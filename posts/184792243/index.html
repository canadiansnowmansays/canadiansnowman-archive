The Iran Crisis

The Carrier

Trump's Military Option Enroute

January 16, 2026

USS Abraham Lincoln is now heading back to the Middle East (Photo by Gabriel R. Piper/U.S. Navy via Getty Images)

The photograph says everything: USS Abraham Lincoln, a 100,000-ton nuclear-powered supercarrier, cutting through blue water with her full air wing visible on deck. The image appeared in Forbes yesterday with a headline that couldn’t be more direct: “U.S. Navy Supercarrier USS Abraham Lincoln Heading To The Middle East.”

Less than a week before Donald Trump’s inauguration anniversary, the United States is positioning overwhelming military capability within striking distance of Iran.

This changes everything I documented in my previous analysis.

The Contradiction

In my recent essay tracking the Iran crisis through mid-January, I noted what appeared to be a quiet de-escalation. After Trump’s January 9 threats and Reza Pahlavi’s calls for U.S. intervention, the situation seemed to stabilize. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi signaled willingness to negotiate. Trump claimed Iran “wants to talk.” The immediate crisis appeared to be cooling.

I was wrong. Or more precisely: the apparent de-escalation was tactical positioning while military capability moved into place.

The USS Abraham Lincoln Carrier Strike Group departed San Diego in late November “with almost no fanfare,” according to Forbes defense correspondent Peter Suciu. The Department of Defense refused to announce where CVN-72 would deploy. She conducted operations in the South China Sea through early January, leaving analysts uncertain whether she would remain in the Indo-Pacific or pivot to the Middle East.

We now have our answer. CVN-72 has been ordered to the Persian Gulf. She will arrive approximately January 22-23, giving Donald Trump immediate military options in his first week as president.

What’s Being Deployed

This is not symbolic. Multiple sources including The New York Times, BBC, Washington Post, and Al Jazeera have confirmed a comprehensive military surge far beyond the carrier alone:

The Abraham Lincoln Carrier Strike Group:

  • Carrier Air Wing 9 operating F/A-18E/F Super Hornets (strike fighters)

  • EA-18G Growlers (electronic warfare/jamming)

  • E-2D Advanced Hawkeyes (airborne early warning)

  • MH-60R/S Seahawks (anti-submarine/search and rescue)

  • USS Spruance (DDG-111), USS Michael Murphy (DDG-112), USS Frank E. Petersen Jr. (DDG-121) - Arleigh Burke-class destroyers with Tomahawk cruise missiles

  • One fast-attack nuclear-powered submarine (identity classified)

  • USS Roosevelt (DDG-80) already operating in Persian/Arabian Gulf

Additional Assets Confirmed by NYT:

  • “An array of warplanes” flowing to region from Europe: fighter jets, attack planes, refueling aircraft

  • Air defense equipment including interceptor missiles for base protection

  • KC-135 Stratotanker refueling aircraft already repositioned from Qatar to Saudi Arabia

Base Evacuations: The United States and United Kingdom have begun withdrawing personnel from al-Udeid Air Base in Qatar, the largest U.S. military installation in the Middle East housing approximately 10,000 troops. Qatar’s government confirmed the move was undertaken “in response to current regional tensions.”

This evacuation carries particular significance: al-Udeid was similarly evacuated before “Operation Midnight Hammer” in June 2025, when the United States struck three Iranian nuclear enrichment sites. Iran retaliated with approximately twelve ballistic missiles targeting al-Udeid. Most were intercepted, but one struck the base causing moderate damage.

The Pentagon is repeating that playbook.

As one defense analyst told multiple outlets: “You don’t reposition a full carrier strike group from the Indo-Pacific for a one-night symbolic operation. This signals readiness for prolonged presence and follow-on capability if required.”

Translation: Trump will have the capability to strike Iran’s nuclear facilities, command and control centers, and IRGC infrastructure very soon.

The Strategic Cost

The deployment reveals how seriously the Pentagon views the Iran crisis. Moving CVN-72 from the South China Sea to the Middle East leaves the United States Navy spread dangerously thin across multiple theaters.

The Indo-Pacific Vulnerability:

  • USS George Washington (CVN-75), the Navy’s only forward-deployed carrier based in Yokosuka, Japan, is sidelined for maintenance until at least summer

  • USS Carl Vinson (CVN-70) returned from deployment in August and won’t be available for several more months

  • USS Theodore Roosevelt (CVN-71) will likely assume eastern Pacific operations from CVN-72

This comes precisely when China operates three aircraft carriers and continues aggressive naval expansion. The Pentagon has explicitly pivoted toward “renewed great-power competition in the Indo-Pacific,” yet Iran’s crisis forces impossible choices.

The Caribbean Gap:

  • USS Gerald R. Ford (CVN-78) remains in the Caribbean following the Venezuela operation

  • Her deployment has been extended to nearly match her 2023-2024 deployment of 239 days at sea

  • USS Dwight D. Eisenhower (CVN-69) or USS George H.W. Bush (CVN-77) expected to relieve her

The Long-Term Strain:

  • USS Nimitz (CVN-68) will make her final journey to Norfolk for decommissioning this spring

  • The future USS John F. Kennedy (CVN-79) won’t be commissioned until March 2027

  • First deployment unlikely until late 2027 or 2028

The Navy cannot maintain this operational tempo indefinitely. Deploying CVN-72 to the Middle East represents a calculated risk that Iran poses a more immediate threat than China - at least for the next several weeks.

The Timing

The carrier will arrive in the Persian Gulf approximately January 22-23.

This is not coincidental. This is deliberate sequencing.

Consider the timeline:

  • June 2025: “Operation Midnight Hammer” - U.S. strikes three Iranian nuclear sites, Iran retaliates with missile attack on al-Udeid

  • Late December 2025: Israel and Iran secretly agree via Russian channel not to strike each other first

  • December 28: Iran’s currency crashes, protests begin

  • January 3: U.S. captures Maduro in Venezuela

  • January 9: Trump threatens Iran, Pahlavi calls for intervention

  • January 10-12: Death toll surges, internet blackout

  • January 14: Al-Udeid evacuation begins, European officials say intervention “likely within 24 hours”

  • Mid-January: Apparent de-escalation, diplomatic signals

  • January 22-23: Carrier strike group in position

The apparent mid-January de-escalation now looks like operational security while military assets transit from the Pacific. The evacuation of al-Udeid—the same base evacuated before June’s strikes—suggests the Pentagon is preparing for Iranian retaliation, not just deterrence posturing.

The Question

Three interpretations remain possible:

1. Genuine Strike Preparation Trump intends to act militarily against Iran’s nuclear program or IRGC leadership while the regime is vulnerable. The carrier provides the capability; the protests provide the pretext; Maduro’s capture demonstrated willingness to act.

Multiple sources suggest timing may be immediate. Reuters reported Wednesday that two European officials said U.S. military intervention in Iran appeared “likely” and could come “within 24 hours.” An Israeli official told Reuters it appeared Trump had decided to intervene, though the scope and timing remained unclear.

However, other reporting suggests deliberate delay. Both The Washington Post and Axios reported that Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu asked Trump to postpone planned strikes, requesting more time to prepare for Iranian retaliation. If accurate, Netanyahu likely wants more U.S. military assets positioned in theater — Israel relied heavily on American forces to intercept Iranian missiles during their June 2025 war, and many still penetrated Israeli defenses.

2. Maximum Pressure Bluff Position overwhelming capability to terrify Iran’s leadership into concessions without intending to strike. The carrier’s presence alone might fracture IRGC unity or force Khamenei to negotiate. Protesters see American backing; the regime sees American missiles.

This interpretation gained credibility when Trump announced Wednesday that “important sources” in Tehran told him the “killing in Iran is stopping” and executions had been postponed. Perhaps the military positioning already achieved its objective.

3. Hedge Strategy Maintain maximum flexibility. Have options ready while leaving diplomatic paths open. Strike if Iran crosses undefined red lines; negotiate if they show weakness; watch if the regime collapses on its own.

Further complicating calculations: The Washington Post reported that in late December — days before protests erupted — Israel and Iran secretly agreed via Russian intermediaries not to launch preemptive strikes against each other. Israel wanted Iran to stay out of its Lebanon operations. Iran responded positively. Whether this private agreement survives the current crisis remains unknown.

I cannot tell you which interpretation is correct. Neither can Trump’s advisors, because Trump himself may not have decided.

What I can tell you: the United States is positioning military capability that could destroy Iran’s nuclear program in a matter of hours. And Iran’s regime knows it.

What This Means

The apparent de-escalation I documented last week was incomplete. While diplomatic signals suggested cooling tensions, the Pentagon was quietly moving lethal capability into position. But so was everyone else.

European officials told Reuters Wednesday that U.S. military intervention appeared “likely” and could come “within 24 hours.” Yet Trump simultaneously claimed Iran had stopped killing protesters and postponed executions. Netanyahu reportedly asked for delays to prepare Israeli defenses. The Washington Post revealed a secret Israel-Iran non-aggression pact brokered by Russia in late December. And through it all, the carrier steams westward.

Whether Trump intends to use this capability, threaten with it, or simply have it available remains unknowable. But the carrier’s deployment transforms every diplomatic exchange from theoretical to immediate. When Iran’s Foreign Minister says Tehran is “ready for war or negotiations,” he’s no longer speaking abstractly. American strike aircraft will be within range. Iran knows what happened at al-Udeid in June 2025 when the United States last struck their nuclear facilities.

The next seven days will reveal whether Operation Midnight Hammer was prologue or warning. The carrier arrives January 22-23.

Then we discover whether this was preparation, deterrence, or bluff.

One way or another, we’re about to find out.
