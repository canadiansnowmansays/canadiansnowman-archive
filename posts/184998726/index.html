The Great Canadian Trade War

Part VII

How Trump Lost Canada - The Numbers Don’t Lie

January 21, 2026

From 64% disapproval to 85% liquor boycott: The math of a broken alliance

By CanadianSnowmanSays | January 21, 2026

In Part VI, I showed you how Mark Carney’s Beijing deal was strategic brilliance—walking into USMCA negotiations with signed alternatives in hand. Today, I’m going to show you the numbers that prove it wasn’t just strategy.

It was survival.

When 64% of your voters hold a negative view of the United States—the highest in 20+ years of polling—and when liquor imports from America drop 85%, you’re not making foreign policy choices. You’re following a mandate written in grocery store aisles and border crossing statistics.

The Great Canadian Trade War just became the Great Canadian Boycott. And the math is absolutely devastating.

The Liquor Massacre: When 85% Means “We’re Done”

Let’s start with the number that captures everything:

US liquor imports to Canada:

  • Late 2024: $63.1 billion

  • Late 2025: $9.5 billion

  • Loss: $53.6 billion (85% collapse)

That’s not a boycott. That’s a national divorce filing.

Breaking it down by spirit category (Q2 2025 vs Q2 2024):

  • Rum exports: -49%

  • Brandy exports: -67%

  • Gin exports: -76%

  • Vodka exports: -71%

  • Overall US spirits to Canada: -85%

The Distilled Spirits Council tried to spin this diplomatically: “The international market is especially critical for U.S. whisky producers, which are facing stagnating domestic sales and record-high inventory levels.”

Translation: Canadian boycott + domestic plateau = bourbon makers are screwed.

Jim Beam literally halted Kentucky production. Brown-Forman’s CEO called the liquor board removals “more damaging than Canada’s retaliatory tariffs.”

When pulling your product from shelves hurts worse than a 25% tariff, you’ve lost more than market access. You’ve lost consumer trust.

The Tourism Collapse: $5.7 Billion in Missing Canadians

Remember when Americans joked about Canada being America’s hat? Canadians just took off the hat and went to Mexico instead.

The raw numbers:

  • Canadian trips to US: -25%+

  • Canadian flight bookings (through September): -70%

  • US tourism industry forecast loss: $5.7 billion

  • Ogdensburg-Prescott Bridge crossings: -37.93% vs 2019

  • Auto crossings at that bridge: -41.21% vs 2019

Where did they go instead?

  • Domestic tourism: Up 6% to record C$59bn (May-August)

  • Mexico trips: +12% surge

The irony is exquisite: Trump threatened to annex Canada, so Canadians started vacationing in Mexico—you know, the other country Trump tried to bully with tariffs.

Goldman Sachs worst-case estimate: Foreign boycotts + tourism decline could cost US economy $28-90 billion in 2025, or 0.1-0.3% of GDP.

For context, that’s roughly equivalent to eliminating the entire state of Vermont’s economic output. Because Trump couldn’t keep his mouth shut about the “51st state.”

The Consumer Uprising: 1.4 Million Facebook Members Can’t Be Wrong

This is where it gets truly unprecedented. Canadians—famously polite, notoriously conflict-averse Canadians—organized a mass consumer movement with the efficiency of a military operation.

The participation rate:

  • 85% of Canadians replacing US products with Canadian alternatives (Angus Reid)

  • 71% plan to buy fewer US products in 2025 (Dunnhumby)

  • 91% want Canada to rely less on US

  • 1.4 million members in “Buy Canadian” Facebook groups

  • Multiple apps created to identify and avoid US products

“Maple Scan” became the #4 downloaded app on Canada’s iPhone App Store. Think about that: Canadians were more interested in boycotting America than whatever game or social media app was trending.

The apps with the most Canadian names ever:

  • “Buy Beaver”

  • “Maple Scan”

  • “O SCANada”

  • “Is This Canadian?”

  • “Check the Label”

If naming your boycott app “Buy Beaver” doesn’t signal you’re seriously committed to avoiding US products, I don’t know what does.

The Grocery Store Revolution

The boycott wasn’t just online virtue signaling. It fundamentally reshaped Canadian retail.

Per Bank, Loblaw Companies CEO:

  • Tariffed US products: Sales down 15-20% on average

  • Some categories: Down 50%

  • Canadian products: Sales up

  • New Canadian suppliers added: 100+ since January

Canadian grocers started labeling products with maple leaves and “Made in Canada” tags. Loblaws put black “T” symbols on tariffed goods—essentially warning labels saying “this came from the country trying to economically destroy us.”

US orange juice imports (September 2025 vs 2024): $7.45M vs $12.6M (-41%)

Some Canadian restaurants stopped serving orange juice entirely rather than buy Florida OJ. That’s not boycotting—that’s culinary scorched earth.

The Automotive Realignment: History Made in July

Here’s a data point that should terrify Detroit:

July 2025: Mexican car imports to Canada exceeded US car imports for the first time in history.

Read that again. Canada—America’s integrated automotive partner since before most of us were born—now imports more cars from Mexico than from the United States.

This isn’t a blip. This is a supply chain realignment happening in real-time.

The Big Three (Ford, GM, Stellantis) begged Trump for USMCA exemptions on their tariffs. He gave them a 30-day reprieve, then slapped them anyway.

Result? Canadian consumers said “fine, we’ll buy Mexican trucks” and rewrote North American automotive trade patterns.

The Polling Apocalypse: When Your Closest Ally Fears You More Than China

Now we get to the numbers that should genuinely terrify American policymakers. These aren’t just economic statistics—they’re existential threats to the relationship.

Pew Research Center (Spring 2025):

  • 64% negative view of US (highest in 20+ years of polling)

  • 34% favorable view (tied with 2020 for lowest since 2002)

  • 77% lack confidence in Trump as president

  • 90% describe Trump as “arrogant”

  • 75% say Trump is “dangerous”

  • 22% have confidence in Trump’s approach to international affairs

Angus Reid Institute (October 2025):

  • 46% want to treat US as “enemy or potential threat”

  • 34% want to treat China as “enemy or potential threat”

  • 24% want to treat India as “enemy or potential threat”

Let that sink in: Canadians now fear America (46%) more than they fear China (34%).

China—with ongoing allegations of foreign interference, hostage diplomacy, and attempted political manipulation—is seen as less threatening than the United States.

Additional Pew finding: 59% of Canadians name the US as their country’s top threat.

That’s not poll

ing error. That’s a relationship in ruins.

Leger Poll (January 2026): 1 in 3 Canadians believe the US could take “direct action” to control Canada in the future.

When a third of your neighbor’s population genuinely fears you might invade, you’ve moved beyond trade disputes into something far darker.

The Electoral Earthquake: Trump Saved the Liberals

Remember where we were a year ago?

Before Trump’s second term:

  • Liberal Party: Facing “potentially historic collapse”

  • Conservatives: Leading by wide margins

  • Pierre Poilievre: Likely next PM

  • Focus: Affordability, inflation

After Trump’s tariff war:

  • Mark Carney: Won near-majority (April 2025)

  • Liberals: “Unthinkable” return to power

  • Poilievre: Lost 4th consecutive election, facing leadership review

  • Focus: Standing up to Trump

The progression is stunning:

  1. Trudeau resigns (January), hastened by finance minister quitting over Trump handling

  2. Carney campaigns as the man who can stand up to Trump

  3. Voters “anxious about Canada’s sovereignty and economy” flock to Liberals

  4. Carney achieves what was “unthinkable just months prior”

Current polling: Carney’s Liberals a few points ahead of Conservatives, though gap narrowing.

Trump didn’t just fail to destroy the Liberal Party. He saved them by being exactly who he is.

The Conservative “elbows down” criticism of Carney? Voters rejected it. They wanted someone who’d negotiate smartly, not posture performatively.

Poilievre ran soft on Trump. Carney ran against him. Canadians chose accordingly.

The “Buy Canadian” Mandate

This isn’t just consumer preference—it’s becoming government policy.

Federal level:

  • Carney prioritizing Canadian suppliers in procurement

  • “Buy Canadian” extending to defense procurement

  • Shifting away from US suppliers as NATO spending ramps up

Provincial responses:

  • Ontario: Pulled US alcohol from LCBO shelves

  • Alberta: “Substantial” ad campaign to identify Canadian products

  • Nova Scotia: Pulled all US alcohol

  • BC: Requiring Canadian-made biofuels instead of US

  • Quebec: Multiple “Buy Quebec” initiatives

This isn’t retaliation. This is institutionalization of the boycott.

When “Buy Canadian” becomes formal procurement policy rather than just consumer sentiment, you’ve crossed from temporary to structural change.

The Global Context: It’s Not Just Canada

European boycotts (from Wikipedia sources):

  • Denmark: ~50% deliberately avoiding US products

  • Sweden: “Boycott USA” Facebook group: 80,000 members

  • Denmark: “Boycott USA” Facebook group: 90,000 members

  • France: “BOYCOTT USA” Facebook group: 30,000 members

  • Reddit r/BuyFromEU: 200,000 members (April 2025)

  • European Central Bank survey: 44% of Europeans turning away from US brands

Tesla as bellwether:

  • Europe sales: -28% (some estimates -45%)

  • January 2025: 9,913 units vs 18,121 in January 2024

  • Volkswagen surpassed Tesla as top EV seller in Europe

When a brand this closely associated with Trump gets hammered this hard in its second-largest market, that’s not market forces—that’s political backlash hitting the bottom line.

European travel to US: -17% overall, with Ireland, Germany, Norway exceeding -20%

Canadian summer flight bookings to US: -70% through September

Goldman Sachs wasn’t exaggerating: This is a global phenomenon, and Canada is just the most dramatic example.

Why Beijing Makes Perfect Sense Now

Return to Part VI with this context. When Carney stood in Beijing calling China “more predictable” than the United States, he wasn’t making a foreign policy choice in a vacuum.

He was responding to these numbers:

  • 91% want less US reliance

  • 64% negative US view

  • 46% see US as enemy/threat

  • 85% replacing US products

  • 71% buying fewer US goods

The China deal wasn’t Carney being opportunistic. It was Carney being responsive to the clearest political mandate a Canadian PM has received in decades.

When your voters are literally downloading apps to avoid American products, signing a deal to diversify away from the US isn’t bold—it’s mandatory.

“We take the world as it is, not as we wish it to be” wasn’t cynicism. It was acknowledging that Canadian voters have already taken the world as it is and decided America is unreliable.

The USMCA Math Just Changed

Now fast-forward to the July 1 USMCA review with this context:

What US negotiators think they have:

  • Geographic proximity leverage

  • Economic dependence (76% of exports)

  • No viable alternatives

What they actually face:

  • A population where 64% hate America

  • Consumers who’ve already reduced purchases 71%

  • An 85% liquor boycott that shows sustained commitment

  • A PM who won election by standing up to Trump

  • A signed China deal providing alternative markets

  • 91% public support for US de-coupling

Every US demand—dairy access, digital tax, supply management, Buy American carve-outs—runs into the political reality that Carney can’t be seen caving to a country 64% of Canadians view negatively.

The boycott numbers give Carney the ultimate negotiating shield: “I’d love to compromise, but my voters will end me if I do.”

Trump created the political environment where reasonable Canadian compromise became electoral suicide.

The Counterargument: Did It Actually Hurt the US?

Trevor Tombe (University of Calgary economist) makes an important point: Government tariffs had measurable impact, but consumer boycotts on non-tariffed goods showed limited evidence in trade data through April 2025.

His finding: Tariffed goods imports -43% vs non-tariffed goods -8% (April 2025)

This suggests government actions (pulling liquor, tariffs) drove most impact, not pure consumer choice on available goods.

However, this misses several points:

  1. Liquor specifically: -85% IS the consumer boycott—implemented via provincial government response to consumer demand

  2. Tourism: -25% is pure consumer choice, costing $5.7bn

  3. Cars: Mexican>US shift (July) happened AFTER his April data

  4. Lag effects: Consumer behavior changes take time to show in import data

  5. Goldman Sachs disagrees: Projects $28-90bn total impact

The real question isn’t “did boycotts hurt the US economy?” It’s “did they change Canadian behavior permanently?”

And on that question, the answer is unequivocally yes.

What This Means Going Forward

Three scenarios for how this plays out:

Scenario 1: The Surface Reconciliation (40%)

  • USMCA extended with minor modifications

  • Some tariffs dropped, others remain

  • Relationship officially “repaired”

  • But consumer trust permanently damaged

  • Diversification continues quietly

Scenario 2: The New Normal (45%)

  • USMCA extended but with clear constraints

  • Canadian diversification accelerates

  • “Buy Canadian” becomes permanent policy

  • 60-65% US export share becomes new baseline (down from 76%)

  • Multiple China/Europe/India deals signed

Scenario 3: The Break (15%)

  • USMCA review fails

  • Sunset countdown begins

  • Rapid diversification to Asia/Europe

  • North American integration unravels over 10 years

  • Historic realignment

My money’s on Scenario 2. The numbers show a permanent shift in Canadian sentiment that no trade deal can fully repair.

You can extend USMCA. You can drop tariffs. You can even get Carney and Trump smiling for cameras.

But you can’t undo 85% liquor boycotts, -70% flight bookings, and 64% negative polling with a press conference.

The Bottom Line

In Part VI, I argued Carney’s Beijing deal was strategic brilliance. Now you see why it was more than that—it was responding to the clearest democratic mandate a Canadian PM has received in modern history.

The mandate, written in data:

  • Stop relying on America (91%)

  • Buy less from America (71%)

  • Treat America as a threat (46%)

  • Find alternatives (China deal approval)

Trump didn’t lose a trade war. He lost a country.

An 85% liquor boycott isn’t economic policy—it’s a relationship ending. When Canadians would rather go to Mexico than Miami, pay more for Canadian orange juice than buy Florida’s, and download apps specifically to avoid your products, you’ve crossed from trade partner to pariah.

The Great Canadian Trade War became the Great Canadian Boycott, which became the Great Canadian Divorce.

And divorces, unlike trade deals, are very hard to undo.

The math always wins. And right now, the math is saying that when you threaten to annex your closest ally, impose arbitrary tariffs, terminate negotiations over accurate Reagan quotes, and generally treat your neighbor like a hostile power, they start acting like you’re a hostile power.

The numbers don’t lie: Trump lost Canada.

And with USMCA review in 5.5 months, he’s negotiating with an ex who’s already dating someone else.
