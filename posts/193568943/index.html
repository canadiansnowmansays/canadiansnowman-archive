Formosa: The Beautiful Problem

The Speech in Nanjing

Part I: In which an opposition leader visits the mainland and tells Beijing exactly what it wants to hear

April 28, 2026

Kuomintang leader Cheng Li-wun lays a floral wreath at the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum in Nanjing, Jiangsu province, on Wednesday. Photo: AP

On a warm Wednesday morning in early April 2026, Cheng Li-wun, chairwoman of Taiwan’s Kuomintang party, the opposition party, stood before the mausoleum of Sun Yat-sen in Nanjing and laid a floral wreath at the foot of a statue of the founder of modern China. She then delivered a speech. In it, she blamed Japanese “imperialist forces” for the division of mainland China and Taiwan, arguing that Taiwan’s separation from the motherland was a wound inflicted by imperial aggression in 1895, not a product of civil war, ideology, or the inconvenient question of what Taiwanese people might actually want.

It was a masterpiece of managed ambiguity. And it tells you almost everything you need to know about why this island — thirty-six thousand square kilometres of mountains, microchips, and unresolved legal status — remains the most dangerous piece of real estate on earth.

Welcome to Formosa: The Beautiful Problem.

Let us begin with what actually happened in Nanjing, because the staging matters as much as the words.

Cheng’s visit did not occur spontaneously. Chinese President Xi Jinping personally invited her, and the timing of that invitation was not coincidental. It fell in April 2026, weeks before a planned summit between Xi and Donald Trump — a summit at which Taiwan’s fate, though it will not be placed formally on the agenda, will hover over every exchange like smoke over a brush fire. Xi wanted a Taiwanese opposition leader on mainland soil, at the most symbolically charged tomb in the Chinese nationalist canon, delivering a speech that validated Beijing’s preferred historical narrative. He got exactly that.

The KMT obliged because the KMT has its own reasons. Taiwan’s domestic politics are a permanent argument about identity that has never been resolved, and the Kuomintang — the Nationalist Party that fled to Taiwan in 1949 after losing the civil war to Mao’s Communists — has always occupied an awkward position in that argument. It is the party that brought the Republic of China government to Taiwan, that imposed martial law for thirty-eight years, that spent decades insisting it was the legitimate government of all of China and would one day reconquer the mainland. Over time, reconquest became untenable and the claim quietly shrank, but the KMT never fully made peace with Taiwanese independence. It remains the party of the “1992 Consensus” — the understanding, reached between KMT and Communist representatives, that Taiwan and mainland China are both parts of “one China,” with each side free to define what that China actually means.

The choreography escalated. Two days after the Nanjing wreath-laying, Cheng met Xi Jinping himself in Beijing — a formal reception, red carpet, gilded backdrop, matching blue suits, the full visual weight of Chinese state protocol. They shook hands. The image circulated immediately. It is the most politically consequential photograph taken in the Taiwan Strait context in years — not because of what was said in the meeting, but because of what the meeting itself communicates. A Taiwanese opposition leader, on mainland soil, receiving the personal endorsement of the man who has stated he will never renounce the use of force against Taiwan. In Beijing’s image architecture, there are no casual handshakes. Every frame is an argument. This one argues that the KMT can be worked with, that cross-strait accommodation is available, and that Taiwan’s current government — not Beijing — is the obstacle to peace.

It will be in every political attack advertisement in Taiwan’s 2028 presidential election. Cheng knows this. She went anyway.

KMT Chairwoman Cheng Li-wun meets Chinese President Xi Jinping at the Great Hall of the People, Beijing, April 10, 2026 — the first official meeting between the sitting heads of the Chinese Communist Party and the KMT in almost a decade. Hours before the meeting, Taiwan’s President Lai Ching-te said on social media that “any compromise with an authoritarian regime would damage Taiwan’s sovereignty.” Xi told Cheng that Taiwan’s “development prospects hinge on a strong motherland.” Both leaders stated their opposition to Taiwan independence. Both leaders opposed “foreign meddling” in Taiwan-China relations — a reference to the United States. Photo: Xie Huanchi / Xinhua / AP

The DPP, the Democratic Progressive Party currently governing Taiwan under President Lai Ching-te, rejects the 1992 Consensus entirely. For the DPP, Taiwan is Taiwan — not a province, not a wayward member of a civilizational family, not a piece of unfinished business from a civil war that ended three-quarters of a century ago. For the DPP, Cheng’s speech in Nanjing was not a nuanced historical observation. It was a gift to Beijing, wrapped in the language of shared grievance.

They are not wrong. When Cheng blamed Japan for the division, she was deploying a framing that carefully sidesteps the civil war — and therefore sidesteps the Communist victory, and therefore sidesteps the question of the CCP’s legitimacy, and therefore makes reunification sound less like conquest and more like healing. It is surgically convenient. Beijing has been making this argument for years. Having a Taiwanese politician make it, in Nanjing, in front of cameras, is worth considerably more than any editorial in the People’s Daily.

KMT officials and military officers pose before the Republic of China flag and a portrait of Sun Yat-sen, c. 1945. The Nationalist Party’s founding mythology — Sun’s revolution, China’s republican destiny — is the same iconography Kuomintang chairwoman Cheng Li-wun invoked in Nanjing eighty years later.

None of this, it must be said, is historically false. Japan did seize Taiwan in 1895, following China’s defeat in the First Sino-Japanese War and the humiliating Treaty of Shimonoseki, in which a weakened Qing dynasty ceded the island to Tokyo in perpetuity. Japan ruled Taiwan as a colony for fifty years — longer than it occupied Korea, longer than it held Manchuria, longer than most of its imperial adventures. During that half-century, Taiwanese intellectuals did watch the 1911 revolution overthrow the Qing dynasty with genuine excitement, hoping that a revitalised China would eventually come to liberate them from colonial rule. That hope was largely disappointed, as hopes for liberation during the age of imperialism had a habit of being.

So Cheng is not fabricating history. She is selecting it — selecting the parts that serve a particular political purpose while declining to mention the parts that complicate it. The parts she declines to mention include the massacre of as many as thirty thousand Taiwanese civilians by Nationalist forces in February 1947, an event known as the 228 Incident, which remains the foundational trauma of Taiwanese democratic identity and the primary reason so many Taiwanese people find the idea of reunification with any version of mainland China so viscerally unwelcome. She also declines to mention that it was not Japan but the Nationalist government she represents’s historical predecessors who imposed martial law on Taiwan from 1949 to 1987 — thirty-eight years during which Taiwanese political dissidents were imprisoned, tortured, and killed.

The KMT’s relationship with Taiwan’s own population is, to put it gently, complicated. But complication is the enemy of choreography, and Nanjing was choreography.

There is a Canadian in this story. There usually is, somewhere near the edges of the Pacific’s defining moments, unnoticed by everyone including Canadians.

Kenneth Kirkwood, Hugh Keenleyside, Herbert Meredith Marler, and J.A. Langley — the complete founding staff of Canada’s first legation in Japan — Tokyo, 1929. Marler (centre, in full diplomatic court dress) funded the legation’s official residence from his personal fortune. Keenleyside (second from left), thirty years old and seven months into his diplomatic career, would spend seven years watching Japan’s militarism transform from political tendency to consuming ideology. Source: Library and Archives Canada / Bibliothèque et Archives Canada, accession 3329722

In May 1929, a thirty-year-old diplomat named Hugh Keenleyside arrived in Tokyo with instructions to open Canada’s first-ever legation in Japan — the third foreign mission Canada had established anywhere in the world. On Dominion Day of that year, Keenleyside raised the Canadian ensign on Asian soil for the first time in history. He was Canada’s first chargé d’affaires in Tokyo, and he would remain at the legation as First Secretary until 1936, watching Japan’s militarism transform from political tendency to consuming ideology, watching the invasion of Manchuria in 1931, watching the Pacific order that European imperial powers had assembled over the previous century begin to come apart at every seam.

Keenleyside came home and spent the war years doing two things simultaneously: serving on the Permanent Joint Board on Defence that bound Canada to the United States, and fighting, largely alone within government, against the internment of Japanese Canadians — the very people whose homeland he had lived in, whose culture he understood better than almost any other Canadian official, and whose Canadian citizenship his government was systematically suspending. He lost that fight. He never stopped fighting it.

He was also, in the war’s darkest weeks of May 1940 — as Dunkirk unfolded and France collapsed — the man Prime Minister Mackenzie King sent to Washington to meet with Secretary of State Cordell Hull and President Roosevelt himself, carrying Churchill’s urgent appeal for aid and returning with Roosevelt’s message about the British fleet. The same man who had raised Canada’s flag in Tokyo, who had watched the Pacific catch fire in slow motion, was threading the needle between the three leaders who would eventually win the war. He saw the catastrophe coming from both ends of the globe. He understood, better than almost any Canadian alive, what imperial ambition looked like when it was still politely denying its own intentions.

He is the reason Canada has a thread running through this series. Not because Canada is central to the Taiwan question — it is not — but because Keenleyside embodies something Canada has never quite resolved: the gap between what this country understands about Asia, accumulated through a century of proximity and engagement, and what it is willing to say out loud when the costs of speaking become real. That gap is not abstract. It has consequences. It is having them now.

Which brings us to the present configuration of the problem, which is genuinely extraordinary.

Taiwan currently exists in a condition that has no precise parallel in international law or diplomatic history. It governs itself. It has a military, a currency, a passport, elected officials, a constitutional order, and twenty-three million people who participate in one of Asia’s most vibrant democracies. It is not formally recognised as a sovereign state by most of the world’s governments, including Canada, because most of the world’s governments maintain diplomatic relations with the People’s Republic of China, which insists that Taiwan is a province of China and that recognising it as anything else is an act of hostility.

The United States, Taiwan’s principal security partner, does not formally recognise Taiwan either. What it has instead is the Taiwan Relations Act of 1979, which commits Washington to providing Taiwan with defensive arms and to maintaining the capacity to resist any resort to force that would determine Taiwan’s future against the wishes of its people. It is a commitment worded carefully enough that every administration since Carter’s has been able to interpret it to its own taste, and the current administration is no exception.

Donald Trump’s approach to Taiwan is, like his approach to most things, transactional in method and opaque in intent. He has demanded that Taiwan raise its defence spending to ten percent of GDP — a figure that would require Taipei to spend more on its military than on everything else its government does combined, a demand so disconnected from fiscal reality that it functions less as policy than as leverage. He extracted a trade deal in which Taiwan committed five hundred billion dollars in high-technology investment in the United States in exchange for a tariff rate of fifteen percent rather than the thirty-two percent he initially threatened. He paused four hundred million dollars in military aid to Taiwan during trade negotiations with China without apparent compunction. He told an interviewer that Xi “considers Taiwan to be part of China, and that’s up to him, what he’s going to do.”

“That’s up to him.” Five words that contain, if you listen carefully, the sound of strategic ambiguity cashing its last cheque.

The National Defence Strategy released in January 2026 outlines a “denial defence along the First Island Chain” — the arc of islands from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines that forms the western boundary of American power projection in the Pacific. It is a strategy document in which Taiwan’s name does not appear. The island that anchors the entire strategic architecture is too politically sensitive to mention in the document describing that architecture. This is either a masterwork of executive discretion or a signal so unambiguous that only diplomats could pretend to misread it. Possibly both.

The speech in Nanjing, then, is not merely a colourful anecdote from the margins of international politics. It is a data point in a rapidly clarifying picture.

Beijing is preparing the ground. Xi has invited the Taiwanese opposition leader who endorses the “one China” framework to visit the mainland weeks before his summit with Trump. He is demonstrating that the KMT can be worked with, that cross-strait dialogue is possible on Beijing’s terms, and that Taipei’s current government is the obstacle rather than the problem itself. He is separating Taiwan’s population from its government in the international imagination, which is precisely what you do before you make a move you want the world to interpret as reluctant.

The Trump administration is preoccupied with a trade deal, an Iran situation, a Greenland fixation, and the daily emergencies of governing while dismantling governance. Taiwan is a variable in a negotiation, not a democracy worth defending on its own terms.

And in Nanjing, a Taiwanese politician is explaining that Japan, not the Communist Party, not the Nationalist Party, not eighty years of strategic ambiguity, is responsible for the division that must now — the implication hangs in the air, unsaid — finally be resolved.

One hundred and thirty-one years after the Treaty of Shimonoseki. Forty-seven years after the Taiwan Relations Act. Twenty-nine years after Taiwan’s first direct presidential election. The beautiful problem remains beautiful, and remains a problem, and is becoming more urgent than at any point since 1949.

This series will try to explain why. We will go back a great deal further than 1895 — back to the Dutch, to Koxinga, to the Qing annexation, to the first unequal treaties, to all the layers of claim and counter-claim that have been deposited over four centuries on this island that has never, not once in recorded history, enjoyed an uncontested status. We will make the strongest honest case for independence. We will make the strongest honest case for reunification. We will examine the silicon shield, the Trump auction, the Canadian silence, and what it might mean if the most dangerous unresolved territorial dispute in the world resolves itself on Beijing’s timeline rather than anyone else’s.

We will also keep returning to Hugh Keenleyside, standing in Tokyo in the summer of 1929, watching the Pacific catch fire in slow motion, and doing his best to make his government understand what was coming.

He was right. His government was not listening.

Some things do not change.
