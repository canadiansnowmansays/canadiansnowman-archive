SCOTUS Strikes Down Trump’s Tariff Empire

The Boy Who Cried Emergency Meets His Reckoning — Now What?

February 20, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays Feb 20, 2026

Back in November, we ended our satirical preview of Supreme Court arguments with three words: Your move, Constitution.

The Constitution moved.

In a 6-3 decision issued this morning, the Supreme Court ruled that Donald Trump cannot use the International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 to impose sweeping tariffs on imports from nearly every U.S. trading partner. Writing for the majority, Chief Justice John Roberts delivered the kind of sentence that will be quoted in constitutional law courses for decades: “Those words cannot bear such weight.”

That’s the juridical equivalent of a judge looking at a defendant and saying: nice try.

For readers who’ve followed this series since the early “Thursdays” days — through the August pariah-state analysis, the October twin-crisis update, and the November children’s guide to constitutional arson — this ruling is the payoff we saw coming from a long way out. We predicted 60-80% odds Trump would lose. He lost. Now the hard part begins.

Bottom Line Up Front: The Supreme Court’s 6-3 ruling strikes down the legal foundation for approximately 80% of Trump’s tariffs, vindicating every court that ruled against him below. But this is not the end of the trade war — it’s a transformation. Trump retains substantial authority under Section 232 and Section 301, his team has been preparing alternatives for months, and over $200 billion in collected tariff revenue now hangs in legal limbo. The ruling creates clarity on constitutional law while plunging trade and refund policy into new chaos.

What the Court Actually Ruled

The rule of law holds: Chief Justice John Roberts rejects the claim of unlimited tariff power from two words in a 1977 statute

The majority opinion, authored by Roberts and joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, Jackson, Barrett, and Gorsuch, rested on a straightforward reading of the statute’s text and the Constitution’s structure.

IEEPA gives the president authority to “regulate” the “importation” of foreign property during a declared national emergency. The Trump administration argued those two words — separated by sixteen others in the statute — granted the president the independent power to impose tariffs of any amount, on any country, for any duration. Roberts rejected this with surgical precision: “The president asserts the extraordinary power to unilaterally impose tariffs of unlimited amount, duration, and scope. In light of the breadth, history, and constitutional context of that asserted authority, he must identify clear congressional authorization to exercise it.”

He could not. The statute doesn’t mention tariffs, taxes, or duties anywhere.

The majority applied the Major Questions Doctrine — which we flagged in our October analysis as the administration’s most dangerous legal exposure — requiring clear congressional authorization before the executive can claim authority over major economic questions. Starting the biggest trade war since the Great Depression, the Court effectively agreed, qualifies as a major question.

The ruling affirmed what three lower courts had already concluded, including the Federal Circuit’s 7-4 decision from August that we covered in Part 1 of our October update. The constitutional quicksand we identified back then has now fully swallowed the policy built upon it.

The Dissenters: Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh

The 6-3 split is notable for who landed where. Justices Clarence Thomas, Samuel Alito, and Brett Kavanaugh dissented — the last of whom Trump appointed. This is not the unified conservative bloc many assumed would protect a Republican president’s executive power claims.

The dissenters’ reasoning, while not yet fully available, appears to accept the administration’s broad reading of presidential emergency authority. That three justices were prepared to grant one man unlimited tariff power — based on two words in a 1977 sanctions statute — is itself a remarkable and troubling fact worth recording.

But the majority held. The rule of law, on this day, held.

What Survives: The Tariff Empire Doesn’t Disappear

Here is where analysis must discipline the celebration. This ruling does not end the trade war. It ends this version of the trade war.

As we explained in painstaking detail in our October updates, the IEEPA tariffs were never the whole picture. What survives the ruling:

Section 232 “National Security” tariffs — the 25% on steel, 25% on aluminum, 50% on copper, and 25% on automobiles — are entirely unaffected. These require a Commerce Department investigation and formal findings, which were completed. They stand.

Section 301 tariffs on China — covering approximately $370 billion in Chinese goods at rates of 7.5-25%, originally imposed during Trump’s first term and retained by Biden — also survive. These are the tariffs that existed before the IEEPA escalation and they remain in full force.

What disappears are the sweeping IEEPA-based “reciprocal tariffs” — the 10% baseline plus country-specific rates up to 50% — and the “fentanyl/trafficking tariffs” on Canada, Mexico, and China that brought total Chinese rates to 55% or higher. For most countries beyond China, this is dramatic: the entire framework of “reciprocal tariffs” collapses.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent telegraphed the pivot months ago at a New York Times conference, noting the administration could redeploy tariffs using Section 301, Section 122, and Section 232 authorities. None of those provide the instant, unlimited, emergency-declaration-by-tweet power Trump wielded under IEEPA — they require investigations, formal procedures, and in some cases congressional reauthorization. But they are real authorities that survive today’s ruling.

Kevin Hassett, Trump’s National Economic Council director, said this morning the administration had a “backup plan that’s just as good.” Whether that’s true or wishful thinking will determine the next chapter.

Rule of law vindicated: The Constitution’s text over creative emergency readings.

The $200 Billion Refund Question

This is where the ruling creates its own chaos.

U.S. Customs and Border Protection collected more than $250 billion in customs duties in 2025, with roughly half — over $125 billion — coming from IEEPA tariffs now ruled unlawful. Companies like Costco and Bumble Bee Foods had already begun filing claims in anticipation of today’s ruling. A coalition of over 800 small businesses called We Pay the Tariffs issued a statement within minutes of the decision demanding “a fast, efficient, and automatic refund process.”

Here is the brutal reality: refunds are not automatic. The Court offered no instruction on refunds in today’s ruling. Every importer seeking its money back must sue individually through the U.S. Court of International Trade, which will manage the process. For small businesses that lack legal departments and litigation budgets, this is cold comfort.

The Trump administration will almost certainly not establish a voluntary refund process. It has already warned that tariff refunds could damage the nation’s fiscal health. With over $200 billion at stake, expect years of litigation — and expect the administration to delay, complicate, and contest every dollar.

The irony is exquisite: the same administration that demanded companies “eat the tariffs” will now ask courts to let it keep the money it collected illegally.

Trump’s Response: Defiance, Delegation, and Denial

The press conference that followed the ruling was, in its own way, as historically significant as the ruling itself.

Trump called the decision “deeply disappointing” and said he was “ashamed of certain members of the court, absolutely ashamed for not having the courage to do what’s right for our country.” He called the majority justices “very unpatriotic and disloyal to our Constitution,” a “disgrace to our nation,” and said the ruling was “an embarrassment to their families.” He called them “lap dogs for RHINOs and the radical left” and “an automatic no.”

He praised the three dissenters — Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh — as having “strength and wisdom and love of our country,” calling Kavanaugh a “genius” and saying he was “so proud” of the appointment. The framing was entirely about personal loyalty: justices who ruled against him were disloyal and unpatriotic; justices who ruled for him demonstrated love of country.

He accused the majority of being “swayed by foreign interests” and claimed unnamed “slimeballs” and “sleazeballs” from other countries had influenced the decision. When pressed to name them, he couldn’t.

He tied the ruling to voter fraud. Unprompted.

This is not a president processing legal defeat and planning a measured response. This is a president who believes the Supreme Court owes him loyalty, that judicial independence is a form of betrayal, and that any ruling against him must be explained by corruption or cowardice rather than law.

Notably: his written-down, prepared statement — not impulsive remarks — called the justices a “disgrace to the nation.” These are the official, considered positions of the administration.

The Pivot: Section 122, Section 301, Section 232

On the substance, Trump announced he would sign an executive order the same day imposing a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — a provision no president has ever invoked. He said it would take effect within approximately three days.

Section 122 carries explicit constraints IEEPA did not: tariffs under this authority are limited to 150 days unless Congress votes to extend them. With midterm elections approaching and Republican support for tariffs already fracturing — only six House Republicans voted last week to rescind Canada tariffs — congressional extension faces serious headwinds.

He also announced Section 301 investigations into unspecified countries’ unfair trade practices, which could yield additional tariffs after the required investigation period. And he confirmed all existing Section 232 “national security” tariffs — steel, aluminum, copper, automobiles — remain in force.

Kavanaugh’s dissent, which Trump praised enthusiastically, helpfully catalogued the alternative authorities available to the administration. Trump’s team had clearly read it before the press conference ended.

The critical caveat: Section 122’s 150-day clock starts immediately. If Congress won’t extend — and the political environment suggests it won’t — the administration faces another cliff in roughly five months. Section 301 investigations take 7-12 months. Section 232 expansions require Commerce Department findings within 270 days. None of these provide the instant, unlimited, emergency-declaration-by-tweet authority IEEPA provided.

The Refund Question: Answered

Trump was asked directly whether he planned to honor refunds to companies that paid illegal tariffs. His answer: “I guess it has to get litigated for the next two years. So they write this terrible, defective decision, totally defective. It’s almost like not written by smart people... We’ll end up being in court for the next five years.”

Translation: no voluntary refunds. Every dollar of the $120-200 billion in potentially refundable duties will be contested individually in court. The businesses waiting for relief should plan accordingly.

This too fits the pattern documented in this series since August: collect the money, claim legitimacy, force everyone else to spend years and legal fees recovering what was taken.

The Spin: Turning Defeat Into Victory

As Trump spoke at the podium, his Truth Social account posted: “The Court has given me the unquestioned right to ban all sorts of things from coming into our Country, a much more powerful Right than many people thought we had.”

The Supreme Court struck down 80% of his tariffs. He is claiming this as expanded authority.

The markets, at least, weren’t convinced. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq rose slightly on Friday — investors read the pivot to Section 122 as a significant downgrade of tariff pressure, not an equivalent replacement. The empire of constitutional quicksand has been replaced with something considerably more constrained.

One final detail worth recording: Trump has a China trip scheduled in approximately five weeks. He goes with substantially less tariff leverage than he had this morning. That meeting just got considerably more complicated.

Saturday Morning Update: The Ceiling, Immediately

Less than 24 hours after announcing a 10% global tariff under Section 122, Trump posted on Truth Social Saturday morning that he was raising it immediately to 15% — the statutory maximum the law allows. He said the administration would “determine and issue the new and legally permissible Tariffs” in the coming months, signaling Section 301 and Section 232 expansions are actively in preparation.

The speed tells you everything about the governing philosophy. The Supreme Court ruled Friday that he exceeded his authority. By Saturday morning he had maxed out his replacement authority’s ceiling via social media post, skipping the press conference, the executive order review process, and the 24-hour cooling period he had promised.

One detail worth recording: for some countries, the new 15% Section 122 rate is actually higher than the IEEPA “reciprocal” rates the Court just struck down. He lost in court and some trading partners are paying more than before the ruling.

A further casualty: Indonesia had signed a trade deal with the United States on Thursday — zero tariffs on American goods in exchange for a 19% rate on its exports. One day later that framework was invalidated by the ruling. One day after that, replaced with a flat 15% regardless of the agreed terms. The credibility cost of this trade war extends far beyond Canada. Every government that negotiated concessions based on IEEPA tariffs now has legitimate reason to question whether the deal they made means anything.

From fentanyl tariffs to national security claims — the pain at the crossing continues

A Brief Word on Canada

Canada’s business community is not celebrating. Canadian Chamber of Commerce president Candace Laing put it precisely: “The Supreme Court’s decision to strike down the use of IEEPA tariff powers is a legal ruling, not a reset of U.S. trade policy. Canada should prepare for new, blunter mechanisms to be used to reassert trade pressure, potentially with broader and more disruptive effects.”

That’s the sober Canadian read. The IEEPA tariffs that targeted Canada are struck down. But the impulse that produced them — and the administration wielding it — hasn’t changed. We’ll examine the full implications for the Canada-U.S. relationship in the Great Canadian Trade War series.

What This Series Got Right

This is not a moment for excessive self-congratulation — the damage to American businesses, families, and relationships has been real and the correct prediction provides no comfort to those who paid the price. But the record is worth acknowledging.

Since August 2025, this series documented:

  • The constitutional fragility of IEEPA tariff authority, calling 60-80% odds of a Trump loss

  • The Major Questions Doctrine as the administration’s critical legal vulnerability

  • The distinction between IEEPA tariffs (struck down today) and Section 301/232 authorities (which survive)

  • The $200+ billion refund exposure that would follow an adverse ruling

  • The alternative authorities Trump would deploy post-ruling

Every element of that analysis has been vindicated today. The trade war built on constitutional quicksand has now sunk — at least in its current form.

Conclusion: A Reckoning, Not an Ending

The Supreme Court has done what the rule of law requires: it held that even a president invoking emergency powers must find those powers in actual statutory text, not in creative readings of two words separated by sixteen others. This is how the constitutional system is supposed to work, and today it worked.

But the trade war is not over. It is transforming. The administration retains real tariff authorities, has a prepared pivot strategy, and shows no sign of moderating its protectionist ambitions. The $200 billion refund fight will consume courts and businesses for years. China’s rare earth controls — imposed in October as mirror retaliation for U.S. semiconductor warfare — remain fully in effect regardless of today’s ruling. The economic damage already inflicted on American families, businesses, and international relationships does not reverse with a court opinion.

The boy who cried emergency has been told by the Constitution’s highest interpreters that he cried once too often. What he does next will determine whether this becomes a genuine reckoning or merely a temporary obstacle on the road to further constitutional crisis.

We’ll be watching.
