The Iran Crisis

The World on Day Two

Part XXI: While Iran Burns

March 1, 2026

Part XX documented what was happening inside Iran on the second day of the war: the confirmed death of the supreme leader, the survival of the president, the IRGC calculating in the dark, the Delcy of Iran running an interim council, two Tehrans existing simultaneously.

This essay documents what was happening everywhere else.

The answer is: the world Iran built against itself in 36 hours of retaliation. The safe havens shattered. The geopolitical alignment hardening. A president at a fundraiser. A warning from history. And a Voice of America that wasn’t ready for the moment its entire existence was built to serve.

Dubai: The Safe Haven Is Gone

Dramatic view of dense smoke rising against illuminated luxury buildings in Dubai’s Palm area following a strike on or near the Fairmont The Palm hotel (March 2026; via WSJ).

For decades, Dubai was the one place in the Middle East where everything worked. Iranian businessmen, American executives, Russian oligarchs, global tourists — all coexisted in a city that had made stability its brand and profited enormously from the arrangement.

That image is gone. It will not return quickly.

Iran targeted the UAE with more than 200 drones and 137 ballistic missiles on Day One and Two. Five-star hotels caught fire. Explosions rattled apartment towers. Dubai International Airport was damaged, injuring seven people. Jebel Ali port — one of the world’s busiest — was struck. Social media influencers and terrified migrant workers posted identical videos of fiery projectiles streaking past the Burj Khalifa.

Fourteen drones reached Emirati territory and waters despite interceptions. Debris from missiles that were successfully shot down rained across the city, causing further damage and injuries. One “Asian national” was killed when drone debris struck the Abu Dhabi International Airport. Debris also struck the facade of Etihad Towers — the complex where the Israeli embassy is located — injuring a woman and her child.

Cinzia Bianco of the European Council on Foreign Relations: “It is really a tough and serious moment. There was nothing that they valued more than that perception of safety — and they prided themselves on maneuvering politically and hedging to be basically on the good books of everyone.”

A yacht sails past thick black smoke rising from Jebel Ali port in southern Dubai after debris from an intercepted Iranian missile ignited a fire at one berth (March 1, 2026). Source: Fadel Senna / AFP via Getty Images

Dubai had cultivated close ties with Iran deliberately, partly to maintain its safe haven status. The Emirates is one of Iran’s largest trading partners. None of it provided protection. Iran targeted everyone who was trying to stay out of the war.

Oman — the country that tried hardest to prevent this war, whose foreign minister said a breakthrough was “within reach” 48 hours before the bombs fell, whose diplomats had spent months mediating — was attacked. A drone hit housing at the Duqm commercial port. Badr Albusaidi said he was “dismayed.” His country was bombed for its trouble.

Nighttime scene outside the heavily damaged Crowne Plaza Hotel in Manama, with visible burn marks, collapsed sections, emergency lights, and crowds/police tape securing the area following the strike (March 1, 2026). (Source: Travel and Tour World)

Bahrain’s Crowne Plaza Hotel in Manama was damaged. Kuwait alone absorbed 97 ballistic missiles and 283 drones — one person killed, more than 30 injured. Qatar intercepted 18 ballistic missiles. Saudi Arabia was targeted. 300 British military personnel were within several hundred yards of a strike in Bahrain.

Iran’s retaliation strategy is now visible: it is not targeting military installations alone. It is targeting the economic geography of the American alliance in the Gulf. Hotels. Airports. Ports. The infrastructure of the stable Middle East that the US has spent decades underwriting. Every strike on a Dubai hotel is a strike on the argument that American protection is worth having.

The coalition Iran built against itself on Day One has now been given fresh grievances on Day Two. But a coalition with grievances against Iran and a coalition willing to act against Iran are two different things.

The Strait: 70 Percent Gone — And The Tanker War May Be Starting

Bitcoin offers the first read on how markets are pricing this war. Day One saw it drop 4% on the war news, recover, and close up 1.15%. As of Sunday morning it is trading at $66,970 — down from pre-war levels but not in freefall. The crypto market is not pricing civilizational collapse. Oil is a different calculation entirely, and the divergence between the two tells you something about where the genuine systemic risk is being located.

MarineTraffic confirmed a 70% drop in vessel traffic through the Strait of Hormuz by Saturday evening. The majority of ships had done U-turns, diverted to alternative routes, or begun idling in the Gulf of Oman. 55 oil tankers remain in Iranian waters — 18 laden with crude, 37 empty.

Two ships have now been struck in two days. The tanker Skylight was targeted Saturday — crew of 20 evacuated, four injured. On Sunday morning, Britain’s maritime security agency reported a second ship near the Strait struck by an “unknown projectile” causing a fire in its engine room. The blaze was brought under control. Whether this is systematic targeting of tanker traffic or opportunistic harassment is the question every shipping company in the world is trying to answer right now.

OPEC+ moved preemptively, announcing a production increase of 206,000 barrels per day beginning in April. The group said it would take a “cautious approach” to further moves. Analysts are not calling it sufficient.

The oil price forecasts arriving Sunday are divergent in ways that matter. Eurasia Group projects a $5-10 rise per barrel when markets open — significant but manageable. Barclays is less sanguine. Analyst Amarpreet Singh: “How this ends is extremely uncertain at this point but in the meantime oil markets will have to face their worst fears.” The Barclays number on the table: above $100 per barrel — a level not seen since the post-Ukraine invasion inflation surge of 2022 that reshaped Western politics for three years.

Brent crude closed Friday at $73, already up 20% on the year. If the Barclays scenario materializes, the economic shock is not a commodity price story. It is a cost-of-living story. It is a midterm election story. It is the Republican strategists gathering in Florida this weekend’s worst nightmare arriving ahead of schedule.

Pakistan: The Consulate in Karachi

Eight protesters were killed on Sunday after pro-Iran demonstrators breached the outer perimeter of the US consulate in Karachi, according to Pakistan’s Edhi Foundation emergency rescue service. Security forces threw tear gas. Eight people died.

Pakistan has the world’s second-largest Shia Muslim population. It shares a border with Iran. It has been attempting to walk a fine line — condemning the strikes without alienating Trump, who has maintained a warm rapport with Pakistani leaders.

That fine line just got considerably harder to walk. Eight Pakistani citizens are dead outside an American consulate in the country’s largest city. The domestic political pressure on the Pakistani government to respond is now significant. The regional spillover that analysts warned about is not confined to the Middle East.

Russia and China: The Alignment Hardens

Vladimir Putin expressed his “deepest condolences” to President Pezeshkian for the assassination of the supreme leader. His statement called the killing “committed in cynical violation of all norms of human morality and international law.” He said Khamenei would be remembered in Russia as “an outstanding statesman” who personally helped elevate the Russian-Iranian relationship “to the level of a comprehensive strategic partnership.”

China’s foreign ministry said it “firmly opposes and strongly condemns” the attacks on Iran and the killing of Khamenei, calling them “a grave violation of Iran’s sovereignty and security.” It called for an immediate stop to the fighting.

These statements are not surprising. They are nonetheless significant when placed in sequence. Russia is currently fighting a war in Ukraine with Iranian-supplied drones. China has been deepening economic ties with Iran throughout the sanctions era. Both governments now have a public record condemning an American military operation that killed a head of state.

The geopolitical alignment that has been forming since 2022 — Russia, China, Iran as a loose anti-American axis — has just had its most dramatic test. The axis’s Iranian partner has been decapitated. The question for Moscow and Beijing is not whether to express condolences. It is what they do next.

Voice of America Wasn’t Ready

Trump called on the Iranian people to rise up and take over their government. He did so via a platform most Iranians inside Iran cannot currently access. The internet is at 4% of normal capacity. The digital wall is holding.

In past conflicts, the United States relied on federally funded media to get its message past enemy lines. Voice of America was created during World War II. Radio Free Europe during the Cold War. Radio Farda — the Persian-language service of Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty — was built specifically to broadcast into Iran, including via a US radio transmitter in Kuwait that could reach Iranian audiences even through internet blackouts.

Voice of America launched in 1942 with a mandate to combat Nazi and Japanese propaganda

The Trump administration spent 2025 dismantling these tools. Trump called Voice of America “a TOTAL, LEFTWING DISASTER” and put nearly all of its 1,400 staff on paid leave. He tried and failed to shutter Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty entirely. Kari Lake, the Trump ally running the parent entity overseeing government-funded media, had been berating RFE/RL for failing to “align their message with American foreign policy.” Radio Farda lost access to the Kuwait transmitter for several months last year amid the dispute.

On Saturday — the day the war began, the day Trump told Iranians their moment of freedom had arrived — Radio Farda scrambled to restore its Kuwait transmitter access. Steve Capus, RFE/RL’s president: “Farda was built for this moment.” Lake’s US Agency for Global Media agreed to carry Farda’s programming on AM radio waves from Kuwait at RFE/RL’s own expense.

The United States called on 93 million people to revolt and then had to emergency-restore the AM radio transmitter it had spent a year trying to shut down in order to reach them.

Ilan Berman of the American Foreign Policy Council: the Trump administration “has dismantled some of the most potent tools that it has” to reach the Iranian people. “This de minimis approach is just not suited for the moment.”

Trump at the Fundraiser

While the war entered its second day, President Trump attended a glitzy fundraising dinner at Mar-a-Lago for MAGA Inc., the pro-Trump super PAC. His press secretary had said the fundraiser was “more important than ever.”

He did not return to the White House. He did not deliver a televised address to the nation. He did not hold a press conference. He confirmed Khamenei’s death on Truth Social and communicated throughout the day via social media posts.

Presidential historian Michael Beschloss: “What Americans of our time are accustomed to is a president giving a White House speech — usually from the Oval Office — that befits the supreme importance of making war. We are in a time where so many political traditions are being sidelined. And this is another.”

A Republican strategist and former State Department official who served in Trump’s first term: “The American public woke up to find the president took major military action with little public engagement or information. This certainly is shoot first and answer questions later.”

White House communications director Steven Cheung posted on X: “NO PANICANS! TRUST IN TRUMP!”

In a phone interview with CBS News conducted from Mar-a-Lago, Trump offered his own diplomatic assessment: “Much easier now than it was a day ago, obviously. Because they are getting beat up badly.” He said the operation had weakened Iran and could open the door to negotiations.

This is the wartime communication strategy of the United States in 2026. A Truth Social post announcing the death of a foreign head of state. A fundraising dinner. A CBS phone interview from the same ballroom. The president who launched the largest American military operation since the invasion of Iraq did not cancel his evening plans — and assessed the diplomatic consequences from Mar-a-Lago.

The Libya Warning

Philip Gordon, who served as assistant secretary of state for Europe when NATO waged its air war against Muammar Qaddafi in Libya in 2011, offered the most precise historical warning on Day Two.

Trump told Iranians to take over their government. Gordon’s response: “Which Iranian people? There’s 90 million of them.” On regime change without ground troops: “It can create even more of a security vacuum into which all sorts of different forces will surge.”

Iraq, Libya, Afghanistan — Gordon served through all of them. His conclusion: “All cases were similar in the sense that they turned into a costly disaster.”

The Libya parallel is exact in one specific way: Qaddafi was killed, the regime fell, the country had no functioning successor government, and the result was a decade of civil war and chaos that continues today. Libya had 6 million people and no nuclear program. Iran has 93 million people, enriched uranium, the IRGC, and three overlapping military command structures.

Dana Stroul called the worst-case scenario “complete chaos.” Gordon called Libya “a costly disaster.” The Atlantic’s Shane Harris and colleagues reported that planners concluded air strikes alone would not produce regime change and built in weeks of sustained bombing because they did not know what would. They still don’t.

Israeli Casualties: The Trajectory Is Escalating

Nighttime rescue scene at collapsed Tel Aviv residential building—rubble, responders, and crater from the first fatality strike

A woman in her 50s was killed in Tel Aviv when an Iranian ballistic missile evaded Israel’s air defenses and hit a residential building. She was the first Israeli fatality of the conflict. By Sunday morning she was not the last.

Six people were killed in Beit Shemesh — a city 18 miles west of Jerusalem — in what Israeli emergency services described as the worst single casualty event of the war so far. More than 20 additional casualties, including two in serious condition, were evacuated to hospitals. The Israeli military dispatched search and rescue forces and a helicopter to the site.

That is the trajectory in 36 hours: one fatality on Day One, six dead in a single strike on Day Two. Iran is firing in waves. Each wave tests the defense architecture. Each successful penetration is both a military and a psychological data point — for Israel, for Iran, and for the Gulf states watching to see whether American protection holds.

Israel’s air defenses have generally managed to repel Iranian attacks, but some missiles and drones have penetrated. Drones are harder to detect than standard missiles and have occasionally threaded through the systems. Sirens warned of incoming fire in Jerusalem for the first time since the conflict began. The escalation curve on Israeli civilian casualties is now visible.

The Closing of the World

Emirates, British Airways, Air France, and Lufthansa all suspended flights. Major airports remain closed across the Gulf corridor. A wide band of airspace — the key hub connecting Europe, Asia, and Africa — is disrupted. The global travel infrastructure that routes through the Middle East is fractured in ways that will take weeks to restore even if the shooting stops tomorrow.

It will not stop tomorrow.

Israel’s defense minister called the killing of Khamenei “a turning point” and said the operation would continue “for as long as necessary.” The stated objective is to “create the conditions” for a change of government. The planners built in weeks. Trump said throughout the week “or as long as necessary.”

The EU’s top diplomat Kaja Kallas called it “a defining moment in Iran’s history” and said there was “an open path to a different Iran.” She was working toward “practical de-escalation.” What practical de-escalation looks like when one side is promising force “they have never experienced before” and the other is bombing Tehran for the second consecutive day is not yet clear.

The Omani foreign minister, who said a breakthrough was within reach 48 hours ago and watched his country get bombed for his trouble, said he was “dismayed.”

The world that existed on Friday morning — with its nuclear negotiations, its shipping lanes, its Dubai safe havens, its Omani mediators, its AM radio transmitters, its presidential Oval Office addresses — does not exist anymore.

Part XXII will document what replaces it.
