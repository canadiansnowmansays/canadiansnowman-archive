The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 11 - The Philosophical Roots

Understanding Miller’s Hobbesian Worldview and Why It Matters

January 12, 2026

Over ten parts from September through October 2025, I examined what fascism actually is, how it’s being built in America, and how to fight back. I measured Trump and MAGA against historical fascism’s ten characteristics and found 9/10 alignment. I documented Russ Vought’s systematic construction of authoritarian infrastructure and traced how Miller redefined anti-fascism itself as terrorism.

But a January 2026 Atlantic analysis by Gal Beckerman reveals something I hadn’t fully explored: the philosophical foundation beneath Miller’s authoritarianism. Understanding why Miller thinks the way he does helps explain how the fascist architecture gets constructed and justified.

The answer lies in a debate that history seemed to have settled centuries ago: Thomas Hobbes versus John Locke.

The Choice That Defines Government

If you want to know a political leader’s governing philosophy, you could cut through a lot of bluster by asking them: Are you a Hobbesian or a Lockean? Anyone who’s taken Poli Sci 101 will understand what this means.

These 17th-century philosophers each offered a picture of human nature in its rawest form, and they came to starkly different conclusions:

John Locke believed people were capable of reason and moral judgment. His ideas were central to the birth of modern democracy—including American democracy. Give citizens accurate information and fair processes, Locke argued, and they will converge on something resembling the common good. Government exists to protect natural rights, and it derives legitimacy from the consent of the governed.

Thomas Hobbes believed humans were vicious creatures who needed protection from themselves by a powerful king. In the “state of nature”—life without government—existence would be “solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.” Only a strong sovereign with a monopoly on violence could prevent us from tearing each other apart. Without such power, life would be a war of all against all.

One way to understand the head-spinning nature of being an American over the past couple of decades is that this debate—one that history seemed to have settled in Locke’s favor—is alive again. Barack Obama was Lockean through and through, insisting repeatedly that if citizens were just given accurate information and a fair hearing, they would converge on the common good. Donald Trump is Hobbesian extraordinaire, constantly portraying life under anyone’s leadership but his own much as Hobbes describes the state of nature: chaotic, dangerous, and requiring a strongman’s intervention.

But it’s Stephen Miller who has now made this philosophy explicit and unapologetic.

Miller’s “Iron Laws of the World”

On January 8, 2026, Stephen Miller—Trump’s Deputy Chief of Staff, often cast as the president’s “brain”—articulated what he called the “iron laws of the world since the beginning of time.” His monologue sounded like something lifted directly from Hobbes’s 1651 treatise Leviathan:

“We live in a world, in the real world, that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power.”

Miller’s might-makes-right declaration came after Trump’s decision to overthrow Venezuela’s president and in anticipation of the United States possibly acquiring Greenland from Denmark “by any means necessary” (a notion that Miller’s wife found fit to turn into a meme). The will to dominate, seize other countries’ resources because you can, and generally bully those that can’t fight back is nothing to worry about, Miller reassured Americans. This is just the natural state of things. This is how it all works. Power does what it wants. The rest is “international niceties.”

As Beckerman notes in the Atlantic, Miller might as well etch Hobbes’s words onto a gold plaque ready to hang among other gilded tchotchkes in the Oval Office: “During the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war; and such a war, as is of every man, against every man.”

This is the philosophical foundation for everything I documented in Parts 2-6 of this series. But it’s worth understanding just how fundamental this worldview is, and how it differs even from the philosopher Miller appears to be channeling.

The Bastardization of Hobbes

Here’s what makes Miller’s invocation of Hobbes particularly revealing: he’s not even reading Hobbes correctly.

Hobbes described the state of nature as a problem to be solved, not a condition to embrace. By showing how terrifying life would be without institutions—an existence of constant fear, insecurity, and watching your back—Hobbes was diagnosing humanity’s predicament. Left to our own devices, without any government or social order, we would have no culture, no science, no peace. This thought experiment was his starting point, and it helped fuel the Enlightenment then just getting under way.

The cure Hobbes proposed won’t seem palatable to most Americans today. He suggested that stability could come only through the empowerment of a strong monarch who would have a monopoly on violence and keep our worst instincts at bay. This is not democracy, obviously. But crucially, the king, as Hobbes dreamed him up, would need to keep and gain the consent of those he ruled over. This was his Leviathan—an earthly sovereign power that humans rationally create to escape the chaos of the state of nature. It was a social contract: there would be good faith in the exchange with a strongman, sacrificing a certain amount of autonomy to ensure that everyone could thrive without killing one another.

That’s Hobbes’s vision. He was trying to offer us an escape from a horror show that resembles William Golding’s Lord of the Flies: boys on an island without grown-ups or any of their rules, driven by privation and instinct to a murderous contest of dominance.

As for Miller’s vision, it’s horror all the way down. He’s not looking to solve for the state of nature. He simply calls it “the real world”—anarchic rules that America should accept and use to its benefit. If Miller has read Hobbes, he’s drawing the wrong lesson. Even if he might like the idea of Trump as the Leviathan-like authoritarian come to bring order to chaos, this is not how Trump understands his role. Otherwise, Trump would be bolstering international institutions and multilateral relationships rather than trashing whatever and whoever does not serve his glory.

If we were all living on the island in Lord of the Flies, Trump would be Jack—the boy who recognizes that power comes not from rules but from fear, spectacle, and aggression.

Even Hobbes wanted to escape the horror show. Miller wants to weaponize it.

The American Rejection of Hobbes

Miller’s worldview represents a fundamental break with American political tradition. The Founders were deeply skeptical of Hobbes. John Adams’s winking remark to his son John Quincy that Hobbes’s ideas were “mischievous” pretty much sums it up. The creators of a more participatory system of government did not take kindly to the notion that all people needed was a Mafia boss to offer them protection.

Thomas Jefferson laid out his objections most clearly in an 1816 letter to Adams. And Miller—if he is actually interested in thinking through the implications of his “iron laws”—might do well to read it.

Jefferson agreed with Hobbes’s main contention: government is necessary to organize human behavior. But he thought that leaders had to do a lot more than just prevent brutality and chaos. A Lockean, Jefferson had a more expansive view of human needs and capabilities. He felt the “principle of Hobbes” was lacking because it didn’t account for humans having a “moral sense.”

People want more than just life without fear, Jefferson wrote. “Every human mind feels pleasure in doing good to another.” Any worthy American government needed to reflect this instinct toward goodness and create conditions for it to flourish. This also happens to be the only way to summon the kind of “real world” that someone would actually want to live in.

This is why Part 10 of my series examined the Founders’ explicit rejection of monarchy. The American experiment was built on Lockean principles—the belief that people, given the right institutions and accurate information, could govern themselves. Kings were precisely what America was designed to prevent.

Miller’s Hobbesian worldview is fundamentally un-American. It rejects the philosophical foundation of the republic.

Philosophy in Practice: Miller’s Daily War Room

Understanding Miller’s Hobbesian worldview helps explain everything I documented in Parts 2-6. But a January 2026 Atlantic profile by Ashley Parker, Michael Scherer, and Nick Miroff reveals how this philosophy operates in practice—through Miller’s daily 10 a.m. conference calls that run “less like a government adviser and more like a wartime general.”

These calls happen every day—yes, even Saturdays. Miller plays “the role of browbeater, inquisitor, and bully.” He accepts no excuses, entertains no dissent. “He pushes everybody to the absolute limit because he knows that the clock is ticking,” one senior official told the Atlantic. “He gets on the phone and he yells at everybody. Nobody is spared from his wrath.”

In May 2025, Miller demanded 3,000 immigration arrests per day—a nearly tenfold increase over 2024 numbers. He expects daily updates on ICE hiring (10,000 new deportation officers), detention capacity, deportation flights, and border crossings. He publicly shames bureaucrats who fall short or resist orders.

This is Hobbesian philosophy in action. In Miller’s world, opposition isn’t democratic disagreement requiring persuasion—it’s an obstacle to be crushed through superior force. The “iron laws” he articulates aren’t just international relations theory; they’re his management style.

“If you say something stupid, he’ll tell you to your face,” a third official explained. “You are expected to perform at a certain level, and there’s no excuse for not meeting those expectations.”

Notice the complete absence of Locke’s “consent of the governed.” There’s no rational deliberation, no moral reasoning, no acknowledgment that government officials might have legitimate concerns or expertise. There’s only power: Miller has it, they don’t, and that’s the only reality that matters.

The Atlantic profile reveals that Miller’s role extends far beyond immigration. Steve Bannon calls him Trump’s “prime minister.” Miller has “a role in nearly every area about which he cares deeply: immigration and border security, yes, but also national security, foreign policy, trade, military action, and policing.”

When Trump complained about homeless encampments near the State Department, he turned to Miller: “Get it done.” Within six hours, as Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt recalled, “there were cranes cleaning them up.”

Most tellingly, during “Signalgate”—when the Trump administration accidentally included The Atlantic‘s editor in chief on a private Signal chat about bombing Yemen—it was Miller, not the national security adviser, Pentagon chief, or vice president, who ended debate and directed the group to move forward with strikes.

This is the Hobbesian sovereign in practice: the one with power decides, and the decision is final because power makes it so.

The Philosophical Roots Run Deep

Miller’s worldview didn’t emerge from nowhere. A resurfaced 2003 video shows 17-year-old Miller on a school bus, discussing Iraq. With a smirk, he suggests the “ideal solution” for Saddam Hussein would be “to cut off their fingers.” He argues that torture is the proper punishment in a “nonbarbaric society.”

Then comes the line that reveals everything: “Torture is a celebration of life and human dignity.”

Watch that video and you see someone who has already internalized the Hobbesian premise that cruelty and domination aren’t unfortunate necessities—they’re the natural order. The teenage Miller can barely contain his delight at the outrage his proclamation generates, “his mouth widening into a toothy grin” before continuing.

This is Miller the troll, who has “confided in friends that he enjoys starting a fire, then dousing it with gasoline.” But as the Atlantic notes, “after more than two decades relishing his role as the gleeful contrarian, the persona has now become more true character than occasional outlandish caricature.”

By the time Miller joined Jeff Sessions’s Senate office, he was buttonholing reporters in Capitol hallways to press his anti-immigrant case, calling them at home to talk for hours about immigration minutiae. Working with Bannon, he made Breitbart News the communications arm of his crusade, understanding that “the more outrageous the headline, the better.”

He helped sabotage the bipartisan “Gang of Eight” immigration bill in 2013, despite broad support from business leaders, tech companies, and donors. When he joined Trump’s 2016 campaign—launched with claims about Mexican “rapists”—his philosophy had found its strongman.

The baby-faced Miller quickly moved from the back of Trump’s plane to the inner circle. By March 2016, he was Trump’s opening act, “riling up crowds across the country with an anti-immigrant, anti-Washington populism that sometimes threatened to overshadow Trump himself.”

“He was so insane over-the-top,” Bannon recalled. “But of course the MAGA base can’t get enough of him.”

How Philosophy Becomes Fascism

Understanding Miller’s Hobbesian philosophy illuminates how the ten characteristics of fascism I examined in Part 2 aren’t random tactics—they flow logically from a worldview that sees humans as inherently vicious and power as the only real law.

Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology: If the world is war of all against all, then the nation becomes the tribe you protect against all others. Defining who is a “real American” becomes existential—you can’t afford sentiment about those outside your group. Miller has described the results of legal immigration programs as “the Somalification of America” and has made clear he wants immigration “from the Nordic countries, and not so much from the Third World countries,” as former Senator Jeff Flake observed. This isn’t policy preference; it’s survival logic in Miller’s Hobbesian framework.

Violent suppression of opposition: In a Hobbesian world, opposition isn’t democratic disagreement—it’s a threat to the sovereign power that keeps chaos at bay. As I documented in Part 5, when Miller deployed troops to Democratic cities despite court orders, he wasn’t just breaking norms—he was following his philosophy. His accusation that federal judges are engaged in “legal insurrection” for ruling against Trump’s policies reveals his view: courts have no army, and legal authority means nothing against raw power.

Scapegoating of minorities: If humans are naturally vicious and the world is defined by power struggles, then “others” aren’t potential citizens or people seeking better lives—they’re threats in the perpetual war. Miller’s rhetoric about immigrants “poisoning the blood of our country” makes perfect sense in a Hobbesian framework. They’re not refugees; they’re invaders in the war of all against all. His demand for 3,000 arrests per day treats human beings as quotas to be met through force.

Fusion of state and corporate power: Hobbes argued for a sovereign strong enough to maintain order. In Miller’s bastardized version, this means corporations serve Trump’s power or become enemies. There’s no free market or worker power—just the question of whether you’re aligned with the strongman’s strength.

Cult of personality and strongman worship: Hobbes argued for a powerful sovereign to impose order. But Miller’s version lacks Hobbes’s social contract—there’s no consent, no rational agreement among citizens. There’s just Trump, the strongman who will dominate or be dominated. Loyalty to him becomes loyalty to order itself. As the Atlantic notes, Trump has described Miller as sitting “at the top of the totem pole” inside the White House, with near-total policy control.

Rejection of democratic norms: This is the logical endpoint. If the world is truly governed only by strength, force, and power, then courts, constitutions, and democratic processes are just pretty lies. The “iron laws” Miller describes don’t recognize legal authority—only raw power. When courts blocked Trump’s deployments and the administration ignored the rulings, that wasn’t lawlessness in Miller’s mind—it was philosophy in action. He describes the Democratic Party as a “domestic extremist organization,” reframing political opposition as existential threat.

Russ Vought’s systematic dismantling of democratic infrastructure, which I examined in Part 4, becomes comprehensible when you understand it’s built on Miller’s Hobbesian foundation. You’re not just changing policies—you’re reconstructing government around the principle that power is the only reality.

When Power Becomes Personal: The Barbara Wien Case

The most chilling revelation in the Atlantic profile isn’t Miller’s policy positions or even his management style. It’s what happens when someone with his Hobbesian philosophy gets state power and faces personal offense.

On a warm September morning, Barbara Wien, a 66-year-old retired gender and peace-studies professor, arrived at the Millers’ Arlington, Virginia home to protest their presence in the neighborhood. She pointed her index and middle fingers at her own eyes, then directed those fingers at Katie Miller, who was on the front porch. The gesture—captured on video—was a simple “I’m watching you.”

Stephen Miller took this as a call to violence. And unlike most Americans who face neighborhood disputes, Miller had unique power to punish.

The Millers had already felt under siege. Someone had posted flyers at neighborhood parks where their kids played, revealing their home address and calling Miller a Nazi. The couple had stopped allowing their children to play in the yard. A Rhode Island man had been indicted for threatening to kill Miller. Katie Miller had been photographed in the neighborhood while going to the gym and walking with her kids.

But Miller wasn’t going to be intimidated by a retired professor in a striped dress.

Days later, Miller appeared on Sean Hannity’s Fox News program. Though ostensibly there to discuss the federal response to Charlie Kirk’s assassination, Miller pivoted to discuss “domestic terrorists,” including those guilty of “doxxing.”

“You want us to live in fear? We will not live in fear,” Miller declared. Then came the threat: “You will live in exile, because the power of law enforcement under President Trump’s leadership will be used to find you, will be used to take away your money, take away your power, and, if you have broken the law, to take away your freedom.”

Miller set about drafting executive orders, later signed by Trump, directing federal law enforcement to refocus counterterrorism efforts on people with “anti-fascist” ideas, including “extremism on migration, race, and gender” and “hostility towards those who hold traditional American views on family, religion, and morality.”

He began describing a central divide in the country between “legitimate state power” and left-wing “street violence”—a category he defined broadly enough to include his neighbor’s finger-pointing gesture. He accused Democratic politicians who called him or Trump “authoritarian” of “inciting violence.” (Never mind that he had repeatedly called the Biden administration “fascist.”)

Miller demanded FBI involvement in investigating Wien. A Democratic Virginia prosecutor became concerned about federal overreach and sought to narrow the scope of a search warrant. A federal magistrate judge refused to approve federal search warrants.

When the investigation stalled, Miller’s ally Jim Jordan, the House Judiciary Committee chair, announced an inquiry into the Democratic prosecutor. “This is so cool,” Katie Miller posted on social media. “Thank you.”

The prosecutor refused to cooperate, noting that Congress lacked authority to intervene in a state law-enforcement matter. There were still some powers of the state that Miller did not control.

This is what Hobbesian philosophy looks like when it meets personal grievance and state power. Wien didn’t threaten violence. She didn’t break any law. She pointed fingers at someone she believed was implementing cruel policies. In a democracy based on Lockean principles, this is protected speech—the kind of dissent that makes self-governance possible.

But in Miller’s Hobbesian framework, the gesture was an attack on the sovereign’s household, and therefore justified the full weight of federal law enforcement. Never mind that using federal power to investigate personal enemies is precisely the kind of abuse that constitutional limits are designed to prevent.

This is why understanding Miller’s philosophy matters. It’s not abstract. It’s the difference between a government official who tolerates dissent because citizens have rights, and one who deploys the FBI against a neighbor because power should face no constraints.

The Wien case reveals the ultimate danger of Miller’s worldview: when you truly believe that the world is governed only by strength, force, and power—when you reject the idea that humans have a moral sense or that government derives legitimacy from consent—then every personal slight becomes an existential threat, and every federal agency becomes a potential weapon.

This is Hobbes without the social contract. It’s the state of nature Miller claims to describe, but directed against citizens by the very government that’s supposed to protect them.

Miller’s philosophy moved from theory to practice with Trump’s decision to overthrow Venezuela’s government. As the Atlantic notes, the stated motive and evident outcome make clear this is “a raw exercise of strength for strength’s sake rather than a bid to stabilize the Western Hemisphere.”

Trump has already put dibs on 30 to 50 million barrels of Venezuelan oil. The naked grab removes any ambiguity. This isn’t realpolitik in the Kissingerian sense—Henry Kissinger’s transactional diplomacy at least aimed for stability through balance of power among strong states. Miller’s Venezuela adventure is pure domination: we can, therefore we will.

This is the international version of what I documented in Part 5: federal troops deployed to Portland, Chicago, and Memphis over local officials’ objections. Whether domestic or foreign, Miller’s principle remains consistent: power does what it wants, and anyone lacking sufficient force to resist simply endures what power imposes.

Why Philosophy Matters

Understanding Miller’s worldview matters for three reasons:

First, it explains the systematic nature of what we’re seeing. These aren’t disconnected authoritarian impulses—they’re the logical application of a coherent philosophy. When you believe the world is governed only by force, every fascist characteristic becomes not just justified but necessary.

Second, it reveals what we’re actually fighting. This isn’t about policy disagreements. We’re fighting a fundamental philosophical rejection of the American founding. Miller wants to replace Lockean democracy with Hobbesian authoritarianism.

Third, it shows the argument we need to make. Parts 7-10 examined how institutions and citizens fight back. But we also need to fight the philosophical battle. We need to articulate why Jefferson was right and Miller is wrong.

The Perversion of “Realism”

Miller frames his worldview as “the real world”—as if anyone who disagrees is naive. This makes authoritarianism seem pragmatic and democracy seem childish.

But Miller’s “realism” is profoundly unrealistic. Human civilization represents our collective effort to escape the Hobbesian state of nature. Every institution, every law, every democratic norm—these aren’t illusions. They’re the reality we’ve constructed to avoid the nightmare Hobbes described.

The European Union, NATO, the United Nations, international trade agreements—these aren’t “international niceties.” They’re the architecture that has delivered unprecedented peace and prosperity. The decades since World War II have seen historically low levels of great power conflict precisely because nations agreed to rules beyond pure force.

Miller’s “real world” is actually regression to a primitive condition we’ve spent centuries overcoming.

The Irony of Miller’s Background

Miller is descended from Jewish immigrants who fled persecution in Eastern Europe. His family benefited from a liberal democratic system that recognized humans as more than power-seeking animals—that recognized refugees as people deserving protection.

The Lockean principles Miller now rejects—human dignity, government by consent, moral sense—these are what saved his ancestors. The Hobbesian world Miller champions—where might makes right—is precisely what his family escaped.

This isn’t just philosophical inconsistency. It’s betrayal of the principles that made his existence possible.

What This Means for Resistance

Understanding Miller’s philosophy clarifies how to fight it:

Challenge the premise. Don’t accept Miller’s framing that his worldview represents “reality.” The evidence of history supports Jefferson, not Miller.

Defend institutions. Every time courts block illegal actions, every time citizens vote, we prove that Locke was right—that humans can govern themselves through reason rather than submitting to strongmen.

Articulate the alternative. It’s not enough to oppose authoritarianism. We must affirmatively defend the Lockean vision. Democracy isn’t weakness—it’s the highest form of political organization humans have achieved.

Expose the con. Miller’s “iron laws” serve one purpose: justifying power in Trump’s hands. It’s propaganda designed to make authoritarianism seem inevitable and resistance seem futile.

The Stakes

When I began this series in September 2025, I wanted to establish what fascism actually is. Eleven parts later, the evidence is overwhelming. But this philosophical analysis adds a crucial dimension: we’re not just facing fascist tactics, we’re facing an ideological alternative to the American founding.

Miller isn’t just Trump’s advisor—he’s the philosophical architect of a project to replace Lockean democracy with Hobbesian authoritarianism. This makes resistance not just pragmatic but existential. We’re defending the philosophical foundation of the republic itself.

The Founders chose Locke over Hobbes, democracy over autocracy, human potential over human vice. Miller wants to reverse that choice.

The Question Before Us

Miller’s articulation of his “iron laws” forces clarity. He’s told us explicitly: the world is governed by strength, force, and power alone. “International niceties” are for the weak. Might makes right.

The question is whether Americans accept this premise. Do we believe Miller’s “real world” is reality? Or do we insist, with Jefferson, that humans are capable of better—that we have a moral sense, that institutions can constrain power, that government derives legitimacy from consent?

Everything flows from how we answer this question. Accept Miller’s philosophy, and fascism becomes logical. Reject it, and democracy becomes necessary.

Miller has made his choice. He chooses Hobbes. He chooses might makes right. He chooses the war of all against all.

The question is: what do we choose?

Because if Miller’s philosophy prevails—if Americans accept that power is the only reality—then everything I’ve documented becomes not a warning but a prophecy. The fascist characteristics, the authoritarian architecture, the rejection of courts—all of it becomes Miller’s “real world.”

But if we reject his premises, if we insist on the Lockean vision that founded this nation, then resistance isn’t futile—it’s fulfilling our obligation to the democratic experiment.

Miller has revealed his hand. Now we must reveal ours.

This concludes the 11-part series “What Fascism Actually Is.” The evidence is comprehensive and the choice is clear.

The warning signs are all there. The philosophical foundation has been made explicit.

The question is whether we choose Locke or Hobbes, democracy or authoritarianism, self-governance or strongman rule.

That choice will determine everything that comes next.
