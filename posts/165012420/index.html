Trump TACO Tuesdays

Episode 1: The Double TACO: When China Tariffs and Steel Policy Hit Reality Simultaneously

June 3, 2025

The moment when you realize TACO Tuesday is becoming a problem

Welcome to the inaugural edition of Trump TACO Tuesdays, where we track the predictable pattern of bold policy announcements followed by inevitable reality checks. For those unfamiliar with the term, "TACO trade" stands for "Trump Always Chickens Out" – originally coined by Financial Times columnist Robert Armstrong to describe the gap between tariff rhetoric and actual implementation. While Wall Street traders initially used this framework to navigate trade policy volatility, the TACO pattern has proven remarkably consistent across multiple policy areas.

This week, we're witnessing a rare phenomenon: the Double TACO. Two major trade policy areas – China tariffs and steel/aluminum duties – are simultaneously hitting the reality wall that typically forces moderation of initial bold positions. The market's reaction suggests investors are learning to trade the pattern, not just the announcements.

TACO #1: China Tariffs Meet Diplomatic Reality

The first TACO began unfolding exactly as expected, but weekend developments have accelerated the pattern dramatically. Throughout the campaign and early presidency, Trump promised immediate and sweeping tariffs on Chinese goods, with threats ranging from 60% across-the-board duties to complete economic decoupling. These weren't conditional statements but absolute declarations delivered with characteristic confidence about America's negotiating leverage.

Then came the reality checks in rapid succession. First, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated last week that talks were "a bit stalled," suggesting Trump and Xi Jinping would need to speak directly before progress could be made. But by Friday, Trump's frustration boiled over in a characteristic social media outburst: "China, perhaps not surprisingly to some, HAS TOTALLY VIOLATED ITS AGREEMENT WITH US. So much for being Mr. NICE GUY!"

This represents the classic TACO acceleration – when initial diplomatic optimism crashes into complex reality. The Geneva trade agreement, which had suspended most tariffs for 90 days, is now disputed by both sides. China counters that the U.S. violated the deal first through increased tech export restrictions and threats to revoke Chinese student visas. Beijing's response was predictably firm: any U.S. actions that "damage China's interests" will be met with measures to "safeguard its rights and benefits."

The linguistic evolution tells the complete TACO story in compressed time. Campaign promises of "day one" tariffs became "we're making progress in Geneva," then shifted to "talks are stalled," and finally devolved into "China HAS TOTALLY VIOLATED" accusations. Each phase represents the predictable stepping stones from bold promise to frustrated reality.

What makes this TACO particularly revealing is the internal coordination chaos it's exposed. Former senior White House intelligence official Dennis Wilder noted that Bessent might not have been involved in decisions about chip export curbs and visa policies – suggesting the Treasury Secretary is desperately seeking a Trump-Xi call to "install some coherence in government policies." This institutional dysfunction often emerges as TACO patterns develop, when different agencies pursue conflicting approaches.

TACO #2: Steel Tariffs and Targeted Reality

The second TACO emerged simultaneously in the steel and aluminum sector, but with an interesting twist. Rather than completely backing down from tariff threats, the administration implemented targeted increases – a classic TACO compromise that splits the difference between bold rhetoric and complete inaction.

The steel tariff increases that were announced hit the market hard, demonstrating that even partial TACO implementations can have significant economic effects. However, the targeted nature of these tariffs, rather than the sweeping industry-wide changes initially suggested, shows the pattern in action. The administration found a middle ground that allows them to claim they're keeping promises while avoiding the broader economic disruption that comprehensive steel tariffs might cause.

This represents what we might call a "Soft TACO" – where the chickening out takes the form of scope reduction rather than complete abandonment. Instead of backing down entirely, the administration narrowed the policy to its most politically defensible and economically manageable elements.

The Market's TACO Education

What's particularly fascinating about this Double TACO moment is how markets are responding to both the policy developments and the accelerating timeline. The China situation's rapid deterioration over the weekend – from stalled talks to mutual accusations of violations – demonstrates how quickly TACO patterns can intensify when diplomatic complexity meets political frustration.

Rather than treating each announcement as an isolated event, sophisticated traders appear to be factoring both the TACO pattern and Trump's emotional responses into their analysis. The market reaction to the China impasse, Trump's frustrated social media posts, and the steel tariff news suggests investors are becoming more adept at reading the gap between initial promises and likely chaotic implementation.

This represents a crucial evolution in how Wall Street processes Trump administration announcements. During the first term, markets often whipsawed on every trade policy tweet. Now, there's evidence that investors are applying a more nuanced TACO framework – recognizing that frustrated presidential outbursts often signal policy difficulties rather than imminent breakthroughs.

The steel sector reaction, in particular, shows this sophisticated analysis. Markets moved on the targeted tariff increases but didn't panic about broader trade war implications, even as the China situation deteriorated. This suggests traders are learning to separate tactical policy moves from strategic relationship breakdown – a crucial distinction as TACOs accelerate.

Institutional Constraints in Action

Both TACOs illustrate how institutional constraints shape policy outcomes regardless of initial presidential intentions. In the China case, the need for Treasury Department analysis, congressional input, and allied coordination all serve to moderate the most extreme positions. The steel tariffs show how industry lobbying, supply chain considerations, and economic impact analyses create pressure for targeted rather than comprehensive approaches.

These constraints aren't necessarily weaknesses – they represent the institutional wisdom that prevents policy from swinging too dramatically in any direction. The TACO pattern, viewed from this perspective, might actually be a feature of the system rather than a bug. It allows for bold leadership rhetoric while ensuring that actual policy implementation considers broader economic and diplomatic consequences.

The Double TACO Implications

The simultaneous emergence of two TACOs in the same week creates interesting analytical opportunities. It suggests that the pattern isn't limited to isolated policy areas but represents a broader approach to governance. When reality checks hit multiple policies simultaneously, it often accelerates the moderation process as competing priorities force trade-offs.

For investors, the Double TACO provides a clear test case for the framework's predictive value. If both China policy and steel tariffs continue to moderate from their initial bold positions, it strengthens the case for treating future announcements with similar analytical skepticism.

The timing is also significant. Early in the administration, when political capital is highest and institutional resistance lowest, represents the optimal moment for implementing dramatic policy changes. If TACOs are emerging this early, it suggests the pattern may be even more pronounced as the administration settles into governing routines.

What to Watch Next

The key indicators for TACO development are evolving rapidly, particularly on the China front. Trump's weekend outburst suggests we're entering the frustration phase of the TACO cycle, where initial diplomatic optimism gives way to blame-shifting and emotional reactions. Watch for continued escalation of rhetoric paired with behind-the-scenes attempts to salvage some kind of face-saving arrangement.

The proposed Trump-Xi call, which National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett suggested could happen "as early as this week," represents a potential TACO inflection point. If the call occurs, watch the language used to describe it – phrases like "productive discussion" or "progress on key issues" typically signal movement toward moderation. If the call doesn't happen, or if it goes poorly, expect further acceleration of the TACO pattern toward complete policy breakdown.

For China policy specifically, monitor whether Trump's frustration leads to actual tariff implementation or remains confined to social media venting. The gap between angry tweets and concrete policy action often reveals the true constraints facing the administration. Additionally, watch for signs that different government agencies are pursuing conflicting approaches – a classic indicator that institutional chaos is forcing unintended moderation.

For steel policy, observe whether the targeted approach expands amid China tensions or remains limited as a strategic hedge. The coordination between different trade policy areas will reveal whether the administration can maintain coherent economic strategy under diplomatic pressure.

The TACO Advantage

Understanding the TACO pattern provides both analytical and investment advantages. It helps separate signal from noise in policy announcements, offers a framework for predicting likely outcomes, and provides context for market reactions that might otherwise seem irrational.

As this Double TACO unfolds, it will test whether the pattern holds under different conditions and policy areas. The results will inform our analysis of future announcements and help refine the framework for maximum predictive value.
