The Kash Patel Files

He Should Be Worried

Preface: These Eyes

April 19, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

🎵 Bette Davis Eyes — Kim Carnes (1981) 🎵 These Eyes — The Guess Who (1969) 🎵 I Only Have Eyes for You — The Flamingos (1959)

There is a particular quality to the eyes of Kashyap Patel — wide, alert, perpetually startled, as though he has just walked into a surprise party he wasn’t expecting and isn’t entirely sure is for him. They are the eyes of a man who sees threats everywhere: in the senior agents who served with distinction for decades, in the congressional oversight he once weaponized and now fears, in the login screen that locked him out on a Friday afternoon in April and convinced him, for one panicked hour, that his presidency — sorry, his directorship — was over.

They are not, however, the eyes of a man who sees what is right in front of him.

I am a retired Canadian journalist watching from Toronto, which is either the worst or the best vantage point from which to assess the director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation. Worst, because I have no sources inside the Hoover Building, no security clearance, no professional stake in the outcome. Best, because I have no sources inside the Hoover Building, no security clearance, and no professional stake in the outcome. I have only the public record — which turns out to be more than sufficient, because the public record of Kashyap Patel is one of the most comprehensively documented institutional catastrophes in modern American governance.

The record is so voluminous, in fact, that it requires a series. This is that series.

What follows across these essays is not a partisan attack. I have no particular brief for the Democratic Party, which has its own institutional failures to account for, and I hold several hunded shares of Tesla, which should establish my bona fides as someone who does not reflexively reject the orbit in which Patel moves. This is, rather, a national security audit — conducted from the outside, in the only voice available to me, which is that of a 74-year-old Canadian sitting at his kitchen table with a husky named Dior, reading article after article and marvelling at the sheer density of the catastrophe.

Let me state the thesis plainly: Kash Patel should be worried about his job. But not nearly as worried as the rest of us should be about him having it.

The man who runs America’s premier law enforcement agency — 38,000 employees, jurisdiction over counterterrorism, counterintelligence, cybercrime, organized crime, public corruption, and civil rights — has, according to reporting from The Atlantic, The New York Times, and multiple other outlets drawing on dozens of current and former officials:

  • Consumed alcohol to the point that his security detail could not wake him and considered requesting breaching equipment — the tools SWAT and hostage-rescue teams use to force entry through locked doors.

  • Assigned a full-time SWAT team to protect his girlfriend, a country singer whose website says “NO UPCOMING SHOWS,” converting Nashville’s tactical unit into her personal escort service for hair appointments and senior centre performances.

  • Used government jets for trips to a wrestling match, a hunting ranch, a golf resort in Scotland, and the Milan Olympics — where his official schedule showed a single 20-minute security briefing and hours of “personal time/cultural activities.”

  • Attempted to post a classified photograph of Five Eyes intelligence chiefs — some of whom are nondisclosed — on social media, and had to be physically prevented from doing so by his own staff.

  • Fired agents who worked on investigations assigned to them by their superiors, dismantled the FBI’s storied public corruption squad, terminated a trainee three weeks from graduation for displaying a Pride flag, and forced out a veteran agent the week after he buried his wife — on allegations the FBI’s own records disproved.

  • Panicked when a login screen malfunctioned, calling aides and allies to announce he had been fired, prompting the White House to field calls from Congress asking who was running the FBI.

And he has done all of this during a war with Iran — a state sponsor of terrorism with demonstrated capability and intent to strike inside the United States — after firing counterintelligence agents devoted to the Iranian threat.

I have written extensively in these essays about a framework I call TACO — Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate — which I developed to describe the behavioural patterns of the Trump administration. Patel is its purest embodiment, a man who operates in all four modes simultaneously:

Threaten: He polygraphs his own employees to find out who has said something unflattering about him. He fires agents for case assignments they did not choose. He told Steve Bannon he would “come after” journalists with criminal charges.

Accommodate: He exists to serve one audience — Donald Trump. Every action, from the immigration diversion to the Epstein file management to the social media strategy during active investigations, is calibrated to please the president. When Trump called after the Italy beer-chugging, Patel accommodated. When Stephen Miller demanded immigration quotas, Patel accommodated. The accommodation is total.

Capitulate: He has capitulated to every institutional temptation the directorship offers — the jets, the travel, the perks, the celebrity — while capitulating away the institutional responsibilities that justify them. William Sessions lost the directorship in 1993 for offences that Patel has exceeded by orders of magnitude.

Obfuscate: “Print it, all false, I’ll see you in court — bring your checkbook.” That was Patel’s response to a detailed investigation by The Atlantic citing more than two dozen sources. It is the response of a man who has confused bravado with rebuttal, who believes that denying a fire while standing in smoke constitutes firefighting.

His yearbook quote, from Garden City High School on Long Island, came from the Jewish theologian Abraham Joshua Heschel: “Racism is man’s gravest threat — the maximum of hatred for a minimum reason.”

It reads now as an artifact from a life unlived. The young man who chose those words — the son of immigrants, one of a handful of students of colour in his class, a kid who caddied at the country club and listened to defence attorneys tell their stories — that young man might have become someone worth admiring. A public defender who fought for people the system forgot. A national security professional who protected the country that had taken his family in.

Instead, he became the wizard who saves King Donald.

And now the wizard is behind a locked door, and nobody can wake him up, and the country he was supposed to protect is at war.

J. Edgar Hoover, whatever his many sins, built a corrupt empire that at least functioned. He knew where the files were. He showed up for work. He did not require breaching equipment.

Hoover is rolling in his grave. But he is rolling with purpose, the way he did everything — methodically, with a file on everyone who watched him do it.

Patel, by contrast, rolls without purpose, without direction, without institutional memory, and occasionally without consciousness. He has managed the worst of both worlds: the authoritarianism without the competence, the corruption without the craft, the fear without the respect.

These eyes — wide, watchful, seeing enemies everywhere — have missed everything that mattered.
