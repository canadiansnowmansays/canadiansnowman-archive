The Incredible Disappearing Witness

Part XXXII: Pam Bondi discovers that the best way to avoid answering questions under oath is to no longer have a job

April 10, 2026

Ms. Bondi had a series of missteps and messaging failures, particularly in her handling of the release of the files from the Jeffrey Epstein investigation. Credit Doug Mills / New York Times

I. The Oldest Trick

There is a moment in every magician’s act when the audience knows exactly what is about to happen. The assistant climbs into the box. The magician raises the saw. The crowd understands the mechanics — the hidden panel, the practiced contortion — even before the blade starts moving. The trick still lands, because knowing how it works is entirely different from being able to stop it.

April 9, 2026. The Department of Justice sends a letter to House Oversight Chairman James Comer explaining that Pam Bondi will not be appearing for her scheduled deposition on April 14. The reason, offered with lawyerly serenity, is that she was subpoenaed in her official capacity as attorney general. As she is no longer attorney general — having been fired by the man she served — the subpoena, the DOJ argues, no longer obligates her to appear.

Read that again slowly.

The administration fired its own attorney general days before she was scheduled to testify under oath. Then the administration’s Justice Department sent a letter explaining that, since she no longer works for the administration, the subpoena is moot.

You have to admire the audacity, if nothing else.

II. A Timeline Worth Sitting With

Let us be precise about the chronology, because precision is the enemy of the kind of foggy plausible deniability this maneuver depends on.

Pam Bondi was subpoenaed by the House Oversight Committee in bipartisan fashion — five Republicans joining Democrats — to testify about her role overseeing the release of the Justice Department’s Epstein investigative files. The subpoena was not casual. It required Republican members to cross the aisle in an era when crossing the aisle costs political capital.

The deposition was scheduled for April 14, 2026.

Bondi was fired last week.

The DOJ letter arrived Wednesday, April 9 — five days before the scheduled testimony.

If you are looking for a coincidence, you will need to look elsewhere. This city is fresh out.

III. The Legal Argument (Such as It Is)

Assistant Attorney General Patrick D. Davis, writing to Chairman Comer, made the administration’s position explicit: Bondi was subpoenaed “in her official role as attorney general and not in a personal capacity.” Since she no longer holds that office, the department’s position is that she is not obligated to appear.

This argument has the superficial quality of legal reasoning while being, upon examination, significantly less sturdy than it appears.

Congressional subpoena power is not a narrow instrument. It flows from Article I and has been interpreted broadly by courts for over two centuries. The critical question is not what title the recipient held when the subpoena was issued, but whether the testimony sought relates to matters within Congress’s legitimate oversight interest.

Bondi’s knowledge of what happened inside the Justice Department’s handling of the Epstein files does not evaporate when she cleans out her desk. She was the attorney general. She made decisions. She was in meetings. She received briefings. None of that is erased by a presidential text message informing her of her termination.

Rep. Nancy Mace, who led the subpoena effort and is not always the most reliable compass in Washington but is correct here, made exactly this point: “Our motion to subpoena Pam Bondi, which was passed by the Oversight Committee, was for Bondi by name, not by title.”

Rep. Robert Garcia, the top Democrat on the panel, was more direct: “Our bipartisan subpoena is to Pam Bondi, whether she is the Attorney General or not. She must come in to testify immediately, and if she defies the subpoena, we will begin contempt charges.”

The committee, for its part, announced it would contact Bondi’s personal counsel to discuss next steps. Which suggests the subpoena is not, in fact, withdrawn — whatever the DOJ’s preferences on the matter.

IV. The Rat That Stephen Smelled

Let us be direct about what the most straightforward explanation here actually is.

Pam Bondi was becoming a problem. Not for the Epstein survivors. Not for the Democratic members of the Oversight Committee. Not even, primarily, for the Republican members who broke ranks to subpoena her. She was becoming a problem for the White House.

A sworn deposition is not a friendly media appearance. It is not a binder on a table. It is not a carefully managed closed-door session after which nothing emerges. A sworn deposition is a legal proceeding in which you are obligated to tell the truth under penalty of perjury, and in which experienced congressional investigators — who have had months to review the Epstein files themselves — ask you questions you have not seen in advance.

That is a fundamentally different environment from anything Bondi had navigated before. And the White House knew it.

The firing was publicly attributed to performance issues — specifically, her stumbles handling the Epstein investigation. Which is either darkly comic (fired for mishandling the files she’ll now never testify about) or perfectly designed (the stumbles were the justification, the testimony was the motive). These are not mutually exclusive.

Todd Blanche, the acting attorney general, when asked about the subpoena on Tuesday, said he would leave the matter “to Chairman Comer and others to figure out.” This is the political equivalent of washing your hands while someone else holds the soap.

V. The TACO Cycle, Completed

Readers of this series will recognize the pattern. The Threaten-Accommodate-Capitulate-Obfuscate cycle that has characterized the Trump administration’s handling of the Epstein files since the beginning did not end with the files’ release. It merely entered a new phase.

Threaten: Congress demands documents. Survivors demand accountability. The world demands answers.

Accommodate: A January 30 release of files. Millions of pages. A binder. A press conference. The appearance of transparency performed with considerable theatrical commitment.

Capitulate: The files contain almost nothing of investigative value regarding the living powerful. The co-conspirators named in the 2007 draft indictment remain redacted. The 2.5 million withheld pages remain withheld. The pattern of exposure is consistent: victims’ faces unredacted, powerful names protected.

Obfuscate: When Congress attempts to secure sworn testimony about how those decisions were made, the mechanism of obfuscation is the firing of the person who made them, followed by a department letter arguing the subpoena is therefore moot.

It is, as cover-up mechanics go, unusually clean. No documents burned, no witnesses intimidated, no break-in required. Just a termination letter, a legal argument, and the quiet hope that the news cycle moves on.

VI. Comer’s Studied Ambiguity

Chairman James Comer did not vote for the Bondi subpoena. He said he didn’t think it necessary. He faced pressure from conservative hardliners to withdraw it after Bondi’s voluntary closed-door session — a session after which, notably, nothing of consequence emerged. He said he planned to “speak with Republicans about next steps.”

When asked this week about the subpoena’s future, Comer said: “I haven’t talked to Republicans, I’m sure I’ll give them a call this weekend. I don’t know what is going to happen.”

This is not the language of a chairman planning to press forward aggressively. It is the language of a man waiting to see which way the wind blows before committing to a direction.

The wind, in this reading, blows from Mar-a-Lago.

Mace and Democratic Rep. Ro Khanna sent a letter to Comer this week urging him to “publicly reaffirm” the deposition commitment. That two members felt it necessary to write that letter tells you something about their confidence in what Comer’s phone calls with Republicans this weekend will produce.

VII. What Bondi Knows

Let us close with the question underneath all the procedural maneuvering.

What does Pam Bondi know?

She was the attorney general. She oversaw a review of millions of files related to Jeffrey Epstein’s network — a network that included, by any fair reading of the documentary record, people of considerable wealth and power across multiple political parties and multiple countries. She made decisions about what to release and what to withhold. She sat in rooms where those decisions were made. She received briefings. She was, for a period of months, the most consequential person in America with respect to what the public would and would not learn about one of the most consequential sex trafficking cases in modern American history.

She is now a private citizen.

And the administration that employed her, and then fired her, and then argued through its legal machinery that her firing relieves her of testifying obligations, has a rather obvious interest in ensuring that the questions her deposition would have answered remain, for the foreseeable future, unanswered.

The survivors who have waited years — decades, in some cases — for accountability have a rather obvious interest in the opposite.

The American people, one suspects, are capable of determining which of those interests deserves precedence.

Congress has the tools. It has contempt authority. It has the power to issue a new subpoena addressed to Bondi as a private citizen. It has members — on both sides — who have publicly committed to exactly that path.

The question, as it always is in this long-running exercise in strategic transparency, is whether the tools will be used.

History is watching. It tends to notice when they aren’t.

VIII. Meanwhile, Across Town

Melania Trump, the first lady, made an unexpected statement about Jeffrey Epstein at the White House on Thursday.Credit...Evan Vucci/Reuters

At approximately 3:59 in the afternoon on April 9, 2026 — the same day the Department of Justice sent its letter arguing that Pam Bondi need not testify — Melania Trump summoned reporters to the entrance hall of the White House to make a statement about Jeffrey Epstein.

Nobody had asked.

There was no prominent story breaking that morning linking Melania to Epstein. No congressional hearing had been announced. No journalist had filed a report. The first lady of the United States simply decided that April 9, 2026 — this particular day, of all available days — was the moment to clear her name.

“The lies linking me with the disgraceful Jeffrey Epstein need to end today,” she said.

Let us sit with the date for a moment before proceeding.

The statement itself raised as many questions as it answered. Mrs. Trump acknowledged that she and the president had attended some of the same parties as Epstein, describing it as the natural consequence of “overlapping in social circles” in New York. She addressed her name’s appearance in the Epstein files — specifically, a 2002 email to Ghislaine Maxwell in which a woman named “Melania” praised a New York magazine profile of Epstein, signed off with “Love,” and received in return the salutation “Sweet pea” from the woman who would later be convicted of sex trafficking.

Mrs. Trump characterized this as “casual correspondence.” Which it may well be. Social New York in 2002 was a particular ecosystem with its own gravitational rules, and a friendly email does not a conspiracy make. These are fair points.

What is less easily explained is the timing.

The first lady also addressed the origin story of how she met her husband — a story that has always centred on Paolo Zampolli, an Italian modeling agent who introduced them at the Kit Kat Club in 1998. What she did not address, because it is uncomfortable, is that Paolo Zampolli appears multiple times in the Epstein files and conducted business with Epstein. This does not implicate Melania in anything. It does complicate the tidy separation between her social world and Epstein’s.

She closed her remarks by calling on Congress to continue its investigation and give victims a hearing.

Congress to continue its investigation. The same investigation that, hours earlier, the DOJ had moved to obstruct by blocking Bondi’s testimony.

The effect of Melania’s statement — whatever its intent — is to insert a sympathetic, humanizing narrative into the Epstein story on the precise day that the accountability machinery was being quietly disassembled. It gives the media a different Epstein angle to chase. It reframes the day’s Epstein news around a first lady defending her honour rather than around an attorney general being shielded from sworn testimony.

Whether that was the design or the coincidence is a question only the people in the White House entrance hall on Thursday afternoon can answer.

What we can say, with confidence, is this: on April 9, 2026, the Trump administration did two things related to Jeffrey Epstein. It moved to kill the deposition of the woman who oversaw the Epstein files. And it put the First Lady in front of cameras to talk about something else entirely.

Make of that what you will. The survivors, one suspects, already have.

The simplest way to avoid answering questions under oath, it turns out, is to lose the job that got you subpoenaed. The second simplest way is for Congress to decide it has better things to do. We are about to learn which of those mechanisms proves more durable.
