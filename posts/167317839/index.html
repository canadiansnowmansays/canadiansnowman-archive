The Big Beautiful Bill Chronicles

64% of Americans Say No, But Senate Republicans Say Yes

July 1, 2025

By Stephen Lawton | July 1, 2025

Senator Lisa Murkowski (R-Alaska) speaks to reporters after Tuesday's historic tie-breaking vote on Trump's legislation. Murkowski cast the crucial vote that allowed the bill to pass 51-50 after VP JD Vance broke the tie.

The Bottom Line Up Front: In a historic tie-breaking vote Tuesday, Senate Republicans passed Trump's signature legislation 51-50 with Vice President JD Vance casting the deciding vote, but not before making significant changes that set up a collision course with the House. With the most unpopular major legislation in decades now facing a deficit increase of $3.3 trillion (up from the House's $2.4 trillion), 11.8 million people losing health insurance, and polling showing 64% opposition nationwide, the bill's final passage remains far from certain despite clearing this critical hurdle.

The Senate's High-Stakes Gamble: What Actually Changed

After weeks of closed-door negotiations, Senate Republicans released an updated version of OBBA on June 27, 2025 that makes several crucial modifications to the House bill—some that could doom its chances when it returns to the lower chamber.

The Major Changes That Matter:

SALT Deduction Drama: The most contentious change involves the State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction. The amended version includes a new SALT agreement which will raise the deduction cap to $40,000 for five years beginning in 2025, but only after Senate Republicans initially proposed keeping it at $10,000. House Republican Mike Lawler has already drawn a line in the sand, stating "If the Senate reduces the SALT number, I will vote NO and the bill will fail in the House"—a threat that could sink the entire package given the House's razor-thin majority.

Medicaid Cuts Get Deeper: Perhaps the most significant departure from the House version, the Senate draft would gradually reduce the maximum amount of provider taxes, which is currently up to 6%, until it reaches a 3.5% threshold by 2031. This represents a much steeper cut than the House approach and has sparked fierce opposition from moderate Republicans. Four Republican senators have openly questioned the Medicaid cuts in the House bill: Sens. Lisa Murkowski of Alaska, Jerry Moran of Kansas, Susan Collins of Maine and Josh Hawley of Missouri.

Bigger Price Tag, Bigger Problems: The Senate version dramatically increases costs. While the House bill would add $2.4 trillion to the deficit, the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office published an analysis finding that the Senate bill would increase the national debt by $3.3 trillion over the next 10 years. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget was even more alarmed, stating "Although we have not produced a full estimate of the bill, it appears to add roughly $4 trillion to the debt through 2034, including interest – which is roughly $1 trillion higher than the House-passed version of the bill".

Child Tax Credit Reduction: In a move that could hurt middle-class families, the Senate bill pares back the child tax credit from $2,500 in the House version to $2,200 per child, though it does increase the senior deduction from $4,000 to $6,000.

Debt Ceiling Explosion: The Senate is proposing raising the nation's debt limit by $5 trillion, a sizable increase compared to the House bill, which agreed to $4 trillion—a change that has already prompted opposition from Sen. Rand Paul, who voted against advancing the bill Saturday night.

Tuesday's Historic Vote: Victory by the Thinnest of Margins

The final vote to pass the bill Tuesday was nothing short of historic. Senate Republicans voted 51-50 to pass President Trump's "big, beautiful bill" — but only after Vice President JD Vance cast the tie-breaking vote, marking one of the most dramatic legislative victories in recent memory. Three Republicans broke ranks: Sens. Thom Tillis of North Carolina, Susan Collins of Maine, and Rand Paul of Kentucky joined all 47 Democrats in opposition.

The vote capped a grueling marathon session that spanned more than 24 hours, with senators voting on dozens of proposed amendments in what's known as a "vote-a-rama." Republican leaders frantically worked until the final moments to secure enough votes, with the outcome hanging in the balance until Sen. Lisa Murkowski of Alaska finally cast her yes vote after intensive overnight negotiations.

Murkowski, who had threatened to vote no over concerns about Medicaid cuts, called it "one of the hardest votes of her Senate career" and said she voted yes "despite some continued reservations." The Alaska senator secured key concessions including $50 billion for rural hospitals and exemptions for Alaska from certain SNAP benefit cuts.

The Polling Disaster: America's Verdict Is Clear

While Senate Republicans celebrated their narrow procedural victory, polling data reveals a sobering reality: this may be the most unpopular major legislation in modern American history.

The Numbers Don't Lie:

Across four recent polls from the Washington Post, Fox News, KFF and Quinnipiac University, the legislation was an average of 24 points underwater. On average, 55% of surveyed Americans opposed it, while 31% supported it. That makes the bill more unpopular than any piece of major legislation passed since at least 1990, according to data crunched by George Washington University political science professor Chris Warshaw.

Fox News Poll Spells Trouble: Only 38 percent favored the bill, while 59 percent opposed it, a 21-point gap against the bill. About half of all voters believed the legislation would be detrimental to their families, and just a quarter thought it would deliver any benefit. Most damaging for Republicans: less than half of Republicans said they believed the bill would help their own families.

Bipartisan Opposition: The KFF poll found even more devastating results. Nearly two-thirds of the public (64%) hold an unfavorable opinion of the tax and budget bill being discussed by Congress, while one-third (35%) hold a favorable view. Crucially, among Republicans and Republican-leaning independents who are not MAGA supporters, two-thirds (66%) have an unfavorable view of the bill.

The Knowledge Problem: Perhaps most telling, the Post/Ipsos poll also shows the people who say they know more about it tend to be more opposed. Those who had heard only "a little" or "nothing at all" opposed it 31-19%, but those who had heard "a great deal" or "a good amount" opposed it 64-33%.

Pew Research Confirms the Trend: Far more Americans oppose the legislation than favor it. Nearly half (49%) oppose it, while 29% favor it. Another 21% are not sure. A 55% majority says the bill would help high-income people, with 38% saying it would help them a lot. Only 11% say it would hurt high-income people.

Elon Musk Doubles Down: The Billionaire Opposition Intensifies

Even as Republicans celebrated Saturday's vote, Elon Musk—Trump's former DOGE chief and $300 million political donor—escalated his attacks. On Saturday, billionaire Elon Musk, with whom Trump had a public falling out this month over his censure of the bill, again doubled down that criticism. The Tesla and Space X CEO called the package "utterly insane and destructive"

This continued opposition from Trump's biggest financial backer represents an unprecedented split in the Republican coalition.

The House Collision Course: Will the Bill Survive Its Return?

Even if the Senate passes the bill this week, it faces an uncertain future in the House. With their 220-seat majority (with two vacancies), Republicans can afford to lose only a few votes. Several factors make passage far from certain:

SALT Rebellion: New York Rep. Mike Lawler has made clear that any reduction in the SALT deduction cap will cost Republican votes in the House. "I will not accept a penny less" than the negotiated $40,000 cap. "If the Senate reduces the SALT number, I will vote NO and the bill will fail in the House".

Deficit Hawks: The substantially higher price tag—potentially $4 trillion including interest—could lose more fiscal conservatives. Rep. Thomas Massie and Warren Davidson, who voted against the original House bill, are unlikely to support an even more expensive version.

Rural Hospital Concerns: The deeper Medicaid cuts in the Senate version could lose rural Republicans whose districts depend on these hospitals. The bill includes a $25 billion fund for rural hospitals and slightly delays the cap on Medicaid provider taxes, but this may not be enough to satisfy concerned members.

What Happens Next: The House's Final Stand

The bill now heads to the House, where Speaker Mike Johnson faces the challenge of his career. With a 220-212 majority and Trump's July 4th deadline looming, Johnson is calling House Republicans back from recess for a Wednesday vote. But several factors make passage far from certain:

The SALT Rebellion: New York Rep. Mike Lawler has made clear that any reduction in the SALT deduction cap will cost Republican votes. The Senate's changes to SALT provisions could prove fatal.

Deficit Hawks: The $3.3 trillion price tag—nearly $1 trillion higher than the House version—could lose fiscal conservatives who were already skeptical.

Medicaid Concerns: The Senate's deeper cuts to Medicaid could cost votes from Republicans in rural districts that depend on these programs.

Trump and Vance are expected to be "deeply involved" in whipping House votes, with the president declaring "It's a great bill. There is something for everyone." But with the bill's historic unpopularity and the Senate's changes, even Trump's influence may not be enough.

The Accountability Question: Why Aren't You Listening to Us?

With 64% of Americans opposing this legislation—including two-thirds of non-MAGA Republicans—millions of constituents across the country are asking their elected representatives the same fundamental question: Why aren't you listening to us?

The disconnect is staggering. In poll after poll, Americans have made their views crystal clear: they don't want this bill. They don't want to lose their healthcare. They don't want trillions added to the debt. They don't want massive tax cuts for the wealthy paid for by cutting programs that help working families. Yet Republican after Republican continues to march in lockstep toward passage.

Is it simply fear of Donald Trump's wrath? The evidence suggests yes. As one Republican aide told reporters off the record, "Nobody wants to be the next target of a Truth Social rant." Senator after senator who privately expressed doubts about the bill's deficit impact or Medicaid cuts ultimately fell in line during Saturday's vote.

Consider the math: if you're a Republican senator and 57% of independents in your state oppose this bill, plus 89% of Democrats, you're looking at roughly 70% opposition among your constituents. Yet you're voting yes anyway. That's not representative democracy—that's political cowardice disguised as party loyalty.

The question isn't just about this bill. It's about whether American democracy can function when elected officials fear their party leader more than they respect their voters. When political survival trumps public service, the entire system breaks down.

The Bigger Picture: Democracy vs. Plutocracy

This battle over the "Big Beautiful Bill" represents more than just a legislative fight—it's a test of whether deeply unpopular policies can be forced through despite overwhelming public opposition. Nonpartisan analysts estimate that a version of Trump's tax cut and spending bill would add trillions to the $36.2 trillion US government debt. They also say that the bill would pave the way for a historic redistribution of wealth from the poorest 10 percent of Americans to the richest.

As Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer put it, "Future generations will be saddled with trillions in debt. Debt is abstract, but what does it mean for the average American? Raising your costs, raising your costs to buy a home, raising your costs to buy a car, raising your costs on credit card bills. And why are they doing all this? Why are they doing the biggest Medicaid cuts in history? Now, it's getting close to a trillion dollars, just in Medicaid alone. All to cut taxes for the ultra-rich and special interests".

The Bottom Line: A Hollow Victory?

Tuesday's 51-50 vote may have given Trump a legislative victory, but it came at enormous cost. With the bill requiring a tie-breaking vote, facing a $3.3 trillion deficit increase, 12 million people losing health insurance, and opposition spanning from Elon Musk to two-thirds of the American public, this "win" exposes the deep fractures within the Republican Party.

The real test now comes in the House, where just a few Republican defections could kill the entire package. In a democracy, sustained opposition of this magnitude—64% of Americans opposing the legislation—usually proves fatal to major legislation. The question now is whether Trump's political muscle can overcome both the math and the will of the American people in the final 48 hours before his July 4th deadline.
