Trump TACO Tuesdays

Episode 9: The China TACO and the Alaska Capitulation

August 12, 2025

Stephen Lawton August 12, 2025

Welcome to Episode 9 of Trump TACO Tuesdays, where we document the most predictable TACO in our series yet: the moment Trump's "final" China deadline became another 90-day extension, followed by the ultimate accommodation—rolling out the red carpet for Vladimir Putin on American soil while nuclear submarines sit idle and sanctions remain theater.

At exactly midnight on August 12th, Trump's China truce expired. By Monday morning, he had quietly signed another 90-day extension. But that was just the appetizer. The main course came when Trump announced Putin would visit Alaska for "direct diplomacy"—the same Putin whose war Trump claimed to oppose, the same Russia he threatened with nuclear submarines just weeks ago.

Episode 9 reveals how TACO psychology scales from trade accommodations to geopolitical capitulation, proving that mathematical constraints don't recognize the difference between tariff deadlines and national security commitments.

The China TACO: Midnight Mathematics

For weeks, Trump insisted the August 12th deadline was different. "China knows I mean business this time," he declared on Truth Social. "No more Mr. Nice Guy. Midnight August 12th—that's it. The art of the deal means knowing when to walk away, and I'm prepared to walk." Administration officials leaked stories about Trump being "personally invested" in following through, with unnamed sources claiming he had learned from previous accommodation cycles.

Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick appeared on Fox Business Friday, declaring, "The President has been crystal clear. August 12th at midnight, Eastern Time. If China hasn't agreed to our terms, the gloves come off. No extensions, no delays, no accommodations." National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett echoed the sentiment on CNBC, insisting "This time is genuinely different. The President understands the stakes."

The mathematical constraints remained unchanged: Wall Street sliding (-2.3% on Friday alone), brutal inflation reports looming (core CPI expected to spike to 4.1%), and the same economic realities that forced every previous accommodation. As @CalltoActivism noted, Trump "bet everything on a 90-day pause before reimposing steep tariffs—hoping Beijing would blink. They didn't."

Beijing's response was masterful in its simplicity: complete silence. No desperate last-minute phone calls to Washington, no emergency trade delegations, no conciliatory statements. Chinese officials simply went about their weekend as if no deadline existed. Foreign Ministry spokesperson Wang Wenbin's Friday briefing mentioned the deadline exactly zero times, focusing instead on routine diplomatic matters.

Beijing didn't need to blink. They had studied eight previous episodes of Trump TACO Tuesdays. They understood the mathematical inevitability better than Trump's own advisors.

The Midnight That Never Came

At 11:58 PM Eastern Time on August 12th, Trump posted on Truth Social: "Great things are happening with China. Very close to a deal. Need just a little more time to get it perfect. The Art of the Deal!"

At midnight, nothing happened. No tariff implementation, no dramatic announcements, no follow-through on weeks of escalatory rhetoric. The promised "gloves coming off" moment passed in silence.

By 9 AM Monday, Trump had signed Executive Order 14127, extending the China trade pause for another 90 days, buried in a routine batch of paperwork with no fanfare, no press conference, no explanation. The order cited "ongoing productive negotiations" and "significant progress toward a comprehensive agreement" as justification for the extension.

The White House press pool wasn't notified. Karoline Leavitt's morning briefing mentioned it only when directly asked by reporters, describing it as "standard diplomatic practice" and "evidence of the President's sophisticated negotiating approach."

Classic TACO: the dramatic buildup, the quiet capitulation, the immediate pivot to new threats to distract from the accommodation.

Market Psychology: TACO Vindicated Again

Markets responded exactly as TACO psychology predicted. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which had dropped 389 points Friday on deadline anxiety, soared 612 points Monday morning on accommodation relief. The S&P 500 gained 2.1%, while the Nasdaq surged 2.8%.

"This is classic TACO pattern recognition at work," said Peter Ricchiuti, senior finance professor at Tulane University. "Investors have learned to bet against Trump's follow-through, regardless of how tough the rhetoric gets. The mathematical constraints are just too powerful."

Currency markets validated the accommodation immediately. The Chinese yuan strengthened 1.2% against the dollar, while emerging market currencies rallied across the board. Bond yields fell as investors moved out of safe-haven positions, pricing in continued trade stability rather than escalation.

The market's TACO literacy has become so sophisticated that some trading algorithms now automatically buy on Trump deadline announcements, betting on eventual accommodations. The "TACO trade" has become a recognized strategy among quantitative hedge funds.

The Alaska Accommodation: From Nuclear Threats to Red Carpets

But the China extension paled beside the week's ultimate TACO: Trump's announcement that Vladimir Putin would visit Alaska for "essential diplomatic dialogue to end the Ukraine conflict through American strength." This from the president who just weeks ago positioned nuclear submarines against Russia, who claimed to be stopping Putin's war machine through military pressure.

The accommodation progression is textbook TACO psychology:

Phase 1 (Episode 8): Nuclear submarine deployment - "President Donald Trump issued a rare threat of nuclear escalation on Friday, saying he had ordered two nuclear submarines to be strategically positioned near Russia."

Phase 2 (Early August): Quiet submarine withdrawal - Pentagon sources confirmed the submarines returned to normal patrol patterns "after completing their strategic positioning objectives." No press coverage, no official announcements.

Phase 3 (August 12): Putin Alaska invitation - complete accommodation disguised as diplomacy. "I'm bringing Putin to American soil because that's what strong leaders do," Trump declared. "We're going to make the greatest deal in history, right here in Alaska."

The progression from nuclear brinksmanship to diplomatic accommodation reveals TACO psychology at its most dangerous. When grandiose military threats meet geopolitical reality, accommodation becomes the psychological escape route—even when it means legitimizing war criminals on American territory.

The Anchorage Disgrace: Rolling Out the Red Carpet

Shame on Trump for letting Putin onto American soil while Ukrainian cities burn and Russian oil flows to fund the war machine. The president who promised to end the Ukraine conflict through strength instead provides Putin with the ultimate propaganda victory: an American president receiving him as an equal on American territory.

The Alaska meeting location itself represents accommodation disguised as symbolism. Trump claims choosing Anchorage shows "American strength on American soil," but the reality is more revealing: Alaska provides Putin with a low-risk venue far from Washington protests, while giving Trump the optics of "home field advantage" without the political cost of a White House visit.

Russian state media immediately seized on the accommodation. RT headlines proclaimed "Putin Accepted as Equal by American President on US Territory," while Sputnik ran analysis pieces titled "Trump's Alaska Invitation Proves Multipolar World Order." The Kremlin's propaganda apparatus understood what Trump apparently didn't: the accommodation was the victory, not whatever "deal" might emerge.

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy's response was diplomatically restrained but privately furious, according to Ukrainian officials. "We hope our American partners remember who is defending democracy and who is destroying it," Zelenskyy said in his evening address, without mentioning Trump directly.

The Accommodation Optics

The Alaska meeting reveals TACO accommodation at its most dangerous. Putin doesn't need to make concessions—Trump provides the platform, the legitimacy, the photo opportunities. Putin gets everything (international standing, sanctions relief pressure, Ukrainian conflict normalization) while giving nothing (continued war, oil sales, territorial occupation).

Consider what Putin gains from the Alaska accommodation:

  • Legitimacy: An American president treating him as a peer despite ongoing war crimes

  • Sanctions Relief Pressure: Meeting with Trump implies eventual sanctions accommodation

  • Propaganda Victory: Domestic Russian media can showcase American presidential acceptance

  • Strategic Patience Validation: Proof that waiting out Trump's threats leads to accommodations

  • Ukrainian Demoralization: Signals to Kyiv that American support has limits

What does America gain? Trump claims "the greatest deal in history," but provides no specifics. The accommodation itself becomes the objective, dressed up as dealmaking.

The Pattern Scales: From Trade to War

What makes Episode 9 particularly troubling is how TACO psychology scales from trade disputes to existential conflicts. The same mathematical constraints that force tariff accommodations—economic pressure, market punishment, political costs—now drive geopolitical accommodations with authoritarian regimes.

The economic constraints forcing China accommodation (market volatility, inflation pressure, recession risks) operate identically on Russia policy. Sustained confrontation with Putin requires sustained economic costs: expanded sanctions, military aid commitments, energy market disruptions. When those costs create political pressure, accommodation becomes the mathematically inevitable path of least resistance.

Trump's advisors understand the pattern even if they won't admit it publicly. "The President is always looking for win-win solutions," Kevin Hassett explained on Meet the Press. "Whether it's China trade or Russia diplomacy, he brings the same dealmaking approach." The euphemism is transparent: "dealmaking" means accommodation when accommodation serves political needs.

The Nuclear TACO: From Submarines to Summits

The nuclear submarine deployment that headlined Episode 8 now looks like classic TACO escalation psychology: deploy maximum threat to create maximum accommodation justification. The submarines were never meant to start a conflict—they were meant to create the conditions for accommodation with maximum face-saving potential.

"The submarine deployment achieved its strategic objective," an unnamed Pentagon official told Defense News. "It demonstrated American resolve and created the space for productive diplomatic engagement." Translation: the threats worked exactly as intended—to force accommodation while maintaining the appearance of strength.

Russian Defense Minister Sergei Shoigu's dismissive response to the submarine deployment now appears strategically brilliant: "Russia possesses significantly more nuclear submarines in the world's oceans than the US." Rather than escalating, Moscow called Trump's bluff, correctly calculating that accommodation would follow threat deployment.

The pattern reveals TACO psychology's most dangerous evolution: when accommodation pressure reaches nuclear brinksmanship levels, the accommodation that follows appears reasonable by comparison. Welcoming Putin to Alaska seems moderate after threatening nuclear escalation.

Other TACO Updates: The Accommodation Cascade

The China and Russia accommodations created a cascade effect across Trump's other impossible promises:

The Pharmaceutical 200% TACO: The 60-day deadline for drug companies to cut prices by "30% to 80%" passed in complete silence. No implementation, no extensions, no explanations. The 200% tariff threats on pharmaceutical imports similarly evaporated once accommodation pressure peaked elsewhere.

The August 7th Reciprocal Tariff TACO: The "country-specific reciprocal tariffs" promised for August 7th got quietly delayed indefinitely. Commerce Department sources confirm no implementation timeline exists, describing the delay as "administrative prioritization of more significant trade negotiations."

The India "Substantial" Tariff TACO: Trump's threats of "substantially" higher tariffs on India over Russian oil purchases never materialized—perhaps because Putin's Alaska visit required maintaining the same Russian oil flows Trump once condemned. Indian refiners continue purchasing Russian crude at pre-threat levels.

The De Minimis Elimination TACO: The August 29th deadline to eliminate the $800 de minimis threshold has been pushed to "later this fall," according to Treasury Department officials. The accommodation came pre-emptively, before the deadline pressure could build.

The cascade effect proves TACO psychology's systematic nature: when core accommodations (China, Russia) become necessary, peripheral accommodations (pharmaceuticals, India, de minimis) become inevitable to conserve political capital.

International TACO Recognition: The Framework Goes Global

Episode 9 marks the complete internationalization of TACO as a predictive framework. Allied governments now plan around accommodation cycles rather than taking threats at face value. Enemy governments wait out threat escalation, knowing mathematical constraints will force retreats.

European Union officials privately describe "TACO planning" in their strategic documents, according to Brussels sources. NATO allies factor accommodation probability into their defense planning. China's Politburo reportedly receives regular TACO analysis briefings to optimize their negotiation timing.

The framework's international adoption constrains Trump's negotiating power while enabling adversary patience. When everyone knows accommodation is mathematically inevitable, threats lose their credibility before they're even issued.

The Psychology of Perpetual Accommodation

Episode 9 reveals TACO evolution from tactical retreat to permanent governing strategy. Trump no longer makes threats intending to follow through—he makes threats intending to accommodate from a position that appears strong. The accommodation becomes the objective, not the byproduct.

This psychological shift explains the Alaska invitation's timing: immediately after the China accommodation, when maximum distraction value was needed. Putin's visit serves dual purposes—accommodate Russia while distracting from China capitulation.

The pattern suggests Trump has internalized accommodation as his primary governing tool. Rather than fighting the mathematical constraints that force retreats, he now leverages them to create accommodation opportunities that can be branded as diplomatic victories.

Conclusion: When Accommodation Becomes Capitulation

Episode 9 proves TACO psychology operates at every level of presidential power. From midnight trade deadlines to nuclear submarine deployments, the same accommodation pressure that creates tactical retreats now drives strategic capitulations.

Trump Always Chickens Out—even when it means welcoming war criminals to American soil while pretending it's strength. The framework that began by predicting tariff accommodations now tracks the accommodation of American foreign policy itself.

When TACO becomes the permanent governing strategy, allies question commitments and adversaries plan for American retreat. The mathematical constraints that once forced trade accommodations now constrain America's global leadership, one accommodation at a time.

The Ukraine Betrayal: Negotiating Without the Victim

The Alaska meeting with Putin represents TACO psychology's most dangerous evolution: the moment accommodation transcends policy disputes to become a tool for legitimizing authoritarian aggression. But perhaps most troubling is what the meeting excludes—any meaningful Ukrainian representation in discussions about Ukraine's future.

Trump has announced Putin's visit without confirming Ukrainian participation, despite the conflict being fundamentally about Ukrainian sovereignty. This represents accommodation at its most morally bankrupt: negotiating the fate of a democracy under assault without including the democracy in the room.

President Zelenskyy's measured response masks profound alarm in Kyiv. Ukrainian officials privately express horror at the prospect of Trump and Putin carving up Ukrainian territory in an Anchorage hotel suite, treating Ukrainian cities like chess pieces in a grand bargain. "Nothing about Ukraine without Ukraine," has been Kyiv's diplomatic mantra—but Trump appears ready to ignore it entirely.

The historical parallel is chilling: Neville Chamberlain's 1938 Munich Agreement, where European leaders negotiated Czechoslovakia's fate without Czech representation. Chamberlain returned to London claiming "peace for our time" while sacrificing democratic principles to authoritarian demands. The accommodation felt like victory until Hitler's tanks rolled into Warsaw.

The World's Terrible Hope

As the world prays for peace, they simultaneously pray that Trump will not repeat history's greatest accommodation failure. The paradox is profound: global leaders desperately want the Ukraine conflict to end, but understand that capitulation disguised as peace merely delays and amplifies future conflicts.

European allies face an impossible calculus. They want American leadership to end the bloodshed, but fear American accommodation will legitimize territorial conquest through violence. NATO Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg's recent comments reflect this tension: "We support all diplomatic efforts for sustainable peace, but sustainable peace requires respecting international law and Ukrainian sovereignty."

The "sustainable peace" qualifier reveals the trap. Trump's TACO psychology seeks immediate accommodation relief—end the conflict, reduce political pressure, claim victory. But sustainable peace requires confronting the mathematical constraints that make accommodation attractive: the costs of sustained confrontation with authoritarian aggression.

The Accommodation Trap: When Peace Enables War

The Alaska meeting threatens to demonstrate TACO psychology's most dangerous characteristic: accommodation that creates larger future conflicts while appearing to solve immediate problems. If Trump accommodates Putin's territorial gains to end the Ukraine conflict, he signals to every authoritarian regime that violence plus patience equals victory.

China watches the Alaska accommodation calculus intensely. If America accommodates Russian territorial conquest, Beijing's Taiwan calculations shift fundamentally. Why negotiate when accommodation rewards aggression? The TACO framework that began with trade disputes could end by accommodating the destruction of the post-1945 international order.

The mathematical constraints forcing Trump toward accommodation are real: American war fatigue, economic costs of sustained support, domestic political pressure to "end endless conflicts." But accommodating Putin's war crimes creates exponentially larger mathematical constraints: containing Chinese expansion, deterring Iranian aggression, maintaining alliance credibility.

The Moral Mathematics of Accommodation

Episode 9 forces a terrible question: when accommodation pressure becomes overwhelming, how do democracies maintain moral clarity? Trump's TACO psychology treats Ukrainian suffering as a negotiating inconvenience, not a moral imperative. The accommodation calculus reduces human rights to political costs.

Ukrainian mothers fleeing bombed hospitals, children separated from families, civilians executed in occupied territories—these become abstract variables in Trump's dealmaking equation. The moral weight that should constrain accommodation instead gets accommodated itself, reduced to "tragic but necessary compromises for peace."

Putin understands this moral accommodation better than Trump. The Russian strategy has always been to make Ukrainian suffering so immense that accommodation feels humane by comparison. End the bloodshed through territorial concessions, Putin suggests, and the killing stops. The accommodation trap closes: opposing the deal means prolonging suffering, even though the deal enables future suffering.

The Anchorage Precedent: What Victory Looks Like to Authoritarians

When Putin's plane lands in Anchorage, authoritarian regimes worldwide will study every detail of the accommodation. The precedent being set extends far beyond Ukraine: American presidents will meet with war criminals on American soil, legitimize territorial conquest, and call it peace.

The visual imagery alone serves Putin's strategic objectives. Putin walking on American territory as Trump's equal, shaking hands with the American president while Ukrainian refugees remain homeless, signing agreements that sacrifice democratic principles for temporary stability. These images will echo through history as the moment American leadership accommodated evil in the name of expedience.

Russian state media is already preparing the narrative: "Putin's Alaska Victory Proves Multipolar World Order." The accommodation will be framed not as compromise, but as American submission to Russian power. Putin returns to Moscow having achieved what Soviet leaders never could—American presidential recognition of Russian territorial conquest.

The Prayer for Failed Accommodation

For the first time in our TACO series, we must pray for accommodation failure. We must hope that the mathematical constraints that made eight previous accommodations inevitable will somehow break when confronted with genocide and war crimes.

The world prays that Trump will look at Ukrainian cities reduced to rubble and find constraints more powerful than political pressure. That he will see Ukrainian children in bomb shelters and discover mathematical equations that don't balance human rights against convenience. That he will confront Putin's war crimes and locate moral principles that resist accommodation pressure.

But eight episodes of evidence suggest accommodation is as inevitable as gravity. The only variable is how much America—and Ukraine—gives away in the process.

The Ultimate TACO Test

Episode 9 presents TACO psychology's ultimate test: when accommodation pressure meets moral imperative, which force proves stronger? Can mathematical constraints that reliably predict tariff retreats and trade extensions also predict the accommodation of democracy itself?

The Alaska meeting will answer whether TACO psychology operates at the level of civilizational principles, or whether some constraints prove too morally bankrupt to accommodate. Putin is betting on the former—that Trump Always Chickens Out, even when chickening out means legitimizing genocide.

As we watch Putin's plane approach Anchorage, the stakes transcend political analysis. This isn't about predicting accommodation cycles anymore—it's about whether accommodation pressure can overcome the moral foundations that separate democracy from authoritarianism.

The world watches Alaska not hoping for a deal, but praying for deal failure. Praying that for once, when it matters most, Trump will discover constraints more powerful than the ones that made accommodation inevitable eight times before.

Episode 10 may write itself, but humanity prays it won't need to. In Alaska, we discover whether Trump Always Chickens Out applies even to the defense of human dignity—or whether some principles prove too sacred to accommodate.

For Ukraine, for democracy, and for history's judgment, we pray this is the TACO that finally breaks. 🙏🙏
