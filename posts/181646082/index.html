The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 13: The Orbital Debris Time Bomb - Why This Could All Fail Catastrophically

December 25, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 25, 2025

Two thousand years ago, shepherds and wise men followed a star to Bethlehem, navigating by the heavens as humans had done for millennia. For most of human history, the stars have been our guides—for navigation, for inspiration, for connection to something larger than ourselves.

Today, we stand at a different crossroads. We’re no longer just looking at the stars—we’re filling the space around them with thousands of satellites, with plans for millions more. And in our rush to harness the cosmos for computing power, we risk making the heavens themselves inaccessible.

In late November 2025, a Chinese rocket stage passed dangerously close to a SpaceX satellite. The near-miss received minimal coverage.

In early December, the European Space Agency released updated collision probability models showing that at current satellite deployment rates, the chance of a catastrophic collision in low Earth orbit approaches 90% by 2035.

And still, SpaceX continues launching more satellites. Google continues testing. Blue Origin advances its secret program. And the collision probability ticks higher.

Everyone in the space industry knows the risk. Nobody wants to talk about it publicly. Because if orbital debris makes low Earth orbit unusable, tens of billions of dollars in current investments become worthless overnight.

In Parts 9-12, I documented how orbital AI data centers are technologically viable, economically approaching breakeven, and being aggressively developed by multiple billionaires. The hardware works. The business case is strengthening. The race is on.

But there’s a problem that makes all of that potentially irrelevant: physics doesn’t care about business cases.

And the physics of orbital debris—specifically a phenomenon called Kessler Syndrome—suggests that putting millions of satellites in low Earth orbit might be the fastest way to make space unusable for generations.

What Is Kessler Syndrome?

A thick ring of space junk orbits Earth in this fanciful artist's concept of a "Kessler syndrome" event

In 1978, NASA scientist Donald Kessler published a paper describing a cascading collision scenario that would make low Earth orbit unusable. The mechanism is simple and terrifying:

Step 1: A collision between two satellites creates thousands of debris fragments traveling at orbital velocities (7-8 km/second, or ~17,000 mph).

Step 2: Those fragments hit other satellites, creating more fragments.

Step 3: Those fragments hit more satellites, creating even more fragments.

Step 4: The cascade becomes self-sustaining. Even if you stop launching satellites entirely, the debris field continues growing from collisions between existing debris.

Step 5: Low Earth orbit becomes unusable for decades or longer. Satellites can’t operate without getting destroyed. The GPS constellation fails. Communications satellites are gone. Earth observation is impossible. The International Space Station becomes uninhabitable.

Kessler called it a “collision cascading scenario.” The industry calls it Kessler Syndrome. And once it starts, there’s no stopping it.

If you've seen the 2013 film Gravity starring Sandra Bullock and George Clooney, you've watched Kessler Syndrome play out in real time. What Alfonso Cuarón dramatized for entertainment—a Russian missile strike triggering a debris cascade that destroys the space shuttle and International Space Station—physicists have been warning about for decades. The movie wasn't science fiction. It was science fact, waiting to happen.

The terrifying part? We might already be in the early stages.

The Current Situation: Already Dangerous

As of December 2025, here’s what’s in low Earth orbit:

  • ~6,000 active Starlink satellites

  • ~1,000 other operational satellites (communications, Earth observation, scientific)

  • ~3,000 dead satellites that failed or completed their missions

  • ~34,000 tracked debris objects larger than 10 cm

  • ~900,000 estimated debris objects between 1-10 cm

  • ~130 million debris objects smaller than 1 cm

Anything larger than 1 cm can catastrophically damage a satellite. Anything larger than 10 cm can destroy it completely.

The International Space Station performs collision avoidance maneuvers multiple times per year. In 2022, they had to dodge debris 25 times. In 2023, it increased to 32 times. The trend is accelerating.

SpaceX’s Starlink satellites perform over 25,000 collision avoidance maneuvers per year. That’s not a typo. Twenty-five thousand maneuvers annually to avoid tracked debris.

And that’s with only 6,000 Starlink satellites operational.

The Math That Guarantees Catastrophe

Here’s where it gets horrifying: the plans call for millions of satellites.

Elon Musk has stated specifically that SpaceX plans to deploy “one megaton per year of satellites” to achieve 100 gigawatts of orbital AI capacity. At 100 kilowatts per satellite and roughly 2,000 kg mass per Starlink V3 satellite, that’s approximately 10,000 satellites per year being added to orbit.

Within five years, that’s 50,000 satellites. Within ten years, 100,000 satellites—just from SpaceX alone. And that’s the conservative 100 GW plan. Musk has suggested the upper bound might reach 300-500 gigawatts per year, which would triple or quintuple those numbers.

Google, Blue Origin, and multiple Chinese companies have parallel ambitions. The combined industry target could easily exceed several hundred thousand to over a million satellites by 2035.

Let’s use conservative math: 100,000 satellites for 100 GW of capacity. That’s still 17 times the current total satellite population of Earth.

100,000 satellites mean:

  • 17× the current total global satellite population

  • Collision probability increases roughly with the square of satellite population (because collisions require two objects)

  • 25,000 avoidance maneuvers/year becomes ~7 million maneuvers/year at that density

  • Every satellite performing multiple avoidance maneuvers daily

At that density, satellites spend more time dodging than operating. The computational overhead for collision tracking and avoidance exceeds the computing capacity the satellites provide.

And that assumes perfect tracking. In reality:

  • Objects below 10 cm aren’t consistently tracked

  • Many debris pieces are too small to track but large enough to kill satellites

  • Chinese and Russian military satellites sometimes don’t broadcast positions

  • Orbital prediction errors accumulate over time

You can’t dodge what you can’t see.

The Chinese Near-Miss: A Warning Shot

The incident that opened this essay wasn’t hypothetical. In late November 2025, a spent Chinese rocket stage—used to launch reconnaissance satellites—crossed paths with a SpaceX Starlink satellite with less than 1 kilometer of separation.

At orbital velocities, 1 km separation is 6 seconds of travel time. If the orbital prediction models were off by 0.1%, the objects would have collided.

The Chinese stage massed approximately 3 tons. The Starlink satellite massed about 260 kg. A collision at 7.5 km/second closing velocity would have released energy equivalent to several tons of TNT.

The result would have been:

  • Complete destruction of both objects

  • Thousands of debris fragments (every piece larger than 1 gram becomes a projectile)

  • Debris field spreading along the orbital path

  • Increased collision probability for every other satellite in similar orbits

One collision. Thousands of new hazards. All traveling at speeds where impact energy is measured in tons of explosive equivalent.

And we got lucky. The objects missed.

How many more near-misses before the luck runs out?

The Thermal Management Problem: The Other Time Bomb

Let’s say we solve the debris problem somehow—maybe with better tracking, active debris removal, or lower satellite populations. There’s another fundamental challenge that makes orbital data centers harder than the optimistic projections suggest: heat dissipation.

On Earth, data centers use water cooling, HVAC systems, and atmospheric convection. Total cooling requirements consume roughly 40% of data center energy.

In space, there’s no air and no water. The only way to reject heat is through thermal radiation. And radiative cooling is extremely inefficient compared to conduction or convection.

The International Space Station generates about 75 kilowatts of heat from electronics and crew. To dissipate that heat, the ISS has enormous radiator panels totaling 477 square meters (5,135 square feet).

That’s 6.4 square meters of radiator surface per kilowatt of heat rejection. And ISS radiators use active cooling loops with ammonia—not simple passive radiation.

Now scale that to AI computing:

A single Nvidia H100 GPU draws 700 watts under full load and generates roughly 700W of heat. A small AI cluster with 100 GPUs generates 70 kilowatts of heat—nearly as much as the entire ISS.

Using ISS radiator scaling, you’d need ~450 square meters of radiator surface for 100 GPUs.

SpaceX’s Starlink V3 satellites are designed for 150 kilowatts of computing capacity. Using ISS scaling, that requires ~960 square meters of radiators—roughly the size of 10 tennis courts per satellite.

Now remember: we need millions of these satellites for meaningful computing capacity.

The radiator area becomes the limiting factor. You can’t make radiators arbitrarily large—they need structural support, they add mass (which adds launch costs), and they create drag in low Earth orbit (which reduces satellite lifespan).

Google’s Project Suncatcher paper acknowledges this: “Advanced thermal interface materials and heat transport mechanisms would be required, preferably passive to maximize reliability.”

Translation: “We don’t actually know how to solve this yet, but we’re hoping better materials will help.”

Jensen Huang’s Skepticism: “This Is Just a Dream”

In October 2025, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang—whose H100 chips are at the center of the orbital data center revolution—was asked about space-based AI computing.

His response: “This is just a dream.”

Coming from the person whose company stands to profit enormously if orbital data centers succeed, that’s revealing. Huang isn’t a skeptic of space in general—Nvidia is actively working with Starcloud, supporting research, providing technical assistance.

But he’s also an engineer who understands thermal management, power systems, and operational reliability. And he’s apparently not convinced the physics works at scale.

What does Huang know that the optimists are ignoring?

Probably several things:

1. Chip reliability in radiation environments is unproven at scale. Google’s tests showed Trillium TPUs surviving 15 krad in a particle accelerator. That’s encouraging. But particle accelerator tests are artificial. Real space radiation includes:

  • Solar particle events (unpredictable intensity)

  • Galactic cosmic rays (constant background)

  • Secondary particles from radiation interactions

  • Cumulative effects over 5-10 year operational periods

Lab tests tell you the minimum survival threshold. They don’t tell you the failure rate in real operational environments.

2. Thermal management at computing densities is harder than current demonstrations. Starcloud-1 has one H100. That’s manageable. A satellite with 100-200 H100 equivalents? The thermal engineering becomes exponentially harder, not linearly harder.

3. Operational reliability without human maintenance is a massive unknown. On Earth, when a server fails, a technician swaps it out. In orbit, failed chips brick the satellite. You can’t open the box and replace components.

Google’s analysis assumes designing for redundancy and accepting that satellites become partially non-functional over time. But at what failure rate? If 20% of computing capacity dies in the first year, the economics collapse. If 5% dies, it’s manageable.

Nobody knows what the real number is. It won’t be known until satellites are operational for years.

4. The collision risk makes long-term investments dangerous. Even if everything works technically, the Kessler Syndrome risk means orbital infrastructure might become uninsurable. Banks won’t finance multi-billion dollar satellite constellations that could be wiped out by debris cascades.

Huang presumably sees all of this. And his “just a dream” comment suggests he thinks the problems are harder than the optimists admit.

The Environmental Contradiction

Here’s the dark irony: orbital data centers are marketed as environmentally friendly—no water usage, no land consumption, no fossil fuel power generation.

But getting satellites to orbit creates enormous environmental costs that nobody wants to calculate honestly.

A Falcon 9 launch burns roughly 400 tons of kerosene and liquid oxygen. CO₂ emissions: approximately 1,000 tons per launch. That’s equivalent to 200 cars driving for a year.

SpaceX’s Starship will be even larger—though using methane instead of kerosene, so slightly better emissions profile per kilogram of payload.

To launch 1 million satellites at 2,000 kg average mass (optimistic for data center satellites), you need:

  • 2 million tons of payload to orbit

  • Roughly 100,000 launches (at 20 tons per Starship launch)

  • 100 million tons of CO₂ emissions (at 1,000 tons per launch average)

That’s the CO₂ emissions of 20 million cars running for a year.

And that’s just the launches. It doesn’t count:

  • Manufacturing emissions for satellites

  • Mining rare earth elements for chips and batteries

  • Transportation and logistics

  • Ground station operations

  • Decommissioning and debris mitigation

A 2023 European Space Agency study (ASCEND) concluded that orbital data centers are carbon-neutral only if:

  • Launchers are fully reusable (100% propellant recovery)

  • Emissions are below 370 kg CO₂ per kg payload

  • Satellites operate for 10+ years without failures

Current rockets don’t meet those thresholds. And the 10-year operational requirement is aspirational, not demonstrated.

The environmental case for orbital data centers assumes technology that doesn’t exist yet at cost levels that haven’t been proven.

The Cascade Scenario: How It Plays Out

Let me walk through what Kessler Syndrome actually looks like when it starts:

Year 1 (2028): A collision between a dead Chinese satellite and an active SpaceX Starlink creates 3,000+ trackable debris pieces and hundreds of thousands of sub-centimeter fragments. SpaceX loses 12 additional satellites in the following month from secondary collisions. Insurance rates for orbital assets increase 50%.

Year 2 (2029): Another major collision—this time involving a defunct Russian military satellite. The debris field overlaps heavily used orbital zones. Collision avoidance maneuvers triple industry-wide. Two commercial Earth observation satellites are destroyed. Launch insurance becomes prohibitively expensive for some operators.

Year 3 (2030): Multiple mid-size collisions in quick succession. The ISS has to perform emergency maneuvers twice in one month. NASA and ESA jointly recommend limiting new launches to essential missions only. Private operators ignore the recommendation because billions are invested in orbital infrastructure.

Year 4 (2031): A cascade event. Debris from a collision hits three satellites simultaneously. Those collisions create debris that hits seven more satellites within 48 hours. Fourteen satellites are destroyed in one week. The industry recognizes the cascade has begun.

Year 5 (2032): Low Earth orbit at 500-600 km altitude becomes functionally unusable. Satellites still operate at higher orbits (800+ km), but those orbits are filling with debris too. Insurance companies stop underwriting orbital assets entirely. Banks recall loans on satellite projects.

Year 7-10 (2034-2037): Complete Kessler Syndrome. Collision rates exceed debris removal capabilities. New satellites launched into LEO have expected lifetimes measured in months, not years. The GPS constellation is damaged. Global communications infrastructure degrades. SpaceX, Google, and Blue Origin abandon orbital data centers as uneconomic.

Decades later: Natural orbital decay slowly clears debris from lower orbits, but higher orbits (above 800 km) remain polluted for centuries. Humanity’s access to space is severely constrained. The ISS is abandoned. Satellite internet is gone. Earth observation capabilities are degraded to pre-space-age levels.

That’s not science fiction. That’s the scenario NASA scientists have been warning about for 45 years.

And we’re voluntarily accelerating toward it by putting millions of satellites in orbit for data centers.

The Counter-Arguments: Why Optimists Think It’s Manageable

To be fair, not everyone thinks Kessler Syndrome is inevitable. The counter-arguments from industry:

1. “Active debris removal will solve it.” Companies like Astroscale are developing satellites that can capture dead satellites and deorbit them. If debris removal keeps pace with new launches, the problem is manageable.

Rebuttal: Debris removal is expensive, slow, and unproven at scale. Removing one dead satellite costs tens of millions of dollars. There are thousands of dead satellites. The math doesn’t work unless removal costs drop by 90%+.

2. “We’ll design satellites to deorbit quickly.” New regulations require satellites to deorbit within 5 years of mission end. If satellites burn up quickly, debris doesn’t accumulate.

Rebuttal: 5-year deorbit doesn’t help if collisions create debris during the operational period. And “designed to deorbit” doesn’t mean “will deorbit”—failures happen. Dead satellites become uncontrollable debris.

3. “Better tracking and AI-powered collision avoidance prevent collisions.” With better sensors and machine learning models, we can track smaller debris and perform avoidance maneuvers with higher precision.

Rebuttal: You still can’t dodge what you can’t see. Sub-centimeter debris is untraceable. And increasing avoidance maneuvers consumes fuel, shortens satellite lifetimes, and increases operational costs. At some point, avoidance becomes the mission.

4. “We won’t need millions of satellites—tech improves.” Better chips, better power systems, better designs mean fewer satellites needed for target computing capacity.

Rebuttal: True, but demand also grows. If AI computing requirements keep doubling, even with better technology, satellite counts still reach unmanageable levels.

The honest assessment: Maybe all of these solutions work. Maybe debris removal, better tracking, faster deorbit, and improved technology keep Kessler Syndrome at bay.

But “maybe” isn’t a risk management strategy when the downside is losing access to space for generations.

Why Orbital Might Be a Short-Term Solution Only

Here’s my conclusion:

Orbital data centers will probably work technically for 2025-2035. SpaceX will deploy hundreds or thousands of satellites. Google will test prototypes. Blue Origin will enter the market. Commercial operations will begin. Revenue will be generated.

But orbital won’t scale to the terawatt levels the AI revolution demands. The debris risk, thermal challenges, and operational complexity make millions of satellites untenable.

Orbital serves as a proving ground—a way to demonstrate that space-based computing works, validate the technology, establish customer acceptance, and build operational experience.

Then humanity transitions to lunar infrastructure for sustainable long-term computing capacity. The Moon doesn’t have the debris problem. The thermal environment is better (permanent shadows for free cooling). Expansion capacity is unlimited.

Orbital is the sprint. Lunar is the marathon. But the sprint matters because it proves the concept.

And if we’re lucky—if the optimists are right and Kessler Syndrome remains manageable—orbital infrastructure remains viable for latency-sensitive applications while the Moon handles the bulk computing load.

That’s the hybrid architecture I described in Part 10. It’s not either/or. It’s both, with each doing what it does best.

But there’s a third path emerging that could reduce debris risk entirely: optical laser communications from higher orbits.

In December 2025, China demonstrated a breakthrough that challenges the “millions of satellites” model. From geostationary orbit—36,000 kilometers up, not the 500-600 km of LEO constellations—a Chinese satellite achieved 1 gigabit per second downlink speeds using just 2 watts of power via laser communications.

That’s five times Starlink’s average speed, from one satellite in GEO, using power equivalent to a nightlight.

The technology uses adaptive optics to overcome atmospheric turbulence, achieving 91.1% signal quality. Earlier in 2025, Chinese scientists reportedly achieved 100 Gbps satellite-to-ground laser transmission—100 times faster than current Starlink performance.

The implications for debris risk are significant. As researchers noted, this approach “instead of saturating LEO, places a smaller number of optical satellites in higher orbit to cover broader areas more efficiently.”

Laser links are also harder to intercept or jam than radio frequency, making them attractive for secure communications. And unlike RF systems hampered by spectrum congestion, optical systems bypass those limits entirely.

This doesn’t solve the data center cooling problem or the latency issue for real-time applications. But it demonstrates that high-performance space communications don’t require millions of LEO satellites creating debris fields.

The race isn’t just SpaceX vs Google vs Blue Origin using traditional RF. China is pursuing a fundamentally different architecture that could sidestep the debris problem entirely—fewer satellites, higher orbits, optical instead of radio.

It’s another example of how rapidly the technology landscape is evolving, and why betting everything on one approach (LEO RF constellations) might be premature.

But if we’re unlucky—if debris cascades start sooner than expected—then orbital becomes a cautionary tale, and the push to the Moon accelerates out of necessity rather than strategic choice.

Either way, the next 10 years will determine whether orbital data centers are humanity’s computing future or a multi-billion-dollar mistake.

The shepherds and wise men had the stars to guide them on their journey. We still have that same sky. The question is whether we’ll have the wisdom to keep it accessible for the generations who’ll come after us, searching for their own guiding lights.
