The Runway

A companion essay to The Trump Accountability Project

March 23, 2026

Debris hangs from a damaged Air Canada Express jet that struck a fire truck at New York's LaGuardia Airport on Sunday. (Adam Gray/Reuters)

There are moments when the right response to a tragedy is silence. Not permanent silence — accountability journalism cannot afford that — but a pause long enough to acknowledge that two people are dead, that families woke this Monday morning to the worst news a family can receive, and that dozens more are recovering in New York hospitals from injuries sustained on a rainy runway at LaGuardia Airport just before midnight on Sunday.

The pilot and co-pilot of Air Canada Express Flight 8646 — Jazz Aviation employees operating a Bombardier CRJ-900 on the Montreal-to-New York run — have not yet been publicly named. Their families deserve that privacy as long as they need it. What is known is that they were professionals doing their jobs, that they brought 72 passengers safely through fog and rain to touchdown, and that they died when their aircraft struck a Port Authority fire truck that had been authorized to cross their runway. They were Canadian. The truck was American. The system that put both of them in the same space at the same moment was entirely the responsibility of the United States federal government.

I want to be precise about what I am doing in this essay, and what I am not doing. The National Transportation Safety Board has sent a go-team to LaGuardia. The investigation is hours old. I am not in a position to assign causation, and I will not pretend otherwise. What I can do — what accountability journalism demands — is document the systemic context in which this tragedy occurred, attribute speculation to those who have publicly offered it, and ask the questions that must eventually be answered.

What the audio tells us

Investigators inspect the wreckage of a fire truck that collided with an Air Canada Express jet that was landing at LaGuardia Airport. (Adam Gray/Reuters)

CNN obtained air traffic control recordings from LiveATC.com that establish the basic sequence of events. A United Airlines flight had aborted its takeoff on another runway due to a warning light and an odor in the cabin that had sickened flight attendants. A Port Authority Aircraft Rescue and Firefighting vehicle responded — and, according to the ATC audio, was granted permission by a controller to cross Runway 4. Simultaneously, Jazz Flight 8646 was on final approach to Runway 4, traveling at approximately 130 miles per hour.

The controller apparently recognized the collision risk too late. The New York Times reported that in the recordings, the controller can be heard telling the vehicle several times to stop — “Stop, Truck 1, stop!” — before the collision. Seconds later, the controller addressed the aircraft directly: “JAZZ 646, I see you collided with the vehicle.”

In a subsequent recording published by Newsweek and LiveATC, a controller is heard saying, simply: “I messed up.”

The investigation will determine what weight to assign those words. A moment of shock. A statement of fact. Possibly both.

One controller, two jobs

Newsweek published a formal analytical piece this morning identifying three urgent questions the NTSB must answer. The first is structural: initial reports suggest that a single controller may have been responsible for both local operations — runway clearances for landing aircraft — and ground operations, meaning taxiway and vehicle movements, at the time of the incident.

This is not automatically a violation. As Newsweek notes, FAA Order 7110.65 permits tower positions to be combined during periods of lower traffic volume. LaGuardia at midnight, in rain and fog, with a simultaneous emergency on another runway, may or may not qualify as such a period. That is precisely what investigators must determine.

What Newsweek’s analysis makes clear is the compounding nature of the scenario: the controller was managing routine arrivals alongside an active emergency response. A United jet with sick crew on one runway. An Air Canada jet on final approach to a second runway. A fire truck crossing between them. One controller.

The margin for error in that combination, at that airport, in those weather conditions, was vanishingly small.

The structural context: years in the making

The NTSB investigation will focus, properly, on what happened in those final seconds. But accountability journalism must look further back.

The FAA’s air traffic controller shortage is not a secret, a controversy, or a matter of partisan dispute. It is a documented, multi-decade institutional failure confirmed by the Government Accountability Office, the National Academy of Sciences, the FAA’s own workforce planning documents, and the union representing controllers themselves.

The numbers are stark. According to a GAO report published in January 2026, the number of American air traffic controllers has fallen approximately six percent over the past decade, while air traffic has increased by roughly ten percent. As of September 2024, more than forty percent of FAA terminal facilities were operating below the agency’s own eighty-five percent staffing threshold. Out of 313 FAA facilities included in the 2024 workforce plan, only 23 met or exceeded their staffing goals.

New York Terminal Radar Approach Control — the facility responsible for managing the airspace serving LaGuardia, JFK, and Newark — has been among the most critically understaffed in the country.

The causes are layered: government shutdowns in 2013 and 2018-2019 froze hiring and training. COVID-19 suspended academy operations for nearly two years. Becoming a fully certified controller takes up to six years. The mandatory retirement age is 56. Only approximately two percent of applicants complete the full training and certification pipeline.

None of this happened under the current administration. The staffing crisis has roots going back more than a decade, through multiple presidencies, multiple Congresses, multiple FAA administrators. That is the honest accounting.

What this administration added

In February 2025, the Department of Government Efficiency — operating outside normal FAA management structures, issuing terminations by email — fired approximately 400 FAA workers. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy stated publicly that no air traffic controllers were among those dismissed. That is technically accurate and substantively incomplete.

Among those fired, according to the Professional Aviation Safety Specialists union: aviation safety assistants, maintenance mechanics, aeronautical information specialists, and workers from the FAA’s Air Traffic Organization en route charting group — the team responsible for maintaining and updating the navigational charts used daily by controllers across the country. Eighteen air traffic control facilities lost their maintenance mechanics. Workers who kept radar systems operational, who updated airspace maps after incidents, who supported the physical infrastructure of the control system — gone.

One terminated charting worker told Washington DC radio station WTOP: “We have hundreds of changes every day across the country that need to be made. These are crucial and critical to what the air traffic controllers do for their routes, and without maps they could not operate.”

There was, we now know, a battle inside the administration about this. Fortune magazine reported in March 2025 that Transportation Secretary Duffy confronted Elon Musk directly in a Cabinet meeting, frustrated that DOGE had attempted to cut air traffic control employees themselves. Duffy reportedly asked what he was supposed to do while dealing with multiple recent plane crashes — this was just weeks after the Reagan National mid-air collision that killed 67 people in January 2025. He also publicly rejected Musk’s claim that air traffic controllers had been hired through DEI initiatives.

The President ultimately sided with Duffy: DOGE would advise, not decide. The 400 supporting workers had already been fired.

A government running on fumes

The disruption at LaGuardia did not occur in isolation. The New York Times noted Monday morning that the more than 400 flight cancellations at LaGuardia were compounding “travel strife at United States airports caused by the partial government shutdown.” A federal government operating without full appropriations was already straining the aviation system before Flight 8646 touched down on Runway 4.

That detail — reported as context by the Times, not as accusation — matters. It means the LaGuardia crash did not land on a stable, fully funded, fully operational system temporarily disrupted by one tragic event. It landed on a system already under stress from a funding impasse that the administration and Congress have failed to resolve. Two simultaneous federal failures, compounding each other, on the same Monday morning.

What the Secretary himself has said

This is not Canadian speculation. This morning, Transportation Secretary Duffy told NBC News that the FAA is actively looking into whether ATC staffing was a factor in the LaGuardia collision. When the Secretary of Transportation raises the question publicly, hours after a crash, the question has left the realm of speculation and entered the realm of official inquiry.

He is on his way to LaGuardia as this essay goes to press.

A note from the comments section

I encountered something this morning that I cannot leave unremarked. A reader of the Washington Post’s coverage posted the following comment about the crash: “You let Canadians fly planes, what do you expect? Dogsled skills don’t transfer to piloting.”

I debated whether to include this. I have decided I must, because it is not an outlier. It is a symptom.

For fourteen months, the Trump administration has conducted a sustained rhetorical campaign against Canada — annexation rhetoric, “51st state” contempt, economic warfare through tariffs, and a consistent messaging strategy that positions Canadians as lesser, dependent, provincial. When that contempt becomes normalized at the political level, it filters downward. It shows up in comments sections. It shows up as the reflexive first response to a tragedy in which the victims are Canadian.

The bitter factual irony requires no elaboration: the two Canadians are dead. The American coordination system that cleared a truck onto their runway is the subject of an investigation. The dogsled comment reveals more about the commenter’s information environment than it does about Canadian aviation.

What accountability requires

I want to be clear about what this essay is and is not claiming.

It is not claiming that DOGE’s FAA cuts caused the LaGuardia crash. The investigation has not established that, and I will not assert it.

It is claiming that two pilots are dead at an airport served by a chronically understaffed ATC system; that a controller working what may have been a combined position made a decision that proved fatal; that the administration has cut supporting FAA infrastructure over the explicit objection of its own Transportation Secretary; that a partial government shutdown was already compounding aviation stress across the system at the moment of the crash; and that the Secretary himself considers ATC staffing a live question in this investigation.

It is claiming that this is the second major fatal aviation incident in American airspace in fourteen months. That the pattern exists whether or not it is convenient to acknowledge it. That a system operating at the edge of its capacity — with supporting infrastructure degraded, political will to fund recovery repeatedly subordinated to the ideology of reduction, and a government shutdown layered on top — is a system that has narrowed its margin for human error to the point where a single controller’s mistake on a rainy midnight runway becomes irrecoverable.

The NTSB will tell us what happened on Runway 4 at LaGuardia on the night of March 22, 2026.

Accountability journalism’s job is to ensure that when that report arrives, no one can claim they didn’t see the conditions that made it possible.

UPDATE — March 25, 2026

Since this essay was published, The Atlantic has released a detailed analysis by aviation writer Colleen Mondor that materially advances the picture.

The NTSB confirmed at a Tuesday news conference that two controllers were in the tower at the time of the collision — not one. But that number is less reassuring than it sounds. The NTSB acknowledged it has received conflicting information about which controller was responsible for ground control at the critical moment, and whether that controller was simultaneously managing arrivals. NTSB Chair Jennifer Homendy cautioned against attributing the crash to distraction, but described conditions at LaGuardia that night as “a heavy workload environment” and raised the agency’s standing concern about fatigue during lightly staffed midnight shifts.

Mondor introduces the operative concept: task saturation. The cascade began not at 11:37pm when the collision occurred, but a full hour earlier, when United Flight 2384 aborted two successive takeoffs and declared an emergency on the taxiway. Multiple emergency vehicles were dispatched. Frontier Flight 4195 was taxiing nearby. Multiple aircraft were inbound. The fire truck needed to cross Runway 4 to reach the United aircraft — and Air Canada Flight 8646 was on final approach to Runway 4. The controller’s full statement after the crash was: “We were dealing with an emergency earlier, and I messed up.”

Transportation Secretary Duffy, standing at LaGuardia on Monday, called the tower “well staffed” — 33 controllers against a target of 37. The NTSB is still investigating how many certified professional controllers were on duty, what happened at shift change, and whether anyone was available to relieve the controller at the time of the collision. Normally, Homendy said, he would have been relieved. He was still on duty several minutes after the crash.

The Atlantic piece closes with a historical footnote that deserves to travel further. In 1967, the FAA requested $100 million to modernize equipment and hire more controllers, reporting that its 14,000 controllers and technicians simply could not keep pace with traffic growth. President Lyndon Johnson denied the request and told the agency to maintain safety with existing funding. An airline source told the New York Times that year that the president had told the agency “not to allow any crashes” while insisting it “make the service fit the system” rather than the reverse.

In 2025, the United States had 10,800 certified professional controllers and 4,869 technicians — figures Mondor notes are shockingly close to those from nearly sixty years ago, despite air traffic having exploded in the interval.

The strategy hasn’t changed. Only the consequences have compounded.

POSTSCRIPT — March 27, 2026

They have names now. Canada brought them home yesterday.

The body of First Officer Mackenzie Gunther, 24, of Peterborough, Ontario, was carried off a plane at Ottawa International Airport Thursday afternoon by fellow pilots. Captain Antoine Forest, 30, of Coteau-du-Lac, Quebec, was then flown onward to Montréal–Trudeau International Airport. CBC News Hundreds of pilots and flight staff stood in the rain in front of Air Canada’s Montreal headquarters to honour Forest as the procession rolled through after sundown. Global News

Gunther had graduated from Seneca Polytechnic in Toronto with a degree in aviation technology in 2023 and joined Jazz Aviation directly after graduation. NBC News Forest had dreamed of flying since childhood. His great-aunt Jeannette Gagnier confirmed his death, and the mayor of Coteau-du-Lac wrote: “Antoine was one of our own. In a community like ours, everyone knows someone who knew him.” ABC7 Chicago

What they did in those final seconds matters. The cockpit voice recorder shows the first officer handed control to the captain just six seconds before impact. Captain Forest immediately applied hard braking. CBC News Passenger Clément Lelièvre credited their “incredible reflexes” with saving lives, saying he believed the braking made the difference between a collision and a massacre. ABC7 Chicago

The system around them had already failed. The fire truck carried no transponder, meaning controllers were tracking it by less precise radar. Two air traffic controllers were each simultaneously working two positions on a late-night shift. CBC News A runway incursion warning system failed to sound an alarm. Wikipedia The partial government shutdown had left the airport operating at reduced capacity. The controller, seconds after clearing the truck across the runway, could be heard on the audio saying “I messed up.”

Two young Canadians absorbed the consequences of American institutional failure. They did not make the system that killed them. They simply flew into it — and in their final six seconds, they chose to fight.

Rest, Captain Forest. Rest, First Officer Gunther.
