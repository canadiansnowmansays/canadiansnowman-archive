Stephen's Sunday Satire

The Department of Gains

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. is winning the war on Twinkies. The Twinkies are winning the war on public health.

March 29, 2026

A dispatch from the nation that invented the six-pack cabinet secretary

Stephen’s Sunday Satire Series

Good news, America. Your children are safe.

Not from measles, obviously. But from a man in a Twinkie costume? Finished. Done. Suplexed into the canvas, punched repeatedly in what I can only assume is the cream filling, and left defeated on the mat while flames shoot up around the triumphant, shirtless, 72-year-old Secretary of Health and Human Services, set — and this detail is load-bearing — to Limp Bizkit.

The Twinkie never stood a chance.

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. posted this video to X last week, where millions watched it. It is AI-generated, which means somewhere an actual human being sat at an actual computer and thought: yes. This. This is the health communication strategy of the United States government. That human being is 26 years old and has the title of Digital Director at the Department of Health and Human Services, which is either the best job in America or a sign that civilization has entered its terminal meme phase. Possibly both.

The Twinkie suplex video — screenshot from Kennedy's X] Secretary Kennedy demonstrates the government's official position on processed snack foods.

Your children are safe, incidentally, from a Secretary of Health who has publicly described snorting cocaine off a toilet seat in his younger years and who now appears, for all the world, as a shirtless wellness icon suplexing snack foods while flames shoot up around him. The arc is presented as inspiration. The man fell, and the man rose, and the man is now jacked, and the jeans are waterproof, and the message is: look what is possible.

What the memes do not address — what they are specifically engineered not to address — is the infrastructure of survival that made the arc possible. Robert F. Kennedy Jr. did not get clean because of personal discipline alone. He got clean because he had money, and family, and lawyers, and the kind of institutional protection that attaches itself to certain surnames like a second skin. He had access to the best treatment facilities in America. He had people around him whose job, formally or informally, was to ensure that a Kennedy did not become a statistic. He had, in short, every resource that the children his agency is supposed to protect do not have — the children in communities where the harm reduction programs Kennedy has been cutting were often the only infrastructure standing between a bad decision at nineteen and a death at twenty-two.

The cocaine survived. The man survived. The system that would have caught a different child did not need to activate, because a different system — one denominated in wealth and access and the specific gravity of a famous name — activated instead.

That system is not available as an action figure accessory.

The message the memes send about drugs is, at best, confused. The message they send about whose children get to survive their mistakes, and whose children we have apparently decided to leave to chance, is considerably clearer. It is just not the kind of clarity that photographs well next to a cold plunge.

A brief tour of America’s most productive Cabinet office

In the months since taking office, Kennedy has been, by any metric you care to apply to a man in blue jeans who works out with Kid Rock, extraordinarily busy.

He has appeared, in AI form, as a Super Smash Bros. character launching frosted donuts into oblivion. He has been rendered as a collector’s action figure — retailing, spiritually if not literally, at $19.99 — whose listed accessories include “waterproof jeans,” one peregrine falcon (for rescue operations), and a conspicuous absence of any functional vaccine policy. He cold-plunged. He pumped iron. He drank whole milk with Kid Rock, which is either a public health initiative or a bet, and I am genuinely unsure which. On Christmas Eve, he called Santa Claus to personally recommend the treadmill. Santa, to his credit, has not publicly responded.

The HHS official X account celebrated Kennedy’s birthday by posting a photograph of him cutting into a steak adorned with birthday candles.

A steak. With birthday candles. Posted by the Department of Health.

Somewhere, a CDC epidemiologist who just had their position eliminated is watching

Waterproof jeans sold separately. Children’s vaccine schedule: consult your lawyer.

This is an official communication from the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. No, really.

The thing about the Twinkies

Here is what makes Kennedy’s meme strategy genuinely impressive as satire — and I say this as someone who writes satire for a living, from a country where the Health Minister’s most viral moment involved explaining the flu shot — it has successfully convinced millions of people that body-slamming a Twinkie in an AI video constitutes doing something about Twinkies.

The Twinkie is still on supermarket shelves.

Kennedy promised — specifically, loudly, on camera, while presumably wearing jeans — that “President Trump and I are going to stop the mass poisoning of American children” through artificial food dyes. The actual result: some food companies have made voluntary pledges to remove synthetic dyes. Most of these pledges take effect next year. Some the year after. Doritos, flagship vessel of the American snack armada, introduced a dye-free version of its chips and also continued selling the original bright-orange version, apparently on the theory that you can have both a concession and a product line.

Telling people to eat real food and actually removing the fake food from existence are, I cannot stress this enough, different things. One requires legislation, regulatory authority, and political will. The other requires an AI animator and a Limp Bizkit licence.

Guess which one Kennedy chose.

The frosted donut: vanquished. The food system: unchanged.

Now about those children

I want to be precise here, because precision is the thing that separates satire from cruelty.

During Kennedy’s tenure as the man responsible for America’s health, the United States has experienced its largest measles outbreak in 33 years. Over 1,200 confirmed cases. Three deaths. Two of them children. Ninety-two percent of cases in people who were unvaccinated or whose vaccination status was unknown. This is happening in the country that eliminated measles in the year 2000 — eliminated it, past tense, done, gone — and is now watching it return like a sequel nobody asked for, to a franchise everyone thought was finished.

There are no HHS memes about this.

No AI-generated Kennedy video in which he suplexes the measles virus. No action figure protecting children from preventable disease. No shirtless cold plunge in the name of herd immunity. Just silence, a steak birthday cake, and the quiet sound of the Trump administration reportedly trying to rein Kennedy in ahead of the midterms because, as a Republican pollster’s memo recently concluded with the clinical bluntness of a man who has seen the crosstabs, “vaccine skepticism is bad politics.”

The Secretary of Health’s anti-vaccine views, it turns out, are unpopular. Who could have predicted this. Who among us saw it coming. Certainly not the man who knew Kennedy’s views on vaccines and made him Secretary of Health.

The meta-joke

Here is the thing that keeps me up at night, in the way that only truly magnificent absurdity can:

Kennedy’s meme team has accidentally produced the most devastating satire of Kennedy imaginable. The action figure with waterproof jeans and no healthcare policy. The Twinkie suplex while children catch measles. The birthday steak while the CDC burns. If a satirist had invented this character — the 72-year-old carnivore falconer with a six-pack who fights processed food in CGI while presiding over a public health collapse — editors would have sent it back. Too on the nose. Nobody would believe it. Dial it back.

And yet here we are.

The genius of it — and I use “genius” in the way one might describe a perfect natural disaster — is that the meme strategy works precisely because it is entertaining. It is genuinely funny. Kennedy’s team knows it is funny. They are in on the joke, right up to the point where the joke is on the children in Texas with measles, at which point they post a picture of a birthday steak and log off.

University of Michigan professor Donald Moynihan, who studies this administration’s approach to social media, has noted that Kennedy’s content lacks the “emphasis on dominance, control, and fear” found elsewhere in the White House’s digital output — which has recently included splicing footage of missile strikes with Call of Duty clips to celebrate the Iran war. Kennedy’s memes are, by comparison, cosy. Wholesome, even. Be healthy! Eat real food! The Secretary of Health has a six-pack and he is using it for good!

The malevolence, in Kennedy’s case, is in the gap. Not in what the memes say. In what they bury.

A note from Canada

I watch all of this from Toronto, where our Health Minister’s most aggressive content strategy is a press release, and where the concept of a Cabinet Secretary going viral for his jeans has not yet penetrated the national consciousness. We have our own political absurdities — I will not pretend otherwise — but we have not yet reached the phase of governance where the action figure is the platform.

I was thirteen years old when they shot Uncle Jack. I mourned him in Toronto, as millions did across the world — because Camelot, it turned out, belonged to everyone. Not just Americans. Not just Democrats. Everyone who believed, however briefly, that a leader could be genuinely serious about serious things without requiring a digital team to make it look that way. The man who stared down Nikita Khrushchev across thirteen days of civilizational terror communicated his authority through something that has not yet been successfully rendered in AI: the absolute, bone-deep credibility of a man who understood that the performance and the substance had to be the same thing, because the cost of the gap was thermonuclear war. He did not need waterproof jeans. He did not need Limp Bizkit. He needed to be real, and he was.

His nephew is fighting Twinkies in CGI.

The Twinkie is still on the shelf.

What I can tell you, from this comfortable northern distance, is that what Kennedy has achieved is a masterwork of distraction. The meme and the mandate exist in parallel universes. In the meme universe, Kennedy is a tireless warrior for American health — jacked, committed, suplexing snack foods into submission with the focused intensity of a man who has never met a Twinkie he couldn’t beat. In the mandate universe, the measles is back, the CDC vaccine advisors have been fired, the food dye ban is a voluntary suggestion, and the Secretary of Health is being quietly managed by political operatives who have read the polls.

The meme version won, though.

Millions of views. Zero accountability. Waterproof jeans.
