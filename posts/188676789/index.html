Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 4: Washington’s Cuba — The Batista Years (1898–1959)

March 4, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Fulgencio Batista surveys his island, 1950s. He ran Cuba as a personal franchise — for himself, for the American mob, and for the corporations whose investments Washington was determined to protect. The man pointing at the map owned everything on it.

To understand why a population would follow a revolution into the unknown — why ordinary Cubans in the 1950s were willing to risk everything to support a young lawyer-turned-guerrilla fighting from the mountains — you have to understand what they were living in. Not the Cuba of tourist brochures, where Havana’s hotels gleamed and its casinos glittered and American celebrities lounged by swimming pools. Not the Cuba that Washington preferred to see, a reliable Caribbean ally and profitable investment destination. But the Cuba that most Cubans actually inhabited: a country of breathtaking inequality, systematic corruption, routine political violence, and a foreign dependency so deep that the island’s basic economic decisions were effectively made in Washington and New York rather than Havana.

The sixty years between the American occupation of 1898 and the Revolution of 1959 are essential reading for anyone who wants to understand Cuba’s modern history, and they are also essential context for understanding why the Cuban government’s narrative of revolutionary liberation — however distorted it may have become in practice — found such genuine resonance. You cannot understand the Revolution without understanding what it was revolting against. And what it was revolting against was not merely Fulgencio Batista, though Batista was monstrous enough. It was a system that had been constructed, maintained, and repeatedly rescued by the United States, and that served American interests at the direct expense of the Cuban people.

The American Occupation and the Platt Amendment

The United States occupied Cuba from 1898 to 1902, and the conditions under which it left — the Platt Amendment — ensured that the occupation never truly ended. It simply changed form.

The Platt Amendment, written by U.S. Senator Orville Platt of Connecticut and inserted into the Cuban constitution under American pressure, gave the United States the right to intervene in Cuban affairs whenever it judged Cuba’s independence or Cuban lives or property to be at risk. It also prohibited Cuba from entering into any foreign treaty without American approval, limited Cuba’s ability to take on public debt, and confirmed American control of Guantánamo Bay as a naval base. These provisions were not suggestions. They were conditions of withdrawal. The Cubans could have their nominal independence, but only on these terms.

The Platt Amendment was formally repealed in 1934 — one of Franklin Roosevelt’s Good Neighbour Policy reforms — but by then its work was largely done. The pattern of American intervention it had established had shaped Cuban political culture for thirty years, accustoming Cuban politicians to operating under external supervision and American investors to treating Cuba as an effectively managed appendage of the U.S. economy.

Between 1906 and 1922, the United States intervened militarily in Cuba three times — in 1906, when a disputed election led to political chaos; in 1912, to protect American property during a rebellion by Afro-Cuban veterans; and from 1917 to 1922, during and after the First World War. Each intervention reinforced the message: Cuba’s sovereignty was real only to the extent that Washington permitted it to be.

Political cartoon of the Platt Amendment as a chain/leash on Cuba—satirical, historical illustration tying to U.S. grip.

The American Economic Grip

The military interventions were, in many ways, less significant than the economic penetration that accompanied them. American capital flooded into Cuba after 1898, and within a few decades had come to dominate the island’s economy to an extraordinary degree.

By the 1920s, American companies owned approximately 70 percent of Cuba’s sugar industry — the foundation of the entire economy. American firms controlled Cuba’s utilities, its railroads, its telephone system, its nickel mines. The United Fruit Company owned vast tracts of agricultural land in eastern Cuba. American banks held Cuban debt. American hotels and casinos catered to American tourists who brought their dollars to an island that had been made hospitable specifically for their comfort.

This economic penetration had two consequences for ordinary Cubans that are crucial for understanding the revolutionary period. The first was structural poverty. The sugar economy was extraordinarily volatile — prices swung wildly with world market conditions, and when sugar prices fell, as they did catastrophically in the early 1920s and again during the Great Depression, the Cuban economy collapsed. Workers who had been employed during the zafra, the harvest season, were unemployed for months at a time. Rural poverty was endemic. The benefits of Cuba’s considerable agricultural wealth flowed primarily to American shareholders and a thin layer of Cuban landowners and professionals, not to the cane cutters, mill workers, and agricultural labourers who did the physical work.

The second consequence was political corruption. The American economic presence created enormous incentives for Cuban politicians to serve American interests rather than Cuban ones — to maintain the conditions that American investors required, to suppress labour organising that threatened American profits, to award contracts and concessions to American firms in exchange for personal enrichment. The result was a political culture of almost operatic corruption, where public office was understood primarily as an opportunity for private accumulation, and where the line between politics, organised crime, and American business interests was often invisible.

The Rise of Batista

The Cuba of the 1930s was a country in crisis. The sugar price collapse of the late 1920s and the Great Depression had devastated the economy. The government of Gerardo Machado, which had begun promisingly with investment in infrastructure, had descended into dictatorship — political opponents were imprisoned, tortured, and killed, and the secret police operated with terrifying impunity.

In 1933, a general strike and military rebellion toppled Machado. What followed was a brief, extraordinary moment — a provisional government with genuine reformist ambitions, led initially by a university professor named Ramón Grau San Martín, that enacted an eight-hour working day, granted women the right to vote, abrogated the Platt Amendment, and generally alarmed American business interests with its nationalism.

The Roosevelt administration, through its ambassador Sumner Welles, declined to recognise the Grau government and made clear its displeasure. Within months, the Grau government had been overthrown — not by American military force, but by a Cuban sergeant named Fulgencio Batista, who mobilised the army against the reformers, installed a more compliant government, and positioned himself as the indispensable strongman of Cuban politics.

Batista is a figure of considerable complexity. He was Afro-Cuban — mixed race, from a poor family in Oriente province — and his rise to power was in some ways a genuine rupture with the white elite that had dominated Cuban politics since independence. He was intelligent and politically nimble, capable of pragmatic flexibility. Between 1940 and 1944, Cuba actually had a democratic constitution that Batista himself had supported, and he governed with sufficient restraint during that period to allow free elections and the peaceful transfer of power to Grau San Martín.

But Batista was also, fundamentally, a man of appetite and ambition, and when the political calculations changed he changed with them. After losing the 1952 election — running a distant third, with no prospect of victory — he staged a coup in March of that year, three months before the vote, cancelled the elections, and established a military dictatorship that would last until January 1, 1959.

The Batista Dictatorship

Iconic Nacional Hotel overlooking the sea—emblem of Batista-era luxury, Mafia ties, and tourist Havana

The Cuba of the 1950s under Batista is often described through the lens of its glamour — and the glamour was real. Havana was, in a certain sense, one of the most exciting cities in the Western Hemisphere. The Nacional Hotel, the Riviera, the Capri — built or renovated with American Mafia money, operated by figures like Meyer Lansky and Santo Trafficante — attracted American celebrities and tourists who could gamble, drink, and pursue pleasures less available at home. Frank Sinatra performed at the Tropicana. Hemingway lived outside Havana and fished in its waters and drank in its bars. Graham Greene visited and wrote Our Man in Havana, a novel that captured the corrupt absurdity of the city with devastating precision.

But beneath the glamour was a reality that most of the tourists did not see, or preferred not to see.

The Batista dictatorship killed between 10,000 and 20,000 Cubans during its seven years — political opponents, student activists, suspected rebels, people who were in the wrong place at the wrong time. Batista’s secret police, the SIM, under the command of Colonel Blanco Rico and later the notorious Rolando Masferrer, operated without legal constraint. Prisoners were tortured and killed, their bodies sometimes left in public places as warnings. Political dissent of any kind was dangerous.

The corruption had reached levels that staggered even in a country accustomed to corrupt government. Cabinet ministers, military officers, and police commanders were openly on the payroll of American gambling interests. Public contracts were allocated by kickback. Customs duties were evaded by arrangement. Batista himself accumulated a personal fortune estimated at hundreds of millions of dollars, much of it transferred abroad in preparation for the inevitable day when the arrangement might end.

Meanwhile, the structural poverty of the Cuban countryside remained essentially unchanged. Cuba’s literacy rate — around 76 percent — was among the highest in Latin America, but education quality in rural areas was dismal. Healthcare was concentrated in Havana. In Oriente province, in the Sierra Maestra mountains where Fidel Castro would eventually establish his guerrilla base, conditions were medieval. Rural families lived in bohíos without electricity or running water, farming subsistence plots on land they did not own, their children suffering from malnutrition and preventable disease.

Washington and Batista

The Eisenhower administration’s relationship with Batista is one of the clearest examples of Cold War realpolitik at its most corrosive. Batista was a brutal dictator with a documented record of torture and political murder. He was also reliably anti-communist, friendly to American business interests, and willing to maintain the conditions under which American capital could operate profitably in Cuba. For the Eisenhower administration, these considerations outweighed the human rights record by a considerable margin.

Havana, 1958. Campaign posters for Batista's hand-picked successor line the streets outside the Presidential Palace. The election was held in November. By January 1, 1959, Batista had fled and Castro was in Havana. Rivero Agüero never took office.

The United States provided Batista’s government with military equipment and training throughout the 1950s. American-made weapons — tanks, aircraft, napalm — were used against Cuban civilians. American officials met regularly with Batista and his ministers, providing the diplomatic legitimacy that helped sustain the regime. When the Cuban army was struggling against Castro’s rebels in the mountains of Oriente, it was American military advisers who tried to help them improve their counterinsurgency tactics.

This support was not a secret. Cubans watched American weapons kill Cuban civilians. They saw American officials smile for photographs with Batista. They understood that the most powerful nation in the world had made a calculation: the suffering of the Cuban people was an acceptable price for an agreeable business climate.

That understanding did more to radicalise Cuban opinion than any revolutionary pamphlet could have. When Castro’s guerrillas came down from the mountains in January 1959, they were greeted by enormous crowds of Cubans who had lived under the system that Washington had sustained, and who wanted something different. Whatever the Revolution’s subsequent failures and crimes, its initial popular support was genuine and comprehensible. It was the accumulated product of sixty years of American-backed exploitation and thirty years of American-backed dictatorship.

Marco Rubio’s family left Cuba in 1956, three years before the Revolution — they were part of the middle and professional class that benefited from and operated within the Batista system. The Cuba they left was a Cuba that the United States had helped build. It is one of the great ironies of the current blockade that its chief architect in the Trump administration is a man whose own family history is inseparable from the system that made the Revolution both possible and, for many Cubans, necessary.

The Approaching Storm

Fidel Castro and rebels in the Sierra Maestra—bearded guerrillas with rifles, jungle setting—evokes the opposition building in the mountains.

By the mid-1950s, the Batista dictatorship was generating opposition from nearly every sector of Cuban society. University students organised and demonstrated, and were beaten and killed by the police. The urban underground — the Movimiento 26 de Julio, named for the date of Castro’s failed 1953 attack on the Moncada barracks — planted bombs and assassinated regime officials. Rural Cubans in Oriente harboured the guerrillas who were beginning to build a genuine military challenge in the mountains.

In the Sierra Maestra, Castro and a small band of survivors from the disastrous Granma landing of 1956 were slowly expanding their operation. They were not communists, at least not yet — they were nationalists in the tradition of Martí, committed to ending the Batista dictatorship and restoring democratic government. Their politics were deliberately vague on the economic questions that would have alarmed American investors, and Castro was careful, in the late 1950s, to cultivate reasonable American journalists — Herbert Matthews of the New York Times visited him in the mountains in 1957 and wrote a profile that presented him as a democratic reformer — while avoiding the ideological clarity that would provoke American intervention.

Whether Castro’s eventual embrace of communism was always his intention, or whether it was a response to American hostility after the Revolution, is a question historians still debate. What is not debatable is that the question would not have arisen in the same way if the United States had not spent sixty years treating Cuba as a commercial appendage rather than a country, if it had not sustained Batista’s dictatorship with weapons and diplomatic cover, and if the accumulated weight of American-backed exploitation had not made radical change the only option that millions of Cubans believed might actually alter their lives.

The storm was coming. Washington had helped build it.
