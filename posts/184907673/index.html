The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 12: What Dies With Khamenei?

April 23, 2026

The Islamic Republic’s new operating structure: a figurehead Supreme Leader who has not appeared in public in seven weeks, statements read by television anchors, AI-generated videos simulating his presence — and behind all of it, the Revolutionary Guards running the show. The clerical republic is, functionally, a security state. Khomeini’s revolution has become its own worst enemy. Source: Rudaw / AFP

Editor’s Note

I wrote this essay prior to the Iran War beginning — asking a hypothetical question: what dies with Khamenei? I was writing about an 86-year-old man in declining health, projecting forward to an inevitable but not-yet-arrived death. I was writing, as journalists do, slightly ahead of events.

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei died on February 28, 2026. His daughter died with him. His son-in-law. His grandson. Top military commanders. The strike was precise and devastating. He died, by most accounts, underground — in or near the compound beneath which he had sheltered before.

Throughout this series, I used the image of the bunker as a metaphor: the man who built a defensive architecture incapable of serving the people it claimed to protect. The metaphor held to the end.

On March 9, the Assembly of Experts — under IRGC pressure — named Mojtaba Khamenei, the Supreme Leader’s son, as his successor. The revolution that overthrew a monarchy just completed a dynastic transfer of power. The Guardian’s final act was to become the Shah.

Seven weeks later, the new Supreme Leader has not appeared in public once. His statements are read on television by news anchors. The regime has deployed AI-generated video to simulate his presence. Sources close to his inner circle report severe injuries from the February 28 strike — facial disfigurement, reportedly a lost leg. Trump, managing a fragile ceasefire and naval blockade, publicly called Iran’s government “seriously fractured.” He was being diplomatic.

What dies with Khamenei? I wrote this answer before I knew it would be the real one. Read it as what it is: a question that history has now answered, written by someone who saw the answer coming before the question was settled. Section VII has not required a word of revision.

— Stephen, April 2026

The Death of Revolutionary Legitimacy

Khamenei has been ruling since 1989. He’s the only leader many young protestors have ever known. The institutions he’s empowered-and empower him-are likeminded hardliners whose main source of diversity is whether their beards and turbans are black or white

Every revolution eventually faces its reckoning. The gap between promise and reality grows until either the revolution reforms itself or the people reject it entirely.

The French Revolution devoured its own children through the Terror, then produced Napoleon’s dictatorship. The Russian Revolution’s promise of workers’ paradise produced Stalin’s gulags. The Chinese Revolution’s Great Leap Forward caused mass starvation.

Iran’s Islamic Revolution promised divine justice, human dignity, and national sovereignty. Thirty-seven years under Ayatollah Ali Khamenei produced poverty, repression, and international humiliation.

When Khamenei dies — and at 86 years old with declining health, that moment approaches — what dies with him?

Not just a man. Not just a regime. But the very idea that Islamic government, as conceived by this revolution, can deliver what it promises.

I. THE THEOLOGICAL FAILURE

The Islamic Republic rests on one foundational claim: rule by Islamic jurists (velayat-e faqih — guardianship of the jurist) produces just, moral, and successful government because it reflects God’s will.

This wasn’t presented as political preference or cultural choice. This was presented as theological truth: Islamic government is superior to secular government because it embodies divine justice.

Thirty-seven years of Khamenei’s rule test that claim.

The Experiment

Think of it as empirical theology. If Islamic government (as theorized by Khomeini and implemented by Khamenei) truly reflects divine will and divine justice, we should observe:

  • Justice in society (Islamic adalah)

  • Mercy toward the weak (Islamic rahmah)

  • Honesty in governance (Islamic sidq)

  • Prosperity through righteous economics (Islamic halal)

  • Social harmony (Islamic silm)

  • Protection of the oppressed (Islamic nusrah)

  • Moral leadership (Islamic qudwa)

The Results

  • Justice: Systematic corruption, rigged elections, political prisoners, torture

  • Mercy: Thousands killed in protests, executed after rushed trials, shot for demanding rights

  • Honesty: Systematic lying — about economy, casualties, responsibility for failures

  • Prosperity: 50% poverty, 60% child malnutrition, worthless currency

  • Social harmony: Generational divide, ethnic tensions, mass emigration, nationwide protests

  • Protection of oppressed: The Guardian kills those seeking economic justice

  • Moral leadership: Elite enrichment, hypocrisy, and violence contradict Islamic ethics

The Verdict

Either:

A) Islamic government (as implemented) doesn’t work — meaning the theory was wrong, or

B) This wasn’t true Islamic government — meaning Khamenei failed to implement it

Either conclusion is devastating for the Islamic Republic’s legitimacy.

  • If (A): The entire revolutionary project was based on false premise.

  • If (B): Thirty-seven years of absolute power by a Supreme Leader who claimed divine guidance produced failure anyway.

The Shia Theological Crisis

Shia Islam emphasizes justice as central principle. The Twelve Imams — especially Imam Ali and Imam Husayn — are revered for standing against tyranny and oppression.

Imam Husayn died at Karbala (680 CE) rather than submit to Umayyad tyranny. His martyrdom defines Shia identity: refusal to accept injustice, even at cost of life.

Every year, millions of Iranians participate in Ashura commemorations, mourning Husayn’s stand against oppression.

The cognitive dissonance is overwhelming: commemorating Husayn’s resistance to tyranny while living under Khamenei’s tyranny, proclaimed in Islam’s name.

Iranian protesters understand this perfectly. When they chant “This country won’t be fixed until we bury the mullahs!” they’re not rejecting Islam — they’re rejecting clerical distortion of Islam.

What Dies: The Claim That Clerical Rule = Divine Justice

When Khamenei dies, this theological pretense dies with him. The experiment ran for 47 years (1979–2026). The results are clear. Islamic government, as theorized and implemented by this revolution, produced outcomes contradicting every Islamic principle it claimed to uphold.

Future Iranian governments may be Islamic in culture or identity. But the specific claim — that rule by Islamic jurists (velayat-e faqih) produces superior governance — will be discredited by Khamenei’s legacy.

II. THE INSTITUTIONAL COLLAPSE

Institutions outlive individuals. That’s their purpose. Strong institutions survive leadership transitions, adapt to changing circumstances, and provide continuity across generations.

Khamenei inherited institutions from the revolution’s founding. He systematically destroyed them.

What Khomeini Left

When Ayatollah Khomeini died (1989), he left:

  • Assembly of Experts: Body of clerics with real deliberative capacity

  • President: Office with genuine executive authority (Rafsanjani, Khatami had power)

  • Majles (Parliament): Legislature with real policy debates

  • Judiciary: Theoretically independent judicial system

  • Political factions: Reformists, conservatives, pragmatists competing in meaningful elections

  • Civil society: Limited but real space for NGOs, student groups, intellectuals

  • Clerical establishment: Senior ayatollahs with independent religious authority

  • IRGC: Revolutionary Guards as military force, not economic empire

These institutions were constrained by velayat-e faqih (Supreme Leader authority), but they existed and functioned.

What Khamenei Destroyed

The Assembly of Experts are 88 male clerics-all Khamenei loyalists-who have the power to anoint/remove the Supreme Leader. The joke about them is their average age is deceased. It's also chaired by 95-year-old Ayatollah Jannati (who makes Khamenei look youthful)

  • Assembly of Experts: Rubber stamp. Never challenged Supreme Leader once in 37 years.

  • Presidency: Reduced to administrator executing Khamenei’s will. Presidents who showed independence (Khatami, Rouhani) were systematically undermined.

  • Majles: Guardian Council vetting eliminates opposition. Parliament debates only what Khamenei permits.

  • Judiciary: Became execution arm of regime. Independence eliminated. Chief Justice appointed by Supreme Leader.

  • Political factions: Reformists barred from office, imprisoned, or silenced. Only regime loyalists permitted.

  • Civil society: Destroyed. NGOs shut down, intellectuals imprisoned, student groups infiltrated.

  • Clerical establishment: Senior ayatollahs who disagreed with regime silenced or placed under house arrest (Grand Ayatollah Montazeri died under house arrest).

  • IRGC: Transformed from revolutionary military into parallel state controlling 40% of economy.

The Personalist System

What Khamenei created: power flowing through one person — himself — with no independent institutional checks.

This is textbook personalist dictatorship. Every authoritarian study shows such systems are:

  • Most unstable during succession

  • Most prone to collapse when leader dies

  • Least capable of institutional adaptation

  • Most likely to produce violence during transition

What Dies: Institutional Continuity

When Khamenei dies, there are no strong institutions to manage transition. There’s only a power vacuum and competing factions with guns.

The Islamic Republic won’t transition smoothly because Khamenei made sure smooth transition was impossible. Any successor either:

  • Continues Khamenei’s methods (personalist dictatorship) = system remains brittle and faces same legitimacy crisis

  • Attempts institutional reform = threatens IRGC and clerical interests = likely coup or internal conflict

  • Presides over democratization = system abandons velayat-e faqih = revolution ends

No path forward preserves both the system and stability.

III. THE GENERATIONAL RUPTURE

Revolutions succeed when younger generations embrace revolutionary values. They fail when younger generations reject them.

The Revolutionary Generation (Born 1940s–1950s)

They remember Shah’s repression, the 1979 revolution’s promise, the Iran-Iraq War sacrifice, and revolutionary idealism. For them, comparing the Islamic Republic to the Shah is meaningful — the revolution delivered something better than SAVAK torture chambers and foreign domination. This generation, now in their 70s and 80s, still includes regime supporters.

The War Generation (Born 1960s–1970s)

They lived through the Iran-Iraq War as children and teenagers, economic hardship, Khomeini’s death, Khamenei’s rise, and some reform hopes (Khatami era, 1997–2005). Mixed loyalties. Old enough to remember the revolution but young enough to be disillusioned.

Generation X (Born 1980s)

They experienced post-war economic stagnation, Khatami’s reforms blocked by Khamenei, the 2009 Green Movement crushed, growing economic crisis, and internet access showing alternatives. Majority skeptical of regime. Limited revolutionary nostalgia.

Generation Z (Born 1990s–2000s)

They have no memory of the Shah, no memory of the revolution’s promise, no memory of the war’s sacrifice — only repression, poverty, and lies. They compare the Islamic Republic not to the Shah, but to the rest of the world they see online. This is the generation burning Khamenei’s pictures, leading “Woman, Life, Freedom” protests, chanting for the regime’s overthrow.

Before she died, Sarina Esmailzadeh, 16, said her generation wanted to live like young people in New York

The Generational Math

60% of Iran’s population is under 30 years old. These young people don’t remember the revolution, don’t fear a return to monarchy, don’t accept the Islamic Republic’s legitimacy, and don’t see any reason to preserve this system. When elderly revolutionaries die, who carries the revolution forward? Not the youth. They want to bury it.

What Dies: Revolutionary Continuity

Every revolution needs younger generations to embrace revolutionary values and carry them forward. The Islamic Revolution failed this test catastrophically.

The youth don’t want to reform the Islamic Republic. They want to end it. When Khamenei dies, the generational bridge collapses entirely. No one born after 1990 will defend velayat-e faqih. No one born after 2000 will mourn the revolution.

IV. THE ECONOMIC POINT OF NO RETURN

Authoritarian regimes can survive without legitimacy if they deliver prosperity. China’s CCP maintains control partly through economic growth. Gulf monarchies buy loyalty through oil wealth distribution.

Iran under Khamenei produced the opposite: economic decline, mass poverty, systemic corruption.

The Structural Problems

  • IRGC economic control: Revolutionary Guards control 40% of economy through front companies, smuggling networks, and bonyads. They won’t surrender this voluntarily.

  • Sanctions architecture: Decades of sanctions created dependency on illicit networks and black markets. These benefit corrupt elites, not the general population.

  • Subsidy system: Government subsidizes fuel, bread, and other essentials. Reforming this — economically necessary — would cause immediate unrest.

  • Banking system: International isolation means Iranian banks can’t transact globally. Foreign investment is nearly impossible.

  • Brain drain: Losing 150,000–180,000 educated professionals annually. Those who could drive economic recovery are leaving.

  • Demographic crisis: Aging population, declining birth rates, youth unemployment exceeding 25%.

The Catch-22

To fix the economy, Iran needs to end sanctions, privatize IRGC assets, end corruption, attract foreign investment, and stop brain drain. Every solution requires political changes the regime cannot survive.

The Tipping Point

December 2025: The rial collapsed to 1.4 million per USD. This isn’t fluctuation. This is a currency death spiral. When money becomes worthless, savings evaporate, trade collapses, government can’t pay salaries, services shut down, and the social contract breaks. The protests that began December 28, 2025 weren’t political. They were economic survival.

What Dies: The Social Contract

Authoritarian regimes offer an implicit deal: surrender political freedom, receive stability and prosperity. Khamenei’s Iran offers: surrender freedom, receive poverty and chaos. That’s not a sustainable contract. When economic collapse intersects with political repression, revolutions happen.

The Islamic Republic’s economic model — IRGC corruption, sanctions isolation, ideological rigidity — is past the point of no return. No successor can fix this without dismantling the system itself.

V. THE REGIONAL IRRELEVANCE

For decades, Iran positioned itself as regional power broker — the “resistance axis” challenging US-Israel dominance, supporting oppressed Muslims, exporting revolution. That pretense collapsed in 2024–2025.

The Empire’s End

  • Syria (December 2024): Assad fled to Moscow. Iran’s 13-year investment — tens of billions of dollars, thousands of fighters, massive political capital — produced nothing. Syria was the linchpin of the land bridge to Lebanon. Now it’s gone.

  • Lebanon (2024): Israel eliminated much of Hezbollah’s senior leadership and destroyed significant military infrastructure. Iran’s most powerful proxy severely weakened.

  • Yemen: Houthis control northern Yemen but can’t win the civil war. Their attacks on shipping invited international retaliation without delivering strategic advantage to Iran.

  • Gaza (2023–2024): Hamas launched the October 7 attack without consulting Iran — proving Iran doesn’t control even its own proxies. Israel’s response devastated Gaza. Iran gained nothing.

  • Iraq: Shia militias exist but can’t prevent Iraq from maintaining relations with the US, Saudi Arabia, and other Iranian rivals.

The Isolation

The Abraham Accords demonstrated Arab states prioritize their own interests over the Palestinian cause or Iranian “resistance.” The Saudi-Iranian détente papered over rivalry without eliminating it. The Arab League majority views Iran as a threat, not a leader. Turkey is a rival, not an ally. The Sunni-majority Islamic world — 85% of global Muslims — doesn’t follow Iran. It fears or opposes Iran.

The Military Reality

June 2025 exposed Iran’s military weakness: it couldn’t defend its own airspace, air defenses failed against F-35s and B-2s, retaliation was intercepted, nuclear facilities were damaged, and no ally provided military support. This wasn’t a close call. This was overwhelming defeat.

What Dies: The Resistance Myth

Iran presented itself as regional leader resisting Western imperialism. Reality: Iran is an isolated, weak, regionally rejected power whose proxies collapsed and whose military was humiliated. No successor can resurrect the “resistance axis.” It’s gone. What remains is an isolated, sanctioned, militarily weak country with domestic crisis and no allies.

VI. THE MORAL BANKRUPTCY

The revolution claimed moral authority. It positioned the Islamic Republic as a morally superior alternative to the Shah’s corruption and Western secular materialism. This claim is dead.

The Hypocrisy

They claimed justice — delivered corruption. IRGC generals live in mansions. Clerical families accumulate billions. Officials’ children party in Dubai. Meanwhile, 50% of Iranians live in poverty.

They claimed Islamic piety — delivered selective enforcement. Strict hijab laws for women while elites ignore Islamic modesty. Alcohol prosecuted for the poor, tolerated for the rich. Islamic banking claimed while IRGC runs smuggling operations.

They claimed to protect the oppressed — they are the oppressors. Thousands killed in protests. Political prisoners tortured. Women beaten for dress choices. Youth imprisoned for music preferences.

They claimed sovereignty — they delivered dependence. Economically dependent on China. Militarily dependent on Russia. Regionally isolated. Internationally sanctioned.

They claimed Islamic government — delivered dictatorship. Elections rigged. Dissent crushed. Power concentrated in one unelected man who admits to killing thousands.

The Symbol: Women Burning His Picture

Remember the videos from January 2026: Iranian women, faces visible, lighting cigarettes with burning images of Khamenei.

This wasn’t just political protest. This was desecration as performance art.

The Supreme Leader’s image — mandated in every school, government office, military base — reduced to lighter fuel for cigarettes (themselves defiance of public morality codes). Those burning images declared: your claim to Islamic authority is worthless.

What Dies: Moral Legitimacy

When the people you claim to serve burn your picture while chanting for your death, moral authority is finished. When traditional supporters — Grand Bazaar merchants, residents of religious cities — join protests against you, moral legitimacy is exhausted. When the international Muslim community views you with suspicion or hostility, moral leadership is over.

No successor inherits moral authority. They inherit moral bankruptcy.

VII. THE SUCCESSION THAT COULDN’T HAPPEN — AND DID

Updated April 2026

This section originally outlined why the succession after Khamenei would be impossible to manage cleanly. History has since supplied the answer — and it is stranger, and more confirming, than anything projected here.

On March 9, 2026, Mojtaba Khamenei — the Supreme Leader’s son — was named Iran’s third Supreme Leader, installed under IRGC pressure after the Assembly of Experts’ attempts to meet in person repeatedly failed due to security concerns. The revolution that overthrew the Pahlavi dynasty because hereditary rule was illegitimate completed a father-to-son transfer of power. The irony doesn’t require elaboration.

What the analysis predicted has materialized — not as one scenario, but as a collapse into all four simultaneously.

Scenario 1 (continuation) is technically in place: there is a Supreme Leader. But the continuity is a fiction. Seven weeks into his tenure, Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared in public once. His statements are read on television by news anchors. The regime has used AI-generated video to simulate his presence. Sources report he was severely injured in the February 28 strike — facial disfigurement, reportedly a lost leg. Whether incapacitated or simply hidden, he cannot function as his father did.

Scenario 3 (military takeover) is effectively underway. Analysts describe the current system as “a hardline coalition trying to manage war, diplomacy, and internal competition simultaneously” — with Mojtaba serving as figurehead for decisions actually made by IRGC commanders and senior security officials. He is, in the precise words of one analyst, “not quite supreme.”

The result is what the original analysis warned: a power vacuum dressed in succession clothing. Trump extended the ceasefire citing Iran’s government as “seriously fractured” — an extraordinary public acknowledgment that the US does not know who is actually making decisions in Tehran.

The original four scenarios were not alternatives — they were simultaneous outcomes. The succession happened. The system is also fracturing. The IRGC is also consolidating. And the possibility of popular uprising has not gone away: the January 2026 protests preceded the war, and the economic conditions that caused them have not improved.

A Supreme Leader who cannot be seen. A government Trump calls fractured. A naval blockade tightening around Iran’s oil exports. A ceasefire extended indefinitely because no one in Tehran can produce a unified position.

This is what dying looks like.

What Dies: The Fiction of Orderly Transition

The Islamic Republic does not have a succession mechanism adequate to its crisis. It has a name on a title, a ghost in a bunker, and a security apparatus pretending the institution still functions.

Every scenario produces one result: the revolution as conceived in 1979 and implemented by Khamenei ends. There is no scenario where the Islamic Republic continues as is, problems get solved, legitimacy restores, people accept the system, or the revolution survives in its current form.

Khamenei built a system that couldn’t outlive him. His son is proving it.
