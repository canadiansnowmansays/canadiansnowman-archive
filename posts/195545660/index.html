Canada: How the Dominion Was Forged

How the Dominion Was Forged

Part I: The Contested Shore

May 2, 2026

The reconstructed Fortress of Louisbourg on a clear day, overlooking the Atlantic. The stone walls and harbour that once sheltered French fleets and cod ships. Fortress of Louisbourg National Historic Site / Tourism Nova Scotia

I stood inside the Fortress of Louisbourg on a grey afternoon in Cape Breton, and the first thing that struck me was the wind. Not the reconstructed walls or the period-costumed interpreters or the cannons pointed out to sea. The wind. It comes off the Atlantic with a kind of indifferent hostility, the way empires come for territory — not out of malice, exactly, but because the geography is there and the wind goes where the wind goes.

Louisbourg is Canada’s first lesson in impermanence. The French built it beginning in 1713 — a massive stone fortress on the coast of Île-Royale, now Cape Breton Island — designed to protect the entrance to the Gulf of St. Lawrence and the fishing grounds that were, in the eighteenth century, worth more than most of the land they bordered. Cod built this fortress. Cod, salted and dried and shipped back to Europe, was the currency of empire on the North Atlantic, and Louisbourg was the vault that was supposed to keep it safe. The French called it the Gibraltar of the North. They poured decades of labour and millions of livres into its construction. They built walls thirty feet high and barracks for thousands and a harbour that could shelter a fleet.

And then they lost it. And got it back. And lost it again. And watched it get demolished, stone by stone, by the people who took it the second time.

The fortress was first captured by New England colonial forces in 1745 — a ragtag militia of fishermen, farmers, and merchants from Massachusetts who besieged it for forty-nine days and took it in a victory so unlikely that it entered colonial mythology as evidence of providential favour. William Pepperrell, the merchant who led the expedition, became the first American-born baronet. The colonists celebrated. They had proven that amateur armies, motivated by faith and profit, could take European fortifications.

Then the Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle in 1748 handed Louisbourg back to France in exchange for territorial concessions in Europe. The colonists were furious. They had bled and died for that fortress, and the diplomats in London had traded it away as a bargaining chip in a continental card game. The resentment planted by that betrayal — the sense that colonial interests were subordinate to imperial calculations made in distant capitals — would grow over the next three decades into something the British did not anticipate: revolution.

Louisbourg was captured again in 1758, this time by a proper British army under Major General Jeffrey Amherst, with a young brigadier named James Wolfe commanding one of the landing forces. The siege lasted seven weeks. The fortress fell. And this time, the British were not going to give it back. They demolished it. Not just the fortifications — the town itself. Walls, buildings, harbour works, all of it reduced to rubble and scattered. The destruction was deliberate and total, an act of strategic erasure designed to ensure that the French could never again use Louisbourg as a base for operations in the North Atlantic.

Aerial view of the Fortress of Louisbourg, showing its strategic position on the windswept coast of Île-Royale (Cape Breton Island). Fortress of Louisbourg National Historic Site

I walked through the reconstructed fortress — rebuilt by Parks Canada in the 1960s and 1970s as a heritage project and employment program — and tried to imagine it in rubble. The walls that now hold interpretive displays and gift shops once held soldiers who fought for two empires that regarded this coastline as a prize worth killing for. The harbour that now holds tourist boats once held warships. The wind that pushed against me as I walked the ramparts was the same wind that filled the sails of the ships that carried empires back and forth across this water, trading this place like a card in a game whose rules were written in London and Paris.

That is Canada’s origin story. Not a declaration of independence. Not a founding myth of liberty and self-governance. A fortress that changed hands between empires, built and destroyed and rebuilt, on a windy shore at the edge of a continent that neither empire fully understood.

The contest that Louisbourg symbolized reached its climax roughly five hundred kilometres to the southwest, on a plateau above Quebec City, on the morning of September 13, 1759.

The British landing and assault on Quebec, September 13, 1759, as the forces of Wolfe scaled the cliffs to reach the Plains of Abraham. Source: Historical engraving, public domain

The Plains of Abraham is, in person, a surprisingly gentle place to have decided the fate of a continent. It sits on the heights above the St. Lawrence, a broad, open expanse that is now a park where families picnic, joggers loop, and tourists photograph the view. The city spreads below — the Château Frontenac, the old walled quarter, the ferry crossing to Lévis. It is beautiful. It is tranquil. It is also the site of one of the most consequential fifteen minutes in the history of the Western world.

Major General James Wolfe had been besieging Quebec City for three months without success. The citadel on the heights was virtually impregnable from the river. Previous attempts to land troops had been repulsed. Wolfe was running out of time — winter was approaching, the St. Lawrence would soon freeze, and the fleet would have to withdraw or be trapped.

In desperation or genius — the line between the two is often visible only in retrospect — Wolfe gambled everything on a night assault up the cliffs at L’Anse-au-Foulon, a narrow path on the cliff face that the French considered impassable and therefore barely guarded. Under cover of darkness, roughly 4,500 British soldiers climbed the heights. By dawn, they had assembled in battle formation on the plains above the city.

The Marquis de Montcalm, commanding the French garrison, faced a decision that has been debated by military historians for two and a half centuries. His fortifications were strong. Reinforcements under the Chevalier de Lévis were hours away. The rational choice was to wait — to let the British stand on the open plains while French forces assembled for a coordinated counterattack.

Montcalm attacked immediately. The reasons remain debated — fear that the British would entrench, concern about morale, personal temperament, the fog of a situation that was evolving faster than any commander could fully process. Whatever the reason, the French advanced across the plains in a formation that broke apart under the disciplined British musket volleys.

The British had been ordered to hold their fire until the French were within forty paces. When the volley came, it was devastating. The French advance shattered. Montcalm was mortally wounded — he died the following morning. Wolfe was killed by a musket ball to the chest during the engagement, dying on the field as word of his victory reached him.

Both commanders were dead. The battle had lasted approximately fifteen minutes. Quebec City surrendered within days. Montreal fell the following year. And the Treaty of Paris in 1763 formally ceded the whole of New France to Britain, ending a century and a half of French sovereignty over the St. Lawrence Valley and the heart of North America.

Benjamin West’s The Death of General Wolfe (1770), capturing the moment of victory and loss on the Plains of Abraham. Source: Public domain / National Gallery of Canada

Two dead generals. Fifteen minutes. A continent redrawn.

But here is where the Canadian story diverges from every other colonial narrative, and where it begins to earn the word “forged.”

In the normal course of imperial conquest, what happens next is assimilation. The victor imposes its language, its laws, its religion, its customs. The conquered are absorbed, marginalized, expelled, or some combination of all three. This is the pattern across the colonial world — in the Americas, in Africa, in Asia, in Australasia. The British Empire was particularly efficient at it. From Ireland to India, the pattern held: conquest, followed by the systematic replacement of local institutions with British ones.

In Quebec, they didn’t. Not fully. Not permanently. Not because the British were uniquely enlightened — they were not, as the Acadians expelled from Nova Scotia a few years earlier could have attested. But because the practical realities of governing 70,000 French-speaking, Catholic habitants in a vast, cold territory that was about to become dangerously unstable required a different approach.

The Plains of Abraham today, overlooking Quebec City — a tranquil park that once decided the fate of a continent. Source: Richard Wong Photography

The American colonies to the south were growing restive. The Stamp Act was passed in 1765, just two years after the Treaty of Paris. The protests, boycotts, and increasingly radical rhetoric that would culminate in revolution were already audible. Britain needed Quebec quiet. Britain needed Quebec loyal. And loyalty, the British calculated — correctly, as it turned out — was more reliably purchased through accommodation than through suppression.

The result, fifteen years later, would be the Quebec Act of 1774 — the formal, legislative expression of the decision to coexist. But the seed was planted in the immediate aftermath of the Plains of Abraham, in the military governance of Murray and then Carleton, in the practical choices of British administrators who discovered that the most effective way to govern a French population was to govern with a French population. To let them keep their language. Their faith. Their civil law. Their seigneurial system. Their identity.

This was not tolerance in the modern sense. It was imperial pragmatism. But pragmatism, in the Canadian story, has a way of hardening into principle over time. The decision not to erase — made for reasons of strategic calculation in the 1760s — became the foundational commitment of a country that did not yet exist. The commitment to bilingualism. To legal pluralism. To the idea that a single state could contain multiple nations without requiring any of them to disappear.

I think about this every time I hear Americans describe their own founding as a story of principle — of self-evident truths, of liberty, of revolution against tyranny. There is something magnificent about that story, and something true. But there is also a Canadian story, less magnificent and differently true, and it begins not with a declaration of independence but with the quiet, unglamorous recognition that the people you just conquered are not going anywhere, and you are going to have to figure out how to live with them.

I grew up in London, Ontario — a city so determinedly British that it has a Thames, a Piccadilly Street, and a general air of having been transplanted from the Home Counties by someone who forgot to include the weather. London was founded by British settlers who brought their institutions, their place names, and their assumptions about how the world should be ordered. But even in London, the French fact was present — in the bilingual labels on every product, in the French immersion programs in the schools, in the constitutional architecture of a country that had been, from its first moment, a negotiation between two peoples who did not choose each other but could not escape each other.

Canada did not begin with a revolution. It began with a negotiation. And everything that followed — Confederation, the railway, the Charter, the trade agreements, and Mark Carney standing at a podium in 2026 rallying a bilingual nation against a president who thinks Canada is a state — everything that followed grew from that original seed, planted on the Plains of Abraham in the blood of two generals who did not live to see what their deaths would produce.

The wind at Louisbourg is still blowing. The fortress has been rebuilt — not by the French or the British, but by Canadians, as a monument to the idea that things that have been destroyed can be reconstructed, and that the contest for this land is never quite over.

That is where Canada begins. Not with a gunshot heard round the world. With a negotiation heard by almost nobody.
