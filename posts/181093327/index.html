When Loyalty Trumps Competence in the Justice System (Part 1)

How an Unlawful Appointment Is Tomorrow's Advisory Position: The Alina Habba Story

December 8, 2025

December 8, 2024

There’s an old biblical adage that warns us about what happens when the blind lead the blind: both fall into a pit. In the case of Donald Trump’s Justice Department appointments, we’re witnessing something even more remarkable—a systematic process where demonstrable incompetence in private practice becomes the qualifying credential for wielding federal prosecutorial power.

Meet Alina Habba and Lindsey Halligan. Both served as Trump’s personal lawyers. Both lost cases spectacularly. Both were then appointed as U.S. Attorneys. Both had their appointments ruled unlawful by federal judges. And both exemplify a disturbing pattern: in Trump’s second administration, loyalty isn’t just valued over competence—incompetence in service to Trump appears to be the actual qualification.

This isn’t hyperbole. This is documented fact.

In this two-part series, I’ll examine both cases in detail. Today, we’ll look at Alina Habba—whose trajectory from losing an $83 million case for Trump to being appointed New Jersey’s top federal prosecutor reveals the selection criteria at work. In Part 2, we’ll examine Lindsey Halligan’s even more alarming case, where prosecutorial misconduct of an extraordinary degree culminated in both her appointment and her indictments being ruled unlawful.

Together, these cases reveal something crucial: Trump hasn’t just made bad appointments. He’s created a pipeline where serving as his personal lawyer—and failing spectacularly at it—becomes the credential for wielding federal prosecutorial power.

The Habba Precedent: From $83 Million Loss to U.S. Attorney

Let’s start with Alina Habba, whose legal career trajectory reads like a case study in the Dunning-Kruger effect.

Habba first gained national attention representing Trump in the E. Jean Carroll defamation case. The result? An $83.3 million judgment against her client—one of the largest defamation awards in American history.

During the trial, her performance was so problematic that Judge Lewis Kaplan repeatedly admonished her for basic procedural errors. The specifics matter because they reveal just how unqualified she was:

  • She didn’t know how to authenticate evidence. At one point, Habba literally asked Judge Kaplan: “How do you suggest I proceed?” when showing an exhibit. The judge had to explain: “Show it to her, ask if she recognizes it.”

  • She failed to pre-mark exhibits. Judge Kaplan snapped at her: “It’s not marked. It should be marked. Do it appropriately. Do it overnight. They. Need. To. Be. Premarked.”

  • She didn’t know basic courtroom procedure. Judge Kaplan had to tell her: “Ms. Habba, when you speak in this courtroom or any other courtroom, you’ll stand up.” She also didn’t know to sit down after the judge ruled.

  • She tried to introduce evidence not in the record. The judge scolded her: “Ms. Habba, this is Evidence 101.”

  • She had 10 consecutive objections sustained—meaning 10 questions in a row were improper.

  • She talked over the judge when he sustained objections, prompting Kaplan to ask: “Did you not hear me?”

  • The judge threatened her with jail. When Habba talked out of turn, Kaplan told her she was “on the verge of spending some time in the lockup.”

Legal experts were brutal in their assessments. Former federal prosecutor Elizabeth de la Vega warned that “Habba might think she’s doing a great job by performing for Trump, but she’s already succeeded in making the jury dislike her intensely.” Attorney Harry Litman called her trial performance a “comedy of bumbling errors.”

And she lost. Badly. $83.3 million badly.

But failure, in Trump’s world, is not disqualifying—it’s a resume builder, provided you remain loyal.

The Appointment That Defied Logic

Despite this spectacular loss, and despite having no prosecutorial experience whatsoever, Trump appointed Habba as U.S. Attorney for the District of New Jersey in his second term. It was an appointment that raised immediate red flags among legal experts. New Jersey handles significant federal cases including public corruption, organized crime, and complex white-collar prosecutions. The position typically goes to seasoned prosecutors with decades of courtroom experience.

Habba had been admitted to the New Jersey bar in 2010 and spent most of her career handling small business litigation before joining Trump’s personal legal team. She had never prosecuted a criminal case. She had never worked in a U.S. Attorney’s office. She had never handled the kinds of complex federal investigations that define the New Jersey office’s work.

None of that mattered. What mattered was that when Trump lost the Carroll case—when the jury found him liable for sexual abuse and defamation and awarded Carroll $83.3 million—Habba remained loyal. She didn’t distance herself from the client. She didn’t quietly exit the legal team. She doubled down, appearing on television to defend Trump and attack the verdict.

That loyalty was the qualification.

The Legal Challenge and Unlawful Appointment

The appointment lasted until last Monday, December 2, 2024, when a federal appeals court ruled that Habba had been serving as U.S. Attorney unlawfully. The court found that her appointment violated federal statutes governing interim U.S. Attorney appointments—specifically the 120-day limitation on such appointments absent Senate confirmation or district court approval.

The statute, 28 U.S.C. § 546, is clear: the Attorney General can appoint an interim U.S. Attorney for 120 days. After that, only the district court can extend the appointment. This limitation exists for a reason—it prevents administrations from installing political loyalists in prosecutorial positions without oversight.

Trump and Attorney General Pam Bondi tried to work around this limitation. They failed.

Habba resigned immediately after the ruling.

The Troubling Postscript: Advisor Roles and the Circumvention of Accountability

But here’s where the story gets even more concerning: resignation from an unlawful appointment doesn’t necessarily mean leaving the administration. There’s been speculation—and it would be entirely consistent with the pattern we’ve seen—that Habba could resurface as an advisor to Attorney General Pam Bondi.

Think about what an impressive resume she’s built for such a role:

  • ✓ Asked federal judge how to authenticate evidence

  • ✓ Didn’t know to stand when addressing the court

  • ✓ Failed to pre-mark exhibits repeatedly

  • ✓ Had 10 consecutive objections sustained

  • ✓ Required “Evidence 101” instruction from judge

  • ✓ Talked over judicial rulings

  • ✓ Threatened with jail time for contempt

  • ✓ Lost $83.3 million for her client

  • ✓ Remained absolutely loyal through all of it

In any normal legal career, this would be a catastrophe. In Trump’s Justice Department, it’s apparently a credential for senior advisory positions.

Because that’s what this resume actually demonstrates: someone who doesn’t let pesky things like “basic courtroom procedure” or “evidence rules” or “professional standards” get in the way of serving Trump’s interests. The incompetence isn’t a bug—it’s a feature. It proves you’ll do what you’re told regardless of legal constraints or professional obligations.

Think about what this would mean: the same person whose appointment as U.S. Attorney was ruled unlawful due to statutory violations could simply sidestep those constraints by operating in an advisory capacity. No Senate confirmation required. No statutory limitations on interim appointments. No direct accountability. Just influence—and a proven track record of not understanding or not caring about legal constraints.

And this presents what could be an even more dangerous scenario: Pam Bondi advised by Alina Habba is potentially worse than Alina Habba as U.S. Attorney.

Why? Because Bondi herself brings her own loyalty-over-law concerns to the position. As Florida Attorney General, she declined to investigate Trump University after Trump’s foundation made a $25,000 donation to her political committee. She later called the investigation into Trump’s charity “a political hit job.” Her loyalty to Trump is well-established.

Now imagine Bondi—already predisposed to use DOJ to serve Trump’s interests—being advised by Habba, who has demonstrated she’s willing to pursue Trump’s enemies regardless of legal merit. You get the incompetence and loyalty of the Habba appointment, but without the statutory constraints that got her official appointment thrown out.

It’s the blind leading the blind, but with plausible deniability built in.

Bondi makes the final decisions, so she bears the official responsibility. But Habba whispers in her ear, provides “analysis” of cases Trump wants brought, and helps identify which career prosecutors are insufficiently loyal. It’s all the weaponization with none of the accountability.

And there’s precedent for this approach. When direct appointments fail or face legal challenges, this administration has shown a pattern of finding workarounds. Can’t appoint someone because of statutory limitations? Make them an advisor. Can’t get Senate confirmation? Use acting officials. Can’t remove civil servants through normal channels? Create new positions that report directly to political appointees.

The Habba resignation, viewed in this light, might not be a defeat at all. It might just be a repositioning—from a vulnerable position that required statutory authority to an advisory role that requires nothing but Trump’s favor.

The courts can invalidate unlawful appointments. But they can’t stop the President from listening to whomever he wants.

The Pattern Begins to Emerge

The Habba case reveals several crucial elements of what appears to be a systematic approach to Justice Department appointments:

1. Loyalty Trumps Competence

Habba’s qualification wasn’t her legal acumen—the Carroll case demonstrated she lacked basic trial skills. Her qualification was that she remained loyal after losing badly. She didn’t blame Trump for the loss. She didn’t distance herself from the case. She defended him publicly, attacked the verdict, and signaled that she would always prioritize Trump’s interests over professional standards or legal constraints.

That’s exactly what Trump needs in someone wielding prosecutorial power.

2. Public Failure is a Credential

In a normal legal career, losing an $83 million case would be career-ending, or at least career-damaging. It would raise serious questions about judgment, preparation, and competence.

In Trump’s world, it’s proof that you’ll stick with him no matter what. The more public the failure, the more valuable the loyalty it demonstrates.

3. Prosecutorial Experience is Unnecessary (or Undesirable)

Habba had zero prosecutorial experience. She’d never brought a criminal case. She’d never worked with grand juries. She’d never navigated the complex world of federal criminal investigation.

This wasn’t an oversight in her appointment—it was the point. Career prosecutors have professional standards that constrain what they’ll do. They decline cases with insufficient evidence. They resist political pressure to indict. They follow established procedures.

Someone with no prosecutorial experience doesn’t know what prosecutors aren’t supposed to do. And more importantly, they don’t care. Their loyalty is to Trump, not to prosecutorial norms.

4. The Workaround is Already in Place

When the courts blocked Habba’s appointment as unlawful, the response wasn’t to acknowledge the problem or reform the appointment process. The response was to find a way around the constraint.

Advisory roles require no Senate confirmation, face no statutory limitations, and carry no formal accountability. But they provide influence over the same decisions that formal positions control.

This is the real lesson of the Habba case: when direct authority is blocked, indirect influence becomes the path. The incompetence and loyalty remain the same—only the organizational chart changes.

What This Means for the Justice Department

The Habba appointment—even though it failed—reveals the fundamental transformation underway at the Justice Department.

Traditionally, U.S. Attorneys are selected based on:

  • Prosecutorial experience

  • Trial experience

  • Legal expertise

  • Professional reputation

  • Institutional credibility

Under Trump, the selection criteria appears to be:

  • Personal loyalty to Trump

  • Willingness to lose publicly for Trump

  • Lack of institutional allegiance to DOJ norms

  • Demonstrated disregard for professional standards

This isn’t just bad hiring. It’s the systematic replacement of one set of institutional values (competence, professionalism, independence) with another (loyalty, compliance, weaponization).

The Justice Department will continue to exist as a formal entity. It will have the same building, the same organizational chart, the same statutory authority. But when you replace professionals selected for competence with loyalists selected for compliance, the institution becomes a shell—it retains the appearance of legitimacy while serving purely political purposes.

The Double Trouble Scenario

Attorney General Pam Bondi faced questions from lawmakers during a hearing before the Senate Judiciary Committee

The Bondi-Habba combination represents a particularly dangerous version of this transformation.

Bondi is a Trump loyalist with her own history of prioritizing Trump’s interests over prosecutorial independence. She’s the Attorney General—she has formal authority over DOJ operations, prosecutorial decisions, and personnel.

Habba is a Trump loyalist with demonstrated willingness to disregard professional standards and legal constraints. She’s potentially an advisor—she has informal influence over the same decisions, but without the statutory constraints or accountability that come with formal positions.

Together, they represent “double trouble”:

  • Bondi provides the formal authority

  • Habba provides the informal pressure

  • Both share the same loyalty-first orientation

  • Neither has strong allegiance to DOJ independence

  • Career prosecutors face pressure from both above (Bondi as AG) and beside (Habba as advisor)

It’s the weaponization of the Justice Department with redundancy built in. If career prosecutors resist Bondi’s directives, Habba can identify them as disloyal. If Bondi faces criticism for politicized prosecutions, Habba remains in the shadows, providing “advice” without bearing responsibility.

And it’s completely legal. Presidents can have advisors. Attorneys General can consult whomever they choose. There’s no statute limiting who can whisper in their ear, no judicial review of informal advisory relationships.

The courts blocked Habba’s formal appointment. But they can’t block her informal influence.

Coming in Part 2: The Halligan Case Gets Worse

If the Habba case reveals the pattern, the Lindsey Halligan case reveals just how dangerous that pattern becomes when fully implemented.

Halligan, like Habba, was Trump’s personal lawyer with minimal federal court experience. Like Habba, she was appointed as interim U.S. Attorney despite having zero prosecutorial experience. Like Habba, her appointment was ruled unlawful by a federal judge.

But Halligan went further. Much further.

In her 63 days as interim U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, Halligan:

  • Secured indictments of both former FBI Director James Comey and New York Attorney General Letitia James

  • Admitted in court that she submitted grand jury indictments that the grand jury never actually voted on

  • Engaged in prosecutorial misconduct so extraordinary that legal experts called it grounds for disbarment or criminal charges

  • Had both indictments thrown out as unlawful

The Halligan case takes everything problematic about the Habba appointment and makes it worse. Where Habba’s appointment was stopped before she could bring cases, Halligan actually weaponized her position—and the manner in which she did so reveals just how dangerous it is to put unqualified loyalists in prosecutorial positions.
