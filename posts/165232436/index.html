A Tax Bill for All Americans

A Fairer, Smarter Path Forward Amid Market Chaos

How Trump's $2.4 Trillion Deficit Bomb United Elon Musk, Democrats, and Market Forces Against the 'Well, We're All Going to Die Bill'

June 4, 2025

Even Elon Musk is demanding lawmakers "KILL the BILL"—exposing the contradictions in Trump's economic spin

By Stephen Lawton June 4, 2025

The Bottom Line Up Front: The GOP's "Big Beautiful Bill" is causing market turmoil and drawing fierce opposition from unlikely allies—including Trump's own former DOGE chief. With the CBO confirming the bill would add $2.4 trillion to the deficit and leave 10.9 million people without health insurance, 30-year Treasury yields spiking to 5.14%, and Moody's stripping America's top credit rating, America needs a deficit-neutral alternative that calms markets while delivering real relief to working families.

The Cracks in Trump's Coalition Are Showing

In a stunning political earthquake that has rocked Washington, Elon Musk—Trump's former Department of Government Efficiency chief and $300 million political donor—has declared war on the president's signature legislation. In a post that garnered 1.3 million views, Musk urged Americans:

"Call your Senator,
Call your Congressman,
Bankrupting America is NOT ok!
KILL the BILL"

This isn't just political theater. When the world's richest man, who spent nearly $300 million electing Trump, calls the president's top priority a "disgusting abomination," you know something is deeply wrong. Musk's escalating attacks have grown increasingly urgent, warning that "This immense level of overspending will drive America into debt slavery!"

To put this crisis in perspective: A million seconds ago was May 23rd. A billion seconds ago was 1993. A trillion seconds ago was 30,000 B.C. The US national debt is now rising by $1 trillion every 180 days. And Trump's bill would dramatically accelerate this nightmare.

The House's passage of the GOP's "Big Beautiful Bill" on May 21, 2025, has sent shockwaves through the markets—and not the good kind. The bond market is in turmoil, with 30-year Treasury yields spiking to 5.14% as investors balk at the bill's $4 trillion deficit increase, according to the Congressional Budget Office (CBO). The stock market isn't faring much better, with the bill passing "despite growing concerns over the U.S. debt, which has reached 124% of GDP, prompting a downgrade of the United States' top-notch credit rating by Moody's last week."

The Trump Administration's Desperate Spin vs. Reality

While markets panic and even Trump's closest allies jump ship, the administration has launched a full-court press to defend the indefensible. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent dismissively told reporters "Who cares?" about Moody's historic credit downgrade—a stunning display of fiscal recklessness from America's top financial official.

The Contradiction Exposed: Trump and his cabinet claim the bill will "cut deficits" and provide "jet fuel for the economy," but every independent analysis tells a different story:

  • Congressional Budget Office: $2.4 trillion deficit increase over 10 years and 10.9 million people losing health insurance

  • Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget: $3.3 trillion added to the debt over the next decade

  • Penn Wharton Budget Model: $4.9 trillion primary deficit increase after economic effects

  • Tax Foundation: $1.7 trillion to $2.6 trillion deficit increase

As one GOP critic noted: "Any Republican who votes for this has no right to ever complain about the debt or deficit again."

Meanwhile, Speaker Mike Johnson has been making the rounds on Sunday talk shows, desperately trying to shame GOP senators into compliance. Johnson told "State of the Union" to make "as few modifications to this package as possible" and emphasized the need to expedite the bill's passage, hoping Trump could sign it by Independence Day.

But the old Trump tactic of "toe the line or else" is cracking. Even loyalist Republicans are breaking ranks:

  • Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY): "I'd love to tell the American people, 'We can cut your taxes and increase spending and everything is going to be just fine.' But I can't do that because I'm here to deliver a dose of reality."

  • Rep. Warren Davidson (R-OH): "Deficits do matter and this bill grows them now. The only Congress we can control is the one we're in. Consequently, I cannot support this big deficit plan."

  • Sen. Ron Johnson (R-WI): "The first goal of our budget reconciliation process should be to reduce the deficit. This actually increases."

When Sen. Rand Paul (R-KY) said he wouldn't vote for a bill with $5 trillion in debt ceiling increases, calling it "not conservative," Trump lashed out viciously on Truth Social: "Rand votes NO on everything, but never has any practical or constructive ideas. His ideas are actually crazy (losers!). The people of Kentucky can't stand him."

Paul's response was telling: "I like the president, supported the president. But I can't in good conscience give up every principle that I stand for and every principle that I was elected upon."

The Bill's Broken Promises and Market Fallout

The GOP bill makes the TCJA's tax cuts permanent, keeping lower individual rates and a doubled standard deduction. It also boosts the pass-through deduction for businesses from 20% to 23%, slashing the effective tax rate for wealthy business owners to 28.5%—well below the 37% top individual rate.

But here's the cruel irony: while showering the wealthy with massive tax breaks, the bill devastates the programs that help working families. The Congressional Budget Office's devastating new analysis confirms the bill would leave 10.9 million people without health insurance and that "8.6 million fewer people would have health insurance with the various changes to Medicaid and the Affordable Care Act" plus "3 million fewer people each month would have SNAP benefits."

Seniors, promised "no tax on Social Security," got a measly $4,000 deduction—pocket change compared to what the ultra-wealthy gain. The bill will "reduce the take-home incomes of bottom 10 percent of income earners by four percent by the end of the decade" while the wealthy see massive gains.

The bond market's reaction is a red flag—yields at 5.14% signal investor fears of increased debt issuance and inflation, reminiscent of the 2022 UK bond crisis under Liz Truss, where unfunded tax cuts triggered a market revolt. Investors are "unnerved by the country's worsening fiscal position and Trump's erratic tariff moves" and "have been selling off U.S. assets that make up the bedrock of the global financial system."

Why Even Elon Musk Has Had Enough

According to Fox News' Brian Kilmeade, "The Elon Musk thing caught the President by surprise and I hear he's furious." And Trump has every reason to be angry—Musk's opposition isn't just about fiscal responsibility, it's personal and strategic. The bill "would undo most of the Biden-era Inflation Reduction Act, which poured federal tax incentives into production of electric vehicles, batteries and solar energy — cornerstones of Musk's businesses." When even your biggest financial supporter realizes you're destroying their business model, maybe it's time to reconsider.

As one analysis noted: "The budget removes the $7,500 tax credit for electric vehicles, which is a big part of Tesla's success in the US. It also kills incentives to build batteries in the US – another incentive that greatly benefited Tesla."

A Fairer, Deficit-Neutral Plan to Calm the Markets

Imagine a tax bill that doesn't just favor the rich but lifts everyone up—without adding a dime to the debt. Here's a blueprint that prioritizes fairness and fiscal responsibility, potentially stabilizing the markets in the process.

1. Real Relief for Low- and Middle-Income Families

Consider a single mom in Ohio, earning $40,000 a year with two kids, struggling to afford groceries. The current bill might give her a $200 tax cut—barely enough for a week's shopping. We can do better:

Boost the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) and Child Tax Credit (CTC): Increase the EITC phase-in rate to 50% for families with incomes under $50,000, and make the CTC fully refundable up to that threshold. This could give our Ohio mom an extra $3,000 a year—enough for school supplies, car repairs, or an emergency fund. The Urban Institute shows this would lift after-tax income for the bottom quintile by 5–10%. Cost: $100 billion annually.

Tiered Standard Deduction Increase: Add a $2,000 boost to the standard deduction for filers with incomes below $75,000. For a middle-income family earning $70,000, this could save $1,000 a year—real money for a summer vacation or holiday gifts. Cost: $50 billion annually.

2. Ask the Wealthy to Pay Their Fair Share

To fund this relief without adding to the debt, we need to look at the top earners, who've seen their after-tax income soar since the TCJA. The Tax Foundation's 2024 report notes the top 0.0002% (average wealth $8 billion) pay $36.8 million in taxes, much from investments taxed at lower rates. Here's how to balance the scales:

Raise the Top Marginal Tax Rate: Increase the top rate from 37% to 39.6% for income over $1 million, affecting just the top 0.5% of earners. This could raise $26 billion a year, per Tax Foundation estimates.

Limit the Pass-Through Deduction: Cap the 23% pass-through deduction for incomes above $500,000, ensuring wealthy lawyers or hedge fund managers don't get a massive tax break while small businesses still benefit. Savings: $25 billion a year, per the Joint Committee on Taxation.

Increase Capital Gains Taxes: Bump the capital gains rate from 20% to 24.2% for income over $1 million, raising $42 billion annually, per Treasury estimates. This targets the ultra-wealthy profiting from stock sales while working families pay higher rates on wages.

Close Corporate Loopholes: Tighten rules on offshore tax deferral and inversions, raising $50 billion a year, per CBO estimates. Corporations shouldn't dodge taxes while regular Americans foot the bill.

Minimum Tax on High Earners: Set a 30% minimum tax on adjusted gross income for filers earning over $5 million, ensuring the ultra-rich can't use loopholes. Revenue: $25 billion a year.

3. Protect Social Programs and Cut Wasteful Spending

The current bill risks slashing Medicaid, hurting vulnerable people—like a diabetic grandmother in rural Georgia who relies on it for insulin. We can protect her while cutting elsewhere:

Cancel Medicaid Cuts: Maintain current funding, costing $50 billion annually compared to proposed cuts, ensuring low-income families aren't left behind.

Eliminate Extra Military Spending: The bill adds military spending with no clear offset, costing $75 billion a year, per historical data. We can support our troops without bloating the budget—redirect that money to debt reduction.

Cancel Trump's Vanity Parade: Trump's planned June 14 military parade for his 79th birthday will cost $45 million, including $16 million just to repair street damage from tanks. As Rep. Steve Cohen noted in his "HAPPY BIRTHDAY Budget Act": "Donald Trump may imagine himself an all-powerful ruler, but he is a president, not a potentate, and the American people don't pay tribute to him as if he were a king." The Army has never held a parade for its own birthday—this is pure ego spending while families lose healthcare.

The Bottom Line: Fairness, Fiscal Health, and Market Stability

Here's the math: This plan generates $243 billion annually ($26B from top rates, $25B from pass-through limits, $42B from capital gains, $50B from corporate loopholes, $25B from minimum tax, $75B from military spending savings), covering the $200 billion in costs ($100B for EITC/CTC, $50B for standard deduction, $50B for Medicaid). That leaves a $43 billion surplus to chip away at the national debt.

Low-Income Families: Your Ohio mom gets $3,000 more a year, and Medicaid stays intact for those who need it most.

Middle-Income Families: A $1,000 tax cut helps them breathe easier, and fiscal stability means no future debt-driven tax hikes.

Wealthy/Super-Wealthy: Their effective tax rate rises slightly (from 50% to 52% for the top 0.001%, per Tax Foundation data), ensuring they contribute to our shared future.

How the Markets Might React to This Plan

The current bill's $4 trillion deficit increase is spooking investors, driving up bond yields and stock market volatility. But what if the Senate adopted this deficit-neutral plan instead?

Bond Market: A deficit-neutral bill would likely calm bond investors. The current 5.14% yield on 30-year Treasuries reflects fears of increased debt issuance and inflation, as seen in the UK's 2022 bond crisis under Liz Truss. A revenue-neutral plan signals fiscal discipline—yields could ease back toward 4.5%, as investors gain confidence in the U.S. government's ability to manage its $37 trillion debt.

Stock Market: The S&P 500's current volatility stems from uncertainty over inflation and future tax hikes to cover the debt. A fairer bill that boosts disposable income for low- and middle-income families—without adding to the deficit—could stabilize markets. Consumer spending, which drives 70% of GDP, would rise as families have more to spend, potentially lifting corporate earnings. Historically, deficit reduction efforts have coincided with stock market gains—a similar plan today might reduce volatility and generate 5–7% gains.

When Chuck Schumer and Elon Musk Agree, Maybe It's Time to Listen

In one of the most surreal moments in recent political memory, Democrats have seized on Musk's opposition with devastating effectiveness. Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer held up printouts of Musk's tweets during a news conference, saying "I agree with Elon Musk" and brilliantly rebranded Trump's legislation as "the Well, We're All Going to Die Bill" in honor of Sen. Joni Ernst's recent comments.

The numbers Democrats are highlighting are devastating:

  • Sen. Chris Murphy: The bill "throws 15 million people off their health care" and "will add $2.4 TRILLION to the national debt. That's more than the entire annual GDP of Canada."

  • Sen. Bernie Sanders: "Is it 'moral' to eliminate health care for 13.7 million Americans so the 1% gets a $664 billion tax break?"

  • Ed Krassenstein: The bill "will take healthcare away from up to 16 million Americans and take affordable food away from 11 million."

Meanwhile, House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries echoed those statements, saying "breaking news: Elon Musk and I agree with each other" and pledging to work with Schumer to kill the legislation.

When Democrats and the world's richest man—who spent $300 million to elect Trump—agree that the bill is a fiscal disaster, maybe it's time to listen. As Speaker Johnson tries to defend the "moral component" of the bill, Bernie Sanders fires back: "Is it 'moral' to cut nutrition assistance for hungry kids? Is it 'moral' to eliminate health care for 13.7 million Americans so the 1% gets a $664 billion tax break?"

Why This Matters Now

This isn't just about numbers—it's about people. It's about ensuring tax policy doesn't leave working families behind while the rich get richer. It's about keeping the promise to seniors, not shortchanging them with a $4,000 deduction when they were promised more. And it's about stopping the debt spiral that's already shaking the bond and stock markets, threatening our economic future.

The Senate can make this bill better—demand they do. Even Treasury Secretary Bessent's dismissive "Who cares?" about America's credit downgrade shows how out of touch this administration has become with fiscal reality.
