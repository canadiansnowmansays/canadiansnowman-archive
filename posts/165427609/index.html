The American Contradiction

How Anti-"Woke" Politics Betrays the Nation's Foundational Promise

Week 5: The Unfinished Business - From Slavery to Anti-"Woke" Backlash

July 2, 2025

August 28, 1963: 'I Have a Dream.' The backlash began immediately.

The Through-Line: America's Continuous Racial Hierarchy

To understand contemporary anti-"woke" politics, we must begin with an uncomfortable truth: it represents the latest evolution in America's continuous system of racial hierarchy that began with slavery. From a Canadian perspective, where our history with slavery was more limited and ended earlier, the persistence of American racial hierarchy appears both tragic and strategically self-defeating.

The pattern is clear: whenever Black Americans gain political or economic power, white America develops new mechanisms to maintain racial hierarchy. Anti-"woke" politics isn't a departure from American tradition—it's the continuation of a 400-year project to preserve white supremacy through evolving means.

Slavery: The Original Sin and Economic Foundation

American slavery wasn't simply labor exploitation—it was a comprehensive system of racial hierarchy that defined Blackness as inherently inferior and whiteness as inherently superior. This wasn't accidental; it was essential to justifying the economic brutality that built American wealth.

The profits from slave labor didn't just benefit Southern plantation owners. Northern banks, shipping companies, textile mills, and insurance companies all profited from the slave economy. Slave labor built the U.S. Capitol, the White House, and much of early American infrastructure. The wealth generated by unpaid Black labor provided the capital for American industrialization and westward expansion.

Crucially, slavery required constant ideological justification. White Americans had to believe that enslaving other human beings was not just economically beneficial but morally justified. This required constructing elaborate racial theories about Black inferiority, white superiority, and the "natural order" of racial hierarchy.

Reconstruction: The First Backlash

When slavery ended, Black Americans briefly gained political power during Reconstruction. Black men voted, held office, established schools and businesses. For a moment, multiracial democracy seemed possible.

The white backlash was swift and violent. The end of Reconstruction in 1877 ushered in the Jim Crow era—a system of legal segregation and disenfranchisement designed to restore white supremacy through law rather than slavery. Black Codes, sharecropping, convict leasing, and lynch law created "slavery by another name."

The arguments used to justify Jim Crow echo in contemporary anti-"woke" rhetoric: states' rights, law and order, protecting white children, maintaining social stability. The language changes, but the goal remains the same: preventing Black political and economic power.

Civil Rights Era: The Dream and the Backlash

The 1960s Civil Rights Movement, epitomized by Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech at the March on Washington, represented the most powerful challenge to American racial hierarchy since Reconstruction. The August 28, 1963 march brought 250,000 Americans to the nation's capital in the largest demonstration for civil rights in American history. King's vision of an America where children would "not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character" captured national imagination and moral authority.

This movement achieved unprecedented victories: President Lyndon B. Johnson signed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 ending legal segregation and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 securing political participation, famously telling aide Bill Moyers that Democrats had 'lost the South for a generation.' These laws opened educational and employment opportunities previously denied to Black Americans, who began entering previously all-white institutions and building genuine political and economic power.

Dorothy Counts, 15, on her first day at Harry P. Harding High School, Charlotte, North Carolina, September 4, 1957. Photo by Douglas Martin.

The human cost of integration was borne by individuals like Dorothy Counts, a 15-year-old Black student who became the first to integrate Harry P. Harding High School in Charlotte, North Carolina, in 1957. A haunting photograph captures Dorothy sitting alone in a crowd of white students, their faces twisted with laughter and sneers—not with her, but at her. Surrounded by hostility, she maintained her composure with quiet dignity.

Dorothy faced constant harassment: students spat on her, threw rocks and trash, hurled racial slurs and threats. Yet she never cried, never stopped, never looked down. Her father had told her: "You are not less than anyone." But the crowds waiting for her didn't believe that. Every step Dorothy took was against hate that wanted to crush her spirit.

Though Dorothy only attended the school for four days before her family withdrew her due to safety concerns, those four days lit a fire that couldn't be extinguished. She walked alone—but she walked for thousands. Her courage, like that of countless other integration pioneers, proved that the dire predictions of integration opponents were wrong while strengthening American institutions. At 82 years old, Dorothy Counts-Scoggins continues to live in Charlotte, mentoring students and working to ensure no child endures what she experienced.

Yet as with every previous era of Black advancement, white backlash was immediate and systematic. The Southern Strategy explicitly appealed to white racial resentment. "Law and order" became code for controlling Black communities. The War on Drugs disproportionately targeted Black Americans. School busing faced massive white resistance. King himself was assassinated in 1968, just five years after his dream speech.

From a Canadian perspective, this pattern of progress followed by backlash appears particularly destructive. Canada achieved racial integration with less systematic resistance, suggesting that American white backlash represents choice rather than inevitability.

The "Colorblind" Era: Racism Without Racists

By the 1980s, explicit racial appeals became socially unacceptable. White supremacy adapted by claiming to be "colorblind"—opposing policies that would help Black Americans while denying racial motivation.

The rhetoric became sophisticated: welfare queens, super predators, urban pathology, achievement gaps, cultural deficiencies. The policies remained unchanged: mass incarceration, residential segregation, educational inequity, wealth gaps. Racism continued, but racists claimed not to exist.

This era saw the rise of "merit" arguments that are central to contemporary anti-"woke" politics. Affirmative action was attacked as "reverse racism." Diversity programs were condemned as "lowering standards." Any policy helping Black Americans was labeled "unfair to whites."

Obama and the Breaking Point

Barack Obama's presidency represented Black political achievement that white supremacy couldn't absorb. A Black man exercising ultimate political power triggered white backlash that eventually produced Trump.

Birtherism wasn't just conspiracy theory—it was the assertion that Black Americans can never be truly American. The Tea Party wasn't just fiscal conservatism—it was white identity politics. Even during Obama's presidency, the persistence of racial violence became undeniable with the deaths of Trayvon Martin, Michael Brown, Eric Garner, and others—leading to the founding of Black Lives Matter in 2013. The movement's simple assertion that "Black Lives Matter" was immediately countered with "All Lives Matter," revealing how even the most basic claim to Black humanity triggered white defensiveness.

The rise of explicit white nationalism wasn't anomaly—it was the logical endpoint of escalating racial resentment that couldn't tolerate Black political power at any level.

From a Canadian perspective, the intensity of anti-Obama backlash revealed how little American racial attitudes had actually changed. The election of a Black president didn't signal post-racial progress—it triggered the racial reaction that produced contemporary anti-"woke" politics.

Black Lives Matter and the "Woke" Backlash

The 2020 protests following George Floyd's murder represented the largest civil rights demonstrations in American history, with an estimated 15-26 million Americans participating. The Black Lives Matter movement, which had grown from a hashtag in 2013 to a decentralized global force, organized much of this unprecedented mobilization. For a moment, white Americans seemed to acknowledge systemic racism. Corporations made diversity commitments. Schools taught about racial history. Police reform seemed possible.

BLM's power lay in its ability to connect individual cases of police violence to systemic patterns of racial oppression. The movement didn't just protest specific deaths—it challenged the entire framework of racial hierarchy that made those deaths possible and predictable.

Then came the backlash, and BLM itself became a primary target. "Woke" became the new code word for Black political power, but so did "Black Lives Matter"—transformed by conservative media into a symbol of anti-American radicalism. Critical Race Theory—an academic framework unknown to most Americans—became the boogeyman for any discussion of racism. Diversity programs were attacked as "anti-white discrimination."

The pattern repeated with particular viciousness: Black progress, white backlash, new mechanisms to maintain racial hierarchy. Anti-"woke" politics specifically targets BLM's core insight—that racism is systemic rather than individual—because acknowledging systemic racism would require systemic solutions that threaten white advantages.

The movement's decentralized structure, which had been a source of strength in organizing massive protests, became a vulnerability as opponents could attribute any controversial statement by any activist to the entire movement. "Defund the police"—a policy proposal for reallocating resources from policing to social services—was weaponized as evidence of BLM's supposed anti-American extremism.

From a Canadian perspective, the American backlash against BLM appears particularly counterproductive. The movement's calls for police accountability and racial justice align with basic democratic values that most Canadians support across party lines. American resistance to these reforms undermines both domestic stability and international credibility.

The DEI Target: Black Economic Advancement

Contemporary attacks on Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion programs specifically target Black economic advancement. When corporations eliminate DEI programs, Black hiring and promotion decline while white advancement increases. When universities end affirmative action, Black enrollment drops while white enrollment rises.

The "merit" argument is particularly pernicious because it suggests that Black achievements are undeserved while white success is earned. This ignores centuries of exclusion that prevented Black Americans from building wealth, attending elite schools, or accessing professional networks.

From a Canadian perspective, where employment equity is legally required, American retreat from diversity programs appears economically self-defeating. We benefit from American exclusion by attracting the talent that American racism discourages.

Voter Suppression: The Political Dimension

Anti-"woke" politics includes systematic efforts to reduce Black political power through voter suppression. Gerrymandering dilutes Black votes. Voter ID laws target Black communities. Polling place closures make voting difficult in Black neighborhoods.

These efforts specifically target Black political gains. States with growing Black populations face the most restrictive voting laws. The goal is maintaining white political control despite demographic change.

This represents a direct attack on democracy itself. If political parties cannot win Black votes, they eliminate Black voters rather than change their policies.

The Wealth Gap: Economic Control

Anti-"woke" policies perpetuate racial wealth gaps that ensure Black economic subordination. The median white family has ten times the wealth of the median Black family. This gap hasn't closed despite civil rights legislation—it has widened.

Eliminating programs designed to address these gaps ensures their persistence. Attacking "woke" financial policies prevents Black wealth building. Restricting diversity programs limits Black access to high-paying jobs.

Economic subordination ensures political subordination. Black families without wealth cannot influence politics, fund campaigns, or build generational power.

The International Implications

America's continuous racial hierarchy has international implications that extend beyond domestic politics. Authoritarian regimes use American racism to discredit democratic values. How can America promote human rights globally while maintaining racial inequality domestically?

From a Canadian perspective, American racial policies affect our relationship with our largest ally and trading partner. American brain drain due to racism benefits Canada, but American democratic failure threatens North American stability.

The Canadian Context and Contrast

Canada is not immune to anti-Black racism, but our approach has generally emphasized inclusion rather than exclusion. Our smaller Black population may make racism less politically central, but it doesn't eliminate racial discrimination.

However, Canada has generally moved toward greater inclusion while America moves toward greater exclusion. Employment equity laws require diversity rather than prohibit it. Multiculturalism policies celebrate rather than suppress racial identity.

This difference suggests that American racial hierarchy represents choice rather than inevitability. Other democracies have chosen inclusion over exclusion with better economic and social results.

Conclusion: The Unfinished Business

Anti-"woke" politics represents America's unfinished business with racial equality. Every previous era of Black progress has faced white backlash designed to restore racial hierarchy. Contemporary anti-"woke" politics continues this pattern with new language but identical goals.

The choice facing America is whether to finally complete the work of racial equality or continue the cycle of progress and backlash that has defined American race relations for four centuries. The historical pattern suggests that Black advancement will eventually overcome white resistance, but the question is how much damage will be done to American democracy and competitiveness before this resolution occurs.

From a Canadian perspective, American racial hierarchy appears both morally wrong and strategically self-defeating. The talent and energy wasted on maintaining white supremacy could be directed toward building a more prosperous and just society. The international credibility lost through racial discrimination undermines American global leadership.
