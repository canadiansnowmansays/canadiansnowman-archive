The Epstein Files

The Numbers That Won't Stop Growing

Part 17: From 100,000 to 5.2 Million Pages in Twelve Days

December 31, 2025

December 31, 2025

On December 19, 2025, the Department of Justice released approximately 100,000 pages of documents related to Jeffrey Epstein investigations. The release was mandated by the Epstein Files Transparency Act, passed by Congress with overwhelming bipartisan support and signed into law by President Trump on November 19.

The DOJ had thirty days to prepare. They knew the scope of the files. They understood their obligations under federal law. And they had a clear deadline: December 19, 2025.

What happened in the days that followed reveals either catastrophic institutional incompetence or deliberate obstruction—and in either case, a pattern of accommodation that now threatens to consume the credibility of the Justice Department itself.

The Numbers That Won’t Stop Growing

The story of the Epstein files release can be told through a simple timeline of numbers:

December 19: DOJ releases approximately 100,000 pages. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche announces they’re reviewing “about a million pages” total and will need “a few more weeks” to complete the process.

December 24 (five days later): DOJ announces the FBI and Southern District of New York have “uncovered over a million more documents potentially related to the Jeffrey Epstein case.” The new total: over 2 million pages.

December 30 (eleven days after the original release): The Washington Post reports DOJ is now seeking to enlist approximately 400 lawyers to review roughly 5.2 million pages of Epstein files.

In eleven days, the Department of Justice’s estimate of how many documents exist quintupled. From one million to 5.2 million pages.

This raises three possibilities, none of them reassuring:

  1. Gross institutional incompetence: The DOJ had no systematic understanding of what documents it possessed when Congress passed the transparency law, despite having access to these files for years.

  2. Deliberate delay tactics: Each “discovery” of millions more pages provides justification for continued violations of the December 19 deadline and buys more time to control the narrative.

  3. Both: The department started with genuine ignorance about the scope of its holdings and is now exploiting that incompetence as cover for obstruction.

Consider what DOJ is now claiming: When Congress passed the Epstein Files Transparency Act on November 19, giving them exactly thirty days to comply, the department apparently believed it had about one million pages to review. By their own account, they immediately assigned nearly 200 lawyers from the National Security Division to begin the review process in late November.

What were those 200 lawyers doing for thirty days if not conducting a systematic inventory? How do you assign lawyers to review documents without first determining what documents exist? And how do you “discover” 4.2 million additional pages after already conducting what should have been a comprehensive search?

The December 30 revelation is particularly telling. DOJ now seeks 400 lawyers total—double the original 200—and is pulling prosecutors from national security cases, criminal prosecutions, and U.S. Attorneys’ offices in New York and Florida. According to sources familiar with the matter, the document review is expected to take “until at least January 20.”

January 20, 2025. Inauguration Day.

One could dismiss this as coincidence. Or one could observe that every estimate provided by DOJ has proven false, every deadline has been missed, and every “discovery” has provided justification for further delay—with the next target date conveniently landing on the day a new administration begins.

When the Government Surveils the Journalist

Julie K. Brown, the Miami Herald reporter who dug deep into the Jeffrey Epstein case

On December 26, Julie K. Brown posted a discovery that should alarm anyone who values press freedom and the rule of law.

Brown is the Miami Herald reporter whose dogged investigation into Jeffrey Epstein led directly to his 2019 arrest and, subsequently, to Ghislaine Maxwell’s prosecution and conviction. Her reporting broke through years of prosecutorial accommodation and political protection that had allowed Epstein to operate with near-impunity.

While reviewing the newly released Epstein files, Brown found her own American Airlines booking information from July 2019—complete with her maiden name and flight itinerary—attached to a grand jury subpoena.

“Does somebody at the DOJ want to tell me why my American Airlines booking information and flights in July 2019 are part of the Epstein files attached to a grand jury subpoena?” Brown asked on social media. “Why was the DOJ monitoring me?”

The timeline matters:

  • July 6, 2019: Jeffrey Epstein arrested at Teterboro Airport in New Jersey

  • July 2019: Alexander Acosta—Trump’s Labor Secretary and the former U.S. Attorney who gave Epstein a sweetheart non-prosecution deal in 2008—resigns under pressure

  • July 2019: DOJ obtains Julie K. Brown’s travel records

  • August 10, 2019: Epstein found dead in his Manhattan jail cell

The Department of Justice was tracking the journalist whose investigation had exposed Jeffrey Epstein. Not just monitoring her reporting—tracking her physical movements through airline booking records.

This is not normal. Federal prosecutors don’t routinely surveil journalists. They don’t attach reporters’ travel itineraries to grand jury subpoenas without cause. And when they do take such extraordinary steps, it’s typically because they believe the journalist poses some threat to an investigation.

What threat did Julie K. Brown pose in July 2019? She had already published the reporting that led to Epstein’s arrest. The investigation was public. The case was proceeding.

Unless the threat wasn’t to the investigation of Epstein. Unless the threat was to someone Epstein could implicate.

The House Oversight Committee Democrats immediately demanded answers: “The Department of Justice needs to explain why travel information and booking itineraries for journalists are in the Epstein files.”

Representative Robert Garcia, the ranking Democrat on the committee, went further. In a video interview, he committed that if Democrats retake the majority in the 2026 midterms, “on day one” the Oversight Committee would subpoena Attorney General Pam Bondi, Deputy AG Todd Blanche, the Federal Bureau of Prisons leadership, and former FBI directors to answer questions about both the Epstein files and the surveillance of Brown.

“That’ll happen day one,” Garcia said.

But day one requires a majority. And until then, the questions remain unanswered.

A President’s Christmas Panic

If there was any doubt about President Trump’s personal investment in controlling the Epstein files narrative, his Christmas week social media posts dispelled it.

On December 24, after DOJ announced the “discovery” of over a million additional documents, Trump posted on Truth Social:

“Now a million more pages on Epste are found, DOJ is being forced to spend all of its time on this Democrat inspired hoax. When did they say no more and work on election fraud, etc. The Dems are the ones who worked with Epste, not the Republicans, release all of their names, embarrass them, and get back to helping our country.”

Read that carefully. The President of the United States is calling for the Department of Justice to selectively release only the files that implicate Democrats, explicitly to “embarrass them,” and then stop releasing files altogether.

This isn’t a defense of transparency. It’s a demand for weaponized selective disclosure followed by suppression.

The post continues: “The radical left doesn’t want people talking about Trump and Republican success. only a long ago dead Jeffrey Epstein, just another witch hunt.”

“Just another witch hunt.” That’s how Trump characterizes the court-mandated release of documents about a convicted sex trafficker who died in federal custody while facing charges for trafficking dozens of underage girls.

But Trump wasn’t finished. On Christmas night—December 25—he posted again:

“Merry Christmas to all, including the many sleazebags who love Jeffrey Epste, gave him bundles of money, went to his island, attended his parties, and thought he was the greatest guy on earth, only to drop him like a dog when things got too hot. Falsely claimed that they had nothing to do with him, didn’t know him, said he was a disgusting person, and then blame, of course, President Donald J. Trump, who is actually the only one who did drop Epstein and long before it became fashionable to do so.”

The projection is breathtaking. Trump is describing people who “attended his parties” and “thought he was the greatest guy on earth” while claiming he was “actually the only one who did drop Epstein.”

The documentary record tells a different story:

  • Trump flew on Epstein’s private jet at least eight times between 1993 and 1996, according to a January 2020 email from a federal prosecutor

  • In 1992, Trump and Epstein were the only two male guests at a Mar-a-Lago party with 28 young women

  • Epstein employees testified that Trump and Epstein spoke on the phone at least three times weekly in the mid-to-late 1990s

  • In 2002—two years after Virginia Giuffre was trafficked from Mar-a-Lago—Trump told New York Magazine: “I’ve known Jeff for fifteen years. Terrific guy. He’s a lot of fun to be with. It is even said that he likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side.”

Trump’s Christmas post ends with a threat: “Enjoy what may be your last merry Christmas.”

This is the President of the United States, on Christmas night, threatening unspecified consequences to unspecified people if the Epstein files continue to be released.

The posts reveal something beyond policy disagreement or political calculation. They reveal panic.

The Pattern Accelerates

We are now watching accommodation psychology operate at the institutional level.

In February 2025, Trump claimed he’d never heard of the Epstein files and couldn’t release what he didn’t know existed. By March, after public pressure, he acknowledged the files and promised transparency. In April, he suggested some files might implicate national security. By summer, he was warning that premature release could harm investigations. In August, he was praising Bill Barr’s judgment in keeping the files sealed during Trump’s first term.

Then came the November surprise: Faced with a bipartisan congressional rebellion, Trump signed the Epstein Files Transparency Act into law, praised Congress for their work, and claimed credit for transparency.

The December releases have followed a pattern documented in Parts 14, 15, and 16 of this series:

  • Non-functional search features that required users to download and manually review thousands of pages

  • Selective emphasis on Bill Clinton material in the Friday release

  • Deleted files and photos, later restored after public outcry

  • Improper redactions that both exposed victim identities and concealed relevant information about powerful men

  • Trump ordering DOJ to investigate Clinton’s “involvement and relationship” with Epstein while DOJ simultaneously releases Data Set 8 allegations against Trump with a disclaimer calling them “unfounded and false”

What’s new in these twelve days is the acceleration. The pattern isn’t just continuing—it’s intensifying:

December 19: Partial release with technical problems December 20: Files and photos deleted, then restored December 21-23: Third release, survivors speak out, legal enforcement mechanisms announced December 24: “Discovery” of over a million more documents December 24: Trump demands selective release targeting only Democrats December 25: Trump’s Christmas night threats December 26: Julie K. Brown reveals DOJ surveillance December 30: Document count explodes to 5.2 million pages, new deadline of “at least January 20”

Each development provides justification for the next delay. Each “discovery” excuses the previous failure. Each timeline extension pushes accountability further into the future.

And through it all, Trump oscillates between demanding the DOJ stop wasting time on Epstein and threatening consequences if the releases continue.

This is not the behavior of someone with nothing to hide.

What Enforcement Looks Like

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, D-N.Y., speaks to reporters

On December 22, Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer introduced a resolution directing the Senate to initiate legal action against the Department of Justice for violating the Epstein Files Transparency Act. The resolution will force consideration when the Senate reconvenes on January 5.

The same day, Representatives Thomas Massie and Ro Khanna announced they are drafting inherent contempt charges against Attorney General Pam Bondi. Unlike traditional contempt, which requires DOJ cooperation to enforce, inherent contempt allows Congress to fine Bondi daily until she complies. Massie and Khanna can introduce it as a “privileged resolution,” forcing a vote within two legislative days and requiring only a House majority to pass.

These mechanisms convert the DOJ’s violation from political scandal to legal confrontation with concrete consequences.

Representative Robert Garcia has made clear what Democrats would do with subpoena power: “When we win that majority, the oversight committee on day one is going to get answers and get very aggressive on bringing these folks in.”

But that requires Democrats winning the 2026 midterm elections. Until then, enforcement depends on the handful of Republicans willing to break with Trump.

So far, that list consists primarily of Thomas Massie.

Meanwhile, eighteen survivors of Epstein’s trafficking sent an open letter to Congress on December 22 noting that the “public received a fraction of the files” and that materials were “riddled with abnormal and extreme redactions” while “numerous victim identities were left unredacted, causing real and immediate harm.”

Two survivors, Jenna Lisa Jones and Marina Lacerda, appeared on MSNBC the same night. Jones described reading the files and discovering “numerous girls that I was friends with that I didn’t even know were victims... I cried last night reading their names.” These were high school classmates whose trauma she never knew about until the DOJ exposed their identities.

“They didn’t ask for this and they’re getting thrown into this circus act,” Jones said. “This is not just retraumatizing of what happened to us. We’re being retraumatized by our government.”

Lacerda was more direct: “They’re protecting the wrong people here. It almost feels like they did this on purpose.”

The survivors called for “immediate congressional oversight, including hearings, formal demands for compliance, and legal action.”

Congress reconvenes in five days.

The Test Continues

This is Part 17 of The Epstein Files series because the story won’t end. What began in February 2025 with Trump claiming ignorance has evolved through eleven months of accommodation psychology into an institutional crisis that now encompasses:

  • The systematic violation of federal law

  • The surveillance of journalists investigating government wrongdoing

  • A president demanding weaponized selective disclosure

  • Document counts that quintuple in eleven days

  • Review processes that conveniently extend to Inauguration Day

  • Survivors retraumatized by their own government

  • A pattern of incompetence and obstruction that has become indistinguishable

In a December 23 MSNBC segment, Lawrence O’Donnell invoked Watergate: “Everyone working for Donald Trump knows that they do not have the same immunity that the Supreme Court gave to Donald Trump. And everyone working for Donald Trump should know that Richard Nixon’s White House chief of staff, his White House counsel, his attorney general, and dozens of other people working for Richard Nixon were convicted of crimes and sent to prison.”

O’Donnell is right about the legal exposure. Trump can pardon his subordinates. He cannot pardon himself from political consequences. And he cannot erase the documentary record.

That record now includes:

  • A federal prosecutor’s January 2020 email revealing Trump flew on Epstein’s jet at least eight times, including flights with Maxwell present and a flight where the only other passengers were Epstein and a 20-year-old woman

  • The New York Times investigation documenting Trump’s parties at Mar-a-Lago with underage models in the mid-1990s

  • Flight logs showing Trump’s children flew on Epstein’s plane

  • Testimony from Epstein employees about Trump and Epstein’s near-daily phone calls where “the conversation always revolved around sex”

  • Grand jury subpoenas seeking employment records from Mar-a-Lago in connection with the Maxwell prosecution

  • Julie K. Brown’s airline records in DOJ files from July 2019

  • Trump’s own Christmas week social media posts demanding selective release and threatening consequences

Congress reconvenes January 5. Schumer’s resolution will force a vote. Massie and Khanna’s inherent contempt charges will follow shortly after. The survivors aren’t stopping their advocacy. Judge Engelmayer has already called the DOJ’s claims about the grand jury transcripts “demonstrably false.”

And somewhere in the Department of Justice, 400 lawyers are being pulled from national security cases and criminal prosecutions to review 5.2 million pages of documents about Jeffrey Epstein—documents the DOJ claimed just eleven days ago totaled only one million pages.

The question is no longer whether DOJ violated the law. They did, beginning December 20. The question is whether anyone with power will hold them accountable for it.

The test continues. The pattern persists. And the files—all 5.2 million pages of them—remain mostly sealed.

For now.
