The Great Canadian Trade War

Halloween Update

Congress Checks Trump’s Tantrum

October 31, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays | October 31, 2025

Senator Amy Klobuchar makes the case for overturning Trump’s spite tariffs: ‘No way to treat an ally and no way to treat the American people.’

Note the timestamp: this was passed last night. As House representatives were presumably home trying on their Halloween costumes, Senator Klobuchar is on the Senate floor fighting 10% grocery bill increases. This is what happens when someone in Congress remembers they have an actual job.

Remember when I gave Congressional override just a 5% probability in my October 24 analysis? Well, the Senate just proved that even long shots come in when presidents throw expensive enough tantrums.

In Part V of this series, I documented how Doug Ford’s $75 million Reagan ad triggered Trump to terminate all trade talks and add 10% spite tariffs. Now, just one week later, the U.S. Senate has passed a bipartisan resolution to overturn those tariffs.

The math always wins—and now it’s winning in Congress.

The Timeline of a Tantrum Unraveling

Seven days. That’s all it took to go from presidential rage-tweet to Congressional rebellion:

  • October 24: Reagan ad airs during ALCS games

  • October 24: Trump terminates all Canada-US trade talks (47 minutes later)

  • October 25: Ford announces he’ll pull ads Monday (after weekend coverage)

  • October 26: Trump adds 10% punishment tariffs via Truth Social

  • October 31: Senate passes resolution to overturn tariffs

Senator Amy Klobuchar, who led the charge with Senator Tim Kaine, delivered the killing blow on the Senate floor: “No way to treat an ally and no way to treat the American people.”

That’s not political posturing. That’s math meeting Main Street.

Why Congress Moved at Lightning Speed

The Senate doesn’t usually move this fast, especially not in a divided government. But Trump’s tantrum created a perfect storm of urgency:

1. The Grocery Bill Reality Adding 10% to Canadian imports doesn’t hurt Canada—we’re already diversifying to Asia and Europe at record pace. It hurts American families buying:

  • Lumber for home repairs

  • Energy for heating

  • Food from integrated supply chains

  • Auto parts for car maintenance

2. The Strategic Stupidity Even GOP senators understand that punishing Canada—while we’re helping secure the continent through NORAD, supplying critical minerals, and providing stable energy—is strategically insane. Especially over accurate Reagan quotes.

3. The Business Revolt The U.S. Chamber of Commerce went ballistic. When you lose the business lobby over a spite tax triggered by a political ad, you’ve lost the plot.

4. The Timing Halloween week. Senators are home, hearing from constituents about rising prices. Nothing motivates Congress like angry voters asking why their groceries cost more because the president got mad at a TV commercial.

The Constitutional Questions

Here’s where it gets interesting. Klobuchar and Kaine aren’t newcomers—they’re seasoned operators who understand trade law. The speed of their move suggests they found something with teeth.

Possible paths:

  1. Congressional Review Act (CRA): If Trump used specific authorities, Congress might be able to overturn with simple majorities

  2. Section 232 Override: Some trade mechanisms have built-in Congressional checks

  3. USMCA Violation Resolution: Terminating talks over an ad could trigger treaty provisions

The fact they moved this fast—on Halloween week, no less—suggests they believe they have a path that doesn’t require Trump’s signature or can survive his veto.

If this needs a veto override, they’ll need 67 Senate votes and 290 House votes. Tough, but when American families face 10% price hikes because of a Truth Social tantrum, strange coalitions form.

Ford’s Gambit Becomes Congressional Catalyst

What makes this delicious is how perfectly it validates Ford’s strategy. He didn’t just trigger Trump—he triggered the entire American system of checks and balances.

Think about what Ford’s $75 million actually bought:

  • Reagan’s message reached 24+ million Americans during ALCS/World Series

  • Trump’s tantrum made global headlines

  • Congressional override now makes those headlines again

  • Canada looks reasonable while America tears itself apart

This is what strategic patience looks like. Ford pulled the ads Monday as promised, showing respect. Trump added spite tariffs anyway, showing rage. Now Congress is showing Trump what consequences look like.

The Ghost That Keeps On Giving

This Halloween, Trump’s learning that some ghosts can’t be banished with a Truth Social tantrum. Reagan’s warnings about tariffs, delivered through Ford’s strategic ad buy, have now manifested in the U.S. Senate chamber. The friendly ghost of free trade past just got very real—and very constitutional.

What Happens Next

And as if scripted by a comedy writer, while the Senate was passing this resolution, Toronto and Los Angeles were preparing for Game 6 of the World Series. Millions of Americans are watching Canadian excellence on their screens while their Congress checks their president’s anti-Canadian tantrum. You literally can’t escape Canada this week—we’re in your ballpark, your Senate chamber, and your grocery bills.

Immediate (This Week):

  • House must reconvene and vote

  • Trump will rage-tweet about “RINO traitors”

  • More GOP senators will distance themselves

  • Canada continues diversification, unperturbed

Short-term (November):

  • If House passes, constitutional showdown begins

  • Business pressure intensifies pre-holidays

  • State governors pile on (especially those facing elections)

  • Ford probably drops a polite “told you so”

Medium-term (Through Year-End):

  • Whether this override succeeds or not, damage is done

  • Trump’s trade authority is now questioned by his own party

  • Canada’s pivot to non-US markets accelerates

  • Other allies (EU, Japan, Mexico) take notes

The Halloween Lesson

As American kids trick-or-treat tonight, their parents are learning about the ultimate Halloween horror story: A president who adds taxes because he got outsmarted by a Canadian premier quoting an American president.

The ghosts haunting Trump tonight aren’t supernatural—they’re:

  • Reagan’s actual words about tariff disasters

  • Congressional Republicans breaking ranks

  • American families checking grocery receipts

  • Canadian negotiators calmly building Asian partnerships

Ford dressed his message in Reagan’s costume, Trump thought he could make it disappear, and now Congress is checking who got the most candy. Spoiler: It isn’t the guy adding 10% to everything.

The Bottom Line

In one week, we’ve gone from “shrewd tactical media buy” to “constitutional crisis over spite tariffs.” That’s not normal. But then again, adding punishment taxes because someone quoted your hero accurately isn’t normal either.

My October 24 prediction was that someone would eventually explain to Trump that tantrums cost money. I didn’t expect that “someone” to be the U.S. Senate, and I didn’t expect it this fast.

But here’s what I know: The math always wins. And right now, the math is winning in the halls of Congress.

Ford spent $75 million on ads. Trump spent political capital on spite. The Senate is now spending Halloween telling Trump that presidential tantrums have constitutional consequences.

For Canada? We’re spending our time building trade relationships with adults. The contrast couldn’t be clearer.

Happy Halloween, America. The scariest thing in your house isn’t a costume—it’s the grocery bill your president just raised because he lost a media strategy battle to Canada.
