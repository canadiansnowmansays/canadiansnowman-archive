The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 8: The Economic Stranglehold

March 31, 2026

Editor’s Note

This essay was written in January 2026, documenting the economic conditions that drove Iranians into the streets in December 2025. The rial in freefall. Inflation above 40 percent. Sixty percent of the population experiencing food insecurity. A budget that increased security spending by 150 percent while offering workers wage increases worth less than half the rate of inflation.

Those conditions have not improved. They were, in fact, the kindling. The January massacres and the subsequent war have only deepened the crisis. The sanctions architecture remains. The IRGC kleptocracy remains. The ordinary Iranian’s economic reality is, if anything, worse.

The numbers here are not historical curiosities. They are the explanation for everything that followed. Read them as the indictment they are.

— Stephen, March 2026
____________________________________________________________________

Women and children stand in a long queue for humanitarian aid and basic staples amid severe food shortages and price surges. Getty Images / AFP

How Ideology Bankrupted a Nation

On December 16, 2024, facing spiraling economic crisis, Ayatollah Khamenei addressed the Iranian people: “We must all reduce consumption of food, water, energy, and fuel. This is our duty as Iranians.”

The order came from a man whose office (Setad) controlled an estimated $95 billion in assets in 2013. From a Supreme Leader whose IRGC had just transferred over $1 billion to Hezbollah while Iranians couldn’t afford eggs. From the Guardian of a revolution fought partly over economic inequality and monarchical excess.

By January 2026, the economic situation had deteriorated further:

  • Inflation: 42-48% annually

  • Poverty: 50%+ of population below poverty line

  • Malnourishment: 60% of Iranians experiencing food insecurity

  • Currency: 1.4+ million rials per USD (from ~70 rials in 1979)

  • Hunger: 7 million Iranians food insecure

  • Unemployment: Official 9-11%, real rate estimated 20-30%

  • Daily blackouts: 3-4 hours nationwide

  • Minimum wage: Below subsistence level

The Islamic Revolution promised economic justice. Forty-six years later, under Khamenei’s 37-year guardianship, economic inequality and suffering exceed what existed under the Shah.

This isn’t external judgment. This is measurement by revolutionary standards—the promises made in 1979, the principles Khamenei claims to uphold.

By those standards, his economic record is catastrophic failure.

I. THE INFLATION DEATH SPIRAL

Pre-Revolution vs. Revolutionary Reality

Under the Shah (1960-1978): Average inflation ~10% annually, sometimes lower.

Under the Islamic Republic (1979-2026): Average inflation ~17-18% annually, with devastating spikes.

Recent years tell the story:

  • 2018-2023: 40-50% annually

  • October 2025: 48.6%

  • December 2025: 42.2%

  • Food inflation (2025): 72% year-over-year

  • Some staples: 100% increases (chicken, eggs, rice)

What This Means

An item costing 100 rials in 1979 costs over 2 million rials in 2026.

Mathematically: 99.995% loss of currency value over 47 years.

Practically: Savings destroyed, wages meaningless, planning impossible, survival precarious.

The 2025 Acceleration

In 2025, multiple shocks hit simultaneously:

  • June war damaged economy and oil infrastructure

  • UN sanctions returned September 2025

  • Government raised gasoline prices 66% (removing subsidies)

  • Global oil prices declined

  • Iranian oil exports constrained

Result: Inflation accelerated, hitting food prices especially hard. Meat became luxury item. Cooking oil unaffordable for many. Bread prices doubled.

By late 2025, Iranian families faced stark choices: pay for food or pay for heating? Buy medicine or buy eggs? The “Resistance Economy” Khamenei promised in 2014 delivered only resistance to survival.

A shopper navigates supermarket aisles with limited stock and high prices visible on shelves. Photo credit: Stock photography illustrating inflation and supply issues.

II. THE CURRENCY COLLAPSE

The rial’s trajectory tells Iran’s economic story:

Historical rates:

  • 1979: ~70 rials per USD

  • 1990s: Thousands per USD

  • 2018 (pre-Trump sanctions): 42,000 (official rate)

  • March 2025: ~700,000

  • June 2025 (post-war): ~850,000

  • January 2026: 1.4+ million

The Human Impact

Workers’ wages: What bought a month’s groceries in 2018 buys a week’s groceries in 2026—if that.

Minimum wage dropped from $180 (March 2025) to $130 (September 2025) in dollar terms—not because rial wages decreased, but because the rial collapsed.

Imports: Medicine, food, machinery all require foreign currency. As rial weakens, imports become prohibitively expensive.

The Government’s Response

Historically, Iran maintained subsidized exchange rates for “essential imports”—initially 42,000 rials per USD while parallel market was much higher. This created:

  • Corruption (those with access to subsidized dollars profited)

  • Distortions (artificial pricing incentivized rent-seeking)

  • Inequality (connected elites benefited, ordinary Iranians suffered)

By 2025, subsidized rates were abandoned for most transactions—the government lacked foreign currency reserves to maintain the fiction.

The result: Even government couldn’t control the rial’s freefall.

III. THE POVERTY EXPLOSION

The Numbers

World Bank estimates and Iranian internal assessments paint grim picture:

Translation: Half of Iran’s 86.5 million people live below poverty line.

The Malnourishment Crisis

In 2024, Iran’s Ministry of Social Welfare announced: 57% of Iranians experience some level of malnourishment.

By August 2025, Shargh newspaper reported severe deficiencies in protein access across the population, citing NGO surveys.

Seven million Iranians face acute food insecurity—genuine hunger, not just inadequate nutrition.

The Protein Crisis

Meat prices surged beyond most families’ reach. Chicken—traditionally affordable protein—became semi-luxury. Eggs, once staple, now expense. Fish, beans, even dairy products increasingly unaffordable.

Result: Protein deficiency, especially affecting children. Malnutrition’s long-term effects—stunted growth, cognitive impacts, weakened immunity—will burden Iran for generations.

Compare to Shah’s Era

Pre-revolution (1970s): Approximately 20-25% poverty, but widespread acute hunger was not reported as systematic problem.

Post-revolution (2025): 50%+ poverty, 57% malnourishment, 7 million food insecure.

The revolution promised to lift up the poor. Instead, it impoverished millions more.

IV. THE DAILY HUMILIATIONS

Beyond statistics, Iranians face daily degradations:

Electricity Blackouts

Since February 2025: Power outages 3-4 hours daily, nationwide.

Impact:

  • Businesses close during blackouts, losing revenue

  • Hospitals struggle to maintain operations

  • Refrigerated food spoils

  • Students can’t study

  • Air conditioning fails in summer heat (45°C+)

  • Heating fails in winter cold

Cause: Mismanagement, underinvestment, selling cheap low-grade fuel (mazut) instead of cleaner options, decades of sanctions limiting equipment imports.

An “energy superpower” with world’s second-largest gas reserves cannot keep lights on.

Water Crisis

Summer 2025: Rotational water cutoffs in Tehran and other cities. Autumn warnings of rationing if drought persists.

Twenty provinces suffered worst drought in 40+ years.

Cause: Climate change, yes—but also mismanagement, over-extraction, failed infrastructure, diversion for agriculture without sustainability planning.

Air Pollution

Tehran and other cities experience 3-7 clean air days per year.

Why? Government switched to cheaper, dirtier fuel (mazut) to keep power on. The air became toxic, but alternatives weren’t affordable.

Children, elderly, those with respiratory conditions suffer most.

Medicine Shortages

Though medicine is technically exempt from sanctions, payment difficulties and banking restrictions make imports challenging.

Result: Chronic shortage of essential medications. Black markets thrive. Patients with cancer, diabetes, heart conditions struggle to find medication.

Some die from treatable conditions because medicine is unavailable or unaffordable.

Food Lines and Hoarding

As prices surge and shortages loom, Iranians queue for staples. Videos from late 2025 show lines at bakeries, fights over discounted chicken, panic buying when items become available.

The government introduced “inflation tax” in August 2025—making inflation itself partially taxable, supposedly to discourage hoarding. In reality, it further squeezed struggling families.

V. WHILE THE ELITE FEAST

As ordinary Iranians suffered in 2025, where did resources go?

Foreign Spending (documented in Part 7):

  • $1 billion+ to Hezbollah (first 10 months)

  • Continued Syria support (until December collapse)

  • Support for other proxies

  • Nuclear program (until June war)

  • Ballistic missile production

IRGC Elite

Investigative reports document Revolutionary Guard commanders’ lifestyles:

  • Luxury compounds in Tehran’s wealthiest neighborhoods

  • Vacation properties on Caspian coast

  • Foreign properties through family members (Turkey, UAE, Europe)

  • Access to subsidized foreign currency

  • Import licenses for luxury vehicles (while ordinary Iranians couldn’t afford fuel)

  • Private education for children abroad

  • Medical treatment in Europe

The Tax Exemption Scandal

Before 2019 protests, it emerged that Khamenei had exempted IRGC entities—including massive economic holdings—from taxation.

Setad, Khatam al-Anbiya, and other IRGC-linked organizations controlling hundreds of billions in assets paid no taxes.

Meanwhile, ordinary Iranians faced:

  • VAT on purchases

  • “Inflation taxes”

  • Subsidy removals (fuel, bread, other basics)

  • Increased fees for government services

The wealthiest entities in Iran—those most able to contribute—were exempt. The poorest—those least able—bore the burden.

The Revolutionary Betrayal Made Explicit

The 1979 revolution was fought partly because the Shah’s family and military-connected elite enriched themselves while ordinary Iranians struggled.

Revolutionary slogans: “Economic justice!” “End corruption!” “Serve the people, not the elite!”

Compare 2025: Military-clerical elite enriched through IRGC monopolies and Khamenei-controlled Setad. 1% hold 33% of wealth. Tax exemptions for connected entities. Billions to foreign proxies while Iranians starve.

The revolutionary promise was betrayed by the revolution’s Guardian.

VI. THE SANCTIONS EXCUSE

Inevitably, Khamenei and defenders blame sanctions for economic crisis.

The Partial Truth

Sanctions hurt—no question. U.S. sanctions (reimposed 2018, intensified 2025) and UN sanctions (returned September 2025) constrain:

  • Oil exports

  • Banking transactions

  • Technology imports

  • International trade

  • Foreign investment

These impose real costs and limit economic options.

The Convenient Lie

But sanctions don’t explain:

Corruption: IRGC monopolies, no-bid contracts, Setad’s $95 billion—all domestic choices.

Mismanagement: Daily blackouts in country with massive energy reserves—policy failure, not sanctions.

Inequality: 1% holding 33% of wealth—distribution choice, not sanctions effect.

Foreign spending: Billions to proxies while Iranians hungry—priority choice, not sanctions mandate.

Tax exemptions: IRGC entities exempt while ordinary Iranians taxed—domestic policy, not sanctions.

Sanctions made bad situation worse. But they didn’t create bad situation.

The Comparative Question

Venezuela and North Korea face comparable or worse sanctions. Both struggle economically. Neither has Iran’s oil and gas resources.

Yet Iran—”energy superpower” with educated population, significant infrastructure, and natural resources—performs worse than countries with fewer advantages.

Why? Policy choices. Corruption. Ideological priorities over pragmatic economics. Military spending and foreign adventurism over domestic investment.

According to Iran Open Data: Iran loses ~20% of potential oil revenue to sanctions-evasion costs—but those costs enrich IRGC networks running shadow tankers and front companies.

The sanctions excuse is true enough to be believable, false enough to hide the deeper truth: the system itself is the problem.

VII. HIS OWN WORDS CONDEMN HIM

On Economic Justice (2014 “Resistance Economy” speech):

“Economic justice is not just about raising GDP. It’s about equitable distribution, about ensuring every Iranian can live with dignity. The Islamic Republic serves the people, especially the oppressed masses.”

Test against 2025:

  • Equitable distribution? 1% hold 33% of wealth

  • Dignified living? 60% malnourished, 7 million hungry

  • Serving oppressed masses? The oppressed masses are in streets demanding his overthrow

On Priorities (2017):

“We prioritize Iranian welfare. Economic policy must serve the people first.”

Test: $1 billion to Hezbollah while Iranians can’t afford eggs. Priorities revealed.

On Leadership (1989):

*”I will use all my capabilities and faith to bear this heavy responsibility”—the responsibility to serve the Iranian people.

Test: December 2024 order to “reduce consumption” while maintaining foreign spending and elite privileges. That’s the opposite of servant leadership.

On the Shah’s Failures (multiple speeches):

Khamenei frequently invoked the Shah’s economic failures to justify the revolution: corruption, inequality, elite enrichment, popular suffering.

Test: By every metric, Khamenei’s Iran features worse corruption, worse inequality, more elite enrichment, more popular suffering than the Shah’s Iran.

The December 2024 Order

“People must reduce consumption of food, water, energy, and fuel.”

This order encapsulates 37 years:

The revolution was fought for economic justice. The revolution’s Guardian, facing economic crisis created by his system’s failures, ordered the hungry to eat less, the thirsty to drink less, the cold to endure without heat.

Not: “I will reduce foreign spending.”

Not: “The elite will share the burden.”

Not: “IRGC will pay taxes.”

Just: “Consume less.”

Marie Antoinette’s alleged “Let them eat cake” has nothing on Khamenei’s documented “Eat less cake.”

VIII. THE CHOICE MADE CLEAR

Iran’s economic crisis isn’t inevitable. It’s the result of choices:

Choice 1: Spend billions on foreign proxies OR invest in domestic infrastructure, education, healthcare.

Khamenei chose proxies. Now Syria’s lost, Hezbollah’s devastated, Hamas is crushed—and Iranians are still hungry.

Choice 2: Exempt IRGC elite from taxes OR require equitable contribution.

Khamenei chose exemptions for the powerful, taxes for the powerless.

Choice 3: Pursue pragmatic economics OR maintain ideological rigidity.

Khamenei chose ideology—”resistance economy,” refusing necessary reforms, doubling down on failed policies.

Choice 4: Prioritize Iranian welfare OR pursue regional hegemony.

Khamenei chose hegemony. Forty years later, no hegemony achieved—just bankrupt nation.

Choice 5: Admit policy failures and reform OR blame foreigners and persist.

Khamenei chose blame. Thirty-seven years of blaming “enemies” for problems created by his own choices.

Every choice sacrificed Iranian welfare for other priorities. Every choice contradicted revolutionary promises. Every choice made by the man claiming to guard those promises.

CLOSING: THE BILL COMES DUE

Crowds gather at night amid burning vehicles and darkened streets during unrest. Photo credit: Citizen video / verified footage from CNN, The New York Times

In January 2026, Iranians protest across all 31 provinces. The primary trigger: economic desperation.

They can’t afford food. They can’t afford heat. They can’t find medicine. They suffer daily blackouts. Their savings are worthless. Their wages buy nothing. Their children go hungry.

And they know—because they’re not stupid—where the money went: foreign proxies now defeated, IRGC commanders’ luxury compounds, nuclear programs now damaged, ballistic missiles mostly intercepted, elite bank accounts, Khamenei’s Setad controlling billions.

The revolution promised economic justice. Khamenei delivered systematic impoverishment justified by ideology and blamed on foreigners.

By the revolution’s own standards—serving the oppressed masses, providing economic equity, ensuring dignified life for all Iranians—his economic record is complete failure.

Worse than the Shah. Measurably, documentably worse by every metric that matters to ordinary people: poverty rate, inflation, currency value, access to food, electricity, water.

When Khamenei ordered Iranians to “consume less” in December 2024, he admitted—inadvertently—what 37 years of his guardianship delivered: a nation so impoverished its leader can only tell citizens to accept hunger.

The revolution promised bread. Khamenei delivered stones. And told them to chew harder.

The bunker beneath his compound protects him from enemies. But it can’t protect him from the economic catastrophe he created, the hungry Iranians he failed, or the revolutionary promises he betrayed.

The bill has come due. And Iranians are done paying.
