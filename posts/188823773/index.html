Submit or Be Destroyed

Volume 4: The Republican Purge — How Trump Eliminated Anyone in His Party Who Challenged Him

March 11, 2026

On January 13, 2021, ten House Republicans voted to impeach Donald Trump for inciting insurrection. One week later, in the Senate trial, seven Republican senators voted to convict. Together, those seventeen legislators represented the most significant intraparty challenge to a president in modern American history.

By the end of 2025, the purge was nearly complete.

Four of the ten House members lost their primaries to Trump-backed challengers. Four more retired rather than face the political destruction Trump had promised. One announced his retirement in December 2025. One — David Valadao of California — filed for reelection, the lone survivor of a generation that dared to vote their conscience.

The success rate of Trump’s retribution against his own party’s impeachment voters: approximately ninety percent.

This volume documents the purge — not just the outcomes, but the mechanics. Because the destruction of Liz Cheney, the submission of Ted Cruz, the fall of Marjorie Taylor Greene, and the siege of Thomas Massie aren’t separate stories. They’re the same story, told through different characters. And the lesson they collectively teach is the most important finding in this entire series: Donald Trump has made opposition within the Republican Party functionally impossible.

The Seventeen

The 2021 impeachment vote divided into two groups. Ten House members voted to impeach. Seven senators voted to convict. Their fates tell the story of the purge.

The House Ten — What Happened to Each:

Liz Cheney (Wyoming): The highest-ranking Republican to vote for impeachment — she was House Republican Conference Chair, the third-ranking position in the caucus. Trump endorsed attorney Harriet Hageman. Cheney was stripped of her leadership position, censured by the Wyoming Republican Party, and ultimately lost her primary by 37 points — the most decisive primary defeat of any sitting member of Congress in recent memory. She now faces potential legal prosecution from the same administration she voted to impeach.

Tom Rice (South Carolina): A reliable conservative whose impeachment vote shocked even his own staff. Trump endorsed state legislator Russell Fry. Rice lost his primary by 25 points.

Jaime Herrera Beutler (Washington): A six-term incumbent and moderate Republican. Lost her primary in Washington’s jungle primary system in 2022.

Peter Meijer (Michigan): A freshman who voted to impeach in his first month in office. Trump endorsed John Gibbs. Meijer lost his primary by less than 4 points. “I would rather lose office with my character intact,” he said, “than stay reelected having made sacrifices of the soul.”

Anthony Gonzalez (Ohio): A former NFL wide receiver in his second term. Trump endorsed his former aide Max Miller within weeks of the impeachment vote. The Ohio Republican Party censured Gonzalez and called for his resignation. He retired in September 2021, less than eight months after the impeachment vote, citing threats to his family.

Adam Kinzinger (Illinois): Retired in October 2021 after redistricting drew him into a district with Trump-endorsed Darin LaHood. Kinzinger served as a member of the January 6th Select Committee alongside Cheney.

Fred Upton (Michigan): A thirty-five-year veteran of the House — first elected in 1987. Retired in April 2022 after redistricting and facing a Trump-aligned primary challenger.

John Katko (New York): A moderate who had won reelection in a swing district. Retired in January 2022.

Dan Newhouse (Washington): Survived his 2022 primary in Washington’s jungle system. Announced retirement in December 2025 — the second-to-last survivor finally stepping aside.

David Valadao (California): The only House impeachment voter who has filed for reelection in 2026. The last one standing.

The final count: four primaried and defeated, four retired preemptively, one announced retirement. One survivor out of ten.

The Senate Seven voted to convict in the trial: Richard Burr (NC), Bill Cassidy (LA), Susan Collins (ME), Lisa Murkowski (AK), Mitt Romney (UT), Ben Sasse (NE), and Pat Toomey (PA). Of these, Burr, Sasse, Toomey, and Romney all left the Senate before facing voters again. Murkowski survived a 2022 challenge. Collins wasn’t up for reelection until 2026. Cassidy faces a 2026 primary in deep-red Louisiana. The purge continues.

The Cheney Destruction: Template for Everything

Liz Cheney’s case deserves extended attention because it established the template for all subsequent purges.

Before her impeachment vote, Cheney was Republican royalty. The daughter of Vice President Dick Cheney, she represented Wyoming — one of the most conservative states in the country, which Trump had won with 70% of the vote. She was the third-ranking Republican in the House. She was, by every conventional measure, one of the most powerful and best-protected Republicans in Congress.

None of it mattered.

The destruction followed the Cohn playbook with mechanical precision. Trump endorsed her primary challenger. The Wyoming Republican Party censured her. She was stripped of her leadership position in a closed-door vote. Republican colleagues who had privately agreed with her impeachment vote publicly abandoned her. The National Republican Congressional Committee, which normally protects incumbents, refused to support her.

Cheney, to her credit, did not submit. She served as vice chair of the January 6th Select Committee, delivering some of the most devastating public presentations of evidence against Trump in any congressional proceeding. She became the face of Republican resistance to Trump.

She lost by 37 points.

The margin matters. It wasn’t a close race. It was annihilation — a demonstration that in a Republican primary, opposing Trump is not a calculated risk. It is political suicide. Every Republican who has considered challenging Trump since Cheney’s defeat has done so with that number etched in their mind: 37 points.

The Submission Template: Cruz, Rubio, Graham, Vance

The purge wasn’t just about destroying opponents. It was equally about showcasing the rewards of submission — creating a parallel narrative in which former critics who bent the knee were elevated to positions of extraordinary power.

The submission cases are, in some ways, more revealing than the destructions, because they demonstrate what Trump actually wants. He doesn’t want to eliminate all opposition; he wants to convert it into servitude. The most valuable allies are not those who were always loyal — they’re those who once opposed and then submitted, because their conversion proves Trump’s dominance over their conscience.

Marco Rubio: Trump called him “a con artist” and “Little Marco” during the 2016 primary. Rubio called Trump “a con man” who was “the most vulgar person to ever aspire to the presidency.” Today Rubio is Trump’s Secretary of State — the nation’s chief diplomat, representing the man he once said was too vulgar to be president.

Ted Cruz: Trump called his wife ugly, accused his father of conspiring in the JFK assassination, and branded him “Lyin’ Ted.” Cruz called Trump “utterly amoral,” “a pathological liar,” and “a narcissist at a level I don’t think this country has ever seen.” He refused to endorse Trump at the 2016 Convention. Then he endorsed him. Then he brought Heidi to dinner with the Trumps. Today, Cruz told The View that Heidi “laughed” when Trump called her ugly and that his father “laughed too.” He campaigns for Trump, advocates for Trump, and has been rewarded with political survival.

Lindsey Graham: Called Trump “a race-baiting, xenophobic, religious bigot” who would “destroy” the Republican Party. Today he golfs with Trump regularly and is one of his most visible Senate allies. The transformation required no public accounting.

J.D. Vance: Called Trump “America’s Hitler” and said he might be “a cynical asshole” who was “nativism leading to fascism.” Today he is Vice President of the United States.

In each case, the more extreme the original opposition, the more valuable the submission. When Vance goes from “America’s Hitler” to Vice President, the hypocrisy isn’t the scandal — it’s the point. It demonstrates, in the most public possible way, that principles are negotiable and that Trump’s power transcends any conviction his allies once held.

Marjorie Taylor Greene: The Most Loyal Soldier Falls

If the Cheney case is the template for destruction of opponents, the Marjorie Taylor Greene case is something more disturbing: the destruction of the most loyal ally imaginable, for a single act of deviation.

For five years — from 2020 through most of 2025 — MTG was Trump’s fiercest defender in Congress. She embraced QAnon theories. She was photographed rubbing a cardboard cutout of Trump at campaign events. She was stripped of her committee assignments by Democrats for inflammatory rhetoric — and Trump defended her throughout. She voted with Trump on everything. She was expelled from the Freedom Caucus for being too pro-Trump. She was, by any reasonable measure, the single most loyal Trump supporter in the entire United States Congress.

In November 2025, Greene signed a discharge petition to release the Jeffrey Epstein files — alongside Thomas Massie and Democrat Ro Khanna. She criticized Trump’s foreign policy as “not America First.” She opposed H-1B visas. She called Israel’s actions in Gaza genocide. She told 60 Minutes that Trump was “extremely angry” about the Epstein files push.

Trump’s response was instantaneous and total. He branded her “Marjorie ‘Traitor’ Greene” and “Marjorie Taylor Brown (Green grass turns Brown when it begins to ROT!).” He called her “low IQ.” He revoked his endorsement and backed primary challenger Clay Fuller. Death threats poured in — including against her son. Greene said Trump “blamed me for the death threats coming in on my own son.”

On January 5, 2026, Greene resigned from Congress. She said she didn’t want her district “to have to endure a hurtful and hateful primary against me by the president we all fought for.” On The View, she was direct: “The straw that broke the camel’s back was the president calling me a traitor for standing with women who were raped when they were teenagers.”

By February 2026, Greene had arrived at a place that would have been unimaginable a year earlier. “I’m not in his cult,” she said. “Him calling me that has become a badge of honor because I’m not in his cult, I refused to bow to his demands to cover up the Epstein files to protect his friends.”

The MTG case proves something the Cheney case didn’t. Cheney’s destruction could be rationalized — she was a dissident, she voted for impeachment, she joined the January 6th Committee. She had crossed a bright line. But Greene? Greene was the truest believer. Five years of total loyalty, destroyed in weeks, because she pushed for the release of documents about the sexual exploitation of minors.

If Marjorie Taylor Greene isn’t safe, no one is safe. That’s not an observation. It’s the operating principle.

Thomas Massie: Fighting, Not Folding

The most instructive case currently unfolding is Thomas Massie of Kentucky — because Massie is doing something almost no Republican has attempted: fighting Trump’s retribution machine head-on while remaining in office.

Massie co-led the Epstein files push with Ro Khanna. He voted against Trump’s “One Big Beautiful Bill.” He opposed the Iran strikes as unconstitutional. He opposed Venezuelan regime change. In short, he voted his conscience — consistently, transparently, and without apology.

Trump’s response has been the most aggressive campaign of intraparty destruction since Liz Cheney. He launched Kentucky MAGA, a dedicated super PAC run by Chris LaCivita to unseat Massie — the first such effort in modern history targeting a sitting Republican incumbent with a purpose-built political operation. A $1 million ad buy was deployed. Trump endorsed Navy SEAL Ed Gallrein as a primary challenger. He called Massie “LOSER,” “moron” (at the National Prayer Breakfast), “weak and pathetic RINO,” and “true hater of Israel.”

Then Trump went after Massie’s family. On Truth Social, he attacked Massie’s wife, claiming she had made him “a Liberal.” At a Lincoln Day Dinner, Massie’s microphone was taken away mid-speech as he tried to defend his wife from Trump’s attacks.

Massie’s response has been characteristically direct. He posted: “I am not suicidal. I eat healthy food. The brakes on my car and truck are in good shape. I practice good trigger discipline... There are no deep pools of water on my farm and I’m a pretty good swimmer.” When Trump announced a supposed alien disclosure, Massie called it “the ultimate weapon of mass distraction” from the Epstein files.

As of this writing, Massie has raised $2.2 million for his primary defense. He has the endorsement of Rand Paul and the backing of Elon Musk. He faces a May 2026 primary that will be the most closely watched congressional race in the country — because its outcome will answer a question that the Cheney destruction left open: can anyone survive the Destruction Machine while remaining in office?

If Massie wins, it proves that resistance is possible.

If Massie loses, it proves that it isn’t.

Jeff Hurd: The Speed of Retribution

On February 21, 2026 — the day this series was conceived — Trump pulled his endorsement of Colorado Representative Jeff Hurd for voting against Trump’s tariff package.

Hurd’s case is notable for its speed and its pettiness. This wasn’t an impeachment vote. It wasn’t a years-long pattern of defiance. It was a single policy disagreement on tariffs. And within hours — hours — Trump revoked his endorsement and signaled that Hurd would face a primary challenge.

The Hurd case demonstrates that the retribution threshold has dropped to zero. In 2021, you had to vote for impeachment to trigger destruction. By 2026, a single tariff vote is sufficient. The purity test has narrowed to a point where any deviation — on any vote, on any issue, at any time — can trigger the full machinery of retribution.

This is the logical endpoint of the system Roy Cohn built. When every disagreement is treated as betrayal, and every betrayal triggers destruction, what remains is not a political party. It’s an obedience structure. Members don’t vote based on their constituents’ interests or their own judgment. They vote based on a single calculation: will this trigger retribution?

The Three Paths, Fully Mapped

This volume completes the Three Paths framework introduced in the Introduction.

Path One: Never Oppose. Rewarded — as long as the loyalty never wavers. MTG followed this path for five years. It worked until it didn’t. The lesson: even lifetime loyalty provides no protection if you deviate once.

Path Two: Oppose, Then Submit. This is the path Trump rewards most lavishly — Rubio as Secretary of State, Vance as Vice President, Cruz as protected senator, Graham as golf partner. The more extreme the original opposition and the more humiliating the reversal, the more valuable you become, because your submission proves that Trump’s power exceeds your principles.

Path Three: Oppose and Stay Principled. Destruction. Cheney by 37 points. Rice by 25. Gonzalez driven out in eight months. Kinzinger redistricted. Upton retired after 35 years. MTG resigned. Massie under siege. Hurd endorsed and un-endorsed within a single congressional term.

Ninety percent of the impeachment voters are gone. The ones who submitted — Cruz, Graham, Rubio, Vance — were rewarded with the most powerful positions in American government. The ones who didn’t submit were driven into oblivion.

The system works. That’s the terrifying conclusion. Not that Trump is cruel — that’s obvious. Not that he demands loyalty — that’s been true since Roy Cohn. The terrifying conclusion is that the system functions with mechanical efficiency. It identifies dissent. It calibrates the response. It executes the punishment. And it rewards the submission — publicly, lavishly, and in full view of everyone who might be considering their own act of conscience.

What This Means for Democracy

I write this series as a Canadian observing American democracy with the combination of proximity and distance that my perspective provides. And what I observe is this: the Republican Party of the United States is no longer a political party in any meaningful democratic sense. It is an obedience structure organized around a single individual, maintained through systematic retribution against dissent, and enforced by the demonstrated willingness to destroy anyone — including the most loyal allies — who deviates from absolute compliance.

This is not normal. It is not normal for a democracy to have a major political party in which ninety percent of legislators who voted their conscience on a constitutional question were eliminated within four years. It is not normal for a political leader to attack the family members of his own party’s elected officials. It is not normal for a single tariff vote to trigger the full machinery of political destruction.

In my “World According to Trump” series, I documented the binary psychology that drives this behavior — the division of the world into loyal and disloyal, winners and losers. In my “Enforcers” series, I documented the cabinet members who have internalized the retribution instinct and run independent destruction operations. In my “Enemies List” series, I documented the most dangerous manifestation — the weaponization of the justice system itself.

This volume documents what may be the most consequential element: the transformation of the Republican Party from a democratic institution into an instrument of one man’s will, enforced through fear and maintained through the systematic destruction of anyone who dares to think independently.

In the final volume, we examine how that instrument is being used — not just against fellow Republicans, but against federal employees, universities, scientists, states, and entire sectors of American life.
