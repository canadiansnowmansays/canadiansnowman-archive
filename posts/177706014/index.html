The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

Trump's 2013 Words vs. His 2025 Actions

The Man Who Blamed Biden and Obama for Everything Takes No Responsibility for Anything

November 1, 2025

If in the last episode of this series we followed the money to expose the Trump administration’s true priorities, this episode follows the lies—the statements, the spin, and the shameless reversals that accompanied the manufactured crisis. Because the hypocrisy wasn’t just in what they did, but in what they said while doing it.

“Problems Start from the Top”—Unless Trump Is on Top

Perhaps no hypocrisy is more brazen than Trump’s complete reversal on who bears responsibility for government shutdowns.

In 2011, speaking to NBC, Trump was unequivocal: “In my opinion I hear the Democrats are going to be blamed and the Republicans are going to be blamed. I actually think the President would be blamed. If there is a shutdown I think it would be a tremendously negative mark on the president of the United States. He’s the one who has to get people together.”

In 2013, during the Obama administration’s shutdown, Trump expanded on this theme in a Fox & Friends interview: “I mean, problems start from the top and they have to get solved from the top. And the president’s the leader. And he’s got to get everybody in a room and he’s got to lead.”

He was even more direct when asked who would be fired during a shutdown: “Well, if you say who gets fired it always has to be the top. I mean, problems start from the top and they have to get solved from the top and the president’s the leader. And he’s got to get everybody in a room and he’s got to lead.”

Trump tweeted repeatedly during that period, saying things like “Leadership: Whatever happens, you’re responsible. If it doesn’t happen, you’re responsible” and agreeing with supporters who said such a thing would never happen if Trump were president.

He told Fox News host Greta Van Susteren in October 2013: “You have to get everybody in a room. You have to be a leader. The president has to lead. He has to get [the Speaker] and everybody else in a room, and they have to make a deal.”

Fast forward to 2025, and Trump—now president during a shutdown lasting over 30 days—has blamed everyone but himself. He spent the first weeks of the shutdown traveling abroad in Malaysia, Japan, and South Korea, meeting with foreign leaders while refusing to negotiate with congressional Democrats.

When Senator Peter Welch expressed frustration that Trump had taken a foreign trip during the crisis, he called it “an indication of how he could care less.” Senator Mark Warner was even more blunt, calling it “outrageous” that Trump would take another foreign trip with the government shut down: “The message is, frankly, President Trump: ‘Stay in America. Put America first. Sit down with us and work this out.’”

Policy experts noted that as of late October, there had not been a single meeting between the administration and Democratic leaders to negotiate an end to the shutdown. Bobby Kogan of the Center for American Progress stated: “There hasn’t been a single meeting to reopen the government.”

Trump simply refused to sit down and talk—the very act he had insisted Obama must do in 2013.

Taxpayer-Funded Partisan Propaganda

The administration went even further, using taxpayer dollars and federal agency resources to spread partisan blame. The Department of Housing and Urban Development posted messages on its website blaming the “Radical Left” for the shutdown, warning that “The Radical Left in Congress shut down the government.”

The White House website itself became a propaganda outlet, posting screeds about “Democrats’ sick political games” and calling the crisis a “demented political game.” One post about air travel delays used “Democrat Shutdown” five times while describing the disruptions as “Democrat-driven chaos.”

The same administration that found creative ways to pay the military and continue ballroom construction claimed Democrats held the “off-switch”—while Trump traveled abroad and refused to negotiate. Federal websites meant to inform citizens became partisan attack platforms, all while Trump himself held the actual power to end the crisis.

Federal employees across multiple agencies—including Interior, Commerce, Labor, State, Treasury, Justice, Agriculture, and Health and Human Services—received emails instructing them to blame “Congressional Democrats” for the lapse in appropriations. The Small Business Administration even provided suggested language for employee out-of-office messages: “I am out of office for the foreseeable future because Senate Democrats voted to block a clean federal funding bill (H.R.5371) leading to a government shutdown.”

Ethics experts said these actions likely violated federal ethics laws designed to keep partisan politics out of day-to-day governing. The consumer advocacy group Public Citizen filed a complaint with the Office of Special Counsel, alleging the HUD website message violated the Hatch Act.

But the administration didn’t care—they were more interested in winning the blame game than following ethics rules or ending the crisis.

“The EXACT SAME Bill”—Except for All the Cuts We Made

Perhaps no single statement better encapsulates the bad faith of Republican arguments than Speaker Mike Johnson’s October 30 post on X: “Just 6 months ago, Chuck Schumer voted for the clean CR funding bill — the EXACT SAME bill on the floor RIGHT NOW. At the time, he said a shutdown would hurt the American people — and he was right. Then the radical left came after him.”

Johnson’s claim—that Democrats are blocking “the EXACT SAME bill” they supported six months earlier—is breathtakingly dishonest. It relies on voters not understanding what changed between May 2025 and October 2025.

What changed was Trump’s “One Big Beautiful Bill Act.”

This massive legislation, signed into law after Schumer’s May vote, delivered the most substantial cut ever to SNAP, projected to kick approximately 2.4 million people off the program. It also included cuts to Medicaid and other health spending worth about $1.1 trillion over a decade. The bill fundamentally transformed the federal budget baseline.

When Schumer supported a “clean CR” in May, he was continuing pre-Big Beautiful Bill funding levels—the status quo everyone had been working with. Now, Johnson wants him to accept a “clean CR” that continues the post-Big Beautiful Bill slashed funding levels. It’s not remotely the same thing.

Additionally, the Affordable Care Act subsidies enhanced during COVID-19 were set to expire by the end of 2025 without congressional action. These subsidies helped double ACA enrollment from 11 million in 2021 to 24 million in 2025. A KFF analysis found that without these subsidies, enrollees would face an average increase in out-of-pocket costs of 79%, with state-by-state increases ranging from 49% to 195%. Democrats were demanding these be addressed as part of any funding deal.

So when Johnson claims Democrats are blocking “the EXACT SAME bill,” he’s deliberately ignoring:

  1. The billions in cuts Republicans made between May and October

  2. The 2.4 million people Republicans kicked off SNAP

  3. The $1.1 trillion in health spending cuts Republicans enacted

  4. The looming expiration of subsidies that would cause insurance premiums to more than double for millions of Americans

This is like cutting someone’s salary in half, then six months later demanding to know why they won’t accept “the exact same paycheck” they accepted before. The number on the top of the bill might be the same, but what it represents has been fundamentally altered.

Johnson’s framing reveals the deliberate strategy: make devastating cuts, then act shocked when Democrats won’t pretend those cuts never happened. Accuse them of moving the goalposts when Republicans are the ones who moved the entire playing field.

It’s gaslighting as legislative strategy. And it depends entirely on Americans not paying attention to what happened in between.

“The Radical Left” vs. The American People

Throughout the shutdown, Trump administration officials and Republican leaders consistently characterized the crisis as Democrats’ fault. Trump himself told reporters that people receiving SNAP benefits were “largely Democrats” and they were “hurting their own people”—a baseless claim that ignored the significant number of red states with high SNAP participation rates, including Oklahoma, Louisiana, West Virginia, and Alabama.

SNAP benefits are not distributed based on political affiliation, and according to USDA data, many red states have higher-than-average SNAP participation rates.

But the American people weren’t buying the spin. Polling consistently showed that more Americans blamed Trump and Republicans for the shutdown than Democrats—by substantial margins. In one ABC News/Washington Post poll, 45% blamed Trump and the GOP. Among independents, Trump and Republicans were held responsible by a 2-to-1 margin (46% to 23%).

Democrats were more united in blaming Trump and Republicans (81%) than Republicans were in blaming Democrats (72%).

Perhaps more tellingly, concern about the shutdown grew as it dragged on, with three-quarters of Americans expressing concern by late October, up from two-thirds at the start. Even among Republicans, 61% were concerned, though far fewer said they were “very concerned” compared to Democrats and independents.

The public could see what was happening. They could see Trump traveling abroad instead of negotiating. They could see construction continuing on a luxury ballroom. They could see the administration moving billions for the military while claiming helplessness for food aid. They could see the hypocrisy, even if Republican leaders pretended not to.

The Refusal to Negotiate

Despite Trump’s 2013 insistence that presidents must “get everybody in a room” and negotiate, the 2025 Trump took the opposite approach. He traveled overseas twice during the shutdown, refused to meet with Democratic leaders, and delegated the crisis to congressional Republicans while publicly undermining any compromise efforts.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries repeatedly requested meetings with Trump to negotiate an end to the shutdown. Trump refused or ignored their requests, instead posting AI-generated mockery videos of Democratic leaders on social media.

One video showed a fake Schumer lamenting how “nobody likes Democrats anymore” while Jeffries was depicted with a sombrero and mustache. When Trump did meet with Democratic leaders in the Oval Office, he seemed more interested in photo ops—including pictures of himself pointing and smiling at Democrats with “Trump 2028” hats on his desk—than in actual negotiations.

The meeting was apparently fruitless, and there were no meaningful follow-ups.

Speaker Johnson claimed there was “no point” in sitting down with Schumer because he had “painted himself into a corner.” Yet Johnson kept the House out of session for six straight weeks, refusing to call members back to vote on any compromise measures or individual funding bills.

Both Johnson and Senate Majority Leader John Thune rejected efforts to pass targeted bills that could fund SNAP or other critical programs, insisting on an all-or-nothing approach. When asked about funding SNAP separately, Johnson said there was “no such legal avenue”—a claim federal judges would soon prove false.

Trump even called for eliminating the Senate filibuster entirely—the 60-vote threshold that required Republicans to negotiate with Democrats. Rather than sit down and work out a compromise, Trump wanted to change the rules so he wouldn’t have to negotiate at all. Johnson immediately backed this call, saying: “This is exactly what President Trump said in his TRUTH post last night — we just want this to END! Democrats don’t even deny they’re causing the pain. They admit it!”

The irony was rich. Republicans controlled all levers of government but wanted to eliminate the one rule that forced them to work across the aisle. They’d rather blow up Senate procedure than compromise. And they called Democrats unreasonable.

The pattern was clear: Republicans controlled the White House, the Senate, and the House, but they refused to negotiate, refused to compromise, and refused to even meet—all while blaming Democrats for the crisis.

The “Democrat Shutdown” That Republicans Own

The administration tried desperately to brand this as the “Democrat shutdown” or “Schumer shutdown.” But the facts told a different story:

  • Republicans controlled all three branches of government

  • The shutdown began when Senate Democrats blocked a Republican bill, but only because they needed 60 votes and Republicans only had 53

  • Trump had the authority to negotiate and compromise but chose not to

  • Trump spent critical weeks traveling abroad instead of negotiating domestically

  • There were no meaningful negotiation meetings between administration officials and Democratic leaders

  • Republicans made massive budget cuts earlier in the year and then demanded Democrats accept those cuts as the new baseline

As Senator Chris Coons noted, the administration had “utterly failed the American family every step of the way.” He added: “If Trump cared even half as much about working Americans as he does his fancy, corporate-bought ballroom, we wouldn’t be in this crisis.”

Johnson tried to blame Democratic fears of primary challenges, specifically pointing to Zohran Mamdani’s candidacy for New York City mayor and suggesting Schumer and Jeffries were afraid of being challenged by progressives. This conspiracy theory conveniently ignored that Republicans, not Democrats, were the ones refusing to negotiate or compromise.

When Your Own Words Condemn You

The resurrection of Trump’s 2013 comments wasn’t just political point-scoring—it revealed a fundamental truth about his character and his administration. When someone else is in charge, standards are absolute. When Trump is in charge, standards disappear.

In 2013, Trump tweeted: “FACT – the reason why Americans have to worry about a government shutdown is because Obama refuses to pass a budget.”

In 2025, Trump controlled the White House but blamed Schumer for the shutdown.

In 2013, Trump tweeted: “Leadership: Whatever happens, you’re responsible. If it doesn’t happen, you’re responsible.”

In 2025, Trump took zero responsibility.

In 2013, Trump agreed with a supporter who said if Trump were president, a shutdown would never happen.

In 2025, under Trump’s presidency, the government experienced its second-longest shutdown in history.

The hypocrisy isn’t accidental. It’s not even really about shutdowns. It’s about a worldview where Trump can never be wrong, can never be responsible, can never be the problem—no matter what his own past statements declared as obvious truth.

As one political observer noted in response to Trump’s old interview: “Trump said a shutdown is the President’s fault, he just wasn’t talking about himself, he blames everyone bar himself when things go wrong.”

The AI Mockery and the Missing Seriousness

While millions of Americans worried about their next meal or their next paycheck, Trump spent time creating and posting AI-generated mockery videos of his political opponents. The fake videos depicting Schumer and Jeffries weren’t just in poor taste—they revealed how unserious Trump was about resolving the crisis.

Schumer responded to one such video: “If you think your shutdown is a joke, it just proves what we all know: You can’t negotiate. You can only throw tantrums.”

Jeffries, meanwhile, posted a real photograph of Trump with Jeffrey Epstein, adding simply: “This is real.”

The contrast was stark: Democrats trying to get Trump to take the crisis seriously, while Trump treated it as content for social media engagement.

Conclusion: When Words Mean Nothing

What this essay reveals is perhaps more disturbing than the financial hypocrisy documented in the companion piece. At least with money, you can follow a trail of decisions and priorities. With words, Trump and his administration have made clear that nothing they say carries any weight or consistency.

Trump’s passionate 2013 statements about presidential responsibility weren’t principles—they were weapons to use against Obama. The moment Trump gained power, those principles evaporated.

Johnson’s claim about the “EXACT SAME bill” wasn’t a misunderstanding—it was a deliberate lie designed to confuse voters about what Republicans had done in the intervening months.

The administration’s use of federal resources to spread partisan propaganda wasn’t an oversight—it was a calculated strategy to win the blame game regardless of ethics laws.

The refusal to negotiate despite controlling all levers of government wasn’t a tactical decision—it was proof that Republicans never intended to negotiate at all.

When you examine both the priorities and the blame-shifting together, the full picture emerges: An administration that weaponized hunger for political leverage while building golden ballrooms. That found billions for some purposes but claimed poverty for others. That demanded others live up to standards Trump himself refused to meet. That lied about basic legislative facts while using taxpayer resources to spread propaganda.

This isn’t mere political hypocrisy. This is hypocrisy as a governing philosophy, where the only consistent principle is power—who has it, how to keep it, and whose suffering can be exploited to gain more of it.

Trump once said that when people talk about government shutdowns in history, “they’re going to be talking about the president of the United States, who the president was at that time. They’re not going to be talking about who was the head of the House, the head the Senate.”

For once, he was right. History will remember who was president during this shutdown. It will remember the ballroom being built while families went hungry. It will remember the deleted legal guidance and the defied court orders. It will remember the president who traveled the world while refusing to negotiate at home.

And it will remember his own words, spoken with such certainty in 2013: “Problems start from the top and they have to get solved from the top.”

Indeed they do. And in 2025, the problem started at the very top.
