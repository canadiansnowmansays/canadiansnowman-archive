How American Chaos Reveals Canadian Contradictions

Part 2: Democratic Deficits

June 20, 2025

How American electoral chaos reveals the undemocratic elements hiding in plain sight within Canadian politics

The irony is almost too perfect.

While Americans spent years debating whether their democracy was under attack, Canadians smugly pointed to our "stable" system as proof of our democratic superiority. We watched gerrymandering scandals, voter suppression efforts, and Electoral College controversies with the satisfied air of people whose elections actually work.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: Canada's democratic system contains structural flaws that would make American reformers weep. We just call them "features" instead of "bugs."

The Undemocratic Democracy

Let's start with the most obvious problem: Canada doesn't elect its government democratically. Full stop.

In 2021, the Liberals won a minority government with 32.3% of the popular vote while the Conservatives received 33.7%. In any actual democracy, this would be considered a scandal. In Canada, we call it Tuesday.

Our First Past the Post (FPTP) system regularly produces results where the party with fewer votes governs the party with more votes. The 2019 election was even more egregious—Trudeau's Liberals won 157 seats with 33.12% of the vote while Scheer's Conservatives won 121 seats with 34.4%.

If this happened in America, Canadian commentators would be writing think pieces about democratic breakdown. When it happens here, we shrug and move on.

The Appointed Senate Comedy

Then there's our Senate—a legislative body that makes the American Electoral College look like a model of democratic representation. At least Americans elect the people who choose their electors. We just... appoint senators because the Prime Minister feels like it.

Justin Trudeau promised an "independent" Senate, which in practice meant he still got to choose who sat there, but they didn't officially belong to his party. Now Carney inherits this same system—he gets to make "independent" appointments that coincidentally align with Liberal priorities. It's like claiming your hiring process is merit-based because you removed the company logo from the interview room.

The regional representation formula is equally absurd. Prince Edward Island, with 165,000 people, gets four senators. Alberta, with 4.4 million people, gets six. This isn't federalism—it's arithmetic dysfunction.

The Governor General Farce

Our head of state is appointed by an elected official to represent a foreign monarch who inherited their position from their mother. When we're feeling particularly democratic, we let a committee suggest names to the Prime Minister, who then suggests one to the King, who rubber-stamps the choice.

The Governor General has reserve powers that could theoretically dissolve Parliament or refuse royal assent to legislation. These powers are supposedly obsolete, but they exist—an unelected official with theoretical veto power over elected representatives.

When this system occasionally malfunctions—as it did during the Payette affair—we treat it as a personnel problem rather than a structural one. Imagine American reactions if their head of state were chosen by the President to represent a foreign monarch.

The Party List Problem

While Americans debate ranked choice voting and proportional representation, Canada operates under a system where party leaders have near-dictatorial control over their caucuses. MPs who step out of line get expelled from caucus, lose their staff, their committee positions, and their ability to run under the party banner.

The "free vote" is so rare in Canadian politics that we announce them like snow days. American legislators regularly break with their party leadership; Canadian MPs who do so become independents.

We've created a system where 338 MPs are effectively appointed by five or six party leaders, who are chosen through processes that most Canadians couldn't explain if their lives depended on it.

The Municipal Vacuum

Canadian cities—where most Canadians actually live—exist at the constitutional mercy of provincial governments. They have no inherent right to exist, no constitutional protections, and can be reorganized or eliminated by provincial decree.

Toronto, Canada's largest city, was forcibly amalgamated by the Harris government against the wishes of its residents. The federal government, despite having MPs representing Toronto ridings, had no say in the matter. Municipal taxation powers are so restricted that cities fund essential services through development charges and property taxes that bear no relationship to residents' ability to pay.

The Constitutional Straitjacket

The Constitution Act requires unanimous provincial consent to reform the Senate or change the monarchy—meaning Canada's democratic reforms require the approval of undemocratic institutions. It's the ultimate catch-22: we can't democratize our system because our undemocratic system won't let us.

Meanwhile, we mock American constitutional gridlock while operating under a document that makes their amendment process look flexible.

The Mirror's Reflection

American electoral chaos forces us to confront an uncomfortable question: if we're so concerned about democratic legitimacy, why do we accept a system that routinely produces illegitimate results?

When Trump claimed the 2020 election was "rigged," Americans correctly pointed out that he lost the popular vote. When Canadian parties govern with minority support, we rationalize it as "how Westminster systems work."

The difference isn't that our system is more democratic—it's that we're more comfortable with undemocratic outcomes as long as they're produced by polite processes.

The Trudeau-Trump Parallel

Here's the mirror effect in action: both Justin Trudeau and Donald Trump governed despite losing the popular vote (Trump in 2016, Trudeau in 2019 and 2021). American democrats spent four years arguing that Trump lacked legitimacy because he didn't win the most votes. Canadian progressives never made the same argument about Trudeau.

It's not that Trudeau is equivalent to Trump—it's that Canadians apply different standards to democratic legitimacy depending on whether we like the outcome.

The Carney Test

Mark Carney's recent victory presents an interesting test case. The Liberals won 169 seats with 43.7% of the popular vote, falling short of the 172 needed for a majority government despite winning more votes than any other party. While Carney can claim a legitimate popular mandate—more Canadians voted Liberal than Conservative—our system produces a minority government that may actually be more democratic than a false majority.

Minority governments force compromise and coalition-building. The previous Trudeau government's confidence and supply agreement with the NDP delivered the Canadian Dental Care Plan, pharmacare initiatives, and other progressive policies that neither party could have achieved alone. When parties must negotiate and share power, more Canadians see their votes reflected in actual policy outcomes.

The democratic deficit isn't minority government—it's the instability it creates. When MPs vote freely or coalitions collapse, governments can fall at any moment. This creates governing uncertainty that majority governments elected with minority support never face. Will Carney build a stable coalition that represents a true majority of Canadian voters? Or will he govern alone and dare other parties to topple his government, risking the very democratic collaboration that could legitimize his mandate?

The Uncomfortable Truth

The mirror reveals this: Canadians aren't inherently more democratic than Americans—we're just more accepting of undemocratic outcomes. We've convinced ourselves that process legitimacy matters more than outcome legitimacy, that politeness trumps representation.

American democracy is messy, chaotic, and frequently dysfunctional. But at least Americans are fighting about democratic principles. Canadians have mostly given up the fight, accepting undemocratic governance as the price of stability.

The question isn't whether Canadian democracy is better than American democracy. The question is whether either system deserves to be called democratic at all.
