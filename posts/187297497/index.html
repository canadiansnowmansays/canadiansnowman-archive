Security Clearances Override

Part 26: “I Don’t Want Them to Know” (2017-2021)

March 5, 2026

President Trump and senior advisor Jared Kushner. Kushner would later lead nuclear negotiations with Iran in Geneva — talks that ended when Trump ordered the bombs to fall.

In a functioning government, security clearances exist to protect national security. Intelligence agencies evaluate whether individuals can be trusted with classified information by examining their backgrounds, foreign contacts, financial vulnerabilities, and potential for compromise by hostile actors.

When security professionals determine someone poses a risk, they deny clearance. This happens to hundreds of applicants annually. The system isn’t perfect, but it exists for a crucial reason: preventing classified information from reaching those who might misuse it, sell it, or be coerced into revealing it.

Donald Trump destroyed this system for his daughter Ivanka and son-in-law Jared Kushner. When CIA and FBI officials raised serious concerns about granting them Top Secret/SCI clearances, Trump ordered his chief of staff to override the denials. When career security officials objected, Trump retaliated against the whistleblower who revealed his interference.

The aftermath vindicated the security professionals’ concerns: after leaving the White House with valuable relationships built using his security clearance, Kushner received a $2 billion investment from Saudi Arabia’s sovereign wealth fund—despite Saudi officials’ own investment committee saying Kushner’s firm was a bad investment.

This essay documents Trump’s security clearance overrides, the professionals’ warnings, the retaliation against whistleblowers, and the corrupt aftermath that proved the security concerns were justified.

“I Don’t Want Them to Know”

The Critical Meeting

President Trump and then-Chief of Staff John Kelly; Trump directed Kelly to override intelligence concerns and grant Jared Kushner clearance, seeking plausible deniability from security professionals.

In spring 2018, President Trump summoned White House Chief of Staff John Kelly and White House Counsel Don McGahn to discuss Jared Kushner’s security clearance. Both Kushner and Ivanka Trump had been operating with interim clearances for more than a year while the background investigation process continued.

Career security officials at the CIA and FBI had raised concerns. They recommended denying Kushner’s clearance based on:

  • Foreign contacts: Kushner had failed to disclose numerous contacts with foreign officials on his SF-86 security clearance application, requiring multiple amendments

  • Financial vulnerabilities: Kushner had significant personal debt, including the troubled 666 Fifth Avenue property

  • Counterintelligence concerns: Intelligence agencies questioned whether Kushner could be compromised through his foreign relationships and financial pressures

Both Kelly and McGahn advised Trump against overriding the professionals’ concerns. The security clearance process exists for a reason. If intelligence professionals believed Kushner posed a risk, Trump should defer to their expertise.

Trump didn’t care. According to Kelly’s later testimony, Trump told Kelly to grant Kushner a clearance. Kelly pushed back, explaining the process and the concerns. Trump’s response was explicit:

“I don’t want to get involved in these. I want you to do it. I don’t want them to know”—”them” being the intelligence professionals and career security officials.

Trump wanted the clearance granted, but he wanted plausible deniability. He wanted to be able to claim he hadn’t interfered while simultaneously ordering his chief of staff to override the security professionals’ recommendations.

The Override

Kelly complied. He ordered the clearance granted. Kushner received Top Secret/SCI clearance in May 2018, despite the CIA and FBI’s concerns. Ivanka received her clearance around the same time.

Trump publicly lied about his involvement. In an interview with the New York Times, Trump claimed: “I was never involved with the security clearances.” This was a direct lie. Trump had personally ordered Kelly to grant the clearances.

The override wasn’t just about one clearance. It was a systemic problem. Tricia Newbold, a career security official at the White House, later revealed that the Trump White House had overridden security concerns for at least 25 individuals who had been denied clearances by career professionals.

Jared Kushner’s Disqualifying Background

The SF-86 Omissions

Jared Kushner’s security clearance application was unprecedented in its incompleteness. The SF-86 form requires disclosure of foreign contacts. Kushner’s initial submission omitted more than 100 contacts with foreign officials.

This wasn’t a minor oversight. Kushner had to amend his SF-86 multiple times—security officials stopped counting after the third major revision. Each amendment revealed additional foreign contacts Kushner had failed to disclose initially.

The pattern suggested either extraordinary carelessness (disqualifying for someone handling classified information) or deliberate concealment (even more disqualifying).

Foreign Contacts

Kushner’s foreign relationships raised red flags:

  • Meetings with Russian officials during the transition, including Ambassador Sergey Kislyak and banker Sergey Gorkov

  • Communications with Russian officials about establishing a back-channel communication system

  • Meeting at Trump Tower in June 2016 with Russians promising “dirt” on Hillary Clinton

  • Extensive contacts with Middle Eastern officials, particularly from Saudi Arabia and UAE

  • Financial relationships with foreign investors

Each contact individually might be explainable. Collectively, they painted a picture of someone with extensive foreign entanglements that could create leverage for hostile intelligence services.

Financial Vulnerabilities

Kushner’s financial situation made him a counterintelligence risk. His company’s flagship property—666 Fifth Avenue in Manhattan—was underwater. Kushner had purchased it for $1.8 billion in 2007, taking on massive debt just before the financial crisis. By 2018, the property was worth far less than the debt it carried.

Intelligence professionals understand that financial desperation creates vulnerability. Someone deeply in debt is susceptible to foreign influence if those foreigners can offer financial relief. Kushner needed money desperately—exactly the situation that makes intelligence officials nervous.

Mohammed bin Salman’s Investment

Jared Kushner with Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman; Kushner received a $2 billion investment from the Saudi PIF shortly after leaving office, despite their committee’s objections—validating warnings about foreign ties and financial vulnerabilities.

The security professionals’ concerns proved prescient. In 2021, six months after leaving the White House, Kushner formed a private equity firm called Affinity Partners. He sought $2 billion from Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund (PIF), controlled by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman.

The Saudis’ own due diligence team reviewed Kushner’s proposal and recommended against the investment. The PIF’s investment committee found:

  • Kushner’s firm was “inexperienced”

  • The proposed fee structure was “excessive”

  • The investment thesis was weak

  • Kushner’s ties to Saudi Arabia created appearance problems

Despite this recommendation, Mohammed bin Salman personally overruled the committee and directed the $2 billion investment to Kushner.

This was obvious pay-for-play. During his White House tenure, Kushner had developed a close relationship with MBS. Kushner had defended MBS after the murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi. Kushner had shaped Middle East policy favorable to Saudi interests. And now, with Kushner out of government, MBS repaid him with $2 billion.

The security professionals had warned that Kushner’s foreign relationships and financial vulnerabilities made him a risk. Their concerns were vindicated when Kushner monetized those relationships immediately after leaving government.

The Whistleblower and Retaliation

Tricia Newbold, White House security specialist and whistleblower, testified that political appointees overrode denials for 25 individuals—leading to her suspension in retaliation.

Tricia Newbold’s Revelations

Tricia Newbold was a career White House security official with 18 years of experience. She managed the adjudication of security clearances and had served under both Republican and Democratic administrations.

In April 2019, Newbold provided testimony to the House Oversight Committee, revealing:

  • The Trump White House had overridden denials for at least 25 individuals

  • Career security professionals had recommended denying clearances based on concerns about foreign influence, conflicts of interest, questionable conduct, financial problems, and criminal conduct

  • Political appointees had overridden these recommendations

  • The overrides were unprecedented in scope and number

Newbold’s testimony was damning. She described a White House that treated security clearances as political favors rather than national security necessities. She revealed a systematic pattern of putting politics over security.

The Retaliation

The Trump administration’s response to Newbold’s whistleblowing was swift and vicious. Within weeks of her testimony:

  • Newbold was suspended without pay

  • She was placed on administrative leave

  • Her access to classified information was revoked

  • She faced potential termination

The retaliation sent a clear message to other potential whistleblowers: if you reveal Trump administration misconduct, we will destroy you.

Newbold filed a complaint, alleging the suspension was retaliation for her protected whistleblower testimony. The Office of Special Counsel later found merit in her complaint. But the damage was done—Newbold’s career was derailed for telling the truth.

The Systemic Failure

Kushner and Ivanka weren’t the only problematic clearances. Newbold revealed 25 overrides. Each represented a political appointee overruling career security professionals. Each potentially compromised national security.

We don’t know the identities of all 25. We don’t know what classified information they accessed. We don’t know if any of them were compromised by foreign intelligence services. The Trump administration ensured these questions wouldn’t be answered by retaliating against the whistleblower who could provide details.

But we know the pattern: Trump put political loyalty and family interest ahead of national security. He corrupted the security clearance process to benefit people who posed risks. And he punished those who tried to expose the corruption.

The Precedent Set

Trump’s security clearance overrides establish a dangerous precedent. Future presidents now know they can:

  • Override security professionals’ recommendations for political reasons

  • Lie about their involvement

  • Retaliate against whistleblowers who reveal the overrides

  • Face no consequences

The security clearance system depends on trusting that presidents will defer to professional security judgments. Trump destroyed that trust. He demonstrated that presidents can corrupt the system for personal benefit and escape accountability.

More fundamentally, Kushner’s $2 billion Saudi payment proved the security professionals were right to worry. They identified financial vulnerabilities and questionable foreign relationships. They warned these created risks. And immediately after Kushner left government, those concerns materialized exactly as the professionals predicted—Kushner monetized his foreign relationships for massive personal profit.

The system worked—career professionals identified the risks correctly. Trump broke the system by overriding their judgment. And the aftermath vindicated everything the professionals warned about.

From refusing to rent to Black tenants in the 1970s to calling for the death penalty for the exonerated Central Park Five to his "very fine people on both sides" response to Charlottesville, Trump has left a clear record. Like the security clearance violations, it's behavior that would disqualify anyone else. Like everything else, Trump faced no consequences.
