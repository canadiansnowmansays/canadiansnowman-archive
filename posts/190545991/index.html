Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 5: The First British Disaster: Retreat From Kabul (1839-1842)

March 18, 2026

Opening: Dr. Brydon Rides Alone

The iconic painting “The Remnants of an Army” (1879) by Elizabeth Butler (Lady Butler), depicting the exhausted assistant surgeon—wounded and alone on his pony—approaching the gates of Jalalabad on January 13, 1842, as the sole survivor to reach safety uncaptured. This image symbolizes the utter destruction of the British column.
Source: Tate Britain

On January 13, 1842, a British sentry at Jalalabad saw a rider approaching from the direction of Kabul. The man was slumped over his horse - actually a wounded pony. His uniform was torn, bloodstained, and frozen. He could barely stay in the saddle.

It was Dr. William Brydon, assistant surgeon in the British Army.

“Where is the army?” the sentry asked.

Brydon gestured weakly behind him, toward the mountain passes from which he’d emerged.

“I am the army,” he replied.

Out of 16,500 people - 700 European soldiers, 3,800 Indian sepoys, and 12,000 camp followers - who had left Kabul nine days earlier under promise of safe passage, Brydon was the only one to reach Jalalabad alive and uncaptured.

It was one of the worst military disasters in British history.

That elderly woman in Badgor holding her child, waiting for medical care that may never come - she lives in a country that destroyed a British army despite British rifles, British discipline, and British confidence. The mountains that isolated her village from doctors also turned British columns into funeral processions. Geography is destiny. The pattern never changes.

The March of Imperial Hubris (1839)

When we left off in Part 4, the British were preparing to invade Afghanistan to replace Dost Mohammad with the unpopular Shah Shuja.

The invasion force assembled:

  • 8,000 British and Indian soldiers

  • 38,000 camp followers (families, servants, merchants)

  • Nearly as many camels to carry supplies

What they brought reveals their mindset:

  • Two camels solely for transporting cigars

  • One officer required 60 camels for personal effects

  • Mess silver, cologne, exotic wines

  • The 16th Lancers brought foxhounds for hunting

This wasn’t an army preparing for mountain warfare - it was an imperial expeditionary force expecting a pleasant colonial posting.

The Wrong Route

Unable to use the natural Khyber Pass route (their Sikh ally controlled the territory), the British took an 800-mile detour through the Bolan Pass in the south.

The march was brutal:

  • 100-degree temperatures forcing night marches

  • Water sources deliberately contaminated

  • Rocky terrain killing 3,000 camels in a single five-mile pass

  • Forcing abandonment of 27,000 rounds of ammunition

Lieutenant Eyre would later remark: “If only they’d known how much they were going to need it.”

Initial Victory

Despite difficulties, the conquest seemed successful:

  • Kandahar fell easily

  • Ghazni was taken by blowing the gates and fighting through streets (182 British casualties, 3,000 Afghan)

  • Kabul fell without a fight as Dost Mohammad fled

  • Shah Shuja installed on throne

By November 1839, the British appeared to have succeeded.

The Illusion of Control (1839-1841)

For nearly two years, occupation seemed stable. The British settled into cantonments outside Kabul. Life became colonial:

  • Officers’ wives and children arrived

  • Horse racing, cricket, ice skating

  • Gardens growing vegetables

  • Amateur theatricals

  • Shopping at Kabul’s bazaars

But Lieutenant Eyre had observed something troubling: The cantonment was “a piece of swampy ground, commanded on all sides by hills or forts.” Political considerations - making Shah Shuja appear independent - had overridden military sense.

The Hidden Resentment

Beneath colonial normalcy, resentment built:

  1. Economic disruption: 16,500 foreigners caused massive inflation, devastating poor locals

  2. Cultural offense: British soldiers consorting with prostitutes outraged conservative Muslims

  3. Financial betrayal: London’s new Tory government cut subsidies to tribal leaders

  4. Puppet government: Shah Shuja remained unpopular with no legitimacy

The British confused occupation of cities with control of the country.

As Captain R.S. Trevor observed, the subsidy system - though appearing corrupt - was “a form of social glue holding the society together.” When subsidies were cut, the social compact collapsed.

The Uprising and Catastrophic Leadership (November 1841)

In November 1841, Afghans killed Alexander Burnes and attacked British positions. The crisis was made exponentially worse by catastrophic leadership.

Major-General William Elphinstone - Commander:

  • 59 years old, suffering dysentery and gout

  • Veteran of Waterloo 27 years earlier

  • Acutely aware of his own inadequacy

  • Indecisive, prone to panic

  • Contemporary officers called him “the most incompetent soldier that was to be found”

Brigadier John Shelton - Second-in-command:

  • Also a Waterloo veteran

  • Openly despised Elphinstone

  • Would fall asleep during war councils

  • When awake, gave contradictory orders

The Tactical Blunder

When Afghans attacked, Shelton drew troops into infantry squares - the formation that broke French cavalry at Waterloo.

Afghan fighters weren’t charging. They were sniping with jezails (long-barreled muskets) from mountain positions at ranges where British muskets were ineffective.

British soldiers stood in neat formations while Afghans picked them off.

The Negotiations

Rather than fighting out or fortifying properly, the British negotiated. Sir William Macnaughten - who’d planned the invasion - tried a double game: negotiating withdrawal while secretly bribing tribal leaders to support Shah Shuja.

When his double-cross was exposed, Akbar Khan (Dost Mohammad’s son) shot him dead. His body was dismembered and hung in the bazaar.

The Agreement

Akbar Khan agreed to safe passage to Jalalabad (90 miles) in exchange for:

  • Hostages (officers’ families, women, children)

  • Payment

  • Leaving behind artillery and supplies

  • Handing over Elphinstone and Shelton as “honored guests”

Whether Akbar Khan intended to honor this, or whether other Afghan leaders he didn’t control planned to attack, remains debated.

What’s certain is what happened next.

The Retreat: Nine Days of Horror (January 6-13, 1842)

Day One (January 6)

16,500 people left Kabul:

  • 700 European soldiers

  • 3,800 Indian sepoys

  • 12,000 camp followers

Conditions:

  • Deep winter, temperatures below freezing

  • Snow and ice

  • Narrow mountain passes ahead

  • 90 miles to safety

The column made only six miles the first day. Camp followers - many barefoot, in thin clothing - began dying from exposure. That night, hundreds more froze.

The Pattern Emerges

Each day:

  1. Column moves slowly through snow

  2. Afghan fighters attack from heights above defiles

  3. Jezail snipers pick off soldiers and followers

  4. Column bunches up, becoming denser target

  5. Officers try maintaining order but lack authority

  6. Night falls, more die from exposure

  7. Morning reveals hundreds of frozen corpses

Akbar Khan sent messages offering to take more hostages under his “protection.” Women, children, and wounded officers who accepted survived. Those who continued died.

The Khord-Kabul Pass (January 8)

A historical illustration of the British retreat column under attack in a snowy defile (likely evoking Khord Kabul Pass, January 8, 1842), with Afghan fighters firing down from the heights on the trapped soldiers and camp followers.

The column entered a narrow gorge with walls rising hundreds of feet. Afghan fighters lined the heights.

The British were trapped, unable to maneuver, unable to return fire upward. The slaughter was systematic. The pass became a killing ground. One soldier described the snow as “pink and crimson” from blood.

The Jagdalak Pass (January 12-13)

By January 12, only a few hundred remained. They reached Jagdalak Pass and found it blocked with holly oak - a massive barrier of thorny vegetation.

With Afghans firing from above, the British hacked and tore through the barrier with bare hands.

By the time they broke through, only about 70 European soldiers and a dozen cavalry remained alive.

Gandamak (January 13)

William Barnes Wollen’s 1898 painting “The Last Stand of the 44th Regiment at Gundamuck”, depicting the final desperate defense by the remaining British soldiers on a snowy hill near Gandamak village on January 13, 1842, surrounded and out of ammunition.

The survivors - about 20 officers and 45 infantry - reached Gandamak village. They had 20 muskets and two rounds of ammunition each.

They climbed a small hill for a defensive stand. It was hopeless.

The Afghans surrounded them and demanded surrender. The British refused. A one-sided battle followed. All the infantry died.

A dozen cavalry tried riding for Jalalabad, just 16 miles away. Six were killed immediately. The remaining six reached Futtehabad, four miles from safety, where villagers initially welcomed then attacked them. Three more killed.

Four officers reached Jalalabad’s outskirts. Three were killed in sight of the fortress.

One Man

Dr. William Brydon rode into Jalalabad on January 13, 1842.

He was the only member of the main column to reach safety without being captured.

The painter Elizabeth Butler later depicted his arrival in “Remnants of an Army” (1879) - the lone figure on horseback approaching Jalalabad. The painting became iconic, symbolizing catastrophic imperial failure.

The Savage Response (1842)

The British public was shocked. The Empire had lost an entire army to “uncivilized tribesmen.”

The Army of Retribution

In April 1842, General George Pollock led forces through the Khyber Pass (the route they should have used in 1839), defeated Afghan forces, and marched on Kabul.

In September 1842, the British:

  • Burned the great bazaar

  • Destroyed parts of the Bala Hissar fortress

  • Released hostages

  • Executed Afghans accused of participating in the uprising

  • Laid waste to villages

But they didn’t stay.

The Pointless Circle

Consider the achievement:

  1. 1839: Invaded to replace Dost Mohammad with Shah Shuja

  2. 1841: Lost nearly an entire army

  3. 1842: Returned to destroy Kabul and rescue hostages

  4. 1842: Reinstalled Dost Mohammad

  5. 1842: Left Afghanistan

Thousands dead. Enormous expense. National humiliation. And the final result: The same emir they’d deposed was back, and Britain had no influence.

The Propaganda Victory

Back in Britain, the disaster was spun as heroism. Richard Macrory describes:

“Astley’s Circus in London mounted spectacular reconstructions. Their show ‘Afghanistan War’ hyped up the invasion, the rescue of prisoners, and the Army of Retribution, while downplaying the disastrous retreat and political failures. The audiences cheered.”

The public got a story of plucky soldiers betrayed by treacherous Afghans, bravely fighting to the last, then gloriously avenged.

What they weren’t told: The invasion was unnecessary, Shah Shuja was a failed puppet, commanders were incompetent, and the entire enterprise achieved nothing.

The Lessons... Temporarily Learned

For about 35 years, the British stayed out. Dost Mohammad ruled until death in 1863. His son Sher Ali Khan succeeded him. British-Afghan relations remained wary but stable.

British officers who served on the frontier understood what happened. But institutional memory is short.

A new generation of hawks, facing Russian expansion, decided the First Afghan War was a fluke - caused by bad leadership and luck, not fundamental lessons about Afghanistan’s unconquerability.

They would invade again. Twice.

What This Disaster Teaches

1. Geography Defeats Technology British rifles were superior to Afghan jezails. But not when fired from indefensible positions in narrow passes. Mountains negate technological advantages.

2. Incompetent Leadership Matters Elphinstone’s indecision and Shelton’s insubordination turned a difficult situation into catastrophe. But even competent commanders couldn’t have held Afghanistan.

3. Occupation Requires Legitimacy Shah Shuja was seen as a British puppet with zero legitimacy. Without legitimacy, occupation requires permanent military force at unsustainable cost.

4. The Pattern Is Ancient The British knew about Alexander’s struggles, about centuries of Persian difficulties. They assumed British superiority made them different. The pattern held.

5. Afghans Always Outlast Empires The British came with confidence. The Afghans waited in their mountains. The British left in defeat. The Afghans remained.

The Bridge to the Second War

The First Anglo-Afghan War should have taught Britain to leave Afghanistan alone.

Instead, it taught them that better leadership, more troops, and different tactics might succeed. In 1878, they would try again, beginning the Second Anglo-Afghan War.

That three-month-old baby, Zakia, vomiting with no medical care - her ancestors survived British rifles in those mountain passes. They outlasted an empire that ruled a quarter of the world.

But she might not survive preventable malnutrition caused by distant policy choices.

Because while geography protects against invasion, it doesn’t protect against starvation when aid is cut.

In Part 6, we’ll see how Britain tried again - and learned the same lesson a second and third time.
