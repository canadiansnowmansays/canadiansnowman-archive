The Iran Crisis

Trump TACO Time

Part LIX: Day Thirty-two Threats, Talks, Tankers, Troops and a Ticking Clock

March 27, 2026

The Deadline That Wasn’t, The Talks That May Not Be, and the Ground War Nobody Wants But Everyone Is Preparing For

Vahid Salemi AP / NYT

I. TACO, Again

On Thursday evening, minutes after the S&P 500 closed its worst day since January and Brent crude hit $108 — its highest level of the week — President Trump posted on Truth Social that he was extending his deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz from Friday to April 6. Ten more days. He said the extension was requested by the Iranian government. Iran, which has publicly denied that any negotiations are taking place, did not immediately comment on having requested anything.

This is the third deadline. The first was Saturday night — 48 hours, obliterate power plants, biggest one first. The second was Monday — five days, productive conversations, Iran denied. The third is now April 6 — ten more days, talks going very well, Iran still denying.

The TACO sequence, for those keeping score at home: Threaten ✓ Accommodate ✓ Capitulate ✓ Obfuscate — ongoing, with diminishing returns.

Michael Crowley, writing in the Times, has documented what this pattern looks like from the outside: “Standoff With Iran Raises Fresh Doubts About Trump’s Freestyle Diplomacy.” The jumble of emissaries Trump has assembled — Witkoff, Kushner, Vance, Rubio — reflects, Crowley writes, “his improvisational approach to foreign dealings and his disdain for career diplomats and their often cumbersome protocols.” Vance is a past opponent of US intervention in Iran. Rubio is an Iran hawk who publicly defended the decision to attack. They are not a negotiating team. They are a casting call.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio at the G7 foreign ministers meeting in France, Friday. His last visit to the Middle East: October. His last foreign trip before this one: St. Kitts and Nevis. Credit: Pool photo by Brendan Smialowski/The New York Times

Former Ambassador Daniel Kurtzer rated Trump’s Iran diplomacy a failure, in part because the president seems unsure of his own goals. “Trump says he wants to de-escalate, but does he even know what that means?” The 15 demands submitted to Tehran, Kurtzer added, “are nonstarters, because they would require Iran essentially to give up on everything.”

The most damning detail in Crowley’s piece concerns the Geneva meeting just before the war began. Jake Sullivan, Biden’s former national security adviser, told Jon Stewart on The Daily Show that just days before the bombing started, the Iranians put a proposal on the table in Geneva that “went a long way towards resolving the nuclear issue.” The US negotiators, Sullivan said, “simply didn’t understand what they were being offered, and they ignored it, and decided to go ahead and strike.”

A senior Trump administration official, briefing reporters anonymously, confirmed the depth of the preparation problem without intending to. The official repeatedly misstated the abbreviation for the International Atomic Energy Agency — the organization that has monitored Iran’s nuclear program for decades — calling it the “I.E.A.E.” or “I.E.A.” He also expressed surprise that Iran had insisted on its right to enrich uranium — a position Iran has declared for decades. After reviewing the Iranian document, the official said: “I went through it. I know enough about nuclear that I was able to absorb.” His conclusion: “It all smelled fishy.” That view was relayed to Trump. The bombing started the next night.

II. The Countdown to a Ground War

The men planning the ground options. Isfahan. Kharg Island. The coastal positions. None of them easy. All of them expensive. (Doug Mills/The New York Times)

Thomas Wright, writing in The Atlantic, has named what the third deadline actually is: not a diplomatic window but a countdown to a ground war.

The timing is not coincidental. Thousands of Marines and much of the 1st Brigade of the 82nd Airborne are en route to the Middle East. The deadline expires as they arrive. “Trump may intend the talks to act as cover for an escalation decision already made,” Wright writes. “Even if he doesn’t, the structural reality is the same: When the deadline expires, he will be close to having significant ground-combat capability in the region and a collapsing diplomatic process to justify using it.”

Three ground operations are most likely, according to media reports on internal administration deliberations. First: a raid on Iran’s nuclear facilities at Isfahan to seize its stockpile of highly enriched uranium. Second: the seizure of Kharg Island, Iran’s principal oil export hub. Third: deployment of troops to Iran’s shoreline to suppress attacks on Hormuz shipping.

Each is more dangerous than it sounds.

On Isfahan: Austin Long, a senior nuclear fellow at MIT, told Wright that Iran’s highly enriched uranium is a white crystalline solid stored in thick steel cylinders that cannot be reliably destroyed with explosives. If the cylinders are pierced, they emit severely hazardous gas. A successful seizure would require US troops to secure a wide perimeter, locate and excavate up to 970 pounds of uranium buried under unknown depth of rubble, protect it from counterattack, load it onto aircraft, and depart under fire. The uranium may also be spread across Isfahan and two other sites — meaning multiple simultaneous raids of unprecedented complexity.

On Kharg Island: forces there would immediately be within range of sustained Iranian fire. Iran could respond by attacking energy infrastructure and desalination plants across the Gulf, or by destroying the island’s oil facilities to deny them to the Americans. Former Israeli Prime Minister Barak’s estimate remains the baseline: two American divisions, at least 20,000 troops, for the long haul.

Wright’s conclusion is the clearest analytical statement yet of where this war stands: “The question worth asking now is not whether the US-Iran talks will fail, but what the United States will do on the other side of that failure. Trump has a long history of claiming victory in the face of overwhelming evidence to the contrary. This may be the rare moment when that instinct serves the country — because the alternative appears to be doubling down on a losing strategy by launching a ground war.”

III. The Turning Point Nobody Wanted

Shane Harris, writing in The Atlantic, has identified the single event that may have changed Trump’s calculus more than any market indicator or congressional briefing: the Iranian missile strike on Ras Laffan.

The Ras Laffan LNG onshore facilities in Qatar before the war. Qatar spent a quarter century building this complex into the world's third-largest LNG export operation. Iran proved in one night it could derail the entire enterprise. Credit: Qatargas/Reuters.

The Ras Laffan industrial facility — approximately 50 miles from the center of Doha — is the backbone of Qatar’s economy and a critical node in global LNG supply. An Iranian missile strike in retaliation for an Israeli attack on Iran’s portion of the shared natural gas field reduced Qatar’s LNG export capacity by 17 percent overnight. The projected revenue loss: $20 billion annually. Estimated repair time: three to five years.

The numbers are worth sitting with. It took Qatar more than a quarter century — from the discovery of the offshore gas field to first shipments — to become the world’s third-largest LNG exporter. It took Iran one night to prove it could derail that entire enterprise. Qatar declared force majeure with customers in Italy, Belgium, South Korea, and China. Global LNG supply, if the facility remains offline through year-end, will effectively revert to 2021 levels, per Columbia University’s Center on Global Energy Policy.

The ripple effects extend beyond energy. Qatar is one of the world’s leading producers of urea — a key component of fertilizer — with spring planting season approaching in the Northern Hemisphere. Qatar also provides a third of the world’s helium, essential for manufacturing computer chips and medical imaging equipment. Neither is shipping from the Gulf.

Fatih Birol, head of the IEA, has now quantified the cumulative damage: “This crisis, as things stand now, is two oil crises and one gas crisis put all together.” More than 40 energy facilities across nine countries have been severely damaged. Even if the war ended this week, it could take months or years to bring supplies back to prewar levels.

Harris’s assessment of where this leaves Trump is blunt: “Despite all Iran has lost, it maintains the military capability to cause a global energy crisis. If Trump hadn’t known this before, the Ras Laffan attack offered a clear demonstration.”

IV. Republicans Know

Michelle Goldberg, writing in the Times, has documented what Republican members of Congress are saying privately that they will not yet say publicly — and what the $200 billion tells us about the war’s actual trajectory.

“On a bipartisan basis, it was pretty clear to us that there was no plan, no strategy,” said Representative Sara Jacobs, Democrat and committee member. The Pentagon briefers, she said, “could not articulate an end game, and we are three weeks into this war.” Representative Jason Crow, a combat veteran and Democratic member of the Armed Services Committee: colleagues express concerns in closed meetings “that they’re unwilling to show publicly.”

The $200 billion Pentagon funding request is Jacobs’ tell: “That’s not a down payment on wrapping things up. That’s a down payment on a long war.”

Senator Lisa Murkowski of Alaska has begun drafting a formal Authorization for the Use of Military Force — an AUMF — describing the move as “an act of desperation.” Under the 1973 War Powers Act, such an authorization must receive an expedited vote within days of being filed. Congress departs for a two-week recess Friday. The vote — if Murkowski introduces the measure — would come after the recess, close to the 60-day mark of the conflict, the deadline the War Powers Act imposes.

People are asking me, 'Is that what we're moving into?' And I can't honestly tell them the answer, because I don't know that answer. Credit: Salwan Georges/The New York Times.

Murkowski’s words are worth quoting directly: “This president came into office saying he was going to be the peace president. How many times has he said, ‘We don’t like these long wars, these never-ending wars?’ People are asking me, ‘Is that what we’re moving into?’ And I can’t honestly tell them the answer, because I don’t know that answer.”

The Pentagon will not hold a press conference this week. Hegseth and General Caine last faced reporters on March 19 — eleven days ago, as of Friday. The administration that describes briefings as “courtesy calls” is now declining courtesy entirely.

V. The Drone Revolution They Missed

Simon Shuster, writing in The Atlantic, has traveled to Rheinmetall’s factory in Germany to document the deeper military-industrial failure that the Iran war has exposed — and which Eliot Cohen’s audit last week only began to describe.

The CEO of Rheinmetall, Armin Papperger, dismissed Ukrainian drone warfare as “playing with Legos.” His company continues to receive enormous contracts for tanks and artillery that Ukrainian operators have demonstrated can be destroyed in minutes by drones costing a few hundred dollars. When Shuster asked whether Rheinmetall had developed drone-protection netting for tanks — a standard improvised adaptation on both sides of the Ukraine front — his guide looked around at the machines waiting for repair and said: “No. We don’t have something like that.”

The Iran war has made the same point at scale. Thousands of Shahed drones have pummeled the Persian Gulf, hitting hotels, airports, seaports, desalination plants, and energy infrastructure. One Iranian drone killed six American soldiers at Port Shuaiba in Kuwait. The US and its allies have depleted expensive interceptor missile stockpiles trying to shoot them down, forcing governments to rush-order cheaper Ukrainian-made drone-killers.

Ukraine now produces 4 million drones annually — up from 150,000 in 2023. Ukrainian drone operators, invited to play the red team in NATO exercises, launched 30 rapid strikes and took 17 NATO armored vehicles out of the game within hours. “Four Ukrainian drone teams,” Ukraine’s drone force commander told a conference, “would take 15 minutes to make another Pearl Harbor.”

The West’s response has been to order more tanks. Rheinmetall’s order backlog is expected to top 135 billion euros by end of 2026. Its stock has risen 15-fold since the Russian invasion of Ukraine. Papperger told investors the war in Iran means sales will grow at least 40 percent this year.

Former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, who has invested in Ukrainian drone manufacturers: “They will be the primary arms supplier to all of Europe. Their drones are so inexpensive; they are so battle-tested.” Rheinmetall’s CEO’s response to the possibility that Ukrainian companies might sell drones to NATO: they would not make it through the alliance’s bureaucracy. “They need a NATO qualification.”

The Iran war has not changed the procurement model. It has enriched the people running it.

VI. The Gulf’s Information War

Vivian Nereim, writing in the Times from Riyadh, has documented a story running parallel to the military one: Gulf governments are arresting their own residents for posting videos of Iranian attacks online.

The UAE has arrested at least 35 people. Qatar has detained more than 300. Kuwait arrested three social media influencers for a satirical video in which they cowered under stairs wearing helmets during an air raid siren — charged with “mocking the current situation.” In Bahrain, the public prosecutor is pursuing the death penalty against four people accused of filming strikes and “promoting and glorifying Iranian terrorist acts.”

The largest proportion of UAE detainees were accused not of spreading misinformation but of “publishing true videos of the attacks and their impact.” The UAE government’s statement to the Times: “Disseminating such materials or inaccurate information can incite public panic.” It did not respond to questions about why accurate videos were also banned.

TikTok and Instagram influencers in Dubai — who require government licenses — have been producing patriotic content venerating the royal family. “Dubai is still the safest place on earth,” declared one video, set to a pop song. It was posted while five-star hotels were on fire.

The head of the UAE national media authority, Abdulla Alhamed: “One image can ignite fear in a city, and one inaccurate word can unsettle an entire society.” He is correct. He is also describing the government’s own promotional videos.

VII. Khasab

On a quiet stretch of coastline in Khasab, Oman — sometimes called the Norway of Arabia for its rocky fjords, separated from Iran by just 21 miles of water at the narrowest point — two boys were skipping stones at sunset into the Strait of Hormuz.

Ismaeel Naar, writing in the Times from Khasab, has provided the essay this war needed at the one-month mark: a dispatch from the people who live closest to the chokepoint the world is fighting over and did not ask for any of this.

The docks are quiet. The tour operators’ desks sit empty. The dolphin-watching trips have stopped. A 65-year-old retired civil servant named Abdullah Alflaiti, who remembered the tanker wars of the 1980s, told Naar: “This, too, shall pass. May God protect us all.”

The Oman Foreign Minister, Badr Albusaidi, wrote in The Economist last week: “America has lost control of its own foreign policy.” He asked: “What can we do to extricate the superpower from this unwanted entanglement?”

He built the off-ramp. He is watching someone drive past it again.
