The World According to Trump

The World According to Trump

Episode 2: Cesspools and Beautiful Places — Trump’s America, Divided by Devotion

February 20, 2026

A series by CanadianSnowmanSays

In the first essay in this series, we examined how Donald Trump reduces the entire planet to a single psychological question: Are you being nice to me? Democratic allies who maintain their own positions earn contempt. Authoritarian strongmen who offer flattery earn warmth. The globe isn’t organized by strategy — it’s organized by ego.

Now we turn the lens inward, to the country Trump actually governs. And what we find is the same operating system running on domestic hardware.

In November 2024, just days before the presidential election, the New York Times published a companion piece to their international analysis. Titled “’Cesspools,’ ‘Hellholes’ and ‘Beautiful Places’: How Trump Describes the U.S.,” it mapped how the then-candidate talked about American cities and states on the campaign trail. The findings were extraordinary — not because Trump’s rhetoric was new, but because the data made the pattern undeniable.

Source: New York Times, Nov. 1, 2024. Screenshots from NYT interactive used throughout this essay for commentary and analysis.

Most presidential candidates — Republican and Democrat alike — stick to positives and platitudes when talking about the United States. They praise American cities. They celebrate American communities. They find something to love in every corner of the country they’re asking to lead. It’s so universal a practice that it’s barely noticed.

Not so for Donald J. Trump.

“We’re like a garbage can for the rest of the world to dump people that they don’t want.”

That’s how the man seeking — and ultimately winning — the presidency described the United States of America. Not an adversary. Not a competitor. His own country. A garbage can.

The Vocabulary of Contempt

NYT Interactive

Trump’s language about American cities isn’t merely negative — it’s apocalyptic. The Times catalogued the terms he used on the campaign trail, and they read less like political rhetoric than like dispatches from a war correspondent embedded in a failed state.

“The cities are rotting and they are indeed cesspools of blood.”

Let that sentence sit for a moment. This is a man asking to be President of these cities. Asking their residents for votes. And his pitch is that the places they live are rotting cesspools of blood.

NYT Interactive

The specific cities he targets tell you everything. Chicago is “worse than Afghanistan.” Washington, D.C. — the capital of the nation he leads — is a “rat-infested, graffiti-infested shithole.” Atlanta is a “killing field.”

NYT Interactive

Portland is “a burned-down hulk of a city.” San Francisco is “destroyed.”

NYT Interactive

Multiple California cities earn placement on a list of “war zones and ganglands.”

NYT Interactive

And it spreads across the map — Northeastern, Midwestern, and Southern cities alike receive the treatment. “Like a fire pit.” “War zones.” “Decimated.” “Filthy.” “Dangerous.” “Hellhole.”

Now, any observer of American politics could look at this list and immediately identify the pattern. These aren’t random cities. They are, overwhelmingly, cities with three characteristics: they vote Democratic, they have large minority populations, and they are led by Black or Democratic mayors.

Chicago. Atlanta. Washington, D.C. Detroit. San Francisco. Portland.

This is not a policy critique. Trump doesn’t cite crime statistics, compare policing models, or propose specific interventions. He doesn’t say “Chicago’s murder rate has increased 15% and here’s my plan to address it.” He says it’s “worse than Afghanistan.” The language isn’t analytical. It’s visceral, dehumanizing, and designed to evoke disgust — not problem-solving.

And for those of us watching from outside America, the racial dimension is impossible to miss. When Trump calls a majority-Black city a “killing field” and a majority-white rural state “beautiful,” he’s not making a subtle point. He’s drawing a line — and everyone knows where it falls.

Beautiful Places, Beautiful People

NYT Interactive

The contrast is where the psychology becomes unmistakable.

When Trump talks about places that support him politically, the vocabulary transforms completely. Idaho, Iowa, and Indiana are “states that you don’t even hear too much of because they’re so good and so well run.”

NYT Interactive

Alaska is “an incredible place and beautiful state.” Montana is “land of cowboys and cattle hands... one of the most beautiful places in all of God’s creation.”

NYT Interactive

And then there’s the full map of places he’s called “beautiful,” “great,” “good,” or said he loves. It blankets the country in a warm glow of generic praise — but look closely, and the geography of affection tracks almost perfectly with the electoral map. Red states are beautiful. Blue cities are hellholes.

This is the domestic version of the international nasty/nice binary, and it operates on identical logic. Abroad, allies who challenge him earn contempt while strongmen who flatter him earn praise. At home, cities that vote against him are cesspools while states that vote for him are God’s creation. The variable isn’t crime rates, economic performance, or quality of life. The variable is loyalty to Donald Trump.

Idaho’s poverty rate is higher than San Francisco’s. Montana has higher per-capita rates of domestic violence than most of those “war zone” California cities. Indiana’s life expectancy is lower than New York City’s. None of this matters. What matters is whether a place makes Trump feel appreciated.

The Decline Narrative

NYT Interactive

Perhaps most revealing is Trump’s tendency to combine praise and condemnation in a single rhetorical move — describing places as having once been great but now being in decline. The Times flagged this pattern specifically, noting it was “perhaps most unusual.”

Aurora, Colorado and Springfield, Ohio: “These were two beautiful, successful towns, idyllic. And they’re in trouble, big trouble.”

NYT Interactive

Detroit is an “once great city.” New York City is a “city in decline.”

This matters because the decline narrative is the engine of the entire MAGA project. Make America Great Again only works if America has stopped being great. Trump needs American cities to be failing. He needs the country to be a garbage can. If things are merely imperfect — fixable through normal governance, incremental improvement, and institutional competence — then there’s no need for a strongman savior.

So the apocalyptic language isn’t an accident, and it isn’t hyperbole that got away from him. It’s the essential predicate for his political offer. You can’t sell yourself as the only person who can save America if America doesn’t need saving. The cesspools, the killing fields, the hellholes — they’re not descriptions of reality. They’re advertisements for Trump.

NYT Interactive

“Our once great country, soon to be greater-than-ever-before country.”

There it is. The complete formula. America was great (past tense). America has fallen (present tense). Only Trump can make it great again (future tense). The decline isn’t a problem to be solved — it’s a story to be told, with one hero and one hero only.

The Alternative Is Apocalypse

NYT Interactive

And if the decline narrative is the predicate, the apocalyptic alternative is the threat. Without Trump, the future isn’t merely suboptimal — it’s civilizational collapse.

“Your country is being turned into a third world hellhole ruled by censors, perverts, criminals and thugs.”

Read that again. “Censors, perverts, criminals and thugs.” This is a man describing the current government of the United States of America — the government he was seeking to lead. Not a political opposition with different policy preferences. A cabal of perverts and thugs engineering national destruction.

This is the rhetoric of authoritarian movements everywhere. The nation is under existential threat. Dark forces are destroying it from within. Only one leader — one man, not a party, not a movement, not a set of institutions — can save it. Everyone who came before has failed or is complicit. The choice isn’t between competing visions of governance. The choice is between salvation and annihilation.

If this rhetoric sounds familiar to students of history, it should.

The Mirror

Now step back and hold the two maps side by side — the international map from Essay 1 and the domestic map from this essay — and the unified psychology becomes impossible to deny.

Internationally: Democratic allies who maintain independence = “nasty.” Authoritarian strongmen who offer deference = “friends.”

Domestically: Blue cities with diverse populations = “cesspools.” Red states with loyal voters = “beautiful.”

Internationally: Countries that show Trump personal respect = proof America is great again. Countries that don’t = evidence of persecution.

Domestically: Places that vote for Trump = “so good and so well run.” Places that vote against him = “rotting.”

Internationally: Conflicts are discussed in terms of the Nobel Prize Trump was denied.

Domestically: Urban problems are discussed in terms of the political opponents Trump despises.

Internationally: White Scandinavian countries produce “nice” immigrants. Black and Brown countries are “hellholes.”

Domestically: Majority-white rural states are “God’s creation.” Majority-Black cities are “killing fields.”

It’s the same binary. The same grievance engine. The same mirror held up to the world, reflecting nothing but Donald Trump back at himself. The only variable that determines whether a place — a city, a state, a country, a continent — is beautiful or a hellhole is whether its people have been sufficiently nice to him.

What a Canadian Sees

I’ll close with an observation that I can make precisely because I’m not American.

Americans are so immersed in their political polarization that the sheer abnormality of this rhetoric can lose its shock value. Blue America hears Trump attack their cities and processes it as partisanship — ugly, but familiar. Red America hears Trump praise their states and processes it as validation — welcome, and deserved.

But from Toronto, watching a sitting president describe his own capital city as a “rat-infested shithole” and his own country as a “garbage can” is not normal partisanship. It is not within the range of democratic political discourse as practiced in any other developed nation. No Canadian prime minister, no British prime minister, no German chancellor, no French president, no Australian prime minister has ever described their own cities in these terms. It doesn’t happen. Not because other democracies lack political division, but because there is a baseline understanding that a leader’s job is to lead the entire country, including the parts that didn’t vote for them.

Trump has abandoned that understanding entirely. In his telling, America doesn’t have cities that face challenges — it has enemy territories occupied by people who deserve contempt. It doesn’t have a country that could be improved — it has a garbage can that only he can rescue.

In the final essay of this series, we’ll pull these threads together and ask the question that the Times, with its commitment to journalistic neutrality, cannot quite ask: What happens to a democracy when its leader views both the world and his own nation through a lens that has room for only one subject — himself?
