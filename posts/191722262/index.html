The 3.5% Question for No Kings, March 28th

Is America Approaching Its Tipping Point?

March 22, 2026

The Actor and the Revolutionary

Robert De Niro is not a subtle man. But then, subtle isn’t what this moment calls for.

In his video recorded for the March 28th No Kings mobilization, De Niro reached back 250 years: when a mentally ill King George threatened American freedoms, he reminded viewers, the colonists didn’t sit on their hands. They took to the streets. A nation was born.

Now, he said, there is another mentally ill tyrant, another corrupt would-be king, another gutless Congress. And his closing line — “It’s time to make America sane again” — landed with a precision that no policy brief ever could.

I’ve been writing this Two Americas series since October 2025, when the first No Kings rallies drew five million people and I framed it as something historically significant — a mass democratic reflex against authoritarian drift. I said then that the question wasn’t whether the protest energy was real. The question was whether it could scale, sustain, and translate into actual political change.

Seven months later, on Saturday March 28th, we get another data point. And the numbers are striking.

The Scoreboard So Far

The No Kings movement has now run three major cycles.

June 14, 2025: Five million Americans participated across more than 2,100 events. It was Trump’s 79th birthday and the U.S. Army’s 250th anniversary military parade — a parade that drew a fraction of the crowd that showed up to oppose the man who ordered it.

October 18, 2025: Seven million people. Over 2,700 events. The movement had grown by 40 percent in four months. It had spread internationally — “No Tyrants” events appeared outside U.S. embassies in London and other capitals.

March 28, 2026: Over 3,000 events are now confirmed in all 50 states and every single U.S. congressional district — including the reddest of the red. Organizers are targeting nine million participants. Indivisible co-executive director Ezra Levin has called it “the largest protest in American history.”

He might be right. The Women’s March of January 2017 drew an estimated 3.3 to 4.6 million. The George Floyd protests of summer 2020 reached an estimated 15 to 26 million — but those were episodic, scattered across months. A single-day, nationally coordinated mobilization of nine million would be genuinely without American precedent.

“In America, we have no kings.” — nokings.org

The Magic Number: 3.5 Percent

Here is where it gets analytically interesting — and where I’ll borrow a framework from political science rather than punditry.

Harvard researcher Erica Chenoweth spent years studying civil resistance movements across the 20th century. Her finding, now widely cited in democratic theory circles, is both simple and startling: no government in modern history has successfully resisted a sustained nonviolent civil resistance movement once it reached 3.5 percent of the national population as active participants.

Not three percent. Not four. Three point five. That’s the threshold at which, historically, movements become impossible to ignore, co-opt, or suppress without crossing lines that delegitimize the government itself.

For the United States, with a population of approximately 335 million, that number is roughly 11.7 million people.

The No Kings movement is currently at seven million. Its March 28th target is nine million. The gap to 3.5 percent is approximately 2.7 million people — or about what the movement grew by between June and October 2025.

Put plainly: if the trajectory holds, the 3.5 percent threshold is not a fantasy. It is a calculable near-term possibility.

What’s Driving March 28th: It’s Not Just Trump

The October rallies were broadly anti-authoritarian — against the erosion of democratic norms, the weaponization of the Justice Department, the dismantling of federal institutions. The anger was real but the target was diffuse.

March 28th has sharper edges.

The proximate trigger is ICE. Specifically, a series of immigration enforcement operations in which federal agents shot and killed civilians — Renée Good, Keith Porter, and Alex Pretti — in circumstances that drew widespread condemnation. Operation Metro Surge in Minnesota, which targeted immigrant communities with a level of force that prompted a general strike on January 23rd, has become the symbol of what critics call state-sanctioned terror against non-white communities.

And then there is Iran. Three weeks ago, the Trump-Netanyahu joint military operation struck Iranian targets and killed Supreme Leader Khamenei. I have been tracking this crisis daily in my Iran series, and I will say this: whatever one’s view of the strategic calculus, the decision to launch what amounts to a regional war without meaningful Congressional authorization has added a second, entirely distinct stream of protest energy to March 28th. Families of servicemembers. Veterans’ groups. Anti-war constituencies that don’t always align with immigration activists.

This convergence matters. Movements that draw from a single grievance are vulnerable to resolution or fatigue. Movements that draw from multiple, reinforcing grievances are structurally more durable.

A Canadian’s Honest Assessment

I have been watching the United States from across the border for most of my adult life. I drove through the American South as a sixteen-year-old on a school band tour in 1967, and I have been puzzling over this country ever since.

The question I keep asking myself as I watch No Kings grow is not whether the anger is authentic. It is. The question is whether anger, even at this scale, translates into durable political change — or whether it dissipates into the ambient noise of a polarized society that has learned to generate spectacle without consequence.

Here is what gives me cautious optimism. This movement is not spontaneous. It is organized. Indivisible, the ACLU, the AFL-CIO, and nearly 300 partner organizations have been systematically training protest marshals, running legal rights clinics, deploying de-escalation coordinators, and building local leadership infrastructure in places that have never seen this kind of civic mobilization. The “Eyes on ICE” program — which trained over 200,000 people in a single session on how to legally monitor federal enforcement operations — is not the work of people who plan to go home and wait for the next election.

What gives me caution is history. The Tea Party of 2009–2010 was also organized, also angry, also locally rooted. It produced a midterm wave in 2010 and then was largely captured by the Republican establishment it claimed to oppose. Protest energy is renewable. Political translation of that energy is not automatic.

The difference — and I think it is a real difference — is that the Tea Party was working with the grain of the American system. These protesters are working against an administration that has explicitly signaled its intention to restructure that system. That changes the stakes, and it may change the staying power.

The Nonviolence Question

I want to address something that is being discussed seriously in organizing circles, and that I think deserves honest treatment rather than reassurance.

The No Kings movement has been explicitly, consistently, emphatically nonviolent. Organizers have trained thousands of marshals specifically to maintain that discipline. The ACLU is running know-your-rights sessions this week. The movement’s public face — De Niro’s video, Indivisible’s messaging, the nokings.org website — is scrupulously peaceful.

But reports from Los Angeles indicate that at least one previous No Kings event involved police deploying tear gas and rubber bullets against what a protest coordinator described as peaceful demonstrators. That version of events is contested. What is not contested is that the conditions exist for escalation — a federal government that has shown willingness to use force against civilian protesters, a protest movement that is growing in scale and anger, and a media ecosystem that profits from conflict.

If March 28th remains nonviolent — and I believe the organizers are working extraordinarily hard to ensure that — it will be one of the most powerful civic demonstrations in American history. If it does not, the story changes entirely, and not in a direction that serves democracy.

This is the knife’s edge that every large-scale protest movement walks. And it is worth naming clearly.

Two Americas, Revisited

When I began this series in October 2025, I framed the polarization of American society as a decades-long process — rooted in the founding, accelerated by the Great Sorting, weaponized by digital media, and now reaching something like a constitutional crisis.

What I see in No Kings is not the resolution of that crisis. It is, perhaps, the beginning of a counter-pressure sufficient to make resolution possible.

The Two Americas that I described — one defined by democratic accountability and shared civic identity, the other by authoritarian nationalism and tribal grievance — are still both fully present. March 28th will be a measurement of which one has more active citizens willing to put their bodies in the streets.

Nine million people is not 3.5 percent. But it is more than seven million. And seven million was more than five million. In a country that has been told, repeatedly, that resistance is futile, that the institutions are captured, that the courts have failed — the fact that participation keeps growing is itself a data point of considerable significance.

Robert De Niro ended his video with a simple line: “See you on the 28th.”

From Toronto, I won’t be there in person. But I will be watching very carefully. And I suspect history will be taking notes.
