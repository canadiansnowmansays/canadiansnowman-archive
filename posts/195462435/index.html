The Rise and Fall of Pam Bondi

The Binders

Part II: She presented three white binders, millions of pages, and zero accountability — then smiled when the survivors stood up

April 30, 2026

Though Pam Bondi may prove another polarizing pick, she is likely to be at least somewhat less controversial than Matt Gaetz. | Alex Brandon/AP

I. The Second Choice

Matt Gaetz withdrew on November 21, 2024. Hours later, Trump announced Bondi.

There is no polite way to frame this. The forty-fifth and forty-seventh president’s first choice for the nation’s chief law enforcement officer was a man under active investigation for statutory rape, drug use, and sex trafficking. When that man became untenable — not because of the allegations themselves, which had been public for years, but because the House Ethics Committee was about to publish its findings — Trump reached for the backup.

Pam Bondi was the backup.

She was, in many respects, an ideal one. She was a loyalist whose loyalty had been tested and proven over more than a decade. She had taken the $25,000 and delivered the silence. She had defended him during his first impeachment. She had promoted his election fraud claims on national television. She had run the legal arm of America First Policy Institute. Every credential on her résumé was a receipt for services rendered.

The Senate confirmed her 54-46 on February 4, 2025. One Democrat, John Fetterman of Pennsylvania, crossed over. The next day, Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas swore her in.

On her first day in office, she shut down the FBI’s Foreign Influence Task Force, the DOJ’s Task Force KleptoCapture, and scaled back enforcement of the Foreign Agents Registration Act — the same act under which she had herself been registered as a lobbyist for Qatar just five years earlier.

The woman who had lobbied for foreign governments was now in charge of the law that governs lobbying for foreign governments. And her first act was to weaken it.

Florida had taught her well.

II. The Weaponization Memo

The hand that holds the marker decides what the memo says — and what it doesn't.

Within days, Bondi released a series of internal memos that announced the new architecture of the Department of Justice.

The centrepiece was the Weaponization Working Group — a unit whose stated purpose was to end “abuses of the criminal justice process” by law enforcement. The memo named, by name, prosecutors who had previously brought charges against Donald Trump.

Read that again. The attorney general created a task force to investigate the investigators who had investigated her boss. The prosecutors who had secured a felony conviction against the president — a conviction that still stands, sentence deferred — were now themselves targets of the department they had served.

This was not unprecedented in the way that political journalists use the word — meaning “unusual” or “norm-breaking.” This was unprecedented in the way that structural engineers use the word — meaning the foundations have shifted and the building may not hold.

Other first-week actions included halting federal funding to sanctuary cities, ending diversity programs across the department, and dropping corruption charges against New York Mayor Eric Adams — whose city happened to be a sanctuary city and whose sudden cooperation with immigration enforcement happened to coincide with his legal troubles disappearing.

The quid pro quo was visible from Toronto. Federal prosecutors resigned in protest. Bondi said they “suddenly decided they didn’t want to support the men and women at ICE.”

She had used almost identical language about the foreclosure investigators she fired in Florida in 2011. The script was the same. Only the stage had changed.

III. The Binders

Three binders. Millions of pages. Nothing inside.

And then there were the Epstein files.

Congress had passed the Epstein Files Transparency Act with bipartisan support, driven in no small part by the dogged legislative strategy of Thomas Massie and Ro Khanna. The law required the release of the Justice Department’s investigative files related to Jeffrey Epstein — the financier, convicted sex offender, and alleged sex trafficker who had died by apparent suicide in federal custody in 2019 while awaiting trial.

On January 30, 2026, Bondi held a press conference. She brought binders. Three of them, white, splayed open on the table. Behind her, a Fox News hype machine that had spent weeks promising the files would reveal the names of the powerful men who had patronized Epstein’s operation.

Millions of pages were released.

They contained almost nothing.

No client list. No new charges. No co-conspirator revelations. The names redacted in the 2007 draft indictment remained redacted. Approximately 2.5 million additional pages were withheld, with the department citing attorney-client privilege. Victims’ faces were left unredacted in the released materials. The powerful were protected. The vulnerable were exposed.

It was, as this writer noted at the time, the longest-running exercise in strategic transparency in American legal history — in which everything is released, nothing is revealed, and the cover-up is the press release.

Bondi, for her part, told Fox News that a client list was “sitting on my desk right now to review.” In July 2025, the FBI and DOJ quietly announced that no such list existed.

She had said it was on her desk. It was not on her desk. She had said it on national television. Nobody was charged with misleading the public, because misleading the public is not a crime when the attorney general does it. It is a press conference.

IV. The Hearing

On February 11, 2026, Bondi appeared before Congress to testify about the Epstein files.

Representative Ted Lieu delivered what may have been the defining statement of the proceedings. He noted that more than a thousand sex trafficking victims remained without justice, and that the department had not held a single man accountable. “Shame on you,” he said. “If you had any decency, you would resign right after this hearing.”

She did not resign. She did not even flinch.

What she did was smile. Not a grimace. Not a diplomatic mask. Cameras captured a genuine smile during testimony about the sexual trafficking of minors.

Representative Pramila Jayapal asked the survivors in the room to stand. They stood. Jayapal asked them to raise their hands if they had been unable to meet with the Justice Department. Every hand went up.

Then Jayapal asked Bondi to turn around and face them.

Bondi refused. She would not look at them.

Attorney General Pam Bondi was grilled by lawmakers as she testified for several hours before the House Judiciary Committee February 11, 2026

“I won’t get into the gutter for her theatrics,” Bondi said of the congresswoman’s request to acknowledge the survivors of the trafficking network she was ostensibly investigating.

There it was. The pattern, fully matured. The foreclosure investigators were fired for investigating. The Trump University complaints were buried for $25,000. The execution was rescheduled for a fundraiser. And now the survivors of sex trafficking were dismissed as “theatrics” by the chief law enforcement officer of the United States, who smiled during their testimony and refused to turn her chair.

Florida had taught her to look the other way. Washington gave her a bigger chair from which to do it.

V. The Internal Memo

Investigations closed. Drawer shut. Nothing to see here.

In July 2025 — five months before the hearing, six months before the binders — something happened inside the Department of Justice that would not become public for months.

An internal memo revealed that the DOJ had quietly closed all ongoing investigations into Epstein’s co-conspirators. Every one of them. The people named alongside Epstein in the 2007 draft indictment, the individuals whose identities remained redacted in the public files, the unnamed powerful men whose accountability the Transparency Act was designed to secure — the investigations into all of them were shut down.

When Bondi told Congress in February that all materials “required to be released under the Epstein Transparency Act” had been published, she was technically correct. What she did not say — and was not asked directly enough to be forced to say — was that her department had already decided not to pursue the people the files implicated.

The binders were not a tool of transparency. They were the final act of a cover-up conducted in plain sight, with white binders and press conferences and Fox News exclusives, performed by a woman who had spent a career learning exactly how to make the absence of accountability look like the presence of justice.

VI. The Cracks

The hearing should have been survivable. The binders, despite their emptiness, had been presented with enough theatrical commitment to satisfy the base. The Fox News appearances continued. The talking points held.

But something shifted after February 11.

The survivors had been in the room. Their hands had been raised. Bondi had refused to turn around. And unlike the foreclosure investigators, unlike the Trump University complainants, unlike the man on death row whose execution was postponed for a party — the Epstein survivors had cameras pointed at them.

The image held. It spread. It became the single visual that defined Bondi’s tenure: a woman who would not turn her chair to face the people she was supposed to protect.

Congressional Republicans who had quietly tolerated the Epstein whitewash began to recalculate. Nancy Mace, who is not always a reliable compass but whose instincts on this issue proved sound, started building a coalition. Five Republicans would eventually join Democrats on the Oversight Committee to subpoena Bondi for sworn testimony.

Five Republicans. In 2026. Crossing the aisle on a subpoena for the president’s own attorney general.

The cracks were showing.

Light gets in where the marble gives way.
