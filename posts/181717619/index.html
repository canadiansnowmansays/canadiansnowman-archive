What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 3: Trump University — The $25 Million Education in Fraud (2005-2018)

December 15, 2025

“I Want to Become an Educator”

In 2005, Donald Trump stood before reporters at the launch of Trump University and made a remarkable claim. Fresh off his success hosting The Apprentice, Trump announced that his new venture was devoted to helping people gain real estate skills and knowledge. His goal, he said, was to create a “legacy as an educator” by “imparting lots of knowledge.”

It was a lie from the beginning.

Trump University wasn’t a university. It offered no accredited degrees. It conferred no college credit. It was a high-pressure sales operation designed to extract maximum money from vulnerable people seeking financial success, using Trump’s name and false promises to lure them into paying up to $35,000 for “secrets” that turned out to be generic information available for free online.

By the time the scheme collapsed under the weight of lawsuits and investigations, Trump University had operated from 2005 to 2010, taking in millions from approximately 7,000 students. Three separate lawsuits — two class actions and one from the New York Attorney General — accused Trump and his organization of fraud, false advertising, and violating consumer protection laws.

In November 2016, days after winning the presidential election, Trump agreed to pay $25 million to settle all three cases. He had vowed never to settle, called the cases “without merit,” and claimed he would win easily at trial. He settled anyway, buying his way out of what would have been a devastating fraud trial scheduled to begin just weeks after his election victory.

This is the story of a systematic fraud that bilked thousands of Americans — and how Trump evaded meaningful accountability by settling without admitting wrongdoing, just as he’d done throughout his career.

The Business Model: Bait and Switch

Trump University’s operation followed a carefully designed playbook documented in internal company materials and court filings:

Stage 1: The Free Seminar Prospective students were invited to free “investor workshops” advertised as opportunities to “learn from Donald Trump’s hand-picked instructor a systematic method for investing in real estate that anyone can use effectively.” The seminars were held in hotel conference rooms across the country.

At these free events, attendees heard testimonials about Trump’s real estate genius and were told they too could achieve similar success. Then came the pitch: for $1,495, they could attend a more comprehensive three-day seminar called the “Apprenticeship Workshop.”

Stage 2: The Three-Day Seminar The three-day workshops promised “insider success secrets” and access to Trump’s proven real estate strategies. Students paid their $1,495 and attended sessions where instructors used high-pressure tactics to upsell them to even more expensive programs.

Students were told the three-day seminar was just an introduction. The real secrets, the instructors said, required additional training. For $9,995 to $34,995, students could purchase the “Gold Elite” or “Gold Plus” packages that promised one-on-one mentorship, access to private funding sources, and Trump’s most closely-guarded strategies.

Stage 3: The Big Squeeze This is where the fraud became most egregious. Instructors were trained to push students to max out their credit cards, take out additional credit lines, and spend everything they could access.

According to court testimony and internal documents, instructors were told to identify students’ financial capacity and pressure them to spend it all. “If they can afford it, we want them to pay for the most expensive program,” internal training materials instructed. Students were told this was their “once in a lifetime opportunity” to learn from Trump himself — even though Trump had virtually no involvement with the program’s content or instruction.

Many students went deep into debt to pay for the advanced programs, believing they were investing in their financial future.

The Promises vs. The Reality

Trump’s Promise: “Learn from Donald Trump’s hand-picked instructors” The Reality: Trump had little to no involvement in selecting instructors. In deposition testimony, Trump admitted he was “relatively unengaged with what went on in Trump University.” When asked about specific instructors, he couldn’t identify them or describe their qualifications.

Trump’s Promise: “Trump’s insider success secrets” The Reality: Bob Guillo, 76, who paid $35,000 for the Gold Elite program, told NPR: “The more and more I got involved in Trump University, the more and more I found out that I had truly been scammed.” He discovered that the “secrets” were generic information easily found on the internet or in library books. “You could go to the IRS website to learn about federal tax deductions,” Guillo said. “That was a ‘secret.’”

Trump’s Promise: “Expert mentoring and one-on-one guidance” The Reality: Plaintiffs testified that the promised mentoring was “worthless or nonexistent.” Instructors were often unqualified and provided no meaningful guidance. One student described spending thousands of dollars only to receive photocopied materials and a few generic phone calls.

Trump’s Promise: “Access to private funding sources and real estate deals” The Reality: No special access materialized. Students were on their own to find financing and properties, using the same methods available to anyone.

Trump’s Promise: “Techniques that work in any market” The Reality: Some techniques taught were illegal in certain states where the seminars took place. Students who tried to implement them faced legal problems.

The Students Who Got Scammed

Sherri Simpson of Florida attended a free Trump University workshop in 2010 and was lured by the promise of learning Trump’s methods. She eventually spent approximately $19,000 on Trump University programs. In her lawsuit, she claimed the program misrepresented what students would receive and used high-pressure tactics to extract maximum money.

Tarla Makaeff was the original lead plaintiff in one of the California class actions. She paid nearly $60,000 to Trump University and alleged that the program used false advertising to defraud students. When she spoke out, Trump’s organization filed a $1 million counterclaim against her for defamation — a classic intimidation tactic. The counterclaim was dismissed, but the emotional toll on Makaeff was severe. In March 2016, she withdrew as lead plaintiff, citing “emotional abuse she had suffered from Trump and his attorneys in the press” as Trump became a presidential candidate.

Art Cohen led another class action on behalf of students who purchased access to Trump University’s “live events.” His case documented systematic deception and false advertising.

The stories were remarkably consistent: vulnerable people seeking financial improvement were targeted with false promises, subjected to high-pressure sales tactics, and left with worthless “education” and mountains of debt.

New York Strikes: The Attorney General’s Investigation

In August 2013, New York Attorney General Eric Schneiderman filed a $40 million civil suit against Trump University, alleging the business engaged in illegal practices and false advertising in violation of New York law.

The investigation uncovered damning evidence:

  • Trump University operated without required educational licenses

  • It falsely claimed to be a university when it offered no accredited programs

  • It used Trump’s name and celebrity to mislead consumers about the quality of instruction

  • Internal documents showed it was designed to extract maximum revenue, not provide genuine education

  • Instructors were incentivized based on how much money they could get students to spend

Schneiderman was blunt in his assessment. The lawsuit alleged Trump had personally orchestrated the scheme and characterized it as systematic fraud. The Attorney General’s office documented “patterns of racketeering activity” and claimed Trump operated the fraudulent organization “knowingly, willfully, and unlawfully.”

Trump’s Defense Strategy: Attack the Judge

When the California class actions moved toward trial in 2016, with Trump now the Republican presidential nominee, he deployed a defense strategy that revealed much about his character: attack the judge personally on racial grounds.

U.S. District Judge Gonzalo Curiel was assigned to hear the case. Curiel was born in Indiana to Mexican immigrant parents and had served as a federal prosecutor before being appointed to the bench by President Obama. He had a distinguished record as a fair and careful jurist.

Trump launched unprecedented attacks on Curiel, claiming the judge had “an absolute conflict” because of his “Mexican heritage” and because Trump had promised to build a wall on the U.S.-Mexico border. “I’m building a wall, okay? I’m building a wall,” Trump told CNN. “I’m trying to keep business out of Mexico... He’s Mexican. We’re building a wall between here and Mexico.”

Trump said Curiel had an “inherent conflict of interest” and claimed the judge was biased against him. When CNN’s Jake Tapper pressed Trump on whether he believed a Muslim judge would also be biased, Trump said: “It’s possible.”

Leading Republicans united in an extraordinary denunciation of Trump’s attacks. House Speaker Paul Ryan called Trump’s comments “the textbook definition of a racist comment.” Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell said Trump should “quit attacking various people you competed with or various minority groups in the country.” Senator Lindsey Graham called the attacks “un-American.”

Trump’s lawyers never filed a formal motion for Curiel to recuse himself — likely because legal experts said such a motion would be considered frivolous and potentially sanctionable. But Trump’s public attacks served another purpose: intimidating the judge and poisoning the jury pool.

The 98 Percent Approval Myth

Throughout his presidential campaign, Trump repeatedly cited a “98 percent student satisfaction rate” as proof of Trump University’s quality. The Trump campaign even launched a website — 98percentapproval.com — featuring selected testimonials from satisfied students.

It was another lie.

The 98 percent figure came from surveys completed immediately after the three-day seminars, before students realized they’d been scammed and before they discovered the “advanced” information they’d paid thousands more for was worthless. The surveys were administered by Trump University staff in a high-pressure environment where students had just been convinced to max out their credit cards for additional programs.

More tellingly, Trump refused to release the broader universe of student feedback. Thousands of students later reported dissatisfaction, filed complaints with consumer protection agencies, and joined lawsuits alleging fraud. The “98 percent approval” claim selectively used early data while ignoring the mountain of later complaints.

“I’ll Never Settle”... Until I Do

During the presidential campaign, Trump repeatedly vowed he would never settle the Trump University cases. At rallies and in interviews, he said:

  • “I don’t settle cases. I don’t do it because that’s why I don’t get sued very often, because I don’t settle.”

  • “I could have settled this case numerous times but I don’t want to settle cases when we’re right. I don’t believe in it.”

  • “We’re going to win the case, and if they want to settle, I’ll do it. But I’m not pushing to settle.”

In May 2016, Judge Curiel set the trial to begin on November 28, 2016 — three weeks after the presidential election. As the trial date approached and Trump won the election, the calculus changed dramatically.

On November 10, 2016, Judge Curiel urged the parties to work toward a settlement. Within days, Trump capitulated. On November 18, 2016, Trump agreed to pay $25 million to settle all three Trump University cases — $21 million to class action members and $4 million to the New York Attorney General’s office.

The settlement was announced with standard language: Trump “did not admit any wrongdoing.” This was crucial for Trump, allowing him to claim he’d been vindicated while buying his way out of a trial that would have been a disaster for a president-elect.

Why Trump Settled: The Trial Would Have Destroyed Him

Trump settled because going to trial would have been catastrophic. Consider what would have been presented to a jury:

  1. Trump’s Own Deposition: Trump had already given deposition testimony showing he knew virtually nothing about Trump University’s operations, couldn’t identify instructors, and had misled students about his involvement.

  2. Internal Documents: Emails and training materials showing the program was designed to maximize revenue extraction, not provide genuine education.

  3. Systematic Fraud Evidence: Testimony from thousands of students with remarkably consistent stories of false promises and high-pressure tactics.

  4. Trump’s Attacks on the Judge: The entire country had heard Trump’s racist attacks on Judge Curiel, which would have colored jury perception.

  5. Timing: The trial would begin just three weeks after Trump won the presidency. Daily headlines about “President-Elect Trump’s Fraud Trial” would have dominated the transition period.

Judge Curiel had already ruled in August 2016 that there was “sufficient evidence against Trump for the case to go to a jury.” Trump’s motion for summary judgment was denied. Trump was going to trial unless he settled, and the evidence against him was overwhelming.

The $25 million settlement allowed Trump to avoid all of this. He paid the money (though Fortune later reported that his business partner Phil Ruffin actually paid the settlement, not Trump personally) and moved on without admitting wrongdoing.

The Settlement: 90% Recovery, But No Justice

Under the settlement terms:

  • Approximately 7,000 former Trump University students who filed claims would receive 80-90% of what they paid for Trump University programs

  • The New York Attorney General received $4 million

  • Plaintiffs’ attorneys agreed to work pro bono to maximize recovery for victims

  • Trump admitted no wrongdoing

  • All three cases were dismissed with prejudice

In April 2018, Judge Curiel finalized the settlement, and payments were distributed to victims. Attorney General Schneiderman called it “a stunning reversal by President Trump, who for years refused to compensate the victims of his sham university.”

“This settlement marked a stunning reversal by President Trump,” Schneiderman said in a statement. “My office won’t hesitate to hold those who commit fraud accountable, no matter how rich or powerful they may be.”

But was Trump really held accountable? He paid money — a lot of money — but faced no criminal charges, no admission of wrongdoing, and no lasting consequences beyond the financial payout. For Trump, $25 million was the cost of making the problem go away.

The Broader Pattern of Educational Fraud

Trump University wasn’t the only Trump educational venture with problems. The Trump Institute operated separately under a licensing agreement with Trump’s name. It also faced fraud allegations and investigations.

In 2010, the office of Texas Attorney General Greg Abbott (later governor of Texas) investigated Trump University for potential violations. After Trump University closed its Texas operations and communications with investigators that included requests for customer lists and internal documents, no suit was brought.

In 2010, Florida Attorney General Pam Bondi’s office was considering whether to join New York’s lawsuit against Trump University. Bondi’s office had received complaints from Florida students who felt defrauded. But Bondi never joined the lawsuit.

In September 2013, Trump’s foundation donated $25,000 to a political committee supporting Bondi’s re-election — a contribution that violated laws against political donations from nonprofit organizations. Trump later reimbursed the foundation and paid the IRS a $2,500 excise tax as penalty.

The timing raised obvious questions: Did Trump’s donation influence Bondi’s decision not to pursue Trump University? Trump denied any connection, and Bondi said the donation had no impact on her decision. But in 2019, Bondi joined Trump’s White House staff to handle communications about his impeachment inquiry — a move that ethics watchdogs criticized.

What Should Have Happened

Trump University represented systematic fraud. Trump should have faced:

Criminal Fraud Charges: The evidence of intentional deception, false advertising, and systematic extraction of money through misrepresentation supported criminal fraud charges. Federal and state prosecutors had ample evidence but declined to pursue criminal cases.

Personal Accountability: Trump was personally involved in Trump University’s formation, marketing, and operations. He should have faced personal liability beyond financial settlement. He didn’t.

Revocation of Business Licenses: Someone who orchestrates systematic consumer fraud should not be allowed to continue operating businesses that interact with the public. No such revocation occurred.

Public Admission of Wrongdoing: The settlement’s “no admission of wrongdoing” clause allowed Trump to continue claiming vindication. Victims deserved a public acknowledgment that they had been defrauded.

Disqualification from Public Office: Operating a fraudulent scheme should disqualify someone from holding the presidency. Trump not only avoided disqualification but settled the case after being elected president.

Why No Criminal Prosecution?

The most critical question: Why weren’t criminal charges filed? The evidence supported criminal fraud prosecutions under both federal and state law. Yet no prosecutor brought criminal charges.

Possible explanations:

  1. Resource Constraints: White-collar fraud cases require enormous prosecutorial resources. Civil cases may have seemed sufficient to achieve victim compensation.

  2. Settlement Pressure: Once civil settlements were reached, victims were made whole (to 90% of their losses). Prosecutors may have felt criminal charges were less urgent.

  3. Political Calculations: By 2016, Trump was a major presidential candidate. Prosecutors may have feared filing criminal charges would appear politically motivated.

  4. Corporate Shield: Trump University was a separate corporate entity. Piercing that shield to reach Trump personally would require proving he personally orchestrated the fraud, which, while supported by evidence, added complexity.

  5. Statute of Limitations: By the time the full scope of the fraud became clear through litigation, statutes of limitations may have run on some early conduct.

But none of these explanations is satisfying. The evidence of systematic fraud was overwhelming. Trump’s personal involvement was documented. Thousands of victims were defrauded. Criminal prosecution was justified and feasible. It didn’t happen.

The Trump University Playbook

The Trump University fraud demonstrated Trump’s refined accountability-evasion tactics:

  1. Deny Everything: Call allegations “ridiculous” and “without merit”

  2. Counterattack: Sue or threaten to sue accusers (like the $1 million counterclaim against Makaeff)

  3. Attack Investigators: Demonize prosecutors and judges (like the racist attacks on Judge Curiel)

  4. Delay: Fight every motion, drag out litigation for years

  5. Selective Evidence: Promote the “98 percent approval” myth while hiding broader complaints

  6. Settle Without Admission: When forced to pay, settle without admitting wrongdoing

  7. Claim Victory: Spin the settlement as vindication rather than accountability

This playbook allowed Trump to operate a fraud scheme for five years, evade criminal prosecution, and ultimately pay his way out of accountability.

The Human Cost

For Bob Guillo and thousands like him, Trump University represented more than financial loss. “At first it was embarrassing,” Guillo told NPR. “Then I became very, very angry that the man that scammed me out of all that money had the audacity to run for president.”

Sherri Simpson fought for years for the right to sue Trump separately rather than accept the class action settlement, believing she deserved more accountability than a check. The court rejected her request.

Art Cohen and Tarla Makaeff spent years as lead plaintiffs, enduring attacks from Trump’s lawyers and watching Trump leverage their lawsuit for political gain by claiming victimhood.

All of them got money back — 80-90 cents on the dollar. None of them got justice. None of them got an apology. None of them saw Trump criminally prosecuted for the fraud he committed against them.

Looking Ahead

Part 4 will examine the Trump Foundation’s operation as a personal slush fund, making illegal political contributions and violating nonprofit laws. Once again, Trump would ultimately settle rather than face criminal prosecution, paying a fine and shutting down the foundation while admitting no wrongdoing.
