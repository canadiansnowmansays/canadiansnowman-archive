When Democracy Fights Back

The Requiem

For the Voting Rights Act, 1965–2026

May 2, 2026

President Lyndon B. Johnson signs the Voting Rights Act into law, August 6, 1965. The law that made it possible would survive for sixty-one years

The reign of Queen Elizabeth II lasted longer than the guarantor of American democracy.

Vann Newkirk, writing in the Atlantic on Friday, opened his requiem for the Voting Rights Act with that sentence. I have been sitting with it since I read it. It is the kind of observation that reorients everything — not because it is polemical, but because it is simply, devastatingly true. Color television, credit cards, and Barbie dolls arrived before the VRA and will outlast it. Of the 250 years since the American founding, fewer than sixty unfolded under the aegis of genuine universal suffrage. The law that made Black voting a practical reality rather than a theoretical right was signed in 1965. The Supreme Court completed its demolition on Wednesday.

Sixty-one years. Elizabeth II reigned for seventy.

In Parts XX and XXI of this series, I examined the legal mechanics of Louisiana v. Callais and the immediate cascade of consequences it triggered: Louisiana’s primaries suspended, Florida’s map passed, Tennessee and South Carolina in motion, the generational pipeline of Black Southern political leadership placed at structural risk. Those essays were about what the ruling did. This one is about what the ruling ended.

The Trap

The most precise description of what the Supreme Court has constructed over the past thirteen years was not written by a legal scholar or a senator. It was written by Newkirk, a journalist, and it is worth reproducing in its full logic:

Partisan gerrymandering is legal, the Court decided in 2019, even when partisanship is a solid proxy for race. Considering race in drawing districts is now impermissible, the Court decided Wednesday, unless intentional racial discrimination can be proven with a specificity that the new standard makes practically unachievable. Therefore: addressing racial disparities in redistricting may be not only difficult in practice but generally illegal. The Voting Rights Act has been transformed into a zombie law — one that exists on the books but now mostly protects white Americans from attempts to break their disproportionate control of the voting machinery it was designed to open.

This is the trap. It was not constructed accidentally. Chief Justice Roberts laid the foundation in Shelby County in 2013 by striking down the preclearance formula on the grounds that Black voter registration and turnout had improved — improvements produced largely by the preclearance requirement he was striking down. Justice Alito completed the architecture on Wednesday by citing the difficulty of proving intentional racial discrimination as evidence that discrimination is no longer the dominant force — in states where discrimination has learned, over sixty years of litigation, to speak the language of partisan advantage rather than racial animus.

The logic is circular. The remedy produced the improvement. The improvement is cited to eliminate the remedy. The elimination will reverse the improvement. When the reversal is complete, it will not be possible to restore the remedy, because the remedy required proof of intentional discrimination, and the states will have documented their intentions in partisan terms.

Roberts built the trap in 2013. Alito closed it on Wednesday.

The Zombie

There is a detail in the Callais ruling that has received insufficient attention, and it is the most clarifying detail in the entire decision.

Justice Clarence Thomas wrote a concurrence. He agreed with the majority’s outcome. He also wrote that although the ruling does not formally strike down Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act — the provision allowing voters to challenge discriminatory maps — it should still “largely put an end to this ‘disastrous misadventure’ in voting-rights jurisprudence.”

Justice Elena Kagan, dissenting, wrote that the ruling rendered Section 2 “all but a dead letter” and called it the culmination of “the majority’s now-completed demolition of the Voting Rights Act.”

Thomas and Kagan agree on what the ruling did. They disagree only on whether it was a good thing.

The majority opinion — written by Alito, joined by Roberts, Kavanaugh, Gorsuch, and Barrett — insists the ruling is limited, that Section 2 survives, that the Court has merely updated the framework. Both the Court’s most conservative justice and its most liberal justice have looked at that claim and rejected it in writing. The majority is alone in its description of what the majority did.

This is what a zombie law looks like in practice. The statute remains on the books. The enforcement mechanism has been removed. The facade stands. The building behind it does not.

What the VRA Actually Was

It is worth pausing here, because the seat-count coverage of the Callais ruling has obscured something important about what was actually lost on Wednesday.

The Voting Rights Act was not primarily a redistricting law. It was not designed to guarantee a specific number of Black congresspeople or to engineer proportional racial representation in legislative bodies. Lyndon Johnson, signing it into law in 1965, described its purpose as forcing the opponents of liberty to “open the gates to opportunity” to all Americans. Voting rights were, in his framing, a matter of the “dignity of man and the destiny of democracy.” The law was meant to be a proactive guarantor of that destiny — not a calculator of seat shares.

Black voting-rights advocates understood this from Frederick Douglass forward: no person’s rights could really be inalienable if any person’s rights were trampled. The VRA was, in Newkirk’s phrase, the true instantiation of the Declaration of Independence. For centuries, Black Americans fought for the ballot not simply to have a say in their government, but to demonstrate their own value — to themselves and to others. The ballot was dignity made concrete and countable.

That is what was lost Wednesday. Not just seats. Not just districts. The structural guarantee that the gates would remain open.

Much of what passes for conventional wisdom in American political science is a recent product made possible by the VRA. The relatively low level of political violence that Americans take for granted was simply not the case when people were lynched for attempting to register to vote. The ease with which many Americans participate in politics — filing online, voting early, choosing from a competitive field of candidates that reflects something of their community’s composition — is a recent invention, built on a legal foundation that was completed in 1965 and is now, effectively, gone.

The Congressional Black Caucus did not exist until 1971. It now has more than sixty members. Those numbers will change. Starting with Louisiana.

Two Witnesses

I am going to do something I rarely do in this series: speak in the first person about memory rather than analysis.

Newkirk ends his essay by telling us about his grandmother. She is in her early eighties. She was a grown woman before Freedom Summer came to her hometown of Greenwood, Mississippi. She was a mother by the time the Voting Rights Act was signed in 1965. Her generation was the last to live entirely on the wrong side of the law that Wednesday’s ruling has ended. His generation — Newkirk’s — was the first to grow up under the fully empowered VRA, when politics could be a reasonable, unexceptional ambition for people like them.

I am a Canadian. I have said throughout this series that I watch from the outside, that the outsider’s perspective is my credential and my limitation simultaneously. But the outside has its own memories.

I was thirteen years old in November 1963 when a president was shot in Dallas. I remember where I was. I remember what the world felt like in the weeks and months that followed — the sense that something had been broken that might not be repaired. I remember watching, from Toronto, as Lyndon Johnson stood before Congress less than two years later and said “we shall overcome” — borrowing the language of the movement, legislating the Voting Rights Act into existence, doing the thing that the assassinated president had not quite managed to do.

I was fifteen years old when the VRA passed. I did not fully understand what it meant. Sixty years later, watching its demolition from the same country, the same distance, I understand it better than I did then.

The thing I understand most clearly is this: the VRA was the moment when American democracy chose to mean what it said. The Declaration of Independence said all men are created equal. The Fifteenth Amendment said the right to vote could not be denied on account of race. For nearly a century after those promises were made, the machinery of Southern democracy was devoted to ensuring they remained theoretical. The VRA was the law that said: the theory will now be enforced.

Wednesday, the enforcement ended.

The columns still stand. The building behind them does not

The Past Tense

Newkirk’s essay closes with four words that I have not been able to stop reading: “For a while, they succeeded.”

The past tense is not rhetorical. It is precise. The success was real. It was also finite. For sixty-one years, the Voting Rights Act made American democracy closer to the thing it claimed to be. That is not nothing. It is, in fact, extraordinary — a genuine expansion of the democratic franchise, achieved through legislation, enforced through courts, defended through decades of civil rights litigation, producing a Congressional Black Caucus of sixty members, a Black president, a generation of Black Southern political leaders who built careers in the machinery of representative government.

And now Justices Thomas and Kagan have agreed, from opposite sides of the ideological spectrum, that it is over. The demolition is complete. What remains is the facade.

I have spent twenty-two parts of this series arguing that democracy fights back — that the immune system works, that the institutions hold, that the courts block the overreach, that the voters show up, that the attorneys general file the lawsuits, that the fifty-year-old from Mississippi knocks on doors and comes back for the next cycle.

I still believe all of that. I believe it about everything in this series except the one thing that Wednesday ended.

The Voting Rights Act is not coming back through litigation. The legal trap is closed. It is not coming back through executive action. The presidency is not the relevant institution. It can come back only through legislation — through a Congress that passes a new VRA designed to meet the Court’s new standards, signed by a president willing to make it a priority, surviving whatever legal challenges follow.

That Congress does not currently exist. That president is not currently in office. Whether either arrives in time to matter is the question that will define American democracy for the next decade.

Democracy fights back. It is fighting back. And it is fighting, for the first time in this series, without one of the weapons it most needed.

For a while, they succeeded. The work of making that sentence past tense again begins now.
