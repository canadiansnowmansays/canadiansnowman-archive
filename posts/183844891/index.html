Rubio's Three-Phase Plan

How Secretary of State Marco Rubio Revealed America’s Strategy to Run Venezuela Indefinitely

January 7, 2026

Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, left, addressing the news media on Wednesday in Washington. Mr. Rubio suggested there would be a far longer-term and more elaborate strategy for Venezuela.Credit...Kenny Holston/The New York Times

January 7, 2026

Wednesday afternoon, Capitol Hill: Secretary of State Marco Rubio emerged from classified congressional briefings with a message for reporters. The Trump administration wasn’t “just winging it” in Venezuela, he insisted. They had a plan. Three phases: (1) Seize Venezuela’s oil, sell it, control disbursement. (2) Ensure Western companies get “fair” market access. (3) “Transition” to democracy—no timeline given. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt clarified how the U.S. would “run” Venezuela: “Their decisions are going to be dictated by the United States of America.” Not advised. Dictated. Wednesday evening, Trump confirmed to The New York Times: U.S. oversight will last “much longer” than a year. “Only time will tell.” This is the blueprint for 21st-century colonialism.

PHASE 1: STABILIZATION (Translation: Seize the Oil, Control the Money)

Rubio’s description Wednesday:

“We are in the midst right now and in fact about to execute on a deal to take all the oil. We are going to take between 30 and 50 million barrels of oil. We’re going to sell it in the marketplace at market rates, not at the discounts Venezuela was getting. That money will then be handled in such a way that we will control how it is disbursed in a way that benefits the Venezuelan people, not corruption, not the regime.”

Parse that carefully:

“Take all the oil” - Not purchase. Take. Worth $1.8 to $3 billion.

“We will control how it is disbursed” - America decides what benefits Venezuelans. America determines what counts as “corruption.”

TRUMP’S EXPLICIT CLAIM: “CONTROLLED BY ME”

Tuesday night, Trump on social media:

Venezuela’s oil “will be sold at its Market Price, and that money will be controlled by me, as President of the United States of America, to ensure it is used to benefit the people of Venezuela and the United States!”

Not “benefit Venezuela.” Benefit both countries. Trump—personally—controls billions from Venezuelan oil sales. What legal authority? None. But he invaded, captured the president, and now claims control over the national revenue stream.

VENEZUELA CONFIRMS: “STRICTLY COMMERCIAL TRANSACTION”

Wednesday, Petróleos de Venezuela (PDVSA) issued its first statement: Negotiating “sale” of crude under “frameworks similar to those currently in effect with international companies, such as Chevron, and is based on a strictly commercial transaction.”

What’s missing: No mention of U.S. controlling disbursement. No mention of Trump managing the money. PDVSA frames this as normal commerce. Rubio describes seizure. Someone’s lying—or PDVSA is saving face while capitulating.

PHASE 2: RECOVERY (Translation: Open Markets to Western Companies)

Rubio’s description: Phase 2 focuses on “recovery” funded by “ensuring that American, Western and other companies have access to the Venezuelan market in a way that’s fair.”

Translation: Reverse Chavista nationalization. Privatize state industries. Give Exxon Mobil, ConocoPhillips, Chevron, Halliburton, and others unfettered access.

“Fair” to whom? American companies who lost assets in 2007? Or Venezuelans who might prefer state control?

The sequence is clear: America seizes oil (Phase 1), controls revenue, then uses that revenue to fund “recovery” that gives Western companies market access (Phase 2). Venezuela doesn’t get to say no. Because America controls the money.

SENATE DEMOCRATS SMELL A RAT: THE OIL COMPANY INVESTIGATION

Wednesday, five Democratic senators sent letters to eight major oil companies:

Exxon Mobil, Chevron, Shell, ConocoPhillips, BP, Citgo, Continental Resources, Halliburton, SLB, Weatherford, Baker Hughes.

The questions:

  • What advance knowledge did you have of the Venezuela operation?

  • Did you help shape foreign policy decisions?

  • What meetings occurred with Trump administration officials?

  • What are the projected costs to rebuild Venezuela’s oil industry?

  • Will American taxpayers foot the bill?

The senators: Sheldon Whitehouse (RI), Ron Wyden (OR), Elizabeth Warren (MA), Peter Welch (VT), Brian Schatz (HI), Edward Markey (MA).

Their concern: “We would like to know the extent to which U.S. oil and gas companies such as yours had either advance knowledge of or the ability to shape American foreign policy decisions—especially given that Congress was kept in the dark concerning the use of force until after the strikes occurred.”

Translation: Did oil companies help plan the invasion? Are they getting payback for campaign contributions? Is this foreign policy or corporate welfare?

Trump already admitted it Sunday: Asked if he spoke with oil companies before the operation, Trump said: “Before and after. And they want to go in.”

So yes, oil companies knew in advance. And yes, they “want to go in.”

Phase 2 is about giving them what they want: Access to Venezuelan oil, on terms favorable to Western companies, funded by money America seized in Phase 1.

The senators wrote: “American taxpayers cannot afford to take on these expenses.”

But that’s the genius of Rubio’s plan: American taxpayers don’t pay. Venezuelan oil does. America seizes the oil, controls the money, uses it to fund Western corporate access, and calls it “recovery.”

PHASE 3: TRANSITION (Translation: Democracy Eventually, Maybe, No Timeline)

Rubio’s description: Phase 3 would focus on “rebuilding civil society” and transitioning to “more representative democratic government” through a “process of reconciliation.”

Notice what’s missing: No timeline. No mention of elections. No commitment to when Venezuelans vote. Just: “Rebuilding.” “Process.” “Transition.”

Representative Jared Moskowitz (D-FL) after the briefing: “There has to be a timeline for elections, but everyone understands that it’s not going to happen immediately.” When? Officials “gave no details.”

Representative Jason Crow (D-CO), former Army Ranger: “It’s like they’ll wave a magic wand and things will turn out the way they want.” He noted: 15,000 troops deployed, dozen warships (largest flotilla since Cuban Missile Crisis), no timeline for how long, no cost estimates.

Phase 3 means: Democracy eventually. When America decides Venezuela is ready. After Western companies secure access. After “civil society” meets American specifications. This could take years. Decades. As long as it takes.

“THEIR DECISIONS ARE GOING TO BE DICTATED BY THE UNITED STATES”

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt, asked how the U.S. will “run” Venezuela:

“Their decisions are going to be dictated by the United States of America.”

Not advised. Dictated. Delcy Rodríguez is nominally president. But Venezuela’s decisions will be dictated by America.

“IT’S ABOUT OIL”

Wednesday morning: Speaker Johnson told reporters the operation was “to bring justice to a criminal.”

Representative Veronica Escobar (D-TX) walked behind him and shouted: “It’s about oil!”

Three words. One truth. Strip away “stabilization,” “recovery,” “transition”—Rubio’s plan is simple: Take the oil, control the money. Open markets to Western companies. Promise democracy eventually, rule indefinitely.

THE IRAQ PARALLEL: PROMISES VS. REALITY

Rubio’s three-phase plan should sound familiar.

March 2003: Bush administration invades Iraq. The official plan:

Phase 1: Remove Saddam Hussein (accomplished in 3 weeks).

Phase 2: Stabilize the country, restore services, prevent chaos.

Phase 3: Transition to democratic Iraqi government.

What actually happened:

Phase 1: Saddam removed. Mission Accomplished banner. Easy.

Phase 2: L. Paul Bremer opens Iraq’s economy to 100% foreign ownership. U.S. companies get contracts. Halliburton, Bechtel earn billions.

Phase 3: Elections held under occupation. American troops remain 8 years. Cost: $2+ trillion, 4,500 American dead. Iraq today: Iran-backed militias, sectarian violence, instability.

Rubio’s plan follows the same template: Remove dictator. Economic control. Western corporate access. Promise democracy eventually.

Crow sees it: “It’s like they’ll wave a magic wand.” They won’t. The goal isn’t democracy. It’s control.

THE COST: NO ANSWERS

15,000 troops. Dozen warships (largest Caribbean deployment since Cuban Missile Crisis). No timeline. No cost estimate.

Venezuela’s degraded infrastructure needs “tens of billions of dollars and significant time” to rebuild, per Energy Secretary Wright.

Who pays? American taxpayers fund invasion. Venezuela’s seized oil funds “recovery.” Western companies get access, profit. Taxpayers get nothing back.

Corporate welfare disguised as humanitarian intervention.

THE RESEARCH CENTER: COLLATERAL DAMAGE

Venezuelan Institute of Scientific Research: Two U.S. missiles hit during raid. Mathematics department “completely devastated.” Physics, chemistry, ecology, nuclear tech departments damaged.

You don’t rebuild a society by bombing its research institutes.

TRUMP CONFIRMS: “MUCH LONGER” - THE INDEFINITE OCCUPATION

Wednesday evening, hours after Rubio unveiled the three-phase plan, President Trump sat down with The New York Times in the Oval Office.

The reporters asked the critical question: How long will the United States demand direct oversight of Venezuela?

Trump’s answer: “Only time will tell.”

They pressed: Would it be three months? Six months? A year? Longer?

Trump: “I would say much longer.”

Much longer than a year. Possibly years. Possibly indefinitely.

The mask fell completely.

“WE’RE GOING TO BE TAKING OIL”

Trump, in his own words Wednesday evening:

“We will rebuild it in a very profitable way. We’re going to be using oil, and we’re going to be taking oil. We’re getting oil prices down, and we’re going to be giving money to Venezuela, which they desperately need.”

Parse that carefully:

“Taking oil.” Not purchasing. Not negotiating terms. Taking.

“Rebuild it in a very profitable way.” Profitable for whom? America and American companies.

“Giving money to Venezuela, which they desperately need.” After we take their oil, sell it, control the revenues, we’ll give them some back—at our discretion.

This is extraction dressed up as charity.

“THEY TOOK THE OIL FROM US YEARS AGO”

Asked why he chose Maduro’s loyalists over the democratic opposition, Trump sidestepped.

But he offered this justification for seizing Venezuela’s oil:

“They’re giving us everything that we feel is necessary. Don’t forget, they took the oil from us years ago.”

He’s referring to Hugo Chávez’s nationalization of oil facilities in 2007—facilities built by American companies like Exxon Mobil and ConocoPhillips.

Trump views Venezuela’s oil as rightfully American. Nationalization was theft. He’s taking it back.

This isn’t about democracy or human rights or fighting narco-trafficking.

It’s about reversing 20 years of economic nationalism. Reclaiming resources Trump believes belong to America.

“They took the oil from us.” As if Venezuela’s oil—beneath Venezuelan soil—is inherently American property.

This is 19th-century colonial logic applied in the 21st century.

“THEY’RE GIVING US EVERYTHING”

Trump on Delcy Rodríguez’s government:

“They’re giving us everything that we feel is necessary.”

Despite Rodriguez’s public statements about sovereignty and Venezuelan control, Trump says she’s cooperating fully.

He’s probably right. Because what choice does she have?

The blockade continues. The warships remain offshore. The threat of ground troops is explicit.

Rodriguez can claim “Venezuela runs Venezuela” all she wants. Trump knows: “They’re giving us everything.”

This is the reality of Rubio’s three-phase plan: Venezuela cooperates or faces economic collapse and possible military occupation.

Phase 3’s “transition to democracy” is conditional on Venezuela giving America “everything that we feel is necessary.”

And what America feels is necessary? Access to oil. Western corporate market access. Expulsion of Russian, Chinese, Cuban advisers. Indefinite U.S. oversight.

NO COMMITMENT ON ELECTIONS

The Times reported: “Mr. Trump also made no commitments about when elections would be held in Venezuela, which had a long democratic tradition from the late 1950s until Hugo Chavez took power in 1999.”

Phase 3 promises “transition to more representative democratic government.”

Trump won’t say when Venezuelans vote.

Because the timeline isn’t based on Venezuelan democracy. It’s based on American interests.

When will elections happen? When American and Western companies have secured long-term contracts. When Venezuela’s military and intelligence agencies have been sufficiently purged or co-opted. When Russia, China, and Cuba are expelled. When the oil is flowing at levels America wants.

Then—maybe—Venezuelans can vote.

For a government that must preserve all those American conditions or face renewed intervention.

GROUND TROOPS: “I CAN’T TELL YOU THAT”

The Times asked Trump: Would he put American forces on the ground in Venezuela?

Trump: “I wouldn’t want to tell you that.”

Would he send troops if Venezuela blocked access to oil?

Trump: “I can’t tell you that. I really wouldn’t want to tell you that.”

Translation: All options remain on the table, including full military occupation.

Currently: 15,000 U.S. troops deployed around Venezuela, dozen warships offshore (largest Caribbean flotilla since Cuban Missile Crisis).

Trump won’t rule out putting those troops on Venezuelan soil.

This is the implicit threat behind Rubio’s three-phase plan: Cooperate, or we occupy.

Rodriguez is “giving us everything” because she knows the alternative is American boots on the ground in Caracas.

PETRO INTIMIDATED: THE DETERRENT EFFECT

Wednesday evening, during the NYT interview, Colombia’s President Gustavo Petro called Trump.

The call lasted about an hour. Trump let the reporters stay in the Oval Office but made the conversation off-the-record.

Petro—a leftist who has clashed with Trump and whom Trump accused of enabling drug trafficking—called to “explain the situation of drugs” coming out of Colombia.

Trump invited him to Washington. The call “appeared to dissipate any immediate threat of U.S. military action” against Colombia.

The Times noted: Trump “indicated he believed that the decapitation of the Maduro regime had intimidated other leaders in the region to fall into line.”

This is the strategic purpose of Venezuela: Not just to seize its oil, but to intimidate every other Latin American leader.

Message to Petro, to Nicaragua’s Ortega, to Cuba’s Díaz-Canel, to any leftist government in the hemisphere:

Cross America, and Delta Force comes for you.

Venezuela isn’t a one-off. It’s a demonstration.

OIL EXECUTIVES HESITANT: THE WEAK POINT

Trump said Wednesday he’s already talking to American oil executives about investing in Venezuelan fields.

But the Times reported: “Many are reluctant, worried that the operation to run the country could falter when Mr. Trump leaves office, or that Venezuela’s military and intelligence services would undercut the effort because they were being cut out of the profits.”

This is the vulnerability in Trump’s plan.

Scenario 1: Trump leaves office in January 2029. New administration doesn’t want to run Venezuela indefinitely. Withdraws. Investments collapse.

Scenario 2: Venezuela’s military and intelligence—currently cooperating under duress—wait for American attention to shift elsewhere, then sabotage operations and seize assets.

Scenario 3: Political opposition in U.S. forces withdrawal before oil infrastructure is rebuilt, stranding investments.

Oil executives see the risk. This isn’t a stable investment environment. It’s an occupation that could end at any moment, leaving them with worthless assets in a hostile country.

So Trump’s Phase 2—”ensuring American and Western companies have access”—faces resistance from the companies themselves.

They want guarantees. Trump can’t give them.

Because guaranteeing long-term stability would require either:

  • Permanent U.S. military presence (politically unsustainable)

  • Genuine democratic government that willingly preserves American access (contradicts the whole plan)

Trump is offering neither. So oil executives hesitate.

And if they don’t invest, Phase 2 fails. Which means Phase 1’s oil revenues can’t fund “recovery.” Which means Phase 3’s “transition” never happens.

The whole plan depends on Western corporate investment. And Western corporations don’t trust the plan.

“THE OIL WILL TAKE A WHILE”

Trump acknowledged Wednesday: Reviving Venezuela’s neglected oil sector will take years.

“The oil will take a while,” he said.

Venezuela’s oil production collapsed from 3+ million barrels per day (pre-Chávez) to current ~1 million barrels per day.

Energy Secretary Wright said Wednesday: Getting back to historical production “takes tens of billions of dollars and significant time.”

So the timeline becomes:

Years 1-3: U.S. controls Venezuela, begins infrastructure investment

Years 4-7: Oil production gradually increases as facilities rebuilt

Years 8-10: Production reaches levels that justify the investment

Year 10+: American companies start seeing returns

That’s the optimistic scenario. Assuming no sabotage, no political opposition, no withdrawal.

Trump saying oversight will last “much longer” than a year is acknowledgment: This is a decade-plus commitment.

Does America have the attention span for a 10-year Venezuelan occupation?

Does Congress have the will to fund it?

Do American voters want their military running South American oil fields indefinitely?

Trump’s answer: “Only time will tell.”

TRUMP WANTS TO VISIT: THE IMPERIAL TOUR

Near the end of the interview, Trump said he’d like to travel to Venezuela.

“I think at some point it’ll be safe,” he said.

The image is clear: Trump landing in Caracas, inspecting “his” oil fields, meeting with the compliant government he installed, celebrating American dominance.

A victory lap. An imperial tour.

Echoes of Bush landing on the aircraft carrier with “Mission Accomplished” banner behind him.

But Bush learned: Capturing Baghdad was easy. Running Iraq was impossible.

Trump is making the same assumption: Capturing Maduro was dramatic. Running Venezuela will be profitable.

It won’t be.

Because occupations never go as planned. Because local resistance endures. Because American attention spans are short. Because Congress eventually balks at the cost.

Trump wants his victory tour. He might get it.

But “much longer”—the years of oversight he’s promising—will turn into a quagmire he can’t escape and doesn’t want to fund.

COLOMBIA: “THAT WOULD BE A VERY UNWISE QUESTION”

Wednesday, White House Press Secretary Leavitt was asked:

Should Colombia’s president Gustavo Petro “be expecting Delta Force anytime?”

Leavitt’s response:

“That would be a very unwise question for me to answer, to weigh in on.”

Not: “Of course not, Colombia is an ally.”

Not: “We have no plans to take military action against Colombia.”

Instead: Refusing to rule it out.

The threat is explicit: If you’re a leftist Latin American leader who criticizes U.S. actions, Delta Force might come for you next.

Rubio’s three-phase plan isn’t just for Venezuela. It’s a template.

The pattern:

  1. Identify leftist government in resource-rich country

  2. Frame leader as criminal/narco-terrorist

  3. Invade

  4. Phase 1: Seize resources, control money

  5. Phase 2: Open markets to Western companies

  6. Phase 3: Promise democracy eventually

Venezuela this week. Colombia next month? Nicaragua after that?

Rubio on Wednesday: “We feel like we’re moving forward here in a very positive way.”

Positive for whom? American oil companies. Western corporate interests. Trump’s image as strongman.

Not positive for: Venezuelans who lost sovereignty. Latin Americans who fear they’re next. Americans who’ll pay for endless occupations.

THE BLUEPRINT FOR 21ST-CENTURY COLONIALISM

Rubio’s three-phase plan is remarkable for its clarity. Usually, empires pretend—claim to spread freedom, hide extraction behind humanitarian language. Rubio mostly dropped the pretense:

Phase 1: “We are going to take between 30 and 50 million barrels of oil.” Not purchase. Take.

Phase 2: “Ensuring that American, Western and other companies have access to the Venezuelan market.” Not helping Venezuelans. Ensuring Western access.

Phase 3: “More representative democratic government.” Not democracy. More representative than Maduro—a low bar.

And Leavitt made it explicit: Venezuela’s decisions will be “dictated by the United States of America.”

This is neo-colonialism: Invade, seize resources, control revenues, open markets to your companies, install compliant government, call it “liberation.” The innovation: No direct colonial administration needed. Just control resource revenues (Phase 1), ensure corporate access (Phase 2), manage “transition” indefinitely (Phase 3). The local government stays. Venezuela keeps its flag, UN seat, formal sovereignty. But its decisions are dictated by America. Its revenues controlled. Its markets opened. Sovereignty without power. Independence without autonomy.

CONGRESS: COMPLICIT THROUGH SILENCE

Republicans are fine with this. Speaker Johnson: It’s “to bring justice to a criminal.” Senator Cornyn: “The U.S. has a lot of levers to persuade [Rodríguez’s] compliance.” No questions about constitutional authority, cost, timeline, or exit strategy.

Democrats object but don’t act. Senator Kaine: Republicans won’t “hold a single public hearing.” Pelosi: “Not clear what comes next.” They’re sending letters to oil companies, demanding hearings that won’t happen, expressing concern. But not cutting funding, not invoking War Powers Resolution, not stopping anything.

Both parties are letting this happen. Republicans actively support it. Democrats complain without acting. And Rubio’s three-phase plan proceeds.

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN PHASE 3 NEVER ENDS

Rubio said Wednesday: “We feel like we’re moving forward here in a very positive way.”

Forward to what? Every indication points to permanent control: No timeline for elections. No commitment to ending oil revenue seizure. No plan for troop withdrawal. Explicit statement that Venezuela’s decisions will be “dictated” by America.

Phase 3 won’t end when Venezuela becomes democratic. Phase 3 will end when America decides it’s done extracting value. That could be: Never.

CONCLUSION: THE PLAN IS CONQUEST, THE TIMELINE IS INDEFINITE

Wednesday afternoon, Marco Rubio laid out how America will control Venezuela: Seize the oil, control the money (Phase 1). Open markets to Western companies (Phase 2). Promise democracy eventually, rule in the meantime (Phase 3).

Wednesday evening, Donald Trump confirmed the timeline.

Asked how long U.S. oversight would last—three months? six months? a year?—Trump answered: “I would say much longer.”

Much longer than a year. Possibly years. “Only time will tell.”

Representative Escobar shouted the truth: “It’s about oil!”

Trump confirmed it explicitly: “We’re going to be taking oil.” Not purchasing. Not negotiating. Taking.

The White House said decisions will be “dictated by the United States.”

Trump said Rodriguez is “giving us everything that we feel is necessary.”

And Congress does nothing.

The three-phase plan proceeds, now with Trump’s admission of indefinite occupation:

Phase 1 underway: Two tankers seized Wednesday. Energy Secretary: U.S. controls sales “indefinitely.” Trump: “We’re taking oil.”

Phase 2 beginning: Oil companies meeting Trump Friday. But many hesitant—worried operation collapses when Trump leaves office or Venezuelan military sabotages once Americans stop watching.

Phase 3 indefinite: No timeline for elections. Trump: “Much longer” than a year. “Only time will tell.”

This is 21st-century colonialism. Not flags and territories. Just control of resources, dictation of decisions, open markets for our companies—for as long as it takes.

Rubio said they’re “not just winging it.”

Trump confirmed: It lasts years.

And Venezuela is just the beginning.
