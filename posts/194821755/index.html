The Kash Patel Files

The Hollow Bureau

Part V: What’s Left When the Adults Have Gone

April 24, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

"We have run off the cliff in many directions at this point, and you can't get back to the cliff." — Peter Strzok, former FBI counterintelligence official. Illustration: CanadianSnowmanSays/Grok.

Peter Strzok, the former FBI counterintelligence official, used a metaphor that deserves to be quoted in full because it captures, better than any statistic or policy analysis, what has happened to the Federal Bureau of Investigation under Kash Patel:

“It’s like climate change. There’s a delayed response. It’s like when the coyote runs off the cliff and he’s still kind of running, but he’s suspended in the air and he hasn’t fallen into the scribbling little puff of smoke. We have run off the cliff in many directions at this point, and you can’t get back to the cliff.”

The coyote has not yet looked down. The puff of smoke has not yet appeared. But the ground is no longer under its feet.

The Zero-Sum Bureau

The FBI has approximately 13,000 special agents. This sounds like a large number until you consider that they are responsible for counterterrorism, counterintelligence, cybercrime, public corruption, white-collar crime, organized crime, violent crime, civil rights enforcement, and — since the second Trump administration — immigration enforcement.

There are not enough agents to cover all of these threats simultaneously. There never have been. As Strzok explained to David Frum of The Atlantic, “the FBI does not have enough personnel and investigators to cover the threats on its plate.” The job of every FBI director has been to prioritize — to decide which threats receive the most resources, knowing that every allocation is a trade-off.

Under Patel, the trade-off has been made explicit: according to The New York Times, more than 20 percent of the FBI’s workforce has been assigned to immigration enforcement, pulling agents and analysts away from counterterrorism, counterintelligence, cybercrime, public corruption, and drug trafficking.

Twenty percent is not a minor reallocation. It is a structural transformation of the bureau’s mission. And it was driven not by threat assessment but by political demand — specifically, by Stephen Miller, Trump’s top domestic policy adviser, who the Times reported considers Patel a “compliant purveyor of his directives on personnel and policy matters.”

The field offices were given quotas. Each region had to produce a certain number of arrests every day. One field office leader, who identified himself as a conservative who believes in tightening the borders, described the execution as “completely asinine.”

What Got Lost

The stories of what was abandoned to meet the immigration quotas are specific, concrete, and devastating.

A case agent in the central United States was working an undercover operation on a neo-Nazi group with a long history of criminal activity. When he resigned from the FBI, he was told the investigation would have to be closed — there was no one else to pick it up.

In Minneapolis, agents were surveilling a network of Somali-run autism centres engaged in a federal kickback scheme — falsely diagnosing children and pocketing the government funding. The agents had to stop investigating for weeks to work immigration shifts. “I did not go to law school, go to Quantico and work counterterrorism operations overseas,” one agent said, “to be doing traffic control for arrest-a-brown-person day.”

A supervisory intelligence analyst in Los Angeles was ordered to investigate anti-ICE protesters who, according to video evidence, had done nothing wrong — they were yelling and taunting, which is protected by the First Amendment. When she pushed back, she was told to open an investigation anyway. She refused. “Then fire me today,” she said. “I’m not doing something that is fundamentally wrong.” Her squad was taken away. She was told “the seventh floor was aware of her” — meaning Patel’s office.

Philip Fields, a former counterterrorism analyst, described additional losses to The New York Times. The Foreign Influence Task Force — the unit responsible for detecting and countering foreign efforts to manipulate American democracy — was disbanded, he said. Investigations under the Foreign Agent Registration Act were pulled back. Counterintelligence tools were curtailed. “People that I worked with in the counterintelligence programme were very upset about a lot of the investigative tools being taken away,” Fields said.

From counterterrorism to clipboard. According to The New York Times, more than 20 percent of the FBI’s workforce has been reassigned to immigration enforcement. Illustration: CanadianSnowmanSays/Grok.

Kayla Staph, a former cyber special agent, noted that former Director Christopher Wray had told Congress that even if every FBI agent and analyst were shifted to Chinese cyber threats, they would be outnumbered 50 to 1. “That’s an insane statistic,” she said, “and that’s just China.” Under Patel, agents who had been working to close that gap were reassigned to photograph immigration arrests for social media.

The damage extends beyond the FBI itself. Quinta Jurecic, a staff writer at The Atlantic, reported that the U.S. Attorney’s Office for the Eastern District of Virginia — historically one of the most important offices in the country for counterterrorism and counterespionage prosecutions because of its proximity to CIA headquarters — has been hollowed out by resignations and departures. Prosecutors left rather than participate in what they viewed as politicised cases against Trump’s opponents. In Washington, agents who would normally work counterintelligence and counterterrorism were pulled from those duties to patrol city streets under the president’s directive to increase visible federal law enforcement presence. The agents scanning for Iranian sleeper cells and tracking Quds Force assets were, in some cases, literally walking a beat.

The Expertise Pipeline

The damage that is hardest to see — and hardest to repair — is not the loss of individual agents but the destruction of the expertise pipeline that produced them.

Strzok described this pipeline in his conversation with Frum with the specificity of someone who spent a career inside it. An FBI agent who reaches the senior level has typically spent 20 years in a progression of assignments: working as an investigator in the field, perhaps against the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps; returning to headquarters to supervise cases across the bureau; being embedded at the CIA or another intelligence agency; interacting with Congress; managing increasingly complex programmes. By the time they are advising the director on threat assessments, they have accumulated institutional knowledge that cannot be accelerated, downloaded, or faked.

“Well, if you force those next two levels out,” Strzok said, “you’ve essentially got two people at the top who have no idea what they’re doing relying now on the fifth level down — not to say they’re not good agents there, but they just haven’t had the time to sort of gain that experience to be able to advise at the level that they’re suddenly being asked to do.”

The analogy to climate change is precise. The carbon was released years ago. The warming happens on delay. The consequences arrive after the cause has been forgotten by everyone except the people who tried to stop it.

The senior counterintelligence experts who understood the IRGC’s operational playbook are gone — some fired, some reassigned, some driven out by the atmosphere of fear. The analysts who tracked Quds Force assets on American soil are gone. The supervisors who could sort genuine threat intelligence from noise are gone. The institutional memory of how the FBI thwarted attacks that Americans never knew about — that memory walked out the door with the people who carried it.

“I was one of the FBI’s top recruiters,” one senior executive told The New York Times. “I just got a message from someone I’d talked to about applying, saying, Wow, I got an interview. I had to say, I can no longer recommend that you go work for the FBI.”

The Inflated Numbers

Against this backdrop of institutional erosion, Patel has promoted his leadership as a historic success. In January 2026, he claimed on social media a “100% increase” in year-to-year arrests and a “210% increase” in the disruption of gangs and criminal enterprises.

The numbers are, according to multiple current and former officials, substantially misleading.

“The FBI is bilking their numbers,” a senior executive told The New York Times. “FBI agents are walking around providing support for local police and ICE. If the local police or ICE makes an arrest, the FBI is counting it in their stats.”

The examples are instructive. An FBI agent accompanies local police officers who arrest someone for shoplifting. The FBI counts it as an arrest. The Highway Patrol pulls someone over, finds a gun, discovers the driver is a felon. FBI, DEA, and ATF agents all show up and all claim the arrest. Immigration arrests — a category that the FBI never previously counted because it never previously did immigration enforcement — are now folded into the statistics.

“When you make FBI agents street cops, you get street-cop numbers,” a field office leader said. “We didn’t used to count immigration arrests, because we didn’t do immigration. Now we do. We didn’t do street patrols in D.C. in the past. Now we do. In the past, we mostly worked the complex investigations the FBI is famous for. Complicated work with wires and sophisticated techniques — all aimed at taking out the entire criminal enterprises or national security threats. Now, under Kash, we are counting stuff that has been historically left to local police departments and other agencies and saying, Wow, look at us.”

The FBI spokesman disputed this, saying the bureau “ONLY counts state arrests when we work cases jointly from partners.” But the distinction between “jointly working” a case and showing up after local police have already made an arrest is, for the agents on the ground, a matter of semantics that Patel’s leadership has deliberately blurred.

The Crime Decline He Didn’t Build

Beyond the inflated arrest statistics, there is a larger deception at the heart of Patel’s performance — one he delivered with particular bravado on Fox News on April 19, when, asked about The Atlantic‘s reporting on his drinking, he responded not with a rebuttal but with a recitation of crime numbers. “This FBI has the most prolific year in crime reduction in the United States history,” he told Maria Bartiromo. “A 20-point percent reduction in the homicide rate.”

The homicide decline is real. According to the Council on Criminal Justice, homicides in their sample of 35 large U.S. cities fell approximately 21 percent from 2024 to 2025. But here is the fact that Patel’s performance is designed to obscure: the decline is now in its fourth consecutive year. It began in 2022. It accelerated in 2023. It continued through 2024 — all under the Biden administration. As TIME reported, Trump has taken credit for the decline, but it began before his reelection, and experts say it cannot be attributed to any single policy or leader.

Crime has declined in cities where the National Guard was never deployed and ICE operations were never ramped up. As one criminologist told TIME: “We’re in the fourth year of a crime decline, and the National Guard and ICE deployments have really been something that’s only happened over the last six months.”

As for the claim that the homicide rate is the lowest “in the history of our country” — a claim Patel and Trump have both made — PolitiFact found that experts expect the 2025 FBI murder rate to be the lowest in approximately 65 years. Whether it is the lowest since 1900, as claimed, is disputed because data prior to 1960 used different methodology and is not comparable. The FBI itself did not produce equivalent data before 1930.

Patel took office in February 2025. The crime decline he claims as his achievement was in its third year before he arrived. The statistics he recites on television to avoid answering questions about his drinking are real numbers generated by a trend he inherited — and which was driven, in significant part, by the same career agents and institutional expertise he has spent fourteen months dismantling.

The Social Media Bureau

Underlying everything — the immigration diversion, the inflated statistics, the premature announcements about suspects — is a transformation in how the FBI communicates that is as fundamental as any policy change.

No previous FBI director or deputy director maintained an active social media presence. The bureau’s public communications were carefully vetted, institutionally approved, and delivered through designated spokespersons or congressional testimony. This was not an accident. It was a deliberate institutional posture — one designed to protect the integrity of investigations, the safety of agents, and the bureau’s reputation as a nonpartisan law enforcement agency.

Patel and his former deputy Bongino dismantled that posture entirely. Both posted prolifically on X. Both appeared regularly on Fox News and other media. During the Charlie Kirk investigation, according to multiple officials who were on the call, Patel and Bongino spent a critical conference call — one that should have been devoted to operational coordination — scripting their social media strategy. “Kash is like: I’m gonna tweet this. Salt Lake, you tweet that. Dan, you come in with this. Then I’ll come back with this.”

Field offices were instructed to photograph immigration arrests so the images could be posted on social media. “I had never been asked to do that during an arrest operation before,” said Kayla Staph. “It’s inappropriate and tactically unsound.”

The most revealing moment came during a conference call about the Kirk investigation, when Patel told assembled field office leaders: “When a crisis happens, the only thing you need to do is call me. The most important thing in any crisis is controlling the narrative.”

Not solving the crime. Not protecting the public. Not deploying resources. Controlling the narrative.

The War

All of this might be filed under “institutional dysfunction” — serious, damaging, but ultimately a domestic political story — if not for the war with Iran.

Jacqueline Maguire, the former FBI executive who spent 25 years at the bureau, wrote in The New York Times that in the days before the United States launched its military campaign, Patel fired members of a counterintelligence squad that was devoted, in part, to Iran. He said the agents had been let go because their work on the classified documents investigation had placed them in violation of bureau ethics rules. But multiple officials told The Atlantic‘s Fitzpatrick that they were concerned the firings had been rushed and would leave the country shorthanded at a crucial moment.

Iran is the world’s most prolific state sponsor of terrorism. Through its proxies and its direct recruitment abroad, it has killed hundreds of Americans. The IRGC’s Quds Force has planned kidnappings and assassinations on American soil. Iranian hackers have attacked American water systems, banks, and government officials’ personal communications — including Patel’s own.

And the bureau responsible for detecting and preventing Iranian operations inside the United States has had its senior counterintelligence leadership gutted, its counterterrorism analysts reassigned to immigration enforcement, its Foreign Influence Task Force disbanded, and its director periodically unreachable behind locked doors.

“That’s what keeps me up at night,” one official said.

Strzok put it with characteristic bluntness: “When it comes to a potential Iranian response — whether that’s through proxies, whether they have sleeper personnel here, whether they have visitors capable of coming into the United States, whether they have established capabilities out of the Iranian intersection or the mission to the UN — the people who know that, the people who are on the street who have that knowledge, one, at a senior level may be gone; two, at a street level, may have gotten pulled to go work immigration.”

The coyote is still running. The cliff is behind it. The ground is somewhere far below.

And the people who used to watch for the edge have been fired, reassigned, or silenced.

On a Friday afternoon in April, the coyote looked down. The director of the FBI tried to log in to his computer, couldn’t, and panicked — calling aides and allies to announce that he had been fired. He hadn’t. It was a technical error. But the panic told the bureau everything it needed to know about the man at the top, and the White House everything it needed to know about what comes next.
