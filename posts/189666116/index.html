The Director

Part 2: When a President Stops Responding to the World and Starts Staging It

March 3, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays

“Oh my God.” — Timothy Naftali, presidential historian, when asked what could go wrong in Cuba

In Part 1, we examined the confidence cycle — the pattern, documented across centuries, in which quick military victory breeds overconfidence that leads to catastrophic miscalculation. We traced how Bush’s 18-month journey from Afghan confidence to the Iraq invasion is being repeated by Trump at compressed speed: Venezuela to Iran to Cuba in a matter of weeks. We documented the absence of a legal predicate for Cuba action, the “friendly takeover” language that masks the absence of a plan, the imperial overstretch of three simultaneous theaters, and Canada’s role as the canary measuring how far American overreach has gone.

But the confidence cycle, powerful as it is as an analytical framework, explains the mechanism without fully explaining the appetite. Why three regime changes? Why simultaneously? Why the explicit competition with Reagan, Carter, and Nixon? To answer those questions, we need to examine something deeper: the psychology of a president who has stopped responding to the world and started directing it.

I. The Director

President Trump on stage, gesturing like a director setting the next scene in his self-narrated historical epic. Source: NPR / 2024 RNC archive

Trump is not, in his own mind, a president responding to threats. He is a director staging a production. The trilogy structure is not accidental. Venezuela was the opening act — fast, dramatic, a proof of concept that demonstrated the lead actor’s willingness to do what others wouldn’t. Iran is the centerpiece — big, explosive, historically resonant, the set piece that trailers are built around. Cuba is the finale — the emotional payoff, the liberation narrative, the scene where the hero completes what everyone said was impossible.

This framing explains details that pure strategic analysis cannot. It explains why Trump’s Iran address spoke of acting “for the future” — that is the language of someone who has begun narrating his own legacy in real time. It explains the explicit invocation of Reagan, Carter, and Nixon — those are not policy comparisons but billing disputes, arguments about whose name goes above the title. It explains the “friendly takeover” language — a director doesn’t describe the climactic scene in terms of legal authorities and force projections. He describes it in terms that sound bold and simple, because the audience isn’t the Joint Chiefs. The audience is history.

Salama’s reporting in The Atlantic reveals a president who has undergone a remarkable transformation from the isolationist who campaigned on ending foreign entanglements to a leader who now believes he alone possesses the vision and the will to reshape the global order. This is not hypocrisy. It is something more coherent and more dangerous: it is the belief that previous presidents failed not because the problems were intractable but because they lacked the nerve. Reagan talked tough but negotiated with the Soviets. Carter brokered peace but couldn’t rescue hostages. Nixon opened China but resigned in disgrace. In Trump’s self-conception, he is the president who combines vision with ruthlessness — the one who doesn’t blink, doesn’t negotiate from weakness, and doesn’t stop at one.

The Director psychology is what transforms the confidence cycle from dangerous to catastrophic. A president who is simply overconfident can be corrected — by advisers, by events, by institutional friction. A president who sees himself as authoring a historical epic cannot be corrected, because setbacks are rewritten as plot complications rather than evidence of flawed judgment. The Bay of Pigs becomes a story about Kennedy’s weakness, not about the foolishness of the operation. Iraq becomes a story about Obama’s premature withdrawal, not about the invasion itself. In the Director’s narrative, failure is never structural. It is always a casting error — the wrong people executing the right vision.

This is why the speed of the current cycle is so alarming. A director doesn’t pause between acts to commission studies or convene review boards. A director maintains momentum. The audience — in this case, the American public, the international community, the historical record — must not be given time to question the narrative. The next scene must begin before the reviews of the last one are in.

II. The Fog of Governance

And here lies a secondary function of the trilogy that deserves acknowledgment: the sheer velocity of regime change operations serves as its own kind of governance.

When three geopolitical crises are unfolding simultaneously, public attention fragments. Congressional oversight cannot track three theaters at once. Media coverage lurches from one front to the next. Accountability becomes structurally impossible — not because anyone is deliberately obscuring anything, but because the bandwidth of democratic institutions has been exceeded. The fog of war becomes the fog of governance, and in that fog, questions that would normally demand answers — about legal authority, about post-conflict planning, about the costs being incurred — simply never get asked with sufficient persistence to require a response.

Consider what the American public and its institutions are being asked to track right now: the ongoing stabilization (or lack thereof) in Venezuela; the military campaign in Iran and its unknowable aftermath; the escalating pressure campaign against Cuba; a trade war with multiple allies simultaneously; the domestic policy agenda; and every other demand on democratic attention that hasn’t paused because the president discovered a taste for regime change.

No democracy was designed to process this volume of consequential action at this speed. The Founders built a system of checks and balances premised on deliberation — on the assumption that major decisions would be debated, scrutinized, and contested before they were executed. That system assumes a pace of governance that allows each action to be evaluated before the next one begins.

The Director doesn’t operate at that pace. And the structural consequence is that democratic accountability ceases to function — not because it has been abolished, but because it has been outrun.

The Director doesn’t need to orchestrate distraction. The production itself is the distraction. When you are staging three regime changes simultaneously, everything else — every scandal, every investigation, every domestic crisis — recedes into background noise. Not by design, necessarily, but by the structural consequence of a presidency that operates at a pace democratic institutions were never built to match

III. The Whispers and the Calendar

Strategic calculation is not absent from these decisions. The advisers surrounding Trump have genuine policy convictions, not just ego. What makes the moment dangerous is that those convictions happen to align perfectly with the Director's psychological needs — and that alignment eliminates the friction that might otherwise slow the cycle.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio is the grandson of Cuban exiles. Ending the Castro regime is not, for him, a policy objective — it is a personal and familial imperative. He has, by multiple accounts, thought about regime change for years. He is already positioned as the architect of the Venezuela operation. The psychological and political distance between “I delivered Venezuela” and “I can deliver Cuba” is vanishingly small.

Salama reports that Rubio has been in contact through unofficial channels with Raúl Guillermo Rodriguez Castro, the grandson of former President Raúl Castro. He has focused on encouraging Cuba’s few remaining dissidents and civil society groups to rise up and lead — the same approach Trump endorsed for Iranian opposition protesters. But as Salama also notes, Rubio has not publicly specified who should lead a post-regime Cuba, nor has he offered a specific economic plan for rehabilitating a country after decades of sanctions. The vision is clear. The plan is not.

The South Florida political ecosystem amplifies this pressure. Cuban and Venezuelan exiles represent an influential and growing voting bloc. Success in Cuba, on Trump’s terms, would lock Florida for the Republican Party for a generation. The 2026 midterms are approaching. Congressional representatives from Miami and Hialeah are pressing Trump through the unique access that Mar-a-Lago proximity provides.

And then there is the legacy question. Salama’s reporting reveals a president who now sees himself in explicit competition with the great foreign policy presidents: Reagan (who defeated the Soviet Union), Carter (who brokered Camp David), Nixon (who opened China). This is a president who has moved from “America First” isolationism to a vision of himself as a world-historical figure whose trilogy of regime changes will be studied alongside the great transformations of the 20th century.

The combination of personal advisers with aligned incentives, electoral pressure with a deadline, and a president intoxicated by his own momentum is precisely the cocktail that produces catastrophic miscalculation. It is the same cocktail that produced Iraq. The only difference is the timeline.

IV. What Could Go Wrong

Mourners in Iran clutch portraits of the slain Supreme Leader—illustrating the human cost and unpredictable aftermath Naftali warns about. Source: The Japan Times / Reuters

When Salama asked Naftali what could go wrong in Cuba, his response began with two words that deserve to be the opening of every intelligence briefing on the subject: “Oh my God.”

What follows is not speculative. It is derived from the historical record of every American regime change operation that lacked adequate post-conflict planning:

The refugee crisis. Cuba is an island of 11 million people, 90 miles from Florida. A military operation or even a severe enough economic crisis could trigger a mass migration event that would dwarf any previous Cuban exodus. This would occur at precisely the moment when the administration has staked its domestic credibility on reversing immigration flows. The contradiction is absolute: you cannot simultaneously destabilize a Caribbean nation and prevent its population from fleeing to your shores.

The governance vacuum. Unlike Venezuela, which retained democratic institutions and a recognized opposition leader, Cuba has been under one-party rule for nearly seven decades. There is no organized opposition infrastructure. There is no government-in-waiting. The dissidents and civil society groups that Rubio hopes will “rise up and lead” have been systematically suppressed for generations. Telling them to take control — as Trump told Iranian protesters to do — is not a plan. It is a wish.

The quagmire. Naftali’s warning about police states deserves repetition: people with no future in a successor government have no reason to surrender. Cuba’s security apparatus is extensive, deeply embedded in civilian life, and experienced in repression. An American military operation might topple the leadership in days. The insurgency that follows could last years — fought on an island with dense urban environments, limited infrastructure, and a population whose relationship with the United States is complicated by six decades of embargo-inflicted suffering.

The precedent. Dozens of civil society organizations have already written to Congress warning that over 60 years of embargo have not achieved their stated goals. Far from promoting reform, the organizations argue, the embargo has reinforced a siege mentality and narrowed the space for exactly the kind of internal reform that might actually produce democratic change. A military operation would not solve this problem. It would calcify it.

Cuba's government says its forces returned fire on a Florida-based speedboat, killing four people. (AP PHOTO) Credit: AAP

The speedboat. There is one more detail from Salama’s reporting that deserves attention, because it illustrates how quickly events can spiral when tensions are this high. Last week, Cuban soldiers opened fire on a speedboat carrying 10 people as it approached shore, killing four and wounding six. Cuba claimed the Florida-registered vessel fired first and was attempting terrorist infiltration. Rubio said it was not a U.S. government operation. The incident was quickly overtaken by other headlines — which is precisely the problem. In the current environment, a single incident like this, slightly differently timed or slightly more provocative, could provide the pretext that the legal framework currently lacks. When you are enforcing a blockade without articulated legal authority, when your military is already forward-deployed, when the president is “on a roll” — the distance between an incident and an invasion is measured in hours, not months.

V. The Speed Is the Story

The Bush administration took eighteen months to move from Afghan confidence to the Iraq invasion. During that time, there were intelligence assessments (however flawed), congressional debates (however performative), coalition-building efforts (however inadequate), and UN deliberations (however futile). The process was deeply insufficient, but it existed.

Trump’s confidence cycle operates on a fundamentally different timeline. Venezuela was weeks ago. Iran is happening now. Cuba is already being discussed. The institutional mechanisms that might slow the cycle — congressional oversight, intelligence community pushback, allied consultation, legal review — have been systematically weakened or bypassed.

The speed itself is the escalation. When a president moves from one regime change to the next in weeks rather than months, there is no time for the lessons of the first operation to inform the second, or the second to inform the third. There is no time for unintended consequences to manifest. There is no time for the political system to process what is happening. There is only momentum — and a president who is “on a roll.”

This is the most dangerous moment in American foreign policy since the run-up to Iraq. It may be more dangerous, because the Iraq analogy at least involved a single theater of operations with years of deliberation. What we are witnessing is three simultaneous regime changes, compressed into months, driven by a president whose confidence grows with each apparent success, surrounded by advisers whose personal and political incentives align with escalation, and operating without the institutional guardrails that — however inadequately — constrained previous administrations.

The Cuban regime, one suspects, is hoping for a quagmire in Iran — another Iraq that bogs down the American military and saps the political will for a third adventure. But the Director doesn’t think in terms of quagmires. The Director thinks in terms of the next scene. And the Cuban regime’s hope may be exactly wrong: if Iran goes badly, the Director may need Cuba more, not less — a quick, clean win to change the narrative, to demonstrate that the vision is still working, to keep the audience from looking too closely at what’s happening on the other stage.

The confidence cycle is not a theory. It is a pattern, documented across centuries of great power behavior. It has a beginning (quick victory), a middle (expanding ambition), and an end (catastrophic overreach). We are currently in the middle.

The question is not whether the pattern will complete itself. The question is how many countries, and how many lives, will be consumed before it does.

As a Canadian observer, I note with particular concern that my country’s decades-long independent relationship with Cuba — one of the few policy areas where Ottawa has consistently charted its own course — may soon become another casualty of this moment. The Great Canadian Trade War has already demonstrated the cost of disagreeing with this administration. Whether Canada can maintain its Cuba policy under the pressure of simultaneous trade warfare and American regime change operations will be a test not just of Canadian sovereignty but of whether any middle power can sustain an independent foreign policy in the face of American overconfidence.
