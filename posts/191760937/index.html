The Iran Crisis

“The Nuclear Shadow”

Part LIV Day Twenty-Five: Iran Strikes Dimona, Trump Threatens Bushehr, and the War Touches the Unthinkable

March 22, 2026

Aerial satellite view of the Dimona nuclear complex in the Negev Desert, showing extensive buildings and construction — the area targeted by Iran’s confirmed strike on nuclear-related facilities. Source: AP News / satellite photo.

I. Dimona

On Saturday evening, Iranian missiles penetrated Israel’s formidable air defense network and struck the city of Dimona — eight miles from the Shimon Peres Negev Nuclear Research Center, widely believed by researchers to be connected to Israel’s nuclear weapons program, which Israel has never publicly acknowledged.

Israel’s emergency rescue service treated 115 patients. Eleven were in serious condition. A 10-year-old boy was listed in serious condition with shrapnel injuries. A woman sustained moderate injuries from glass fragments. In Arad, 25 miles northeast of Dimona, a second missile caused “extensive destruction” and “chaos” — at least 10 people seriously injured, rescue workers searching rubble for additional casualties.

“This is a very difficult evening,” Netanyahu said in a social media post.

The Israeli military said it had unsuccessfully tried to intercept the missiles and had opened an investigation into what went wrong. That sentence deserves to sit alone. After three weeks of war, after 8,000 targets struck, after Iran’s air force has been declared extinct and its navy declared sunk, Iran fired missiles that Israel’s air defense system — the most battle-tested in the world — could not stop. They struck a city eight miles from a nuclear weapons facility. The investigation into what went wrong is, in one sense, the most important sentence of Day Twenty-Five.

Iran’s state broadcaster confirmed Saturday that the strike on Dimona was deliberately intended to target Israel’s nuclear facilities. This appears to be the first time Iran has publicly confirmed targeting Israel’s nuclear weapons infrastructure. The IAEA said it had received no indication of damage to the nuclear research center and called for “maximum military restraint.” The Tasnim news agency, affiliated with Iran’s security forces, said the missiles were fired in retaliation for the strikes on Natanz on Saturday and the Bushehr nuclear power plant on Tuesday.

Nuclear for nuclear. That is the logic Iran has now stated publicly.

Israel’s military began a new wave of strikes on Tehran overnight, targeting Iranian infrastructure. The escalation cycle, which has been accelerating since the South Pars strike last week, has now reached the threshold neither side was supposed to cross: deliberate targeting of nuclear facilities on both sides, confirmed by both sides.

II. The 48-Hour Clock

On Saturday evening, as reports of the Dimona strike were coming in, Trump posted on social media threatening to “obliterate” Iran’s power plants if it did not “FULLY OPEN, WITHOUT THREAT, the Strait of Hormuz” within 48 hours. He said US strikes on Iranian plants would start “WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST.”

Iran’s biggest plant is Bushehr — its only operating nuclear power plant. Bushehr is fueled by Russian-provided uranium, monitored by the IAEA, and not considered part of Iran’s nuclear weapons program. The spent fuel is returned to Russia.

For decades, nuclear power plants have been considered completely off-limits in warfare because of the obvious risk of environmental catastrophe. The United States has led international efforts to keep Russia and Ukraine from firing near the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant since 2022. Trump is now threatening to strike a nuclear power plant himself — and to start with the largest one — as a 48-hour ultimatum.

David Sanger noted the whiplash: days earlier, Trump was calling on Israel to avoid targeting Iranian energy sites for fear of triggering an escalating cycle of counterstrikes across the Gulf. He is now threatening to strike the most environmentally sensitive energy site in Iran.

Iran responded with its own warning: if Iran’s fuel and energy infrastructure is attacked, Tehran will target all energy infrastructure belonging to US and Israeli allies in the region. The threat that has been implicit since the war began — Iran’s ability to set the entire Gulf’s energy infrastructure on fire — has now been made explicit, in direct response to Trump’s 48-hour clock.

The 48 hours began Saturday evening. They end Monday evening. The Marines are approximately one week from the Gulf. The Natanz strike has not been attributed. Bushehr is being threatened. And somewhere in Tehran, a committee that may or may not include a badly wounded supreme leader is deciding how to respond.

III. The ICBM Question

The Diego Garcia attack, initially reported as a failed strike by Iran using unknown weapons, has now acquired a specific and alarming shape.

Iran fired two missiles. The first failed mid-flight. The second was shot down by an American warship. A US official, speaking on condition of anonymity, confirmed the launch had “surprised the United States because of its range.”

Lt. Gen. Eyal Zamir, the Israeli military chief of staff, provided the most specific public assessment: Iran had fired a “two-stage intercontinental ballistic missile with a range of 4,000 kilometers” at an American target in the Indian Ocean. He said the attack underscored that Iran’s military capabilities could threaten Europe, not just Israel.

Four thousand kilometers. That is 2,500 miles — the distance to Diego Garcia. It is also the distance from Iran to London, Paris, Berlin, Warsaw.

The previous consensus, affirmed by the Defense Intelligence Agency last year, was that Iran did not have ballistic missiles capable of hitting the United States and that developing such capability could take up to a decade. Senator Tom Cotton had argued Iran could develop a functioning ICBM in six months by pairing space launch technology with medium-range missiles. CIA Director Ratcliffe said Cotton was right to be concerned.

Tom Karako of CSIS said the 2,500-mile distance was “beyond what we and they usually advertise” as the range of Iranian missiles. Nicholas Carl of the American Enterprise Institute said it “upends some of the assumptions that many have long had about the Iranian threat.” Even if Iran cannot reliably hit precise targets at that range, he noted, the question now is whether it can reach that far with cluster munition warheads — which it has fired repeatedly at Israel to maximize civilian impact.

The United States has missile-defense facilities in Romania and Poland nominally designed to address Iranian missile threats. Those facilities now look less theoretical and more immediately relevant. Europe, which has been watching this war with alarm but declining to participate, has learned Saturday that it is within range.

A senior Western military official, speaking on condition of anonymity, said the Diego Garcia attack may indicate Iran is trying to force the United States to spread out its defenses — not merely focus on protecting bases in the Middle East. The war that began as a campaign against Iran’s nuclear program has produced, as one of its outputs, public confirmation that Iran can threaten European capitals.

IV. The Trump Doctrine

David Sanger, in a New York Times news analysis published Saturday, named what Trump’s Friday social media post actually represented: the Trump Doctrine for the Middle East.

Richard Haass, former president of the Council on Foreign Relations, summarized it on social media in eleven words: “We broke it, but you own it.”

The post omitted regime change through popular uprising. It omitted the requirement that Iran’s nuclear fuel stockpile be removed from the country. It softened the nuclear goal to “never allowing Iran to get even close to Nuclear Capability” — which is, Sanger noted, essentially where the United States was after burying Iran’s nuclear program in rubble last June. It handed the Strait of Hormuz to the countries that use it. It named six allies the United States would protect — Israel, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait — without mentioning how.

The Washington Week panel on Friday night, featuring Sanger, Reuters Pentagon correspondent Idrees Ali, Atlantic staff writer Vivian Salama, and Dispatch editor Stephen Hayes, provided the sharpest collective assessment of where the war stands.

Ali: “Watch what he’s doing, not what he’s saying. He is sending 5,000 Marines. The strikes have started to ramp up. If he is looking to wind down, he’s not doing a very good job.”

Salama: “He does not know what comes next. He can say we’re winding down because he has never quite defined what the endgame looks like. He realizes they’ve taken on a lot more than they can chew.”

Sanger: “Think about the previous two American attacks. The Air Force took out three major nuclear sites, then went home. Maduro — pulled him out of his bed in Caracas. One-for-one matches. But this time, what we’re trying to do is apply so much military pressure that the government itself would collapse. And we haven’t found in history a single time in modern times that you could bring about regime change just from the air.”

Hayes: “Short-term military success — they’re a degraded power. If they were a third rate, they’re a fifth rate now. The real question is what now?”

Goldman Sachs warned Thursday that if ships remain reluctant to transit the Strait of Hormuz, oil prices could remain elevated into 2027. The administration that began this war expecting it to last weeks is now receiving financial projections measured in years.

Sanger’s verdict, delivered without qualification: “There is no real end in sight.”

V. Saudi Arabia Breaks With Iran

Saudi Arabia’s foreign ministry announced Saturday it was expelling Iran’s military attaché, his deputy, and three other embassy staff from Riyadh — declaring them persona non grata with 24 hours to leave. The statement cited “Iran’s continuing flagrant attacks against Saudi Arabia.”

Saudi Arabia follows Qatar, which expelled Iranian diplomats last week after Ras Laffan was struck. The two countries that host the largest US military installations in the Middle East — Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia — have now both formally broken with Iran at the diplomatic level.

The countries that re-established diplomatic relations with Iran in 2023, in a Chinese-brokered deal that was hailed as a regional realignment, have now expelled Iranian diplomats in the span of one week. The 2023 normalization lasted less than three years. It ended when Iranian missiles started landing on their capitals.

Saudi Arabia’s foreign minister had already warned that the kingdom reserved the right to military action. He said “what little trust” existed with Iran had been “completely shattered.” He declined to say how many days of patience remained.

VI. The Press Goes Back to the Pentagon

On Friday, US District Judge Paul Friedman ruled that the Pentagon’s media restrictions under Hegseth were unconstitutional — a violation of the First and Fifth Amendments. He ordered the immediate reinstatement of press passes for seven New York Times journalists who had surrendered their passes rather than comply with rules that allowed the Pentagon to declare journalists “security risks” and revoke credentials for conduct deemed to threaten national security.

The Pentagon Press Association, representing journalists who cover the Defense Department, wrote to Pentagon counsel Saturday seeking immediate restoration of all surrendered press passes. “Our clients and the public face ongoing, irreparable harm,” counsel David Schulz wrote, “so long as the experienced military reporters of the PPA are excluded from the Pentagon while active combat operations are being conducted in multiple arenas.”

The Pentagon’s chief spokesman wrote on Friday that the department “disagrees with the decision and is pursuing an immediate appeal.” Any appeal would include a petition for a stay of Judge Friedman’s order.

Hegseth has held press conferences since the war began — but has invited pro-Trump outlets to replace national media organizations that refused to accept his restrictions. His briefings have been performance, not accountability. He has said the same thing at each one: “Today will be the largest strike package yet.” He has provided no timeline for the war’s conclusion. He has called questions about strategy “noise.”

The judge’s ruling is a reminder, delivered during the largest American military operation since Iraq, that there is a reason the First Amendment is first. The Pentagon is appealing it.

VII. The Blackout Record

The internet blackout in Iran has now broken Iran’s own record from January — and is on track to become the most severe digital blackout globally, according to NetBlocks, the watchdog organization that tracks internet censorship.

This is the first time an internet shutdown has coincided with Nowruz celebrations in Iran. The holiday that marks renewal, family gathering, and the Persian new year — celebrated since at least the fifth century BC — is being observed this year in near-total digital silence. Three weeks have passed since the blackout came into effect. Only a few circumvention tools are still functioning, as authorities intensify the crackdown on satellite use and VPNs.

“The lack of accurate information and the absence of collective and immediate public communication have caused us to feel trapped in suffocation and silence,” said Saeed Souzangar, a tech entrepreneur and digital rights activist in Tehran, responding to a Times reporter via text message.

The irony that Iran’s foreign minister defended the blackout via Zoom — a platform inaccessible to most Iranians — has not faded. Officials retain internet access. Ordinary Iranians do not. Citizens are being arrested for sending images to foreign news organizations. Five hundred people have been detained for transmitting information to “enemy countries.”

“Internet shutdowns during Nowruz cut people off from their most basic need: connection,” said Amir Rashidi of the digital-rights group Miaan. “At a time when families should be sharing joy across distances, they are instead left isolated and uncertain.”

The war has two information sieges running simultaneously. Iran’s government is preventing its citizens from knowing what is happening to their country. The US Defense Department is preventing American journalists from independently documenting what the American military is doing in their name. One is a blackout. The other is a media policy under appeal. The difference is meaningful. The direction of travel is not reassuring.

VIII. Cuba Waits

Vivian Salama, on Washington Week Friday night, said something that deserves more attention than it received: “While we are all looking over here in the Middle East, the wheels are very much in motion for a Venezuela-style operation in Cuba that would potentially result in regime change.”

Cuban President Miguel Diaz-Canel has publicly acknowledged for the first time that he is talking to the Trump administration. Cuba, which lost its Venezuelan fuel supplies when Maduro was seized, has been experiencing cascading power grid failures. The Cuban electric grid collapsed earlier this week. Administration officials have been openly suggesting the government will follow.

Jeffrey Goldberg noted the connection precisely: “I assume that successor failure in Iran will very much determine whether there’s an appetite for hemispheric adventure.” Idrees Ali agreed: “He’s had successes — Venezuela and Iran was a success. This might be his first major military failure potentially. And if that fails, how do you do regime change in Cuba? It’s tough.”

The lazy Susan is turning. Iran is not finished. Cuba is waiting its turn. The administration that came to office promising to end foreign wars is now managing an expanding theater of military operations with no defined exit from any of them.
