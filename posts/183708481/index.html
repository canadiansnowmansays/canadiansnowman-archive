The Institutions Maduro Left Behind

Why Trump Can’t Just “Run” Venezuela

January 7, 2026

How 27 Years of Chavismo Created a System That Survives the Leader’s Capture

January 6, 2026

MONDAY’S DEFIANCE: THE NATIONAL ASSEMBLY SENDS A MESSAGE

Maduro’s son, Nicolás Maduro Guerra, took the floor. He’s a legislator and a defendant in the U.S. indictment—”the Prince,” accused of cocaine conspiracy.

His message: “If we normalize the kidnapping of a head of state, no country is safe.”

Then came the gesture. Jorge Rodríguez, National Assembly president and brother of interim President Delcy Rodríguez, stood with Maduro’s son. They unveiled a portrait of Maduro and Flores.

Rodríguez made the sign: One hand forming a “V,” the other pointing toward it. “Nosotros venceremos”—Together we will win.

It’s a revolutionary symbol dating to Cuba, to Chávez. It visually resembles Chávez’s signature, ubiquitous after his 2013 death.

Legislators repeated the gesture. Chants erupted: “This isn’t bullshit, it’s the truth; these are the people who defend Nicolás!”

One legislator singled out Trump: “We never envisioned that he would violate his own Constitution—that he could effectively establish a dictatorship in the very nation that served as the democratic model for the world.”

Another sent an “abrazo” to the families of the 32 Cubans killed in the U.S. attacks—a rare public acknowledgment of Cuba’s deep involvement.

The contrast was stark: In New York, Maduro as criminal defendant. In Caracas, his loyalists in complete control, defiant, gesturing victory.

Trump said Saturday: “We’re in charge.”

The National Assembly said Monday: Not yet you’re not.

THE SYSTEM MADURO LEFT BEHIND: 27 YEARS OF INSTITUTIONAL CAPTURE

Here’s what Trump inherited when he captured Maduro:

Not a country ready for democratic transition. Not institutions waiting to embrace American guidance. Not a power vacuum he can fill with his preferred leaders.

What he inherited was a system built over 27 years specifically to resist exactly this scenario.

The National Assembly: 277 of 285 Seats

Only 32 of 285 legislators belong to opposition parties—and even those are viewed as “alacranes” (scorpions), collaborators who maintained seats by accepting chavista control.

The vast majority—277 legislators—are loyal to the Bolivarian movement. They spent Monday demanding Maduro’s return and condemning American barbarism.

Even the few opposition legislators who spoke delivered platitudes, avoiding any mention of U.S. control. Translation: Even the opposition won’t openly endorse Trump’s authority.

The Judiciary: Completely Captured

Venezuela’s judiciary has been under chavista control since the early 2000s. Chávez systematically packed the Supreme Court, expanded justices, removed dissenters.

Key positions—prosecutor general, attorney general, Supreme Court—all chavista appointees.

This matters: Trump has no Venezuelan court system to prosecute Maduro-era officials or legitimize new governance. The legal system is a regime tool, not a check on it.

The Military: Ideologically Indoctrinated

Venezuela’s military has undergone 27 years of Bolivarian indoctrination. Chávez promoted officers based on loyalty, not competence. He created parallel structures—militias, communal councils—bypassing traditional chains of command.

Maduro continued this. Senior officers control ports, airports, smuggling routes, drug networks, gold mines—deeply embedded in the regime’s economy.

The Cuban factor: At least 32 Cubans were killed Saturday—armed forces and interior ministry personnel protecting Maduro. He didn’t trust his own military enough, needed Cuban bodyguards.

What this tells Trump: The Venezuelan military is compromised and ideologically hostile. Yes, U.S. forces humiliated them Saturday. But humiliation doesn’t equal loyalty. Humiliated officers will resent, resist, and undermine American direction.

Intelligence Agencies: Still Operational

Monday’s proof: Fourteen journalists covering the National Assembly were detained by military counterintelligence. Phones searched—photos, contacts, emails, Instagram, cloud files. One reporter “lost custody of his phone.”

This happened hours after Maduro’s capture, with Rodríguez supposedly seeking “cooperative agenda” with the U.S.

The intelligence apparatus doesn’t answer to Trump. It’s a parallel power structure operating with impunity. Trump captured Maduro. But SEBIN and DGCIM still control who moves freely in Venezuela.

PDVSA: The State Oil Company

PDVSA employs tens of thousands—engineers, technicians, administrators. Leadership is chavista. The workforce is ideologically aligned with the revolution.

Trump wants oil flowing to Chevron. But the infrastructure is “rotten, rusty” (Trump’s own words). The expertise is Venezuelan. Firing all chavistas would require tens of thousands of replacements who don’t exist, years of training, billions in investment.

So Trump needs the existing PDVSA workforce—people who spent Saturday chanting “Defend Nicolás!”

Grassroots Surveillance: The CLAP System

Local Supply and Production Committees (CLAPs) ostensibly distribute subsidized food. In reality, they’re surveillance networks embedded in every community since 2016.

CLAP leaders know: Who opposes the government, who attended rallies, who spoke critically. Distribution is conditional: Support the government, get food. Oppose it, starve.

Trump can’t dismantle this without triggering humanitarian crisis and violent resistance. The surveillance network remains operational—watching, reporting, controlling access based on political loyalty.

THE ENCODED DEFIANCE: WHAT THE “V” GESTURE MEANS

Jorge Rodríguez, the President of the National Assembly, gestures during the inaugural session for the 2026-2031 legislative term, in Caracas, Venezuela, on Monday.Credit...Jesus Vargas/Getty Images

Monday’s “V” gesture wasn’t random. It was coded communication.

“Nosotros venceremos”—Together we will win. Revolutionary iconography from Cuba, Che, Castro. In Venezuela, it evokes Chávez—his signature became ubiquitous after 2013 death.

When Rodríguez made that gesture, he signaled:

  • The revolution continues despite Maduro’s capture

  • Chavismo survives beyond any individual leader

  • Resistance is collective, not personal

This is critical: Trump didn’t just capture a dictator. He challenged a 27-year ideological movement built around resisting American imperialism.

Chavismo’s founding narrative is anti-American. For 27 years: America is the enemy, wants Venezuela’s oil, supports oligarchs, starves Venezuelans with sanctions.

Now America invaded and says “we’re in charge.” From chavistas’ perspective, this validates everything they were told.

The “V” gesture is their response: We will resist. We will win. Together.

Trump thinks capturing Maduro broke resistance. Monday’s gesture suggests it energized it.

TRUMP’S IMPOSSIBLE TASK: GOVERNING WITHOUT LEGITIMACY

Trump needs Venezuelan institutions to function—but they’re staffed by people who view American intervention as illegitimate imperialism.

Option 1: Work with existing chavista institutions
Pro: They control the levers, can keep oil flowing.
Con: They despise U.S. intervention, will undermine objectives, view cooperation as temporary.

Option 2: Purge chavistas and install new leadership
Pro: Creates loyal administrators.
Con: Destroys institutional knowledge, triggers violent resistance, requires tens of thousands of replacements who don’t exist.

Option 3: Install Machado/González opposition government
Pro: Democratic legitimacy, popular support.
Con: Opposition controls no institutions, would require massive U.S. military presence, contradicts Trump’s “oil not democracy” priorities.

Trump chose Option 1 with Rodríguez: Work with existing institutions, accept pragmatic chavista leadership.

But this creates a paradox: Rodríguez has no independent legitimacy. She can’t fire legislators, can’t purge the military, can’t dismantle intelligence agencies. She governs at the sufferance of institutions that want Maduro back.

How long before they decide they don’t need her?

THE CUBAN FACTOR: FOREIGN SUPPORT RUNS DEEP

Cuba’s admission that 32 personnel were killed—armed forces and interior ministry—reveals how deeply foreign support was embedded.

Cuba has provided this support since Chávez’s 2002 coup attempt: presidential security, intelligence analysis, military training, counterintelligence.

For Trump, this creates complications:

Cuba spent 25 years integrating with Venezuela’s security apparatus. Letting the U.S. control Venezuela means losing that investment, oil subsidies, strategic positioning.

Cuban personnel are still embedded throughout Venezuela’s institutions. Cuba could actively undermine U.S. efforts through intelligence operations, resistance networks, providing sanctuary.

Trump said Cuba “looks like it is ready to fall.” But Cuba survived the Soviet collapse. From Havana’s perspective: Wait out Trump. Support Venezuelan resistance. Outlast American attention span.

This is a long game. And Cuba plays long games better than America does.

BUSH’S IRAQ MISTAKE, REPEATED

2003: Bush invades Iraq, topples Saddam in three weeks. Declares “Mission Accomplished.”

Then came governing Iraq. Bush’s de-Ba’athification purged all senior Ba’ath Party members. Result: Hundreds of thousands of experienced administrators suddenly unemployed. Institutions stopped functioning. Humiliated officers formed the insurgency core. Iraq descended into chaos.

The U.S. occupied Iraq for eight years. Over 4,000 American troops died. Cost: $2 trillion. Iraq never became the stable democracy promised.

The lesson: Toppling a dictator is easy. Building new institutions is hard. Occupying a country whose people resent your presence is nearly impossible.

Trump faces the same dynamic. Capturing Maduro was easy. But now he must govern—or find someone who can.

The difference: Bush at least pretended to care about democracy. Trump doesn’t even pretend. “For Mr. Trump, the focus in Venezuela is oil, not promoting democracy.”

So Trump’s strategy: Work with Maduro’s institutions, don’t build new ones, extract oil, declare victory.

But Maduro’s institutions don’t work for Trump. They work for Maduro.

Monday proved it: Legislators loyal to the captured president, gesturing defiance, chanting victory, demanding his return.

Someone is wrong about who’s in charge. And Iraq’s eight-year quagmire suggests it’s not the people who control the institutions.

WHAT HAPPENS NEXT?

The most likely scenario: Gradual erosion of U.S. control through surface compliance and subsurface sabotage. Venezuela outwardly cooperates—oil shipments resume, political prisoners released—while quietly undermining American objectives. Corruption skims profits. Infrastructure “accidentally” fails. Activists quietly re-arrested.

This is how occupied countries resist: They wait out the occupier. Local actors know they’ll be there long after American attention fades.

Iraq proved the pattern: Eight years of trying to build new institutions. Eventually the U.S. left. Today’s Iraqi institutions look remarkably like Ba’athist structures—just under different management.

The alternatives are worse: A military coup installing hardline chavistas would trigger escalation. Rodríguez consolidating real power is unlikely—she has no independent base. Popular uprising is impossible while repression apparatus remains intact.

The question isn’t if Trump controls Venezuela. It’s how long before he loses interest.

CONCLUSION: CAPTURING THE LEADER ISN’T CAPTURING THE SYSTEM

Monday’s National Assembly session crystallized Trump’s fundamental problem:

He captured Nicolás Maduro. But he didn’t capture Venezuela.

Venezuela isn’t just a country with a dictator. It’s a country with a 27-year-old ideological movement that has systematically captured every institution:

The legislature: 277 of 285 seats chavista

The judiciary: Completely controlled

The military: Ideologically indoctrinated, economically dependent on regime

The intelligence agencies: Still operational, still repressing

The oil company: Staffed by chavistas whose careers depend on the system

The grassroots: Surveillance networks embedded in every neighborhood

Trump said “we’re in charge.”

But every one of these institutions answered Monday: “Nosotros venceremos”—Together we will win.

Trump thinks this is like a corporate takeover: Remove the CEO, install new management, redirect the profits. But Venezuela isn’t a company. It’s a revolutionary movement that spent 27 years building institutions specifically designed to resist American control.

Hugo Chávez’s entire political project was anti-American. He built institutions loyal to revolution, not to any individual leader. He indoctrinated the military, captured the judiciary, embedded surveillance networks, created parallel power structures.

Maduro inherited that system and maintained it for 12 years.

Now Trump has captured Maduro but inherited the system.

And the system doesn’t work for him. It works for itself.

Monday’s “V” gestures—repeated by legislators, by Maduro’s son, by the National Assembly president—weren’t surrender. They were defiance.

Encoded defiance, yes. Wrapped in diplomatic language about “cooperative agenda” and “peaceful coexistence.” But defiance nonetheless.

Because the institutions Maduro left behind understand something Trump doesn’t:

Dictators come and go. Institutions endure.

Saddam Hussein was captured in December 2003, found hiding in a hole. Bush said we’re in charge of Iraq.

Eight years and $2 trillion later, the U.S. left. Iraqi institutions today look remarkably like Ba’athist institutions—because institutions don’t disappear just because you capture their leader.

Trump captured Maduro on Saturday.

By Monday, Venezuela’s institutions were already signaling: This isn’t over.

Trump may be in charge in Manhattan, where Maduro sits in a federal courthouse awaiting trial.

But in Caracas, where 277 chavista legislators control the National Assembly, where the military still follows chavista commanders, where intelligence agencies still detain journalists, where PDVSA is still staffed by regime loyalists?

In Caracas, the answer to “who’s in charge?” remains very much in question.

And “Nosotros venceremos”—together we will win—echoes from the legislature to the streets to the military barracks to the oil fields.

Trump captured the leader. The institutions Maduro left behind are just getting started.
