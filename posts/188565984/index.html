CRTC Responds - And Proves My Point

The real fight is just beginning

February 19, 2026

February 19, 2026

Last month I published my policy submission to CRTC about advance billing practices in Canadian telecommunications. Today I received their response.

And honestly? It proves exactly what I’ve been saying about the regulatory gap.

What CRTC Said

Director Nanao Kachi responded on February 18th with the following key points:

“As you stated, advance billing is a common industry practice for communications services. How telecommunications service providers bill for their services is a business decision.”

“Generally, the CRTC does not impose business practices; its objective is to facilitate competition, differentiation and innovation to ensure that Canadians have access to high quality services.”

“Customers encountering issues obtaining this refund who could not find a solution with the service provider can bring the matter to the Commission for Complaints for Telecom-television Services (CCTS).”

The letter then encourages me to participate in the upcoming unified Consumer Protection Code consultation.

Why This Response Actually Proves My Argument

Let me break down what just happened:

1. “Common industry practice”

This is the classic appeal to universality. Because every carrier does it, CRTC treats it as settled. But this is exactly my point: universal adoption of a practice doesn’t make it right, fair or consumer-protective.

Every carrier also used to lock customers into 3-year contracts until CRTC banned it. That was “common industry practice” too - until CRTC decided consumer protection required intervention.

2. “Business decision” outside CRTC’s mandate

Except... CRTC already regulates billing practices.

Telecom Decision 2016-171 required carriers to provide prorated refunds for advance-billed services. That wasn’t a “suggestion” - it was a mandate. CRTC told carriers how to handle refunds following cancellation.

So they DO regulate “business decisions” about billing. They just regulate the CONSEQUENCES of advance billing (refunds) without examining the PRACTICE itself (billing in advance).

3. The circular logic

Here’s what happens when you try to address this issue:

  • CCTS: “This is a systemic policy issue, not an individual complaint. Take it to CRTC.”

  • CRTC: “This is a business decision. Take individual complaints to CCTS.”

Nobody examines whether advance billing serves consumer protection principles. The regulatory gap persists.

What This Means

CRTC’s response is exactly what I expected: a polite acknowledgment that defaults to “industry practice” without first-principles examination.

But here’s the thing: they’re inviting participation in the unified code consultation.

That consultation is where this battle will be won or lost. When CRTC asks for public input on combining all the consumer protection codes, that’s when we need to force the question they’ve never asked:

Should telecommunications carriers be allowed to bill consumers for service before providing it?

Not “how should we manage the problems this creates?”

But “should this practice exist at all?”

The Path Forward

I’ll be responding to CRTC to:

  • Acknowledge their points about industry practice

  • Push back on the “business decision” framing given Decision 2016-171

  • Ask for clarification on how they distinguish “business decisions” from “consumer protection mandates”

  • Reaffirm my intention to participate actively in the consultation

When CRTC opens the formal consultation process (Spring 2026), I’ll provide detailed instructions on how to submit comments.

The more Canadians who participate, the harder it becomes for CRTC to dismiss this as one person’s complaint about one carrier.

This is about a systemic practice that affects every single Canadian with telecommunications service.

Your Role

If you’re reading this and thinking “yeah, I’ve dealt with this nonsense too” - start documenting your experience.

When consultation opens, we need:

  • Personal stories about advance billing creating problems

  • Instances of being unable to determine actual amounts owed

  • Late fees charged on amounts never owed

  • Refunds that took weeks or months

  • The impossibility of reaching customer service after cancellation

Individual stories aggregate into systemic evidence.

CRTC says advance billing is “common industry practice.” Let’s show them that common consumer harm from that practice is even more universal.

Stay tuned. The real fight is just beginning.
