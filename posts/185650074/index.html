The Great Canadian Trade War

Part VIII

The Davos Meltdown: When Truth Triggers Tariffs

January 24, 2026

Trump and Carney pictured in June at the Group of Seven (G7) Summit in Canada.

Seven days from “Good for him” to 100% tariff threat—Trump proves Carney right about predictability

By CanadianSnowmanSays | January 24, 2026

January 16, 2026: Trump on Carney’s China deal: “Good for him.”

January 24, 2026: Trump on the same deal: 100% tariff threat on ALL Canadian goods.

What changed in seven days? Carney told the truth about Trump in Davos.

Let me show you the most predictable tantrum in trade war history—and how it just made USMCA renewal virtually impossible.

The Seven-Day Collapse

Here’s the timeline that validates everything I’ve written in this series:

Thursday, January 16:

  • Carney announces China trade deal in Beijing

  • Canada cuts Chinese EV tariffs from 100% to 6.1%

  • China cuts Canadian canola tariffs from ~85% to ~15%

  • Carney calls China “more predictable” than US

Trump’s immediate reaction: “Good for him. That’s what he should be doing.”

Friday, January 17 - Tuesday, January 21:

  • Relative calm

  • Canada briefs US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer on China deal details

  • No complaints from Washington

Wednesday, January 22:

  • Carney delivers Davos speech

  • Declares US-led world order is “over”

  • Calls out great powers using “tariffs as leverage”

  • Urges middle powers to band together

  • Gets standing ovation

  • Never mentions Trump by name

Thursday, January 23:

  • Trump responds in own Davos speech: “Canada lives because of the United States”

  • Withdraws Canada’s invitation to “Board of Peace”

Saturday, January 24 (Today):

  • Trump posts on Truth Social threatening 100% tariffs on all Canadian goods “if Canada makes a deal with China”

  • Calls Carney “Governor Carney” for first time

  • Threatens over deal he praised 8 days earlier

  • No policy change from Canada—only a speech criticizing US behavior

The Real Timeline: This Started in October

Prime Minister Mark Carney shakes hands with Chinese President Xi Jinping at the start of a meeting in Gyeongju, October 31, 2025. The two leaders spoke for almost 40 minutes.

But wait—it gets worse for Trump’s credibility. The China deal didn’t come out of nowhere. It was months in the making, and Trump knew about it the entire time.

October 30, 2025: Trump meets with Xi Jinping at APEC summit in South Korea

October 31, 2025: Carney meets with Xi Jinping at APEC (the very next day)

  • First formal Canada-China leader contact since 2017

  • 39-minute meeting in Gyeongju

  • Xi invites Carney for formal state visit to Beijing

  • They discuss specific “irritants”: canola tariffs, EV tariffs, agriculture

  • Both leaders direct officials to “move quickly to resolve trade issues”

  • Carney calls it a “turning point” that’s “long overdue”

  • Trump raises no objections

November-December 2025:

  • Canadian and Chinese officials negotiate deal details

  • Topics: Exactly what was discussed at APEC (canola, EVs)

  • Trump silent throughout

January 16, 2026:

  • Carney arrives in Beijing for the state visit Xi invited him to in October

  • Announces deal on exactly the topics discussed 2.5 months earlier

  • Trump’s reaction: “Good for him”

January 24, 2026:

  • Trump threatens 100% tariffs over the deal he:

    • Knew was being negotiated for months

    • Never objected to

    • Praised when announced

    • Only got angry about after Davos speech

What This Extended Timeline Reveals

Trump had multiple off-ramps to object:

October 31: Could have told Carney at APEC not to pursue China deal

  • He didn’t

November-December: Could have raised concerns during negotiation period

  • He didn’t

January 16: Could have criticized the deal when announced

  • He didn’t—he praised it

January 17-21: Could have expressed concerns during briefing to US Trade Rep

  • He didn’t

January 22: Carney gives Davos speech

  • NOW Trump has a problem

January 24: Threatens 100% tariffs

  • Not over the deal itself

  • Over being accurately described in public

This isn’t Trump objecting to Canadian trade policy. This is Trump punishing Carney for telling the truth in Davos.

What Actually Happened

Let’s be clear about the sequence of events:

The China deal negotiation (Oct-Jan): Trump silent

The China deal announcement: Trump approved (”good for him”)

Canada briefing US on details: No problem

Carney saying the quiet part out loud in Davos: Immediate meltdown

Trump didn’t threaten 100% tariffs because Canada made a China deal. He threatened them because Carney accurately described Trump’s behavior in public.

The specific passages that triggered him:

“The old order is not coming back. We should not mourn it. Nostalgia is not a strategy.”

“More recently, great powers have begun using economic integration as weapons, tariffs as leverage, financial infrastructure as coercion, supply chains as vulnerabilities to be exploited.”

These aren’t controversial statements. They’re observable facts. Trump uses tariffs as leverage—he literally does this weekly. The old US-led order IS fractured—that’s obvious to everyone.

But saying it out loud, in Davos, to a standing ovation? That’s what triggered the tantrum.

The “Predictable” Validation

In Part VI, I analyzed Carney’s Beijing comment calling China “more predictable” than the US. I argued it wasn’t praise of Beijing—it was an indictment of Washington.

The last three months just proved me right.

China’s behavior on the deal:

  • October 31: Extended state visit invitation, outlined deal framework

  • November-December: Negotiated in good faith

  • January 16: Delivered on commitments made in October

  • January 24: Still supporting agreement

  • Change in position: Zero

Trump’s behavior on the deal:

  • October 31: Silent (day after meeting Xi himself)

  • November-December: Silent during negotiation

  • January 16: “Good for him”

  • January 22: Angry about Davos speech (not the deal)

  • January 24: 100% tariff threat

  • Change in position: From silence to approval to economic warfare

What changed about the deal? Nothing.

What changed about Trump’s ego? Everything.

Carney said you can’t predict Trump’s behavior. Trump’s response was to... be spectacularly unpredictable about something he’d known about for months, never objected to, and then praised.

You literally cannot script better validation.

The “Governor” Signal

Trump calling Carney “Governor Carney” is significant. This was his signature insult for Justin Trudeau, part of the “51st state” rhetoric that helped destroy that relationship.

The pattern with Trudeau:

  • Started with “Governor Trudeau” mockery

  • Escalated to annexation threats

  • Led to Trudeau’s political collapse

  • Ended with Carney winning election by running AGAINST Trump

Now with Carney:

  • Months of relatively respectful relationship

  • Multiple White House meetings

  • Trump expressing admiration for electoral victory

  • First use of “Governor Carney”: Today, alongside 100% tariff threat

This isn’t banter. This is Trump signaling the relationship is over.

And unlike Trudeau, Carney won election specifically by promising to stand up to this behavior.

The Impossible Threat

Let’s analyze what Trump actually threatened:

“If Canada makes a deal with China, it will immediately be hit with a 100% Tariff against all Canadian goods and products coming into the U.S.A.”

Problems with this:

1. Canada already made the deal (8 days ago, which Trump praised, after 3 months Trump knew about)

2. The deal is modest:

  • 49,000 Chinese EVs at reduced rate

  • Agricultural tariff reductions

  • Not a comprehensive trade agreement

3. Legal authority unclear:

  • USMCA exempts most Canadian goods

  • How does he impose 100% on USMCA-compliant products?

  • Courts already ruled IEEPA tariffs questionable

4. Economic suicide:

  • Canada is 2nd largest US trading partner

  • ~$2 trillion in annual North American trade

  • Integrated supply chains would collapse

  • US consumers would face massive price increases

5. Political timing:

  • USMCA review is July 1 (5 months away)

  • This threat makes negotiated renewal nearly impossible

  • Congress already blocked spite tariffs once (Halloween 2025)

6. The “drop off port” theory:

  • Trump claims Canada will be transshipment point for Chinese goods

  • But the deal is for 49,000 Chinese EVs FOR CANADA

  • These aren’t being re-exported to US

  • It’s a solution looking for a problem

Why This Changes Everything

In Part VII, I showed you the boycott data: 85% liquor collapse, 71% of Canadians buying less US products, 64% negative view of America.

I argued Carney’s Beijing deal made sense given that mandate.

This threat proves I understated the case.

Here’s what Trump just did:

To Canadian voters: Validated every fear about American unreliability

  • “We can’t trust them” → Trump threatens deal he knew about for months

  • “They use tariffs as weapons” → Literally what just happened

  • “We need alternatives” → Trump proves it

To Carney politically: Gave him the ultimate shield for USMCA talks

  • Can’t compromise after 100% threat

  • Voters will see any concession as weakness

  • “See? This is why we needed China” becomes unanswerable

To US negotiators: Destroyed their July 1 leverage

  • How do you negotiate after threatening economic annihilation?

  • Why would Canada trust any US commitment?

  • What’s the point of USMCA if Trump ignores it for tariffs anyway?

To international observers: Confirmed US unreliability

  • Knew about deal for months, said nothing

  • Praised deal publicly → threatened over it days later

  • Punishes speeches, not actions

  • Emotional rather than strategic

The USMCA Death Spiral

July 1, 2026 is the mandatory USMCA review date. Here’s why this threat makes renewal nearly impossible:

Before this threat, the dynamics were:

  • US wants concessions (dairy, digital tax, rules of origin)

  • Canada has limited alternatives (76% of exports to US)

  • Negotiated compromise seemed possible

  • Business community pushing for renewal

After this threat, the dynamics are:

  • US demonstrated it will threaten 100% tariffs over speeches

  • USMCA legal protections mean nothing if Trump ignores them

  • Any Canadian concession looks like caving to threat

  • Why negotiate when Trump changes position based on ego?

Carney’s political calculation just became:

“If I compromise in USMCA talks after Trump threatened 100% tariffs over my Davos speech, I look weak. If USMCA renewal fails because Trump is unreasonable, I look like I was right to diversify to China.”

Trump just made USMCA failure politically beneficial to Carney.

The Doug Ford Wild Card

Remember Part V? Doug Ford’s Reagan ad triggered Trump’s October tantrum and trade talk termination.

Now Trump just gave Ford his own nuclear option: electricity.

Ford once threatened to cut off Ontario’s electricity exports to the US. He even implemented temporary tariffs on it. Trump’s 100% threat just made that leverage live ammunition.

The math:

  • Ontario supplies significant electricity to US Northeast

  • Michigan, New York depend on Canadian power

  • Winter demand peaks are covered by Canadian supply

  • Ford faces re-election and needs to look strong

If Trump actually implements this threat, watch Ford cut power exports. It’s his political and economic leverage play, and Trump just gave him the excuse.

The premier who triggered the October meltdown with a Reagan ad could trigger the winter meltdown by flipping a switch.

Carney’s thesis: US-led order is over, middle powers must band together, American behavior is unpredictable

Trump’s response: Unpredictable threat over speech, proving order is broken, forcing alternatives

Result: Trump validated Carney’s analysis while trying to punish him for it.

Every Trump response proved Carney’s point:

  • Carney said China is more predictable → Trump was wildly unpredictable

  • Carney said powers weaponize tariffs → Trump weaponized tariffs

  • Carney said middle powers must diversify → Trump made diversification mandatory

October 31, 2025: Carney and Xi agree to pursue trade deal at APEC. Trump silent.

But Carney isn’t Trudeau. He won election by promising not to back down. And his voters—the ones who dropped liquor imports 85%, reduced US purchases 71%, and view America negatively at 20-year highs—just got validation for every fear about American unreliability.

Trump threatened 100% tariffs against a country whose population already hates America and is actively boycotting US products.

That’s not leverage. That’s gasoline on a fire.

Trump didn’t just prove Carney right about predictability. He made Carney’s Beijing strategy politically mandatory.

The math always wins. And the math says you can’t partner with someone who threatens economic warfare over accurate speeches—especially when he knew about, approved of, and praised the deal for months before his ego got bruised.
