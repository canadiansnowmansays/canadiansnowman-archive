The Baseline Problem

When Dementia Becomes Diplomacy

Part 8: How Cognitive Decline Stopped Being a Medical Issue and Started Dismantling the World Order

January 19, 2026

The Missing Diagnosis

Robert Kagan’s Atlantic cover story, published January 18, 2026, documents in devastating detail how Donald Trump is destroying the American-led liberal world order that has maintained global stability for eight decades. Kagan describes “the most dangerous world [Americans] have known since World War II,” one that will make “the Cold War look like child’s play.”

What Kagan’s 6,000-word analysis doesn’t address is why Trump is making these decisions—and the answer may be more terrifying than geopolitical ambition or authoritarian ideology.

Since June 2025, I’ve been documenting what neuropsychologists call “the baseline problem”—when someone’s cognitive functioning starts from such an unconventional level that further decline becomes nearly impossible to detect. Across seven parts, I’ve shown how Trump’s cognitive deterioration has accelerated from concerning speech patterns to constitutional confusion to self-reported medical dysfunction.

Now that cognitive decline isn’t just affecting Trump’s communication. It’s driving American foreign policy—and as Kagan documents, transforming the United States “from international leader into international pariah.”

The cognitive decline and geopolitical catastrophe aren’t separate phenomena. They’re cause and effect.

What Kagan Documents

Kagan’s analysis shows how Trump has officially ended the American-dominated liberal world order—not because the United States lacks capability, but because it “has decided that it no longer wishes to play its historically unprecedented role of providing global security.”

For 80 years, America provided security guarantees to allies who gave up territorial ambitions and military power in return. This “grand bargain” was “truly aberrant behavior” that “defied all theories of international relations.”

Trump has shattered it by:

  • Demanding allies pay the U.S. for protection

  • Launching tariff wars against virtually all American allies

  • Threatening territorial aggression against NATO allies Canada and Denmark

  • Conducting “ideological and political warfare against European governments”

The consequence will be a return to 19th-century multipolarity—where major powers fought at least one significant war per decade. Germany will rearm, possibly with nuclear weapons. So will Japan, Poland, and South Korea. These won’t be allies sharing security burdens—they’ll be “independent great powers” viewing the United States “with the same antagonism and fear that they direct toward Russia and China.”

Most remarkably, Kagan notes that despite “America First” rhetoric, Trump “evinces seemingly unlimited global ambition.” In his first year, he launched strikes against Iran and Syria, threatened to seize Canada and Greenland, and meddled in wars across three continents.

Kagan asks: “Is this what ‘restraint’ looks like?”

The Nobel Prize Letter: Where Dementia Meets Diplomacy

On January 19, 2026—eighteen days after Trump's WSJ interview—PBS News reported that Trump had sent a letter to Norwegian Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre

The letter, forwarded to multiple European ambassadors, states:

Every Cognitive Dysfunction Marker in One Letter:

Institutional Confusion: Norway doesn’t award the Nobel Prize. The Norwegian Nobel Committee—an independent body—does. Trump is threatening a prime minister for a decision that PM doesn’t control. This mirrors Part 1’s documentation of him asking if he could “appoint himself” Fed Chair, not understanding Senate confirmation requirements.

Grandiose Delusion: Trump claims he “stopped 8 Wars PLUS.” This is false. His presidency saw continued conflicts in Afghanistan, Syria, and Iran. The claim represents exactly what Dr. John Gartner warned about: delusion that “props up his grandiosity.”

Geographic Confusion: Trump threatens to seize Greenland (owned by Denmark) because Norway didn’t give him an award. He’s conflating two Nordic countries in the same policy threat—mirroring Part 2’s documentation of him confusing Iran with India, Azerbaijan with Albania, Cambodia with Armenia.

Reality Testing Failure: Trump claims Denmark has “no written documents” establishing Greenland ownership. This is false. The Treaty of Kiel (1814) and subsequent agreements establish Danish sovereignty.

Temporal Context: In Part 2 (June 2025), I documented Trump’s “obsessive focus on not receiving Nobel Prize recognition.” Six months later, that obsession has escalated from complaint to policy threat—a petty grievance about an award becoming justification for threatening NATO allies.

The Pattern: The Nobel Prize letter isn’t an aberration. It’s the predictable manifestation of cognitive patterns documented across this series:

  • Institutional confusion + foreign policy = threatening allies over awards they don’t control

  • Grandiose delusion + diplomacy = claiming false achievements to justify aggression

  • Geographic confusion + military power = threatening wrong countries

  • Reality testing failure + nuclear weapons = decisions based on false premises

Why This Is Historically Unprecedented

Kagan compares Trump’s approach to 19th-century multipolarity and warns we’re entering a world marked by great power competition and regular warfare. But there’s a crucial difference: Previous leaders who dismantled world orders had intact cognitive function.

Adolf Hitler - Evil and ultimately self-destructive, but cognitively functional. His decisions followed coherent (if twisted) strategic logic rooted in consistent ideology.

Joseph Stalin - Paranoid and brutal, but strategically coherent. His Yalta diplomacy was calculating and effective.

Vladimir Putin - Ruthless and imperialist, but capable of long-term strategic planning.

Trump Is Different:

Hitler didn’t confuse which country he was threatening or make military decisions based on television coverage.

Stalin didn’t threaten allies over awards they didn’t control or claim geographic facts that were demonstrably false.

Putin doesn’t confuse basic institutional structures or self-medicate against medical advice while explaining biology incorrectly.

The danger isn’t just authoritarianism. It’s cognitively impaired authoritarianism with nuclear weapons.

As I documented in Part 4, when someone doesn’t understand he can’t appoint himself Fed Chair, how can he navigate nuclear deterrence, alliance management, or sphere-of-influence diplomacy?

The World in the Rabbit Hole

Kagan documents the global consequences of Trump’s policies. But he frames these as responses to American “strategic choices.” What if they’re actually responses to visible cognitive dysfunction?

Germany’s Calculation

Kagan predicts German rearmament, possibly nuclear. But German leaders aren’t just responding to American strategic realignment. They’re watching:

  • A president who can’t distinguish Norway from the Nobel Committee

  • Someone who makes foreign policy based on Fox News (documented in Part 4: Iran policy changed after Trump watched coverage celebrating Israeli strikes)

  • A leader who explains biology like a cartoon (”nice, thin blood”)

  • Someone who threatened NATO allies over perceived slights

When Germany decides to go nuclear, it’s not just because Trump abandoned NATO. It’s because they’re witnessing cognitive unpredictability combined with nuclear weapons.

Japan’s Dilemma

Kagan notes Japan’s election of nationalist Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi suggests military buildup. But Japanese planners aren’t just factoring American “unreliability”—they’re observing:

  • A president who confused Iran with India

  • Someone who can’t describe his own medical tests

  • A leader who threatened Greenland because Norway didn’t give him a prize

  • Someone whose positions change based on television

There’s no game theory for cognitive decline.

The Unpredictability Problem

Kagan’s analysis assumes nations can prepare through rearmament and new alliances. That works when major powers make rational calculations. But how do you plan around:

  • Positions that change based on TV coverage?

  • Threats against allies over imagined slights?

  • Geographic and institutional confusion?

  • Decisions based on demonstrably false premises?

National security planners worldwide aren’t just preparing for American withdrawal. They’re preparing for cognitive unpredictability in the world’s most powerful military.

The Manipulation Mechanism

But cognitive unpredictability doesn’t operate in a vacuum. As I documented in Parts 4 and 5, Trump’s positions are actively manipulated by two forces: television coverage and ideological handlers.

The Iran Example

Trump’s position on Iran changed in real-time based on Fox News coverage. He sought diplomatic solutions until Israel launched strikes and Fox provided “wall to wall imagery” celebrating Israeli “military genius.” Trump then changed positions, claimed credit, and escalated—culminating in ordering B-2 bombers to drop bunker busters on Iranian nuclear facilities.

Tucker Carlson himself explained Fox’s strategy: “turning up the propaganda hose to full blast and just trying to, you know, knock elderly Fox viewers off their feet and make them submit.”

Lawrence O’Donnell’s assessment: “The elderly 79-year-old Fox viewer living in the White House has a mind that is at least as vulnerable to Rupert Murdoch’s persuasion as anyone else.”

The Wrong Whisperers

But television isn’t the only manipulation. Stephen Miller and Russell Vought—the architects of Project 2025—aren’t just advising policy. They’re exploiting cognitive vulnerability to implement authoritarian agendas.

Someone with intact cognition might question Miller’s mass deportation proposals or Vought’s executive power expansions. Someone with documented institutional confusion (Fed Chair, Nobel Committee) and reality-testing failure (Joan Rivers voting) can be told that their instincts are correct and legal constraints don’t apply.

The pattern: Trump’s confusion about constitutional limits → Miller/Vought say those limits don’t exist. Trump’s grievance about slights → Miller/Vought channel it into policy. Trump’s susceptibility to television → Miller/Vought ensure he watches the right shows.

As I wrote in August: “When power brokers sense weakness at the top, they don’t slow down. They rush. They lock in control. They dismantle guardrails.”

Trump’s cognitive confusion about which country controls the Nobel Prize becomes, in their hands, justification for threatening NATO allies and pursuing territorial expansion.

They’re not managing his decline. They’re weaponizing it.

The Acceleration Timeline

When this series began in June, I documented concerning patterns. Seven months later, those patterns have escalated to the geopolitical crisis Kagan describes:

June 2025: Fed Chair confusion → Constitutional knowledge deficit
August 2025: Nuclear submarines over tweets → Disproportionate military response
October 2025: Quantico speech → Governor demands 25th Amendment
November 2025: Death threats to Congress → Capitol Police protection required
January 2026: “Nice thin blood” → Self-reported medical dysfunction
January 2026: Nobel Prize letter → NATO allies threatened over institutional confusion

Each escalation applies documented cognitive dysfunction to higher-stakes decisions:

Domestic confusion → International confusion
Personal grievances → Foreign policy threats
Constitutional ignorance → Alliance destruction

Dr. John Gartner predicted Trump would “fall off the cognitive cliff.” We’re watching it happen as foreign policy.

What Kagan Misses

Kagan’s analysis brilliantly documents the what. But by framing it as strategic choice (however destructive), he misses the medical dimension that makes it uniquely dangerous.

Kagan: “Trump’s megalomania is transforming the United States from international leader into international pariah”

Reality: The Nobel Prize letter suggests cognitive dysfunction severe enough to confuse which country controls which institution while threatening allies.

Kagan: “Trump and his supporters seem to believe that the rest of the world will simply accommodate this new American approach”

Reality: Trump doesn’t have a coherent “approach.” His positions change based on television, personal slights, and institutional confusion. You can’t “accommodate” cognitive unpredictability.

Kagan: “For all the talk of ‘America First,’ Trump evinces seemingly unlimited global ambition”

Reality: Part 4 documented Trump’s “ambition” as reactive. Iran policy changed after Fox News. Greenland threats followed Nobel grievance. These aren’t calculated power plays—they’re impulsive responses.

The Missing Variable

Kagan assumes Trump’s decisions follow from some coherent worldview. The Baseline Problem series documents they don’t.

When you layer cognitive decline onto geopolitical analysis, the danger multiplies:

  • Unpredictability: Allies can’t plan around positions that change based on TV

  • Irrationality: What deters someone who threatens allies over imagined slights?

  • Institutional confusion: Sphere-of-influence diplomacy requires understanding which institutions control what

  • Reality testing failure: Strategic planning fails when based on false premises

You can’t deter someone who doesn’t understand deterrence.
You can’t negotiate with someone who confuses which country he’s negotiating with.
You can’t plan strategy around someone whose positions change based on television.
You can’t trust guarantees from someone who threatens allies over awards they don’t control.

The Question No One’s Asking

Kagan asks: “Can the world survive Trump’s destruction of the liberal order?”

The Baseline Problem asks: “Can the world survive that destruction being driven by accelerating dementia?”

These aren’t the same question.

If Trump’s foreign policy followed coherent (if destructive) ideology, nations could adapt through rearmament and strategic repositioning. That’s what Kagan predicts.

But if it’s driven by cognitive dysfunction—institutional confusion, geographic mix-ups, television-influenced decisions, personal grievances, reality-testing failure—adaptations become much harder.

The 25th Amendment Was Built For This

In Part 5, I documented Governor Pritzker’s demand for 25th Amendment invocation. Three months later, with the Nobel Prize letter as evidence, it looks like prescient medical assessment.

The 25th Amendment exists for scenarios where presidential incapacity threatens national security. Section 4 provides for removal when the President is “unable to discharge the powers and duties of his office.”

The Evidence:

  • Institutional confusion (Fed Chair, Nobel Committee)

  • Geographic confusion (Denmark vs. Norway)

  • Temporal confusion (Joan Rivers voting)

  • Historical confusion (Declaration/Civil War)

  • Medical decision-making based on cartoon biology

  • Foreign policy based on grievances and television

  • Reality testing failure

If these aren’t indicators of inability to discharge duties, what would be?

Governor Pritzker was concerned about domestic military deployment. Now the dysfunction is driving decisions that will force German rearmament, trigger Japanese military buildup, destabilize European security, and return the world to multipolar great-power competition.

The 25th Amendment isn’t just about domestic governance. It’s about whether cognitive decline in one office can be allowed to dismantle eight decades of global stability.

From O’Donnell’s Question to Kagan’s Warning

Lawrence O’Donnell asked in June: “If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?”

Kagan answered (without realizing it): “We’ll know when the American order begins to collapse, and we enter the most dangerous world we’ve known since World War II.”

The cognitive decline documented across this series and the geopolitical catastrophe Kagan describes are not separate phenomena.

They are cause and effect.

The rabbit hole isn’t metaphorical. It’s clinical. And the entire world is entering it.

When dementia becomes diplomacy:

  • NATO allies face threats based on institutional confusion

  • Nuclear decisions are influenced by television

  • Sphere-of-influence negotiations are conducted by someone who confuses countries and institutions

  • Security guarantees come from someone who makes policy based on personal slights

Kagan writes: “Americans are entering the most dangerous world they have known since World War II.”

He’s right. But the danger isn’t just geopolitical realignment.

The danger is that these forces are being unleashed by someone whose cognitive capacity—by medical expert assessment, self-reported symptoms, and documented patterns—is severely and progressively impaired.
