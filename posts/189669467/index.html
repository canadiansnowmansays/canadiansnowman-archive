The Democratic Promise

Introduction — The Question Steve Jobs Asked in 1983 That We Still Haven’t Answered

March 2, 2026

In 1983, Steve Jobs predicted the next 50 years of technology

Stephen Gordon — CanadianSnowmanSays March 2026

(Image credit: Doug Menuez)

In the summer of 1983, a twenty-eight-year-old Steve Jobs stood before an audience at the International Design Conference in Aspen, Colorado, and described the next four decades of human civilization with a precision that still takes your breath away.

He described the iPhone twenty-four years before it existed. He described the Internet before most people had heard the word. He described the App Store, social media, electronic mail, algorithmic content filtering, and—in a passage so prescient it borders on the uncanny—he described artificial intelligence that could capture a person’s “underlying way of looking at the world” so that future generations could ask it questions after the person was dead and gone.

He was twenty-eight years old. The personal computer was seven.

But here is what matters most about the Aspen speech, and it is the reason this series exists: embedded in every one of those predictions was a value. Jobs was not merely describing what technology would do. He was describing what technology was for. And what it was for, in Jobs’s vision, was democratization.

The Democratic Vision

Jobs built his entire presentation around an analogy that was deliberately chosen. He compared the personal computer to the fractional horsepower electric motor. The electric motor, he explained, was first invented in the late 1800s, and when it was first invented, it could only be built very large—which meant it could only be justified for very large applications. Power was concentrated. Then someone figured out how to share a single large motor across a factory floor through belts and pulleys. Power was distributed to medium-scale operations. But the real revolution—the one that mattered—was the invention of the fractional horsepower motor, which brought power directly to the individual.

“There’s about fifty-five or so fractional horsepower motors now in every household,” Jobs told his audience. The revolution wasn’t the machine. The revolution was that everybody got one.

This was not an idle metaphor. It was a theory of history. Jobs was arguing that concentrated power is the primitive state, and distribution of power is the advanced state. That the natural arc of transformative technology bends toward individual empowerment. And that the personal computer was the latest—and potentially the most consequential—chapter in that story.

When an audience member asked about the privacy implications of centralized databases, Jobs’s answer was revealing. He didn’t talk about regulation. He didn’t talk about oversight. He said: “The first thing we’ve got to do is give tools to people to help them do that. Because right now those tools are centralized.”

Distribute that intelligence. That was the answer. Don’t regulate concentrated power—dissolve it. Put the tools in the hands of every citizen, every student, every household. Make the machine so intuitive that a person could learn to use it in twenty minutes. Put it in a book with a radio link. Let the citizen filter congressional testimony, find out their representative said something objectionable, write a nasty letter, and “zing it on the email system” so an aide reads it the next morning.

That was Steve Jobs’s vision of the future. Technology as the great equalizer. The computer as a tool of democratic citizenship.

He was willing to spend twenty-five percent of Apple’s annual profits to put a computer in every school in America—a hundred thousand schools—because, as he put it, “the kids can’t wait.” Bob Dole killed the federal legislation. California passed it anyway.

The question this series asks is simple: what happened to that vision?

What Happened Instead

Every one of Jobs’s predictions came true. Every single one. The computer in a book with a radio link—that’s the iPhone. The electronic distribution of software with free sampling and credit card payment—that’s the App Store. The interest-based distribution lists that multiply until there are more lists than people—that’s social media. The ability to filter all published information through your personal interests—that’s the algorithmic feed. The machine that captures a thinker’s “underlying way of looking at the world” so you can ask it questions after they’re gone—that’s a large language model.

The predictions came true. The values didn’t.

Social media was supposed to connect people. It became a surveillance capitalism engine that monetized attention, amplified outrage, and destabilized democracies. The algorithmic feed was supposed to empower citizens with filtered knowledge. It became the most sophisticated propaganda delivery system in human history. The computer in everyone’s pocket was supposed to be a tool of individual empowerment. It became a tracking device that records your movements, your purchases, your relationships, and your thoughts, and sells that information to the highest bidder.

And the people who built these things—the heirs to Jobs’s vision—did not, in the main, turn out to be democrats. They turned out to be oligarchs.

Mark Zuckerberg promised to “connect the world.” He built the most powerful tool for mass manipulation ever created, then bent the knee to authoritarian governments to protect his market access. Elon Musk promised clean energy, sustainable transport, and the democratization of space. He became the richest man in the world and used that wealth to purchase a global communications platform, deploy it as a political weapon, and install himself as an unelected shadow official in the United States government. Sam Altman promised “AI for the benefit of all of humanity.” He built the most powerful AI system on Earth, pursued profit over safety at every critical juncture, and when a competitor was blacklisted by the Pentagon for refusing to allow AI to be used for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, he signed the Pentagon contract within hours.

Jobs said “distribute that intelligence.” What happened instead was the greatest concentration of technological power in human history, in the hands of a vanishingly small number of men who answer to no one.

The Exception

And then there is Dario Amodei.

In February 2026, the CEO of Anthropic—the company that builds Claude, the AI system I use as a research tool throughout my work—was given an ultimatum by the Pentagon. Allow your AI to be used for any lawful military purpose without restriction, or face the consequences. The Department of Defense demanded compliance within three days.

Amodei said no.

Anthropic drew two red lines. No fully autonomous weapons. No mass domestic surveillance. The company told the Pentagon that it would rather lose the contract—a $200 million contract embedded in classified military operations—than cross those lines.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth branded Anthropic a “supply chain risk to national security”—a designation normally reserved for foreign adversaries, used for the first time in American history against a domestic company. The Pentagon called Amodei a “liar” with a “God complex.” President Trump posted on Truth Social: “We don’t need it, we don’t want it, and will not do business with them again!”

Hours later—hours later—OpenAI’s Sam Altman announced he had signed a deal to supply the Pentagon with AI for classified military networks.

Amodei’s response, in a CBS interview that will be remembered long after the news cycle forgets it: “Disagreeing with the government is the most American thing in the world. And we are patriots. In everything we have done here, we have stood up for the values of this country.”

In a separate interview, Amodei expressed deep discomfort with what he called the “overnight” and accidental concentration of power in the AI industry—language that could have come directly from Steve Jobs’s 1983 speech about distributing intelligence to prevent exactly that concentration.

Steve Jobs said: distribute the intelligence. Forty-three years later, one tech CEO appears to have been listening.

About This Series

The Democratic Promise is a series in eight parts. It traces a single question from a conference room in Aspen in 1983 to the Pentagon standoff of February 2026: was Steve Jobs’s democratic vision of technology ever realistic, or was oligarchic capture inevitable?

Each essay profiles a figure or moment that advanced or betrayed that vision.

Part 1: The Sermon — Steve Jobs at the 1983 International Design Conference in Aspen. The speech that predicted everything. The fractional horsepower computing analogy, the Aristotle machine, the democratic citizen on email. We examine what Jobs got right, what he got wrong, and most importantly, the values he embedded in every prediction.

Part 2: The Builders — The people who kept faith with the democratic promise. Tim Berners-Lee, who invented the World Wide Web and deliberately refused to patent it. Jimmy Wales and the radical experiment of Wikipedia. The open-source movement. The handful of visionaries who had the power to concentrate and chose to distribute.

Part 3: The Complicated Cases — Bill Gates, who built a monopoly and then spent his second act trying to give it away. Steve Jobs himself, who returned to Apple and created the most democratizing device in history—the iPhone—inside one of the most controlled ecosystems ever designed. The tension between democratizing access and controlling the platform.

Part 4: The Betrayers — Mark Zuckerberg and the transformation of “connecting the world” into the most powerful surveillance capitalism engine ever built. The broader story of how Silicon Valley’s democratic rhetoric became a cover for extraction, manipulation, and the systematic erosion of privacy.

Part 5: The Fallen Visionary — Elon Musk. From solar energy and electric vehicles and the democratization of space to the richest man on Earth, the purchaser of a global communications platform, and the most powerful unelected official in the United States. The most dramatic arc from Jobs’s vision to its opposite.

Part 6: The Dealmaker — Sam Altman and OpenAI. “AI for the benefit of all of humanity” as founding rhetoric. The transformation from nonprofit to capped-profit to whatever OpenAI is now. The board coup. The safety departures. And the Pentagon contract signed within hours of a competitor’s principled refusal.

Part 7: The Patriot — Dario Amodei and Anthropic. The CEO who left OpenAI over safety concerns, built a competing company on the principle that AI development could be done responsibly, drew two red lines against autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, and held those lines when the full weight of the United States government came down on him. What it means that the person most faithful to Jobs’s democratic vision is being punished for it.

Part 8: The Verdict — The overarching question resolved. Was Jobs’s democratic promise naive? Was oligarchic capture inevitable? Or does the Anthropic precedent suggest there is still a path—narrow, contested, under siege—back to the vision of distributed intelligence that a twenty-eight-year-old articulated in Aspen forty-three years ago?

A Prequel, in Retrospect

Regular readers will recognize the questions this series raises. They are the same questions I have been exploring in The Asimov Gap, my ongoing series examining whether humanity can govern artificial intelligence through democratic institutions or whether oligarchic capture of this technology is inevitable.

The Asimov Gap takes Isaac Asimov’s fifty-year thought experiment about robots, power, and governance and applies it to the current AI moment — the concentration of AI development in the hands of a few companies, the failure of democratic institutions to keep pace, the tension between innovation and accountability, and the very real possibility that the most transformative technology in human history will be controlled by a handful of unelected men operating without meaningful oversight.

What I did not fully appreciate when I began The Asimov Gap was that the question Asimov was asking — can democratic societies govern technologies more powerful than the institutions designed to regulate them? — did not originate with AI. It did not even originate with Asimov. It originated with the personal computer. And the person who articulated it most clearly was a twenty-eight-year-old Steve Jobs, standing in Aspen in 1983, describing a world in which distributed intelligence would empower every citizen.

The Democratic Promise is, in retrospect, the prequel to The Asimov Gap. It traces the democratic technology question from its origin — Jobs’s fractional horsepower computing analogy — through four decades of fulfillment and betrayal, arriving at the same doorstep where The Asimov Gap begins: the AI era, the concentration crisis, and the question of whether democratic governance of technology is still possible.

If The Asimov Gap asks “can we govern AI democratically?”, The Democratic Promise asks the prior question: “when did we stop trying?”

The two series are designed to be read independently. But together, they tell a single story — the story of a democratic promise made in 1983, betrayed by the very industry it inspired, and now being tested, in real time, by the most consequential technology humans have ever built.

A Note on Perspective

As with all my work, I should be transparent about where I stand.

I am a Canadian. I write about American politics and technology from across the border, with the analytical distance that position provides and the limitations it imposes. I am a retired businessman, not a technologist, and I approach these subjects through the lens of power, accountability, and institutional behavior rather than engineering.

I should also disclose that I use Claude—Anthropic’s AI system—as a research and drafting tool throughout my work, including this series. That relationship does not make me an advocate for Anthropic. It makes me a user with direct experience of the product and, therefore, a direct stake in the questions this series raises about who controls these tools and under what constraints. I have endeavored throughout to apply the same evidentiary standard to Dario Amodei that I apply to every other figure in these essays. The reader should judge whether I have succeeded.

Finally, a word about Steve Jobs. This series is not hagiography. Jobs was a complicated, often difficult, sometimes cruel human being. He is not the hero of this story. He is the person who, at twenty-eight, articulated a set of values about technology and democracy with such clarity that they serve as a standard against which everything that followed can be measured. Whether he himself would have lived up to that standard is one of the questions Part 3 will address.

The hero of this story, if there is one, is the idea itself: that technology should distribute power, not concentrate it. That the tools of intelligence belong in the hands of every citizen, not locked behind the gates of a few men who answer to no electorate and obey no law but their own ambition.

Steve Jobs called it “fractional horsepower computing.” He meant something much larger.

He meant freedom.
