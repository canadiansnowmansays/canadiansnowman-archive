Episode 9: The Global TACO - When Even Following Through is Chickening Out

August 5, 2025

Stephen Lawton
August 5, 2025

Welcome to Episode 9 of Trump TACO Tuesdays, where we document the most remarkable evolution in our analytical framework yet: the moment TACO went global, became an international meme, and proved that even Trump's attempts to avoid accommodation create perfect accommodation patterns.

Just five days ago, as Trump approached his much-hyped August 1st tariff deadline, something extraordinary happened in Brazil. After signing off on the tariffs but including a long list of exceptions covering nearly 700 products, Brazilians adopted the phrase Trump Always Chickens Out (TACO) to mock the American president; the acronym became one of the seven most-used terms on X (formerly Twitter) that week.

Our framework—originally developed to track Trump's accommodation patterns—had become international shorthand for predicting American trade policy. Foreign governments, markets, and citizens worldwide now use "TACO" to describe what they see as inevitable: Trump's retreat from bold promises when reality intervenes.

But here's the twist that makes Episode 9 so remarkable: Trump knew about the TACO reputation, tried desperately to avoid it, and in doing so created the most sophisticated accommodation pattern we've yet documented—one where "following through" itself becomes a form of chickening out.

The Anti-TACO Strategy: When Reputation Management Meets Reality

As Wall Street traders continued to share the accusation that "Trump Always Chickens Out," the president falsely insisted that he did not delay a tariff deadline. This wasn't accidental rhetoric—Trump's team was acutely aware that the TACO framework had become a market-moving prediction tool.

The anti-TACO strategy was clear: implement something on August 1st to avoid the appearance of complete retreat, while maintaining enough flexibility to avoid economic disaster. What emerged was perhaps the most elaborate accommodation disguised as toughness in modern presidential history.

The Swiss Surprise: When Even Neutral Allies Get TACO'd

Switzerland was left stunned on Friday after Trump hit the country with one of the highest tariffs in his global trade reset, with industry associations warning of tens of thousands of jobs being put at risk. Switzerland was hit with a jump from 31% to 39%, among the highest rates of the new tariffs.

The Swiss TACO reveals how accommodation pressure creates indiscriminate policy outcomes. Switzerland—a neutral, wealthy ally with minimal strategic impact on U.S. trade deficits—received one of the harshest tariff treatments simply because mathematical formulas rather than strategic thinking now drive policy decisions.

Swiss President Karin Keller-Sutter said that she spoke to Trump on Thursday but did not reach an agreement with him to forestall that spike. The country's cabinet will hold a special meeting on Monday to discuss its next steps, demonstrating how TACO policies force even friendly nations into crisis management mode.

Court Skepticism Building: Legal Reality Closing In

On July 31, a panel of 11 judges heard an appeal by the government to protect its IEEPA tariffs, but seemed hesitant to agree. Even the courts that stayed the lower court ruling seem skeptical of Trump's claimed authority.

The judicial skepticism adds another constraint forcing accommodation: when both lower courts and appeals courts question your legal authority, the pressure to modify policies rather than face definitive judicial rejection becomes overwhelming.

Market Devastation Despite "Following Through"

Despite Trump's attempt to appear tough through partial implementation, markets delivered their verdict on Friday: The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped more than 500 points, or 1.2%, while the S&P 500 fell around 1.6%. The tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite tumbled more than 2.2%. All three major averages posted losses for the week.

"The market is tired of the tariff drama," Peter Ricchiuti, senior professor of finance at Tulane University, told CNN. "There is also the recognition that the tariff damage will get worse."

European stocks pulled back Friday, with the pan-European Stoxx 600 trading around 1.3% lower. Asia-Pacific markets ended the day lower, with South Korea's Kospi index retreating 3.88%.

However, markets staged a sharp recovery on Monday, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average soaring 585.06 points, or 1.34%, closing at 44,173.64. The S&P 500 moved up 1.47% to end at 6,329.94, and the Nasdaq Composite surged 1.95% to settle at 21,053.58."Today is sort of a bounce-back day," said Sam Stovall, chief investment strategist at CFRA Research. "Stocks tend to pop after a drop, so that's what's happening."

The recovery pattern itself validates TACO psychology: markets crashed when Trump "followed through," then bounced when investors realized the implementation was less comprehensive than initially feared. Even "following through" triggered the same market punishment that forced previous accommodations, followed by relief when accommodation patterns became apparent.

The Anti-TACO Paradox: Why Avoiding Accommodation Creates Accommodation

The August 1st implementation reveals a fundamental paradox in Trump's approach: his desperate attempt to avoid the TACO label led him to create accommodation patterns so elaborate they became more TACO-esque than simple retreat.

Consider the logical progression:

Classic TACO (Episodes 1-8): Bold promise → Reality constraint → Clean retreat → Face-saving rhetoric

Anti-TACO (Episode 9): Bold promise → Reality constraint → Partial implementation with massive exemptions → Extended deadlines → Market punishment → International mockery

The anti-TACO strategy required more accommodations, more complexity, and more reputation damage than simply admitting the original timeline was unrealistic.

What This Means: The Evolution of Presidential Accommodation

Episode 9 represents the evolution of TACO from a simple prediction tool to a complex international phenomenon that constrains presidential behavior in ways we're still discovering.

For Markets: TACO logic has become so embedded in investor psychology that even partial implementation triggers selling. The framework creates its own reality through self-fulfilling prophecy—investors bet against Trump's follow-through, removing market pressure that might support tougher policies.

For Foreign Policy: Trading partners now plan for American accommodation, reducing the negotiating power of bold threats while increasing the political cost of retreat.

For Presidential Psychology: The attempt to avoid TACO created more elaborate accommodation patterns that damaged Trump's reputation more than simple retreat would have.

The August 7th Setup: TACO Cycle Continuation

Country-specific "reciprocal tariffs" are expected to resume on August 7, 2025. The August 1st accommodation simply moved the mathematical impossibility forward by six days while adding Mexico exemptions, Brazilian carve-outs, and market punishment.

The fundamental constraints that forced Episode 7's accommodations remain unchanged:

  • Economic Reality: Markets punish comprehensive tariff implementation

  • Mathematical Reality: Complex trade negotiations require months, not days

  • Legal Reality: Court challenges to tariff authority continue

  • Political Reality: International mockery of accommodation patterns

August 7th faces identical constraints with reduced credibility.

The Weekend TACO Setup: Pharmaceutical Ultimatum as Diversion

As markets crashed and international mockery peaked, Trump deployed a classic TACO diversion tactic over the weekend: threatening an entirely different industry to distract from tariff accommodations.

President Donald Trump sent letters to 17 major pharmaceutical company CEOs on Thursday, demanding that they bring US drug prices in line with the far lower ones available to patients in other countries. Trump also wants the companies to guarantee that Medicaid, Medicare and commercial-market insurers pay such prices for all new drugs. He gave the companies 60 days to comply.

The pharmaceutical ultimatum followed the exact TACO pattern we've documented:

Bold Numerical Promise: 17 companies, 60-day deadline, "30% to 80%" price cuts

Mathematical Impossibility: The president, however, does not have the legal authority nor the regulatory tools to require drugmakers to sell their products at "Most Favored Nation" prices in any market

Threat Escalation: "But if you refuse to step up, we will deploy every tool in our arsenal to protect American families from continued abusive drug pricing practices," Trump wrote.

The timing reveals classic TACO psychology: deploy a new impossible promise exactly when previous impossible promises collapse. The pharmaceutical threat allows Trump to appear tough while quietly accommodating on tariffs.

The 200% Pharmaceutical TACO: Mathematical Impossibility Squared

Trump's weekend pharmaceutical offensive included an even more dramatic threat: U.S. President Donald Trump warned again this week that long-awaited industry-wide tariffs would be announced "very soon." That levy, he said, could be up to 200% and may hit in 12 to 18 months.

The 200% pharmaceutical tariff threat exhibits every TACO characteristic:

  • Mathematically Absurd: It is estimated that a tariff of just 25% on pharmaceutical imports would drive up U.S. drug prices by almost $51 billion annually, increasing domestic prices by as much as 12.9%

  • Implementation Delay: 12-18 month grace period acknowledges impossibility

  • Industry Panic: The pharmaceutical industry is scrambling with scenario planning as U.S. President Donald Trump's 200% tariff proposal threatens to drive up drug prices and rip out corporate profit margins

The 200% threat creates the perfect TACO cycle setup: a promise so economically destructive that accommodation becomes inevitable, disguised as giving companies time to "adjust."

The August 7th Double TACO: When Following Through Gets Pushed Back Again

Even Trump's accommodation to his accommodation got accommodated. On July 31, Trump signed Executive Order, Further Modifying the Reciprocal Tariff Rates, which ordered that country-specific "reciprocal" tariffs resume on August 7 instead of August 1 as expected.

The August 1st "hard deadline" became August 7th at the last minute—another week-long delay disguised as administrative necessity. As we publish this on August 5th, Trump faces the August 7th deadline with the same mathematical constraints that forced previous accommodations.

The Jobs Report TACO: Shoot the Messenger

Erika McEntarfer was confirmed as commissioner of the B.L.S. last year in an 86-to-8 vote in the Senate.Credit...U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics

When Trump's tariff policies contributed to devastating employment data, he deployed classic TACO blame-shifting: Trump fired Dr. Erika McEntarfer, the commissioner of the Bureau of Labor Statistics, whom he accused, without evidence, of manipulating the monthly jobs reports for "political purposes." US job growth stalled in July, with just 73,000 jobs added, while May and June totals were revised down by a combined 258,000. The Republican capitulation was immediate: Senator Markwayne Mullin, who voted for McEntarfer's confirmation last year, accused her on Fox News of generating "fake reports," declaring "I'm glad she's out of a job.

The National Association for Business Economics slammed President Donald Trump's firing of Bureau of Labor Statistics Commissioner Erika McEntarfer "and the unfounded accusations leveled against the work of the agency" in a statement Friday, calling it "This unprecedented attack on the U.S. statistical system."

Just last week, Vice President JD Vance promoted data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics. After Mr. Trump fired its leader, Mr. Vance’s spokesman said the vice president he “completely aligned with President Trump and was glad to see him dismiss the B.L.S. commissioner.”Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Classic TACO psychology: when your policies create bad economic data, fire the messenger rather than acknowledge policy failure. Trump has a pattern of accepting results that benefit him and denigrating those he dislikes as rigged. Just hours before firing McEntarfer, Vice President JD Vance was promoting Bureau of Labor Statistics data, yet his spokesman immediately declared Vance was "completely aligned with President Trump" in dismissing the commissioner.

The Hidden Shipping Accommodation: October 5th Loophole

Even if the August 7th tariffs technically "go into effect," Trump has quietly built in a massive accommodation that makes the implementation largely theatrical: Items arriving by ship, which account for more than half of US imports, will not be affected by the new tariff rates until October 5 if they depart by August 7.

This represents the most sophisticated TACO yet documented: announce tough implementation while simultaneously creating a two-month grace period for the majority of affected imports. The appearance of following through covers the reality of continued accommodation.

Breaking: The India Escalation - TACO Psychology in Real Time

President Trump in Allentown, Pa., on Sunday. He has a pattern of accepting results that benefit him and denigrating those he dislikes as being rigged or part of a scam.Credit...Haiyun Jiang/The New York Times

Trump has provided a perfect real-time demonstration of TACO psychology with breaking news about India. Donald Trump said he planned to "substantially" raise tariffs on imports from India because of its purchases of Russian oil, in a new escalation of tensions between Washington and New Delhi.

"India is not only buying massive amounts of Russian Oil, they are then, for much of the Oil purchased, selling it on the Open Market for big profits. They don't care how many people in Ukraine are being killed by the Russian War Machine," the US president wrote on Truth Social on Monday. "Because of this, I will be substantially raising the Tariff paid by India to the USA," he said.

This represents classic TACO escalation psychology: when accommodation pressure builds around existing promises (August 7th deadline), deploy new threats against different targets to maintain appearance of toughness. The timing—exactly 48 hours before the August 7th deadline—reveals diversionary tactics rather than strategic calculation.

Strategic Incoherence: Trump threatens a "pivotal strategic partner" that Washington increasingly sees as crucial for containing China. Senior Trump officials had long touted India as one of the large trading partners closest to reaching a tariff pact, even after a high-profile visit by Vice President JD Vance.

Relationship Destruction: "So much for the bromance between Modi and Trump and the ostensibly close relationship between two budding geopolitical allies," said Eswar Prasad, a professor of trade policy and economics at Cornell University.

India's Response: Classic TACO Accommodation in Progress

India's response perfectly demonstrates TACO accommodation psychology in action—public defiance coupled with quiet accommodation:

Public Defiance: India's commerce ministry said it remains "committed to reaching a fair, balanced and mutually beneficial bilateral trade agreement." Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri cited "double standards" in Trump's threats, while petroleum minister Hardeep Singh Puri declared: "I'm not worried at all. If something happens, we will deal with it."

Quiet Accommodation: Despite public statements, Indian Oil Corporation just bought 7 million barrels from the US, Canada, and Middle East for September delivery. Indian state refiners stopped buying Russian oil after July discounts narrowed, while framing the shift as commercial decisions driven by pricing rather than political capitulation.

The India escalation demonstrates how TACO accommodation pressure creates policy chaos: when constraints force retreat on core promises, random escalation against strategic partners becomes the psychological alternative to admitting failure, followed by predictable accommodation disguised as business decisions.

Conclusion: When Following Through is Chickening Out

Episode 8 proves that TACO psychology operates at levels deeper than simple retreat. When accommodation pressure becomes severe enough, even "following through" becomes a form of accommodation—partial implementation with massive exemptions designed to avoid the economic and political costs of comprehensive action.

Trump's anti-TACO strategy created the most elaborate accommodation pattern we've documented: a seven-day delay disguised as implementation, country-by-country exemptions disguised as toughness, 700-product carve-outs disguised as 50% tariffs, pharmaceutical ultimatums disguised as tough leadership, and statistical commissioner firings disguised as accountability.

The weekend pharmaceutical threats reveal TACO evolution in real time: when one impossible promise collapses, immediately deploy another impossible promise to maintain the appearance of toughness while quietly accommodating the first.

As we publish on August 5th, Trump faces three imminent TACO tests: August 7th tariff implementation (with a hidden October 5th shipping accommodation), August 12th China truce expiration, and August 29th de minimis elimination. The framework that began by predicting single accommodations now tracks a complex web of overlapping deadlines, each creating its own accommodation pressure.

Most remarkably, the international adoption of TACO as a predictive framework has created new constraints on presidential behavior. When foreign governments plan for your accommodations and mock them in real time, the political cost of retreat multiplies while the political benefit of toughness diminishes.

When TACO Becomes Nuclear: A Sobering Reality

But Episode 9 also reveals the most troubling evolution of TACO psychology yet documented. When trade accommodation pressure peaked, Trump deployed nuclear submarines against Russia in response to social media posts from Dmitry Medvedev. President Donald Trump issued a rare threat of nuclear escalation on Friday, saying he had ordered two nuclear submarines to be strategically positioned near Russia.

Russia's response was dismissive rather than escalatory: Viktor Vodolatsky, a senior Russian lawmaker, stated that Russia possesses "significantly more nuclear submarines in the world's oceans" than the US, claiming US subs have "long been under their control" and, therefore, no specific response is required.

The mathematical constraints that forced Episode 9's accommodations remain unchanged for the August 7th deadline. When grandiose promises meet immovable constraints—whether legal, economic, or mathematical—accommodation becomes inevitable regardless of political will. Trump's attempt to break the TACO cycle only proved its fundamental laws: reality doesn't negotiate.

As Brazil's viral hashtag demonstrated, Trump Always Chickens Out—even when he doesn't. Episode 10 practically writes itself: when Trump Always Chickens Out becomes a permanent management strategy rather than occasional retreat, the framework evolves from predicting accommodations to predicting accommodation cycles.

Yet as we document these patterns with analytical precision, we must acknowledge a sobering reality: when TACO psychology escalates to nuclear brinksmanship, the stakes transcend political analysis. The world now prays that any talk of repositioning nuclear submarines as strategy against Russia represents the ultimate TACO—that when it comes to nuclear escalation, Trump will indeed chicken out.

For once, accommodation would not be failure. It would be survival.
