The Rise and Fall of Marjorie Taylor Greene

The Rise and Fall of Marjorie Taylor Greene

Part 2: When MAGA Eats Its Own

November 22, 2025

A CanadianSnowmanSays Investigation

In January 2025, when Donald Trump returned to the White House for his second term, Marjorie Taylor Greene seemed to have everything she’d fought for. A Republican president who shared her contempt for establishment norms. A Republican House majority, however slim. Committee assignments with real oversight power. A national media platform. And most importantly, the president’s continued favor.

Ten months later, she was gone—driven out not by Democrats, not by scandal, not even by her own inflammatory rhetoric. She was driven out by Trump himself, in a public humiliation that demonstrated the iron law of MAGA politics: there is room for only one star, and that star brooks no dissent.

The story of how Greene went from Trump loyalist to “Marjorie ‘Traitor’ Greene” in less than a year reveals everything about the nature of the movement she helped build—and the limits of ideological purity when it conflicts with personal loyalty to Donald Trump.

The Honeymoon Period: January-June 2025

The early months of Trump’s second term looked like vindication for Greene and the MAGA hardliners. After years of Democratic control, Republicans finally had the trifecta: White House, Senate, and House. The agenda they’d promised—border security, spending cuts, America First foreign policy—could finally be enacted.

Greene threw herself into the work with characteristic energy. On the Oversight Committee, she launched investigations into Biden administration officials. She pushed for aggressive action on immigration. She sponsored bills to eliminate federal funding for transgender treatments and to investigate COVID-19 origins. She was, by all appearances, a loyal soldier in Trump’s second-term army.

But cracks were already forming, visible to anyone paying attention. Trump’s second term was revealing something that his first term had obscured: his ideological commitments were fungible. The man who’d campaigned as an isolationist was maintaining robust aid to Israel. The president who’d promised to “drain the swamp” was cutting deals with establishment Republicans on continuing resolutions. The leader who’d railed against Big Pharma was quietly preserving Obamacare subsidies.

For most MAGA Republicans, this was fine. Trump’s flexibility was a feature, not a bug—he was getting things done, working the system, making deals. But for the true believers, the ones who’d taken the “America First” rhetoric literally, it looked like betrayal.

Greene, it turned out, was a true believer.

The First Crack: Ukraine Aid

The break started over Ukraine. Throughout 2024, Greene had been one of the House’s most vocal opponents of continued military aid to Ukraine, calling it a “money laundering operation” and demanding that every dollar spent abroad be redirected to domestic priorities. It was a popular position with the MAGA base, who saw the Ukraine conflict as exactly the kind of endless foreign entanglement Trump had supposedly ended.

But Trump’s second-term position on Ukraine was more nuanced than his campaign rhetoric. He wanted to negotiate an end to the war, yes, but he also wanted to maintain America’s credibility as an ally and avoid the appearance of abandoning a democratic partner to Russian aggression. That meant continued military support, at least in the short term.

When a Ukraine aid package came up for a vote in early 2025—part of a larger continuing resolution to keep the government funded—Greene voted no. Loudly. She went on Bannon’s “War Room” and called it a betrayal of everything Trump had campaigned on. She filed amendments to strip the Ukraine funding. She recruited other Freedom Caucus members to join her opposition.

Trump was reportedly furious. He’d spent political capital negotiating the deal, and here was one of his most visible supporters undermining him publicly. But he didn’t break with her yet. The majority was too slim, and Greene’s fundraising prowess and media reach were too valuable.

So he tried a different approach: he called her. Repeatedly. According to later Trump statements, Greene became a near-constant presence on his phone, arguing with him about Ukraine, about spending, about why he was abandoning true conservative principles.

For Trump, who values loyalty above all else and expects deference from subordinates, this was intolerable. You could be wrong, but you had to be wrong quietly. Greene was being wrong at full volume.

The Epstein Files: Where Loyalty Met Conspiracy

If Ukraine was the policy break, the Jeffrey Epstein files were the personal one.

Throughout Trump’s first term and his 2024 campaign, Greene had been a vocal defender against any suggestion of Trump’s involvement with Epstein. When critics pointed to photos of Trump with Epstein, to Trump’s past comments about Epstein liking women “on the younger side,” to flight logs and party guest lists, Greene dismissed it all as fake news and Democratic smears.

But Greene had also built part of her brand on opposition to elite pedophile networks—the very conspiracy theory that had drawn her to QAnon in the first place. And she had an ally in this fight: Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY), a libertarian-leaning Republican with his own history of bucking party leadership.

In mid-2025, Greene and Massie introduced the “Epstein Files Transparency Act,” calling for the release of all remaining sealed documents related to the Epstein case. The bill was framed as a good-government transparency measure, but everyone understood the subtext: it would potentially expose powerful figures, including potentially damaging information about people in Trump’s orbit.

Trump’s response was swift and definitive: absolutely not. The files would remain sealed. The matter was closed. Move on.

For Greene, this created an impossible cognitive dissonance. She’d spent years defending Trump against Epstein allegations while simultaneously demanding exposure of elite pedophile networks. Now Trump himself was blocking the very transparency she’d demanded.

She could have quietly dropped it. She could have found a face-saving way to move on. Instead, she doubled down. She went on podcasts and explained that releasing the files was about justice, about the victims, about holding the powerful accountable—even if that made some people uncomfortable.

The message was clear: even Trump.

The Alliance with Massie

Trump had particular contempt for Thomas Massie, the Kentucky Republican who’d made a career of being the lone “no” vote on bills with broad bipartisan support. Massie voted against aid to Israel, against Ukraine funding, against emergency spending bills. He was principled to the point of stubbornness, and Trump viewed him as a grandstanding pest.

When Greene aligned herself with Massie on the Epstein files and started voting with him on spending bills, Trump saw betrayal. In his view, Massie was a loser—he’d lost Trump’s endorsement in previous cycles, he had no real power, and he was basically irrelevant to the Republican agenda. For Greene to choose Massie’s friendship over Trump’s favor was, to Trump, inexplicable and unforgivable.

The “never-ending barrage of phone calls” that Trump later referenced weren’t just about policy. They were Greene trying to explain herself, trying to maintain her relationship with Trump while sticking to her principles. They were the calls of someone who genuinely believed she could make Trump understand why she was right.

She was wrong.

The Other Breaks: Gaza, E-Verify, Tariffs

Ukraine and Epstein were the big breaks, but they weren’t the only ones. As Trump’s second term progressed, a pattern emerged: wherever Trump chose pragmatism over purity, Greene chose purity.

On Gaza and Israel policy, Trump maintained strong support for Israeli military operations. Greene, increasingly influenced by the anti-interventionist wing of MAGA, started questioning unlimited military aid and American involvement in Middle East conflicts.

On immigration, Trump negotiated a deal that included E-Verify mandates for employers but also provided a path for certain agricultural workers—a compromise with Republican business interests. Greene wanted E-Verify with no compromises, no special carve-outs, no exceptions.

On tariffs, Trump’s approach was tactical—threatening tariffs to extract concessions, then sometimes backing down. Greene wanted the full protectionist agenda, no negotiation, no flexibility.

Each issue alone was manageable. Together, they painted a picture of someone who’d mistaken Trump’s campaign rhetoric for governing doctrine. Trump had always been transactional; Greene had become ideological. And in Trumpworld, that made her dangerous.

November 14, 2025: The Break

On November 14, 2025, Donald Trump withdrew his endorsement of Marjorie Taylor Greene. The announcement came via Truth Social, naturally, and it was characteristically brutal:

“I am withdrawing my support and Endorsement of ‘Congresswoman’ Marjorie Taylor Greene,” Trump said in a post to social media, adding that Greene, who is running for reelection in 2026, has “gone Far Left.”

He added: “She has told many people that she is upset that I don’t return her phone calls anymore, but with 219 Congressmen/women, 53 U.S. Senators, 24 Cabinet Members, almost 200 Countries, and an otherwise normal life to lead, I can’t take a ranting Lunatic’s call every day.”

The nickname “Marjorie ‘Traitor’ Greene”—a play on “Marjorie Taylor Greene” and “traitor”—was Trump at his most cutting. He’d turned her own name into an insult.

For Greene, this wasn’t just a political problem. It was an existential crisis. Her entire political identity was built on being Trump’s most loyal defender. She’d stood by him through impeachments, indictments, and defeats. She’d defended him when other Republicans wavered. And now he was calling her a traitor and backing a primary challenger.

The internal polls confirmed what Trump already knew: his endorsement was political death in GA-14. Greene’s numbers were plummeting. Republican primary voters in her district didn’t care about her principled stands on Ukraine or Epstein files. They cared about Trump’s opinion. And Trump’s opinion was clear: Marjorie Taylor Greene had to go.

November 21, 2025: The Resignation

Greene had two choices: face a brutal primary battle she would almost certainly lose, or leave on her own terms. She chose the latter.

On November 21, 2025, she posted a 22-minute video and a four-page letter to X announcing her resignation effective January 5, 2026. The production values were high—this was clearly planned, not impulsive. And the message was pure MTG: defiant, aggrieved, and apocalyptic.

From peak influence to political exile: Greene at a 2024 Trump rally (left) versus announcing her resignation in November 2025 (right). The arc from ‘future Republican star’ to ‘traitor’ took less than a year.

The key passage from her letter captured her state of mind:

“I was elected to shake things up, not to go along to get along. I’ve been called every name in the book, survived two impeachment votes against me, faced death threats against me and my family, and been subjected to lawfare from people who claim to be on my side. I refuse to be a battered wife in an abusive relationship with a party that has lost its way. The Political Industrial Complex—the neocons, Big Pharma, Big Tech, the military-industrial complex, and the elite donor class—is ripping this country apart, and too many Republicans are willing participants.”

The “battered wife” metaphor was striking and deliberate. Greene was casting herself as a victim of abuse—not from Democrats or the media, but from her own party and, implicitly, from Trump himself.

She continued: “I’m returning to the people I love, to live life to the fullest, and to fight for America’s future in ways that don’t require me to compromise my principles or bow to those who value power over principle.”

It was resignation as martyrdom, exit as moral victory.

The Reactions: A Movement Divided

The responses to Greene’s resignation revealed the fault lines in the MAGA coalition.

Trump was gleeful, almost giddy. In an ABC News interview, he called her departure “great news for the country” and tied it to her “plummeting poll numbers” and fear of a primary challenge. But he also added, somewhat contradictorily, that he’d “always appreciate her early support.” It was Trump’s version of grace: backhanded and transactional.

Thomas Massie was elegiac and pointed: “MTG has spoken more honesty in her time in Congress than most politicians will speak in a lifetime. I’m sad for our country but happy for my friend. She deserves better than this place.”

The subtext was clear: Massie saw Greene as a fellow truth-teller in a city of liars, someone punished for refusing to bend.

Steve Bannon was defiant and forward-looking. On his “War Room” podcast, he predicted that Greene’s influence would only grow: “The House is not big enough for her ambitions or personality. MTG’s national platform just got bigger. She’s not going anywhere.”

Georgia GOP Chairman Josh McKoon called her a “tireless fighter” who’d “stood up for Georgia values,” a carefully worded statement that didn’t mention Trump at all.

Among House Republicans, reactions were muted. No one wanted to defend Greene too vigorously and risk Trump’s wrath, but no one wanted to kick her too hard either. Speaker Mike Johnson released a bland statement thanking her for her service.

The Democratic response was predictable: good riddance to a conspiracy theorist and chaos agent. But even Democrats acknowledged, privately, that Greene’s departure was a gift to Republicans—she’d been a walking attack ad, and her primary challenger would likely be less controversial and therefore harder to defeat in messaging terms

The Political Implications

Greene’s resignation has concrete consequences beyond the drama:

For the House majority: Republicans’ margin in the House shrinks from 219-213 back to its pre-special-election levels. A special election in GA-14 will be held, likely in March or April 2026. The district is solidly Republican (Trump won it by 50+ points), so the seat won’t flip. But the interim period creates headaches for Speaker Johnson on close votes.

For Trump: This is a test case for his second-term authority. He forced out a high-profile ally who’d become inconvenient. The message to other Republicans: don’t test me. But it also reveals vulnerability—Greene didn’t go quietly, and her martyrdom narrative will resonate with some of the MAGA base.

For the MAGA movement: The Greene-Trump split exposes the tension between ideological populism and personal authoritarianism. Greene believed in America First policies; Trump believes in Trump First loyalty. When those conflicted, loyalty won. But that victory has costs—every principled conservative who sides with Greene is a potential future problem.

For Greene personally: She’s 51 years old, wealthy, famous, and now has the freedom to build a media empire without the constraints of congressional service. Bannon’s prediction may prove correct—her platform could grow. Or she could fade, another cautionary tale about crossing Trump. The verdict is years away.

Conclusion: The Scorpion and the Frog

There’s a fable about a scorpion who asks a frog for a ride across a river. The frog refuses: “You’ll sting me.” The scorpion promises he won’t—if he stings the frog, they’ll both drown. The frog agrees. Halfway across, the scorpion stings the frog. As they both sink, the frog asks why. The scorpion replies: “It’s my nature.”

Marjorie Taylor Greene spent five years building a political identity around disruption, chaos, and refusal to compromise. She attacked establishment Republicans, embarrassed party leaders, and blew up legislative deals. She was the scorpion, and she was proud of it.

When she applied that same approach to Donald Trump—calling him out on Ukraine, demanding Epstein file releases, aligning with Trump’s enemies—she discovered that Trump is also a scorpion. And in a contest between two scorpions, the bigger one always wins.

The rise and fall of MTG is really the story of MAGA in miniature: a movement that rewards disruption until disruption threatens the leader, that champions principle until principle conflicts with power, that celebrates truth-tellers until they tell truths the leader doesn’t want heard.

Greene entered Congress as a QAnon conspiracist who believed in secret wars against pedophile cabals. She leaves as a principled populist betrayed by the very movement she helped build. Both narratives are true, and both are incomplete. The complete truth is simpler and sadder: she believed the rhetoric, and Trump never did.

On January 5, 2026, Marjorie Taylor Greene will leave Congress. Whether she becomes a powerful voice in conservative media, a footnote in political history, or something in between remains to be seen. But one thing is certain: she’ll do it at full volume, without apology, and while insisting she was right all along.

She probably was, on some things. But in Trump’s Republican Party, being right matters less than being loyal. And loyalty, it turns out, only flows one way.
