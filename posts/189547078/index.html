The Rot Within

Part XXIII: How the Islamic Republic Betrayed Itself

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

Illustration by The Atlantic. Source: Getty.

The CIA intelligence story, as reported by the New York Times, is being told as a technological triumph. Months of tracking. High-fidelity information on Khamenei’s location. The decision to shift from a planned nighttime strike to a daylight attack to exploit a specific window — a leadership meeting, a room full of targets, a compound in the heart of Tehran at 9:40 AM on a Saturday morning.

All of that is true. But Graeme Wood, writing for The Atlantic on Sunday, offers the interpretation that cuts deeper: the penetration of Iran’s highest command was not only technological. Someone told. And the reason someone told is that the state Khamenei built — the surveillance apparatus, the loyalty networks, the IRGC’s parallel empire — deserved to be betrayed. It had been betraying its own people, including its own true believers, for decades. Eventually the betrayal ran the other direction.

“The regime he represented deserved betrayal,” Wood writes. That sentence is the analytical key to everything that follows.

Graeme Wood, The Atlantic staff writer, whose March 1, 2026 essay “Why Khamenei Is Dead” frames the regime’s internal betrayal as deserved rot—highlighting how true believers like Jaber Rajabi were punished while opportunists thrived (source: Lavin Agency profile photo).

The Dossier Under the Leg

Jaber Rajabi in a video interview, the true believer who fought for Iranian-backed militias in Iraq and later exposed corruption directly to Khamenei—tucked under the leader’s leg and ignored, leading to Rajabi’s betrayal of the regime he once supported (source: Graeme Wood’s March 1, 2026 Atlantic essay “Why Khamenei Is Dead”).

Wood’s source is Jaber Rajabi — a true believer who fought with Iranian-backed militias in Iraq because he genuinely favored the establishment of a Shia theocracy modeled on Iran’s. He was not a dissident. He was not a Western plant. He was the real thing: a man who believed in the project and wanted it to succeed.

What he found in Iraq was not the project he believed in. Iran wanted Iraq to remain weak and submissive, not to become a genuine Shia theocracy. Corrupt Iranian officials were extracting its resources, profiting personally, and leaving the country an impoverished wreck. Rajabi — naive, Wood notes, in ways that distinguish true believers from the career opportunists — decided to do what a true believer would do. He took his evidence directly to the supreme leader.

He had a connection: he knew Mojtaba Khamenei, the supreme leader’s son, from religious study in Qom. He used it to put himself in the receiving line after evening prayers at the compound. He brought a dossier. He was shaking when he handed it over.

Khamenei addressed him by the wrong name. He scanned the executive summary. Then — and this is the detail Wood emphasizes — he did not hand it to an assistant as he normally would. He kept it. He tucked it under his leg.

That was the whole interaction.

Within weeks, Rajabi learned that Qassim Suleimani had heard about the document and wanted him dead. Over the following years, Rajabi survived multiple assassination attempts, including a poisoning that nearly succeeded.

This is what the Islamic Republic did with a loyal man who brought it evidence of its own corruption. It tried to kill him for the trouble.

The Logic of Authoritarian Rot

Wood draws out the structural implication with precision. In an authoritarian system, admitting that corruption exists is an admission of incompetence — because the leader is responsible for everything. There is also a permanent incentive to promote incompetents, because competent people eventually get competent at removing you. The system therefore selects, systematically and relentlessly, for people who will not threaten the leader — which means people who are mediocre, corruptible, and loyal only to their own enrichment and survival.

“No one familiar with the Islamic Republic will be surprised,” Wood writes, “that the whole system was riddled with people who believed in nothing but their own enrichment and survival, and who achieved their exalted positions through mediocrity.”

Karim Sadjadpour’s framework — which this series has applied throughout — arrives at the same place from a different direction. The regime that began as 80% true believers and 20% opportunists inverted over four decades: 20% believers, 80% opportunists. Wood’s Rajabi story is a case study in the mechanism of that inversion. True believers who tried to fix the rot were destroyed. Opportunists who profited from it were promoted. After enough cycles of that selection process, the institution you are left with is not an ideological movement. It is a criminal enterprise wearing ideological clothing.

A criminal enterprise can be very durable — the IRGC’s economic empire, its construction holdings, its telecommunications networks, its smuggling operations, are evidence of that. But a criminal enterprise has a different relationship to loyalty than an ideological movement does. Its members stay because the money is good and the alternatives are worse. When the money starts burning — when American strikes target the economic assets systematically, building by building — the calculus changes.

And when the man at the top of the enterprise is dead, the question of who gets what is no longer settled.

What the Intelligence Penetration Actually Means

The CIA tracked Khamenei for months. Israel knew where everyone was. The attack on the compound killed Khamenei in his office, senior officials in the nearby building, and — if the New York Times graphic is confirmed — Mojtaba Khamenei as well.

That level of penetration does not happen through satellite imagery alone. Wood is right: someone was talking. Whether it was a device in a hearing aid, a tracker on a vehicle, a human asset in the compound, or some combination of all three, the intelligence picture that allowed the US and Israel to shift an entire operation from nighttime to daylight to catch a specific meeting — that picture required human cooperation at some level inside the Iranian system.

The Rajabi story tells us why that cooperation was available. The Islamic Republic spent four decades building a system that punished loyalty and rewarded corruption. At some point, the people inside that system began making the same calculation that Sadjadpour’s 80% opportunists are making now in the bunkers: what is the regime actually worth to me, weighed against what I could gain by cooperating with its enemies?

The answer, for someone inside the compound’s security apparatus — someone who knew the meeting schedules, the locations, the faces in the rooms — was apparently: not enough.

This is the rot within. Not a single traitor, not a single device, not a single intelligence coup. A system so comprehensively corrupted that its own participants were available to be turned. Khamenei built it. He tucked the evidence of it under his leg in 1989 or 1990 or whenever Rajabi handed him the dossier. He spent the next three decades promoting the people who would not challenge him and destroying the people who would.

In the end, the people he promoted sold him to his enemies.

What This Means for the Succession

The rot-within analysis matters for what comes next because it reframes the succession question.

Part XX asked whether the IRGC is coherent enough for someone to step forward. The answer was: yes, but complicated. The complication is now clearer. An institution built on the selection of opportunists and the destruction of true believers is not an institution capable of unified ideological resistance. It is an institution full of people making individual calculations.

Some of those calculations will favor fighting. Some will favor dealing. The man or faction that can offer the most credible guarantee of personal safety and preservation of assets — whether that offer comes from Larijani’s interim council, from an external power, or from the Americans — has a significant advantage in the auction now underway inside Iran’s command structure.

This is both the hopeful and the terrifying version of the situation simultaneously.

Hopeful: an institution of 80% opportunists is an institution that can be negotiated with. The true believers — the 20% — are the obstacle to any deal. If the strikes have eliminated the true believer leadership (Khamenei, Mojtaba, the hardline IRGC commanders), the opportunists may be willing to deal faster than anyone expects.

Terrifying: an institution of 80% opportunists with access to enriched uranium, ballistic missiles, and a proxy network across the region is an institution without a coherent center. There is no single person whose signature ends the war. There is no single command whose order stops the launches. The opportunists are not a unified bloc — they are competing factions, each calculating independently, each with different assets and different enemies and different visions of what the best deal looks like for them personally.

Dana Stroul’s “complete chaos” scenario is not a failure of the war to achieve its objectives. It is what happens when the war achieves a partial objective — the decapitation of the clerical leadership — and the institution left behind is too fragmented and too corrupt to produce a coherent successor.

The Irony Wood Names

Wood’s closing observation deserves to be in the record: “He was a talented leader in other ways. He looked at first like a non-entity, a caretaker to stand in for his much more charismatic and religiously accomplished predecessor. Instead he outlasted almost every dictator of his generation, and he created a network of proxies so ferocious that no enemy of Iran dared disturb it, until Israel decided it had no choice.”

Khamenei survived because he was good at survival. He neutralized rivals, rotated commanders, prevented anyone from accumulating enough power to threaten him. He built a system designed to protect him from the people around him.

The system worked. Right up until it didn’t — because the same mechanisms that protected him from his subordinates also ensured that his subordinates had no particular reason to protect him. When the moment came, the men he had cultivated through four decades of careful management made their own calculations.

Some of them, apparently, calculated that the Americans were going to win.

The Islamic Republic was not destroyed from outside. It was hollowed out from within, over decades, by its own logic. The bombs on Saturday found a structure that was already largely empty at its core. What they destroyed was the shell.

What fills the space the shell occupied is the question this series will continue to document.
