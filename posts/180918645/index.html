Trump's Foreign Policy

From Strategic Blueprint to Moscow Implementation

Part 14: When Doctrine Explains Accommodation and Allies Confront Betrayal

December 6, 2025

December 6, 2025

The Framework Applied

Part 13 examined what Trump’s National Security Strategy says about Europe, Russia, and democratic allies. This installment analyzes what the NSS means: how strategic doctrine translates into the Moscow accommodation documented in Part 12, why expert assessments range from alarmed to devastating, and what this framework signals to allies—including Canada—about America’s strategic priorities.

The question isn’t whether the NSS represents a major shift in American foreign policy. Every serious analyst agrees it does. The question is whether this shift represents coherent strategy or systematic betrayal dressed in strategic language.

The evidence suggests the latter.

Connecting Strategy to Moscow

Now the five-hour Moscow meeting makes perfect strategic sense.

Witkoff and Kushner weren’t there to negotiate. They were there to receive Russian feedback on American surrender terms, because the NSS framework treats Russia as a reasonable partner whose concerns about NATO expansion are legitimate, while treating European support for Ukrainian sovereignty as “unrealistic expectations” driven by antidemocratic governance.

The pattern I documented in Part 12—Europe excluded from negotiations about European security, Secretary of State Marco Rubio skipping NATO meeting rather than defending the indefensible, Putin threatening nuclear escalation against NATO allies, American envoy coaching Russian officials on Trump management—wasn’t operational chaos. It was strategic implementation.

The NSS explains why Trump’s team spent five hours hearing out Putin’s objections without a single European representative present: because the strategic framework treats Europe as the problem, not the partner.

The details tell the story:

Europe excluded from negotiations → NSS calls for “cultivating resistance to Europe’s current trajectory within European nations”

Rubio skipping NATO meeting → NSS questions whether European countries will remain “reliable allies”

Putin threatening European nuclear annihilation → NSS casts America as mediator between Russia and European states, not as NATO ally

American envoy coaching Russian officials → NSS prioritizes “reestablishing strategic stability with Russia” over alliance solidarity

Territorial concessions without military restrictions → NSS goals are Ukraine’s “survival as a viable state” and “expeditious cessation of hostilities,” not sovereignty or justice

Peace plan Russians view as “really hearing” their position → NSS seeks to “mitigate the risk of conflict between Russia and European states” by accommodating Russian security demands

My September TACO analysis concluded: “Trump’s foreign policy is not strategy. It’s psychology. It’s not doctrine. It’s disorder.” I was half right. The psychology is doctrine now. The disorder is strategic.

The Vance Connection: From Munich to NSS

The NSS formalizes themes Vice President JD Vance introduced at the Munich Security Conference in February 2025. CNN reported that Vance told European leaders that the biggest threat to their security was “from within,” rather than from China or Russia. “What I worry about is the threat from within, the retreat of Europe from some of its most fundamental values,” Vance told a stone-faced audience at the Munich Security Conference. CNN

At the time, Vance’s comments could be dismissed as freelancing by an ideological vice president. The NSS makes clear they represented administration policy. CFR analysis noted: “Vance’s speech in Munich could have been interpreted back in February as the ideological views of just the vice president and parts of the radical MAGA base. But now, these views have become the administration’s official policy, which will only accelerate Europe’s efforts to hedge against the United States and to build up its own autonomy.” Council on Foreign Relations

The evolution from Vance’s provocative speech to formal NSS doctrine reveals systematic planning, not impulsive rhetoric. The administration spent ten months translating Munich’s ideological attack into Washington’s strategic framework.

CFR’s analysis continued: “This framework accuses Europe of censorship and suppressing political opposition, expanding on Vice President JD Vance’s much-publicized criticism at the 2025 Munich Security Conference. It characterizes the European Union in adversarial terms, claiming the body has undermined ‘political liberty’ and ‘sovereignty.’” Council on Foreign Relations

Most alarmingly: “Particularly worrisome for Europeans is the endorsement of far-right (’patriotic’) parties and the stated goal to ‘cultivate resistance... within European nations.’ This will be perceived by European allies as an unacceptable attempt to meddle in their internal affairs.” Council on Foreign Relations

The CFR assessment concluded with stark clarity: “If the test for a good European ally is the extent to which it aligns with the nationalist conservative values of the Trump administration, as this NSS suggests, this does not bode well for the future of the transatlantic relationship. Not only does this new strategy mark the end of the transatlantic alliance based on liberal values, but also a reorientation towards an alliance of illiberals.” Council on Foreign Relations

An alliance of illiberals. That’s not hyperbole—it’s the documented policy preference revealed through comparing how the NSS treats democratic versus authoritarian allies.

Expert Assessments: Strategic Shift or Strategic Disaster?

Foreign policy experts across the ideological spectrum recognized the document’s significance, even as they differed on its wisdom.

The Council on Foreign Relations convened seven experts whose analyses ranged from cautious to alarmed. CFR’s Rebecca Lissner noted: “The north star of great power competition with China and Russia—around which the first Trump administration built bipartisan consensus—is gone. Rather than describing the scope and scale of China’s systemic challenge to the United States and our allies and partners, the 2025 NSS makes clear that economics are ‘the ultimate stakes.’ The new paramount objective of Washington’s China policy is a ‘mutually advantageous’ economic relationship.” Council on Foreign Relations

She continued: “That strategic clarity is entirely missing from the new NSS, which is more polemic than policy... And far from the grave tone struck by all its predecessors, this NSS veers at times into screed.” Council on Foreign Relations

CSIS called the document “a real, painful, shocking wake-up call for Europe” and “a moment of cavernous divergence between Europe’s view of itself and Trump’s vision for Europe. If Europe had any doubt that the Trump administration is fully committed to a tough love strategy, it now knows for sure.” Center for Strategic and International Studies

Democratic Representative Jason Crow, who sits on House committees overseeing intelligence and the armed forces, called the strategy “catastrophic to America’s standing in the world and a retreat from our alliances and partnerships.” “The world will be a more dangerous place and Americans will be less safe if this plan moves forward.” NPR

Foreign Policy’s analysis was even more stark, describing the implications for European security: “Rather than old-fashioned superpower competition, Trump is keen to pursue imperial collusion with both Russia and China. The rest of the world, including Europe, is on the colonial menu. Strategically, this has a certain short-term logic. Ideologically, it aligns with support for far-right parties and governments in Europe and beyond. Not only do these forces share the nationalist and socially conservative views championed by MAGA, they are also working to divide Europe and hollow out the European integration project.” Foreign Policy

The analysis continued with devastating clarity: “It is long past time for Europe to realize that, when it comes to the Russia-Ukraine war and the continent’s security, it is, at best, alone. At worst, it now faces two adversaries: Russia in the east and Trump’s United States in the west.” Foreign Policy

Two adversaries. That assessment—from a mainstream foreign policy publication, not partisan commentary—represents how fundamentally the NSS has transformed American-European relations. Allies who thought they shared strategic interests now confront the possibility that Washington views European security as negotiable in pursuit of “strategic stability” with Moscow.

The TACO Pattern Codified

My September analysis identified the TACO pattern: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. The NSS doesn’t break this pattern—it institutionalizes it as strategic doctrine.

Threaten: The NSS threatens Europe with strategic abandonment unless it “corrects its trajectory” toward MAGA-aligned politics. The document questions whether European countries will remain “reliable allies” and warns they may be “unrecognizable in 20 years or less.”

Accommodate: The NSS seeks to “reestablish strategic stability with Russia” and prevent NATO expansion—accommodating Russia’s core demands while dismissing European security concerns as “unrealistic expectations.”

Capitulate: Ukraine’s “survival as a viable state” (not sovereignty, not territorial integrity) becomes the goal, while “expeditious cessation of hostilities” takes priority over just peace terms. The document prioritizes stabilizing European economies and preventing escalation—goals that require Ukrainian territorial concessions.

Obfuscate: The strategy uses euphemisms like “burden-shifting” and “flexible realism” to obscure abandonment as adaptation. It frames mediation between “Russia and European states” as if America isn’t a NATO member with treaty obligations.

But there’s a crucial evolution: TACO is no longer just presidential psychology. It’s now official American strategy, documented in the administration’s foundational foreign policy text.

The pattern that seemed like personality-driven disorder in September is revealed as ideologically coherent doctrine in December: democratic allies are problems requiring internal political “correction,” while authoritarian accommodation represents strategic wisdom.

The Canadian Perspective: Continental Implications

From a Canadian perspective, this NSS is genuinely alarming—not because it mentions Canada (it barely does, lumping us with Europe and Asia in trade complaints), but because it reveals the worldview of the superpower with whom we share a continent and civilization.

The NSS elevates the Western Hemisphere through what it calls the “Trump Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine” The White House, declaring: “The United States must be preeminent in the Western Hemisphere as a condition of our security and prosperity.” The White House

This means “a readjustment of our global military presence to address urgent threats in our Hemisphere, especially the missions identified in this strategy, and away from theaters whose relative import to American national security has declined in recent decades” CNN—meaning away from Europe, toward our shared hemisphere.

It includes “targeted deployments to secure the border and defeat cartels, including where necessary the use of lethal force” CNN—military operations in our hemisphere justified by domestic security concerns. The administration has already conducted deadly boat strikes in the Caribbean, with CNN reporting the administration “has pursued a deadly campaign against alleged drug boats in international waters, so far, destroying at least 23 boats and killing 87 people. Outside legal experts and some members of Congress have questioned the legality of the effort.” CNN

Canada shares more than a border with the United States. We share democratic values, commitment to rule of law, support for international institutions, and belief in alliance solidarity. The NSS treats all these as either irrelevant or actively problematic.

If Trump’s administration views European democracies as civilizationally doomed and strategically unreliable, what prevents similar assessment of Canada? We have immigration policies Trump opposes (and which the NSS links to “civilizational erasure”), support for Ukraine he considers “unrealistic,” and multilateral commitments he despises as “sovereignty-sapping.”

The NSS doesn’t offer reassurance that shared democratic values or geographic proximity protect allies from ideological condemnation. It suggests the opposite: democratic allies who question accommodation of authoritarians become targets for internal political interference, while authoritarian regimes that don’t challenge accommodation become partners deserving respect for their “traditions.”

The document’s treatment of European democracies versus Gulf monarchies establishes the precedent: Trump’s America prefers cooperative authoritarians to questioning democrats.

Canada watched European allies get excluded from Moscow negotiations about European security. We watched the NSS question whether racially diverse NATO members will remain aligned with American values. We watched democracy promotion get abandoned as an American objective while “cultivating resistance” in allied democracies became official policy.

The message for Canada is clear: shared values don’t guarantee partnership when those values include supporting Ukrainian sovereignty, maintaining immigration policies Trump opposes, or questioning American accommodation of authoritarian regimes.

Conclusion: Blueprint for Betrayal

The December 5th National Security Strategy provides the strategic architecture that explains Trump’s foreign policy behavior. The five-hour Moscow meeting wasn’t aberration—it was application. Europe’s exclusion wasn’t oversight—it was policy. Russian accommodation wasn’t weakness—it was preference.

The document achieves something remarkable: it makes alignment with Russian strategic objectives while attacking democratic allies seem like coherent strategy rather than incoherent capitulation.

But coherence doesn’t equal wisdom. Making betrayal systematic doesn’t make it strategic.

The NSS establishes that:

  • Democratic allies face civilizational condemnation while authoritarian regimes get respectful partnership

  • Russia’s security concerns are legitimate while Europe’s are “unrealistic”

  • American interference in allied democracies’ internal politics is official policy

  • Democracy promotion is abandoned as an American objective

  • NATO expansion must end—granting Russia effective veto power

  • Ukraine’s “survival as a viable state” is the goal, not sovereignty or justice

  • Alliance solidarity matters less than ideological alignment with MAGA preferences

These aren’t adjustments to American strategy. They’re demolition of American strategy, replaced with accommodation of authoritarian powers and abandonment of democratic allies.

Aaron Parnas asked the essential question: “If the United States no longer sees itself as the anchor of global order, the question becomes unavoidable. Who, or what, fills the vacuum? And what happens to the alliances that have preserved peace and prosperity for generations?”

The answer emerging from Trump’s NSS is clear: Russia and China fill the vacuum in their respective regions, “patriotic” (far-right) parties fill the vacuum in Europe’s internal politics, and authoritarian governance models fill the vacuum left by abandoned democratic promotion.

This isn’t “America First.” It’s America alone—isolated from democratic allies, aligned with authoritarian preferences, implementing a strategic framework that treats the free world as the problem Putin and Xi are helping solve.

My September analysis concluded that Trump’s foreign policy was psychology masquerading as strategy. The NSS proves I underestimated the danger: the psychology became the strategy. Accommodation isn’t a bug in Trump’s approach—it’s the entire operating system, now formally documented as American strategic doctrine.

And Europe is taking notes. So is Canada. So is every democratic ally wondering if shared values and decades of partnership matter less than demographic composition and ideological alignment with MAGA preferences.

The NSS answers that question definitively: they don’t.

From a Canadian perspective, watching America’s foreign policy deteriorate from strategic doctrine to ideological warfare against democratic allies is genuinely alarming. We share a continent with a superpower whose foundational strategy document questions whether racially diverse allies can be trusted, endorses interference in allied democracies’ internal politics, abandons democracy promotion as an objective, and prioritizes accommodation of authoritarian powers over alliance solidarity.

The pattern never broke. It evolved. And it’s now official United States policy, documented in the strategic framework that will guide American foreign policy for years to come.

Unless democratic allies—in Europe, in Canada, globally—recognize that Trump’s America views them as problems requiring correction rather than partners deserving respect, they’ll continue misunderstanding accommodation as strategy and betrayal as diplomacy.

The NSS should eliminate any such confusion. It’s not a strategy document. It’s a declaration of ideological war against the democratic alliance system America spent seventy-five years building.

And Moscow is taking notes too—because the NSS essentially grants Russia what it’s sought since the Soviet collapse: American acceptance that Europe is neither Russia’s adversary nor America’s primary strategic concern, but rather a “civilizationally” failing region whose objections to Russian aggression represent “unrealistic expectations” that American mediation should overcome.

That’s not strategy. That’s surrender with a table of contents.
