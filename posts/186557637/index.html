The Asimov Gap

The Warfare Wildcard

Part 9: Autonomous Weapons and Democracy

February 3, 2026

The Video That Should Terrify You

Libya, March 2020. A Turkish-made Kargu-2 drone... was widely reported as potentially the first fully autonomous lethal attack in warfare. According to a UN report, retreating forces were 'hunted down and remotely engaged' by drones operating in autonomous mode. While the manufacturer disputes full autonomy in this instance and details remain contested, the incident represents a threshold being crossed.

Once, warriors decided who lived and died.
Now algorithms do—and the line was crossed in silence

This is the line humanity just crossed.

For all of human history, humans decided when to kill. Warriors, soldiers, commanders—humans made the choice to take life.

Now we’ve delegated that decision to algorithms.

And we did it quietly, without much public deliberation, without democratic debate about whether this is acceptable.

Five tech companies are racing to build AGI. Militaries worldwide are racing to weaponize AI. These races are connected and reinforcing.

Sanders’ warning about oligarchic AI control becomes exponentially more dangerous when you add weapons to the equation.

Because democratic oversight of weapons has always been essential. Autonomous weapons threaten to make that oversight impossible.

What Autonomous Weapons Actually Are

Definitions matter. Let’s be precise:

Remote weapons (drones like Predator):

  • Human pilot controls from distance

  • Human sees what weapon sees

  • Human decides when to fire

  • Human responsible for kill decision

Semi-autonomous weapons (Aegis defense system):

  • AI detects targets

  • AI recommends action

  • Human approves or rejects

  • Human in the loop

Autonomous weapons (Kargu-2, future systems):

  • AI detects targets

  • AI decides to attack

  • AI executes strike

  • No human decision required

The shift from semi-autonomous to fully autonomous is profound: When AI makes kill decision, who is responsible? Can there be accountability? Can there be proportionality judgment? Can there be mercy?

More importantly: Can there be democratic control?

Why Autonomous Weapons Change War Itself

Every previous weapon, no matter how powerful, had political constraints:

Nuclear weapons: Powerful enough to destroy civilization. But humans decide whether to launch. Political pressure prevents use: fear of retaliation, moral horror, domestic opposition.

Conventional military: Can invade other countries. But soldiers die. Political pressure constrains aggression: grieving parents, body bags, anti-war movements.

Drones: Can strike anywhere. But humans pilot them. Pilots can refuse orders. Accountability remains: war crimes can be prosecuted, responsibility is clear.

Autonomous weapons eliminate the main political constraint on warfare: casualties.

At Georgetown, Hinton referenced a Vietnam War protest song—Country Joe and the Fish’s “Be the first one on your block to have your boy come home in a box.”

He explained: “If you ask why don’t rich countries invade poor countries all the time? Because they don’t want their boys coming home in a box. That’s the main political pressure against doing it.”

Autonomous weapons remove that constraint.

No grieving parents demanding explanations. No body bags on the news. No anti-war protests because no one’s children are dying. No political cost to military adventurism.

Just expensive robots and dead people in poor countries.

Hinton continued: “If you have an army of drones or humanoid robots, then rich countries can invade poor countries and the poor countries’ people may die, but the rich countries’ people won’t die.”

Of course that wouldn’t happen if you had liberal democratic rich countries. But as soon as you have authoritarian countries, they’re going to invade other countries if they can get away with it.

Putin would be delighted if there were just dead Russian robots instead of dead Russians. No domestic opposition. No mothers demanding their sons return. No political pressure to end the war.

And in the United States? “The military-industrial complex would think that was great because there wouldn’t be any political blowback and the robots would be expensive.”

Counterarguments and Rebuttals

Proponents argue autonomous weapons could reduce civilian casualties through superior precision, eliminate human errors driven by fear or anger, and provide deterrence against adversaries. These aren’t frivolous points—AI systems can process battlefield data faster and potentially with less bias than stressed humans.

But these arguments miss the fundamental problem: precision without accountability enables abuse, not restraint. Speed without human judgment creates accidental war risk, not safety. And deterrence through autonomous systems creates a hair-trigger world where machines respond to machines faster than humans can intervene. The technology may be precise, but precision without democratic control is just efficient authoritarianism.

Think about the implications:

Without casualties:

  • Wars become easier to start

  • Harder to end (no domestic pressure)

  • More frequent (lower political cost)

  • Less restrained (no concern for own soldiers’ safety)

Hinton’s stark conclusion: “It will remove one of the main barriers to rich powerful countries just invading little countries like Grenada.”

This isn’t about whether autonomous weapons work technically. It’s about what they enable politically: warfare without political constraint.

And here's the terrifying part: This is already happening. The Kargu-2 drone deployed in Libya, 2020. Autonomous target identification. Autonomous attack decision. No human in the loop.

The line has been crossed. The only question is whether democratic institutions can reassert control before autonomous warfare becomes normalized.

How Autonomous Weapons Threaten Democracy

Autonomous weapons create unique democratic dangers through speed, scale, and diffused accountability:

Speed eliminates oversight

AI makes decisions in milliseconds. No time for human review. Attack happens before democratic institutions can respond. War can start and end before public knows it’s happening.

Military Drone Swarm

Scale overwhelms accountability

One AI can control thousands of weapons simultaneously. Swarms coordinating attacks. Too many decisions happening too fast for human responsibility. Who is accountable when algorithms make 10,000 kill decisions per hour?

Arms race dynamics eliminate choice

If one side deploys autonomous weapons, others must respond with autonomous weapons (speed advantage). Creates race toward faster, more autonomous systems. Even if you want human control, competitive pressure pushes toward autonomy.

Domestic use against citizens

Currently, militaries/police face constraints:

  • Soldiers/officers can refuse illegal orders

  • Humans reluctant to fire on civilians

  • Moral weight of killing creates hesitation

  • Human conscience is brake on state violence

Autonomous weapons eliminate this:

  • Algorithms don’t refuse orders

  • AI doesn’t hesitate to fire on protesters

  • No moral judgment in code

  • Perfect obedience to authority

Historical context: Every authoritarian regime faces the problem that soldiers might refuse to shoot their own people. Autonomous weapons solve this problem for authoritarians.

Asymmetric power

Government with autonomous weapons has overwhelming force advantage over citizens:

  • Protesters can’t resist drone swarms

  • Insurrection becomes technically impossible

  • Armed citizenry irrelevant (can’t shoot down autonomous drone swarms)

  • Second Amendment obsolete in face of autonomous weapons

Speed eliminates democratic deliberation

War historically allowed time for democratic debate:

  • Mobilization takes time

  • Deployment takes time

  • Public can debate whether war is justified

  • Democracy can constrain military action

Autonomous weapons eliminate this:

  • Engagement happens in seconds

  • By time public knows, war already started

  • Democratic oversight becomes impossible

  • Military/executive branch acts without democratic constraint

Covert action becomes easier

Deniable autonomous weapons:

  • No soldiers to capture

  • No bodies to repatriate

  • No witnesses to testify

  • Plausible deniability for aggression

Makes secret wars easier. Makes accountability harder. Democracy requires transparency. Autonomous weapons enable opacity.

Pattern: Autonomous weapons shift power from democratic institutions (legislatures, courts, citizens) to executives (military leaders, intelligence agencies, authoritarian rulers).

The International Law Problem

Laws of war require human moral judgment. AI can’t provide it.

Geneva Conventions require distinguishing combatants from civilians, proportionality in attacks, military necessity, and humanity (mercy when possible).

These aren’t algorithmic decisions. They’re moral judgments requiring context, empathy, and understanding.

Of course, we could simply rely on human moral judgment in warfare—except recent history suggests that's no guarantee. When leadership takes strategic counsel from advisors like Stephen Miller, Project 2025 architect Russell Vought, and autocrats like Vladimir Putin rather than from established ethical and legal frameworks, the 'human in the loop' becomes meaningless. If AI lacks moral judgment, what do we make of leaders who demonstrate none while commanding the world's most powerful military?

Example: Is an armed civilian defending their home a combatant? Is destroying a building worth the civilian deaths? Should you show mercy to a surrendering or wounded enemy?

AI can be trained on rules. But rules are imperfect specifications of moral principles. Novel situations arise. Context matters. Moral judgment requires understanding, not just pattern matching.

And when AI violates laws of war: Who is responsible? Programmer? Commander? Manufacturer?

Accountability requires human in decision loop. Autonomous weapons eliminate that accountability.

Currently, international law assumes humans make kill decisions. Autonomous weapons break this assumption. New laws needed—but development is racing ahead of regulation.

The Arms Race Nobody Voted For

At least 30 countries are developing military AI. Major players:

United States: Investing billions. Already deploying semi-autonomous systems. Moving toward full autonomy.

China: Matching US investment. Fewer ethical constraints. Aggressive development timeline.

Russia: Developing autonomous weapons. Fewer resources but motivated by military competition.

Israel: Pioneer in autonomous systems. Combat-tested drones with increasing autonomy.

Turkey: Kargu-2 proves they’ve deployed. Exporting to other countries.

Pattern: Arms race is happening. No international agreement constraining it. No democratic debate about whether this is wise.

This is happening without democratic input:

Decision-makers:

  • Military leaders

  • Defense contractors

  • Intelligence agencies

  • Small group of politicians (usually in secret)

Not involved:

  • Public

  • Most elected representatives

  • International community

  • Democratic institutions

Classic security dilemma: Each country fears others developing autonomous weapons. So each develops them. Result: Everyone has them, everyone less secure.

Historical parallel: Nuclear arms race. US and USSR raced to build more bombs. Both ended up with enough to destroy world many times over. Mutually Assured Destruction.

Difference with autonomous weapons: Not just about how many. About how fast and autonomous. Speed and autonomy create instability.

Because: If AI makes decisions, and AI is fast, and both sides have autonomous weapons, war could start and escalate before humans can intervene.

This is exactly what Sanders warns about with AI generally: Oligarchic decision-making about civilizational questions.

With weapons, stakes are even higher: Oligarchic decision-making about who lives and dies.

The Accidental War Scenario

WarGames (1983) depicted the nightmare scenario: Military AI can't distinguish simulation from reality. Released the same year Stanislav Petrov saved the world by not trusting Soviet AI that falsely detected US missile launches. Fiction became near-reality within months.

How autonomous weapons create catastrophic risk:

Setup: Two countries both have autonomous weapons. Relations are tense.

Incident: AI system misidentifies target. Autonomous weapon attacks. Other side’s AI responds automatically (defending against attack).

Escalation: Both sides’ AIs responding to each other. Humans try to intervene but decisions happening too fast.

Result: War neither side wanted, started by algorithms, escalating automatically.

This is not hypothetical:

1983: Soviet early warning system detected US missile launch. AI indicated high confidence. Officer on duty (Stanislav Petrov) decided it was false alarm. Prevented nuclear war by refusing to trust AI.

With fully autonomous weapons: No human in loop. AI detects threat. AI responds. War starts.

Pattern: Autonomous weapons create “hair trigger” dynamics. Accidental war becomes more likely. And happens too fast for democratic institutions to stop.

What Democratic Governance of Military AI Looks Like

Not “ban all military AI”—that’s neither feasible nor wise.

But: Democratic constraints on how military AI is used:

1. Ban Fully Autonomous Weapons

Require meaningful human control: AI can assist, detect, recommend. But human must make kill decision.

Why: Preserves accountability. Ensures proportionality. Allows mercy. Maintains democratic oversight.

Model: International treaty banning fully autonomous weapons, like Chemical Weapons Convention bans chemical weapons.

2. International Verification

Inspections regime: Like nuclear non-proliferation. Verify compliance with autonomous weapons ban.

Why: Arms control only works with verification. Otherwise defection inevitable.

3. Transparency Requirements

Public disclosure of military AI capabilities (within security constraints).

Why: Democracy requires knowing what weapons exist. Secret weapons avoid democratic oversight.

4. Congressional Authorization

Military AI development requires congressional approval, not just executive authority.

Why: War powers belong to legislature. Autonomous weapons shift power to executive. Requiring authorization restores balance.

5. Independent Oversight

Civilian board reviewing military AI before deployment.

Why: Military culture prioritizes effectiveness. Independent oversight balances with legality, ethics, democratic values.

6. Operator Accountability

Clear responsibility when autonomous weapon causes harm. Can’t hide behind “algorithm decided.”

Why: Accountability requires identifiable responsible party. Prevents war crimes becoming algorithmic.

7. Prohibition on Domestic Use

Military autonomous weapons cannot be used against domestic population.

Why: Prevents authoritarian use of AI weapons against citizens.

None of this prevents legitimate military AI use. All of it prevents anti-democratic deployment.

The Campaign to Stop Killer Robots

International coalition working on this:

Campaign to Stop Killer Robots: Coalition of NGOs, activists, researchers advocating for treaty banning autonomous weapons.

Their proposal: “Meaningful human control” requirement. Humans must make all kill decisions.

Support:

  • 30+ countries support ban

  • UN discussions ongoing

  • Many AI researchers signed petition

  • Some military leaders support

Opposition:

  • Major military powers (US, Russia, China, Israel) resist treaty

  • Defense contractors (profit from autonomous weapons)

  • Some military leaders (fear losing advantage)

Pattern: Same dynamic as other arms control efforts. Moral case clear. Strategic concerns create resistance. Democratic pressure needed to overcome resistance.

This is exactly where Sanders-style organizing matters: Public pressure on governments to support treaty. Democratic choice to constrain military AI.

Why This Connects to Sanders’ Broader Warning

Military AI isn’t separate from civilian AI—same companies, same technology, same oligarchs:

Google: Worked on military AI (Project Maven). Employees protested. Google eventually stopped. But technology developed for civilian AI still applicable to military.

Palantir (Peter Thiel): Sells AI to military and intelligence agencies. Same AI used for targeting as for commercial analytics.

Amazon: Sells cloud computing to Pentagon. AI infrastructure powers both commercial and military applications.

Microsoft: Major defense contractor. AI developed for both purposes.

Pattern: Same oligarchs controlling civilian AI also involved in military AI. Same lack of democratic oversight. Same race dynamics.

Sanders’ warning about oligarchic AI control applies doubly to weapons:

If five billionaires shouldn’t control technology shaping civilian life without democratic input, they definitely shouldn’t control technology that kills without democratic input.

Military AI is extreme case of broader problem Sanders identifies: Concentrated unaccountable power making civilizational decisions.

Closing: The Warfare Wildcard

Autonomous weapons are wildcard in AI governance debate:

Why wildcard: Everything moves faster. Stakes are existential. Democratic oversight harder. Arms race dynamics prevent caution.

Current trajectory: Racing toward autonomous weapons without:

  • International treaty constraining them

  • Democratic deliberation about acceptability

  • Meaningful human control

  • Clear accountability

This is exactly what shouldn’t happen with powerful technology.

But military imperatives override democratic process. National security justifies secrecy. Competition justifies speed.

Result: Most dangerous AI applications developing with least democratic oversight.

Sanders’ warning is urgent here: Oligarchic control of AI is dangerous. Oligarchic control of weaponized AI is catastrophic.

The path forward exists: International treaty. Meaningful human control. Democratic oversight. Accountability.

But requires asserting democratic governance against military secrecy and arms race dynamics.

Difficult? Yes. Impossible? No. Necessary? Absolutely.

Because weapons that kill autonomously, controlled by unaccountable AI, deployed without democratic input, are incompatible with democracy itself.

This is the warfare wildcard: Technology that could make democratic control of violence impossible.

Choose now. Build weapons with human control and democratic oversight. Or build weapons that make democracy obsolete.

The warfare wildcard isn’t just about war. It’s about whether democracy survives the age of AI.
