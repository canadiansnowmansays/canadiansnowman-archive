The Rise and Fall of Matt Gaetz

The Rise and Fall (then Pivot) of Matt Gaetz

Part 1: The Dynasty

November 19, 2025

Matt Gaetz, then a Florida congressman, outside the Capitol last year. A bipartisan House Ethics Committee investigation determined there was substantial evidence that he had sex with the 17-year-old in exchange for money.Credit...Haiyun Jiang for The New York Times

The Inheritance

Matt Gaetz didn’t build himself. He was built.

His grandfather, Jerry Gaetz, was a North Dakota politician who ran for lieutenant governor in 1964 under a campaign slogan that would echo through three generations: “Unbought, Unbossed, Unbeatable.” Jerry died of a heart attack during the Republican state convention that year—his son Don watching the coverage on television.

Don Gaetz carried his father’s ambition south to Florida, where he transformed himself from a hospital administrator into a healthcare entrepreneur, launching a for-profit hospice company in 1983 that would eventually make him a multimillionaire. He pivoted to politics in the 1990s, winning a seat on the Okaloosa County School Board, then becoming superintendent—a position he leveraged into a Florida State Senate seat in 2006.

By 2012, Don Gaetz was president of the Florida Senate, one of the most powerful positions in state government. He and his wife Victoria raised their family in a house in Seaside, Florida—the same house used in The Truman Show, complete with a white picket fence bearing a sign that reads “the Truman house.”

Into this world of inherited political machinery and carefully cultivated connections, Matthew Louis Gaetz II was born on May 7, 1982, in Hollywood, Florida. Growing up in Fort Walton Beach, he earned a nickname that would follow him: “Baby Gaetz.”

The Heir Apparent

The younger Gaetz followed the prescribed path of Florida Republican royalty. He graduated from Niceville High School, earned a bachelor’s degree in interdisciplinary sciences from Florida State University in 2003, then headed to the College of William & Mary Law School in Virginia, graduating with his J.D. in 2007.

He was admitted to the Florida Bar in February 2008 and briefly practiced law at a Fort Walton Beach firm. But the law was never the destination—it was a credential, a waystation on the road to what the Gaetz family actually did: politics.

In March 2010, at age 27, Gaetz ran in a special election for the Florida House of Representatives, District 4. He won with 43 percent of the vote. The following November, he won a full term unopposed. He would win again in 2012 and 2014, each time without opposition.

The Florida statehouse was where Matt Gaetz developed the persona that would define him. He developed a reputation for partying—”I’m a legislator, not a monk,” he once joked—and for his aggressive, combative style in committee hearings. He cultivated a lively presence on Twitter. He sponsored legislation to accelerate executions of death row inmates and pushed to eliminate ethanol-blending requirements for gasoline suppliers.

Perhaps most revealing: in 2015, Gaetz was one of only two Florida legislators to vote against a bill criminalizing revenge porn. The bill’s sponsor said Gaetz’s position was that former lovers should be able to use intimate photos however they want.

During this period, the Gaetz political machine operated at full capacity. While Matt served in the Florida House, his father was president of the Florida Senate—the chamber Matt was being groomed to inherit. In 2013, Matt announced he would run in 2016 for his father’s Senate seat once Don was term-limited out.

But a better opportunity emerged.

The Leap

On March 10, 2016, Representative Jeff Miller, the Republican incumbent in Florida’s 1st Congressional District, announced he would not seek reelection. Eleven days later, Matt Gaetz withdrew from the state Senate race and filed for Congress.

The primary was crowded—eight candidates vying for this ruby-red Panhandle district. But Gaetz had advantages none of them could match: his father’s donor network, his own $200,000 personal contribution to his campaign, and $380,000 from two political action committees he’d chaired that conveniently transferred their funds to a federal super PAC supporting his congressional bid.

On August 30, 2016, Gaetz won the Republican primary with 35.7 percent of the vote. In November, he crushed the Democratic nominee with 69 percent. At 34, Matt Gaetz was heading to Washington.

A financial disclosure form he filed that year showed a net worth of $388,000. His father’s net worth: approximately $35 million.

The Trumpiest Congressman

Matt Gaetz arrived in Washington in January 2017, weeks before Donald Trump’s inauguration. He would spend the next eight years building an identity so intertwined with Trump’s that GQ magazine dubbed him “the Trumpiest Congressman in Trump’s Washington” in 2018—a label he wore as a badge of honor, featuring it on his campaign website.

Trump called Gaetz an “Absolute Warrior.” Gaetz returned the affection with a series of increasingly theatrical acts of loyalty that made him a fixture on Fox News and a hero to the MAGA base.

He defended Trump during the Russia investigation with a fervor that sometimes exceeded his grasp of the subject matter. During one CNN appearance attacking the FBI, Gaetz referenced “the Immaculate Conception”—apparently unaware that the Catholic doctrine refers to Mary’s birth, not Jesus’s—and found himself confused on live television.

He invited Charles C. Johnson, a right-wing activist who had questioned whether six million Jews actually died in the Holocaust, to Trump’s 2018 State of the Union address.

At a 2019 hearing on gun control, Gaetz argued that a border wall would save more lives than the proposed legislation. When two fathers whose children were murdered in the Parkland school shooting objected, Gaetz tried to have them thrown out.

But it was his willingness to stage spectacles—to treat congressional procedure as performance art—that truly set him apart.

The Stunts

October 23, 2019: During the House impeachment inquiry into Trump, Gaetz led approximately two dozen Republican colleagues in storming a Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility (SCIF)—the most secure room in the Capitol—where testimony was being taken. They brought their personal cell phones into the classified space, creating what security experts called a major breach that could have allowed foreign intelligence services to eavesdrop on U.S. government proceedings.

The Republicans ordered pizza and refused to leave for hours. The hearing was delayed for five hours while security swept the room. National security professionals were apoplectic. Gaetz was triumphant.

“We are going to try and go in there,” Gaetz had announced at a press conference beforehand, “and we’re going to try to figure out what is going on on behalf of the millions of Americans that we represent.”

What he didn’t mention: roughly a dozen of the Republicans who stormed the SCIF already had legitimate access to the hearings. The stunt was pure theater.

March 4, 2020: As the House voted on an $8.3 billion emergency funding bill to address the emerging coronavirus pandemic, Gaetz appeared on the floor wearing a full gas mask. He tweeted a photo of himself in the mask, reviewing the legislation.

“Members of Congress are human petri dishes,” he told reporters afterward. “We fly through the dirtiest airports. We touch everyone we meet.”

The Surgeon General had just urged Americans to stop buying masks. Vice President Pence had reiterated that “there is no need for Americans to buy masks.” Health officials were warning of shortages for healthcare workers.

Five days later, Gaetz announced he was self-quarantining after coming into contact with someone at the Conservative Political Action Conference who had tested positive for COVID-19. One of his constituents had already died from the virus.

At the peak of his performative powers, Matt Gaetz had mastered the art of turning outrage into attention and attention into influence. He had proven he would do anything for the cause.

He was about to prove it again—and the consequences would finally begin to catch up with him.
