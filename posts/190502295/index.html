The Quiet War

“The Rare Earth Chokehold”

Part II: The Supply Chain Intersection Between the Five-Year Plan and the Iran Munitions Burn Rate. What It Costs to Defend the Strait With Weapons Built From Materials China Processes.

March 17, 2026

I. The Arithmetic Nobody Is Doing

On March 5, 2026 — Day Six of Operation Epic Fury — Pentagon officials told Congress the first week of the war had cost approximately $6 billion. Of that, roughly $4 billion went to munitions and advanced missile interceptors. The Center for Strategic and International Studies estimated the first 100 hours alone at $3.7 billion, with a daily replenishment cost of $758 million for expended munitions.

These numbers are staggering. They are also incomplete. Because none of them account for the material that makes the munitions possible in the first place — or who controls it.

Every F-35 Lightning II requires approximately 920 pounds of rare earth materials. Every Arleigh Burke-class destroyer requires 5,200 pounds. Every Virginia-class submarine requires 9,200 pounds. The Tomahawk cruise missiles being fired into Iranian air defences, the Patriot interceptors being launched against Shahed drones, the THAAD missiles being expended against ballistic salvos — all of them depend on rare earth elements for their guidance systems, flight controls, radar, and propulsion.

China processes approximately 90 percent of the world’s rare earths. It manufactures 94 percent of the world’s sintered permanent magnets. The 15th Five-Year Plan, published the same week the bombs started falling, declares “extraordinary measures” to maintain and extend that dominance.

The United States is fighting a war that consumes rare-earth-dependent weapons at historic rates while the country that processes the materials for those weapons tightens its grip on the supply chain. This is the arithmetic nobody is doing. It is the most important arithmetic of the war.

II. The Cost Asymmetry

The Iran war has produced the most vivid demonstration of cost asymmetry in the history of modern warfare.

A Shahed-136 drone costs Iran between $20,000 and $50,000 to produce. Iran manufactures between 200 and 500 per month. A Patriot PAC-3 MSE interceptor costs the United States between $3.7 million and $5.5 million. Lockheed Martin produced approximately 600 of them across all of 2025. The Middle East burned through more than 800 in the first three days of Operation Epic Fury — more than Ukraine received in four years of war.

A THAAD interceptor costs $12.7 million. Annual THAAD production stood at roughly 96 missiles before the war began, with a framework agreement targeting 400 per year — a ramp-up that will take years to achieve. During the June 2025 Twelve-Day War, approximately 150 THAAD interceptors were fired, consuming an estimated 25 percent of the total American inventory. In the first 100 hours of Epic Fury, the Payne Institute estimates another 40 were expended — along with 90 Patriot missiles and 180 naval interceptors.

Kelly Grieco of the Stimson Center calculated that for every dollar Iran spent manufacturing a Shahed drone, the UAE spent roughly $20 to $28 shooting it down. One analyst compared the economics to using Ferraris to intercept e-bikes.

Iran’s strategy is not to win individual engagements. It is to ensure that the cost of winning each engagement is unsustainable over time. The drone swarms are not primarily weapons of destruction. They are weapons of depletion — designed to drain interceptor magazines until the expensive missiles run out and the defence collapses.

This strategy has a name in military doctrine: attrition. It has a precondition in supply chain economics: the attacker must be able to produce threats faster and cheaper than the defender can produce interceptors. Iran meets that precondition. And it meets it in part because China controls the materials that make the interceptors difficult and slow to build.

III. What China Controls

Excavators at a major Chinese rare earth mining site—core to the 90% global processing dominance. Source: Yale Environment 360 (representative of sites like Maoniuping)

The rare earth story is widely misunderstood. Rare earth elements are not rare — they are more common than copper or lead in the Earth’s crust. The scarcity is not geological. It is industrial.

China did not discover rare earth deposits that nobody else had. It built the processing infrastructure over three decades while the rest of the world outsourced, underinvested, or regulated its own capacity out of existence. The Mountain Pass mine in California, once the world’s leading producer, closed in 2002 after environmental violations and cost pressures. China filled the vacuum deliberately, methodically, and completely.

Today, China controls approximately 60 percent of rare earth mining and 90 percent of rare earth refining. For heavy rare earths — the elements most critical to defence applications, including dysprosium, terbium, and samarium — the concentration is even tighter. The International Energy Agency found that China produces 94 percent of the world’s sintered permanent magnets, up from 50 percent two decades ago. The European Central Bank estimated that over 80 percent of large European firms are no more than three intermediaries away from a Chinese rare earth producer.

The April 2025 export controls targeted seven medium and heavy rare earth elements in direct retaliation for Trump’s tariffs. The October 2025 expansion added five more — holmium, erbium, thulium, europium, and ytterbium — and introduced something unprecedented: extraterritorial reach. For the first time, China required foreign companies to obtain export licenses for products made outside China if they contained Chinese-origin rare earth materials or were produced using Chinese technologies. Foreign firms would need approval to export magnets containing even trace amounts — as little as 0.1 percent — of Chinese-sourced rare earths.

The White & Case legal analysis described it as China’s version of the American foreign direct product rule — the same mechanism Washington had used for years to restrict semiconductor exports to China, now turned back on the United States.

The October controls were temporarily suspended in November 2025 as part of a broader US-China diplomatic engagement. But the April controls — covering the seven heaviest and most defence-critical elements — remain in force. Companies exporting these materials must apply for licences from China’s Ministry of Commerce, a process that takes 45 days and provides no guarantee of approval. Starting December 2025, any application involving exports to foreign military users faces presumptive denial.

The suspension is not a resolution. It is a pause for recalibration. The infrastructure of control is built and demonstrated. Reactivation requires only a political decision.

IV. The DFARS Deadline and the Vulnerability Window

The Pentagon is aware of the problem. Its response is a deadline.

Under DFARS regulation 252.225-7052, starting January 1, 2027, defence contractors will be banned from using magnets, tantalum, and tungsten sourced from China, Iran, North Korea, or Russia. The ban covers the full supply chain for neodymium-iron-boron magnets — from mining through production of finished components. It affects an estimated 78 percent of Pentagon weapons programmes.

The policy intent is unambiguous: sever the dependency. The implementation timeline is the problem.

The Government Accountability Office has estimated that overhauling the defence rare earth supply chain would take 15 years. The Pentagon’s own 2024 National Defense Industrial Strategy set a target to establish a fully integrated mine-to-magnet domestic supply chain by 2027 — the same year the ban takes effect. Since 2020, the Department of Defence has invested over $439 million to strengthen domestic supply chains. In July 2025, it took a $400 million equity stake in MP Materials, the operator of the Mountain Pass mine, making the US government the company’s largest shareholder.

Aerial of Mountain Pass mine—U.S. efforts to reduce dependency amid the chokehold.Source: War on the Rocks (2025)

These are real investments. They are also insufficient at current scale. MP Materials is building separation capacity for light rare earths. Heavy rare earth separation — the dysprosium and terbium that stabilise the magnets in fighter jet engines and missile guidance systems — remains years from domestic production at defence-relevant volumes. Australia’s Arafura Rare Earths project, backed by $840 million in federal funding, projects supplying four percent of global neodymium and praseodymium demand — starting in 2032.

Industry analysts expect the 2027 deadline to be met primarily through waivers and exemptions. One assessment predicted “nothing but exceptions” in the initial period. The Pentagon itself has acknowledged that exemptions may be unavoidable. The alternative — halting production of F-35s, Patriot systems, and guided munitions until non-Chinese sources come online — is not an option any defence planner would accept, particularly during an active war.

The result is a vulnerability window measured not in months but in years — conservatively through the end of this decade, realistically well into the early 2030s. During this window, the United States depends on Chinese-processed materials for the weapons it is currently expending at historic rates in the Middle East.

V. The Burn Rate

This is where the strategic threads converge.

Operation Epic Fury is consuming interceptors at a pace that structurally cannot be sustained. The first week cost $6 billion. CSIS estimates daily munitions replenishment at $758 million. The Pentagon has already summoned major defence contractors to the White House to discuss ramping up production — an acknowledgment that current production rates cannot keep pace with wartime consumption.

Consider the specific intersection: THAAD interceptors contain rare earth magnets essential to their guidance and propulsion systems. Annual production before the war was 96 missiles. The war has already consumed dozens in its first week. The planned ramp-up to 400 per year requires years of investment, facility construction, and — critically — a stable supply of the rare earth materials that China processes.

PAC-3 MSE production was approximately 600 missiles per year in 2025. The DoD framework with Lockheed Martin targets 2,000 per year — but that target is unfunded and will take six to seven years to achieve. Meanwhile, the Middle East consumed more than a year’s production in three days.

Every interceptor expended over Iran is an interceptor that must be replaced. Every replacement requires rare earth magnets. Every rare earth magnet, under current supply chain realities, passes through Chinese processing at some point in its production. Every Chinese export control — active or suspended — represents a lever that can be pulled at any moment.

The Five-Year Plan did not create this vulnerability. It formalised its exploitation as national strategy. The “extraordinary measures” for rare earth dominance are not abstract policy language. They are the codification of a chokehold that the Iran war is tightening in real time — not because China is participating in the war, but because the war is accelerating the consumption of the very materials China controls.

VI. The Chokepoint That Is Not a Chokepoint

The rare earth supply chain is not the only chokehold tightening while the war burns through resources. There is another — financial rather than material — and it may prove harder to reverse than any supply chain disruption.

Shanaka Anslem Perera, writing in his Substack publication, identified a mechanism that mainstream coverage has entirely missed: the Strait of Hormuz did not close because of Iranian mines or fast-attack craft. It closed because seven Protection and Indemnity club cancellation letters, triggered by Solvency II capital requirements, made it uninsurable to transit. The reinsurers could not satisfy EU regulatory capital charges against unlimited tail exposure from a war zone. The insurance cascade accomplished what the entire Iranian navy could not.

The implications for the rare earth chokehold are direct. Even if alternative rare earth supply chains existed at scale — and they do not — the materials would still need to move through maritime shipping routes. The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20 percent of the world’s daily oil supply. It is also a critical transit corridor for bulk mineral shipments. When the insurance market closes a chokepoint, it does not matter how many warships patrol it. No P&I coverage means no vessel transits. No vessel transits means no cargo moves. The mechanism is financial, not kinetic, and its reversal requires sequential multi-party reinsurance recapitalisation — a process that takes months to years regardless of military outcome.

Perera’s analysis reveals a deeper structural point: financial infrastructure exercises sovereignty over physical chokepoints more effectively than navies do. The rare earth chokehold is a supply chain problem. The insurance cascade is a financial architecture problem. The Iran war has activated both simultaneously — tightening the material supply chain through accelerated consumption while closing the maritime routes through actuarial mathematics. The intersection of the two produces a vulnerability that no military victory in Iran can resolve, because the reinsurance market operates on its own timeline and its own logic.

China does not need to blockade shipping routes or impose physical restrictions on rare earth transport. It needs only to maintain its processing dominance while the financial architecture of global maritime insurance — which China did not design and does not control — prevents alternative supply routes from functioning. The war provides the conditions. The insurance market provides the mechanism. The Five-Year Plan provides the patience.

VII. What Ukraine Understood First

There is a grim irony in recent developments. As the United States struggles to defend Gulf installations against Shahed drone swarms using interceptors that cost hundreds of times more than the drones they destroy, Ukraine — which has fought this exact problem for four years — dispatched interceptor drone teams to Jordan within 24 hours of an American request.

Ukraine’s Sting interceptor drone costs approximately $2,500. It has achieved a combat-proven track record, accounting for more than 70 percent of Shahed kills over Kyiv by early 2026. Eleven countries have formally requested Ukraine’s counter-drone assistance since Epic Fury began.

The lesson is not merely about cost-effective interception. It is about industrial logic. Ukraine built a scalable counter-drone system under wartime conditions because it could not afford the alternative — expending million-dollar interceptors against thousand-dollar threats until the interceptors ran out. The constraint forced innovation. The innovation works.

The United States faces the same constraint but has been slower to recognise it, in part because the rare earth supply chain obscures the true cost of the interceptor-dependent model. The sticker price of a PAC-3 missile is $3.7 million. The strategic price includes the dependency on Chinese-processed materials required to build it, the years-long timeline to establish alternative sources, and the vulnerability that dependency creates during a conflict China can influence without firing a single shot.

VIII. The Tightening

Chinese rare earth export/storage site—stockpiles tied to export controls.Source: East Asia Forum (2022-2026)

Return to the 141 pages.

The 15th Five-Year Plan does not mention the Iran war. It does not need to. Its rare earth provisions — the extraordinary measures, the export control architecture, the extraterritorial licensing requirements — were designed for precisely the kind of strategic environment the war has created: one in which the United States is consuming defence materials at unsustainable rates while attempting to build alternative supply chains that do not yet exist.

Every week the war continues, American interceptor stocks decline. Every interceptor expended must be replaced. Every replacement depends on materials China processes. Every dollar spent on replacement is a dollar not spent on the semiconductor fabs, the AI infrastructure, or the domestic rare earth processing capacity that would reduce the dependency.

The chokehold does not require China to do anything aggressive. It requires only that China continue to do what it has been doing for thirty years — processing rare earths more efficiently, more cheaply, and at greater scale than anyone else — while the United States burns through the products of that processing in the skies over Iran.

Trump posted “Death, Fire, and Fury.” The fire requires interceptors. The interceptors require magnets. The magnets require rare earths. The rare earths require Chinese processing.

One hundred and forty-one pages. That is how long the document is that explains why the country supplying the materials for American fire and fury is content to watch it burn.
