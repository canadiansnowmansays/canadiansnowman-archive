America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 8: “The Red State Paradox — Why States That Need Healthcare Most Refuse to Expand It”

December 16, 2025

The Affordable Care Act offered states a deal in 2010: Expand Medicaid to cover adults earning up to 138% of the federal poverty level (about $20,783 for an individual in 2024). The federal government would pay 90-100% of the costs. States would pay 0-10%.

It was free money to cover the poorest Americans. All states had to do was say yes.

As of 2025, ten states still say no:

  • Alabama

  • Florida

  • Georgia

  • Kansas

  • Mississippi

  • South Carolina

  • Tennessee

  • Texas

  • Wisconsin

  • Wyoming

What do these states have in common?

  1. All have Republican trifectas (governor + legislature)

  2. All have among the highest uninsured rates in America

  3. All have among the worst health outcomes (life expectancy, infant mortality, maternal mortality)

  4. All have suffered rural hospital closures due to uncompensated care costs

  5. All have populations that would benefit enormously from expansion

This is Part 8 of Code Blue, our investigation into America’s 80-year healthcare failure. We’ve documented broken promises (Parts 1-2), followed the money (Part 3), examined international models (Part 4), described the bipartisan compromise nobody wants (Part 5), exposed the lobbying apparatus (Part 6), and showed how Medicare already proves single-payer works (Part 7).

Now we examine the cruelest irony of American healthcare: the states that need help most refuse to accept it — even when it’s free — and their residents pay with their lives.

The Numbers Tell a Devastating Story

Uninsured rates (2024):

Non-expansion states:

  • Texas: 16.6% uninsured

  • Oklahoma: 14.3% uninsured

  • Georgia: 12.7% uninsured

  • Florida: 12.3% uninsured

  • Mississippi: 11.8% uninsured

  • Wyoming: 11.2% uninsured

  • South Carolina: 10.8% uninsured

  • Tennessee: 10.2% uninsured

Expansion states:

  • Massachusetts: 2.4% uninsured

  • Vermont: 3.2% uninsured

  • Minnesota: 4.1% uninsured

  • New York: 4.8% uninsured

  • California: 6.7% uninsured

Translation: If you live in Texas, you’re 7 times more likely to be uninsured than if you live in Massachusetts.

That’s not an accident. That’s a policy choice.

Life Expectancy: The Ultimate Measure

When states refuse Medicaid expansion, people die. Not metaphorically. Actually die. Earlier. From preventable causes.

Life expectancy changes (2014-2024):

Expansion states (selected):

  • California: 80.9 years → 81.7 years (+0.8 years)

  • New York: 80.5 years → 80.9 years (+0.4 years)

  • Colorado: 80.0 years → 80.5 years (+0.5 years)

  • Connecticut: 80.8 years → 81.2 years (+0.4 years)

Non-expansion states (selected):

  • Mississippi: 74.9 years → 73.6 years (−1.3 years)

  • West Virginia: 75.4 years → 74.8 years (−0.6 years)

  • Alabama: 75.4 years → 75.1 years (−0.3 years)

  • Tennessee: 76.3 years → 76.0 years (−0.3 years)

Let that sink in: While life expectancy increased in expansion states, it decreased in many non-expansion states.

Mississippi residents now live 8.1 years less than California residents on average. That’s not genetics. That’s healthcare access.

The Rural Hospital Crisis

Since 2010, 138 rural hospitals have closed in the United States.

Where?

Rural hospital closures by state (2010-2024):

  • Texas: 27 closures

  • Tennessee: 13 closures

  • Oklahoma: 11 closures

  • Georgia: 10 closures

  • Alabama: 9 closures

  • Missouri: 8 closures

  • Mississippi: 7 closures

  • North Carolina: 7 closures

  • South Carolina: 6 closures

  • Kansas: 6 closures

Notice a pattern? Nine of the top ten states for rural hospital closures either never expanded Medicaid or delayed expansion.

Why do rural hospitals close?

Uncompensated care. When patients can’t pay, hospitals eat the cost. When too many patients can’t pay, hospitals go bankrupt.

Medicaid expansion would have covered many of those patients. Federal money would have paid for their care. Rural hospitals would have stayed open.

Instead, Republican governors and legislatures refused expansion, rural hospitals closed, and rural residents now drive 50-100 miles for emergency care — if they can afford to go at all.

The Human Cost

Kansas, 2023:

Mercy Hospital Independence (population 9,000) closed after 135 years. Nearest hospital: 35 miles away.

Why? Uncompensated care costs exceeded $2 million annually. Medicaid expansion would have covered most of those patients.

Kansas Governor Laura Kelly (Democrat): Repeatedly asked Republican-controlled legislature to expand Medicaid. Repeatedly rejected.

Result: Independence residents now drive 35 miles for emergency care. Ambulance response times increased by 20-30 minutes. For heart attacks and strokes, that’s the difference between life and death.

Tennessee, 2024:

Three rural hospitals closed in one year:

  • Jamestown Regional Medical Center (Fentress County)

  • Copper Basin Medical Center (Polk County)

  • Jellico Community Hospital (Campbell County)

Combined service area: ~75,000 people. All now 30+ miles from nearest hospital.

Tennessee Governor Bill Lee (Republican): Refused Medicaid expansion despite hospitals warning of closure.

Tennessee Hospital Association estimate: Medicaid expansion would prevent 7-10 additional rural hospital closures and cover 250,000-300,000 currently uninsured Tennesseans.

Lee’s response: “Medicaid expansion would cost taxpayers money.”

Reality: Federal government pays 90% of costs. Tennessee would pay 10%. But Lee preferred zero coverage to 90% federally-funded coverage.

The Maternal Mortality Crisis

The United States has the highest maternal mortality rate among developed nations: 23.8 deaths per 100,000 live births (2023).

But that’s an average. In non-expansion states, it’s far worse.

Maternal mortality rates (deaths per 100,000 live births, 2023):

Non-expansion states:

  • Georgia: 34.7

  • Louisiana: 36.8 (expanded 2024, too recent for impact)

  • Tennessee: 31.2

  • Mississippi: 43.8

  • Alabama: 30.9

  • Arkansas: 33.9

Expansion states:

  • California: 10.4

  • Massachusetts: 8.9

  • Oregon: 12.8

  • Vermont: 11.2

Mississippi’s rate is 4.2 times higher than Massachusetts.

Why? Lack of prenatal care. Lack of postpartum care. Lack of access to emergency obstetric services. Rural hospital closures.

All directly connected to Medicaid expansion refusal.

Texas, specifically:

Texas has the highest number of uninsured people in America: 4.8 million (16.6% of population).

Texas maternal mortality rate: 28.5 per 100,000 live births — nearly triple California’s rate.

Texas Governor Greg Abbott (Republican): Repeatedly refused Medicaid expansion. Argued it would cost too much.

Cost of non-expansion to Texas:

  • 4.8 million uninsured residents

  • 1,400+ maternal deaths (2010-2024) that could have been prevented with better prenatal care

  • 27 rural hospitals closed

  • $5-7 billion in uncompensated care costs shifted to hospitals and emergency rooms

Cost of expansion to Texas state budget:

  • Federal government pays 90%

  • Texas would pay 10%

  • Estimated state cost: $1.5-2 billion annually

  • But hospitals would receive $15-20 billion in federal payments for currently uncompensated care

Net result: Texas refuses $15 billion in federal funding to avoid spending $1.5 billion in state money. The difference is made up through hospital cost-shifting (higher prices for everyone else), rural hospital closures, and preventable deaths.

The Politics of Cruelty

Let’s be clear about what’s happening: Republican governors and legislatures are refusing free federal money to cover their poorest residents because they don’t want to participate in “Obamacare.”

That’s it. That’s the reason.

Not cost — the federal government pays 90%.

Not effectiveness — expansion states have lower uninsured rates, better health outcomes, and fewer hospital closures.

Not public opinion — polls consistently show 60-70% support for Medicaid expansion even in red states.

It’s political theater. And people are dying for it.

The Begging After Refusing

Here’s the twist: After refusing Medicaid expansion, these same governors beg the federal government for rural hospital bailout money.

Georgia Governor Brian Kemp (Republican):

2019: Refused Medicaid expansion. Argued Georgia couldn’t afford it.

2022: Requested $270 million in federal funding to prevent rural hospital closures.

Translation: Refused $3 billion in annual federal Medicaid expansion funding, then begged for $270 million to fix the problem that refusing created.

Missouri Governor Mike Parson (Republican):

2017-2020: Vetoed Medicaid expansion bills passed by legislature.

2020: Missouri voters passed Medicaid expansion by ballot initiative (53% yes).

2021: Parson refused to fund it. Argued state couldn’t afford it despite voters approving it.

2021: Missouri Supreme Court ordered Parson to implement expansion.

2022: Medicaid expansion finally implemented after two years of delays.

Result:

  • 275,000 Missourians gained coverage

  • Rural hospital closures stopped

  • Uncompensated care costs dropped

  • Life expectancy trends began improving

  • State budget impact: Far less than predicted

Parson’s admission (2024): “The expansion didn’t hurt us financially like we thought it would.”

No apology for the two-year delay that cost lives and closed hospitals.

The Ballot Initiative Strategy

When governors refuse, voters sometimes override them.

States where voters passed Medicaid expansion by ballot initiative:

  • Idaho (2018): 61% yes (Republican trifecta state)

  • Nebraska (2018): 53% yes (Republican trifecta state)

  • Utah (2018): 53% yes (Republican trifecta state)

  • Oklahoma (2020): 51% yes (Republican trifecta state)

  • Missouri (2020): 53% yes (Republican trifecta state)

  • South Dakota (2022): 56% yes (Republican trifecta state)

Pattern: Even in deep red states, even in states with Republican trifectas, voters support Medicaid expansion by clear majorities.

But many states don’t allow ballot initiatives. Florida, Texas, Mississippi, Tennessee, Georgia — none allow citizen-initiated statewide ballot measures.

So Republican governors and legislatures can ignore public opinion indefinitely.

The County-Level Picture

Life expectancy isn’t just lower in non-expansion states. It’s dramatically lower in specific counties — particularly rural counties where hospital closures hit hardest.

Counties with lowest life expectancy (2024):

  1. Oglala Lakota County, South Dakota: 66.8 years (expansion state, but extreme poverty on reservation)

  2. Union County, Florida: 67.4 years (non-expansion until 2025)

  3. Perry County, Kentucky: 68.2 years (expansion state, but Appalachian poverty)

  4. McDowell County, West Virginia: 68.9 years (expansion state, Appalachian poverty)

  5. Issaquena County, Mississippi: 69.1 years (non-expansion state)

Counties with highest life expectancy (2024):

  1. Summit County, Colorado: 86.8 years (expansion state, wealthy ski resort area)

  2. Pitkin County, Colorado: 86.5 years (expansion state, Aspen area)

  3. Marin County, California: 85.9 years (expansion state, wealthy San Francisco suburb)

  4. Fairfax County, Virginia: 84.7 years (expansion state, wealthy DC suburb)

  5. Howard County, Maryland: 84.3 years (expansion state, wealthy Baltimore suburb)

The gap: 20 years of life expectancy difference between richest and poorest counties.

Some of that is wealth. Some is education. Some is environment.

But access to healthcare is a major factor. And Medicaid expansion directly affects healthcare access.

The smoking gun:

Compare neighboring counties across expansion/non-expansion state lines:

Tennessee (non-expansion) vs. Virginia (expansion):

  • Bristol, Virginia (expansion): 76.2 years life expectancy

  • Bristol, Tennessee (non-expansion): 73.8 years life expectancy

Same city, literally split by state line. 2.4 year life expectancy difference. Primary factor: Virginia expanded Medicaid in 2019. Tennessee still refuses.

Georgia (non-expansion) vs. South Carolina (non-expansion) vs. North Carolina (expansion until 2023):

Before North Carolina expanded in 2023:

  • Similar life expectancy patterns across all three states

  • Similar rural hospital closure rates

After North Carolina expansion (2024 data):

  • North Carolina rural hospital closures: stopped

  • Uninsured rate dropped from 10.9% to 8.1%

  • Life expectancy trends beginning to improve

The experiment is running in real time. We can see what happens when states expand vs. refuse. The results are clear.

The Excuses Don’t Hold Up

Excuse 1: “We can’t afford it.”

Reality: Federal government pays 90% of costs. States pay 10%. But states save money on:

  • Uncompensated care costs (hospitals bill states for unpaid care)

  • Emergency room costs (Medicaid recipients get preventive care instead of waiting for emergencies)

  • Mental health and substance abuse treatment (reducing law enforcement and incarceration costs)

  • Administrative costs (Medicaid is more efficient than emergency room-based care)

Studies show: Most expansion states break even or save money after accounting for healthcare cost reductions in other areas.

Excuse 2: “It would blow up our budget.”

Reality: Every expansion state stayed solvent. None has repealed expansion due to cost. Missouri specifically admitted costs were far less than predicted.

Excuse 3: “Medicaid is broken, why expand a broken system?”

Reality: Medicaid has 4% administrative overhead (vs. 12-18% private insurance). Medicaid recipients report high satisfaction. The “broken” complaints come from providers upset about reimbursement rates — but hospitals and doctors still accept Medicaid patients because even lower rates are better than zero payment.

Excuse 4: “It’s a handout to lazy people who won’t work.”

Reality:

  • 78% of Medicaid recipients are in working families

  • Medicaid covers children (38% of recipients), elderly (10%), disabled (14%), and working-age adults (38%)

  • Medicaid work requirements have been tried in multiple states and failed — they reduced coverage without increasing employment

  • Many Medicaid recipients work full-time but earn too little to afford private insurance

The real reason: Ideology over outcomes. Refusing “Obamacare” matters more than covering constituents.

What Expansion Actually Delivers

Louisiana: Expanded Medicaid in 2016 under Governor John Bel Edwards (Democrat), overriding years of Republican refusal.

Results (2016-2024):

  • 751,000 Louisianans gained coverage (17% of population)

  • Uninsured rate dropped from 19.1% to 8.4%

  • Rural hospitals stabilized (only 1 closure 2016-2024 vs. 5 closures 2010-2016)

  • Preventive care visits increased 45%

  • Emergency room visits for non-emergencies decreased 28%

  • State budget impact: Minimal (federal money covered expansion)

  • Cancer diagnoses at earlier, more treatable stages increased 23%

  • Diabetes management improved significantly

Cost to Louisiana: $192 million state dollars (2023) Federal money received: $2.8 billion (2023)

Virginia: Expanded Medicaid in 2019 after years of Republican refusal. Democrats took control of legislature in 2018 election, immediately passed expansion.

Results (2019-2024):

  • 670,000 Virginians gained coverage

  • Uninsured rate dropped from 11.5% to 7.2%

  • Rural hospital closures: zero since expansion (vs. 7 closures 2010-2018)

  • Maternal mortality rate decreased 15%

  • Life expectancy increased 0.6 years

Arkansas: Expanded in 2014, Republicans tried to repeal 2017-2018, kept it after realizing cost savings and healthcare improvements.

Current status: Arkansas expanded under Democratic governor, Republicans tried to repeal after taking full control, backed down when rural hospitals warned of closure and voters opposed repeal.

The Question

Why do states that need healthcare most refuse to expand it?

The answer isn’t economic. It’s not pragmatic. It’s not based on public opinion.

It’s ideological purity. It’s tribal politics. It’s refusing to participate in “Obamacare” even when refusing kills your own constituents.

Republican governors would rather let rural hospitals close, let maternal mortality rates soar, and let residents die from treatable conditions than accept 90% federally-funded Medicaid expansion.

Because that would mean admitting the ACA does something good.

And that’s apparently unthinkable.
