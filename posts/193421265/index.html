The Baseline Problem

The Press Conference

Part 11: April 6, 2026: Ninety-Three Minutes at the Lectern

April 6, 2026

White House Press Conference, Mr. Trump said Iran was “negotiating we think in good faith.”Credit...Kenny Holston/The New York Times

A note before we begin. This series applies a consistent analytical framework — the baseline problem — to the documented public behavior of the 47th President of the United States. It does not diagnose. It does not speculate about what cannot be observed. It documents what is on the record, and it asks the question that the framework requires: does this behavior represent a departure from a previously established baseline, and if so, in what direction?

The April 6, 2026 White House press conference was called ostensibly to celebrate the rescue of an Air Force colonel whose F-15E fighter jet had been shot down over Iran on Good Friday. It lasted approximately ninety-three minutes. The president of the United States was at the lectern for most of it.

What follows is a close reading of the record.

I. The Analytical Frame

The baseline problem, as this series has defined it since Part I, is not a partisan construct. It is a clinical and journalistic concept: in order to assess whether behavior is abnormal, you must first establish what normal looks like for the subject in question. You cannot assess deviation without a baseline.

Donald Trump’s baseline — his established normal — is well documented across decades of public life, two presidencies, and thousands of hours of recorded speech. He has always been hyperbolic. He has always been contradictory. He has always been comfortable with statements that would be disqualifying for other politicians. He has always been transactional, theatrical, and self-referential.

The baseline problem is not that Trump says remarkable things. He always has. The baseline problem is when the pattern of his remarks shifts in ways that suggest something other than rhetorical choice — when the contradictions within a single statement become incoherent rather than strategic, when the digressions lose their connective tissue, when the responses to direct questions produce answers to different questions entirely.

Aaron Parnas, writing on his Substack after watching the full press conference, was explicit about his framing: “This is not a partisan statement. I would say the same thing if President Joe Biden spoke about becoming the President of Venezuela during a press conference about a military operation in Iran.”

That is the baseline problem’s operating standard. Not partisan. Not comparative. Observational.

The April 6 press conference provides a substantial observational record.

II. The Record

The press conference opened with the president describing the rescue operation in cinematic terms. “You would call it central casting if you were doing a movie for location,” he said of the mountains of southwest Iran where the colonel had hidden for thirty-eight hours. The description was vivid and coherent. He was clearly engaged.

Then the record begins to accumulate.

On the war’s objectives and status:

“They can’t fight back. They have no capability. I mean, they have some missiles left, they have some drones left, but essentially they have no capability.”

This was said on Day Thirty-Eight of a war in which Iran had, in the preceding seventy-two hours: shot down a US F-15E with a shoulder-fired missile, fired hundreds of missiles and drones at Israel and Gulf states, struck Kuwait’s government ministries complex, damaged Bahrain’s petrochemical facilities, killed four civilians in Haifa, and threatened power plants across the Gulf. The claim that Iran has “essentially no capability” is not hyperbole in service of a strategic argument. It is a statement in direct contradiction with events the president himself had described moments earlier.

On escalation versus de-escalation:

A reporter asked Trump to explain the apparent contradiction between saying the war might end soon and threatening the most massive infrastructure bombing in modern history. His response: “Can’t tell you. I don’t know.”

This series has documented Trump’s strategic use of ambiguity — “I don’t want to say” is a familiar phrase, used to preserve negotiating leverage. “I don’t know” is different. It is not strategic ambiguity. It is the absence of an answer.

On the Strait of Hormuz tolls:

Iran’s 10-point proposal included a $2 million per ship toll, split with Oman. A reporter asked if Trump would accept a deal in which Iran charged tolls. Trump’s response was to propose that the United States charge tolls instead. “We have a concept where we’ll charge tolls,” he said. He did not explain how a country that does not control the strait, has no territorial claim to it, and whose allies are the primary users of it would charge tolls for passage.

“I’d rather do that. Why shouldn’t we? We’re the winner. We won.”

The United States does not control the Strait of Hormuz. The strait has been effectively closed to US-allied shipping since February 28. This is not the record of a winner controlling the waterway.

On seizing Iran’s oil:

“To the victor belong the spoils.” He then added: “We haven’t heard that in, I think, maybe hundreds of years.” He said he would “keep the oil” if it were up to him, but acknowledged that “the American people would like to see us come home.”

This is the third or fourth time in recent weeks that Trump has described taking Iran’s oil as a personal preference held in check only by public opinion. The Venezuela model — seizing a country’s assets — has become the organizing framework for how he describes the endgame. This is not a new idea for Trump, but the frequency and specificity with which he returns to it has increased.

On running for president of Venezuela:

Parnas’s newsletter flagged this without full context, but the record shows Trump made a passing reference to Venezuela in the midst of a discussion about Iran that veered into his relationship with Kim Jong Un, South Korea’s defense burden, and Biden’s autopen. The Venezuela reference did not connect to the preceding or following sentences in any reconstructible way.

On the reporter jail threat:

Trump said a leak about the first pilot’s rescue — published before the second crew member was safe — had endangered the mission. He then said: “We’re going to go to the media company that released it, and we’re going to say, national security, give it up or go to jail. The person that did the story will go to jail if he doesn’t say.”

The First Amendment does not work this way. Reporters are not legally required to reveal sources. The president was charged federally with mishandling classified documents he kept upon leaving office. That case was dismissed. These facts were not mentioned during the press conference. Trump did not identify the reporter or the outlet.

On God:

“God is good. And God wants to see people taken care of.”

“I do because God is good” — in response to whether he believes God supports the US-Israeli war against Iran, which has killed thousands of civilians.

“I don’t like seeing people get killed.”

These three statements were made in sequence, at the same press conference, with no apparent awareness of their collective incoherence.

On Biden:

At the Easter Egg Roll, squeezed onto a bench between two elementary-school children writing cards to members of the military, Trump said: “You know, Biden would use the autopen. He was incapable of signing his name.” One child said: “What?”

This is not the record of a man who is focused on Tuesday’s deadline.

On Kim Jong Un:

“You know who else didn’t help us? South Korea didn’t help us. We’ve got 45,000 soldiers in South Korea to protect them from Kim Jong Un, who I get along with very well. He said very nice things about me. He used to call Joe Biden a mentally retarded person.”

This was said at a press conference about a military deadline involving Iran.

On the Nobel Peace Prize:

“I think I deserve the Nobel Peace Prize. We have one more to end, by the way” — without specifying whether he meant Iran, Ukraine, or another conflict.

III. The Pattern

This series does not diagnose. It observes patterns and asks whether they represent a departure from baseline.

The April 6 press conference contains the following documented features:

Internally contradictory statements within the same press conference — Iran has no capability / Iran shot down our plane with a shoulder-fired missile. We will destroy every power plant / we may help rebuild the nation. A deal must meet my terms / I don’t know what the direction is.

Responses to direct questions that answer different questions — the toll question answered with a counter-toll proposal for which no mechanism exists. The escalation question answered with “I don’t know.”

Non-sequitur insertions that do not connect to the surrounding discourse — Venezuela, Biden’s autopen to elementary-school children, Kim Jong Un calling Biden a mentally retarded person, the Nobel Peace Prize.

Repetition of grievances in contextually inappropriate settings — the autopen critique delivered to children writing cards to soldiers, with the Easter Bunny standing nearby.

Difficulty sustaining a complex argument across its component parts — the spoils claim, the toll counter-proposal, the we-won framing all presuppose a strategic victory whose existence the same press conference simultaneously denied.

The baseline question is: does this pattern represent Trump being Trump — theatrical, hyperbolic, digressive — or does it represent something that has shifted from that established baseline?

This series does not answer that question definitively. It documents the record and notes that the question is being asked — not only by opposition Democrats but by independent journalists, by a Nebraska Republican who backed the war, by the President of the European Council, and by the nation’s largest bank.

Jamie Dimon’s annual letter to JPMorgan Chase shareholders, released the same day, warned of “stickier inflation and ultimately higher interest rates than markets currently expect.” He was writing about the war’s economic consequences. But the phrase is also, without any intended irony, an apt description of what the Baseline Problem series has been tracking: a pattern that is stickier than the market currently expects, and whose eventual rate is unknown.

IV. The Other Players

This series tracks one subject. But the press conference produced a secondary record worth noting.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth compared the colonel’s rescue to the Easter Resurrection. Shot down Good Friday. Hidden in a cave or crevice all of Saturday. Rescued as the sun rose Easter Sunday. “A pilot reborn. All home and accounted for, a nation rejoicing. God is good.”

Hegseth has “Deus vult” — God wills it — tattooed on his right bicep, his description of the Crusaders’ battle cry. He wrote a book titled “American Crusade.” He is directing a bombing campaign against a majority-Shiite Muslim nation. He has asked Americans to pray for victory “in the name of Jesus Christ.”

Pope Leo XIV — the first American-born pontiff — has repeatedly called for an end to the conflict. In a recent homily, Leo said the Christian mission had often been “distorted by a desire for domination, entirely foreign to the way of Jesus Christ.”

The Secretary of Defense is more enthusiastic about the Crusades than the Pope.

The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, General Dan Caine, was asked by Trump at the press conference how many people were sent on the rescue mission. Caine said: “I’d love to keep that a secret.” Trump turned to the reporters and said it was hundreds.

António Costa, President of the European Council: “Any targeting of civilian infrastructure is illegal and unacceptable. Escalation will not achieve a ceasefire and peace.”

Iranian musician Ali Ghamsari staged a sit-in at the Damavand power plant in Tehran — one of Iran’s largest — in response to Trump’s threat. He sat on a carpet with a blanket and his instrument in front of the plant. “If there is no power, there will be no water.”

The Artemis II crew, the same day, set a new record for the farthest distance humans have ever traveled from Earth, surpassing the Apollo 13 milestone. Trump mentioned the mission in the first thirty seconds of his prime-time address five days earlier, then moved on. The crew was somewhere beyond the moon while the press conference was happening.

V. The Standard

This series will continue as long as the public record warrants continued observation. It does not predict outcomes. It does not advocate for removal or any specific action. It holds the record open.

The baseline question, asked again: is the behavior at the April 6 press conference within the established range of what this president does, or has something shifted?

The record documents: a man who said Iran has essentially no capability on the same day Iran demonstrated capability. A man who said he does not know the direction of a war he is directing. A man who proposed charging tolls for a waterway he does not control. A man who told elementary-school children that his predecessor could not sign his name, while the Easter Bunny stood nearby. A man who said God is good, then said he doesn’t like seeing people killed, then authorized the largest volume of strikes since Day One of the war.

The record is the record.

What the market currently expects, and what the actual rate turns out to be, remain to be seen.
