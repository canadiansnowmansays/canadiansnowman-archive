The Iran Crisis

The Currency Crash That Broke Iran

How Economic Crisis Became Revolution

January 12, 2026

December 28, 2025. The Iranian rial collapsed.

Within hours, the currency hit 1.4 million rials per U.S. dollar on the parallel market—a historic low. By week’s end, it had recovered slightly to 1.35 million, but the damage was done. Savings evaporated overnight. Prices for food, medicine, and fuel surged. The purchasing power that Iranians had painstakingly preserved through years of sanctions and inflation simply vanished.

The bazaars of Tehran—Iran’s commercial heart for centuries—went dark. Shopkeepers pulled down their metal shutters and walked away. Not in protest. In desperation. There was nothing left to sell at prices people could afford.

Within 48 hours, what began as merchants closing shops became a nationwide uprising that would leave hundreds dead and threaten to topple a 46-year-old theocracy.

This is the story of how economic crisis became revolution.

The Chronic Inflation Crisis: A Decade of Erosion

To understand what happened on December 28, you must first understand what came before.

Iran hasn’t had a normal economy since 2018. For seven years, the country has experienced what analysts call “one of the most persistent inflation episodes in the developing world.” Not the hyperinflation of Zimbabwe or Venezuela—but a grinding, relentless erosion that averages 40-45% annually.

The numbers tell the story:

  • Official inflation rate (October 2025): 48.6%, up from 45.3% in September

  • Point-to-point inflation (late December): 52.6%

  • Food inflation: Over 50%, with some estimates at 70%

  • Housing inflation: Approximately 35%

These aren’t abstract statistics. They represent the lived reality of 80 million Iranians watching their currency, their savings, and their futures dissolve.

Consider what 40-45% annual inflation means in practice. If you earned 100 million rials in 2018, and your salary kept perfect pace with inflation (it didn’t), you’d need 600 million rials by 2025 to maintain the same purchasing power. If your salary didn’t keep pace—and most Iranians’ didn’t—you’re now earning perhaps 200-300 million rials while needing 600 million. Your standard of living has been cut by more than half.

Food and housing account for approximately 60% of household budgets in Iran. When food inflation exceeds 50% and housing approaches 35%, these two essentials alone explain most of the overall inflation. And because low-income households spend the largest share of income on necessities, price increases hit them hardest.

This is before we factor in the currency crash.

The Rial’s 46-Year Collapse

Iran’s currency has been in structural decline since the 1979 Islamic Revolution. The rial has lost 99% of its value against the dollar over 46 years—a near-complete destruction of monetary value.

1979: Approximately 70 rials per dollar 2015: ~30,000 rials per dollar (before nuclear deal) 2018: ~100,000 rials per dollar (after Trump withdrew from nuclear deal) 2022: ~300,000 rials per dollar 2024: ~600,000 rials per dollar December 28, 2025: 1.4 million rials per dollar

That final jump—from 600,000 to 1.4 million in weeks—represented not just depreciation but collapse. The rial lost more than half its remaining value in a matter of days.

The immediate trigger, as reported by The Washington Post, was straightforward: “A plunge in the value of Iran’s currency on Dec. 28 incited the current protests.”

But why did it plunge?

The Perfect Storm: Five Drivers of Currency Collapse

According to economic analysts, including a comprehensive analysis published by the SDG Knowledge Hub, Iran’s December currency crash resulted from five converging factors:

1. Sanctions Limiting Foreign Exchange

U.S. sanctions have restricted Iran’s ability to export oil—its primary source of foreign currency. When you can’t sell oil freely, you can’t earn dollars, euros, or yuan. When foreign exchange reserves dwindle, currency depreciates.

The June 2025 U.S.-Israeli military strikes on Iran’s nuclear facilities resulted in reimposition of comprehensive UN economic sanctions. This further limited Iran’s ability to access international banking and trade.

2. Exchange Rate Pass-Through

Iran depends heavily on imported food, medicine, and industrial inputs. When the rial depreciates, import costs surge. Firms adjust prices preemptively in anticipation of further currency weakness, creating a feedback loop between exchange rate and consumer prices.

As The Washington Post explained: “Iranians have endured years of soaring inflation, weak economic growth and international isolation, driven by mismanagement, corruption and sanctions imposed by the United States and other countries over Tehran’s nuclear program.”

The International Monetary Fund has consistently documented how price spikes in Iran closely follow currency depreciation episodes. It’s not just correlation—it’s direct causation.

3. Weak Monetary Policy and Central Bank Independence

Although Iran’s Central Bank introduced open market operations in 2020, these tools remain constrained by fiscal pressures and limited institutional credibility. When governments need to print money to cover deficits, central banks cannot effectively control inflation.

The state coffers are essentially empty. Years of sanctions, corruption, and economic mismanagement have drained reserves.

4. Psychological Panic and Capital Flight

When inflation becomes chronic, people expect more inflation. This creates self-reinforcing behavior: households and businesses rush to convert rials into dollars or gold to protect what they have. As economist Mohammad Kohandal told Tasnim News Agency: “As long as inflation remains a chronic problem in the economy, expecting a stable exchange rate is not realistic.”

The December crash triggered panic. People who had held rials—hoping the currency would stabilize—suddenly realized they’d waited too long. The rush to dollars accelerated the depreciation.

5. Security and Political Uncertainty

The risk of regional tensions, combined with public warnings by Iranian officials of possible Western sanctions, reinforced concerns that access to foreign currency would tighten further. Fears of escalation involving Iran and Israel, stalled nuclear diplomacy, and weakening confidence in the economy accelerated the sell-off.

Iran’s proxies had been decimated: Hezbollah and Hamas weakened by Israeli strikes, Assad’s regime toppled in Syria. The country looked vulnerable militarily and isolated diplomatically.

All these factors converged on December 28, 2025.

Who It Hurt Most: The Mathematics of Misery

The currency collapse hit every Iranian, but it devastated certain groups:

The Middle Class: Years of inflation had already eroded savings. The December crash wiped out what remained. Consider a typical middle-class household: According to Iranian Central Bank data, median household savings in urban areas averaged approximately 800 million rials in late 2025. At the pre-crash rate (600,000:1), this represented roughly $1,333 in purchasing power. After the crash (1.4 million:1), the same 800 million rials bought only $571 worth of goods—a loss of 57% of purchasing power in a matter of days. For families who’d spent years saving for a home down payment, a child’s education, or medical emergencies, decades of careful financial planning evaporated in a week.

Bazaar Merchants: These small business owners—the backbone of Iran’s commercial economy—faced impossible math. They’d purchased inventory at one exchange rate, but by the time they could sell, the rial had collapsed. Selling at pre-crash prices meant bankruptcy. Raising prices meant customers couldn’t afford goods. Either choice spelled ruin.

The Working Poor: Those living paycheck to paycheck saw their wages become worthless. If you earned 50 million rials monthly (about $83 pre-crash), your December salary bought perhaps $35 worth of goods. Food prices surged immediately—rice, cooking oil, meat jumped 20-40% within days.

Retirees and Pensioners: Fixed incomes became starvation rations. A pension of 30 million rials that barely covered expenses suddenly couldn’t feed a family of four for a week.

The Sick: Iran’s healthcare system depends heavily on imported medicines and medical supplies. Currency depreciation meant medications doubled or tripled in price. People with chronic conditions—diabetes, heart disease, cancer—faced impossible choices between medicine and food.

This is what economic collapse looks like in human terms. Not just numbers declining on spreadsheets, but families watching their children go hungry, elderly couples choosing which medication to skip, small business owners who’d spent lifetimes building businesses watching everything dissolve.

The Bazaaris: Why the Merchants’ Closure Mattered

When Tehran’s Grand Bazaar shut down on December 28-29, it wasn’t just an economic event. It was a political earthquake.

The bazaaris—traditional merchants who operate in Iran’s sprawling covered markets—have been central to Iranian politics for centuries. They helped fund and organize the 1979 revolution that toppled the Shah. They represented a conservative, religious, but economically pragmatic class that formed the backbone of the Islamic Republic’s support.

When the bazaaris turn against the government, the regime knows it’s in trouble.

The Washington Post noted the symbolic significance: “The crash of the Iranian rial on Dec. 28 was the last straw. The fact that it was Tehran’s bazaar merchants who helped kick off the protests was symbolically charged—bazaar merchants were also central to the protests that led to the 1979 revolution.”

These weren’t radical students or secular opposition figures. These were the people who’d supported Khomeini in 1979, who’d benefited from the Islamic Republic’s economic policies, who’d maintained loyalty through decades of sanctions and pressure.

When they walked away from their shops, they were walking away from the regime.

“Let Them Eat Cake”: When Elites Lose Touch with Economic Reality

The most famous quote never actually spoken illuminates Iran’s December 2025 crisis with uncomfortable precision.

Marie Antoinette almost certainly never said “Let them eat cake” when told French peasants had no bread. But the phrase endures because it captures a revolutionary truth: when ordinary people are starving and elites appear indifferent, governments fall.

The parallel between revolutionary France and contemporary Iran is striking. In both cases, economic crisis didn’t happen overnight—it accumulated over years. France’s fiscal collapse resulted from decades of expensive wars, court extravagance, and structural economic dysfunction. Iran’s inflation crisis built across seven years of sanctions, corruption, and mismanagement.

In both cases, the merchant and commercial classes—initially regime supporters—turned against the government when economic conditions became untenable. The French Third Estate included wealthy bourgeois merchants whose economic interests aligned with the monarchy until bread riots made clear the system was broken. Iran’s bazaaris similarly supported the Islamic Republic for decades until the December currency crash made business impossible.

And in both cases, the trigger was bread. Literally.

When French women marched on Versailles in October 1789, they marched because bread prices had become unaffordable. When Iranian protesters took to Tehran’s streets in December 2025, their first chants were “We can’t afford bread!”

The progression from economic grievance to political revolution follows a pattern that transcends culture and century. Hunger is tolerable when everyone suffers equally and when people believe conditions might improve. Hunger becomes revolutionary when elites eat while ordinary people starve, and when people conclude the system itself prevents improvement.

Iranian President Pezeshkian tried offering modest economic reforms—the equivalent of promising cheaper cake. But when your currency has lost 99% of its value and food inflation exceeds 50%, reforms feel like mockery. When the bazaaris close their shops, when middle-class families choose between medicine and food, when retirees can’t survive on pensions—you’re not in reform territory anymore.

You’re in revolution territory.

Supreme Leader Khamenei, living in heavily-guarded compounds while ordinary Iranians faced starvation-level poverty, embodied the disconnect that makes revolutions possible. Like Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette residing at Versailles—with its Hall of Mirrors, its fountains, its feasts—while French peasants rioted over bread prices, Khamenei’s isolation from economic reality wasn’t just tone-deaf. It was incendiary. His Friday speech blamed protesters for trying to “please the president of the United States”—a response that completely missed the point. People weren’t in the streets because of American influence. They were in the streets because they couldn’t afford bread.

The lesson of 1789 France applies to 2025 Iran: You can survive unpopular policies. You can survive military defeats. You can even survive political repression. But you cannot survive mass starvation combined with elite indifference.

That’s not a recipe for reform. That’s a recipe for revolution.

How Economic Grievance Became Political Uprising

The protests began as pure economics. Merchants closing bazaars. Workers unable to afford food. Students facing impossible futures.

But economic protests in authoritarian states rarely stay purely economic.

The New York Times documented the evolution: “Demonstrators started taking to the streets on Dec. 28, spurred by a sudden plunge in the value of Iran’s currency. But their calls quickly broadened to demanding the overthrow of Iran’s authoritarian clerical rulers.”

Within 72 hours, the slogans changed:

December 28-29: “We can’t afford bread!” “The economy is collapsing!” “We can’t survive!”

December 30-31: “Death to the dictator!” “Clerics get lost!” “We don’t want Islamic Republic!”

January 1-3: “Death to Khamenei!” “Long live the Shah!” “The street will prevail!”

The progression is familiar to scholars of revolution. Economic crisis creates mass mobilization—people take to streets because they’re desperate. But once in the streets, facing security forces, the demands escalate. If we’re risking our lives already, why not demand everything? Why settle for economic reform when the problem is the entire system?

President Masoud Pezeshkian initially tried to strike a sympathetic tone when protests began two weeks ago. He acknowledged economic grievances, promised relief measures, talked about addressing corruption and mismanagement.

But by January 11, his tone had hardened completely. Speaking to Iranian state television, he blamed “the enemy” for bringing “trained terrorists into the country” and vowed “not to let rioters destabilize the country.”

The regime’s economic narrative deserves closer examination: Iranian state media (Tasnim News Agency, Fars News) consistently framed the crisis not as policy failure but as foreign economic warfare. Their argument: U.S. sanctions intentionally collapsed the currency to create unrest. The December 28 crash wasn’t market forces—it was coordinated manipulation by Western intelligence agencies working with exile groups.

This narrative serves multiple purposes for the regime. First, it deflects responsibility for economic mismanagement. If sanctions cause inflation, the problem is external. Second, it frames protesters as foreign agents rather than citizens with legitimate grievances. Third, it appeals to Iranian nationalism—resistance to foreign pressure, even if costly, becomes patriotic.

The problem with this narrative: It offers no solution. If sanctions cause the crisis, and sanctions won’t be lifted without major concessions, what’s the path forward? The regime’s answer—endure, resist, blame foreigners—provides no relief to families who can’t afford bread.

The shift from Pezeshkian’s sympathy to accusation signals regime panic. When governments abandon acknowledgment of legitimate grievances and resort purely to conspiracy theories, they’ve lost confidence they can address underlying problems.

Why This Economic Crisis Is Different

Iran has faced economic crises before. The 1979 revolution itself was partly triggered by economic grievances. The 2009 Green Movement had economic undertones. The 2022 “Women, Life, Freedom” protests occurred amid inflation and hardship.

So why did December 2025 break differently?

First, the accumulation of pain. Seven years of 40-45% inflation isn’t like one year of 100% inflation. Hyperinflation is dramatic but can potentially be fixed quickly. Chronic inflation grinds people down slowly, year after year, until there’s nothing left. By December 2025, Iranian society had been bled dry.

Second, the regime’s weakened position militarily and diplomatically. As The New York Times noted: “Iran’s military and nuclear facilities were battered by a 12-day war with Israel last June, and the country has been sinking into a severe economic crisis after the reinstatement of U.N. economic sanctions last year.”

Assad’s fall in Syria. Hezbollah decimated in Lebanon. Hamas weakened in Gaza. These weren’t just foreign policy setbacks—they were demonstrations that Iran’s “Axis of Resistance” had collapsed. The regime looked vulnerable in ways it hadn’t before.

Third, the demographics. A huge segment of Iran’s population is under 30—people who’ve lived their entire adult lives under sanctions, inflation, and limited opportunities. They have no memory of the revolution’s idealism. They see only economic dysfunction and political repression. When 60% of your population is under 30, and they see no future, you’re sitting on a powder keg.

Fourth, the speed of the December collapse. Gradual decline allows adjustment. Sudden collapse creates panic. The rial’s depreciation from 600,000 to 1.4 million in weeks was too fast for adaptation. People who thought they had modest savings suddenly had nothing. The psychological shock mattered as much as the economic damage.

Fifth, and perhaps most important: Venezuela. Just six days after the December 28 currency crash, the United States invaded Venezuela and captured President Nicolás Maduro. As I documented in Part 2 of this series, that event fundamentally changed Iranian protesters’ calculations about whether international intervention might actually come.

Economic crisis created the conditions for uprising. Venezuela created the belief that uprising might succeed.

The Government’s Impossible Position

By early January, the Iranian government faced an impossible choice.

Option 1: Address the economic crisis seriously. This would require:

  • Ending sanctions (impossible without nuclear concessions)

  • Stopping corruption (threatens the elite)

  • Reforming subsidy system (politically explosive)

  • Allowing currency to find market level (devastating for imports)

  • Opening economy to foreign investment (requires security concessions)

None of these were feasible. Sanctions depend on U.S./UN decisions. Corruption is systemic and protects powerful interests. Currency depreciation would accelerate inflation. Economic opening threatens regime security.

Option 2: Crack down hard on protests. This would:

  • Kill protesters (triggering Trump’s intervention threats)

  • Isolate Iran further (driving away remaining partners)

  • Prove regime illegitimacy (validating protesters’ demands)

  • Risk splits in security forces (if violence becomes extreme)

  • Potentially trigger U.S. military strikes (after Venezuela precedent)

Also deeply problematic. Violence might work in the short term, but at enormous cost.

The regime’s response—internet blackout, massive violence, blame foreign agents—suggests they chose Option 2. But this choice came with real uncertainty about whether it would work.

As a senior Western official told journalists: “Iranian authorities initially appeared reluctant to use lethal force, but once the protests threatened the regime, authorities shifted course.”

The economic crisis had created a political crisis that military force might not be able to solve.

Conclusion: When Economic Collapse Meets Political Opportunity

Iran’s economic crisis didn’t start in December 2025. It started in 2018, when the Trump administration withdrew from the nuclear deal and reimposed sanctions. For seven years, inflation averaged 40-45% annually. The currency lost 99% of its value since 1979. The state coffers emptied. The middle class was destroyed. The poor became destitute.

What happened on December 28 was simply the breaking point.

Currency crashed. Savings evaporated. Prices surged. Bazaars closed. And suddenly millions of Iranians who’d been suffering in relative silence decided they had nothing left to lose.

Economic crises create conditions for political change. They don’t guarantee it—regimes can survive brutal economic conditions if they maintain fear and control. But they create vulnerability.

What made Iran’s December crisis potentially revolutionary was not just its severity, but its timing. It coincided with military weakness (after June’s war with Israel), diplomatic isolation (after Syria’s collapse), demographic pressure (young population with no opportunities), and—perhaps most crucially—Trump’s demonstration in Venezuela that regime change through military force was back on the table.

The currency crash of December 28 was the spark. But the kindling had been accumulating for seven years. And as we’ll see in Part 3 of this series, when the regime tried to extinguish that spark, they reached for the most brutal tools available—and may have set Iran ablaze in the process.

The economic collapse didn’t just hurt Iranians financially. It destroyed their patience, their fear, and their willingness to accept the status quo.

That’s how economic crisis becomes revolution.
