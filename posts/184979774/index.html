How Tom Ridge’s DHS Became Kristi Noem’s Weapon

Tom Ridge founded DHS to protect Americans from terrorism. Twenty-three years later, it kills American citizens and calls them “terrorists.”

January 26, 2026

Official DHS social media post, December 31, 2025

“America After 100 Million Deportations — The peace of a nation no longer besieged by the third world.”

The post received more than 20 million views.

On New Year’s Eve, as Americans prepared to celebrate 2026, the Department of Homeland Security posted an image on social media: a classic car on a sandy beach with palm trees, overlaid with a banner reading “America After 100 Million Deportations” and the caption “The peace of a nation no longer besieged by the third world.”

One hundred million people. Nearly one-third of the entire U.S. population.

The post accumulated more than 20 million views—not as outrage, but as aspiration from the administration’s supporters. DHS, the agency created to protect Americans from foreign terrorists after September 11, was now fantasizing publicly about removing one-third of the people who live in America.

Six days later, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot and killed Renee Nicole Good in Minneapolis. Within hours, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem called Good—a 37-year-old American mother—a “domestic terrorist.”

This is the story of how an agency created to unite Americans against foreign threats became a weapon deployed against Americans themselves.

The Original Vision: “Thank You for Keeping Us Safe”

Tom Ridge, March 12, 2002, unveiling the Homeland Security Advisory System.

“We don’t do politics in the Department of Homeland Security.”

Think of Tom Ridge

“We don’t do politics in the Department of Homeland Security,” Ridge liked to say whenever reporters asked how he handled pressure from the White House. Ridge, a moderate Republican and Vietnam veteran with a square jaw and gentle manner, was Pennsylvania’s governor when nearly 3,000 Americans were killed on September 11, 2001. The nation was gripped with fear and horror, and President George W. Bush put the bipartisanship-seeking Ridge in charge of making sure there wouldn’t be another terrorist strike.

Ridge is now 80 and has mostly retired from public life following a 2021 stroke. I expect it would trouble him deeply to see how the department he built—whose public communications once focused on terror-threat levels and protecting Americans—has been twisted into a trolling operation infused with white nationalism, calling American citizens terrorists and deploying paramilitary forces against communities.

September 11, 2001, changed America. The trauma was visceral, immediate, and unifying. For weeks afterward, Americans of all political persuasions shared a single overwhelming emotion: fear. Fear of another attack. Fear that our systems had failed. Fear that we were vulnerable in ways we’d never imagined.

Out of that fear came DHS—the largest reorganization of the federal government since the creation of the Department of Defense in 1947. The new Cabinet-level entity mashed together more than 20 federal agencies under one roof, all serving a single mission: protect the homeland from terrorist attacks.

The name itself—”homeland security”—was chosen deliberately. It wasn’t universally admired; some found it vaguely authoritarian, reminiscent of how other nations described their domestic security apparatus. But Bush administration officials believed the term evoked something shared that every American would want to protect, transcending partisan politics.

Ridge embodied that vision. He was careful, measured, bipartisan. He understood that DHS’s mission to protect Americans from terrorism enjoyed broad support across the political spectrum. The department’s agencies—Border Patrol, Coast Guard, Secret Service, TSA, FEMA, and others—benefited from renewed national admiration for people in uniform who served their communities.

Jeh Johnson, who served as DHS Secretary during Obama’s second term, captured the original spirit in an anecdote he shared with Washington Post reporter Nick Miroff. Johnson was riding Amtrak one day between Washington and New Jersey with a gym bag emblazoned with the DHS logo. A family sitting nearby noticed, including a young boy. “Toward the end of the trip—and his parents clearly put him up to it—the little boy walked across the aisle and said to me, ‘Sir, thank you for keeping us safe.’”

“That was the first instinct of people when they thought of Homeland Security,” Johnson recalled. “They keep us safe.”

That was the promise. That was the mission. That was what Tom Ridge built.

Ridge’s Last Words on DHS: March 2023

In March 2023, Tom Ridge published a commentary in the Orlando Sentinel reflecting on DHS’s 20th anniversary. Writing three years after his stroke, Ridge expressed one regret: America’s failure to develop comprehensive immigration policy. But about DHS itself, he remained deeply optimistic.

“I reach this 20-year anniversary with optimism that our department will continue to protect us from terrorists, help us recover from the challenges of nature, and deal smartly and compassionately with immigration, despite the lack of a cohesive road map,” Ridge wrote.

He praised DHS workers whose “dedication” he called “unmatched in our federal government.” He described the department’s creation as producing “a stronger and safer America.” He recalled how all four congressional leaders—”Republican and Democratic alike”—had united to create DHS, producing “monumental change” for the country’s security.

Ridge concluded: “I am blessed to have served with our nation’s best, and I know that today’s DHS workers continue the tradition of selfless service to our nation.”

Tom Ridge wrote those words in March 2023.

Less than three years later, on New Year’s Eve 2025, DHS would post fantasies about deporting 100 million people. Six days after that, DHS would kill Renee Nicole Good and call her a “domestic terrorist.” Seventeen days later, DHS would kill Alex Jeffrey Pretti and claim he wanted to “massacre law enforcement.”

Ridge’s optimism—his faith that DHS would “protect us from terrorists” and act “smartly and compassionately”—would be betrayed completely by the agency he founded.

The dedication Ridge praised would manifest as ICE Officer Jonathan Ross recording Renee Good on his phone before shooting her three times, then cursing her corpse.

The “selfless service” Ridge celebrated would become Border Patrol agents wrestling a VA nurse to the ground and firing ten shots in five seconds while he lay motionless.

The “stronger and safer America” Ridge envisioned would become an America where DHS kills citizens and calls them threats.

Ridge is 80 now, recovering from his stroke, watching the department he built with patriotic purpose transformed into something unrecognizable. His 2023 words—full of optimism about DHS’s mission and dedication—now read like an epitaph for a vision that died with Renee Good and Alex Pretti on Minneapolis streets.

The Transformation: Three Stages of Mission Creep

The shift from “keeping us safe” to “domestic terrorism” didn’t happen overnight. It occurred in stages, each seemingly logical at the time, each moving DHS further from its counterterrorism mission toward something else entirely.

Stage One (2001-2014): Counterterrorism Dominance

For DHS’s first decade, the counterterrorism mission dominated. America’s immigration politics were divisive, as they’d always been, but DHS’s border and immigration agencies operated largely in the shadow of counterterrorism work.

The color-coded terror-threat levels became cultural touchstones. DHS officials gave public briefings on threats to the United States. Every year, the Secretary, along with the FBI Director and the head of the National Counterterrorism Center, testified before Congress about worldwide threats.

Border security was part of the mission—preventing terrorists from entering the country was obviously crucial. But it was framed as part of the broader counterterrorism portfolio, not as an end in itself. Immigration enforcement existed, but it wasn’t what DHS was about.

The department succeeded at its stated mission: There has been no major foreign terrorist attack on U.S. soil since 9/11. By 2014, as Stewart Baker, the first DHS policy chief under Bush, told Miroff: “People tend to think that’s yesterday’s problem. We have lost the sense, as a country, that DHS is carrying out a protective mission that is in the interest of all Americans.”

Success bred complacency. And complacency created space for mission creep.

Stage Two (2014-2017): Immigration Rises

In 2014, everything changed. Central American families and unaccompanied children began arriving at the southern border in unprecedented numbers, fleeing violence in Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador. The surge overwhelmed border facilities designed for single adult men from Mexico.

Images of children in chain-link detention facilities shocked the nation. The humanitarian crisis demanded response. DHS’s border and immigration agencies, previously secondary to counterterrorism work, suddenly received massive attention and resources.

Congressional hearings focused increasingly on immigration enforcement rather than terrorism. Budget debates centered on detention facilities and deportation capacity. The agencies that handle immigration—ICE and Border Patrol—grew in size, visibility, and political importance.

But the transformation remained incomplete. DHS still maintained its broader mission. The counterterrorism portfolio continued. Other agencies—TSA, FEMA, Coast Guard, Secret Service—still performed their functions. Immigration enforcement was growing, but it hadn’t yet consumed the department.

That changed with Donald Trump.

Stage Three (2017-Present): Complete Weaponization

Stewart Baker, Bush’s first DHS policy chief, traces the complete transformation to Kirstjen Nielsen, who became DHS Secretary in late 2017. Nielsen had a background in cybersecurity and had served as chief of staff to General John F. Kelly, Trump’s first DHS Secretary.

When Nielsen testified before Congress, lawmakers’ questions focused overwhelmingly on immigration enforcement—particularly Trump’s family separation policy, which deliberately separated thousands of children from their parents to deter asylum seekers. Nielsen would sometimes joke, Baker recalls, that she welcomed inquiries about any other DHS topic.

But the joke masked a grim reality: DHS had become, in the public mind and political discourse, the Department of Immigration Enforcement. Not homeland security. Not counterterrorism. Immigration.

“Nobody could take that job after that without expecting to be the face of immigration policy,” Baker told Miroff. “That was going to be your job, and if you didn’t want to do that, then you really shouldn’t be taking the job.”

The transformation accelerated through Trump’s first term and reached completion in his second. By 2025, DHS’s primary function—the reason it dominated headlines, received Congressional scrutiny, and consumed political oxygen—was immigration enforcement. Counterterrorism became an afterthought. Protecting Americans from foreign threats became secondary to removing immigrants from American communities.

And then came the New Year’s Eve post: “America After 100 Million Deportations.”

This wasn’t border security. This wasn’t even immigration enforcement as traditionally understood. This was ethnic cleansing as aspiration, posted on official government social media, cheered by millions.

Tom Ridge’s department—created to protect all Americans—was now fantasizing about removing one-third of them.

Calling Americans “Terrorists”: The Complete Perversion

On an unseasonably warm Wednesday in Minneapolis, ten blocks from George Floyd Square, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot Renee Nicole Good three times. Good was a 37-year-old American citizen, a mother, someone her own mother described as “one of the kindest people I’ve ever known.”

Video from multiple angles shows Good attempting to leave after masked federal agents surrounded her vehicle and gave conflicting orders. The video shows Ross positioning himself in front of her vehicle, firing as she tried to drive away, and then leaving the scene. Federal agents blocked bystanders—including a physician—from providing medical care for 15 minutes.

Within hours, before any investigation, DHS called Good a “domestic terrorist.”

Secretary Noem amplified: Good was “stalking and impeding” agents, “weaponized her vehicle,” and committed an “act of domestic terrorism.” Vice President Vance repeated the characterization. Trump called her a terrorist. The entire administration, in unison, applied the label.

For Americans who remember the visceral trauma of September 11, “terrorism” carries particular weight. It describes people who hijack planes and fly them into buildings. People who plant bombs in public spaces. People who murder thousands of innocents to advance political goals through violence and fear.

Renee Good was driving a Honda Pilot SUV with a glove box full of stuffed animals, trying to leave a situation where federal agents surrounded her car.

Tom Warrick, a former DHS policy official who worked under Presidents Bush, Obama, and Trump, told Miroff: “Terrorism is such a powerful label. Applying that to Renee Good just defies the average American’s understanding of what that term ought to mean.”

The perversion is complete. DHS was created to protect Americans from terrorists. Now it calls Americans terrorists to justify killing them.

By 2021, domestic terror threats had surpassed foreign ones, according to the FBI. Mass shootings motivated by racism and hate killed dozens in El Paso, Pittsburgh, Charleston, and elsewhere. After the January 6, 2021 attack on the Capitol, FBI Director Christopher Wray called it an act of domestic terrorism—an unprecedented step.

But even then, DHS officials remained hesitant to apply “terrorism” to Americans. They preferred “domestic violent extremism” to avoid calling citizens terrorists. The label was reserved for genuine threats—organized groups, planned violence, intent to terrorize.

Until Renee Good. Until DHS decided that an American mother trying to leave a chaotic situation qualified as “domestic terrorism” because she might have bumped an agent’s leg with her vehicle while fleeing.

The gap between Ridge’s vision and Noem’s application couldn’t be wider. From “thank you for keeping us safe” to “she’s a domestic terrorist for trying to leave.”

The Technology of Oppression

Seth Stodder was crossing the Queensborough Bridge in New York City when a plane hit the North Tower on September 11. A young attorney with a background in trade policy, he arrived at the federal courthouse in Lower Manhattan three blocks away when the towers fell.

Stodder joined the government two weeks later. He went on to work for Customs and Border Protection under Bush and DHS under Obama, remaking U.S. border policy to tighten security without choking off trade and travel. He helped build the systems, acquire the technology, and establish the authorities that DHS would use to secure America’s borders.

Now teaching law at the University of Southern California, Stodder watches those same tools being deployed against Americans in American cities, and he’s horrified.

“In creating DHS, we didn’t want to create the KGB or the FSB,” he told Miroff, referring to Soviet and Russian secret police agencies.

But the technology DHS acquired to protect borders is now being used domestically against citizens:

  • Surveillance drones originally deployed to monitor border crossings now hover over American neighborhoods during immigration raids

  • Facial recognition software designed to identify people entering the country illegally now scans Americans’ faces in their own communities

  • Phone-cracking software purchased to investigate terrorists now extracts data from Americans’ devices without warrants

  • License plate readers installed at borders now track Americans’ movements in their own cities

  • Tactical units trained for border operations now conduct raids at Target stores, hospitals, and apartment buildings

These tools were acquired with the promise they’d be used against foreign threats, subject to constitutional constraints and oversight. Over the years, at conferences and in classrooms, Stodder received questions about potential civil liberties threats from America’s expanding surveillance capabilities.

“My answer to questions was always: Well, we have a constitutional framework here,” he said. Due process, Fourth Amendment protections, judicial oversight—these would prevent abuse.

That answer now seems naive. “You have these institutions and these technologies and these authorities,” Stodder told Miroff, “then some guy like Trump or Stephen Miller can say, ‘Hey, we could use this stuff to do something that nobody’s ever done before in the United States.’”

“To suddenly see DHS become this kind of mechanism of authoritarian intimidation and incipient fascism is disorienting, and frightening,” Stodder said.

Then he added: “It makes me think that maybe DHS was a bad idea.”

The Fears From 2001: Realized

When DHS was created, critics—including many on the political right—feared exactly this: an all-encompassing domestic security apparatus that could become an authoritarian monster. The United States had never had the kind of interior ministry common in autocracies, whose domestic security departments function as political police.

Those fears were swept aside by the broader anxieties of Americans desperate for protection from another 9/11-style attack. The FBI remained within the Justice Department, partly to assuage concerns that DHS could become too powerful and omniscient. But the consolidation went forward.

For twenty years, those fears seemed unfounded. DHS prevented terrorist attacks. Its agencies served legitimate functions. The constitutional framework held. The fears of 2001 looked like overreaction born of post-9/11 paranoia.

Then Trump returned, and every warning proved prescient.

DHS now operates as exactly what critics feared: a massive federal police force deployed domestically for political purposes, using counterterrorism tools against Americans, labeling citizens as terrorists, taking orders from a president who uses it to punish states whose leaders criticize him.

The constitutional framework Stodder trusted? Insufficient. The oversight mechanisms? Bypassed or ignored. The institutional norms? Demolished. The careful distinction between domestic and foreign threats? Erased.

Baker, Bush’s policy chief, attributes the transformation partly to Democrats’ “evolving attitudes toward immigration enforcement.” He describes “an uneasy sense that there is something racist about preferring the people who live here over people who don’t.”

That opposition to once-uncontroversial immigration enforcement has spread, Baker argues. “It’s not just a principle of the Democratic Party. It’s now a fighting principle in the streets.”

But this analysis misses the crucial distinction: Opposition to immigration enforcement has intensified precisely because enforcement has changed from targeting dangerous criminals at borders to grabbing people with no criminal records in Target stores, shooting American citizens who try to leave, and calling mothers “domestic terrorists.”

The opposition isn’t to immigration enforcement. It’s to what immigration enforcement has become under Trump and Noem: authoritarian, unaccountable, lethal, and aimed at anyone who lacks perfect documentation—including American citizens.

“Fucking Bitch”: Twenty Years of Service Ends Here

After Jonathan Ross shot Renee Good, as her Honda careened into a parked car, a voice was heard on the audio—reportedly Ross’s—saying: “Fucking bitch.”

But before he shot her, Ross did something revealing: He recorded her on his phone.

According to the Washington Post’s reporting, Ross had his phone out, filming Renee Good in her vehicle, before he reached for his weapon and fired three times. He documented the encounter—then killed her.

Ross had twenty years of federal service at that point. He joined the Border Patrol in 2007, working near El Paso targeting smuggling organizations and drug traffickers. He joined ICE in 2015, became a member of the elite Special Response Team, served on combat patrol in Iraq with the Indiana National Guard.

By all traditional measures, Ross was an ideal DHS officer: experienced, trained, decorated, trusted with the agency’s most dangerous missions.

And his twenty-year career in homeland security—twenty years of serving the mission Tom Ridge envisioned—ended with him recording an American mother on his phone, shooting her three times, and calling her a “fucking bitch” as she died.

Ross documented the encounter before killing Renee Good. He had his phone out, filming her, before he reached for his weapon. Twenty years of DHS service taught him to record the evidence—then produce it—then kill anyway.

Seven months earlier, Ross had been involved in another vehicle incident. A man dragged Ross 100 yards with his car. Ross was examined medically, cleared physically, and returned to duty without any psychological evaluation—just self-certification that he was mentally fit.

JD Vance later admitted Ross was “sensitive” about vehicles after being dragged. Yet DHS deployed him to Minneapolis, where he positioned himself in front of Renee Good’s vehicle and fired as she tried to leave.

That’s what DHS has become.

Not “thank you for keeping us safe.”

Not protection from foreign threats.

Not counterterrorism or border security or any of the missions Ridge imagined.

Just a traumatized agent with inadequate training, deployed to terrorize a community, killing an American citizen and cursing her corpse while federal officials call her a terrorist and threaten military occupation of the state whose leaders object.

Reform or Dismantle?

Jeh Johnson, Obama’s DHS Secretary, was asked about renewed calls to abolish ICE. His answer invokes a familiar argument: “If you don’t like the Vietnam War, you don’t get rid of the Department of Defense.”

It’s a reasonable analogy, and for agencies serving legitimate functions, it’s persuasive. You reform them, change their leadership, fix their policies—but you don’t eliminate institutions that serve necessary purposes.

But what happens when an agency’s actual function becomes the opposite of its stated purpose?

DHS was created to protect Americans from terrorism. It now calls American citizens terrorists. It was created to unite Americans against foreign threats. It now deploys paramilitary forces against Americans in American cities. It was created to keep Americans safe. It now kills Americans and lies about it.

At what point does “reform” become insufficient?

Seth Stodder—who helped build DHS’s border security systems, who dedicated years to making them work effectively and constitutionally—now says: “Maybe DHS was a bad idea.”

Stewart Baker—Bush’s first DHS policy chief, an architect of the department—watches the current transformation with evident discomfort, defending some changes while acknowledging others have gone too far.

Tom Ridge, the founding Secretary who insisted “we don’t do politics in the Department of Homeland Security,” watches from retirement as his department posts fantasies about deporting 100 million people and calls American mothers “domestic terrorists.”

The counterterrorism mission that justified DHS’s creation has been superseded. The protection of Americans that was its founding purpose has been perverted into terrorizing Americans. The constitutional framework that was supposed to constrain it has proven inadequate against determined authoritarianism.

Johnson is right that you don’t eliminate the Department of Defense because of Vietnam. But you do eliminate agencies when their actual function becomes antithetical to their stated purpose. You do dismantle institutions that have been captured by authoritarians and twisted into weapons against the people they were created to protect.

The question isn’t whether to reform ICE’s tactics or adjust DHS’s priorities. The question is whether an agency that calls American mothers “domestic terrorists,” deploys combat troops in retail stores, and fantasizes about ethnic cleansing can be reformed at all—or whether it has become exactly what critics feared in 2001 and must be dismantled before it does further damage.

Conclusion: The Test We Failed

This picture of Renee Nicole Good, who was shot and killed on Wednesday by a U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agent in Minneapolis, was posted to her mother's Facebook page. (Donna Ganger/Facebook)

When Tom Ridge stood up DHS in 2001, he built it on a promise: This agency will protect all Americans, without politics, focused on genuine threats to our safety. The department would have awesome power—surveillance technology, federal police forces, authority to operate domestically—but it would be constrained by constitutional limits, institutional norms, and the shared mission of keeping Americans safe.

Twenty-three years later, on January 7, 2026, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot Renee Nicole Good three times in Minneapolis. She was trying to leave. He was positioned in front of her vehicle. He fired as she attempted to drive away. He left the scene. Federal agents blocked medical care. She died.

And the Department of Homeland Security—created to protect Americans—called her a domestic terrorist.

Tom Ridge’s vision died with Renee Good on that Minneapolis street. The department he built to unite Americans against foreign threats has become a weapon deployed by an authoritarian president against Americans who criticize him. The technology acquired to secure borders is being used to surveil citizens in their own communities. The federal police forces created to prevent terrorism are terrorizing American cities.

The constitutional framework wasn’t strong enough. The institutional norms weren’t resilient enough. The oversight mechanisms weren’t robust enough. The distinction between foreign threats and domestic dissent wasn’t clear enough.

We failed the test. The fears from 2001 were justified. DHS has become exactly what critics warned it could become.

And now we face the choice: Reform an agency that has proven itself fundamentally opposed to its founding mission? Or acknowledge that some institutions, once corrupted this completely, cannot be salvaged?

Tom Ridge isn’t here to answer that question. He’s in retirement, recovering from a stroke, watching the department he built with patriotic purpose transformed into something unrecognizable.

But I suspect he knows the answer. You can hear it in the contrast between his words—”We don’t do politics in the Department of Homeland Security”—and DHS’s actions today. Between “thank you for keeping us safe” and “she’s a domestic terrorist.” Between protecting Americans from threats and calling Americans threats.

Seth Stodder’s conclusion echoes through the years: “Maybe DHS was a bad idea.”

Maybe it was. And maybe Renee Nicole Good—an American citizen killed by an agency created to protect her—has finally proven it beyond doubt.
