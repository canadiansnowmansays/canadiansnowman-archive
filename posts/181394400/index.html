The One Who Can’t Escape

Why John Bolton Faces 18 Counts While Trump’s Circle Walks Free

December 12, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays
December 12, 2025

BREAKING: Yesterday, December 11, 2025

For the second time in a week, a grand jury refused to indict Letitia James.

Seven days earlier, citizens in Norfolk, Virginia rejected the Justice Department’s case. Yesterday, after prosecutors went grand jury shopping to Alexandria—exactly what Abbe Lowell warned they would do—a second set of citizens said no.

Two grand juries. Two cities. One week. Both refused.

The New York Times called it “a striking rejection of the administration’s retribution campaign.” CNN noted the Justice Department’s “unusual strategy of pursuing second indictments despite earlier failures.” AP wrote that the “back-to-back failures” show prosecutors would “face major hurdles in bringing charges against President Trump’s foes.”

Three attempts. Three failures. Judge dismissal, Norfolk grand jury rejection, Alexandria grand jury rejection.

But while Letitia James has now been protected three times—by a federal judge and by ordinary citizens in two different cities—John Bolton remains trapped.

James Comey is free. His case was dismissed for illegal prosecutor appointment. Letitia James is free. Grand juries in two cities refused to indict her despite the DOJ shopping for a friendlier venue.

But John Bolton? He’s still facing 18 Espionage Act counts. At 76 years old, he cannot leave the United States. His passport was seized over two months ago. His trial is “at least a year” away, meaning he’ll spend 2026 in legal limbo while his legal bills mount into the millions.

His crime? Sharing diary entries with his wife and daughter.

The FBI has known about these diary entries since 2021. The Biden Justice Department investigated and declined to prosecute. Then Trump took office, reopened the case, and suddenly sharing your personal notes with family became 18 federal felonies.

But here’s the cruelest irony: While Bolton faces potential decades in prison for diary entries, Trump and his entire inner circle who actually took classified documents face no consequences at all.

Welcome to the enemies list’s most grotesque double standard.

The Bathroom Versus the Diary

Let’s be precise about what we’re comparing.

Donald Trump:

  • Took hundreds of classified documents to Mar-a-Lago

  • Stored TOP SECRET/SCI materials in a bathroom and ballroom

  • Documents included nuclear weapons information and war plans

  • Showed classified military attack plans to unauthorized people at his golf club

  • Told an interviewer “these are the papers” while waving classified documents

  • Refused to return documents when subpoenaed

  • Directed employees to move boxes to hide them from FBI

  • Instructed his IT director to delete security footage

  • Charged with 40 federal counts

  • Result: All charges dismissed by Judge Aileen Cannon

John Bolton:

  • Kept personal diary entries from his time as National Security Advisor

  • Shared them with his wife and daughter

  • FBI knew about them since 2021

  • Biden DOJ investigated: no charges filed

  • Trump reopened the case in 2025

  • Charged with 18 Espionage Act counts

  • Result: Still facing trial, passport seized, cannot leave country

One man hoarded America’s most sensitive secrets in a bathroom next to a photocopier. The other shared his personal diary with his wife.

One walks free. The other faces potential life in prison.

But It’s Not Just Trump

The Trump double standard is obvious. But look at everyone else in Trump’s orbit who took classified materials—and faced no consequences.

And while we document this list, remember: yesterday, the Justice Department tried for the third time to prosecute Letitia James. They went to Alexandria after Norfolk rejected them. The Alexandria grand jury also refused. Meanwhile, everyone on this list below walks free:

Mike Pence:

  • Classified documents found at his Indiana home in January 2023

  • Included materials from his time as Vice President

  • Result: No charges. Case closed after he “cooperated.”

Mike Pompeo:

  • As Secretary of State, reportedly took materials related to his duties

  • Details remain murky, but no public investigation

  • Result: No charges. No investigation announced.

Steve Bannon:

  • Multiple reports of document retention issues

  • Result: Silence. No prosecution.

Mark Meadows:

  • Text messages and documents related to January 6

  • Questions about handling of classified materials

  • Result: No Espionage Act charges.

Now add the Trump family:

Jared Kushner:

  • Senior advisor who dealt with highly classified intelligence

  • Questions about document handling never seriously investigated

  • Result: No charges.

Ivanka Trump:

  • Also a senior advisor with security clearance

  • Used personal email for official business

  • Result: No consequences.

The pattern is unmistakable: If you were in Trump’s inner circle, you can take documents with impunity. If you criticized Trump, even keeping a diary shared with family can become a federal crime.

Why Bolton Can’t Fight Back Like Comey and James

When Comey and James were indicted, they had multiple lines of defense. Bolton has fewer options.

What Worked for Comey and James:

  1. Illegal Appointment: Lindsey Halligan was unconstitutionally appointed. Judge Currie dismissed both cases on this ground alone.

  2. Grand Jury Corruption: Judge Fitzpatrick documented “profound investigative missteps.” The grand jury never saw Comey’s final indictment.

  3. Grand Jury Rejection: Citizens in Norfolk refused to re-indict James after seeing the evidence.

  4. Statute of Limitations: Comey’s charges were time-barred once the invalid indictment was thrown out.

Bolton’s Weaker Position:

  1. No Illegal Appointment Issue: Bolton’s prosecutors in Maryland were properly appointed. No Judge Currie to dismiss on constitutional grounds.

  2. Different Jurisdiction: He’s in Maryland, not Virginia. Different judges, different grand juries, different prosecutors.

  3. Espionage Act Charges: These are more serious than perjury (Comey) or mortgage fraud (James). The statutory penalties are severe.

  4. No Statute of Limitations: The Espionage Act has no statute of limitations for retention charges. They can prosecute him indefinitely.

  5. National Security Veneer: While Comey and James charges were obviously political, Bolton’s involve “classified information,” giving prosecutors a national security pretext.

Bolton has one major argument: vindictive prosecution. He can cite the Trump double standard, the pattern documented in Parts 8-11 of this series, and the fact that Biden’s DOJ already investigated and closed this case.

But proving vindictive prosecution is notoriously difficult. He’ll need to show:

  • The prosecution was motivated by Trump’s animus toward him

  • The decision to prosecute was influenced by improper considerations

  • The charges wouldn’t have been brought absent the political motivation

The evidence is there. Trump has publicly called for Bolton’s prosecution for years. He posted “Bolton is guilty as hell” on September 20, 2025. The Biden DOJ investigated these exact diary entries and found no case. Trump reopened it within weeks of taking office.

But will a Maryland judge be as willing as Judge Currie to rule against the administration? Will a Maryland grand jury show the same courage as the Norfolk citizens who refused to indict James?

Those are open questions. And Bolton’s freedom may depend on the answers.

The Human Cost: 76 Years Old and Trapped

Here’s what John Bolton’s life looks like right now:

Since October 17, 2025:

  • Cannot leave the United States

  • Passport surrendered to federal authorities

  • Cannot travel even for medical treatment abroad

  • Cannot visit family members in other countries

  • Cannot fulfill speaking engagements overseas

  • Cannot conduct foreign policy work that defined his career

Legal Proceedings:

  • Trial scheduled for “at least a year” away (likely late 2026)

  • Facing 18 separate counts under the Espionage Act

  • Each count carries potential 10-year sentence

  • Legal fees with Abbe Lowell: easily $2+ million over course of case

  • Pretrial motions, discovery, security clearances, classified information procedures

At 76 years old:

  • Health concerns that require him to stay near his doctors

  • Every month in legal limbo is another month of his limited remaining time

  • The stress of facing potential life in prison for sharing diary with wife

  • Watching everyone around Trump walk free while he faces decades behind bars

Bolton is a wealthy man. He can afford Abbe Lowell. He can pay the legal bills. But he cannot buy back his freedom to travel. He cannot purchase the year of his life that will be consumed by this prosecution. He cannot erase the fact that at an age when most people are enjoying retirement, he’s facing the possibility of dying in federal prison.

For sharing his diary with his wife and daughter.

The Chilling Effect Is Working

Since Bolton’s indictment on October 17, something has changed in Washington.

Former officials are making different choices. The word is spreading: if you keep records of what you saw in government service, those records can be weaponized against you years later.

What’s happening:

  • Diary destruction: Former officials who kept detailed notes are reportedly destroying them rather than risk future prosecution

  • Memoir silence: Publishers report fewer former Trump officials willing to write tell-all books

  • Lawyer consultations: Record numbers of former officials seeking legal advice about what materials they kept

  • The Bolton question: “What happened to Bolton?” is now asked in every conversation about documenting government service

The fear is rational. If Bolton can face 18 Espionage Act counts for diary entries the FBI knew about since 2021, then:

  • Can your memoir notes become evidence?

  • Can your personal calendar entries be called classified?

  • Can sharing government anecdotes with your spouse become transmission of national defense information?

  • Can keeping a journal to process the stress of government service make you a criminal?

The answer, apparently, is yes—if you criticize Trump.

This is the chilling effect in action. Not the theoretical chilling effect that civil liberties advocates warn about. The actual, documented, measurable chilling effect where people change their behavior because they’ve watched the government prosecute someone for something that was obviously legal when they did it.

The unwritten history: How many future memoirs will never be written because of what happened to Bolton? How much truth about Trump’s second term will be lost because people are afraid to document it?

We’ll never know. That’s the point.

The Maryland Test: Will His Defense Work?

Bolton’s legal team, led by Abbe Lowell (who also represents Letitia James), now has powerful ammunition.

What happened in Virginia:

  1. Judge Currie found Lindsey Halligan illegally appointed (Part 8)

  2. Judge Fitzpatrick documented grand jury corruption (Part 10)

  3. Norfolk grand jury refused to re-indict James (Part 11)

  4. The entire enemies list pattern was exposed and rejected

What Bolton’s defense can argue:

“Your Honor, we present this as evidence of vindictive prosecution. The Department of Justice:

  • Investigated Bolton’s diary in 2021 and closed the case

  • Reopened it after Trump publicly demanded prosecution

  • Has now seen two political prosecutions collapse in Virginia

  • Saw a federal judge document ‘profound investigative missteps’

  • Watched a grand jury refuse to re-indict despite political pressure

  • Is pursuing my client while Trump, Pence, Pompeo, and others who took actual classified documents face no charges

This is not law enforcement. This is revenge. The pattern is documented. The double standard is clear. The Virginia cases prove that when prosecutors are politically motivated, even grand juries recognize it and refuse to participate.

This prosecution should be dismissed.”

The question: Will Maryland judges be as willing as Virginia judges to call out political prosecution?

Judge Cameron McGowan Currie was brought in from South Carolina specifically to review the Comey and James cases. She’s a Clinton appointee with a reputation for independence. She didn’t hesitate to rule against the Trump administration.

Bolton’s case is assigned to judges in Maryland. Will they show the same courage? Or will they defer to prosecutorial discretion and let the case proceed?

The stakes: If Bolton’s vindictive prosecution defense fails in Maryland, it establishes that the pattern documented in Virginia won’t protect targets in other jurisdictions. It means the enemies list can continue operating—just not in Virginia.

If it succeeds, it means the dam has broken. It means judges across the country are willing to recognize political prosecutions and dismiss them regardless of which administration brings them.

We’ll find out in 2026.

The Cruelest Irony

Step back and consider the complete picture:

The people who worked for Trump and took documents:

  • Trump: bathroom full of nuclear secrets → charges dismissed

  • Pence: classified materials at home → no charges

  • Pompeo: document questions → no investigation

  • Bannon, Meadows, Kushner, Ivanka: various issues → silence

The people who criticized Trump:

  • Comey: allegedly lied about a conversation → indicted, then dismissed for illegal prosecutor

  • James: paid mortgage early → indicted, then grand jury refused to re-indict

  • Bolton: shared diary with wife → 18 Espionage Act counts, still facing trial

The enemies list isn’t just about who gets prosecuted. It’s about the grotesque disparity in how similar conduct is treated based solely on whether you’re loyal to Trump.

And the cruelest irony? The closer you were to Trump, the more you saw, the more dangerous you become.

Bolton wasn’t some outside critic. He was Trump’s National Security Advisor. He sat in the Situation Room. He attended the most sensitive meetings. He saw Trump’s incompetence, his ignorance, his corruption up close.

And he told the truth about it.

That’s why he’s facing 18 counts while Trump’s loyalists walk free. Not because he committed worse crimes. Not because he endangered national security. But because he saw too much and refused to stay silent.

The message to future insiders could not be clearer: You can steal classified documents with impunity if you stay loyal. But keep a diary and tell the truth? We’ll destroy you.

What Comes Next

Bolton’s case will proceed slowly through the Maryland federal court system. Pretrial motions. Discovery battles. Classification reviews. Security clearance issues.

Each step will cost him money, time, and freedom. Each month that passes is another month he cannot travel. Another month closer to trial. Another month of his life consumed by this prosecution.

Key dates to watch:

  • Pretrial motions: Bolton’s team will file to dismiss on vindictive prosecution grounds. This is the critical moment. If the judge agrees, the case ends. If not, it proceeds to trial.

  • Discovery: Bolton’s lawyers will demand documents showing the decision-making process behind reopening this case. Who ordered it? When? What role did Trump’s public statements play?

  • Trial (late 2026?): If it gets this far, Bolton will face a jury. Will they see through the political motivation? Or will the “classified information” veneer convince them?

Meanwhile:

  • Comey is free, his case dismissed

  • James is free, a grand jury having refused to indict her

  • Trump, Pence, Pompeo, and the rest face no consequences

  • And Bolton sits unable to leave the country, facing potential life in prison for diary entries

Conclusion: The Enemies List’s Perfect Crime

The enemies list has evolved.

When Comey was indicted, judges caught the illegal appointment. When James was first re-indicted in Norfolk, a grand jury said no. When prosecutors went shopping to Alexandria yesterday, a second grand jury also refused. The system found a way to reject the most blatant political prosecutions—three times for James alone.

But Bolton’s case is more carefully constructed. The prosecutors are properly appointed. The charges invoke national security. The jurisdiction is different. The timeline avoids the statute of limitations problems that doomed Comey’s case. And most importantly: there’s no grand jury standing between him and trial because he was indicted back in October before the pattern became clear.

This is the enemies list learning from its mistakes. This is political prosecution 2.0: just careful enough to avoid the constitutional and procedural problems that killed the earlier cases, but still fundamentally about revenge against a critic.

And it might work.

That’s the terrifying part. Bolton could go to trial. He could be convicted. He could spend his final years in federal prison—not for selling secrets to enemies, not for leaking to journalists, but for sharing his personal diary with his wife and daughter.

While Trump and everyone around him who took actual classified documents walk free.

That would be the enemies list’s perfect crime: a conviction that looks legitimate on paper, achieved through proper procedures and proper prosecutors, against someone who technically violated a law—even though the only reason he was prosecuted is because he told the truth about Donald Trump.

Three critics. Three prosecutions. Two escaped through multiple protections. One remains trapped.

Comey: dismissed for illegal prosecutor. James: dismissed by judge, rejected by Norfolk grand jury, rejected by Alexandria grand jury. Bolton: still facing 18 counts with no such protections.

John Bolton’s case will test whether our justice system can recognize political prosecution even when it’s dressed up in proper legal clothing and doesn’t have the obvious flaws that doomed the others.

If the Maryland courts dismiss this case, it will prove the system can still identify revenge prosecutions and reject them.

If they don’t, it will prove that careful enough execution can turn the Espionage Act into a weapon against anyone who criticizes the president—as long as you find some technical violation to charge and avoid the procedural mistakes that killed the Comey and James cases.

Bolton’s freedom—and the future of political dissent in America—may depend on which way Maryland goes.

This is the twelfth essay in “The Enemies List” series. While Comey escaped through constitutional protections and James was protected by citizen courage in two different cities, Bolton faces the most dangerous version of political prosecution: one that learned from earlier failures and might actually succeed.
