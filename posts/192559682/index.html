The Charlie Kirk Debates

#28: Systematic Dehumanization of Opponents - Part II: The Challenge

April 2, 2026

Challenge #1: Words Create Permission Structures, Not Just Atmospheres

Kirk occasionally noted that his rhetoric was not calls to violence. This disclaimer does not engage with how dehumanization actually works.

Research on political violence consistently shows that direct incitement (”go hurt these people”) is not required for rhetoric to create violence. What is required is a permission structure: language that positions a group as non-human, threatening, or evil reduces the psychological barriers to violence against that group among the most radicalized listeners.

The person who attacked Paul Pelosi was motivated by right-wing conspiracy theories about Democrats—theories Kirk had been amplifying. The January 6th mob was motivated by the stolen election narrative Kirk had been promoting. The mass shooters who cited Great Replacement theory were motivated by the demographic replacement narrative Kirk endorsed. In each case, the connection was not Kirk ordering violence but Kirk’s rhetoric creating the cognitive framework in which violence became logical.

This is why Kirk’s disclaimers (”I’m not calling for violence”) are insufficient. The question is not whether he ordered violence. The question is whether his rhetoric—demons, satanic opponents, immigrant rapists, child-corrupting alphabet mafia—created permission structures for violence. The evidence that it did is documented in multiple acts of real-world harm.

Challenge #2: The “Alphabet Mafia” Label Violates Every Standard Kirk Claimed

Kirk claimed to believe in human dignity grounded in the image of God (imago dei)—the theological principle that every human being possesses inherent dignity as a creation of God. He cited this principle in opposition to abortion. He invoked it in discussions of poverty. He claimed Christian values of love and truth-seeking as his foundation.

The “alphabet mafia” label is incompatible with imago dei. Mafia members are criminals. They are not people deserving of dignity—they are organizations deserving of prosecution. By applying this label to LGBTQ+ Americans, Kirk categorically denied the dignity of an entire population. This includes gay Americans, the children of gay Americans, transgender veterans, and the LGBTQ+ children of his own audience who would hear themselves described as criminals in league with an organized crime syndicate.

Kirk’s God-given human dignity apparently had exceptions. Those exceptions are the definition of conditional dignity—which is not dignity at all.

Challenge #3: The Demonic Framework Makes Democracy Impossible

Democracy requires, at minimum, that you treat political opponents as fellow citizens with legitimate standing to disagree. You don’t have to like them, agree with them, or vote for them. But democratic politics assumes that they are human beings in good faith attempting to advance their understanding of the public good—and that when they win elections, you accept the result and try again next time.

Kirk’s “doing the bidding of Satan” framework eliminates this minimum requirement. If your opponents are demons, accepting their electoral victories is capitulation to evil. If the progressive coalition is a satanic force of civilizational destruction, then resisting by extraordinary means is not just permissible—it’s holy obligation.

This is precisely the psychological framework that produced January 6th. The participants who attacked police officers and stormed the Capitol were not, in their own minds, engaged in insurrection. They believed they were fighting satanic forces. Kirk’s rhetoric contributed to building that belief system, even if he did not order the attack.

Challenge #4: The Immigrant Rapist Framing Has No Empirical Foundation

Kirk’s characterization of immigrants as men who “will go into your communities and rape your women and take your children” is not honest description—it is fear fabrication unsupported by evidence.

Multiple studies, including research from the libertarian Cato Institute, consistently find that immigrants—including undocumented immigrants—commit crimes at lower rates than native-born Americans. A 2020 study in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences found that undocumented immigrants are substantially less likely to be arrested for most crimes than native-born citizens. Immigration researchers attribute this to the rational incentive structure: immigrants, especially undocumented ones, have strong reasons to avoid police contact.

Kirk’s “rape your women” framing is a direct echo of Donald Trump’s 2015 campaign announcement (”They’re bringing crime. They’re bringing drugs. They’re rapists.”) and has the same factual foundation: none. It is not description. It is propaganda—designed to trigger fear responses that bypass rational evaluation of actual immigration data.

Challenge #5: The Christian Standard Kirk Violated

Kirk invoked Christianity constantly as the foundation of his values and his vision for America. He cited Scripture, led prayer at events, called America a Christian nation, and positioned his movement as defending Christian civilization.

The Christianity he claimed as his standard contains explicit teachings on how opponents, enemies, and strangers should be treated.

Matthew 5:44: “Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you.” Luke 10:27: “Love your neighbor as yourself.” Matthew 25:35: “I was a stranger and you welcomed me.” Galatians 3:28: “There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus.”

The New Testament contains no passage supporting “alphabet mafia,” “immigrant rapists,” “doing the bidding of Satan,” or calling George Floyd a “scumbag.” It contains extensive passages about loving strangers, dignifying the poor, and the equal standing of all people before God.

Kirk selected from Christianity the elements that served his political project and discarded the elements that conflicted with it. The resulting “Christianity” is not a recognizable version of the faith he claimed as his foundation—it is political tribalism dressed in scriptural language.

Challenge #6: State Senator Wiener Was Right

After Kirk’s assassination, California State Senator Scott Wiener wrote: “Charlie Kirk did not deserve to die. Also Charlie Kirk was a vile bigot who did immeasurable harm to so many people by normalizing dehumanization.”

Wiener’s statement was condemned as inappropriate post-death criticism. But it described an observable pattern that Kirk’s supporters cannot explain away: Kirk normalized language that characterized specific populations as subhuman, criminal, demonic, and deserving of divine punishment. This normalization had real consequences for LGBTQ+ youth who heard their existence described as delusion and God’s perfect law suggested execution, for immigrant families who heard themselves described as rapist invaders, and for political opponents who heard themselves described as satanic agents requiring not persuasion but defeat.

Conclusion

Charlie Kirk claimed human dignity as a foundational value. He invoked imago dei. He positioned Christianity as the moral foundation of his movement.

He then spent years calling LGBTQ+ Americans a mafia that corrupts children, characterizing political opponents as demonic agents of Satan, describing immigrants as rapists who would invade American homes, dismissing a man’s murder with “scumbag,” and invoking God’s perfect law as justification for executing gay people.

These are not honest descriptions. They are systematic dehumanization—stripping targeted populations of the humanity required for empathy, moral consideration, and democratic standing. Whatever else Kirk’s legacy contains, this pattern contradicts every value he claimed as his foundation.

He was wrong. The evidence proves it.
