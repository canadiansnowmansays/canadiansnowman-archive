Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 3: The Long Fight for Freedom — and Its Betrayal (1868–1898)

March 2, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Carlos Manuel de Céspedes (1819–1874). On October 10, 1868, he freed his slaves, handed them machetes, and started a war. Cuba has never fully stopped fighting it.

On October 10, 1868, a sugar planter named Carlos Manuel de Céspedes walked to the slave quarters on his estate near Bayamo, in eastern Cuba, and freed every enslaved person he owned. Then he rang the bell of his sugar mill — the sound that normally summoned workers to begin the grinding day — and delivered a speech to the assembled crowd of freedmen, farmhands, and neighbours. Spain had ruled Cuba for 370 years, he said. The time had come to end it.

This moment — which Cubans call El Grito de Yara, the Cry of Yara — is one of the founding acts of Cuban national identity. Céspedes was not the only conspirator or the only planter willing to risk everything, but he was the one who moved first, and the symbolism of his action — a wealthy landowner simultaneously freeing his enslaved workers and declaring war on the colonial power — gave the independence movement a moral coherence it might otherwise have lacked. It said, in effect, that this struggle was not just for the freedom of Cuba’s white criollo elite. It was for everyone.

What followed was thirty years of intermittent war — a cost in human life and suffering that is staggering by any measure — culminating in an outcome that was, for most of the people who had sacrificed so much, a profound betrayal. Cuba fought for its independence and got something considerably less. The power that supplanted Spain was not Cuban. It was American. And it would prove, in its own way, just as damaging to the aspiration that had driven Cubans to sacrifice so much.

The Ten Years’ War (1868–1878)

The war that Céspedes launched in October 1868 would last a decade and claim somewhere between 200,000 and 250,000 lives — a figure that, for a population of approximately 1.4 million, represents a demographic catastrophe. It was fought almost entirely in eastern Cuba, in the mountains and forests of what is now Oriente province, where the Spanish held the cities and the Cuban Liberation Army — the Ejército Libertador — controlled the countryside.

The Cuban forces were a genuinely remarkable coalition. They included white criollo landowners like Céspedes, free Black Cubans who had lived under the constant surveillance and suspicion of the colonial system, and formerly enslaved people whose freedom Céspedes and others had made central to the independence cause. The general who became the war’s greatest military hero, Antonio Maceo Grajales, was the son of a free Black Cuban father and a mixed-race mother. His tactical brilliance was matched by his moral clarity: he believed unshakeably that a free Cuba must be a Cuba of racial equality, and he said so, loudly, even when it made powerful white Cubans uncomfortable.

The Spanish response was merciless. General Valeriano Weyler — who would later become infamous for his tactics in the second independence war — served in Cuba during the Ten Years’ War and helped develop the scorched earth strategies that the Spanish would deploy on a larger scale later. Villages suspected of supporting the rebels were burned. Crops were destroyed. Civilians caught between the two forces suffered enormously.

The rebels, for their part, had their own brutal strategy: they would carry the war westward, into the sugar heartland of central and western Cuba, destroying the sugar economy that sustained Spanish colonialism. This westward invasion — the invasión — never fully materialized during the Ten Years’ War, partly because of military setbacks, partly because of political divisions within the Cuban independence movement itself. Céspedes, for all his historic significance, was a difficult man — autocratic, prone to dismissing advisers who challenged him — and the Constituent Assembly of the rebel government eventually removed him from the presidency. He died in a Spanish ambush in 1874, fighting alone on a hillside, his cause still unresolved.

By 1878, after ten years of war, both sides were exhausted. The Pact of Zanjón ended the fighting on terms that gave Cuban rebels amnesty and promised reforms — more political representation, gradual emancipation of the enslaved — without delivering independence. Most of the Cuban rebel leaders accepted the peace, calculating that reform was better than continued bloodshed.

The monument to Antonio Maceo in Santiago de Cuba. The "Bronze Titan" fought in both the Ten Years' War and the War of Independence, surviving twenty-six wounds before being killed in battle in 1896 — two years before the Americans arrived to claim his victory.

Antonio Maceo did not. He met with the Spanish commander and delivered what became known as the Protest of Baraguá — a declaration that he would not accept any peace that did not include full independence and the immediate abolition of slavery. It was a gesture of magnificent futility that changed nothing militarily. But it established a principle that would define Cuban political culture for generations: there are things that cannot be surrendered, regardless of the cost.

The Interwar Years and José Martí

The decade and a half between the Ten Years’ War and the resumption of full independence struggle was not peaceful. There was a shorter uprising — the Little War — in 1879–1880, which was quickly suppressed. Slavery was finally abolished in Cuba in 1886, eight years after the Pact of Zanjón — not through the revolutionary upheaval that Cubans had fought for, but through gradual Spanish reform. The formerly enslaved became nominally free, but with no land, no capital, and no political power, their freedom was largely theoretical.

Meanwhile, in New York City, a Cuban exile named José Martí was building something remarkable.

Martí is Cuba’s great poet-hero — its Simón Bolívar and its Abraham Lincoln compressed into a single, slight, extraordinarily intense figure. He had first been arrested by the Spanish at sixteen for sedition, imprisoned, put to forced labour in the Havana quarries, exiled. He spent much of his adult life in exile in Spain, Guatemala, Venezuela, and the United States, writing poetry and political journalism, organizing the Cuban exile community, and thinking deeply about what kind of Cuba was worth fighting for.

His politics were unlike those of most revolutionary leaders of his era. He was anti-imperialist in a sense that explicitly included the United States — he wrote, with prescience that bordered on prophecy, about the danger of trading Spanish colonialism for American domination. He was passionately committed to racial equality, arguing that there was no such thing as a Black Cuban or a white Cuban, only Cubans — a position that was genuinely radical in the 1880s and 1890s. He was suspicious of military caudillos and committed to civilian democratic governance. And he was an organizer of genius, who spent fifteen years carefully building the infrastructure — the Cuban Revolutionary Party, the networks of exile clubs and associations, the fundraising, the planning — for another attempt at independence.

He also wrote some of the finest poetry in the Spanish language, including the Versos Sencillos from which the lyrics of Guantanamera were later drawn. He was, by any measure, one of the extraordinary figures of the nineteenth century, and Cuba’s claim on his memory is one of the genuinely legitimate foundations of its national identity.

The Second War of Independence (1895–1898)

The war Martí had spent fifteen years preparing began in February 1895. Within months, the Liberation Army, now led by the aging Máximo Gómez and the indomitable Antonio Maceo, was carrying the conflict westward — achieving the invasión that had eluded the rebels in the Ten Years’ War — sweeping through the sugar heartland toward Havana, destroying estates and infrastructure, forcing the Spanish onto the defensive.

Martí landed in Cuba in April 1895, almost against the advice of the military commanders who felt he was more valuable as a political and organizational leader than as a soldier. On May 19, barely six weeks after landing, he was killed in a cavalry skirmish at Dos Ríos — charging Spanish lines on horseback, wearing a black suit, as if offering himself to history. He was forty-two.

José Martí (1853–1895). Poet, revolutionary, Cuba’s national hero. He predicted exactly what the United States would do with Cuba’s independence struggle — and died six weeks after returning to fight for it.

His death was catastrophic for the independence cause in ways that went beyond the loss of his talents. Martí had been the one figure capable of holding together the movement’s competing factions — the military men, the civilian politicians, the Black Cuban leaders, the exile community — and balancing its competing visions of what independence should mean. Without him, those tensions intensified. But the war went on.

Spain’s response, under General Weyler, escalated to systematic atrocity. Weyler implemented the reconcentración policy — the forced relocation of the rural Cuban population into fortified towns, or “reconcentration camps,” where they could not support the rebels. Hundreds of thousands of Cuban civilians were forcibly moved. The camps were massively overcrowded, with minimal food and sanitation. Disease spread rapidly. Historians estimate that somewhere between 150,000 and 400,000 Cuban civilians died in the reconcentration camps — of starvation, of dysentery, of yellow fever, of the cumulative effects of displacement and deprivation. Weyler was recalled after two years, partly because the atrocities were generating enormous international outrage, but by then the damage was done.

It was this outrage — amplified by the sensationalist American press, particularly the newspapers of William Randolph Hearst and Joseph Pulitzer — that began drawing the United States into the Cuban conflict. American public opinion, appalled by Spanish brutality and moved by sympathy for Cuban independence, was shifting toward intervention. American business interests, which had invested heavily in Cuban sugar and were watching the war destroy their assets, were calculating whether a change of colonial power might serve them better than continued Spanish rule.

The American Entrance and Its Consequences

The explosion that sank the USS Maine in Havana harbour on February 15, 1898, killing 268 American sailors, provided the pretext — and despite the historical uncertainty about its cause (a Spanish mine, an internal accident, something else entirely), the American press and public demanded war. “Remember the Maine!” became the rallying cry. In April, the United States declared war on Spain.

The Spanish-American War lasted four months. Spain was no match for American military and naval power, and the outcome was never genuinely in doubt. What was in doubt — and what the Cuban independence movement failed, catastrophically, to anticipate — was what American victory would mean for Cuba.

The Cubans had been fighting for thirty years. They had put an estimated 300,000 or more total dead into the independence cause across two wars. Their Liberation Army, under Generals Calixto García and others, had been fighting effectively for three years and had the Spanish largely pinned down when the Americans arrived. Cuban forces participated in the Santiago campaign — the Americans depended on Cuban intelligence and cooperation — but the final battles, including the famous charge up San Juan Hill by Teddy Roosevelt’s Rough Riders, were presented to the American public as American victories.

Theodore Roosevelt came to Cuba in 1898 to fight Spain and left having secured American dominance over the island instead. He called it liberation. The Cubans who had been fighting for thirty years called it something else.

More tellingly, when the Spanish surrendered Santiago, Cuban general Calixto García and his troops were barred from entering the city for the surrender ceremony. An American general accepted the Spanish surrender. The Cuban fighters who had bled for thirty years were not invited.

It was a preview of what was to come.

The Treaty of Paris, which ended the Spanish-American War in December 1898, transferred Cuba from Spanish to American control. The Cubans were not a party to the treaty. They were not consulted. They were not present. Their thirty years of revolutionary struggle had produced, in the end, a transfer of sovereignty from one outside power to another.

Cuba was not independent. Cuba was occupied.

The signing of the Treaty of Paris, December 10, 1898. Spain and the United States divided the spoils of the Spanish-American War. Cuba was not represented. The island's thirty-year independence struggle was settled by the two empires it had been fighting against.

The Meaning of the Betrayal

The Cuban independence movement had been driven by a vision — Martí’s vision, primarily — of a free, racially equal, democratic Cuba that would serve as a model for all of Latin America. It was an idealistic vision, perhaps an impossible one, but it had given the liberation struggle its moral force and its popular support.

What arrived in 1898 was something very different. American military occupation. American administrators making decisions about Cuban governance. And, most damagingly, the Platt Amendment of 1901 — a set of conditions imposed by the U.S. Congress on the new Cuban constitution that gave the United States the right to intervene in Cuban affairs at will, to maintain naval bases on Cuban soil (Guantánamo Bay, which remains in American hands to this day), and to approve or disapprove Cuban treaties with other nations. The Platt Amendment made Cuba a nominally independent country that was operationally a protectorate of the United States.

Antonio Maceo was not alive to see it — he had been killed in battle in December 1896, cut down by Spanish fire in a skirmish in Punta Brava. Martí was not alive to see it either. But the men who were alive to see it understood what had happened. Cuba had fought thirty years for independence and been handed a leash.

This betrayal is the foundation on which the Cuban revolutionary consciousness of the twentieth century was built. When Fidel Castro invoked the memory of Martí, he was invoking a figure who had explicitly warned about American imperialism. When the Revolution presented itself as the completion of 1895’s unfinished business, it was drawing on a genuine historical grievance — the thirty years of sacrifice that had been answered not with independence but with occupation.

Whether the Revolution actually delivered on those aspirations is a different question, and one this series will address. But the grievance itself was real. The betrayal of 1898 was real. And the Cubans who lived through it, who had sacrificed so much for something that turned out to be so much less than promised, had every reason to feel that the island always paid, and the island was never allowed to keep what it had earned.
