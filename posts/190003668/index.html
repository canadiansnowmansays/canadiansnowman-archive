The Contribution Index

Introduction

Who Actually Helps? A Framework for Measuring What Nations Give to Humanity — and What They Take Away

March 6, 2026

Costa Rica’s protected rainforests and cloud forests exemplify the “Costa Rica Principle” — world-class biodiversity stewardship on modest resources.

In early March 2026, a content creator named Way Yuhl posted a seventy-five-second video on his Substack, Cultural Perspective, that lodged itself in my brain and refused to leave.

His proposition was elegantly simple: What if we ranked nations not by GDP or military power, but by whether they make the world better? What if we treated nations as assets or liabilities?

Yuhl sketched the outlines of a framework — five human metrics, five environmental metrics, equal weighting — and offered three quick examples. Costa Rica as an asset. Indonesia as mixed. Russia as a liability. Then he drove to his sign-off.

It was a provocation, not an analysis. A napkin sketch, not an architectural drawing. And yet the question he raised is, I think, one of the most important questions we can ask in 2026: Is your nation making the total sum of humanity, and the planet, better or worse?

I want to thank Way Yuhl for planting the seed. What follows is my attempt to grow it into something more: a series of essays that applies a rigorous, transparent, evidence-based framework to twenty nations — ten that are genuinely contributing to human and planetary wellbeing, and ten that possess the resources and capacity to lead but are choosing not to.

The series unfolds as two interleaved countdowns, building from number ten to number one in each list. Like any good countdown, the tension increases as the stakes rise. The early entries are solid but relatively straightforward. The later entries are where the contradictions deepen, the debates intensify, and the conclusions hit hardest.

Which nations made which list? You will have to read to find out. But I will tell you this: some of the answers will surprise you. And at least one of them will make me deeply uncomfortable to write.

This is The Contribution Index. And it starts here, with the methodology.

Why This, Why Now

We live in an era that measures national success almost exclusively by dominance. GDP. Military spending. Geopolitical influence. The metrics we celebrate tell nations that power is the point — that the goal of sovereignty is to accumulate enough leverage to impose your will on others.

The result is a world where the wealthiest nation in human history ranks 132nd on the Global Peace Index while accounting for 43 percent of global arms exports. Where a former superpower, sitting on vast natural resources and world-class scientific talent, has become the least peaceful nation on Earth. Where nations lift hundreds of millions from poverty while building surveillance states and expanding coal production. Where petrostates accumulate sovereign wealth on a scale that could reshape entire continents while suppressing half their own populations.

Power rankings tell us who dominates. They tell us nothing about who helps.

The Contribution Index asks a different question: What is your nation actually doing for humanity and the planet? Not what could you do. Not what do you claim to do. What are the measurable, evidence-based outcomes of your national choices?

And crucially: How do those outcomes compare to your capacity?

The Capacity-to-Contribution Gap

This last point is the moral architecture of everything that follows, so let me be explicit about it.

A nation like Chad or Haiti scoring poorly on any global index is not a story. It is an obvious consequence of colonialism, resource extraction, structural disadvantage, and — in many cases — deliberate exploitation by wealthier nations. To rank struggling nations alongside their exploiters and call them both “underperformers” would be intellectually dishonest and morally grotesque.

The Contribution Index does not do this.

Instead, the second half of this series focuses on what I call the Capacity-to-Contribution Gap: the distance between what a nation could contribute to humanity given its wealth, institutions, education, infrastructure, and natural resources, and what it actually contributes.

To make the capacity assessment transparent rather than impressionistic, I derive it from four measurable factors: GDP per capita (economic resources available), total GDP (absolute scale of potential impact), tertiary education rates (human capital), and institutional quality as measured by the World Bank’s Governance Indicators (ability to execute). A country that ranks in the global top fifteen across these four dimensions has no excuse for a low contribution score. It is a nation making choices. Those choices have consequences for every other nation on Earth, and they deserve scrutiny.

This is punching up, not down. And it is — I believe — the most important distinction this series makes.

The Framework: Twenty Parameters, Four Categories

The Contribution Index evaluates nations across twenty parameters organized into four categories: Human Development, Environmental Stewardship, Systemic Behaviour, and The Neighbour Test. Each parameter is scored on a scale of 1 to 10, yielding a maximum possible score of 200.

The scoring draws on data from established international indices — the UN Human Development Index, the Yale Environmental Performance Index, the Economist Intelligence Unit’s Democracy Index, the Good Country Index, the Global Peace Index, and SIPRI’s arms transfer database, among others — but the framework is my own. No existing index captures all four dimensions, and none applies the Capacity-to-Contribution lens that I believe is essential to honest assessment.

Each parameter is scored on both level (where a nation stands today) and trajectory (the direction and pace of change over the past three to five years). A nation at 40 percent renewable energy and accelerating earns a higher score than one at 50 percent that has stalled. Trajectory matters because a static snapshot rewards inertia and punishes nations that are genuinely improving from a lower baseline. Where relevant, the individual essays will note when level and trajectory diverge significantly.

Category One: Human Development (5 parameters, maximum 50 points)

Life expectancy and healthcare access. Are your people living long, healthy lives? Is healthcare available to all citizens or only to those who can afford it? This metric looks at both outcomes (life expectancy, infant mortality, maternal mortality) and access (universal coverage, out-of-pocket costs, rural availability).

Educational attainment and access. Not merely university enrolment rates, but literacy, equity of access across gender and socioeconomic lines, and the quality of educational outcomes. A nation where the wealthy receive excellent education while rural populations remain functionally illiterate scores differently from one where educational opportunity is broadly distributed.

Democratic governance and civil liberties. Press freedom, rule of law, judicial independence, voting rights, protection of minority rights, and government accountability. The Economist’s Democracy Index provides a useful baseline, but this parameter also considers trajectory — is democratic governance strengthening or eroding?

Poverty reduction trajectory. Not just current poverty rates but direction of travel. A nation with 30 percent poverty that has halved its rate in two decades tells a fundamentally different story from one with 15 percent poverty that has stagnated for a generation. Movement matters as much as position.

Gender equality and minority rights. How a nation treats its most vulnerable populations tells you nearly everything about its actual values, as opposed to its stated ones. This parameter examines legal protections, economic participation, representation in governance, and lived outcomes for women, ethnic minorities, Indigenous populations, LGBTQ+ communities, and religious minorities.

An iconic image from Bhutan: Taktsang Monastery clinging to sheer rock face — vibrant prayer flags framing the misty Himalayan backdrop, a visual of peaceful orientation toward humanity and the planet.

Category Two: Environmental Stewardship (5 parameters, maximum 50 points)

Renewable energy share and trajectory. Where you are matters, but where you are heading matters more. A nation at 40 percent renewable energy and accelerating scores differently from one at 60 percent that has plateaued. The Yale Environmental Performance Index provides foundational data here.

Emissions per capita. Absolute emissions favour small countries unfairly. Per capita measurement levels the playing field and reveals the true carbon footprint of national choices. A nation of 5 million producing the same emissions as a nation of 300 million is making profoundly different choices per citizen.

Biodiversity and habitat protection. Percentage of land and marine areas under genuine protection — not “paper parks” that exist in legislation but not in enforcement — species trajectory, and the effectiveness of conservation programs.

Deforestation and land use. Are you expanding or contracting your natural capital? A nation that is steward to critical ecosystems like the Amazon or the Congo Basin carries particular weight here, because its choices affect not just its own citizens but the entire planet’s carbon cycle and biodiversity.

Water and air quality. What your citizens are actually breathing and drinking on a daily basis. This is where environmental policy meets human health, and where the consequences of industrial choices become most immediately visible.

Category Three: Systemic Behaviour (5 parameters, maximum 50 points)

This category measures the concrete, quantifiable ways a nation engages with the rest of the world. It captures what a nation does — the tangible outputs of its foreign policy, its economic architecture, and its military choices.

A note on scale: systemic behaviour is inherently harder to assess for small nations than large ones. A country of 5 million people cannot resettle refugees or fund development aid at the same absolute level as a country of 330 million. Where relevant, I adjust for this — scoring disposition and per-capita effort rather than raw volume. The essays will note where this scaling applies.

Arms exports and conflict contribution. Are you fuelling wars elsewhere? SIPRI data shows that the five largest arms exporters — the United States, France, Russia, China, and Germany — account for roughly 72 percent of all international arms transfers. Many of those weapons end up in conflict zones, prolonging wars and multiplying civilian casualties. A nation’s contribution to global peace is inseparable from its arms export policies.

Tax haven and corruption facilitation. Are you enabling global wealth extraction? Some of the world’s wealthiest and most “respectable” nations serve as conduits for money laundering, tax evasion, and kleptocratic wealth storage. This parameter examines not just domestic corruption but the degree to which a nation’s financial architecture enables corruption elsewhere.

Refugee and humanitarian contribution. When crises emerge — wars, famines, climate disasters — does your nation accept responsibility? Refugee acceptance per capita, humanitarian aid as a percentage of GDP, and the quality of asylum processes all factor into this assessment.

Adherence to international law and institutions. Does your nation participate in or undermine the rules-based international order? Treaty compliance, support for international courts, contributions to UN peacekeeping, and respect for multilateral agreements all reflect a nation’s commitment to collective governance.

Exploitation versus investment in developing nations. Is your foreign engagement extractive or developmental? Belt-and-road debt traps, resource extraction without local benefit, and trade agreements that primarily serve the wealthier party all count against a nation here. Genuine development investment, technology transfer, and equitable partnership count in its favour.

Category Four: The Neighbour Test (5 parameters, maximum 50 points)

This is the category that, I believe, makes the Contribution Index not just original but necessary — and it is the category that required the most deliberation in the design of this framework.

The first three categories measure what a nation does: how it develops its people, how it stewards its environment, and how it behaves internationally in concrete, measurable terms. The Neighbour Test measures something different: what a nation is. Its disposition. Its orientation toward the rest of humanity. The root character from which all other choices grow. Put simply: Systemic Behaviour scores what a nation does. The Neighbour Test scores why it does it — the underlying orientation that makes the actions predictable.

Every underperformer in this series shares a common trait that transcends their individual failings on arms, environment, or human development. It is the trait of treating the rest of the world as a resource to be extracted rather than a community to be served. “America First.” “The Chinese Dream.” Russia’s imperial revanchism. The specific expressions differ. The disposition is identical: we exist for ourselves, and the rest of you exist for our benefit.

Every top performer shares the inverse trait: a disposition toward solidarity, cooperation, and the recognition that sovereignty comes with responsibility — that the capacity to help creates the obligation to help.

The Neighbour Test gives this disposition the weight it deserves. It is scored across five parameters:

Multilateral orientation. Does the nation participate constructively in international institutions, or does it undermine, withdraw from, or weaponize them? Treaty participation, UN voting patterns, institutional support versus obstruction. A nation that builds multilateral frameworks earns high marks. A nation that dismantles them — especially ones it built — earns the lowest.

Response to global crises. When humanitarian emergencies, pandemics, or climate disasters strike, does the nation step forward or step back? This captures real-time disposition — what a country does when the moment demands solidarity rather than self-interest.

Treatment of allies and partners. Does the nation build relationships based on mutual benefit, or does it coerce, threaten, and extract? This is where tariffs imposed on allies, sovereignty threats against neighbours, and the weaponization of trade relationships all get scored.

Consistency of values versus interests. Does the nation apply its stated values consistently, or only when convenient? A nation that champions human rights while arming dictators, or that promotes democracy while embracing autocrats, scores low. This parameter captures hypocrisy as a measurable systemic behaviour — and it applies to top performers as well as underperformers.

Long-term global stewardship. Does the nation make decisions with future generations and the broader human community in mind, or does it optimize for short-term national advantage? Sovereign wealth funds, intergenerational climate commitments, and investment in global public goods reflect stewardship. Resource extraction, debt accumulation, and environmental degradation reflect its absence.

I want to be transparent: the Neighbour Test contains more editorial judgment than the other three categories. I have heard the critique that it risks coding “multilateral equals virtuous” without always quantifying net benefit. The objection is fair. Transactional realism has sometimes produced genuine global public goods — military deterrence has kept peace in certain regions, and reserve currencies provide global financial stability.

But disposition matters. A nation that builds alliances to share burdens is making a fundamentally different moral choice from one that builds alliances to extract leverage. The Neighbour Test captures that distinction across five measurable dimensions rather than a single subjective score — and where the distinction is genuinely ambiguous, the editorial essay for that nation will say so.

The Neighbour Test carries equal weight with each of the other three categories: 50 points out of 200. This is a deliberate architectural choice. I believe that how a nation orients itself toward the rest of humanity is not merely one factor among many — it is the factor that predicts all others. A nation that chooses solidarity will, over time, invest in human development, protect its environment, and behave constructively in international systems. A nation that chooses self-interest above all else will, over time, fail on all three — no matter how much wealth or capacity it possesses.

Twenty-one essays and twenty-one scorecards will test that proposition. I believe the evidence will bear it out.

A Note on Innovation Spillovers

One legitimate criticism of any contribution framework is that it risks under-counting what nations generate as a byproduct of their own self-interested activity. Some nations account for enormous shares of global research and development spending. Their institutions have invented or distributed technologies — from satellite navigation to vaccine platforms — that have lifted billions of lives.

The Contribution Index does not ignore this. But it distinguishes between intentional contribution — the deliberate choice to direct national resources toward global benefit — and incidental spillover — the byproducts of self-interested investment that happen to benefit others.

Both have value. But a nation that invents a world-changing technology while simultaneously arming 107 countries is making a different moral choice from one that invests in international development directly. The spillover is real. It is not, however, a substitute for choosing to help. The individual essays will address innovation contributions where they are significant, and readers can weigh for themselves whether those spillovers offset the deficits documented elsewhere in the scorecard.

On Weighting

The four categories of the Contribution Index carry equal weight: 50 points each, for a maximum of 200.

This is a deliberate architectural choice, and it deserves explanation.

A single parameter buried inside a larger category — worth, say, 10 points out of a 200-point index — would give a nation’s fundamental disposition toward humanity roughly 5 percent of the total weight. Less than water quality. Less than deforestation. Less than any individual metric. That would be mathematically indefensible given the analytical importance of what the Neighbour Test measures.

The evidence across twenty-one scorecards confirms why: the single most predictive factor in whether a nation contributes to humanity or exploits it is not any specific policy outcome but the underlying disposition toward global citizenship. Nations that see themselves as part of a community behave like it across every dimension. Nations that see themselves as solo operators behave like that across every dimension.

Giving the Neighbour Test equal categorical weight — 25 percent of the total — makes the framework match the thesis.

Within each category, all parameters carry equal weight: 1 to 10, no multipliers. A reader can look at any nation’s scorecard and immediately understand how every number was calculated. Transparency is not negotiable.

The Nordic Question

Norway’s fjords — pristine, hydropower-rich, and protected — help explain its ranking despite North Sea oil.

Let me address something that will become obvious as this series unfolds: the top ten is dominated by Nordic and European nations.

This is not Eurocentric bias. It is what the data consistently shows across every major international index — the HDI, the Democracy Index, the EPI, the Good Country Index, the Global Peace Index. The same cluster of nations appears near the top of virtually every measure of human and planetary wellbeing.

The more interesting question is why.

The nations that score highest on the Contribution Index share several structural characteristics: small to medium populations, strong social contracts, proportional representation electoral systems, post-war investment in multilateral institutions, high levels of social trust, robust press freedom, and — critically — the choice to direct national wealth toward collective wellbeing rather than military dominance or wealth concentration.

These are not accidents of geography or culture. They are policy choices, made over decades, often against significant resistance. And they are choices of disposition as much as of policy — the decision to see national success as inseparable from the wellbeing of others.

The question the Contribution Index asks is not “why do certain nations score well?” It is “why do so few nations with similar or greater resources make similar choices?”

That question — the question of choice, of capacity squandered or directed — is the engine of this entire series.

The Costa Rica Principle

Costa Rica’s cloud-forest canopy overlooking the Pacific — a visual reminder that contribution is about choices, not GDP per capita.

Before the countdown begins, I want to establish one foundational insight that will recur throughout the series.

Contribution is not correlated with wealth or military power.

There is a nation in Central America — five million people, a GDP per capita roughly one-fifth that of the world’s wealthiest democracy — that outperforms that democracy on nearly every metric that actually matters to human and planetary wellbeing. It has no military. It runs on 98 percent renewable energy. It provides universal healthcare. Its democracy is strengthening while others classified as “flawed” continue to erode.

If contribution were a function of resources, the wealthiest nation on Earth should score five times higher than this small Central American country. It does not. It scores significantly lower.

This single comparison is the thesis of this series in miniature: the choices nations make matter infinitely more than the resources they possess. And the nations with the most resources have the most to answer for when those choices serve only themselves.

I call this the Costa Rica Principle. You will see it demonstrated again and again across this series.

The Countdown Begins

The twenty-one essays that follow — plus a special edition and a concluding reflection — will each apply this framework to a single nation. Every essay follows the same three-part structure:

The scorecard — twenty parameters across four categories, the numbers laid bare.

The analysis — where the contradictions, trajectories, and hidden stories emerge from behind the numbers.

The editorial conclusion — where I step back and assess what kind of global citizen this nation actually is, what the aggregate score reveals, and what it conceals.

Top performers and underperformers will alternate throughout the series, building toward the twin climaxes at number one.

And because I believe that any framework worth applying to others must first be applied to yourself, the countdown begins with the nation I know best and care about most.
