The Kash Patel Files

The Reckoning

Part VI: Where This Ends

April 25, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

On April 10, 2026, the FBI director tried to log in to his computer, couldn't, and panicked — calling aides and allies to announce he'd been fired. He hadn't. It was a technical error. Illustration: CanadianSnowmanSays/Grok

On Friday, April 10, 2026, the director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation tried to log into an internal computer system and failed.

What happened next tells you everything you need to know about the man, the institution he leads, and the country that is depending on both.

Patel panicked. He became convinced that he had been locked out — that the White House had fired him. He began frantically calling aides and allies to announce that he was out. Nine people familiar with his outreach described the episode to The Atlantic‘s Sarah Fitzpatrick. Two of them described his behaviour as a “freak-out.”

News of the emotional outburst ricocheted through the bureau. Officials at FBI headquarters exchanged messages. In some corners of the building, there were expressions of relief. The White House fielded calls from the bureau and from members of Congress asking who was now in charge of the FBI.

It turned out that the answer was still Patel. He had not been fired. The access problem appears to have been a technical error. It was quickly resolved.

“It was all ultimately bullshit,” one FBI official said.

But the episode — trivial in its mechanics, devastating in its implications — was not bullshit at all. It was a diagnostic. It told the country what the country already suspected: that the director of the FBI is a man so consumed by paranoia, so disconnected from the institutional norms of the agency he leads, and so aware of his own precariousness that a login screen malfunction was enough to trigger a full-blown crisis of confidence — not just in himself, but in the chain of command of the nation’s premier law enforcement agency.

An FBI director who is capable of being undone by a password error is an FBI director who is not capable of leading 38,000 people through a war.

The Paranoia

Patel’s fear is not irrational. He has good reasons to believe his job is in jeopardy — and some of those reasons, as Fitzpatrick reported, have to do with what witnesses described as his bouts of excessive drinking.

He has watched the trajectory of his predecessors in Trump’s orbit. Jeff Sessions was the first senator to endorse Trump and was rewarded with the attorney general’s office. He was then publicly humiliated for months and ultimately pushed out because he did what the law required and recused himself from the Russia investigation. Kristi Noem was fired as DHS secretary. Pam Bondi — who, as Fitzpatrick and Ashley Parker reported, “did everything right, or at least everything Trump asked her to do” — was fired because Trump perceived her as lacking “smarts and guts.”

The pattern is clear: loyalty to Trump is a necessary condition for survival, but it is never a sufficient one. The president demands results that are often impossible, blames his subordinates when the impossible fails to materialise, and discards them when they become more liability than asset. “No one can succeed in this job,” someone close to the White House mused to the Atlantic reporters. “Why would anyone want this job?”

Patel’s specific vulnerabilities are accumulating. Trump has complained that Patel seemed unprepared for television appearances and that investigations the president directed him to pursue — particularly those targeting former Biden administration officials — have not moved quickly enough. The headlines about his use of the FBI jet, the whispers about his partying, and the growing file of public embarrassments have reached the West Wing. “They fear that Trump would react badly were he to focus on those storylines,” officials told Fitzpatrick.

Senior members of the Trump administration are already discussing who might replace him.

“We’re all just waiting for the word,” an FBI official said.

A former official called Patel “rightly paranoid.”

The Hacked Emails

In March 2026, hackers claiming affiliation with Iran's Ministry of Intelligence posted more than 300 emails from the FBI director's personal account — and signalled they were holding back more. Source reporting: The New York Times. Illustration: CanadianSnowmanSays/Grok.

The paranoia has a material basis beyond internal politics. On March 27, 2026, hackers claiming affiliation with Handala Team — a group associated with Iran’s Ministry of Intelligence and Security — posted more than 300 messages stolen from a personal Gmail account used by Patel. The emails were from 2010 to 2022, predating his time as FBI director.

The emails themselves were largely unremarkable — apartment hunting, travel bookings, job application materials from his years as a federal public defender in Miami. One contained photographs from what appeared to be a visit to Cuba in 2013.

But there were indications that the hackers were holding back additional material. The website displayed images of attachments not included in the initial release, including a version of Patel’s résumé from 2016 that listed a classified CIA award. The domain was registered by an entity that appeared to identify itself as the Kingdom of Tonga. Cybersecurity tools flagged the website as potentially capable of implanting malware on visitors’ devices. And despite the group’s claimed Iranian affiliation, the website was hosted on a Russian server — a country with extensive experience in hack-and-dump operations.

The FBI acknowledged the breach. “The information in question is historical in nature and involves no government information,” the spokesman said.

This may be true. It is also beside the point. The strategic significance of the hack is not what the emails contain. It is what the hack demonstrates: that a foreign adversary — one the United States is actively at war with — has compromised the personal communications of the man responsible for defending America against that adversary’s operations.

The inspector general’s warning about alcohol creating vulnerability to foreign exploitation takes on a different weight when the foreign adversary has already demonstrated its ability to penetrate the director’s digital life. What else do they know about his patterns, his habits, his vulnerabilities? What leverage might that knowledge provide? These are the questions that counterintelligence professionals are trained to ask. They are questions that the FBI’s counterintelligence professionals — at least those who haven’t been fired or reassigned — are presumably asking right now.

The irony is sharp enough to cut. The man who polygraphs his own agents to find out who has said something unflattering about him has been compromised by the very adversary he was supposed to be protecting the country against.

The Convergence

In the taxonomy of Trump-era firings, there are distinct failure modes. Sessions was fired for principled recusal — for doing the legally correct thing. Noem was fired for incompetence. Bondi was fired for insufficient results in carrying out impossible directives. Pete Hegseth — whose tenure as defence secretary has been its own parade of dysfunction — has survived, for now, primarily because the cost of replacing him during a war exceeds the cost of tolerating him.

Patel represents the convergence of all these failure modes, plus a fourth that is uniquely his own: personal incapacitation.

He has Sessions’ vulnerability to presidential displeasure — Trump has complained about his television appearances and the pace of political investigations. He has Noem’s incompetence — the premature tweets, the botched announcements, the inability to manage the bureau’s basic operations without melodrama. He has Bondi’s insufficient results — the investigations Trump ordered have not produced the convictions Trump wanted. And he has what The Atlantic has documented — the drinking, the absences, the breaching equipment, and the locked doors — a category of liability that none of his predecessors shared.

The betting, according to multiple sources in Fitzpatrick’s reporting, is not whether Patel will be fired but when. His own assessment appears to track with this: he has recently told people in his orbit that he intends to stay at least through the 2026 midterm elections, “while acknowledging that the president could change his mind at any time.”

This is not the language of a man who feels secure in his position. It is the language of a man who is counting the days.

The Pattern

There is a pattern that emerges when Patel feels the walls closing in, and it has been identified by reporters and former agents alike. Former FBI agent Rob D’Amico described it to MS NOW: “When he gets jammed up on something, he literally fires people right after.” The Atlantic‘s Quinta Jurecic noted the same pattern in the timing of the Department of Justice’s indictment of the Southern Poverty Law Centre, announced at a press conference days after Fitzpatrick’s story dropped — with Patel standing beside Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche in what was staged as a vote of confidence. Bad headlines, followed immediately by an action designed to give the MAGA base red meat. The indictment displaces the drinking. The firing displaces the jet. The spectacle displaces the substance.

Meanwhile, the substance only deepens. In a Radio Atlantic interview this week, Fitzpatrick said that since her story was published, she has been “inundated” with additional sources — “going up to the highest levels of the government” — offering corroborating information. “This was an open secret in Washington,” she said. The $250 million defamation suit was filed on Monday. By midweek, the reporter he is suing was fielding calls from officials who wanted to confirm everything she had written and add more.

Fitzpatrick described the current mood inside the FBI in terms that should stop every American in their tracks: “The entire building is panicking. The entire building is freaking out. People are just waiting for the moment when he will be fired, and it’s creating a lot of chaos and a lot of instability. There are really key, important, life-or-death decisions that need to be made.”

The reckoning, when it comes, will not arrive as a quiet resignation or a dignified departure. If the pattern holds, it will be preceded by escalation — more firings, more indictments, more spectacle, more aggression — because that is the only response Patel knows. When cornered, he does not retreat. He attacks. He sued The Atlantic for $250 million. He claimed, without evidence, that the FBI had proof the 2020 election was stolen. He announced arrests that may never materialise. Each escalation is a tell — not of strength, but of desperation.

The Institutional Wreckage

But the question that should preoccupy the country is not whether Patel will survive. It is what survives him.

The FBI that Patel will leave behind — whether he departs next week or next year — will be smaller, less experienced, less trusted by allies, less capable of performing its core mission, and more thoroughly politicised than at any point since J. Edgar Hoover.

The senior leadership pipeline has been severed. The agents and analysts who spent decades building expertise in counterterrorism, counterintelligence, and cybersecurity have been fired, reassigned, or driven out. The institutional knowledge they carried — the understanding of how threats are assessed, how investigations are built, how allies are managed, how constitutional boundaries are maintained — left with them.

The bureau’s international partnerships have been damaged. The British are furious about the broken promise on the London position and the attempted social media breach of Five Eyes security. The signals that Patel sends at every international engagement — that he would rather go jet skiing than attend intelligence briefings, that his staff’s priorities are meals, workouts, and entertainment — erode the trust that underpins intelligence cooperation between allies.

The domestic workforce is demoralised, frightened, and shrinking. Agents spend their days on LinkedIn instead of investigations. Recruiters cannot recommend the bureau to qualified candidates. The message of the purge — that you can be fired for any case, any friendship, any flag, any act of conscience — has produced exactly the culture it was designed to produce: one of silence, compliance, and diminished ambition.

And the threats have not paused to accommodate the bureau’s dysfunction. Iran’s Quds Force is still operational. Chinese intelligence is still stealing American technology and recruiting American assets. Russian hackers are still probing American infrastructure. Domestic extremists — the neo-Nazi group that one agent was investigating before he was reassigned to immigration enforcement — are still organising.

The coyote has run off the cliff. It has not yet looked down. But the ground is coming.

These Eyes

These eyes — wide, watchful, seeing enemies everywhere — have missed everything that mattered. Illustration: CanadianSnowmanSays/Grok.

There is an irony that runs through the entire Patel story, and it is this: the man who sees enemies everywhere has never been able to see himself.

He saw deep state conspirators in the career professionals who tried to tell him he was unqualified. He saw persecution in the judge who berated him for showing up to court without a tie. He saw betrayal in the speaker of the house who wouldn’t punish a deputy attorney general for pushing back on congressional overreach. He saw corruption in the FBI agents who investigated the president because they were assigned to investigate the president.

He saw enemies in every direction — and in seeing enemies everywhere, he missed the actual threats: to the institution he was entrusted to lead, to the country he was sworn to protect, and to his own capacity to do either.

J. Edgar Hoover — corrupt, paranoid, authoritarian — at least understood that the FBI’s power depended on its perceived competence. He built a machine that functioned, however perversely. He showed up for work. He knew where the files were. He maintained relationships with allies and adversaries alike because he understood that intelligence is transactional and that a reputation for reliability, however undeserved, is a strategic asset.

Patel has none of this. He has the authoritarianism without the competence, the paranoia without the cunning, the ambition without the discipline. He has built nothing, maintained nothing, and protected nothing — except his own fragile sense of importance, which he guards with polygraphs and SWAT teams and the reflexive aggression of a man who knows, somewhere behind those wide and watchful eyes, that he is in over his head.

“Part of me is glad he’s wasting his time on bullshit,” one FBI official told Fitzpatrick, “because it’s less dangerous for rule of law, for the American public. But it also means we don’t have a real functioning FBI director.”

He should be worried. He should be very worried.

But somewhere tonight, behind a locked door or at a private club or on a government jet bound for somewhere that has nothing to do with the safety of the American people, the director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation may not be worrying about anything at all.

And somewhere tonight, the people who mean this country harm are counting on exactly that.

The Canadian Security Intelligence Service has not, to my knowledge, produced a formal assessment of the American FBI director. But if they did — if they applied the sober, clinical, bureaucratic language of an allied intelligence agency to the open-source record of Kashyap Patel — the result might read something like what follows tomorrow.
