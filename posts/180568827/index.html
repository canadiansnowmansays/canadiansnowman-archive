Now This is (Not) Fake News

Part 7: The Coverup, The Lies, The Mockery

December 4, 2025

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted an AI-generated children’s book cover showing Franklin the Turtle firing on drug-carrying boats as he faces allegations that the U.S. military’s campaign against drug traffickers has committed extrajudicial murder and war crimes that target survivors (X/@PeteHegseth)

In Part 6, we documented the “kill everybody” order and the legal consensus calling it a war crime or murder. Now we examine the congressional investigations, the administration’s increasingly contradictory defenses, and the evidence they refuse to release.

The Congressional Response: Investigations Launched

Both the House and Senate Armed Services Committees announced immediate investigations. The statements are notable for their bipartisan nature—something increasingly rare in this political environment.

Senate Armed Services Committee (Chair Roger Wicker, R-MS, and Ranking Member Jack Reed, D-RI): “The Committee is aware of recent news reports—and the Department of Defense’s initial response—regarding alleged follow-on strikes on suspected narcotics vessels in the SOUTHCOM area of responsibility. The Committee has directed inquires to the Department, and we will be conducting vigorous oversight to determine the facts related to the circumstances.”

House Armed Services Committee (Chair Mike Rogers, R-AL, and Ranking Member Adam Smith, D-WA): “This committee is committed to providing rigorous oversight of the Department of Defense’s military operations in the Caribbean. We take seriously the reports of follow-on strikes on boats alleged to be ferrying narcotics in the SOUTHCOM region and are taking bipartisan action to gather a full accounting of the operation in question.”

Senator Wicker confirmed on December 1 that Hegseth told him there was a second strike. Wicker said his committee expects “full access to the audio and video of the strikes” as part of its investigation.

Senator Mark Warner, the top Democrat on the Senate Intelligence Committee, is coordinating with Chair Tom Cotton to arrange a briefing with Admiral Bradley. Warner called on the Trump administration to “release an unedited video of the strike to help determine whether these individuals were in the water, on the boat, still combatants or not.”

Admiral Bradley is scheduled to provide a classified briefing to lawmakers on December 5.

The Administration’s Evolving Defense: Contradictions and Scapegoating

The administration’s response has been a masterclass in deflection, contradiction, and blame-shifting.

The Crystal Clear Vision (September 3, One Day After the Strike): On Fox News, Hegseth was proud and specific: “I watched it live. We knew exactly who was in that boat. We knew exactly what they were doing, and we knew exactly who they represented. And that was Tren de Aragua, a narco-terrorist organization designated by the United States trying to poison our country with illicit drugs.”

The Fog of War (December 2, After Scandal Breaks): At a White House Cabinet meeting, Hegseth claimed: “I did not personally see survivors... because that thing was on fire and was exploded, and fire, smoke, you can’t see anything. You got digital, there’s — this is called the fog of war.”

Let that sink in. He went from “we knew exactly who was in that boat” to “you can’t see anything” in 90 days. As Lawrence O’Donnell noted on MSNBC: “There is no fog in Pentagon offices where Pete Hegseth was watching that boat. And there is no fog in that video that we have seen of that small boat destroyed by an American missile.”

The “I Left the Meeting” Defense: Hegseth’s new story is that he authorized the first strike, watched it live, then moved on: “I watched that first strike live. As you can imagine, at the Department of War, we got a lot of things to do. So I didn’t stick around for the hour and two hours, whatever... A couple of hours later, I learned that that commander had made the [decision] — which he had the complete authority to do. And by the way, Admiral Bradley made the correct decision to ultimately sink the boat and eliminate the threat.”

This is remarkable: Hegseth is claiming he was so busy he couldn’t stick around to see the results of the first lethal strike in this new military campaign. But he “stands by” Bradley’s decision to kill the survivors.

Trump’s “I Don’t Know Anything” Defense: Trump did what he always does when in trouble—claimed ignorance: “I didn’t know about the second strike. I didn’t know anything about people. I wasn’t involved, and I knew they took out a boat.” Then in the same breath: “I wouldn’t have wanted that, not a second strike,” while insisting the first strike was “fine.”

As Senator Rand Paul pointed out: “There’s a very distinct statement said on Sunday. Secretary Hegseth said he had no knowledge of this and it did not happen. It was fake news. It didn’t happen. And then the next day, from the podium of the White House, they’re saying it did happen. So either he was lying to us on Sunday or he’s incompetent and didn’t know what had happened.”

Paul knows they don’t have to choose between lying and incompetent. Hegseth has proved he’s capable of both.

White House Throws Bradley Under the Bus: On December 2, White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt confirmed there was a second strike but claimed the order came from Admiral Bradley, not Hegseth: “Secretary Hegseth authorized Adm. Bradley to conduct these kinetic strikes. Adm. Bradley worked well within his authority and the law directing the engagement to ensure the boat was destroyed and the threat to the United States of America was eliminated.”

Pentagon Internal Revolt: The Washington Post reported that Leavitt’s statement “elicited a furious backlash within the Defense Department, where officials described feeling angry at the uncertainty over whether Hegseth would take responsibility for his alleged role in the operation or leave the military and civilian staff under him to face the consequences.”

“This is ‘protect Pete’ BS,” one military official told the Post. Another said: “It’s throwing us, the service members, under the bus.” A separate person said some of Hegseth’s top civilian staff “appeared deeply alarmed about the revelations.”

The Protocol Change That Proves They Knew It Was Illegal: The Post also reported: “The protocols were changed after the strike to emphasize rescuing suspected smugglers if they survived strikes, according to three people.” Why would you change your protocol if the original approach was legal? The Pentagon’s own War Manual states: “Orders to fire upon the shipwrecked would be clearly illegal.” They changed the protocol because they knew the second strike violated their own rules.

George Will’s Assessment: Conservative dean George Will wrote in the Washington Post: “Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth seems to be a war criminal without a war... The killing of the survivors by this moral slum of an administration should nauseate Americans.”

A moral slum of an administration. That’s from the dean of conservative political writers, not a partisan Democrat.

The Franklin the Turtle Incident: A Defense Secretary Unserious About Accountability

As bipartisan outrage mounted, as legal experts called for war crimes investigations, as senators from both parties demanded answers, Pete Hegseth did something that perfectly encapsulates both his character and this administration’s approach to accountability.

On December 1, he posted to X an AI-generated image of Franklin the Turtle—the beloved Canadian children’s book character known for teaching preschoolers about kindness and friendship—dressed in military gear, standing on a helicopter, firing a rocket-propelled grenade at drug boats.

The caption: “For your Christmas wish list...”

The publisher of Franklin responded swiftly. Kids Can Press, the Toronto-based publishing house, issued a statement: “Franklin the Turtle is a beloved Canadian icon who has inspired generations of children and stands for kindness, empathy, and inclusivity. We strongly condemn any denigrating, violent, or unauthorized use of Franklin’s name or image, which directly contradicts these values.”

The Pentagon’s response? Chief Pentagon Spokesman Sean Parnell told CBS News: “We doubt Franklin the Turtle wants to be inclusive of drug cartels... or laud the kindness and empathy of narco-terrorists.”

Let that sink in. The Pentagon spokesperson responded to a children’s book publisher with a defense of using a cartoon turtle to mock concerns about potential war crimes.

Senator Mark Kelly, a retired Navy captain, captured the absurdity: “He is in the national command authority for nuclear weapons, and last night he’s putting out, on the internet, turtles with rocket-propelled grenades. I mean, have you seen this? This is the secretary of defense. This is not a serious person.”

Representative Adam Smith, ranking Democrat on the House Armed Services Committee, was more direct: “I’ve read books to my children, but not that one apparently. This is serious business. I mean, even if it’s justified, even if it’s right, seriously, you’re killing people. And this is your response to tweet out some joke about a cartoon turtle. I mean, we need a secretary of defense who understands the seriousness and the importance of the job that he has. We don’t have one.”

Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer called Hegseth a “national embarrassment” and described the Franklin meme as a “sick parody”: “Tweeting memes in the middle of a potential armed conflict is something no serious military leader would ever even think of doing. The only thing this tweet accomplishes is to remind the whole world that Pete Hegseth is not up to the job.”

The mock book cover has been viewed more than 2.5 million times. People have created their own versions: “Franklin Goes to the Hague,” “Franklin Violates the Geneva Convention,” “Franklin on Trial at the ICC.”

This is America’s Secretary of Defense. This is the man with authority over the world’s most powerful military. And his response to credible allegations of war crimes is a meme.

What We Still Don’t Know—And What That Tells Us

Here’s what the administration refuses to disclose:

1. The Execute Order: Congress has requested the written execute order Hegseth gave to Special Operations Forces. The administration has refused to turn it over. This document would show exactly what Hegseth ordered, in writing, before the strike occurred. Its absence from public view is deafening.

2. The Audio and Video: Senator Wicker says his committee expects “full access to the audio and video of the strikes.” As of December 2, that access hasn’t been granted. The administration has the footage. They watched it in real time. They saw the survivors clinging to wreckage. They saw the second strike. But they won’t release it.

3. The Radio Intercepts: One U.S. official told the New York Times that the military intercepted radio communications from one of the survivors to what the official said were “narco-traffickers.” If this communication exists, it should be easy to produce. It would justify Bradley’s claim that the survivors were still “legitimate targets.” It hasn’t been produced.

4. The Intelligence: What intelligence justified the strike? How did they know the boat carried drugs? How did they identify the occupants as members of Tren de Aragua or any designated terrorist organization? The administration says they “knew exactly who was in that boat.” They’ve provided no evidence.

5. The Evidence of Drugs: To date, not a single ounce of drugs has been recovered from any of the 22 boats destroyed. Not one. The administration claims the boats carried drugs, but every second strike conveniently destroys all evidence.

6. The Names: Who were the 11 people killed on September 2? What were their names? How old were they? Was there a 14-year-old on that boat? A 12-year-old? Hegseth told Fox News “we knew exactly who was in that boat.” If that’s true, release the names. Show the evidence. Prove they were Tren de Aragua members.

The refusal to provide evidence isn’t incidental—it’s central to the operation. Each second strike doesn’t just kill survivors; it destroys evidence. No one can now prove they weren’t carrying drugs. But critically, no one can ever prove they were carrying drugs.

This is deliberate evidence destruction in service of a “take no prisoners” policy that violates centuries of established law.

The Internal Dissent That Was Silenced

The skepticism about these strikes isn’t just coming from senators and legal experts. It’s coming from inside the military itself—and those voices are being systematically silenced.

NBC News reported that the senior military lawyer at Southern Command—the combatant command that oversees all these boat strikes—specifically told his superiors that the strikes were illegal. This JAG officer expressed concern that “strikes against people on boats in the Caribbean Sea and the Eastern Pacific could amount to extrajudicial killings and therefore legally expose service members involved in the operations.”

He was overruled and pushed aside from the decision-making process.

Think about that: The senior legal advisor whose job is specifically to ensure military operations comply with the law said these strikes were illegal. And he was removed from the process so the strikes could continue.

The Five Eyes Alliance Breakdown

The international consequences are already severe. The United Kingdom—America’s closest intelligence partner and part of the Five Eyes alliance—has stopped sharing intelligence with the U.S. about suspected drug trafficking vessels in the Caribbean.

CNN reported: “The United Kingdom is no longer sharing intelligence with the US about suspected drug trafficking vessels in the Caribbean because it does not want to be complicit in US military strikes and believes the attacks are illegal.”

The Five Eyes alliance—the United States, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand—is described as “the world’s oldest and most significant intelligence alliance.” It originated in World War II and for decades these five nations have freely shared intelligence about everything from Soviet spies to terrorism plots to drug trafficking.

The UK’s decision “marks a significant break from its closest ally and intelligence sharing partner and underscores the growing skepticism over the legality of the US military’s campaign around Latin America.”

When your closest ally, with whom you’ve shared intelligence seamlessly for 80 years, pulls out of intelligence sharing because they believe your operations are illegal and don’t want to be complicit in war crimes, that tells you everything about how serious this is.

The Retaliation Against Senator Kelly

The Pentagon announced it is investigating Senator Mark Kelly—a decorated retired Navy captain who flew 39 combat missions and spent 25 years in the Navy—for his participation in a video urging service members to refuse illegal orders.

The Pentagon said they are reviewing “serious allegations of misconduct” against Kelly and that he could be recalled to active duty for court-martial proceedings.

Kelly’s offense? He and five other members of Congress—all former military or intelligence officers—released a video reminding service members: “Our laws are clear. You can refuse illegal orders. You must refuse illegal orders.”

Trump responded by calling it “seditious behavior punishable by death” and said Kelly should be “executed” and “hanged.” He told people to “go get them”—sending a mob after sitting members of Congress.

In his first TV interview after the threat, Kelly told Rachel Maddow: “I said something that was pretty simple and non-controversial, and that was that members of the military should follow the law. And in response to that, Donald Trump said I should be executed, I should be hanged.”

Kelly captured the absurdity perfectly: “How does reciting the UCMJ... how is that in violation of the uniform code of military justice? It’s absurd.”

But Kelly refused to be intimidated: “I’ve had a missile blow up next to my airplane. I’ve been shot down, nearly shot down multiple times. I’ve flown a rocket ship into space four times. My wife Gabby Giffords, meeting with her constituents, shot in the head, six people killed around her. I’ve been stressed by things more important than Donald Trump trying to intimidate me into shutting my mouth and not doing my job.”

This is the pattern: Internal military lawyers who say it’s illegal get pushed aside. Allies who say it’s illegal stop sharing intelligence. Senators who remind troops they can refuse illegal orders face court-martial threats and execution rhetoric.

What Happens Next

Admiral Bradley’s classified briefing on December 5 will be crucial. Senators will have the opportunity to question him directly about the sequence of events, the orders he received, and the communications with Hegseth.

The White House has said Bradley “worked well within his authority and the law.” Bradley himself has been promoted to commander of U.S. Special Operations Command since September 2. Hegseth has declared his “100% support” for Bradley.

But legal experts are clear: if Bradley ordered the second strike specifically to kill survivors—whether following Hegseth’s broader directive or his own initiative—everyone in the chain of command could be liable for war crimes or murder.

The congressional investigations will continue, though their effectiveness depends on cooperation from an administration that has refused to provide the execute order, the audio and video evidence, and basic information about the intelligence that justified the strikes.

The 2026 Midterm Factor

Democratic control of the House after the 2026 midterms—currently projected by virtually all polling—would dramatically change the investigative landscape. Seth Moulton’s prediction that “Americans will be prosecuted for this” becomes more likely if Democrats gain subpoena power.

With Democratic control would come:

  • Real subpoena power, not requests the administration can ignore

  • The ability to hold Hegseth and Bradley in contempt if they refuse to cooperate

  • Public hearings that would force testimony under oath

  • Access to classified materials through the House Intelligence Committee

  • The political will to pursue accountability even if Republicans resist

We’re a year away from that possibility. The question is: how many more people will die in the Caribbean before then? The death toll went from 57 in late October to 83 by early December. That’s 26 more people killed in five weeks.
