The Helen Keller Administration

This is the latest installment in our “Blind Leading the Blind” series, which has documented systematic incompetence across Trump’s 2025-2026 administration. For the full analysis of the original 22 appointments, see our comprehensive report from May 2025, with updates and deep dives published throughout the year.

Introduction: Beyond Blindness

When we launched this series in May 2025, we chose the biblical metaphor of “the blind leading the blind” to describe an administration that systematically prioritized loyalty over competence. Ten months later, that metaphor feels insufficient. Blindness implies merely that you cannot see. What we’ve documented over the past year goes far beyond that.

This administration doesn’t just fail to see the damage it’s doing — it refuses to hear the warnings from experts, allies, courts, and the public. And when confronted with its own failures, it remains stubbornly, defiantly silent about accountability, choosing instead to blame Democrats, the media, the “deep state,” and anyone else within tweet-length reach.

They can’t see the consequences. They won’t hear the criticism. They don’t speak the truth.

They are blind, deaf, and dumb.

Which brings us, with sincere apologies to one of the greatest Americans who ever lived, to what I’ve come to think of as the Helen Keller Administration. And before anyone clutches their pearls at the comparison, consider this: Helen Keller, despite being literally blind, deaf, and unable to speak for much of her early life, became one of America’s most brilliant thinkers, a fierce political activist, a socialist, a suffragist, and a tireless advocate for the disabled, the poor, and the marginalized. She overcame her limitations through sheer determination, intellectual rigor, and moral courage.

Donald Trump’s appointees have none of those limitations — and none of those qualities.

Helen Keller couldn’t see or hear, but she understood the world with extraordinary depth and compassion. Trump’s nominees can see and hear perfectly well — they simply choose not to. Helen Keller’s condition was a tragedy she transcended. This administration’s condition is a choice they celebrate.

The real insult to Helen Keller’s legacy isn’t the comparison — it’s the fact that a woman who literally couldn’t see, hear, or speak demonstrated more awareness, more empathy, and more competence than the entire roster of officials we’re about to examine.

As we’ll see, the administration has continued to find new ways to validate our thesis. Today alone — February 25, 2026 — two more nominees sit before Senate committees: Casey Means, a wellness influencer with no active medical license, seeking to become America’s top doctor; and Steve Pearce, an oil industry veteran who once criticized Teddy Roosevelt for creating national parks, seeking to oversee 245 million acres of public land. Both are second choices, replacements for earlier nominees who were too controversial even for this administration.

And they are far from alone.

Since our last comprehensive update, the administration has nominated a Fed chair while criminally investigating the current one, put forward a State Department nominee who promotes “white culture” and replacement theory, and promoted the man who created the Signal group chat that leaked classified military operations to serve as America’s voice at the United Nations.

Let’s meet the new members of the Helen Keller Administration — where seeing, hearing, and speaking the truth are not just optional, they’re actively discouraged.

Casey Means: The Nation’s Doctor Can’t Practice Medicine

Deanna Donegan/The New York Times; Photographs by Getty

The Position: Surgeon General of the United States

The Hearing: Today, February 25, 2026

The Qualification: Wellness influencer, entrepreneur, ally of RFK Jr.

The Disqualification: No active medical license, didn’t complete residency, promotes vaccine skepticism

As these words are being written, Dr. Casey Means sits before the Senate Health, Education, Labor and Pensions Committee, making her case to become America’s top doctor. The hearing was originally scheduled for October 2025, but was postponed when Means went into labor — an irony not lost on critics who note that the woman seeking to lead 6,000 members of the U.S. Public Health Service Commissioned Corps couldn’t even manage to be present for her own confirmation hearing.

To be fair, going into labor is a perfectly valid reason to postpone a hearing. What’s less valid is everything else about this nomination.

Means, 38, graduated from the Stanford School of Medicine — a genuine credential that she has leveraged into a career that has progressively distanced her from actual medicine. She began a surgical residency at Oregon Health and Science University but dropped out in 2018, reportedly frustrated that she was “cutting people open instead of understanding why they were sick in the first place.” She then opened a small “functional medicine” practice in Portland that she “phased out” a year later. Her medical license lapsed in January 2024. She has not treated a patient in years.

What she has done is build a substantial following as a wellness influencer. She co-authored a best-selling book called “Good Energy” with her brother Calley Means, who serves as a senior adviser to Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. She co-founded Levels, a company that sells wearable glucose monitors. She promotes supplements, wellness products, and a philosophy that essentially tells Americans their doctors are the problem.

She titled one chapter of her book: “Trust Yourself, Not Your Doctor.”

This is the woman who wants to be the nation’s doctor.

The Hearing Reveals the Pattern

The early moments of today’s hearing crystallized everything. When asked whether she would encourage mothers to vaccinate their children, Means would not give a direct answer. When pressed on whether vaccines cause autism — a claim debunked by every major medical institution in the world — she replied that “science is never settled.”

Science, in fact, is settled on this question. The original 1998 study linking vaccines to autism, published by Andrew Wakefield in The Lancet, was retracted after investigators found Wakefield had manipulated data and violated ethical protocols. His medical license was revoked. Subsequent studies involving millions of children across multiple countries have found no link between vaccines and autism. The science is settled. What’s “never settled” is whether political appointees will acknowledge it.

Senator Bernie Sanders, the ranking member of the committee, captured the core concern: “I have very serious questions about the ability of Dr. Means to be the kind of surgeon general this country needs.”

Two former surgeons general have publicly stated Means is unfit for the role. Dr. Rich Carmona, who served under George W. Bush and had been a practicing physician for over 20 years when appointed, said Means lacks the credentials. Trump’s own first-term surgeon general, Dr. Jerome Adams, noted that the role requires a medical license — which Means does not have.

The Second Choice Problem

Means is Trump’s second pick for surgeon general. His first choice was Dr. Janette Nesheiwat, a Fox News medical contributor. Nesheiwat was withdrawn not because she was unqualified — she was, in fact, a practicing physician — but because Trump’s allies, particularly in the MAHA (Make America Healthy Again) movement, considered her insufficiently critical of mainstream medicine. She had, for instance, recommended COVID vaccines on Fox News.

In other words, the first nominee was too qualified. Too connected to actual medicine. Too willing to recommend things that doctors recommend.

So they found someone who wouldn’t make that mistake.

The Real Purpose

The surgeon general doesn’t make policy directly. But the position carries enormous influence over public health messaging. Past surgeons general leveraged the role to slash smoking rates, combat AIDS stigma, and address the loneliness epidemic. The power lies in the bully pulpit — in being a trusted medical voice that Americans listen to.

As Dr. Kathleen Hall Jamieson of the University of Pennsylvania’s Annenberg Public Policy Center observed, the surgeon general’s voice has historically spoken “for what we knew from the best available science.” It said, “I’m a doctor you can trust. I’m speaking for a kind of science that is grounded in our traditional understanding of how science works.”

Jamieson then posed the key question: “Now imagine that in this context, that voice was being used to say, ‘Drink raw milk.’”

Casey Means has spoken out against bans on raw milk. She has questioned childhood vaccine schedules. She has condemned birth control pills. She has told Americans not to trust their doctors. She has promoted supplements from companies that paid to sponsor her newsletter. And she has done all of this while not actually practicing medicine herself.

She is the Helen Keller Administration’s contribution to public health — someone who can see perfectly well that Americans need better health outcomes, but who refuses to hear what the scientific establishment says about how to achieve them, and who won’t speak clearly about whether vaccines are safe.

The Senate is expected to confirm her.

Steve Pearce The Man Who Wants to Sell Your Public Lands

Steve Pearce, the nominee for the Bureau of Land Management, during a news conference in 2023 in Albuquerque, N.M., when he was the chair of the state’s Republican Party.Credit...Susan Montoya Bryan/Associated Press

The Position: Director, Bureau of Land Management

The Hearing: Today, February 25, 2026

The Qualification: Founded an oil and gas drilling services company

The Disqualification: 4% lifetime environmental rating, voted to sell public lands below market value, criticized Teddy Roosevelt for creating national parks

While Casey Means answers questions about vaccines in one Senate hearing room, Steve Pearce sits in another, defending his fitness to oversee 245 million acres of federal land and 700 million acres of subsurface minerals. The Bureau of Land Management controls more American territory than any other federal agency — and Trump wants to hand it to a man whose career has been dedicated to extracting profit from that territory.

Pearce, 78, is a former Republican congressman from New Mexico and a decorated Air Force combat pilot. His military service is honorable and genuine. But his post-military career tells a different story: he founded an oil and gas drilling services company in the Permian Basin, one of the most productive petroleum regions in the world. He then entered politics, where he consistently voted to expand drilling, loosen environmental protections, and — most alarmingly — sell public lands to private interests.

The League of Conservation Voters, which tracks lawmakers’ environmental records, gave Pearce a 4% lifetime rating. Four percent. Out of a hundred. To put that in perspective, you could score higher by accidentally voting the wrong way on a single environmental bill.

The Record That Matters

Pearce’s legislative history reads like a wish list for the extractive industries:

In 2005, he voted for a provision that would have allowed the Bureau of Land Management to sell public lands containing minerals to companies below market value.

In 2012, he criticized former President Theodore Roosevelt — perhaps America’s greatest conservationist — for popularizing “big ideas of big forests and big national parks.” He called for reversing the “trend” of public ownership of land, particularly in the West.

In 2016, he cosponsored a bill to speed up the sale or exchange of lands managed by the very bureau he now seeks to lead.

In 2017, he voted for a House rules package that would have made it easier to sell off public lands.

He voted multiple times to restrict the Antiquities Act, the century-old law that allows presidents to create national monuments.

He introduced legislation to prevent any president from establishing national monuments in New Mexico — his own state.

Following the Money

Pearce’s financial disclosures reveal that he earned between $100,000 and $1 million from leasing “frac tanks” — storage containers for petroleum products. He and his wife also held investments in oil and gas leases in the Permian Basin and interests in more than a dozen energy companies, including American Electric Power and Chevron.

He has pledged to divest. As we’ve learned from this administration, pledges to divest are roughly as reliable as pledges to “drain the swamp.”

The Unusual Coalition

What makes Pearce’s nomination particularly notable is who’s opposing it. This isn’t just environmentalists and Democrats. An unusual coalition of hunters, military veterans, and outdoor recreation groups has organized against his confirmation.

The Vet Voice Foundation, made up of military veterans, has created a “Sell-Off Steve” website opposing his nomination. Zac Fort, president of the New Mexico Shooting Sports Association, put it bluntly: “Hunters and sport shooters across our state treasure these lands. We use them constantly and we do not want to see them sold off.”

These are not typical Democratic constituencies. These are gun owners, hunters, and veterans who understand that public lands belong to the public — not to oil companies, mining operations, or real estate developers. They are the people Trump claims to champion, and they are telling him this appointment is wrong.

Trump’s White House, naturally, isn’t listening.

The Second Choice (Again)

Like Casey Means, Pearce is Trump’s second pick for this position. The first choice was Kathleen Sgamma, president of the Western Energy Alliance. Sgamma was asked to withdraw after a memo surfaced in which she wrote that she was “disgusted” by Trump’s role in spreading the misinformation that led to the January 6 riots.

Note the pattern: the first BLM nominee was withdrawn not for being unqualified or corrupt, but for having expressed disgust at January 6. The replacement is someone who, on January 9, 2021, posted on social media: “God bless President Donald J. Trump. He will be our President FOREVER and no one can take that away from us.”

The loyalty test, once again, supersedes the competence test.

Kevin Warsh: The Fed Chair Nomination That Comes With a Criminal Investigation

Fed Candidate Kevin Warsh draws opposition from left and right / Bloomberg

The Position: Chair of the Federal Reserve

The Status: Nominated January 30, 2026; confirmation blocked

The Qualification: Former Fed governor, Stanford and Harvard credentials

The Complication: The administration is criminally investigating the man he’d replace

If Casey Means represents the administration’s assault on public health expertise and Pearce represents its assault on public lands, Kevin Warsh represents something potentially more dangerous: its assault on the independence of America’s central bank.

On the surface, Warsh is the most conventionally qualified nominee in this essay. He served on the Federal Reserve’s Board of Governors from 2006 to 2011, navigating the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression. He has a law degree from Harvard. He’s a visiting fellow at the Hoover Institution and a lecturer at Stanford’s business school. Trump called him “central casting.”

The problem isn’t Warsh’s credentials. The problem is everything surrounding his nomination.

The Powell Investigation

To understand the Warsh nomination, you must first understand what happened to Jerome Powell, the current Fed chair. Trump appointed Powell during his first term but grew to despise him for not cutting interest rates as aggressively as Trump demanded. In his second term, Trump’s attacks escalated from social media insults — calling Powell a “numbskull,” a “moron,” and a “jerk” — to something unprecedented in American history: the Department of Justice launched a criminal investigation of the sitting Federal Reserve chairman.

The investigation centers on cost overruns in the Fed’s $2.5 billion renovation of two Washington, D.C., office buildings. This is, to put it charitably, an extraordinarily unusual basis for a criminal probe of the nation’s chief central banker. Senator Thom Tillis, a Republican on the Banking Committee, observed that “if we’re going to accuse someone of criminal behavior because of a project overrun in federal government, we better start budgeting a lot more for prisons.”

The investigation’s real purpose is transparent: it’s a message. Comply with Trump’s demands on interest rates, or face criminal prosecution.

The Warsh Contradiction

Enter Kevin Warsh, nominated on January 30, 2026, to replace Powell when his term ends in May. Warsh has historically been a monetary policy “hawk” — someone who favors higher interest rates to combat inflation. This would ordinarily put him at odds with Trump, who wants rates dramatically lower.

But Warsh has undergone a convenient transformation in recent months. Where he once criticized loose monetary policy, he now increasingly echoes Trump’s calls for rate cuts. He told Fox Business in October that “the president’s policies are working” and “are leading to stronger growth and lower prices” — a statement made while the annual inflation rate had actually been rising.

Senator Elizabeth Warren called Warsh what he is: a “sock puppet.” She noted that “he has taken 15 years of his consistent view on inflation and interest rates and turned them on a dime in order to bow down to Donald Trump.”

The Threat That Says Everything

The most revealing moment came during Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent’s Senate testimony. Warren asked Bessent a simple question: Could he commit, right then, that Trump’s Fed nominee Kevin Warsh would not be investigated by the Department of Justice if he didn’t cut interest rates the way Trump wanted?

Bessent’s answer: “That is up to the president.”

Read that again. The Treasury Secretary of the United States would not rule out criminal prosecution of the incoming Federal Reserve chair for making independent monetary policy decisions. The firewall between the White House and the Fed — a cornerstone of American economic stability since the 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord — was openly dismantled in a single sentence.

The Confirmation Blockade

Warsh’s nomination is currently stalled because Republican Senator Thom Tillis has vowed to block any Fed nominees until the criminal investigation of Powell is dropped. The irony is exquisite: the administration launched a bogus investigation to intimidate the current Fed chair, and that investigation is now preventing confirmation of his replacement.

This is the Helen Keller Administration’s approach to monetary policy: blind to the consequences of politicizing the Fed, deaf to warnings from economists across the political spectrum, and dumb — in every sense of the word — about how independent central banking protects economic stability.

Jeremy Carl: The “White Culture” Nominee

The Position: Assistant Secretary of State for International Organizations (i.e., the UN)

The Hearing: February 12, 2026

The Qualification: Right-wing think tank fellow, brief first-term Interior Department stint

The Disqualification: Promotes replacement theory, described as anti-Semitic, tweeted 850+ times with controversial racial content after being nominated

If the Helen Keller Administration has a pattern, it’s this: find someone who says the quiet part loud, then nominate them to a position where volume matters.

Jeremy Carl, Trump’s pick to represent the United States at international organizations including the United Nations, had his hearing on February 12. It did not go well — not because Carl was unprepared, but because his preparation consisted of years of documented statements about race, religion, and “white culture” that senators from both parties found alarming.

Carl, a Montana resident who has worked at conservative think tanks including the Claremont Institute, has written extensively about his concern that “white culture” is disappearing and being “erased” from America. When Senator Chris Murphy asked him to define “white culture” and provide examples of the government erasing it, Carl offered: “Anytime you have mass immigration, you’re going to have a change in cultures.” Pressed for specifics, he cited “things like going to a certain type of Christian church.”

Senator Jeanne Shaheen’s questioning was more pointed: “You continued to make anti-Semitic and racist comments even after your nomination was announced last year. Since your nomination, you’ve tweeted more than 850 times, appeared on five podcasts and repeated this language. This is a pattern.”

Carl has promoted the “great replacement” conspiracy theory — the same ideology cited by mass shooters in El Paso, Christchurch, and Buffalo. He would be representing the United States before international bodies that include representatives from 193 nations, the vast majority of which are not white.

The Helen Keller Administration apparently believes the best person to represent America’s diversity to the world is someone who fears that diversity is destroying “white culture.”

The Already-Confirmed: Those Who Slipped Through

While the nominees above are still navigating the confirmation process, several appointees have already been confirmed who deserve inclusion in any comprehensive accounting of the Helen Keller Administration. These are individuals whose qualifications raised serious questions, whose performance has validated those concerns, or whose appointment represents the triumph of loyalty over competence in particularly striking ways.

Mike Waltz: From Signal Scandal to United Nations

UN Ambassador, confirmed September 2025

Mike Waltz’s career trajectory in this administration reads like a reward system for failure. As National Security Advisor, Waltz created the Signal group chat that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth used to leak classified information about Yemen airstrikes — a security breach that we documented extensively in our original series. The group accidentally included a journalist, turning a classified military discussion into a front-page story.

In any normal administration, the person who created the channel through which classified information was leaked would face consequences. In the Helen Keller Administration, Waltz was promoted. Trump moved him from National Security Advisor to UN Ambassador — a position requiring Senate confirmation, which he received in September 2025.

The message is unmistakable: create a security breach that compromises military operations, and you get a prestigious diplomatic post. The Helen Keller Administration is blind to the breach, deaf to the intelligence community’s concerns, and dumb about the signal it sends to allies who share sensitive information with the United States.

Lee Zeldin: The Climate Skeptic Running the EPA

EPA Administrator, confirmed 56-42

Lee Zeldin, the former New York congressman who lost his gubernatorial race to Kathy Hochul, was confirmed to lead the Environmental Protection Agency despite having no scientific background and a record of climate skepticism. Trump promised Zeldin would “ensure fair and swift deregulatory decisions” — which is a polite way of saying “dismantle environmental protections that inconvenience industry.”

Zeldin was a member of the Bipartisan Climate Solutions Caucus during his time in Congress, which might suggest some environmental awareness. But his appointment was celebrated by industry groups and opposed by environmental organizations almost universally. His primary qualification appears to be a willingness to prioritize deregulation over protection — which is, of course, the opposite of what the “P” in EPA stands for.

Sean Duffy: From Reality TV to Transportation Secretary

Transportation Secretary, confirmed 77-22

Sean Duffy’s path to running America’s transportation infrastructure began on “The Real World: Boston,” MTV’s reality show. He later became a Fox News contributor and served as a Wisconsin congressman. His confirmation to oversee the Department of Transportation — which manages aviation safety, highway infrastructure, rail systems, and maritime operations — sailed through with bipartisan support.

Duffy is affable and telegenic. Whether affability qualifies someone to manage the nation’s transportation infrastructure during a period of aging bridges, FAA staffing crises, and the emergence of autonomous vehicles is another question. The Helen Keller Administration has an answer: it doesn’t matter, as long as you’re loyal.

Kelly Loeffler: The Insider Trading Senator Runs Small Business

SBA Administrator, confirmed 52-46

Kelly Loeffler’s brief Senate career was defined by one of the most notorious insider trading controversies in recent congressional history. After attending a classified Senate briefing about the emerging COVID-19 pandemic in January 2020, Loeffler and her husband dumped millions of dollars in stock before the market crashed. The couple made trades in companies that would be affected by the pandemic — selling hospitality stocks while buying shares in a company that makes protective equipment.

Loeffler was investigated by the Senate Ethics Committee and the Justice Department. Both investigations were ultimately dropped without charges, though the optics were devastating. She lost her Senate seat to Raphael Warnock in the January 2021 Georgia runoff.

Now she runs the Small Business Administration, the agency charged with helping America’s small businesses succeed. One might argue that someone who used privileged government information to protect her personal wealth while small businesses were about to be devastated by a pandemic is not the ideal champion for small business owners.

The Helen Keller Administration disagrees.

Doug Collins: The Impeachment Defender Runs Veterans Affairs

VA Secretary, confirmed 77-23

Doug Collins is a former Georgia congressman, an Air Force Reserve chaplain, and a perfectly decent human being whose primary qualification for running the Department of Veterans Affairs appears to be his aggressive defense of Trump during the first impeachment proceedings. Collins served as the ranking Republican on the House Judiciary Committee during impeachment, where he became one of Trump’s most visible and vocal defenders.

His military service as a chaplain and his congressional support for veterans’ benefits are genuine credentials. But the VA is one of the largest and most complex agencies in the federal government, serving millions of veterans with healthcare, benefits, and support services. Running it requires deep managerial expertise and healthcare administration experience that Collins does not possess.

He was confirmed with broad bipartisan support, suggesting that even in the Helen Keller Administration, some nominees are inoffensive enough to escape serious scrutiny. But “inoffensive” and “qualified” are not the same thing.

Scott Turner: The NFL Player Running Housing

HUD Secretary, confirmed 55-44

Scott Turner, a former NFL player who served in a relatively minor role during Trump’s first term as executive director of the White House Opportunity and Revitalization Council, was confirmed to lead the Department of Housing and Urban Development.

HUD oversees federal housing policy, public housing, fair housing enforcement, and programs that serve millions of low-income Americans. Turner’s primary government experience consisted of running a council that, by most accounts, produced limited results during its existence.

The pattern is familiar: loyalty from the first term, combined with a telegenic presence, equals a Cabinet position regardless of relevant expertise.

The Pattern Deepens: Second Choices and the Loyalty Filter

One of the most revealing aspects of recent nominations is the number of “second choices” — nominees who replaced earlier picks that were withdrawn or forced out. In every case, the replacement is more loyal, more aligned with Trump’s agenda, and often less qualified than the original.

Tom Homan: The Unconfirmed Power

The Position: “Border Czar”

The Qualification: Former ICE Director who oversaw family separation

The Unique Danger: No Senate confirmation required

No examination of the Helen Keller Administration would be complete without addressing Tom Homan, who exercises enormous power over immigration enforcement without ever having been confirmed by the Senate for his current role.

Homan previously served as acting ICE Director during Trump’s first term, where he oversaw the “zero tolerance” family separation policy that separated thousands of children from their parents at the border. The policy was so cruel that it generated bipartisan condemnation and was eventually reversed by executive order — though hundreds of children were never reunited with their families.

Trump brought Homan back as his “border czar,” a title that carries no statutory authority but enormous practical power. Homan coordinates immigration enforcement across multiple agencies, directs ICE operations, and shapes policy — all without the accountability that comes with Senate confirmation.

This is the Helen Keller Administration’s workaround for the confirmation process: when you can’t get your loyalists past the Senate, give them a title that doesn’t require Senate approval. The power is the same; the oversight is zero.

Mike Huckabee: The Fox News Host as Ambassador to Israel

Ambassador to Israel

Mike Huckabee, former Arkansas governor and Fox News host, was appointed to one of the most sensitive diplomatic positions in the world during a period of active military conflict in the Middle East. Huckabee is a Christian Zionist who has denied the existence of Palestinians as a people, stating that “there really is no such thing as a Palestinian.” He has compared abortion to the Holocaust while visiting the Yad Vashem memorial in Israel.

Whatever one’s views on Middle East policy, the position of Ambassador to Israel during wartime requires diplomatic sophistication, regional expertise, and the ability to navigate extraordinarily complex relationships with multiple governments, military forces, and international organizations.

Huckabee’s qualification is that he hosted a show on Fox News, governed Arkansas, and is loyal to Trump.

Conclusion to Part 1: The Deeper Pattern

As we survey these twelve additional members of the Helen Keller Administration, the pattern that we identified in May 2025 has not merely continued — it has intensified and metastasized.

The original “Blind Leading the Blind” analysis documented 22 cases of loyalty trumping competence. With these additions, we now track 34 appointments and nominations that demonstrate the same thesis. And the pattern has evolved in troubling ways:

The Second-Choice Escalation: When first nominees are withdrawn, replacements are selected not for greater competence but for greater loyalty. The filter tightens with each iteration. The template was set by the very first second-choice: Matt Gaetz for Attorney General, withdrawn only because a sex trafficking investigation and drug use allegations made him too personally toxic even for a Republican Senate. His replacement, Pam Bondi, brought her own well-documented loyalty credentials — she dropped a fraud investigation into Trump University after Trump’s foundation made a $25,000 donation to her political committee — but without the personal scandal that made Gaetz unconfirmable. The critical point is this: Bondi has proven every bit as willing as Gaetz would have been to weaponize the Department of Justice for Trump’s personal vendettas. The Comey and Letitia James indictments, the criminal investigation of Fed Chair Jerome Powell, the “86 47” seashell prosecution — Bondi has delivered everything Trump wanted from an Attorney General without the baggage that sank Gaetz. She is not the moderate alternative; she is the competent loyalist who replaced the incompetent one. The second choice isn’t better — it’s smarter about being worse.

The Workaround Mentality: When Senate confirmation presents obstacles (Warsh blocked by Tillis, Halligan’s appointment ruled unlawful), the administration finds workarounds — advisory roles, unconfirmed “czar” titles, or simple defiance of court rulings.

The Expertise Purge: Across every agency, career professionals with institutional knowledge continue to flee. The Helen Keller Administration doesn’t just appoint unqualified leaders; it drives out the qualified staff who might compensate for leadership failures.

The Cascade Effect: Each incompetent appointment creates conditions that make future failures more likely. When the Surgeon General questions vaccines, it reinforces the HHS Secretary’s anti-vaccine agenda. When the EPA Administrator rolls back protections, it validates the Interior Secretary’s push for extraction. When the Fed Chair nominee genuflects to presidential pressure, it emboldens the Treasury Secretary’s unconventional theories.

Helen Keller, the real one, wrote: “The only thing worse than being blind is having sight but no vision.”

She could have been describing this administration.
