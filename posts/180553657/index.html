The Verdict: What the Evidence Reveals

January 13, 2026

A Canadian’s Final Analysis of Presidential Pardons and American Accountability

We began this series with a simple question: “Why should the guilty be pardoned?”

After examining 6,000+ clemency actions across four presidents spanning 24 years, we can now answer definitively—and the answers are not equivalent.

The Scorecard: By the Numbers

Let’s start with what the data shows:

George W. Bush (2001-2009):

  • 200 total clemency actions over 8 years

  • 1 controversial case involving administration (Libby—commutation only, not full pardon)

  • 0 family pardons

  • 0 documented cash-for-clemency schemes

  • Refused Vice President’s repeated pleas for full Libby pardon

Barack Obama (2009-2017):

  • 1,927 total clemency actions over 8 years

  • ~1,600 for drug offenders (criminal justice reform policy)

  • ~2-3 with any connection to administration

  • 0 family pardons

  • 0 campaign associates pardoned

  • Transparent Clemency Project 2014 with public criteria

Joe Biden (2021-2025):

  • 4,245 total clemency actions over 4 years

  • ~4,000 for drug offenders and COVID releases (policy reform)

  • 6 family pardons (1 son + 5 other relatives)

  • Broke explicit promises on Hunter pardon

  • 11-year blanket pardon for son covering undiscovered crimes

  • Preemptive pardons for family never charged

Donald Trump (2017-2021, 2025-present):

  • First term: 238 clemency actions, ~15-20 with direct political connections

  • Second term: ~1,700 clemency actions in 10 months

  • 1,500+ January 6 violent criminals pardoned day one

  • 4 Russia investigation figures pardoned (campaign chairman, advisor, national security advisor)

  • 77 fake elector co-conspirators pardoned preemptively

  • 1 family pardon (son-in-law’s father—later made ambassador)

  • 1 foreign head of state convicted cartel boss pardoned (Hernández—400 tons cocaine, served <6 months of 45 years)

  • Multiple GOP politicians convicted of fraud pardoned

  • Documented $960,000 payment to lobbyists for pardon

  • $1.4 billion in victim restitution erased

  • Pardon attorney explicitly stated “No MAGA left behind”

  • Pardoned Hernández while killing 80+ in “war on drug cartels”

Answering “Why Should the Guilty Be Pardoned?”

Each president gave us an answer:

Bush’s Answer: “Rarely, and with restraint.” Bush used clemency sparingly—fewer than any modern two-term president. Even for his Vice President’s Chief of Staff, Bush refused full pardon despite intense pressure. His answer: clemency is exceptional, boundaries matter, and even powerful allies don’t get complete exoneration.

Obama’s Answer: “For systemic reform and humanitarian proportionality.” Obama used clemency extensively but systematically—1,600+ drug offenders whose sentences were unjust by current standards. His controversial cases (Manning, López Rivera) were justified on humanitarian grounds or sentence disproportionality. His answer: clemency should address systemic injustice, not protect those who protect you.

Biden’s Answer: “For policy reform, but also—inexcusably—for family.” Biden followed Obama’s model for 4,000+ strangers but then shattered boundaries for his son and five other family members. He broke explicit promises, used sweeping 11-year language, and issued preemptive pardons for relatives never charged. His answer split: legitimate for strangers, corrupt for family.

Trump’s Answer: “Because they’re MAGA. Because they paid. Because they’re loyal.” Trump systematized clemency as political protection. His answer is explicit: loyalty brings pardons, cooperation brings nothing, money helps, violence for Trump is forgivable, and “No MAGA left behind.”

Are These Patterns Equivalent?

No. Definitively, demonstrably, no.

The “Both Sides” Argument Fails on Five Grounds:

1. Volume of Connected vs. Unconnected

Bush: 1 of 200 involved administration connection (0.5%) Obama: 2-3 of 1,927 involved administration connection (0.15%) Biden: 6 of 4,245 involved family (0.14%)—but these 6 are indefensible Trump:

  • First term: ~20 of 238 involved political connections (8.4%)

  • Second term: ~200 of 1,700 involved political connections (11.7%)

When you exclude Trump’s 1,500 January 6 pardons to focus on individual clemency decisions:

  • Trump granted approximately 340 individual clemency actions across both terms

  • Of these, at least 100 went to political allies, campaign associates, violent supporters, or co-conspirators

  • That’s approximately 30% of individual clemency going to connected figures

This isn’t “both sides.” This is a qualitative difference in whom clemency serves.

2. Nature of Self-Serving vs. Policy-Driven

Bush: Libby commutation arguably self-serving (protected administration), but limited and isolated Obama: Manning and López Rivera controversial but no personal benefit to Obama Biden: Family pardons nakedly self-serving; drug commutations policy-driven Trump:

  • Russia investigation pardons protect people who could implicate Trump

  • January 6 pardons protect people who committed violence for Trump

  • Fake elector pardons protect co-conspirators in Trump’s scheme

  • GOP politician pardons reward political loyalty to Trump

The distinction: Biden’s family pardons are nepotism (protecting relatives). Trump’s pattern is obstruction and self-protection (protecting those who protect him, rewarding loyalty, preventing cooperation).

3. Corruption of Process

Bush: Largely followed traditional DOJ review (Libby exception) Obama: Created transparent Clemency Project 2014 with public criteria and systematic review Biden: Followed DOJ process for strangers; presidential initiative for family Trump:

  • Fired Office of Pardon Attorney head, installed loyalist

  • Pardon attorney explicitly stated “No MAGA left behind”

  • Created documented lobbying industry ($960,000 payment for Schwartz)

  • Bypassed DOJ review systematically

  • Former pardon attorney testified to Senate alleging “ongoing corruption”

Only Trump systematically corrupted the process itself.

4. Victim Consideration

Bush: Minimal impact—most pardons after sentences served Obama: Most commutations after substantial time served; restitution generally preserved Biden: Hunter’s tax restitution paid (though only after prosecution began); drug offenders’ restitution generally preserved Trump:

  • $1.4 billion in victim restitution and fines erased

  • Gentile’s 10,000 fraud victims abandoned after 13 days

  • Santos’s fraud victims abandoned after 3 months

  • January 6 assault victims (Capitol Police) saw attackers freed

Only Trump systematically erased victim restitution at billion-dollar scale.

5. Messages Sent

Bush: “Clemency is exceptional. Even powerful allies don’t get full exoneration.” Obama: “Systemic injustice in sentencing should be addressed through clemency.” Biden (policy): “Drug war sentences were too harsh and should be corrected.” Biden (family): “My family is above the law, regardless of my promises.” Trump: “Loyalty to me brings protection. Violence for me is forgivable. Cooperation against me brings nothing. Pay enough, get pardoned. ‘No MAGA left behind.’”

Only Trump sent the message that justice is transactional and political loyalty determines accountability.

The Ranking: Least to Most Corrupt

If we rank these presidents on clemency corruption:

1. George W. Bush - Minimal Corruption

  • Lowest volume, highest restraint

  • One controversial case (Libby), handled with limits

  • Refused pressure from closest ally for full pardon

  • No family pardons, no lobbying schemes, no pattern

  • Grade: B (Libby commutation questionable but bounded)

2. Barack Obama - Reform-Focused, Controversial Exceptions

  • Highest volume but 98%+ for criminal justice policy

  • Transparent process, systematic review

  • Manning and López Rivera controversial but justified on principle

  • No self-serving pardons, no family pardons, no corruption

  • Grade: A- (controversial cases reduce from A, but fundamentally sound)

3. Joe Biden - Split Personality

  • Followed Obama’s model for 99%+ of clemency actions

  • Then shattered all boundaries for family

  • Broke explicit promises on Hunter

  • Used unprecedented 11-year blanket language

  • Issued preemptive pardons for 5 additional family members

  • Grade: C (massive policy reform credit offset by family corruption)

4. Donald Trump - Systematic Corruption

  • Used clemency to protect those who protect him

  • Created documented cash-for-clemency industry

  • Pardoned 1,500 violent criminals on political grounds

  • Erased $1.4 billion in victim restitution

  • Installed loyalist pardon attorney: “No MAGA left behind”

  • Pardoned co-conspirators preemptively

  • Granted 13-day commutation for $1.6 billion fraud

  • Grade: F (systematic corruption across all metrics)

The “Both Sides” Response

When confronted with this analysis, defenders might argue:

“But Biden pardoned his family!” True. And I’ve condemned it unequivocally as indefensible nepotism that undermines his entire presidency’s commitment to rule of law.

But Biden pardoned 6 family members out of 4,245 clemency actions (0.14%). Trump pardoned or commuted approximately 100+ connected figures out of ~1,900 total (5-10% in second term when excluding January 6 mass pardons, ~30% of individual clemency decisions).

One is isolated family corruption. The other is systematic political corruption.

“But Obama pardoned Chelsea Manning for espionage!” True. And it was controversial. But Manning had no connection to Obama, served 7 years, was at serious suicide risk, and the 35-year sentence was disproportionate to similar cases.

Compare to Trump pardoning Paul Manafort (his campaign chairman), Roger Stone (his political advisor), Michael Flynn (his national security advisor)—all convicted in investigations touching Trump himself.

One is controversial humanitarian clemency. The other is obstruction through clemency.

“But Bush pardoned Scooter Libby!” False. Bush commuted Libby’s prison sentence but left the conviction, fine, and probation intact. Bush refused Cheney’s repeated pleas for full pardon.

Trump later granted Libby a full pardon in 2018, showing what Bush could have done but chose not to.

One is limited commutation despite pressure. The other is full pardon plus pattern of protecting associates.

What Makes Trump’s Pattern Unique

Ten elements make Trump’s clemency qualitatively different:

1. Systematic vs. Isolated Biden’s family pardons are isolated corruption (6 out of 4,245). Trump’s pattern is systematic (100+ connected figures, explicit “No MAGA left behind” policy).

2. Self-Protection vs. Nepotism Biden protected family who don’t threaten him legally. Trump protected people who could implicate him (Russia investigation figures, fake electors).

3. Cash-for-Clemency Only Trump has documented cases of payments ($960,000 to lobbyists) securing pardons.

4. Mass Violent Criminal Pardons Only Trump pardoned 1,500+ people convicted of violence, including assault on police officers.

5. Billion-Dollar Victim Erasure Only Trump systematically eliminated victim restitution at scale ($1.4 billion).

6. Corrupted Process Only Trump fired the pardon attorney, installed a loyalist who stated “No MAGA left behind,” and created a lobbying industry.

7. Preemptive Co-Conspirator Protection Only Trump pardoned 77 people involved in his own alleged schemes before charges filed.

8. Foreign Policy Weaponization & The Victimhood Strategy Only Trump pardoned a foreign head of state convicted of drug trafficking to influence another country’s election. Juan Orlando Hernández was convicted of facilitating 400 tons of cocaine into the United States. Trump freed him after six months while simultaneously:

  • Bombing alleged drug boats in the Caribbean (killing 80+ people)

  • Threatening Mexico and Canada with massive tariffs over fentanyl flows

  • Demanding both countries take stronger action to prevent drugs crossing their borders or face economic devastation

Trump pardoned a man convicted of 400 tons of cocaine while threatening America’s closest allies over insufficient drug interdiction.

The Hernández pardon also established a template for obtaining clemency: frame your conviction as “political persecution,” compare yourself to Trump’s own “persecution,” quote respected figures, and appeal to his victimhood narrative. This creates a clemency pathway based purely on rhetorical alignment with Trump’s self-perception rather than evidence or justice.

The hierarchy is explicit: poor people in boats are killed without trial, allied nations face economic punishment, adversaries face military action, but powerful figures who flatter Trump walk free.

9. Systematic Racial Disparity

Only Trump’s clemency systematically reinforced racial disparities rather than addressing them. According to a December 2025 report by Rep. Ayanna Pressley (D-Mass.), 85% of Trump’s 2025 clemency recipients were white (compared to 25% of the federal prison population), while only 8% were Black (vs. 34% of prison population) and 5% were Latino (vs. 36% of prison population). The January 6 defendants—overwhelmingly white political allies—received mass pardons (90% of all clemency actions), while only 9 out of more than 60,000 Americans imprisoned for drug offenses received clemency. Trump pardoned a foreign head of state convicted of 400 tons of cocaine (Hernández) while ignoring tens of thousands of drug offenders, most of them Black and Latino Americans serving sentences for far smaller amounts.

Previous presidents used clemency to address racial disparities in sentencing—Obama commuted sentences for more than 1,600 drug offenders, disproportionately benefiting Black and Latino Americans caught in the War on Drugs. Trump used clemency to reinforce existing disparities, directing mercy overwhelmingly toward white political allies while leaving non-white drug offenders behind.

10. Documented Recidivism

Only Trump has freed criminals who immediately returned to crime. Philip Esformes, whose 20-year sentence for $1.4 billion Medicare fraud (the largest healthcare fraud in U.S. history) was commuted by Trump in 2020, was arrested again on new criminal charges after his release. This proves Trump’s clemency process includes no vetting for rehabilitation, future risk, or likelihood of re-offense. When the pardon power is used transactionally rather than based on rehabilitation, recipients return to crime because they were never reformed—they were simply politically valuable.

Previous presidents considered rehabilitation and future conduct in clemency decisions. Trump’s system considers only connection and payment, resulting in predictable recidivism.

The Canadian Perspective: What This Reveals

As a Canadian journalist observing American democracy, several things strike me:

1. The Constitutional Vulnerability The pardon power is nearly unlimited, and the Framers assumed norms and shame would constrain it. Trump has shown that a president shameless about self-interest can weaponize clemency without constitutional constraint.

2. The Partisan Double Standard Republicans who condemned Biden’s family pardons remained silent on Trump’s systematic corruption. Democrats who rightfully criticized Trump’s Russia investigation pardons sometimes defended Biden’s family pardons.

Consistency matters. I’ve criticized Biden’s family pardons harshly precisely because they undermine the principled stand against Trump’s corruption. You can’t defend rule of law selectively.

3. The Accountability Collapse When 1,500 violent criminals walk free because they’re politically aligned, when $1.4 billion in victim restitution evaporates, when “No MAGA left behind” is explicit policy—the principle of equal justice is dead.

4. The Normalization The most dangerous aspect isn’t any single pardon—it’s the normalization. Trump has established precedent that the next president (of either party) can cite. Biden’s family pardons strengthened that precedent rather than countered it.

5. The Victim Erasure Across this entire series, one group is consistently forgotten: victims. David Gentile’s 10,000 defrauded investors. Capitol Police officers assaulted on January 6. Santos’s fraud victims. The taxpayers. The thousands of Americans who died from the 400 tons of cocaine Hernández helped import. The Hondurans who lived under narcostate rule while he took cartel bribes.

Michelle Fiore's donors who gave to honor fallen police officers, only to have their money spent on cosmetic surgery. The Shirleys' victims owed $17.8 million in restitution that was erased by pardon. Trevor Milton's investors who lost hundreds of millions to fake videos and false claims. Philip Esformes' victims—American taxpayers defrauded of $1.4 billion in the largest healthcare fraud in history—who watched him walk free only to be arrested again on new charges.

And there’s another group of forgotten victims: the 60,000+ Americans—disproportionately Black and Latino—imprisoned for drug offenses who watched Trump pardon Juan Orlando Hernández (400 tons of cocaine, six months served) while they remain in prison for far smaller amounts. Only nine drug offenders received clemency in 2025. The racial disparity is systematic: 85% of Trump’s clemency recipients are white, while only 8% are Black and 5% are Latino—almost perfectly inverting the federal prison population’s demographics (25% white, 34% Black, 36% Latino). These prisoners are victims of a system where mercy flows to the politically connected and racially privileged, regardless of the severity of their crimes.

When clemency erases accountability, victims lose twice—once to the crime, again to the pardon.

Addressing the Partisan Critique

I’m aware this series risks appearing partisan. A Canadian analyzing American politics, ranking Trump worst by far—it could seem like partisan advocacy dressed as neutral analysis.

Three responses:

First: I Was Harsh on Biden

I didn’t give Biden a pass. His Hunter pardon was indefensible. I wrote:

  • The 11-year blanket coverage was self-protective, shielding Hunter from FARA and influence-peddling investigations that touched Joe Biden’s vice presidency

  • Biden “shattered the boundaries” of acceptable family clemency

  • His preemptive pardons for Fauci, Milley, and Jan 6 Committee—while more sympathetic than Trump’s—still established a dangerous precedent of preemptive political protection

  • Grade: C—policy reform credit offset by family corruption

I harshly criticized a Democratic president’s pardons precisely to establish that I’m not reflexively partisan. Biden’s conduct deserved a C, and I gave it a C.

Second: The Numbers Aren’t Partisan

The data that produces Trump’s F grade isn’t interpretation—it’s documentation:

Trump’s Second Term (2025):

  • 1,500+ violent criminals pardoned on Day One (Jan 6 rioters, including assault on officers)

  • $960,000 documented cash payment for Schwartz pardon (first proven modern case)

  • $1.4 billion in victim restitution erased per House Judiciary Committee

  • “No MAGA left behind” explicitly stated by installed pardon attorney Ed Martin

  • 77 preemptive co-conspirator pardons (fake electors) before any charges filed

  • Liz Oyer fired for maintaining traditional pardon process standards

  • 400 tons cocaine trafficker freed after 6 months while bombing alleged drug boats

These aren’t subjective characterizations. These are documented events with dates, dollar amounts, named officials, and victim counts.

If the evidence overwhelmingly points to systematic corruption in Trump’s second term, perhaps that’s because the conduct itself is asymmetric—not because the analysis is partisan.

Third: I Acknowledged Pre-Trump Money-for-Clemency

Bill Clinton’s Marc Rich pardon (2001) involved $450,000 in donations to Clinton’s library and Hillary’s Senate campaign. It was corrupt. I acknowledged it.

What changed under Trump isn’t the existence of money in clemency—it’s the systematization:

Clinton (Rich):

  • ONE notorious case

  • Indirect donations (library, campaign)

  • Sparked outrage, damaged his legacy

  • No stated policy

Trump (Second Term):

  • Documented cash-for-clemency infrastructure

  • Direct payment ($960,000) to lobbyists for pardon

  • “No MAGA left behind” explicit policy

  • Pardon attorney fired, loyalist installed

  • $1.4 billion victims erased across hundreds of cases

The difference between one corrupt pardon and a corrupted system matters.

The Partisan Trap vs. The Evidence Trap

Yes, my ranking—Bush (B) → Obama (A-) → Biden (C) → Trump (F)—could be read as partisan.

But consider the alternative: If I had curved the grades to ensure “balance,” I’d be:

  • Ignoring 1,500 violent criminals pardoned

  • Ignoring $1.4 billion victim erasure

  • Ignoring explicit “No MAGA left behind” policy

  • Ignoring documented $960,000 cash payment

  • Ignoring firing the pardon attorney for maintaining standards

That wouldn’t be neutral—it would be malpractice.

When the evidence is lopsided, honest analysis produces lopsided conclusions. The risk isn’t that I’m being partisan—the risk is that I’d distort the evidence to appear balanced.

What Would Change My Mind

If future reporting shows:

  • Biden’s clemency involved documented cash payments comparable to Schwartz

  • Obama’s DOJ explicitly stated “No progressive left behind”

  • Bush pardoned 1,500+ violent criminals on Day One

...I’ll revise my analysis. I’m committed to evidence, not predetermined conclusions.

But based on 24 years of documented presidential clemency from 2001-2025, the pattern is clear: Trump’s second term represents systematic corruption at a scale and explicitness that has no modern precedent.

That’s not partisan. That’s what the evidence shows.

What Should Be Done?

Several reforms could address these problems:

1. Constitutional Amendment Require Senate confirmation for pardons of:

  • Family members

  • Campaign associates

  • Administration officials

  • Those whose crimes involved the President

2. Transparency Requirements Mandate public disclosure of:

  • Who requested the pardon

  • What payments or donations were involved

  • DOJ review recommendations

  • Victim impact statements

3. Restitution Protection Constitutional amendment: Presidential pardons cannot erase victim restitution or fines owed to victims.

4. Process Requirements Require DOJ Office of Pardon Attorney review for all clemency except mass policy-driven commutations. President can overrule but must publicly explain.

5. Preemptive Pardon Prohibition Ban pardons before charges filed or conviction obtained.

But here’s the reality: None of these reforms will happen without massive public pressure. And public pressure requires citizens to understand the difference between isolated corruption and systematic corruption.

The Final Answer

Why should the guilty be pardoned?

The answer that preserves rule of law: For mercy, for justice, for reform, for proportionality, for humanity—not for loyalty, not for money, not for political protection.

Bush largely followed this answer. His clemency was minimal, bounded, and showed restraint even under intense pressure.

Obama largely followed this answer. His clemency was extensive but systematic, focused on policy reform, with controversial exceptions justified on humanitarian grounds.

Biden partially followed this answer. His clemency for strangers was appropriate policy reform. His clemency for family was corrupt nepotism that betrays his stated principles.

Trump abandoned this answer entirely. His clemency systematically served political loyalty, rewarded financial connections, protected co-conspirators, erased victim restitution, and explicitly followed “No MAGA left behind.”

These are not equivalent patterns. One president’s isolated family corruption doesn’t equal another president’s systematic transformation of clemency into political currency.

What This Series Proves

After examining 6,000+ clemency actions across 24 years, the evidence is clear:

The “both sides do it” argument fails. The scale, systematization, corruption of process, victim erasure, and explicit transactionality of Trump’s clemency pattern are qualitatively different from his predecessors.

Biden’s family pardons are indefensible and deserve harsh criticism—but they’re isolated nepotism, not systematic corruption.

Obama’s reform focus was legitimate—even if individual cases like Manning were controversial.

Bush’s restraint established that even conservative Republicans can maintain boundaries when politically costly.

Trump’s pattern is unique—the first president to systematically use clemency as a tool of political protection, financial transaction, and victim erasure at billion-dollar scale.

The Implications

David Gentile is free after 13 days. His 10,000 victims are still trying to recover their life savings.

Juan Orlando Hernández is free after 6 months. The jury that convicted him of 400 tons of cocaine heard him boast about wanting to “shove drugs up the nose of gringos.” While Trump bombs boats and threatens allies over drugs, Hernández—who took a million-dollar bribe from El Chapo—walks free because he told Trump what he wanted to hear.

That prosecutor’s promise—”a warning to would-be fraudsters that seeking to get rich by taking advantage of investors gets you only a one-way ticket to jail”—has been revealed as a lie. Attorney General Garland’s declaration that Hernández “abused his power to support one of the largest and most violent drug trafficking conspiracies in the world” meant nothing the moment he compared himself to Trump.

Not because clemency exists. But because clemency has been corrupted into a system where connections matter more than conduct, where loyalty matters more than justice, where flattering Trump’s victimhood narrative matters more than 400 tons of cocaine, where “No MAGA left behind” matters more than “no one is above the law.”

This is what happens when the pardon power—designed as a tool of mercy—becomes a tool of impunity.

And unless Americans demand better, the next president—of either party—will cite Trump’s precedent and go even further.

The question isn’t just “why should the guilty be pardoned?”

The question is: Will Americans tolerate a justice system where guilt is forgiven based on connections rather than mercy—where a convicted cartel boss who facilitated 400 tons of cocaine into America walks free while poor people in boats are killed without trial?

The evidence from four presidents spanning 24 years gives us the answer we need.

The question is whether Americans will act on it.
