The Defector

Part III: George Conway and the Absolution Question

May 3, 2026

George Conway at his January 6, 2026 campaign launch

On January 6th, 2026 — the fifth anniversary of the Capitol attack — George Conway launched his campaign for Congress.

He chose the date deliberately. Of course he did. George Conway has spent years performing opposition to Donald Trump on cable news, in op-eds, in Lincoln Project ads designed by people who understand exactly how a signal lands. A man who operates at that level of political sophistication does not pick a launch date by accident.

The signal was clear: I was there. I saw what this became. I am the witness the Democratic Party needs in Congress.

The question the Twelfth District has to answer before June 23rd is whether a witness and a legislator are the same thing — and whether the Democratic Party of the Upper West Side of Manhattan is in the business of granting absolution.

Who George Conway Was

Archival photo of a younger George Conway in conservative-lawyer mode

Before we examine the candidate, we need to examine the biography — because Conway is asking voters to trust the transformation, and transformations require a before.

George Conway is a Yale undergraduate and Harvard Law graduate who became one of the most respected conservative litigators of his generation. He co-founded the Federalist Society chapter at Harvard Law — the pipeline that has produced a generation of conservative judges, including three current Supreme Court justices. He clerked for a federal circuit judge. He was considered for Solicitor General in Trump’s first administration. He was, in short, a pillar of the conservative legal establishment at the precise moment that establishment was building the architecture that produced the last decade of American jurisprudence.

This matters. Not because it disqualifies him — people change, careers evolve, and the conservative legal world of the 1990s was not identical to what it became — but because the specific credential Conway is offering voters is his track record of opposition to Trump. That track record begins in approximately 2017. The forty years before it were spent, professionally, on the other side.

While Kellyanne Conway served as Trump’s campaign manager and then as his White House counsellor — she coined “alternative facts” for the American political vocabulary — George Conway was on Twitter, increasingly publicly, calling the president mentally unfit. He co-founded the Lincoln Project. He appeared on cable news. He wrote op-eds. He donated to Democrats. Last year, he renounced his party entirely and registered as a Democrat.

And now he is asking the people who were on the losing side of the politics he spent his career advancing to send him to Congress to fix what he helped build.

This is not an unreasonable request. It is, however, a large one.

The Pitch and Its Problems

Conway’s campaign pitch, in his own words, is this: “We need people who are going to fight to save our democracy. And that requires proven fighters, proven people who have shown the ability and the wherewithal to stand up to Trump.”

Three observations.

First: Every candidate in this race opposes Trump. This is the Upper West Side of Manhattan, one of the most Democratic precincts in the United States. Opposing Trump is the entry requirement, not the differentiator. When a rival candidate noted that “being a Trump critic is hardly a unique qualification — everyone running in this race shares that credential,” she was not being uncharitable. She was being accurate.

Second: The “proven fighter” framing assumes that cable news opposition, op-eds, and Lincoln Project advertising are the relevant credentials for legislating. They are not. Congress does its work in committee hearings, floor votes, constituent services, budget negotiations, and the grinding procedural machinery of lawmaking. Conway has never done any of it. He is 62 years old, with four decades of legal and political experience — and the specific experience most relevant to the job he is seeking is entirely absent from his résumé.

Third, and most interesting: Conway is running for what the Atlantic describes as a seat so safe that whoever wins the primary will likely keep it as long as they want. He is asking for a congressional seat in perpetuity on the basis of having opposed one president. Donald Trump will not be president forever. What does George Conway do on January 20th, 2029, when Trump is gone and the Twelfth District’s constituents need someone to negotiate healthcare policy, fight for affordable housing, or hold a hearing on financial regulation? The answer to that question is nowhere in Conway’s campaign materials, because the campaign has not been designed to answer it.

The Absolution Question

Here is the frame I keep returning to, watching this race from Toronto.

George Conway spent the formative decades of his career helping build the conservative legal infrastructure that produced the Supreme Court majority that just gutted the Voting Rights Act. He helped elect — through his association with, and proximity to, the Trump campaign’s leadership — the president who triggered the redistricting war now convulsing American democracy. He was, for the years that mattered most, on the winning side.

He has since changed his position. This is genuine. The public record of his opposition to Trump is extensive and, in many instances, brave — it cost him his marriage, his professional community, and his party affiliation. These are not trivial sacrifices.

But absolution is a transaction that requires something from both parties. The person seeking it must offer something more than the acknowledgment of error. And the person granting it must decide what they are granting it for.

What Conway is offering the Twelfth District is not a legislative record. It is not policy expertise in the specific issues that affect his constituents. It is witness. He has seen the inside of the conservative legal apparatus. He knows how the Federalist Society pipeline works because he helped build it. He knows how Trump’s orbit operates because he was adjacent to it, in ways more intimate than any other candidate in this race.

That knowledge has genuine value — in oversight hearings, in judicial nominations fights, in the specific congressional work of understanding and unwinding what the last decade constructed. A George Conway who spent four years on the House Judiciary Committee would be genuinely useful in ways that his public profile does not fully capture.

But witness is not the same as legislating. And the Twelfth District, which has just lost a thirty-four-year incumbent who knew the machinery of Congress in his sleep, needs a legislator.

The Schlossberg Parallel

Side-by-side portrait of George Conway and Jack Schlossberg

It is worth noting that Conway and Jack Schlossberg — the race’s current polling leader — are running structurally similar campaigns, though neither would put it that way.

Both are asking voters to trust a signal the candidate’s identity sends rather than a record of legislative accomplishment. Schlossberg is the Kennedy grandson. Conway is the Trump apostate. Both campaigns are built primarily around what the candidate represents rather than what they have done.

The difference — and it is significant — is that Schlossberg is thirty-three and has had no opportunity to build a legislative record. His thin résumé is a function of age. Conway is sixty-two, with four decades of professional experience. His is a function of career choices.

From outside the American political system, this asymmetry looks like the central unresolved tension in Conway’s candidacy. He is asking to be judged by his most recent chapter. But a sixty-two-year-old is the sum of all his chapters, and the earlier ones are not erased by the later ones.

The Kellyanne Question

This cannot be avoided, so it will be handled directly and briefly.

Kellyanne Conway helped elect Donald Trump twice. She ran his 2016 campaign. She served as his White House counsellor. She is, by any reasonable assessment, one of the most consequential architects of the political era that produced everything this series has been examining.

Kellyanne Conway and her husband, George, in Washington in 2017. Matt Rourke / AP file

The marriage is over. George Conway is not responsible for Kellyanne Conway’s professional choices, any more than any former spouse is accountable for the other’s career. People who have been married to each other are not the same person.

But in a campaign built on the credential of having opposed Trump — of having stood up when it cost something — the biographical fact that the candidate spent years in a household where Trump’s most effective political operative was also making a home deserves acknowledgment rather than evasion. Conway has been publicly candid about the contradictions of his marriage. He does not pretend they were not there. That candour is to his credit.

What the Twelfth District has to weigh is whether the private contradictions of that decade inform the public candidate standing before them now. I do not think they disqualify him. I do think they are part of the full accounting that voters are owed.

The Polling and the Money

As of March 2026, Schlossberg leads at 25%, Conway sits second at 16%, with Lasher and Bores tied at 11% and a third of voters undecided. The poll was commissioned by Conway’s own campaign — and he published the results showing him in second place. This is either unusual confidence or a sophisticated media play: the story became “Conway releases poll showing Schlossberg ahead,” generating coverage of Conway while appearing to cede the spotlight. The close-up magician’s trick, performed by a man who has never met Micah Lasher.

The money is real. Conway raised over $2.4 million since launching in January — more than Lasher’s $1.4 million, less than Bores’s $2.2 million who leads in fundraising. For a candidate who launched after the last FEC filing period, that number represents genuine financial credibility. Manhattan donors are not sentimental. They are writing checks because they believe this race is winnable and this candidate is viable.

Whether the money and the name recognition are enough to close the gap with Schlossberg — or to overtake Bores and Lasher from below — is what the next seven weeks will determine.

The View From Here

New York City Upper West Side skyline looking west over the Hudson

I have been watching this race from Toronto, where I started this series because Jack Schlossberg was running for Congress and the question of what the Kennedy legacy means in 2026 seemed worth examining.

George Conway has complicated that question in an unexpected way.

The Schlossberg candidacy is about inheritance — what is passed down, what is owed, what a name carries across generations. The Conway candidacy is about something different: what is redeemed, what can be atoned for, whether the Democratic Party is an institution that rewards conversion or demands a longer record before it grants trust.

I find myself genuinely uncertain about the answer. Conversions matter. The conservative-to-Democrat pipeline has produced genuine public servants. A party that refuses all converts is a party that cannot grow.

But the Twelfth District is not being asked to accept Conway as a Democrat. It is being asked to send him to Congress. Those are different requests. The first requires only a changed registration. The second requires evidence that the skills and values he brings to the job are the ones the district needs.

On the evidence available, George Conway is a brilliant lawyer, a genuine Trump critic, a man who paid a real personal price for his convictions, and a candidate who has not yet fully answered the question of what he does when the fight he was built for is over.

June 23rd is coming. The Twelfth District will need an answer before then.
