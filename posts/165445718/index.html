Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 1: The AI Energy Crisis - Understanding the Scale of the Challenge

June 11, 2025

The Numbers That Should Terrify Everyone

Goldman Sachs Research forecasts global power demand from data centers will increase 50% by 2027 and by as much as 165% by the end of the decade. To put this in perspective, we're talking about adding the equivalent of entire countries' worth of electricity demand in just a few years.

The culprit? A single ChatGPT query requires 2.9 watt-hours of electricity, compared with 0.3 watt-hours for a Google search. Creating an image with generative AI uses the energy equivalent of fully charging a smartphone. As AI capabilities explode and adoption spreads globally, these small numbers multiply into massive demand.

Regional Hotspots:

  • Ireland: In Ireland, it now accounts for over 20% of all electricity consumption

  • United States: The sector has already surpassed 10% of electricity consumption in at least five US states

  • Europe: Goldman Sachs Research estimates a data center pipeline for Europe amounting to about 170 GW, equivalent to about one-third of the region's power consumption

The Perfect Storm: Three Converging Crises

The AI energy surge isn't happening in isolation. Three massive trends are converging to create unprecedented electricity demand:

1. The AI Revolution From text generation to video creation to scientific discovery, AI is moving from niche applications to powering entire economies. Accelerated servers, which is mainly driven by AI adoption, is projected to grow by 30% annually.

2. The Great Electrification Transportation, heating, industrial processes—everything is shifting from fossil fuels to electricity. Electric vehicle adoption, heat pump installations, and industrial electrification are adding massive new loads to the grid.

3. Economic Growth Global aggregate electricity demand grows by 6 750 terawatt-hours (TWh) by 2030 in our Stated Policies Scenario, equivalent to more than the combined demand from the United States and European Union today.

The Grid Isn't Ready

Here's the kicker: our electricity infrastructure was designed for a different world. The growth of data centres could therefore lead to considerable strain on local power networks, exacerbated by the huge mismatch between rapid data centre construction times and the often sluggish pace of expanding and strengthening grids and generation capacity.

The Investment Gap: Goldman Sachs Research estimates that about $720 billion of grid spending through 2030 may be needed. That's just for the transmission and distribution infrastructure—before we even talk about generating the electricity.

Beyond Data Centers: The Broader Energy Transition Challenge

While AI grabs headlines, the broader energy transition faces its own supply chain nightmares. In 2021, the world consumed around 500,000 tonnes of lithium, a figure that is expected to reach over 3 million tonnes by 2030. This six-fold increase in lithium demand for energy storage faces geographic concentration risks and processing bottlenecks.

According to Claudio Spadacini, Founder and CEO of Energy Dome, "one of the most critical bottlenecks in the energy transition is the lack of available solutions for long-duration energy storage. While lithium-ion batteries and pumped hydro have shaped the past decade, they cannot address the full range of challenges the grid now faces".

The Climate Clock Is Ticking

All of this is happening against the backdrop of climate change. We need to triple renewable energy capacity by 2030 while simultaneously meeting exploding AI energy demands. It's the greatest engineering challenge in human history.

But here's the remarkable thing: we might actually be able to do it. The next parts of this series will show how a renaissance of clean energy technologies—from solar and wind to nuclear and fusion—could meet this challenge head-on. The scale of the challenge is unprecedented, but so is the opportunity.
