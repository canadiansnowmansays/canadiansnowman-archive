American Flag Blue

The Initiative of the Week, Week Two

April 26, 2026

A reflecting pool, by its nature, shows you exactly what stands before it.

That is, perhaps, why this one needed fixing.

The Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool has been, by all accounts, filthy for years. Murky. Leaking. A visitor this week photographed it from water level — the surface the colour of something you’d find at the back of a neglected refrigerator. The Washington Monument, that great obelisk of national aspiration, shimmered somewhere in the distance, indistinct. The reflection itself was the problem. Too accurate. Too honest. A dirty pool in the capital of a nation conducting an illegal war in Iran, suppressing its own health agencies, burying the Epstein files, and coating grocery shelves with tariff costs while replaying a 2024 campaign promise — prices down, Day One — on a loop that grows quieter each week.

A dirty pool only mirrors what surrounds it. And what surrounds it, this spring, is not a city at its finest.

So: the pool is being fixed.

This is, it should be noted, Week Two of what has become a reliable Friday tradition. Last week it was the triumphal arch — a monument to American greatness, announced with suitable fanfare, the kind of news that travels well on social media and sits uncomfortably alongside questions about bond markets and grocery bills. The week before that, something else. There is always something else. The cadence is deliberate: a monument a week keeps the scorecard off the front page, and the scorecard — circulating widely on X this same morning, red box after red box, No, No, No — is not a document the administration wishes to discuss. Balanced budget: No. End the Ukraine war: No. Release the Epstein files: No. Release the JFK files: No. And then there is this one — bring prices down, starting on Day One. The quote was right there, August 2024, the man’s own words, delivered with the full confidence of someone standing before a table of grocery products arranged like props in a campaign commercial. Folgers. Maxwell House. Kraft. Jimmy Dean. The American pantry, laid out as evidence of a promise.

One hundred days later, the tariffs have arrived and the grocery prices have not come down. The promise, like so many others, has been quietly replaced. Not retracted — never retracted — simply crowded out. By an arch. By a pool. By the next initiative, and the one after that. One wonders, in quieter moments, whether the grocery table is still somewhere in a White House storage room, waiting for a press briefing that will not come. The only green boxes on the scorecard, in any case, were DOGE, which identified $300 billion in waste without, as the scorecard dryly noted, actually cutting much of it, and border security, which achieved its results by spending $38 billion to maintain what was already a $35 billion annual increase.

Someone on X this week cut through the scorecard’s careful accounting and produced a simpler version. It is worth noting here that Trump never said the words attributed to him in the image below. He didn’t have to. He said retribution from the podium, repeatedly and proudly. He converted the presidency into a revenue stream — the crypto coins, the Gulf golf courses, the tariff exemptions, the Truth Social pump. He buried the Epstein files. He weaponized the DOJ against his enemies while shielding his allies. The meme is fictional. The underlying arithmetic is not. Someone simply did the math out loud and rendered it in plain English, which is occasionally more honest than anything said at an actual press briefing.

Against this backdrop, a gleaming pool is not nothing.

The President announced the initiative himself, with characteristic attention to the numbers. The Reflecting Pool, built in 1922, is 2,000 feet long and 167 feet wide — “that’s like taller than any skyscraper,” he observed, in a comparison that repays no further examination. It has been filthy for years. It has leaked for years. The previous restoration plan carried a price tag of $300 million and a projected timeline of more than three years, involving the removal and replacement of the original granite. The President found this unacceptable. He visited the site personally, accompanied by the Secretary of the Interior, Doug Burgum, who confirmed that yes, there had been problems, and yes, it hadn’t been working for quite some time. The President surveyed the scene and reached a conclusion.

He called three men he knows from installing swimming pools.

“That’s all they can do is a swimming pool,” he explained, in what may be the most efficient summary of a federal procurement process in the history of the republic. The three men gave him a good price. The granite was ground down rather than replaced. The surface was cleaned and smoothed. And then — here is where the initiative achieves a kind of terrible poetry — it was lined with an industrial-grade swimming pool coating. The kind available at any suburban pool supply store. The kind a homeowner in Scottsdale applies to a backyard installation before the July Fourth weekend.

One to two million dollars. Two weeks of work.

“What colour would you like, sir?” they asked.

His aide supplied the answer before he could: American Flag blue.

“That’s the colour I want,” the President confirmed. “I want American Flag blue.”

It will last forty to fifty years, we are assured. No leaks. No anything.

Pause here, if you will, and consider what has just occurred. The Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool — 2,000 feet long, visited by millions annually, the body of water before which Martin Luther King stood on August 28th, 1963 and told a nation what he dreamed — has been resurfaced with a swimming pool liner by contractors selected on the basis of their prior work at a Florida resort. The monument that absorbed the echo of the greatest speech of the twentieth century will now reflect the sky in the same product used to refurbish backyard pools across the Sun Belt.

American Flag blue, incidentally, does not appear on any American flag. But it sounds as though it should, and in this administration, that is generally sufficient.

The metaphor nearly completes itself. When the reflection becomes too troubling — when the pool shows you the Epstein files still sealed, the CDC in freefall, the bond markets buckling, a hundred days of red No after red No, a meme that cuts too close to the bone to be entirely dismissed as fiction — you do not clean the water. You do not ask what the water has been reflecting, or why the reflection disturbs you, or what it would mean to genuinely address what stands before the pool. You coat the bottom. You choose a colour. You call it beautiful. You call it more beautiful, in fact, than it was in 1922 when they first built it, because in this telling everything that came before was inferior and everything that follows will be magnificent, and the numbers — one million versus three hundred million, two weeks versus three years — are delivered with the satisfaction of a man who has never once considered that some things resist the logic of the swimming pool contractor.

The White House posted a photograph of the empty pool this week. Two words beneath it: Coming Soon.

The internet, which reflects everything, responded immediately. One user posted a photograph taken from pool level the same week — the water still in, still the colour of long neglect, the Lincoln Memorial visible at the far end like a reproach. Another superimposed the President himself — flag-patterned swim trunks, MAGA cap, arms churning — doing the butterfly stroke toward the Washington Monument. The image gathered thousands of likes before lunchtime. The pool, still empty, still honest in its way, reflected whatever stood before it — the Monument, the sky, the accumulated weight of what this city has become in one hundred days.

Soon it will reflect only blue. American Flag blue. Forty to fifty years, guaranteed.
