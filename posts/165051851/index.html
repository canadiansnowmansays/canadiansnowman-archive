The $37 Trillion Question

Episode 8: Taxing the 1% - A Proven Path to Fiscal Sanity

July 15, 2025

A weekly series examining how America broke its postwar promise of fiscal responsibility. Each week, we trace the bipartisan policy choices that created today's debt crisis and explore evidence-based solutions that could restore fiscal sanity. This analysis is updated regularly with the latest economic and political developments.

America's $37 trillion debt crisis demands a return to the proven postwar strategy that once made the United States the world's most successful economy: taxing the wealthy at rates sufficient to fund government operations while maintaining growth incentives for productive investment. This isn't radical socialism or punitive confiscation—it's a restoration of the fiscal framework that drove 4% annual growth in the 1950s while keeping debt-to-GDP at manageable 35% levels.

The evidence is overwhelming: top tax rates of 91% in the 1950s coincided with America's greatest economic expansion, while Republican tax cuts since Reagan have delivered slower growth alongside exploding deficits. Today's 37% top rate—down from 70% in 1980—represents a failed experiment in fiscal irresponsibility that prioritized concentrated wealth over broad-based prosperity.

As a Canadian observing from across our shared border, I've watched this experiment unfold with growing alarm. Our 53% combined top tax rate funds universal healthcare and fiscal sustainability while America's lower rates produce debt crisis and social division. The contrast couldn't be clearer: progressive taxation works, while tax cuts for the wealthy fail spectacularly.

The solution isn't complicated—it requires political courage to implement what economic evidence clearly supports. A modern progressive tax structure could generate $600 billion annually while addressing inequality and restoring fiscal balance. This represents practical policy rather than wishful thinking, based on careful analysis of what America's economy can sustain while maintaining international competitiveness.

The postwar model offers a blueprint, but modern conditions require adapted approaches that reflect current economic realities while maintaining the core principle: those who benefit most from economic success should contribute proportionally to maintaining the systems that enable that success.

The Postwar Model: When Progressive Taxation Worked

Understanding how progressive taxation can solve America's current crisis requires examining how it once made America great. The period from 1945-1980 demonstrates conclusively that high tax rates on the wealthy can coexist with—indeed, can drive—exceptional economic performance.

The 91% Rate and 4% Growth

From 1945 through the early 1960s, America maintained top marginal tax rates of 91% on incomes over $200,000 (roughly $2 million in today's dollars). Far from stifling growth, this period produced the fastest sustained economic expansion in American history—4% annual GDP growth that lifted all income levels while funding massive public investments.

The Interstate Highway System, launched in 1956, required $25 billion in federal investment (over $250 billion in today's dollars) that created jobs while establishing infrastructure that supported decades of subsequent growth. The GI Bill educated millions of veterans, creating the skilled workforce that drove technological innovation through the 1960s and beyond.

Space exploration, funded through progressive taxation, employed 400,000 people directly while spinning off technologies that birthed entire industries. These weren't government handouts—they were strategic investments that generated returns far exceeding their costs while demonstrating American technological leadership globally.

Revenue That Matched Ambitions

High tax rates generated government revenue of roughly 18% of GDP—sufficient to fund ambitious projects while maintaining fiscal discipline. The top 1% of earners paid effective rates around 45%, contributing substantially to public investments that supported the economic growth from which they benefited.

Critically, this wasn't punitive taxation but partnership between successful individuals and the society that enabled their success. High earners retained significant after-tax income while contributing to infrastructure, education, and research that created opportunities for future success.

The Eisenhower administration—led by a Republican president—maintained these tax rates while achieving balanced budgets in 1956 and 1957. This bipartisan consensus reflected understanding that progressive taxation represented fiscal responsibility rather than ideological warfare.

Shared Prosperity and Social Cohesion

Progressive taxation during this era produced broadly shared prosperity that strengthened social cohesion and democratic governance. The top 1%'s income share remained stable around 10%, while middle-class incomes rose consistently throughout the period.

This shared growth created positive feedback loops where economic success generated political support for continued public investment. When everyone benefited from growth, politics focused on optimizing policy rather than redistributing existing wealth through zero-sum conflicts.

Importantly, high tax rates didn't prevent wealth accumulation—they channeled it toward productive investment rather than financial speculation. The postwar era produced substantial fortunes while ensuring that wealth creation served broader economic development rather than just individual accumulation.

Analysis: The correlation between high tax rates and robust economic growth in the postwar era demolishes arguments that progressive taxation necessarily hinders prosperity. The data shows that 70-91% top rates coincided with America's greatest economic expansion.

Modern Proposal: A 21st Century Progressive Structure

A modern progressive tax structure must reflect current economic conditions while maintaining the core principle that made postwar taxation successful: asking those who benefit most from economic success to contribute proportionally to its maintenance.

The 75% Solution for Ultra-High Earners

Economists Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman have calculated that a 75% tax rate on incomes over $10 million could generate approximately $300 billion annually—enough to cut current deficits by roughly 18% while targeting only the top 0.1% of earners who have captured disproportionate shares of economic growth.

This rate targets genuinely ultra-high incomes rather than affecting successful professionals, small business owners, or upper-middle-class families. Someone earning $10 million annually would pay the 75% rate only on income above that threshold, maintaining strong incentives for productive economic activity while ensuring appropriate contribution to public investments.

International evidence supports this rate's feasibility. France maintained 75% rates on high earners from 2012-2014 without economic collapse, while several Nordic countries have sustained rates above 60% for decades while achieving exceptional economic performance.

Corporate Loophole Elimination: $100 Billion Annually

Corporate tax reform could generate an additional $100 billion yearly through closing loopholes that allow profitable companies to avoid taxation while benefiting from public infrastructure, education systems, and legal frameworks that enable their success.

The current corporate rate of 21%—down from 35% before Trump's 2017 cuts—already ranks among the lowest in developed nations. However, effective rates often fall far below statutory levels due to deductions, deferrals, and offshore accounting strategies that have no economic justification beyond tax avoidance.

Minimum tax rates on large corporations, international coordination to prevent profit-shifting, and elimination of special preferences for particular industries could restore corporate taxation to levels that fund the public investments businesses require for long-term success.

Wealth Tax Implementation: $200 Billion Potential

A modest 2% annual wealth tax on billionaires could yield roughly $200 billion according to Saez-Zucman analysis, while affecting fewer than 1,000 individuals who have accumulated unprecedented fortunes during decades of tax cuts and favorable policies.

Wealth taxation addresses a fundamental problem with income-based systems: extremely wealthy individuals often realize minimal taxable income while accumulating vast fortunes through unrealized capital gains. A wealth tax ensures that accumulated success contributes to maintaining the systems that enabled such accumulation.

Implementation challenges exist—valuation difficulties, enforcement complexities, international coordination requirements—but these represent technical problems rather than fundamental barriers. Several European countries have successfully implemented wealth taxes, providing models for American adaptation.

Total Revenue Potential: $600 Billion Annually

Combined, these progressive tax reforms could generate roughly $600 billion in new annual revenue—sufficient to cut federal deficits by approximately 35% while funding infrastructure investment, climate action, and social programs that drive long-term growth.

This revenue estimate assumes no behavioral changes from taxpayers, making it conservative. Higher tax rates might actually increase economic activity by reducing incentives for financial speculation while increasing returns to productive investment and work.

More importantly, $600 billion represents sustainable revenue from progressive sources rather than one-time measures or policies that undermine long-term growth. This tax structure could be maintained indefinitely while providing fiscal space for addressing national priorities.

Implementation Strategies: Overcoming Political Obstacles

Progressive taxation faces substantial political obstacles, but strategic implementation could build momentum while demonstrating practical benefits that generate broader public support.

State-Level Innovation and Demonstration

Individual states could implement progressive tax reforms that demonstrate effectiveness while building political momentum for federal action. California, New York, and other large states have sufficient economic scale to show how progressive taxation can fund superior public services while maintaining competitiveness.

State-level wealth taxes on billionaire residents could fund infrastructure, education, and healthcare improvements that attract business investment while improving quality of life. Success stories at state level could overcome ideological resistance to progressive taxation.

Connecticut, Massachusetts, and other states have successfully implemented millionaire taxes that generate substantial revenue without triggering the exodus that opponents predicted. These examples provide evidence that progressive taxation can work in American political contexts.

Federal Minimum Rates and Interstate Coordination

Federal policy could establish minimum tax rates on high earners and large corporations while allowing states to implement higher rates if they choose. This approach prevents tax competition races to the bottom while preserving federalism principles.

Interstate compacts could coordinate wealth taxation to prevent avoidance through residency changes while ensuring that mobile wealthy individuals contribute appropriately regardless of their chosen domicile.

International tax coordination—through OECD frameworks and bilateral agreements—could prevent corporate profit-shifting while ensuring that American tax reforms don't encourage relocations that provide no economic benefits.

Gradual Phase-In with Performance Metrics

Rather than immediate implementation of full progressive tax rates, reforms could phase in gradually while measuring economic impacts and adjusting as necessary. This approach reduces uncertainty while providing evidence about effects that can inform public debate.

Initial rates of 50% on incomes over $10 million could increase to 75% over several years if economic performance remains strong. Similarly, corporate and wealth tax reforms could begin modestly while expanding as administrative capacity develops.

Performance metrics—economic growth, business investment, employment levels, revenue generation—could guide implementation while providing objective measures of success that overcome ideological arguments about progressive taxation effects.

Addressing Economic Arguments Against Progressive Taxation

Common economic arguments against progressive taxation often reflect ideological commitments rather than empirical evidence, but addressing them seriously helps build political support for reform.

The Growth Question: Does Progressive Taxation Reduce Economic Performance?

Historical evidence clearly shows that progressive taxation can coexist with robust economic growth. The 4% growth rates of the 1950s-1960s occurred during periods of much higher tax rates than current levels, while tax cut periods since Reagan have generally delivered slower growth.

International comparisons reinforce this conclusion. Germany, France, Canada, and Nordic countries maintain higher tax rates than the United States while achieving comparable or superior economic performance across most metrics that matter for ordinary citizens.

Modern economic research confirms that tax rate effects on growth are modest compared to impacts of education, infrastructure, and institutions that progressive taxation can fund. Quality public investment often generates higher returns than private alternatives while supporting long-term growth.

The Investment Incentive Concern

Critics argue that higher tax rates discourage productive investment, but evidence suggests the opposite effect. Progressive taxation reduces returns to financial speculation while maintaining incentives for business investment and expansion that generate employment and productivity growth.

Capital gains taxation—currently favored through lower rates—often encourages short-term speculation rather than long-term investment. Higher rates on capital gains, particularly for short holding periods, could redirect investment toward productive uses rather than financial manipulation.

Moreover, progressive taxation funds public investments—infrastructure, education, research—that support private sector productivity and profitability. Businesses benefit from quality transportation systems, educated workers, and technological innovation that progressive taxation can finance.

The Mobility and Competition Challenge

International tax competition represents a genuine challenge for progressive taxation, but coordination mechanisms can address these concerns while maintaining fiscal sovereignty.

Wealthy individuals and corporations have limited ability to avoid well-designed progressive taxes through mobility. High earners depend on access to American markets, legal systems, and infrastructure that provide value justifying appropriate tax contributions.

International evidence shows that tax-motivated relocations often prove temporary or ineffective when properly designed systems prevent abuse. France's wealth tax experience provides lessons about implementation rather than evidence against progressive taxation principles.

The Administrative Complexity Issue

Progressive taxation does require sophisticated administration, but American tax authorities possess technical capacity to implement complex systems effectively when provided adequate resources and political support.

The current tax code already contains extensive complexity that primarily benefits wealthy taxpayers through specialized deductions and preferences. Progressive reform could actually simplify taxation by reducing special provisions while raising rates on high earners.

Modern technology enables tax administration approaches that weren't feasible during earlier progressive tax periods. Automated reporting, international information sharing, and sophisticated audit techniques can address avoidance strategies more effectively than historical systems.

Revenue Uses: Strategic Investment for Long-Term Growth

Progressive taxation's benefits depend not only on revenue generation but on strategic use of those resources to drive long-term economic growth while addressing pressing national priorities.

Infrastructure Investment for 21st Century Competitiveness

America's infrastructure deficit—estimated at $2.6 trillion by engineering societies—represents both urgent necessity and significant opportunity. Progressive tax revenue could fund comprehensive infrastructure modernization that drives productivity growth while creating employment opportunities.

Transportation infrastructure—roads, bridges, airports, ports—requires sustained investment that private markets won't provide at necessary scale. Progressive taxation offers stable funding for long-term projects that generate returns over decades while supporting economic activity.

Digital infrastructure—broadband expansion, 5G networks, computing capacity—increasingly determines economic competitiveness but requires public investment to ensure universal access rather than leaving rural and low-income areas behind.

Climate Action and Energy Transition

Climate change requires massive public investment in renewable energy, building retrofits, electric vehicle infrastructure, and industrial transformation that private markets alone cannot finance at necessary speed and scale.

Progressive taxation could fund a Green New Deal-style investment program that creates employment while addressing environmental challenges. These investments generate long-term economic returns while positioning America for leadership in growing clean energy industries.

Energy independence—reducing reliance on fossil fuel imports—provides national security benefits while creating domestic employment in manufacturing, construction, and technology sectors that cannot be outsourced internationally.

Education and Human Capital Development

Progressive taxation could fund educational investments that drive long-term growth while addressing inequality that undermines social cohesion and economic opportunity.

Universal pre-K education, expanded higher education access, and workforce retraining programs represent investments in human capital that generate returns exceeding their costs while ensuring that technological change creates opportunities rather than displacement.

Student debt relief—funded through progressive taxation rather than general borrowing—could liberate young Americans to start businesses, buy homes, and make career choices based on aptitude rather than debt obligations.

Healthcare System Reform

Progressive taxation could fund universal healthcare that reduces costs while improving outcomes, freeing businesses from health insurance obligations while ensuring that medical needs don't determine family financial security.

Medicare expansion—funded through progressive taxation—could achieve cost savings through administrative efficiency while providing universal coverage that enhances labor market flexibility and entrepreneurship.

Public health investment—disease prevention, mental health services, addiction treatment—generates substantial economic returns while addressing health disparities that impose economic costs on communities and families.

International Coordination and Tax Competition

Effective progressive taxation requires international coordination to prevent avoidance while maintaining appropriate fiscal sovereignty for democratic governance.

OECD Frameworks and Multilateral Cooperation

The OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) initiative provides frameworks for coordinating corporate taxation while preventing the profit-shifting strategies that enable tax avoidance by multinational corporations.

Minimum tax rates on large corporations—recently agreed internationally—represent progress toward ensuring that profitable companies contribute appropriately to public investments regardless of their accounting strategies.

Individual tax coordination proves more challenging but bilateral agreements can address specific avoidance strategies while maintaining overall progressive tax principles. Information sharing mechanisms enable enforcement that wasn't possible during earlier progressive tax periods.

Addressing Tax Haven Abuse

Progressive taxation requires addressing tax haven abuse that enables wealthy individuals and corporations to avoid obligations while benefiting from public investments in their home countries.

Financial transparency requirements—beneficial ownership disclosure, automatic information exchange, country-by-country reporting—can expose tax haven strategies while making avoidance more costly and risky.

Sanctions on non-cooperative jurisdictions—limiting market access, imposing withholding taxes, restricting financial transactions—provide enforcement mechanisms that make tax haven strategies less attractive.

Maintaining Fiscal Sovereignty

International coordination must preserve democratic control over taxation while preventing destructive competition that benefits only wealthy taxpayers and corporate interests.

Minimum standards rather than identical policies can address tax competition while allowing different countries to pursue approaches that reflect their political preferences and economic conditions.

American leadership in progressive taxation—demonstrating that higher rates can coexist with prosperity—could encourage other countries to adopt similar approaches while reducing pressure for competitive tax cutting.

Political Economy: Building Coalitions for Reform

Progressive tax reform requires building political coalitions that include middle-class families, business communities, and civic organizations that would benefit from public investments that progressive taxation can fund.

Emphasizing Benefits Rather Than Just Burdens

Successful progressive tax advocacy must emphasize benefits—infrastructure, education, healthcare, climate action—that progressive taxation can fund rather than focusing only on asking wealthy individuals to pay higher rates.

Middle-class families pay substantial amounts for healthcare, education, and other services that progressive taxation could provide more efficiently through public investment. Total costs often decrease when progressive taxation replaces private payments.

Business communities benefit from infrastructure, educated workers, and stable social systems that progressive taxation can fund. Emphasizing these benefits helps overcome ideological resistance while building practical support for reform.

State and Local Demonstration Effects

Successful progressive taxation at state and local levels can demonstrate practical benefits while building momentum for federal reform. California's experience with higher tax rates on wealthy residents provides evidence that progressive taxation can work in American contexts.

Local infrastructure projects, education improvements, and public services funded through progressive taxation create visible benefits that generate political support while proving that public investment can deliver superior results.

Business Community Engagement

Forward-thinking business leaders increasingly recognize that progressive taxation can fund the public investments that support long-term competitiveness while addressing inequality that threatens social stability and market demand.

Businesses benefit from infrastructure, education, and research that progressive taxation can fund while suffering from inequality, political instability, and deteriorating public services that inadequate taxation produces.

International business experience—companies operating in higher-tax countries like Germany or Canada—provides evidence that progressive taxation can coexist with business success while supporting superior public services.

Addressing Wealth Inequality Through Progressive Taxation

Progressive taxation represents the most effective tool for addressing wealth inequality that threatens both economic stability and democratic governance.

Concentration and Democracy

Current wealth concentration—38% held by the top 1%—provides disproportionate political influence that undermines democratic equality while distorting policy toward narrow interests rather than broad public welfare.

Progressive taxation can address this concentration while funding public investments that create opportunities for wealth building among middle-class families who currently face barriers to asset accumulation.

Inheritance taxation—currently minimal due to high exemptions—could prevent aristocratic wealth concentration while maintaining incentives for productive economic activity during lifetime wealth creation.

Economic Efficiency and Stability

Extreme wealth concentration creates economic instability through boom-bust cycles driven by speculative investment rather than productive economic activity. Progressive taxation can redirect investment toward productive uses while reducing financial speculation.

Consumer demand depends on broad-based income distribution rather than concentrated wealth that generates lower consumption propensities. Progressive taxation supports consumer demand while funding public investment that drives economic growth.

Financial instability—demonstrated in 2008 and recurring financial crises—often stems from concentrated wealth seeking high returns through risky speculation rather than productive investment that progressive taxation can encourage.

Implementation Timeline and Political Strategy

Effective progressive tax reform requires strategic implementation that builds momentum while demonstrating benefits that generate broader political support.

Year One: Foundation Building

Initial implementation could focus on closing corporate loopholes and implementing modest rate increases on ultra-high earners while building administrative capacity for more comprehensive reform.

Revenue from initial measures could fund visible infrastructure projects and public services that demonstrate progressive taxation benefits while generating political support for expanded reform.

Years Two-Three: Expansion and Coordination

Successful initial implementation could enable expansion to full progressive rate structure while developing international coordination mechanisms that prevent avoidance strategies.

State-level innovation during this period could provide additional demonstration effects while building political momentum for comprehensive federal reform.

Years Four-Five: Full Implementation and Optimization

Complete progressive tax structure implementation could provide full revenue potential while optimizing policies based on initial experience and changing economic conditions.

International coordination could expand to comprehensive frameworks that prevent tax competition while maintaining appropriate fiscal sovereignty for democratic governance.

Conclusion: The Proven Path Forward

Progressive taxation offers America's most realistic path toward fiscal sanity, reduced inequality, and restored international competitiveness. The evidence—historical, international, and economic—overwhelmingly supports progressive approaches over the tax cut experiments that created current fiscal crisis.

The postwar model proves that high tax rates on the wealthy can drive rather than hinder economic growth while funding public investments that benefit everyone, including those who pay higher rates. Modern adaptations can achieve similar results while addressing 21st-century challenges that require sustained public investment.

Most importantly, progressive taxation represents practical policy rather than ideological commitment. The $600 billion revenue potential from progressive reform could address fiscal imbalances while funding infrastructure, climate action, and social programs that drive long-term growth.

Implementation requires political courage and strategic coalition-building, but growing public awareness of inequality and fiscal crisis creates opportunities for progressive reform that didn't exist during previous decades.

The alternative—continued commitment to tax cuts that have failed repeatedly while debt accumulates toward crisis levels—leads toward fiscal collapse and social instability that would harm everyone, including the wealthy individuals who currently benefit from inadequate taxation.

Recent congressional actions—particularly the Senate's passage of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" adding $4.6 trillion to the debt—provide real-time validation of this analysis while demonstrating the bipartisan fiscal dysfunction that makes progressive reform not just advisable but increasingly urgent. The bill's passage confirms that without fundamental tax reform, America will continue its path toward fiscal crisis regardless of which party controls Congress.
