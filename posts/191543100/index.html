When the President Forgot He Was President

Part 10: Seventy-Four Days from “Nice, Thin Blood” to Forgetting His Own Office

March 21, 2026

In Part 9, I documented the clinical diagnosis that medical experts are documenting but cable news won’t fully explain: Donald Trump doesn’t have Alzheimer’s disease. He has frontotemporal dementia—a progressive cognitive disorder that doesn’t diminish the person, but augments them by removing inhibitions. What’s being disinhibited in Trump’s case is his underlying malignant narcissism.

And the deterioration is accelerating week over week.

This is what that acceleration looks like when it reaches the point where even the baseline problem—the fact that Trump’s starting point was so unconventional—can no longer mask the decline.

This is what happens when the President of the United States forgets he’s the President of the United States.

March 16, 2026: The Oval Office

Behind the Resolute Desk. Cameras rolling. The President of the United States on one of his extended riffs.

The target: California Governor Gavin Newsom, who has been open about his dyslexia. Trump had spent the week attacking Newsom over his learning disability, saying “a president should not have learning disabilities” and claiming presidents must be “sharp as hell” The Daily Beast.

On March 16, Trump brought this argument into the Oval Office. He told reporters: “I think a president should not have learning disabilities, OK?” Yahoo!

Then he said this: “And I know it’s highly controversial to say such a horrible thing - the president of the United States. Gavin Newscum admitted that he has learning disabilities, dyslexia, everything about him is dumb.” U.S. News & World Report

Read that again.

“The president of the United States, Gavin Newscum.”

Donald Trump, sitting behind the Resolute Desk in the Oval Office, called Gavin Newsom—the Governor of California—”the president of the United States.”

While arguing that presidents need to be cognitively sharp.

While forgetting that he himself is the president.

Newsom’s response on X: “NO THANK YOU, WE BELIEVE IN FREE ELECTIONS!” The Daily Beast

His press office went further, posting in all caps: “NOW THAT I, GAVIN C. NEWSOM, AM OFFICIALLY PRESIDENT OF THE UNITED STATES (THANK YOU DONALD!), I HAVE MANY BIG ANNOUNCEMENTS TO MAKE! FIRST, EVERY TRUMP EXECUTIVE ORDER IS NULL AND VOID, STEPHEN MILLER AND EVERY TRUMP GOON IS FIRED...” Marijuana Moment

The mockery was deserved. But beneath the humor lies something much darker.

Playing into the gaffe, Newsom said legalizing marijuana was among the “many big announcements” his White House was making.

The Context That Can’t Be Ignored

This didn’t happen in isolation.

According to multiple reports, during the same Oval Office session, Trump was “appearing to struggle to keep his eyes open as others spoke” The Daily Beast.

The Newsom gaffe was part of a week-long obsession. Trump had made similar comments about Newsom’s dyslexia at least three times in recent days U.S. News & World ReportTime, including at a rally where he said “I don’t want the president of the United States to have a cognitive deficiency. I’m for everybody, but when it comes to president you gotta be sharp. You gotta be sharp as hell.” The Daily Beast

Newsom had responded to that comment with: “Too late,” The Daily Beast suggesting Trump already has a cognitive deficiency.

Two days later, Trump proved Newsom’s point by forgetting he’s the president.

The National Center for Learning Disabilities responded to Trump’s attacks on Newsom with a statement: “Learning disabilities, including dyslexia, affect how the brain processes information. They do not reflect a person’s intelligence, judgment, or ability to lead. Every day, people with learning disabilities serve their communities as teachers, entrepreneurs, scientists, members of the military, and public servants.” Time

Dyslexia is a learning disability that affects how the brain processes written language. Frontotemporal dementia is a neurodegenerative disease that destroys the brain’s ability to function.

They are not the same thing. One is a difference in neurological wiring. The other is progressive brain destruction.

Donald Trump appears to have the latter while attacking people with the former.

The Lawyer, the Niece, the Cardiologist

The March 16 incident didn’t happen in a vacuum. It came after weeks of escalating medical expert assessments.

Ty Cobb served as Trump’s White House attorney. He’s not a Democratic operative or a Never Trumper. He worked for the man. Defended the man. Knows the man.

On January 21, 2026, Cobb went on MSNBC’s “The Beat” with Ari Melber. “I think there’s been a significant decline,” Cobb said. “He’s always been driven by narcissism. But I think the dementia and the cognitive decline are, you know, palpable, as do many experts, including many physicians.” The Hill

“Palpable.” Not speculative. Not theoretical. Palpable.

Mary Trump—clinical psychologist, Trump’s niece—appeared on CNN’s OutFront on the same day. She said she had noticed her uncle’s cognitive health “rapidly getting worse” in recent months The Daily Beast.

Her clinical assessment: “Just his behavior—the way he speaks, his inability to reign himself in, his inability to stay on topic. And often times it seems that he’s not exactly aware of where he is or the audience he’s speaking to.” The Daily Beast

“Not exactly aware of where he is.”

Remember that phrase.

On March 16, he wasn’t exactly aware that he’s the President of the United States.

But the most significant statement came from Dr. Jonathan Reiner, the cardiologist who served Vice President Dick Cheney. Reiner wrote on X: “This letter, and the fact that the president directed that it be distributed to other European countries, should trigger a bipartisan congressional inquiry into presidential fitness.” The Hill

Read that again. A physician who served at the highest levels of government, who understands the constitutional implications of what he’s saying, is calling for a congressional inquiry into presidential fitness.

That’s not medical speculation. That’s a professional assessment that the situation has become a national security threat.

When Public Opinion Catches Up to Expert Assessment

For months, medical experts, former colleagues, and family members had been sounding alarms. By February 2026, the American public was starting to hear them.

YouGov polling conducted in February showed a dramatic shift:

49% of Americans now believe Trump is experiencing cognitive decline, up from 40% before the election Yougov.

But the real story is in the Independent voters—the people least invested in partisan narratives: 51% of Independents now say Trump is suffering cognitive decline, up from 34% in October 2024 Yougov. That’s a 17-point swing in four months.

63% say Trump’s health and age are affecting his ability to govern Yougov.

And crucially: a majority of Americans say they do not trust information from the White House about Trump’s health Yougov.

Why would they? The White House response to every documented incident has been the same: denial, deflection, and attacks on anyone raising questions.

When Dr. Gupta and other medical professionals raised concerns after Davos, White House spokesperson Liz Huston responded: “President Trump is the sharpest, most accessible, and energetic president in modern American history. While deranged and failing Daily Beast journalists and disgraced doctors with Trump Derangement Syndrome push blatant lies—President Trump is dominating on the world stage and brokering historic deals to advance the interests of the American people.” The Daily Beast

“Sharpest president in modern American history.”

Eighteen days before he forgot he was president.

The Timeline of Acceleration

Let me lay out what we’ve documented in this series:

January 1, 2026: WSJ interview. Trump admits wanting “nice, thin blood,” confuses his own medical procedures (CT scan vs. MRI), explains his “delicate skin” and constant bleeding, uses makeup to hide bruising, and defends falling asleep as “blinking.”

January 19, 2026: The “Dear Jonas” letter—demanding Greenland, citing his Nobel Prize snub, declaring “The World is not secure unless we have Complete and Total Control of Greenland.”

January 21-22, 2026: Davos—Iceland/Greenland confusion, worst hand bruising yet, rambling speeches, manic posting.

January 21, 2026: Medical experts go public with clinical assessments. Dr. Gupta: “It’s getting worse.” Ty Cobb: “The dementia and cognitive decline are palpable.” Dr. Reiner: calls for congressional inquiry.

February 6, 2026: Medical professor documents “deterioration almost week over week” and concludes “the rate of decline is accelerating.”

Mid-February: Kentucky rally—Trump admits falling asleep during Iran war planning briefings. Struggles to pronounce “Kentucky.”

March 16, 2026: While arguing presidents must be “sharp as hell,” Trump forgets he’s the president.

March 17, 2026: Trump refers to “Jeffrey’s island”—revealing linguistic intimacy with Jeffrey Epstein despite years of claiming he barely knew him.

From the WSJ interview to forgetting his own office: 74 days.

From Davos to the Newsom gaffe: 54 days.

From the “week over week” assessment to forgetting he’s president: 38 days.

The intervals are shrinking. The acceleration is measurable.

What We’re Watching

In Part 1 of this series, I opened with Lawrence O’Donnell’s question: “If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?”

The answer, increasingly, is this: we’ll know when medical experts stop hedging and start using clinical terms. When former colleagues say it’s “palpable.” When physicians call for congressional inquiries. When the deterioration becomes so rapid it’s measurable week over week.

And when the President of the United States forgets he’s the President of the United States.

The baseline problem made early detection almost impossible. Trump’s unconventional baseline meant that behaviors that would have been alarming in anyone else were dismissed as “just Trump being Trump.”

But there’s a limit to how far that rationalization can stretch. And we’ve reached it.

When you’re sitting behind the Resolute Desk, cameras rolling, and you call someone else “the president of the United States” while arguing that presidents need to be cognitively sharp—the baseline problem stops being a theoretical framework and becomes a documented fact.

The experts are no longer speculating. They’re documenting.

The public is no longer uncertain. They’re watching.

And the acceleration is no longer debatable. It’s undeniable.

The Question No One’s Asking

Here’s what should terrify every American: all of this has happened in 74 days.

What happens in the next 74 days?

What happens when the rate of decline continues to accelerate? When “week over week” becomes “day over day”? When forgetting you’re president becomes forgetting who the president is supposed to be?

The 25th Amendment exists for exactly this scenario. Section 4 allows the Vice President and a majority of the Cabinet to declare the president “unable to discharge the powers and duties of his office.”

But using it requires two things:

First, officials willing to act despite the political consequences.

Second, a clear and unmistakable triggering event—something so undeniable that even Trump’s most loyal defenders can’t rationalize it away.

Is forgetting you’re the president while sitting in the Oval Office enough?

Apparently not. Because 48 hours after it happened, Trump is still president.

The baseline problem, it turns out, isn’t just a diagnostic challenge. It’s a constitutional crisis waiting to happen.

Because when the baseline is already so unconventional that forgetting you hold the most powerful office on Earth can be dismissed as “just Trump being Trump,” then there’s no behavior so alarming it can’t be rationalized.

And that means there’s no ceiling to how bad this can get before someone in a position of authority decides to act.

The Reality of Frontotemporal Dementia

Frontotemporal dementia (FTD) is a group of neurologic disorders associated with changes in personality, behavior, language or movement. Some FTD forms are inherited, and some are not.

As I documented in Part 9, this isn’t Alzheimer’s disease. Dr. Frank George, a psychologist and cognitive neuroscientist who has studied Trump for a decade, has explained why the diagnosis of frontotemporal dementia makes this uniquely dangerous.

Alzheimer’s would mean gradual diminishment. Memory loss. Eventually, a trajectory toward incapacity that would make the 25th Amendment inevitable.

But frontotemporal dementia means the disease is removing the inhibitions from Trump’s malignant narcissism. Making him more of what he already was. More vengeful. More erratic. More dangerous.

And as Dr. George noted, “as the frontotemporal dementia progresses, that disinhibition just gets worse and worse. His decisions will become more erratic, more vengeful. It’s just getting expressed with no guard rails.”

No guard rails.

On January 1, Trump admitted wanting “nice, thin blood” because he doesn’t want “thick blood pouring through my heart.”

On March 16, he forgot he was president.

Seventy-four days.

And the acceleration continues.

Conclusion: When the Undeniable Becomes Undeniable

The acceleration is undeniable.

The clinical diagnosis is documented.

The public polling shows majority awareness.

The expert assessments use words like “palpable” and call for congressional action.

The timeline shows measurable, accelerating decline.

And the President of the United States has forgotten he’s the President of the United States.

The question is: what will it take for that to matter?

How much worse does it have to get before the 25th Amendment isn’t a political risk but a constitutional necessity?

How many more incidents like March 16 will we witness before someone in authority acts?

The baseline problem predicted this moment. It told us that Trump’s unconventional starting point would make detecting further decline almost impossible. That behaviors alarming in anyone else would be rationalized as “just Trump.”

But even the baseline problem has limits.

And we’ve reached them.

The President forgot he was President.

In 74 days, we’ve gone from “nice, thin blood” to forgetting the office he holds.

The acceleration is undeniable.

The diagnosis is documented.

The question is whether anyone with the constitutional authority to act will do so before the next 74 days produce something even worse.
