The Great Canadian Trade War

Part XI — The Court Giveth, The Bully Taketh Away

Canada Wins the Legal Battle — and May Face a Harder War

February 20, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays Feb 20, 2026

This morning I published Part X arguing that USMCA renewal is nearly dead. The math was clear. The trust was gone. Canada was preparing for breakup.

Soon after, the Supreme Court of the United States handed Canada what looked like a gift: a 6-3 ruling striking down Trump’s IEEPA tariffs as unconstitutional.

The IEEPA tariffs — the “reciprocal” and “fentanyl” duties that have strangled Canadian exports since early 2025 — are legally dead.

So why is Canada’s top business lobby not celebrating?

Because Candace Laing, president and CEO of the Canadian Chamber of Commerce, said exactly what needed to be said within minutes of the ruling:

“The Supreme Court’s decision to strike down the use of IEEPA tariff powers is a legal ruling, not a reset of U.S. trade policy. Canada should prepare for new, blunter mechanisms to be used to reassert trade pressure, potentially with broader and more disruptive effects.”

Read that last phrase again: broader and more disruptive effects.

That’s not pessimism. That’s the correct analysis. And here’s why.

What the Ruling Actually Does for Canada

Candace Laing is the Canadian Chamber of Commerce's president and CEO. Photo provided by Candace Laing.

The IEEPA tariffs struck down today include the “fentanyl/trafficking” tariffs Trump imposed on Canada in February 2025 — the ones used to justify 25% duties on most Canadian goods, later adjusted for USMCA-compliant products. Those tariffs are now constitutionally invalid.

That’s real relief for Canadian exporters. The legal foundation that justified over a year of economic punishment has been demolished by six of nine Supreme Court justices. The majority said what we’ve argued in this series since the beginning: IEEPA was a sanctions and emergency law, not a blank check for unlimited tariff authority.

But here is the critical distinction, and it matters enormously for the USMCA endgame I analyzed this morning.

What survives the ruling:

The Section 232 “national security” tariffs — 25% on steel, 25% on aluminum, 50% on copper — remain fully in force. Canada is explicitly named in these. They don’t touch IEEPA at all.

More importantly: nothing in today’s ruling prevents Trump from launching new Section 232 investigations declaring Canadian goods a national security threat. Section 232 requires a Commerce Department investigation and formal findings — but it has been used before, it will be used again, and the threshold for “national security” in this administration has proven to be whatever is politically convenient.

The Blunter Tools Laing Is Warning About

When the Canadian Chamber of Commerce says “blunter mechanisms,” they mean specifically this:

Section 232 expansions. Trump already used Section 232 to justify steel and aluminum tariffs. He can use the same authority to target Canadian lumber, Canadian oil, Canadian automotive parts — anything his Commerce Department can frame as a national security threat. These tariffs don’t require an emergency declaration. They require a study and a signature.

Congressional legislation. Trump’s team will now pursue what they couldn’t win in court: statutory authority from Congress to impose broad tariffs. If Republicans hand him that authority through legislation, no court can strike it down the same way.

USMCA withdrawal. As I detailed in Part X this morning, Trump’s own trade representative has floated bilateral deals with Mexico that would leave Canada with separate — and worse — terms. Today’s ruling doesn’t change Trump’s “half inclined to leave” posture. It may harden it: he lost his fast-acting emergency tool, and USMCA restructuring becomes one way to reassert leverage.

Direct bilateral pressure. With IEEPA gone, expect more targeted threats: bridge leverage, energy leverage, dairy demands, digital services tax demands. The tools become narrower and more sector-specific — which in some ways makes them harder to fight, because each one affects a different Canadian industry with different political constituencies.

How This Changes the USMCA Math

In Part X, I gave three scenarios and their updated probabilities:

  • Grand Bargain: 20% (down from 35%)

  • Bilateral Endgame: 65% (up from 50%)

  • Full Break: 15%

Today’s ruling forces another update — and the picture is genuinely mixed.

The case that it helps USMCA renewal: Trump just lost his most powerful unilateral lever. Section 232 and Section 301 authorities are slower, narrower, and more legally constrained. He has less ability to threaten Canada with instant 100% tariffs by declaring an emergency over a Davos speech. That could make negotiation more rational, because Trump can no longer credibly threaten to blow up the entire relationship overnight.

The case that it hurts USMCA renewal: Trump doesn’t respond to losing by moderating. He responds by escalating with whatever tools remain. The administration was already preparing for this ruling — Kevin Hassett said this morning they have a “backup plan that’s just as good.” If that backup plan involves Section 232 expansions targeting Canada specifically, Canadian negotiators face new threats before the IEEPA wounds have healed.

The trust problem I identified this morning is unchanged. Canada “questions whether it could trust any fresh trade agreements with Mr. Trump.” A Supreme Court ruling that strikes down his tariffs doesn’t restore that trust — it may deepen it, because Canada just watched Trump spend 14 months arguing he had unlimited emergency tariff power. That he lost doesn’t mean he won’t try again through different means.

Updated scenarios:

  • Grand Bargain: 20% → hold. The ruling doesn’t move this needle. Trump would need to fundamentally change his negotiating approach, which there’s no evidence of.

  • Bilateral Endgame: 65% → slight downward revision to 60%. Loss of IEEPA reduces Trump’s leverage slightly, making the brutal “Mexico deal, Canada gets worse terms” scenario marginally less certain. But Greer’s strategy remains unchanged.

  • Full Break: 15% → slight upward revision to 20%. Without IEEPA, Trump may view USMCA’s formal structure as an obstacle rather than an asset, making unilateral withdrawal more attractive as a way to reassert tariff authority through other means.

Trump’s Response: What It Means for Canada Specifically

Trump’s press conference confirmed and sharpened Laing’s warning.

The Section 122 “10% global tariff” he announced will apply to Canada — it is explicitly global. However, at 10% it is substantially lower than the IEEPA rates Canada faced, and the 150-day expiration clock means this is a temporary measure unless Congress extends it. That extension faces significant political obstacles with midterms approaching.

More relevant for the USMCA endgame: Trump’s attack on the Supreme Court as “swayed by foreign interests” and staffed by justices who are “unpatriotic and disloyal” tells Canadian negotiators something important. This is a president who interprets legal constraints as betrayal. When the next constraint arrives — a Section 122 expiration, a Section 301 investigation finding, a congressional refusal to extend tariffs — expect the same response: attack the institution, claim the power anyway, find another workaround.

Laing was right. McConnell said it plainly from the Republican side: “If the executive would like to enact trade policies that impact American producers and consumers, its path forward is crystal clear: convince their representatives under Article 1.” Trump’s response to that advice, delivered in real time at the same press conference: “I don’t have to. I have the right to do tariffs.”

He said this while standing in the White House briefing room, minutes after the Supreme Court told him he does not.

Canada is not negotiating with a president who accepts constraints. It is negotiating with a president who treats every constraint as a temporary obstacle and every institution that enforces one as an enemy. The 150-day Section 122 clock is not relief — it is the next deadline on the road to the next crisis.

The diversification strategy isn’t just smart. Given what was on display this afternoon, it is the only rational response.

The Irony Canada Has to Live With

Here is the brutal irony of today’s ruling from Canada’s perspective.

Canada has been fighting Trump’s tariffs in court, through diplomatic channels, through boycotts, through trade diversification. The Supreme Court just validated every legal argument Canada made: Trump exceeded his authority. The tariffs were unconstitutional. The emergency declaration was a fraud.

Canada was right.

And it doesn’t change much, because the underlying problem was never just legal. It was political. It was psychological. It was a president who uses trade as punishment for personal slights, who threatened 100% tariffs because Carney gave a Davos speech, who endorsed a bridge and then threatened to block it for leverage.

The Supreme Court can strike down IEEPA tariffs. It cannot make Trump a rational actor. It cannot restore trust that was deliberately destroyed. It cannot rebuild the “partners not adversaries” relationship that this administration torched in the first month of its second term.

And Canada knows it. That’s why Ottawa is already planning for Section 232 expansions. That’s why the China trade deal is operational. That’s why 91% of Canadians want less reliance on the United States — not because of IEEPA, but because of the country using it.

What Canada Does Now

The correct Canadian strategy hasn’t changed. If anything, today’s ruling validates it.

Keep diversifying. The China canola deal, the India discussions, the EU relationship strengthening — these aren’t reactions to losing IEEPA. They’re preparations for whatever blunter tool comes next. Section 232 on Canadian lumber? Less damaging if Europe is buying more. Direct pressure on the dairy sector? Less devastating if Asian markets are growing.

Keep the political discipline. Carney can’t cave on USMCA even if he wanted to — his mandate was won on standing firm, and the voters who gave him that mandate have empirical proof that accommodation doesn’t work. The apology after the Reagan ad bought zero goodwill. The legal victory today will buy approximately the same amount.

Let the refund chaos unfold on the American side. Over $200 billion in tariffs that may need refunding, hundreds of companies filing claims, and an administration that will resist every dollar. That’s America’s problem to manage. Canada’s focus should be on what comes next, not on litigating what just ended.

One More Reason Not to Bank on the Ruling

Canadian officials and businesses should absorb one more uncomfortable reality before treating today’s ruling as durable relief: this administration has a documented pattern of treating court orders as obstacles rather than verdicts. Federal judges have held administration officials in contempt over immigration enforcement. Congressional funding orders have been ignored. DOGE access to sensitive payment systems continued despite judicial orders to halt it.

A Supreme Court ruling carries different weight — defying it openly would be a constitutional crisis of historic proportions. But “different weight” doesn’t mean guaranteed compliance. The more likely path is procedural: delay the ruling’s implementation, contest its scope aggressively, immediately redeploy Section 232 authorities to replicate IEEPA’s effect before the legal dust settles, and resist refund claims at every turn. Canada cannot build its trade strategy on the assumption that a court ruling translates automatically into changed American behavior. Laing said it best: this is a legal ruling, not a reset. The chamber’s advice — prepare for blunter mechanisms — implicitly accounts for the possibility that the administration finds ways to blunt the ruling itself.

Breaking update—The 24-Hour Confirmation

If any further evidence were needed that Laing’s warning was correct, it arrived Saturday morning. Less than 24 hours after the Supreme Court struck down his IEEPA tariffs, Trump announced via Truth Social that he was raising his Section 122 replacement tariff from 10% to 15% — the statutory maximum — effective immediately. The ruling had not yet been formally implemented. The ink was barely dry.

This is what “blunter mechanisms” looks like in real time. Canada should plan accordingly.

The Bottom Line

Friday morning I wrote that the path to USMCA renewal is vanishing.

Soon after, the Supreme Court struck down the legal foundation for over a year of tariff punishment against Canada.

And Canada’s top business voice said: prepare for broader and more disruptive effects.

That’s not contradiction. That’s clarity.

Canada won a battle — a real one, a significant one, one that will eventually mean real tariff relief. But it’s winning the battle in a war that hasn’t ended. The opponent just lost his favored weapon. That makes him more dangerous in some ways, not less.

Laing is right. The ruling is not a reset of U.S. trade policy. It’s a reset of which tools that policy uses.

And Canada, after 14 months of economic warfare, boycotts, diversification deals, and one very good Davos speech, has learned something important: you don’t win this war in court. You win it by making yourself less dependent on the country waging it.

That work continues. Court ruling or not.
