CRTC’s Second Response

Why This Fight Just Got More Interesting

March 4, 2026

March 4, 2026

After my first response to CRTC pushed back on their “business decision” framing and asked whether my January submission would be included in the consultation record, I received a second letter.

The answer? No.

“For clarity, your letter of 19 January 2026 will not be included as part of the record of the upcoming Consumer Protection Codes Code harmonization proceeding.”

Translation: Everything you wrote doesn’t count. Start over when we say you can.

The Process Over Substance Approach

Director Kachi’s letter directs me to:

  • Wait for the formal consultation to open

  • “File an intervention” through their official process

  • Join a mailing list to be notified

  • Check their webpage periodically

Notice what’s NOT in this response:

  • Any engagement with the substance of my argument

  • Any acknowledgment of the regulatory gap I identified

  • Any response to my question about how they distinguish “business decisions” from “consumer protection mandates”

Just: follow the process.

Why This Proves My Point

This is exactly the dynamic I’ve been describing.

When you raise a systemic consumer protection issue in telecommunications:

  1. CCTS says: “That’s a policy issue, take it to CRTC”

  2. CRTC says: “That’s a business decision, take individual complaints to CCTS”

  3. You submit a policy analysis

  4. CRTC says: “That submission doesn’t count, wait for the formal process”

  5. Formal process opens... eventually... maybe in Spring 2026... they’ll let you know...

Nobody wants to examine whether advance billing serves consumer protection principles. They just want to manage the paperwork.

Why This Actually Makes the Intervention Stronger

Here’s what CRTC’s bureaucratic response inadvertently revealed: they have no substantive counter-argument.

If advance billing served consumer protection objectives, they could have said so. If my analysis was flawed, they could have pointed out the flaws. Instead, they retreated to procedure.

Which brings me to the most important part of my argument—the part that makes this not just consumer protection, but good policy for everyone involved.

The Win/Win Nobody Talks About

Eliminating advance billing isn’t a zero-sum fight between consumers and carriers. It’s a win for both sides.

Here’s why:

For Consumers (Obvious):

  • Pay only for service actually received

  • No overpayments requiring refunds

  • No late fees on amounts never owed

  • No credit bureau risk during processing delays

  • Account balances that reflect reality

For Carriers (Not Obvious, But True):

According to CCTS’s 2024-25 Annual Report:

  • 23,647 complaints (record high)

  • 46% are billing-related (approximately 10,878 billing complaints)

  • Top billing complaints: “incorrect charges and missing account credits”

These are precisely the problems advance billing creates.

Now here’s the kicker: Carriers pay fees to CCTS for every accepted complaint.

Do the math:

  • Advance billing creates systematic billing disputes

  • Each dispute costs the carrier CCTS fees

  • Each dispute requires customer service time

  • Each refund requires administrative processing

  • All of this is ongoing, year after year

Eliminating advance billing would:

  1. Systematically reduce CCTS complaint volumes

  2. Reduce CCTS fees carriers pay

  3. Reduce administrative costs of refund processing

  4. Reduce customer service costs dealing with billing disputes

  5. Improve customer satisfaction scores

Yes, switching to arrears billing creates a one-time cash flow adjustment. For one billing cycle, carriers collect payment for last month while providing this month’s service.

But that’s a one-time impact for multi-billion dollar corporations with access to capital markets.

The ongoing cost savings from reduced complaints, administrative overhead, and CCTS fees would offset that transition cost relatively quickly.

This is why the intervention should succeed: it’s not asking CRTC to choose between consumer protection and carrier interests. It’s showing how consumer protection and operational efficiency align.

Carriers resist because “this is how we’ve always done it,” not because advance billing is actually good for their bottom line.

Building Coalition

I’m not fighting this alone.

I’ve reached out to Professor Michael Geist (University of Ottawa Faculty of Law, leading Canadian telecom policy expert) to ask whether he would consider amplifying this position—either through his blog or his own intervention filing.

The more voices CRTC hears making this argument, the harder it becomes to dismiss as one person’s complaint.

If you have connections to consumer advocacy groups, telecom policy experts, or organizations that file CRTC interventions, send them this analysis. OpenMedia, Public Interest Advocacy Centre, Consumers Council of Canada—these groups have the resources to make this argument at scale.

Making This Public

I’m publishing my full intervention draft here

Why make it public?

  1. Transparency: You can see exactly what I’m arguing

  2. Template: Others can use this framework for their own interventions

  3. Collaboration: Feedback makes the argument stronger

  4. Accountability: CRTC can’t claim nobody told them

If you’re planning to file an intervention when the consultation opens, feel free to reference this analysis, use the research, or adapt the arguments. That’s the point.

The Path Forward

Fine. I’ll play by their rules.

When CRTC opens the formal Consumer Protection Code consultation, I will:

  • File this intervention through their official process

  • Include both preliminary CRTC responses as evidence of regulatory avoidance

  • Submit updated CCTS data and carrier cost analysis

  • Coordinate with anyone else who wants to make this argument

They want a formal proceeding? Let’s give them one they can’t ignore.

What You Can Do Now

1. Join the CRTC mailing list to be notified when consultation opens: CRTC Consumer Protections Action Plan

2. Document your experience. Start writing down specific instances where advance billing created problems:

  • Amounts you overpaid

  • Refunds that took weeks/months

  • Late fees on amounts you never owed

  • Inability to reach customer service after cancellation

  • Confusion about what you actually owed

When the consultation opens, these stories become official record.

3. Share this analysis with anyone who might care:

  • Consumer advocacy groups

  • Policy experts

  • Friends who’ve been screwed by telecom billing

  • Journalists covering telecom issues

4. If you have expertise in telecom policy, regulatory economics, or consumer protection law—and you’re willing to file an intervention—let me know in the comments. Coalition building matters.

Why This Matters

CRTC wants process? We’ll give them process.

But we’ll make sure that process includes:

  • Hundreds of Canadian consumer voices

  • Data showing how advance billing creates systematic harm

  • Evidence that eliminating it would actually reduce carrier costs

  • Coalition of credible policy experts

  • Analysis showing CRTC has never examined this from first principles

The question CRTC will have to answer:

Why should Canadian consumers pay in advance for telecommunications service when:

  1. No other essential utility requires it

  2. It creates systematic consumer harm

  3. It increases carrier operational costs

  4. The historical rationale is obsolete

  5. Real-time billing systems already exist

I’m betting they won’t have a good answer.

Stay tuned.
