The Canadian Snowman’s Amended Guide to Classic Fairy Tales

In which we observe that David Graham left the best ones on the table

April 26, 2026

Brendan Smialowski / Getty

David Graham of The Atlantic published a delightful exercise: a reinterpretation of classic fairy tales through the moral lens of the forty-seventh president of the United States. It was clever, pointed, and—like most American commentary—incomplete. Graham covered eight tales and then went home, as though the Western canon of cautionary fiction begins and ends with what you can fit between commercial breaks.

Here in Canada, where we learn our fairy tales in two official languages and occasionally a third if you count Albertan, we feel an obligation to finish the job. What follows is the remainder of the curriculum.

“The Emperor’s New Clothes”

Plot: Two swindlers convince a vain emperor that they have woven him a magnificent suit of clothing invisible to anyone who is stupid or incompetent. The emperor parades through town naked while his courtiers praise the garment’s beauty. A child finally shouts that the emperor has no clothes.

Moral: The child is a member of the fake news media and should be investigated by the DOJ. The clothes are real, perfect, possibly the most beautiful clothes anyone has ever seen—many people are saying so—and the crowd that gathered to admire them was the largest crowd in the history of crowds. The swindlers, meanwhile, have been awarded a $2.3 billion DOGE contract to redesign the uniforms of the federal workforce. Elon says they’ll be done by Thursday.

“The Tortoise and the Hare”

Plot: A slow tortoise challenges a fast hare to a race. The overconfident hare, so far ahead that he takes a nap, loses to the plodding but persistent tortoise.

Moral: This race was rigged. Everybody knows the hare was winning by a lot, and then, very late at night, after all the animals had gone to sleep, suitcases full of new distance appeared from nowhere. The tortoise should be investigated, the race decertified, and the finish line seized by the federal government under the National Emergencies Act. The tortoise is also suspected of being from Greenland, which, as everyone knows, we’re going to be buying very soon, whether the Danes like it or not.

“The Boy Who Cried Wolf”

Plot: A shepherd boy, bored with his duties, repeatedly raises false alarms about a wolf attacking the flock. The villagers come running each time, only to find there is no wolf. When a real wolf finally appears, no one believes the boy, and the sheep are devoured.

Moral: The boy was right every time. Those were all wolves. The fact that nobody could see them proves how deep the conspiracy goes. The boy should cry wolf louder, more often, and preferably in all caps on Truth Social at 3 a.m. If the sheep get eaten, that’s the villagers’ fault for watching CNN. Besides, the wolves have been treated very unfairly, and several of them—including the ones from January 6—have already received presidential pardons.

“Jack and the Beanstalk”

Plot: A poor boy trades the family cow for magic beans. The beans grow into a beanstalk reaching a castle in the clouds, where a giant lives with a golden-egg-laying goose, a bag of gold, and a singing harp. Jack steals all three, chops down the beanstalk as the giant pursues him, and the giant falls to his death.

Moral: This is essentially the Greenland strategy. The cow was worthless. The beans were speculative. The castle belongs to someone who doesn’t appreciate it. You go up, take what you want, destroy the only connection behind you, and if the giant dies, that’s a negotiating problem he should have anticipated. The golden goose should be leveraged into a cryptocurrency. The harp should be branded. And the beanstalk should have had a tariff on it. Jack would make an excellent Secretary of State, provided he’s willing to annex the cloud kingdom, rename it, and tell the press he was invited. The same approach applies to the Panama Canal, the Gulf of Mexico, and—let us not forget—your neighbour’s backyard, should it contain minerals or a Northwest Passage.

“Little Red Riding Hood”

Plot: A girl in a red hood is sent to visit her grandmother but encounters a wolf, who races ahead, eats the grandmother, and disguises himself in her clothing. When Red Riding Hood arrives, she is deceived until she notices the wolf’s oversized features. In most versions, a huntsman arrives just in time to save them both.

Moral: The grandmother was in the country illegally and has been rendered to a maximum-security wolf stomach in El Salvador without due process. Her family has been told she was never in the cottage. A Supreme Court order requiring her return has been acknowledged and ignored. The red hood shows excellent political instincts. The huntsman represents the Second Amendment and is the only hero in this story. The wolf has been appointed to a senior advisory role and describes his relationship with the grandmother as “beautiful,” adding that they “fell in love,” much like a certain president and a certain North Korean dictator who exchanged what were described, without apparent irony, as “love letters.”

“The Ugly Duckling”

Plot: A duckling, mocked and ostracized for being different, endures a miserable youth until he matures into a beautiful swan, more magnificent than any of the ducks who tormented him.

Moral: Correct. The duckling was always the most beautiful bird on the pond, and the fact that nobody recognized it earlier was a witch hunt—the likes of which no waterfowl has ever endured in the history of our country. Now that the duckling is a swan—the biggest, most powerful swan anyone has ever seen—he is entitled to go back, impose 245% tariffs on the ducks, annex the pond, threaten to rename it, and demand that all the other birds acknowledge they are, and always have been, the fifty-first flock. Any duck who disagrees will be subjected to “economic coercion that will make your little duck head spin.”

“The Three Little Pigs”

Plot: Three pigs each build a house—one of straw, one of sticks, and one of bricks. A wolf comes along, blows down the first two houses, but cannot destroy the brick house, where all three pigs take refuge.

Moral: The third pig has the right idea about building a wall. But he made a crucial error: he paid for it himself. The wolf should have been made to pay for the wall. This is basic statecraft. The first two pigs were not “blown down”—they were victims of an open-border policy that let wolves in without proper vetting. The brick house should be extended along the entire southern perimeter of the farmyard, the pigs’ children on the other side should be separated from their parents as a deterrent, and the whole project should be funded by a national emergency declaration after Congress declines to appropriate straw.

“Rapunzel”

Plot: A girl with impossibly long hair is locked in a tower by a sorceress. A prince discovers her, climbs her hair to reach her, and they fall in love. The sorceress, enraged, banishes Rapunzel to the wilderness and blinds the prince. After years of wandering, they are reunited, and Rapunzel’s tears restore the prince’s sight.

Moral: The tower is fine. The tower is, in fact, a very well-run facility—much like CECOT, which has received excellent reviews from people who have never been inside it. The sorceress had every right to put Rapunzel there. She was a suspected member of a gang, probably, and the paperwork will turn up eventually, or it won’t, because the administration is not required to explain itself to liberal fairy-tale courts. The prince’s attempts to reach her constitute illegal entry. If Rapunzel had simply gone through the proper channels—which do not exist—none of this would have happened. She was sent to the wilderness in full compliance with applicable law, and any Supreme Court ruling to the contrary will be described as “an opinion” and then disregarded.

“Pinocchio”

Plot: A wooden puppet is brought to life and told he can become a real boy if he proves himself brave, truthful, and unselfish. His nose grows whenever he tells a lie. After many misadventures—including being turned into a donkey—he earns his humanity through acts of selflessness.

Moral: The growing nose is a form of government overreach and possibly election interference. A man should be free to say whatever he wants—including that he won an election he didn’t, that a crowd was the largest in history when it wasn’t, that a hurricane was heading somewhere it wasn’t, that he had nothing to do with a hush-money payment he orchestrated, and that the boxes of classified documents in his bathroom were just “newspapers and magazines”—without his face being regulated by some deep-state fairy with a clear political agenda. Also, the puppet was already a real boy. The realest boy. People are coming up to him, big strong people, with tears in their eyes, saying, “Sir, you are the realest boy we’ve ever seen.” The nose, by the way, is a perfectly normal size.

“The Frog Prince”

Plot: A princess reluctantly befriends a frog after he retrieves her golden ball from a well. When she finally kisses him—or, in some versions, throws him against a wall—he transforms into a handsome prince. They marry and live happily.

Moral: The frog was always a prince. A strong leader. A very smart guy. He runs a tough lily pad, sure, but that’s what it takes to maintain order in a swamp. The princess didn’t throw him against a wall—they exchanged “beautiful letters,” and they fell in love. It doesn’t matter that the frog feeds dissidents to herons, conducts weapons tests in the shallows, or holds political prisoners in submerged cages. What matters is that the frog said very nice things at the summit, and the princess got a beautiful commemorative coin out of it. The frog prince now receives regular congratulatory phone calls and has been described as “very honourable,” which is a word that means whatever you need it to mean at any given press conference.

“The Fox and the Grapes”

Plot: A hungry fox spots grapes hanging high on a vine. After several failed attempts to reach them, he walks away, declaring that they were probably sour anyway.

Moral: The grapes were always sour. The fox never wanted them. He was never even trying to get them. He was actually offered the grapes—very strongly, by many people—and he turned them down because they were low-quality grapes, probably from Denmark, which has been very unfair on the Greenland question. The vine is too high, which constitutes a non-tariff trade barrier and will be subject to a 150% reciprocal grape tariff effective Wednesday, unless it’s paused on Thursday, reimposed on Friday under a different executive order with the same name, and then modified on Saturday after the bond market has what can only be described as a nervous breakdown.

“Stone Soup”

Plot: A hungry traveller arrives in a village where no one will share food. He announces he will make “stone soup,” placing a stone in a pot of boiling water. Curious villagers gather, and when the traveller suggests the soup would be even better with a carrot, a potato, some meat, each villager contributes something. The result is a hearty communal soup.

Moral: The traveller is the greatest negotiator the village has ever seen. He convinced an entire community to provide free ingredients for a product made of nothing, which he branded, bottled, and is now selling for $59.99 alongside a limited-edition Trump Bible, a pair of golden sneakers, a non-fungible digital trading card of himself as a soup-themed superhero, and a $100,000 commemorative meme coin that fluctuates in value based on his mood. The villagers should be grateful. Many of them are now shareholders in $DJT, which is currently trading at a valuation that has no discernible relationship to the contents of the pot.

“Sleeping Beauty”

Plot: A princess, cursed at birth by an uninvited fairy, pricks her finger on a spindle and falls into a deep sleep that lasts a hundred years, until a prince arrives and wakes her with a kiss.

Moral: The uninvited fairy was simply retaliating for being excluded, which is perfectly understandable. Many people have been excluded from things and have had to retaliate—for instance, by inciting a mob to march on Congress after losing an election, or by threatening military force against an ally whose island territory you find geographically convenient. The princess slept for a hundred years because she didn’t have Fox News, which is the only reliable information source in any kingdom. The prince was very handsome and strong, and it is important to note that he did not ask for consent before kissing her, because, as has been explained on a now-famous Access Hollywood tape, when you’re a prince, they let you do it.

A note from the Canadian sidelines: Up here, we tell our children the same fairy tales, but with a different emphasis. In our version, the three bears file a police report. The emperor’s advisors resign in quiet dignity. The tortoise and the hare tie and then apologize to each other. The boy who cried wolf gets therapy, which is covered. Rapunzel has legal representation. The frog prince is politely declined at the border. And when someone suggests buying the beanstalk kingdom, we send a firm but unfailingly polite diplomatic note explaining that cloud kingdoms are not for sale, the canal is ours, the gulf has a name, and we are not—will never be—your fifty-first state.

We find these endings less exciting but considerably more sustainable.

Acknowledgments

The tales above were borrowed, with gratitude, from Aesop (sixth century BC, who started all of this), the Brothers Grimm (who wrote down what German grandmothers already knew), Hans Christian Andersen (who gave us the Emperor’s wardrobe malfunction and the ugly duckling, and who was Danish, which now feels relevant), Charles Perrault (who got to Sleeping Beauty and Little Red Riding Hood first, because the French always do), Carlo Collodi (who understood, in 1883, that a puppet whose nose grows when he lies would one day feel less like fiction), and the anonymous oral traditions of England and Europe, which gave us Jack, his beanstalk, three pigs of varying architectural ambition, and a stone that somehow feeds a village—all without a single licensing agreement.

And to Donald J. Trump, forty-fifth and forty-seventh President of the United States, without whom none of these reinterpretations would be necessary, believable, or—regrettably—satirical. Mr. President, the Western canon of children’s literature has endured for centuries without requiring a update. It took a singular talent to make “The Boy Who Cried Wolf” feel like a documentary, “Jack and the Beanstalk” like a policy blueprint, and “Pinocchio” like a biography. We are, in our way, grateful. You have made fairy tales relevant again—though not, perhaps, in the manner you intended.

The wolves, we note, remain at the border. They have been very patient.

Stephen Gordon writes the CanadianSnowmanSays newsletter from Toronto, where the fairy tales are bilingual, the wolves wait politely at the border, and nobody—not even the giant—is interested in becoming a state.
