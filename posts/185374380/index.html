The Greengrocer Took Down the Sign

Part 10: What It All Means

January 22, 2026

Part 10 of the Davos Rupture Series
CanadianSnowmanSays

For five days in January 2026, two visions of international order collided in Davos.

One demanded subordination through coercion. The other offered partnership through collective strength.

By the time the snow cleared, we knew which vision worked.

Greenland became the test case. Trump spent 71 minutes demanding full ownership. Denmark stood firm. Europe coordinated. Within 24 hours, Trump backed down to vague frameworks that preserved Danish sovereignty.

The frameworks outlined in Parts 2-4—Carney’s variable geometry, Macron’s refusal to brutalize, von der Leyen’s capacity building—worked exactly as described. Trump’s five core misunderstandings about power, outlined in Part 8, all proved costly.

This isn’t the end of American power. It’s the emergence of something different: middle powers discovering they have agency, coordination neutralizing coercion, sovereignty maintained through solidarity.

This is what the post-American order actually looks like.

  • Build Asia-Pacific relationships (CPTPP, bilateral agreements)

Havel’s greengrocer framework guides decisions: Where are we living within the lie? Where can we stop pretending?

Whoever governs will face the same challenges. Carney’s framework provides answers.

Pre-Davos: Canada debated whether to resist American demands.

Post-Davos: Canada debates how to resist while building alternatives.

Von der Leyen’s €890 Billion:

Real budgets, actual timelines:

  • Economic diversification accelerates

The Article 5 Demonstration:

Here’s the profound irony Trump missed: While questioning whether NATO allies would defend the United States, Europe was demonstrating that Article 5 works—by defending Denmark against Trump.

Denmark increased military deployment to Greenland. Germany cancelled meetings in solidarity. European Parliament suspended trade deals. Nordic countries coordinated responses. The EU issued statements backing Danish sovereignty.

This wasn’t symbolic. This was Article 5 in practice:

Article 5: “An armed attack against one or more of [the Allies] in Europe or North America shall be considered an attack against them all.”

Trump was the attack. Europe was the collective defense.

Not military force—Trump wasn’t invading. But coercion, territorial demands, and sovereignty threats against a NATO member activated the same solidarity mechanisms. When one ally faces pressure meant to subordinate them, others respond collectively.

The Message to Moscow:

If anyone was watching closely, it was Vladimir Putin.

For years, Putin has bet NATO solidarity is fiction—that when tested, Western allies would fracture, prioritize bilateral interests, abandon collective commitments.

Greenland proved otherwise.

When Trump demanded Denmark surrender territory, Europe didn’t negotiate bilaterally, pressure Denmark to accommodate, stay silent, or fragment into competing responses. Instead: immediate coordination across institutions, suspended economic agreements, cancelled diplomatic meetings, and demonstrated that attacks on one member activate collective response.

Putin watched Denmark face the wrath of the world’s most powerful military, backed by threats and economic coercion—and watched Europe stand firm.

The Strategic Lesson:

Russia’s entire Ukraine strategy assumes NATO wouldn’t actually defend the Baltics, Poland, or Romania if tested. That America might pressure allies to accept territorial concessions. That European solidarity would crack under bilateral pressure.

Greenland demonstrated the opposite:

If NATO allies will defend Denmark against American pressure—against their most important security partner, against the country that underwrites European defense—they’ll certainly defend Estonia against Russian pressure.

Article 5 isn’t conditional on who’s applying pressure. It’s a commitment to collective defense of sovereignty, period.

The Unintended Validation:

Trump spent 71 minutes questioning NATO’s reliability: “We will be there for them 100%, but I’m not sure that they would be there for us.”

Within 24 hours, NATO proved it works—by defending a member against Trump.

The irony is exquisite: Trump demonstrated Article 5’s strength while trying to undermine it. Russia learned that NATO solidarity is real while Trump tried to prove it’s transactional.

As one European official texted after Trump’s retreat: “We just showed Putin that Article 5 isn’t American generosity toward Europe. It’s European commitment to each other that includes America when America honors alliance principles.”

Denmark didn’t just defend Greenland. Europe didn’t just coordinate against coercion.

They proved the alliance works.

Thank you, Mr. Trump, for the demonstration.

The Transformation:

Pre-Davos: Europe debated whether to resist or accommodate.

Post-Davos: Europe debates how to build independent capacity while maintaining partnership.

The Cyprus Model Under Discussion

The New York Times reported Thursday on what NATO officials were actually discussing in Brussels—the concrete proposals that might constitute the vague “framework” Trump announced:

The Concept:

Create “sovereign base areas” for U.S. military installations in Greenland, modeled after British sovereign bases in Cyprus. Under this arrangement, small pockets of Greenlandic territory would transfer sovereignty to the United States, creating American soil in Greenland similar to how British bases in Cyprus are British territory.

This is fundamentally different from Trump’s Wednesday demand for “full ownership” of all Greenland, but it’s also fundamentally different from the existing U.S. base arrangement under the 1951 defense pact.

The Current Arrangement:

Right now, the United States maintains Thule Air Base (renamed Pituffik Space Base) in Greenland under a 1951 agreement that grants military access without sovereignty transfer. About 150 U.S. personnel operate a remote missile defense station.

The Proposed Change:

Under the Cyprus model, the United States wouldn’t just have access—it would own the land the bases sit on, giving greater control than current embassy sites or military access agreements provide.

Why This Matters to Trump:

Trump has said he wants guaranteed land for his “Golden Dome” missile defense system. Current agreements could theoretically be terminated if Greenland gains full independence from Denmark. Sovereign U.S. bases would ensure permanent American presence regardless of Greenland’s future political status.

The Problem:

Denmark says no. Greenland says no. And even if they eventually said yes to small sovereign bases, that’s not what Trump demanded Wednesday.

General Alexus G. Grynkewich, NATO’s top commander in Europe, confirmed the Cyprus model was discussed but emphasized “we’ve done no planning yet” for joint Arctic operations and “we have not received political guidance to move out.”

Translation: It’s been discussed. Nothing’s been decided. Nobody’s agreed to anything. Trump’s “framework” may be NATO officials discussing possibilities that Denmark rejects.

The Broader NATO Arctic Plan:

Multiple officials described plans for “Arctic Sentry”—a significant new NATO mission in the Arctic, modeled on similar missions in the Baltic Sea and Eastern Europe meant to counter Russia.

This would expand NATO’s presence, increase coordination on Arctic security, and formalize what’s been informal cooperation.

Denmark could agree to this. Greenland could support it. It advances actual security interests without requiring sovereignty transfer.

But it’s not what Trump said he wanted. And it’s not what his “framework” claim suggested he got.

Denmark Denies Direct Negotiations

Here’s what makes Trump’s “framework” announcement even more questionable: A senior Danish official told the New York Times that “there have been no direct discussions between Denmark and the U.S. about the possibility of granting the U.S. sovereign parcels of land.”

Read that carefully. NATO officials are discussing the Cyprus model as a possibility. But Denmark—the country that would actually have to agree to it—says no direct discussions with the United States about sovereign land transfer have occurred.

The Disconnect:

  • NATO officials: Discussing hypothetical sovereign base arrangements

  • Trump: Announced he has a “framework”

  • Denmark: Says no actual negotiations on sovereign bases have happened

This is the pattern playing out in real time: Trump announces outcomes before creating them, hopes the announcement creates momentum toward reality.

It’s why Frederiksen had to issue her statement clarifying “We can negotiate on everything political; security, investments, economy. But we cannot negotiate on our sovereignty.” She’s responding to Trump’s announcement by restating what Trump already knows: Denmark hasn’t agreed to anything involving sovereignty transfer.

Trump’s “framework” appears to be: NATO officials discussed possibilities → Trump announced framework → Denmark says wait, we haven’t discussed this → Everyone scrambles to figure out what Trump actually announced.

The emperor has no clothes. Again.

European Responses: Cautious Relief, Firm Principles

European leaders’ responses to Trump’s Wednesday evening framework announcement revealed a pattern: relief at de-escalation paired with absolute clarity that sovereignty remains non-negotiable.

Dick Schoof (Netherlands): “Positive that the path to de-escalation has been embarked upon and that the 10 percent import tariffs are off the table.” The Dutch response captured European sentiment: relief without celebration.

Giorgia Meloni (Italy): “It is essential to continue fostering dialogue between allied nations.” Even Meloni—typically more sympathetic to Trump—emphasized “dialogue” over demands, “allied nations” over subordination.

Emmanuel Macron - Unity Works: As Macron arrived at the emergency European Council meeting in Brussels on Thursday—a summit specifically convened to discuss Trump’s Greenland threats—he articulated what the week had proven:

“We started the week with some kind of escalation, threats, threats of invasion and tariff threats, and we have returned to a situation that seems to me much more acceptable, even though we remain vigilant. So the conclusion to be drawn is that when Europe reacts in a united manner, using the instruments at its disposal, and when it is threatened, it can command respect, which is a very good thing.”

This wasn’t celebratory. It was diagnostic: European unity commanded respect. Fragmentation would have invited exploitation.

France - Pushback on False Claims:

The office of President Emmanuel Macron took the unusual step of publicly contradicting Trump’s Davos claim about French drug prices. Trump had claimed he’d forced France to raise prescription drug prices by threatening wine tariffs, and that Macron agreed “in three minutes.”

Macron’s office issued a statement calling it “fake news,” noting that “the president does not set drug prices in France. They are regulated by the social security system and have, in fact, remained stable.”

The pushback mattered: European governments weren’t just accepting Trump’s framework announcement at face value. They were actively fact-checking his claims and correcting false narratives publicly.

European Parliament - Waiting for Substance:

The European Parliament, which had suspended the E.U.-U.S. trade deal ratification immediately after Trump’s Wednesday speech, indicated that simply dropping the tariff threat wouldn’t automatically resume the process.

Bernd Lange’s position remained: no resumption of trade deal work “until the U.S. decides to reengage on a path of cooperation rather than confrontation.”

The message: Trump’s retreat from immediate threats is necessary but not sufficient. Actual partnership requires more than just not threatening allies.

Denmark - The Red Line Holds:

Danish Foreign Minister Lars Løkke Rasmussen captured the relief-but-firm-principle balance: “Trump’s removing the tariff threat ‘takes the heat out,’” he acknowledged. But then the clarity: The United States will not “own” Greenland. “That is a red line.”

His fuller assessment: “This morning we had a president who would not rule out taking Greenland by force. Now he says he will not take Greenland by force. That creates a small sliver of hope.”

Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen’s statement was even more explicit about what’s negotiable and what isn’t:

“We can negotiate on everything political; security, investments, economy. But we cannot negotiate on our sovereignty. Our red lines are the same as they have been all along. We will of course not cede sovereignty. And Greenland’s future must be defined by Greenland, and Greenland is part of Denmark.”

When asked directly if Denmark would be open to ceding territory in Greenland to the United States, Frederiksen was unequivocal: “We have said from the very beginning that a discussion about our status as a sovereign state cannot be discussed—it cannot be changed.”

What This Reveals:

The Danish position offers a masterclass in diplomatic clarity: We’ll discuss expanded partnership, increased security cooperation, economic investment, infrastructure development—anything except sovereignty itself.

This isn’t inflexibility. It’s defining the difference between partnership (negotiable) and subordination (non-negotiable).

The Unified European Message:

Across these responses, a pattern emerged:

  1. Relief at de-escalation - glad tariffs are suspended, military force ruled out

  2. Firm on principles - sovereignty non-negotiable, partnership requires mutual respect

  3. Skeptical of claims - actively fact-checking Trump’s statements (France on drug prices)

  4. Conditional engagement - trade deal remains suspended pending substantive change

  5. Coordination continuing - each response reinforced others

What’s notable is what’s missing from European responses: nobody celebrated Trump’s framework as a breakthrough. Nobody thanked him for his “deal.” Nobody suggested this resolved fundamental questions about American reliability.

The message was clear: We’re relieved you’ve backed away from your worst threats. We remain ready for partnership. But we’re not pretending this week didn’t happen or that our view of American alliance commitments hasn’t fundamentally changed.

The Long-Term Shift:

Von der Leyen’s €890 billion European defense commitment didn’t suddenly become unnecessary because Trump backed down from Greenland. Macron’s call for European strategic autonomy didn’t evaporate. Carney’s variable geometry framework didn’t become obsolete.

The frameworks outlined in Parts 2-4 remain necessary precisely because Europe learned that American demands can escalate rapidly, that sovereignty can be questioned even among close allies, and that coordination works when coercion arrives.

Trump’s retreat validated the approach. It didn’t make the approach unnecessary.

Zelensky’s Warning: Europe Must Step Up

President Volodymyr Zelensky used his Thursday appearance at Davos to deliver one of his most scathing critiques of Europe—surprising given that Europe has become Ukraine’s most reliable ally as Trump stepped back.

His diagnosis was blunt:

“Instead of becoming a truly global power, Europe remains a beautiful but fragmented kaleidoscope of small and middle powers. Instead of taking the lead in defending freedom worldwide, especially when America’s focus shifts elsewhere, Europe looks lost, trying to convince the U.S. president to change. But he will not change.”

On Europe’s Greenland Response:

Zelensky was particularly critical of how Europe handled Trump’s territorial demands:

“If you send 30 or 40 soldiers to Greenland—what is that for? What message does it send? What’s the message to Putin? To China?”

His point: Symbolic gestures aren’t sufficient responses to actual threats. Europe’s coordination on Greenland worked, but the scale of response must match the scale of challenge.

On NATO’s Reliability:

Most strikingly, Zelensky—who has long pressed for Ukraine’s NATO membership—questioned whether NATO actually works when tested:

“Today, Europe relies only on the belief that if danger comes, NATO will act. But no one has really seen the alliance in action. If Putin decides to take Lithuania or strike Poland, who will respond? Who will respond? Right now, NATO exists thanks to the belief that the United States will act, that it will not stand aside and will help. But what if it doesn’t? Believe me, this question is everywhere, in the minds of every European leader.”

The Irony:

Zelensky delivered this critique even as Europe was demonstrating that Article 5 solidarity works—by defending Denmark against American pressure. His point wasn’t that Article 5 is fiction, but that Europe must be prepared for scenarios where American participation can’t be assumed.

The Call to Action:

“You need Ukraine’s independence too—because tomorrow, you may have to defend your way of life. And when Ukraine is with you, no one will wipe their feet on you.”

Zelensky’s message: Europe proved coordination works this week. Now build the capacity to make that coordination decisive without depending on American involvement.

What This Reveals:

Zelensky sees what Carney diagnosed: The rupture is real. The post-American order requires Europe to develop autonomous capability while maintaining partnership. Variable geometry requires middle powers to be powerful enough that their coordination matters.

Greenland proved the concept works. Now Europe must scale the capacity to match the concept.

The Emergency Brussels Summit

That Thursday’s events required an emergency European Council meeting in Brussels—specifically convened to discuss Trump’s Greenland threats—demonstrates how seriously Europe took the crisis.

This wasn’t a scheduled meeting where Greenland got added to the agenda. This was a crisis summit called in direct response to an American president threatening a NATO ally’s sovereignty.

Leaders arrived expressing relief at de-escalation but also deep concern about trans-Atlantic relations. Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk captured the broader worry: “The fact that Ukraine has faded into the background is very bad news. We’ll need to persuade our American friends—and all Europeans—to once again focus on what’s fundamental for our security.”

The message: Greenland was a crisis. But the underlying issue—American reliability, alliance solidarity, European agency—remains unresolved.

The summit itself validated the coordination that defeated Trump’s pressure. But it also acknowledged that one successful coordination doesn’t solve the fundamental rupture Carney diagnosed.

Or he could learn the wrong lesson: didn’t threaten hard enough, need more leverage next time.

Early indications suggest Trump learned the wrong lesson.

Rather than recognizing that coercion unified opposition and coordination defeated pressure, Trump appears to have internalized a different narrative: he “won” a framework, demonstrated American strength, and secured his objectives.

Early indications from his subsequent statements suggest Trump learned the wrong lesson.

The Victory Narrative:

Trump repeatedly characterized the framework as a win, using language like “great deal for America,” “exactly what we needed,” and “solved the Arctic security problem.”

Notably absent from these victory claims: any acknowledgment that he’d backed down from full ownership, any admission that his tariff threats failed, any recognition that allied coordination forced the retreat.

The Blame Deflection:

When asked about contradictions—between his “59 countries” Board of Peace claim and his aide’s “maybe 25,” between his “framework with Rutte” and Rutte’s spokesperson denying sovereignty compromise—Trump didn’t acknowledge discrepancies. He moved to other topics.

This pattern suggests Trump hasn’t learned that credibility erosion compounds. Each contradicted claim makes the next claim less believable. Each spokesperson denial makes future announcements more suspect.

The Next Threat:

Perhaps most tellingly, Trump’s broader approach to tariffs and alliance pressure didn’t change. Within days, he was threatening tariffs on other allies over different issues, suggesting the Greenland retreat was tactical (this specific threat didn’t work) rather than strategic (threats don’t work).

What This Means:

If Trump learned the right lesson—that coercion backfires among democratic allies—we’d expect:

  • Shift toward partnership language

  • Consultation with allies before announcements

  • Respect for sovereignty even when inconvenient

  • Building actual coalitions rather than demanding compliance

If Trump learned the wrong lesson—that he won despite backing down—we’d expect:

  • Continued threat-based diplomacy

  • More “frameworks” announced before they exist

  • Escalating demands when initial pressure fails

  • Pattern repeating with different targets

The evidence suggests the latter. Which means middle powers will get more practice using the Denmark playbook. Which means variable geometry networks will strengthen. Which means American influence will continue declining even as Trump declares victories.

The Credibility Problem:

Each retreat teaches allies: Trump’s threats aren’t final. His red lines can be resisted. Time favors middle powers.

This makes future coercion less effective. The boy who cried wolf loses audience.

The Pattern:

Healthcare: “Great plan” → “concept of a plan” Greenland: “Full ownership” → “concept of a deal”

When Trump doesn’t get what he wants, “concept” emerges. It’s admission disguised as confidence.

THE RUPTURE CARNEY DIAGNOSED

Carney was right: This isn’t transition. It’s rupture.

Not between America and allies. Between two visions of how alliances work:

Old Vision:

  • America sets agenda, allies comply

  • Sovereignty conditional on American approval

  • Protection in exchange for deference

  • Bilateral relationships, hierarchy

New Vision:

  • Middle powers coordinate, America negotiates as partner

  • Sovereignty non-negotiable

  • Mutual benefit, not transactional exchange

  • Multilateral networks, variable geometry

The Historical Moment:

Post-WWII order lasted 75 years on this foundation: American leadership, allied support, mutual benefit, shared values.

That order required American restraint. America had power but used it to build institutions, (mostly) respect sovereignty, enable partnership.

When America demands territorial concessions from NATO allies, questions collective defense, treats partnerships as protection rackets—the foundation cracks.

The rupture isn’t allies abandoning America. It’s America abandoning the principles that made the alliance work.

Middle powers are building frameworks that preserve those principles.

THE BOARD OF PEACE POSTSCRIPT: THE CEREMONY THAT WASN’T

Trump announced a Board of Peace charter signing ceremony for Thursday. The ceremony occurred, but the limited media coverage and unclear details spoke volumes.

What Was Promised vs. What Occurred

The Promise: Charter signing ceremony Thursday in Davos with 59 countries, $1 billion permanent seats, major diplomatic event establishing Trump’s peace legacy.

The Reality: No major media coverage, no photographs, no list of attending countries, no details on what was signed or by whom.

The absence of coverage is telling. Trump’s team publicizes every accomplishment. The ceremony either didn’t happen, happened at drastically reduced scale, or occurred without Trump’s claimed participation.

The Numbers That Don’t Add Up

By week’s end, officials settled on “nearly three dozen countries” expressing interest in at least temporary participation—40% fewer than Trump’s “59 countries” claim, roughly equal to his aide’s “north of 20, maybe 25” estimate.

Countries “temporarily” joining are hedging, watching what this becomes before committing.

Who Actually Showed Up

Rushed to accept: Hungary (Orbán), Belarus (Lukashenko), Uzbekistan—authoritarian states.

Declined or questioned: France, Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Slovenia, Ukraine, Poland—democratic allies.

Pattern: Democracies declined. Autocracies rushed to join.

This isn’t the coalition of “highly respected world leaders” Trump promised. It’s leaders who benefit from normalizing transactional diplomacy over principle-based alliances.

What Actually Happened at the Ceremony

The Board of Peace signing ceremony did occur Thursday—with leaders including Argentina’s Javier Milei, Hungary’s Viktor Orbán, and representatives from Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Qatar physically present.

But the substance revealed why democratic allies stayed away.

Jared Kushner’s Gaza Vision:

Trump’s son-in-law presented slides depicting “gleaming skyscrapers rising on Gaza’s coast and the construction of entirely new cities,” requiring at least $25 billion in investment.

Rafah—the southern city Israel largely razed during the war—would be rebuilt in two to three years, Kushner said. “We said, ‘Let’s plan for catastrophic success.’ There is no ‘Plan B.’”

The Problems:

Hamas hasn’t agreed to disarm or disband. The United States has struggled to convince countries to contribute peacekeeping troops. It’s unclear who provides the billions required.

Most tellingly, Kushner himself acknowledged moving money into Gaza could be “a little risky” while calling it “amazing investment opportunities.”

What Trump Said:

“Once this board is completely formed, we can do pretty much whatever we want to do, and we’ll do it in conjunction with the United Nations. You know, I’ve always said the United Nations has got tremendous potential. Has not used it.”

This encapsulates the problem: “Do pretty much whatever we want to do” is the opposite of multilateral institution-building. It’s unilateral authority disguised as international cooperation.

Why Democratic Allies Declined:

France, UK, Norway, Sweden saw what this was: Not genuine multilateral peace-building requiring consensus and process, but Trump-chaired body where permanent membership costs $1 billion and the chairman “can do pretty much whatever we want.”

That’s not an alternative to the UN. That’s a rejection of why international institutions exist—to constrain unilateral action through collective process.

Ali Shaath’s Announcement:

The head of a Palestinian technocrat committee said the Rafah border crossing with Egypt is “scheduled to reopen next week.”

Israel hasn’t confirmed this. Israeli authorities have vowed to keep the border closed until Hamas returns the body of Ran Gvili, the last Israeli captive whose remains are still in Gaza.

Another announcement. Another contradiction pending.

The Charter’s Missing Heart

The Times of Israel obtained the Board of Peace charter. The most striking detail: no mention of Gaza.

This matters because the Board was conceived to oversee Gaza ceasefire, the UN Security Council authorized it as “transitional administration” for Gaza, and Trump repeatedly cited Gaza as justification.

Instead, the charter describes a vague international peace organization. Translation: Too difficult to get countries to commit to Gaza, so we made it vague enough that nobody commits to anything.

The $1 Billion That Became Optional

Original pitch: $1 billion for permanent Board seat.

Clarification after pushback: “Membership does not carry any mandatory funding obligation beyond whatever a state or partner chooses to contribute voluntarily.”

Canada’s finance minister: Canada won’t pay $1 billion.

The mandatory fee became voluntary the moment countries pushed back. Just like Trump’s “full ownership” of Greenland became a “framework” he won’t detail.

The Executive Board Problem

The four-person executive board includes Marco Rubio (appropriate), Steve Witkoff (Trump loyalist), Jared Kushner (Trump’s son-in-law—massive conflict of interest), and Tony Blair (controversial former PM with Iraq War baggage).

Trump’s son-in-law on an “independent” peace organization raises obvious questions about conflicts of interest and whether this is diplomacy or family business.

What It Reveals

The Board of Peace followed the exact same pattern as the Greenland framework:

Big Announcement:

  • “59 countries” joining

  • $1 billion permanent seats

  • Major ceremony Thursday

  • Rival to the UN

Contradicted Reality:

  • Maybe 25-35 countries, “temporarily”

  • $1 billion became “voluntary”

  • Ceremony unclear if it even happened

  • Charter so vague it commits to nothing

Pattern Recognition:

This is Trump’s approach to foreign policy:

  1. Announce grand initiative

  2. Inflate participation and scope

  3. When reality doesn’t match, quietly scale back

  4. Hope initial announcement is what people remember

  5. Move to next announcement before scrutiny intensifies

The Deeper Meaning

The Board of Peace failure reveals something more important than another Trump exaggeration.

It shows what happens when you try to build international organizations on personal loyalty rather than shared principles:

  • Democratic allies won’t join

  • Autocracies will (because it normalizes their approach)

  • Nobody commits real money

  • Charter stays vague because specifics require actual agreement

  • “Ceremony” becomes unclear because celebration requires substance

Compare this to von der Leyen’s €890 billion European defense commitment:

  • Specific euro amounts

  • Actual budget allocations

  • Clear timelines

  • Binding commitments

  • Democratic accountability

Or Carney’s variable geometry framework:

  • Based on shared principles

  • Builds on existing relationships

  • Requires actual coordination

  • Measured by actions, not announcements

The Board of Peace is announcement without substance. Variable geometry is substance without big announcements.

Davos revealed which approach works.

The Ultimate Irony

Trump created the Board of Peace because he’s “bored of peace”—the actual hard work of diplomacy, the tedious negotiations, the patient relationship-building.

The Board was meant to be the trophy: Trump the Peacemaker, Nobel Prize winner, historic statesman.

Instead it became another case study in why actual peace requires actual work:

  • Careful negotiation (not ultimatums)

  • Building consensus (not demanding compliance)

  • Honoring commitments (not making promises then walking back)

  • Including affected parties (not excluding them from decisions about their future)

The Board of Peace failed for the same reason Trump backed down on Greenland: When you skip the substance and go straight to the trophy, you end up with neither.

The greengrocer applies to international organizations too. You can’t build peace on a foundation of living within the lie.

The reason? Mark Carney’s Davos speech diagnosing the rupture.

Let that sink in: Trump created a “peace” organization, then disinvited a NATO ally because their incoming Prime Minister gave a speech Trump didn’t like.

This perfectly encapsulates the Board of Peace problem: It’s not an international institution based on shared principles. It’s a Trump loyalty club where membership depends on not criticizing Trump.

The “most prestigious Board of Leaders ever assembled” includes Hungary, Belarus, and Uzbekistan—but not Canada, because Carney spoke honestly about American alliance behavior.

Variable geometry vs. personal loyalty. Substance vs. announcement. Partnership vs. subordination.

The contrast couldn’t be clearer.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS

For Journalists:

Stop framing every middle power action as “defying America” or “anti-American.”

Denmark defending sovereignty isn’t anti-American. It’s pro-sovereignty. Canada building alternatives isn’t abandoning alliance. It’s exercising agency.

The framework isn’t “with America or against America.” It’s “partnership with America and coordination beyond America.”

For Policymakers:

The Greenland playbook is now public and proven. Middle powers facing American pressure have framework, proof, and agency.

Demand from voters: Maintain principle while building capacity. Don’t accept false choice between submission and abandonment.

For Citizens:

Your government doesn’t have to choose between subordination and isolation. Variable geometry offers third way: sovereignty through coordination, partnership without subordination.

For America:

The choice is clear:

Option A: Continue coercion. Demand subordination. Threaten allies. Question collective defense.

Result: Middle powers coordinate in opposition. Variable geometry networks grow. American influence declines. Alliances hollow out.

Option B: Return to partnership principles. Respect sovereignty. Treat allies as equals. Lead through example.

Result: America remains central to global order. Alliances strengthen. Influence grows through leadership, not coercion.

Greenland showed which path leads where. Within 24 hours, coercion failed.

Whether America learns this lesson remains to be seen.

THE WORLD THAT’S COMING

The future isn’t American unilateralism or Chinese authoritarianism. It’s not subordination or chaos.

It’s middle powers discovering they don’t have to choose.

They can build a different world:

  • Partnership means partnership, not protection racket

  • Sovereignty isn’t conditional on great power approval

  • Strength comes from coordination, not subordination

  • Alliances based on principle, not transaction

  • Variable geometry enabling flexible cooperation

  • Capacity building creating real alternatives

This world doesn’t exclude America. It includes America—as partner, not hegemon. As leader through example, not coercer through threat.

Davos 2026 was the moment we saw that world coming into focus.

Trump spent 71 minutes trying to prevent it. He failed in 24 hours.

On January 21, 2026, Trump spoke for 71 minutes about why America must own Greenland. He invoked World War II falsely, questioned NATO despite its defense of America, threatened tariffs, called Denmark ungrateful, declared that without America “most countries don’t even work.”

He demanded full ownership. He said anything less wouldn’t suffice.

Twenty-four hours later, he announced a vague “framework” he refused to detail, dropped his tariff threats, and wouldn’t confirm American ownership.

In between:

Mark Carney spoke for 15 minutes about greengrocers and variable geometry.

Emmanuel Macron spoke for 25 minutes about refusing to brutalize ourselves.

Ursula von der Leyen spoke for 20 minutes and committed €890 billion to European strength.

The rupture Carney diagnosed is real. But it’s not between America and allies.

The other says: Partners coordinate. Sovereignty matters. Principle prevails. Threats unify opposition.

For 75 years, the Western alliance functioned because America chose partnership over coercion.

When America chooses differently, the alliance adapts—preserving the principles America is abandoning.
