The Iran Crisis

The Endgame Question

Part XXX: “Most of the People We Had in Mind Are Dead”

March 3, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 3, 2026

This meme captures Day Four of Operation Epic Fury better than any headline. Shot from a driver’s seat, it shows a rearview mirror reflecting a gas station receding into the distance, its price sign reading $1.99. Through the windshield ahead, another station’s sign glows: $3.11.

The AAA reported Tuesday morning that the national average for a gallon of regular gasoline had jumped eleven cents overnight, reaching $3.11 — up from just under $3.00 on Monday. Brent crude closed the day near $80 a barrel after briefly touching $83, up roughly $11 per barrel since Friday. Trump told reporters Tuesday that oil prices would drop “as soon as this ends.” By Tuesday afternoon, he was posting on Truth Social that the United States Navy would begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz “as soon as possible.” He had read the political weather.

The war that began Friday night over the skies of Tehran had arrived, four days later, at the pump. And on Tuesday, the man who launched it sat across from the first foreign leader to visit the White House since the bombs started falling, and answered questions in public for the first time.

What Donald Trump said in that Oval Office press conference with German Chancellor Friedrich Merz will be quoted in the history books — not for its clarity or vision, but for its stunning absence of both.

I. “No Aborts” — How It Was Decided

The most important piece of reporting published in four days of wall-to-wall coverage came Monday from a six-reporter New York Times reconstruction of how Trump decided to go to war. It deserves to be read in full. Its central findings go to the heart of every accountability question this series has been tracking since Part XXI.

Benjamin Netanyahu Feb 11th meeting with Donald Trump, ABC News

Benjamin Netanyahu walked into the Oval Office on the morning of February 11, determined. For weeks, the United States and Israel had been secretly discussing a military offensive against Iran. But Trump administration officials had recently begun negotiating with Tehran over the future of its nuclear program, and the Israeli prime minister wanted to make sure that the diplomatic effort did not derail the plans. Over nearly three hours, the two leaders discussed the prospects of war, possible dates for an attack, and the possibility — however unlikely — that Trump might reach a deal. Days later, Trump dismissed the history of negotiating with Iran as merely years of “talking and talking and talking.”

By mid-February, the Pentagon had assembled a force in the Middle East capable of sustaining a campaign of several weeks: two aircraft carriers, a dozen supporting ships, fighter jets, bombers, refueling tankers, and air defense batteries. This buildup was happening while Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner were conducting nuclear talks with Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi in Geneva. Barbara Leaf, who oversaw Middle East policy for the Biden State Department, told the Times: “That was evidence of war planning. You don’t need two carrier strike groups for diplomacy leverage. I was never in any doubt he would go for a military strike.”

On February 18, in the Situation Room, General Dan Caine — Chairman of the Joint Chiefs — laid out the options. A limited strike to pressure Iran in negotiations. A larger campaign aimed at toppling the government. He told the president clearly: the larger option carried high risk of American casualties, could destabilize the region, and would significantly deplete US munitions stockpiles. The option was much more difficult than the capture of Nicolás Maduro in Venezuela, which Trump viewed as a template for success.

Trump then told the American public that Caine had said a military action against Iran would be “something easily won.”

That is not a mischaracterization or an exaggeration. It is a documented lie, sourced to people with direct knowledge of the Situation Room discussion.

JD Vance — the vice president, the self-styled “America First” skeptic of military intervention, the man whose skepticism of foreign entanglements was central to his political identity — argued in that same meeting that a limited strike was a mistake. If the United States was going to hit Iran, he told the group, it should “go big and go fast.” His conversion is politically significant. The ideological anchor of MAGA restraint endorsed the biggest American military operation since the 2003 invasion of Iraq.

On February 24, Secretary of State Marco Rubio briefed the Gang of Eight — congressional leaders plus intelligence committee chairs — on the coming operation. He discussed the intelligence, the possible timing, and the potential “offramp” if Iran gave up nuclear enrichment. He never mentioned that the administration was considering regime change.

Three days later, on Air Force One en route to Corpus Christi, Texas, Trump gave the order: “Operation Epic Fury is approved. No aborts. Good luck.”

The Geneva talks, it emerged, had ended days before with the Iranians presenting a seven-page plan on nuclear enrichment levels that alarmed Witkoff and Kushner — who told Trump a deal could not be reached. Trump told the Times on Sunday: “Toward the end of the negotiation, I realized that these guys weren’t going to get there. I said, ‘Let’s just do it.’”

What the Mazzetti reconstruction makes clear — and what Tuesday’s press conference confirmed — is that the diplomatic track was theater from the beginning. The military buildup proceeded in parallel with the talks. The CIA was tracking Khamenei’s movements throughout February. The date was moved twice to accommodate military readiness, then moved again when intelligence confirmed Khamenei’s location for Saturday morning. The supreme leader was in his office, waiting for a briefing on a meeting that was already underway, when the missiles struck.

Khamenei had told a close circle that, in the event of war, he preferred to stay in place and become a martyr rather than be judged as a leader who had gone into hiding. History obliged him.

There is one precedent worth noting. In February 1976, President Gerald Ford signed Executive Order 11905 prohibiting any employee of the US government from engaging in, or conspiring to engage in, political assassination. Ronald Reagan later broadened the scope — dropping the qualifier “political” — and that ban technically remains in effect, last amended by George W. Bush in 2008. Trump never revoked it. Tim Naftali, writing in The Atlantic, observed the logical consequence: “As killing foreign leaders gets easier for us, harming our leaders presumably gets easier for others.” The international taboo against foreign political assassination has had a stabilizing effect, despite states that have flouted it. That taboo is now gone.

II. The War Powers Letter — Three Rationales, Zero Coherence

On Monday, Trump submitted his legally mandated War Powers notification to Congress. The document is worth examining in full, because it bears almost no resemblance to what the administration has told the American public.

The letter justified the strikes on the grounds that they were undertaken to protect the US homeland and forces in the region and to “advance vital United States national interests, including ensuring the free flow of maritime commerce through the Strait of Hormuz.” The letter cited “collective self-defense of our regional allies, including Israel.”

The letter made no mention of regime change. No mention of Khamenei’s assassination. No mention of an imminent threat to the American homeland — which Rubio had cited as the justification on Monday, and which officials with access to US intelligence have said Trump exaggerated.

Rubio on Monday: the United States struck Iran “proactively in a defensive way” because Israel was going to strike regardless and Iran would target US forces in retaliation. The United States had to go first.

Trump on Tuesday, when asked if Israel forced his hand: “No. If anything, I might have forced Israel’s hand.”

Rubio on Tuesday, walking back Monday’s statement: “The president determined we were not going to get hit first. It’s that simple.”

Senator Brian Schatz of Hawaii, leaving the classified Senate briefing Tuesday: “They have had three or four or five justifications for this act of war over the last four or five days, and nothing in this classified session alleviated that confusion.”

Senator Chuck Schumer: “very unsatisfying,” with “different answers every day.” Senator Chris Murphy: administration officials described “an open-ended conflict” with Iran and said the military campaign “hasn’t even really started in earnest yet.”

Elbridge Colby, Pentagon policy chief, testifying before the Senate Armed Services Committee — the first public congressional questioning of any administration official since the war began: “We are at the earliest stages of this campaign.”

The letter itself acknowledged: “Although the United States desires a quick and enduring peace, it is not possible at this time to know the full scope and duration of military operations that may be necessary.”

In thirteen words buried in a War Powers notification, the administration conceded what four days of press conferences had obscured: no one knows when this ends, or how.

III. The Hidden Clock — Munitions and the Race Against Time

There is a variable in this war that has received less public attention than the death tolls and the diplomatic drama, but which may ultimately determine the outcome more than any of them: the missile interceptor supply.

Missy Ryan and Nancy Youssef, reporting for The Atlantic, have laid out the mathematics clearly. At the outset of Operation Epic Fury, Iran possessed roughly 2,000 medium-range ballistic missiles capable of hitting Israel, and between 6,000 and 8,000 short-range missiles capable of striking Gulf states. Iran has also launched approximately 2,000 drones in the first four days. The United States produces an average of 270 advanced Patriot interceptor missiles per year. Air defenders typically fire two interceptors at each incoming projectile to ensure destruction. The arithmetic is not comforting.

General Caine raised this concern in the Situation Room on February 18 — before the war began. He told Trump that a sustained campaign would significantly deplete US munitions stockpiles. Trump’s Monday Truth Social post acknowledged the issue while trying to minimize it: US munitions “have, at the medium and upper medium grade, never been higher or better,” he wrote, but then added, “At the highest end, we have a good supply, but are not where we want to be.” He blamed Biden for not replacing weapons sent to Ukraine. The tell is in the admission itself.

Senator Angus King of Maine told The Atlantic that congressional intelligence briefings had flagged the interceptor concern months ago: “We were told some months ago that they were worried about what we could supply to Ukraine because it would deplete our magazine capacity. That implies that there is a limit to what we have.”

The US military buildup for Operation Epic Fury was funded partly by pulling interceptor supplies from Asia and the Western Hemisphere — the two areas the Trump administration has publicly identified as its national-security priorities. Taiwan and the Pacific, in other words, are now somewhat less defended so that Gulf states can intercept Iranian Shaheds.

Iran appears to understand this dynamic. According to Danny Citrinowicz, a former senior Israeli military intelligence official, Tehran has shifted its firing pattern from the massive synchronized barrages of previous conflicts to smaller, more frequent clusters — preserving firepower, maintaining steady rhythm, and bleeding interceptors over time. The strategy is asymmetric endurance: not to win militarily, but to outlast the political will to sustain the war.

Tom Karako of the Center for Strategic and International Studies told The Atlantic that if interceptor strain becomes severe enough, it may force the one outcome Trump has repeatedly ruled out: substantial ground forces inside Iran, hunting underground missile launchers and Soviet-designed Scud batteries that air power cannot reliably eliminate. “We can’t afford to keep doing this,” Karako said. “That’s why there’s such an urgency to finish the job.”

Iran’s Ali Larijani stated the strategy plainly on social media Monday: Iran “has prepared itself for a long war,” including plans for “gradual escalation and expansion of the battlefield.”

The war is, at its core, a race. The US and Israel are racing to destroy Iran’s launch capacity before Tehran exhausts the interceptor stockpiles of American, Israeli, and Gulf forces. Every day the war continues, the clock ticks in Iran’s favor.

IV. The Succession Problem — The IRGC Wins

Mojtaba Khamenei, son of the slain former leader, in Tehran in 2019.Credit...Morteza Nikoubazl/NurPhoto, via Associated Press

Tuesday evening, Farnaz Fassihi reported that the Assembly of Experts — Iran’s 88-member clerical body constitutionally responsible for selecting the supreme leader — had convened virtually, and that Mojtaba Khamenei, the 56-year-old son of the slain Ayatollah, had emerged as the clear frontrunner. An announcement was being considered as early as Wednesday morning, though some clerics expressed reservations: naming a successor might expose him as a target.

Vali Nasr, the Iran scholar at Johns Hopkins, offered a blunt assessment: “If he is elected, it suggests it is a much more hard-line Revolutionary Guard side of the regime that is now in charge.”

Mojtaba Khamenei is not the pragmatic technocrat this series has been tracking in the Larijani-Qalibaf faction. He has close ties to the IRGC. The Guards, according to officials familiar with the deliberations, pushed for his appointment. He has operated in the shadows of his father’s empire for decades — managing security and military apparatus, largely out of public view.

This is the CIA’s identified worst-case scenario, presented to Trump and his advisers in the Situation Room on February 18: a hard-line cleric replacing Khamenei, potentially more bent on acquiring a nuclear weapon than his predecessor.

Trump was asked directly, at the Oval Office press conference Tuesday, to describe his worst-case scenario in Iran. His answer: “I guess the worst case would be we do this and somebody takes over who’s as bad as the previous person. Right, that could happen. We don’t want that to happen.”

He was then asked who he would like to see run Iran. His answer: “Most of the people we had in mind are dead.” He continued: “Now we have another group. They may be dead also, based on reports. So you have a third wave coming. Pretty soon we’re not going to know anybody.”

After the press conference ended, two senior administration officials were asked by reporters to expand on the president’s worst-case scenario — to identify who might lead Iran after the fighting stops. The officials told reporters to refer back to the president’s answer.

Which was, of course, not really an answer at all.

Friedrich Merz, the German chancellor who had just spent hours with Trump discussing the war’s aftermath, concluded his post-meeting remarks to reporters with characteristic directness: “In my knowledge and understanding today, the American government does not have a clearly formulated strategy for the future civilian leadership of this country.”

The first world leader to visit Washington since the war began had seen enough.

V. The Democracy That Isn’t There — What Iranians Actually Face

Arash Azizi, speaking with The Atlantic’s Stephanie Bai on Tuesday, offered the most honest assessment of the democratic opposition’s position available in English-language media: “The idea that they could rise up in the midst of this war and stage a revolution and take power — it’s not based on reality.”

Azizi, himself an anti-regime Iranian, described a movement divided not just tactically but fundamentally — over whether Iran should be a republic or restore a constitutional monarchy, whether it should be a federal or unitary state, over the meaning of the pre-1979 period, over the role of the diaspora versus the domestic opposition. “These differences over the past have tragically stopped Iranians from uniting over their future.”

His family, he noted, was leaving Tehran for the north to escape the bombardments. Not to join an uprising. To survive.

The internet remains partially blocked. Phone lines are more open than during the January massacres — Azizi can reach his family to confirm they are alive — but organized communication, coordination, economic activity: all severely disrupted. His grandmother, he noted with quiet affection, “believes she can take care of herself.”

David Frum, also in The Atlantic Tuesday, circulated an Iranian joke that captures the situation better than any policy brief: An agent asks an Iranian if he’s willing to work to overthrow the Khamenei regime. The Iranian says yes. The agent offers $100,000. The Iranian looks troubled, hesitates, then agrees — but asks if he can pay in installments.

The joke is not cynical. It is accurate. Iranians want liberation. They would help bring it about if they could. But decades of repression, the massacre of at least 7,000 protesters in January alone, the systematic destruction of civil society infrastructure, and now the daily bombardment of their cities have left them with no organized capacity to translate desire into power.

What has the Trump administration done to build that capacity? Azizi was asked directly. His answer: “There’s no evidence whatsoever that the Trump administration has shown interest in a democratic transition in Iran.” The administration defunded human rights organizations. It placed a figure hostile to Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi in charge of Voice of America Persian. It cut Radio Free Europe’s transmission capability. It went to war without building the alternative.

The democratic transition window, if it ever existed, required years of preparation, a unified opposition, and American institutional support. The Trump administration had none of the above, and the IRGC — which has been preparing for exactly this scenario for two decades — moved immediately to consolidate around Mojtaba Khamenei the moment his father was killed.

VI. The Four Names

The Pentagon released them Tuesday afternoon.

Captain Cody A. Khork, 35, of Winter Haven, Florida.

Sergeant First Class Noah L. Tietjens, 42, of Bellevue, Nebraska.

Sergeant First Class Nicole M. Amor, 39, of White Bear Lake, Minnesota.

Sergeant Declan J. Coady, 20, of West Des Moines, Iowa.

All four were assigned to the 103rd Sustainment Command in Des Moines, Iowa. Army Reserve soldiers. They were killed by an Iranian drone that detonated at Port Shuaiba, Kuwait, on Sunday — a commercial port about 68 miles from Iranian territory across the Persian Gulf. Not a fortified military base. A temporary office space.

The Pentagon had dispersed troops and personnel away from military bases in anticipation of Iranian retaliation — a reasonable precaution that created a different vulnerability. Bases are fortified against drone attacks. Makeshift office spaces at commercial ports are not. A Shahed drone, costing tens of thousands of dollars, found the gap. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth acknowledged that the “squirter” that got through had “hit a tactical operations center that was fortified, but these are powerful weapons.” A US military official added that the operations center “was not as fortified as most American bases.”

Declan Coady was 20 years old. He was from West Des Moines, Iowa.

Two other service members killed in the same attack have not yet been publicly identified.

Trump said Sunday, before the names were released: “Sadly, there will likely be more before it ends. That’s the way it is.”

VII. Tehran Under Siege — What the Bombs Are Hitting

Smoke rising on Tuesday after a strike west of Tehran.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

Farnaz Fassihi, who has covered Iran for three decades, has been compiling testimony from Tehran residents that no official briefing will provide.

Yasaman, a 40-year-old photographer: “I haven’t slept for four nights.”

Kamran, texting from inside the city: “Tonight, the attacks are even more massive. It feels like bunker bombs or something — explosion after explosion.”

Mina, a 61-year-old engineer watching television in her upscale apartment in northern Tehran when an office building three doors away was struck — the force blowing out every window and door on her block: “The attacks are very near, the sounds are massive, pray we make it through the night.”

The Gandhi Hospital — a private facility known for its fertility treatments — sustained significant damage when an airstrike targeted a state television tower across the street. The hospital president, Dr. Mohammad Hassan Bani Assad, described evacuating patients: “We have newborn babies. We had eight patients in the ICU, two in critical condition. Women giving birth. Embryos in our fertility department.”

Rubio’s response to such accounts, on Monday: “The hardest hits are yet to come.”

The Golestan Palace — a UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the oldest monuments in Tehran, standing since the 16th century, surviving the Constitutional Revolution, two World Wars, the fall of the Shah, and forty-seven years of the Islamic Republic — was damaged by debris and shockwave from a nearby airstrike. Its famed hall of mirrors sustained significant damage.

In Minab, a town in southern Iran, thousands filled the streets Tuesday for the funeral of victims of Saturday’s airstrike on the Shajarah Tayyebeh girls’ elementary school. Workmen dug rows of graves at a cemetery five miles away. The procession gathered around a truck loaded with coffins. Some mourners wailed; others showered the caskets with sweets and rose petals. At least 175 people died in that strike. Most of them were believed to be children.

Neither the Israeli nor US government has directly addressed the school strike. US Central Command said on Saturday it was “aware of reports concerning civilian harm” and was “looking into them.”

VIII. The Gulf Between Allies

The Trump administration is simultaneously managing a war in Iran, a new Israeli ground operation in Lebanon, a full-scale evacuation of American citizens from 14 countries, a diplomatic confrontation with Spain, and the fraying of alliances that took generations to build.

The Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman — are, as Vivian Nereim reported Tuesday, caught in what one analyst called “a strategic squeeze between the United States and Iran in a conflict they did not choose.” Iran has now fired more than a thousand missiles and drones at Gulf nations in four days. At least seven people have died in those countries from Iranian strikes. The UAE alone reported 170 intercepted ballistic missiles and 57 drones that slipped through and struck Emirati territory. Dubai — a city that had maintained effective immunity from regional conflict for decades — watched five-star hotels catch fire and apartment windows shatter.

UAE Minister Reem Al Hashimy delivered the Gulf states’ collective verdict Tuesday: “Diplomacy remains the only viable path. The military solution will only lead to more crises.”

Former Qatari Prime Minister Sheikh Hamad bin Jassim Al-Thani went further, warning on social media that once this war ends, “new dynamics will emerge in the region” and “Israel is likely to wield considerable influence.” He urged Gulf states toward self-reliance: “This reassessment requires building a credible deterrent capability that relies less on external allies and more on our own collective capacities.”

That is a founding Gulf partner nation questioning, in public, whether the American security guarantee is worth its cost.

The American evacuation of its own citizens was, until Tuesday afternoon, an embarrassment. The State Department told Americans across 14 countries to leave “using available commercial transportation” — while airspace was closed across much of the region. Ambassador Mike Huckabee told Americans in Israel to take a bus to Egypt. The US Embassy in Jerusalem said it was “not in a position to evacuate or directly assist Americans.”

Retired Major General Randy Manner, stranded in the UAE since Friday when his flight to Bangkok was canceled: “It is the epitome of absurdity. ‘Leave the country’ but the airspace is closed. This is nothing less than a total failure of the US government to provide the expected assistance to American civilians who are caught in harm’s way.”

France, by contrast, had 400,000 citizens in the region and began charter evacuation flights Tuesday evening. Germany organized chartered flights prioritizing sick and elderly. Britain deployed HMS Dragon with counter-drone capabilities and sent helicopters to Cyprus. President Macron deployed an aircraft carrier to the Mediterranean and a frigate to Cyprus, while declaring the US-Israeli strikes “outside of international law, which we cannot approve.”

Trump’s response to European hesitation: “We’re going to cut off all trade with Spain. We don’t want anything to do with Spain.” He added: “I’m not happy with the UK either. This is not Winston Churchill that we’re dealing with.”

Spain’s prime minister Pedro Sánchez had denied American aircraft use of Spanish bases because the operation, in his government’s assessment, violated international law. Trump told reporters he could simply fly in and use the bases anyway: “Nobody is going to tell us not to use it.” Spain’s trade policy is handled by the European Union. Spain runs a trade deficit with the United States. The European Commission responded: “A deal is a deal.”

The Trump administration is managing the most consequential American military operation in two decades with the diplomatic bandwidth of a trade dispute.

IX. What Day Four Settled

German chancellor Friedrich Merz talks to Donald Trump during a meeting in the Oval Office on Tuesday. Merz said he told the US president Ukraine has to preserve its territorial integrity. Photograph: Mark Schiefelbein/AP

Friedrich Merz flew home with a candid assessment that the White House could not contradict: the United States has no clearly formulated strategy for what comes after.

Trump’s own words confirmed it. Most of the potential replacement leaders are dead. The backup group may also be dead. The succession frontrunner is a hardline IRGC-backed son of the man the operation was designed to eliminate — which is the CIA’s documented worst-case scenario, presented to the president in the Situation Room before the first bomb fell.

The War Powers letter to Congress bears no resemblance to the public rationale. The legal justification has been rewritten three times in four days. The Senate Armed Services Committee’s three questions — objectives, theory of victory, legal authority — emerged from classified briefings no more answered than when they were asked.

The munitions clock is ticking. Iran is firing in measured clusters precisely to make it tick faster. The interceptor mathematics favor the side willing to absorb losses over the side that must prevent them.

Six Americans are dead. Four of them are from Iowa.

Gas is $3.11. The International Monetary Fund warned Tuesday of potential “very impactful” consequences for the global economy across “a range of metrics.” CMA CGM — the world’s third-largest container shipping company — suspended all Middle East port bookings, extending the disruption from oil tankers to everything that moves in a shipping container through the region.

Colby told the Senate Armed Services Committee: “We are at the earliest stages of this campaign.”

Fifty thousand US troops are now in theater.

The man who launched this war told reporters Tuesday, without evident irony, that his proudest legacy would be that of a peacemaker.
