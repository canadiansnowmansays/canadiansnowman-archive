The Counterfactual President

The Accountability Question

Part 7: Justice Deferred or Delivered

May 1, 2026

January 6, 2021 happened.

We all watched it. The violent assault on the Capitol. The attempt to prevent certification of a democratic election. The Confederate flags in the Rotunda. The gallows erected for the Vice President. The injuries to police officers. The deaths that followed.

Then the investigation happened. The House January 6th Committee documented the plot. The criminal prosecutions followed. Over 1,000 people faced charges. Some were convicted. Some served prison sentences.

But the central question of accountability was definitively answered during Trump’s first year back: The people most responsible for January 6th faced no consequences.

Donald Trump was indicted for his role. So were some of his advisors and allies. The cases were building. Evidence was mounting. And then Trump won the election.

Now, after one year back in power, we can assess what happened: Those prosecutions were dismissed. The January 6th defendants were pardoned. The coup attempt was memory-holed. And the message became crystal clear: If you’re powerful enough, if you’re loyal enough, if you wait long enough — you’re above the law.

But this past year was about more than January 6th. It was about the systematic dismantling of the mechanisms that make accountability possible at all. DOGE didn’t just cut spending — it fired Inspectors General, defunded oversight offices, eliminated the administrative infrastructure that allows wrongdoing to be detected, documented, and reported. You cannot have accountability in a government that has deliberately destroyed its own oversight capacity.

This is what we watched over twelve months: not just impunity for specific crimes, but the demolition of the architecture that makes accountability structurally possible.

The counterfactual isn’t just what President Harris would have done differently during her first year. It’s what accountability looks like when it’s allowed to function — and what its complete absence looks like after twelve months of watching it disappear.

The January 6th Accountability Cascade: What Year One Produced

What Trump did during his first year back:

He issued blanket pardons within weeks of inauguration. Not case-by-case review. Not mercy for nonviolent first-time offenders. Blanket pardons including for those who assaulted police officers, who planned the attack in advance, who came armed and organized.

Throughout that first year, the message was reinforced constantly:

  • Political violence in service of Trump is rewarded, not punished

  • Attacking police officers defending the Capitol is forgivable if done for Trump

  • Conspiracy to overturn elections faces no consequences if you’re loyal

  • Democracy itself is negotiable depending on who wins elections

This wasn’t mercy. It was erasure of accountability. It was signaling that the next coup attempt won’t face consequences either.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, January 6th prosecutions would have continued based on evidence. Career prosecutors would have made individual determinations about charges and sentencing throughout those twelve months. Organized conspirators would have faced full accountability. The investigation into coordination at higher levels would have continued.

But Harris would have faced intense political pressures throughout that first year: Republican accusations of weaponizing justice. Progressive pressure to prosecute Trump and allies aggressively. Media framing any prosecution as partisan revenge. Legal challenges at every stage. The difficulty of prosecuting a former president. The line between accountability and perceived persecution would have been contested constantly.

This would have been rule of law — the principle that violent attempts to overturn democratic elections must face consequences.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, January 6th was forgotten and rewarded. Under Harris, it would have been prosecuted.

And we spent a year watching it forgotten and rewarded.

Trump’s Own Criminal Liability: What Twelve Months Produced

Donald Trump faced multiple criminal investigations and indictments when he returned to power.

What happened during his first year back:

All federal cases were dismissed within months. Trump installed attorneys general who ended investigations. State cases were delayed, appealed, and undermined through federal interference. The 34 felony convictions were appealed throughout the year.

Trump faced no accountability during those twelve months for: attempting to overthrow a democratic election, stealing and sharing classified information, extorting election officials to falsify vote counts, committing business fraud for decades.

The message became clear over that year: Get enough power and you’re untouchable. Crime at the highest levels faces no consequences. The law applies to everyone except those who control law enforcement.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, Trump’s criminal cases would have followed normal legal procedures. A continuing Special Counsel would have made prosecutorial decisions based on evidence and law. State cases would have proceeded without federal interference. The key distinction: Harris wouldn’t have ordered prosecutions — she would simply not have interfered with law enforcement. The cases would have succeeded or failed based on evidence and law.

This is what accountability looks like: letting the legal system function without political interference.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, he was above the law. Under Harris, law would have applied equally.

And we spent a year watching him above the law.

DOGE and the Demolition of Oversight Architecture

The accountability story of Trump’s first year cannot be told without DOGE, because DOGE did something that pardons and prosecutorial interference couldn’t do alone: it structurally dismantled the government’s capacity for self-oversight.

What DOGE did to accountability during year one:

Inspectors General were fired or defunded across multiple agencies. The IGs who investigate waste, fraud, and abuse — whose job is literally to document government misconduct — were removed or rendered toothless. Congressional oversight was hampered by agencies that no longer had the staff to respond to information requests. The administrative infrastructure that allows whistleblowers to report wrongdoing was hollowed out.

This matters because accountability in government doesn’t rely solely on the Justice Department. It relies on a network of oversight mechanisms: IGs, ethics offices, agency counsel, congressional investigators, administrative law judges. When you dismantle that network, you don’t just enable current misconduct — you make future accountability structurally impossible.

What Trump spent a year building: A government in which wrongdoing cannot easily be detected, documented, or reported through institutional channels. A government in which the choice is between going public (and facing retaliation) or staying silent (and watching misconduct continue).

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, the oversight architecture would have been strengthened, not demolished. Inspectors General empowered. Ethics offices staffed. Whistleblower protections enforced. Congressional oversight supported. The network of institutional checks that makes accountability possible would have remained intact.

This isn’t glamorous governance. It doesn’t generate headlines. But it is the foundation on which all other accountability rests.

And we spent a year watching that foundation demolished.

The Pardon Power: What Twelve Months of Obstruction Produced

Presidential pardon power is nearly unlimited. It’s a constitutional check on prosecutorial excess, a tool for mercy.

What Trump did during his first year back:

Blanket pardons for January 6th defendants within weeks. Pardons for anyone prosecuted for Trump-related crimes throughout the year. Pardons as tools of obstruction — rewarding those who refused to cooperate, signaling to others that loyalty is rewarded with immunity.

This transformed pardon power from mercy into obstruction over twelve months. It enabled future criminality by guaranteeing no consequences for Trump allies.

The counterfactual: Harris would have used pardon power traditionally during her first year — case-by-case review, consideration of rehabilitation and remorse, focus on nonviolent offenders serving excessive sentences, clemency for those demonstrating genuine reform. No pardons for political allies.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, pardons obstructed justice. Under Harris, pardons would have served justice.

And we spent a year watching obstruction.

The Enemies List: What Twelve Months of Weaponization Produced

Trump maintained his extensive enemies list throughout year one: journalists, prosecutors, judges, election officials, former staff who testified against him, members of Congress who investigated him, whistleblowers.

What happened during that year:

The Justice Department was directed to investigate Trump’s enemies throughout those twelve months — Jack Smith faced investigations, Liz Cheney was targeted, Adam Schiff faced harassment, journalists were investigated, judges faced threats, election officials were targeted.

These investigations weren’t based on probable cause or evidence. They were based on Trump’s desire for revenge — textbook authoritarian capture: using law enforcement to punish opposition and deter future accountability efforts.

The counterfactual: Under Harris, the enemies list wouldn’t have existed. People who opposed Trump wouldn’t have been investigated simply for opposition. DOJ would have followed evidence, not grudges. Only actual crimes would have been crimes.

And we spent a year watching opposition prosecuted.

The Accommodation Psychology Framework: What Twelve Months Confirmed

My accommodation psychology analysis reveals why accountability matters beyond individual cases:

When crimes at the highest levels face no consequences, everyone recalculates what’s possible. Officials who might have maintained boundaries now accommodate. Those who might have spoken up now stay silent.

What we watched during year one:

The accommodation cascade accelerated across all twelve months. Every unpunished crime made the next one easier. Every pardoned crony emboldened others. Every protected official encouraged more corruption. Every demolished oversight mechanism removed another check on misconduct.

The TACO pattern spread systematically: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. And the end state after one year was severe collapse of institutional integrity — not total, but enough to make the system functionally unreliable.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, the opposite pattern would have emerged: accountability reinforcing boundaries. Officials maintaining standards because violations faced consequences. Whistleblowers speaking up because protection existed. Institutional integrity strengthening because corruption was punished rather than rewarded. Oversight mechanisms functioning because they hadn’t been defunded.

This is how systems heal rather than collapse.

And we spent a year watching collapse.

The International Dimension: What the World Watched for Twelve Months

From my Canadian perspective, I watched American accountability collapse over twelve months.

When the world’s most powerful democracy demonstrated across a full year that crimes at the highest levels face no consequences, that violent coup attempts are pardoned and forgotten, that oversight mechanisms can be deliberately destroyed, that rule of law is negotiable based on who holds power — it sent a message to every authoritarian and every democrat worldwide.

To authoritarians after year one: Democracy is weak. Rule of law is pretense. Power is all that matters. Trump proved you can attempt a coup, face prosecution, wait it out, regain power with immunity, systematically eliminate accountability, and demolish the oversight infrastructure over twelve months. The lesson is complete.

To democrats after year one: America can’t be counted on. Democratic norms are tissue paper. Constitutional systems collapse easily. And when they collapse, they take the oversight mechanisms with them.

The counterfactual would have sent different signals: Democracy is resilient. Rule of law holds even under pressure. Accountability applies regardless of power. Oversight mechanisms are strengthened, not demolished.

Which set of signals did the world learn after the year we just lived through?

The Republican Rule-of-Law Question After Year One

I return to Republican readers who claimed to believe in law and order, in accountability, in constitutional governance.

Trump attempted to overthrow a democratic election. Then he spent his first year back pardoning those who carried out the violence, firing those who prosecuted the cases, punishing those who held him accountable, and dismantling the oversight architecture that makes accountability structurally possible.

If you actually believed in rule of law, this past year should have horrified you. We watched accountability disappear for powerful Republicans systematically across twelve months. We watched the principle “no one is above the law” proven to be pretense. And we watched the mechanisms designed to enforce that principle deliberately destroyed.

The counterfactual asks: Would you rather have lived through a year where Trump faced accountability for attempting a coup, or the year we actually experienced — where he was rewarded with returning power, immunity, and the authority to dismantle the oversight infrastructure he spent years evading?

If you can’t answer honestly after watching this past year, you never believed in rule of law.

And we spent a year confirming you never believed it.

The Exhaustion of Documenting a Year of Unpunished Crimes

The architecture remains. The occupants don't. Grok AI

I need to acknowledge this exhaustion because it’s real and it’s grown throughout the year. I’ve documented thousands of crimes, corrupt acts, constitutional violations.

And none of it mattered this past year. The crimes continued. The perpetrators faced no consequences. The system produced no accountability across twelve months. And then the mechanisms that might have produced future accountability were systematically dismantled.

This is what killed faith in institutions during the year we just experienced — not the crimes themselves, but the systematic absence of consequences month after month, combined with the deliberate destruction of the capacity for consequences in the future.

The counterfactual offers what we missed:

Not perfect justice — no system achieves that. But functional accountability across a year. Investigations that follow evidence. Prosecutions that proceed based on law. Consequences that apply regardless of power. Oversight mechanisms that survive rather than get defunded. The basic principle that crimes have costs.

That’s not utopian. That’s baseline rule of law. It’s what functioning democracies require.

And we spent a year watching it disappear completely.
