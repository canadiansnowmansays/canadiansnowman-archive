Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 6: The Second And Third Anglo-Afghan Wars (1878-1919)

March 25, 2026

A formal seated portrait of five figures (Major Cavagnari second from left, Amir Yakub Khan in the centre, the tall Daoud Shah next to the Amir, and Jenkyns and Habibullah Moustafi at extreme left and right), taken by John Burke at Gandmak in Afghanistan in May 1879

Opening: They Came Back

Thirty-six years after Dr. Brydon rode alone into Jalalabad, after the catastrophic retreat that killed 16,500 people, after the Army of Retribution’s savage reprisals, after Britain finally left Afghanistan in defeat - they came back.

A new generation of British politicians and generals, gripped by the same Great Game paranoia about Russian expansion, decided that the First Anglo-Afghan War had been an aberration. Bad leadership. Bad luck. Poor planning.

Surely, with better commanders, more troops, and lessons learned, Britain could succeed where their predecessors had failed?

They invaded again in 1878. With 40,000 troops this time, determined not to repeat 1842’s mistakes.

They would learn the same lesson anyway.

And then, incredibly, they would come back a third time in 1919.

Three wars across 80 years. Three different outcomes tactically. But the same strategic result every time: Britain unable to hold Afghanistan, eventually withdrawing, with Afghans still there.

That woman in Badgor, that closed clinic in Nalej, that starving three-month-old Zakia - they live in a country that defeated the British Empire three times. Not through superior firepower, but through geography, endurance, and the simple fact that Afghans had nowhere else to go. They lived there. The British didn’t.

The Second Anglo-Afghan War: Invasion (1878)

His Highness the Amir Shere Ali, circa 1878

The Trigger

In 1878, Russia sent a diplomatic mission to Kabul. Afghanistan’s emir, Sher Ali Khan (son of Dost Mohammad), received them cordially.

The British, still paranoid despite 36 years of peace, demanded to send their own mission. When Sher Ali hesitated - reasonably pointing out that hosting rival empires’ diplomats was dangerous - the British issued an ultimatum.

When it expired unanswered, they invaded. November 1878. With 40,000 troops.

The Military Success

This time, the British invasion was professionally executed:

  • Three columns invaded from different directions

  • Superior firepower overwhelmed Afghan resistance

  • Sher Ali fled north and died in exile

  • His son, Yakub Khan, became emir and signed the Treaty of Gandamak (May 1879)

The treaty gave Britain:

  • Control of Afghan foreign policy

  • British diplomatic mission in Kabul

  • Strategic border territories

  • In return, Britain recognized Yakub Khan and promised support

It seemed the British had learned from 1839. They wouldn’t try occupying the whole country, just control foreign policy. A lighter touch. A sustainable approach.

The Envoy: History Repeats (September 1879)

The British envoy to Kabul was Major Sir Louis Cavagnari - swashbuckling, multilingual, understanding Afghanistan’s dangers.

Unlike Alexander Burnes in 1841, Cavagnari took precautions:

  • Lived in the Bala Hissar fortress, not among civilians

  • Maintained strict security

  • Kept low profile (apart from arriving on an elephant)

  • Avoided brothels that had caused such offense in 1839-41

Rory Stewart notes in ‘Afghanistan: The Great Game’ that Cavagnari “swore off the ladies” trying to avoid Burnes’ fate.

It didn’t matter.

On September 3, 1879, Afghan troops in Kabul - angry about unpaid wages, resentful of foreign presence - attacked the British residency.

Cavagnari and his staff fought for hours. They were overwhelmed. Cavagnari was killed. His body dismembered. The residency burned.

Once again, a British diplomatic mission in Kabul had ended in massacre.

General Frederick Roberts: The Competent Commander

General Frederick Roberts - “Bobs” to his soldiers - led forces to Kabul.

When he arrived and saw Cavagnari’s mangled remains, Roberts ordered reprisals: 100 Afghans hanged. He occupied Kabul and prepared to hold it.

But then Afghan resistance exploded.

The 60,000 Watch Fires

One winter evening, Roberts and his officers looked out from their fortified camp. On the ridgeline surrounding Kabul, stretching for miles, they saw watch fires.

Sixty thousand fires.

Afghan tribal forces gathering - Pashtuns, Tajiks, Hazaras, Uzbeks - temporarily united against the foreign invader.

Roberts faced potential siege in Kabul, in winter, with tens of thousands of hostile Afghans surrounding him.

Unlike Elphinstone, Roberts was competent. He didn’t panic. He fortified positions and waited for reinforcements.

The Battle of Maiwand: Disaster Strikes (July 1880)

“Saving the Guns at Maiwand,” 27 July 1880: One of the worst British defeats of the Victorian era, where a brigade of 2,500 was routed by Ayub Khan’s larger force.

While Roberts held Kabul, disaster struck in the south.

At Maiwand village, a British brigade of about 2,500 men (1,000 British, 1,500 Indian sepoys) encountered a much larger Afghan force under Ayub Khan.

The battle was a rout:

  • Afghan cavalry and infantry overran British positions

  • When the British retreated, they were cut down

  • Nearly 1,000 killed out of 2,500

  • One of the worst British defeats of the Victorian era

The Maiwand Lion memorial in Reading, England, still commemorates the fallen.

The defeat shocked Britain as much as the 1842 retreat had.

Roberts’ March to Kandahar

Lord Roberts’ legendary 313-mile march from Kabul to Kandahar in 23 days, August–September 1880 — a feat of endurance that restored British prestige but could not change the strategic reality.

Roberts made a bold decision: March from Kabul to Kandahar (about 300 miles) and defeat Ayub Khan.

In August-September 1880, he led 10,000 men on a forced march, covering 313 miles in 23 days.

On September 1, 1880, Roberts attacked Ayub Khan outside Kandahar and won decisively.

British military prestige was restored.

The Crucial Difference

Unlike 1842, the British didn’t retreat in panic through mountain passes. They withdrew in good order, on their own terms.

But - crucially - they still withdrew.

General Roberts understood what many British politicians didn’t. After his Kandahar victory, he observed:

“We have nothing to fear from Afghanistan, and offensive though it may be to our pride, the less they see of us, the less they will dislike us.”

Then he left Afghanistan.

The Compromise Solution (1880-1919)

The British had learned - partially - that they couldn’t occupy Afghanistan. But they still wanted to prevent Russian influence.

The solution: Control Afghan foreign policy while leaving internal affairs to Afghan rulers.

Abdur Rahman Khan became emir with British support. He agreed:

  • Afghanistan would have no foreign relations except through Britain

  • In exchange, Britain paid subsidies

  • Britain left him alone to rule internally (often brutally)

This compromise lasted until 1919. Britain got what it really needed - a buffer state that wouldn’t ally with Russia. Afghanistan got autonomy in internal affairs.

Neither side was happy. Both could live with it.

The Cost of the Second War

In two years (1878-1880):

  • Thousands of British and Indian soldiers killed

  • Tens of thousands of Afghans killed

  • Enormous financial cost

  • Result: A compromise that could probably have been negotiated without war

But at least this time, Britain hadn’t lost an entire army. Roberts’ competence and the decision to withdraw on British terms made the Second War less catastrophic than the First.

The lesson seemed learned: Control foreign policy, leave internal affairs alone, don’t try to occupy.

It lasted 39 years.

The Third Anglo-Afghan War: Afghanistan Wins (1919)

Emir (later King) Amanullah Khan in 1919, who declared Afghanistan’s full independence from Britain in May, repudiated the old treaties, and launched the Third Anglo-Afghan War with a call for jihad.

After World War I, the British Empire was exhausted:

  • Financially drained

  • Militarily overstretched

  • Politically unstable

  • Public weary of war

Afghanistan’s new emir, Amanullah Khan, saw opportunity.

The Declaration (May 1919)

Amanullah declared Afghanistan’s complete independence and launched an invasion of British India.

His goals:

  • Expand Afghan territory into Pashtun regions of British India

  • Inspire Muslim uprisings against British rule

  • Force Britain to recognize complete sovereignty

He declared jihad - holy war against infidel British.

The Brief War (May-June 1919)

The war lasted one month.

Militarily, Britain still had advantages:

  • Better-equipped army

  • Artillery

  • Aircraft (bombing Kabul and Jalalabad)

The British use of aircraft shocked Afghans - warfare from the sky was new and terrifying.

But Britain had no will to fight. After WWI:

  • British public exhausted

  • Treasury bankrupt

  • Army demobilizing

  • No appetite for another Afghan occupation

A long war in Afghanistan? Politically and practically impossible.

Royal Air Force biplanes (such as the Bristol F2B or DH9A) operating on the North-West Frontier in 1919. The use of aircraft for bombing and strafing was a terrifying new technology for Afghans, yet Britain still lacked the political will for another occupation.

The Treaty of Rawalpindi (August 1919)

The war ended in negotiated settlement:

  • Afghanistan gained complete independence in foreign affairs

  • Britain ended subsidies

  • The Durand Line border (drawn 1893) was recognized

  • Britain withdrew all claims to Afghan policy

For the first time since 1747, Afghanistan was fully independent and internationally recognized.

What Britain Had Achieved

After three wars across 80 years:

  • First war: Complete disaster

  • Second war: Military success, political compromise

  • Third war: Brief stalemate, Afghan independence

Final result: Afghanistan independent, exactly what Dost Mohammad had wanted in 1839.

Britain could have achieved this through diplomacy without a single death.

Instead: 80 years of conflict, thousands dead, enormous expense, same outcome.

The Voices That Warned

British officers who served in Afghanistan tried warning their superiors about the futility of occupation.

Winston Churchill served on the Afghan frontier in 1897 and wrote:

“Every influence, every motive, that provokes the spirit of murder among men, impels these mountaineers to deeds of treachery and violence. The strong aboriginal propensity to kill, inherent in all human beings, has in these valleys been preserved in unexampled strength and vigor.”

This reveals the British mindset - they saw Afghans as primitives driven by base instincts, never considering that resisting foreign occupation might be rational.

Rudyard Kipling, poet laureate of empire, wrote in “The Young British Soldier” (1892):

“When you’re wounded and left on Afghanistan’s plains, And the women come out to cut up what remains, Jest roll to your rifle and blow out your brains An’ go to your Gawd like a soldier.”

Even imperial cheerleaders knew Afghanistan was different. It broke armies. It destroyed reputations.

General Roberts - the only British commander to win in Afghanistan - said it plainly: “The less they see of us, the less they will dislike us.”

But institutional memory is short. Each generation thought they could succeed where predecessors failed.

The Historical Count: The British Century

From 1839 to 1919: 80 years of British involvement.

Actual British control:

  • 1839-1842: ~3 years (and only cities)

  • 1879-1880: ~1 year (contested)

  • Total: ~4 years out of 80

That’s 5% of the time period. And “control” is generous - more like “besieged occupation while countryside remained hostile.”

The cost:

  • Three wars

  • Thousands of British and Indian soldiers killed

  • Tens of thousands of Afghans killed

  • Enormous financial drain

  • National humiliation (especially 1842)

The result:

  • Afghanistan independent

  • Britain with no influence

  • Same situation as 1839

The lesson: Eighty years to learn what 2,300 years of history had already proven.

Why the British Wars Matter for Today

The British experience matters because it’s so well-documented. We have:

  • Official military reports

  • Personal letters and journals

  • Parliamentary inquiries

  • Newspaper accounts

  • Paintings and photographs

We know exactly what went wrong, why it went wrong, and how officers on the ground warned it would go wrong.

And yet it happened anyway. Three times.

This tells us: Historical knowledge doesn’t prevent empires from repeating mistakes.

The Soviets would study British records and invade anyway.

The Americans would study both British and Soviet records and invade anyway.

The Pattern Persists Because Fundamental Factors Never Change:

  • Geography

  • Tribal structure

  • Afghan resistance

  • Impossibility of occupation without legitimacy

  • Unsustainability of foreign military presence

The Quote That Explains Everything

Stephen Tanner observes: “The Afghan people... present the same problems to foreign antagonists today as they did 2,500 years ago.”

The Lessons Britain Finally Learned

After three wars, Britain learned:

1. Occupation Is Impossible Even with overwhelming force, you can’t hold Afghanistan long-term without legitimacy.

2. Control Foreign Policy, Not Internal Affairs The 1880-1919 compromise worked because Britain didn’t try to change Afghan society.

3. Technology Doesn’t Overcome Geography British rifles, artillery, and eventually aircraft didn’t make the mountains navigable or valleys controllable.

4. Afghan Resistance Never Ends Every time Britain thought resistance was crushed, it reignited.

5. The Costs Always Exceed Benefits Even successful campaigns (Second War) cost more than they gained.

6. Eventually, You Have to Leave And when you leave, Afghans are still there.

The Bridge to Independence

Afghan peace delegates at Murree (and later Rawalpindi), 1919. The resulting Treaty of Rawalpindi granted Afghanistan complete independence in foreign affairs — ending 80 years of British attempts to control the country.

After 1919, Afghanistan was finally, genuinely independent for the first time in centuries.

The next 59 years (1919-1978) would be Afghanistan’s longest period of sustained peace and self-determination.

It would include the “golden age” when Kabul had universities, cinemas, women in miniskirts. It would prove Afghanistan could develop when given peace and resources.

But it would also prove how fragile that peace was.

When the communist coup came in 1978, triggering Soviet invasion in 1979, everything would collapse - and the devastation would make the British wars look gentle by comparison.

That three-month-old baby Zakia, her grandmother might remember stories of British times passed down. Her great-great-grandparents lived through it.

And now she faces starvation because another empire - one that should have learned from British history - created dependency then cut aid.

The pattern never changes. The empires never learn. Afghans always pay the price.

In Part 7, we’ll see Afghanistan’s golden age - the brief period when Afghanistan finally had peace to develop on its own terms.
