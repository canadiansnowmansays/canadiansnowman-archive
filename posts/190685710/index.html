The Iran Crisis

The Excursion

Part XLII: How the President Described a War That Has Killed Seven Americans and 1,300 Iranians

March 12, 2026

President Trump speaking in Kentucky, March 11, 2026 / Doug Mills / The New York Times

On Wednesday, March 11, 2026 — the twelfth day of Operation Epic Fury — the President of the United States traveled to Ohio and Kentucky to give economic speeches. Between remarks about pharmaceutical manufacturing and trade policy, he characterized the war he had started thirteen days earlier.

“For them, it is a war,” he said, at a pharmaceutical company in Ohio. “For us, it’s turned out to be easier than we thought.”

Later, in Kentucky: “Over the past 11 days, our military has virtually destroyed Iran. Their air force is gone. Totally gone.”

Later still, returning to Washington: “Most people say it’s already been won.”

On the same day, the Pentagon told Congress in a closed-door briefing that the first six days of the operation had cost the United States more than $11.3 billion. Seven American service members were dead. One hundred and forty were wounded, eight of them severely. Iran’s representative to the United Nations told the Security Council that 1,348 civilians had been killed in Iran. Lebanon’s death toll had passed 630, with more than 800,000 people displaced. Three cargo ships had been struck in or near the Strait of Hormuz. The new supreme leader of Iran, injured on the opening day of strikes, had not appeared in public since his appointment two days earlier. Oil briefly crossed $100 a barrel overnight. The International Energy Agency described the situation as “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.”

The President called it an excursion.

He was not confused. He was not misinformed. He was doing what he has done consistently since February 28: constructing a narrative in which the observable facts of the war — the costs, the casualties, the unresolved strategic situation, the $100 oil — are replaced by a performance of dominance so complete that the gap between the performance and the reality requires active effort to maintain.

This essay is about that gap. And about what fills it.

I. The Finding

On Wednesday morning, as the President prepared his Ohio remarks, the Pentagon released the preliminary findings of its investigation into the strike on the Imam Reza Elementary School in Abyek, Qazvin Province — the strike that killed 175 people, most of them children, on Day Ten.

The finding was unambiguous: the United States was responsible. The strike was based on outdated targeting information.

The children of Abyek were killed by an American weapon guided by American intelligence that had not been updated to reflect the school’s current use. This is the accountability finding the series has been building toward since Part XL documented Mahyar Zanganeh falling by the soccer goalpost, since Shiva Amelirad in Toronto lost contact with her family in Qazvin, since the Tomahawk fragments with their Ball Aerospace SDL antenna and Globe Motors actuators were photographed on the school’s grounds and confirmed by Trevor Ball at Bellingcat.

The investigation is in. The finding is in. The United States killed 175 people, most of them children, with a missile guided by outdated information.

Leaving the White House for Ohio on Wednesday morning, a reporter asked the President about the finding.

“I don’t know about that,” Trump said.

Ten days earlier, he had told the country that Iran might be responsible for the strike — without evidence, without details. The Pentagon investigation has now established what the Tomahawk fragments established on Day Ten: American weapons. American targeting. American accountability.

Senator John Kennedy on Iran school strike: ‘A terrible, terrible mistake’

Senator John Kennedy of Louisiana, the sole Republican to have called the war “a terrible, terrible mistake” and asked the administration to “look the American people in the eye and say we’re sorry,” remains alone in his caucus. No other Republican senator or representative has joined him. The finding exists. The accountability does not.

II. The Plan They Didn’t Have

On February 18, 2026 — ten days before Operation Epic Fury began — Chris Wright, the Secretary of Energy, told an interviewer he was not concerned about oil market disruption from the looming war. He cited the June 2025 twelve-day war as his evidence: “Oil prices blipped up and then went back down.” Other Trump advisers, according to the New York Times, privately dismissed warnings that Iran might wage economic warfare by closing the Strait of Hormuz — the strategic chokepoint through which one-fifth of the world’s oil supply transits.

The dismissal was not uninformed. It was a choice. Trump had been briefed on the oil price risk before February 28. He acknowledged it — and characterized it as “a short-term concern that should not overshadow the mission to decapitate the Iranian regime.” He directed Wright and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent to develop options for a potential price spike. He did not speak publicly about those options — including political risk insurance backed by the US government and potential Navy escorts — until more than 48 hours after the conflict started.

The escorts have not taken place. The insurance has not been implemented.

What has taken place, instead, is a series of improvisations that have made the situation worse rather than better. Wright posted on social media Tuesday that the Navy had “successfully escorted” an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz. Stocks rose. Oil markets steadied. Then administration officials said no escorts had taken place. Wright deleted the post. Markets went back into turmoil. A false signal, issued by a Cabinet secretary, that briefly moved global markets and then had to be retracted.

The President’s response to the Strait crisis has been to tell tanker crews to “show some guts” and sail through it.

After a classified Pentagon briefing on Tuesday, Senator Christopher Murphy of Connecticut said on social media that the administration had “no plan for the Strait of Hormuz” and “did not know how to get it safely back open.” Murphy is a Democrat and can be dismissed as partisan — except that what he described was the content of the Pentagon’s own classified briefing to Congress. The people who gave the briefing did not contest his characterization.

Inside the administration, according to the Times report based on interviews with a dozen officials, some are “growing pessimistic about the lack of a clear strategy to finish the war.” They have been careful, the officials said, not to express that pessimism directly to the president, “who has repeatedly declared that the military operation is a complete success.”

The gap between what officials believe and what they tell the president is the gap this series has been documenting since Day One — the gap between the Wright tweet and its deletion, between the unconditional surrender demand and Leavitt’s acknowledgment that Iran will not be expected to say so themselves, between “the straits are in great shape” and the oil terminals closing in Iraq and Oman on Day Thirteen.

Secretaries Rubio and Hegseth appear to have recognized the gap and attempted to construct an off-ramp. On Monday and Tuesday, they coordinated around three discrete, narrower objectives — destroy Iran’s missiles and launchers, destroy the factories that make them, destroy the navy — that the State Department presented in bullet-point format on an official social media account. The presentation was designed to give the president a framework within which to declare victory on achievable terms.

Trump, at his own news conference, appeared to use the framework — and then, in the same remarks, began “circling back to a rationale for fighting a bit longer,” as Matthew Pottinger, former deputy national security adviser, told the Times. “He doesn’t want to have to fight a ‘sequel’ war,” Pottinger said.

The off-ramp was built. The president appears to be driving past it.

Hegseth’s own admission, at a Pentagon press conference Tuesday, is the most precise summary of the miscalculation: “I can’t say that we anticipated necessarily that’s exactly how they would react, but we knew it was a possibility.”

They knew it was a possibility. They dismissed the possibility. The possibility is now the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.

III. The Bill

The $11.3 billion figure disclosed in Tuesday’s closed-door congressional briefing covers only the first six days. It does not include the pre-war military buildup. It does not include the long-term cost of replacing expended munitions. It does not include the economic cost of what is happening to oil.

On Thursday morning, Brent crude briefly crossed $100 a barrel for the first time since the war began — up from below $73 on February 27. The International Energy Agency’s Thursday monthly report described the situation with a precision the administration has declined to match: “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.” Global supplies, the agency said, are set to decrease by 8 million barrels per day. Oil traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has plummeted to “a trickle.”

The 400 million barrel coordinated reserve release announced Wednesday — the largest in IEA history, covering approximately four days of global demand — did not bring prices down. It went up anyway. The arithmetic is visible: four days of reserve coverage against a sustained 8-million-barrel daily supply disruption is not a solution. It is, as the IEA’s own director said, a bridge — and the bridge is considerably shorter than the gap.

American drivers are paying for that arithmetic directly. Gas prices hit $3.60 a gallon on Thursday, up 21 percent since Day One. Diesel reached $4.86 a gallon, up 29 percent. The twelfth straight day of increases. The President said Wednesday that prices were “coming down very substantially.” They went up.

Senator Schumer said Trump’s reserve release “should have come days earlier” and that Trump had “created many more problems than the strategic petroleum reserve will solve.”

The supplemental funding request — not yet submitted to Congress — is expected to exceed $50 billion. Speaker Johnson says the timetable is “yet to be determined.” A group of six Democratic senators warned Majority Leader Thune Wednesday that if Hegseth and Rubio are not called to testify publicly before the March 27 recess, they will force floor votes on war powers resolutions. Senator Reed, the top Democrat on the Armed Services Committee, wrote separately to Hegseth demanding the cost information the Pentagon has not shared with Congress — which is constitutionally required to authorize and appropriate funds for military operations.

IV. The Russia Room

Vladimir Putin’s senior economic advisor Kirill Dmitriev with Donald Trump’s envoy Steve Witkoff, St. Petersburg, Russia, April 11, 2025

On Wednesday, in Florida, Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner — the President’s two special envoys — met with Kirill Dmitriev, the Kremlin’s special envoy on foreign investment and economic cooperation.

The subject, Dmitriev said in a statement published Thursday, was “the current crisis in global energy markets.” The United States and other countries, he said, were “beginning to better understand the key systemic role of Russian oil and gas in ensuring the stability of the global economy, and the inefficiency and destructive nature of sanctions against Russia.” He noted that “promising projects” had also been discussed.

Witkoff confirmed the meeting on social media. He provided no details.

The Russia sanctions loosening has now moved from a Bessent float to an active negotiation — with the president’s son-in-law in the room, with the Kremlin’s investment envoy at the table, in a Florida meeting held on the same day that France’s president told the G7 that sanctions on Russia must not be lifted under any circumstances.

The loop that the Iran Crisis series has been documenting since Part XXXV is now fully visible: Iran is fighting this war in part with Shahed drones whose production was enabled by Russian technical assistance; Russia is sharing satellite imagery with Iran; and Russia is simultaneously negotiating with Trump’s inner circle for the removal of the sanctions that were imposed after its invasion of Ukraine. The war in Iran is, among other things, creating the leverage Russia has been seeking since February 24, 2022.

Macron said no. Witkoff met with Dmitriev the same day.

The series has used the TACO framework — Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate — to track the administration’s negotiating patterns since Part I. What the Florida meeting represents is not yet capitulation. It is the accommodation phase, conducted at the highest level of the president’s personal circle, in the middle of a war, with oil at $100.

V. The Obvious Takes Its Revenge

Samuel Corum / Sipa / Bloomberg / Getty

Franklin Foer, writing in The Atlantic on Wednesday, offered the most precise strategic diagnosis of the war’s twelfth day and its unresolved endgame.

Every president before Trump had been deterred from military action against Iran by obvious risks — by the geography of the Strait, by the regime’s preparation for exactly this war, by the absence of a plausible successor state, by the strategic costs of regime change without a plan for what follows. Trump believed those obvious risks would not apply to him. The obvious is now taking its revenge.

The geography was always going to be a hard fact. The Strait is thirty-five miles wide at its narrowest, surrounded on three sides by Iranian territory. Admiral Cooper told Congress on Wednesday that the United States has struck 5,500 targets, 60 Iranian navy ships, and 16 mine-laying vessels. The Strait is still closed. The mines may or may not have been fully deployed — American officials were “spooked” by intelligence showing Iranian mine-laying preparations that were “only in their earliest stages,” according to the Times. The history of Persian Gulf mining suggests caution: during the 1980s Tanker War, Iranian mines damaged commercial ships, nearly sank the USS Samuel B. Roberts, and struck an American-escorted convoy on its very first run. “This is the challenge when you lay mines,” said naval historian Stephen Phillips. “They’re indiscriminate.”

Trump said Wednesday: “The straits are in great shape. We’ve knocked out all of their boats.”

Iraq closed its oil terminals Thursday morning after two tankers — one American-owned — were found burning off the Iraqi coast. Iraqi officials believe Iran was responsible. Oman closed its Mina Al Fahal port. Saudi Arabia intercepted two drones heading toward the Shaybah oil field. The straits are not in great shape.

The regime was always going to celebrate martyrdom. The decapitation strategy succeeded only in replacing one Ayatollah Khamenei with another — an injured, hiding Mojtaba, leading a security state from an undisclosed location, whose silence since his appointment is not a sign of collapse but of careful survival. He does not need to appear publicly to lead. He needs only to remain alive. Every day he does so is a day the unconditional surrender has not occurred.

What the strategic analysis of Mojtaba tends to omit is the personal dimension. He did not only lose his father on Day One. The same strikes killed his mother, his wife, and his son. He was wounded himself. The man who needs to be, in the Foer formulation, a willing negotiating partner for the regime’s survival is a man who lost his entire immediate family to American and Israeli bombs in the opening hours of this war. Erika Solomon, the Times’ Iran bureau chief, spoke to intelligence and military experts across the region. Their assessment: “from an American perspective, Mojtaba Khamenei is going to be worse.” The Revolutionary Guards, who had long favored him, read his appointment as a signal of continuity and defiance. So did everyone else.

The strategic error at the center of the war remains what Foer identified: by declaring unconditional surrender and regime change as his objectives, Trump gave Iran the ability to claim survival as victory while making it impossible for himself to declare victory on achievable terms. Rubio and Hegseth appear to understand this. They built the three-goal off-ramp. The president is driving past it because he does not want a sequel war — which means he wants a definitive end that the unconditional surrender framing makes indefinable.

The most plausible path out of the war, Foer argues, requires finding an Iranian official willing to quietly negotiate the regime’s survival — the Delcy Rodríguez scenario. After twelve days of strikes that have killed the supreme leader and most of the senior military leadership, the administration has no plausible candidate for that role. The people who might have played it are dead. The people who replaced them have no incentive to negotiate survival with a government that has publicly committed to their regime’s end.

“Calling for revolution after the revolution has been crushed,” Foer wrote, “is belated timing, to say the least.”

There is a second strategic trap that the Foer analysis does not fully capture, because it concerns not the war’s endgame but its aftermath. Iran’s uranium enrichment facilities were severely damaged in the June 2025 war. But Iran still holds 441 kilograms of near-weapons-grade uranium. Its scientists still have the knowledge. The IAEA is blind. Rubio told Congress that “people are going to have to go and get it.” The administration has debated sending ground troops for exactly this purpose. An exit from the war that does not account for that stockpile leaves the United States and Israel in a position they cannot easily describe as improved. Danny Citrinowicz, a former head of the Iran branch of Israeli military intelligence, put it with precision: a war that was meant to prevent Iran from having a bomb could be the war that actually pushed Iran past the threshold toward building one. Mojtaba Khamenei — with personal losses, with Revolutionary Guard backing, with a damaged but intact scientific infrastructure — has every reason to sprint.

The Gulf states that have been absorbing Iranian attacks throughout this war did not ask for it. Saudi Arabia, the largest, actively opposed it. They are now trapped in it — their oil fields targeted, their airspace penetrated, their economic stability hostage to a Strait they do not control. Vivian Nereim, the Times’ Gulf bureau chief in Riyadh, described their position with clarity: they do not want a collapsed Iran any more than they wanted this war. A failed state across the water — with refugee crises, armed insurgencies, and a nuclear stockpile unaccounted for — is the nightmare they are now trying to avoid having paid for with Saudi Shaybah drones and Bahraini fuel tanks. They want it finished. What “finished” means is the question no one in Washington has answered.

VI. What the Excursion Looks Like From Inside

The word the President used — excursion — deserves to sit with what Day Twelve actually contained, so its weight can be properly assessed.

In Tehran, drone strikes hit Basij militia street checkpoints during Iftar — the Ramadan evening meal, when families visit. Several people were killed. Nader Kalhori, a business owner in the traditional bazaar, announced on Clubhouse that the bazaar would close until Saturday and employees stay home. Iraj, 48, a Tehran resident reached by the Times through Iran’s twelve-day internet blackout, said: “Day by day we have become more discouraged and hopeless. We only go outside when it’s absolutely necessary.” Saeed, 38, said: “Our lives have been paralyzed, and work has completely stopped.”

In Lebanon, Israel sent an additional infantry brigade to the border and launched what the military called “extensive strikes” on Beirut’s southern Dahiya district. An apartment building in the densely populated Aisha Bakkar neighborhood in central Beirut was struck; four people were wounded. Ahmad Ballout, 67, woke up on the floor. “We didn’t expect this at all,” he said. Ali Khodor, 40, told his seven-year-old and three-year-old that the explosions were fireworks. He is now suspicious of every stranger who enters his building. A Red Cross paramedic, Youssef Assaf, was killed in southern Lebanon after rushing to treat the wounded in Majdal Zoun. Lebanon’s death toll passed 630. More than 800,000 people have been displaced.

Iran fired cluster munitions at Israel — a weapon more than one hundred countries have banned under the 2008 Convention on Cluster Munitions. At least two Israeli civilians have been killed in cluster munition attacks. Twelve Israeli civilians have been killed in Iranian attacks total.

Narges Mohammadi, the Nobel Peace Prize laureate, remains at an unknown location in Zanjan Province. Her family does not know if she is alive.

Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington, 26, of Glendale, Kentucky, died Saturday night from injuries sustained at Prince Sultan Air Base on March 1. He is the seventh American killed in the war the President called an excursion.

At the United Nations Security Council on Wednesday, Bahrain’s representative addressed the council while sirens were sounding in his country. Thirteen of fifteen members voted to condemn Iran’s strikes. Russia’s proposed resolution calling for a ceasefire — which did not even name the parties — received four votes. The US opposed it. Nine countries abstained.

US Ambassador Mike Waltz called the 13-0 vote “exactly what the United Nations should be about.” It was a notable endorsement of multilateral institutions from an administration that has withdrawn from most of them. It was also the administration claiming the UN’s authority when the vote went its way, having spent two years dismantling its obligations when it did not.

VII. The Unconditional Surrender That Cannot End

Karolyne Leavitt said the administration was “not claiming the Iranian regime is going to come out and say that themselves.” What she described is a war whose stated termination condition is one the administration does not expect to be observably met. Trump decides when Iran has surrendered. There are no external markers. There is no threshold. There is no event that Iran can produce to demonstrate compliance.

This is not a military strategy. It is a rhetorical position that allows the war to continue indefinitely, its end condition defined by presidential declaration rather than by anything Iran can do. Iran cannot surrender unconditionally to an adversary publicly committed to regime change, because surrendering means the end of the regime. The loop is closed. The demand and the objective are mutually reinforcing. The war sustains itself.

Trump has said the war could go on for more than a month. He has also said it is “very complete, pretty much.” He has said “practically nothing left to target.” He has said Iran’s military is down 90 percent on missiles and 85 percent on drones. He has said the Strait is in great shape. He has said oil prices are coming down. He said the escorts had happened, and then they hadn’t.

The administration has been at war with its own statements since Day One. What has remained consistent — the one thread that has not frayed — is that no Republican senator other than Kennedy has demanded that it account for any of them.

Day Thirteen Begins

Oil crossed $100. Iraq’s terminals are closed. Tankers are burning off Basra. The Kremlin’s envoy left a Florida meeting with Witkoff and Kushner describing “promising projects” and the inefficiency of sanctions. Macron said sanctions must not be lifted.

Seven Americans are dead. The Pentagon has found that American weapons killed 175 children on outdated targeting information. The President does not know about that.

The largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market is in its thirteenth day. The Strait is closed. The off-ramp was built. The President is driving past it.

Investors have been pricing in a TACO moment since Tuesday — TACO being the term, now appearing in mainstream financial and political analysis, for the pattern this series has been tracking since Part I: Trump Always Chickens Out, the president’s habit of walking back his most dramatic positions when the market blowback becomes too intense. He did it with tariffs. He did it with Greenland. Tuesday, after he hinted at an expedited end to the war, stocks rallied and oil fell. The TACO trade seemed vindicated. By Thursday morning oil was back above $100.

The difference between tariff TACO and Iran TACO is the difference between a policy and a war. A tariff can be walked back with a social media post. What cannot be walked back: seven dead Americans, 1,348 Iranian civilians, a Strait that has been closed for thirteen days, a new supreme leader who lost his mother, wife, and son to American bombs, and 441 kilograms of uranium whose location the IAEA cannot verify. The facts on the ground do not delete when the post does.

Senator Kennedy is still alone.
