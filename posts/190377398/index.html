The Iran Crisis

“The Third”

Part XXXIX: Day Nine Evening / Day Ten Morning: March 8–9, 2026

March 9, 2026

Mojtaba Khamenei had been named Iran’s third supreme leader.

I. The Announcement

Early Monday morning, Tehran time, Iranian state television switched from somber war coverage and religious mourning to upbeat revolutionary anthems. The Assembly of Experts — 88 senior Shiite clerics who had been conducting their deliberations virtually because Israel bombed their office in Qom last Tuesday — released a statement through state media. Mojtaba Khamenei, 56, son of the assassinated supreme leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, had been named Iran’s third supreme leader.

Nine days into the war. Under continuous bombardment. Meeting in writing and by video because their traditional gathering place had been struck. The clerics finished what they came to do.

In Tehran, two things happened simultaneously. Government supporters poured into the streets, cheering and waving large flags as state television cut to scenes of celebration in public squares across the country. And from apartments and rooftops across the capital, a different sound: opponents of the government chanting “Death to Mojtaba” into the night air.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps issued an immediate statement praising the selection as “a new dawn and a new phase for the revolution and the Islamic Republic’s rule.” The Iranian armed forces pledged allegiance, vowing to “appear more powerful, resolute and committed than before in safeguarding the achievements of the Islamic Revolution, and will resist and stand firm under the command of their leader until their last breath and drop of blood.”

Ali Larijani, Iran’s top national security official and the country’s effective wartime manager, framed the announcement as a defeat for the enemy: “Despite the enemies’ tactics to kill Imam Khamenei in hopes of bringing the country to a dead end, Mojtaba Khamenei was elected in a legal process.”

The war had a new supreme leader. The war would continue.

II. The Dynastic Break

There is a historical dimension to this selection that the military and economic news risks obscuring.

The Islamic Revolution of 1979 was explicitly and foundationally a rejection of hereditary rule. It toppled a monarchy. It promised to return power to the people rather than pass it through bloodlines. In 47 years, there have been two supreme leaders: Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, the revolution’s founder, and Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, who succeeded him in 1989. There is now a third — and he is the son of the second.

The late Ayatollah had told close advisers that he did not want his son to succeed him. He did not want the role to become hereditary. His own revolution had been launched against hereditary rule. But under the pressure of war — with the assembly bombed out of their offices, their deliberations forced online, the country under continuous bombardment — those who held real power in Iran closed ranks. The clerics, the Guards, Larijani: all converged on continuity. Vali Nasr of Johns Hopkins University: “I don’t think anybody in Iran right now wants to do something that suggests that the system is breaking.”

The system did not break. It calcified. And in doing so, it broke the revolution’s founding promise in the most literal way possible: a son inheriting his father’s absolute power in a republic that was created to end exactly that.

Sanam Vakil of Chatham House had anticipated this logic before the announcement: Mojtaba “is deeply integrated into the regime’s networks and he represents continuity, and he will be supported by Iran’s deep state. He really will bring the support of elites, the security establishment, and the broad system. This is what he ultimately represents.”

What he represents, stripped to its structure, is the IRGC’s candidate. He served in their ranks during the final years of the Iran-Iraq war. He has spent decades coordinating military and intelligence operations from his father’s office. He is not a public figure — he seldom speaks or appears in public, and his personality and politics outside his father’s inner circle are largely unknown even within Iran. He is now the commander-in-chief of Iran’s armed forces.

One more detail, because it is in the record and it matters: his wife, his mother, and a son were killed in US-Israeli strikes since the war began. The clerics who selected him cited this explicitly as a reason for their choice — that honoring the father’s legacy required selecting the son. The new supreme leader of Iran takes command of a military conflict that has already killed three members of his immediate family.

III. What Trump Produced

President Trump declared Mojtaba Khamenei “unacceptable.” He told ABC News on Sunday — hours before the announcement — that whoever was chosen as supreme leader “is not going to last long” without American approval. “We want to make sure we don’t have to go back every 10 years,” he said.

The Assembly of Experts heard this and selected Mojtaba anyway.

Ayatollah Mohsen Heidari Alekasir, one of the assembly members, addressed Trump’s interference directly in the days before the vote: the “Great Satan” had done the assembly “a kind of service” by publicly objecting to certain candidates. “Someone who is opposed by the enemy is more likely to be beneficial for Iran and Islam.”

This is not a rhetorical flourish. It is the operating logic of the selection. Trump’s public declaration that Mojtaba was unacceptable made Mojtaba more appealing to the men deciding. Israel’s threat to assassinate everyone who attended the meeting — issued by military spokesman Avichay Adraee on social media Sunday morning — accelerated rather than delayed the process, because indefinite delay meant indefinite vulnerability and the assembly understood that finishing quickly was safer than waiting.

The National Intelligence Council told the administration before the war began that even a large-scale military assault on Iran was unlikely to topple its theocratic government. Here, nine days in, is the proof of that assessment. Under maximum military pressure, with their offices bombed, their leader assassinated, their oil infrastructure burning, their navy degraded, their air defenses systematically destroyed — the clerics selected the most continuity-oriented, IRGC-aligned candidate available and completed the process in nine days.

Trump’s Tuesday comment now reads as its own epitaph for the succession strategy: “Pretty soon we’re not going to know anybody.” Asked about a worst-case scenario, he said: “I guess the worst case would be we do this and somebody takes over who’s as bad as the previous person. Right, that could happen. We don’t want that to happen.”

It happened.

IV. The MbS Argument — and Why It May Not Matter

There is one contrarian read in the record, and it deserves its place precisely because it is the only available off-ramp argument.

Abdolreza Davari, a politician described as close to Mojtaba, argued in a phone interview from Tehran that the new supreme leader might emerge as a figure in the style of Saudi Arabia’s Mohammed bin Salman — bringing structural change and some degree of liberalization. “If there is anyone who could move toward some sort of de-escalation with the United States, it is him — any other person would face backlash from the ruling class and conservatives. He intends to bring structural change.”

The argument rests on a generational logic: Mojtaba is of a younger, more pragmatic cohort of clerics; because of his lineage, he faces less resistance from hard-liners; precisely because the conservative establishment supports him, he has room to maneuver that a reformist would never have.

Against this: the IRGC praised his selection as a “new dawn.” The armed forces pledged their last drop of blood. Larijani celebrated it as proof of the enemy’s failure. These are not the statements of a system preparing to negotiate. An Iranian engineer named Alireza — opposition-aligned, hoping the war would bring the regime’s end — sent a text message from Tehran after the announcement: “This is a sign that things will get much worse.”

The MbS path remains theoretically available. It is the only path that ends this war without either Iranian collapse — which the NIC said was unlikely — or permanent American military entanglement. But the conditions for it do not currently exist, and nothing in Sunday night’s announcement brought them closer.

V. One Hundred Dollars

As markets opened Sunday evening, Brent crude briefly exceeded $110 a barrel before settling around $104. It was the first time oil had crossed $100 in almost four years. Oil is now 50 percent more expensive than it was on February 27, the last day before the war.

Stock futures fell 1.5 percent across the S&P 500, Nasdaq, and Dow Jones simultaneously with the succession announcement — the markets read Mojtaba’s selection as continuity of conflict, not a path to resolution. Investors now expect inflation of 4.5 percent over the next twelve months, up from a forecast of 2.3 percent at the start of the year. The two-year Treasury yield has risen 0.2 percentage points since the war began. Diesel is up 22 percent. US gas is at $3.45 nationally, up 16 percent in nine days.

The Federal Reserve is caught. Inflation is running hot and accelerating — driven by energy costs the Fed cannot control — while the February jobs report showed the economy shedding 92,000 jobs. Raising rates fights inflation but deepens the employment crisis. Cutting rates addresses unemployment but accelerates inflation. The war has locked the Fed out of its own toolkit.

Trump posted on Truth Social Sunday that higher oil prices were “a very small price to pay for U.S.A., and World, Safety and Peace.” He campaigned on lowering energy costs. The national average was $3.03 per gallon on Election Day. It is now $3.45 and rising, with oil above $100 and the Strait of Hormuz closed for the ninth consecutive day.

Energy Secretary Chris Wright told CNN on Sunday that the Strait could see more regular traffic within “a few weeks at most.” He cited as evidence a tanker that had transited the strait in the previous 24 hours. A shipping analyst identified the vessel as the Parimal — a small Iranian-connected tanker whose transponder happened to be on. “I don’t believe this vessel can be used as an indication that traffic is returning to normal.” The administration is managing the energy narrative with a single Iranian-connected tanker as evidence of progress.

VI. Riyadh

The State Department issued mandatory departure orders for American diplomatic personnel in Saudi Arabia on Sunday — the first such order for the Kingdom since the war began. The US Embassy in Riyadh has been attacked multiple times; two drones hit the building on Tuesday causing “limited fire and minor material damage.” A drone was shot down over the diplomatic quarter early Sunday. American employees at the embassy and consulates in Jeddah and Dhahran are being told to leave.

The US mission in Saudi Arabia has no Senate-confirmed ambassador. Career diplomat Michael Ratney was forced out at the start of the Trump administration. The mission is led by a career acting chief of mission. It is now being evacuated.

Top diplomats in Saudi Arabia had requested mandatory departure authorization weeks ago given the frequency of attacks. The request was only approved Sunday. The gap between the request and the approval is its own accountability record: American diplomatic personnel were living under drone attack in a mission without an ambassador while Washington delayed the authorization to leave.

Meanwhile in Karachi, Pakistan, Reuters has confirmed that US Marines fired on protesters during the storming of the US Consulate on March 1. At least ten people were killed. This is the first confirmation of American military personnel firing on civilian protesters since the war began.

VII. The Seventh, and What the Pentagon Said

The Pentagon confirmed Sunday that a seventh American has died in the war. The service member was wounded on March 1 when Iran struck a Saudi military base where US troops were stationed — the same day as the Karachi consulate attack, the same day Kataib Hezbollah claims it began its campaign of 28 strikes against American positions. He died Saturday night while being prepared for transfer to a military hospital in Germany. His name has not been released.

In disclosing the death, the Pentagon made a statement that deserves to stand on its own: Iran was “more prepared for war than the Trump administration anticipated,” and its strikes have been “widespread and less predictable” compared to past practice, when Iran gave advance warning of which bases it intended to hit.

The administration launched a war against a country whose retaliatory behavior it misread. The National Intelligence Council said the war was unlikely to produce regime change. The CIA said killing Khamenei would not collapse the government. The Pentagon now acknowledges Iran was more prepared than anticipated. Seven Americans are dead. Iran has a new supreme leader who is the IRGC’s preferred candidate. Oil is at $104. The Strait is closed. CENTCOM is planning through September.

The total war death toll as of Sunday: at least 1,759 people killed, the vast majority in Iran. Iran’s ambassador to the UN puts the Iranian civilian death toll above 1,300. Lebanon: 394 dead, including 83 children and 9 medical professionals. Gulf states: at least 14 dead, the majority foreign nationals — migrant workers from South Asia who came to the region for economic opportunity. An 11-year-old girl in Kuwait. One Indian national and one Bangladeshi national killed when a military projectile hit their residence near the Saudi air base at Kharj, where they worked for a cleaning and maintenance company.

VIII. What Comes Next

Israel has threatened to kill Mojtaba Khamenei. Trump has said he will not last without American approval. The IRGC has pledged to follow him until their last drop of blood. The armed forces have pledged the same. Oil is at $104.

The succession question — the central uncertainty of the war’s first nine days — is resolved. It resolved in the direction the NIC predicted: continuity, not collapse. The regime did not fracture under bombardment. It selected its most continuity-oriented leader, accelerated by the very threats meant to prevent the selection, and announced the result to revolutionary anthems while opponents chanted from their rooftops.

The question was never whether Iran would fight. The question was what came after the fighting. Nine days in, the answer is beginning to take shape: a new supreme leader with IRGC roots, oil above $100, the Strait closed, seven Americans dead, 1,759 total dead, and a war that the Pentagon is planning to last through September.

Trump told his base on Sunday that what he is doing is “a very MAGA thing. A very, very MAGA thing.” He said he is “at the highest point I’ve ever been with MAGA.”

He is also at the highest oil price in four years. The seventh American died Saturday night. The new supreme leader of Iran, whose family was killed in Trump’s war, has just taken command of the forces that will continue fighting it.

Day Ten begins.

IX. Day Ten: First Light

Three things happened as Day Ten began that cannot wait for the next dispatch.

The school. Iran’s semiofficial Mehr News Agency released a video Sunday night that Bellingcat and the New York Times independently verified. It shows a Tomahawk cruise missile striking the naval base beside the Shajarah Tayyebeh elementary school in Minab on February 28 — Day One. The Tomahawk is a US-only weapon. Neither Israel nor Iran deploys it. The NYT assembled satellite imagery, social media posts, and a timeline of strikes confirming the school was hit at the same time as the base. Gen. Dan Caine acknowledged at his Wednesday briefing that Minab was in the strike zone and that “the first shooters at sea were Tomahawks unleashed by the United States Navy.” Two weapons experts independently identified the missile in the video.

On Saturday, at Dover Air Force Base, standing at the ceremony for his six dead soldiers, Trump said: “In my opinion and based on what I’ve seen, that was done by Iran. They’re very inaccurate, as you know, with their munitions.” Pete Hegseth, standing beside him, said: “The only side that targets civilians is Iran.”

The video was released the next day. One hundred and seventy-five people reported killed at that school. Many of them children.

Oil to $120. As Asian markets opened Monday, Brent crude briefly surged to almost $120 per barrel — the highest since the Covid pandemic — before retreating to approximately $103 after the Financial Times reported G7 finance ministers would meet Monday to coordinate a strategic petroleum reserve release. The retreat from $120 to $103 is not relief; it is the distance between a panic ceiling and a sustained crisis floor. Gasoline in the United States is up 47 cents in the last week alone, per AAA. RBC Capital Markets on Sunday: “With no clear definition of what winning looks like, it is hard to forecast whether this will be a multi-week or multi-month conflict.”

Putin. Vladimir Putin issued a formal statement congratulating Mojtaba Khamenei on his selection as supreme leader, pledging “unwavering support for Tehran and solidarity with our Iranian friends.” Russia and Iran deepened their partnership after the 2022 Ukraine invasion; Iran supplied the Shahed drones Russia now uses against Ukrainian civilians — the same drone design currently flying against American positions across the Gulf. Putin is congratulating the IRGC’s preferred supreme leader while Russian satellites track US warship positions for Iranian targeting. Trump has not addressed this.

Day Ten is underway.

End of Day Nine / Dawn of Day Ten. Mojtaba Khamenei is Iran’s third supreme leader. Brent crude: $103, briefly $120 overnight, up 50%+ since Day One. US gas: $3.45 per gallon, up 47 cents in one week. Investor inflation expectations: 4.5% over the next year. Stock futures down 1.5%. Americans killed: 7. Total war dead: 1,759+. Lebanon: 394 dead including 83 children. The Strait of Hormuz: closed, Day Nine. The supplemental funding request: not yet submitted. Congressional authorization: twice blocked. CENTCOM planning horizon: September. A Tomahawk struck beside an elementary school on Day One. The war continues.
