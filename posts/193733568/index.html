The Steel Deal

Part 12: How a Luxembourg Billionaire Donated $37 Million in Foreign Steel to the American President's Vanity Project — and What He Got Two Days Later

April 10, 2026

The construction site for the new White House ballroom last month.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Part 11 of this series documented how the president’s sons invested in drone companies, helped screen the Pentagon officials who award drone contracts, and grew their net worth by hundreds of millions as the Iran war consumed the weapons their portfolio produces. That is the grift operating through the machinery of war.

Part 12 is the grift operating through the machinery of tariffs — the very tariffs the president has staked his economic legacy on.

A Luxembourg-based steel company donated tens of millions of dollars worth of foreign steel for the construction of Donald Trump’s $400 million White House ballroom. Two days after the president publicly described the gift in glowing terms at a donor event, his administration quietly issued a proclamation authorizing the commerce secretary to cut in half the tariffs applied to that company’s Canadian steel exports.

The company is ArcelorMittal. The proclamation was signed on October 23, 2025. No investigation has been opened. No conflict of interest has been acknowledged. The White House called the connection “tenuous.”

It is not tenuous.

The Setup: A Temple to Himself, at No Cost to Taxpayers

The ballroom project was announced as an act of presidential generosity. Trump would build a magnificent new ballroom on the White House grounds — the most recognizable building in the United States — and he would do it without costing the American taxpayer a single dollar. Wealthy donors would fund the construction. Corporations would donate the materials. The president would get the glory, and the country would get a ballroom.

The cost estimate began at $200 million. By the time the president was speaking at his October donor event, it had risen to $400 million — a 100 percent increase that Trump presented not as a warning sign but as evidence of ambition.

“I already raised the money,” he told the assembled donors. “Businesses pledged to donate all of the steel and air conditioning for the structure.”

He identified Carrier, an American company, as the air conditioning donor. He did not identify the steel donor by name. The White House has made some donor names public and allowed others to remain secret.

The steel donor, it turns out, had reasons to remain secret.

The Donor: A Globe-Spanning Empire with a Canadian Problem

ArcelorMittal is the world’s second-largest steel maker. It was formed in 2007 when Lakshmi N. Mittal — an Indian-born billionaire and business magnate who chairs the company — engineered the acquisition of the European steel giant Arcelor. The combined company produces steel in France, Germany, Brazil, Canada, India, and elsewhere. The bulk of its production is concentrated in Europe.

Mittal is not a stranger to American political circles. He praised Trump effusively at a business roundtable in New Delhi in 2020. He has long been aligned with the trade protectionism that Trump champions — urging the European Union to step up its own steel protections in response to American tariffs, and commending Trump’s efforts to restrict Chinese steel exports. Trump’s tariffs, while making it more expensive for ArcelorMittal to export steel into the American market, have also raised global steel prices — a dynamic that benefits Mittal’s broader business even as it constrains his American sales.

ArcelorMittal also has a specific and significant American exposure: it makes automotive steel at a plant in Canada that is exported to American car and truck manufacturers. That Canadian production is subject to Trump’s steel tariffs. Getting relief from those tariffs — even a partial reduction — would be worth considerable money to a company of ArcelorMittal’s scale.

The company also completed the acquisition of a facility in Alabama last year, and is planning another nearby. The American footprint gives it a political argument: ArcelorMittal is not merely a foreign company extracting from the American market. It is, the White House was eager to emphasize, creating American jobs.

It was also, it turns out, donating American — or rather European — steel to the American president’s ballroom.

The Timeline: Forty-Eight Hours

The sequence of events is not complicated. It simply requires that you pay attention to the dates.

On October 21, 2025, President Trump spoke at a White House donor event for the ballroom project. He described receiving a visit from the representative of “a great steel company” who had offered to donate the metal for the structure.

“He said, ‘Sir, I’d like to donate the steel for your ballroom,’” Trump told the crowd. “I said: ‘Whoa, that’s nice.’ And I found out — ‘How much is the steel?’ I called the contractor. ‘Sir, it’s down for $37 million.’ I said, ‘This is a nice donation, right?’”

Trump was characteristically specific about quality. The donor “wants to make sure it’s high quality,” he explained — “great steel as opposed to garbage steel, because they dump a lot of garbage around. You know, steel is like everything else, including human beings. Steel could be high quality, and it can be low quality.”

The steel, according to two people familiar with the arrangements who spoke to the New York Times on condition of anonymity, was produced in Europe.

Two days later, on October 23, 2025, the White House issued a proclamation making various adjustments to tariffs on trucks, buses, and auto parts. Buried within the document was a provision authorizing Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick to reduce by up to half the tariffs applied to aluminum or steel producers that operate facilities in Canada or Mexico and supply American car and truck manufacturers — provided the quantities corresponded to “newly committed United States production capacity, as determined by the secretary.”

ArcelorMittal makes automotive steel in Canada that is exported to American manufacturers. It has also committed to expanding its Alabama facility, which would help it qualify for the provision.

The White House official who addressed the connection between the donation and the proclamation described it as “tenuous” and noted that no company had yet received the exemption, and that other steel companies could benefit from the provision as well.

Both things can be true — no exemption has yet been received, and other companies could theoretically qualify — while the underlying architecture of the arrangement remains exactly what it appears to be.

The Template: Three Generations, One Move

Fred Trump extracted wealth from housing by manipulating federal programs designed to help others. He overbilled, underdeclared, and moved money through structures designed to obscure what was happening until long after it had happened.

Donald Trump extracted wealth from the presidency by creating leverage — tariffs, regulations, market-moving announcements — and then accepting payment from the people most affected by that leverage. The $37 million in steel is simply a more elegant version of the Mar-a-Lago membership fee that doubled on inauguration day 2017.

The elegance lies in its reversibility. The White House official is technically correct: no exemption has yet been received. If one never arrives, ArcelorMittal donated $37 million in European steel to an American president’s ballroom and received nothing in return — a generous if eccentric act of corporate philanthropy. If the exemption does arrive, it will arrive through a process administered by Howard Lutnick, the commerce secretary, under authority granted by a proclamation signed two days after the president publicly celebrated the gift. The causal chain will be administratively invisible and politically deniable.

This is the refinement the second term brought. The first term grift was often crude — the hotel down the street from the White House that foreign governments booked as tribute, the golf courses billing the Secret Service to guard the man they were built to protect. The second term grift has learned to write itself into proclamations, bury itself in tariff schedules, and describe itself as coincidence.

The Irony the Steel Workers Are Living

The president has imposed a 25 percent tariff on foreign steel since 2018. Last June, he doubled it to 50 percent. The explicit purpose was to protect American steel workers and American steel companies from foreign competition — to make it more expensive to import foreign steel and thereby force buyers to source domestically.

Brandon Farris, the executive vice president of the Steel Manufacturers’ Association, issued a statement in response to the ArcelorMittal story noting that the domestic steel industry was “experiencing a resurgence” thanks to the tariffs, and that American producers “stand ready to supply the high-quality, American-made steel needed to bring the president’s infrastructure and manufacturing priorities to life.”

The president chose European steel instead — for a building that is not merely infrastructure, but the symbolic residence of the American presidency itself.

The man who gave speeches about garbage steel versus great steel, who doubled tariffs to protect American producers, who has made steel tariffs a cornerstone of his economic nationalism, is building his vanity project with Luxembourg steel. The workers whose jobs those tariffs were designed to protect are being told that the protection is working, that the industry is in resurgence, that the president is on their side.

They are also being told, implicitly, that their steel was not good enough for the most famous building in the country.

What the Marks Don’t Know

The White House spokesman, Davis Ingle, responded to the ArcelorMittal story with the following statement: Trump is “making the White House beautiful and giving it the glory it deserves at no cost to the taxpayer — something everyone should celebrate.” He added that only people with “a severe case of Trump Derangement Syndrome would find a problem with that.”

There is a problem with that.

The president told his donors that taxpayers would not pay for the ballroom. He did not tell them that a foreign steel company would effectively subsidize the project in exchange for a tariff exemption written two days later. He did not tell American steel workers that the iconic American building would be framed with European steel. He did not disclose which donor was providing the steel, or what that donor might receive in return.

The marks — the workers who believed the tariffs were for them, the donors who believed the project was pure philanthropy, the citizens who believed the White House was being restored as an act of patriotism rather than self-dealing — are still being told there is nothing to see.

There is plenty to see. You just have to read the proclamation dates.
