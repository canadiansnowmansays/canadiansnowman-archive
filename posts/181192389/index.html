America's 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 10: “The American Health Security Act of 2026 — A Bill to Introduce or Refuse”

December 18, 2025

BREAKING NEWS: December 11-17, 2025

As I finalize this series, Congress is failing in real time.

December 11: The Senate voted on healthcare. Two bills. Both failed.

The Republican bill: Expand Health Savings Accounts, let enhanced ACA subsidies expire. Rejected.

The Democratic bill: Extend enhanced ACA subsidies for three years. Rejected 51-48.

But Senator Hawley, you voted yes. You, Susan Collins, Lisa Murkowski, and Dan Sullivan voted with Democrats to extend the subsidies while 47 of your Republican colleagues voted no.

December 16: Speaker Mike Johnson blocked House Republicans from voting on subsidy extensions. The House Rules Committee—which Johnson controls—rejected amendments from GOP centrists who had spent days crafting compromise proposals with income limits and reforms.

December 17 (yesterday): In what the New York Times called a “striking rebellion,” four House Republicans revolted against their own Speaker.

Reps. Brian Fitzpatrick (R-PA), Mike Lawler (R-NY), Ryan Mackenzie (R-PA), and Rob Bresnahan Jr. (R-PA) signed a Democratic discharge petition to force a floor vote on ACA subsidies—against Johnson’s wishes. They signed just minutes before the House voted on Johnson’s healthcare bill.

Hours later, Johnson’s bill passed 216-211, with one Republican defection. It now heads to the Senate where it faces certain death.

Rep. Fitzpatrick’s statement: “We have worked for months to craft a two-party solution to address these expiring health care credits. Our only request was a floor vote on this compromise. That request was rejected.”

The discharge petition now has 218 signatures—enough to force a floor vote in January. But by then, the subsidies will have already expired.

One week. Three dramatic failures. Open rebellion in the House.

Inside the Capitol: chaos. Rep. Chip Roy (R-TX): “Milquetoast garbage, not even a concept of a plan.” Speaker Johnson blaming Democrats for “breaking the system 15 years ago.” President Trump demanding direct payments to people.

In 13 days (January 1, 2026), 22 million Americans will see their premiums double. Two million will lose coverage entirely.

Senator Hawley, here’s my question:

You voted to extend subsidies. Four of your House colleagues just defied their own Speaker to force a vote. That takes political courage.

But extending subsidies for three years just delays the crisis.

Will you introduce comprehensive reform that actually solves it?

The bill below doesn’t extend subsidies for three years. It solves the problem permanently. Universal coverage. Deficit reduction. Proven international models.

You voted the right way on December 11. Now finish the job.

Senator Hawley, we need to talk.

Earlier this year, you proposed the “No Taxes on Healthcare Act”—allowing Americans to deduct up to $25,000 in healthcare expenses. You were right about the problem: American families are crushed by medical costs.

But your solution treats a symptom, not the disease.

Tax relief doesn’t address the fundamental problem: American healthcare costs $14,885 per capita—double what every other developed country pays. Tax deductions just shift who pays. A family earning $50,000 annually with $15,000 in medical expenses still can’t afford care, even with a $25,000 deduction they can’t fully utilize.

But you saw the problem. You proposed something. You tried.

That’s more than most of your colleagues have done in 80 years.

THE AMERICAN HEALTH SECURITY ACT OF 2026

Summary for Legislative Counsel

Short Title: American Health Security Act of 2026

Sponsors:

  • Senator Josh Hawley (R-MO)

  • Senator Brian Schatz (D-HI)

Co-Sponsors:

  • Senator Mike Braun (R-IN)

  • Senator Bill Cassidy (R-LA)

  • Senator Lisa Murkowski (R-AK)

  • Senator Susan Collins (R-ME)

  • Senator Shelley Moore Capito (R-WV)

Committees of Jurisdiction:

  • Senate: Finance Committee (primary), HELP Committee (secondary)

  • House: Ways and Means (primary), Energy and Commerce (secondary)

Legislative Vehicle: Budget reconciliation (Phase 1), Regular order with 60 votes (Phase 2)

Implementation Timeline: 10 years (2027-2036)

Fiscal Impact:

  • Gross cost: $200-300 billion annually (subsidies, public option, administration)

  • Savings: $500-700 billion annually (price controls, administrative simplification, drug negotiation)

  • Net impact: $200-400 billion in annual deficit reduction

Coverage Impact: Universal coverage for all 330 million Americans

TITLE I: IMMEDIATE REFORMS (Year 1)

Section 101: Enhanced ACA Enrollment

(a) Automatic Enrollment: The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall automatically enroll individuals receiving federal benefits (unemployment, SNAP, SSI, SSDI) in subsidized health insurance plans unless the individual opts out.

(b) Year-Round Enrollment: Open enrollment for ACA marketplace plans shall be year-round, eliminating the current 6-week window.

(c) Pre-Populated Applications: The Secretary shall pre-populate marketplace applications with IRS and Social Security Administration data to reduce administrative burden.

Estimated Impact: 3-5 million newly insured, $8-12 billion annual cost

Section 102: Medicaid Expansion Incentives

(a) Enhanced Federal Match: For states expanding Medicaid after January 1, 2027, the federal government shall pay 95% of costs for the first three years (instead of 90%).

(b) Rural Hospital Stabilization Fund: The Secretary shall establish a $500 million annual fund to prevent rural hospital closures in states that expand Medicaid within 24 months of this Act’s passage.

(c) Section 1115 Waiver Fast-Track: The Secretary shall approve Section 1115 waivers for expansion within 30 days of state application.

Estimated Impact: 3-5 states expand, 2-4 million newly insured, 50-80 rural hospitals saved

Section 103: Expanded Drug Price Negotiation

(a) Accelerated Timeline: The Secretary shall negotiate prices for 50 drugs by 2028 (instead of 20 by 2029 under current law).

(b) International Reference Pricing: Negotiated prices shall not exceed the average of prices in Canada, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom.

(c) Insulin Price Cap: All insulin formulations shall be capped at $35 per month for all Americans (extending current Medicare cap to all coverage).

Estimated Impact: $15-20 billion annual savings, 50-70% reduction in patient out-of-pocket costs

Section 104: Surprise Billing Elimination

(a) Ground Ambulance Coverage: The No Surprises Act is amended to include ground ambulance services (currently exempt), with existing contracts grandfathered for 24 months to allow industry transition.

(b) Increased Penalties: Penalties for surprise billing violations shall increase from $300 per day to $10,000 per day.

(c) Federal Arbitration: The Secretary shall establish fast-track federal arbitration for billing disputes, with 30-day resolution requirement.

Estimated Impact: $40-50 billion in eliminated surprise bills, 15-20% reduction in medical bankruptcy

Section 105: Hospital Price Transparency

(a) Mandatory Public Posting: All hospitals shall post machine-readable price files for all services with no exemptions.

(b) Pre-Procedure Price Disclosure: Hospitals must provide patients with written price estimates for non-emergency procedures before treatment.

(c) Public Comparison Website: The Secretary shall establish a public website comparing hospital prices for common procedures.

(d) Increased Penalties: Penalties for non-compliance shall increase from $300 per day to $10,000 per day.

Estimated Impact: 15-25% price reduction over 3-5 years through competitive pressure

TITLE II: PUBLIC OPTION AND MEDICARE EXPANSION (Years 2-3)

Section 201: Medicare Part E (Public Option)

(a) Establishment: There is hereby established “Medicare Part E” (Everyone), a public health insurance option administered by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services.

(b) Eligibility: Any U.S. resident not eligible for traditional Medicare may enroll in Medicare Part E.

(c) Phase-In:

  • Year 1: Available to individuals aged 50-64

  • Year 2: Available to individuals aged 40-49

  • Year 3: Available to all U.S. residents under age 65

(d) Benefits: Medicare Part E shall provide benefits identical to traditional Medicare Parts A, B, and D.

(e) Premiums: Premiums shall be set at actuarial cost, with no federal subsidy beyond existing ACA premium subsidies.

(f) Provider Networks: Medicare Part E shall use the same provider networks as traditional Medicare.

Estimated Impact: 10-15 million enrolled by Year 5, 10-15% premium reduction through competition, revenue-neutral by Year 4

Section 202: Medicare Age Reduction

(a) Eligibility Expansion: Medicare eligibility age is hereby lowered to age 60.

(b) Buy-In Option: Individuals aged 60-64 may enroll in Medicare with premiums set at actuarially determined rates (approximately $400-500 per month).

(c) Premium Subsidies: Subsidies shall be available based on income using the ACA subsidy schedule.

Estimated Impact: 5-7 million newly insured, $5-8 billion net annual cost

Section 203: Permanent ACA Subsidies

(a) Enhanced Subsidies Made Permanent: The enhanced ACA premium subsidies enacted in the American Rescue Plan and extended through 2025 are hereby made permanent.

(b) Subsidy Expansion: Premium subsidies are extended to households earning up to 600% of the federal poverty level (approximately $90,000 for individuals).

(c) Elimination of Subsidy Cliff: The current subsidy cliff at 400% FPL is eliminated.

Estimated Impact: 3-4 million retain coverage who would otherwise lose it, $30-35 billion annual cost

Section 204: Comprehensive Drug Price Negotiation

(a) Scope Expansion: The Secretary shall negotiate prices for all drugs covered by Medicare Part D.

(b) Timeline: Full implementation within 3 years of enactment.

(c) International Reference Pricing: Negotiated prices shall not exceed the average of prices in Canada, Germany, France, and the United Kingdom.

Estimated Impact: $100-150 billion annual savings, 60-70% reduction in patient drug costs

Section 205: Out-of-Pocket Cap

(a) Federal Maximum: All health insurance plans (ACA marketplace, employer, Medicare, public option) shall have maximum out-of-pocket costs of $2,000 per person and $4,000 per family annually.

(b) Included Costs: Out-of-pocket maximums shall include deductibles, co-pays, and co-insurance.

(c) Federal Subsidy: The Secretary shall subsidize plans to ensure affordability at these caps.

Estimated Impact: Virtual elimination of medical bankruptcy, 50-60 million Americans with reduced financial burden, $40-50 billion annual cost

TITLE III: UNIVERSAL COVERAGE (Years 4-6)

Section 301: Individual Mandate

(a) Requirement: Every U.S. resident shall obtain health insurance coverage.

(b) Coverage Options: Qualifying coverage includes employer-sponsored insurance, ACA marketplace plans, Medicare Part E (public option), traditional Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, VA coverage, or TriCare.

(c) Penalty: Individuals who fail to obtain coverage shall pay a penalty of $850 per year or 2.5% of household income, whichever is greater.

(d) Exemptions: Exemptions shall be granted for financial hardship, religious objections, or other circumstances determined by the Secretary.

Estimated Impact: 20-25 million remaining uninsured Americans gain coverage

Section 302: Standardized Coverage Requirements

(a) Basic Health Plan: The Secretary shall define a “Basic Health Plan” that all insurers must offer.

(b) Covered Services: The Basic Health Plan shall include:

  • Hospital care

  • Physician services

  • Prescription drugs

  • Preventive care

  • Mental health services

  • Substance abuse treatment

  • Rehabilitation services

  • Emergency services

  • Maternity and newborn care

  • Pediatric services

  • Dental services

  • Vision services

  • Hearing services

(c) No Limitations: The Basic Health Plan shall have:

  • No annual or lifetime limits

  • No denial for pre-existing conditions

  • No coverage exclusions based on health status

  • No waiting periods for pre-existing condition coverage

(d) Supplemental Coverage: Insurers may offer supplemental plans covering services beyond the Basic Health Plan.

Legal Basis: Swiss Federal Health Insurance Act (KVG 1996), Article 61

Section 303: Federal Price Controls

(a) Hospital Services: The Secretary shall establish maximum prices for all hospital procedures, using Medicare rates as the baseline.

(b) Physician Services: The Secretary shall establish maximum prices for all physician services using the Medicare physician fee schedule as baseline, with annual adjustments of 2-3% tied to the Medicare Economic Index (MEI) and a $5 billion annual bonus pool for primary care production incentives.

(c) Prescription Drugs: The Secretary shall establish maximum prices for all prescription drugs using international reference pricing (average of Canadian, German, French, and UK prices).

(d) Medical Devices: The Secretary shall establish maximum prices for all medical devices using German pricing as the baseline.

(e) Universal Application: All insurers (private, public option, Medicare, Medicaid) shall pay the same prices for the same services.

(f) Provider Participation: All hospitals and physicians accepting any federal healthcare dollars (Medicare, Medicaid, public option, ACA subsidies, VA, TriCare) must accept these prices.

Legal Basis: German Social Code Book V (SGB V), Section 72

Estimated Impact: 30-40% reduction in healthcare spending over 5 years, 35-45% reduction in premiums

Section 304: Premium Subsidies

(a) Subsidy Schedule: Premium subsidies shall be provided on a sliding scale based on household income as a percentage of the federal poverty level:

  • 0-138% FPL: Free coverage (Medicaid or public option)

  • 138-300% FPL: Premiums capped at 4% of income

  • 300-400% FPL: Premiums capped at 6% of income

  • 400-500% FPL: Premiums capped at 8% of income

  • 500-600% FPL: Premiums capped at 10% of income

  • Above 600% FPL: No subsidy

(b) Federal Funding: The federal government shall fund 90% of subsidy costs, with states funding 10%.

Legal Basis: Swiss subsidy system (approximately 30% of Swiss residents receive subsidies)

Estimated Impact: Ensure affordability for all Americans, $150-200 billion annual cost

Section 305: Administrative Simplification

(a) Single Billing Code System: The Secretary shall establish a single, standardized billing code system for all healthcare services, eliminating proprietary insurer coding systems.

(b) Standardized Claim Forms: The Secretary shall establish a single, standardized claim form for all insurers.

(c) Electronic Claims Processing: All insurers shall process claims electronically within 48 hours of submission.

(d) Prior Authorization Restrictions: Prior authorization shall be prohibited for evidence-based care and required only for experimental treatments.

(e) Appeals Process: The Secretary shall establish a fast-track federal appeals process for coverage denials, with 30-day resolution requirement.

Estimated Impact: Reduce administrative costs from 12-18% to 4-6%, save $200-250 billion annually, free physicians to practice medicine

TITLE IV: MEDICAL EDUCATION AND PROVIDER SUPPORT (Years 7-10)

Section 401: Medical School Debt Relief

(a) Loan Forgiveness: The Secretary shall provide $100,000 in medical school loan forgiveness to any physician practicing in the United States, paid over 10 years ($10,000 per year).

(b) Eligibility: All licensed physicians in active practice are eligible.

Estimated Impact: Reduce physician debt burden, enable gradual alignment of physician compensation with international norms, $12-15 billion annual cost

Section 402: Medical School Expansion

(a) Federal Funding: The Secretary shall provide $5 billion annually to expand medical school capacity by 25% over 10 years.

(b) Primary Care Focus: At least 40% of expanded slots shall be designated for primary care training.

Estimated Impact: Increase physician supply by 8,000-10,000 per year, improve access to care, particularly in underserved areas

Section 403: National Health Service Corps Expansion

(a) Free Medical School: The Secretary shall establish a National Health Service Corps program offering free medical school tuition in exchange for 5 years of practice in rural or underserved areas.

(b) Funding: The Secretary shall fund 2,000 positions annually.

Estimated Impact: Improve rural healthcare access, reduce physician debt burden for participants

Section 404: Malpractice Reform

(a) Federal Malpractice Standards: The Secretary, in consultation with medical societies and patient advocates, shall establish federal malpractice standards for cases involving federal healthcare dollars.

(b) Safe Harbor: Physicians following evidence-based clinical guidelines shall have safe harbor from malpractice liability.

(c) Damage Caps: Non-economic damages shall be capped at $500,000 (adjustable for inflation).

(d) Alternative Dispute Resolution: The Secretary shall establish health courts for fast-track resolution of malpractice claims.

Legal Basis: German malpractice system (lower costs, faster resolution, similar patient outcomes)

Estimated Impact: Reduce malpractice insurance costs by 50-70%, reduce defensive medicine costs, align U.S. physician costs with international norms

TITLE V: FUNDING AND IMPLEMENTATION

Section 501: Revenue Provisions

(a) Top Marginal Tax Rate: The top marginal income tax rate is hereby restored to 39.6% for income above $400,000 (individual) or $450,000 (married filing jointly).

  • Revenue: $60 billion annually

(b) Carried Interest: The carried interest tax loophole is hereby closed, requiring all carried interest to be taxed as ordinary income rather than capital gains.

  • Revenue: $18 billion annually

(c) Medicare High-Income Surcharge: A 3% Medicare surcharge is hereby established on individual income exceeding $500,000 (individual) or $600,000 (married filing jointly).

  • Revenue: $75-85 billion annually

(d) Employer Payroll Savings: Employers shall continue paying healthcare costs through adjusted payroll taxes equivalent to their current healthcare spending, phased down over 5 years as system efficiencies reduce total costs.

  • Revenue: $200-250 billion annually (declining as costs drop)

Total New Revenue: $153-163 billion annually from new taxes, plus $200-250 billion redirected from current employer healthcare spending

Section 502: Deficit Impact

(a) Total Cost: Estimated $300-400 billion in new annual federal spending for subsidies, public option administration, and program implementation.

(b) Total Savings: Estimated $500-700 billion in annual savings from:

  • Price controls: $400-500 billion

  • Administrative simplification: $200-250 billion

  • Drug price negotiation: $100-150 billion

(c) Net Impact: $200-400 billion in annual deficit reduction

Section 503: CBO Scoring Requirement

The Congressional Budget Office shall score this Act using dynamic scoring that accounts for:

  • Reduced emergency room costs

  • Reduced uncompensated care

  • Improved preventive care

  • Reduced administrative burden

  • Increased labor productivity from healthier workforce

  • Reduced job lock (workers can change jobs without losing coverage)

Section 504: Implementation Timeline

(a) Year 1 (2027): Title I provisions (immediate reforms) implemented (b) Year 2 (2028): Title II provisions (public option, Medicare expansion) begin phase-in
(c) Year 3 (2029): Title II fully implemented (d) Year 4 (2030): Title III provisions (universal mandate, price controls) begin phase-in (e) Year 6 (2032): Title III fully implemented, universal coverage achieved (f) Years 7-10 (2033-2036): Title IV provisions (medical education reform) implemented

Section 505: Oversight and Reporting

(a) Annual Report: The Secretary shall submit an annual report to Congress on:

  • Coverage rates (by state, age, income)

  • Healthcare costs (per capita, as percentage of GDP)

  • Health outcomes (life expectancy, infant mortality, preventable deaths)

  • Provider supply (physician availability, hospital access)

  • System performance (wait times, patient satisfaction)

(b) Public Dashboard: The Secretary shall establish a public website displaying all data in real-time.

(c) Enforcement: The Secretary shall enforce price controls, coverage standards, and administrative requirements with penalties of up to $10,000 per day for violations.
