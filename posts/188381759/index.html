When Democracy Fights Back

What the Rest of the World Already Knows

Part 14: The Problem America Won't Solve — And the Country Next Door That Did

February 19, 2026

I’ve spent the past thirteen parts of this series documenting the redistricting wars of 2026 — the gerrymandered maps, the court battles, the referendums, the scorecards. The Texas power grab, the California counterpunch, the Virginia gambit, the Callais time bomb.

But here’s the thing I keep coming back to, watching all of this from Toronto: none of it had to happen.

Not the arms race. Not the lawsuits. Not the Supreme Court deciding which party’s gerrymander gets blessed and which one doesn’t. None of it. Because the rest of the democratic world figured this out decades ago.

The New York Times published a useful survey this week of how other democracies handle redistricting. It’s a good primer. But I want to go deeper — because I don’t just report on this from the outside. I live in the country that solved it.

What Canada Figured Out in 1964

Let me tell you a story about a country that used to gerrymander exactly the way America does now — and then stopped.

For nearly a century after Confederation, Canadian redistricting was a partisan bloodsport. The party in power drew the maps. Conservatives did it when they were in charge. Liberals did it when they were in charge. Constituencies were carved and packed with the same cold precision you see in Texas and Virginia today. Scholars have documented that every redistricting between 1872 and 1952 was, in the words of political scientist John Courtney, “carefully managed by the government of the day, whether Conservative or Liberal, in its own interest.”

Sound familiar?

By the early 1960s, Canadians had had enough. Both parties had had enough. The back-and-forth had exhausted everyone — politicians included — because every time power changed hands, the other side’s maps got torn up and redrawn. Nobody’s seat was safe. Nobody’s map was permanent. The system was eating itself.

So in 1964, Lester Pearson’s Liberal government introduced the Electoral Boundaries Readjustment Act. It took a full year to get through Parliament because members on all sides fought over the details. But the core principle survived: take redistricting away from politicians entirely.

Here’s how it works, and has worked for sixty years:

After every census, ten independent commissions — one per province — are established to redraw the maps. Each commission is chaired by a judge appointed by the chief justice of the province. The other two members are appointed by the Speaker of the House of Commons. No sitting politician, federal or provincial, can serve on a commission. The commissions hold public hearings, publish proposed maps, accept objections — including from members of Parliament — and then make final decisions that no legislature can override.

That’s it. The politicians can complain. They can show up at hearings and argue their case. But they can’t draw the lines. The judge draws the lines.

Is it perfect? No. There have been debates about population variance between ridings, about how to balance community-of-interest concerns with strict population equality, about whether the commissions have too much discretion. The McGill Law Journal published a detailed analysis of these tensions as recently as 2019.

But here’s what Canada doesn’t have: a redistricting arms race. We don’t have legislatures gerrymandering in the middle of a decade. We don’t have governors calling special sessions to rig the maps. We don’t have Supreme Court justices deciding which party’s gerrymander is constitutionally acceptable. We don’t have trillion-dollar elections turning on whether a judge in Tazewell County, Virginia, or a three-judge panel in Austin, Texas, thinks a particular map crosses an invisible line between “partisan” and “racial” motivation.

We had all of that. We chose to stop.

The Rest of the World Got the Memo

Canada wasn’t alone. The New York Times piece surveys several countries, and the pattern is consistent.

In Britain, politicians are barred from any role in redistricting. Boundary commissions — one for each country in the United Kingdom — are staffed by people whose job depends on having no political affiliations. Past or present partisan connections disqualify you from serving. The rules are set at the national level, so there’s no equivalent of fifty states running fifty different processes with fifty different standards.

In Australia, an independent elections commission oversees the process. The commission includes a judge and a statistician — not a governor and a legislative majority leader. Proposed maps go out for public comment, get revised, go out again, and are finalized only after multiple rounds of public input. Politicians can comment. They cannot draw.

In France, redistricting is handled through the interior ministry, but an independent commission reviews the maps, and a constitutional council ensures they comply with the law. Changes must be approved by Parliament. There have been accusations of political bias, but as Vincent Pons, a Harvard economist who studies French democracy, told the Times: “You never see in France a government drawing a map and saying, ‘Oh, we’re doing this because we think it’s going to help us win additional districts.’ This would be completely unacceptable.”

Read that quote again. “Completely unacceptable.”

In America, it’s Tuesday.

The Hungary Warning

But the Times piece also includes a cautionary tale that should make Americans deeply uncomfortable.

Hungary. Since 2010, Viktor Orbán and his Fidesz party have used redistricting as one of several tools to engineer a permanent supermajority. After winning a two-thirds parliamentary majority in 2010 — legitimately — Orbán rewrote the electoral laws, reduced the size of parliament from 386 seats to 199, and redrew every district map in the country. The maps were drawn in secret. A Journal of Democracy analysis found that if Fidesz and the opposition won exactly equal numbers of votes under the new maps, Fidesz would come out ten seats ahead.

The redistricting was reinforced by media capture, judicial appointments, restrictions on civil society, and the elimination of runoff elections. Orbán didn’t need to steal votes. He just rigged the architecture so that the same number of votes produced an unbeatable majority.

In 2022, the European Parliament voted to declare Hungary no longer a full democracy. The official designation: “electoral autocracy” — a system in which elections are held but respect for democratic norms is absent.

Orbán’s admirers include Donald Trump, who has hosted him at Mar-a-Lago and praised his leadership. Several Republican strategists have openly described Hungary as a model for conservative governance. The Heritage Foundation’s Project 2025 — the blueprint for Trump’s second term — drew explicit parallels to Orbán’s institutional playbook.

I’m not saying America is Hungary. America has a Constitution, an independent judiciary (mostly), a free press (still), and a federal structure that makes total capture much harder than in a parliamentary system. But here’s what the comparison reveals: the techniques are the same. Gerrymander the maps. Stack the courts. Restrict who can vote. Capture the referees. Each step makes the next step easier. Each step is technically legal.

The question isn’t whether America could become Hungary. The question is how many of the same tools have already been deployed.

Why America Can’t Fix This

So if Canada, Britain, Australia, France, and New Zealand have all figured out how to take redistricting out of partisan hands, why can’t America?

The answer is structural, not intellectual. Americans know what independent commissions are — several states have tried them. California’s citizen redistricting commission, ironically, produced the nonpartisan maps that Gavin Newsom just suspended by ballot initiative. Arizona, Michigan, and Colorado have independent commissions that work reasonably well. Virginia created a bipartisan commission in 2020 that Democrats are now trying to override.

The problem is federalism. In Canada, one act of Parliament created a national system. In America, redistricting is a state function, protected by the Constitution. There is no federal mechanism to impose independent commissions on fifty states. Congress could theoretically pass legislation requiring them — the For the People Act included such provisions — but that bill is dead, and the Supreme Court has shown no interest in imposing structural reforms through the judiciary.

Which means reform only happens when both parties in a given state agree to disarm simultaneously. And that almost never happens — because the party in power always believes it can draw maps that lock in its advantage for a decade.

This is the trap. It’s a classic prisoner’s dilemma. If both sides cooperate, both benefit from stable, fair maps and competitive elections. But if one side defects — gerrymanders while the other side plays fair — the defector wins big. So nobody cooperates. Texas defects. California retaliates. Virginia follows. Florida prepares to escalate. And the spiral continues.

Canada broke the spiral in 1964 because both parties were exhausted by it. The Liberals were in power, but they’d been out of power recently enough to remember what it felt like to have the other side’s maps used against them. The Conservatives agreed because they knew they’d eventually be back in opposition. Everyone was tired of the game.

America isn’t tired yet. Or rather, Americans are tired — polls consistently show large majorities favor independent redistricting commissions — but their elected representatives aren’t. Because the people who would have to give up the power to draw maps are the people who currently benefit from drawing them.

What This Series Has Really Been About

I’ve written fourteen parts now about the redistricting wars. I’ve tracked lawsuits, counted seats, built scorecards, analyzed Supreme Court decisions. I’ve tried to give you the most complete picture I can of what’s happening state by state, court by court, map by map.

But the real story isn’t the scorecard. The real story is that the most powerful democracy on earth is engaged in an exercise that every other mature democracy abandoned decades ago. And the reason it can’t stop isn’t that the problem is unsolvable. It’s that the people with the power to solve it profit from it remaining unsolved.

From up here in Toronto, I watch this with a mix of fascination and sadness. Canada isn’t perfect. Our politics have their own pathologies. But when it comes to drawing the lines that determine who represents whom, we made a choice sixty years ago that Americans still can’t bring themselves to make: we decided the referees shouldn’t also be the players.

That’s not a radical idea. It’s the foundation of every fair system ever designed. And until America adopts it — not state by state, not referendum by referendum, but structurally — the redistricting wars will never end. They’ll just get more expensive, more litigious, and more corrosive to the democracy they’re supposedly conducted in the name of.

The guardrails exist. Other countries built them. The blueprints are available.

All that’s missing is the will.
