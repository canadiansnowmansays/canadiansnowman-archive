Now This is (Not) Fake News

Part 6: "Kill Everybody" — The Crime and The Law

December 3, 2025

A Canadian’s Analysis of the September 2 Boat Strike

By CanadianSnowmanSays December 3, 2025

In Parts 1-5, I documented the press purge and warned what happens when nobody’s watching. Hegseth called investigative reporting ‘fake news.’ Now we have the proof this is (not) fake news: the order, the strike, the law.

Remember when I told you 57 people were dead and counting? When I warned you what happens when nobody’s watching?

We now know exactly what happens. We have the order. We have the sequence. We have the survivors clinging to burning wreckage. And we have the second missile.

“The order was to kill everybody.”

The Washington Post Revelation

On November 28, The Washington Post published what will become one of the defining investigative pieces of the Trump administration’s second term. National security reporters Alex Horton and Ellen Nakashima—both banned from the Pentagon under Hegseth’s new press restrictions—obtained accounts from two people with direct knowledge of the September 2 operation.

The Post’s Alex Horton, who previously served in Iraq as an Army infantryman and has shared three Pulitzer Prizes, and Ellen Nakashima, a veteran national security correspondent, got this story by doing what good journalists do: finding sources willing to speak because they witnessed something they believe was wrong.

Here’s what their sources revealed:

The Surveillance: “The longer the U.S. surveillance aircraft followed the boat, the more confident intelligence analysts watching from command centers became that the 11 people on board were ferrying drugs,” the Post reported. The analysts were gaining confidence over time, but at no point had confirmation. They believed. They assessed. But they didn’t know.

The Spoken Directive: Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth gave a spoken directive, according to two people with direct knowledge of the operation. “The order was to kill everybody,” one of them said. Not “neutralize the threat.” Not “destroy the vessel.” “Kill everybody.”

The First Strike: A missile screamed off the Trinidad coast, striking the vessel and igniting a blaze from bow to stern. For minutes, commanders watched the boat burning on live drone feed.

The Jolt: As smoke cleared, they got a jolt: Two survivors were clinging to the smoldering wreck. The survivors were visible, no longer operating the boat, clinging to wreckage, wounded, defenseless. Under any reasonable interpretation of the laws of war, they were “hors de combat”—out of combat.

The Justification: Admiral Frank M. “Mitch” Bradley, overseeing the attack from Fort Bragg, told people on the secure conference call that the survivors were still “legitimate targets” because they could “theoretically call other traffickers to retrieve them and their cargo.” Not because they posed an immediate threat. Not because they were armed. Because they could theoretically make a phone call.

The Second Strike: Bradley ordered a second strike to comply with Hegseth’s instructions, two people familiar with the matter told the Post. The two men were blown apart in the water.

The Aftermath: The Sept. 2 attack was “the opening salvo in the Trump administration’s war on suspected drug traffickers in the Western Hemisphere,” the Post noted. Hegseth’s order “adds another dimension to the campaign against suspected drug traffickers,” one that raises “new questions about the legality of the strikes and whether U.S. personnel involved could face war crimes charges.”

The Earlier Reporting

The Washington Post wasn’t the first to report that survivors were killed in a second strike. The Intercept’s Nick Turse first reported in early September that “several people on the boat survived the initial strike” and documented that the attack came from an unmanned U.S. aircraft. But the Post’s November 28 revelation added the critical detail: Hegseth’s spoken order to “kill everybody,” turning what was already a controversial strike into an explicit war crime allegation.

This wasn’t an isolated incident. This was the first strike—the one that established the pattern for everything that followed.

Since September 2, the U.S. military has conducted at least 21 strikes on alleged drug boats, killing a minimum of 83 people. We now know those operations began with an explicit order to leave no survivors.

And here’s what’s chilling: in at least one subsequent strike on October 16, two survivors were captured and later sent to Ecuador and Colombia. Which means the administration can take survivors. They can conduct arrests. They chose to execute the survivors on September 2.

The New York Times Follow-Up

On December 1, The New York Times published additional reporting that both confirmed and complicated the Post’s account. Five U.S. officials told the Times that Hegseth “ordered a strike that would kill the people on the boat and destroy the vessel and its purported cargo of drugs.”

But critically, these officials said Hegseth’s written execute order “did not specifically address what should happen if a first missile turned out not to fully accomplish all of those things.” According to these sources, Hegseth gave no further orders after the initial strike; Admiral Bradley ordered “several follow-up strikes that killed the initial survivors and sank the disabled boat.”

This is the administration’s defense: Hegseth ordered killing everyone on the boat, but didn’t specifically order killing survivors if the first strike failed. As if that distinction matters when two men are clinging to burning wreckage and someone gives an order to finish them off.

The question becomes: Did Bradley act on his own initiative, or was he following through on Hegseth’s broader directive to “kill everybody”? The Post’s sources say he was complying with Hegseth’s instructions. The administration claims Bradley acted independently within his authority.

Either way, the result is the same: two wounded, defenseless men blown apart in the water.

The Legal Consensus: This is a War Crime

The legal analysis is remarkably unified across ideological lines. From former military lawyers to constitutional scholars, from Republicans to Democrats, the consensus is clear: if the facts are as reported, this is a war crime—or murder, depending on whether you accept the administration’s claim that we’re in an “armed conflict” with drug cartels.

The Former JAGs Speak

The Former JAGs Working Group—an organization of retired military judge advocates—issued a statement that leaves no room for interpretation:

“If the U.S. military operation to interdict and destroy suspected narco-trafficking vessels is a ‘non-international armed conflict,’ as the Trump Administration suggests, orders to ‘kill everybody,’ which can reasonably be regarded as an order to give ‘no quarter,’ and to ‘double-tap’ a target in order to kill survivors, are clearly illegal under international law.”

If the attacks are occurring outside an armed conflict—which most legal experts contend is the case—such orders “to kill helpless civilians clinging to the wreckage of a vessel our military destroyed would subject everyone from SECDEF down to the individual who pulled the trigger to prosecution under U.S. murder laws.”

The Geneva Conventions Are Unambiguous

The Pentagon’s own Law of War Manual states: “For example, orders to fire upon the shipwrecked would be clearly illegal.”

It also says: “It is prohibited to conduct hostilities on the basis that there shall be no survivors, or to threaten the adversary with the denial of quarter.”

The Geneva Convention provides that wounded, sick, or shipwrecked combatants who are “hors de combat” (out of combat) must be collected and cared for. Deliberately killing survivors who pose no threat constitutes a grave breach—recognized as a war crime under international law and the U.S. War Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. §2441).

Even If You Accept the Armed Conflict Theory

Here’s what makes the September 2 strike particularly indefensible: even if you accept the Trump administration’s dubious legal theory that we’re in an armed conflict with drug cartels, killing shipwrecked survivors violates the laws of war.

Harvard professor Jack Goldsmith, who served as a lawyer in both the Department of Justice’s Office of Legal Counsel and the Department of Defense after 9/11 under George W. Bush, has previously suggested there could be legal justifications for attacking drug boats. But he struggled to see how the second strike didn’t violate the law.

He quoted the Civil War-era Lieber Code of 1863, by which President Abraham Lincoln decreed that inflicting additional wounds or killing an enemy “wholly disabled” should be punishable by death if convicted.

Michael Schmitt, a former Air Force lawyer and professor emeritus at the U.S. Naval War College, told PBS: “I can’t imagine anyone, no matter what the circumstance, believing it is appropriate to kill people who are clinging to a boat in the water. That is clearly unlawful.”

CNN legal analyst Elie Honig stated flatly: “In a word, they were illegal. It is a core principle of the rule of law and of the law of war that you cannot target and kill people who have been rendered incapacitated or defenseless.”

Daniel Maurer, a retired Army judge advocate general and associate professor at Ohio Northern University, explained the legal framework clearly: “Whether they are narco-terrorists designated by the president or not, whether they’re war criminals or not, it doesn’t matter. Killing them while shipwrecked, while they’re hors de combat—while they’re out of the fight—is a war crime.”

But Maurer added a crucial point: he doesn’t believe this would be a war crime because he doesn’t consider the U.S. to be legally engaged in an armed conflict with narco-terrorists. “If the facts ultimately show the strike was taken to kill the survivors, it would be the equivalent of Hegseth or Bradley condoning or ordering murder, which could implicate everyone down that chain of command who participated in, who planned, who executed that strike.”

The Senators Weigh In

The bipartisan consensus is striking:

Senator Tim Kaine (D-VA): “This rises to the level of a war crime if it’s true.”

Senator Angus King (I-ME): “If the facts are as have been alleged, that there was a second strike specifically to kill the survivors in the water, that’s a stone-cold war crime. It’s also murder.”

Senator Chris Van Hollen (D-MD): “I think it’s very possible there was a war crime committed... Of course, for it to be a war crime, you have to accept the Trump administration’s whole construct here, which is we’re in armed conflict, at war, with this particular—with the drug gangs. But it could be worse than that, right? If that theory is wrong, then it’s plain murder.”

Representative Mike Turner (R-OH), former chair of the House Intelligence Committee: “Obviously if that occurred, that would be very serious, and I agree that that would be an illegal act.”

Representative Seth Moulton (D-MA), a Marine Corps veteran who received a classified briefing on the strikes: “Mark my words, it may take some time, but Americans will be prosecuted for this either as a war crime or outright murder.”

The legal consensus is clear. The question now becomes: will anyone be held accountable?
