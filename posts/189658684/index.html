The Confidence Cycle

Part 1: How Quick Victory Breeds Overconfidence, Overconfidence Breeds Miscalculation, and Why Trump Is Repeating the Pattern at Triple Speed

March 3, 2026

Norlys Perez / Reuters

“The president is feeling like, ‘I’m on a roll’; like, ‘This is working.’” — Senior administration official, March 2026

There are moments in history when a single quote captures something so precisely dangerous that it deserves to be studied not just as journalism but as a warning. When a senior administration official told The Atlantic’s Vivian Salama that the president was feeling “on a roll,” they weren’t describing confidence. They were describing the most lethal pattern in American foreign policy: the conviction, born of early military success, that the next operation will be just as easy.

We have seen this before. And we know how it ends.

I. The Pattern

In October 2001, the United States invaded Afghanistan. The Taliban fell in weeks. American casualties were minimal. The Northern Alliance did most of the ground fighting. It was, by almost any military measure, a stunning success.

Presidential historian Timothy Naftali, now at Columbia University’s School of International and Public Affairs, remembers what came next. Speaking to Salama about the current moment, Naftali drew a direct line to December 2001, when the ease of the Afghan operation began transforming the Bush administration’s ambitions. Saddam Hussein, he observed, had been a persistent irritant to American foreign policy for over a decade. In the wake of the Afghan victory, the country’s appetite for tolerating threats had plummeted while its ambitions to reshape the world had soared.

The logic was seductive: if the Taliban could be toppled in weeks, why not Saddam? If Afghanistan could be liberated with minimal American casualties, why not Iraq? The intelligence assessments were uncertain, the post-war planning was thin, the regional dynamics were poorly understood — but none of that mattered against the intoxicating certainty that this is working.

Iraq became the defining catastrophe of 21st-century American foreign policy. Thousands of American lives lost. Hundreds of thousands of Iraqi civilians dead. Trillions of dollars spent. A region destabilized for a generation. An occupation that lasted nearly a decade. And it all began with the same five words now echoing through the corridors of the White House: I’m on a roll.

The pattern has a name in strategic studies: the confidence cycle. Quick victory breeds overconfidence. Overconfidence breeds miscalculation. Miscalculation breeds catastrophe. It is not unique to the United States — the Soviets experienced it moving from Eastern Europe to Afghanistan, the British experienced it across their imperial history — but the American version has a particular flavor. It combines genuine military superiority with a profound inability to distinguish between winning a war and winning a peace.

What makes the current moment uniquely alarming is the speed. Bush took eighteen months to move from Afghan confidence to the Iraq invasion. Trump has compressed the cycle into weeks. Venezuela to Iran to Cuba — one regime change executed, one military campaign underway, and a third under active contemplation, in a matter of months.The confidence cycle isn’t just repeating. It’s accelerating.

II. The Trilogy

To understand where Cuba fits, you have to understand the sequence — because the sequence is the argument. Each operation builds the case for the next, and each success (or apparent success) lowers the threshold for the one that follows.

Venezuela was the proof of concept. The seizure of Nicolás Maduro was dramatic, unprecedented, and — in the narrow sense of removing a leader from power — effective. The operation was justified on the basis of a federal arrest warrant for narco-trafficking charges. There was a legal predicate, however controversial. There was a clear opposition leader. There were decades of democratic institutional memory in the country. And critically, the international community’s response was limited to condemnation without consequence: UN meetings that would be vetoed, ICC considerations from a court the U.S. doesn’t recognize, and strongly worded statements from regional neighbors with no enforcement mechanism.

The lesson Trump absorbed was not about Venezuela’s unique circumstances. The lesson was: I can do this and nothing happens.

Iran was the escalation. Where Venezuela was a surgical extraction, Iran was a full military campaign — U.S. and Israeli missiles falling on a nation of 88 million people, culminating in the death of Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. The justification shifted from law enforcement (a warrant) to national security (nuclear weapons, regional destabilization, the historical memory of the 1979 hostage crisis). The legal and moral framework was entirely different from Venezuela, but the underlying psychology was identical: this is working.

Trump’s video address announcing military action in Iran was revealing. He acknowledged there might be American casualties but stressed the action was being taken “for the future.” This is the language of a leader who has moved beyond tactical calculation into legacy-building — a president who has begun to see himself not as a decision-maker weighing costs and benefits but as a historical figure whose boldness will be vindicated by posterity.

A necessary acknowledgment: Venezuela's Maduro was a designated narco-terrorist whose removal disrupted documented drug trafficking networks. Iran's nuclear program represented a genuine proliferation threat, and the strikes degraded enrichment capacity that multiple administrations had failed to contain diplomatically. These are not invented justifications. The danger is not that the operations lacked any rationale — it is that genuine rationale, combined with apparent success, feeds the psychology that makes the next operation feel equally justified even when the case is fundamentally weaker.

Cuba is where the pattern becomes most dangerous, precisely because it is the weakest case on the merits and the strongest case on the psychology.

III. The Weakest Case

Every previous operation in this trilogy had something Cuba lacks: a clear predicate for action.

Maduro was seized on a federal arrest warrant based on narco-trafficking charges. Whatever one thinks of the operation’s legality, there was a document with a judge’s signature. Iran was targeted over its nuclear weapons program and its documented role in regional destabilization through proxy forces. Again, one can debate the proportionality, but the threat was articulable.

Cuba has neither.

William LeoGrande, co-author of Back Channel to Cuba, a comprehensive history of Washington-Havana negotiations, put it bluntly to The Atlantic: there is no predicate comparable to what existed for Venezuela or Iran. The administration has pointed to Cuba’s state sponsor of terrorism designation, but even the rationale for that listing, LeoGrande notes, doesn’t hold up under scrutiny. The declaration of a national emergency over Russian signals-intelligence facilities and alleged safe harbor for groups like Hezbollah and Hamas is a justification in search of evidence, not evidence in search of a response.

This matters enormously, because the absence of a clear legal or security predicate means the real driver is something else entirely: momentum. The president is “on a roll.” The operation in Venezuela worked. Iran is underway. Cuba is 90 miles from Florida, economically collapsing, militarily weak, and diplomatically isolated. The gravitational pull of the confidence cycle does the rest.

The administration has already begun applying pressure that goes beyond diplomacy. Punitive tariffs have been authorized on imports from any third country supplying oil to Cuba. U.S. forces have begun intercepting vessels en route to the island. Salama reports — and this detail should alarm anyone paying attention — that there has been no articulated legal authority for such interdiction. When a great power begins enforcing a blockade without legal justification, it is not engaged in diplomacy. It is engaged in the preliminary stages of something else.

IV. The “Friendly Takeover”

Havana skyline at dusk, featuring the iconic Capitol building—90 miles from Florida and now in the crosshairs of the confidence cycle. Source: Getty Images (Nikada)

Pay close attention to language, because language reveals intent — and sometimes it reveals the absence of a plan.

When Trump floated the possibility of a “friendly takeover” of Cuba while speaking with reporters, he was doing something that has become characteristic of his foreign policy: applying the vocabulary of real estate to the governance of nations. A “friendly takeover” is a term from corporate mergers and acquisitions. It describes a transaction where both parties agree to the deal. It implies willing buyers and willing sellers. It assumes that sovereignty is a commodity to be transacted.

This is the same linguistic framework that produced the Greenland acquisition proposal, the Panama Canal recovery rhetoric, and the recurring suggestion that Canada should become the 51st state. In each case, the language performs a kind of conceptual magic trick: it transforms what would be an act of imperial domination into the language of business, making the unthinkable sound merely unconventional.

But the “friendly takeover” framing also reveals something more specific about Cuba: the administration doesn’t have a plan. A military operation requires a predicate they don’t have. A negotiated settlement requires a willing counterparty — and while Rubio is reportedly in contact with Raúl Castro’s grandson through back channels, there is no indication that the Cuban regime is prepared to negotiate its own dissolution. What remains is this linguistic placeholder, a phrase that sounds purposeful while describing nothing concrete.

The Venezuela model — where the U.S. seized the president and then worked with his vice president to maintain order — is reportedly tempting. But as Salama’s reporting makes clear, that model has its own problems. Months after Maduro’s capture, the administration has provided no timeline for democratic transition. Some political prisoners have been released and the economy has been stabilized through U.S.-arranged oil sales, but the fundamental character of governance hasn’t changed. If “friendly takeover” means “we replace the person at the top and call it a day,” then what Trump is describing isn’t regime change. It’s regime renovation.

For the Cuban exile community in South Florida — the political constituency whose support drives much of this ambition — regime renovation would be bitterly insufficient. They have waited 65 years not for a new face atop the same system but for the end of the system itself. Trump knows this. He told reporters he wants to change the “fundamental character” of Cuba. But nothing in the current approach suggests a mechanism for achieving that goal.

V. The Canary in the Caribbean

There is a useful way to gauge how far American overreach has gone at any given moment: watch Canada.

For more than six decades, Canada has maintained diplomatic and economic relations with Cuba, explicitly rejecting the American embargo as counterproductive. This was not a radical position — it was shared by most of America’s allies — but it was distinctively Canadian in its persistence. Through ten American presidents and every iteration of Cuba policy from assassination attempts to détente, Ottawa held its independent line.

That independence is now under extraordinary pressure. The Great Canadian Trade War has already strained the bilateral relationship to its most precarious point in generations. If Trump moves toward forcible regime change in Cuba, Canada faces an impossible choice: abandon a 60-year foreign policy principle under economic duress, or become one of the few Western voices opposing the operation and absorb the consequences.

Watch what Ottawa does. If even Canada — historically the most independent voice on Cuba among Western nations — begins hedging, it tells you something about the gravitational force of American overconfidence in this moment. And if Canada resists, it tells you something about how far Trump has pushed beyond the boundaries that even close allies will tolerate.

VI. The Overstretch

Here is what the confidence cycle always obscures: capacity.

The United States military is currently conducting or supporting operations in the Persian Gulf (Iran), maintaining a presence in the Caribbean (Venezuela aftermath, Cuba interdiction), and managing ongoing global commitments that haven’t disappeared simply because the president has discovered a taste for regime change. Salama notes, almost in passing, that the military is “already stretched thin from the Caribbean to the Persian Gulf.”

Three simultaneous regime changes is not a thing that modern great powers do successfully. It is, in fact, the precise pattern that defines imperial overstretch — the moment when ambition outpaces the institutional capacity to consolidate gains. Rome did it. Spain did it. Britain did it. And in each case, the period of maximum expansion was also the beginning of contraction.

The Bush administration learned this in real time. Afghanistan was manageable. Afghanistan plus Iraq was not. The resources, attention, and institutional bandwidth required to occupy, stabilize, and rebuild even one country proved staggering. Two proved impossible. The United States spent twenty years in Afghanistan and ultimately left with the Taliban back in power.

Trump is now contemplating a third theater of operations while the first (Venezuela) remains unstabilized, the second (Iran) is so fresh that outcomes are unknowable, and the military is already deployed across both. LeoGrande’s observation carries the weight of history: no one has used open military operations against Cuba since the Bay of Pigs, and that, he noted dryly, didn’t go very well.

The Cuban regime, presumably, is watching this calculation closely. Naftali identified the dynamic precisely: when you have a police state populated by people who have no future in a pro-American successor government, they have no incentive to surrender — and they hold the monopoly on firepower. Cuba’s military may be weak by American standards, but the question isn’t whether the U.S. can defeat it. The question is what happens the day after the defeat, and the month after that, and the year after that — with American forces already committed in two other theaters.

The confidence cycle explains the mechanism — how quick victories breed overreach. But it doesn’t explain the appetite. Why three regime changes? Why simultaneously? Why the explicit competition with Reagan, Carter, and Nixon for historical legacy?
