The Contribution Index

Israel

Part VI: The Innovation Paradox

March 27, 2026

Tel Aviv skyline at dusk, hub of Israel’s startup ecosystem Modern high-rises representing the “Startup Nation” vibrancy, where per-capita innovation in tech, cybersecurity, and medicine rivals global leaders. Source: The Times of Israel

Underperformer #8

Israel presents the Contribution Index with its most complex scoring challenge. No nation on either list generates a sharper tension between what it creates and what it destroys.

The innovation output is staggering. Israel produces more startups per capita than any nation on Earth. Its agricultural technology — drip irrigation, desalination, precision farming — has been adopted across the developing world and has literally made deserts bloom. Its cybersecurity and medical technology sectors are globally significant. Its scientific research output, relative to population, is extraordinary.

And yet Israel ranks 155th out of 163 nations on the 2025 Global Peace Index. Its military operations have produced civilian casualties on a scale that has drawn condemnation from the International Court of Justice. Its occupation of Palestinian territories has endured for nearly six decades. And in early 2026, its strikes against Iran — coordinated with the United States in what became Operation Epic Fury — helped ignite the most dangerous military escalation the Middle East has seen since the 1973 Yom Kippur War.

The innovation paradox: a nation that gives the world technology to grow food in deserts while setting its entire region on fire.

The Scorecard

Note: Scores on arms exports, international law adherence, and the Neighbour Test revised downward from earlier estimates to reflect the regional escalation of early 2026, including Israel’s role in the strikes against Iran and the cascading consequences across the Middle East.

The Analysis

Human Development: 37 out of 50

Israel’s domestic human development is strong — for its Jewish citizens. Life expectancy exceeds 83 years. The healthcare system is universal and effective. The education system produces extraordinary scientific talent. The technology sector, centred around Tel Aviv, has generated innovation that rivals Silicon Valley on a per capita basis. These are genuine achievements that the scorecard must acknowledge.

The 6 on democratic governance reflects a democracy that functions well for part of the population under its control but operates a fundamentally different system for the rest. Palestinians in the occupied territories live under military administration without political rights. This is not a peripheral detail — it is the central fact of Israeli governance. A democracy that applies to some of the people under its authority but not all of them is, by any honest definition, incomplete. The Economist Intelligence Unit classifies Israel as a “flawed democracy,” and the flaw is not subtle.

The 6 on minority rights reflects the same structural division. Arab citizens of Israel face systemic disadvantages in housing, employment, and public investment. Palestinians in the territories face conditions that every major international human rights organisation — Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, B’Tselem — has described using language that the Israeli government rejects but that the organisations stand behind with exhaustive documentation.

Israeli philanthropists have invested heavily in domestic institutions. The Peres Center for Peace and Innovation and the Rashi Foundation fund health and education programmes that are meaningful within Israel’s borders. American-Israeli philanthropic flows are enormous, channelling billions into Israeli universities, hospitals, and cultural institutions. But philanthropy that strengthens a society domestically while that same society maintains a military occupation externally is not contribution in the sense this index measures — it is investment in one population at the expense of another.

Environmental Stewardship: 30 out of 50

Large-scale desalination facility in Israel. Industrial water treatment plant turning seawater into freshwater, a major environmental contribution adopted globally to combat scarcity. Source: Scientific American (article on Israel’s desalination era)

Middling. Israel’s water technology is globally admired — desalination and water recycling systems developed in Israel are genuine contributions to environmental innovation, and they have been adopted across arid regions of the developing world. This is real, and it matters.

But domestic environmental performance has lagged. Renewable energy adoption has been slow relative to Israel’s extraordinary solar potential — the Negev desert receives more solar irradiation than virtually any populated territory on Earth, yet solar’s share of the energy mix remains modest. Per capita emissions are moderate. And the environmental impact of military operations — the destruction of agricultural land in Gaza, the contamination from munitions, the infrastructure devastation — does not factor positively into any environmental assessment.

Systemic Behaviour: 12 out of 50

This is where Israel’s scorecard does not merely decline. It collapses.

And it is here that I must speak plainly about what has happened in the Middle East in the months preceding this essay, because the scores cannot be understood without it.

Arms exports and conflict contribution: 1 out of 10.

Israel is not merely a participant in regional conflict. It is, in 2026, the primary catalyst of the most dangerous military escalation the Middle East has experienced in half a century.

Devastated buildings and rubble in Gaza from military operations. Aerial view of widespread destruction in civilian areas, illustrating the scale of infrastructure damage and humanitarian crisis central to the essay’s critique. Source: Al Jazeera

Begin with Gaza. The military campaign that followed October 7, 2023 produced civilian casualties that the UN, the International Court of Justice, and humanitarian organisations worldwide have documented in terms that do not require repetition here — the numbers are a matter of record, and they are staggering. The destruction of civilian infrastructure — hospitals, schools, refugee shelters, residential blocks — has been systematic. The humanitarian crisis is, by any measure, among the worst on Earth.

But Gaza was not the end. It was the beginning.

The campaign in Gaza drew Hezbollah into active engagement from Lebanon — a re-entry that escalated throughout 2025 and into 2026, destabilising a country already in economic freefall. Israeli strikes in Lebanon expanded. The northern front, dormant for years, reignited. Southern Lebanon, barely recovered from the 2006 war, was devastated again.

And then came Iran.

In late February 2026, US and Israeli strikes against Iran decimated much of Iran’s top leadership, including Supreme Leader Khamenei and over forty senior officials. Operation Epic Fury — as the broader military campaign became known — represented a direct military assault on a sovereign nation of 88 million people, undertaken without UN authorisation, without a declaration of war, and with consequences that are still cascading as this essay is written.

What Israel ignited — and what the United States fuelled — is not a contained military operation. It is a regional conflagration. Iran has vowed retaliation. Global oil markets have convulsed. The diplomatic architecture that might have contained this — the JCPOA, the Abraham Accords’ momentum, the back-channel relationships that professional diplomats spent decades building — has been shattered.

One nation did not cause all of this. But one nation sits at the geographic and strategic centre of every escalation. Gaza. Lebanon. Iran. The thread runs through Jerusalem, and the Contribution Index must say so.

Adherence to international law: 1 out of 10.

Israeli military tanks and soldiers in conflict zone near Lebanon. Armored forces in a northern operational area, evoking the escalations into Lebanon and beyond, including the 2026 regional conflagration tied to Operation Epic Fury. Source: NBC News

The International Court of Justice has issued advisory opinions on the illegality of the occupation. The International Criminal Court has opened investigations. UN resolutions condemning Israeli actions have been passed repeatedly, blocked in the Security Council only by American vetoes. The 2026 strikes against Iran were conducted without UN Security Council authorization. The military campaign in Gaza has been the subject of genocide proceedings at the ICJ.

Israel’s position — that these international legal frameworks are biased, that the ICJ is politically motivated, that the ICC lacks jurisdiction — does not change the factual record of non-compliance. A nation that rejects the legitimacy of every international institution that rules against it is not engaging with international law. It is rejecting it.

The Neighbour Test: 7 out of 50

This is the lowest Neighbour Test score of any nation assessed in this series except Russia.

Multilateral orientation earns a 2. Israel participates in some international frameworks selectively but rejects the jurisdiction of every institution that rules against it — the ICJ, the ICC, the UN Human Rights Council.

Response to global crises earns a 1. In 2026, Israel is not responding to a global crisis. It is creating one. The cascading escalation from Gaza through Lebanon to Iran has produced the most dangerous military crisis the Middle East has experienced in half a century. A nation at the epicentre of an escalation that threatens global energy markets, regional stability, and tens of millions of civilian lives cannot score above the floor on crisis response.

Treatment of allies earns a 2. Israel’s relationship with its primary patron — the United States — is one of dependency rather than partnership. Its relationships with Arab neighbours who had begun normalizing through the Abraham Accords have been functionally suspended. Its strikes in Syria, Lebanon, and now Iran have been conducted without meaningful coordination with regional partners.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 1. A nation that calls itself the only democracy in the Middle East while maintaining a military occupation without political rights for the occupied population, that invokes self-defence while conducting operations that the ICJ is investigating for genocide, that champions innovation while its military destroys the infrastructure of two million people in Gaza — the gap between stated values and observed behaviour is not inconsistency. It is the deliberate weaponization of democratic language to provide cover for fundamentally undemocratic actions.

Long-term stewardship earns a 1. The strikes against Iran have not made Israel safer. They have made the region more dangerous, the diplomatic landscape more hostile, and the prospect of long-term peace more remote than at any point since 1973. A nation optimizing for short-term military dominance at the cost of long-term regional stability is making the opposite of a stewardship choice.

The Innovation Question — Israel Edition

Rows of crops thriving in arid land thanks to Israeli-developed drip irrigation technology, a prime example of innovation making deserts bloom and aiding global food security. Source: EurekAlert! (scientific news release)

The innovation spillover argument finds its sharpest and most painful application here.

Israel’s agricultural technology has genuinely improved food security across the developing world. Drip irrigation systems developed in Israeli labs water crops in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Israeli desalination technology provides drinking water to millions. Israeli medical research — particularly in oncology and neuroscience — has saved lives. Israeli cybersecurity innovations protect critical infrastructure globally.

These contributions are real. They matter. And they cannot offset what is happening in Gaza, in Lebanon, and now in Iran.

A nation that exports drip irrigation to Africa while conducting military operations that destroy water infrastructure for two million Palestinians in Gaza is not making a net positive contribution to global water security. A nation whose medical researchers develop treatments that save lives worldwide while its military operations produce mass civilian casualties is not making a net positive contribution to global health. These are not offsetting ledger entries. They are parallel choices, made simultaneously, by the same nation. The Contribution Index scores both. And when both are scored, the destruction overwhelms the innovation — not because the innovation is unreal, but because the destruction is on a scale that no amount of drip irrigation can remediate.

The Editorial Conclusion

Israel scores 86 out of 200. The eighth-ranked underperformer.

The innovation paradox is not, in the end, a paradox at all. It is two sets of choices producing two sets of outcomes simultaneously. Israel has chosen to invest in technology, education, and healthcare for its own citizens — and it has excelled. It has also chosen occupation, military escalation, and systematic non-compliance with international law — and it has excelled at those too. It is now choosing to participate in strikes against a nation of 88 million people, with consequences that will reverberate for decades.

The Contribution Index does not net the one against the other. It scores both. And when both are scored, a nation can be brilliant and catastrophically destructive at the same time.

Israel is proof.

The ancient rabbinical teaching holds that whoever saves a single life saves the entire world. Israeli innovation has saved lives. Israeli military action has ended them — on a scale, in 2026, that makes the innovation contribution feel less like a counterweight and more like a bitter footnote.
