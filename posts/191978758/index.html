The Buyout

Essay #21 1/2: Your Government Just Paid $928 Million to Kill Clean Energy

March 24, 2026

Under the terms, TotalEnergies would forfeit its leases in federal waters for two wind farms that would have been built off New York and North Carolina.Credit...Randi Baird for The New York Times

I published Essay #21 a week ago. It was called “The Price of Not Building,” and it documented how decades of EV hesitation and a deliberate policy unravelling left America paying an Iran tax at every gas station in the country. I thought that was the low point.

I was wrong. It can always get worse.

On March 23, 2026, the Trump administration announced that it will pay the French energy giant TotalEnergies $928 million to abandon its plans to build two wind farms off the East Coast. Nearly a billion dollars in American taxpayer money, handed to a foreign corporation, to not build American clean energy.

In exchange, TotalEnergies will invest that money in oil and gas projects in the United States — including a facility in Texas for exporting liquefied natural gas to global markets, and gas-burning power plants to meet rising electricity demand from data centres.

Let me say that again, slowly, because I want to make sure every reader of this series absorbs it.

The United States government is paying a French company to stop building wind power off New York and North Carolina. The French company will use that money to build gas plants for AI data centres and LNG export terminals. The taxpayer funds the transaction. The fossil fuel industry gets the investment. The wind farms that would have powered more than 1.3 million homes disappear. And Interior Secretary Doug Burgum stands at a Houston energy conference and declares: “The era of taxpayers subsidizing unreliable, unaffordable and unsecure energy is officially over.”

The era of taxpayers subsidizing fossil fuels, apparently, is just getting started.

The Deal

The two wind projects being killed are Attentive Energy, which would have been built 54 miles south of Jones Beach, New York — producing enough electricity for over a million homes and businesses in New York and New Jersey — and Carolina Long Bay, 22 miles south of Bald Head Island, North Carolina, which could have powered around 300,000 homes starting in the early 2030s.

TotalEnergies’ CEO, Patrick Pouyanné, called the agreement “pragmatic.” He said his company had concluded that offshore wind was “not the most affordable way to produce electricity” and would require federal subsidies that are now being phased out. To be clear, he added, TotalEnergies still invests in onshore wind and solar elsewhere. Just not offshore wind in the United States. Not anymore.

What Pouyanné didn’t say — but what every reader of this series knows — is that the reason offshore wind is “too expensive” in America is largely because this administration has spent fourteen months making it too expensive. They halted five projects under construction in December. They cited classified national security reports that federal judges found unpersuasive in every single case. They created regulatory uncertainty that drives up financing costs. And now they’re offering buyouts. First they poisoned the well. Now they’re paying to cap it.

The Pattern

For readers who have followed this series, the TotalEnergies deal is not an isolated event. It is the third stage of a pattern I’ve been documenting since Essay #19.

Stage one: Block it. In December 2025, the administration halted five East Coast offshore wind projects under construction, including the Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind project — 2.6 gigawatts of clean power heading to the largest data centre cluster on earth. Dominion Energy sued, calling the halt “arbitrary and capricious.” Federal courts agreed. Every legal challenge went against the administration. The CVOW delay alone cost $228 million, plus $137 million in tariff impacts — costs passed to Virginia ratepayers.

Stage two: Pledge to fix it. On March 4, the administration hauled tech CEOs to the White House to sign the Ratepayer Protection Pledge — a voluntary, unenforceable commitment for data centre companies to provide their own power. Experts called it theatre. The Harvard Law School Electricity Law Initiative called it “an unenforceable corporate ESG commitment intended to trick the public.” The White House has no jurisdiction over state-regulated electricity markets.

Stage three: Pay to kill it. On March 23, the administration paid $928 million to a French company to abandon wind projects that would have powered 1.3 million homes, redirecting the investment into gas plants and LNG exports.

Block it. Pledge to fix it. Pay to kill it.

That is not energy policy. That is demolition with extra steps.

And here is where the series needs to say something plainly that I have danced around for twenty-one essays. This is not ideology. It is not strategy. It is policy so internally contradictory that it can only be explained by spite — and the spite has a traceable origin. Donald Trump has been publicly hostile to wind power since 2012, when he tried and failed to stop an offshore wind project visible from his golf course in Aberdeenshire, Scotland. He sued. He lost. He has been angry about wind turbines ever since. Over the years, the grievance metastasised into a portfolio of absurdities: wind turbines kill birds, wind turbines drive whales crazy, wind turbines are ugly, wind turbines lower property values. None of these claims have survived contact with evidence. Federal judges reviewing his administration’s classified national security report on offshore wind were, in every case, unpersuaded.

The wind turbines were visible in the background when Trump visited the Menie course during his 2025 visit / Getty Images

But here we are. A personal aesthetic grudge born on a Scottish golf course in 2012 has become a billion-dollar federal policy in 2026. The leader of the free world is spending nearly a billion taxpayer dollars to kill wind farms because he once didn’t like the view from the 14th tee. The entire American offshore wind industry — and by extension, a significant chunk of the clean energy transition — is being held hostage by one man’s taste in coastal scenery.

I have tried, across twenty-one essays, to give this administration’s energy policy the benefit of analytical doubt. I am running out of doubt.

The Timing

The timing is almost comically bad. As I documented in Essay #21, the war in the Middle East has shocked global oil markets. Gas prices are spiking. Americans are paying an Iran tax at every pump in the country. Energy experts have argued that investments in renewable energy — including offshore wind — can help countries protect against exactly this kind of oil price volatility.

Seth Kaplan of Grid Strategies told the New York Times that the lesson from Europe’s experience after the Ukraine invasion was clear: “They really needed to look at their own native energy resources.” Wind blowing off the Atlantic coast is about as native as it gets.

Instead, the administration is paying to eliminate domestic energy and redirecting the investment into LNG export infrastructure — facilities that will ship American natural gas to global markets, making it less available domestically, not more. In the middle of an energy price crisis driven by Middle Eastern instability, the United States is paying to reduce its own energy independence.

From Canada, I keep waiting for the contradiction to become too obvious to sustain. It never does.

The Response

New York Governor Kathy Hochul called it “an outrageous abuse of taxpayer dollars” and a “pay-not-to-play scheme.” North Carolina Governor Josh Stein called it “ludicrous and wasteful,” noting that the administration is “spending $1 billion in taxpayer money to pay off a company to stop it from investing private dollars to create the clean energy we need.”

Those are Democratic governors, so the administration will dismiss them. But the math doesn’t care about party affiliation. The wind farms were private investment. The buyout is public money. The administration is replacing private clean energy investment with public fossil fuel subsidy. That is the opposite of the free market economics this administration claims to champion.

And here’s the detail that should haunt every fiscal conservative: the $928 million will likely come from the Justice Department’s Judgment Fund — a pot of money used to pay court settlements, funded by taxpayers, with no congressional appropriation required. Nearly a billion dollars, no vote, no debate, no oversight.

What This Means for the Series

I started The Great Energy Challenge to document the collision between AI’s insatiable energy demands and the world’s capacity to meet them through clean power technologies. Twenty-one essays in, the technological solutions are clearer than ever — solar, wind, nuclear, batteries, sodium-ion, space-based systems. The obstacle is not physics. It is not engineering. It is not economics.

The obstacle is a government that has moved from indifference to obstruction to active demolition of the clean energy infrastructure that the market, the military, the tech industry, and the grid all need.

Block it. Pledge to fix it. Pay to kill it.

The next time you hear someone say “all of the above” energy strategy, remember what “all of the above” looks like in practice: nearly a billion dollars to a French company to make sure the wind turbines never turn.

Those turbines would have generated electricity for decades. The gas plants they’re being replaced with will generate emissions for decades. And the taxpayers who funded the buyout will be paying higher electricity bills for decades, wondering why nobody built the wind farms that were right there, permitted and ready, before someone paid to make them disappear.
