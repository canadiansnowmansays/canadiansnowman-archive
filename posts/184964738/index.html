The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Essay #15: The Storage Solution: Why Battery Innovation Is the Missing Link in the Clean Energy Revolution

January 18, 2026

Introduction: The Half-Built Bridge

We’ve made extraordinary progress generating clean electricity—solar costs down 90% in a decade, renewables now supplying over 30% of global electricity. But we’ve built only half the bridge. The missing half? Energy storage.

Throughout this series, we’ve examined AI’s unprecedented energy demands, explored orbital solar solutions, and documented offshore wind destruction. But there’s a critical component we haven’t addressed: the battery revolution that must accompany any realistic clean energy future.

Without massive advances in energy storage, renewable energy won’t replace fossil fuels, AI data centers will keep running on natural gas backup, and even space-based solar struggles to compete economically. The battery problem isn’t just important—it’s existential.

Right now, a small Finnish start-up is making claims that sound too good to be true, promising the “holy grail” of battery technology. Whether Donut Lab delivers or disappoints, their announcement illuminates why battery innovation matters so profoundly for everything we’ve discussed in this series.

The Fundamental Problem: Intermittency

Solar panels produce electricity when the sun shines. Wind turbines generate power when the wind blows. But AI data centers need electricity 24/7 with near-perfect reliability. This mismatch between renewable supply and constant demand creates the “intermittency problem.”

Batteries could solve this—storing excess energy when generation exceeds demand, then releasing it when production drops. But the scale required is daunting. A single AI data center consuming 100-500 megawatts needs battery installations costing hundreds of millions just for four hours of backup. For 24-hour backup? The numbers become astronomical.

Grid-scale storage faces worse mathematics. California’s peak demand hits 50,000 megawatts. Storing enough energy to power the state through a windless night would require battery installations larger than anything currently existing—and that’s one state, one night.

More renewable energy requires exponentially more storage capacity. Current battery technology makes that storage prohibitively expensive at required scales. This is why battery innovation determines whether the entire renewable transition succeeds or fails.

The Evolution: From Lead-Acid to Lithium-Ion

The rechargeable battery is older than most realize. French physicist Gaston Planté invented lead-acid batteries in 1859—the same chemistry still starting cars today. Heavy, low energy density, toxic materials, but cheap and reliable. For over a century, the only option.

Nickel-metal hydride (NiMH) batteries, commercialized in the 1980s, powered early hybrids like the Prius but suffered from “memory effect”—partial discharge cycles reduced capacity.

Everything changed with lithium-ion. Commercialized by Sony in 1991 based on Nobel Prize-winning research, lithium-ion revolutionized electronics and enabled modern EVs through:

  • Energy density: Three times better than NiMH, five times better than lead-acid

  • No memory effect: Maintains capacity through thousands of partial charges

  • Low self-discharge: Retains energy for months

  • Scalability: Same chemistry works in smartphones or semi-trucks

These advantages explain lithium-ion’s dominance. But fundamental limitations constrain its future role.

Current Limitations: Why Lithium-Ion Isn’t Enough

Four critical constraints limit lithium-ion’s future:

Energy density ceiling: Today’s cells store about 250-300 watt-hours per kilogram—only 2% of gasoline’s energy density (though electric motors are far more efficient). We’re approaching theoretical limits; the chemistry can only store so much before you’re just adding weight.

Charging speed: Fast-charging generates heat and degrades electrodes, reducing lifespan. Current EVs charge to 80% in 20-30 minutes under ideal conditions—still far slower than pumping gas and a meaningful constraint on commercial operations.

Degradation: All lithium-ion batteries lose capacity over time. Typical EV batteries retain 70-80% capacity after 100,000 miles—sufficient for passenger vehicles but a significant economic liability when the battery is often the most expensive component.

Supply chain vulnerability: Lithium-ion requires lithium, cobalt, nickel, and graphite. Much cobalt comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo with serious ethical concerns. China controls most lithium refining and rare earth processing. These bottlenecks could constrain growth and create geopolitical dependencies.

There’s also fire risk. Lithium-ion stores immense energy chemically. Damage or thermal runaway can cause intense fires. While statistically rare, battery fires spark legitimate safety concerns.

These limitations aren’t fatal—lithium-ion will dominate for years. But they explain why researchers race to develop next-generation technologies. The most hyped contender? Solid-state batteries.

The Solid-State Promise: Why Everyone’s Chasing the Holy Grail

Conventional lithium-ion batteries have three components: cathode, anode, and liquid electrolyte allowing ions to flow between electrodes. That liquid is both essential and problematic—it enables ion movement but is flammable, can leak, requires heavy containment, and limits electrode packing density.

Solid-state batteries replace liquid electrolyte with solid material—ceramic, glass, or solid polymer—that still conducts ions but eliminates liquid disadvantages. This simple substitution theoretically unlocks transformative improvements:

  • Higher energy density: Solid electrolytes enable lithium metal anodes instead of graphite, potentially doubling energy per pound

  • Faster charging: Solid materials can handle much higher current densities, enabling minutes-not-hours charge times

  • Longer lifespan: Eliminating liquid electrolytes reduces degradation, potentially extending life to hundreds of thousands of cycles

  • Safety: Solid electrolytes aren’t flammable and resist thermal runaway

  • Temperature tolerance: Solid materials operate across wider temperature ranges than liquids

  • Simplified design: Potentially fewer components and less heavy containment

These advantages are so compelling that virtually every major automaker and battery manufacturer has solid-state programs. Toyota has invested billions (repeatedly pushing back deadlines). Nissan targets 2028. Samsung, Panasonic, and CATL all have major programs. Well-funded start-ups like QuantumScape (backed by Volkswagen) and Solid Power (backed by BMW and Ford) have raised billions.

So why hasn’t solid-state already replaced lithium-ion? The gap between theoretical advantages and practical manufacturing is vast.

Why It’s Been “5 Years Away” for 20 Years

Finding solid electrolyte materials that conduct ions efficiently, remain stable through thousands of cycles, don’t crack, can be manufactured at scale, and don’t cost a fortune has proven fiendishly difficult. Interface issues where solid meets electrodes, maintaining contact as materials expand and contract, create resistance and reduce performance.

Manufacturing complexity is severe. Liquid electrolytes are simple to work with. Solid electrolytes require entirely new production processes, equipment, and quality control—retooling costs billions and takes years. Defects or variations that don’t matter in small lab batches become critical at scale.

New materials, new processes, and lower volumes mean solid-state batteries cost significantly more than lithium-ion. Unless performance advantages justify the premium, markets won’t adopt the technology.

This explains the pattern: promising announcements, laboratory results, prototype demonstrations, then years of silence wrestling with manufacturing realities. Timelines slip. Cost targets prove unattainable. Competitors announce breakthroughs that also fail to materialize.

It’s become an industry joke. “Solid-state batteries are the future—and always will be.”

Tesla’s Battery Day: A Cautionary Tale

Before Donut Lab, consider what happened when the world’s most successful EV manufacturer promised revolutionary batteries.

September 2020: Tesla’s Battery Day unveiled the 4680 cell—a new format (46mm diameter, 80mm tall) with revolutionary “tabless” design eliminating connection tabs for better electron flow. The promises: 5x energy capacity, 6x power output, 16% range improvement, 56% cost reduction, path to $25,000 Tesla within three years, cobalt elimination, silicon anodes, and “dry electrode” coating for simpler manufacturing.

Battery experts called it “brilliant.” Sandy Munro calculated 4680s could nearly double energy storage in the same space. Professor Shirley Meng gave it “an A-plus.” Musk promised pilot production would hit 10 GWh within a year, eventually reaching 200+ GWh at full scale.

Then reality hit. The dry electrode process—essential for promised cost reductions—became a manufacturing nightmare. Traditional batteries use wet paste that must dry before assembly. Tesla’s dry process was supposed to eliminate this step but proved extraordinarily difficult at scale.

By July 2021, Musk was tempering expectations: “Our focus is now on improving the 10 percent of manufacturing processes that currently bottleneck production output.” That “10 percent” consumed years of engineering.

Production crawled. October 2023: 20 millionth cell produced—enough for maybe 2,000 Cybertrucks monthly, four years after Battery Day promised 12-18 months. By September 2024, production hit 500,000 cells daily—respectable but years late.

Worse: the cells reaching production didn’t match specifications. A 2022 teardown revealed “ordinary, but larger, NMC 811 cells”—no silicon anode, no cobalt reduction, no dry electrode initially. The larger format delivered worse energy density than smaller 2170s they were supposed to replace.

The Cybertruck launched with disappointing range and charging performance—cells held less energy, charged slower, generated more heat than promised.

November 2025, Tesla shareholder meeting: Musk admitted pursuing dry electrode process “was way harder than I thought” and may have been a mistake. Other manufacturers pursuing 46mm cells with conventional processes had already surpassed Tesla’s performance.

The lesson? If Tesla—with unlimited capital, world-class engineers, vertical integration, and Musk’s intensity—struggled five years to deliver on credible battery promises, what does that tell us about a 100-person Finnish start-up claiming they’ve leapfrogged everyone?

Tesla’s 4680 isn’t about failure—production is finally ramping. It’s about the enormous gap between theoretical improvements and practical manufacturing at scale, even for the most capable companies backed by experts.

Which brings us to Donut Lab.

The Donut Lab Gambit: Too Good to Be True?

January 4, 2026: Finnish start-up Donut Lab (fewer than 100 employees, spin-off from Verge Motorcycles) released a slick CES video claiming commercially viable, mass-producible solid-state batteries ready for immediate delivery.

The specifications sound like fantasy: Roughly twice lithium-ion energy density, zero-to-100% charge in five minutes, 100,000 charge cycles (versus ~1,000-2,000 for lithium-ion), full performance from -22°F to 212°F, no rare or geopolitically constrained elements, cheaper than lithium-ion, deliveries starting March 2026.

If even half these claims are accurate, Donut Lab solved problems stumping the world’s largest technology companies for two decades. CEO Marko Lehtimäki insists every specification is factual and third-party validation comes within weeks.

Scientists are skeptical that Donut Lab’s solid-state battery meets all the specifications the company has claimed. (Verge Motorcycles)

Scientists are deeply skeptical. Paul Braun (University of Illinois): “Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence... I need to see a lot more data.” Kelsey Hatzell (Princeton): the combination of properties “sounds impossible.”

Red flags: No published data, no peer-reviewed research, no detailed specifications beyond marketing. No major automotive or battery manufacturer partnerships. Just $60 million in funding—a rounding error compared to what established players invested. Claims of Finland production starting when major companies spent years and billions on pilot lines strains credulity.

Yet they’re taking $30,000 deposits for electric motorcycles claiming this battery technology delivers by March. Either they shock the world, or it’s one of the most spectacular failures in recent technology history.

We’ll know soon. Unlike vague future promises, Donut Lab’s March deadline is hard. Customers will receive motorcycles (or won’t). Independent reviewers will test batteries (or won’t have access). Claims will be validated or debunked in weeks, not years.

This makes Donut Lab less important as a company than as a test case: Can a small start-up with limited resources actually leapfrog global giants? Or is solid-state’s “5 years away” reputation still accurate?

Alternative Paths: Beyond the Holy Grail

While solid-state captures headlines, other approaches offer distinct advantages:

Sodium-ion batteries replace lithium with abundant, cheap, geographically dispersed sodium. Lower energy density makes them less suitable for vehicles but potentially excellent for grid storage. Chinese battery giant CATL is already producing them commercially.

Lithium-sulfur batteries promise higher energy density at lower cost (sulfur is cheap and abundant) but degrade rapidly as sulfur cathodes dissolve. Solving this could unlock 2-3x lithium-ion’s energy density.

Flow batteries store energy in external liquid tanks rather than within cells—scale energy storage independently of power output. Expensive and low energy density for vehicles, but potentially excellent for grid-scale storage with extremely long lifespans.

Beyond batteries, complementary technologies offer solutions:

Pumped hydroelectric storage (95% of grid-scale storage globally) pumps water uphill during excess generation, flows back through turbines when needed. Proven and reliable but requires specific geography and decade-long permitting.

Compressed air energy storage uses excess electricity to compress air in underground caverns, releases it to spin turbines. Requires specific geology but could provide massive capacity.

Green hydrogen uses excess renewable electricity to split water, stores hydrogen, then burns it in fuel cells when needed. Poor round-trip efficiency (30-40%) but stores indefinitely and solves seasonal storage challenges batteries can’t address.

The point: we likely need multiple solutions optimized for different use cases. Batteries for short-duration stability and vehicles. Pumped hydro for multi-hour storage. Green hydrogen for seasonal storage. Waiting for a single “holy grail” may be foolish when diverse, complementary solutions could meet different needs more effectively.

Scale and Economics: The Real Challenge

Even if breakthrough batteries arrive, we face a more fundamental challenge: the sheer scale required.

Current AI data centers consume perhaps 50-100 terawatt-hours annually—roughly 1-2% of global electricity. Projections suggest 500-1,000 terawatt-hours by 2030. Powering this with renewables requires massive storage. Conservative estimate: 4 hours of backup for AI data centers needs 200-400 gigawatt-hours of batteries by 2030. Global battery manufacturing capacity in 2024 was ~1,500 gigawatt-hours annually—but most goes to EVs, consumer electronics, and other grid storage.

Fully decarbonizing the entire U.S. electric grid likely requires 2,000-5,000 gigawatt-hours of storage, possibly much more for multi-day backup during prolonged cloudy, windless weather.

At current prices (~$120-150 per kilowatt-hour), building 3,000 gigawatt-hours of storage costs ~$400 billion—just for batteries, not installation, electronics, land, or grid connections. If solid-state costs twice as much initially, that doubles.

This is where storage intersects everything else in this series. Space-based solar offers continuous generation without intermittency. Terrestrial renewables plus storage require massive infrastructure adding significantly to costs. The economic comparison isn’t simple—orbital solar needs unprecedented launch capacity and in-space construction; terrestrial renewables need unprecedented mining and battery deployment.

Which proves more viable—or whether we need both at different scales—remains genuinely uncertain. But battery innovation directly determines that equation’s outcome.

Implications: What Battery Breakthroughs Mean

Donut Lab’s claims, regardless of validity, matter to the broader energy story.

If solid-state (or any next-generation technology) delivers dramatic improvements in density, charging, lifespan, and cost, terrestrial renewable energy strengthens considerably. Grid storage becomes more economical. EVs achieve true parity with internal combustion. The intermittency problem shrinks from existential to manageable.

Conversely, if battery technology continues incrementally improving but never achieves breakthrough gains, orbital solar and continuous-generation approaches become relatively more attractive. Space-based infrastructure investment might prove cheaper than building sufficient terrestrial storage to handle continental-scale intermittency.

Third scenario: battery breakthroughs enable revolutionary applications we haven’t imagined—electric aviation, seasonal energy storage capturing summer sun for winter, autonomous vehicles and mobile robotics reshaping logistics and labor.

For AI specifically, implications are profound. If data centers can rely on renewable energy plus massive battery installations for reliable 24/7 power, pressure to develop orbital solar diminishes. If batteries remain expensive and limited, continuous-generation alternatives become essential.

This uncertainty is why we can’t “wait and see.” We need multiple paths simultaneously—advancing battery science, developing terrestrial renewables at scale, exploring space-based alternatives, investing in diverse storage technologies—because we won’t know which approaches prove viable until we’ve actually deployed them at scale.

Betting everything on a single solution risks discovering ten years from now that our chosen approach doesn’t scale economically. Diversification isn’t hedging—it’s acknowledging honest uncertainty about which technologies succeed when confronted with brutal realities of physics, economics, and continental-scale implementation.

Conclusion: The Missing Link Remains Missing (For Now)

Energy storage isn’t a supporting player in the clean energy transition—it’s the co-star. Without massive advances in batteries or alternative storage, renewables remain constrained by intermittency, fossil fuels maintain baseload dominance, and AI’s energy demands keep driving natural gas consumption regardless of climate commitments.

The lithium-ion revolution enabled smartphones and electric vehicles, but we’re approaching that chemistry’s theoretical limits. The next revolution—solid-state, sodium-ion, or something we haven’t envisioned—needs to happen soon for serious decarbonization timelines.

Donut Lab’s announcement, skeptically received and lacking evidence, nevertheless illustrates how desperately the world needs battery breakthroughs. That a small Finnish start-up generates global headlines with unverified claims reflects both legitimate hunger for solutions and the battery industry’s credibility problem after decades of overpromising.

We’ll know within weeks whether Donut Lab achieved something extraordinary or added another vaporware chapter. Either way, the fundamental challenge remains: we need massive improvements in energy storage capacity, cost, and performance to make renewable energy work at AI’s demanded scales.

Throughout this series, we’ve examined orbital solar, offshore wind, nuclear renaissance, and energy infrastructure politics. But none work optimally without solving storage. You can generate unlimited clean energy, but without effective storage, you haven’t solved the reliability problem keeping fossil fuels economically competitive.

The bridge remains half-built. Battery innovation—real, verified, scalable innovation, not marketing hype—represents the construction materials needed to complete the crossing. Whether those materials arrive from Finland, Japan, California, or some unheard-of laboratory matters less than whether they arrive in time.

The AI energy crisis won’t wait for perfect solutions. The question is whether good-enough solutions arrive before we’ve locked in another fossil fuel infrastructure generation that will be economically and politically impossible to retire.

Battery technology holds part of the answer. But only part. And right now, that part remains frustratingly incomplete.
