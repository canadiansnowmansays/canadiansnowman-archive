License to Kill in Trump's Wonderland

In the James Bond films, “00” agents have license to kill in service of their country. In Trump’s America, ICE agents have learned they have the same license—except they’re killing American citizens.

January 24, 2026

Secretary Noem's ICE Wonderland: Where recording federal agents gets you killed

Let me show you two pictures. You tell me what’s wrong.

But first, let me warn you: What follows will feel like falling down a rabbit hole into a world that makes no sense. Like Alice tumbling into Wonderland, you’ll encounter a reality where logic inverts, where words mean their opposite, where the authorities are the threat and the helpers are the victims.

Except this isn’t Wonderland. This is Minneapolis. This is America. This is today.

In Lewis Carroll’s tale, Alice encounters a world of absurdist tyranny: a Queen who demands absolute obedience, a justice system where sentence comes before verdict, a reality where words mean whatever those in power say they mean.

In Trump’s America, we’ve fallen into that same nightmare. Federal agents kill American citizens and call it self-defense. A VA nurse caring for veterans is labeled a would-be cop killer. Video evidence is ignored in favor of official narrative. Critics of state violence are accused of insurrection. And the magic phrase “I feared for my life” grants absolute immunity to kill.

The difference is that Alice woke up. We’re still falling.

Picture One: The Nurse

Alex Jeffrey Pretti, 37 years old, wearing scrubs at the Minneapolis Veterans Affairs hospital. He’s an ICU nurse—the person you want by your bedside when you’re at your most vulnerable. He cares for America’s veterans, the men and women who served their country and now need someone to serve them.

His colleagues describe him as capable, competent, friendly. “The kind of person who cared deeply about his work and his patients,” says Dr. Dimitri Drekonja, who worked with Alex for years. “He was a really great colleague and a really great friend. The default look on his face was a smile.”

Alex started at the VA as a research assistant in his early 20s, delivering pizzas at night to make ends meet while pursuing his dream of helping people. He spent years working his way up, enrolling patients in medical studies, learning the profession, earning his nursing degree. By Saturday morning, he’d achieved his goal: ICU nurse, caring for veterans in their most critical moments.

He was working extra shifts, saving for a home and a new car. “He was happy, and I was happy for him because his life was just starting,” remembers Dr. Aasma Shaukat, who hired him to his first job at the hospital.

In his free time, Alex loved mountain biking on Minneapolis trails. He lived with his dog, Joule. His next-door neighbor Jeanne Wiener talked with him over the fence several times a week: “He’s the sweetest, kindest, most unoffensive, most nonviolent person you’d ever want to meet.”

Alex had no criminal record. He was a legal gun owner with a permit to carry, as allowed by Minnesota law. He followed the news closely and cared deeply about social justice and fighting for fairness. “I’m not surprised he was out there protesting and observing,” says his colleague Ruth Anway.

Two weeks before his death, Alex’s father warned him: “Go ahead and protest, but do not engage, do not do anything stupid.” Alex assured his father he understood.

This is the picture of a man who dedicated his life to caring for others.

Saturday morning, January 24, 2026, 9:05 a.m., 26th Street and Nicollet Avenue, Minneapolis. Temperature: 10 degrees below zero.

Alex Jeffrey Pretti, ICU nurse who cared for America’s veterans, is dead on a Minneapolis sidewalk.

This is the picture of a man killed by federal agents while exercising his First Amendment rights.
