The Enemies List

Zero Evidence

Part 18: The courts keep saying the same thing. The White House keeps not listening.

March 14, 2026

U.S. Attorney for D.C. Jeanine Pirro and Federal Reserve Chair Jerome H. Powell. (Eric Lee and Valerie Plesch for The Post)

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 14, 2026

There is a sentence in a federal court ruling unsealed yesterday that deserves to be read slowly, and then read again.

“The government has offered no evidence whatsoever that Powell committed any crime other than displeasing the president.”

Chief Judge James Boasberg of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia wrote those words. He is a federal judge. He was appointed by Barack Obama. He has now become one of the most consequential figures in American jurisprudence — not because he sought the role, but because the Trump administration keeps handing it to him.

Yesterday, Boasberg quashed the Justice Department’s grand jury subpoenas against Jerome Powell, Chair of the Federal Reserve. He ruled that prosecutors had produced “essentially zero evidence” of a crime, that their justifications were “so thin and unsubstantiated” as to be pretextual, and that a “mountain of evidence” pointed to the real purpose: harassing Powell until he either slashed interest rates or resigned and made way for someone who would.

Seventeen parts into this series, I’ve documented a through-line that the Trump administration seems genuinely incapable of learning from: the courts keep saying no. Grand juries keep saying no. Judges keep saying no. And each time, the response from the White House is to attack the judge, claim victimhood, and try again.

Friday was no different. U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro — Trump’s handpicked loyalist running the D.C. federal prosecutor’s office — held a hastily called news conference up the block from the courthouse and declared that Powell was now “bathed in immunity,” that Boasberg had “neutered the grand jury’s ability to investigate crime,” and that this was “the antithesis of American justice.”

She plans to appeal. She also plans to file a motion for reconsideration.

In other words: same play, different defendant.

How We Got Here

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell with his signature nonpartisan purple tie, speaks at the Economic Club of Washington, Tuesday, Feb. 7, 2023, at the Renaissance Hotel in Washington. (AP Photo/Jess Rapfogel) · ASSOCIATED PRESS

Jerome Powell has been on the enemies list longer than most people who appear in this series. His offence was institutional rather than personal: as chair of the Federal Reserve, he declined to do what Trump wanted. Specifically, he declined to dramatically slash interest rates on command.

Trump has attacked Powell more than 100 times in public statements. He has called him “TOO LATE,” “TOO ANGRY,” “TOO STUPID,” and “TOO POLITICAL.” He has demanded his firing. He has called for his resignation. He has publicly mused about replacing him.

None of it worked. Powell, whose term runs until May 15, holds a position that Congress specifically designed to be insulated from presidential pressure, precisely because monetary policy corrupted by politics tends to produce catastrophic results.

So the White House found another tool.

Late last year, Pirro’s office served two subpoenas to the Federal Reserve’s Board of Governors. The ostensible target was a $2.5 billion renovation of the Fed’s Washington headquarters — a project running approximately $600 million over its 2022 estimate. Prosecutors wanted records about the renovations and transcripts of Powell’s congressional testimony, in which he briefly addressed the project’s cost overruns.

The theory, such as it was, involved false statements to Congress and fraud. Powell, they suggested, had misled lawmakers about the nature and scope of the project.

Powell didn’t take it quietly. In January, he released an extraordinary video message — unprecedented for a sitting Fed chair — in which he made the subtext explicit: “This is about whether the Fed will be able to continue to set interest rates based on evidence and economic conditions — or whether instead monetary policy will be directed by political pressure or intimidation.”

That statement earned him something in Trump’s Washington. It earned him enemies on Capitol Hill — and, crucially, allies.

The Judge Who Keeps Saying No

Chief Judge James Boasberg of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia, who quashed the subpoenas against Jerome Powell, stating the government offered “no evidence whatsoever” of a crime beyond “displeasing the president.” / Newsweek

If you’ve been following this series, Boasberg is not a new name.

He blocked the administration’s attempt to mass-deport alleged gang members under an 18th-century wartime statute, the Alien Enemies Act. That ruling sent the White House into a fury. Trump called him a “troublemaker and agitator.” Republicans in Congress called for his impeachment. The Justice Department filed a misconduct complaint against him — which was dismissed. Chief Justice John Roberts, in a rare direct rebuke of the president, rejected the calls for judicial impeachment.

None of that deterred Boasberg from doing his job yesterday.

His 27-page ruling, dated Wednesday and unsealed Friday, is a methodical, at times scathing, accounting of what the evidence actually showed — and didn’t. He began, as he often does, with the factual record. He quoted nearly 100 public statements by Trump and his deputies attacking Powell. He noted that Bill Pulte, the director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency — a man with no law enforcement role whatsoever — posted to social media asking Congress to investigate Powell over “his political bias” and the building renovations, hinting it might be a way to remove him.

Then Pirro’s office “promptly complied.”

Boasberg drew a direct line: the president spent years asking, in essence, if someone would rid him of this troublesome Fed chair. He suggested a specific line of investigation. That suggestion came from a political appointee with no prosecutorial authority. The U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia then opened a criminal inquiry.

“Those facts strongly imply that this investigation was launched for an improper purpose, as were the resulting subpoenas,” Boasberg concluded.

Then came the line that should be placed in a museum case next to the other artifacts of this era: “The Government might as well investigate him for mail fraud because someone once saw him send a letter.”

He wasn’t finished. Boasberg had offered prosecutors a lifeline — an opportunity to submit additional evidence against Powell directly to the judge, privately, so they wouldn’t have to tip their hand to the Fed or to Powell’s legal team. If there was something real here, something beyond the renovation cost overruns and the testimony, they could show it to him alone.

They declined.

“The Court is thus left with no credible reason to think that the Government is investigating suspicious facts as opposed to targeting a disfavored official,” Boasberg wrote.

Pirro’s Press Conference

Jeanine Pirro, the U.S. attorney for the District of Columbia, speaks during a news conference on Aug. 26 in Washington, D.C. Andrew Harnik/Getty Images

I want to spend a moment on Jeanine Pirro’s news conference, because it tells you something about the culture of the office she runs.

She called it hastily, up the block from the courthouse. She came out swinging. She accused Boasberg of harboring “animus” toward the president. She called him an “activist judge.” She said his ruling was “untethered to the law.” She claimed the grand jury’s ability to investigate crime had been “neutered.”

She described Powell publishing a “woe-is-me video” and calling “friends” in Washington and around the world to “gin up support for himself.”

Then, asked whether politics had influenced her decision to open the investigation, she said: “Politics is not the lane I’m in right now. We are focused on the law.”

This, from the woman who launched a criminal probe into the Fed chair based on a social media post from the director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency, a man who has no jurisdiction over the Federal Reserve, no law enforcement authority, and whose public motivation — reducing interest rates — was identical to the president’s.

“Politics is not the lane I’m in right now.”

The press conference was, in miniature, the entire Trump legal strategy: assert the investigation is legitimate, attack the judge who found it wasn’t, announce an appeal, and declare yourself the victim of an activist judiciary.

It has not been working.

The Republican Problem

One of the things that distinguishes the Powell case from earlier entries in this series is the degree to which it has fractured the president’s own coalition.

Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina sits on the Senate Banking Committee, which oversees the Federal Reserve. In January, after Powell revealed the subpoenas, Tillis announced that he would block any Federal Reserve nominee — including Kevin Warsh, Trump’s handpicked choice to replace Powell when his term expires in May — until the criminal investigation was dropped.

Yesterday, after the ruling, Tillis was blunt: the decision “confirms just how weak and frivolous the criminal investigation of Chairman Powell is and it is nothing more than a failed attack on Fed independence.” He called on Pirro’s office to “save itself further embarrassment and move on.”

And then the threat, directed not at Powell or Boasberg but at his own president’s team: if DOJ appeals, it will delay Warsh’s confirmation. Powell’s term ends May 15. The clock is ticking. The appeal Pirro announced today will consume weeks the White House does not have.

The Iran war has made this worse. With American forces engaged at the Strait of Hormuz and energy costs climbing, the Federal Reserve’s cautious approach to rate cuts is backed by the very economic logic Trump claims to champion. Slashing rates into an inflationary war economy would be precisely the kind of reckless political intervention that the Fed’s independence was designed to prevent.

Trump wanted a compliant Fed chair. Instead, he has a stalled confirmation, a bipartisan coalition defending Fed independence, a judge who has twice now rejected his legal strategy, and an approval to appeal that will accomplish nothing except delaying the outcome he was trying to achieve.

The Pattern, Complete

Let me restate what this series has now documented across 18 parts.

James Comey: Indicted. Case dismissed after the judge ruled the prosecutor was illegally appointed.

Letitia James: Indicted. Two separate grand juries in two weeks refused to indict. Cases dismissed.

John Bolton: Indicted on 18 counts. Trial pending.

Mark Kelly: Censured in the Senate. A federal judge blocked the censure on First Amendment grounds.

Jerome Powell (Part 14): Criminal investigation opened via Jeanine Pirro’s office.

Ilhan Omar: Investigation opened by DOJ and Congress. Physically attacked 24 hours later.

Don Lemon and Georgia Fort: Arrested despite a magistrate judge and a federal appeals court both rejecting probable cause.

The Six Lawmakers: Grand jury returned zero votes on all six counts after DOJ tried to criminalize a 90-second video reminding troops to refuse illegal orders.

Jerome Powell (Part 18, today): Subpoenas quashed. “Zero evidence of a crime other than displeasing the president.”

The through-line is not complicated. Every time this administration has tried to use the criminal justice system as a political weapon — to punish critics, silence opponents, or coerce independent institutions — the courts, the grand juries, or both have rejected it. Not every time. Bolton is still facing trial. Lemon was arrested after two courts said no, which remains the most alarming development in this entire series. But the pattern of judicial rejection is now so consistent that it constitutes something close to a verdict — not on any individual case, but on the strategy itself.

Boasberg’s ruling this week is particularly important because of what it documents. This wasn’t a case where prosecutors overreached on the charges. This was a case where prosecutors had, in the judge’s assessment, no legitimate investigative purpose at all. The subpoenas were not a clumsy prosecution. They were a pressure campaign dressed up as a prosecution, and Boasberg said so plainly.

The White House keeps testing whether the judiciary will eventually break. It hasn’t. The grand juries keep testing whether ordinary citizens will rubber-stamp political prosecutions. They won’t. And today, a federal judge looked at a criminal investigation into one of the country’s most powerful financial institutions and found, after examining all the evidence, exactly nothing.

What Comes Next

Pirro will appeal. The appeal will likely fail, for the same reason the underlying case failed: there is no evidence. Boasberg offered to look at whatever they had in private. They passed.

Powell will serve out his term, which ends in May. Warsh’s confirmation, already delayed by the investigation, will now be delayed further by the appeal Pirro has promised. Senator Tillis has made clear he considers that a self-inflicted wound.

And Boasberg will continue to be attacked by the people whose cases he keeps ruling against, which is to say: he will continue doing his job.

There is something almost clarifying about today’s ruling. After 17 parts of documented escalation — from indictments to arrests to criminalizing speech — the Powell case ends not with a dramatic constitutional confrontation but with a judge writing, in plain English, what everyone already knew.

The government offered no evidence. The justifications were pretextual. The purpose was harassment.

And the court found that it did not.
