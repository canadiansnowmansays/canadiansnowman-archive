The Democratic Promise

The Builders

Part 2: Berners-Lee, Wikipedia, and the Open-Source Movement

March 16, 2026

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon - March 2026

Tim Berners-Lee at CERN — inventor of the World Wide Web, who gave it away to keep it universal and open. Source: CNN

The Decision That Changed Everything

In 1989, six years after Steve Jobs described a world in which computers would be hooked together and people would form communities around shared interests until “there were more lists than people,” a thirty-four-year-old British physicist named Tim Berners-Lee sat in an office at CERN, the European particle physics laboratory in Geneva, and wrote a proposal titled “Information Management: A Proposal.”

His supervisor’s handwritten note on the cover page read: “Vague, but exciting.”

The proposal described a system for linking documents across a network of computers using something Berners-Lee called “hypertext” — text that contained embedded links to other text, stored on other computers, anywhere in the world. Click a link, and you would be transported to a different document on a different machine in a different country, seamlessly, instantly, for free.

He built it. By 1991, the World Wide Web was operational. And then Tim Berners-Lee made a decision that most people have never heard of, but that shaped the modern world more profoundly than almost any business decision in history.

He gave it away.

Berners-Lee did not patent the Web. He did not license it. He did not start a company to monetize it. He convinced CERN to release the underlying code into the public domain, free for anyone to use, modify, or build upon, with no royalties, no restrictions, and no strings attached.

Consider what this meant. By the mid-1990s, the World Wide Web was generating trillions of dollars in economic activity. Every website, every e-commerce transaction, every search engine query, every social media post, every streaming service — all of it runs on the infrastructure that Berners-Lee invented and chose not to own. If he had patented the Web and collected even a fraction of a cent per transaction, he would have become the wealthiest human being who ever lived, by orders of magnitude.

He chose not to. Deliberately. On principle.

“The decision to make the Web an open system was necessary for it to be universal,” Berners-Lee explained. “You can’t propose that something be a universal space and at the same time keep control of it.”

That sentence is the purest expression of Steve Jobs’s democratic promise ever articulated by someone in a position to betray it. Berners-Lee had the power to concentrate. He chose to distribute. He had the opportunity to become an oligarch. He chose to become a public servant.

Berners-Lee in the lab where he chose not to patent hypertext, ensuring no one could own the internet. Source: The Guardian

The Philosophy of Open

Berners-Lee was not operating in a vacuum. His decision emerged from a tradition — a community of thinkers and builders who believed that the most powerful technologies should be shared rather than owned. This tradition predates the personal computer, but it found its fullest expression in the decades following Jobs’s Aspen speech.

The open-source software movement began in earnest in 1983 — the same year Jobs gave his speech — when Richard Stallman, a programmer at MIT’s Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, launched the GNU Project with a radical proposition: software should be free. Not free as in “no cost,” but free as in “freedom.” Users should have the right to run software, study how it works, modify it, and share it with others. Stallman called this “free software” and founded the Free Software Foundation to promote it.

Stallman was a purist, and his purism sometimes made him difficult. But the core insight was profound: if software is the medium through which modern life is conducted — and Jobs had argued in Aspen that it would be — then controlling software is controlling people. Open software is a prerequisite for open society.

Linus Torvalds in 2018 Photograph by Kimmo Mäntylä / REX / Shutterstock

In 1991, a twenty-one-year-old Finnish student named Linus Torvalds posted a message to an internet newsgroup: “I’m doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won’t be big and professional).” The operating system was Linux. It became the foundation of the modern internet. Today, Linux runs the majority of the world’s servers, powers every Android phone, operates inside most of the world’s supercomputers, and underlies the infrastructure of virtually every major technology company on Earth — including the ones that would later betray the democratic promise.

Linux was built by thousands of volunteers, coordinated through the internet, contributing their labor for free. No corporation directed it. No executive profited from it. It was — and remains — a collectively owned piece of infrastructure so fundamental to modern civilization that it is almost invisible, like plumbing or the electrical grid.

The open-source movement produced Apache (the web server software that powered the early internet), Mozilla Firefox (the browser that broke Microsoft’s monopoly), Python (the programming language that underlies modern AI), and hundreds of thousands of other tools that form the invisible scaffolding of the digital world. All free. All open. All built on the principle that the tools of the digital age should be collectively owned.

This was Jobs’s fractional horsepower computing vision made real at the infrastructure level. Not just distributing the hardware, but distributing the software, the protocols, the standards — the intelligence itself.

The Encyclopedia Anyone Could Edit

Jimmy Wales: the builder who proved distributed knowledge could rival traditional encyclopedias — without enclosure. Source: Big Think

In January 2001, Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger launched Wikipedia with an idea so audacious it was widely mocked: an encyclopedia that anyone could edit.

The experts said it would never work. How could you trust content written by anonymous volunteers? How would you prevent vandalism, propaganda, misinformation? The Encyclopaedia Britannica had taken 244 years and thousands of credentialed experts to build its authority. Wikipedia proposed to replace it with... the internet.

Twenty-five years later, Wikipedia is the largest reference work in human history. It contains more than sixty million articles in over three hundred languages. It is the fifth most visited website on Earth. It has effectively replaced every traditional encyclopedia ever published. And it has done all of this without advertising, without a paywall, without selling user data, and without making its founder a billionaire.

Wales could have monetized Wikipedia at any point. The traffic alone — billions of page views per month — would have generated enormous advertising revenue. Every business advisor he consulted told him he was leaving money on the table. He refused. Wikipedia operates as a nonprofit, funded by donations, governed by its community of volunteer editors.

The result is imperfect. Wikipedia has biases. Its coverage is uneven. Its editing community can be insular and sometimes hostile to newcomers. Articles on controversial topics are perpetual battlegrounds. These are real problems.

But consider what Wikipedia represents in the context of this series: a tool of knowledge that is free, open, collectively governed, available to anyone with an internet connection, in hundreds of languages, without surveillance, without manipulation, without algorithmic distortion. It is the closest thing the internet has produced to Jobs’s vision of distributed intelligence — the citizen sitting at home, filtering the world’s knowledge, empowered to learn anything and contribute to the collective understanding.

Wikipedia is what the internet was supposed to be.

That it stands almost alone in this regard is the measure of how thoroughly the democratic promise was betrayed elsewhere.

The Cathedral and the Bazaar

In 1997, an essay by Eric S. Raymond called “The Cathedral and the Bazaar” crystallized the philosophical divide that would define the next three decades of technology.

Raymond described two models of software development. The “cathedral” model: a small group of experts working in isolation, releasing a polished product to passive consumers. The “bazaar” model: a large, decentralized community of contributors working in the open, iterating rapidly, with anyone free to participate.

The cathedral was Microsoft. The bazaar was Linux. The cathedral was proprietary. The bazaar was open. The cathedral concentrated control. The bazaar distributed it.

Raymond argued that the bazaar model produced better software — “given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow” — but the deeper argument was political. The cathedral model reproduced the power structures of industrial capitalism: a small elite controlling the means of production, distributing finished products to passive consumers. The bazaar model was democratic: distributed participation, collective ownership, transparent process.

For a brief, shining period in the late 1990s and early 2000s, it looked like the bazaar was winning. The open internet, powered by open-source software, governed by open standards, accessible to anyone. Blogs democratized publishing. Forums democratized discussion. Wikipedia democratized knowledge. The whole thing ran on Linux, Apache, and protocols that nobody owned.

Jobs’s democratic promise appeared to be fulfilling itself.

And then something happened.

The Enclosure

In the history of England, there is a period known as the Enclosure Movement, spanning roughly from the fifteenth to the nineteenth century, during which common lands — fields and forests that had been shared by communities for centuries — were systematically fenced off and converted to private property. Peasants who had grazed their animals and grown their food on common land were expelled. The shared resource became the private asset. The many lost access so the few could profit.

What happened to the open internet between approximately 2004 and 2014 was a digital enclosure.

The common land of the early web — the open protocols, the decentralized architecture, the democratic culture — was systematically enclosed by a small number of companies that built proprietary platforms on top of the open infrastructure. Facebook enclosed social communication. Google enclosed search and advertising. Amazon enclosed commerce. Apple enclosed the app ecosystem. Twitter enclosed public discourse.

Each of these platforms was, in its early days, positioned as a tool of democratic empowerment. Facebook would connect the world. Google would organize the world’s information and make it universally accessible. Twitter would give everyone a voice. The rhetoric was pure Jobs — distribute, empower, democratize.

But the business model was the opposite of Jobs’s vision. The open web had no central point of control. The platforms created central points of control. The open web let users move freely between sites. The platforms created walled gardens designed to keep users inside. The open web distributed data across millions of independent servers. The platforms concentrated data in proprietary databases controlled by a handful of companies.

The builders — Berners-Lee, Torvalds, Wales, Stallman, and thousands of anonymous contributors — had created the commons. The platforms enclosed it.

And the builders watched it happen.

What the Builders Tried to Do About It

Tim Berners-Lee, to his credit, has spent the last two decades trying to reverse the enclosure. In 2009, he founded the World Wide Web Foundation to fight for an open internet. In 2018, he launched a project called Solid, designed to give users control over their own data by storing it in personal “pods” rather than corporate databases. In 2022, he published a “Contract for the Web” calling on governments, companies, and citizens to protect the open internet.

None of it worked. Not because the ideas were wrong, but because the economic incentives were overwhelming. The platforms had discovered that controlling user data was the most profitable business model in human history. Giving that control back to users was asking companies to voluntarily dismantle the source of their wealth and power.

Berners-Lee’s frustration has been palpable. In a 2018 interview, he said: “The web was designed to be decentralized so that everybody could participate by having their own domain and having their own web server and this has been very much taken away from ordinary people.”

Read that sentence alongside Jobs’s 1983 warning: “The first thing we have got to do is give tools to people to help them do that. Because right now those tools are centralized.”

Forty years apart, two of the most important figures in the history of computing identified exactly the same problem. Jobs warned about it before it happened. Berners-Lee watched it happen despite having built the infrastructure to prevent it.

The Lesson of the Builders

The builders proved that Jobs’s democratic vision was technically achievable. The open internet, open-source software, Wikipedia — these are not hypotheticals. They exist. They work. They demonstrate that technology can distribute power, can empower individuals, can create collectively governed institutions that serve the public interest.

The builders also proved something more uncomfortable: that the democratic vision is not self-sustaining. It requires active defense. The open commons does not protect itself. Left undefended, it will be enclosed by those with the resources and the incentive to enclose it.

Berners-Lee gave away the Web. Torvalds gave away Linux. Wales gave away Wikipedia. Stallman gave away GNU. They chose distribution over concentration, public good over private wealth, the democratic promise over the oligarchic temptation.

But choosing to distribute is not enough if others choose to enclose. The builders gave us the commons. They could not prevent the enclosure. That required a different kind of power — political power, regulatory power, collective power — that the technology community has never been willing or able to exercise.

The builders kept faith with Jobs’s vision. They proved it could work. And they watched, helpless, as it was systematically dismantled by people who claimed to share that vision but never meant a word of it.

The story of those people begins in the next essay.
