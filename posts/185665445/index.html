ICE Kills Alex Jeffrey Pretti, VA Nurse Who Cared for Veterans

White House and DHS Repeat the Same Lies

January 24, 2026

Seventeen days after killing Renee Nicole Good, they did it again.

Saturday morning, January 24, at 9:05 a.m., in 10-degree-below-zero cold, Border Patrol agents shot and killed a 37-year-old Minneapolis man on the sidewalk at 26th Street and Nicollet Avenue. His name was Alex Jeffrey Pretti. He was an ICU nurse at the Veterans Affairs hospital, caring for the veterans who served this country. He was an American citizen. A legal gun owner with a permit to carry. A Minneapolis resident with no criminal record.

Within minutes, the Department of Homeland Security deployed the same lies that worked with Renee Good. The same narrative. The same blame-the-victim framing. The same refusal to provide information to local authorities. The same mockery of protesters. The same tear gas and pepper spray—violating a federal judge’s order from two days earlier.

And they’re getting away with it again.

This is no longer about one tragic shooting. This is about a systematic pattern of killing American citizens, lying about the circumstances, and facing zero consequences. This is about federal agents who have learned they can kill with impunity as long as they stick to the script.

Renee Good wasn’t an aberration. She was the beginning.

What DHS Claims Happened

Border Patrol Commander at Large Gregory Bovino

The Department of Homeland Security issued its official narrative within hours, delivered by spokesperson Tricia McLaughlin—the same official who lied about Renee Good, who lied about chemical weapons on Sunday morning, who lies reflexively because lying works:

“At 9:05 AM CT, as DHS law enforcement officers were conducting a targeted operation in Minneapolis against an illegal alien wanted for violent assault, an individual approached US Border Patrol officers with a 9 mm semi-automatic handgun. The officers attempted to disarm the suspect but the armed suspect violently resisted. More details on the armed struggle are forthcoming. Fearing for his life and the lives and safety of fellow officers, an agent fired defensive shots. Medics on scene immediately delivered medical aid to the subject but was pronounced dead at the scene.”

DHS elaborated: “The suspect also had 2 magazines and no ID—this looks like a situation where an individual wanted to do maximum damage and massacre law enforcement.“

Border Patrol Commander Gregory Bovino—the same official who led the Target store raids, who oversees these operations—held his own press conference. He claimed the man “approached” agents with a handgun, that officers tried to disarm him, that the man “violently resisted,” and that fearing for his life, an agent fired “defensive shots.”

Bovino added that federal law enforcement has been “under attack” in Minneapolis. He characterized the shooting as necessary self-defense against an armed threat.

President Trump posted a photo of the gun on Truth Social, captioned “ready to go,” suggesting premeditated violence against law enforcement. He called the victim’s weapon proof of intent to “massacre” federal agents.

Every element of this narrative is designed to justify the killing: Armed man approaches officers. Violent resistance. Fear for life. Defensive shots. Case closed.

There’s just one problem: The video shows something completely different.

Several agents wrestled the man onto the sidewalk. One officer struck him with an object. Then ten shots were fired within five seconds.

Not “defensive shots” after an armed approach. Ten shots in five seconds while the man was on the ground surrounded by agents.

TIME magazine’s description: “Video of the shooting shows Border Patrol officers pepper-spraying the victim as he attempts to assist a woman who had been pushed to the ground by an agent. A group of around seven agents then takes him to the ground as he is disoriented. A shot rings out, then several more in quick succession as he lies motionless on the floor.”

“Bring Me The News” reported what video actually shows before the shooting: “The man approached agents with his phone out, appearing to record their actions.”

Not a gun. A phone. Recording federal agents—exercising his First Amendment rights.

Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey watched the video and described what he saw: “I just saw a video of more than six masked agents pummeling one of our constituents and shooting him to death.“

Minneapolis Police Chief Brian O’Hara, when asked about the discrepancy between DHS claims and video evidence, said simply: “The video speaks for itself.“

The video shows:

  1. Man approaches with phone to record (not gun)

  2. Seven agents wrestle him to ground

  3. Agent pepper-sprays him while he’s disoriented

  4. Agent strikes him with object

  5. Ten shots fired in five seconds

  6. Man lies motionless

  7. Agents continue firing

This isn’t self-defense. This is an execution.

Who They Killed: Alex Jeffrey Pretti

Seventeen days after killing Renee Good, Border Patrol agents shot an ICU nurse caring for America's veterans. He approached them with his phone. They fired 10 shots in 5 seconds

His name is Alex Jeffrey Pretti.

He was 37 years old. A registered nurse who worked in the intensive care unit at the Minneapolis Veterans Affairs hospital, caring for veterans in their most vulnerable moments. He had no criminal record. He was a lawful gun owner with a permit to carry, as required by Minnesota state law.

Alex graduated from high school in Green Bay, Wisconsin, in 2006, making the honor roll. He started working at the Minneapolis VA hospital in his early 20s as a research assistant—delivering pizzas at night to make ends meet while pursuing his dream of helping people.

Dr. Aasma Shaukat, who hired him to his first job in the hospital’s research department, remembered: “He was your typical struggling young person with a lot of ambition, but no direction yet. But he knew he wanted to help people in some way or another.”

Alex spent years working on medical studies and enrolling patients. Ruth Anway, a nurse colleague who encouraged him to pursue nursing, said: “He really thrived in that environment. He wanted to be helpful, to help humanity and have a career that was a force of good in the world.”

By Saturday morning, Alex was an ICU nurse—the job he’d worked so hard to achieve. Dr. Dimitri Drekonja worked with him for years: “He was capable, competent and friendly, the kind of person who cared deeply about his work and his patients. He was a really great colleague and a really great friend. The default look on his face was a smile.”

The last time Dr. Shaukat spoke with Alex, he’d been working extra shifts as a nurse, saving up to buy a home and a new car. “He was happy, and I was happy for him because his life was just starting. This feels so senseless and just so wrong.”

In his free time, Alex loved mountain biking on trails around Minneapolis. He lived in an apartment with his dog, Joule. His next-door neighbor, Jeanne Wiener, talked with him over the fence several times a week: “He’s the sweetest, kindest, most unoffensive, most nonviolent person you’d ever want to meet.”

Alex followed the news closely and cared deeply about social justice and fighting for fairness. Ruth Anway said: “I’m not surprised he was out there protesting and observing.”

Two weeks before his death, Alex’s father—Michael Pretti, now living in Colorado—warned him to be careful in Minneapolis. “Go ahead and protest, but do not engage, do not do anything stupid, basically,” Michael told him. Alex assured his father he understood. He knew to be careful.

On Saturday morning, January 24, Alex Jeffrey Pretti was observing federal agents conducting an operation in his neighborhood. He approached them with his phone to record their actions—exercising his First Amendment right to observe law enforcement.

Seven Border Patrol agents wrestled him to the ground. They pepper-sprayed him while he was disoriented. They struck him with an object. They fired ten shots in five seconds while he lay on the sidewalk.

Alex Jeffrey Pretti, who spent his career caring for American veterans, was killed by federal agents—many of them veterans themselves.

Federal agents killed him on a Saturday morning in 10-below-zero weather, then lied about how he died, and are now being protected by an administration that refuses any accountability.

Alex is the second American citizen killed by federal agents in Minneapolis in 17 days. He will not be the last.

The Timing: One Day After 50,000 Marched

Saturday’s killing came exactly one day after the largest protest in Minneapolis since George Floyd’s murder.

On Friday, January 23, an estimated 50,000 people marched through Minneapolis in subzero temperatures. The “ICE Out of Minnesota: Day of Truth and Freedom” demonstration, organized by community leaders, clergy, and labor unions, called for a “no work, no school, no shopping” economic blackout.

Thousands of businesses closed. Workers stayed home. Schools shut down. Families marched together with children despite temperatures hovering near zero. The protest started near U.S. Bank Stadium and ended with a rally at Target Center.

The message was clear: Get federal agents out of Minnesota. Stop the killings. End the occupation.

Saturday morning at 9:05 a.m.—less than 24 hours after the march ended—Border Patrol agents killed another Minneapolis resident.

The timing sends its own message: Your protests don’t matter. Your marches change nothing. We will kill who we want, when we want, and there’s nothing you can do about it.

Rachel Sayre, director of Minneapolis’s emergency management department, worked in conflict zones including Yemen, Haiti, Syria, Iraq, and Ukraine. She described what’s happening in Minneapolis: “A powerful entity is violently and intentionally terrorizing people, making them afraid to go outside so they can’t earn a living and kids can’t go to school. Many of our residents, my neighbors, my friends, are ceasing participation in public life, afraid to even go to the doctor.”

This is what state terror looks like. Not in Yemen or Syria. In Minneapolis.

Trump’s Response: Accusing Critics of Insurrection

Hours after the killing, President Trump posted a lengthy statement on Truth Social. He did not express condolences. He did not call for investigation. He did not acknowledge an American citizen died.

Instead, he attacked Minnesota officials:

“The Mayor and the Governor are inciting Insurrection, with their pompous, dangerous, and arrogant rhetoric! Instead, these sanctimonious political fools should be looking for the Billions of Dollars that has been stolen from the people of Minnesota, and the United States of America. LET OUR ICE PATRIOTS DO THEIR JOB! 12,000 Illegal Alien Criminals, many of them violent, have been arrested and taken out of Minnesota. If they were still there, you would see something far worse than you are witnessing today!”

Read that again: Trump accuses the Governor and Mayor of “inciting Insurrection” for criticizing federal agents who killed their constituents.

Criticizing state violence = insurrection. Killing American citizens = patriotism.

This is the language of authoritarian regimes. When criticism of government killings becomes “insurrection,” you no longer live in a democracy. You live under occupation.

Trump also claimed “12,000 Illegal Alien Criminals, many of them violent, have been arrested.” This is false. According to reporting in December, ICE had arrested 400 people in the Minneapolis operation. In January, DHS claimed 103 out of 2,000 arrestees (5%) had records of violent crimes. The math doesn’t support Trump’s claims—but truth doesn’t matter when propaganda serves the purpose.

Walz Draws the Line: State Will Prosecute Federal Agents

Governor Tim Walz spoke with White House officials twice on Saturday. His message was unambiguous and unprecedented:

“Minnesota’s justice system will have the last word on this. It must have the last word on this. As I told the White House in no uncertain terms this morning, the federal government cannot be trusted to lead this investigation. The state will handle it, period.“

Walz went further: “Minnesotans are witnessing and we’re creating a log of evidence for the future prosecution of ICE agents and officials responsible for this.“

This is a governor announcing his state’s intent to prosecute federal agents. This is a direct challenge to federal supremacy. This is a constitutional crisis unfolding in real time.

Walz characterized the situation starkly: “Minnesota has had it. This is sickening. The President must end this operation. Pull the thousands of violent, untrained officers out of Minnesota. Now.”

He accused the administration of deliberately creating chaos: “We want calm, peace and normalcy back to our lives. They want chaos. We cannot and we will not give them what they want by meeting violence with violence.”

Senators Amy Klobuchar and Tina Smith echoed Walz’s demands. Klobuchar: “To the Trump administration and the Republicans in Congress who have stood silent: Get ICE out of our state NOW.”

Representative Ilhan Omar called it “an execution by immigration enforcement” and said: “Minnesota was once a place of refuge, and Trump has turned it into a war zone where unchecked federal forces murder our neighbors.”

This isn’t partisan rhetoric. This is Minnesota’s entire leadership—from governor to senators to representatives—united in saying: The federal government is killing our citizens and lying about it.

The Aftermath: “Boo Hoo” and Chemical Weapons

Hundreds of protesters gathered at the scene within an hour. Federal agents blocked off the intersection with barricades. Protesters chanted “ICE out now” and “They’re killing my neighbors!”

Minneapolis resident Josh Koskie, watching federal agents control the scene of another killing, said simply: “They’re killing my neighbors!”

When protesters shouted at federal agents, calling them “cowards” and telling them to go home, one officer responded mockingly as he walked away: “Boo hoo.”

DHS called the protesters “rioters” and claimed 200 people “began to obstruct and assault law enforcement” necessitating “crowd control measures for the safety of the public.”

The video shows peaceful protesters horrified by another killing. It shows federal agents deploying chemical weapons against citizens. It shows the government treating Minneapolis residents as enemy combatants.

Minneapolis Police Shut Out—Again

Minneapolis Police Chief O’Hara revealed a crucial detail: “We have not been told any official reports of what led to the shooting” by federal agents.

Federal agents killed someone in Minneapolis and refuse to tell the city’s police chief what happened.

O’Hara also revealed that federal officials tried to dismiss Minneapolis police from the scene entirely. He refused. The standoff required intervention from Hennepin County Attorney Mary Moriarty, who demanded the scene be secured by local law enforcement for evidence collection.

Hours after the shooting, three armored FBI vehicles appeared at the scene. O’Hara told CNN that federal officials were blocking local police from accessing the scene.

This is the same pattern as Renee Good: Kill American citizen, control the scene, lock out local investigators, conduct self-investigation, declare shooting justified.

The Minnesota Bureau of Criminal Apprehension is investigating—but only after Walz demanded it. And only if federal agents don’t obstruct them the way they obstructed investigations into Renee Good’s killing.

The Hennepin County Sheriff’s Office requested assistance from the Minnesota National Guard on Saturday “due to the potential for continuing and growing conflict.”

The National Guard has been on standby since Renee Good’s death. Now they’re being deployed to protect the federal ICE headquarters at the Whipple Federal Building, where protests have been constant for 17 days.

This is the Guard protecting federal buildings from American protesters after federal agents killed American citizens. This is the government using military force to suppress dissent after committing acts of violence.

The NBA postponed Saturday’s Timberwolves-Warriors game “to prioritize the safety and security of the Minneapolis community.” The game was rescheduled for Sunday.

A major professional sports league postponed a game because a city isn’t safe after federal agents killed someone. That’s the level of crisis Minneapolis faces.

The Third Shooting: Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis

Saturday’s killing was actually the third shooting by federal agents in Minneapolis since Renee Good’s death.

On January 14—one week after Good’s killing—ICE agents shot Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis, a Venezuelan man, in the leg. DHS claimed Sosa-Celis was driving a vehicle during a chase, that he “violently assaulted” an officer, and that agents shot him in self-defense while being “ambushed” by three people.

But videos from the scene contradict DHS claims about the identity of the driver and details of the shooting itself. Family members called 911 frantically, saying federal agents had been following Sosa-Celis for 30 minutes trying to crash into him, that he got home, and “because we closed the door on them, they shot him!”

Sosa-Celis later claimed from the hospital that he was inside the house with the door closed when the agent shot him through the door.

CNN obtained videos showing the aftermath contradicting at least some of ICE’s claims. But without video of the shooting itself, DHS’s narrative stands—another case of “feared for safety, defensive shots, justified.”

Three shootings in three weeks. Two Americans killed. One immigrant wounded. All justified by DHS. All defended by the administration. All featuring similar patterns of conflicting accounts and limited evidence.
