The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 14: Which Future Arrives - Three Scenarios for AI's Energy Revolution The Great Energy Challenge Series

December 26, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 2025

We’ve traveled a long way together through this series.

In Parts 2-8, I documented the terrestrial energy crisis: how AI’s computing demands threaten to overwhelm Earth’s power grids, why nuclear provides the only realistic baseload solution at scale, and why natural gas serves as the essential bridge fuel while renewables and nuclear capacity ramp up.

In Parts 9-13, I explored the space solution: how the first AI data center is already operational in orbit, why the Moon offers the technically superior long-term answer, what the Antarctic Treaty tells us about “impossible” cooperation, how SpaceX/Google/Bezos are racing for orbital dominance, and why orbital debris could make it all catastrophically fail.

Now, in this essay, it’s time to integrate everything and answer the fundamental question: Which future actually arrives?

I’ll present three scenarios based on different assumptions about technology development, geopolitical cooperation, and debris management. Then I’ll assess which scenario is most probable and what it means for humanity’s energy and computing future.

Scenario 1: Orbital Scales Successfully (The Optimistic Case)

Key assumptions:

  • Starship achieves $100-200/kg launch costs by 2028-2030

  • Thermal management challenges are solved with better materials

  • Debris tracking and active removal keep Kessler Syndrome at bay

  • Tesla AI chips (AI5-AI8) prove space-viable at volume

  • Insurance and financing markets remain stable despite collision risks

Timeline:

2026-2027: SpaceX launches first Starlink V3 satellites with substantial computing capacity (100+ kW per satellite). Initial deployments focus on edge processing for Starlink’s own network. Google’s prototype satellites with Planet Labs demonstrate TPU viability in orbit.

2028-2029: SpaceX deploys 500+ AI-capable satellites, offering commercial computing services. Major customers include satellite imaging companies (processing in orbit), financial services (low-latency trading), and AI companies (inference for latency-sensitive applications). Blue Origin enters the market with its first operational satellites.

2030-2032: The industry reaches 5,000-10,000 orbital AI satellites collectively (SpaceX dominates with ~70% market share, Google ~20%, Blue Origin and others ~10%). Combined computing capacity: 10-20 gigawatts equivalent.

Terrestrial data center growth slows as companies shift new capacity to orbital. Energy-intensive AI training increasingly moves to space. Terrestrial power grids experience relief from expected demand growth.

2033-2035: Active debris removal services become profitable. Regulations require operators to purchase “debris insurance” that funds removal operations. The industry collectively funds a “space traffic management authority” modeled on air traffic control systems.

Collision rates stabilize at manageable levels. The insurance market adjusts to orbital risks with higher premiums but continued availability. Banks finance orbital infrastructure based on 10-year operational lifetimes and proven reliability data.

2035-2040: Orbital data center capacity reaches 50-100 gigawatts. This represents 30-40% of AI computing demand. Terrestrial data centers handle the remaining 60-70% where low latency or physical proximity matters.

Launch costs continue falling as Starship production scales and competitors emerge. The cost crossover point is reached: orbital computing becomes cheaper per kilowatt-hour than terrestrial for energy-intensive workloads.

2040+: Orbital infrastructure is mature, profitable, and operationally stable. Humanity has successfully expanded computing capacity beyond Earth’s energy constraints without triggering Kessler Syndrome. The Moon remains an interesting possibility but isn’t necessary because orbital proved sufficient.

What has to go right:

  1. Technical breakthroughs in thermal management. Current radiator requirements are prohibitive. New materials or heat rejection methods must dramatically improve efficiency.

  2. Debris removal becomes economically viable. Cost per removed satellite must drop from tens of millions to single-digit millions or lower.

  3. Chip reliability exceeds expectations. Real-world failure rates in space must be below 10% over 10-year periods.

  4. No major cascade events. Even one Kessler-syndrome-initiating collision could collapse the industry.

  5. Geopolitical cooperation on space traffic management. US, China, Russia, EU must agree on orbital slot allocation, debris responsibility, and collision protocols.

Probability assessment: 15-20%

This is the scenario the industry wants to believe. But it requires almost everything to go right simultaneously. History suggests that’s unlikely.

Scenario 2: Moon-Based Dominance (The Patient Strategy)

Key assumptions:

  • Orbital proves technically viable but doesn’t scale due to debris concerns

  • Launch costs fall to $200/kg by mid-2030s enabling lunar economics

  • Antarctic Treaty-style cooperation emerges for lunar governance (2033-2037)

  • Permanently shadowed craters prove as advantageous as predicted

  • Lonestar’s early success demonstrates customer acceptance

Timeline:

2026-2029: Orbital data centers deploy as per Scenario 1 early stages. SpaceX, Google, Blue Origin all make progress. But debris incidents escalate. Three significant collisions occur. Insurance rates spike. Public concern grows.

Meanwhile, Lonestar expands from its 2025 Freedom mission to multi-petabyte facilities in cislunar space. NASA's Artemis program achieves the goals set in Trump's December 2025 executive order: astronauts land at lunar south pole by 2028, water ice is confirmed in permanently shadowed craters, and initial elements of a permanent outpost are established by 2030. Nuclear reactors are deployed to provide continuous power during 14-day lunar nights.

China's ILRS program also achieves lunar surface operations by 2029. Both US and Chinese missions validate that the lunar environment is operationally feasible for industrial infrastructure.

2030-2032: A major orbital collision destroys 10+ satellites, creating massive debris field. ESA and NASA jointly recommend limiting orbital data center expansion. The incident triggers serious discussions about long-term sustainability.

Google and SpaceX both announce “lunar research initiatives” to explore moving infrastructure to the Moon. Blue Origin reveals that its secret program has been lunar-focused all along. Bezos commits $10 billion to lunar infrastructure development.

2033-2035: The geopolitical window opens as predicted in Part 11. New US administration (post-Trump) signals willingness to cooperate with China on lunar frameworks. Leadership changes in China and Russia create diplomatic opportunities.

Business pressure from SpaceX, Blue Origin, Google, and others demands legal certainty for lunar resource extraction. Insurance industry won’t underwrite missions without governance framework.

2036-2037: A “Lunar Framework Agreement” is signed, modeled on the Antarctic Treaty. It doesn’t resolve ownership disputes but establishes operational protocols, resource extraction guidelines, safety zones, and conflict resolution mechanisms.

The framework is explicitly pragmatic: allows commercial activity while requiring international oversight. US, China, Russia, EU, and 40+ other nations sign. It’s not ideal but it’s workable.

2037-2040: First large-scale data centers are constructed in lunar lava tubes near permanently shadowed craters. Solar power from crater rims provides continuous energy. Natural cooling reduces energy requirements by 85% compared to terrestrial facilities.

SpaceX leverages Starship for lunar cargo delivery at $200/kg costs. Blue Origin’s New Glenn also services lunar logistics. Competition drives costs lower.

2040-2045: Lunar data center capacity reaches 50-100 gigawatts equivalent—matching what Scenario 1 achieved with orbital, but with zero debris risk and sustainable expansion potential.

Orbital infrastructure stabilizes at 5,000-8,000 satellites (not millions) handling only latency-critical applications—real-time communications, gaming, HFT, interactive AI. The bulk computing load (training, batch processing, storage) operates from the Moon.

2045+: The hybrid architecture is established: minimal orbital for latency-sensitive tasks, lunar surface for everything else. Humanity has solved the AI energy crisis without exhausting terrestrial power grids or creating orbital debris catastrophe.

Manufacturing on the Moon begins (using lunar regolith for solar panels and construction materials). The economic center of computing gradually shifts off-planet.

What has to go right:

  1. Orbital debris becomes undeniable problem. Has to be serious enough to force change but not catastrophic enough to destroy space access entirely.

  2. Geopolitical cooperation emerges. Leadership changes create diplomatic window. Business pressure forces governments to negotiate.

  3. Lunar economics work as predicted. Permanently shadowed craters must deliver the cooling benefits. Launch costs must fall as projected.

  4. Technical challenges are solved. Radiation shielding in lava tubes, power transmission from crater rims, water ice extraction—all must work as theorized.

  5. Long-term commitment sustained. Lunar infrastructure requires decades of patient investment. Political support must continue through multiple election cycles.

Probability assessment: 40-45% (increased from 35-40% based on December 2025 policy developments)

The Trump executive order of December 18, 2025 significantly increases this scenario’s probability. A formal presidential directive with specific timelines (2028 landing, 2030 outpost, nuclear reactor deployment) transforms lunar infrastructure from speculative technology to national policy priority.

This scenario still requires patience, cooperation, and long-term thinking—not humanity’s natural strengths. But the forcing events (debris problems, business pressure, government commitments, leadership changes) make cooperation increasingly likely over a 10-15 year timeframe.

The executive order doesn’t guarantee success—technical challenges and political sustainability across administrations remain serious obstacles. But it demonstrates that lunar infrastructure has moved from “interesting idea” to “actual policy” faster than anticipated when this series began.

Scenario 3: The Hybrid Muddle (The Pragmatic Middle)

Key assumptions:

  • Neither orbital nor lunar achieves clean dominance

  • Debris remains manageable but constrains orbital growth

  • Lunar develops slowly but proves viable for specific applications

  • Terrestrial improvements reduce space infrastructure urgency

  • Geopolitical cooperation is partial and inconsistent

Timeline:

2026-2030: Orbital data centers deploy successfully but growth is slower than Scenario 1. SpaceX reaches 2,000-3,000 AI-capable satellites by 2030 (not 10,000). Google and Blue Origin deploy hundreds, not thousands.

Total orbital capacity: 5-8 gigawatts by 2030. Meaningful but not revolutionary.

Simultaneously, terrestrial energy infrastructure improves faster than expected. Small modular reactors (SMRs) achieve commercial deployment by 2028-2029. Natural gas plants with carbon capture become economic. Solar + battery storage scales rapidly.

The terrestrial energy constraint remains serious but isn’t catastrophic. Data centers can still expand on Earth—just at higher cost and slower pace than desired.

2030-2035: Orbital debris incidents occur but don’t trigger full Kessler Syndrome. Three major collisions destroy 20+ satellites total. Industry responds with better tracking, stricter deorbit requirements, and modest debris removal efforts.

Lunar infrastructure develops but slowly. Lonestar expands to petabyte-scale facilities. NASA and China both operate small-scale computing test beds on the Moon. But large-scale data centers don’t materialize—costs remain higher than hoped, technical challenges persist.

Governance cooperation is partial. Artemis and ILRS remain separate frameworks. Limited bilateral agreements emerge (US-UK-Australia sharing lunar south pole region, China-Russia-Pakistan cooperating on separate locations) but no comprehensive treaty.

2035-2040: The industry settles into a three-way split:

  • Terrestrial data centers: Still handle 60-70% of computing load. SMRs and renewables have expanded capacity enough to absorb AI growth, albeit at high cost.

  • Orbital infrastructure: Handles 15-20% of computing—primarily edge processing, low-latency applications, and Earth observation data processing. Satellite population stabilizes at 10,000-15,000 (manageable but constrains growth).

  • Lunar facilities: Handle 10-15% of computing—primarily disaster recovery, long-term data storage, and batch processing for jobs that don’t need low latency. Growing slowly but operational.

No single approach dominates. Each serves its niche. The transition to space is gradual, not revolutionary.

2040-2050: This mixed model persists. Terrestrial remains dominant because it’s proven, understood, and continuously improving. Orbital provides valuable supplements for specific use cases. Lunar grows steadily as infrastructure matures but doesn’t displace terrestrial.

AI computing demand continues growing but not at apocalyptic rates—efficiency improvements and algorithmic breakthroughs reduce energy requirements per unit of computing.

The energy crisis is managed, not solved. We muddle through with incremental improvements across multiple approaches rather than a single transformative solution.

What has to go right:

  1. Terrestrial energy scales adequately. SMRs, natural gas, renewables must collectively deliver enough capacity to prevent crisis.

  2. Orbital avoids catastrophic debris cascade. Collisions happen but don’t trigger Kessler Syndrome.

  3. Lunar proves viable but not economically dominant. Works for niche applications; doesn’t become the obvious winner.

  4. AI efficiency improves. Better algorithms and chips reduce energy requirements faster than raw computing demand grows.

  5. No geopolitical disasters. US-China tensions remain manageable. No major wars disrupt space cooperation.

Probability assessment: 45-50%

This is the “muddle through” scenario. Nothing works perfectly but nothing fails catastrophically. Multiple partial solutions collectively address the problem.

It’s not satisfying narratively—no clear winners, no transformative breakthrough. But it’s how large-scale infrastructure transitions usually happen: messy, incremental, pragmatic.

Integrating the Terrestrial Energy Solutions

Before rendering final judgment, let’s remember Parts 2-8 of this series:

The terrestrial energy expansion is happening regardless of space solutions.

Natural gas plants are being built now (Part 2-3). Nuclear renaissance is underway with SMRs, new large reactors, and fusion progress (Part 4-5). Renewables continue scaling with storage improvements (Part 6-7). Grid infrastructure is modernizing (Part 8).

This terrestrial expansion reduces the urgency of space-based solutions. If SMRs deliver on their promise—providing 1-2 GW per facility with factory production scaling to dozens per year by 2030—terrestrial data centers can expand substantially without exhausting grid capacity.

Space infrastructure becomes an option, not a necessity.

That fundamentally changes the risk calculus. You don’t take massive risks with orbital debris or lunar infrastructure if terrestrial solutions adequately meet demand.

The space solution becomes most attractive if:

  1. Terrestrial energy costs remain prohibitively high (>$50/MWh)

  2. Environmental constraints prevent adequate terrestrial expansion

  3. Space achieves clear cost advantages ($200/kg launch makes economics favorable)

  4. Political/regulatory barriers slow terrestrial infrastructure deployment

Space becomes less necessary if:

  1. SMRs deploy at scale with costs below $40/MWh

  2. Natural gas + carbon capture provides adequate bridge capacity

  3. AI efficiency improvements reduce computing energy requirements

  4. Public acceptance of nuclear improves, speeding deployment

The interplay between terrestrial and space solutions determines which scenario unfolds.

My Assessment: Which Scenario Is Most Probable?

Based on the analysis in this series—the technical constraints, economic projections, geopolitical dynamics, and historical precedents—here’s my assessment:

Scenario 3 (Hybrid Muddle) is most probable at 45-50% likelihood.

We muddle through with partial solutions:

  • Terrestrial data centers continue expanding with SMRs, gas, renewables

  • Orbital provides valuable supplements for specific applications

  • Lunar develops slowly as disaster recovery and niche computing

  • No single approach dominates; each serves its strengths

Scenario 2 (Moon-Based Dominance) is second most probable at 40-45%.

The Trump executive order of December 18, 2025 significantly strengthens this scenario by making lunar infrastructure official U.S. policy with specific deadlines. If orbital debris becomes serious enough to force change, and if geopolitical cooperation emerges through leadership changes and business pressure, lunar infrastructure could dominate by 2040s. The Antarctic Treaty precedent proves this isn’t fantasy.

Scenario 1 (Orbital Scales Successfully) is least probable at 15-20%.

Requires too many things to go right simultaneously. Debris risk is real and growing. Thermal challenges are harder than acknowledged. Insurance markets will constrain growth as collision probability increases.

Why I favor the Hybrid Muddle:

1. History suggests incremental transitions, not revolutionary ones. Major infrastructure changes—electrification, internet deployment, mobile communications—took decades and involved multiple competing approaches before standards emerged.

2. Risk aversion favors diversification. No single entity wants to bet everything on one approach. SpaceX pursues orbital, Google researches both orbital and lunar, Blue Origin stays flexible. Diversification is rational.

3. Geopolitical cooperation is hard but not impossible. Partial cooperation (bilateral agreements, limited frameworks) is more achievable than comprehensive treaties. That favors hybrid rather than clean outcomes.

4. Terrestrial improvements reduce pressure for revolutionary solutions. SMRs are real, deployable, and scaling. That takes pressure off space solutions to be perfect.

5. Debris constrains orbital but doesn’t eliminate it. We’ll probably avoid catastrophic Kessler Syndrome (through better tracking, modest removal efforts, constrained growth) but debris fears limit orbital scaling.

What This Means for the Energy Future

Whichever scenario unfolds, several conclusions are clear:

1. The terrestrial energy expansion is essential and inevitable. We need SMRs, natural gas, renewables, and grid modernization regardless of space solutions. That’s Parts 2-8 of this series, and it remains true even if space infrastructure succeeds.

2. Space-based computing is transitioning from science fiction to engineering reality. Starcloud-1 proves it works. Google’s radiation testing shows commercial chips survive. The economic analysis suggests viability below $200/kg launch costs. It’s real.

3. The Moon is the long-term sustainable solution if cooperation emerges. Orbital has fundamental scaling limits (debris, thermal, operational complexity). The Moon doesn’t. Antarctic Treaty proves cooperation is possible. Timeline is 2030s-2040s.

4. The next 5 years (2025-2030) determine the path. Orbital deployments either work or face serious debris problems. Terrestrial SMRs either deploy at scale or don’t. Geopolitical relationships either improve or deteriorate. These near-term outcomes lock in longer-term trajectories.

5. Humanity will likely solve this through multiple partial solutions rather than one transformative breakthrough. That’s the Hybrid Muddle scenario—not as satisfying as “the Moon saves us” or “orbital transforms everything” but more consistent with how complex infrastructure transitions actually happen.

The Broader Implications

This series began with a simple question: How do we power the AI revolution without destroying Earth’s energy systems?

The answer turns out to be: Multiple approaches, each contributing, none sufficient alone.

Terrestrial: SMRs + natural gas + renewables + grid improvements = baseline capacity growth

Orbital: Limited deployment for latency-sensitive applications = 10-20% of demand

Lunar: Long-term sustainable infrastructure for bulk computing = potential 40-60% of demand by 2040s-2050s

Efficiency: Better algorithms, improved chips, optimized workloads = reduces total energy requirements 20-30%

The combination solves the problem. No single solution does.

That’s frustrating for those who want a clear winner. It’s unsatisfying for narratives that need heroes and villains. But it’s how the world actually works.

The energy transition is messy, incremental, and pragmatic.

We build SMRs while also researching fusion. We deploy orbital satellites while planning lunar infrastructure. We expand natural gas while scaling renewables. We do all of it simultaneously because the stakes are too high to bet on one approach.

And probably—if we’re lucky and reasonably competent—we muddle through successfully.

Not with a transformative breakthrough that solves everything cleanly. But with a messy combination of partial solutions that collectively keep the lights on and the AI models training.

A Final Thought: The Stakes

This series began with a concerning observation: AI’s energy demands were growing faster than most acknowledged. The projections were daunting. The solutions seemed inadequate.

After examining both terrestrial energy (Parts 2-8) and space infrastructure (Parts 9-14), the picture looks more manageable—cautiously optimistic, even.

Not because any single solution is guaranteed to work. But because humanity is pursuing enough different approaches that some combination will probably succeed.

SMRs might deploy slower than hoped, but natural gas fills the gap. Orbital might face debris constraints, but the Moon offers alternatives. Geopolitical cooperation might be partial, but bilateral frameworks emerge. Efficiency improvements might be incremental, but they compound over time.

The energy crisis is solvable. Not easily. Not cleanly. Not with one transformative breakthrough. But solvable through persistent, pragmatic effort across multiple fronts.

That’s not a Hollywood ending. But it’s a realistic one.

And given the stakes—humanity’s computing future, climate goals, economic prosperity, and technological progress—realistic and achievable is good enough.

The AI revolution will be powered. Probably through a messy combination of nuclear plants in Iowa, solar farms in Arizona, satellites in orbit, and data centers on the Moon.

And that’s okay. In fact, it’s probably the best outcome we could reasonably hope for.

Conclusion: The Work Continues

This series ends but the story continues. The next five years will determine which scenario unfolds.

Watch for these signals:

  • SMR deployment rates by 2028: If 5+ facilities are operational in the US, terrestrial solutions are working

  • Orbital collision incidents by 2029: If major cascades occur, Moon becomes necessary

  • Launch cost trends by 2030: If Starship achieves $200/kg, space economics transform

  • Geopolitical cooperation by 2032: If US-China frameworks emerge, Moon becomes viable

  • AI efficiency improvements by 2030: If energy per FLOP drops 50%+, crisis pressure reduces

The future isn’t predetermined. It depends on choices we make—as individuals, companies, and nations—over the next decade.

The energy challenge is real. The solutions exist. Whether we deploy them successfully remains to be seen.

But the analysis suggests we can. Not perfectly. Not effortlessly. But adequately.

And in a world facing existential challenges—climate change, technological disruption, geopolitical tensions—adequately is sometimes enough.

Thanks for following this journey from Iowa nuclear plants to lunar data centers. The real work begins now.
