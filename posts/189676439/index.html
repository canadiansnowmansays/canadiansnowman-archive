The Sermon

Part 1: Steve Jobs at Aspen, 1983

March 9, 2026

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon - March 2026

The Room

The International Design Conference in Aspen has been running since 1951. It draws architects, industrial designers, graphic artists, filmmakers - the people who shape the physical and visual world. In 1983, the conference theme was “The Future Is Not What It Used to Be,” which, given what was about to happen in that room, turned out to be the understatement of the century.

The speaker was Steve Jobs. He was twenty-eight years old, the co-founder and chairman of Apple Computer, and he had been invited to explain to a room full of designers what this “personal computer” thing was and why they should care about it.

He opened with a joke about his speaking fee - sixty dollars - and the fact that he had worn a tie for the occasion. Then, over the next fifty-four minutes, he delivered what may be the most prescient technology speech ever recorded.

The speech was not widely known for decades. The audio was recorded by the conference but sat in relative obscurity until it resurfaced in the age of digital archives. Listening to it today - with the full benefit of forty-three years of hindsight - is an experience that oscillates between admiration and something closer to vertigo. The man at the podium is not guessing. He is not speculating. He is describing, with the calm confidence of someone reading from a script that has not been written yet, the world we now inhabit.

But the speech is not important because Jobs predicted the future. Lots of people predict the future, and most of them are wrong about the details and right about the general direction, or right about the details and wrong about the timing. Jobs was right about both. What makes the Aspen speech historically significant - and what makes it the foundation of this series - is that Jobs embedded a moral philosophy in his technological predictions. He did not just describe what was coming. He described what it should be for.

Steve Jobs, 28, at the 1983 International Design Conference in Aspen — where he predicted the iPhone, internet communities, algorithmic feeds, and AI that captures a thinker’s worldview.” Source: Wallpaper* (from Steve Jobs Archive footage)

The Electric Motor Analogy

Jobs began with a history lesson disguised as a metaphor.

The electric motor, he explained, was first invented in the late 1800s. In its early form, it could only be built very large, which meant it could only serve very large applications. A factory might have one enormous motor. Power was concentrated by necessity.

The next breakthrough was the shared motor - a single large unit connected to multiple workstations through a system of belts and pulleys running through a central shaft. This distributed the motor’s power across a factory floor, making it cost-effective for medium-scale applications. But the power still flowed from a central source. The workers depended on the shaft.

The real revolution - the one that changed civilization - was the fractional horsepower motor. Suddenly, power could be delivered directly to the point of use. Individual applications. Individual people. No shaft. No dependency on a central source. By 1983, Jobs noted, there were approximately fifty-five fractional horsepower motors in every American household.

The fractional horsepower motor — Jobs’s 1983 metaphor for the shift from concentrated factory power to power in every household. The personal computer was supposed to do the same for intelligence. Source: IQS Directory (educational diagram)

Then he mapped the analogy onto computing.

The ENIAC, built in 1947, was the giant motor - a room-sized machine accessible to almost no one, built for military ballistic calculations. Time-sharing in the 1960s was the belt-and-pulley stage - one large computer shared across many users through terminals connected by “umbilical cords” to a mainframe somewhere else. The illusion of individual access, but the reality of central control.

And the personal computer - Apple’s invention, Jobs argued - was the fractional horsepower motor. Computing power delivered directly to the individual. No umbilical cord. No mainframe. No dependence on a central authority.

“The reason Apple exists,” Jobs said, “is because we stumbled onto fractional horsepower computing five years before anybody else.”

This was not merely a business narrative. It was a theory of progress. And the theory had a clear moral direction: concentrated power is the primitive state. Distributed power is the advanced state. The arc of technology bends toward the individual.

It was an argument about democracy dressed up as a product presentation.

The Predictions

What followed was a series of predictions so accurate that reading the transcript today produces a peculiar sensation - the feeling of watching someone describe your life before you were born into it.

The iPhone and iPad. Jobs described Apple’s strategy in language that could serve as a product brief for the device that would not exist for another twenty-four years:

The iPhone 17 Pro Max in cosmic orange (Image credit: Apple / Future)

“What we want to do is we want to put an incredibly great computer in a book that you can carry around with you, that you can learn how to use in twenty minutes. That is what we want to do. And we want to do it this decade. And we really want to do it with a radio link in it, so you do not have to hook up to anything. You are in communication with all these larger databases and other computers.”

A great computer. In a book. Twenty minutes to learn. Radio link. Connected to all other computers and databases. He was describing the iPhone in 1983. He even described the iterative path to get there: first a breadbox-sized machine at $10,000 (the Lisa), then a shoebox at $2,500 (which would become the Macintosh), then finally the book. The engineering roadmap was twenty-four years long, and he had it mapped out at twenty-eight.

The Internet and social networks. Jobs told a story about Xerox PARC, where approximately a hundred computers had been connected on a local area network. What happened, he said, was that formal work distribution lists quickly gave way to informal interest groups - a volleyball distribution list, a Chinese food cooking list. “Before long, there were more lists than people.”

That single observation - “more lists than people” - is a one-sentence description of the internet as it actually evolved. Reddit. Facebook Groups. Discord servers. Slack channels. Subreddits. The entire architecture of online community, predicted from a single observation at Xerox PARC.

He then projected outward: “We are about five years away from really solving the problems of hooking these computers together in the office, and we are about ten to fifteen years away from solving the problems of hooking them together in the home.” He was speaking in 1983. The World Wide Web was invented in 1989. Consumer internet access became widespread in the mid-1990s. His timeline was almost exactly right.

The App Store. This prediction is so specific it almost feels like time travel. Jobs described the software distribution problem: there were twenty thousand programs for the Apple II, but consumers had no way to evaluate them before buying. You would go to a dealer, ask which one to buy, and the person behind the counter had no idea - “they are out selling computers, they are not looking at software.”

His solution: “We need a software radio station.” He then described, step by step, the model that would become the App Store:

Software would be transmitted electronically over phone lines - “we take our ones and zeros and send tones over the phone that the computers will understand and go directly from computer to computer.” There would be free sampling - “we will give you thirty seconds of this program for free, or we will give you five screenshots and we will let you play with it for a day.” And purchase would be instant - “if you want to buy it, just type in your Visa number and you got it.”

Electronic distribution. Free trial periods. One-click purchase with a credit card. He described the App Store’s business model in 1983, twenty-five years before it launched.

Social media and algorithmic feeds. When asked about privacy and centralized databases, Jobs pivoted to a description of information filtering that precisely anticipated the algorithmic feed:

“I can come home and on a weekend peruse the weekly outpouring of information, but put a filter on it because I am only interested in gun control. And I can find out that my Congressman gave some testimony last week about gun control that I did not agree with, so I can get on and write a pretty nasty letter and zing it on the email system and make sure that at least one of his aides will read it tomorrow.”

Personalized content filtering. Algorithmic curation based on individual interests. Instant electronic communication with elected officials. He was describing Google News, Twitter, and constituent email campaigns - all in a single paragraph, all in 1983.

Artificial intelligence. And then there is the passage that, in 2026, reads less like prediction and more like prophecy.

Jobs was discussing the difference between television programming, which captures specific experiences, and computer programming, which captures “the underlying principles of an experience” - the rules that generate thousands of individual experiences. He used the example of video games following the laws of physics, and a children’s economics game called Hammurabi.

Then he made the leap:

“I think as we look towards the next fifty to a hundred years, if we really can come up with these machines that can capture an underlying spirit, or an underlying set of principles, or an underlying way of looking at the world, then when the next Aristotle comes around, maybe if he carries around one of these machines with him, his or her whole life, and types in all this stuff, then maybe someday after the person’s dead and gone, we can ask this machine, ‘Hey, what would Aristotle have said? What about this?’ And maybe we will not get the right answer, but maybe we will. And that is really exciting to me.”

Read that again. He is describing a machine that ingests a person’s entire body of thought and work over a lifetime - “types in all this stuff” - and then, after the person is gone, can be asked questions and generate responses consistent with that person’s worldview.

He is describing a large language model. He is describing the fundamental concept behind ChatGPT and Claude. He is describing the technology that I am using, right now, to research and draft this essay.

In 1983.

“And that is one of the reasons I am doing what I am doing,” he said.

The Democratic Ethic

But the predictions, remarkable as they are, are not what make this speech the foundation document of the democratic technology movement. What makes it foundational is the ethic that runs beneath every prediction like an underground river.

Every technology Jobs described was a technology of individual empowerment. Every application he envisioned was an application of democratic citizenship. Every problem he identified was a problem of concentrated power, and every solution he proposed was a solution of distribution.

Consider the specific examples he chose when describing how people would use these technologies:

The citizen filtering congressional testimony on gun control. Not a consumer buying products. Not a business optimizing workflows. A citizen monitoring their representative and holding them accountable through direct communication.

The student who would never think of writing a paper without a computer, just as they would never think of going to science class without a calculator. Not a corporate knowledge worker. A student. The next generation, empowered.

The hundred thousand schools that needed computers - and Apple’s willingness to spend twenty-five percent of its annual profits to put one in every school, because “the kids can’t wait.” Not for the educational bureaucracy. Not for parents to understand. Not for the market to provide. The kids can’t wait. The urgency was moral, not commercial.

And when confronted directly with the question of concentrated power - what about these giant databases that know everything about us? - Jobs’s answer was not regulation, not oversight, not trust in institutions. His answer was: distribute the tools. Give people the ability to process information for themselves. The antidote to concentrated data power is distributed data intelligence.

“The first thing we have got to do is give tools to people to help them do that. Because right now those tools are centralized.”

This is not a business strategy. It is a political philosophy. And it places Steve Jobs, at twenty-eight years old, squarely in the tradition of democratic thinkers who believed that the distribution of capability is the foundation of liberty.

The Missionary

The Aspen conference tent/audience (wide shot of the crowd listening) — symbolizes the moment reaching out to the world.

There is a passage near the end of the speech that reveals something about Jobs that the standard biographies tend to miss, or at least to underweight.

An audience member asked about employee retention - how Apple kept its people. Jobs’s answer began with economics (everyone owned stock) and organizational structure (three layers of management, modeled on the Catholic Church). But then it shifted into something more personal:

“Most of us did not make the clothes we are wearing, and we did not cook or grow the food that we eat, and we are speaking a language that was developed by other people. We use a mathematics that was developed by other people. We are constantly taking. And the ability to put something back into that pool of human experience is extremely neat.”

This is not the language of a businessman. It is the language of someone who sees technology as a moral obligation - a way of contributing to the collective inheritance of humanity. Jobs was describing Apple’s mission not as market dominance or shareholder returns, but as participation in the ancient human project of building on what came before and leaving something better for what comes after.

He continued: “Everyone knows that in the next ten years, we have the chance to really do that. And we can look back and say, ‘God, we were a part of that.’ And while we are doing it, it is pretty fun too.”

The urgency in this passage is not commercial. It is historical. Jobs believed he and his team were at a hinge point in human civilization, and the window to get it right was finite. “In fifteen years, it is going to be all over in terms of this first phase, getting these tools out into society in large numbers. But during the next fifteen years, we have an opportunity to do it great or to do it so-so.”

Do it great or do it so-so. That was the choice, as Jobs framed it. Not do it or don’t do it. The technology was coming regardless. The question was whether it would be done with intention, with beauty, with democratic purpose - or whether it would be done carelessly, producing “one more piece of junk object” in people’s lives.

He was a missionary. And the mission was not Apple. The mission was civilization.

What He Got Wrong

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that Jobs was not a perfect prophet. He got several things wrong, and some of what he got wrong is instructive.

He underestimated the timeline for voice recognition and natural language processing. When asked about voice interfaces, Jobs said it was “the better part of a decade away” and acknowledged the difficulty: “Understanding language is much harder than understanding voice. We can sort of sort out the words, but what do they all mean?” He was right about the difficulty. He was wrong about the timeline. It took not one decade but four before voice assistants like Siri (which Apple itself would introduce in 2011) became functional, and even today, in 2026, natural language understanding remains imperfect.

He was too optimistic about American industrial competitiveness. Jobs expressed concern that most consumer objects - televisions, watches, cameras, bicycles - were no longer designed in America. He believed the personal computer industry offered a chance to reverse that trend. In fact, while Apple became the most valuable company in the world, the broader pattern of offshoring manufacturing continued and accelerated. The computers themselves are designed in Cupertino and manufactured in Shenzhen.

He was naive about the information filtering problem. Jobs described citizens filtering information to find what interested them as though the challenge were purely technical - build the filter, empower the citizen. He did not anticipate that the filter itself would become the instrument of manipulation. That algorithmic curation would not merely help citizens find truth but would actively distort their perception of reality. That the “software radio station” would become a propaganda machine. This is perhaps the most consequential thing Jobs got wrong: he assumed good faith in the system. He assumed the tools would be used as designed.

He did not anticipate surveillance capitalism. This is related but distinct. Jobs understood that personal data would become important - he addressed the privacy question directly. But he framed it as a problem of centralized databases controlled by institutions. He did not imagine a world in which the personal computer itself - the tool of liberation - would become the primary instrument of surveillance, voluntarily carried by billions of people who trade their privacy for convenience without fully understanding the exchange.

The Unasked Question

And yet, the most important thing Jobs got wrong was not a prediction. It was an assumption.

Jobs assumed that the democratizing tendency of technology was inherent - that distributing computing power to individuals would naturally produce democratic outcomes. The fractional horsepower motor analogy carried an implicit argument: just as the electric motor inevitably moved from concentrated to distributed, computing power would follow the same arc, and democratic empowerment would follow computing power.

This turned out to be profoundly wrong.

Distributing the hardware did not distribute the power. It redistributed the interface while concentrating the infrastructure. Everyone got a computer in their pocket. But the data those computers generated flowed upward to a handful of companies. The algorithms that determined what information people saw were written by a handful of engineers and optimized for a handful of executives. The platforms on which people communicated, organized, and expressed themselves were owned by a handful of men.

Jobs’s fractional horsepower motor analogy breaks down at a critical point: when you have a fractional horsepower motor in your kitchen, you control it. When you have a smartphone in your pocket, you operate it - but you do not control it. The operating system is controlled by Apple or Google. The apps are controlled by their developers. The data flows to their servers. The algorithm decides what you see. The terms of service can change without your consent.

The user has the experience of empowerment. The reality is dependency.

Jobs could not have foreseen this, because the business model that made it possible - the exchange of free services for personal data, monetized through targeted advertising - had not yet been invented. The World Wide Web did not exist. Google would not be founded for another fifteen years. Facebook for another twenty-one. The idea that the tool of liberation would become the instrument of extraction was, in 1983, genuinely unimaginable.

But it is the central fact of the story this series tells. And it is why Jobs’s 1983 speech is not merely a historical curiosity but a moral benchmark - a record of the road not taken, the future that was promised and then betrayed.

The Standard

Steve Jobs stood in Aspen in 1983 and articulated, with extraordinary clarity, a vision of technology in the service of democracy. Distribute the intelligence. Empower the citizen. Put the tools in every hand. Make them beautiful. Make them intuitive. Get them into schools. The kids can’t wait.

Every major technology company founded in the decades that followed claimed to share this vision. “Connecting the world.” “Organizing the world’s information.” “AI for the benefit of all of humanity.” “Accelerating the world’s transition to sustainable energy.”

The question this series will now pursue, figure by figure and decade by decade, is simple: who meant it?
