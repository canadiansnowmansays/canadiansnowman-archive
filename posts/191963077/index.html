The War Dividend

Part 11: How the President Started a War His Sons Are Cashing In On — and Why the Pentagon’s Contractors Were Selected by the Men Who Profit From Them

March 27, 2026

U.S. military personnel operating attack and surveillance drones — the exact capabilities boosted by the Pentagon’s $1.1 billion Drone Dominance initiative and contracts awarded to companies backed by the Trump sons. Source: U.S. Marine Corps / Department of Defense (official military imagery).

Recent reporting documented five specific incidents of market-moving announcements preceded by suspicious trading activity — from the $TRUMP meme coin launch to the Iran TACO announcement — and the $300-400 million extracted from a single Truth Social post. That is the grift operating in financial markets.

This is the grift operating in the military-industrial complex.

The president started a war. His sons invested in the weapons the war requires. The Pentagon — whose senior leadership was screened, in part, by one of those sons — awarded those companies hundreds of millions in contracts. The sons are now worth multiples of what they were worth before their father returned to office.

Senator Lindsey Graham, on Fox News Sunday, described the Iran war with the kind of candor that usually stays in private: “When this regime goes down, we’re gonna make a ton of money. Venezuela and Iran have 31 percent of the world’s oil reserves. We’re gonna have a partnership with 31 percent of the known reserves.”

Graham was talking about America. He was also, whether he intended to or not, describing the Trump family business model.

Senator Lindsey Graham speaking candidly about the financial upside of regime change in Iran, echoing the Trump family business model described in the essay. Source: Fox News appearance (public domain broadcast still).

The Setup: Creating the Market

To understand how the sons’ drone portfolio works, you first need to understand the policy decisions that made it valuable — decisions made by the administration they are part of.

In December 2024 — the month before inauguration — the Trump administration announced a ban on new Chinese-made drone models and components. China had dominated the global consumer and commercial drone market. The ban eliminated the competition overnight, creating a vacuum that American manufacturers could fill.

Then came the Drone Dominance initiative — a Pentagon program budgeted at $1.1 billion to field over 200,000 American-made attack drones by 2027. The Department of Homeland Security added hundreds of millions more for drone and counter-drone technology.

Then came the Iran war on February 28, 2026. The war the Pentagon’s own internal assessments described as requiring urgent drone capability. The war in which one-way attack drones were used in combat by US forces for the first time. The war that the sons invested into.

Create the market. Fill the market. Collect from the market.

Don Jr.: The Portfolio That Screened Its Own Regulators

Donald Trump Jr., partner at 1789 Capital and advisor to drone companies that later received major DoD contracts. Source: Professional portrait (public/business photo)

Donald Trump Jr. joined 1789 Capital as a partner in November 2024 — the month his father was re-elected. The fund’s assets under management grew from $150 million to $2 billion in a single year. It is now closed to new investors.

Trump Jr. has been explicit about what “patriotic capitalist” means in practice. At the Future Investment Initiative conference in Riyadh in October 2025, he told the audience: “We understand what the administration wants to do, because we helped craft some of that messaging. So we can be on the outside but still have that connection.”

The connection runs deeper than messaging. Following the 2024 election, Trump Jr. reportedly helped screen candidates for senior Pentagon positions on behalf of his father — checking whether candidates supported his own personal investment priorities, specifically looking for candidates who wanted to spend in areas aligned with his portfolio.

The people who decide which defense companies receive federal contracts were selected, in part, by the man whose investments depend on those decisions.

When the Associated Press asked about language in a regulatory filing stating that 1789’s new investment firm would explicitly target companies seeking federal grants, Trump’s chief business lawyer filed a new document with that language quietly removed. Senator Elizabeth Warren wrote formally to Defense Secretary Hegseth in January 2026 documenting the conflicts and requesting an explanation of how these contracts were awarded. No substantive response has been documented.

The Contracts — Four Companies, Four Awards:

Unusual Machines — a drone parts startup that had received minimal Pentagon business before 2025. Trump Jr. joined its advisory board in November 2024 and received 200,000 shares. It subsequently won its largest DoD contract in company history.

Vulcan Elements — a 30-employee startup producing rare-earth magnets used in drones, radar systems, and other military equipment. 1789 Capital invested in August 2025. Three months later, Vulcan received a $620 million loan from the Pentagon’s Office of Strategic Capital — the largest contract that office had ever awarded in its history. Three months from investment to record-breaking federal contract.

Anduril Industries — unmanned combat systems manufacturer. 1789 Capital backed. Received a ten-year US Army contract worth up to $20 billion. Anduril’s CEO told Bloomberg the company has “all sorts of primarily counter-air systems that are present in conflict zones” and is “actively working day to day” with the DoD — in the Iran war.

Powerus — a Florida-based drone company founded by US Army Special Operations veterans approximately one year ago. Both Eric and Don Jr. invested through American Ventures, the family’s investment vehicle. Powerus bought three competitors in six months, raised $60 million, and is planning a Nasdaq debut via reverse merger with Aureus Greenway Holdings — the Trump-backed golf course holding company already publicly traded. It is targeting the Pentagon’s $1.1 billion Drone Dominance program directly.

Powerus CEO Andrew Fox, when asked why his company chose a Trump vehicle for its public listing rather than any of the hundreds of other shell companies available on Nasdaq, said he was “not a finance specialist and didn’t know.”

Eric: The Israeli Drone Company and the One-Way Attack

Eric Trump, whose investments include Xtend (AI-enabled one-way attack drones deployed in the Iran war) and whose net worth grew tenfold since inauguration. Source: Recent professional portrait (business event coverage)

In February 2026, Eric invested in Xtend — an Israeli AI-driven drone company — as part of a $1.5 billion deal to take it public. Xtend makes an AI-enabled operating system for one-way attack drones — the kamikaze weapons that fly to a target and detonate. It had already secured a multi-million-dollar Department of War contract for one-way attack drone kits before Eric’s investment.

According to US Central Command, one-way attack drones are the first of their kind the Pentagon has ever deployed in combat. The Iran war created the demand. Xtend supplies the product. Eric invested as the war was entering its fourth week.

When asked directly about the conflict of interest accusations, Eric said: “I am incredibly proud to invest in companies I believe in. Drones are clearly the wave of the future.” He was more pointed in his Wall Street Journal response: “I happen to believe drones will be a much better investment than companies that still print newspapers.”

A few days after the US went to war with Iran, he wrote on X: “Drones are the wave of the future — Proud of this company and the work that they are doing to keep America safe.”

He was talking about his investment.

Eric’s American Bitcoin cryptocurrency mining company had already briefly pushed his personal stake to nearly $950 million at its Nasdaq peak. Forbes now estimates his net worth at approximately $400 million — a tenfold increase since inauguration day.

The Numbers

Before their father returned to the White House, Forbes estimated Don Jr.’s net worth at approximately $50 million and Eric’s at approximately $40 million.

By end of 2025, Don Jr.’s net worth had grown sixfold — driven by crypto ventures and 1789 Capital portfolio companies that received at least four federal contracts from the Trump administration.

Eric’s tenfold increase brings him to approximately $400 million. The Iran war, the Chinese drone ban, the Drone Dominance program, and the Pentagon contract pipeline are not yet fully reflected in those figures. The contracts keep coming.

Israeli-made Xtend AI-enabled one-way attack (kamikaze) drones, the type Eric Trump invested in during the early weeks of the Iran war and which the Pentagon has now deployed operationally. Source: Xtend / Israeli defense industry imagery (public promotional).

Combined, the two sons have added approximately $500-600 million to their personal net worth since inauguration day. The weapons being used in that war — the one-way attack drones, the AI-enabled kamikaze systems, the drone components — flow through a supply chain that includes companies owned, in part, by the president’s sons.

William Hartung of the Quincy Institute put it precisely: “If they can recruit the president’s son to join in boosting a particular firm, whether or not its product has been proven effective, they have a whole new level of influence, which can be wielded to serve their financial interests rather than the public interest.”

Kedric Payne of the Campaign Legal Center: “Presidents are expected to avoid even the appearance that they are using their office to financially benefit themselves or their family.”

The Reckoning

Anduril Industries high-speed combat drone in production — one of the major unmanned systems companies backed by 1789 Capital (Don Jr.) that received a potential $20 billion U.S. Army contract during the Trump administration. Source: Anduril Industries / Reuters

Fred Trump extracted $4 million from federal housing programs through inflated invoices, shell companies, and political connections that ensured the right people looked the other way. The template he built: use government programs to create leverage. Extract payment from the gap between policy and reality. Transfer the costs to taxpayers and the system. Claim credit for building something.

Fred applied the template to housing. Donald applied it to the presidency itself. Eric and Don Jr. have applied it to the wars the presidency starts, the contracts the Pentagon awards, and the policies the White House writes.

There is no investigation. There is no independent oversight. The SEC answers to Trump. The Pentagon’s senior leadership was screened by Trump Jr. The DOJ answers to Trump. Congress has not held a single hearing.

The Iran war has killed 13 American service members as of March 23, 2026. It has killed somewhere between 1,400 and 3,320 Iranians. It has wounded 200+ Americans and displaced 3.2 million Iranians. It has sent gas to $3.96 per gallon and diesel to $5.29 — costs transferred to every American who drives to work, every trucking company, every airline, every business that depends on fuel.

Fred Trump extracted $4 million over decades. His grandsons are extracting the equivalent in weeks, from a war their father started, with weapons their companies make, awarded by a Pentagon their selections populate.

Don Jr., when asked directly about profiting from his father’s position: “They created this monster.”

They did not. He did.

The Geography of Impunity

There is one remaining question this series cannot answer but accountability journalism requires asking.

The Trump sons are smart enough to have built this. They are presumably smart enough to see what comes after 2029. The statute of limitations runs for years — but it runs. The receipts are permanent. A new SEC, a new DOJ, a new administration uninterested in protecting the previous one — these are not hypotheticals. They are the predictable consequence of everything this series has documented across twelve installments.

The family’s combined wealth now runs to the billions. Jared Kushner, through his Gulf investment vehicle Affinity Partners, has raised $3 billion — primarily from Saudi Arabia’s sovereign wealth fund — since leaving the White House in 2021. It is, as Don Jr. acknowledged, a monster.

Monsters need somewhere to live.

The geography of the family’s business empire is instructive. The UAE has no extradition treaty with the United States. Saudi Arabia has no extradition treaty. Qatar has no extradition treaty. These are precisely the three countries where the Trump family has its most significant and fastest-growing business relationships — real estate towers in Dubai and Abu Dhabi, sovereign wealth fund investments, luxury golf developments outside Riyadh, a $400 million aircraft from the Qatari royal family, a $5.5 billion joint venture with Qatari DR announced this year.

Whether this is coincidence or contingency planning, only the family knows.

What is documentable is this: the countries where American prosecutors cannot reach the Trump family are the same countries where the Trump family has been most aggressively building its financial future. The exits from accountability have been under construction simultaneously with the grift itself.

Fred Trump died in 1999, before the full accounting of his schemes arrived. His son may not be present for his own reckoning — for reasons this series has documented elsewhere. But the grandsons are young, healthy, and very, very rich.

And they have been very carefully choosing their friends.

The house always wins.

Even when it has already planned which country to win from.
