The Counterfactual President

Domestic Policy Divergence

Part 5: Healthcare to Climate

April 17, 2026

In 2017, John McCain walked onto the Senate floor at 1:30 AM and gave a thumbs down.

That single gesture saved the Affordable Care Act from repeal. Trump and Congressional Republicans had spent months trying to eliminate healthcare coverage for millions—no replacement plan, just destruction because Obama’s name was attached to it.

McCain’s vote stopped them. Barely.

Now, after Trump’s first year back in power, we can assess what happened when he finally had the opportunity to resume his attacks on the ACA. We watched him systematically sabotage it throughout the year: weakened coverage requirements, destabilized insurance markets, expanded junk insurance plans, eliminated subsidies.

The human cost mounted over twelve months: coverage lost, medical bankruptcies increasing, people dying from treatable conditions because care became unaffordable.

This is where policy divergence became starkly visible during year one. Not philosophical differences about government’s role. Not competing visions. Simple, measurable questions: Do people have healthcare? Can they afford prescription drugs? Are they dying from preventable causes?

The counterfactual isn’t whether President Harris would have created perfect healthcare policy in her first year. It’s whether she would have actively tried to destroy what existed.

Let’s examine what that first year actually produced versus what it could have been.

Americans mobilized repeatedly to defend the Affordable Care Act — a law that required a single senator's courage to survive. Source: PBS NewsHour / KFF Health News

Healthcare: What a Year of Destruction Produced

Start with the most immediate, tangible difference: healthcare access.

What Trump did during his first year back:

He resumed attacks on the ACA from every angle throughout those twelve months:

  • Legal challenges to eliminate pre-existing condition protections

  • Administrative actions weakening coverage requirements

  • Funding cuts destabilizing insurance markets

  • Medicaid work requirements reducing coverage

  • Expansion of junk insurance plans

  • Elimination of subsidies

The goal wasn’t reform. It was demolition sustained over twelve months. The human cost was measurable: millions lost coverage throughout the year. Medical bankruptcies increased. People died from treatable conditions because care was unaffordable.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have strengthened and expanded the ACA during her first year:

  • Enhanced subsidies making coverage more affordable

  • Public option expanding competitive pressure

  • Prescription drug negotiation expanded

  • Medicaid expansion incentivized

  • Coverage gaps closed

But Harris would have faced enormous challenges throughout that first year: Progressive pressure for Medicare for All versus moderate concerns about feasibility. Labor unions divided. Healthcare industry lobbying against changes. Even with Democratic Congress, internal party tensions would have made comprehensive reform difficult throughout year one. The public option might have been the limit of achievable progress, disappointing progressives while enraging Republicans.

This isn’t revolutionary—it’s incremental improvement. Not transformation. Just making what exists work better while expanding access.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, millions lost coverage. Under Harris’s year one, millions would have gained it. That’s lives saved or lost.

And we spent a year watching lives lost.

Reproductive Healthcare: What a Year of Criminalization Produced

Trump appointed three Supreme Court justices to overturn Roe v. Wade. He succeeded. During his first year back, he supported state-level abortion bans, federal restrictions, criminalization of travel for care, and prosecution of healthcare providers.

What we watched during year one:

Federal policy supported state-level criminalization throughout those twelve months:

  • FDA restrictions on medication abortion

  • Enforcement actions against providers

  • Federal funding cuts

  • Support for fetal personhood legislation

Women died throughout that year—from sepsis when doctors refused treatment for miscarriages, from complications when forced to carry nonviable pregnancies, from unsafe abortion attempts when legal care was inaccessible.

After Dobbs, marching became the only legal remedy left in many states. Source: WHYY Philadelphia

The counterfactual:

Harris would have been explicit during her first year: She would have signed legislation codifying Roe protections. She would have defended access to medication abortion. She would have protected healthcare providers.

Through executive authority throughout that year she would have:

  • Protected FDA approval of medication abortion

  • Defended providers from state prosecution

  • Ensured veterans’ access to care

  • Supported travel for legal care

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, women faced criminalization and death. Under Harris, reproductive healthcare would have remained accessible.

That’s whether women lived or died based on policy choices.

And we spent a year watching women die.

Prescription Drugs: What a Year of Price Gouging Produced

The data is not ambiguous. Americans pay multiples of what other developed nations pay for identical medications. Source: KFF (Kaiser Family Foundation)

For decades, Medicare was prohibited from negotiating drug prices—a gift to pharmaceutical companies. Biden changed this through the Inflation Reduction Act, allowing Medicare to negotiate.

What Trump did during his first year back:

He sided with pharmaceutical companies throughout those twelve months:

  • Weakened Medicare drug price negotiation

  • Attempted to remove insulin price caps

  • Worked to repeal out-of-pocket spending limits

  • Blocked drug importation

The result over that year: Americans continued paying far more than other developed nations for identical drugs while pharmaceutical executives received massive bonuses.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have expanded drug price negotiation during her first year:

  • Increased drugs subject to negotiation

  • Extended negotiation to private insurance

  • Capped out-of-pocket costs

  • Allowed drug importation

  • Closed patent loopholes

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, patients paid more for essential medications. Under Harris, they would have paid less. That determined whether people could afford to stay alive.

And we spent a year watching people unable to afford medications.

Climate Policy: What a Year of Sabotage Produced

NASA's satellite record doesn't editorialize. It simply documents what accelerating climate disaster looks like from space. Source: NASA

Trump called climate change a hoax. During his first year back, he withdrew from the Paris Agreement again, gutted environmental regulations, promoted fossil fuel production, mocked climate science, and defunded climate research.

What we watched over twelve months:

Climate policy was actively reversed throughout the year:

  • Clean energy investments eliminated

  • Renewable energy development obstructed

  • Electric vehicle incentives removed

  • Building efficiency standards weakened

  • Climate adaptation defunded

  • International leadership abandoned

The human cost mounted during that year: More extreme weather, more deaths from heat and disasters, accelerating ecological collapse, billions in preventable damages.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have continued and expanded climate investment during her first year:

  • Maintained clean energy tax credits

  • Accelerated renewable deployment

  • Supported EV transition

  • Invested in adaptation and resilience

  • Rejoined international cooperation

But Harris would have faced significant obstacles throughout that first year: Fossil fuel industry opposition. Republican obstruction. Some labor union concerns. International coordination challenges. Balancing growth with emissions reduction. Managing regional impacts. Progressive pressure for faster action versus moderate concerns about disruption.

This isn’t eliminating fossil fuels overnight. It’s managed transition.

The Inflation Reduction Act was already producing results before Trump. Harris would have built on it during year one. Trump demolished it.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, climate disaster accelerated. Under Harris, mitigation would have proceeded.

And we spent a year watching acceleration.

Economic Policy: What a Year of Upward Redistribution Produced

The Pew Research Center has tracked American wealth inequality for decades. The line runs in one direction. Source: Pew Research Center

Trump’s 2017 tax cut: massive benefits for corporations and the wealthy, minimal benefits for everyone else, exploding deficits, no sustained wage growth.

What he did during his first year back:

Economic policy served the wealthy and corporations throughout those twelve months:

  • Extended and expanded tax cuts for top earners

  • Reduced corporate tax rates further

  • Weakened labor organizing protections

  • Eliminated overtime pay protections

  • Blocked minimum wage increases

The pattern was consistent over that year: concentrate wealth upward, weaken worker power, reduce corporate accountability.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have pursued policies addressing inequality during her first year:

  • Tax increases on high earners and corporations

  • Enhanced child tax credits

  • Expanded earned income tax credits

  • Protected union organizing rights

  • Maintained overtime protections

  • Supported minimum wage increases

This isn’t socialism—it’s returning to rates that existed under Republican presidents.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, wealth concentrated further at the top. Under Harris, policies would have addressed inequality.

And we spent a year watching inequality accelerate in a K economy. This is not OK

Immigration: What a Year of Cruelty Produced

The people crossing the border are fleeing conditions American foreign policy often helped create. Cruelty as deterrence has a three-decade track record of failure. Source: CNN

Trump’s immigration policy during his first year back was performance cruelty: raids, indefinite detention, mass deportations, border wall theater, elimination of asylum protections.

What we watched over twelve months:

Immigration policy escalated throughout the year:

  • Raids targeting long-term residents

  • Workplace enforcement disrupting industries

  • DACA protections eliminated

  • Refugee admission reduced

  • Asylum process gutted

  • Military deployed to border

The human cost mounted throughout that year: families destroyed, communities devastated, industries disrupted. And border security didn’t improve because cruelty doesn’t deter desperate people.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have pursued comprehensive reform during her first year:

  • Path to citizenship for long-term residents

  • DACA made permanent

  • Asylum process reformed but functional

  • Border security enhanced through technology

  • Legal immigration channels expanded

  • Root cause investments

This isn’t open borders—it’s functional immigration policy.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, cruelty as policy. Under Harris, policy as problem-solving.

And we spent a year watching cruelty.

Education: What a Year of Privatization Produced

The consequences of underfunding public education compound across generations. That's a feature of the policy, not a bug. Source: Parents for Public Schools / New Jersey Policy Perspective

Trump’s education policy during his first year back: weaken public schools, expand vouchers, eliminate the Department of Education, ban teaching about race and history, politicize curriculum.

What we watched over twelve months:

Education policy served privatization and culture war throughout the year:

  • Federal funding redirected to vouchers

  • Public school support reduced

  • Student loan forgiveness eliminated

  • Higher education funding cut

  • Title IX protections weakened

  • Curriculum mandates enacted

The goal wasn’t improving education—it was undermining public education while directing resources to private alternatives.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have strengthened public education during her first year:

  • Increased federal funding

  • Universal pre-K expansion

  • Teacher pay increases

  • Student loan forgiveness expanded

  • College affordability addressed

  • Title IX protections maintained

This isn’t federal takeover—it’s federal support.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, public education was weakened. Under Harris, it would have been strengthened.

And we spent a year watching public education undermined.

Social Safety Net: What a Year of Shredding Produced

Trump and Congressional Republicans targeted social safety net programs during his first year back: Medicaid work requirements, SNAP cuts, housing assistance reductions, disability benefit restrictions.

What we watched over twelve months:

Safety net programs faced systematic cuts throughout the year:

  • Medicaid coverage reduced

  • SNAP benefits restricted

  • Housing assistance cut

  • Disability benefits harder to maintain

  • Programs serving children defunded

The justification: reducing dependency. The reality: increased poverty, hunger, homelessness throughout that year.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have strengthened safety net programs during her first year:

  • Medicaid expanded

  • SNAP benefits maintained

  • Housing assistance expanded

  • Child poverty addressed

  • Social Security and Medicare protected

This isn’t creating dependency—it’s ensuring basic security.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, vulnerable Americans lost support. Under Harris, they would have gained it.

And we spent a year watching vulnerable people lose support.

The Human Cost: What Twelve Months Produced

These aren’t abstract debates. They’re questions of life and death, dignity and desperation, opportunity and deprivation.

What we measured after year one:

Healthcare: Millions lost coverage. People died.

Reproductive rights: Women died from preventable complications.

Prescription drugs: Seniors couldn’t afford medications.

Climate: Disasters accelerated.

Economic policy: Inequality grew.

Immigration: Families were separated.

Education: Public schools weakened.

Safety net: Poverty increased.

These are measurable outcomes over twelve months. Lives saved or lost. Families intact or destroyed. Futures possible or foreclosed.

The counterfactual isn’t utopia versus dystopia. It’s better versus worse, more people thriving versus more people suffering, problems addressed versus problems ignored.

And we spent a year watching worse, suffering, and problems ignored.

The Republican Cognitive Dissonance After Year One

I want to address Republican readers who claim to care about fiscal responsibility, life, family values, limited government, free markets.

What you watched during Trump’s first year back:

Tax cuts exploding deficits. Policies increasing maternal mortality. Family separation and child poverty. Government intervention in reproductive healthcare. Subsidies for favored industries.

The policy divergence exposed contradictions over twelve months. You can’t claim fiscal responsibility while supporting tax cuts for the wealthy. You can’t claim to be pro-life while opposing healthcare access. You can’t claim to support families while supporting policies that separate and immiserate them.

The counterfactual asks: Do you actually believe your stated principles, or do you just support whatever Trump does?

We spent a year getting the answer.

Why Domestic Policy Mattered Internationally This Year

My Canadian perspective: We watched American domestic policy throughout the year because it affects us and because American choices influence global trends.

When America demonstrates healthcare can be universal, it strengthens arguments globally. When America demonstrates the opposite, it empowers those arguing against access.

When America invests in climate, it accelerates transition. When America sabotages climate, it slows global action.

When America addresses inequality, it shows growth and shared prosperity are compatible. When America concentrates wealth upward, it encourages inequality everywhere.

American domestic policy sets precedents. The counterfactual mattered internationally because billions of people were affected by whether America showed democracy can address challenges or whether it demonstrated democratic governance fails.

And we spent a year demonstrating failure.

The Exhaustion of Watching a Year of Harm

I need to acknowledge: Writing about what we watched this past year feels like explaining that food prevents starvation, that medicine heals illness, that shelter protects from weather.

We spent twelve months debating whether people should have healthcare. Whether women should control their own bodies. Whether children should have enough food. Whether anyone should afford to stay alive.

These shouldn’t be controversial. In every other developed democracy, they’re not.

Only in America, only under Republican governance, are these basic aspects of civilized society treated as radical impositions.

Billions of people conducted the same thought experiment throughout that year: What would it be like to live in a country where government tried to solve problems rather than accelerate them? Where policy served broad public interest rather than narrow elite benefit? Where basic security and dignity were rights rather than luxuries?

That’s not utopian fantasy. That’s normal functioning governance in most developed democracies.

The counterfactual reminds us this past year didn’t have to be this way.

And we spent a year watching it be this way anyway.
