The Venezuela Invasion Series

The Stories Behind the Headlines

Week 1: While the World Watched Maduro’s Capture, the Invasion Set Off Cascading Effects Across Two Continents

January 9, 2026

January 09, 2026

CUBA: THE DOMINO FALLS

“It’s never been as bad as it is now.”

Omar Everleny Pérez, 64, Cuban economist who has lived in Cuba his entire life, described the island’s current state Wednesday.

The numbers tell the story:

  • 14-15 hour daily blackouts

  • Ration cards worthless (basics unavailable at subsidized prices)

  • Gasoline queue: One Havanan is 5,052nd in line—after signing up three months ago

  • 2.75 million fled since 2020 (out of 9.7 million population = 28%)

  • Economy declined three consecutive years

The Gasoline Queue

Cuba’s lifeline for decades: Venezuelan oil. Subsidized. Reliable. Essential.

Saturday: Trump cut it off. Venezuela’s blockade continues. Rodríguez’s government, desperate to accommodate Trump, can’t help Cuba even if it wanted to.

Trump on Wednesday: “Cuba looks like it is ready to fall.”

He’s right. Without Venezuelan oil, Cuba faces complete economic collapse. The blackouts worsen. The exodus accelerates. The regime has no options.

This is the secondary effect of Venezuela’s invasion: Cuba, already suffering from decades of U.S. embargo, loses its last major economic partner. The island enters free fall.

And Trump watches. Waiting for Cuba to collapse without firing a shot.

THE CARIBBEAN: CAPITULATION UNDER PRESSURE

Before Saturday: Caribbean nations largely refused to accept Venezuelan asylum seekers, citing capacity concerns.

After Saturday: Two nations reversed course.

Antigua & Barbuda

Announced it would accept 10 Venezuelan asylum seekers per year.

What changed? U.S. visa restrictions. The Trump administration made clear: Cooperate on Venezuela, or lose visa access for your citizens.

Ten asylum seekers per year isn’t humanitarian generosity. It’s symbolic compliance.

Dominica

Announced it would accept Venezuelan refugees. Number undisclosed.

Same pressure. Same result.

The pattern: Small Caribbean nations, economically dependent on tourism and remittances from citizens working in the U.S., cannot afford to defy Trump. When he applies pressure—visa restrictions, economic threats—they fold.

Venezuela’s invasion demonstrated the stick. Caribbean visa policy is the carrot’s absence.

These nations got the message: Accommodate Trump on Venezuela, or face consequences at home.

OAS: HEMISPHERE CONDEMNS (EXCEPT EL SALVADOR)

Monday, the Organization of American States convened emergency session to address Venezuela invasion.

Brazil, Mexico, Colombia strongly condemned the U.S. operation.

Mexico’s representative: “Intervention has never brought lasting stability to any nation.”

OAS Charter, Article 3: An act of aggression against one American state is an act of aggression against all.

The U.S. ambassador faced interruption: A protester shouted “Imperial oil grab!” during his remarks.

Only one nation backed the United States: El Salvador.

El Salvador’s president, Nayib Bukele, has cultivated close relationship with Trump. He sees alignment with U.S. as strategic. The rest of Latin America condemned. Bukele stood alone.

The vote revealed the divide: The hemisphere opposes U.S. military intervention. But opposition doesn’t mean action. Brazil, Mexico, Colombia condemned—then did nothing.

Because they watched what happened to Petro. Condemn publicly. Accommodate privately. Survive.

EUROPE: PANICKING PRIVATELY, SILENT PUBLICLY

The New York Times’ Steven Erlanger reported Wednesday: “There is a massive gap between public and private reactions from European leaders.”

Mark Leonard, director of the European Council on Foreign Relations: “Privately, European leaders are panicking about what happens next, especially in Greenland. But publicly on Venezuela, they are desperate not to say anything critical or invoke international law on Trump at a time of maximum peril for Ukraine.”

Nathalie Tocci, director of Italy’s Institute of International Affairs: “U.S. foreign policy now is imperial, and consistently imperial.”

Translation: Europe knows what Trump is doing. They know it violates international law. They know it sets dangerous precedent.

But they need U.S. support for Ukraine. So they stay silent on Venezuela. And issue carefully worded statements on Greenland.

The Seven-Leader Joint Statement

Monday: Seven European leaders signed joint statement defending Greenland’s sovereignty:

  • Emmanuel Macron (France)

  • Friedrich Merz (Germany)

  • Giorgia Meloni (Italy)

  • Donald Tusk (Poland)

  • Pedro Sánchez (Spain)

  • Keir Starmer (United Kingdom)

  • Mette Frederiksen (Denmark)

The statement: “Greenland belongs to its people” and invoked UN Charter on sovereignty.

Wednesday: Britain provided air surveillance, naval support, and bases for U.S. forces seizing Russian-flagged tanker Marinera in the North Atlantic.

The split screen is stark: Britain signs statement defending sovereignty Monday. Britain helps U.S. seize ships in international waters Wednesday.

Ursula von der Leyen, EU Commission President, Wednesday in Cyprus: “Law is stronger than force.”

But when force is applied by America, Europe accommodates.

France’s Foreign Minister: “Long Gone Are Those Times”

Jean-Noël Barrot, French Foreign Minister Wednesday: “Long gone are the times where you could buy yourself Louisiana.”

Except: Trump isn’t buying Greenland. He’s threatening military force unless Denmark sells.

And Europe, needing U.S. for Ukraine, won’t stop him.

Canada: The Exception

Canada is backing Denmark. Prime Minister Mark Carney visiting Copenhagen. Canada opening consulate in Greenland in February.

Why Canada acts where Europe hesitates: Canada shares Arctic with Greenland. U.S. control of Greenland threatens Canadian sovereignty. Europe can avoid Greenland issue. Canada cannot.

But even Canada’s support is limited: Diplomatic backing, not military. If Trump seizes Greenland, Canada will condemn. Then accommodate.

THE MILITARY OPERATION: DETAILS EMERGE

80+ Killed in Saturday’s Raid

Venezuelan soldiers, Cuban advisers, intelligence personnel. Killed defending Maduro’s compound.

Wednesday: Funerals held in Caracas. Families and military gathered. Rodríguez’s government used funerals for propaganda—portraying defenders as martyrs.

But the defenders failed. Delta Force breached the compound. Maduro was extracted. The funerals, while emotionally powerful, proved the futility of resistance.

Two U.S. Special Ops Troops Still Hospitalized

Pentagon update Wednesday: Two American Special Operations forces injured in Saturday’s raid remain hospitalized. Conditions not disclosed.

Trump hasn’t mentioned them. He celebrated the operation’s success (”no Jimmy Carter disaster”). But American casualties—even non-fatal—undercut the narrative of surgical, cost-free intervention.

The Kentucky Training Facility

Trump revealed Wednesday in New York Times interview: He tracked the training of forces for the operation “down to the creation of a life-size replica of the compound at a military facility in Kentucky.”

This wasn’t improvised. Months of planning. Purpose-built training facility. Full-scale mockups.

Trump: “I don’t know that [Carter] would have won the election, but he certainly had no chance after that disaster.”

He was referring to April 1980 Iran hostage rescue failure—helicopter crash in desert, eight American dead, operation aborted.

Trump contrasted Saturday’s success: “You didn’t have a Jimmy Carter crashing helicopters all over the place, that you didn’t have a Biden Afghanistan disaster.”

80+ defenders dead, 2 Americans hospitalized, Venezuelan research center destroyed—but Trump considers it success because Maduro was captured and no American helicopters crashed.

THE RESEARCH CENTER: COLLATERAL DAMAGE

Wednesday, Venezuela’s Institute of Scientific Research announced: Two U.S. missiles hit its facilities during Saturday’s raid.

One missile identified: AGM-154C-1, precision-guided glide bomb.

Damage:

  • Mathematics department “completely devastated” (housed servers, computer networks)

  • Physics, chemistry, ecology, nuclear technology departments damaged

Director Alberto Quintero released video showing metal fragments from missile.

The institute’s statement: “This is an unprecedented act of imperial aggression. There is no justification to attack a sanctuary of science.”

Why did precision-guided munitions hit a research institute?

Possible explanations: Intelligence failure. Dual-use targeting (nuclear technology department). Collateral damage. Intentional degradation of Venezuela’s scientific capacity.

None explain how “precision” weapons hit civilian research center.

But it reveals the cost of Trump’s “success.”

MADURO’S POW STATUS CLAIM

Maduro, now in Brooklyn detention center, is claiming prisoner-of-war status under Geneva Conventions.

Legal basis: Manuel Noriega precedent. When U.S. invaded Panama (1989), captured Noriega, tried him in Miami—Noriega claimed POW status. Got special prison conditions. Wore military uniform at trial.

Legal experts say Maduro will lose: There’s no state of war between U.S. and Venezuela. Congress didn’t declare war. No armed conflict recognized under international law.

But if granted: Could affect prison conditions, trial procedures, post-conviction detention.

The claim itself is strategic: Maduro framing his capture as military operation between nations, not law enforcement action against criminal.

He’s partly right. This was military invasion, not arrest. But admitting that would undercut Trump’s justification: bringing “criminal” to justice under U.S. indictment.

THE SHADOW FLEET: BREAKOUT PARTIALLY SUCCEEDS

Monday morning: At least 16 sanctioned oil tankers disappeared from Venezuelan ports where they’d been docked for weeks.

Desperate gamble: Flee simultaneously using fake names, spoofed coordinates, radio silence. Overwhelm U.S. surveillance through saturation.

Wednesday: Two seized. Marinera (Russian-flagged) in North Atlantic. Sophia (stateless) in Caribbean.

Thursday: New York Times confirmed via satellite imagery—at least nine tankers have evaded the naval blockade so far. Four tankers sailing hundreds of miles into the Atlantic Ocean. Several heading northeast through Caribbean. At least one changed flag to Russia.

According to official Russian vessel registry: At least four tankers switched to Russian flags after Saturday, attempting to evade U.S. seizure by claiming Russian protection.

U.S. boarded Marinera anyway. Russia had sent naval escort vessel—but it wasn’t present when U.S. Coast Guard boarded. Avoided standoff.

The score so far: 9 escaped (for now), 2 captured. The blockade isn’t 100% effective—but it’s still devastating. Venezuela’s internal projections show 75% production collapse by year’s end if blockade holds. Nine tankers escaping doesn’t change that math. U.S. naval dominance remains overwhelming. Satellite tracking comprehensive. Most escapees will eventually be intercepted or unable to unload at destination ports where U.S. pressure prevents buyers from accepting delivery.

OIL EXECUTIVES: PRIVATE DOUBTS

It was ALWAYS about oil

Friday, Trump is scheduled to meet oil executives at White House. Chevron, Exxon, ConocoPhillips, Halliburton, SLB, Weatherford, Baker Hughes expected.

The pitch: Invest in Venezuela’s oil fields. Trump controls the government. Security guaranteed. Profit opportunities enormous.

The executives’ private concerns:

Scenario 1: Trump leaves office January 2029. New administration doesn’t want to run Venezuela indefinitely. Withdraws. Investments collapse.

Scenario 2: Venezuela’s military and intelligence—currently cooperating under duress—wait for American attention to shift, then sabotage operations. Assets seized or destroyed.

Scenario 3: Political opposition in U.S. forces withdrawal before oil infrastructure rebuilt. Investments stranded.

Trump said Wednesday: Reviving Venezuela’s oil sector “will take a while.” Energy Secretary Wright: “Tens of billions of dollars and significant time” to reach historical production levels.

That’s a decade-plus commitment. And executives don’t trust Trump’s ability—or willingness—to maintain control that long.

So they’re hesitant. Attending White House meeting. Listening to pitch. But not committing.

This is the vulnerability in Trump’s plan: Phase 2 (Western corporate access) requires corporate buy-in. Corporations aren’t buying in.

SENATE DEMOCRATS INVESTIGATE

Wednesday, five Democratic senators sent letters to eight major oil companies:

Senators Sheldon Whitehouse (RI), Ron Wyden (OR), Elizabeth Warren (MA), Peter Welch (VT), Brian Schatz (HI). Senator Edward Markey (MA) sent separate inquiries.

The questions:

  • What advance knowledge did you have of Venezuela operation?

  • Did you help shape foreign policy decisions?

  • What meetings occurred with Trump administration officials?

  • What are projected costs to rebuild Venezuela’s oil industry?

  • Will American taxpayers foot the bill?

Trump admitted Sunday: He spoke with oil companies about Venezuela “before and after. And they want to go in.”

So they knew. Question is: How much influence did they have on the decision to invade?

The senators: “American taxpayers cannot afford to take on these expenses.”

But Trump’s plan: Make Venezuela pay through seized oil revenues. Use that money to fund “recovery” (Phase 2) that gives Western companies access.

Corporate welfare disguised as humanitarian intervention.

FLORIDA REPUBLICANS: TRAPPED

Saturday night, Miami’s three Republican members of Congress faced repeated questions: Why did Trump dismiss María Corina Machado?

Representative Mario Diaz-Balart insisted: “I’m convinced that when there are elections...the next democratically elected president of Venezuela is going to be María Corina Machado.”

Notice what’s missing: Any explanation for Trump’s rejection. Any theory for how Machado gets from hiding to presidency. Any timeline for elections.

Trump said Wednesday: U.S. oversight of Venezuela will last “much longer” than a year. “Only time will tell.”

Florida Republicans are trapped between constituents who love Machado (Nobel Prize winner, 93% primary victory, democratic opposition leader) and Trump who rejected her for Maduro’s vice president.

Nelson Jiménez, 55, Venezuelan immigrant: “We were a little surprised by what he said about María Corina. I think he’s wrong.”

But Trump doesn’t care about Venezuelan-American voters in Miami. He cares about oil. And Machado couldn’t deliver oil without demanding democracy.

So Florida Republicans offer vague assurances (”she’ll be president eventually”) without explaining how, when, or why Trump would allow it.

GREENLAND: THE NEXT TARGET

Trump’s Greenland demands escalated this week alongside Venezuela operation.

Rubio: Trump Wants to BUY Greenland

Monday, Rubio told lawmakers: Trump wants to purchase Greenland from Denmark, not invade militarily.

White House: Won’t rule out military force.

The contradiction is strategic: Rubio offers diplomatic path (purchase). White House maintains military threat. Denmark faces choice: Sell, or risk U.S. military action.

U.S. Already Has “Sweeping Access”

1951 defense agreement gives U.S. military bases, radar installations, surveillance systems in Greenland. Analysts noted: “The U.S. has such a free hand there... can pretty much do what it wants if it just asked nicely.”

So what does Trump want that he doesn’t already have?

White House Press Secretary Leavitt Wednesday: Acquiring Greenland would give U.S. “more control over the Arctic region” and deterrence against Chinese and Russian influence. “There would be many other benefits as well that the president and his national security team are currently talking about.”

Translation: Full sovereignty. Not just bases. Control.

Greenland’s Opposition

85% of Greenlanders oppose U.S. takeover according to recent polling.

Denmark defending sovereignty. But with Venezuela demonstrating Trump’s willingness to use military force, how long can Denmark resist?

And Europe, needing U.S. for Ukraine, is “panicking privately” but staying quiet publicly.

Venezuela was the demonstration. Greenland is the next application.

KEY VENEZUELAN PLAYERS: THE POWER STRUCTURE ENDURES

Saturday’s raid captured Maduro. But the institutions he built remain intact.

Diosdado Cabello (Interior Minister)

Controls internal security, intelligence networks, colectivos (armed militias). Second most powerful chavista after Maduro. Hasn’t been removed. Continues running repression apparatus.

Wednesday this week: Colectivos established checkpoints across Venezuela. Boarding buses. Searching phones for keywords: “invasion,” “Maduro,” “Trump.” Anyone celebrating Maduro’s capture faces arrest.

Cabello’s survival proves: Capturing leader doesn’t capture system.

Vladimir Padrino López (Defense Minister)

Controls military. Hasn’t publicly defected despite Maduro’s capture. Military remains under his command, loyal to chavista structure if not to captured leader.

This is Trump’s problem: He can’t replace Padrino López without risking military revolt. So he works with him. Which means military remains chavista.

Jorge Rodríguez (National Assembly President)

Brother of Delcy Rodríguez (interim president). Led Monday’s National Assembly session—277 of 285 members chavista. Made “V” gesture: “Nosotros venceremos” (Together we will win).

The siblings run Venezuela now: Delcy as president, Jorge as legislative power. Both longtime chavistas. Both accommodating Trump to survive. But both embedded in system that wants Maduro back.

Nicolás Maduro Guerra (”The Prince”)

Maduro’s son. Legislator. Indicted in U.S. for cocaine conspiracy. Monday: “If we normalize the kidnapping of a head of state, no country is safe.”

He’s right. But he’s also indicted. Will U.S. extract him next? Or work with him because removing him destabilizes Rodríguez government?

These four—Cabello, Padrino López, Jorge Rodríguez, Maduro Guerra—represent the power structure Trump must either co-opt or remove. He’s chosen co-option. Which means chavismo endures.

Accommodation in Action: Political Prisoners Released

Thursday morning, Jorge Rodríguez announced: Venezuela would release “an important number” of political prisoners, both Venezuelans and foreign citizens.

Among those freed:

  • Rocío San Miguel - one of Venezuela’s most prominent political prisoners, investigated authoritarian government even as others fled. Detained nearly two years. Now at Spanish embassy in Caracas with daughter.

  • Five Spanish nationals - Spain confirmed, called it “positive step in new phase Venezuela is currently undergoing”

Venezuelan state TV: Gave short statement, took no questions, moved on to pro-Maduro rally.

This is accommodation. Rubio’s demands from Monday included: expel foreign advisers, reopen oil trade, release political prisoners, hand over oil control. Venezuela is complying. Release prisoners to satisfy Trump. Prove cooperation. Survive.

The message works both ways: For Venezuela—accommodation keeps regime alive. For other nations watching—resistance is futile, accommodation is survival.

THE LEGAL QUESTIONS: UNRESOLVED

War Powers

Constitution requires congressional authorization for military action except in cases of imminent threat to U.S. or Americans.

Trump claimed Venezuela operation was law enforcement: Executing warrant for Maduro’s arrest under 2020 indictment.

But: 15,000 troops deployed. Dozen warships. Airstrikes. Military invasion by any definition.

Congress hasn’t authorized war with Venezuela. Republicans control both chambers. Haven’t held public hearings. Aren’t demanding answers.

Democrats object but don’t act: Letters to oil companies (investigative theater). Demands for hearings (Republicans refuse). No moves to cut funding or invoke War Powers Resolution.

So Trump governs Venezuela without congressional authorization.

Ted Lieu Draws Constitutional Red Line - For Greenland

Thursday, Representative Ted Lieu (D-CA) issued stark warning about potential Greenland invasion:

“If any military member, from the generals on down, participates in using force against Greenland without congressional authorization, they are following illegal orders.”

Lieu continued: “That is not rhetoric. That is the law. And he is putting everyone on notice.”

“Orders that violate the Constitution are not orders. They are illegal commands, and obedience is not a defense.”

This is the Nuremberg principle: “I was following orders” doesn’t absolve responsibility for illegal acts.

Lieu emphasized timing: “This is not said after the damage is done. It is a preemptive warning. A flare shot into the sky saying: think very carefully before you cross this line.”

The constitutional argument is sound. Article I, Section 8 gives Congress power to declare war. War Powers Resolution allows president to use force without authorization only for 60 days in cases of attack on U.S. or imminent threat.

Neither applied to Venezuela. Neither applies to Greenland.

But here’s the problem: Lieu’s warning is too late for Venezuela (damage done, 80+ killed, no consequences) and may be too early for Greenland (military already followed unconstitutional orders for Venezuela, why refuse for Greenland?).

Still, Lieu’s statement establishes: At least some Democrats recognize the constitutional crisis. And military officers now have public legal warning that Greenland orders without congressional authorization would be illegal.

Whether they refuse remains to be seen. Venezuela proved Trump can invade without authorization and military will comply. Lieu is betting Greenland—a NATO ally—will be different.

Senate Advances War Powers Resolution (52-47)

Thursday afternoon, hours after Lieu’s warning, the Senate voted to advance a resolution requiring Trump to seek congressional authorization for continued Venezuela military operations.

Five Republicans broke ranks to join Democrats:

  • Susan Collins (Maine) - up for re-election 2026

  • Lisa Murkowski (Alaska)

  • Rand Paul (Kentucky)

  • Josh Hawley (Missouri)

  • Todd Young (Indiana)

The vote was “a rare assertion of congressional authority over the president’s war powers,” reflecting “worries in Mr. Trump’s own party about his bellicose and seemingly open-ended action in Venezuela, undertaken without consultation or authorization by Congress.”

Senator Rand Paul: One factor that tipped his vote was Trump’s comment to the New York Times that the U.S. might remain involved in Venezuela for years. “The vote sent a clear signal that only Congress could order the country to go to war.”

Senator Chuck Schumer: “Donald Trump promised no more endless wars. Yet he’s sliding straight into another one in South America.”

Trump’s immediate response: Attacked the five senators on social media. “Susan Collins, Lisa Murkowski, Rand Paul, Josh Hawley, and Todd Young should never be elected to office again. They should be ashamed.” Accused them of undermining national security and “taking away our Powers to fight and defend the United States of America.”

VP JD Vance dismissed the resolution: Called War Powers Act “a fake and unconstitutional law” that “is not going to change anything about how we conduct foreign policy.”

The reality: Vote is largely symbolic. House unlikely to pass. Trump would veto. But it puts senators on record, reflects GOP anxiety about open-ended commitment, and establishes constitutional marker for future accountability.

Venezuelan state TV celebrated: “Today, the Senate has clearly stated, by a majority vote: ‘No more war.’ That all hostilities and any type of attack within Venezuelan soil must cease.”

Control of Venezuelan Oil Revenues

Trump announced Tuesday: He will personally control billions of dollars from Venezuelan oil sales.

Constitutional problem: Article I gives Congress control over government spending. No money can be expended except as appropriated by legislation.

Trump has no legal authority to seize another nation’s oil revenues and control their disbursement.

But he’s doing it anyway. And Congress isn’t stopping him.

UN Human Rights Office Condemnation

UN human rights office declared Venezuela invasion a violation of international law. Sovereignty violations. Unlawful use of force.

Trump doesn’t care. U.S. isn’t party to International Criminal Court. UN Security Council can’t act (U.S. veto power).

International law matters only if enforced. Trump’s calculation: No one will enforce it against America.

He’s right.

Saturday: Delta Force extracts Maduro. Demonstration of power.

Sunday: Trump threatens Colombia. Delcy Rodríguez accommodates.

Monday: Caribbean nations capitulate. Europe issues statements. National Assembly defies but doesn’t act.

Tuesday: Trump announces oil seizure. Petro posts about “senile brain.”

Wednesday: Two tankers seized. Rubio unveils three-phase plan. Trump confirms “much longer” than year. Petro capitulates after hour-long call. Europe “panics privately.” Cuba in free fall.

Thursday: Senate rebukes Trump 52-47, five Republicans break ranks. Trump attacks them. Vance dismisses War Powers Act as "fake." Venezuela releases political prisoners (Rocío San Miguel, five Spanish nationals). Nine tankers confirmed escaped blockade. Ted Lieu draws constitutional red line for Greenland. Trump tells NYT his power is limited only by "my own morality."

Friday: Trump meets oil executives. Pitch: Invest in Venezuela. Reality: Executives hesitant.

The pattern: Each day brought new evidence that Venezuela’s invasion wasn’t about Venezuela. It was about reordering the hemisphere. Demonstrating power. Forcing accommodation. Opening markets. Seizing resources. And intimidating anyone who might resist.

Week One achieved Trump’s objectives:

CONCLUSION: THE CASCADING EFFECTS

Saturday’s headlines were simple: Maduro captured.

Week One’s reality was complex: Cuba enters free fall. Caribbean reverses asylum policies. Europe panics privately. Colombia capitulates. Military details emerge. Legal questions multiply. Oil executives express private doubts. Shadow fleet partially escapes (9 tankers evade, 2 seized). Venezuelan power structure endures. Venezuela releases political prisoners to accommodate. Senate rebukes Trump 52-47 but likely symbolic. And Trump tells the Times his power is constrained only by “my own morality”—not international law, not Congress, not treaties.

These are the stories behind the headlines. The secondary effects. The cascading consequences.

Venezuela’s invasion wasn’t an isolated operation. It was the first domino.

Cuba, losing Venezuelan oil, faces economic collapse.

Caribbean nations, facing U.S. pressure, reverse asylum policies.

Colombia, watching Delta Force extract Maduro, capitulates to Trump within 72 hours.

Europe, needing U.S. for Ukraine, stays silent despite recognizing Trump’s imperialism.

Venezuela releases political prisoners—accommodation working.

Senate rebukes Trump with rare bipartisan vote—but Vance dismisses War Powers Act as “fake.”

Oil executives, invited to profit from occupation, privately doubt Trump can maintain control long enough to justify investment.

And Latin America recalibrates: Every leftist leader watching Venezuela, watching Colombia, watching accommodation work and resistance fail, calculating whether to submit or resist.

Most will accommodate. Because Venezuela demonstrated the cost of resistance. Colombia demonstrated the speed of capitulation. And Thursday demonstrated Congress can object but not stop.

Week One showed what Trump’s “America First” foreign policy means for Latin America:

Not partnership. Domination.

Not alliance. Subordination.

Not cooperation. Compliance.

And Thursday showed Trump’s philosophy: “My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

Venezuela was the demonstration. Week One was the cascade. And the pattern continues to accelerate.
