Israel/Palestine: The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

A Series Introduction

April 9, 2026

By Stephen Gordon

Jerusalem, viewed from the Mount of Olives. The golden Dome of the Rock marks the Temple Mount — the single most contested square kilometre on earth, sacred to three faiths, claimed by all of them, peacefully shared by none. Everything in this series begins here.

Let me tell you what this series is not.

It is not a brief for Hamas or Hezbollah. It is not a denial of the Holocaust, the millennia of Jewish persecution, or the legitimate human need for refuge that drove the Zionist project. It is not anti-Semitic — a charge so promiscuously deployed against anyone who questions Israeli state policy that the word itself is in danger of losing its meaning precisely when the world needs it most. It is not a declaration that Israel has no right to exist.

It is also not going to pretend that what is happening in Gaza and Lebanon is anything other than what it appears to be — the systematic destruction of civilian populations and the infrastructure that sustains human life — while the Western democracies that built the modern international legal order either applaud, equivocate, or quietly write the cheques. Gaza has been reduced to rubble. Lebanon has been targeted with a ferocity that its civilian population did not choose and cannot escape. Both are consequences of a chain of decisions that this series will trace to its origins.

A Canadian journalist of a certain age, watching the Middle East burn in real time on a screen in Toronto, has no dog in this fight. That is precisely the point. What I have instead is the stubborn conviction that complexity is not a reason for silence, that “it’s complicated” has become the most morally cowardly sentence in the English language, and that the people dying — in Gaza, in Lebanon, in Israel — deserve better than our carefully managed discomfort.

So let us begin honestly.

On Abraham and What He Does and Does Not Prove

Three of the world’s great religions — Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — share a common ancestor in the patriarch Abraham, and all three locate their deepest spiritual roots in a narrow strip of land on the eastern shore of the Mediterranean. This is a historical and anthropological fact of staggering importance. It explains why Jerusalem is not just a city but a wound that three billion people feel personally. It explains why this conflict generates a heat entirely disproportionate to the land mass or population involved. It explains why rational actors do irrational things when Jerusalem is on the table.

What it does not explain — what it does not justify, sanction, or make morally coherent — is the removal of living people from land they have inhabited for generations on the basis of a divine promise recorded in an ancient text.

I say this with full respect for faith, including the faith of those who hold that promise sacred. But the moment we accept theological claim as the operating basis for 21st century territorial law, we have opened a door through which every religious nationalist on earth is entitled to walk. The Crusaders walked through it. They were certain God was on their side too. The results, as we shall see, were not encouraging.

The Abraham narrative matters in this series because it is genuinely, consequentially believed — by Jewish religious Zionists, by forty million American evangelical Christians, by Muslim believers with their own Abrahamic inheritance in the same land. You cannot understand the conflict without understanding the depth and sincerity of those beliefs. But sincerity is not justification. People have sincerely believed catastrophically wrong things throughout human history, and the job of journalism — the job of anyone trying to make honest sense of the world — is to say so plainly, with respect for the believer and clear eyes about the belief.

So: Abraham will appear in this series. He will not be settling it.

On Jerusalem, Where We Will Begin

There is a square kilometre of real estate in the Old City of Jerusalem that contains the Western Wall — the last remnant of the Second Temple, Judaism’s holiest site. Directly above it sits the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque, the third holiest site in Islam, built on the Temple Mount that Jews call Har haBayit and Muslims call Haram al-Sharif. A ten minute walk away stands the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, built on the site where Christians believe Jesus was crucified, buried, and resurrected.

Three faiths. One square kilometre. Every claim genuine. Every claim non-negotiable. Every claim, in the minds of its believers, superseding every other.

If you want to understand why the Middle East has not known peace, stand in that square kilometre and look slowly in every direction. The conflict is not about land, not really. It is about the meaning of land — about which human stories get to count, which inheritances get honoured, which suffering gets weighted most heavily in the scales of history.

That is the problem this series attempts to trace honestly. Not to solve it — no writer in Toronto, or anywhere else, is going to solve it — but to follow the chain of decisions, most of them made by powerful men who bore none of the consequences, that transformed a genuine theological and human complexity into the specific catastrophe we are watching unfold in real time.

In 2019, a South African producer named Master KG released a song called Jerusalema — a prayer in Zulu asking the city to be his home, to guide him, not to leave him behind. It became the most danced-to song on earth during the pandemic lockdowns. People danced to it in hospital corridors, on rooftops, in refugee camps, in village squares on six continents. Three billion people already knew instinctively what he meant. Nobody needed a translation. That is the city this series is about — the one that every human tradition reaches toward, that no political arrangement has ever successfully resolved, and that continues to cost lives at a rate that should shame every government that has had the power to act and chosen comfort instead.

On Method

Each instalment of this series will focus on a specific period, decision, or actor. The aim throughout is accountability without demonization — a distinction that has become nearly impossible to maintain in current discourse, where any criticism of Israeli state policy is called antisemitism and any acknowledgment of Israeli security concerns is called complicity in genocide.

I reject both of those shortcuts.

The history here is documented. The decisions were made by named people in named places for articulable reasons, many of them cynical, some of them genuinely well-intentioned and catastrophically wrong. The suffering — Palestinian and Israeli — is real. The Western role in creating and sustaining this situation is not a conspiracy theory. It is the foreign policy record of the last hundred years, available in any serious archive.

I will name what I see. I will cite what I claim. I will distinguish between what is documented and what is my analytical judgment. I will not pretend to a neutrality I do not possess — no honest writer does — but I will apply the same standards of evidence and accountability to every party.

The people of Gaza deserve that rigour. So do the Israelis living under the genuine threat of a regional war they did not entirely choose. So do the Lebanese, the Iranians, and the Canadians wondering what their government’s silence makes them party to.

This series has thirteen parts. It begins in Jerusalem, where everything begins. It will not end with answers, because the honest answers do not yet exist. It will end, I hope, with a clearer understanding of how we got here, who got us here, and what it would actually take — not in fantasy but in political reality — to get somewhere else.

That is the most a journalist can offer. It is also, in times like these, not nothing.
