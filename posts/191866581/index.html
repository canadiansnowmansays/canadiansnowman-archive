The Iran Crisis

“TACO”

Part LVI: Day Twenty-Seven: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate — All Before Noon. Then the War Continued Anyway.

March 24, 2026

An apartment building hit by a strike in Tehran on Monday.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

I. The Move

At approximately 7:17 AM Eastern on Monday, as the 10-year Treasury yield approached 4.40 percent — the precise threshold that caused Trump to reverse his most bruising tariffs last April — and as Brent crude traded above $114, President Trump posted on Truth Social that the United States and Iran had held “VERY GOOD AND PRODUCTIVE CONVERSATIONS REGARDING A COMPLETE AND TOTAL RESOLUTION OF OUR HOSTILITIES IN THE MIDDLE EAST” and that he had instructed the Pentagon to postpone any strikes on Iranian power plants for five days.

Brent fell from $114 to below $96 within minutes. S&P 500 futures swung from negative to positive 1.5 percent. The 10-year yield retreated from 4.40 to 4.35 percent. The bond market exhaled. The S&P 500 closed up 1.15 percent — its best single day since the war began.

Iran said no such talks had taken place.

This series has used the TACO framework — Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate — since its earliest essays. Day Twenty-Seven of the Iran war is the purest single-day expression of that framework this series has documented.

The threat: Saturday night — 48-hour ultimatum, obliterate power plants, biggest one first, deadline approximately 7 PM Eastern Monday.

The accommodation: Sunday — messages exchanged through intermediaries. Vivian Salama and Jonathan Lemire, writing in The Atlantic, confirmed that Trump’s Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff communicated with the Iranian government through Pakistan and other regional intermediaries, presenting a 15-point plan based on the proposal offered to Iran before the war began.

The capitulation: Monday 7:17 AM — five-day extension dressed as diplomatic breakthrough. Trump told reporters the talks had produced “many, like 15 points of agreement” and that “we’ll see how that goes, and if it goes well, we’re going to end up with settling this. Otherwise, we’ll just keep bombing our little hearts out.”

The obfuscation: Iran denied talks occurred. Iran’s Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf called Trump’s announcement an attempt to “escape the quagmire in which the US and Israel are trapped” and an effort to calm rattled energy markets. Iranian state broadcaster IRIB said Trump “out of fear of Iran’s response, backed down from his 48-hour ultimatum.” Three Iranian officials confirmed to the Times that Araghchi and Witkoff had spoken by phone — but characterized those as “preliminary discussions on de-escalation,” explicitly not negotiations.

David Sanger, writing in the Times, identified the box Trump had painted himself into: striking power plants would have violated the Geneva Conventions and triggered exactly the Gulf-wide infrastructure retaliation he had been trying to prevent when he scolded Israel for hitting South Pars. Backing down while claiming diplomatic progress was the only available exit. The 10-year yield provided the timing. The announcement provided the cover.

Israel launched a new wave of strikes on Tehran’s IRGC headquarters during the announcement of productive peace talks. The war continued. The markets moved. By Tuesday morning, Brent had rebounded to $102 — the TACO bounce already fading as reality reasserted itself.

One question this series cannot answer but accountability journalism requires asking: the market moved sharply and predictably on Trump’s announcement. Brent fell from $114 to below $96 in minutes. S&P futures swung from negative to plus 1.5 percent. The 10-year yield retreated from 4.40 to 4.35. Who knew the announcement was coming, and when did they buy?

II. Iran in the Living Room

Morteza Nikoubazl / NurPhoto / Getty

Karim Sadjadpour, writing in The Atlantic, has published the most analytically complete account of Iran’s strategic theory — and it reframes Monday’s events precisely.

Iran’s strategy in every confrontation with the United States since 1979 has been the same: not to win on the battlefield, but to make the war too costly for the American public to continue. The 1983 Beirut barracks bombing killed 241 Americans. Reagan had resolve until Congress didn’t. Iran-supplied IEDs killed as many as 1,000 Americans in Iraq. Bush had resolve until six in ten Americans called his war a mistake. “Iran wins by not losing,” Sadjadpour writes. “Trump loses by not winning.”

The weapons this time are missiles, drones, and geography. The Strait closure has removed 11 million barrels per day from global supply. Americans are paying $3.98 per gallon nationally and $5.35 for diesel — up 34 and 42 percent respectively since the war began.

The opinion data Sadjadpour cites is striking: nine in ten Democrats oppose the strikes, as do most independents. Fifty percent of Americans oppose the war and only 40 percent support it. Even within the Republican Party, non-MAGA Republicans are split — about 54 percent supportive. Iran’s foreign minister has been actively cultivating MAGA fractures, posting that Trump has turned “America First” into “Israel First.” Iranian state TV has amplified Tucker Carlson and Joe Kent. Tehran doesn’t just want to turn Americans against the war. It wants to turn Americans against one another.

“This is ultimately a war between a democracy’s impatience and a theocracy’s ruthless endurance,” Sadjadpour writes. “The question is whether, for the first time since 1979, Tehran has finally met a U.S. president more committed to destroying the regime than the regime is to destroying him.”

Monday’s TACO move — the capitulation timed precisely to bond market pressure — suggests the answer, at least for now, is no.

III. The 82nd Airborne

Troops from the 82nd Airborne Division deploying from Fort Bragg, N.C., to Eastern Europe in response to the war in Ukraine in 2022.Credit...Kenny Holston for The New York Times

While Trump was announcing peace talks, the Pentagon was planning ground operations.

Greg Jaffe and Eric Schmitt, writing in the Times, reported that senior military officials are weighing a possible deployment of a combat brigade from the Army’s 82nd Airborne Division to support US military operations in Iran. The forces would come from the 82nd’s Immediate Response Force — approximately 3,000 soldiers capable of deploying anywhere in the world within 18 hours.

The likely mission: seize Kharg Island, Iran’s main oil export hub, in conjunction with the 2,200 Marines from the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit now approaching the Gulf. The Marines would go in first — their combat engineers can repair the airfield that US bombing raids have already damaged. Once the airfield is repaired, the Air Force begins flowing supplies by C-130. The 82nd would augment and relieve the Marines, providing the sustained ground presence the expeditionary unit cannot maintain beyond two weeks.

Salama and Lemire confirmed in The Atlantic that Senator Lindsey Graham has pushed vigorously, in public and in private, for a Kharg Island seizure. Trump has expressed reluctance to risk numerous American casualties. The fighting, officials note, would likely be fierce.

By Friday — the new deadline — approximately 4,500 Marines will be in the region, Sanger noted in the Times. The five-day extension is not a pause in preparation. It is a preparation window.

In early March, the Army abruptly canceled the 82nd Airborne headquarters’ participation in a training exercise at Fort Polk — keeping the command element at Fort Bragg in case the order came. That cancellation was three weeks ago. The balloon, as military planners say, has not yet gone up. But the string has been cut.

IV. The Point of No Return

Erika Solomon, writing in the Times, documented what the infrastructure strikes have already done — before Trump’s announcement, before the five-day pause, before any diplomacy real or claimed.

Israeli strikes on Tehran’s fuel depots turned the skies orange and enveloped the capital in toxic fumes and acid rain. The Andimeshk hospital was damaged Saturday. The South Pars strike sparked an energy crisis in Iran. And on the first day of the war, a US strike on a girls’ elementary school killed approximately 175 people, most of them children.

The International Committee of the Red Cross on Monday issued its most direct warning of the war: “What we have seen in recent days in the Middle East risks reaching a point of no return. War on essential infrastructure is war on civilians. It must stop.” Its president, Mirjana Spoljaric, noted the particular danger of nuclear facilities — both sides have now deliberately targeted each other’s nuclear infrastructure.

Omid Memarian of DAWN named the strategic self-destruction embedded in this approach: the infrastructure attacks “have achieved what the Islamic Republic had long tried and failed to do: convincing people that the military campaign is not against the government, but against the Iranian people, the Iranian state, and their very existence.”

Esfandyar Batmanghedjli of the Bourse & Bazaar Foundation calculated the asymmetry: Iran has nearly 500 power plants compared to 50 in Israel. Its largest plant provides 4 percent of Iran’s total capacity; Israel’s largest provides a fifth of its country’s energy. “If Trump hits a major power plant, Iran will respond by hitting utilities in the Gulf states and Israel — including power plants and desalination facilities — and will count on the fact that it has to hit fewer targets to have a bigger impact.”

The five-day pause may have averted a catastrophe. Or it may have simply deferred it to Friday.

V. What a Deal Would Actually Require

Trump has said the talks have produced “15 points of agreement” and that he seeks “a complete and total resolution.” Sanger mapped what that would actually require in the Times.

Getting oil flowing through the Strait of Hormuz is the most urgent issue. But equally important: the 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade uranium stored at Isfahan and Natanz. Trump’s stated demand remains an end to Iranian nuclear enrichment and elimination of all uranium stockpiles — terms Iran has previously rejected. It was the breakdown of precisely these negotiations between Kushner, Witkoff, and Araghchi that led to the February 28 strikes in the first place.

Salama and Lemire confirmed in The Atlantic that Turkey and Pakistan have floated proposals for in-person meetings — Araghchi with Witkoff and Kushner in one scenario, Vice President Vance with Ghalibaf in another. Vance, whose long-held isolationist views have put him at odds with some in the administration, may also travel to Islamabad in the coming days. None of the meetings have been scheduled.

Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group identified the distance remaining: “Not attacking energy infrastructure is a low bar. The terms of a cease-fire, or an agreement that would resolve the longer-term problems including the fate of the stockpile or reopening of the strait — none of those things are anywhere close to the finish line right now.”

VI. Jiu-Jitsuing the Iranians

While Trump was announcing productive peace talks, his Treasury Secretary was announcing something that deserves its own paragraph of silence before analysis.

The Trump administration has temporarily lifted sanctions on 140 million barrels of Iranian oil currently sitting at sea. At approximately $100 a barrel, this represents a potential $14 billion windfall — to the country the United States is simultaneously bombing.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent described the move on NBC’s Meet the Press as an act of martial artistry: “In essence, we are jiu-jitsuing the Iranians. We are using their own oil against them.”

Richard Nephew, a former State Department and National Security Council official who helped negotiate the Iran nuclear agreement under Obama, offered a more precise description: “This is asking them to please, please, please sell oil because the market is going haywire.” He added: “The result of all of this is they are conceding the fact that the Iranians have leverage over whether the U.S. can continue to prosecute the war. You have gone to the Iranians and told them, ‘You have our Achilles’ heel.’”

The Foundation for Defense of Democracies noted the license contained “no escrow mechanism and no obvious restrictions on payment channels” — meaning the $14 billion flows to Iran without the blocking mechanisms Bessent described on television.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and other Trump administration officials are struggling to explain the logic of lifting sanctions on Iranian oil.Credit...Eric Lee for The New York Times

Alan Rappeport, writing in the Times, documented the irony precisely: in 2016, Trump called the first $400 million installment of Obama’s $1.7 billion payment to Iran a “scandal.” He revived that criticism three days before lifting sanctions on $14 billion in Iranian oil. “Remember when they sent Boeing 757s over there, loaded with cash?” Trump said. “That’s not going to happen with Trump.”

Senator Mark Warner: “I remember when my Republican colleagues blasted Barack Obama over $400 million tied to hostages. Where’s the outrage now?”

The outrage is not coming. The oil is flowing. The war continues.

VII. Ofer Moskovitz

Before the diplomacy and the market moves and the TACO analysis, there was a man named Ofer Moskovitz.

He was 60 years old, a farmer, a kibbutz official, known to everyone as “Poshko.” He lived in Misgav Am on Israel’s border with Lebanon, a 400-person community. He had been featured in Israeli news in recent days as an unofficial spokesman for the border region.

“A missile or a drone could fall on me at any moment,” he told a Haifa radio station on Friday. “It’s Russian roulette.”

On Sunday, a car he was riding in was blown up. Hezbollah claimed responsibility. The Israeli military initially agreed. Then on Monday, the military said an initial review showed “flaws in the planning and execution” of Israeli artillery fire — five rounds fired “at an incorrect angle” aimed at the wrong ridge. Ofer Moskovitz was killed by his own country’s military. He was buried in Kibbutz Amiad, near the Sea of Galilee. He had celebrated the birth of his second grandchild the week before.

His brother Ron, reached after the funeral, said the family harbored no animosity toward the soldiers responsible. “In war, these are things that happen.” But he was less forgiving of the political leadership.

“The residents of the North were promised that the threat had been removed. People want to be told the truth. There are nuances, there are complexities. Leaders who are incapable of communicating complexity to their people — they should not be leaders.”

He was talking about Israeli leaders. He was talking about all of them.

VIII. The War Expands

The five-day pause covers US strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure. It covers nothing else.

Overnight Monday into Tuesday, Iran fired a new type of missile at Tel Aviv — carrying a warhead of approximately 100 kilograms, described by Israeli military Colonel Miki David as “something we have not yet encountered in the war.” Four impact sites were reported across Israel. At least three residential buildings sustained extensive damage. Six people were treated for injuries in Tel Aviv. Search and rescue teams were deployed to southern and central Israel.

Iran struck the Kurdish region of Iraq with six ballistic missiles in two separate pre-dawn attacks — killing six Kurdish fighters and wounding 30 others. The Kurdistan Regional Government condemned the strikes “in the strongest terms.” Iran did not comment. This is the first documented Iranian strike on the US-aligned Kurdish autonomous region since the war began — a significant geographic expansion of the conflict.

Kuwait’s electricity ministry reported that seven power lines in several areas of the country were taken out of service after sustaining damage from falling debris from air defense interceptions. The Kuwaiti army said it was confronting drone and missile attacks for the second consecutive night since about midnight.

In Anbar Province, Iraq, a Popular Mobilization Forces commander and several of his fighters were killed in a strike on their headquarters. The group, an umbrella organization of Iran-backed militias under Iraqi state supervision, blamed the United States. US forces have previously targeted PMF positions that have attacked American assets including the US Embassy in Baghdad.

US Central Command confirmed it had conducted more than 9,000 strikes in Iran since February 28, damaging or sinking more than 140 Iranian naval vessels. Israeli strikes hit Hezbollah infrastructure in Beirut Monday night — at least seven explosions heard around the Lebanese capital. The Dallafa Bridge in southern Lebanon was struck, the latest in a series of attacks on transit routes. A person was killed in a strike east of Beirut, which Israel said targeted an IRGC member.

The United Nations peacekeeping force in southern Lebanon reported that a projectile hit a building at its headquarters in Naqoura. The UN said it believed the projectile was fired by “a non-state actor” and recorded “intense gunfire and explosions” near its headquarters in recent days.

Qatar’s remote work mandate — in place since the war began — was terminated. Qataris return to in-person work Tuesday. A small signal of normalcy, in a region where normalcy has become unrecognizable.

IX. Lax Americana

Naila Ruechel for The New York Times

Carlos Lozada, writing in the Times, has published the analytical capstone that this war’s fourth week demands: “The U.S. Is No Longer the Leader of the Free World.”

His argument is not partisan. It is historical. Pax Americana — the US-led system of alliances and institutions that promoted American interests and values and helped avoid major conflicts in the decades after World War II — is gone. In its place, Lozada argues, is what he calls Lax Americana: “a careless and uninhibited and incurious U.S. superpower that struts across the chess board, threatening old friends and enabling old rivals, seeking short-term gains, heedless of the dangers it is creating for itself and for the world.”

The distinction he draws is precise: “The United States wants the benefits of hegemony, but without accepting the responsibilities — ensuring collective security, promoting economic openness, nurturing vital alliances — that come with it. Trump doesn’t care to be a superpower; he just likes to wield superpowers.”

The evidence from this war is abundant. The 22-country Hormuz coalition remains a declaration, not a fleet — confirmed warships in the strait: zero. European leaders who initially declined to help drew Trump’s fury: “We don’t need anybody. We’re the strongest nation in the world.” The EU Commission President, speaking in Canberra this week, called on Iran to cease attacks and allow free navigation — but said the EU could “envisage an operation” only after hostilities end. Australia moved an aircraft to the UAE and supplied defensive missiles. No one is joining the war.

Lozada invokes Robert Kagan’s warning from January: “The American might that upheld the world order of the past 80 years will now be used instead to destroy it.” He cites the foreign-policy theorist Immanuel Wallerstein on the consistency beneath Trump’s apparent contradictions between isolationism and intervention: “Fear and disdain, combined with the assumption that our way of life is pure and should not be defiled by involvement in the miserable quarrels of others, unless we are in a position to impose on them our way of life.”

His closing assessment deserves to stand alone: “We are not entering a post-American world, one in which the United States recedes from the stage or stops wielding its military might. Far from it. But we may be entering a post-America world, one in which the meaning of America, the principles and values the country has long stood for — sometimes in reality, sometimes in aspiration — are fading. And the loss of that America may prove just as damaging, and far more lasting, than any harm Donald Trump’s excursions can inflict.”

The world’s policeman, as Lozada puts it, is on the take.
