Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 8: The Canadian Exception — Friends Across the Water

March 18, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau looks on as Cuban President Fidel Castro gestures during a visit in Havana on Jan. 27, 1976. THE CANADIAN PRESS/Fred Chartrand

In the winter of 1976, as the Cold War was entering one of its more frigid phases, the Prime Minister of Canada arrived in Havana with his wife and their four-month-old son. Pierre Elliott Trudeau was the first leader of a NATO country to visit Cuba since the Revolution, and the optics were everything Washington feared they would be. Two hundred and fifty thousand Cubans lined the streets of Havana to watch the motorcade. Trudeau addressed a crowd at a rally in Cienfuegos and, carried away by the moment or by calculated provocation — with Trudeau it was often hard to tell — shouted into the microphone: “Viva Cuba! Viva Fidel Castro!”

The diplomatic fallout was immediate. The U.S. State Department expressed displeasure. John Diefenbaker, the former Canadian Conservative prime minister, denounced the visit as lending legitimacy to a communist dictatorship. Members of Parliament demanded explanations. The visit, they said, was inappropriate, embarrassing, incompatible with Canada’s alliance obligations.

Trudeau was entirely unmoved. Canada had maintained diplomatic and trade relations with Cuba since 1945 — since before the Revolution, and continuously through it — and he saw no reason to abandon them simply because the United States had decided Cuba was an enemy. Canada was an ally of the United States, not a satellite. It had its own foreign policy, its own interests, and its own judgment about how to engage the world. Engagement, Trudeau believed, served people better than isolation. And the people of Cuba, whatever one thought of their government, deserved to be treated as people.

This was the Canadian Exception — the stubborn, principled, occasionally infuriating insistence on a different path — and it is worth examining carefully, because it illuminates something important both about Cuba’s story and about what kind of country Canada has been and is choosing, in this difficult moment, to remain.

Why Canada Stayed

Canada’s decision to maintain relations with Cuba after the Revolution was not a reflex or an accident. It was a deliberate choice, made in the context of enormous American pressure to join the embargo, and rooted in several distinct Canadian interests and values.

The most pragmatic was trade. Canadian companies — banks, mining firms, agricultural exporters — had significant business interests in Cuba, and the embargo created an opportunity as much as a problem. With American firms excluded from the Cuban market, Canadian companies found themselves in a position of unusual advantage. The Royal Bank of Canada and the Bank of Nova Scotia maintained operations in Cuba long after American banks were forced out. Canadian mining interests operated in Oriente province. Canadian agricultural exporters supplied food that Cuba could not obtain from the United States. The bilateral trade that resulted was never enormous by Canadian standards, but it was consistent, and it established patterns of economic relationship that persisted through decades of American hostility.

There was also a foreign policy calculation that went deeper than trade. Canada had spent the better part of the twentieth century carefully constructing an independent foreign policy identity — distinct from both the British imperial tradition it was leaving behind and the American hegemonic tradition it was determined not to simply adopt. The Cuba question was, in this context, a test case. If Canada abandoned its relationship with Cuba simply because Washington demanded it, the message to the world — and to Canadians — was that Canadian foreign policy independence was notional. If Canada held its course, the message was different: Canada was a country that made its own decisions.

Diefenbaker, for all his criticism of Trudeau’s 1976 visit, had actually established this principle himself in the early 1960s, when he resisted American pressure to join the embargo. He was suspicious of Castro and had no illusions about the Revolution’s authoritarianism. But he was equally suspicious of American demands for automatic allied compliance, and he recognised that Canada’s credibility as an independent actor depended on its willingness to occasionally say no.

And underneath the trade interests and the foreign policy calculations was something harder to articulate but genuinely real: a Canadian moral discomfort with the use of civilian suffering as a foreign policy weapon. The embargo was designed to make ordinary Cubans miserable enough to revolt. Canadians, looking at that design, found it troubling — not because they were naive about Cuban politics, but because they had a different instinct about how you treat people you disagree with.

Pierre and Fidel

Trudeau and his wife, Margaret, and Castro listen to the national anthems of both countries after the Trudeaus arrived in Havana. (Fred Chartrand/Canadian Press)

The friendship between Pierre Trudeau and Fidel Castro was, by all accounts, genuine — one of the more improbable personal relationships in Cold War history, and one that resists easy characterisation.

They were superficially similar in certain ways: both intellectuals, both lawyers by training, both men of considerable personal charisma and absolute certainty in their own judgment, both capable of inspiring intense loyalty and intense frustration in equal measure. Both had transformed their countries’ political cultures through sheer force of personality. Both were, in their different ways, romantics about history and their own place in it.

But their politics were almost diametrically opposed. Trudeau was a liberal democrat, committed to individual rights, the rule of law, and a vision of Canada as a pluralist society where citizens could flourish in freedom. Castro was a revolutionary authoritarian who had built a one-party state, imprisoned political opponents, and decided that the rights of the collective outweighed the rights of the individual in every contest. They disagreed, fundamentally, about almost everything that mattered.

And yet they liked each other enormously. Castro respected Trudeau as one of the few Western leaders who talked to him as an equal rather than treating him as a problem to be managed. Trudeau found in Castro a mind genuinely worth engaging — a man who had thought seriously about colonialism, development, and the inequities of the international economic order in ways that connected with his own intellectual concerns. Their friendship was, as Trudeau’s son Alexandre later wrote, “extra-political” — it existed in a space that was not about ideological alignment but about mutual recognition.

The friendship had practical consequences. Canada provided a back-channel between Cuba and the United States during several tense moments in the 1970s and 1980s. Canadian officials, trusted by both sides to some degree, could carry messages that neither superpower could deliver directly. This quiet diplomatic role — the role of honest broker, of the country that talks to everyone — was one of the most valuable things Canada did in the Cold War, and it was only possible because Canada had not joined the embargo.

Fidel Castro and Justin Trudeau at Pierre Trudeau's state funeral, Montreal, September 2000. The man who had held Justin’s brother Michel as a four-month-old infant in Havana had come to say goodbye to his father.

When Pierre Trudeau died in September 2000, Castro flew to Montreal for the state funeral. He was an honorary pallbearer, one of the very few foreign leaders present. He was photographed embracing a grieving Justin — then twenty-nine, who had met Castro at his father’s side since childhood, who had grown up with this extraordinary figure as something like a family friend, and who now stood in the autumn rain of a Montreal street saying goodbye to both of them at once.

Varadero and the Tourist Connection

Varadero, Cuba — where generations of Canadians escaped the winter. The vintage American car is a reminder of everything that stopped in 1960, and everything that didn't.

While the diplomatic relationship between the two governments was the formal expression of the Canada-Cuba connection, the deeper and more personal one was built on beaches.

Canadian tourism to Cuba grew steadily through the 1980s and exploded after the Soviet collapse in the early 1990s, when Cuba desperately needed foreign currency and opened its tourism sector to international investment. Canadian hotel chains — Sunwing, Air Transat, WestJet charters — built a mass market for Cuban sun holidays that became, for a generation of Canadians, the default answer to the question of where to spend February.

At the peak, Canadians made approximately 1.3 million visits to Cuba per year — accounting for more than 40 percent of all tourist arrivals. The image of the Canadian in Varadero became a cultural fixture: the pale northerner, emerging from a long winter, discovering the particular pleasure of rum, warm water, and music played by musicians who could not easily leave the country they were playing in. The relationship was transactional, obviously, and not without its complications — the ethical questions about supporting an authoritarian economy through tourism are real, and Canadians discussed them, though not always in ways that changed their travel plans. But it was also, at its best, genuinely human. Canadians and Cubans talking across the pool, playing dominoes, sharing meals, developing the kind of person-to-person connections that official policy can never fully replicate.

This was what Justin Trudeau was describing in November 2016, when he stood in Havana as Prime Minister and told his audience that of all the links between Canada and Cuba, “the most significant ones exist not by way of commercial or development ties, but through one-on-one, person-to-person connections.” He was speaking of something real. Generations of Canadians had a relationship with Cuba — personal, particular, accumulated over years of winter holidays — that Americans simply did not have and could not have, because their government had decided Cuba did not exist.

The Trudeau Visit of 2016 and Castro’s End

Justin Trudeau’s official visit to Cuba in November 2016 was the forty-year echo of his father’s historic trip, and it was shadowed from the beginning by the knowledge that Fidel Castro, now ninety years old and gravely ill, might not survive much longer.

The visit lasted thirty hours. Trudeau met with President Raúl Castro — Fidel had formally transferred power to his brother in 2008 — and held substantive talks on climate change, gender equality, and economic cooperation. He spoke at the University of Havana, charming the crowd with his colloquial Spanish and his evident genuine affection for the country. His wife Sophie held her own parallel events on gender equality.

Fidel was too ill to receive him. The old revolutionary, who had held Michel Trudeau as an infant in 1976 and had wept at Pierre’s funeral in 2000, could not come downstairs for one last conversation with the son of his closest friend. The two men communicated through intermediaries. It was the last near-miss of a relationship that had spanned three generations.

Ten days later, on November 25, Fidel Castro died.

Justin’s statement of condolence described Castro as “a larger than life leader who served his people for almost half a century” and noted his “significant improvements to the education and healthcare of his island nation.” The statement drew immediate and fierce criticism internationally, particularly from Cuban-Americans and from politicians across the Western world who felt it glossed over Castro’s authoritarianism, his political prisoners, his firing squads. The criticism was not entirely unfair — the statement was notably short on acknowledgment of the regime’s human rights record.

But the reaction also revealed something about the different ways in which Cuba is seen from different vantage points. From inside the Cuban exile community in Miami, Castro was unambiguously a monster. From inside Cuba itself, the reality was more complicated — a leader who had genuinely improved some lives, genuinely destroyed others, and presided over a system of control that left little room for the kind of nuanced judgment that Trudeau was attempting. From Canada, from the perspective of a country that had never broken relations, never joined the embargo, never required its citizens to see Cuba through the binary lens of Cold War enmity — the picture was different again.

None of these perspectives is the whole truth. All of them contain part of it.

The Changed Equation

The Canada-Cuba relationship of 2026 is not what it was in 1976, or even in 2016. The calculus has shifted in ways that illuminate both the crisis Cuba is now facing and the broader deterioration of the Canadian-American relationship under Trump.

Cuban tourism has been in freefall. The accumulated crises of the past decade — hurricanes, economic collapse, the COVID shutdown, blackouts, fuel shortages — have made Cuba a difficult and sometimes uncomfortable destination even for the most committed Canadian sun-seekers. The Varadero hotels still stand, but the lights go off unpredictably, and the certainty of a reliable holiday that once made Cuba the automatic choice for February escapes has evaporated.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the ledger, Canadians are staying away from the United States in numbers that are genuinely unprecedented. American border crossings by Canadians have dropped sharply as Trump’s tariffs, his annexation rhetoric, and the general hostility of his administration toward Canada have made Americans feel less like neighbours and more like adversaries. Canadians who once chose Cuba over Florida, then Florida over Cuba, are now choosing neither.

The irony is sharp: a generation of Canadians built their sense of the Caribbean through Cuba, the place their American friends couldn’t go. Now they are building their sense of themselves in relation to an America they are beginning to feel they can’t go to either — not comfortably, not safely, not without a sense of crossing into hostile territory.

Pierre Trudeau, who spent his career insisting that Canada was not and would never be a satellite of American power, might have recognised something in this moment. The pressure is different now — tariffs and rhetoric rather than Cold War alliance demands — but the essential challenge is the same: how does a smaller country maintain its values, its independence, and its relationships with the world when the large country next door has decided that loyalty means compliance?

Canada’s answer, then and now, is the same one Trudeau gave in 1976 when he stood in Cienfuegos and shouted into the Caribbean air. We make our own choices. We talk to everyone. We do not abandon people because it would be convenient to do so.

Viva Cuba. It remains, in 2026, a principled thing to say.
