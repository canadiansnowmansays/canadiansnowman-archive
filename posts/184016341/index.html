The Paradox, Emboldening Both Democracy and Authoritarianism

One Week After Maduro’s Capture, Iran Erupts and Russia Escalates—Opposite Effects From the Same Demonstration of Power

January 9, 2026

January 9, 2026

THURSDAY NIGHT: IRAN GOES DARK

Thursday evening, Iran’s internet went offline. NetBlocks and Georgia Institute of Technology monitoring systems showed “an abrupt and near-total drop in connection levels.” The country was almost completely cut off from the global internet.

The reason: Protests demanding regime change had exploded across multiple cities. Tehran, Mashhad, Bushehr, Shiraz, Isfahan, Karaj. Crowds chanting “Death to Khamenei.” Government buildings burning. Security forces firing weapons. At least 40 people killed since late December.

Friday morning, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei addressed the nation: “Last night in Tehran and in some other places, a group of vandals came and damaged buildings belonging to their own country just to please the president of the United States.”

He wasn’t entirely wrong.

FRIDAY MORNING: RUSSIA ESCALATES

Friday, Russia’s Defense Ministry announced it had struck western Ukraine with an Oreshnik—a nuclear-capable intermediate-range ballistic missile.

This was only the second time in the war Russia had fired this type of missile. The first was November 2024, hitting central Ukraine with dummy warheads as a symbolic nuclear threat.

But Friday’s target was different: Western Ukraine. Near the Polish border. Near NATO.

The New York Times noted: “Russia is escalating the fighting in Ukraine even as it has offered a muted response to challenges in other places around the globe, including in Venezuela.”

Where, last Saturday, the Trump administration ousted Russia’s ally, President Nicolás Maduro.

Where, Wednesday, the United States seized a Russian-flagged oil tanker in the North Atlantic.

Russia’s response to Venezuela: Muted. Request for crew release (granted Friday). No military escalation.

Russia’s response in Ukraine: Nuclear-capable missile near NATO border.

ONE WEEK, TWO OPPOSITE EFFECTS

Saturday, January 3: Delta Force extracted Nicolás Maduro from his bedroom in Caracas. Eighty Venezuelan and Cuban defenders killed. U.S. warships blockaded Venezuela. Trump announced indefinite American control.

The intended message: American power is absolute. Accommodate or face extraction.

The intended audience: Latin America. Specifically, leftist governments that might resist U.S. demands.

The intended effect: Colombia capitulated within 72 hours. Caribbean nations reversed asylum policies. Venezuela released political prisoners. Accommodation worked.

But demonstrations of raw power send messages to multiple audiences.

Week One ended with two simultaneous, contradictory global effects:

Iran’s opposition emboldened: Protests reaching unprecedented scale. Regime shutting down internet. Khamenei blaming Trump. Opposition betting Trump might actually intervene.

Russia’s regime emboldened: Escalating in Ukraine with nuclear-capable weapons. Accepting Venezuela loss. Compensating by escalating where it can. Sending message: If U.S. ignores international law, so can we.

This is the Venezuela paradox.

IRAN: OPPOSITION EMBOLDENED

The country severed nationwide internet access as anti-government demonstrations calling for regime change swept through multiple cities.CreditCredit...Kamran/Middle East Images, via Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

The Timing

Late December: Protests began over currency collapse. Merchants closing bazaars. Economic grievances.

January 3: Trump invades Venezuela.

January 6-8: Protests explode in scale and scope. Multiple opposition groups coordinate. Reza Pahlavi (Shah’s son), Kurdish groups, Nobel laureate Narges Mohammadi (from detention) all calling for Thursday 8 PM demonstrations.

January 8 (Thursday night): Massive crowds. Tehran, Mashhad, Bushehr, Shiraz, Isfahan, Karaj. Affluent neighborhoods joining for first time. “Death to Khamenei.” “Long live the Shah.” Government buildings burning.

January 9 (Thursday night): Iran shuts down internet nationwide.

January 9 (Friday morning): Khamenei blames protesters trying to “please” Trump.

The correlation is not coincidental.

Trump’s Statement

Last week, Trump said the United States “would come to the aid of protesters in Iran” if the government used lethal force against demonstrations.

This is not 2009. When Barack Obama offered rhetorical support to Iran’s Green Movement but made clear the U.S. would not intervene. The regime cracked down. The movement collapsed.

This is 2026. A week after Trump invaded a sovereign nation, captured its president, killed 80+ defenders, and announced indefinite U.S. control.

Iranian protesters believe Trump’s statement. Because Venezuela proved Trump acts, not just talks.

What Protesters Saw

Saturday in Venezuela:

  • Delta Force breached heavily-fortified presidential compound

  • Killed 80+ Venezuelan and Cuban defenders

  • Extracted Maduro and wife within hours

  • Trump announced indefinite U.S. control

  • International law irrelevant

Wednesday, Trump told the New York Times: His power is limited only by “my own morality. My own mind.”

Pressed about international law: “I don’t need international law.” Then: “It depends what your definition of international law is.”

Iranian protesters’ calculation: If Trump invaded Venezuela to remove authoritarian leader, if Trump says his power is limited only by his own judgment, if Trump promised to help Iranian protesters—maybe he actually will.

This is the opposite of 2009. Obama promised support but wouldn’t act. Trump promises support after demonstrating willingness to invade.

Khamenei Confirms the Connection

A picture made available by the office of Iran’s supreme leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei on Saturday. Credit...Office Of The Iranian Supreme Leader, via Reuters

Friday speech to supporters in Tehran:

“There are people whose job is only about destruction. Last night in Tehran and in some other places, a group of vandals came and damaged buildings belonging to their own country just to please the president of the United States.”

Then: “He wants to side with the rioters and harmful individuals. If he can, he should go and run his own country.”

Khamenei recognizes: Venezuela emboldened Iranian opposition. Trump’s intervention in Venezuela makes his promises to Iranian protesters credible.

“If he can, he should go and run his own country” = Khamenei worried Trump might intervene in Iran like Venezuela.

The Internet Blackout

Thursday afternoon, Iran went offline. NetBlocks: “Near-total drop in connection levels.” Georgia Tech: Country “almost completely offline.”

The regime’s calculation:

  • Protesters coordinating via internet

  • Outside support (U.S., Israel) via internet

  • Video evidence of crackdown reaching world via internet

  • Possibility of U.S. intervention (like Venezuela) if crackdown visible

So cut the internet. Isolate protesters. Prevent coordination. Hide crackdown.

This is not routine repression. This is regime panic.

Omid Memarian, Iranian human rights expert: “The Iranian government uses internet shutdowns as a tool of repression. Whenever protests reach a critical point, authorities sever the country’s connection to the global internet.”

Thursday was that critical point. Because Venezuelan opposition watched Maduro extracted. And Iranian opposition thought: That could be Khamenei.

The Scale

At least 40 killed since late December (Amnesty International says 28 between Dec. 31-Jan. 3, other groups say 40+ total).

Cities affected: Tehran, Mashhad, Bushehr, Shiraz, Isfahan, Karaj, Tabriz, Kerman, Gonabad.

Demographics: Young and old. Men and women. Affluent neighborhoods (Shahrak Gharb) joining for first time.

Slogans: “Death to Khamenei.” “Long live the Shah.” “Freedom, freedom.” “The street will prevail.” “Clerics get lost, the shah is coming back.”

Violence: Government buildings burned. Seminary in Gonabad stormed, clerics beaten. Security forces firing weapons, tear gas.

Bazaar strikes: Tehran, Tabriz, Isfahan, Mashhad, Kerman. Heart of Iran’s economy. Could paralyze country if continued.

Regime response: Judiciary head Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Eje’i: “This time it’s different. This time there are no excuses left. I tell the people and the families that this time no one will be spared.”

Revolutionary Guards taking over from regular security forces. This is not police action. This is military crackdown.

All of this one week after Venezuela.

RUSSIA: REGIME EMBOLDENED TO ESCALATE

A Ukrainian soldier firing a howitzer toward the front lines near Pokrovsk, Ukraine, last month.Credit...Tyler Hicks/The New York Times

Friday’s Oreshnik Strike

Friday morning, Russia’s Defense Ministry announced: Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile struck western Ukraine.

The missile: Nuclear-capable. Can carry conventional, dummy, or nuclear warheads. Based on RS-26 Rubezh intercontinental ballistic missile, redesigned with shorter range. Part of Russia’s strategic nuclear force.

The target: Western Ukraine. Near Lviv. Near Polish border. Near NATO.

Previous use: November 2024, hit central Ukraine (Dnipro) with dummy warheads as symbolic nuclear threat after U.S./UK granted Ukraine permission to strike deep into Russia.

Friday’s use: Unclear if dummy or conventional warheads. But target choice deliberate.

Why western Ukraine? To send message to Europe—specifically Poland (NATO/EU member backing Kyiv in peace talks).

Ukraine’s Foreign Minister Andriy Sybiha: The strike is “a grave threat to the security of the European continent and a test for the trans-Atlantic community.”

President Zelensky: “A clear reaction from the world is needed. Above all from the United States, whose signals Russia truly pays attention to.”

Russia’s “Muted Response” to Venezuela

Saturday: U.S. invaded Venezuela, ousted Russia’s ally Maduro.

Wednesday: U.S. seized Russian-flagged oil tanker Marinera in North Atlantic. Russia had sent naval escort but wasn’t present when U.S. Coast Guard boarded. Avoided standoff.

Friday: Russia requested release of two crew members from Marinera. U.S. granted request. Russia thanked Washington.

The New York Times noted: “Russia is escalating the fighting in Ukraine even as it has offered a muted response to challenges in other places around the globe, including in Venezuela.”

Russia’s calculation:

  • Can’t stop U.S. in Venezuela (Western hemisphere, overwhelming U.S. naval dominance)

  • Can’t stop U.S. from seizing Russian-flagged tankers (international waters, U.S. Coast Guard superiority)

  • Accept losses in Venezuela, negotiate crew release

  • But escalate where Russia has leverage: Ukraine

Compensatory escalation: Lose in Venezuela, escalate in Ukraine.

The Message to Europe

Target: Western Ukraine near Polish border.

Timing: One week after Venezuela, amid U.S.-led peace talks.

Weapon: Nuclear-capable Oreshnik.

Russia’s message to Europe:

  • You’re backing Kyiv in peace talks

  • U.S. invaded Venezuela without congressional authorization

  • U.S. seized our tanker in international waters

  • U.S. says international law applies only when Trump decides it does

  • If U.S. ignores international law, so can we

  • This nuclear-capable missile near your border is reminder: We can escalate

Zelensky recognized it: Demanded U.S. response. “Russia must receive signals that it is its obligation to focus on diplomacy, and must feel consequences every time it again focuses on killings.”

But Russia watched Trump’s NYT interview Wednesday: “My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.” “I don’t need international law.” “It depends what your definition of international law is.”

Russia’s response: If Trump’s power is limited only by his own judgment, Putin’s is too.

Putin’s Calculation

Venezuela showed:

  • U.S. will invade without congressional authorization

  • U.S. will ignore international law when convenient

  • U.S. will kill 80+ defenders without consequence

  • U.S. will impose indefinite control and call it “stabilization”

Putin’s takeaway: International law doesn’t constrain major powers. Only strength matters.

So Putin escalates in Ukraine: Fire nuclear-capable missile near NATO border. Claim it’s response to fake “attack on Putin residence” (U.S. intelligence concluded no such attack occurred). Assert Russia’s right to escalate unilaterally.

Trump invaded Venezuela based on “my own morality.”

Putin fires Oreshnik based on his own judgment.

Both leaders watched the other demonstrate that international law is optional for powerful nations.

THE DUAL MESSAGE

Trump invaded Venezuela to demonstrate American power. The intended audience was Latin America. The intended effect was accommodation. It worked: Colombia capitulated, Caribbean reversed policies, Venezuela released prisoners.

But power demonstrated without legal constraint sends messages to multiple audiences.

To Opposition Movements

Iranian protesters saw:

  • Trump invaded Venezuela to remove authoritarian leader

  • Trump captured Maduro despite fortified compound

  • Trump announced indefinite U.S. control

  • Trump promised to help Iranian protesters if regime uses lethal force

  • Trump proved willingness to act (not just rhetoric like Obama 2009)

Iranian opposition’s calculation: Maybe Trump will help us like he helped Venezuela.

Result: Protests exploding. Regime panicking. Internet shut down. Khamenei blaming Trump. Opposition emboldened.

To Authoritarian Regimes

Putin saw:

  • Trump invaded Venezuela without congressional authorization

  • Trump ignored international law (claimed power limited only by “my own morality”)

  • Trump killed 80+ defenders without consequence

  • Trump imposed indefinite control

  • Trump seized Russian tanker without legal challenge

Putin’s calculation: If international law doesn’t constrain U.S., it doesn’t constrain Russia.

Result: Russia fires nuclear-capable missile at western Ukraine near NATO border. Message to Europe: We can escalate too.

The Paradox

Venezuela was meant to demonstrate American power. It did.

But power demonstrated without legal constraint has opposite effects on opposite audiences:

Opposition movements: Emboldened to rise up (betting Trump will help)

Authoritarian regimes: Emboldened to escalate (betting international law doesn’t matter)

Iran burns. Russia escalates. Both direct consequences of Venezuela.

TRUMP’S “MY OWN MORALITY” WORLDVIEW

Wednesday evening, Trump told the New York Times:

Asked about limits on his global powers: “Yeah, there is one thing. My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

On international law: “I don’t need international law. I’m not looking to hurt people.”

Pressed further: “I do” need to abide by international law. But: “It depends what your definition of international law is.”

Translation: Trump decides when international law applies. His power is constrained only by his own judgment.

The worldview: National strength, not laws or treaties, should decide outcomes when powers collide.

Applied to Venezuela: U.S. is stronger, therefore U.S. can invade, capture leader, impose control.

Applied to Greenland: U.S. is stronger, therefore U.S. can demand Denmark sell, threaten military force.

Applied to Colombia: U.S. is stronger, therefore U.S. can threaten invasion until leftist president capitulates.

This is “my own morality” foreign policy: Power matters. Law doesn’t.

Iran and Russia both watched. Both adjusted calculations.

IRAN: THE UNINTENDED CONSEQUENCE

Trump didn’t invade Venezuela to help Iranian protesters.

Trump invaded Venezuela to demonstrate power to Latin America. To force accommodation. To seize oil. To impose U.S. control.

But Iranian opposition watched. And thought: If Trump intervened in Venezuela, maybe he’ll intervene in Iran.

Thursday night, protests exploded. Friday morning, Khamenei blamed Trump. The connection is explicit.

This is the unintended consequence of demonstrating power without legal constraint:

Opposition movements in authoritarian countries see the demonstration and think: Maybe America will help us too.

Iranian protesters are betting Trump’s promise (”would come to aid of protesters if lethal force used”) is credible because Venezuela proved Trump acts.

Whether Trump actually intervenes in Iran is irrelevant to the current crisis. What matters is Iranian opposition believes he might. That belief emboldened unprecedented protests. Those protests forced regime to shut down internet, kill 40+, deploy Revolutionary Guards.

Venezuela made Trump’s Iran promise credible. That credibility destabilized Iran.

The Risk

If Trump doesn’t intervene in Iran, protests likely crushed. Regime crackdown. 40 dead becomes 400. Internet stays dark. Opposition leaders arrested or killed.

Then: Iranian opposition learns Trump’s promises meaningless. Venezuela was exception (Western hemisphere), not precedent. Disillusionment. Decades to rebuild opposition movement.

If Trump does intervene in Iran: Another invasion. More killed. Indefinite U.S. control of another country. Potential war with Iran (not raid, actual war). Oil markets disrupted. Global crisis.

Either way, Venezuela created the Iran crisis. By making Trump’s promise credible to opposition that bet on his intervention.

RUSSIA: THE CALCULATED RESPONSE

Putin didn’t escalate in Venezuela. He escalated in Ukraine.

This is calculated, not impulsive.

Russia’s spheres:

  • Western hemisphere: U.S. dominance overwhelming. Can’t stop Venezuela invasion.

  • International waters: U.S. Coast Guard can seize tankers. Can’t prevent it without naval confrontation.

  • Ukraine: Russia has leverage. Can escalate. Can demonstrate power.

So Putin accepts Venezuela loss. Negotiates crew release from seized tanker. Avoids confrontation where Russia is weak.

Then escalates where Russia is strong: Fires nuclear-capable Oreshnik at western Ukraine near NATO border.

The timing is deliberate: One week after Venezuela. One day after Trump’s “my own morality” interview. While U.S.-led peace talks ongoing.

The target is deliberate: Western Ukraine near Poland (NATO/EU member). Not eastern front lines. Message to Europe, not just Ukraine.

The weapon is deliberate: Nuclear-capable. Second use ever. Symbolic threat, not military necessity.

Putin’s message: If Trump’s power is limited only by “my own morality,” Putin’s power is limited only by his own judgment. If international law doesn’t constrain U.S. in Venezuela, international law doesn’t constrain Russia in Ukraine.

This is mirror-image logic. Trump demonstrates power without legal constraint. Putin does the same.

The Danger

If both U.S. and Russia operate under “my own morality” framework, international law becomes meaningless.

Disputes resolved by power, not law.

Weaker nations have no recourse.

Arms control agreements unenforceable.

Nuclear escalation risks increase.

Friday’s Oreshnik strike is warning: Russia can escalate to nuclear-capable weapons when it chooses. If Trump decides constraints unilaterally, so can Putin.

THE VENEZUELA EFFECT: GLOBAL RECALIBRATION

Saturday’s Venezuela invasion was meant to reorder the Western hemisphere. It succeeded: Colombia capitulated. Caribbean accommodated. Venezuela released prisoners.

But it also triggered global recalibration.

Every opposition movement in authoritarian countries watched. Thought: Maybe Trump will help us like Venezuela.

Every authoritarian regime watched. Thought: If international law doesn’t constrain U.S., it doesn’t constrain us.

Iran erupts. Russia escalates. Both Week One.

This is the Venezuela effect:

One invasion. Multiple audiences. Opposite interpretations.

Opposition movements: Emboldened to rise (Trump might help).

Authoritarian regimes: Emboldened to escalate (law doesn’t matter).

Both adjusting calculations based on same demonstration of power.

The Pattern Continues

Week One:

  • Saturday: Venezuela invaded

  • Wednesday: Colombia capitulated, Trump “my own morality” interview

  • Thursday: Iran shuts down internet, protests explode

  • Friday: Khamenei blames Trump, Russia fires nuclear-capable missile near NATO

Week Two begins with:

  • Iran offline, burning, 40+ dead

  • Russia escalating with nuclear-capable weapons

  • Trump meeting 14 oil executives Friday to rebuild Venezuela

  • Machado visiting Trump next week (after he rejected her)

  • Second wave of Venezuela attacks canceled (accommodation working)

The pattern: Power demonstrated without legal constraint creates cascading global effects—some intended (Latin America accommodation), some unintended (Iran destabilized, Russia escalating).

CONCLUSION: THE PARADOX OF POWER

Trump invaded Venezuela to demonstrate American power. He succeeded. Colombia capitulated within 72 hours. Caribbean nations reversed policies. Venezuela released political prisoners. Accommodation worked exactly as predicted.

But demonstrations of raw power are visible globally. And different audiences draw different conclusions.

Iranian opposition saw: Trump acts, not just talks. Maybe he’ll help us. Let’s rise up.

Putin saw: International law doesn’t constrain major powers. Let’s escalate in Ukraine.

This is the Venezuela paradox: One invasion, two opposite global effects.

Opposition movements emboldened (Iran erupting).

Authoritarian regimes emboldened (Russia escalating).

Both consequences of same demonstration of power.

Trump’s worldview—articulated Wednesday as “my own morality”—is that national strength, not law, should decide outcomes. Venezuela demonstrated that worldview in action.

Iran and Russia both watched. Both adjusted. Both acted within a week.

Khamenei accused protesters of trying to “please” Trump. He’s right. Because Venezuela showed Trump might actually intervene.

Putin fired nuclear-capable missile near NATO border. Because Venezuela showed international law is optional for powerful nations.

Venezuela achieved its hemispheric goal. Latin America intimidated. Accommodation secured. U.S. control imposed.

But the global consequences are just beginning.

Iran burns. Russia escalates. Trump claims power limited only by his own morality. And the world recalibrates accordingly.

When the most powerful nation declares international law applies only when it decides, everyone adjusts their calculations—opposition movements and authoritarian regimes alike.

This is the Venezuela effect: One week. Two continents. Opposite audiences. Opposite interpretations. Same demonstration of power.

The invasion was Saturday. The global cascade continues.
