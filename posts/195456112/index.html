Loyalty Trials

Thomas, Can You Spare a Pair?

In a party where 90% loyalty is treason, one Kentucky Republican refuses to read his lines off someone else's phone.

April 28, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays

Series Introduction

In a functioning democracy, a primary election is how a party chooses its best candidate to face the opposing party. It is, in theory, a mechanism of selection.

In the spring of 2026, the Republican Party is running a different kind of primary. Not selection — elimination. Not who best represents the voters but who sufficiently fears the president. The ballot has been repurposed as a loyalty instrument: a way of making examples, enforcing silence, and demonstrating to every Republican officeholder in America what happens to those who deviate from the line.

Donald Trump has been using the federal government to punish his enemies since January 20, 2025 — firing them, investigating them, prosecuting them, erasing them from the institutional memory of the state. The Loyalty Trials are the electoral extension of that same campaign. This time, instead of federal prosecutors, he sends super PACs. Instead of a court, he uses a ballot box. Instead of a sentence, he issues an endorsement — for your opponent.

The cases being heard this May span three states and four distinct species of Republican transgression. In Indiana, the crime was blocking a gerrymandering scheme. In Louisiana, it was a single impeachment vote cast five years ago. In Georgia, it was refusing to help steal an election. In Kentucky, it was the audacity of independent thought.

The defendants are all Republicans. The judge is Donald Trump. The jury is the Republican primary electorate.

This series tracks the trials — the charges, the defendants, the verdicts, and what they tell us about whether anything in the American political system is still capable of saying no.

Dreaming of Sleepy Joe

Study this photograph.

Four men stand behind the Resolute Desk. Hands clasped. Eyes forward. Spines straight. The posture of loyalty — literal, physical, unquestioning. They are behind their president in every sense the word allows. And the president himself? He is nodding off. The leader of the free world, the man to whom all this vertebral obedience is directed, appears to be losing a fight with consciousness. They stand. He sleeps. Nobody moves. Nobody speaks. Nobody breaks formation. This is what compliance looks like in the age of the Ministry of Truth — you hold your position even when the man you’re standing behind isn’t awake to notice.

Now look at this man.

Photograph by Caroline Gutman for The Atlantic

He is sitting on his own desk, in his own office, looking directly at you. Behind him: framed diplomas, a “Don’t Tread On Me” flag, and what appears to be a framed copy of the Epstein Files Transparency Act — legislation he wrote and forced through a Congress that didn’t want it, past a Speaker who tried to stop it, and over a White House that would have preferred the files stay sealed.

Nobody else is in the frame. He doesn’t need anyone standing behind him.

His name is Thomas Massie, and he may be the last man in Washington who owns his own words.

There is a moment in Orwell’s masterpiece when Winston Smith, alone in his cramped apartment, commits the most dangerous act imaginable. He opens a notebook and writes a thought that is entirely his own. Not the Party’s thought. Not Big Brother’s thought. His.

In the spring of 2026, this pudgy-turned-grandfatherly Republican from northern Kentucky is doing roughly the same thing — except instead of a notebook, he’s using a clip-on debt clock he built himself, and instead of a cramped apartment, he’s standing in a bridal suite inside a converted barn, explaining to a hundred or so Republicans why agreeing with the President of the United States ninety percent of the time should not be a capital offence.

The Vocabulary of Obedience

The Ministry of Truth doesn’t announce itself. It never does. It arrives disguised as loyalty, wrapped in flags, and enforced not by jackboots but by super PACs. Chris LaCivita’s operation started running attack ads against Massie last summer — softening up the target months before there was even a challenger, because the first rule of modern propaganda is that you don’t need a counter-argument when you have a counter-narrative.

And what is the counter-narrative? Listen to the official White House statement, delivered by spokesperson Davis Ingle with all the subtlety of a telegram from the Ministry of Love: Massie “cares more about peacocking for his radical Democrat friends and liberal media allies than delivering for the men and women of Kentucky’s 4th district.”

Let’s unpack that sentence the way Orwell would.

Peacocking. A man who installed a jail water heater himself to save taxpayers $6,500 — and then had the inmates strip the old unit for parts — is peacocking. A man who built a wearable national debt counter because he thinks you ought to see the number ticking upward while your representatives spend your money — that man is performing for attention.

Radical Democrat friends. This refers to Ro Khanna, with whom Massie co-authored the legislation that forced the release of the Epstein files. You remember the Epstein files — the ones that the Trump White House and Speaker Johnson tried to keep sealed. The ones that the American public overwhelmingly wanted released. Apparently, working across the aisle to deliver transparency that your own party was blocking makes you a collaborator. In the Ministry’s lexicon, bipartisanship is betrayal.

Liberal media allies. Massie sat for an interview with The Atlantic. He answered questions. On the record. With photographs. In the current Republican ecosystem, this qualifies as consorting with the enemy. The proper protocol, as established by the Ministry, is to restrict press access, revoke credentials, and grant exclusives to approved outlets. Talking to journalists is the new thoughtcrime.

The 90 Percent Heresy

Here is the fact that the Ministry of Truth must work hardest to obscure: Thomas Massie votes with Republican leadership approximately ninety percent of the time. He supported the Save America Act — Trump’s top legislative priority. He endorsed Trump for president. Trump endorsed him right back. They discussed Massie becoming Secretary of Agriculture. They chatted about the timing of announcements. “Just tweet it. I’ll retweet you,” the President told him.

Ninety percent.

In any functioning organization — a business, a marriage, a hockey team — ninety percent agreement would be considered a miracle of alignment. But the Ministry does not deal in percentages. The Ministry deals in absolutes. You are loyal or you are a traitor. You vote the party line or you are, in the President’s own Newspeak, “a totally ineffective LOSER,” “the worst,” a “moron.”

The specifics of Massie’s dissent are instructive. He opposed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act because it would balloon an already catastrophic deficit. He questioned the constitutionality of launching military strikes without congressional authorization. He pushed for the release of files that the American public had a right to see.

In the old Republican Party — the one that existed before the Ministry’s renovation — these positions had names. Fiscal conservatism. Constitutional originalism. Government transparency. They were not heresies. They were the catechism.

The Warm Body Primary

Unable to defeat Massie’s arguments, the Ministry has deployed the next best thing: a warm body. Those are Trump’s words, not mine. “I wanted just — give me somebody with a warm body to beat Massie,” the President told a rally crowd, before generously upgrading his own candidate to include “a big, beautiful brain and a great patriot.”

Ed Gallrein is a Navy SEAL and a political novice whom Trump personally summoned to the Oval Office and asked to run. This is how the Ministry fills its ranks — not through democratic selection, but through presidential appointment. The candidate’s qualifications are, in descending order: (1) willingness to comply, (2) a résumé that looks good on a yard sign, and (3) warm blood at approximately 37 degrees Celsius.

When Gallrein was scheduled to appear at the Grant County Republican fundraiser where Massie was speaking, he pulled out the day of, citing funerals. The county GOP chair, who is backing Massie, hinted that Gallrein withdrew after learning he’d have to answer questions from the crowd. His deputy campaign manager showed up instead and read a speech off her phone while Massie sat directly in front of her.

When she accused Massie of voting against the President’s border plan, he interrupted. “False,” he said.

“Please. I did not interrupt you,” she replied.

“I didn’t lie about you,” Massie answered.

This is the difference between a man who owns his words and a campaign that reads them off a phone.

The Price of Winston’s Notebook

Massie has a theory about what happens if he wins on May 19th. He believes six to a dozen other Republican members of Congress would feel emboldened to occasionally vote their conscience instead of the party line. He’s spoken with them directly, though he won’t name them.

Think about that for a moment. There are Republican members of Congress who privately agree with Massie’s positions — on spending, on constitutional war powers, on government transparency — but who will not vote accordingly unless someone else goes first and survives. They are waiting to see whether thoughtcrime is still punishable by political death.

This is how the Ministry works. Not through direct censorship — although that’s coming, and we’ve tracked it in previous entries in this series — but through the simple, devastating efficiency of fear. You don’t need to rewrite every Republican’s thoughts. You just need to destroy one of them publicly enough that the rest rewrite their own.

The President has attacked Massie’s new wife, calling her “Radical Left.” He has suggested that Massie remarried too quickly after the death of his first wife. These are not policy arguments. These are not even political attacks in any traditional sense. They are the weapons of personal annihilation — designed not to persuade, but to demonstrate what happens to those who deviate.

And yet.

Thomas Massie, the MIT-trained engineer who once told the Cincinnati Enquirer “I’m ready to be unpopular,” is still standing in a bridal suite in Grant County, still wearing his handmade debt clock, still telling voters exactly what he thinks, still refusing to let the Ministry rewrite his words.

He even does a Trump impression. Apparently, it’s closer to Alec Baldwin than to reality, which tells you something about how seriously he takes the performance of power.

Can He Spare a Pair?

The question isn’t really whether Massie can spare a pair for the rest of the Republican caucus. He’s demonstrated that he has them. The question is whether any of his colleagues are willing to accept the loan.

Massie’s internal polling shows Trump’s approval in the district has dipped from the mid-90s during his first term to the low 70s now. Republican turnout has sagged in special elections. The President’s political juice, while still formidable, is not what it was. The fear remains, but the power behind the fear is diminishing — and Massie, ever the engineer, can read the gauges.

“If you’re praying for me to stay in the fight, and God answers your prayer, I’ll win my reelection,” Massie tells supporters who offer their prayers. “If you’re praying for my soul, I’ll be on my farm next year and out of politics.”

It’s a good line. It’s also a genuine offer. Here is a man who has made peace with the possibility that speaking his own mind will cost him his career. He has already calculated the price and decided he can afford it.

In Orwell’s novel, Winston Smith loses. The Ministry breaks him. He learns to love Big Brother.

Thomas Massie, from the evidence, has not learned to love Big Brother. He has learned to install his own water heater, build his own debt clock, write his own legislation, and tell his own voters exactly where he stands — even when where he stands is ten points away from the party line.

Whether the voters of Kentucky’s 4th district reward that independence on May 19th will tell us something important about the Ministry’s reach. Not whether it exists — we’re well past that question. But whether it can be beaten by one stubborn man who refuses to read his lines off someone else’s phone.

From up here in Toronto, watching the last remaining exhibit of Republican independence fight for survival against a machine designed to enforce total compliance, I find myself oddly moved. Not because I agree with Thomas Massie on guns, or raw milk, or most of his policy positions. I don’t.

But because he owns his words. In the Ministry of Truth, that’s the most dangerous thing a man can do.

And the most necessary.
