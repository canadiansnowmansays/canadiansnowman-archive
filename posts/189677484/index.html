The Democratic Promise

The Patriot

Part 7: Dario Amodei and Anthropic

April 20, 2026

Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic. "Disagreeing with the government is the most American thing in the world." Source: Getty Images / via Startuppedia

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays, April 2026

The Line

On February 27, 2026, Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, did something that no major technology CEO in the age of AI had done before.

He said no.

The Pentagon had asked Anthropic to remove safety guardrails from its AI model, Claude, to enable applications in mass surveillance and autonomous weapons systems. The contract was worth approximately two hundred million dollars. Saying no meant losing the revenue. It meant losing government favor. It meant inviting retaliation from an administration that had shown, repeatedly and viciously, that it punished disobedience.

Amodei said no anyway.

Within hours, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a “supply chain risk” — a classification previously reserved for foreign adversaries and sanctioned entities. It was the first time in American history that a domestic technology company had been formally designated a security risk for refusing a government contract on ethical grounds.

The President’s response was blunter: “We don’t need it, we don’t want it.”

And Sam Altman, as documented in Part 6, rushed to sign the contract that Anthropic had rejected — reportedly hours before the United States began its war with Iran. The timing was not lost on observers. Altman later admitted that OpenAI's move "looked opportunistic and sloppy." That may be the most honest thing he has said in years.

The Context

To understand what Amodei did, you have to understand what he was refusing, and what it cost.

The Pentagon’s request was not for a general-purpose AI assistant. It was for AI systems without the safety constraints that prevent misuse — systems that could be deployed for mass surveillance of civilian populations and integrated into autonomous weapons platforms. Removing the guardrails was the specific ask. Not “help us with logistics.” Not “improve our intelligence analysis.” Remove the constraints that prevent your technology from being used to watch everyone and kill without human oversight.

Anthropic’s position was that these red lines were not negotiable. The company had been founded in 2021 by Amodei and his sister Daniela, both former OpenAI executives, specifically because they believed AI safety required institutional commitment — not just rhetoric, but structural guarantees embedded in the company’s governance and operating principles.

The cost of that commitment became real on February 27th. Two hundred million dollars in revenue, gone. Government partnership, gone. And something worse: the designation as a supply chain risk effectively signaled to other government agencies and defense contractors that doing business with Anthropic carried political danger. It was not just a lost contract. It was an attempt at institutional destruction.

The damage extended beyond the Pentagon. 1789 Capital, a venture capital firm associated with Donald Trump Jr., abandoned an investment in Anthropic worth hundreds of millions of dollars. The message to the rest of the technology industry was unmistakable: cross this administration and the cost will be measured not just in lost contracts but in lost capital. It was a lesson that every other AI company absorbed in real time — and that made Anthropic's refusal to capitulate all the more remarkable.

“The Most American Thing in the World”

Amodei at the World Economic Forum, Davos. He expressed genuine unease about the speed at which AI power was concentrating — including in his own company's hands. Source: World Economic Forum / via Startuppedia

Amodei’s public response was remarkable for its clarity and for its framing.

“Disagreeing with the government,” he said, “is the most American thing in the world.”

That sentence does something precise. It reframes the confrontation from a business dispute into a constitutional principle. The Pentagon framed Anthropic’s refusal as a security risk — disloyalty, essentially. Amodei reframed it as the exercise of a right that the American system was designed to protect. The right to say no. The right to dissent. The right of a private citizen or a private company to refuse a government demand that violates their principles.

This is the democratic promise in its purest form — not as a technology philosophy, but as a political act. Jobs said distribute the intelligence. Berners-Lee gave away the Web. Torvalds gave away Linux. Those were acts of distribution. Amodei’s refusal was an act of restraint — a refusal to distribute power to an institution that intended to use it in ways that violated democratic norms.

Distribution and restraint are two sides of the same coin. The democratic promise is not just about putting tools in everyone’s hands. It is about refusing to put certain tools in certain hands. It is about recognizing that some applications of technology are incompatible with democratic governance, and that the people who build those technologies have a responsibility to draw lines that governments and markets will not draw for themselves.

The Courts

Amodei said no. Then he did something that distinguished his stand from mere corporate posturing: he sued.

Anthropic filed two lawsuits against the federal government — one in the Northern District of California challenging the supply chain risk designation and the presidential directive banning agencies from using Claude, and a second in the D.C. Circuit challenging the Pentagon’s designation directly. The company argued that the government’s actions constituted illegal retaliation for the exercise of First Amendment rights — that Anthropic was being punished not for any security failing but for disagreeing with the Pentagon in public.

On March 26, Judge Rita F. Lin of the Northern District of California granted Anthropic a sweeping preliminary injunction. Her forty-three-page opinion is one of the most remarkable judicial documents of the AI era.

Lin called the supply chain risk designation “likely both contrary to law and arbitrary and capricious.” She noted that the designation had historically been reserved for foreign adversaries and terrorists, not American companies engaged in contract disputes. She observed that the Pentagon had previously praised Anthropic as a partner and subjected it to rigorous national security vetting — and that it was not until the company publicly raised concerns about how its technology would be used that the government moved to destroy it.

Her language was extraordinary. “Nothing in the governing statute supports the Orwellian notion that an American company may be branded a potential adversary and saboteur of the U.S. for expressing disagreement with the government,” she wrote. And then the sentence that may define this chapter of the AI governance story: “Punishing Anthropic for bringing public scrutiny to the government’s contracting position is classic illegal First Amendment retaliation.”

The government cannot simultaneously argue that a company is an existential threat to national security and that its technology is indispensable to national security. Lin saw through the contradiction. She issued an injunction barring the Trump administration from enforcing the ban on non-Defense agencies using Anthropic.

But the legal picture is not clean. Two weeks later, on April 8, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals denied Anthropic’s request to block the Pentagon’s blacklisting. The appeals court acknowledged that Anthropic “will likely suffer some degree of irreparable harm” but ruled that its interests “seem primarily financial in nature” and that intervening would “force the United States military to prolong its dealings with an unwanted vendor of critical AI services in the middle of a significant ongoing military conflict.”

That “significant ongoing military conflict” is Iran. The war that Sam Altman’s contract was signed to support.

So the legal landscape, as of this writing, is split. Lin’s injunction protects Anthropic from the broader federal government. The D.C. Circuit lets the Pentagon enforce the ban. The case will likely take a year or more to resolve. In the meantime, the most important question in AI governance — whether the government can punish a technology company for refusing to remove safety constraints — remains unanswered.

Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI. He rushed to sign the contract Anthropic rejected, then admitted the timing "looked opportunistic and sloppy." Source: OpenAI (event screenshot)

The Public

Something else happened after February 27 that no one in Silicon Valley predicted.

The public responded.

Within twenty-four hours of Anthropic’s refusal and OpenAI’s countermove, Anthropic’s Claude climbed to the number-one position on the U.S. Apple App Store, displacing ChatGPT. More than 1.5 million users joined what became known as the QuitGPT movement — cancelling their OpenAI subscriptions and switching to Anthropic’s products. A Reddit thread urging people to cancel and delete ChatGPT crossed thirty-three thousand upvotes and kept climbing.

This matters for the argument of this series. The democratic promise, as I have framed it, depends on citizens who understand what is at stake. For most of the digital age, the public response to corporate concentration of technological power has been indifference. People complained about Facebook’s privacy violations and kept using Facebook. People complained about Amazon’s labor practices and kept ordering from Amazon. The gap between public concern and public action has been one of the defining features — and one of the most depressing features — of the platform era.

The QuitGPT movement was different. Not because it was large enough to threaten OpenAI’s business — it was not — but because it demonstrated that a meaningful number of people were willing to act on principle. They understood what the Pentagon had asked for. They understood what Anthropic had refused. They understood what OpenAI had accepted. And they voted with their wallets and their app downloads.

Whether that is a one-time reaction or the beginning of a sustained shift in public engagement with AI governance is a question that cannot yet be answered. But it is evidence — the first real evidence in the AI era — that the democratic promise has a constituency.

The Discomfort

There is another dimension to Amodei’s position that connects directly to Jobs’s 1983 vision, and it is worth quoting at length.

In the weeks before the Pentagon confrontation, Amodei had spoken publicly about his discomfort with the speed at which AI power was concentrating. He described the “overnight” emergence of a world in which a handful of companies controlled the most powerful technology on Earth, and he expressed genuine unease about the implications — including the implications for his own company.

This is not the language of a typical CEO. CEOs do not usually express discomfort with the power their own companies wield. They celebrate it. They frame it as evidence of success, of market validation, of strategic positioning. Amodei’s willingness to articulate the problem — even when his company was part of the problem — suggests a level of self-awareness that is rare in the technology industry and almost nonexistent among its leaders.

That discomfort is not always expressed with diplomatic precision. In early March, as tensions with the administration escalated, Amodei posted an internal message on Anthropic’s Slack platform suggesting that the government’s hostility was driven in part by the company’s refusal to offer what he described as “dictator-style praise.” The message leaked. The Wall Street Journal and other outlets reported it. Amodei subsequently apologized for the tone, saying the post did not reflect his considered views.

It is worth pausing on this moment. A different kind of CEO would never have written that message — too risky, too candid, too easily weaponized. A more polished operator would have kept the frustration private and the public statements anodyne. Amodei wrote what he was thinking and put it where his employees could see it. That it leaked is a reminder of how exposed any honest statement becomes in a politically charged environment. That he apologized is a reminder that he operates in a world where candor carries costs. But the original instinct — to name what he saw, even intemperately — is consistent with everything else in this essay. Amodei’s defining quality is not polish. It is an inability to pretend that what is happening is not happening.

Jobs said “distribute that intelligence” when asked about the danger of centralized databases. Amodei, forty-three years later, was grappling with the same question from the inside — as someone who was building the intelligence and watching it concentrate in real time, including in his own hands.

The honesty of that discomfort matters. It does not resolve the tension. Anthropic is still a private company building increasingly powerful AI systems. It still concentrates capabilities that affect billions of people. It is still, by any structural analysis, part of the oligarchic pattern this series documents. But the discomfort — the willingness to name the problem, to draw lines, to accept punishment for maintaining those lines — distinguishes Amodei from every other figure in this series except the builders.

The Mythos Paradox

Dario Amodei arrives at the White House, April 17, 2026. Seven weeks after the Pentagon branded his company a threat to national security, the administration wanted access to its most powerful model. Source: Jessica Koscielniak / Reuters

As this essay goes to publication, the Anthropic story has taken a turn that even the most cynical observer of Washington could not have scripted.

On April 17, 2026 — less than two months after the Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk — Dario Amodei walked into the West Wing for a meeting with White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent. The subject: Anthropic’s new AI model, Mythos Preview, a cybersecurity tool so powerful that Anthropic itself restricted its release, providing access only to a small group of partners under a new initiative called Project Glasswing.

The same government that branded Anthropic an adversary now wants access to its most advanced technology.

The White House called the meeting “productive and constructive.” President Trump, asked about Amodei’s visit while on a runway in Phoenix, responded, “Who?” and said he had no idea the meeting had taken place. Wiles, according to reporting by Axios, made clear that the government needs a relationship with Anthropic and wants an open line of communication. She was circumspect about the Pentagon dispute, saying only that “it’s in court.” Both sides sought to wall off the Pentagon fight from the way the rest of the government engages with Anthropic.

One Trump adviser described the situation to Axios with characteristic Washington bluntness: “This is a big problem. Everyone’s complaining. There’s all this drama. So this got elevated to Susie to hear Dario out, determine what is bullsh-t and start to plot a way forward.”

A U.S. official was less diplomatic: “They’re using this Mythos cyber weapon to find friendly ears in the government. They’re succeeding.”

The irony is structural, not incidental. Anthropic’s refusal to remove safety guardrails was framed by the Pentagon as a threat to national security. Now Anthropic’s commitment to those same principles of restraint — its willingness to restrict even its own most powerful model rather than release it indiscriminately — is precisely what makes the government want access. The qualities that got Anthropic blacklisted are the qualities that make its technology trustworthy. The administration is discovering, in real time, that a company willing to say no to the Pentagon is also a company that can be trusted with tools capable of breaching the nation’s financial system.

This is not resolution. The lawsuits continue. The supply chain risk designation remains in effect for the Pentagon. The D.C. Circuit has declined to block it. Anthropic is still, legally and practically, at war with the Department of Defense while simultaneously negotiating with the rest of the executive branch.

But it is a development that speaks directly to the democratic promise. The builders — Berners-Lee, Torvalds — proved that technology could serve democratic purposes. Amodei is proving something different: that the refusal to compromise on principle can itself become a source of strategic power. Not immediately. Not cleanly. But in a way that no amount of accommodation could have achieved.

Sam Altman signed the contract. He got the revenue. And he lost something that turns out to matter more than revenue: the credibility to be trusted with the next generation of tools.

The Standard Applied

Steve Jobs at the International Design Conference, Aspen, 1983. He made a promise. Forty-three years later, Amodei was the one who kept it. Source: Aspen International Design Conference, 1983 (video still)

This series has used Steve Jobs’s 1983 Aspen speech as a moral benchmark. How does Amodei measure against it?

Jobs said distribute the intelligence. Amodei builds intelligence that is, by its nature, concentrated in the companies that train it. The infrastructure costs alone — billions of dollars in computing hardware — ensure that only a handful of organizations can build frontier AI systems. This is not a choice Amodei made. It is a structural reality of the technology. But it is a reality that places Anthropic, like OpenAI, like Google DeepMind, in tension with the democratic promise.

Jobs said empower the citizen. Claude — the AI system Anthropic builds — does empower individuals in meaningful ways. It provides access to analysis, writing, research, and reasoning that was previously available only to those who could afford expert consultants. In this respect, Anthropic advances the democratic promise more directly than most of the companies in this series.

Jobs said the tools should serve democratic purposes. Amodei drew a line at tools that would undermine democratic purposes — mass surveillance, autonomous weapons — and accepted severe consequences for holding that line. No other major AI company has done this.

Jobs said the kids can’t wait. Amodei’s commitment to AI safety includes a long-term perspective — a willingness to slow down, to test, to constrain — that prioritizes getting it right over getting it first. This is a form of the same urgency Jobs expressed, directed not at speed of deployment but at quality of outcome.

The honest assessment is this: Amodei is not a builder in the mold of Berners-Lee or Torvalds. He has not given away his technology. He runs a private company that charges for its products. He operates within the same market system that bent Gates, enclosed the open web, and transformed OpenAI from a nonprofit into a corporation.

But within that system — within the constraints of running a company that must generate revenue to survive — Amodei has drawn lines that no one else has drawn, held positions that no one else has held, and accepted consequences that no one else has accepted. He has done this not for competitive advantage (the Pentagon contract would have been pure advantage) but for principle.

In a series populated by people who broke the democratic promise — for profit, for power, for convenience, for reasonable-sounding reasons — Amodei is the figure who, when the moment came, kept it.

Whether that is enough — whether individual acts of conscience can substitute for the structural reforms that democratic governance of technology requires — is the question the final essay must address.
