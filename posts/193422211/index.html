To the Moon, Alice

Part II: We Will Always Choose Earth

April 6, 2026

Earthset, April 6, 2026, as seen by the crew of NASA’s Artemis II spacecraft as it swung around the far side of the moon. NASA

April 6, 2026 — Day 6 of the Artemis II Mission

There are days when history announces itself with trumpets and press releases. And then there are days like this one — when it arrives quietly, through a cracking voice 252,000 miles away, naming a crater for a woman who died of cancer at forty-six and never got to see any of it.

Today was the second kind.

Forty Minutes of Silence

At 6:44 p.m. Eastern time, the Orion spacecraft Integrity slipped behind the far side of the moon and fell silent.

For forty minutes, four human beings — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen — were the most isolated people who have ever lived. Not just far from home. Unreachable. The moon’s mass interposed between them and every other human being on Earth, blocking every signal, severing every thread. Mission control in Houston went quiet. The screens kept updating telemetry that was already minutes old. There was nothing to do but wait.

Victor Glover had asked for that silence before it came. In a video recorded months earlier, he had issued a challenge: “I would love it if we could all agree, for that time, let’s just be humans. I’m challenging all of us to think more about unity.”

Whether the world obliged is debatable. But the request was the right one.

When Integrity emerged on the other side — when the signal reconnected and Houston exhaled — Christina Koch’s first words were not technical. They were not a systems report or a checklist item. They were this:

“It is so great to hear from Earth again.”

And then, speaking to the people of Asia, Africa, and Oceania who could look up at that moment and see the moon hanging in their sky: “We see you, too.”

She went on. She described a future of lunar exploration — outposts, rovers, companies, radio astronomy, ships. She enumerated the ambitions of the species with the quiet confidence of someone who had just seen the moon with her own eyes and believed every word of it.

And then she ended with the line that will outlast this mission, outlast this decade, and possibly outlast most of what any of us write this year:

“We will always choose Earth. We will always choose each other.”

Carroll

Reid and Carroll Wiseman. Ms. Wiseman died of cancer before the Artemis II mission.Credit...via NASA

Earlier in the day, before the blackout, before the solar eclipse and the Earthshine and the meteors flashing on the lunar surface, there was a moment that had nothing to do with geopolitics or orbital mechanics or the billionaire space economy.

Jeremy Hansen’s voice broke as he called down to mission control.

The crew had been observing a crater that straddled the boundary between the near and far sides of the moon — a crater that could, at certain times, be seen from Earth. They wanted it named. Not for a mission. Not for an agency. For a woman.

“It’s a bright spot on the moon,” Hansen said, his voice fragmenting across 250,000 miles of vacuum, “and we would like to call it Carroll.”

Carroll Wiseman. Pediatric nurse practitioner. Mother of Ellie and Katherine. Wife of the mission commander, who had been ready to give up his astronaut dream when she was diagnosed with cancer — and who she refused to let do so. She died in 2020 at forty-six, six years before her husband climbed into a rocket and rode fire into history.

The crew embraced. Mission control observed a moment of silence, the Wiseman family nearby. And somewhere on the boundary between the moon’s near and far sides, there is now a crater with a woman’s name on it — a bright spot, permanently.

Before launch, Wiseman had posted a photo on X: himself and his two daughters in front of the Space Launch System rocket. “I love these two ladies,” he wrote, “and I’m boarding that rocket a very proud father.”

His elder daughter had responded to the mission assignment, years earlier, by baking moon-shaped cupcakes.

“These two kids, I thought, were going to pull me,” Wiseman said in a NASA podcast. “But they were pushing me.”

What They Saw

Via NASA

The science of today will take months to fully process. But the human reports came in real time, and they were something.

The far side of the moon — the hemisphere that has faced away from Earth for the entirety of human existence — was illuminated today in ways it was not during any of the Apollo missions. Those missions were timed for near-side landings at lunar dawn. Today, with the moon in a different orbital position, portions of the far side caught sunlight that human eyes have never seen fall on them before.

Of the more than 100 billion humans thought to have ever lived, twenty-four have seen the far side with their own eyes. Today that number became twenty-eight. Three Americans and a Canadian.

Reid Wiseman looked at the Orientale Basin — a 600-mile-wide ringed impact crater straddling the near and far sides, formed 3.8 billion years ago — and said: “It’s very three dimensional.”

Christina Koch described fresh impact craters that looked nothing like their photographs. “What it really looks like is like a lampshade with tiny pinprick holes and the light shining through,” she said. “They are so bright compared to the rest of the moon.”

Victor Glover, who is a pilot and tends toward understatement, watched meteor strikes flash on the lunar surface in real time — rocks from space hitting a world that has no atmosphere to burn them up, just silent detonations of light — and abandoned understatement entirely.

“Humans probably have not evolved to see what we’re seeing,” he said. “It is truly hard to describe. It is amazing.”

Later, during the 53-minute solar eclipse that no mission planner had originally planned for but that a precise April 1st launch date made possible, the crew watched the solar corona emerge as the moon swallowed the sun. They saw Earthshine — the ghostly illumination of the moon’s surface by light reflected from our own planet. They saw Mars, reddish, in the starfield behind the moon. They saw Saturn, orange. They saw Venus, alone and brilliant in the dark.

“After all of the amazing sights that we saw earlier,” Glover said, “we just went sci-fi.”

A Voice From August

The day began with a voice from the dead.

When the crew woke up at 10:50 a.m. Eastern — roused by a song called “Good Morning” — they heard, unexpectedly, the voice of Jim Lovell. Apollo 8 pilot. Apollo 13 commander. The man who circled the moon in 1968 and survived an oxygen tank explosion in 1970 and spent the rest of his long life as a living argument for human resilience.

Lovell had recorded a message before he died in August. NASA had kept it for this moment.

“Hello, Artemis II! This is Apollo astronaut Jim Lovell. Welcome to my old neighborhood!”

He passed the torch. He told them not to forget to enjoy the view. He signed off as he always did: “Good luck and Godspeed from all of us here on the good Earth.”

Four astronauts, 215,000 miles from home, heard a dead man’s voice and continued the work.

The View From Here

NASA, via Associated Press

I have five photographs in front of me as I write this.

The first is NASA’s own image: the Orion spacecraft and the crescent moon side by side in the absolute black of space. Hardware and wonder sharing a frame.

The second is Reid and Carroll Wiseman on a beach, heads together, the ocean behind them. They are smiling the way people smile when they have no idea what is coming and are simply happy in the present moment. She is gone. Her name is on the moon.

The third is the moon itself, as seen from Earth — full, cratered, ancient, approximately indifferent to all of this.

The fourth is the one that moves me most. A human ear, a human face turned toward a window, and the moon outside — small, round, unreachable — framed in a car window or an airplane porthole. The most democratic image of the week. No agency logo. No mission patch. Just a person and the moon and the glass between them, which is the glass that has always been between us and the moon, until five days ago.

The fifth is the moon seen through Orion’s porthole window from inside the capsule — framed by wiring, bolts, cable bundles, the beautiful organized clutter of human engineering. The sublime packaged in the utilitarian. The eternal glimpsed through the temporary.

This is what it looks like when the species tries.

What Comes Next

Integrity is now earthbound, as mission control put it — “we are ready to bring you home.” Splashdown in the Pacific off San Diego is scheduled for Friday, April 10th. Day 7 will be quieter, giving the crew time to rest. The toilet, which has caused intermittent problems throughout the mission, continues to be monitored. Even at 252,000 miles, plumbing is plumbing.

The geopolitics, the data centers, the lunar land grab, the Artemis Accords and their Chinese counterpart, the water ice in the permanently shadowed craters — all of that will be here when they get back. It will all be covered in this series, because it matters enormously.

But today was not the day for any of that.

Today was the day a Canadian astronaut’s voice broke naming a crater for a dead woman. Today was the day a dead Apollo astronaut said welcome to my old neighbourhood to four people passing through his old neighbourhood at 961 miles per hour. Today was the day Victor Glover said we just went sci-fi while watching meteors hit the moon. Today was the day Christina Koch looked back at a crescent Earth rising over the lunar horizon and told Asia, Africa, and Oceania: we see you too.

In 1941, John Gillespie Magee wrote about putting out his hand and touching the face of God.

Today, four people touched the face of the moon.

And then they turned around and chose Earth.
