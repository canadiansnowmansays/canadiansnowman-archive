The World According to Trump

The World According to Trump

Episode 4: The Mirror — When Ego Becomes Geography and Geography Becomes Policy

March 6, 2026

A series by Stephen Gordon, CanadianSnowmanSays

In the first three essays of this series, we examined three maps.

The first was international. Using the New York Times’ analysis of more than 300 presidential statements, we watched Trump sort the entire planet into a binary: allies who maintain their independence are “nasty,” strongmen who offer personal deference are “friends,” and every conflict on Earth is evaluated not by its humanitarian stakes but by whether it earned Donald Trump a Nobel Prize.

The second was domestic. Using the Times’ companion analysis of campaign rhetoric, we watched Trump apply identical logic to his own country: blue cities with diverse populations are “cesspools” and “killing fields,” red states with loyal voters are “beautiful” and “God’s creation,” and the entire nation is cast as a garbage can that only one man can rescue.

The third was personal. On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court struck down Trump’s sweeping tariffs 6-3, and within hours Trump sorted nine named human beings into the same two columns he has applied to 195 nations and 50 states. Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh — dissenters — earned “strength, wisdom, and love of country.” The six in the majority, including two of his own appointees, earned “disgrace,” “disloyal,” and “an embarrassment to their families.” Neil Gorsuch and Amy Coney Barrett — confirmed through Trump’s own political capital, reliable conservative votes across dozens of cases — were sorted into the nasty column on the afternoon they ruled against him on one.

Now it’s time to hold those three maps on top of each other and ask the question that ties this series together: What is the actual governing principle here? Not what Trump says it is. Not what his supporters believe it is. Not what his opponents fear it is. What does the data tell us?

The data tells us something simple, consistent, and profoundly alarming: there is no worldview. There is no domestic vision. There is no foreign policy doctrine and no national agenda. There is only a mirror — and everything that passes before it is evaluated by a single criterion: What does this reflect back about Donald Trump?

One Psychology, Three Geographies

Let me lay the parallels out plainly, because the symmetry is the argument.

The Loyalty Test

Internationally, Trump’s warmth flows toward leaders who demonstrate personal loyalty. Xi Jinping gets “great relationship.” Kim Jong Un gets “very good relationship.” Orbán, Erdogan, and MBS get “friend of mine.” These men have one thing in common: they govern without democratic accountability, which means they can offer Trump personal allegiance without the inconvenience of electorates, free presses, or legislative opposition qualifying their devotion.

Domestically, Trump’s warmth flows toward places that demonstrate electoral loyalty. Idaho, Iowa, Indiana — “so good and so well run.” Montana — “one of the most beautiful places in all of God’s creation.” Alaska — “incredible.” These states have one thing in common: they voted for Trump by overwhelming margins.

Personally, Trump’s warmth flows toward individuals who vote with him at the moment of maximum consequence. Not those who have served him longest. Not those who share his ideology. Not even those he appointed. Those who, on the day that counts most, choose him over their obligations. Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh dissented on the tariff ruling and earned presidential praise. Gorsuch and Barrett ruled on the merits — as any judge is constitutionally required to do — and earned contempt. The criterion is identical at every scale: loyalty in, praise out; independence in, contempt out.

The Contempt Pattern

Internationally, democratic allies who maintain their own negotiating positions earn derision. Canada is “very nasty.” Macron “always gets it wrong.” Norway loses Trump’s “respect.” Spain is freeloading. These nations’ actual contributions to American security, trade, and diplomatic interests are irrelevant. What matters is that they didn’t bend the knee.

Domestically, cities that vote against Trump earn apocalyptic condemnation. Chicago is “worse than Afghanistan.” D.C. is a “rat-infested shithole.” Atlanta is a “killing field.” Portland is “a burned-down hulk.” These cities’ actual economic output, cultural contributions, and quality of life metrics are irrelevant. What matters is that they didn’t vote for him.

Personally, individuals who exercise independent judgment at a consequential moment are transformed overnight, regardless of their prior record. James Mattis was “the closest thing to General Patton we have” — until he resigned over Trump’s treatment of allies, whereupon he became “the world’s most overrated general.” Jeff Sessions was “a great man” — until he recused himself from the Russia investigation as the law required, whereupon he became “the biggest mistake” of Trump’s presidency. Mike Pence served four years of unflinching public loyalty and was repaid, on January 6, with a mob chanting for his execution. No accumulation of prior service insulates anyone. The account resets with every consequential decision.

The Racial Undercurrent

Internationally, the countries Trump wants immigrants from — Norway, Sweden, Denmark — are the whitest nations in Europe. The countries he calls “hellholes” — Haiti, Somalia, Afghanistan, the Congo — are overwhelmingly Black and Brown. European nations are evaluated primarily by whether they’ve accepted non-white immigration, with Poland and Hungary earning praise for keeping people out and Germany and France condemned for letting people in.

Domestically, the cities Trump attacks most viciously — Chicago, Atlanta, Detroit, Washington D.C. — are among the most prominent majority-Black cities in America. The states he praises — Idaho, Montana, Iowa, Indiana — are among the whitest. He doesn’t say the quiet part out loud. He doesn’t have to. The map says it for him.

The Savior Narrative

Internationally, Trump presents himself as the sole guarantor of global peace. He’s “settled” eight wars. The Ukraine invasion wouldn’t have happened under him. The October 7 attack wouldn’t have happened under him. Without Trump, the world falls apart. With him, conflicts resolve through the sheer force of his presence.

Domestically, Trump presents himself as the sole savior of a nation in terminal decline. “Our once great country, soon to be greater-than-ever-before country.” Without him, America is a garbage can ruled by “censors, perverts, criminals and thugs.” With him — and only with him — greatness returns.

Personally, Trump presents himself as the sole legitimate authority over every institution he encounters. The Supreme Court, he suggested after the tariff ruling, had been “swayed by foreign interests.” The justices who ruled against him weren’t exercising constitutional judgment — they were corrupted, disloyal, cowardly. The only valid ruling is the one that confirms his authority. Any institution that checks him is, by definition, broken.

The parallel is exact across all three maps. Each narrative requires catastrophe as its premise and Trump as its conclusion. The world needs him because it’s on fire. America needs him because it’s rotting. The courts need him because they’ve been compromised. This is why the apocalyptic language isn’t an excess — it’s a requirement.

The TACO Framework Applied to Geography — and to People

For readers who have followed my earlier work on Trump’s behavioral patterns, this analysis maps directly onto the TACO framework I’ve been developing: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate.

Threaten: Countries, cities, and individuals that resist Trump’s authority receive escalating rhetorical attacks. Canada goes from trading partner to “very nasty.” Chicago goes from major American city to “worse than Afghanistan.” Gorsuch and Barrett go from celebrated appointees to family embarrassments. The threats aren’t proportional — they’re performative, designed to establish dominance and punish independence.

Accommodate: Leaders, places, and people that accommodate Trump receive disproportionate rewards. Orbán gets defended against all criticism. Montana gets described in quasi-religious terms. Kavanaugh, whose dissent read more as a warning about institutional chaos than as a defense of Trump’s tariff policy, gets “strength and wisdom and love of country.” The accommodation doesn’t require substantive alignment on policy — it requires personal deference.

Capitulate: When the threatening works — when a leader, a city, or an institution adjusts its behavior to please Trump — the capitulation is celebrated as evidence of Trump’s dominance. Iraq “has been a much friendlier place. They talk to us.” The framing is always the same: resistance was foolish, submission was inevitable, and Trump’s pressure is what made it happen.

Obfuscate: When the pattern becomes too obvious, Trump deploys distraction. He claims his endorsements swung elections. He claims to have settled conflicts he had no role in. He suggests the justices who ruled against him were bribed by foreign powers. These aren’t verifiable claims — they’re fog machines, designed to obscure the simplicity of the underlying psychology by burying it under a blizzard of grandiose assertions.

The maps — geographic and personal — strip away the obfuscation and reveal the pattern bare. Whether you’re plotting nations on a globe, cities on a map, or justices on a 6-3 split, the same terrain emerges every time.

Accommodation Psychology in Action

In my series “The Ultimate Whisperer,” I’ve explored what I call accommodation psychology — the pattern by which institutions, individuals, and even entire nations gradually adjust their behavior to manage an unreasonable actor, ultimately normalizing the unreasonable demands and forgetting that the baseline ever existed.

The three maps are accommodation psychology made visible.

Consider what has already happened internationally. NATO allies have learned that public criticism of Trump results in punitive rhetoric and potential trade retaliation — so they mute their criticism. Democratic leaders have learned that personal flattery earns better treatment than substantive cooperation — so the savvier ones have begun performing deference. Countries seeking favorable trade terms have learned that the path runs not through policy alignment but through personal courtship of the president — so they send delegations to Mar-a-Lago.

Domestically, the accommodation is even more advanced. Blue-state governors have learned that criticizing Trump publicly means their disaster relief requests get scrutinized, their federal funding gets questioned, and their cities get publicly branded as hellholes. Some have begun moderating their tone. Red-state governors have learned that performing loyalty — appearing at rallies, echoing Trump’s rhetoric, refusing to criticize even his most extreme positions — earns praise and preferential treatment. So the performance intensifies.

And now, institutionally, the Supreme Court has received its first direct experience of the pattern. Three justices ruled with Trump and were praised. Six ruled against him and were called disgraces. Two of those six — Gorsuch and Barrett — hold lifetime appointments and cannot be removed for their rulings. They ruled as they believed the Constitution required. They did so knowing the response. The question now is whether other institutions, watching this play out, draw the right lesson — that the binary is absolute but the power is not — or the wrong one, that it is simply safer to accommodate.

This is how accommodation psychology works at scale. The unreasonable actor doesn’t change. The world changes around him. And the longer it goes on, the more the accommodation looks like normal politics — until someone steps back far enough to see the full picture.

The three maps provide that step back. They show us, in aggregate, what daily news coverage cannot: the complete pattern, spanning years, spanning continents, applied with mechanical consistency to every person, place, and nation that crosses Trump’s field of vision.

The Danger of the Mirror

So what? Presidents have had egos before. Politicians have played favorites. Partisanship has always colored how leaders talk about the opposition. Why does it matter that Trump’s mental map is organized by narcissism rather than strategy?

It matters because maps have consequences.

When Trump calls Canada “very nasty,” that language shapes the negotiating environment for real trade deals that affect real workers in both countries. When he calls Chicago “worse than Afghanistan,” that language shapes federal resource allocation to a city with real crime problems that require real solutions. When he calls Gorsuch and Barrett family embarrassments, that language shapes the environment in which every federal judge — and every institution that depends on judicial independence — must now operate.

When he praises Orbán and Erdogan while denigrating Macron and Carney, he is actively reorienting the alliance structure that has kept the peace in Europe for eighty years — not because he’s calculated that authoritarians make better partners, but because they make him feel better about himself. When he praises Thomas and Alito while attacking Barrett and Gorsuch, he is signaling to every court in the country that the criterion for a good ruling is not constitutional fidelity but personal loyalty to the executive.

The narcissistic map isn’t just embarrassing. It’s operationally dangerous. Foreign leaders who understand the pattern can exploit it — and they do. A grand state visit, a flattering phone call, a public statement of admiration, and suddenly a nation that is actively working against American interests earns “great relationship” status. Meanwhile, an ally that quietly cooperates on intelligence sharing, military basing, and trade facilitation but refuses to perform public worship gets labeled “nasty.”

The incentive structure is catastrophic at every scale. It rewards performance over substance. It punishes independence. It ensures that every decision — from tariff levels to military deployments to constitutional rulings — is filtered through the single question that organizes Trump’s entire world: How does this make me feel about me?

What a Canadian Sees, One Last Time

I’ve closed each essay in this series with the outsider’s perspective, and I’ll do so one final time.

From Toronto, what is most striking about this series of events — the NYT analyses, and now the Supreme Court’s tariff ruling and Trump’s response — is not what they reveal about Trump. Anyone paying attention already knew his psychology. What is striking is the scale at which the binary now operates.

When this series began, we were mapping nations and cities. Trump’s sorting mechanism was visible in his rhetoric, but it operated at enough remove — in the space between a president and a sovereign nation, between a president and a major city — that it could still be mistaken for policy, however erratic.

The Supreme Court ruling removed that distance entirely. Nine named individuals, appointed to the highest court in the land, exercising their constitutional function, were sorted in real time into the two columns that have organized every map in this series. The criterion was identical. The language was identical. The speed was identical — within hours of the ruling, the binary was complete.

This is what a Canadian observer sees that Americans, living inside the map, sometimes cannot: the pattern is not getting more sophisticated with time and experience. It is not evolving into something more strategic or more stable. It is simply expanding — to new domains, new institutions, new individuals — while remaining exactly the same.

The John Irving novel from which this series borrows its title — The World According to Garp — is about a man who tries to make sense of a chaotic, dangerous world through storytelling. Garp imposes narrative on the randomness of life because the alternative — accepting that the world is fundamentally ungovernable — is too frightening to bear.

Trump does something similar, but inverted. He doesn’t impose narrative to make sense of the world. He imposes himself on the world to make the world make sense of him. Every country, every city, every individual, every institution is processed through the single question of what it says about Donald Trump. The world isn’t chaotic and dangerous — it’s a stage, and he is the only character that matters.

Garp at least understood that his stories were fiction.

Trump believes the mirror shows reality.

That’s the difference. And for the 7.8 billion people who share this planet with him — and the 330 million Americans who live in his cesspools and beautiful places and depend on his appointees to uphold their constitutional rights — that difference is everything.

Postscript — March 5, 2026

The ink on this series was barely dry when I felt the need for an update>

On March 5, 2026 — yesterday — Donald Trump fired Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem. She had overseen his mass deportation agenda for thirteen months, including the militarized Minneapolis operation that left two American citizens dead. She had spent $220 million in taxpayer funds on an ad campaign featuring herself. She had cycled through three acting FEMA administrators while bottlenecking disaster relief. None of that ended her tenure.

What ended it was two days of congressional testimony in which she stated, under oath, that Trump had personally approved the ad campaign. The president told Reuters the same day: “I never knew anything about it.” A sitting Cabinet secretary had drawn a direct line between a $220 million vanity project and the man who authorized it, in a legally significant forum, on the record.

As I documented in The Noem Files, Trump does not fire people for killing American citizens. He fires people who create evidence of his own involvement in their failures.

True to the pattern, the firing came wrapped in praise. Noem “has served us well,” Trump wrote, and would be moving to a new role as Special Envoy for the Shield of the Americas — a position that did not exist before Thursday afternoon. A promotion-shaped firing. The language was warm; the meaning was identical to every other entry in the individuals map. You no longer reflect what I need to see. You’re gone.

Sen. John Kennedy of Louisiana, who drew out the testimony that sealed Noem’s fate, understood the criterion immediately. The ads, he said, put “the president in a terribly awkward spot.” Not Noem. Not the families of Renee Good and Alex Pretti. The president.

That is the only column in Trump’s map that has ever mattered.
