The Iran Crisis

Why Iran Won’t Fold

Part XIV: What War Would Actually Look Like

February 23, 2026

President Trump’s threat is the latest example of how he is trying to force Iranian leaders to see that they have no choice but to give up every aspect of their nuclear program.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

The question that has confounded Washington for weeks was finally spoken out loud on Saturday by Steve Witkoff, President Trump’s lead negotiator with Iran. Standing before Fox News cameras, he searched for the right word, then abandoned the search.

“He’s curious as to why they haven’t... I don’t want to use the word ‘capitulated,’ but why they haven’t capitulated.”

The admission was more revealing than Witkoff intended. The entire architecture of American pressure — two carrier strike groups, 100-plus aircraft, the largest military buildup in the Middle East in two decades, explicit threats of strikes — rests on the assumption that Iran will eventually calculate that the cost of defiance exceeds the cost of surrender. Washington is genuinely puzzled that Tehran has not yet performed that calculation and arrived at the expected answer.

The answer, analysts say, is that Iran has performed the calculation. It just doesn’t agree with Washington’s arithmetic.

The Rational Logic of Refusal

Ali Vaez is Crisis Group’s Iran Project Director and Senior Adviser to the President.

“For Iran, submitting to U.S. terms is more dangerous than suffering another U.S. strike.”

That assessment from Ali Vaez, the Iran director of the International Crisis Group, cuts through weeks of diplomatic noise to the core strategic reality. It is not that Iran’s leadership is reckless or suicidal. It is that they have looked at what Washington is demanding and concluded that compliance is the existential threat, not the bombs.

The Trump administration’s position, as publicly stated by Witkoff, is zero enrichment — Iran permanently surrenders its entire nuclear program. Iran’s foreign minister Abbas Araghchi told CBS on Sunday that enrichment is a right the supreme leader upholds and that his officials cannot abandon. In a 2024 speech, Ayatollah Khamenei made the ideological stakes plain: “Nuclear energy is not the problem, nor are human rights; America’s problem is with the very existence of the Islamic Republic.”

Danny Citrinowicz, who headed the Iran branch of Israel’s Defense Intelligence and is now at the Atlantic Council, argues that uranium enrichment has become “a pillar of the regime itself.” If Iran’s leaders concede on that point, he said, “they will actually undermine the existence of the regime itself.” The logic flows directly from Venezuela: Trump removed Maduro. If Iran surrenders the only leverage it has — nuclear capability — it becomes the next Maduro.

Vaez extends the analysis further. Iran does not believe that once it capitulates, Washington will ease the pressure. It believes capitulation would “only encourage the U.S. to go for the jugular.” Under that calculus, another American strike is survivable. The regime has prepared multiple layers of leadership to replace anyone killed. It learned that lesson from June’s 12-day war. Surrender, by contrast, offers no path back.

The University of Tehran’s Sasan Karimi, who served as deputy vice president for strategy in Iran’s previous government, framed it in terms that Washington rarely considers: “At times, a political state — especially an ideological one — may weigh its place in history as heavily as, or even more heavily than, its immediate survival.”

This is not the psychology of a government about to capitulate.

The Ally Fracture

While Washington puzzles over Tehran’s intransigence, its own coalition is fracturing in ways that carry direct operational consequences.

Britain has formally refused to grant the United States permission to use Diego Garcia in the Indian Ocean or RAF Fairford in Gloucestershire for strikes on Iran. The refusal is grounded in a specific legal argument: international law does not distinguish between a state that carries out a strike and one that supports it with knowledge of the circumstances. London’s legal counsel has concluded that granting base access makes Britain a co-participant in any internationally wrongful act.

Trump’s response was immediate and transactional. He withdrew his backing for Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s Chagos Islands deal — the arrangement securing Britain’s 99-year lease on Diego Garcia — posting “DO NOT GIVE AWAY DIEGO GARCIA” on Truth Social.

The operational significance is not symbolic. June’s Operation Midnight Hammer was a single overnight mission; B-2 bombers flew round-trip from Whiteman Air Force Base in Missouri. The planning now underway, according to Reuters, envisions weeks of sustained operations targeting Iranian state and security facilities across a country slightly larger than Venezuela and Afghanistan combined. That operational scope requires forward basing that Britain is withholding.

Mike Huckabee, the US ambassador to Israel, waves upon his arrival in the West Bank town of Taybeh, in 2025. Photograph: Nasser Nasser/AP

Meanwhile, US Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee told Tucker Carlson that Israel has a right to much of the Middle East. Arab diplomats across the region — the states Washington needs to at minimum stay quiet during any strike — reacted with fury. The coalition that was already absent is now actively fracturing.

What War Would Actually Look Like

Nancy Youssef’s reporting in The Atlantic this week revealed something that has gone largely unremarked: during Trump’s first term, Pentagon officials took the extraordinary step of sharing their war plans for Iran with White House advisers specifically to deter the president from acting. They reasoned that if his advisers saw the risks, they would counsel restraint. It worked twice.

This time, those same war plans are being used as a menu of options.

The administration has floated four separate aims for any military campaign, each requiring a fundamentally different approach: a negotiating lever, regime decapitation, missile program destruction, or nuclear site re-strikes. The problem, as current and former defense officials told Youssef, is that America cannot achieve all four simultaneously — and Trump has not indicated which he is actually pursuing.

Here is what each scenario looks like in practice.

A limited pressure strike — the option Trump described on Friday as what he is “considering” — would target IRGC bases and production facilities over a period of days. The Navy would lead, with F-35s and EA-18G Growlers suppressing Iran’s air defenses first, followed by Tomahawk missiles from submarines and strike aircraft hitting fixed targets. Bryan Clark of the Hudson Institute assessed this as the most likely scenario based on the current force posture: “going after softer targets, like IRGC bases and production facilities, over a protracted period of time.” Such strikes could limit Iran’s ballistic missile capability to only the missiles already on mobile launchers — but Iran could rebuild those facilities within months.

Targeting Iran’s leadership would require precision weapons after air defenses are suppressed — laser-guided bombs against specific individuals, inevitably risking civilian casualties. Trump has spoken openly about Khamenei as a target since June, writing on Truth Social that he “knew EXACTLY where he was sheltered.” An Axios source confirmed this weekend that options presented to Trump include “one scenario that takes out the ayatollah and his son and the mullahs.” But if the IRGC survives while the clerical leadership is removed, it could take control and steer Iran toward an even more hostile posture. The regime would have little left to lose.

Re-striking nuclear facilities would again require B-2 bombers carrying GBU-57 bunker-busters against hardened underground sites. Satellite imagery from Vantor, a Colorado-based firm, shows Iran has spent recent months pouring fresh concrete at tunnel entrances near Natanz and constructing a concrete sarcophagus around the Taleghan 2 facility at Parchin — hardening that directly targets the vulnerabilities exposed in June. And IAEA Director-General Rafael Grossi confirmed this week that most of Iran’s pre-bombing nuclear stockpile remains in place, directly contradicting Trump’s “obliterated” claim. A second strike would contradict Trump’s own assertions about the first.

Former deputy assistant secretary of defense Mick Mulroy offered the assessment that cuts through all four scenarios: “We can degrade something. We can destroy something. But that doesn’t mean they can’t rebuild it.” Nuclear programs, he said, “ultimately end through negotiations or political change” — not through military force alone.

The Human Stakes

In Ramat Aviv, a quiet neighborhood in northwest Tel Aviv, some of the buildings hit by Iranian missiles last June still stand in ruins. One resident, Chen, 44, whose apartment was damaged while his family was away, described the aftermath: “It took us a lot of time to stabilize.” Now, as the buildup accelerates, he is weighing whether to evacuate his wife and two children. “You don’t know when it will actually happen,” he said, “and you also don’t want to get stuck.”

At Soroka Medical Center in Beersheba — which sustained a direct missile hit last June — a quarter of the hospital’s beds and more than a third of its operating rooms remain unusable. Director Shlomi Codish is preparing to move 400 to 500 patients on short notice, including premature babies and patients on ventilators. “It will take several more years until we finish rebuilding everything that was destroyed,” he said.

Amalya Liebermann, 27, a video director and editor from Tel Aviv, with her friend, Rani Assa Polansky, 26, also from Tel Aviv. Amid the preparations, most Israelis continue their daily routines. (Heidi Levine/FTWP)

Israeli officials have made their strategic position clear: this time, they are second fiddle by design. “For 30 years, all Israeli governments tried to ensure that the Iranian problem would not just be an Israeli problem, and they succeeded,” said Brigadier General Ran Kochav, the former IDF Air and Missile Defense Commander. Israel will join if needed, but the US is leading. The moral and strategic responsibility sits entirely in Washington.

That transfer of responsibility comes with a specific Israeli concern that has received insufficient attention: a prolonged conflict risks exhausting Israel’s supply of missile interceptors. During the June war, Iran fired hundreds of long-range missiles; Israel intercepted more than 80 percent, but the attrition was real. A weeks-long campaign is a categorically different defensive problem.

The Last Off-Ramp

Against this backdrop, a potential diplomatic escape route is being quietly debated by both sides. It originated from IAEA Director-General Grossi: Iran would be permitted to produce small amounts of nuclear fuel for medical purposes at the Tehran Research Reactor — a facility first supplied to the Shah by the United States under the Atoms for Peace program in the 1960s. Iran could claim it still enriches. Trump could claim Iran has shuttered everything capable of weapons production. Both sides declare victory.

The proposal faces one immediate obstacle: Witkoff said publicly on Fox News Sunday that “zero enrichment” is Trump’s only acceptable outcome. But Araghchi told CBS the same day that the US had not actually demanded zero enrichment in the talks themselves — a direct contradiction of Witkoff’s public position. Either there is a gap between what the US says publicly and what it is actually negotiating, or the two sides are not hearing the same conversations.

Abbas Araghchi, the Iranian foreign minister, last year. He said on Sunday that his country was not ready to give up what he said was its “right” to make nuclear fuel under the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

A third round of talks is confirmed for Thursday in Geneva. Iran’s draft proposal is expected to be transmitted to Washington on Monday or Tuesday. Araghchi told CBS he believed a “fast deal” was still possible: “We can work on those elements and prepare a good text.”

The Situation Room meeting that took place on Wednesday of last week tells a different story about the administration’s actual posture. Vice President Vance, Secretary Rubio, Joint Chiefs Chairman General Dan Caine, CIA Director John Ratcliffe, and Chief of Staff Susie Wiles gathered to discuss military options. Vance did not oppose a strike but pressed Caine and Ratcliffe hard on risks. Caine’s assessment carried a specific weight: when asked about the Venezuela operation, he told Trump there was a high likelihood of success. For Iran, he has been unable to offer the same reassurance. It is a far more difficult target.

The Axios source who described the targeting options put the uncertainty plainly: “What the President chooses no one knows. I don’t think he knows.”

The Urgency That Wasn’t

Before accepting the premise that war is necessary or imminent, a question posed this morning by Thomas Wright of the Brookings Institution — who served as senior director for strategic planning at the National Security Council under Biden — deserves a direct answer: “What exactly has changed since June that justifies a more extreme course of action now?”

The buildup itself demands context. The force Trump has assembled represents, by one account, 40 to 50 percent of all deployable US airpower in the world. That is not a pressure campaign — that is a significant portion of America’s entire global military capacity concentrated around one country, while China watches the Indo-Pacific and Russia watches Europe.

On the urgency of Iran’s nuclear timeline, the administration is sending contradictory signals that cannot both be true. Witkoff told Fox News on Saturday that Iran is “probably a week away from having industrial-grade bomb-making material.” The IAEA director-general told French television this week that most of Iran’s pre-bombing stockpile remains in place but that the June strikes set the program back years. One of these assessments is dramatically wrong. If Witkoff is right, the Geneva talks are theater and strikes are already decided. If the IAEA is right, there is no week-to-week urgency — there is time.

Wright’s strategic argument adds a dimension missing from the current debate. A comprehensive zero-enrichment deal requiring sweeping sanctions relief could paradoxically stabilize the very regime it seeks to constrain. Tens of billions of dollars unlocked, global markets reopened, isolation ended — all at the precise moment when internal pressure from the January massacre, economic collapse, and a looming succession crisis is mounting organically. The administration may be about to offer the Iranian regime a lifeline in exchange for nuclear concessions, when patience and sustained pressure might achieve both goals at lower cost and lower risk.

Wright’s conclusion is the sharpest single sentence published this weekend on the Iran crisis: the United States faces “a choice between recognizing the leverage created by recent events and discarding it.” June 2025 created that leverage. The question Thursday’s Geneva talks must answer is whether Washington is about to spend it wisely — or waste it on a war whose objectives remain undefined.

The Structural Problem

The original essay in this series documented the 30-day military buildup that transformed the Iranian standoff from a diplomatic dispute into a military countdown. This essay documents why that countdown has not resolved the fundamental problem — and may have deepened it.

Trump entered these negotiations believing maximum pressure would produce maximum concessions. Iran entered them believing that the lesson of Venezuela is precisely the opposite: that states which surrender their leverage get removed, while states that retain it survive. Both governments are rational actors. They simply have completely different models of how the other side will respond to pressure.

Youssef’s most important observation is also the most understated: “without ground troops, there are limits to what U.S. strikes can achieve.” The 2003 Iraq invasion involved five carrier groups, many more aircraft, and 170,000 ground troops. Even then, it produced outcomes that most historians now consider one of the gravest American strategic errors of modern times. What Trump is contemplating is a sustained air campaign against a country of 90 million people, with no ground forces, no allied support from Britain, no Arab coalition, no clear objective, and a Supreme Leader who has concluded that fighting is safer than folding.

Thursday’s Geneva talks may produce a face-saving formula. Araghchi’s language on CBS was more flexible than his public statements of recent weeks. The Grossi medical enrichment proposal gives both sides a ladder to climb down from their stated positions.

But the Witkoff quote will linger. The president of the United States is “curious” why Iran hasn’t capitulated.

Tehran has an answer. Washington doesn’t appear to have heard it.
