When Democracy Fights Back

The Virginia Verdict

Part XIX: Yes Wins — By the Width of a Fingernail

April 21, 2026

Virginia has spoken.

By a margin of 50.7 to 49.3 percent — roughly 40,000 votes out of nearly 2.7 million cast — Virginians approved the redistricting referendum on April 21st. The Associated Press called the race with 88 percent of votes counted. The new map, drawn by Democratic state legislators and approved by Governor Abigail Spanberger, will now govern Virginia’s congressional elections through 2030, giving Democrats a projected advantage in ten of the state’s eleven House seats.

Don Scott, the Speaker of the Virginia House of Delegates, put it plainly: “Virginia just changed the trajectory of the 2026 midterms.”

He is not wrong. But the trajectory was changed by less than one and a half points. That number deserves to sit alongside the headline.

Whatever the final margin, the sentence above does not contain the whole story. It contains the conclusion. The story — the one worth telling the morning after — is about everything that led to that result, and everything it sets in motion.

In Part XVIII of this series, written eight days ago, I promised a postmortem. This is it

What $75 Million Bought

Let’s start with the money, because the money is the clearest measure of how seriously both parties understood what was at stake.

By election day, the Virginia redistricting referendum had attracted more than $98 million in combined spending — making it one of the most expensive off-cycle ballot measure campaigns in American history. For context, that is only $3 million less than what was spent on last year’s Virginia governor’s race, and $16 million more than the New York City mayoral race. For a single-question April referendum in one state, that figure is staggering. It reflects the arithmetic that both parties have been running since Texas fired the opening shot last summer: in a House where the majority is measured in single digits, four seats is a majority-maker.

Representative Hakeem Jeffries, the House Democratic minority leader, backed the Yes campaign directly. Speaker Mike Johnson backed No. Donald Trump called into a Virginia radio host’s streaming channel the night before the vote to warn that a Democratic House majority would be “a disaster.” The president who triggered this entire redistricting war by demanding Texas Republicans redraw their maps at his explicit direction then added, with an unwitting candour that deserved a wider audience: “I don’t know if you know what gerrymandering is, but it’s not good.”

He is correct. It is not good. It is what he started.

Jeffries, to his considerable credit, responded by offering to broadcast Trump’s statement to every Virginia voter. Sometimes the opposition writes the best ads.

The Shame Economy

Before we assess the result, it is worth recording — for the historical account, if nothing else — the extraordinary psychological discomfort that defined the Yes campaign from start to finish.

Michelle Maldonado, a Democratic member of the Virginia House of Delegates, delivered a pep talk to campaign volunteers near her home in Manassas that contained the following sentence: “Nobody wants to do this. I don’t want to do this.”

She then spent the next hour helping those volunteers prepare to knock on doors in support of doing exactly that.

This is not hypocrisy in the classical sense. It is something more interesting and more troubling: the complete internalization of a lose-lose logic. Democrats calculated, not unreasonably, that if Republicans gerrymander without apology and without restraint, then procedural restraint on the Democratic side simply means permanent minority status. The math is brutal and the conclusion follows from it.

But the performance of reluctance has a cost. Campaigns built on “I’m sorry to have to ask” are not, historically, campaigns built for comfortable margins. They are campaigns where the volunteers knock on doors and the candidates hold their noses and everybody understands, at some level, that something is being sacrificed in the name of necessity.

State Senator Creigh Deeds framed it this way at the University of Virginia: “I believe that people should choose their representatives. Representatives shouldn’t choose their people.” He then spent the rest of his remarks urging Virginians to vote yes on an amendment that would let Democrats choose quite a lot of their own representatives. Nobody pointed out the contradiction. Or perhaps someone had, and he simply didn’t care anymore. After eighteen parts of this series, I’m not sure which interpretation is more alarming.

The Maryland Contrast

The most instructive data point in this story was never in Virginia at all. It was across the Potomac.

Maryland is a stronger Democratic bastion than Virginia. Democrats hold seven of its eight House seats. The lone Republican-held seat — in the western panhandle — was genuinely vulnerable. National Democratic leaders, including the party apparatus in Washington, pushed hard for Maryland to join the redistricting offensive. Governor Wes Moore was on board. The opportunity was real.

Maryland’s Senate president said no.

Now that the Virginia result is in, the Maryland contrast is no longer hypothetical — it is analytical. If Virginia’s referendum passed, Maryland left a seat on the table that it will not recover before November. If Virginia’s referendum failed, Maryland’s Senate president looks not merely principled but prescient: he read the room in his own state, resisted the national party’s pressure, and preserved his state’s redistricting integrity at no political cost.

The Maryland comparison also permanently deflates the “we had no choice” argument that animated the Virginia campaign. They did have a choice. A neighbouring Democratic state made a different one. Whether Maryland was right or wrong, principled or strategically naive, is a debate that will now continue into the fall. But the choice existed. It always did.

Eric Cantor’s Revenge Tour

The opposition to Virginia’s redistricting amendment was led, with magnificent irony, by Eric Cantor.

For readers who need a refresher: Eric Cantor was the House Majority Leader who, more than almost any other Republican of his generation, helped build the modern architecture of congressional obstruction, partisan warfare, and procedural bad faith that created the conditions for the redistricting war now consuming his state. He is not a neutral civic actor defending bipartisan principles. He is one of the principal architects of the system he now decries.

“Democrats have overreached,” Cantor told the Atlantic in the weeks before the vote. “There’s no doubt that gives us an advantage.”

He was not wrong about the political dynamics. But the man who helped gerrymander Republican dominance across the Sun Belt for a decade, now complaining that Democrats have “overreached” by doing roughly the same thing, deserves to have his participation in the historical record noted. In any serious country, this would be disqualifying commentary. In America in 2026, it is a press release.

Representative Ben Cline, whose conservative district would have become a Democratic-leaning battleground under the proposed map, launched a separate group to defeat the referendum. “Virginia had a Republican governor less than three months ago,” he said, “but Democrats now want to take 91 percent of the House seats. That’s insane.”

This is also not wrong. It is also exactly what Republicans did in Texas, North Carolina, Ohio, and Missouri without, as far as I can determine, a single public statement from Ben Cline registering any comparable objection.

The Obama Problem — Resolved

Much was made, in the weeks leading up to the referendum, of the awkward position Barack Obama occupied in the campaign. He appeared in Yes campaign ads urging Virginia voters to fight back against Republican redistricting. He also appeared, in old footage dredged up by the No campaign, delivering eloquent speeches against partisan gerrymandering. Both uses of Obama were accurate. Neither was dishonest. Both were deeply American.

What the Obama paradox actually illustrated — and this is the thing neither set of ads could say plainly — is that principled positions have a shelf life in a system where principles are not universally observed. Obama was against gerrymandering when Republicans were doing it to Democrats. He supports this referendum because Democrats are doing it to Republicans in response to Republicans doing it to Democrats.

This is called reciprocity. It is not admirable. It is not the politics Obama campaigned on in 2008. It is, however, coherent — if you accept the premise that unilateral disarmament in a redistricting war is not principle, but surrender.

Eric Holder — Obama’s own Attorney General, and a man who spent years building the National Redistricting Foundation in explicit support of independent commissions and fair maps — arrived at the same place. He announced his support for the Virginia referendum with a statement that contained exactly three words worth preserving: “We have to do something.”

That is not the slogan of a man who wanted this fight. It is the slogan of a man who looked at what was happening and decided that the alternative to doing something was allowing it to happen unopposed.

The Confusion That Wasn’t (Quite)

The Republican No campaign ran an operation that deserves documentation regardless of the outcome, because it will serve as a template in the next state where this playbook gets deployed.

The primary Republican-aligned group opposing the referendum — Virginians for Fair Maps — raised more than $24 million, almost entirely from a nonprofit of the same name that declined to disclose its donors. A second Republican group, Justice for Democracy, collected $9.4 million, also almost entirely from dark-money sources. On the Democratic side, the main Yes group, Virginians for Fair Elections, raised over $64 million — at least $61 million of it from dark-money nonprofits, including $38 million from House Majority Forward, a nonprofit aligned directly with Hakeem Jeffries. A New York Times analysis found that of the $98 million raised by the three groups that bought the most advertising, at least 96 percent came from nonprofits that do not disclose their donors.

Both sides. Ninety-six percent. In a campaign nominally about democratic accountability.

In heavily Democratic Arlington, No yard signs appeared in traditional Democratic blue and green, reading “Don’t Divide Arlington” — designed to catch inattentive progressive voters who might oppose the referendum without fully registering which side they were supporting.

You do not run that advertising if you are confident in your argument. You run it when you need to confuse voters before they can think clearly. Confusion is the strategy when the facts are inconvenient.

The facts, in this case, were as follows: Virginia Democrats drew a map that favoured their party. They did so transparently. They put it on the ballot. They invited voters to approve or reject it. The opposition, by contrast, was funded in significant part by people who would not say who they were.

Draw your own conclusions about who was afraid of transparency.

The Scoreboard, Redrawn

The national redistricting arithmetic, as it stands the morning after Virginia:

Republican gains: Texas (+5), North Carolina (+1), Missouri (+1), Ohio (+1-2) Democratic gains: California (+5), Utah (+1, court-ordered), Virginia (+4, confirmed) Pending: Florida (+1-5R potential)

Virginia’s four seats have erased the Republican structural advantage built since Texas last summer. The redistricting war has arrived, as the NYT put it, at “a rough draw.” The Cook Political Report now rates Democrats at approximately 217 seats — one short of a majority — meaning a single toss-up seat could flip the House, before a single campaign dollar is spent in November.

Florida is the last remaining front. Governor Ron DeSantis — whose special session opened April 20th and runs through April 24th — has promised a new map. If Florida moves aggressively, Republicans could claw back some of what Virginia cost them. DeSantis is not a man typically accused of insufficient aggression.

The Supreme Court wildcard also remains live. A ruling striking down the Voting Rights Act provision that bans racial gerrymandering — possible this spring — could open additional southern states to Republican remapping before November. That ruling is the subject of Part XX.

What Deeds Got Right

I want to return to Creigh Deeds one more time, because his discomfort was the most honest thing said in this entire campaign, and it deserves a fair accounting now that the votes are in.

Deeds was right that the redistricting commission Virginia built in 2020 was good. He was right that something important was being sacrificed. He was right that some Democratic voters would not be convinced no matter how carefully he explained the stakes — and on a 50.7-to-49.3 result, those voters very nearly cost the Yes campaign everything. He was right about all of it, and he asked voters to vote yes anyway.

The margin tells you something the victory lap will not. This was not a mandate. It was a decision, made by the narrowest of democratic pluralities, to fight fire with fire. The bipartisan redistricting commission Virginia pioneered in 2020 is now suspended until 2030. Whether that was the right trade depends on what happens in November — and on whether Florida and the Supreme Court give Democrats any room to breathe.

The View From Here

Democracy is fighting back. But it is fighting back by adopting the weapons of its attackers.

That sentence has been building across eighteen parts of this series. It is now, after Virginia, no longer a prediction. It is a description.

50.7 to 49.3. Forty thousand votes. That is the margin by which the redistricting war arrives at a rough draw. It is not the margin of a movement. It is the margin of a calculation — made by just enough voters, in just enough counties, to tip the balance of a $98 million campaign fought almost entirely in the dark, by nonprofits that will not say who they are, over a map that will govern who holds power in Washington.

The redistricting war will not end here. Florida’s session runs through April 24th. The Supreme Court’s Voting Rights Act ruling could reset the board entirely. The Callais case will reach a verdict this summer. Each of those developments will be examined in Part XX.

From the outside — and I am always, in this series, speaking from the outside — what happened in Virginia last night looks less like a democratic victory and more like a successful act of democratic self-defence. Which is not quite the same thing. A country that requires its citizens to gerrymander back in order to avoid being permanently gerrymandered out is a country whose democratic architecture is under serious strain. Virginia held. The architecture is still under strain.

Virginia voted. The redistricting war continues.

We will be watching Florida.
