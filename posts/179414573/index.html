Two Dominoes Fall

Part 8: Judge Dismisses Both Comey and James Cases Over ‘Unlawful’ Prosecutor

November 24, 2025

One illegal appointment. Two cases dead. Judge rules Trump prosecutor Lindsey Halligan was unlawfully installed.

November 24, 2025: A federal judge has dismissed criminal cases against both former FBI Director James Comey and New York Attorney General Letitia James, ruling that Lindsey Halligan, the interim U.S. attorney who brought the charges, was illegally appointed by the Justice Department.

In what the Associated Press calls “a stunning rebuke of the Trump administration’s efforts to target Trump’s political opponents,” U.S. District Judge Cameron McGowan Currie found that Halligan’s appointment violated the Constitution’s Appointments Clause, rendering both prosecutions null and void.

The simultaneous dismissal of both cases marks a catastrophic failure of what I’ve been documenting as Trump’s systematic prosecution of his critics. In just eight weeks, two of the three enemies list cases have collapsed—not on their merits, but because the administration couldn’t even appoint prosecutors legally.

The Constitutional Violation

The dismissals turn on a fundamental constitutional issue: Who has the power to appoint federal prosecutors?

Under the Appointments Clause, principal officers like U.S. Attorneys must be nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate. While the law allows for temporary appointments, these must follow specific procedures and limitations.

Judge Currie—a Clinton appointee from South Carolina brought in specially to review these cases—found that Halligan’s appointment violated these requirements. Both defendants had asked for the cases to be dismissed with prejudice, meaning the Justice Department couldn’t bring them again. But the judge instead dismissed them without prejudice, though it remains unclear if or how the Justice Department might attempt to revive the prosecutions.

This isn’t a technicality. It’s a constitutional safeguard designed to prevent exactly what happened here: a president installing loyalists to prosecute his enemies without Senate oversight.

The Pattern Shattered

Seven weeks ago, I documented how this pattern began:

September 20, 2025: Trump posts on social media demanding Attorney General Pam Bondi prosecute Comey, James, and Adam Schiff: “They’re all guilty as hell, but nothing is going to be done.” He adds: “JUSTICE MUST BE SERVED, NOW!!!”

September 20-21: Erik Siebert, the U.S. Attorney who refused to bring charges, is forced out.

September 21: Lindsey Halligan—a former White House aide and Trump personal lawyer with zero criminal prosecution experience—is installed as interim U.S. Attorney.

September 25: Comey is indicted after just four days.

October 9: James is indicted for allegedly defrauding a bank by paying off her mortgage early—saving $50 per month.

November 24: Both cases dismissed as unlawfully brought.

The speed was the tell. Real federal prosecutions take months or years to build. These took days. Now we know why: they weren’t building cases. They were executing orders.

The Hollow Victories

Both Comey and James are free today, their cases dismissed. But consider what these “victories” cost:

James Comey:

  • Faced federal charges for allegedly lying to Congress

  • Months of legal proceedings

  • Hundreds of thousands in legal fees (defense attorneys estimate over $450,000 in the first month alone)

  • Reputation forever linked to criminal prosecution

Letitia James:

  • Indicted for allegedly misleading lenders about her finances to secure better mortgage rates—a decades-old transaction

  • Forced to fight federal “bank fraud” charges while serving as New York’s Attorney General

  • Massive legal costs

  • The absurdity of prosecuting a 20-year-old mortgage overshadowed by the gravity of federal indictment

They won. But the process was the punishment.

One Enemy Remains Standing

With Comey and James freed, attention turns to the third member of the enemies list: John Bolton.

Unlike Comey and James, Bolton wasn’t prosecuted by Halligan. His case is being handled by different prosecutors in Maryland. But the pattern is the same:

  • Biden DOJ investigated his diary sharing and closed the case in 2021

  • Trump reopened it after taking office

  • Bolton now faces 18 Espionage Act counts (8 for transmission, 10 for retention) for sharing personal diary entries with two unauthorized individuals, reportedly his wife and daughter

  • At 76, he cannot leave the United States, his passport seized

Will Bolton’s prosecutors watch their colleagues’ cases collapse and reconsider? Or will they press forward, hoping Maryland judges are more accommodating than Virginia ones?

The Appointments Clause Strikes Back

Today’s ruling is a reminder that the Constitution still matters. The Appointments Clause isn’t some arcane technicality—it’s a crucial check on presidential power.

By requiring Senate confirmation for principal officers, the Founders ensured that no president could unilaterally staff the government with loyalists. Temporary appointments are allowed, but they’re supposed to be just that—temporary fixes, not end-runs around Senate oversight.

Trump tried to bypass this system. He installed Halligan without Senate confirmation specifically to prosecute his enemies. Today, a federal judge said no.

As the Associated Press reports, “The orders make Lindsey Halligan the latest Trump administration prosecutor to be disqualified because of the manner in which they were appointed.” Judges have separately disqualified interim U.S. attorneys in New Jersey, Los Angeles and Nevada—but in those cases, the prosecutions were allowed to continue. Here, because Halligan was “the sole signer of the indictments and the driving force behind them,” the cases died with her authority.

Where Were the Lawyers?

The most stunning aspect of this dismissal is how predictable it was. Any competent lawyer should have flagged the appointments issue immediately. Constitutional law students learn about the Appointments Clause in their first year.

Yet somehow, the Trump Justice Department either:

  1. Didn’t realize Halligan’s appointment was unlawful

  2. Knew and proceeded anyway, hoping judges wouldn’t notice

  3. Were so focused on prosecuting Trump’s enemies that they ignored basic constitutional requirements

None of these options inspire confidence in the rule of law.

The Broader Implications

Today’s dismissals reveal something important about Trump’s enemies list: It’s not just morally wrong. It’s legally incompetent.

In their rush to prosecute Trump’s critics, they:

  • Violated the Appointments Clause (Comey and James)

  • Admitted the grand jury never saw the final indictment (Comey)

  • Reopened closed cases for political reasons (Bolton)

  • Brought absurd charges that career prosecutors rejected (all three)

This isn’t the behavior of a Justice Department. It’s the behavior of a political hit squad that doesn’t even know how to execute hits properly.

Questions for the Aftermath

With two cases dismissed on constitutional grounds, urgent questions remain:

  1. Will the DOJ appeal? The dismissals were without prejudice, leaving the door technically open. But appealing would mean defending Halligan’s illegal appointment.

  2. Can Comey and James be re-indicted? While the judge dismissed without prejudice, the statute of limitations has now passed on Comey’s alleged 2020 false statements. The five-year window (18 U.S.C. § 1001) expired around September 25, 2025—just days after Halligan rushed to indict him. The timing wasn’t coincidental; it was desperate. Any new prosecution would need a properly appointed prosecutor and face the same vindictive prosecution arguments.

  3. Will Bolton’s case survive? Different prosecutors, different jurisdiction—but the same political motivation. Will Maryland judges follow Virginia’s lead?

  4. Will Halligan face consequences? She brought two unlawful prosecutions. As CNBC notes, she’s “President Donald Trump’s former personal lawyer” with “no prior prosecutorial experience.” The bar should investigate.

  5. Where is Congress? Two federal cases just collapsed because of an unlawful appointment. Will anyone investigate how this happened?

  6. What about Schiff? He was the third person named in Trump’s September 20 post. Is he next?

The System Holds—Barely

Today’s dismissals prove our institutions haven’t completely failed. A federal judge examined these prosecutions and found them constitutionally deficient. The rule of law prevailed.

But we came dangerously close to a different outcome. If Halligan’s appointment had been even marginally more careful—if they’d followed proper procedures—Comey and James might be facing trial on trumped-up charges.

The enemies list didn’t fail because the system automatically rejects political prosecutions. It failed because Trump’s team made a rookie constitutional error.

Next time, they might not be so sloppy.

Conclusion: Two Down, One to Go

When I began documenting the enemies list, critics said I was being alarmist. They said our institutions would hold. They said judges would never allow political prosecutions to proceed.

Today, they’re partially right. Judge Cameron McGowan Currie stopped these prosecutions cold. The system held.

But it shouldn’t have been this close. As the AP reports, these rulings amount to “a stunning rebuke of the Trump administration’s efforts to target Trump’s political opponents as well as its legal maneuvering to hastily install a loyalist prosecutor willing to file the cases.”

It shouldn’t take constitutional violations to stop political prosecutions. It shouldn’t require federal judges to do what Congress, the Justice Department’s own ethics rules, and basic decency should have prevented in the first place.

James Comey is free. Letitia James is free. But John Bolton still can’t leave the country. And somewhere in the White House, they’re learning from their mistakes.

The enemies list is wounded but not dead. And the fact that it ever became operational at all should terrify anyone who believes in the rule of law.

Because next time—and there will be a next time—they’ll remember to appoint their prosecutors properly. And then what will stop them?
