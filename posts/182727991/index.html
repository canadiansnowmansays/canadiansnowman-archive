Trump 2025-2026: Scorecards State of the Union and Reality Checks

A First-Year Scorecard

Part 3 of 3: Justice, Rights, and the Path Forward

January 1, 2026

An Unofficial State of the Union: Annual Review and Resolutions for 2026

Recap from Parts 1 and 2

Part 1 examined immigration enforcement (historic lows through questionable means), federal workforce gutting (249,000 net job losses), USAID elimination (500,000-1,000,000 preventable deaths), and chaotic trade policy (failed objectives with economic disruption).

Part 2 covered affordability failure (K-shaped economy benefiting only the wealthy), extrajudicial drug war killings (105 dead in boat strikes), diplomatic mixed results (Gaza success, Ukraine failure, exaggerated claims), and domestic military normalization (troops in American streets).

Part 3 concludes with justice system weaponization, civil rights reversals, fundamental constitutional questions, genuine achievements worth acknowledging, and comprehensive 2026 resolutions for moving forward.

9. Weaponization of Justice: Revenge Through Prosecution

The Promise: Turn the Justice Department on political adversaries in retaliation for prosecutions against him.

The Reality: Trump abandoned all pretense of prosecutorial independence, openly ordering investigations and indictments of perceived enemies. Unlike his first term’s informal, sometimes furtive efforts, Trump’s second term featured direct, public demands that Attorney General Pam Bondi sought to fulfill.

In April, Trump signed executive orders requiring DOJ scrutiny of officials from his first administration he viewed as disloyal. In September, he pushed out the U.S. Attorney in the Eastern District of Virginia after the prosecutor found no legitimate basis to indict James Comey and Letitia James. Trump told Bondi to move “now” to indict them.

Bondi installed Lindsey Halligan, a personal Trump lawyer with no prosecutorial experience, as the new U.S. Attorney. Days later, Halligan obtained grand jury indictments of both Comey and James. A judge dismissed the cases, ruling Halligan was improperly appointed, but DOJ continues attempting prosecution.

In Miami, a Trump appointee opened an investigation into what loyalists deemed a “grand conspiracy” by officials who led the Russia inquiry and criminal cases against Trump—a fishing expedition for revenge rather than evidence-based prosecution.

This open weaponization demolished the post-Watergate norm of Justice Department independence. Trump explicitly justified this as reciprocal retaliation for what he claimed was Biden’s weaponization—a claim without evidence that nonetheless provided political cover for genuine abuse.

Verdict: Total abandonment of prosecutorial independence and rule of law principles. Represents the most dangerous concentration of power in the Justice Department since Watergate.

2026 Resolution: Restore post-Watergate norms of prosecutorial independence. Let DOJ professionals make charging decisions based on evidence, not presidential directives. Acknowledge that weaponization—whether real or claimed—damages institutions more than it damages political opponents. Rebuild public trust through transparent, apolitical law enforcement.

10. Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion: Purge and Rewrite

The Promise: Eliminate DEI initiatives across government and pressure private sector to do the same.

The Reality: Trump executed a comprehensive campaign to make diversity initiatives taboo and erase discussion of racism from American institutions. The administration threatened federal employees with “adverse consequences” for failing to report colleagues who defied DEI purges.

Trump cut billions from schools and colleges with diversity programs, declaring they could not run “racially charged” orientation programs or reserve scholarships for minorities. The Pentagon reviewed military libraries for books addressing racism and sexism. Research grants mentioning “equity” or “racial minority” were systematically eliminated.

Trump ordered Confederate statues restored and celebrated, explicitly honoring those who fought to preserve slavery. He accused the Smithsonian of focusing too much on “how bad slavery was” and incorporating “divisive, race-centered ideology” in exhibits on race.

Private corporations and law firms faced pressure to abandon diversity practices. Trump signed executive orders restricting racism education in classrooms and limiting transgender issues teaching.

The administration gutted Biden-era policies protecting transgender students, reinterpreting Title IX to exclude LGBTQ+ protections. Government documents were ordered to reflect “biological sex” only. Project 2025’s extreme position equating “transgender ideology” with pornography wasn’t formally adopted, but Trump took steps to exclude trans Americans from public life.

These actions abandoned core tenets of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, redefining the Act’s protections through executive reinterpretation rather than legislative change.

Verdict: Systematic erasure of civil rights progress and institutional memory about racism. Legal challenges ongoing, but damage to diversity initiatives already extensive.

2026 Resolution: Distinguish between legitimate concerns about implementation and wholesale abandonment of diversity goals. Address affirmative action through courts and legislation, not executive fiat. Teach accurate history including difficult chapters. Recognize that civil rights protections serve national interests by ensuring equal opportunity.

The Constitutional Questions: Presidential Power and Institutional Limits

Beyond individual policy outcomes, Trump’s first year raised fundamental constitutional questions about the balance of power between branches. Unlike presidents who push executive power limits to solve specific problems, Trump pursued power accumulation across multiple domains simultaneously.

He routinely invoked emergency authorities for situations not traditionally considered emergencies, fired officials in ways that appeared to conflict with statutory limits, centralized control by challenging pockets of independence created by Congress, claimed authority to effectively nullify statutes (TikTok ban), delayed or refused to release some appropriated funds, sought to dismantle agencies without explicit congressional approval, and declared armed conflicts without congressional authorization.

The Supreme Court faces decisions that will either reimpose traditional limits or validate more expansive executive claims—setting precedents that will affect future presidents regardless of party affiliation. The Court has shown mixed responses: skepticism toward some claims (tariff emergency powers) while enabling others (broad immunity for official acts).

Congressional Republicans’ reluctance to defend institutional prerogatives against executive encroachment represents a concerning development that transcends partisan advantage. When the legislature declines to protect its constitutional authorities, the system of checks and balances weakens regardless of which party benefits temporarily.

The “unitary executive theory”—a constitutional interpretation holding that the president embodies all executive power—provided legal framework for actions that previous eras might have considered impeachable. Whether this theory represents legitimate constitutional interpretation or dangerous overreach will shape American governance for generations.

Resolution for 2026: Both parties should recognize that precedents empowering the executive branch will eventually be used by presidents of the opposing party. Restoring institutional checks serves everyone’s long-term interest.

Achievements Worth Acknowledging

Honest assessment requires recognizing genuine accomplishments alongside failures:

Border Security: Whatever one’s views on methods, driving illegal crossings to historically low levels represents achievement of a stated objective. The infrastructure created—tripled ICE budget, Laken Riley Act detention requirements—provides tools for future enforcement.

Gaza Ceasefire: Trump’s team played a significant role in achieving the October 2025 ceasefire that released hostages and reduced violence. While fragile and imperfect, this represented more progress than had been achieved in two years of war.

Economic Growth: Third-quarter GDP growth of 4.3% exceeded expectations, demonstrating economic resilience despite policy disruptions. Though benefits distributed unevenly, growth occurred.

Deregulation: The administration achieved significant regulatory rollback, fulfilling promises to reduce business compliance burdens. Whether this proves beneficial long-term remains debatable, but it fulfilled campaign commitments.

Judicial Appointments: Continuing his first-term pattern, Trump reshaped the federal judiciary with conservative jurists who will influence American law for decades.

Tax Policy: Extension of first-term tax cuts maintained campaign promises, though deficit impact and distributional questions persist.

These achievements deserve recognition even as we acknowledge their costs and question their sustainability. Policy assessment requires weighing both successes and failures honestly.

Legislative Record: Room for Improvement

Trump’s record-setting executive action coincided with limited legislative accomplishment. He signed only five bills into law, none representing major policy initiatives—among the weakest starts for a new president in over a century. The “One Big Beautiful Bill” tax package, while claimed as a signature achievement, represents the primary legislative output.

This legislative gap meant Trump governed almost entirely through executive action, making many changes vulnerable to future reversal or judicial invalidation. The lack of legislative foundation for consequential policies—immigration enforcement expansions, agency reorganizations, spending cuts—leaves them on uncertain legal ground.

2026 Resolution: Invest in legislative strategy. Durable change requires Congressional approval. Work with legislators to codify successful policies into law. Build bipartisan coalitions where possible. Accept that major reforms require compromise and negotiation, not just executive directive.

Personnel Challenges

October reporting documented 49 nominee withdrawals during 2025, including high-profile collapses over offensive communications. This personnel turbulence slowed implementation, complicated agency leadership, and highlighted that even Senate Republicans had limits to their support. The chaos contradicted claims of seamless administrative accomplishment.

2026 Resolution: Improve vetting and nomination processes. Recognize that personnel is policy—appointments matter. Work with Senate to confirm qualified nominees efficiently.

Economic Reality: The K-Shaped Recovery

The administration’s economic messaging collided with mathematical reality. Third-quarter GDP growth of 4.3% appeared strong, driven by consumer spending. But demographic analysis revealed a bifurcated economy where wealthy Americans maintained spending while lower-income households cut back.

Real disposable income remained flat as inflation eroded wages. Credit card balances rose $24 billion in Q3, suggesting spending was debt-financed. The manufacturing sector continued shedding jobs despite tariffs supposedly designed to revive it. Consumer confidence hit its lowest level since April’s tariff announcement.

Treasury Secretary Bessent predicted 3% annual GDP growth, but this optimism failed to address the fundamental affordability crisis. Inflation ranged between 2.8% and 3.1% all year—a modest improvement from pandemic peaks but well above the Federal Reserve’s 2% target and insufficient to restore purchasing power lost during 2021-2023.

2026 Resolution: Acknowledge and address the K-shaped recovery. Prosperity that flows only to the wealthy undermines social cohesion and political stability. Economic policy must serve working families, not just stockholders.

International Relations and Complicity Questions

Trump’s policies strained alliances and raised complicity questions. The United Kingdom stopped sharing drug-trafficking intelligence to avoid complicity in extrajudicial killings. European allies expressed horror at USAID elimination’s death toll. Canadian officials condemned Trump’s “economic betrayal” through tariffs.

Traditional allies faced a dilemma: continue cooperation and risk complicity in human rights violations, or distance themselves and weaken the alliance structure built over 75 years. Many chose distancing, accelerating American isolation.

2026 Resolution: Rebuild alliance relationships damaged by unilateral actions. Recognize that American power depends partly on allied cooperation. Consult before acting on matters affecting shared interests.

Project 2025: The Quiet Implementation

While Trump distanced himself from the Heritage Foundation’s Project 2025 during the campaign, his administration quietly implemented substantial portions. PBS News tracking found extensive alignment in:

  • LGBTQ+ policy: transgender service members denied care, identity documentation restricted, Title IX reinterpreted

  • Reproductive health: EMTALA guidance rescinded, Task Force on Reproductive Healthcare Access eliminated, Planned Parenthood Medicaid funding blocked

  • Education: Department functions redistributed, curriculum monitoring imposed, federal oversight weakened

  • Military: DEI programs eliminated, healthcare access reduced for women and LGBTQ+ service members

The systematic implementation of Project 2025 priorities—despite public disavowals—revealed the administration’s actual ideological agenda and suggested coordination with conservative movement infrastructure.

2026 Resolution: Honest governance requires acknowledging actual policy agenda. If implementing Project 2025 priorities, own it rather than deny it. Allow voters to judge based on actual policies, not rhetorical deflections.

The Human Cost: A Body Count Administration

More than any recent presidency, Trump’s 2025 can be measured in lives lost:

  • 500,000-1,000,000 deaths from USAID elimination

  • 105 extrajudicial killings in boat strikes

  • 406 Palestinians killed during “ceasefire”

  • Increased mortality from federal workforce cuts degrading disease control and disaster response

  • Projected 28,785 annual deaths if Medicaid cuts proceed

No modern peacetime administration has presided over this scale of preventable death. The casualties from USAID elimination alone exceed American combat deaths in Vietnam, Korea, Iraq, and Afghanistan combined. These weren’t lives lost pursuing national security objectives—they resulted from ideological preferences implemented without regard for humanitarian consequences.

2026 Resolution: Value human life above ideological purity. When policies produce mass casualties, acknowledge reality and reverse course. Some principles matter more than political consistency.

Durability and Reversibility

Which Trump changes prove permanent depends on three factors:

Legal Challenges: Courts have blocked some actions (Los Angeles troop deployment, improper appointment of prosecutors) while allowing others. The Supreme Court’s rulings on tariff authority, agency elimination, and executive power will determine whether Trump’s precedents constrain future presidents.

Legislative Codification: Without congressional action, most Trump changes remain vulnerable to reversal. The infrastructure for immigration enforcement may persist, but refugee policy, federal workforce size, and foreign aid can be restored by future administrations.

Institutional Damage: Some harm proves irreversible. Lives lost to USAID elimination cannot be recovered. Diplomatic relationships damaged by tariffs and extrajudicial killings will take decades to repair. Precedents normalizing domestic troop deployment and Justice Department weaponization will tempt future presidents regardless of party.

The most enduring Trump legacy may not be specific policies but the demonstration that constitutional limits can be exceeded without consequence if courts and Congress refuse to intervene. This lesson will outlast his presidency.

Conclusion: From Assessment to Action—America’s 2026 Resolutions

Trump promised radical change and delivered it—achieving some objectives while failing catastrophically on others. He drove border crossings to historic lows through means that may not survive judicial review. He slashed the federal workforce but left government unable to perform basic functions. He achieved diplomatic success in Gaza while conducting extrajudicial killings elsewhere.

The first-year assessment reveals genuine accomplishments alongside profound failures. More importantly, it identifies areas demanding immediate course correction regardless of partisan affiliation.

America’s Essential 2026 Resolutions:

  1. Restore Life-Saving Aid: The 500,000-1,000,000 deaths from USAID elimination represent a humanitarian catastrophe requiring immediate reversal. This transcends politics—children are dying from preventable causes.

  2. End Extrajudicial Killings: The boat strikes violate fundamental legal principles and serve no drug interdiction purpose. Return to lawful interdiction methods immediately.

  3. Rebuild Government Capacity: Strategic workforce reductions make sense; mass purges that cripple essential services do not. Restore capacity for disease control, disaster response, and basic governance.

  4. Restore Prosecutorial Independence: DOJ weaponization—whether real or perceived—damages institutions more than political opponents. Rebuild post-Watergate norms protecting prosecutorial independence.

  5. Replace Chaos with Strategy: Trade policy requires Congressional input and coherent strategy, not tariff whiplash. Economic policy needs focus on working families, not just wealthy Americans.

  6. Respect Constitutional Limits: Presidential power requires checks. Congress must defend institutional prerogatives. Courts must enforce constitutional boundaries. Norms matter because they protect democracy.

  7. Govern Through Legislation: Five bills passed represents historic failure. Durable change requires legislative action, not executive orders vulnerable to judicial invalidation or future reversal.

  8. Acknowledge Costs: Good governance requires honest accounting. When policies produce hundreds of thousands of deaths, acknowledge reality and adjust course—don’t double down on failure.

The Path Forward

This assessment isn’t partisan attack—it’s evidence-based analysis identifying what works and what doesn’t. Some Trump policies achieved stated goals; these successes should be acknowledged and built upon. Others failed catastrophically; these require immediate correction.

From a vantage point outside America’s partisan warfare—where constitutional principles matter more than tribal loyalty—the question becomes clear: it’s not whether Trump governs from the right or left, but whether governance follows constitutional limits, respects human life, operates through proper channels, and achieves stated objectives at reasonable cost.

America’s 2026 Practical Improvement Agenda:

  • Maintain immigration enforcement gains while restoring humanity

  • Right-size government strategically, not chaotically

  • Rebuild humanitarian capacity that saved 92 million lives over two decades

  • Develop coherent trade strategy replacing tariff chaos

  • Address affordability through policies helping working families

  • End legally indefensible military operations

  • Restore institutional norms protecting democracy

  • Govern through legislation achieving durable, constitutional change

The assessment also reveals systemic concerns transcending any single administration. Presidential power has expanded dangerously when courts and Congress refuse to enforce limits. Legislative gridlock drives excessive reliance on executive action. Partisan polarization prevents honest policy assessment.

These problems predate Trump and will outlast him. But 2025’s extreme examples—extrajudicial killings, agency elimination without Congressional approval, prosecutorial weaponization, unprecedented deaths from program cuts—demonstrate the urgency of restoration and reform.

Looking Forward: Questions That Demand Answers

Whether America recovers from 2025’s damage depends on choices ahead. Courts can reimpose constitutional limits or validate expansive executive claims. Congress can reassert institutional prerogatives or continue abdicating authority. Future presidents can restore norms or exploit precedents set.

The historical verdict will ultimately rest not just on Trump’s actions but on America’s response. Did we treat extraordinary as normal? Did we accept humanitarian catastrophe as acceptable collateral damage? Did we allow constitutional limits to erode without resistance?

As we enter 2026, these questions demand answers. Not through partisan lens, but through commitment to governance that respects law, values human life, operates through proper institutions, and serves the national interest rather than ideological preference or personal grievance.

Sometimes distance provides the clarity that proximity obscures. From outside America’s borders, the stakes are obvious: what happens to American democracy and constitutional governance matters far beyond American shores. The precedents set today will echo globally for decades.

The time for assessment has passed. The time for resolution and action has arrived. Will we take it?
