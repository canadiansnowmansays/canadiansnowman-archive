When the Tankers Don’t Turn Around

The Cuban Missile Crisis in reverse — and why no one in Washington seems to notice

March 20, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 20, 2026

Iconic TV broadcast still of JFK explaining the Cuban blockade, with the American public watching—mirroring the adult authority (or lack thereof) in both eras.

I was eleven years old in 1962. I remember the thirteen days. I remember the particular quality of the silence between news bulletins — the way it pressed down on you, even as a child, the sense that the world was genuinely fragile, that the adults in charge might not be able to hold it together. I remember Kennedy on television, controlled and grave, explaining that Soviet ships carrying nuclear missiles were headed toward Cuba and that the United States Navy would stop them.

I remember the moment the Soviet ships turned around.

That moment felt like breathing again. Even a pre-teen understood it. It felt like the adults had chosen, at the last possible moment, to let the species survive.

I am thinking about that moment today, because two Russian tankers are currently heading toward Cuba — and this time, no one looks like they’re turning around.

The blockade that dare not speak its name

Let’s be precise about what is actually happening, because the language being used in official Washington obscures more than it reveals.

The United States has imposed a naval blockade on Cuba. Not an embargo — a blockade. Since January 2026, no fuel has legally reached the island. Venezuela’s oil, which Cuba depended on, ended when the US conducted a military raid and captured Nicolás Maduro. Russia has pledged to fill the gap. Two tankers — the Sea Horse, carrying liquefied gas, and the sanctioned Anatoly Kolodkin, carrying 730,000 barrels of crude — are now at sea and headed toward Havana.

On Thursday, the US Treasury’s Office of Foreign Assets Control amended its general license for Russian oil shipments, explicitly naming Cuba as a prohibited destination. The message was unambiguous: those tankers are not to be received. Cuba, currently enduring its worst energy crisis since the collapse of the Soviet Union, with ten million people losing power in a single grid collapse on March 16th, is being told by Washington that help is not coming.

Donald Trump, for his part, said this week that he believes he will have the “honor” of taking Cuba. “Whether I free it, take it, I think I could do anything I want with it,” he told reporters. “They’re a very weakened nation right now.”

There is a word for the deliberate starvation of a civilian population to force regime change. International law has several of them. None of them are flattering.

What is different this time

In 1962, the Soviet Union was bringing nuclear missiles to Cuba — weapons capable of striking Washington, New York, Chicago. The existential military threat was real and documented. Kennedy’s blockade was a response to a genuine strategic provocation ninety miles from American soil.

In 2026, the United States is blocking oil tankers from reaching Cuba — not because Cuba poses a military threat, but because Washington has decided it wants a different government in Havana and has calculated that freezing a population in darkness is an acceptable method of achieving that goal.

The blockade is American. The ships being challenged are Russian. The island being strangled is not armed. The logic of 1962 has been turned inside out.

But here is what has not changed: the underlying escalatory mechanics are identical. In 1962, Soviet ships approached a US naval blockade line. The question was whether they would cross it. Everything that followed — the back-channel negotiations between Bobby Kennedy and Ambassador Dobrynin, the secret agreement to remove US Jupiter missiles from Turkey, the public pledge not to invade Cuba — was architecture built to give both sides a way to step back without humiliation.

That architecture existed because both Kennedy and Khrushchev understood that neither of them actually wanted a nuclear war, and that the system needed mechanisms to prevent one from happening by accident.

I am looking at the current situation and I cannot find that architecture.

The signal from Moscow that Washington may be ignoring

Two days ago, Nikolai Patrushev — Putin’s assistant and the head of Russia’s Maritime Collegium, one of the most senior security figures in Moscow — gave an interview to Kommersant that should have landed on the front page of every newspaper in the Western Hemisphere.

Modern Russian shadow fleet tanker at sea (representing the Sea Horse and Anatoly Kolodkin heading to Cuba) Aerial views of large oil tanker underway, evoking the vulnerable merchant vessels now challenging the blockade—potentially escorted.

“In the hunt for tankers, bulk carriers and container ships,” Patrushev said, “some countries simply played too much.” He announced that Russia would strengthen the protection of its merchant fleet, including naval escorts. Mobile firing groups are being considered. The possibility of placing special protective equipment on ships is being worked out.

This was not a hypothetical. Russia has already begun escorting shadow fleet tankers with naval vessels. In June 2025, a Russian missile corvette accompanied two sanctioned tankers through the English Channel. The precedent is established. The question now is whether Moscow will extend that escort to the Caribbean.

Think carefully about what that scenario looks like. A US Navy destroyer intercepts a Russian oil tanker in international waters in the Caribbean Sea. Alongside the tanker is a Russian corvette. The corvette does not yield. The destroyer does not yield. There is no hotline being used. There is no back-channel. There is no pre-negotiated framework for how this situation resolves without someone making a catastrophic decision.

In 1962, the Soviet ships turned around because Kennedy and Khrushchev had, through the most dangerous thirteen days in modern history, constructed just enough diplomatic scaffolding to make turning around possible. Khrushchev needed to be able to say he had secured guarantees. Kennedy needed to be able to say the missiles were gone. Both needed the other to survive the crisis politically.

What does Putin need right now to turn the tankers around? What is Trump offering him? What is the off-ramp?

As best I can determine: there is none.

The silence that should terrify us

What distinguished the Cuban Missile Crisis from a nuclear catastrophe was not American military superiority, though that existed. It was not Soviet weakness, though that was a factor. It was the fact that both sides had senior officials — not leaders, but officials — who were in direct, secret, continuous communication throughout the crisis. It was the existence of people on both sides who understood that their job was to prevent the logic of the confrontation from running to its conclusion.

Robert McNamara, reflecting on the crisis decades later, said that the lesson of those thirteen days was not that American resolve had prevailed. The lesson, he said, was that the world had survived through a combination of skill, judgment, and sheer luck — and that luck was not a strategy.

I am 74 years old. I was a child when I lived through the first Cuban crisis — old enough to be frightened, young enough to believe the adults would figure it out. I watched the world pull back from the edge.

Modern U.S. warship on patrol in regional waters, symbolizing the absent “architecture” for de-escalation and the risk of confrontation

I am watching it approach that edge again, and the things that saved us last time — the architecture, the back-channels, the mutual recognition that both sides needed to survive — are conspicuously absent. What I see instead is a US president who thinks he can take Cuba whenever he wants, a Russian president who has staked his credibility on protecting his allies, two tankers at sea, and a Treasury Department that has just told them they cannot dock.

The Soviet ships turned around in 1962 because someone on both sides was working frantically to give them a reason to.

Who is doing that work now?

I don’t know the answer. I’m not sure anyone in Washington does either. And that silence — that absence of visible, serious, sustained diplomacy — is the most frightening thing I have encountered since I was eleven years old watching Soviet ships approach a blockade line sixty-four years ago.

The tankers are still at sea.
