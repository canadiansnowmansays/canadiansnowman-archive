America’s Crisis of Political Violence

Part III: The Golf Course and the Ghost

May 1, 2026

Ryan Wesley Routh in the custody of the Martin County Sheriff's Office. (NBC South Florida)

Two months after Butler, it happened again. And almost nobody remembers.

On September 15, 2024, Ryan Wesley Routh, a 58-year-old from Hawaii by way of North Carolina, positioned himself in the shrubbery along the perimeter of Trump International Golf Club in West Palm Beach, Florida. He had a rifle. He had a scope. He had a go-pro camera, two backpacks, a loaded SKS-style weapon, and the kind of patience that suggests planning rather than impulse. He had been there, according to investigators, for approximately twelve hours.

Trump was playing golf. A Secret Service agent walking the course ahead of the president spotted the barrel of a rifle poking through the fence line. The agent fired. Routh fled. He was apprehended on the highway shortly afterward, driving a black Nissan.

No shots were fired at Trump. No one was injured. No blood. No iconic photograph. No fist pump.

And that, in the cruel calculus of American attention, was the problem.

Ryan Wesley Routh was, in almost every respect, the inverse of Thomas Matthew Crooks. Where Crooks was young, obscure, and motivationally opaque, Routh was middle-aged, voluble, and ideologically hyperactive. He had a long history of social media posts expressing intense hostility toward Trump. He had travelled to Ukraine in 2022, apparently attempting to recruit Afghan soldiers to fight against Russia — a quixotic and possibly delusional enterprise that attracted brief media attention at the time. He had written a self-published book. He had a criminal record in North Carolina.

He was, in short, exactly the kind of figure who in any other era would have dominated the national conversation for weeks. A man with an international backstory, a documented ideology, a loaded weapon, and twelve hours of patience in the bushes outside a golf course where the former president was playing the back nine.

Instead, he became a ghost.

The West Palm Beach attempt received coverage, certainly. It was a major story for roughly forty-eight hours. But it never achieved the gravitational pull of Butler, and the reasons are instructive.

First, there was no blood. American political narratives run on imagery, and the West Palm Beach incident produced no image remotely comparable to the Butler fist pump. A rifle barrel poking through shrubbery is not a photograph that sells T-shirts. A man apprehended on a highway is not a figure who inspires mythology.

Second, Trump was not injured. In the hierarchy of American political violence, a near-miss that produces no contact with the principal is categorically different from a wound. Butler had blood. West Palm Beach had a golf game interrupted. The emotional registers are not comparable, even though the intent — to kill the president — was identical.

Ryan Wesley Routh, seen here in a screengrab from a rally held in central Kyiv in April of 2022, was identified as the suspect in what federal officials say was an apparent assassination attempt against former President Donald Trump in Florida. AFPTV/AFP via Getty Images

Third, and most importantly, the narrative was complicated. Routh was not a figure who fit neatly into the mythology that had been constructed around Butler. He was older. He was ideological. He had a history that invited uncomfortable questions about the radicalizing effects of political rhetoric on unstable individuals. If Butler could be absorbed into a story about Trump’s providential survival, West Palm Beach pointed toward something messier — a country producing aspiring assassins at a rate that suggested systemic dysfunction rather than individual aberration.

And systemic dysfunction, in American political discourse, is nobody’s preferred story. The preferred story is always about the individual — the lone wolf, the bad actor, the isolated case. The preferred story preserves the fiction that the system itself is sound and that violence is an anomaly rather than a feature.

Routh was convicted of attempted assassination and sentenced to life in prison. That sentence was handed down with remarkably little fanfare. By the time the trial concluded, the country was deep into the 2025 political cycle, and the West Palm Beach attempt had been filed in the same mental category as the kind of thing that happens now — unfortunate, alarming in the moment, ultimately forgettable.

But consider what forgetting it requires. It requires accepting that a man with a rifle and a twelve-hour stakeout plan was positioned within firing range of a former president at a golf course in Florida, and that this is simply the sort of thing that occurs in the normal course of American life. It requires accepting that the security perimeter of a venue regularly used by a former president and active candidate was permeable enough that an armed man could establish a concealed position and wait half a day. It requires accepting that the only thing separating this incident from another Butler — or worse — was the alertness of a single Secret Service agent who spotted a rifle barrel through a fence.

That is not security. That is luck. And luck is not a system.

The contrast between Butler and West Palm Beach reveals something important about how America processes political violence, and it is the through-line that connects both events to the Washington Hilton.

Violence that produces a usable image gets absorbed into mythology. Violence that doesn’t gets forgotten. The criterion is not severity. It is not proximity to death. It is not even the identity of the target. The criterion is narrative utility. Can this event be converted into a story that serves someone’s interests? If yes, it endures. If no, it evaporates.

Butler produced the most politically useful photograph in a generation. It endured. West Palm Beach produced a man in a Nissan on Interstate 95. It evaporated.

And the Washington Hilton? The Washington Hilton produced a president in a tuxedo, pitching a ballroom from behind the White House podium while a Secret Service agent was being treated for a bullet impact to his vest. The narrative utility was instantaneous: courage, defiance, the need for better infrastructure, the comparison to Lincoln. Within ninety minutes of the shooting, the event had already been fully processed and repurposed.

Cole Tomas Allen from Torrance, California is the man who has been arrested and accused of shooting a guard while charging a security checkpoint at the White House correspondents dinner. Allen has been charged with firearms and assault charges.

The gunman — Cole Tomas Allen, 31, a Caltech graduate and indie game developer from Torrance, California — will likely follow the Routh trajectory. His name will circulate for a few days. His social media will be excavated. His neighbours will express shock. And then, unless his motive proves to be something genuinely novel, he will recede into the same grey archive where Routh already resides: the file marked attempted political violence, non-fatal, no iconic image, narratively inconvenient.

Here, then, is the pattern as it has established itself across three incidents in twenty-two months:

A gunman approaches the president. The security apparatus responds — sometimes brilliantly, sometimes after catastrophic failures, always after the fact. The president survives. The event is immediately converted into political capital by whatever means the specific circumstances allow. The structural conditions that produced the gunman — the guns, the rhetoric, the radicalization pipelines, the accessibility of targets, the culture of grievance — are acknowledged in the abstract and ignored in the particular. And the country moves on, its baseline tolerance for political violence ratcheted one notch higher.

Butler. West Palm Beach. The Washington Hilton.

Three incidents. One pattern. Zero structural change.

From Canada, where a prime minister was last assassinated never — where the very concept of a prime ministerial assassination attempt is so foreign to the national experience that it reads as fiction — the pattern is not just alarming. It is incomprehensible. Not because Canadians are morally superior. We are not. We have our own violence, our own failures, our own capacity for cruelty. But we have not yet built a political culture in which shooting at the head of state has become a recurring event that the country processes in forty-eight hours and files under normal.

That distinction — the distinction between a country where political violence is an aberration and a country where it has become a rhythm — is what this series is about.
