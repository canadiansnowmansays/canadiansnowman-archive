Now This is (Not) Fake News

Part 8: The Pattern Complete — How Press Restrictions Enable War Crimes

December 5, 2025

A Canadian’s Analysis of Accountability’s Death

By CanadianSnowmanSays December 5, 2025

In Parts 6 and 7, we documented the “kill everybody” order, the legal consensus calling it a war crime, and the administration’s contradictory defenses. Now we examine how the Justice Department provided pre-emptive legal cover, why whistleblowers had no choice but to leak to banned journalists, and how this revelation validates everything I’ve been documenting about the press purge.

This is where all seven previous parts of this series come together.

The Justice Department’s Pre-Emptive Protection

We now know why the administration felt emboldened to proceed with these strikes: they had legal cover, or at least thought they did.

In late November, The Washington Post revealed the existence of a classified Justice Department memo from the Office of Legal Counsel. The memo, penned in the summer of 2025 before the strikes began, indicated that U.S. personnel involved in the strikes would not be prosecuted down the line.

According to people who have read the memo, it accepts Trump’s “determination” that the United States is in a formal armed conflict with drug cartels and that boat crews are “combatants.” It concludes that suspected drug shipments are lawful military targets because they finance cartel “war efforts.”

A broad range of legal experts reject this analysis entirely. But even accepting it for the sake of argument, CNN legal analyst Elie Honig explained why it doesn’t provide legal cover for the September 2 strike:

“First of all, while that memo relates to the overall effort to target these Venezuelan drug boats, it apparently does not relate to this double tap, to this effort to go back and attack people who had been rendered helpless in the water. The second thing is that Office of Legal Counsel memo actually was issued on September 5th, which was three days after this attack. And so you can’t provide retroactive cover for something that happened three days ago.”

The memo’s existence raises a disturbing question: Did the Office of Legal Counsel know about the “kill everybody” order? Did they know second strikes on survivors were planned? Or did they provide broad legal cover assuming the operations would be conducted within the bounds of law, only to have Hegseth exceed even that generous interpretation?

The Washington Post also reported that the Trump administration “sidestepped skeptical lawyers across national security agencies as it pursued a military campaign against alleged narcotraffickers.” This wasn’t a case of getting legal advice and following it. This was a case of shopping for legal opinions until they found lawyers willing to bless the operation, then ignoring the lawyers who raised concerns.

The Whistleblowers Who Had No Choice

The Washington Post’s reporting came from “two people with direct knowledge of the operation” who provided the “kill everybody” quote. These are insiders—people who were there, who watched the operation unfold, who heard the order.

Their decision to speak to banned journalists wasn’t taken lightly. As Lawrence O’Donnell noted on MSNBC, these sources are like “the helicopter pilot flying over the My Lai massacre and knowing that he had to do something, knowing that what he saw happening on the ground was a crime.”

O’Donnell elaborated: “The people who are witnessing the order to kill everybody see only one way for them to try to stop such orders. And so they spoke to the kinds of reporters who have been banned from the Defense Department under Donald Trump. Reporters who are not allowed into the Defense Department press briefing room by Pete Hegseth.”

He continued: “The Washington Post reporting on this story proves once again that access to the press briefing room is access to the least useful reporting that can be done in Washington in the Defense Department or in the White House or anywhere else that has a press briefing room. Alex Horton and Ellen Nakashima of the Washington Post got this story without setting foot inside the Pentagon because they are banned from setting foot inside the Pentagon.”

The whistleblowers had no other option. With no adversarial press corps inside the Pentagon, with no daily briefings where aggressive questioning could surface concerns, the only accountability mechanism left was whistleblowing to reporters who have been barred from the building.

This is the perverse effect of the press purge: it doesn’t stop information from getting out—it just ensures that the only information that emerges comes from people brave enough to risk their careers by leaking to banned journalists.

How This Validates Everything From Parts 4 and 5

Let me walk you through how the September 2 revelation confirms the entire thesis of this series.

September 2: The First Strike

When the September 2 strike occurred, there was no Pentagon press corps to demand immediate answers. Hegseth posted a video on X with dramatic music. That was it. No press conference. No questions answered.

If there had been a functioning press briefing system:

  • Reporters would have asked: How many people were on the boat?

  • Follow-up: Were there survivors after the first strike?

  • Follow-up: Were there multiple strikes?

  • Follow-up: What intelligence justified the strike?

  • Follow-up: Was any contraband recovered?

Instead: Silence. Social media announcements. No questions.

September-October: The Escalation

As strikes accelerated—from one every few weeks to three in a single day on October 27—there was no daily briefing where reporters could track the pattern. No one inside the building to notice the escalation. No one to ask why the pace was increasing. No one to demand evidence that any of these boats actually carried drugs.

Late October: My Coverage

On October 29 and 30, I published Parts 4 and 5 of this series. Part 4 documented 57 dead at that point, the lack of evidence, the international condemnation, and the complete absence of Pentagon briefings. Part 5 showed how major policy announcements—the 23,500-person Quick Reaction Force and the return to nuclear testing—were made via leak and Truth Social, with no press conferences and no accountability.

I wrote then: “This is what the press purge enables: military operations with body counts but no accountability, claims without evidence, and a death toll that keeps rising while the ‘next generation’ press corps stays silent.”

I didn’t know then about the “kill everybody” order. I didn’t know about the second strike on survivors clinging to wreckage. I didn’t know the specific details because no one with Pentagon press access knew—or if they knew, they weren’t asking.

November 28: The Washington Post Breaks Through

It took investigative journalism from reporters banned from the Pentagon to expose the truth. Alex Horton and Ellen Nakashima couldn’t attend Pentagon briefings because there aren’t any meaningful briefings, and they’re not on the approved list anyway.

They found sources. They documented the order. They reported the truth.

And in doing so, they proved the central argument of this entire series: The press purge didn’t eliminate information—it eliminated the mechanism to challenge information in real time.

The Pattern Complete: How Accountability Dies

The September 2 strike wasn’t an aberration. It was the establishment of a pattern that has now killed 83 people across 21 strikes.

Here’s how the system works:

1. Press Restrictions: Purge adversarial journalists. Replace them with propagandists who won’t ask hard questions. The “next generation” press corps Hegseth hand-picked asks nothing, challenges nothing, demands nothing.

2. Information Control: Announce operations via social media with dramatic music. Refuse briefings. Ignore requests for evidence. Post videos declaring victory. Move on.

3. Legal Pre-Emption: Get Office of Legal Counsel memos blessing operations. Shop around until you find lawyers willing to provide cover. Sidestep the skeptical lawyers. Proceed with the knowledge that even if you exceed the memo’s scope, you’ve got something to point to.

4. Evidence Destruction: Conduct second strikes that eliminate all physical evidence. Can’t prove there were drugs? Can’t prove there weren’t drugs either. Every boat destroyed, every survivor killed, every piece of evidence obliterated.

5. Retaliation: When senators question legality, threaten court-martial. When sources leak, investigate them. When press reports the truth, call it “fake news” and post cartoon turtle memes.

Each piece enables the others. The press purge enables operations without scrutiny. Operations without scrutiny enable evidence destruction. Evidence destruction enables deniability. Deniability enables escalation. Escalation enables the next atrocity.

This is how accountability dies. Not dramatically, but methodically. One restriction at a time. One purge at a time. One body at a time.

The Ministry of Truth in Practice: Not Controlling Information, Eliminating Challenge

This is what I’ve been documenting across eight parts of this series. The elimination of the Pentagon press corps isn’t about controlling information—it’s about eliminating the mechanism by which information gets challenged.

In George Orwell’s 1984, the Ministry of Truth rewrites history, burns books, and controls all information. But that’s not how modern information control works. You don’t need to rewrite history when you can prevent questions from being asked in the first place.

You don’t need to burn books when you can ensure no one with credentials will write the damning chapter.

You don’t need to control all information when you can control access to the people who make the decisions.

The Trump administration’s approach is more sophisticated than Orwell imagined. They don’t ban all media—they just ban the adversarial ones. They don’t prohibit all questions—they just eliminate the forums where hard questions get asked. They don’t censor all reporting—they just ensure the reporters with daily access won’t report anything uncomfortable.

The “next generation” press corps Hegseth invited in asked zero questions about the September 2 strike. Zero questions about the 83 dead. Zero questions about the lack of evidence. Zero questions about the “kill everybody” order.

They’re not journalists; they’re stenographers. And that’s exactly what the administration wants.

Meanwhile, the real journalists—the ones who asked hard questions, who demanded evidence, who wouldn’t accept social media announcements as adequate—are banned from the building. So when they finally break the “kill everybody” story, the administration can claim it’s “fake news” from biased outlets.

The Ministry of Truth doesn’t need to control information. It just needs to discredit the people brave enough to report it.

The Broader Implications: A Democracy Without Watchers

In Part 1, I documented the Pentagon press purge. In Part 2, I examined the constitutional violations. In Part 3, I tracked the first month without oversight. In Part 4, I revealed the mounting death toll—57 dead with no evidence and no briefings. In Part 5, I showed how major policy announcements happened via leak and Truth Social.

Parts 6, 7, and 8 document the inevitable result: an explicit order to “kill everybody,” a second strike on survivors clinging to wreckage, legal consensus that it’s a war crime, an administration responding with contradictory lies and cartoon turtle memes, and a refusal to release any evidence.

This is the logical endpoint of eliminating adversarial press coverage. Not just propaganda replacing journalism, but war crimes proceeding unchecked because there’s no one left in the building to ask hard questions.

The Trump administration has built a comprehensive system to avoid accountability:

  • No Pentagon press corps to demand immediate answers

  • Military operations announced via social media

  • Evidence systematically destroyed through second strikes

  • Intelligence never verified because no one can ask follow-up questions

  • Body counts rise while propagandists with credentials stay silent

  • Whistleblowers forced to leak to banned journalists

  • Legal memos provide pre-emptive cover

  • Retaliation against senators who question legality

Every piece of this system serves one purpose: ensuring operations can continue without oversight, without challenge, without consequences.

International Accountability

Colombian and Venezuelan governments have formally accused the United States of extrajudicial murder. The International Criminal Court could theoretically pursue investigations, though U.S. non-cooperation would make this challenging.

The United Kingdom has reportedly stopped sharing intelligence on suspected drug vessels with the U.S. because it believes the attacks are illegal and does not want to be complicit.

International isolation over these strikes could have broader diplomatic consequences, particularly in Latin America where the U.S. already faces skepticism over its interventionist history.

The Questions That Should Haunt Us

I’ll end where I began in October, with questions that remain unanswered:

Were the boats actually carrying drugs, or were they fishing vessels on established routes?

Who were the 83 dead people? What were their names, ages, nationalities?

How old was the youngest person killed? Was there a child on any of these boats?

What intelligence justified each strike?

Why was bombing chosen over boarding when the Coast Guard successfully interdicts drug vessels regularly through arrest and prosecution?

What happens to intelligence value when everyone dies instead of being interrogated?

Has killing suspects without trial become official U.S. military policy?

If Hegseth ordered “kill everybody,” who else heard that order? Who else is complicit?

Will anyone face accountability, or will this be another atrocity that gets memory-holed?

And most importantly: How many more will die before someone with power says “enough”?

The Pattern Revealed, The System Exposed

Fifty-seven dead became 83 dead. Eighty-three dead will become more unless something changes. Unless the press restrictions are challenged in court. Unless Congress acts with real subpoena power after 2026. Unless the American people demand accountability.

But that requires people to pay attention. It requires people to care that their military is killing people based on unverified intelligence, destroying all evidence, refusing to answer questions, and responding to war crimes allegations with cartoon turtle memes.

It requires people to understand that this isn’t just about 83 dead people in the Caribbean. It’s about what kind of country America is becoming when its military can operate without oversight, without accountability, without even having to pretend to answer questions.

The press purge created the conditions. The “kill everybody” order was the result. And unless something fundamental changes, this is just the beginning.
