The $37 Trillion Question

Episode 6: Tariffs Won't Fix This - Why Trump's Trade War Makes Everything Worse

July 1, 2025

A weekly series examining how America broke its postwar promise of fiscal responsibility. Each week, we trace the bipartisan policy choices that created today's debt crisis and explore evidence-based solutions that could restore fiscal sanity. This analysis is updated regularly with the latest economic and political developments.

Donald Trump's 2025 tariff experiment—initially pitched as a cure for America's $37 trillion national debt—has become a masterclass in economic chaos rather than fiscal salvation. What began in April with sweeping proclamations about fixing trade imbalances through protective barriers quickly devolved into market panic, diplomatic retaliation, and consumer price spikes that left ordinary Americans bearing the cost of yet another misguided policy experiment.

The numbers tell the story starkly: Trump's tariffs, which escalated to 145% on Chinese goods before settling at current 30% rates after May's trade deal, generated roughly $50 billion in revenue while imposing an estimated $2,237 annual cost on American families according to Yale's Budget Lab. Against a $1.7 trillion deficit, this represents fiscal homeopathy—diluting the medicine until it becomes worthless while the patient continues deteriorating.

As a Canadian observing from across our shared border, I've watched this trade war unfold with growing alarm. Our $400 billion annual trade relationship with America—representing millions of jobs across both countries—faced unprecedented uncertainty as policy veered wildly between extremes. More troubling, Trump's approach revealed a fundamental misunderstanding of both trade economics and fiscal policy that recalls the worst mistakes of the 1930s.

The tragedy is that tariffs can't address America's underlying fiscal crisis because they target the wrong problem entirely. The $37 trillion debt didn't accumulate through trade deficits—it grew through systematic revenue reductions (Republican tax cuts) combined with increased spending obligations (both parties' contributions). Tariffs might redistribute trade flows, but they cannot restore the fiscal balance that requires progressive taxation and disciplined spending.

Historical Warnings: From Smoot-Hawley to 2018

History provides clear warnings about tariff-based economic policy. The most infamous example remains the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930, which raised average tariff rates to 19% and helped transform a stock market correction into the Great Depression.

The 1930s Disaster

Smoot-Hawley's advocates promised the same benefits Trump touted in 2025: protecting American jobs, raising government revenue, and forcing trading partners to negotiate better deals. Instead, global trade collapsed by 67% according to Bureau of Economic Analysis data, unemployment soared to 25%, and the world economy fell into its deepest recession in modern history.

The policy failed because it ignored basic economic principles that remain valid today. Tariffs function as sales taxes paid by domestic consumers, not foreign governments. When America raises import duties, American families and businesses pay higher prices while foreign producers often find alternative markets for their goods.

More fundamentally, trade wars create retaliatory spirals where each side imposes escalating barriers that eventually destroy the commercial relationships that benefit all participants. Nations that attempt unilateral trade restrictions typically find themselves isolated economically and weakened internationally.

The 2018 Preview

Trump's first-term tariff experiment provided a more recent warning. His 2018 trade war generated $41 billion in revenue while imposing $50 billion in costs on American consumers according to Congressional Budget Office analysis—a net economic loss that foreshadowed 2025's larger-scale failure.

Steel and aluminum tariffs, imposed to protect domestic producers, instead raised construction costs across the economy while triggering retaliation that harmed agricultural exports. Farmers—supposedly protected by trade barriers—required $28 billion in government subsidies to offset export losses caused by foreign retaliation.

The lesson was clear: tariffs redistribute costs and benefits rather than creating net economic gains. Protected industries may benefit temporarily, but these gains come at the expense of consumers and export-dependent sectors that face higher costs and foreign retaliation.

Trump's 2025 Trade War: Escalation and Chaos

Trump's return to tariff policy in 2025 demonstrated how lessons from both distant and recent history had been completely ignored. The policy trajectory revealed either stunning economic illiteracy or deliberate indifference to public welfare in pursuit of political theater.

The April Escalation

On April 9, 2025, Trump announced a "90-day tariff pause" that would implement 10% duties on most imports while exempting China from previous arrangements. Hours later, the White House confirmed that tariffs on Chinese goods would actually hit 145%—combining a 125% general tariff with a 20% fentanyl-related surcharge.

This policy whiplash—promising restraint while delivering unprecedented escalation—sent markets into free fall. The S&P 500 dropped from $545.90 on April 9 to $522.10 on April 10, representing a 4.3% decline that wiped out $6.6 trillion in market value according to Tax Foundation analysis. The Dow Jones fell 1,640 points in two days, while Treasury yields surged to 4.45% as investors fled risky assets.

Market Panic and Policy Confusion

The market reaction reflected rational assessment of policy irrationality. Lawrence Summers, former Treasury Secretary, described the situation as a "serious financial crisis wholly induced by US government tariff policy." Delta Air Lines CEO Ed Bastian warned of recession risks as transportation costs spiked, while the company's stock fell roughly 40% year-to-date.

More troubling, speculation emerged that the tariff threats followed by partial reversals represented deliberate market manipulation. Senators Adam Schiff and Elizabeth Warren called for investigations into potential White House profiteering, noting the suspicious timing of policy announcements relative to market movements.

Global Retaliation

International response was swift and severe. China imposed 125% counter-tariffs on all U.S. goods by April 11, affecting roughly $730 billion in bilateral trade. The European Union implemented €20 billion ($22 billion) in retaliatory measures targeting American agricultural exports. Canada responded with $60 billion in counter-tariffs, despite our historically close trade relationship.

These retaliations revealed tariffs' fundamental flaw: they invite escalating responses that ultimately harm all participants. By May 2025, global trade flows faced the highest barrier levels since the 1930s, with predictable consequences for economic growth and international cooperation.

The May Retreat

Facing economic chaos and political pressure, Trump negotiated a partial retreat in May 2025. Chinese tariffs were reduced to 30% while other countries faced 10% rates—still historically high but below the peak 145% levels that had triggered market panic.

However, even these reduced rates impose substantial costs. The 30% China tariff alone costs American families an estimated $2,237 annually according to Yale Budget Lab calculations, while the average effective U.S. tariff rate stands at 17.8%—the highest since 1934.

Legal Challenges and Uncertainty

Federal courts ruled Trump's IEEPA tariffs illegal in May 2025, finding that the president exceeded his authority in imposing them. However, the Court of Appeals granted a temporary stay, allowing the tariffs to remain in effect pending oral arguments scheduled for July 31, 2025. This legal uncertainty adds another layer of chaos to an already unstable policy framework.

Domestic Economic Damage

Trump's tariff experiment imposed immediate and measurable costs on American families and businesses while failing to address underlying fiscal problems:

Consumer Price Increases

Tariffs function as regressive sales taxes that disproportionately burden lower-income families. Essential goods—food, clothing, electronics, automobiles—all faced price increases as import costs rose. Car prices increased $3,000-$6,000 according to Kiplinger analysis, while everyday consumer goods climbed across categories.

By June 2025, Trump expanded the 50% steel tariffs to household appliances including refrigerators, dishwashers, washing machines, dryers, stoves, and garbage disposals, directly hitting consumers with higher costs on essential goods. The tariffs apply to the steel and aluminum content within these products, adding significant costs to items families depend on daily.

The $2,237 annual cost calculated by Yale economists represents roughly 3.4% of median household income—a significant burden for families already struggling with inflation and stagnant wages. This cost persists regardless of whether tariffs generate government revenue, making them among the most inefficient forms of taxation.

Business Investment Uncertainty

Perhaps more damaging than immediate price increases, tariffs created investment uncertainty that reduced business confidence and economic planning. Companies couldn't predict future input costs or market access, leading to delayed expansion plans and reduced hiring.

The VIX fear index—measuring market volatility—hit 35 during April's tariff chaos, triple its normal range. This uncertainty translated into reduced business investment, slower job creation, and weaker economic growth that persisted even after tariff rates were partially reduced.

As the July 8-9 deadlines approached for renewed tariff escalation, Trump signaled potential flexibility, telling reporters "we can do whatever we want" about extending tariff pauses—demonstrating the policy uncertainty that continues plaguing businesses and markets.

Export Industry Devastation

American exporters faced devastating retaliation that offset any benefits to import-competing industries. Agricultural exports—soybeans, pork, wheat—encountered 125% Chinese tariffs that effectively eliminated market access. Manufacturing exports faced similar barriers from European and Canadian retaliation.

The pattern repeated 2018's experience where protected industries gained modestly while export sectors suffered massively. Since export industries typically employ higher-skilled workers and pay better wages, this redistribution harmed economic development and income growth.

Fiscal Ineffectiveness

Most critically for addressing America's debt crisis, tariffs proved utterly inadequate as revenue sources:

Minimal Revenue Generation

Even at peak rates of 145% on Chinese goods, tariff revenue reached only $50 billion according to preliminary Congressional Budget Office estimates. Against a $1.7 trillion deficit, this represents roughly 3% of the financing gap—essentially meaningless for fiscal policy purposes.

After May's rate reductions to 30% on China and 10% elsewhere, revenue fell further while economic costs persisted. Current tariff levels generate perhaps $30-40 billion annually—less than 2% of federal revenue and insufficient to fund even modest government programs.

Economic Drag Reduces Other Revenue

More problematically, tariffs' economic drag reduced other tax collections. Lower business investment meant reduced corporate income taxes. Decreased consumer spending due to higher prices meant lower sales tax revenue for states. Economic uncertainty reduced capital gains realizations, cutting individual income tax collections.

The net fiscal impact of tariffs often proves negative when these secondary effects are considered. Revenue from import duties is offset by reduced collections from other sources, making tariffs counterproductive even on narrow fiscal grounds.

Compound Costs Through Retaliation

Export losses from foreign retaliation imposed additional fiscal costs through increased government spending. Agricultural subsidies—required to offset export losses—reached $28 billion during Trump's first-term trade war. Similar programs would be necessary to address 2025's larger-scale retaliation.

These offsetting expenditures further reduce tariffs' net fiscal contribution, potentially making them fiscally negative when all costs are considered.

The Navarro Influence: Ideology Over Evidence

Trump's tariff obsession owes much to Peter Navarro's ideological influence rather than evidence-based economic analysis. Navarro, who rejoined the administration as Senior Counsellor for Trade and Manufacturing, has promoted protectionist policies regardless of their economic consequences.

Academic Isolation

Navarro's economic views place him outside mainstream academic consensus. His claims about trade deficits and tariff benefits contradict decades of empirical research and theoretical understanding. Most tellingly, Navarro created a fictional economist—"Ron Vara," an anagram of his own name—to support his arguments when real economists disagreed.

This academic isolation should have disqualified Navarro from policy influence, but Trump valued loyalty and ideological agreement over expertise. The result: policies based on discredited theories rather than evidence-based analysis.

China Obsession

Navarro's particular focus on China—evident in books like "Death by China" and "The Coming China Wars"—drove much of 2025's trade war escalation. His warnings about Chinese "predatory trade practices" and "mercantilist juggernaut" provided intellectual cover for policies that harmed American interests while failing to address legitimate concerns about Chinese behavior.

Even Elon Musk—hardly a China apologist—described Navarro as "dumber than a sack of bricks," reflecting business community frustration with ideologically-driven policies that ignored economic realities.

International Consequences: Damaging Alliances

Trump's tariff policies damaged America's international relationships while weakening collective responses to genuine global challenges:

Allied Frustration

Canada, America's closest ally and largest trading partner, faced $60 billion in retaliatory measures despite our historically cooperative relationship. Our response reflected genuine frustration with American policy unpredictability that threatened established economic partnerships.

European allies expressed similar concerns through their €20 billion retaliation package. These weren't hostile actions by adversaries but defensive responses by friends forced to protect their own economic interests from American policy chaos.

Chinese Strategic Benefits

Paradoxically, Trump's trade war may have strengthened China's international position by demonstrating American policy unreliability. While Chinese exports to America declined, Beijing successfully diversified toward other markets and positioned itself as a more stable trading partner than the United States.

China's 125% retaliation—affecting $730 billion in bilateral trade—demonstrated their capacity to inflict reciprocal damage while maintaining domestic support for government policies. Meanwhile, American allies questioned whether they could rely on stable economic relationships with the United States.

Global Trade System Fragmentation

Most dangerously, Trump's approach contributed to global trade system fragmentation that reduces economic efficiency and increases geopolitical tensions. When major powers abandon rules-based trade in favor of bilateral power politics, smaller nations must choose sides rather than benefiting from open markets.

This fragmentation particularly hurts middle powers like Canada, which depend on stable trade rules to compete effectively with larger economies. Regional trading blocs and bilateral arrangements cannot replicate the efficiency gains from multilateral free trade systems.

The Tariff-for-Income-Tax Fantasy

Perhaps most revealingly, Trump proposed in April 2025 to replace federal income taxes entirely with tariff revenue—demonstrating fundamental misunderstanding of both trade economics and fiscal arithmetic.

Mathematical Impossibility

The federal government collected $2.2 trillion in individual income taxes during fiscal 2024, while total imports were $3.8 trillion. To replace income tax revenue entirely, tariffs would need to average roughly 58%—a level that would collapse trade volumes and eliminate the tax base.

Historical experience confirms this impossibility. When tariffs funded 95% of federal revenue from 1870-1913, government spending represented only 2-3% of GDP compared to today's 20-25%. Modern government operations require revenue sources that tariffs cannot provide without destroying the economic activity they tax.

Regressive Impact

Even if mathematically feasible, replacing progressive income taxes with tariffs would represent the largest regressive tax shift in American history. Wealthy Americans—who pay substantial income taxes—would benefit enormously while poor and middle-class families—who spend larger portions of income on imported goods—would face crushing tax burdens.

This would accelerate inequality trends that already threaten social stability while reducing government's capacity to address economic disparities through progressive taxation.

Economic Collapse Risk

Tariff rates sufficient to replace income tax revenue would trigger economic collapse through multiple channels. Import volumes would plummet, eliminating the tax base. Export industries would face devastating retaliation. Consumer spending would decline sharply as import prices soared.

The result would resemble the 1930s collapse rather than sustainable fiscal policy, demonstrating how ideological extremism can lead to proposals that threaten basic economic functioning.

Why Tariffs Can't Address Fiscal Crisis

Fundamentally, tariffs cannot address America's fiscal crisis because they target symptoms rather than causes:

Wrong Problem Diagnosis

America's $37 trillion debt didn't accumulate through trade deficits—it grew through systematic revenue reductions (primarily Republican tax cuts) combined with spending increases (both parties' contributions). Trade policy cannot address problems created by domestic fiscal policy choices.

Even eliminating all trade deficits wouldn't significantly reduce government borrowing needs. The fiscal deficit and trade deficit represent different phenomena with different causes requiring different solutions.

Revenue Inadequacy

Tariffs cannot generate revenue sufficient to address fiscal imbalances without destroying the economic activity they tax. Import volumes are simply too small relative to government revenue needs, while tariff rates sufficient to generate substantial revenue would eliminate imports entirely.

This represents a fundamental constraint that no amount of policy creativity can overcome. Modern government operations require broad-based taxation of domestic economic activity rather than narrow taxation of international trade.

Economic Inefficiency

Even if tariffs could generate adequate revenue, they represent among the most economically inefficient forms of taxation. Protective tariffs distort production decisions, reduce competitive pressures, and create deadweight losses that reduce overall economic welfare.

Progressive income taxation—the postwar American approach—generates revenue while maintaining economic efficiency and addressing inequality. Tariffs achieve none of these objectives while imposing substantial economic costs.

The Pattern of Policy Failure

Trump's tariff experiment fits a broader pattern of American policy failure over recent decades:

Ideological Override of Evidence

Like Reagan's tax cuts and Bush's deregulation, Trump's tariffs prioritized ideological commitments over empirical evidence. Clear historical and economic analysis predicted their failure, but political considerations overwhelmed technical expertise.

This pattern—ideology trumping evidence—explains much of America's fiscal crisis. Policies are chosen for political benefits rather than economic effectiveness, creating compound problems that worsen over time.

Short-term Politics Over Long-term Consequences

Tariffs provided immediate political benefits—satisfying protectionist sentiments and appearing to address trade concerns—while imposing costs that emerged gradually and fell on dispersed constituencies. This temporal mismatch enables irresponsible policies that survive initial implementation.

Similar dynamics drove tax cuts that provided immediate benefits while creating long-term fiscal problems and spending increases that delivered visible benefits while imposing hidden costs through debt accumulation.

Elite Capture of Policy Process

Trump's tariff policies, like previous policy failures, reflected elite capture rather than broad public interest. Navarro's ideological influence and business interests that benefit from protection shaped policy regardless of broader economic consequences.

This capture explains why policies persist despite obvious failure—beneficiaries have concentrated interests in maintaining them while costs are dispersed across the broader population.

International Lessons: The Canadian Alternative

Canada's approach to trade and fiscal policy demonstrates superior alternatives to American policy choices:

Multilateral Trade Engagement

Canada maintains extensive trade relationships while avoiding zero-sum confrontations that damage all participants. Our approach—working within established rules while addressing specific concerns through negotiation—preserves beneficial relationships while addressing legitimate problems.

This multilateral engagement has enabled Canada to maintain trade relationships with all major economies while avoiding the costly retaliation that American unilateralism has triggered.

Fiscal Responsibility Through Progressive Taxation

Canada funds government operations through progressive taxation rather than attempting shortcuts like tariff revenue. Our 53% combined top tax rate generates sustainable revenue while maintaining economic competitiveness and social cohesion.

This approach proves that responsible fiscal policy can coexist with international economic integration and domestic prosperity—exactly the combination that American policy has failed to achieve.

Looking Ahead: The Tariff Trap

Trump's tariff experiment reveals how policy failures compound over time. Initial mistakes create political constituencies that benefit from continuation, making correction increasingly difficult even as evidence of failure accumulates.

Tariff-protected industries will lobby for continuation regardless of broader economic costs. Workers in protected sectors will support politicians who maintain barriers even if overall employment suffers. This creates political lock-in that makes bad policies self-perpetuating.

Meanwhile, retaliation becomes institutionalized as foreign governments implement counter-measures that develop their own political constituencies. Trade wars are easier to start than stop because both sides develop interests in maintaining barriers that originally represented temporary responses.

The Real Solution: Progressive Taxation

While tariffs fail spectacularly as fiscal policy, proven alternatives exist. Progressive taxation—the postwar American approach—can address fiscal imbalances while maintaining economic efficiency and international cooperation.

A 75% tax rate on incomes over $10 million could generate $300 billion annually according to economists Saez and Zucman—six times current tariff revenue while targeting those most able to pay. Corporate loophole closures could add another $100 billion yearly, while wealth taxes on billionaires could yield $200 billion.

This $600 billion total—generated through efficient, progressive taxation—would cut deficits by 35% while avoiding the economic distortions and international conflicts that tariffs create. More importantly, progressive taxation addresses inequality while tariffs worsen it.

Conclusion: Choosing Evidence Over Ideology

Trump's tariff experiment demonstrates what happens when ideology overrides evidence in economic policy. Despite clear historical warnings and economic analysis predicting failure, political leaders pursued policies that served narrow interests while imposing broad costs.

The $37 trillion debt crisis requires abandoning such failures and embracing proven alternatives. Progressive taxation, strategic spending, and international cooperation—the postwar American model—delivered superior results until political leaders abandoned evidence-based policy for ideological experiments.

Recovery requires acknowledging these failures honestly and rejecting the policies that created current problems. Tariffs represent the latest attempt to avoid fiscal responsibility through magical thinking rather than difficult choices necessary for sustainable policy.

The Canadian model proves that American fiscal crisis represents policy choice rather than economic inevitability.
