Israel/Palestine: The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

Part II — The Crusades: When God Became Military Doctrine

April 29, 2026

Gustave Doré, “The Crusaders Arriving in Jerusalem” (1877)

On November 27, 1095, Pope Urban II addressed a crowd assembled at Clermont, in the Auvergne region of what is now France, and delivered a speech that would kill hundreds of thousands of people over the next two centuries and establish a template for Western intervention in the Middle East that has never entirely been abandoned.

We do not have the precise text of what he said. We have five accounts written afterward by chroniclers, each shaped by the politics and theology of its author. What is consistent across all versions is the essential argument: that Christians in the Holy Land were suffering at the hands of the Muslim Seljuk Turks, that Jerusalem — the city of Christ’s passion and resurrection — was in infidel hands, that God himself demanded its liberation, and that any man who took the cross and marched east to reclaim it would receive full remission of all his sins.

The crowd’s response, according to the chroniclers, was immediate and unanimous: Deus vult. God wills it.

Thus began the Crusades — the series of religiously sanctioned military campaigns that would last from 1096 to 1291, that would write some of the most savage chapters in medieval history, that would permanently poison the relationship between the Christian West and the Muslim East, and that would establish, with remarkable precision, the ideological template that Western powers have been unconsciously replaying in the Middle East ever since.

God wills it. We are bringing civilization. The people already there don’t count in the way that we count. These ideas did not end at Clermont in 1095. They evolved, secularized, and reappeared in different clothing in Balfour’s letter of 1917, in the rhetoric of every American administration that has described Israel as a democratic beacon in a region of darkness, and in the evangelical Christian Zionism that currently shapes United States foreign policy toward a region its most influential adherents have never visited.

History, as William Faulkner nearly said, is not even past. Nowhere is that more precisely true than in the Holy Land.

What the Crusaders Found When They Got There

The Jerusalem that the First Crusade reached in June 1099, after three years of marching, starving, fighting, and dying across Europe and Anatolia, was not the barbaric wasteland of Muslim tyranny that Urban’s rhetoric had conjured. It was a prosperous, cosmopolitan city under Fatimid Egyptian rule — less repressive toward Christian pilgrims, in fact, than several earlier periods of Byzantine Christian governance had been toward Jews and religious minorities.

The Crusaders besieged it for five weeks. On July 15, 1099, they breached the walls.

What followed was a massacre that contemporaries on all sides recorded with horror. The Crusader chronicle Gesta Francorum — written by a participant — describes the killing with a satisfaction that tells you everything about the ideology driving it. Muslim residents were slaughtered wholesale. The Jewish population, which had fought alongside the Muslim defenders, was herded into a synagogue and burned alive. Blood, according to multiple accounts, ran ankle-deep in the streets near the Temple Mount.

Raymond of Aguilers, a chaplain who was present, wrote that the carnage was God’s just judgment. The killing was the point. The liberation of Jerusalem required, in the Crusader theological framework, its purification — the removal of the unbelievers who had defiled it by their presence.

The resonance with certain contemporary Israeli rhetoric about Gaza — about human animals, about eliminating the threat comprehensively, about divine sanction for collective punishment — is not something I am inventing. It is something historians of the Crusades have been noting with increasing unease since October 2023.

I am not calling the Israeli Defence Forces Crusaders. I am noting that when religious nationalism provides the ideological framework for military action, it generates a recognizable grammar of justification, a familiar structure of dehumanization, that transcends the specific theology involved. That grammar was written at Jerusalem in 1099 and it has been revised and republished many times since.

The Crusader States: Colonialism Before the Word Existed

After the massacre, the Crusaders established what historians call the Crusader States — the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the County of Tripoli, the Principality of Antioch, and the County of Edessa. These were, in the most precise sense available to the 12th century, colonial settler states.

The Crusader nobility were European — French, Norman, Flemish, Italian. They occupied land they had not previously inhabited. They built castles, established feudal hierarchies, imported their own legal systems and religious institutions, and governed a population that was predominantly Muslim and Eastern Christian and had no say in the arrangement. The local population was useful as labour and as taxable subjects. It was not consulted about governance. Its existing social and legal arrangements were subordinated to those of the newcomers.

This is not a polemical description designed to make a contemporary point. It is the straightforward historical characterization that medieval historians have applied to the Crusader States for decades. The parallels to other colonial settler projects — in the Americas, in Africa, in Australia, and yes, in mandatory Palestine — are structural, not accidental. Colonial settler projects share certain features across centuries and continents: the claim that the land was divinely or historically destined for the newcomers, the characterization of the existing population as backward or barbaric or simply not fully human in the relevant sense, the construction of a legal and military apparatus that protects the settlers at the expense of the indigenous population, and the genuine bewilderment of the settlers when the indigenous population fails to be grateful.

The Crusader States lasted roughly two hundred years, which is considerably longer than most people realize. They were not a brief adventure. They were a functioning colonial system that extracted wealth, maintained hierarchies, and generated the complex cultural and commercial relationships — including a thriving trade with the Muslim world that the Crusader nobility found economically indispensable even while fighting it militarily — that characterize all sustained colonial projects.

They fell not because their ideology was defeated but because they were militarily overwhelmed. Saladin’s reconquest of Jerusalem in 1187 was the decisive moment.

Saladin and the Road Not Taken

Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub — Saladin — was a Kurdish Muslim military commander who unified Egypt and Syria under his rule and dedicated himself to recovering Jerusalem from the Crusaders. He defeated the Crusader army catastrophically at the Battle of Hattin on July 4, 1187, capturing the True Cross — the holiest relic in the Crusader world — and taking prisoner most of the Crusader nobility. Then he marched on Jerusalem.

What happened next is worth pausing over, because it is almost entirely absent from Western popular understanding of the Crusades.

Saladin did not massacre the population of Jerusalem. He negotiated its surrender, guaranteed the safety of its Christian inhabitants, allowed the Crusader garrison to ransom themselves and depart, and — crucially — permitted Jews to return to the city from which the Crusaders had expelled them eighty-eight years earlier. He wept, by several accounts, upon entering the city. He ordered the Church of the Holy Sepulchre left open to Christian pilgrims. He entrusted the key to a Muslim family — the Nuseibeh family mentioned in Part I, who still hold it today.

Adeeb Joudeh holds the keys to Church of the Holy Sepulchre. Victoria Eastwood/CNN

The contrast with 1099 could not be more complete. The Crusaders had liberated Jerusalem through massacre and exclusion. Saladin reclaimed it through negotiation and inclusion. The 12th century Muslim commander behaved with more restraint, more legal sophistication, and more genuine religious tolerance than the 12th century Christian army had.

This fact was deeply inconvenient to the Crusading ideology and remains deeply inconvenient to anyone who frames the conflict between Islam and the West as a story about Western civilization bringing enlightenment to barbarism. It does not fit the narrative. So it is generally omitted from the narrative.

Saladin’s example is also inconvenient to those who frame the current conflict as one between an inherently violent Islamic civilization and an inherently humane Western-aligned Israel. History declines to arrange itself conveniently for any party’s propaganda.

The Later Crusades and the Pattern of Failure

The Second through Ninth Crusades — the exact numbering is a matter of historical convention rather than precision — followed over the next century and a half, and they establish a pattern that modern readers of Middle Eastern geopolitics will find disturbingly familiar.

Each Crusade was launched with enormous rhetorical investment, genuine religious fervour, and the confident assumption that Western military power, properly directed, could resolve a complex political situation in a distant land by force. Each Crusade encountered the fundamental problem that military force, however overwhelming at the point of application, cannot create the political conditions for stable governance if those conditions do not exist. Each Crusade left the underlying situation worse than it found it while generating enormous profits for the military suppliers, naval contractors, and Italian merchant cities that transported and provisioned the armies.

The Fourth Crusade in 1202-1204 is perhaps the most instructive. It never reached the Holy Land at all. The Crusaders, unable to pay the Venetian fleet that was transporting them, were persuaded — or manipulated, depending on whose account you trust — into sacking Constantinople, the capital of the Byzantine Christian Empire, instead. The Crusaders looted and burned the greatest Christian city in the world in the name of a Crusade to liberate a different Christian city from Muslim rule. The Byzantine Empire never fully recovered. The Eastern and Western branches of Christianity, already formally schismatic since 1054, were permanently embittered. And Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks in 1453, in part because the Latin West had so thoroughly destroyed its capacity to resist.

The operation was a strategic catastrophe of the first order. It was pursued by men who were absolutely certain God was on their side.

The lesson compounds with every Crusade: righteous certainty combined with military power and ignorance of the local political landscape is not a formula for liberation. It is a formula for destruction, blowback, and consequences that outlast the original actors by centuries.

The United States military discovered this formula anew in Iraq in 2003. It had been tried and found wanting eight hundred years earlier in the same general neighbourhood. The geography changes. The pattern does not.

What the Crusades Left Behind

The fall of Acre, 1291

The Crusades ended formally with the fall of Acre in 1291 — the last Crusader stronghold in the Holy Land. The Crusader States were extinguished. The dream of a Christian Jerusalem under Western control was over, and would not seriously revive for six hundred years, until a British general named Edmund Allenby walked through the Jaffa Gate on December 11, 1917 — twelve days after the Balfour Declaration — and announced that Jerusalem had been liberated.

He used that word deliberately. The resonance with the Crusades was not accidental. Allenby’s staff officers reportedly discussed it. Some accounts have Allenby himself remarking on it, though historians dispute whether he actually said “the wars of the Crusaders are now complete.” What is not disputed is that the imagery of Christian liberation of the Holy Land was consciously evoked, that it played in the Western press as a triumphant historical echo, and that it was received in the Muslim world as exactly the continuation of Crusader colonialism that it structurally resembled.

The Crusades left four specific legacies that run directly into the modern conflict.

The first is the template of religious justification for colonial conquest. The idea that a Western power can claim divine or civilizational mandate for intervention in the Middle East — bringing light to darkness, liberating the oppressed, restoring the rightful order — was not invented by the Crusades but was perfected by them and has been available for reuse ever since. British colonialism recycled it. American exceptionalism recycled it. Israeli religious nationalism has its own version of it.

The second legacy is the deep Muslim memory of the Crusades as traumatic conquest. Western commentators are sometimes surprised by how vividly the Crusades figure in contemporary Arab and Muslim political rhetoric. Osama bin Laden referred to Americans as “Crusaders.” The term is not metaphor in this context. It is historical memory of a specific, documentable, savage experience of European Christian armies killing Muslim populations in the name of God, and it informs the reception of every subsequent Western military intervention in the region regardless of the intervening centuries.

The third legacy is the destruction of the sophisticated multi-religious coexistence that had characterized Jerusalem and much of the Levant before the Crusaders arrived. The Fatimid and later Ayyubid Islamic administrations were not perfect by any standard, ancient or modern. But they had developed functional if unequal systems for managing the competing religious claims of Muslim, Jewish, and Christian populations. The Crusaders destroyed that system by eliminating the Muslim and Jewish populations of Jerusalem entirely. The Ottoman system we will examine in Part III was built in part on the ruins of what the Crusaders had wrecked.

The fourth legacy is the most directly relevant to this series. The Crusades established, as a matter of Western cultural memory, the idea that Jews in the Holy Land are a displaced people whose return is cosmically appropriate. This idea — not consciously Crusader in its modern expression, but structurally descended from it — was available to be mobilized by European Jewish nationalists in the late 19th century and by the British government in 1917 when it needed a justification for what it was about to do in Palestine. The Balfour Declaration was not a Crusade. But it drew on the same reservoir of Western Christian sentiment about the Holy Land and its rightful inhabitants, and it was received in the Arab world as exactly the continuation of European presumption — the decision by a distant Western power about the fate of people and land it did not own.

The Crusade as Mirror

I want to be precise about what I am and am not arguing here, because the Crusade analogy is one of the most inflammable in the entire conflict and is regularly deployed dishonestly by all sides.

I am not arguing that the State of Israel is a Crusader state. The Crusader states were created by European Christian armies with no prior connection to the land. The Zionist movement, whatever its European origins, was driven by Jews with a three-thousand-year documented historical and spiritual connection to the land. That distinction matters and is not a minor quibble.

I am not arguing that the Crusades justify contemporary Muslim violence against Jewish or Israeli targets. History does not create moral permission slips, and the memory of the Crusades is regularly exploited by political actors — including Hamas and its sponsors — who have no genuine interest in historical justice and considerable interest in perpetual conflict.

What I am arguing is this: the Crusades established a set of ideas, a grammar of justification, a structure of thought about the Middle East that Western powers have never fully abandoned. The idea that Western intervention in this region is inherently civilizational — bringing order, democracy, the rule of law, enlightenment — rather than strategic and self-interested. The idea that the existing population’s preferences are subordinate to the correct historical or divine arrangement. The idea that military force can resolve in weeks or months what centuries of layered human habitation have made immensely complex. The idea that God — or history, or democracy, or the war on terror — is on our side and therefore our conduct requires less scrutiny than our enemies’.

These ideas killed hundreds of thousands of people in the 11th through 13th centuries. They have continued killing people, in varying configurations, ever since.

The Crusades are not ancient history. They are the first draft of the story we are still living.
