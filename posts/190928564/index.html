The Iran Crisis

“The Crown Jewel”

Part XLV: Day Fourteen: A Strike on Iran’s Oil Lifeline, A Doctrine Named, and the War That Feels Like It Was Launched Without Thought

March 14, 2026

A satellite image of Kharg Island in Iran last month.Credit...Planet Labs PBC, via Associated Press

I. The Crown Jewel

Late Friday night, President Trump posted a video on Truth Social. It was geolocated by CNN to Kharg Island, in the northern Persian Gulf — a five-mile stretch of coral and industrial infrastructure about fifteen miles off Iran’s coast that handles approximately 90 percent of Iran’s crude oil exports. The footage showed explosions and black smoke. Trump’s caption: “Moments ago, at my direction, the United States Central Command executed one of the most powerful bombing raids in the History of the Middle East, and totally obliterated every MILITARY target in Iran’s crown jewel, Kharg Island.”

He added, in the next sentence, a qualifier. “For reasons of decency, I have chosen NOT to wipe out the Oil Infrastructure on the Island.” And then, in the sentence after that, a threat. “However, should Iran, or anyone else, do anything to interfere with the Free and Safe Passage of Ships through the Strait of Hormuz, I will immediately reconsider this decision.”

A US military official confirmed the targets: missile storage sites and mine storage sites — the weapons being used to block shipping. The oil infrastructure was deliberately spared. A senior Iranian Oil Ministry official, speaking anonymously, said the strikes were “enormous and destructive,” that employees of the island’s refineries had reported nearly two hours of nonstop explosions that shook the island like an earthquake, and that an attack on the oil and gas infrastructure would immediately halt a major part of Iran’s exports.

Iran’s Revolutionary Guards issued their response within hours. If the United States followed through with its threat against the oil infrastructure, Iran would retaliate by attacking “all oil, energy and economic infrastructure of oil companies across the region that have American stock shares or cooperate with America. We will turn them into a pile of ash.”

The IRGC separately informed the United Arab Emirates that US military facilities in the country were “legitimate targets.” UAE civilians were warned to stay away from ports and docks. Iran’s parliament speaker had already warned the country would “abandon all restraint” if Persian Gulf islands were attacked.

The island had been untouched for the first two weeks of the war. The White House had been privately discussing not merely striking Kharg but seizing it entirely — a ground operation, four sources told Axios on March 7. Trump said seizing the fuel depot was “not high on the list” but added that he could “change my mind in seconds.” The Kharg strike on Friday was, in the language of strategists, a shot across the bow. The oil infrastructure is the bow itself. Trump has now told Iran how close he is to firing at it, and Iran has told Trump what happens when he does.

Iran’s Fars News reported the strike caused 15+ explosions, hitting army defenses, the Joshen Sea Base, an airport control tower, and a helicopter hangar. The oil infrastructure was undamaged, Iranian sources said. Multiple supertankers had been loading at Kharg as recently as Wednesday.

Brent crude closed Friday at $103.14 — up 40 percent since the war began.

Julie Walton Shaver/The New York Times

II. The Enemy Gets a Vote

In wartime, James Mattis used to say, the enemy gets a vote.

Astonishingly, as The Atlantic’s Phillips Payson O’Brien reported, the Trump administration acknowledged in classified briefings that it did not make provisions for a Strait of Hormuz closure because officials assumed it would hurt Iran more than the United States. The world’s most strategically vulnerable oil chokepoint, identified by military planners for decades as the nightmare scenario, was treated as an implausible retaliation option because the administration assumed Iran would reason the same way Washington did.

Iran did not.

O’Brien names the parallel directly. Trump made the same assumption that Vladimir Putin made when he invaded Ukraine four years earlier: that superior weaponry and military experience would crush any opposition the target government could muster. In his video address on the first night of strikes, Trump said Iran would “soon learn that no one should challenge the strength and might of the United States Armed Forces” and that “no military on Earth” was “even close to its power, strength, or sophistication.” In February 2022, Putin said something functionally identical about Ukraine. Both were right about the military balance. Both were wrong about what that meant.

The US and Israel quickly gained air supremacy over Iran. They have the ability to hit almost any target they choose. They killed much of Iran’s top leadership, including Ali Khamenei himself. And none of it produced capitulation. Iran’s drones and missiles struck American forces across the region. Its mines are going into the Strait faster than the US can clear them. Its small boats are attacking tankers. Its new supreme leader, injured and in hiding, issued a written statement naming the Minab school children specifically. And now Iran is considering a yuan-denominated oil access scheme that, if it takes hold, would represent a permanent shift in the architecture of global energy markets. The enemy is voting with everything it has.

The US has responded to one dimension of this by reaching out to Ukraine. American and Gulf State forces are now being trained by Ukrainians on cheap, effective drone-defense equipment — the same systems Ukraine developed to counter the Iranian drones Russia used against its cities. The Trump administration spent the past year scaling back support for Ukraine, expressing sympathy for Putin’s position, and treating the Atlantic alliance as a nuisance. It now requires Ukrainian military expertise to fight a war against Iran.

The Strait of Hormuz has no alternative. Geography set that truth before any of this administration was born. Rebecca Elliott and Vivian Nereim documented it clearly in the New York Times on Saturday: the strait is the only exit from the Persian Gulf to the wider world. Saudi Arabia’s East-West pipeline has five million barrels per day of export capacity after domestic consumption — against the twenty million barrels that transited the Strait daily before the war. The UAE’s Fujairah pipeline handles a fraction of that. Qatar has not been cooling liquefied natural gas for shipment since the early days of the conflict. Shipping through the Strait has fallen to less than ten percent of prewar levels. Gulf countries have collectively cut production by at least ten million barrels per day. Oil industry veteran John Browne, former chief executive of BP, put it plainly: “There’s nothing which is totally secure here. In the end, someone with bad intention can do all sorts of things to oil and gas infrastructure.”

Daniel Yergin, the Pulitzer Prize-winning energy historian, identified the founding assumption that the administration apparently shared: “The diversification or security came from the fact that the consumers would be there to protect the oil.” Meaning: the United States would use its military might to keep the Strait passable. The irony requires no elaboration. The United States is the reason the Strait is not passable. It was the United States that instigated the conflict on February 28. Energy Secretary Chris Wright, asked when escorts for oil tankers would begin, told CNBC on Thursday: “It’ll happen relatively soon, but it can’t happen now. We’re simply not ready.”

The enemy got a vote. The vote was cast on February 28, when Iran declined to capitulate. It has been cast every day since.

III. Destroy and Deal

Secretary of State Marco Rubio is at the helm of President Trump’s aggressive campaigns to reshape the governments of Iran, Venezuela, Cuba and beyond.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

The war has a doctrine. It was named this week, and naming it clarifies what has otherwise appeared to be improvisation.

Edward Wong and Michael Crowley of the New York Times identified it: regime compliance rather than regime change. Not the George W. Bush model of democracy promotion at gunpoint. Not nation-building. Not the transformation of adversaries into liberal democracies. Instead: use American military and economic power to turn authoritarian countries into client states. Emma Ashford of the Stimson Center: “For Rubio and other members of this younger group, foreign policy isn’t as much about regime change as it is about power. It is about sustaining American military primacy, making other states fear and respect us.”

The architect is Marco Rubio — secretary of state, national security adviser, and the man who told Congress as the war began: “We’re going to unleash Chiang on these people.” The reference was arcane — it traced to a tennis shout by George H.W. Bush, through a golden sword Jeb Bush gave Rubio at the Florida legislature in 2005, to Chiang Kai-shek, the US-backed Chinese military leader who lost the civil war to Mao. The neoconservative lineage is direct and self-conscious. But the doctrine is something new: a merger of neoconservatism with Trumpian transactionalism. The goal is not to spread freedom. The goal is submission.

Wong and Crowley documented the pattern in operation. In Venezuela, Maduro was captured and Delcy Rodríguez — a leftist Maduro ally — now governs, while Rubio works with her rather than pushing for pro-democracy leader María Corina Machado, whom he cosigned a Nobel Peace Prize nomination for in 2024. In Cuba, Rubio is blocking oil shipments while quietly negotiating to open Cuba’s economy with the grandson of Raúl Castro. In Iran, regime change language has been abandoned; the administration is simultaneously demanding unconditional surrender and exploring deals with officials of the current government.

John Bolton, Trump’s first-term national security adviser and a true believer in regime change, identified the gap in this approach with characteristic directness: “On Trump’s resolve, he clearly is playing it day by day, which is no way to maximize the pressure on the regime or to help the opposition.”

The Israeli newspaper columnist Nahum Barnea distilled what this looks like from Jerusalem. “Israel is in the war, but is not leading it. It is operating in the dark. Over every one of its actions hangs a large question: When will Trump, the supreme leader on our side, decide to declare victory and stop the fire?”

David Halbfinger in the New York Times documented the arc: Netanyahu went to war promising to “remove the existential threat posed by the terrorist regime in Iran.” Two weeks later, he is talking about degrading Iran’s nuclear and ballistic missile capability and hoping the Iranian people eventually find a way to overthrow their own government. Yaakov Katz, an Israeli analyst, named the pattern: “hit, degrade, declare victory, build up, prepare, and then do it again.” Netanyahu’s Channel 14 allies are comparing him to Churchill for bringing America into the war from the start — while Netanyahu publicly says the idea that he dragged Trump in is “ridiculous.” Churchill, as one Israeli commentator noted, was ousted before World War II ended.

The doctrine of destroy and deal requires a partner for the deal. In Venezuela, Rodríguez played that role. In Iran, as The Atlantic’s Franklin Foer observed weeks ago, there is no Delcy Rodríguez. The man issuing instructions from an undisclosed location to a military that is mining international waterways and threatening to turn regional oil infrastructure to ash is not a man preparing to deal. He is a man with a list.

IV. The War Comes Home

The violence that began over Iran on February 28 did not stay in Iran.

Within 24 hours of the first strikes, a man wearing a shirt bearing the colors of the Iranian flag killed three people and wounded more than a dozen others at a bar in Austin, Texas. A week later, two teenagers from Pennsylvania claimed ISIS inspiration and tried to set off an explosive device at a protest in New York. On Thursday, a former National Guard member who had spent years in prison for providing material support for terrorism walked into a classroom of ROTC cadets in Norfolk, Virginia, and killed the instructor. Less than two hours later, in West Bloomfield Township, Michigan, a man plowed a car into Temple Israel synagogue and killed himself in a gunfight with security guards. His four family members in Lebanon had been killed by Israeli strikes targeting Hezbollah.

The motives are different. The ideologies are different — the Islamic State, which may have inspired two of the attacks, is a Sunni organization that is Iran’s sectarian enemy, not its ally. But Javed Ali, a former senior counterterrorism official now at the University of Michigan, said the distinction may not matter: if the war continues, there will be more attacks from “lone offenders.” Robert Pape of the University of Chicago, who has studied political violence for thirty years, warned that rising support for political violence is highly correlated with rising antisemitism, and that the more political violence occurs in connection with the Middle East, the more it can draw actors from both left and right into antisemitic attacks. “That is a really dangerous situation,” he said.

Into this climate, the administration has deployed its counterterrorism architecture. Thousands of agents within the Homeland Security and Justice Departments have been shifted from counterterrorism to immigration enforcement. The department is partially shut down amid Senate Democratic demands over immigration enforcement tactics. Many national security officials have resigned or been fired. A 22-year-old with no professional background in national security was appointed to oversee terrorism prevention programs. Alex Plitsas of the Atlantic Council, a former Pentagon official, described counterterrorism as having been “moving steadily down the list as we move further away from 9/11.” He added: “Everybody I talk to — and these are not pearl-clutchers — everybody is worried about it.”

V. What the Water Knows

Note: Includes plants that are “presumed online”. Does not include plants with a capacity below 1,000 cubic meters per day. Source: Global Water Intelligence, DesalData.com.

The war’s civilian toll is not measured only in deaths. It is measured in what is being unmade.

Two desalination plants have been struck. A US-attributed strike on Qeshm Island on March 7 affected the water supply to 30 villages in Iran. An Iranian drone caused material damage to a desalination plant in Bahrain. Qatar and Bahrain each rely on desalination for more than half of their fresh water. Bahrain’s Al Dur plant alone supplies more than a million people daily. An expert at the Center for Strategic and International Studies said that even if plants aren’t directly targeted, oil spills from struck tankers or infrastructure could contaminate the seawater intakes and “practically kill the desalination plant.” He called the existing strikes “intentional, direct targeting” of civilian survival infrastructure. International law prohibits exactly this.

Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

At least 56 historical buildings and cultural sites in Iran have been seriously damaged in US and Israeli strikes, according to Iran’s Ministry of Cultural Heritage. Tehran leads with 19 damaged sites. UNESCO confirmed that several World Heritage Sites have been struck, including the Golestan Palace. The Safavid-era Rashk Palace has been destroyed. The Chehel Sotoun pavilion, the Timurid Hall, the windows of the Ali Qapu Palace — all damaged. Blue Shield International, the international organization that protects cultural heritage in armed conflict, called the destruction a war crime.

Three point two million Iranians are displaced inside their own country. Nearly two thousand are dead. A businessman in Tehran who spoke anonymously described waking most mornings at 5 or 5:30 to the sound of explosions. The streets, he said, are often empty during the day. At night, the city belongs entirely to security forces and no one dares to protest. Some Iranians who were initially hopeful when Ali Khamenei was killed are now simply exhausted. Some have come to believe the war facilitated the very succession the regime might otherwise have struggled to engineer.

This is what 6,000 targets and fifteen days looks like from inside.

VI. The War That Feels Like It Was Launched Without Thought

The writer John Ganz observed that the Iran war is “the first war that feels like it’s been launched by AI: It’s all been done on a level less than thought.” He means that the logic is generative rather than deliberative — producing outputs without a model of what they are for or where they lead. The administration cannot say why it went to war with Iran, cannot say what victory looks like, and, in David Graham’s formulation, now “can’t even decide whether the war is still going on” on any given day.

This is not incompetence alone. It is doctrine. The Warzel piece in The Atlantic this week named the information environment in which the war is being waged and sold: a culture that has developed an insatiable need for instant information while simultaneously losing the capacity to process it. “You do not need to justify a war,” Warzel wrote, “if you believe that, ultimately, people will lose interest in it and move on to the next outrage.” The White House’s official X account published a video superimposing US bombing footage over Wii Sports this week. It was intended to be enjoyed.

In Washington, Senator Tim Kaine of Virginia has forced the Senate into five votes on limiting Trump’s military authority using the 1973 War Powers Resolution. The latest resolution would have immediately halted the Iran war. It failed. Kaine has been doing this since early last year — in Venezuela, in Nigeria, and now in Iran. Republicans accuse him of theater. He calls it an obsession, rooted in twenty years of attending deployments, homecomings, and funerals in a state with more than 100,000 active duty and reserve military personnel. “I came to the Senate with 100 interests and passions, and one obsession,” he said. “And this is my obsession — getting this decision correct.” He hasn’t won yet. He keeps going.

While he does: Jared Kushner, the president’s son-in-law and “peace envoy,” is raising $5 billion or more for his private equity firm Affinity Partners from the sovereign wealth funds of Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar — governments that are currently absorbing Iranian missile and drone strikes because of a war that Kushner helped plan. In December 2024, he told a podcaster he would “not seek more capital for the next four years” to avoid conflicts. Weeks before the war began, in his role as peace envoy, he met with Iran’s foreign minister in Geneva. The talks ended without a deal. The bombs fell. The fundraising continues.

The S&P 500 recorded its worst single-day performance since the war began on Thursday. Gas is $3.68 per gallon nationally — up 23.5 percent since February 28. The US Embassy in Baghdad was attacked by Kataib Hezbollah on Saturday morning. A NATO air defense system intercepted an Iranian missile over Turkey — the third such interception in ten days. The State Department has ordered non-emergency government employees out of Oman. Russia is receiving more than $150 million in extra revenue every day.

Thirteen Americans are dead. All six KC-135 crew members — aircraft with no ejection systems, no parachutes — have been confirmed killed in the probable midair collision over the western Iraqi desert. The Pentagon is sending 2,500 Marines and three warships from the Indo-Pacific.

On Saturday, Trump was asked when the war would end.

“When I feel it in my bones,” he said.

Senator Kennedy of Louisiana remains the only Republican to have voted against the war.

Phase two of four. The longest phase.
