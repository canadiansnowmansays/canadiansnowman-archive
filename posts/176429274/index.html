The Great Sorting (1970s-1990s)

How Americans Retreated into Separate Realities

October 17, 2025

It’s worth considering whether Obama’s presidency acted as an accelerant for divisions that had been building for decades—and yes, it absolutely did, amplifying fault lines already etched deep into the political landscape. (Data from Pew Research shows racial resentment as the top Tea Party predictor, not taxes.) But to understand why America’s first Black president triggered such explosive polarization, we need to examine how Americans had already begun segregating themselves politically, geographically, and culturally in the preceding decades.

The Big Sort Begins (1970s)

In the 1970s, something unprecedented began happening: Americans started moving to communities that shared their political values. Political scientist Bill Bishop documented this phenomenon in “The Big Sort”—showing how Americans increasingly lived in landslide counties where one party dominated.

From a Canadian perspective, this is striking. While Canadians might cluster by language or economic opportunity, Americans were literally relocating based on political ideology. Austin became a blue dot in red Texas. Rural California turned deep red while its cities went blue. This wasn’t government policy—it was millions of individual choices creating political segregation. We’ve seen hints of this in our own urban-rural drifts, but it hasn’t hardened quite as much.

The Southern Strategy’s Long Game (1968-1990s)

Following the Civil Rights era, Republicans executed their “Southern Strategy”—appealing to white Southern voters uncomfortable with racial progress. This wasn’t subtle; Republican strategist Lee Atwater later admitted they used coded racial appeals, transitioning from explicit racism to talking about “states’ rights” and “welfare queens.”

This cynical strategy worked, flipping the South from Democrat to Republican. But it also racialized American partisan identity in a way that would explode during Obama’s presidency. The party of Lincoln became the party of white grievance, setting the stage for the Tea Party and eventually MAGA. Canada’s party realignments, like the West’s conservative shift, feel milder by comparison.

Media Fragmentation (1980s-1990s)

The fairness doctrine’s elimination in 1987 unleashed partisan media. Rush Limbaugh launched his national show in 1988, creating a new form of political entertainment that profited from outrage. Fox News followed in 1996 with its “Fair and Balanced” slogan that was anything but.

While Canada’s CRTC keeps a firmer leash on broadcasters—flaws and all, like stifling some voices—America’s post-1987 free-for-all turned media into a choose-your-own-reality buffet. Both systems have their blind spots: We’ve got our echoey talk radio too, just with more maple syrup.

Republicans had Fox News and talk radio; Democrats had MSNBC and NPR. Americans stopped sharing the same basic facts, let alone interpretations of those facts.

The Gingrich Revolution (1994)

Newt Gingrich transformed American politics by treating Democrats not as opponents but as enemies. His leaked memo advised Republicans to use words like “corrupt,” “destroy,” “sick,” and “traitors” when describing Democrats. He ended the tradition of congresspeople socializing across party lines, moving their families to Washington.

Thank goodness Canadian politics hasn’t fully embraced this scorched-earth vibe—though we’ve had our Harper-era grudge matches—but imagine if it did: Would our minority governments still hash it out over Tim Hortons, or would Parliament Hill turn into a no-holds-barred arena?

This approach worked—Republicans took the House in 1994. But it normalized a level of partisan warfare that strains any system.

Religious Politics (1979-1990s)

The Moral Majority’s founding in 1979 marked conservative Christianity’s full entry into partisan politics. American evangelicals built a powerful political machine, making abortion, gay rights, and “family values” into partisan litmus tests.

This created what would become the “God Gap”—religious Americans increasingly voted Republican while secular Americans went Democratic. Faith became another sorting mechanism, adding moral absolutism to political disagreements. Canada’s secular lean has softened similar tensions, but our faith-based divides (e.g., in education policy) remind us it’s universal.

Clinton and Cultural Warfare (1992-2000)

Bill Clinton’s presidency intensified cultural divisions. To conservatives, Clinton represented everything wrong with liberal America: a draft-dodging, pot-smoking, philandering baby boomer. The Monica Lewinsky scandal and impeachment transformed politics into reality TV, making the personal political in unprecedented ways.

The Clintons weren’t just political opponents—they were cultural enemies, foreshadowing today’s personal attacks.

Pre-Obama Kindling

By 2008, all the kindling was in place:

  • Geographic political segregation

  • Separate media ecosystems

  • Politics as total war

  • Religious partisan alignment

  • Cultural identity as party identity

Then America elected Barack Hussein Obama.

The Obama Acceleration

Obama’s election was seismic not just because he was Black, but because of what he represented to different Americas. To progressives, he embodied hope, change, and America transcending its racist past. To many conservatives, he was the ultimate “other”—urban, intellectual, Black, with a Muslim-sounding name and an elite education.

The Tea Party explosion in 2009 wasn’t really about fiscal policy. “I want my country back” became their rallying cry—but back from whom? The first Black president triggered existential panic among whites who saw demographic change as dispossession.

The birther conspiracy—championed by Donald Trump—perfectly captured this othering. Despite clear evidence Obama was born in Hawaii, millions believed he was a secret Kenyan Muslim. This wasn’t normal political opposition; it was rejection of Obama’s fundamental Americanness.

Digital Gasoline on the Fire

Obama’s presidency coincided with social media’s explosion. Facebook’s news feed algorithm, Twitter’s real-time outrage machine, and YouTube’s recommendation engine created perfect conditions for polarization. Every racist comment at a town hall, every progressive call-out, every conspiracy theory got amplified and monetized.

Unlike previous eras, Americans couldn’t escape politics. It followed them on their phones, targeted them with ads, and sorted them into increasingly pure ideological bubbles.

The tragedy is that Obama genuinely tried to bridge divides—his 2004 “no red states or blue states” speech inspired millions. But his very existence amplified every division that had been building since the 1970s. He was the spark, but Americans had been soaking themselves in gasoline for decades.

Thoughts for my Next Essay

This geographic and cultural sorting was dramatic enough, but it was about to collide with the most powerful force for division ever created: the internet. The careful social dances that once allowed Americans to coexist—like those my I witnessed in 1967 Mississippi—were about to be obliterated by algorithms that profit from rage. The digital revolution wouldn’t just reflect America’s divisions; it would weaponize them...
