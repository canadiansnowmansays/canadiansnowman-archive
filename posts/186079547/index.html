The Iran Crisis

The Betrayal

Trump’s Pattern and the Question That Remains

January 28, 2026

Part 2 of 2 - January 28, 2026

On at least eight occasions over three weeks in late December 2025 and early January 2026, President Donald Trump encouraged Iranian protesters to take to the streets.

“If they start killing people like they have in the past,” he warned on January 1, “we will get involved. We’ll be hitting them very hard where it hurts. And that doesn’t mean boots on the ground, but it means hitting them very, very hard where it hurts.”

On January 9, as protests swelled, Trump posted on Truth Social that the United States was “locked and loaded” and ready to support Iranian demonstrators if the regime began killing them.

“HELP IS ON THE WAY,” he wrote, urging Iranians to intensify their demonstrations and “take over government institutions.”

Then Iran’s Islamic Republic executed what may be the largest mass killing of protesters in modern history. More than 36,500 people were slaughtered in 48 hours according to classified IRGC documents obtained by Iran International.

On January 9, The New York Times learned from Iranian officials familiar with security matters, Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei ordered the Supreme National Security Council to crush the protests “by any means necessary.”

Security forces shot wounded patients in hospitals. They deployed chemical weapons. The Times obtained photographs of more than 300 bodies at Tehran’s main morgue—a majority showing severe head trauma and collapsed eye sockets. They systematically executed thousands while the world watched through a communications blackout.

Trump did nothing.

“The Most Callous Presidential Betrayal”

Karim Sadjadpour, senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace and one of America’s foremost Iran experts, wrote his warning on January 16—when the world still believed the death toll was in the thousands.

“If Trump chooses not to act, his encouragement of the Iranian people to rise up, his repeated promises of U.S. support, and his subsequent abandonment of them will be remembered as one of the most callous examples of presidential betrayal in modern history,” Sadjadpour wrote in The Atlantic.

“Expressing moral support for protesters was the right thing to do. But inciting them to rise up and promising intervention, only to watch them get mowed down by the thousands, will be counted as an act of cruelty.”

That was written when estimates suggested 5,000-12,000 dead.

Now we know the IRGC’s own classified documents show more than 36,500 killed in two days—a scale of killing that approaches genocide.

Trump’s moral authority to condemn Iran’s crackdown is further complicated by his own history. In 2020, he deployed federal forces against largely peaceful protesters in Minneapolis and Portland following George Floyd’s killing, cleared Lafayette Square with tear gas for a photo opportunity, and threatened that “when the looting starts, the shooting starts.” His selective outrage—violent crackdown acceptable at home, unacceptable abroad—undermines his credibility as a defender of protesters’ rights. Yet the scale of Iran’s atrocity transcends questions of Trump’s hypocrisy: 36,500 killed in 48 hours demands accountability regardless of who delivers it.

The consequences of Trump’s promises were visible in individual stories.

“Siavash hoped until the very end that Trump’s help would arrive”

Siavash Shirzad, 38, lived in Bukan in western Iran. His family tried to dissuade him from joining the January 2 protests. But Shirzad insisted.

“This is a celebration of a revolution,” he told them. “Trump said he supports us. I’m going.”

That faith cost him his life. After he was killed, Iranian authorities forced his family to pay to retrieve his body, then denied them the right to bury him in his birth city. They were required to bury him in silence in a remote ancestral village, with no demonstrations permitted.

Shirzad’s story became emblematic of a broader tragedy: Iranians who believed Trump’s promises altered their risk calculations. They went into the streets thinking American support was imminent. Instead, they were massacred by the tens of thousands while Trump watched.

“Iranian grievances are local, but revolutions are about psychology, and Trump’s exhortations altered the risk calculus of many protesters,” Sadjadpour wrote.

The massacre had another consequence. Security officials who may have been uncertain whether to put down their weapons or continue killing now clearly chose a side. Without a credible threat of U.S. intervention, they calculated that the regime would survive—making defection a death sentence and ruthless loyalty their only path to survival.

Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei’s orders on January 9 made the calculation explicit: “al-nasr bil-ru’b”—victory through terror. “Fight them until there is no sedition.”

Security forces obeyed. They didn’t just suppress protests. According to classified IRGC documents obtained by Iran International, they executed more than 36,500 people in 48 hours. They shot wounded patients in hospitals. They deployed chemical weapons that left people collapsed and unable to flee. They forced a teenage protester to hide in a body bag among corpses for three days while summary executions continued around him.

This wasn’t crowd control that got out of hand. This was industrial-scale extermination.

The Carrier That Arrived Too Late

On January 15, I published an essay documenting Trump’s deployment of the USS Abraham Lincoln Carrier Strike Group from the South China Sea to the Middle East. The carrier was expected to arrive approximately January 22-23, giving Trump immediate military options as the crisis reached its peak.

The timing seemed deliberate: the carrier would be in position just as protests intensified, allowing Trump to make good on his threats of intervention.

But the timing revealed a different story.

The carrier arrived in the U.S. Central Command area of responsibility on Monday, January 26—eleven days after the massacre ended. By then, Iran’s prosecutor general had declared “the sedition is over now.” More than 36,500 were dead. The butchery was finished.

Trump’s military option arrived after it mattered.

The carrier’s location in the Middle East now provides strike capability, but not the deterrent that might have prevented the slaughter. Ambrey, a private security firm, issued an assessment on January 27 stating the United States “has positioned sufficient military capability to conduct kinetic operations against Iran while maintaining the ability to defend itself and regional allies from reciprocal action.”

“Supporting or avenging Iranian protesters in punitive strikes is assessed as insufficient justification for sustained military conflict,” Ambrey wrote. “However, alternative objectives, such as the degradation of Iranian military capabilities, may increase the likelihood of limited U.S. intervention.”

The carrier is in position. The question is whether Trump will use it to avenge what he failed to prevent.

What the Intelligence Said

Behind the scenes, Trump received briefings that should have alarmed him—or emboldened him, depending on his objectives.

Multiple U.S. intelligence reports indicated that Iran’s government was at its weakest point since the shah was overthrown in 1979, according to several people familiar with the information reported by The New York Times on January 26.

The protests had shaken elements of the Iranian government, especially as demonstrations reached into areas officials thought were strongholds of support for Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Iran’s economy was historically weak. The regime had just suffered its worst military defeat in decades.

The intelligence suggested an opportunity. Economic hardship had ignited protests in late December that Iran’s government had few options to address. Officials would be forced to choose between reform and repression.

They chose repression at a scale unprecedented in modern history.

Yet Trump hesitated.

He nearly approved strikes against Iran earlier in January, only to cool to the idea on January 14, as advisers and Middle East partners warned it would be difficult to manage subsequent counterstrikes without more combat power in the region. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of Israel also asked Trump to postpone an attack, according to a senior U.S. official—likely wanting more U.S. military assets positioned to help defend Israel from Iranian retaliation.

By the time the carrier arrived, 36,500+ were dead.

The Strategic Paradox

Iran’s Islamic Republic now exists in a state of profound paradox: simultaneously at its weakest point in decades, yet still possessing a “fearsome capacity for repression,” as Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group put it.

But the January 8-9 massacre revealed something darker. The regime didn’t just possess capacity for repression. It demonstrated willingness to execute industrial-scale slaughter of civilians—more than 36,500 killed in 48 hours according to its own classified documents.

The June 2025 war with Israel and the United States delivered the greatest single blow to Iran’s military in decades. Israeli warplanes and U.S. bombers wiped out the top leadership of the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps (IRGC)—the feared praetorian guard to Ayatollah Khamenei.

That leadership void matters. The new generation of IRGC commanders, catapulted to the top unexpectedly, responded to protests with what can only be described as genocide-level violence. They massacred tens of thousands not from strength, but from acute awareness of their weakness.

“Right off the bat, they saw the protests as an existential threat,” said Afshon Ostovar, an Iran expert at the Naval Postgraduate School. “They turned to live fire really quickly because their weakness was acute, and they knew it.”

But they didn’t just turn to live fire. According to Iran International’s review of classified documents and witness testimony:

  • Security forces entered hospitals and executed wounded patients receiving medical treatment—”finishing shots” fired at people with medical tubes still attached

  • Chemical weapons were deployed that went beyond standard tear gas, causing people to collapse before they could flee

  • Iraqi and Syrian proxy forces were brought in to expand killing capacity

  • A systematic program of hospital executions, body disposal, and family extortion followed

The regime’s network of regional proxies—Hezbollah, Hamas, militias in Iraq and Yemen—is in tatters. Its nuclear program, which cost tens of billions of dollars, failed to provide deterrence. Water and electricity supplies are running low.

Today, Iran’s currency collapsed to a record low of 1.5 million rials to the dollar—down from 32,000 to the dollar just a decade ago. The economic catastrophe that sparked protests in late December has only accelerated.

“The regime is ideologically bankrupt, economically at a dead end, and unable to rescue itself,” said Vaez. “But it still has the will, and a fearsome capacity for repression.”

Comparisons to the Soviet Union in the 1980s before its collapse have proliferated among Iran experts. The similarities are striking: an aging leadership clinging to revolutionary ideology, economic stagnation masked by propaganda, military overextension, loss of legitimacy even among supporters, and generational divides within the security forces.

But the Soviet Union didn’t massacre 36,500+ people in a weekend. Iran did.

The New York Times’ visual investigation found only one comparable moment in Iran’s post-revolutionary history: the 1988 mass execution of thousands of political prisoners ordered by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini. Those killings occurred behind closed doors during the Iran-Iraq war as the Islamic Republic consolidated power.

The January 2026 massacre differs in one critical respect: it occurred in public, visible through smuggled communications to the world.

The Islamic Republic now faces what The Times characterized as “its most fragile geopolitical moment in years, and perhaps ever.” At home, it confronts a profound legitimacy crisis. Abroad, it faces weakening regional proxies and the prospect of more attacks from the United States and Israel.

As The Times concluded from analyzing both history and the January crackdown: “When it feels weak, it seems to grow more ferocious.”

The Iranians Who Want Intervention

Something unprecedented has happened in Iranian opposition politics: a broad consensus for foreign intervention has emerged where none existed before.

Over the years, the question of whether Iranians wanted foreign help has sharply divided opponents of the Islamic Republic. For most, the answer was firmly no—memories of the 1953 CIA-backed coup that overthrew Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh, and decades of American sanctions and hostility, made foreign intervention deeply suspect.

That has changed.

Arash Azizi, writing in The Atlantic on January 20—before the full scale of the massacre was known—interviewed about a dozen Iranians. “First, most of those I spoke with favored one form or another of intervention by the United States,” he wrote. “Second, many favored what would have been unthinkable not long ago: for Trump to take out Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei.”

That was when the world believed 5,000-12,000 had been killed.

Now we know more than 36,500 were slaughtered in 48 hours. Security forces executed wounded patients in hospitals. Chemical weapons were deployed. A teenage protester survived by hiding motionless in a body bag for three days while summary executions continued around him.

Melika, a 21-year-old Iranian student in Europe who had strongly opposed the Israeli-American attacks on Iran in June, told Azizi: “I’d frankly like Trump to kill Khamenei. I am worried about a broader war that would hurt Iran’s infrastructure. I wouldn’t like for us to become an Iraq, happy that Saddam is gone but becoming worse. But the Islamic Republic has to pay a price.”

Shahrzad, who participated in protests and lost two friends along with nine others in her broader circle, said: “The people of Iran would like to see a Venezuela-style operation, even if it seems like a strange demand for Iran.”

The shift isn’t limited to young activists. Nobel Peace Prize laureate Shirin Ebadi, celebrated filmmaker Mohsen Makhmalbaf, scholar Mohammad Javad Akbarin, and four others issued an open letter calling for “humanitarian intervention.”

Conservative commentator Mark Levin told Iran International on January 23: “Most revolutions, including America’s own, needed outside help. What is happening in Iran is a counter-revolution against a regime that rules by force and fear.”

Venezuelan opposition leader Maria Corina Machado, fresh from Trump’s operation to capture Maduro, told Iranian activist Masih Alinejad: “When you’re dealing with criminals, the only way they will leave is when the cost of staying in power is higher than the cost of leaving.”

Not everyone agrees. But the emergence of any significant constituency for military intervention represents a seismic shift—driven entirely by the regime’s willingness to massacre civilians by the tens of thousands.

“Until a few years ago, millions of Iranians repeatedly voted in the country’s mostly unfree elections, hoping that regime insiders could pave a path for reform,” Azizi wrote. “They wouldn’t have dreamed of demanding change from American bombers or drones, or of asking the U.S. president to please kill the supreme leader for them.”

“Khamenei’s obstinate refusal to effect any meaningful change and his bloody massacre of civilians in droves have brought Iranians to this point. The grand ayatollah has brought them only death, disaster, and misery, and so they are desperately seeking a way out—by any means necessary.”

That was written before we knew that “droves” meant 36,500+ in 48 hours.

Trump’s Choice

The USS Abraham Lincoln carrier strike group arrived in the Middle East on Monday, January 26, accompanied by guided missile destroyers. The carrier is now positioned to strike Iran.

Ambrey, a private security firm, issued an assessment on January 27 stating the United States “has positioned sufficient military capability to conduct kinetic operations against Iran while maintaining the ability to defend itself and regional allies from reciprocal action.”

Senator Lindsey Graham met with Reza Pahlavi on January 12, both men holding matching black “Make Iran Great Again” baseball caps. After the meeting, Graham told reporters the U.S. should attack the Iranian regime “sooner rather than later.”

“The goal is to end the regime,” Graham said. “They may stop killing them today, but if they’re in charge next month, they’ll kill them then.”

Graham has spoken with Trump “in recent days” about Iran and expects the president to fulfill his promise to help Iranian protesters. The South Carolina senator, one of Trump’s closest allies on foreign policy, has become the most prominent voice pushing for military action.

He’s not alone. A faction within Trump’s administration and Republican circles views the intelligence assessments of Iran’s weakness—combined with the regime’s industrial-scale massacre of 36,500+ civilians—as a closing window of opportunity.

The argument is straightforward: Iran’s regime is at its most vulnerable point since 1979. The protests revealed the depth of popular anger. The IRGC leadership was wiped out in June and replaced with commanders willing to execute tens of thousands of civilians in 48 hours. The economy is collapsing. Regional proxies are shattered. Khamenei is 86 years old and hiding in an underground bunker.

More importantly: a regime that executes 36,500+ civilians in two days, shoots wounded patients in hospitals, and deploys chemical weapons has crossed lines that change the moral calculus of intervention.

Strike now, this faction argues, and the regime might actually fall. Wait, and Iran rebuilds its strength, Khamenei’s succession solidifies a new leadership willing to use the same tactics, and the opportunity passes.

The carrier strike group is now in theater with overwhelming combat power. F-15E Strike Eagles operate from bases throughout the Middle East. Air defense systems protect U.S. bases and Israel from potential Iranian retaliation. The Pentagon has positioned enough firepower to conduct sustained operations, not just symbolic strikes.

A second aircraft carrier, the USS George H.W. Bush, is preparing to deploy from the Atlantic coast, with pilots conducting landing qualifications in recent days. Its destination remains unclear, but the timing suggests it could provide follow-on capability if the Abraham Lincoln conducts strikes—or relieve it for extended operations in the region.

Pentagon officials told The Washington Post that additional military assets are still moving into position and won’t all be in theater for at least another week. The deliberations could extend even longer—or Trump could order strikes tonight.

The carrier isn’t theater. It’s capability, in position, waiting for a decision.

The Risks

His track record suggests caution. Trump’s established pattern has been to threaten extensively, position forces dramatically, then find an off-ramp. In this case, the off-ramp was Iran’s declaration that “the sedition is over” and Trump’s claim that his threats prevented 830 executions—a claim Iran’s prosecutor general called “completely false.”

But this time may be different. The intelligence briefings emphasize opportunity that won’t last. Graham and other hawks are pushing hard. The scale of killing—36,500+ in 48 hours according to Iran’s own documents—fundamentally changes the moral equation. And Trump has already demonstrated willingness to strike Iran once, in June 2025, when U.S. bombers hit three nuclear sites.

The question isn’t whether Trump has the capability. It’s whether Graham’s faction can convince him that a regime willing to execute 36,500+ civilians in two days cannot be allowed to survive.

A billboard in Tehran depicts strikes on an American aircraft carrier.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

The risks are real. A massive billboard unfurled in Tehran’s Enghelab Square on January 26 depicted a bloodied aircraft carrier in American flag colors, with a slogan in Persian and English: “If you sow the wind, you will reap the whirlwind.”

Iran’s defense and foreign ministries vowed that any U.S. attack would trigger “more decisive and more painful” retaliation than in June. At the United Nations late Monday, Iran’s ambassador told the Security Council that Trump’s repeated threats to use military force “are neither ambiguous nor misinterpreted.”

Iranian-backed militias across the region issued their own warnings today. Ahmad “Abu Hussein” al-Hamidawi, leader of Iraq’s Kataib Hezbollah militia, threatened: “The war on the (Islamic) Republic will not be a picnic; rather, you will taste the bitterest forms of death, and nothing will remain of you in our region.”

Yemen’s Houthi rebels warned they could resume attacks on shipping in the Red Sea if the U.S. strikes Iran.

Hezbollah in Lebanon refused to specify its response but said it is preparing for “possible aggression.”

Regional allies nervously urged restraint. Gulf Arab states have signaled they want to stay out of any attack despite hosting American military personnel and bases. The United Arab Emirates reaffirmed Monday that it would not allow “its airspace, territory, or waters to be used in any hostile military actions against Iran.”

This complicates U.S. military options. The carrier provides strike capability without requiring Gulf state cooperation, but limits the range and scale of potential operations.

What Comes Next

Three scenarios now dominate speculation about Iran’s future—but the January 8-9 massacre has fundamentally altered the calculations for each.

Scenario One: Stagnation

The Islamic Republic survives, Khamenei remains in power, and the cycle of repression continues. The regime has demonstrated extraordinary capacity to project and absorb pain—including willingness to execute 36,500+ civilians in 48 hours.

But the cost of survival grows steeper each time. Iran’s economy cannot recover under current sanctions. Its regional influence is shattered. Its legitimacy among its own population evaporated the moment security forces began shooting wounded patients in hospitals.

And Khamenei, 86 years old and now hiding in an underground bunker according to sources who spoke to Iran International, cannot live forever.

“This is not a sustainable situation where you wait for an elderly leader to die to put things back in order,” said Ali Vaez, drawing comparisons to the final years of Mao Zedong in China or Leonid Brezhnev in the Soviet Union. “The problem is the country doesn’t have the time to wait him out.”

Scenario Two: Internal Transformation

Khamenei’s succession—whenever it comes—could present an opportunity for change from within.

But the IRGC commanders who executed 36,500+ people in 48 hours are not reformers. They are men who have crossed lines that can never be uncrossed. Their survival depends on the system’s survival. They will fight to the death to preserve it.

The Revolutionary Guards increasingly dominate not just security, but media, the economy, oil exports, seaports, intelligence, and even an air force. They control everything required to seize power.

One possibility: a figure favored by the Revolutionary Guards takes power after Khamenei’s death, transforming Iran from a theocracy to a military-dominated state like Pakistan or Egypt.

Another, more ominous scenario: a faction of the Revolutionary Guards, fearful of American-backed regime change, moves against Khamenei first. “While a military coup was unthinkable a few years ago,” Ostovar said, “it’s becoming increasingly likely now because of all the pressures building on the regime.”

Scenario Three: Violent Collapse

The Islamic Republic falls, but what replaces it remains uncertain—and potentially catastrophic.

Michael Doran of the Hudson Institute warns that Iran may not be the cohesive nation it appears. Persians constitute only 47 percent of the population. Azerbaijanis account for roughly 23 percent, concentrated in the northwest. Kurds, Arabs, Baluch, and Turkmen populate other border regions.

If the regime collapses, Iran could fragment like Yugoslavia in the 1990s. Azerbaijan—now a NATO-standard military power—might feel compelled to intervene to protect ethnic Azerbaijanis. Turkey could follow.

The nightmare scenario: Khamenei falls but the IRGC survives, sheds its clerical skin, and reemerges as enforcers for a Persian nationalist dictatorship—welcoming Reza Pahlavi as a symbolic figurehead while retaining real power.

But all three scenarios now operate in the shadow of January 8-9. A regime that executes 36,500+ civilians in 48 hours, shoots wounded patients in hospitals, and deploys chemical weapons has revealed itself as something beyond authoritarian. It has revealed itself as genocidal.

February 17

One date looms: February 17, forty days after the peak of the violence.

In Shiite Muslim tradition, families hold memorial services forty days after a death. These gatherings have historically become flashpoints for renewed demonstrations.

That’s why authorities are forcing families to bury their dead in remote locations, requiring silence, monitoring burials, and charging impossible sums to retrieve bodies. They’re trying to prevent the forty-day memorial services from becoming mass demonstrations.

But satellite imagery shows large numbers of cars appearing daily at Behesht-e Zahra cemetery’s southern reaches, where victims are buried. Videos show mourners chanting “Death to Khamenei” at burial sites despite security presence.

If Trump intends to act, February 17 would provide perfect cover: Iranians returning to the streets on their own, giving him a pretext for the intervention he promised.

Or it could pass quietly, with mourners intimidated into silence by the memory of 36,500+ killed in 48 hours and the regime’s threats.

Either way, the carrier will still be there.

Breaking: “A Massive Armada”

As this essay was being finalized on January 28, Trump escalated dramatically.

“A massive Armada is heading to Iran. It is moving quickly, with great power, enthusiasm, and purpose,” Trump posted on Truth Social Wednesday morning. “It is ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary.”

The language marks a significant escalation from previous threats. Trump explicitly invoked the Venezuela operation: “Like with Venezuela, it is ready to rapidly fulfill its mission.” The message was clear—he has demonstrated willingness to capture foreign leaders, and Iran should take the threat seriously.

Trump demanded Iran “Come to the Table” to negotiate “a fair and equitable deal – NO NUCLEAR WEAPONS.” Then came the ultimatum: “Time is running out, it is truly of the essence! As I told Iran once before, MAKE A DEAL! They didn’t, and there was ‘Operation Midnight Hammer,’ a major destruction of Iran. The next attack will be far worse!“

Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi responded within hours, telling state media he had no recent contact with U.S. envoy Steve Witkoff and had made no request for negotiations.

“Our stance is clear: Negotiations don’t go along with threats, and talks can only take place when there are no longer menaces and excessive demands,” Araghchi said.

The exchange eliminates the most likely off-ramp. Iran is refusing to negotiate under threat. Trump is threatening attack if Iran doesn’t negotiate. Both sides are locked into positions that make conflict increasingly probable.

Regional tensions accelerated immediately. Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps warned that neighboring countries would be “considered hostile” if their territory is used during any U.S. attack. Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates have already signaled they will not allow their airspace to be used.

Egypt’s foreign minister spoke separately with both Araghchi and Witkoff on Wednesday, trying to “work toward achieving calm, in order to avoid the region slipping into new cycles of instability.”

Turkey’s Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan told Al Jazeera that “Iran is ready to negotiate,” directly contradicting Araghchi’s statement—suggesting either internal Iranian divisions or diplomatic miscommunication at a moment when clarity is essential.

The USS Abraham Lincoln is in position. The USS George H.W. Bush is preparing to deploy. Additional military assets continue flowing to the region. And this morning, Trump threatened an attack “far worse” than the June operation that killed 430 people.

The carrier isn’t theater. The threats aren’t posturing. Trump is escalating toward a decision point, not backing away from one.

The Question That Remains

Two arguments compete for Trump’s decision.

The Hawks’ Case:

Lindsey Graham and his faction present straightforward moral logic: A regime that executes 36,500+ civilians in 48 hours, shoots wounded patients in hospitals, deploys chemical weapons, and orders “victory through terror” cannot be allowed to survive.

The intelligence confirms the regime is at its weakest point since 1979. The protests revealed massive popular opposition. The IRGC’s new commanders are brutal but inexperienced. The economy has collapsed. Regional proxies are shattered. Khamenei is 86 and hiding in a bunker.

Strike now—sustained operations targeting the IRGC command structure, missile capabilities, and possibly Khamenei himself—and the regime might fall. Or at minimum, fracture enough that internal forces can finish the job.

The Venezuelan precedent reinforces this argument. Trump’s willingness to capture Maduro showed he’ll act decisively when opportunity presents. Iran is a far bigger prize. The domestic politics favor action—Americans won’t protest defending protesters who were massacred by the tens of thousands.

And the moral argument has changed. This isn’t intervention to support protesters anymore. This is accountability for what may be the largest mass killing of civilians in modern history.

The Cautionary Case:

But Trump’s advisers warn of unpredictable consequences. Most U.S. interventions to exact justice on foreign tyrants have ended poorly. No silver bullet will cleanly depose Tehran’s leaders and peacefully transition Iran to stable democracy.

Even successful regime change could trigger ethnic fragmentation—Iran as Yugoslavia, not Iran as post-communist Poland. Regional allies in the Gulf fear destabilization. And Iran, despite its weakness, still has capacity for retaliation that could hit U.S. bases and drag Israel into expanded war.

The precedent Trump set in June argues against the hawks. Operation Midnight Hammer struck nuclear facilities—a clear U.S. security interest with international legitimacy. Striking the IRGC and Khamenei is direct intervention in domestic politics, exactly what Trump threatened to do and then didn’t.

What Decides?

The machinery of war is in motion. The USS Abraham Lincoln arrived in the Middle East on January 26 with overwhelming strike capability. The USS George H.W. Bush prepares to deploy. F-15Es fly from regional bases. Intelligence briefings emphasize opportunity. Lindsey Graham whispers that the window is closing.

Iranians call for help—many specifically asking Trump to kill Khamenei.

The death toll reached 36,500+ according to classified IRGC documents. Iran’s currency collapsed to a record low today. The massacre happened three weeks ago, but its consequences accelerate daily.

Thousands believed Trump’s promises and died while he watched. If he acts now, he’s avenging a massacre he allowed to happen. If he doesn’t act, he confirms the betrayal—but avoids the unpredictable consequences of another Middle East war.

But the scale of killing has changed the equation. This isn’t suppressing protests. According to the regime’s own classified documents, more than 36,500 people were executed in 48 hours. Security forces shot wounded patients in hospitals. Chemical weapons were deployed. A regime willing to commit atrocities at this scale has crossed lines that fundamentally alter the moral calculus of intervention.

The carrier isn’t theater. It’s capability, in position, waiting for Trump to choose between Graham’s accountability and his advisers’ warnings.

February 17 may force that choice.

Either way, as journalist Masih Alinejad wrote: “We are left with graveyards.”
