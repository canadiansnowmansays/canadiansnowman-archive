The Democratic Promise

The Complicated Cases

Part 3: Gates and the Later Jobs

March 23, 2026

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon

The Problem with Heroes and Villains

The story this series tells would be simpler if every figure in it could be cleanly categorized. Builders who kept the faith. Betrayers who broke it. But two of the most consequential figures in the history of personal computing refuse to fit neatly into either box, and intellectual honesty requires dealing with them on their own terms.

Bill Gates and Steve Jobs — the two men who did more than anyone else to put computers into the hands of ordinary people — both advanced and undermined the democratic promise, sometimes simultaneously, sometimes sequentially, and sometimes in ways that remain genuinely difficult to judge even with the benefit of decades of hindsight.

Their stories are instructive precisely because they are complicated. The democratic promise was never a simple binary between good and evil. It was a tension — between openness and control, between distribution and concentration, between mission and money — and Gates and Jobs lived that tension more fully than anyone.

Gates: The Monopolist Who Became a Philanthropist

Bill Gates’s relationship to the democratic promise begins with a letter.

In February 1976 — seven years before Jobs’s Aspen speech — a twenty-year-old Gates published “An Open Letter to Hobbyists” in the Altair Users Newsletter. The hobbyist computing community had been freely sharing software, copying programs, and distributing them without payment. Gates was furious. His company, then called Micro-Soft, had written a BASIC interpreter for the Altair 8800, and hobbyists were copying it without paying.

“As the majority of hobbyists must be aware, most of you steal your software,” Gates wrote. “Who can afford to do professional work for nothing?”

This letter is often cited as the beginning of the proprietary software movement — the philosophical opposite of Richard Stallman’s free software vision. Where Stallman would argue that software should be shared because sharing builds capability, Gates argued that software should be owned because ownership funds development. Where the open-source builders saw software as a commons, Gates saw it as a product.

Both arguments had merit. And both had consequences.

Gates’s proprietary model built Microsoft into the most powerful technology company on Earth. Windows became the operating system on more than ninety percent of personal computers worldwide. Microsoft Office became the standard suite for business productivity. Internet Explorer became the dominant web browser. Gates became, for a time, the richest person in the world.

And here is the complication: Microsoft’s dominance genuinely advanced the democratic promise in one crucial respect. By creating a standard operating system that ran on cheap, commodity hardware from dozens of manufacturers, Windows made personal computing affordable for hundreds of millions of people who could never have afforded an Apple product. The IBM PC and its clones, running Microsoft software, were the fractional horsepower motors that actually reached the masses. Not elegant, not beautiful, not intuitive — but cheap, ubiquitous, and functional.

Jobs built the computer for the privileged few. Gates built the platform for the many. If the democratic promise is measured by how many people got access to computing power, Gates delivered more than Jobs ever did.

But Gates delivered access while concentrating control. The antitrust case that the United States Department of Justice brought against Microsoft in 1998 documented a pattern of behavior that was the precise opposite of the open, distributed vision that Jobs had articulated and that Berners-Lee and Torvalds had built. Microsoft used its operating system monopoly to crush competitors, bundling Internet Explorer with Windows to destroy Netscape, threatening PC manufacturers who installed competing software, and leveraging its market position to extend its dominance into adjacent markets.

The judge’s finding of fact was blunt: Microsoft had maintained its monopoly through anticompetitive means. The company had used its power not to distribute capability but to concentrate control — not to empower users but to eliminate their choices.

Gates was a democratizer of access and a monopolist of control, simultaneously. He put a computer on every desk and then made sure that computer ran his software, used his browser, connected to his services, and operated within his ecosystem. He distributed the hardware while enclosing the software.

This is the Gates paradox, and it has never been fully resolved. His defenders argue that standardization itself was a form of democratization — a common platform that enabled interoperability and lowered costs. His critics argue that monopoly is monopoly regardless of the product’s price point, and that Microsoft’s dominance stifled innovation for a generation.

Both are right. That is what makes Gates a complicated case.

The Second Act

And then Gates did something that no other technology oligarch has done before or since. He left.

In 2008, Gates stepped down from his day-to-day role at Microsoft and devoted himself full-time to the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, which he and his then-wife had established in 2000. The foundation became the largest private charitable foundation in the world, with an endowment exceeding fifty billion dollars, focused on global health, poverty reduction, and education.

The scale of the giving is staggering. The Gates Foundation has spent billions fighting malaria, polio, tuberculosis, and HIV/AIDS. It has funded vaccine development and distribution across the developing world. It has invested in agricultural research to improve crop yields in Sub-Saharan Africa. By any reasonable accounting, Gates’s philanthropy has saved millions of lives — not metaphorically, but literally, measurably, documentably.

In the context of this series, the philanthropy raises a question that has no clean answer: does giving away the fortune redeem the means by which it was accumulated?

Gates built his wealth through monopolistic practices that concentrated power, stifled competition, and undermined the open computing ecosystem that the builders had created. He then used that wealth to distribute capability — not computing capability, but human capability: health, education, agricultural productivity — on a global scale that no open-source project could match.

The monopolist became the philanthropist. The man who enclosed the software commons used the proceeds to invest in the human commons. It is not hypocrisy, exactly. It is something more complicated: a man who concentrated power in one domain and distributed it in another, who undermined the democratic promise in technology and advanced it in global development, who was both the problem and — partially, imperfectly — the solution.

No other figure in this series will present this particular challenge. The betrayers who follow in Parts 4 and 5 are not complicated. They are straightforward.

Jobs: The Return

Jobs with iPhone 4, 2010: The prophet holds the walled garden in his hand — and smiles. Photo: Matthew Yohe, Wikimedia Commons, 2010. CC BY-SA 3.0.

Steve Jobs’s relationship to his own democratic promise is, if anything, more complicated than Gates’s.

In 1985, two years after the Aspen speech, Jobs was forced out of Apple in a boardroom coup. He spent the next twelve years in the wilderness — running NeXT Computer, buying Pixar, learning, evolving, and nursing a grievance that would fuel one of the most remarkable comeback stories in business history.

When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company was ninety days from bankruptcy. What he built over the next fourteen years — the iMac, the iPod, iTunes, the iPhone, the iPad, the App Store, the Apple retail experience — transformed Apple into the most valuable company in the world and reshaped the daily life of billions of people.

But the Jobs who returned to Apple was not the same man who had stood in Aspen in 1983. The missionary had become a perfectionist. The democrat had become a monarch. The man who said “distribute that intelligence” built the most controlled computing ecosystem in history.

The iPhone, which fulfilled Jobs’s 1983 prediction of a great computer in a book with a radio link, was a masterpiece of design and a monument to centralized control. Users could not install software that Apple had not approved. They could not modify the operating system. They could not replace the battery. They could not use the device on a carrier Apple had not sanctioned. Every app passed through Apple’s review process. Every purchase went through Apple’s payment system. Every piece of content was subject to Apple’s content guidelines.

The App Store, which fulfilled Jobs’s prediction of a “software radio station,” was both a revolutionary distribution platform and a toll booth. Apple took thirty percent of every transaction — a commission that developers had no power to negotiate and no ability to avoid. The “software radio station” turned out to be a company store.

Jobs would have argued — and did argue — that this control was necessary for quality. The closed ecosystem ensured security, reliability, and a consistent user experience. Open systems, Jobs believed, produced mediocrity. Closed systems produced excellence. And in terms of product quality, he was largely right. The iPhone was, and remains, a superior product in many respects precisely because Apple controls the entire stack from hardware to software to services.

But quality and democracy are not the same thing. A benevolent monarch can build beautiful palaces. That does not make the kingdom a republic.

The Walled Garden

The term “walled garden” entered the technology lexicon to describe what Apple built under Jobs’s second tenure. It is a revealing metaphor. A walled garden is beautiful. It is carefully tended. It is pleasant to inhabit. And you cannot leave it without climbing the wall.

Apple’s walled garden solved many of the problems that plagued the open computing ecosystem. No malware. No fragmentation. No confusing driver installations. No registry errors. For hundreds of millions of people who wanted a computer that simply worked — the people Jobs had described in Aspen, who should be able to learn to use the device in twenty minutes — the walled garden was a genuine gift.

But the wall was real. And what it enclosed was not just a superior user experience. It was user data, user choice, user agency. Apple decided what software you could run, what content you could access, what repairs you could make to your own device. The democratic promise — distribute the intelligence, empower the individual — had been replaced by a different promise: trust us, and we will take care of you.

This is paternalism, not democracy. Benevolent paternalism, perhaps. Effective paternalism, certainly. But paternalism nonetheless.

Jobs’s defenders will note that users chose Apple products voluntarily, that alternatives existed, that the market provided options. This is true. But it misses the deeper point. Jobs’s 1983 vision was not about consumer choice among competing products. It was about distributing power — the ability to process information, to communicate, to create, to participate in civic life — without dependency on a central authority. The iPhone distributed capability while creating dependency. It empowered the user in a thousand ways while ensuring that empowerment flowed through Apple’s infrastructure, on Apple’s terms, subject to Apple’s control.

The twenty-eight-year-old Jobs would have recognized the problem immediately. It was the mainframe with a better user interface. The belt-and-pulley system with a touchscreen. Fractional horsepower computing — but with Apple holding the power switch.

The Judgment

How do you judge these two men against the standard Jobs himself set in 1983?

Gates failed the democratic promise in technology and partially redeemed himself in philanthropy. He concentrated power in software and distributed it in global health. The net accounting depends on what you value more — the damage done by a computing monopoly that lasted two decades, or the lives saved by a philanthropic enterprise that may continue for a century. Reasonable people will disagree.

Jobs articulated the democratic promise more clearly than anyone, built tools that fulfilled his own predictions with breathtaking precision, and then enclosed those tools in a system of control that contradicted the values he had articulated. He democratized access to computing while centralizing control of the computing experience. He was both the prophet and the first apostate.

Neither man is the villain of this series. The villains are coming. What Gates and Jobs demonstrate is something more subtle and perhaps more important: that the democratic promise is difficult to keep even for people who sincerely believe in it. The pressures of business — the need for revenue, the pursuit of quality, the logic of competitive advantage — push relentlessly toward concentration. Staying open, staying distributed, staying democratic requires active resistance against the gravitational pull of power.

The builders in Part 2 — Berners-Lee, Torvalds, Wales — resisted that pull by refusing to monetize. They kept the promise by opting out of the market. Gates and Jobs stayed in the market and the market bent them. Not entirely. Not uniformly. But enough.

The figures who follow in the next three essays did not bend. They broke. They took the language of the democratic promise — connect, organize, empower, liberate — and used it as marketing copy for the largest concentration of private power in human history.

The complicated cases are over. What comes next is straightforward.
