Part 3

Barack Obama: The Reform Revolution

December 9, 2025

When Volume Served Policy, Not Self-Interest

Barack Obama speaks as he tours the El Reno federal correctional institution in El Reno, Oklahoma, the first visit to a federal prison by a sitting US president. Photograph: Saul Loeb/AFP/Getty Images

If George W. Bush’s clemency record is defined by restraint, Barack Obama’s is defined by reform. The contrast could not be more stark.

The Numbers: Unprecedented Scale

Barack Obama granted 1,927 clemency actions across his eight years:

  • 1,715 commutations

  • 212 pardons

Obama granted more commutations than the previous thirteen presidents combined. His 1,715 commutations dwarf every modern president:

  • Both Bushes: 14 commutations total

  • Bill Clinton: 61 commutations

  • Ronald Reagan: 13 commutations

  • Jimmy Carter: 29 commutations

But this raw number obscures the crucial question: Whom did Obama’s clemency serve?

The Criminal Justice Reform Initiative

Obama came into office with an explicit agenda to address mass incarceration, particularly for non-violent drug offenses. The crack cocaine sentencing disparity—where crack offenses (more common in Black communities) carried 100-times-harsher penalties than powder cocaine offenses (more common in white communities)—had created a generation of people serving decades for crimes that, if committed later, would have resulted in far shorter punishment.

In 2010, Obama signed the Fair Sentencing Act, reducing the crack-to-powder disparity from 100:1 to 18:1. But the law didn’t apply retroactively, leaving thousands serving sentences under the old, unjust regime.

Obama used his clemency power to address this. In 2014, he announced Clemency Project 2014, a program that would systematically review petitions from federal inmates serving sentences for non-violent drug crimes who would have received shorter sentences under current law.

The results were dramatic:

2016 Alone: Obama commuted 1,176 sentences—more than any president in a single year. On his final full day in office (January 19, 2017), Obama commuted 330 sentences in a single day.

The Typical Case: A non-violent drug offender, often Black or Hispanic, serving 20+ years under mandatory minimums, with good prison conduct and substantial time already served. These weren’t famous people or political allies. They were strangers most Americans have never heard of.

Applying Our Framework to Drug Commutations

1. Connection to President: None. Strangers whose only connection was meeting program criteria.

2. Nature of Crime: Non-violent drug offenses, predominantly crack cocaine distribution.

3. Victim Impact: Drug crimes have diffuse victims—communities, families, individuals struggling with addiction. Direct harm is real but difficult to quantify.

4. Stage of Justice: After substantial time served—typically 10-20+ years. Not early releases.

5. Process: Systematic, transparent through Clemency Project 2014. Thousands of petitions reviewed by volunteer lawyers. Public criteria. DOJ review.

6. Timing: Throughout second term, with acceleration toward end—traditional pattern.

7. Justification: Clear policy rationale: sentences unjust under current standards, imposed under laws since revised, with substantial time served.

The Legitimacy of Drug Commutations

The case for legitimacy is strong:

  • Proportionality: Sentences were excessive by current standards

  • Policy Consistency: Congress had agreed crack-powder disparity was unjust

  • Rehabilitation Evidence: Most recipients had good prison records and substantial time served

  • Systematic Process: Transparent criteria, legal review, consistent application

  • No Self-Interest: These were strangers; Obama gained no personal benefit beyond advancing policy

Critics argued jury verdicts should be respected, that commutations might undermine deterrence, and that changes should come through legislation. But even critics generally acknowledged Obama was using clemency for its traditionally recognized purpose of addressing unjust sentences.

This is a critical distinction that will become stark when we compare it to Trump’s pattern.

Chelsea Manning: The Controversial Exception

While bulk of Obama’s clemency went to unknown drug offenders, several high-profile cases generated controversy. The most contentious: Chelsea Manning.

The Crime and Conviction

In 2010, Chelsea Manning (then Bradley Manning), a U.S. Army intelligence analyst, transmitted to WikiLeaks approximately 700,000 classified documents—one of the largest leaks in American history.

The materials included battlefield reports, diplomatic cables, and the “Collateral Murder” video showing a 2007 U.S. helicopter attack killing civilians in Baghdad.

Manning was convicted in August 2013 of 17 charges, including Espionage Act violations. She was sentenced to 35 years—the longest sentence ever for a leak to media.

The Humanitarian Complication

During incarceration, Manning announced she was transgender and began transitioning while imprisoned in an all-male military facility. The military initially denied hormone therapy. Manning attempted suicide twice in 2016, citing conditions of confinement and denial of adequate treatment for gender dysphoria.

Manning’s lawyers argued she was at serious risk if she remained incarcerated for the full 35-year term.

Obama’s Decision

On January 17, 2017—three days before leaving office—Obama commuted most of Manning’s sentence. Manning would be released May 17, 2017, after serving approximately seven years.

This was not a pardon. Manning’s convictions remained. The commutation simply reduced the sentence from 35 years to time served plus four months.

Senior officials stated Obama’s decision was based on:

  • Manning had served almost seven years—longer than anyone else convicted of leaking to media

  • Manning had accepted responsibility and expressed remorse

  • The 35-year sentence was disproportionate compared to similar cases

  • Manning’s treatment in custody raised humanitarian concerns

  • Manning’s mental health and suicide risk were serious

The Reaction

Critics:

  • House Speaker Paul Ryan: “Chelsea Manning’s treachery put American lives at risk and exposed some of our nation’s most sensitive secrets.”

  • Senator John McCain: Called it “a grave mistake that may encourage further acts of espionage.”

  • Defense Secretary Ash Carter reportedly opposed the commutation.

Supporters:

  • ACLU: “Chelsea has been subjected to long stretches of solitary confinement... President Obama was right to commute her sentence.”

  • Amnesty International: “Chelsea Manning exposed serious abuses.”

Applying Our Framework

1. Connection: None. Low-level Army analyst with zero connection to Obama.

2. Nature of Crime: Espionage Act violations. Serious national security crimes that potentially endangered lives and damaged U.S. relationships.

3. Victim Impact: National security harm uncertain but potentially severe. Government argued leaks damaged security; defenders argued no specific casualties proven.

4. Stage of Justice: After serving 7 years of 35-year sentence. Substantial time but less than one-fifth of sentence.

5. Process: Some DOJ review, but Defense Secretary opposed. Not typical clemency case.

6. Timing: Final days—announced January 17, 2017, three days before Trump’s inauguration.

7. Justification: Sentence disproportionality, time served, humanitarian concerns, acceptance of responsibility.

Was It Justified?

This case is genuinely difficult. Reasonable people can disagree:

Arguments for:

  • Proportionality: 35 years dramatically longer than comparable leak cases. CIA Director Petraeus received probation for sharing classified information and lying to FBI.

  • Humanitarian: Manning’s treatment and serious suicide risk created compelling grounds.

  • No Self-Interest: Obama gained nothing politically—it was controversial and occurred as he left office.

Arguments Against:

  • Severity: One of largest leaks in history—700,000 documents.

  • National Security: Even if no specific deaths resulted, damage to intelligence relationships was real.

  • Deterrence: Commuting sentence might encourage other leakers.

The Key Distinction

Whatever one thinks of Manning commutation’s wisdom, it wasn’t self-serving. Manning had no connection to Obama. It didn’t protect Obama from investigation or legal jeopardy. It didn’t reward a political ally or donor.

It was a policy decision about proportional punishment and humanitarian concerns. One can disagree without believing it corrupted clemency power.

Other Controversial Cases

Oscar López Rivera: Obama commuted the sentence of López Rivera, a FALN member (Puerto Rican independence group) responsible for 100+ bombings in the 1970s-80s. After serving 36 years of 55-year sentence (plus 15 for escape), Obama commuted his sentence January 17, 2017.

Critics noted López Rivera never renounced violence. Supporters argued 36 years was sufficient for crimes committed decades earlier.

General James Cartwright: Obama pardoned retired four-star Marine General Cartwright, former vice chairman of Joint Chiefs, who pleaded guilty to making false statements to FBI during leak investigation about Stuxnet cyberattack on Iran.

This was controversial because Cartwright was senior official who lied to investigators. However, Cartwright had already pleaded guilty, cooperated, and expressed remorse. The pardon came after his case was resolved.

What Obama’s Record Reveals

Obama’s clemency approach reveals several patterns:

Systematic Reform Focus: The overwhelming majority (1,600+ of 1,927 cases) went to non-violent drug offenders with no connection to him. Policy-driven, not self-serving.

Transparency and Process: Clemency Project 2014 had public criteria, volunteer lawyer review, systematic processing. No lobbying industry, no cash-for-clemency schemes, no political loyalty test.

Controversial but Principled Exceptions: Cases like Manning and López Rivera generated criticism, but were justified on humanitarian and proportionality grounds. Not political allies being protected.

No Self-Protection Pattern: Obama didn’t systematically pardon associates, campaign officials, or people who could implicate him. No pattern of protecting the administration emerged.

Traditional Timing: Bulk came in second term, ramping up toward end—traditional pattern.

No Family Favoritism: Obama pardoned no family members.

Victim Consideration: Because most commutations went to drug offenders who served substantial time, Obama’s clemency didn’t systematically erase restitution to identifiable fraud or theft victims.

Bush vs. Obama: The Comparison

Bush and Obama represent opposite ends of clemency spectrum within traditional boundaries:

Bush: Extreme Restraint

  • 200 total over 8 years

  • Resisted pressure from powerful allies

  • Traditional practice of pardoning after completed sentences

  • Controversial Libby commutation but otherwise unremarkable

Obama: Extensive Reform

  • 1,927 total over 8 years

  • Focused on systemic criminal justice issues

  • Transparent process for systematic review

  • Controversial high-profile cases but justified on principle

What Both Shared: Neither created clemency system primarily serving personal or political interests. Neither systematically pardoned campaign associates or co-conspirators. Neither created pardons-for-profit industry. Neither erased billions in victim restitution. Neither pardoned family members.

Their approaches differed dramatically—one minimalist, one expansive—but both largely remained within traditional boundaries of what clemency should accomplish.

The Baseline Established

Bush’s restraint and Obama’s reform establish what clemency looks like when used for traditional purposes: mercy, proportionality, criminal justice policy, humanitarian concerns.

This is the baseline. When we examine Biden and Trump, we’ll measure against these standards:

  • Did clemency primarily serve policy goals or personal interests?

  • Was process transparent and systematic, or opaque and transactional?

  • Were controversial cases isolated exceptions or systematic patterns?

  • Did pardons protect the president’s associates and co-conspirators?

  • Was family given special treatment?

  • Were victims’ interests considered or erased?

Bush showed you can use clemency sparingly and resist powerful pressure. Obama showed you can use it extensively without corrupting it. Both demonstrated that presidential clemency can serve legitimate purposes even when controversial.

Now we examine what happens when these boundaries collapse.

Coming Next: Part 4 - “The Biden Dilemma: When Family Becomes Policy”

We’ll confront a president who used clemency appropriately for policy reform but egregiously for family protection—breaking explicit promises and using unprecedented language covering eleven years of potential crimes.
