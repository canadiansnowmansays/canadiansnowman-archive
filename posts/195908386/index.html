The Oakland Transcript

The Cross

Day 2 — April 29, 2026

April 30, 2026

Elon Musk enters the courthouse in Oakland, Calif., on Wednesday for his second day of testimony.Credit...Jason Henry for The New York Times

Ronald V. Dellums Federal Courthouse, Oakland, California

A companion series to The Asimov Gap, CanadianSnowmanSays

On the second morning of his testimony, Elon Musk arrived at the courthouse through the basement again. So did Sam Altman — who had been absent for Day 1 but took his seat today beside Greg Brockman, occasionally furrowing his brow at the proceedings. Outside, a new group had joined the lawyers and reporters in the queue: AI safety researchers, there to watch what this trial might mean for the future of the technology they study. The gallery also attracted what appeared to be fans of Musk — young people who had come to watch the world’s richest man testify. Someone was caught trying to photograph him as he left the courtroom. The judge threatened to shut down video access to the overflow room if it happened again.

OpenAI’s president, Greg Brockman, enters the courthouse on Wednesday. He sat behind OpenAI’s lawyers as Mr. Musk testified.Credit...Jason Henry for The New York Times

Inside, Musk resumed his direct examination before the cross began. He described the moment Microsoft’s $10 billion investment landed — texting Altman “What the hell is going on?” and calling it a “bait and switch.” He described the equity offer that followed as feeling “like a bribe.” He said he continued donating to OpenAI after he left and receiving updates on its progress, but that a profit cap and an AGI dissolution clause provided reassurance that the for-profit arm would be kept in check. He acknowledged that xAI, his own AI company, was founded as a traditional for-profit. That was not a problem, he said. “A problem occurs when you create a nonprofit and turn it into a for-profit.”

Then William Savitt stood up.

The Cross-Examination

Savitt opened by showing Musk a social media post he had made the previous month: “Tesla will be one of the companies to make AGI.” Musk had testified the day before that Tesla was not pursuing AGI. The jury was watching.

From there, Savitt worked through the emails methodically. He showed that as OpenAI explored a shift to a for-profit model in late 2017, Musk wanted not just a stake but full control — including the ability to choose a majority of the board members. Musk agreed. Savitt then showed that Musk had promised the other founders he would create a for-profit company with himself in control — intended as a “public benefit corporation.” Savitt pointed out that Musk had testified a public benefit corporation was essentially the same as a traditional for-profit. Musk’s lawyers objected. The judge allowed the question. Musk agreed he had said that.

Savitt produced the Tesla merger email — “the most promising option would be for OpenAI to attach to Tesla as a cash cow” — and asked Musk to confirm it. Musk confirmed. He showed emails from 2015 and asked Musk to agree they could be interpreted differently from how he had described them. “Your questions are not simple,” Musk said. “They are designed to trick me, essentially.”

The most significant credibility moment came when Savitt raised a direct contradiction in sworn testimony. Musk had said this morning he donated $38 million to OpenAI in total. In an earlier deposition, he had said under oath that he donated $100 million. Savitt put both statements to him. Musk grew combative.

“The classic answer to a yes-or-no question is not so simple,” Musk said. “For example, if you ask the question, ‘Will you stop beating your wife?’” Judge Gonzalez Rogers cut him off. “No, we’re not going to go there.” The gallery laughed.

The afternoon session brought more sparring. After Savitt apologized for an unclear question and said “that wasn’t a fair question,” Musk said: “I find it funny you said it was an unfair question, since you’re only asking unfair questions.” Savitt said he was doing his best. Musk replied: “That is not true.” The gallery laughed again, perhaps nervously.

In the most combative exchange of the day, Savitt pressed Musk on whether he had read a document about the for-profit structure created in 2018. Musk said he had gotten the gist by reading the top. Savitt needled him on whether he had read it in full. Musk raised his voice. “Let me talk.” The judge said: “OK, let’s everybody calm down.” Eventually, Savitt withdrew the question. “OK, after all that?” Musk said. The courtroom laughed.

Savitt tried a new tactic in the afternoon: reminding the jury of Musk’s association with President Trump and AI adviser David Sacks. Jury selection had struggled to find members who could set aside their views on Musk’s political affiliations. Musk’s lawyers objected that the association was irrelevant. The judge did not immediately rule.

Savitt also showed a 2018 email in which Shivon Zilis — Musk’s employee, later mother of four of his children, former OpenAI board member — asked Musk whether she should stay close to OpenAI to keep feeding him information on the company. Musk lashed out at Altman in response, saying Altman “did not keep the board informed, which was why he was fired from the company.” Savitt asked the judge to strike the comment. The exchange was testy.

On SpaceX, Musk repeatedly said he could not discuss it because the company is about to go public and the SEC had forbidden him from commenting ahead of the IPO. But when Savitt asked simply whether Musk would control SpaceX, Musk acknowledged that he would. The judge noted, with some amusement: “Well, you did say that one thing.” The gallery laughed. SpaceX, which recently acquired xAI, will compete directly with OpenAI once public.

Savitt pressed the competitive motive argument directly: the lawsuit is a delay tactic while xAI plays catch-up. He asked Musk if he agreed that Grok “lags much farther behind” ChatGPT. “Not anymore,” Musk said.

After court ended for the day, Savitt complained to the judge about “how difficult it is to get concise answers out of the witness.” Judge Gonzalez Rogers replied: “That is the challenge you have.” Musk’s cross-examination will continue Thursday morning.

Who Is William Savitt

OpenAI’s lead defense counsel, William Savitt, has a long history with Mr. Musk and once represented Tesla.Credit...Jason Henry for The New York Times

The lawyer running the cross-examination is worth a paragraph. Savitt played guitar in a New York City rock band that performed around the East Village, including at CBGB. He drove a yellow cab while pursuing music. He applied to a PhD program at Columbia, went to law school instead, and clerked for Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg on the Supreme Court. He has a complicated history with Musk specifically: he represented Musk and Tesla in a securities fraud lawsuit over the SolarCity acquisition, then switched sides to represent Twitter against Musk when he tried to back out of his $44 billion acquisition. After Musk completed the purchase, he sued Savitt’s firm, Wachtell Lipton, claiming its $90 million fee for representing Twitter under previous management was “unjust enrichment.” That suit was dismissed last year.

The lawyer now cross-examining Musk about his trustworthiness has represented him, argued against him, and watched him attempt to avoid paying the bill. He is not intimidated. The exchanges confirm it.

What This Confirms

Cross-references to The Asimov Gap

The $38 million versus $100 million discrepancy is unresolved. Two sworn statements in the same proceeding directly contradict each other. Savitt raised it and did not let it go. Musk’s redirect on Thursday will need to address it. This is the trial’s first clear credibility rupture — and it is Musk’s, not Altman’s. The Farrow-Marantz investigation documented in Part 28 described a pattern of telling different people different things. That pattern is now emerging under oath from the plaintiff himself.

The control argument is confirmed as structural, not personal. Musk testified he needed control of a for-profit OpenAI to stop bad decisions. He wanted majority board control and a 55 percent stake. He acknowledged he will control SpaceX. He told Tesla analysts he needed more shares to influence its “AI robot army.” Every major figure in the AI governance story — Musk, Altman, Amodei, Hassabis — has argued that they personally needed to control frontier AI to ensure its correct development. Part 23 traced this through Hassabis. Part 28 traced it through Altman. Musk’s testimony confirms it is the industry’s operating assumption, not any individual’s character flaw. Savitt made this point to the jury. It landed.

The AGI dissolution clause is now testimony. Musk said the original Microsoft partnership included a provision to dissolve the arrangement if OpenAI reached AGI. He cited it as reassurance. That clause was deleted on the morning this trial opened — as documented in Part 30. The governance structure Musk was comfortable with no longer exists. He is suing to restore a promise whose last contractual enforcement mechanism was removed the day his trial began.

The Trump association tactic is significant. Savitt’s decision to remind the jury of Musk’s relationship with Trump and David Sacks was deliberate. It connects the trial to the broader political economy of AI governance examined in Part 27 — the partisan geometry of who benefits from ungoverned AI development. Musk’s lawyers objected. The judge has not ruled. Whether this line of argument is allowed to continue will shape how the jury frames the larger question of whose interests the lawsuit actually serves.

The judge remains the most important person in the room. She has now cut off Musk’s wife-beating analogy, told the courtroom to stop talking over each other, noted with evident amusement that Musk admitted SpaceX control despite his stated reluctance, and responded to Savitt’s complaint about concise answers with the driest possible judicial empathy. Her record — Epic v. Apple, where she found Apple had “outright lied under oath” — suggests she does not give technology principals special treatment. Musk is not her first difficult witness. The cross-examination continues Thursday.

Watch For Thursday

Savitt will finish his cross-examination. Musk’s lawyers will then have redirect — their opportunity to rehabilitate his credibility on the donation discrepancy and the Tesla AGI post. The degree to which they succeed will signal how the jury is likely to weigh Musk’s testimony overall.

After Musk steps down, the trial moves to other witnesses. Altman’s testimony is the session this series has been anticipating since the proceedings began. Everything Savitt has built in three days of cross-examination is preparation for that confrontation. The AI safety researchers in the queue Wednesday morning were right to be there.
