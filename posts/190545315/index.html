Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 4: The Great Game Begins (1500-1839)

March 11, 2026

Opening: The Empire That Never Learned

By 1837, the historical record was clear. For 2,400 years, every empire that had tried to control Afghanistan had failed. The Persians had struggled for centuries. Alexander the Great had spent three years in bloody mountain warfare. The Mongols had resorted to genocidal destruction. Even Afghan rulers couldn’t hold their own empire together for more than a generation.

The pattern was unmistakable to anyone who bothered to look.

The British Empire bothered to look. They had scholars who could read Persian histories. They had diplomats who studied every previous failure. They had access to detailed accounts of what happened to armies that marched into the Hindu Kush.

And they decided to invade anyway.

The result would be one of the most catastrophic military disasters in British history - 16,500 people leaving Kabul in January 1842, and only one man reaching safety alive.

But before we examine that disaster in Part 5, we need to understand how Afghanistan arrived at this moment. How did a country that had endured 2,000 years of foreign conquest briefly become a regional power in its own right? And how did the paranoid competition between the British and Russian empires - the “Great Game” - turn Afghanistan back into a battlefield?

That baby in Badgor, waiting with her mother for one of the last mobile doctors in Kunar Province, lives in a country where British confidence met Afghan reality three times. The British had every advantage - superior technology, vast resources, professional armies. They had studied the history. They knew about Alexander’s struggles and the Mongol devastation.

They invaded anyway. Three times. And learned nothing.

But before the British disasters, Afghanistan experienced something rare in its long history: A moment when Afghans weren’t victims of empire, but builders of one.

The Hindu Kush mountains - the same terrain that stalled Alexander, sheltered resistance against the British, and remains Afghanistan's greatest defensive advantage.

The Interregnum: Caught Between Empires (1500-1747)

After the collapse of Timur’s empire in the early 16th century, Afghanistan became contested territory between two new regional superpowers:

The Safavid Empire - Shi’a Persian rulers controlling western Afghanistan, including the great city of Herat

The Mughal Empire - Sunni Muslim rulers from India controlling eastern Afghanistan, including Kabul and Kandahar

This period saw Afghanistan reduced to what one historian called “indigenous warriors who could swing the balance of power among outside empires.” Afghan tribes weren’t strong enough to expel both empires, but they were too formidable to be fully controlled by either.

The result was a careful balancing act. Afghan tribes played the Mughals against the Safavids, sometimes allying with one, sometimes with the other, always maintaining as much autonomy as the shifting balance of power allowed.

The Divided Country

The partition of Afghanistan reflected the strategic interests of both empires:

Western Afghanistan (Safavid)

  • Herat, the “Pearl of Khorasan,” became a Safavid cultural center

  • Persian language and culture dominated

  • Shi’a Islam promoted (though most Afghans remained Sunni)

  • Connected to the Persian trade networks

Eastern Afghanistan (Mughal)

  • Kabul served as the Mughal Empire’s winter capital (the Hindu Kush made summer residence impractical)

  • Gateway to India through the Khyber Pass

  • Strategic military importance for controlling the Indian subcontinent

  • Mix of Persian and Indian cultural influences

But here’s what neither empire could accomplish: Control of the mountain regions. The valleys remained semi-autonomous. Tribal leaders acknowledged whichever empire seemed more powerful at the moment, paid occasional tribute when forced, and maintained their independence in practice.

The Swiss cheese model endured. The cities could be garrisoned. The passes could be held. But the countryside remained Afghan, governed by tribal codes that predated either empire.

The Cultural Flowering

Despite being divided between empires, this period wasn’t entirely negative for Afghanistan. The competition between the Mughals and Safavids created opportunities:

  • Trade flourished as both empires tried to control Silk Road routes

  • Architecture and art benefited from imperial patronage

  • Persian became the language of literature, administration, and high culture

  • Kabul developed as a cosmopolitan city, blending Persian, Indian, and Central Asian influences

But this prosperity was concentrated in urban centers and dependent on the empires’ continued strength. When either empire weakened, Afghanistan suffered. And the fundamental pattern persisted: Afghanistan was valuable to both empires, but neither could fully control it.

The Afghan Revolt: A Warning Unheeded

In the early 18th century, Afghan tribes in the west - particularly the Ghilzai Pashtuns - revolted against increasingly oppressive Safavid rule. What started as a tax revolt became something extraordinary.

In 1709, Mir Wais Khan, a Ghilzai tribal leader, successfully expelled the Safavid governor from Kandahar and established Afghan independence in the south. His son Mahmud decided to go further - he would invade Persia itself.

In 1722, Mahmud’s army of 20,000 Afghan horsemen defeated a Persian force of 42,000 at the Battle of Gulnabad, then besieged Isfahan, the Safavid capital. After a six-month siege that killed nearly 100,000 people - mostly from starvation - the city fell.

For a brief moment, Afghan tribesmen controlled the Persian Empire.

The Madness of Mahmud

But Mahmud proved catastrophically unstable. Once in power, he:

  • Slaughtered Persian nobility

  • Personally massacred the royal family, including children

  • Spent 40 days in a cave “communing with God”

  • Emerged more deranged than before

  • Began killing his own supporters

His own men murdered him in 1725, ending the Afghan occupation of Persia.

The lesson was clear, though few paid attention: Afghan warriors could conquer outward, but their tribal structure made them terrible empire-builders. The same decentralization that made them formidable resisters made them incapable of sustained governance.

This would prove true even for Afghanistan’s greatest leader.

Ahmad Shah Durrani: The Birth of a Nation (1747-1772)

Ahmad Shah Durrani. Made in Punjab or Delhi. Early to mid-19th century. Toor Collection. Source

In 1747, the Persian conqueror Nadir Shah - who had expelled the Afghans from Persia and reconquered Afghanistan - was assassinated by his own officers. His death created a power vacuum across Central Asia.

His elite bodyguard, the Abdali tribe of Afghanistan, suddenly found themselves without a master but in possession of considerable military power and the late shah’s treasury.

The tribal leaders gathered at Kandahar to choose a new leader. After nine days of deliberation, they crowned Ahmad Khan, a 25-year-old military commander who had distinguished himself in Nadir Shah’s service.

He became Ahmad Shah Durrani - “Pearl of Pearls” - and the Abdali tribe became the Durranis.

This was the closest thing to a founding moment for Afghanistan as a nation.

The Empire Builder

Ahmad Shah proved to be an exceptional leader - perhaps the most capable ruler Afghanistan has ever produced. Within fifteen years, he had created an empire that stretched from Khorasan in Persia to Kashmir and Delhi in India.

The Durrani Empire at its peak (c. 1762) encompassed:

  • All of modern Afghanistan

  • Iran’s Khorasan province

  • Nearly all of modern Pakistan

  • Parts of northern India

  • Kashmir and the Punjab

For once in its long history, Afghanistan wasn’t the prize being fought over - it was the center of an empire expanding outward.

Military Genius and Political Wisdom

Ahmad Shah’s success rested on several qualities:

Militarily: He was brilliant. His victory over the Marathas at the Third Battle of Panipat (1761) was one of the largest and bloodiest battles of the 18th century, fought between armies totaling over 100,000 men. The Marathas controlled much of India and seemed unstoppable. Ahmad Shah destroyed them in a single day.

Politically: He understood Afghan tribal psychology. Rather than trying to centralize power (which would have provoked rebellion), he ruled through a confederation. Tribal leaders kept autonomy in exchange for military service and tribute. It was a delicate balance, but Ahmad Shah maintained it through personal charisma, strategic marriages, and judicious use of force.

Personally: Unlike most conquerors, Ahmad Shah was modest in his habits despite enormous wealth. He genuinely cared about his subjects’ welfare. He promoted trade, built infrastructure, and patronized arts and scholarship.

A Poet’s Love for Afghanistan

What sets Ahmad Shah apart from other empire-builders is his genuine love for Afghanistan. He wrote poetry that reveals not just patriotism, but a deep emotional connection to the land itself.

One of his most famous poems:

“By blood, we are immersed in love of you, The youth lose their heads for your sake. I come to you and my heart finds rest. Away from you, grief clings to my heart like a snake. I forget the throne of Dehli When I remember the mountain tops of my Afghan land. If I must choose between the world and you, I shall not hesitate to claim your barren deserts as my own.”

This wasn’t a warlord writing propaganda. This was a man who genuinely saw Afghanistan as worthy of love and sacrifice. He chose Kandahar over Delhi, the mountains over the riches of India.

Ahmad Shah imagined Afghanistan as a nation, not just a collection of tribes to be exploited.

The Fragile Achievement

But even Ahmad Shah couldn’t overcome geography and tribal structure completely. His empire required:

  • Constant military campaigns to keep it together

  • Personal leadership that couldn’t be institutionalized

  • A delicate balance of tribal interests that depended on continued success

  • Wealth from Indian raids to pay tribal leaders and armies

When Ahmad Shah died in 1772 - possibly from skin cancer so severe he wore a silver nose after his original nose “wasted away” - he left an empire that was already showing cracks.

The Rapid Collapse: When Greatness Isn’t Enough (1772-1826)

Ahmad Shah’s son, Timur Shah, inherited the empire but not his father’s genius. His first mistake was moving the capital from Kandahar (the Pashtun heartland) to Kabul (more cosmopolitan but less traditionally Afghan).

This angered the Pashtun tribes who had been the foundation of Durrani power. They saw it as a betrayal, a rejection of Afghan identity in favor of Persian cultural influence.

The Succession Disaster

When Timur Shah died in 1793, he left over 30 sons with no designated successor and no clear mechanism for choosing one.

What followed was predictable: Civil war.

Brothers fought brothers. Tribal alliances shifted constantly. Provincial governors declared independence. The empire Ahmad Shah had spent 25 years building fragmented in less than a generation.

By the 1820s, the Durrani Empire had collapsed to roughly the borders of modern Afghanistan - and even that was precariously held. The provinces were semi-autonomous. The treasury was depleted. The army was fractious and unreliable.

The Pattern Reasserts Itself

The collapse of the Durrani Empire demonstrated a fundamental truth about Afghanistan: Even when Afghans ruled Afghanistan, even when they had a genuinely capable leader, even when they created an empire - it couldn’t last.

The tribal structure that made Afghans formidable warriors made them terrible subjects. The mountains that protected them from invaders also prevented centralization. The ethnic and linguistic diversity that enriched Afghan culture made unified governance nearly impossible.

Ahmad Shah had overcome these challenges through personal genius. His successors couldn’t.

Dost Mohammad: Picking Up the Pieces (1826-1837)

Shere Ali Khan, successor of Dost Mohammad Khan as Amir. Shere Ali Khan ruled until exiled by the British in 1879 when he backed the wrong side in a British-Russian conflict. (By John Osborne)

Out of the chaos of succession wars emerged Dost Mohammad Khan, who seized control of Kabul in 1826. He was competent, politically astute, and genuinely tried to rebuild what the Durrani civil wars had destroyed.

But he faced an impossible situation:

Internally:

  • A depleted treasury

  • Fractious tribes who saw no reason to obey him

  • Rival claimants to power in other Afghan cities

  • No functioning administration beyond Kabul

Externally:

  • The Sikh Empire controlled the Punjab and Kashmir (formerly Durrani territory)

  • Russia was expanding south through Central Asia

  • The British Empire in India was growing increasingly paranoid about Russian intentions

  • Afghanistan was becoming a chess piece in imperial competition

Dost Mohammad tried to play the empires against each other, seeking aid from whichever seemed most useful at the moment. He asked the British for help against the Sikhs. He received Russian diplomats. He tried to maintain Afghan independence while acknowledging the reality that Afghanistan was too weak to stand alone against external threats.

This pragmatic approach would prove to be his undoing.

The Great Game: When Paranoia Trumps Reason

The term “Great Game” was coined by a British intelligence officer to describe the strategic competition between the British and Russian empires for influence in Central Asia. For the British, Afghanistan mattered for one reason: It was a buffer between British India and expanding Russian power.

British Paranoia

By the 1830s, Russia had been steadily conquering Central Asian territories, moving closer to Afghanistan’s northern borders. British strategists in India became convinced that Russia intended to:

  • Gain influence over Afghanistan

  • Use it as a base to invade British India

  • Incite Muslim revolts on India’s northwestern frontier

These fears were largely irrational. Russia had no realistic capability to invade India. The logistics of moving an army through Afghanistan and then fighting British forces in India were impossible. The terrain alone made it absurd.

But paranoia doesn’t require rationality.

British policy toward Afghanistan became driven not by Afghan reality, but by imagined Russian threats. Every Russian diplomatic mission was seen as a prelude to invasion. Every Afghan ruler who showed independence was suspected of being a Russian puppet.

This paranoid mindset would lead directly to catastrophe.

The Trigger: Russian Diplomats in Kabul (1837)

In 1837, Dost Mohammad received a Russian diplomatic mission in Kabul. The mission was minor - Russia was exploring commercial relations and trying to understand the region. Dost Mohammad received them cordially, as he received all foreign envoys.

To the British, this was intolerable.

Never mind that Dost Mohammad was also negotiating with the British. Never mind that he preferred British friendship and alliance. Never mind that he had explicitly asked for British support against the Sikhs.

The mere presence of Russian diplomats in Kabul was treated as a hostile act, as evidence that Dost Mohammad was unreliable.

The British Governor-General of India, Lord Auckland, decided that Dost Mohammad must be replaced with someone more “compliant.”

The Puppet King

The British chose Shah Shuja, Dost Mohammad’s half-brother, who had been deposed in 1809 and was living in exile in British-controlled India.

This was a catastrophically bad choice.

Shah Shuja had been “spewed out” (to quote contemporary British observer Sir John Kaye) by the Afghan people. He was deeply unpopular. His rule had been tyrannical. He had no support base among Afghan tribes. He was seen, correctly, as a British puppet.

Even British officials who knew Afghanistan warned that installing Shah Shuja would require permanent British military presence. The Afghan people would never accept him voluntarily.

The warnings were ignored.

The Decision to Invade

In October 1838, Lord Auckland issued what became known as the “Simla Manifesto,” announcing Britain’s intention to replace Dost Mohammad with Shah Shuja.

The manifesto’s language is revealing:

“The British Government has therefore resolved... to interfere decisively... for the restoration of Shah Shuja to the throne of his ancestors.”

Note the word: “Restoration.” The British convinced themselves they were restoring a legitimate ruler, not imposing a puppet. They believed their own propaganda.

What the British Knew (and Ignored)

By 1838, British officials had access to comprehensive information about Afghanistan:

They knew:

  • Alexander the Great had spent three years in bloody mountain warfare there

  • The Mongols had resorted to genocidal destruction

  • Even the Mughals and Persians, with cultural affinity, had struggled to control it

  • Ahmad Shah Durrani’s empire had collapsed despite his genius

  • Afghan tribes were fiercely independent and resented foreign interference

  • The terrain favored defenders and made supply lines vulnerable

  • Shah Shuja was universally hated

They believed:

  • British military superiority would overcome these challenges

  • Modern weapons and discipline would succeed where ancient armies failed

  • Afghan tribesmen would accept British-imposed order once they saw its benefits

  • The occupation would be brief - just long enough to install Shah Shuja and ensure stability

  • Afghan geography was less formidable than it appeared

This combination of knowledge and willful ignorance would prove fatal.

The Army Assembles

The British assembled a massive force:

  • 8,000 British and Indian soldiers (the “Army of the Indus”)

  • 38,000 camp followers (servants, families, merchants, prostitutes)

  • Tens of thousands of camels and other transport animals

  • Shah Shuja’s own contingent of exiled Afghans

What the British brought revealed their expectations. One officer required 60 camels just for his personal effects. Two camels were devoted solely to transporting cigars. The 16th Lancers brought foxhounds for hunting.

This wasn’t an army preparing for mountain warfare against guerrilla fighters. This was an imperial expeditionary force expecting a pleasant colonial posting, a brief military demonstration, and a triumphant return to India.

They planned to march to Kabul, install Shah Shuja, receive the grateful submission of Afghan tribes, and leave a small garrison to ensure order.

The Historical Moment: 2,400 Years of Pattern

Before we follow the British army into Afghanistan in Part 5, let’s count what should have been obvious by 1839:

2,400 years since the first Persian conquest 2,168 years since Alexander’s costly campaign 618 years since the Mongol apocalypse 67 years since Ahmad Shah Durrani’s death and the collapse of the last stable Afghan state

In all that time, periods of genuine Afghan peace and self-determination totaled perhaps 250 years - roughly 10% of recorded history.

The British had detailed records of all of this. They knew that Afghanistan had broken every empire that tried to hold it. They knew that the mountains made occupation costly. They knew that tribal structure meant defeating one leader didn’t end resistance.

They knew all of this.

They invaded anyway.

The Lesson Unlearned

There’s a particular tragedy in the British decision to invade Afghanistan in 1839. Unlike earlier empires, the British had no excuse of ignorance. They couldn’t claim they didn’t know the history. They couldn’t claim they didn’t understand the terrain or the people.

British officers had written extensive accounts of Afghan geography and culture. British diplomats had studied every previous invasion. British scholars could read Persian histories of Afghan resistance.

The information was available. The pattern was clear. The warnings were explicit.

But institutional arrogance trumped historical knowledge. The British belief in their own superiority, their confidence in modern military technology, their certainty that they were different from previous invaders - all of this overwhelmed the clear lessons of 2,400 years of history.

This is why the British experience in Afghanistan is so instructive. It proves that knowledge of history isn’t enough. Understanding the pattern isn’t enough. Even experiencing disaster firsthand isn’t enough.

The British would invade Afghanistan three times. They would lose catastrophically every time. And between wars, they would convince themselves that the next time would be different.

The Bridge to Catastrophe

As the Army of the Indus assembled on the Afghan border in late 1838, officers wrote letters home describing their confidence. Some worried the campaign would be over before they arrived. Others planned how they would spend their leave time in Kabul.

Shah Shuja, the puppet king, prepared to return to the throne he believed was rightfully his. He genuinely thought the Afghan people would welcome him.

Dost Mohammad, in Kabul, watched the British preparations with growing alarm. He had preferred British alliance. He had asked for British support against the Sikhs. He couldn’t understand why the British were now determined to destroy him.

He would soon flee north, beginning a resistance that would last years.

And that woman in Badgor, holding her three-month-old daughter Zakia who has been vomiting since birth, is the latest in an unbroken line stretching back through the British disasters we’re about to examine, through Ahmad Shah’s brief moment of Afghan greatness, through the Mongol apocalypse, through Islamic conquest, through Alexander’s mountain wars, to the first Persian governors who learned that Afghanistan bows to no one.

The British had studied the history. They knew about Alexander, the Mongols, the countless empires that had struggled and failed. They were certain that British discipline, British technology, British organization would finally succeed where 2,400 years of previous empires had failed.

They were wrong.

Catastrophically wrong.

In Part 5, we’ll follow the Army of the Indus into Afghanistan, through initial victories that seemed to prove British confidence justified, and then through the uprising, the retreat, and one of the worst military disasters in British history - 16,500 people leaving Kabul, one man arriving at Jalalabad.

The pattern was about to repeat. The mountains were waiting. And Afghan resistance was about to teach the British Empire a lesson they would have to learn twice more.
