The Quiet Part Out Loud

Trump Confesses the Objective: Oil

December 17, 2025

December 17, 2025

On December 10th, I published a satirical analysis comparing the Iraq WMD narrative to the Venezuela drug crisis narrative. The central argument: This isn’t about drugs or democracy. It’s about oil. And they’re using the exact same playbook from 2003.

On December 16th, President Donald Trump posted this on Truth Social:

“Venezuela is completely surrounded by the largest Armada ever assembled in the History of South America. It will only get bigger, and the shock to them will be like nothing they have ever seen before — Until such time as they return to the United States of America all of the Oil, Land, and other Assets that they previously stole from us.“

Read that again. Slowly.

“Return to the United States of America all of the Oil, Land, and other Assets that they previously stole from us.”

THE SMALL PROBLEM

Venezuela never stole American oil.

Venezuela never stole American land.

Venezuela’s oil is Venezuelan oil. It’s in Venezuelan territory. It belongs to Venezuela.

Axios had to run a fact-check: “Reality check: Trump incorrectly suggested in his statement that Venezuela stole ‘Oil, Land, and other Assets’ from the U.S. government.”

Argus Media noted: “It is not clear what Trump meant by claiming that Caracas ‘stole’ US-owned oil, land or assets, especially his reference to ‘land’.”

This is the President of the United States claiming ownership of another sovereign nation’s natural resources and threatening military force—”the largest Armada ever assembled in the History of South America”—unless they hand it over.

WHEN SATIRE CAN’T KEEP UP

In my piece, I wrote:

“Here’s a radical idea that will never happen: If you want their oil, just say so.”

I was being satirical. I thought they’d at least maintain the pretense. The drugs narrative. The Iran connection. The humanitarian crisis. The usual marketing campaign.

Six days later, the President just... said it.

Give us your oil or face our armada.

Not “stop the drug trafficking.” Not “restore democracy.” Not “address the humanitarian crisis.”

“Return our oil.”

Oil that was never ours. Oil that sits under their land. Oil that belongs to Venezuela under every principle of international law and sovereignty.

THE PLAYBOOK, ACCELERATED

In my article, I outlined the formula:

  1. Take a real problem (drugs)

  2. Connect it to a dictator with oil

  3. Connect that dictator to America’s enemies (Iran)

  4. Make it existential

  5. Invade

Trump just skipped steps 1-4 and went straight to: “We want the oil. Give it to us or face the largest armada in South American history.”

THE “AMERICAN SWEAT” DELUSION

On December 17th, White House homeland security adviser Stephen Miller doubled down on Trump’s claim, writing on social media:

“American sweat, ingenuity and toil created the oil industry in Venezuela. Its tyrannical expropriation was the largest recorded theft of American wealth and property. These pillaged assets were then used to fund terrorism and flood our streets with killers, mercenaries and drugs.”

Let’s parse this carefully.

“American sweat, ingenuity and toil created the oil industry in Venezuela.”

No. American companies operated in Venezuela under contracts with the Venezuelan government. They didn’t “create” the oil—nature did that. They didn’t own the land—Venezuela did. They had extraction rights. That’s not the same as ownership.

“Its tyrannical expropriation was the largest recorded theft of American wealth and property.”

It wasn’t theft. It was nationalization—a sovereign act that dozens of countries undertook in the 1970s. According to The New York Times, when Venezuela put its oil industry under state control in the 1970s, American companies (Exxon, Mobil, Shell, and Gulf Oil) lost $5 billion in assets and were compensated $1 billion.

Was the compensation adequate? That’s debatable. Is it “theft”? Only if you believe foreign companies have a permanent claim on another country’s resources—which would be, again, colonialism.

Countries have the sovereign right to nationalize industries within their borders. It happened in Mexico. It happened in the Middle East. It happened across Latin America. The usual recourse is international arbitration and compensation—not military invasion 50 years later.

But Miller’s framing is revealing: American companies operating in Venezuela equals American ownership of Venezuela’s oil. When Venezuela exercised sovereignty, it “stole” from America.

This is imperial logic in its purest form.

THE WHISPERER AT WORK

In September, I wrote about Stephen Miller as “The Political Whisperer”—someone who doesn’t just execute Trump’s impulses but shapes them, giving crude instincts intellectual frameworks and historical justifications.

This is Miller at his most effective.

Trump’s instinct: “I want Venezuela’s oil.”

Miller’s framing: “Venezuela stole our oil. American sweat and ingenuity created their oil industry. They owe us. Taking it back isn’t imperialism—it’s justice.”

Miller takes Trump’s transactional greed and transforms it into righteous reclamation. He provides the historical narrative that makes conquest sound like restitution. He’s not just justifying policy—he’s rewriting history to make the policy seem justified.

This is why Miller is so dangerous. He doesn’t restrain Trump’s worst impulses. He intellectualizes them. He gives them scholarly veneer. He makes “give us your oil” sound like legitimate grievance.

And now that framing—”they stole our wealth”—is official White House policy, delivered via Trump’s Truth Social post and reinforced by Miller’s follow-up statement.

The Whisperer whispered. Trump listened. Venezuela pays the price.

“WE KEEP IT, I GUESS”

The framing got even more brazen. When Trump announced the December 10th seizure of the oil tanker Skipper off Venezuela’s coast, a reporter asked what would happen to the oil.

Trump’s response: “We keep it, I guess. I don’t know.”

The Skipper was carrying an estimated 1.8 million barrels of Venezuelan crude oil—valued at approximately $95 million. Trump casually announced the U.S. would just... keep it.

Venezuela’s foreign minister called it “a blatant theft” and “an act of international piracy.”

When Iran seizes tankers in the Persian Gulf, American politicians call it:

  • Piracy

  • An act of war

  • Proof of a rogue state

  • International terrorism

  • A threat to global shipping

When we seize a Venezuelan tanker off Venezuela’s coast and Trump says “we keep it, I guess,” we call it:

  • Sanctions enforcement

  • Legal authority

  • Protecting American interests

  • Completely normal and nothing to see here

The hypocrisy is so blatant it almost demands admiration.

THE DRUG NARRATIVE COLLAPSES

Remember how this was supposedly about drugs? About stopping the flow of fentanyl that’s killing Americans?

White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles told Vanity Fair: Trump “wants to keep on blowing boats up until Maduro cries uncle.”

Not “until the drug flow stops.”

Not “until Venezuela addresses narcotrafficking.”

Until Maduro cries uncle.

This is regime change. It always was. The drugs were the marketing.

The structure of Trump’s statement itself reveals the priority:

First: The military threat and demand for oil
Then: The justifications (drugs, terrorism, trafficking)

The oil is the objective. The threats are the marketing.

THE “FOREIGN TERRORIST ORGANIZATION” DESIGNATION

Trump also announced that Venezuela had been designated a “Foreign Terrorist Organization.”

Here’s where it gets legally complicated.

Technically, the Trump administration designated something called the “Cartel de los Soles” (Cartel of the Suns) as a Foreign Terrorist Organization on November 24th. According to the State Department, this cartel is “headed by Nicolás Maduro and other high-ranking individuals” of the Venezuelan government.

But here’s the problem: The “Cartel de los Soles” doesn’t really exist as a formal organization.

As CNN reported, experts say the term “is more a description of allegedly corrupt government officials than an organized crime group.” Jeremy McDermott of InSightCrime told CNN that it “is not a traditional, vertically organized drug trafficking organization. It is... a series of normally disconnected cells embedded within the Venezuelan military.”

Phil Gunson of the International Crisis Group was even more direct: “Cartel de los Soles, per se, doesn’t exist. It’s a journalistic expression created to refer to the involvement of Venezuelan authorities in drug trafficking.”

Venezuela’s Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello responded: “Suddenly, they dusted off something they call the Cartel de los Soles, which they have never been able to prove, because it doesn’t exist. It’s the narrative of imperialism.”

So what did Trump actually do? He designated a non-existent organization as a terrorist group, claimed it’s “led” by Venezuela’s government, and is now using that designation to justify military action against the entire Venezuelan state.

As CNN noted, “The foreign terrorist organization designation has been historically reserved for non-state actors that do not have sovereign immunities conferred by either treaties or United Nations membership.”

This is legally unprecedented and strategically calculated. By designating Venezuelan government officials as members of a “terrorist organization,” Trump creates a framework where:

  • Attacking Venezuela isn’t a war—it’s counterterrorism

  • Different legal frameworks apply

  • Different authorization requirements

  • Different international law obligations

It’s branding for military action. It’s the legal architecture for bypassing Congressional war powers and international law.

  • At least one nuclear attack submarine

  • F-35 fighter jets

  • B-1 Lancer bombers (flew near Venezuela’s coast in October)

  • V-22 Ospreys

  • Multiple helicopters

This isn’t a drug interdiction operation. This is an invasion force.

THE NEOCON PERSPECTIVE: THEY’RE NOT EVEN HIDING IT

While Trump is at least being inadvertently honest, establishment voices are being deliberately honest. Foreign Affairs published a piece titled “How to Topple Maduro: And Why Regime Change Is the Only Way Forward in Venezuela.”

Key excerpts:

“Economic and diplomatic pressure is simply insufficient against Maduro.”

“The use of American military force to overthrow Maduro would not be without risk... But regime change would not require any ground deployments of U.S. forces except, at most, Special Forces raids...”

“The potential gain for the United States from the collapse of the Maduro regime far outweighs the risk...”

The article openly discusses military strikes against Venezuelan targets and seizing oil fields. Not as a hypothetical. As policy recommendations.

The New York Times reported that the administration has “prepared a series of military plans aimed at Venezuela... seeking control of oil fields central to the country’s ailing economy.”

It’s Iraq 2003 all over again, except this time they’re just openly admitting it’s about the oil.

VENEZUELA’S RESPONSE

Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro addressed the situation on state television:

“The true reasons for the prolonged aggression against Venezuela have been laid bare. It is not migration. It is not drug trafficking. It is not democracy. It is not human rights. It has always been about our natural wealth, our oil, our energy, the resources that belong exclusively to the Venezuelan people.”

Even Maduro—a dictator who has destroyed his own country’s economy and brutalized his own people—is making more sense about international law than the President of the United States.

Look at the sequence of events:

The pattern from my original article:

Iraq: Take a real problem (terrorism) → Connect to dictator with oil → Connect to bigger villain (Al Qaeda) → Make it existential → Invade

Venezuela: Take a real problem (drugs) → Connect to dictator with oil → Connect to bigger villain (Iran) → Make it existential → Invade

Trump just speed-ran it. He didn’t even bother with the pretense. He just said: Give us your oil or face our armada.

And his advisers are now explicitly arguing that Venezuela’s oil belongs to America because American companies once operated there.

THE TIMING IS EVERYTHING

Let’s talk about what else is happening this week.

December 19, 2025: A federal judge has ordered the release of additional sealed documents from the Jeffrey Epstein case. Documents that could contain names, details, and connections involving powerful people.

December 16, 2025: Trump announces the largest armada ever assembled in South American history, demands Venezuela hand over its oil, and threatens a naval blockade.

The bigger the Venezuela crisis becomes, the less media oxygen there is for Epstein documents.

Is it coordinated? Does it matter?

What matters is that it works. Cable news will spend days covering “BREAKING: TRUMP ORDERS VENEZUELA BLOCKADE” instead of covering what’s in those documents. The news cycle will be dominated by armada updates, naval positioning, Maduro responses, and oil market fluctuations.

The Epstein story—which involves actual crimes, actual victims, actual accountability—gets buried under an imperial adventure in the Caribbean.

This is the oldest play in the authoritarian handbook: When domestic scandals threaten, create an international crisis. When the spotlight gets too uncomfortable, start a war. When people start asking questions about your past, give them something else to focus on.

Trump didn’t invent this technique. But he’s mastered the timing.

Posted on December 16th. Three days before sealed documents drop.

Venezuela gets the armada. Epstein gets the memory hole.

THE IRAQ PRECEDENT, ACCELERATED

It took years for the Iraq War lies to unravel. Years for people to admit there were no WMDs. Years for the Saduro-Al Qaeda connection to be exposed as fabrication. Years for people to acknowledge it was about oil and regional control.

With Venezuela, Trump compressed that timeline into a single Truth Social post.

The lie and the truth arrived simultaneously.

“This is about drugs and terrorism and humanitarian concerns.”
“Also give us your oil or face our armada.”

It’s a confession disguised as a threat.

THE FINAL OBSERVATION

I published “The Threat Matrix” as satire because I thought the parallels between Iraq 2003 and Venezuela 2025 were so obvious that highlighting them would be devastating.

Trump read it as an instruction manual.

Or more likely, he didn’t read it at all. He just did what American presidents do when they want another country’s oil: threatened military force and dressed it up as humanitarian concern.

Except this time, he forgot to dress it up first. And he timed it for maximum distraction value.

“Return to the United States of America all of the Oil, Land, and other Assets that they previously stole from us.”

That sentence will be in history books. Right next to “We don’t want the smoking gun to be a mushroom cloud.”

Both lies. Both pretexts. Both about oil.

The only difference is that Trump said the quiet part loud before anyone had to expose it. And Stephen Miller added a history lesson claiming Venezuela’s oil was created by American labor, so nationalizing it was theft.

Sometimes reality doesn’t validate satire. Sometimes it just skips ahead and does the punchline itself. And sometimes it does the punchline specifically to distract you from something else.

Venezuela gets the armada. Iraq got “Mission Accomplished.” Epstein victims get buried under both.

The pattern holds.
