America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 9: "The 10-Year Plan — How to Actually Achieve Universal Healthcare"

December 17, 2025

For eight parts, we’ve documented the problem:

  • 80 years of broken promises (Parts 1-2)

  • $14,885 per capita costs, double the OECD average (Part 3)

  • Five countries achieving universal coverage at half the cost (Part 4)

  • A bipartisan compromise nobody wants (Part 5)

  • $700 million in lobbying preventing reform (Part 6)

  • Medicare proving single-payer works for 67 million Americans (Part 7)

  • Red states refusing free money while their residents die (Part 8)

Now comes the hard part: The actual solution.

Not concepts. Not principles. Not aspirations.

Specific legislation. Committee jurisdictions. Vote counts. Executive actions. Swiss and German legal frameworks America can copy-paste. A plan Congressional staffers can print and hand to senators.

This is Part 9 of Code Blue. This is the roadmap.

The Three-Path Strategy

American healthcare reform can follow three possible paths, depending on political conditions:

Path A: Full Single-Payer (Medicare for All)

  • Requires: Democratic trifecta + elimination of filibuster OR 60+ Democratic senators

  • Likelihood (2025-2034): 15-20%

  • Timeline if passed: 4-6 years implementation

Path B: Regulated Multi-Payer (Swiss-German Model)

  • Requires: Bipartisan coalition of 10-15 senators + House majority

  • Likelihood (2025-2034): 30-35%

  • Timeline if passed: 6-8 years implementation

Path C: Incremental Expansion (Public Option + Reforms)

  • Requires: Reconciliation (50 votes) OR narrow bipartisan support

  • Likelihood (2025-2034): 50-60%

  • Timeline if passed: 8-10 years implementation

This plan focuses on Path C — the most achievable route — while laying groundwork for Path B or A if political conditions shift.

Phase 1: Immediate Executive Actions (Year 1)

No legislation required. President can implement on day one.

Action 1.1: Expand ACA Enrollment

Authority: Section 1311 of ACA grants HHS broad authority to facilitate enrollment

Implementation:

  • Automatic enrollment in subsidized plans for anyone receiving unemployment benefits, SNAP, or other federal assistance

  • Pre-populate ACA applications with IRS and SSA data (opt-out instead of opt-in)

  • Extend open enrollment to year-round (currently 6 weeks)

  • Increase funding for navigators and community enrollment assistance

Impact:

  • Cover 3-5 million currently uninsured Americans immediately

  • Cost: $8-12 billion annually (within existing ACA framework)

Action 1.2: Expand Medicaid Through Federal Incentives

Authority: Section 1115 waivers allow HHS to approve state innovations

Implementation:

  • Offer holdout states 95% federal match (instead of 90%) for first 3 years of expansion

  • Provide $500 million in rural hospital stabilization grants contingent on expansion

  • Allow states to implement expansion via Section 1115 waivers with flexibility on work requirements (but prohibit barriers that reduce coverage)

Impact:

  • Potentially flip 3-5 holdout states (Kansas, Wyoming, Wisconsin most likely)

  • Cover 2-4 million additional Americans

  • Save 50-80 rural hospitals from closure

Action 1.3: Expand Prescription Drug Negotiation

Authority: IRA grants HHS authority to negotiate drug prices; can expand aggressively within existing statute

Implementation:

  • Accelerate negotiation timeline: 20 drugs by 2026 (instead of 2029)

  • Add 30 more drugs by 2027

  • Include insulin (all formulations, not just some)

  • Negotiate prices for drugs covered by federal employees, military, and VA simultaneously

Impact:

  • Save $15-20 billion annually by Year 3

  • Reduce patient out-of-pocket costs 50-70% on negotiated drugs

Action 1.4: Ban Surprise Medical Billing (Full Implementation)

Authority: No Surprises Act (2021) requires HHS to issue implementing regulations

Implementation:

  • Aggressive enforcement of existing law

  • Expand definition of “surprise billing” to include:

    • Ground ambulances (currently exempt)

    • Lab work sent to out-of-network labs

    • Anesthesiologists and radiologists in all circumstances

  • Establish federal arbitration for billing disputes (fast-track resolution)

Impact:

  • Eliminate $40-50 billion in surprise bills annually

  • Reduce medical bankruptcy by 15-20%

Action 1.5: Require Hospital Price Transparency (Enforced)

Authority: Hospital Price Transparency Rule (2021) exists but poorly enforced

Implementation:

  • Increase penalties for non-compliance from $300/day to $10,000/day

  • Require machine-readable price files with no exemptions

  • Create public website comparing hospital prices for common procedures

  • Mandate that hospitals show prices to patients before non-emergency procedures

Impact:

  • Enable actual price competition

  • Reduce hospital pricing 15-25% over 3-5 years as consumers can compare

Total Year 1 Impact:

  • Coverage: 5-9 million newly insured

  • Cost reduction: $25-35 billion annually

  • Legislative votes required: Zero (all executive authority

Phase 2: Reconciliation Legislation (Year 2)

Requires: 50 Senate votes + House majority. Uses budget reconciliation to bypass filibuster.

Bill Title: “American Health Security Enhancement Act of 2026”

Legislative vehicle: Budget reconciliation (bypasses 60-vote threshold)

Senate Committee: Finance Committee (budget reconciliation jurisdiction)

House Committee: Ways and Means Committee + Energy and Commerce Committee

Provision 2.1: Make ACA Subsidies Permanent

Current status: Enhanced ACA subsidies expire December 2025

Reconciliation change:

  • Make enhanced subsidies permanent

  • Eliminate subsidy cliff (currently cuts off at 400% FPL)

  • Extend subsidies to households earning up to 600% FPL (~$90,000 for individuals)

Impact:

  • Keep premiums affordable for middle-class families

  • Prevent 3-4 million people from losing coverage

  • Cost: $30-35 billion annually

Reconciliation compliance: Directly affects federal budget (spending increase)

Provision 2.2: Create Public Option

Implementation:

  • Allow anyone to buy into Medicare (called “Medicare Part E” for Everyone)

  • Premium set at actuarial cost (no subsidy beyond existing ACA subsidies)

  • Same provider networks as traditional Medicare

  • Same benefits as Medicare

  • Administered by CMS (existing infrastructure)

Phase-in:

  • Year 1: Available to individuals 50-64

  • Year 2: Available to individuals 40-49

  • Year 3: Available to everyone

Impact:

  • Provide genuine competition to private insurance

  • Cover 10-15 million people by Year 5 (estimate based on modeling)

  • Reduce premiums 10-15% through competitive pressure

Cost to federal government:

  • Modest initial subsidy for market stabilization: $10-15 billion

  • Becomes revenue-neutral by Year 4 as premiums cover costs

Reconciliation compliance: Creates new revenue stream (premiums) and affects Medicare spending

Provision 2.3: Lower Medicare Eligibility to Age 60

Implementation:

  • Year 1: Eligibility at age 60 (voluntary buy-in)

  • Premium: Actuarially determined (roughly $400-500/month without subsidy)

  • Subsidies available based on income

Impact:

  • Cover 5-7 million early retirees currently uninsured or under-insured

  • Reduce emergency room costs for 60-64 age group

  • Cost: $20-25 billion annually (offset by premiums: $15-20 billion)

  • Net cost: $5-8 billion annually

Reconciliation compliance: Affects Medicare spending and revenue

Provision 2.4: Expand Drug Price Negotiation to All Medicare Drugs

Implementation:

  • Medicare negotiates prices for all Part D drugs (currently only 10-20 drugs)

  • Use international reference pricing (average of Canadian, German, French, UK prices)

  • Implementation timeline: 3 years

Impact:

  • Save $100-150 billion annually by Year 5

  • Reduce patient out-of-pocket costs by 60-70%

Reconciliation compliance: Directly reduces federal spending

Provision 2.5: Cap Out-of-Pocket Costs

Implementation:

  • Federal maximum out-of-pocket cap: $2,000/year per person, $4,000/year per family

  • Applies to all ACA plans, Medicare, and public option

  • Includes deductibles, co-pays, and co-insurance

Impact:

  • Virtually eliminate medical bankruptcy

  • Reduce financial burden on 50-60 million Americans with high out-of-pocket costs

Cost: $40-50 billion annually (government subsidizes gap)

Reconciliation compliance: Affects federal health spending

Total Cost of Reconciliation Bill:

  • Gross cost: $200-250 billion annually

  • Savings from drug negotiation: $100-150 billion annually

  • Net cost: $50-100 billion annually

Offsets:

  • Restore top marginal tax rate to 39.6% (raises $60 billion annually)

  • Close carried interest loophole ($18 billion annually)

  • Implement 1% wealth tax on assets over $50 million ($80 billion annually)

  • Total revenue: $158 billion annually

Net budget impact: Deficit-neutral or slight reduction

Phase 3: Comprehensive Reform (Years 3-5)

Requires: Bipartisan support (60 votes) OR narrow Democratic majority + filibuster reform

Bill Title: “American Universal Healthcare Act of 2028”

Model: Swiss-German hybrid (see Part 5)

Provision 3.1: Mandate Universal Coverage

Implementation:

  • Every American must have health insurance

  • Options: Employer coverage, ACA exchange plan, Public option (Medicare Part E), Traditional Medicare (65+)

  • Individual mandate penalty: $850/year or 2.5% of income (whichever is higher)

  • Exemptions: Financial hardship, religious objections

Impact:

  • Cover remaining 20-25 million uninsured Americans

  • Stabilize insurance markets (healthy people in pool)

Provision 3.2: Standardize Coverage Requirements

Implementation:

  • Federal government defines “Basic Health Plan” that all insurers must offer

  • Covers: Hospital, physician, prescription drugs, preventive care, mental health, dental, vision

  • No annual or lifetime limits

  • No denial for pre-existing conditions (already law, but reinforced)

  • No coverage exclusions based on health status

Based on: Swiss KVG 1996 (Federal Health Insurance Act)

Provision 3.3: Implement Price Controls

Implementation:

  • Federal government negotiates maximum prices for:

    • All hospital procedures (using Medicare rates as baseline)

    • All physician services (using Medicare physician fee schedule)

    • All prescription drugs (using international reference pricing)

    • All medical devices (using German pricing as baseline)

Hospitals and providers must accept these prices from all insurers

Based on: German SGB V (Social Code Book V, sections governing healthcare pricing)

Impact:

  • Reduce healthcare spending by 30-40% over 5 years

  • Lower premiums by 35-45%

  • Eliminate price variation between insurers (all pay same rates)

Provision 3.4: Subsidize Premiums for Affordability

Implementation:

  • Premium subsidies based on income:

    • 0-138% FPL: Free coverage (Medicaid or public option)

    • 138-300% FPL: Premium capped at 4% of income

    • 300-400% FPL: Premium capped at 6% of income

    • 400-500% FPL: Premium capped at 8% of income

    • 500-600% FPL: Premium capped at 10% of income

    • Above 600% FPL: No subsidy (but still must buy coverage)

Based on: Swiss subsidy system (30% of Swiss receive subsidies)

Cost: $150-200 billion annually

Provision 3.5: Simplify Administration

Implementation:

  • Single billing code system (eliminate proprietary insurer coding)

  • Standardized claim forms (one form for all insurers)

  • Electronic claims processing (required within 48 hours)

  • Eliminate prior authorization for evidence-based care

  • Require prior authorization for experimental treatments only

Impact:

  • Reduce administrative costs from 12-18% to 4-6% (Swiss/German levels)

  • Save $200-250 billion annually

  • Free physicians to practice medicine instead of fighting insurance companies

Total Cost of Comprehensive Reform:

  • Gross cost: $350-400 billion annually

  • Savings from price controls: $500-600 billion annually

  • Savings from administrative simplification: $200-250 billion annually

  • Net savings: $350-450 billion annually

Yes, savings. Universal coverage costs LESS than the current system.

Phase 4: Optimization (Years 6-10)

Objective 4.1: Fine-Tune Subsidy Levels

  • Monitor premium affordability

  • Adjust subsidy schedules based on real-world data

  • Ensure no one pays more than 10% of income for basic coverage

Objective 4.2: Adjust Provider Payment Rates

  • Monitor access to care (wait times, provider availability)

  • Adjust payment rates if shortages develop

  • Ensure adequate physician supply through medical school funding

Objective 4.3: Expand Coverage Scope

  • Add dental, vision, hearing to basic coverage (if not included in Phase 3)

  • Add long-term care coverage (for elderly and disabled)

  • Add mental health parity (full coverage, no limitations)

Objective 4.4: Address Medical Education Costs

Problem: American doctors graduate with $200,000-300,000 debt

Solution:

  • Federal medical school loan forgiveness ($100,000 over 10 years of practice)

  • Expand medical school slots by 25% (increase supply, reduce costs)

  • Create “National Health Service Corps” — free medical school in exchange for 5 years of rural/underserved practice

Impact:

  • Reduce physician debt burden

  • Increase primary care physician supply by 30-40%

  • Improve rural healthcare access

Cost: $20-25 billion annually

Savings: Allows physician reimbursement rates to align with international norms over time (currently inflated due to debt service)

Objective 4.5: Monitor and Enforce

  • Annual reporting on coverage rates, costs, outcomes

  • Enforcement of price controls (penalties for overcharging)

  • Insurance company oversight (medical loss ratio, claim denial rates)

  • Public transparency dashboard (all data publicly available)

The Legislative Path

Senate Strategy:

For Reconciliation Bill (Phase 2):

  • Requires: 50 votes + VP tiebreaker

  • Target: All Democrats + independents

  • Vulnerable votes: Joe Manchin types (moderates in swing states)

  • Strategy: Emphasize deficit-neutrality, cost controls, choice preservation

For Comprehensive Reform (Phase 3):

  • Requires: 60 votes (or filibuster reform)

  • Target: 10-15 Republican senators

Possible Republican votes:

  • Lisa Murkowski (AK): Moderate, supports healthcare access

  • Susan Collins (ME): Moderate, voted to preserve ACA

  • Mitt Romney (UT): Implemented similar system in Massachusetts

  • Bill Cassidy (LA): Physician, understands healthcare policy

  • Todd Young (IN): Pragmatist, open to bipartisan solutions

  • Thom Tillis (NC): North Carolina expanded Medicaid under his watch

  • Josh Hawley (MO): Proposed healthcare tax relief (Part 1), might support broader reform

  • Mike Braun (IN): Businessman, understands cost issues

  • Shelley Moore Capito (WV): West Virginia benefits from expansion

  • John Cornyn (TX): Texas facing rural hospital crisis

Strategy:

  • Emphasize market competition (Swiss model keeps private insurance)

  • Emphasize cost controls (benefits employers, reduces taxes long-term)

  • Emphasize rural hospital preservation (critical for Republican states)

  • Allow moderate Republicans to claim “we fixed Obamacare” instead of “we passed single-payer”

House Strategy:

Reconciliation Bill:

  • Requires: Simple majority (218 votes)

  • Currently: Republican control

  • Strategy: Wait for Democratic majority OR find 10-15 moderate Republicans

Possible Republican House votes:

  • Problem Solvers Caucus members (25-30 moderate Republicans)

  • Republicans from expansion states

  • Republicans facing rural hospital closures in districts

Comprehensive Reform:

  • Requires: Simple majority (218 votes)

  • Easier than Senate (no filibuster)

The Copy-Paste Strategy

Why reinvent the wheel? Copy proven legal frameworks:

From Switzerland (KVG 1996):

Article 3: Mandatory Insurance “All persons domiciled in Switzerland must take out health insurance.”

Article 61: Benefits Package “The Federal Council determines the scope of compulsory health insurance benefits.”

Article 72: Premium Subsidies “Cantons shall reduce premiums for low-income persons.”

American version: “All persons residing in the United States must obtain health insurance. The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall determine minimum coverage standards. States shall provide premium subsidies on a sliding scale based on income, with federal government providing 90% of subsidy costs.”

From Germany (SGB V):

Section 28: Medical Benefits “Insured persons are entitled to treatment by physicians, dentists, and other healthcare providers.”

Section 72: Fee Schedules “The Federal Joint Committee shall establish uniform fee schedules for physician and hospital services.”

Section 87: Drug Pricing “The Federal Joint Committee shall negotiate maximum prices for pharmaceuticals based on therapeutic value and international reference pricing.”

American version: “All insured persons are entitled to comprehensive medical care including preventive, diagnostic, therapeutic, and rehabilitative services. The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall establish uniform fee schedules for all healthcare services. The Secretary shall negotiate maximum prices for all prescription drugs using international reference pricing.”

It’s literally that simple. Copy-paste working legal frameworks. Adapt to American federal-state structure. Pass it.

The Political Reality Check

This plan requires:

  • 1 year of executive action (achievable with any president)

  • 1 reconciliation bill (achievable with 50 votes)

  • 1 comprehensive reform bill (requires 60 votes OR filibuster reform)

  • 10 years of implementation

This plan provides:

  • Universal coverage

  • Cost reduction of 40-50%

  • Better outcomes

  • Administrative simplification

  • Preservation of private insurance (Swiss model)

  • Market competition

This plan threatens:

  • Insurance company profits on basic coverage

  • Hospital price gouging

  • Pharmaceutical company monopoly pricing

  • Medical device manufacturer markups

  • PBM middleman profits

Which is why it won’t pass without massive public pressure.

But it’s achievable. It’s tested. It’s working in Switzerland and Germany.

The question isn’t whether it can be done.

The question is whether Americans will demand it.

December 17, 2025 Update:

As I write this, Washington is in chaos.

Last week (Dec 11): The Senate rejected both parties’ healthcare bills. Senator Josh Hawley crossed party lines to vote for extending subsidies—choosing people over politics.

Yesterday (Dec 16): Speaker Mike Johnson actively blocked his own members from voting on subsidy extensions. The House Rules Committee—which Johnson controls—rejected amendments from GOP centrists that would have extended subsidies with reforms.

The centrists had spent days altering their proposal to meet leadership demands. They found ways to pay for it. They added income limits. They compromised on everything.

Johnson rejected it anyway.

The scene in Washington:

  • Tense closed-door meeting last Tuesday: GOP centrists confronted Johnson. Shouting could be heard outside the room. Rep. Nicole Malliotakis (R-NY): “It’s the frustration from members who feel that we have a good compromise solution... and leadership is shutting it down.”

  • Only 2-3 work days left in 2025. No path to a vote this year.

  • Subsidies WILL expire. This is no longer uncertain. On January 1, 2026—14 days from today—22 million Americans will see their premiums double. Two million will lose coverage entirely.

  • GOP moderates now considering discharge petition to force a vote against their own Speaker. Rep. Brian Fitzpatrick (R-PA): “The only thing worse than a clean extension would be expiration. And I would make that decision.”

  • Electoral panic spreading: Rep. Don Bacon (R-NE): “It will be used like a sledgehammer a year from now. The reality will be bad.”

  • Even Jim Jordan (R-OH) warned conservatives: If you block the extension, moderates will bypass you with Democrats and you’ll get even less.

Both parties still blaming each other:

  • Speaker Johnson: “Democrats broke the system 15 years ago”

  • Rep. Chip Roy (R-TX): GOP offers “milquetoast garbage, not even a concept of a plan”

  • President Trump (last Friday): “I want to give the money to the people and let the people buy themselves great health care”

The House may vote this week on a GOP bill that doesn’t extend subsidies. Even if it passes the House, the Senate won’t act. The subsidies expire regardless.

Fifteen years since the ACA. Both parties admitting failure. Johnson blocks votes. Roy admits no plan exists. Trump demands direct payments. Centrists revolt.

14 days until 22 million Americans see premiums double.

Meanwhile, the roadmap to fix this is in this essay. The legal frameworks exist. The international evidence exists.

What doesn’t exist is political courage.

And apparently, what also doesn’t exist is a Speaker willing to let his own members vote.
