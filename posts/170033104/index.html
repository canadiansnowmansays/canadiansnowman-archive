Going After Jack Smith

When "Law and Order" Becomes Revenge

A federal prosecutor did his job investigating Trump. Now Trump's people want to investigate him. This is how democracy dies.

August 3, 2025

Something deeply troubling happened this weekend that deserves more attention than it's getting. The U.S. Office of Special Counsel announced it's investigating Jack Smith—the former special counsel who prosecuted Donald Trump—for allegedly violating the Hatch Act.

Let that sink in for a moment. A prosecutor who brought legitimate criminal cases against Trump is now being investigated by Trump's own appointees for... doing his job.

This isn't just petty revenge (though it's certainly that). It's a dangerous precedent that could fundamentally change how federal law enforcement works in America.

The Setup: What Actually Happened

Here's the timeline that should worry everyone:

Jack Smith was appointed special counsel in November 2022 to investigate Trump's handling of classified documents and his attempts to overturn the 2020 election. Smith brought serious, evidence-based indictments in both cases. The cases never went to trial—not because they lacked merit, but because Trump's delay tactics worked until he won the election.

Now Senator Tom Cotton (R-Arkansas) has asked the Office of Special Counsel to investigate Smith for "unprecedented interference in the 2024 election." The agency—now run by Trump appointees after he fired the previous head—has obliged.

The allegation? That Smith violated the Hatch Act by continuing his prosecutions during election season, somehow turning legitimate criminal cases into illegal political activity.

Why This Is Completely Bogus

The Hatch Act prohibits federal employees from using their official positions for political purposes. It's supposed to prevent, say, IRS agents from targeting political opponents or government employees from campaigning on taxpayer time.

It is not supposed to prevent prosecutors from prosecuting crimes.

Think about the implications here. If continuing a criminal case during an election cycle violates the Hatch Act, then political candidates would have effective immunity during campaign seasons. Rob a bank in October of an election year? Sorry, prosecuting you might influence the election!

Richard Painter, who served as chief ethics lawyer under George W. Bush (hardly a liberal), put it perfectly: he's "never seen a prosecutor found to violate the Hatch Act for pleading with a court."

Smith didn't hold press conferences attacking Trump. He didn't campaign against him. He filed court documents in ongoing criminal cases—you know, his actual job.

The Real Problem: Institutional Capture

But here's what makes this really scary. The Office of Special Counsel isn't some independent watchdog carefully weighing the evidence. Trump fired the previous head (a Biden appointee) and installed his own people to run it.

So Trump's appointees are now investigating the prosecutor who investigated Trump, at the request of Trump's political ally, using a legal theory that would make it impossible to prosecute political candidates.

This isn't oversight. It's revenge with official letterhead.

What This Means for Everyone

If you're thinking "well, Smith was going after Trump, so maybe he deserves it," you're missing the bigger picture. This isn't really about Smith or even Trump.

It's about whether prosecutors can do their jobs without fear of retaliation when powerful people don't like the results.

Imagine you're a federal prosecutor and you see evidence that a senator took bribes, or a governor embezzled funds, or a presidential candidate committed serious crimes. Now imagine knowing that if you pursue those cases and that person regains power, you could face investigation yourself.

How many prosecutors are going to take that risk?

The Chilling Effect Is Already Here

This investigation sends a crystal-clear message to every federal prosecutor in America: investigate powerful political figures at your own peril. Even if you follow every rule, respect every procedure, and base your decisions purely on evidence and law, you could still find yourself under investigation if your target doesn't like the outcome.

That's not how the justice system is supposed to work in a democracy. When prosecutors have to worry about political retaliation for doing their jobs, we don't have equal justice under law—we have a system where political power provides practical immunity.

The Waste of It All

Beyond the constitutional concerns, this is just a spectacular waste of resources. The Office of Special Counsel is supposed to protect whistleblowers and prevent actual political corruption in federal employment. Instead, it's chasing a prosecutor who followed established legal procedures because a senator asked them to.

Meanwhile, real Hatch Act violations—like Trump administration officials using government resources for campaign events during his first term—got minimal consequences. Thirteen Trump officials violated the Hatch Act in his first term, including Kellyanne Conway. The same office recommended she be fired; Trump ignored it.

But Smith files court documents in an ongoing criminal case? Investigation time!

This Is How It Happens

Here's the thing about democratic backsliding—it doesn't usually happen all at once with tanks in the streets. It happens gradually, through the slow corruption of institutions that are supposed to check government power.

First, you capture the agencies that provide oversight. Then you use those agencies to investigate your enemies. Then prosecutors learn not to investigate you because they know what happens to prosecutors who do.

Before you know it, you have a system where some people are above the law not because the law says so, but because everyone knows better than to try to enforce it.

What Happens Next

The Smith investigation should be immediately dropped as legally baseless and institutionally corrupted. But even if it continues, Smith himself will probably be fine—he's already resigned and has private sector options.

The real damage is to the system itself. Every federal prosecutor now knows that investigating powerful political figures could end their career. Every defense attorney for a political figure now knows they can cry "politicization" and potentially get investigators investigated themselves.

And every American should know that we're one step closer to a system where political power means never having to face legal consequences.

The Bottom Line

This isn't about whether you liked Smith's investigations or thought they were well-founded. It's about whether we want to live in a country where prosecutors can investigate crimes without fear of political retaliation.

Because once that principle is gone, it's very hard to get back.

The Smith investigation is being sold as a matter of accountability and preventing the "weaponization" of justice. But the real weaponization is using government agencies to investigate prosecutors for prosecuting. That's not accountability—it's revenge.

And revenge dressed up as law and order is still just revenge.
