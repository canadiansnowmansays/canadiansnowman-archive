The Court That Did What Congress Wouldn’t

How a Federal Judge in Boston Accomplished in One Ruling What 535 Elected Representatives Failed to Do in a Year

March 16, 2026

The medical societies argued that Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and his appointees have made arbitrary and capricious changes to the childhood vaccine schedule.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 16, 2026

On Saturday night, Saturday Night Live aired a four-minute sketch called “MAHAspital” — a withering parody of Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s Make America Healthy Again movement, complete with a doctor ordering beef tallow for a hypertensive patient and a colleague pulling the plug on a vegan. It was, by any measure, one of the sharpest pieces of political satire the show has produced in years, and I wrote about it yesterday with considerable appreciation.

Forty-eight hours later, a federal judge in Boston did something even more devastating. He wrote it into law.

U.S. District Judge Brian Murphy’s ruling on Monday, March 16, 2026 — delivered in response to a lawsuit brought by six of America’s most prominent medical organizations — blocked Kennedy from implementing virtually everything he has spent the past year systematically constructing. The childhood vaccine schedule overhaul: blocked. The handpicked advisory committee’s decisions: stayed. The scheduled committee meeting this week: cancelled. The entire architecture of Kennedy’s vaccine agenda: halted, pending appeal, with a clarity of judicial language that leaves very little room for interpretation.

Readers of this series will recall that I have been documenting Kennedy’s dismantling of American public health infrastructure since August 2025. Four essays across eight months, each one tracking a different dimension of the same catastrophe: the national security implications of gutting mRNA research, the CDC leadership exodus, the firing of seventeen independent vaccine experts, and the president who enabled all of it with a promise to let Kennedy “go wild.”

I did not, at the time, anticipate that the courts would move this decisively this quickly. I am, for once in this series, pleasantly surprised.

What Kennedy Actually Did: A Brief Recap for New Readers

For those joining this series at Part Five, a compressed timeline of events:

In June 2025, Kennedy fired all seventeen members of the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices — ACIP — the independent panel of scientific experts that has shaped American vaccine policy for decades. He replaced them with individuals who shared his deep skepticism of vaccines and mandates, a group whose collective qualification for the role was, generously speaking, ideological alignment rather than scientific expertise.

In the months that followed, his reconstituted committee proceeded to rescind several childhood vaccine recommendations, including immunization of all newborns against hepatitis B. In January 2026, acting CDC director Jim O’Neill — another Kennedy appointee — announced a new childhood vaccine schedule that reduced the number of diseases against which children are routinely immunized from seventeen to eleven, bypassing the ACIP process entirely.

Separately, Kennedy announced on social media that COVID vaccines would no longer be recommended for healthy children or pregnant women. He did this via a post on X. Not through a peer-reviewed process. Not through the evidence-based deliberation that has underpinned American vaccine policy for generations. Via social media, on a platform owned by one of the president’s most prominent supporters.

Dr. Kirk Milhoan, speaking at an A.C.I.P. meeting in Atlanta in September.Credit...Alyssa Pointer/Reuters

The chair of Kennedy’s handpicked committee, Dr. Kirk Milhoan, then suggested that all childhood vaccines — including those against polio and measles — should be optional, because the diseases no longer pose the dangers they once did. This is a position that most public health experts immediately denounced as not merely wrong but actively dangerous, given that the reason these diseases no longer pose the dangers they once did is precisely because of the vaccines Dr. Milhoan now considers optional.

This is the man Kennedy selected to chair the committee that advises America on what shots its children should receive.

The Cocaine Interlude

I would be professionally remiss not to note, for the record, that the man overseeing all of the above recently appeared on a podcast hosted by comedian Theo Von and offered the following assessment of his own relationship with infectious disease:

“I’m not scared of a germ, you know. I used to snort cocaine off of toilet seats.” Scripps News

This remark was made in the context of explaining why Kennedy was not concerned about COVID-19 during the pandemic. His personal germ theory, as best as one can reconstruct it from the available evidence, appears to be that surviving one’s own substance use constitutes adequate immunological preparation for novel respiratory pathogens. This is not, to my knowledge, a position currently supported by the scientific literature, though I acknowledge that the scientific literature is not Kennedy’s primary reference material.

Almost four decades ago, a Supreme Court nomination was derailed because the nominee had smoked marijuana. In 2026, a health secretary saying he used to snort cocaine off of toilet seats barely generated news coverage. MS NOW

I mention this not to mock Kennedy’s past struggles — addiction is a serious disease and his recovery is genuinely commendable — but because the juxtaposition is both factually relevant and historically instructive. The man who does not believe in the germ theory of disease and who cited toilet seat cocaine as evidence of his robust immunity is the same man who fired seventeen independent scientific experts and replaced them with people who share his views. The personal and the institutional are not separate stories. They are the same story.

What the Judge Found

U.S. District Judge Brian E. Murphy at his Senate Judiciary Committee confirmation hearing, April 17, 2024. On March 16, 2026, Judge Murphy blocked Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s overhaul of America's childhood vaccine schedule, finding HHS had acted "contrary to law." (C-SPAN / Senate Judiciary Committee)

Judge Murphy’s ruling was not a close call dressed up in judicial hedging. It was a systematic demolition of the legal arguments the Trump administration had advanced in Kennedy’s defence, delivered with the kind of clarity that makes for uncomfortable reading in the HHS communications office.

The administration’s central argument was audacious in its sweep. Their lawyer, Isaac Belfer, argued that Kennedy and his committee possessed what he called “unreviewable” authority — effectively absolute power — to make vaccine policy, even if that included recommending that Americans become infected with measles rather than vaccinated against it.

Judge Murphy’s response to this argument was three words: “Suffice it to say.”

“Suffice it to say that the Court disagrees,” he wrote, a formulation that in judicial prose carries roughly the same weight as a doctor’s expression of withering contempt for a patient who has presented a TikTok video as a second opinion.

The ruling went further. The vaccine committee, Murphy found, has historically made decisions through careful review of scientific evidence — “a method scientific in nature and codified into law through procedural requirements.” But “unfortunately, the government has disregarded those methods and thereby undermined the integrity of its actions.”

On the January 2026 schedule change — O’Neill’s unilateral reduction of recommended vaccines from seventeen to eleven — the judge was equally direct: “It is undisputed that Director O’Neill issued the January 2026 Memo without sufficiently consulting ACIP. Therefore, he lacked authority to issue the January 2026 Memo and, in so doing, acted contrary to law. The CDC cannot simply bypass ACIP in altering the immunization schedules.”

The government had removed all duly appointed ACIP members and summarily replaced them without undertaking any of the rigorous screening that had been the hallmark of ACIP member selection for decades. Axios

The lawsuit was brought by six medical organizations including the American Academy of Pediatrics, which filed the original suit in July 2025 after Kennedy announced via social media that COVID vaccines would no longer be recommended for healthy children or pregnant women. The suit expanded as Kennedy’s actions accumulated. An amicus brief representing more than a hundred public health experts and organizations was filed in February. More than a dozen states including California and Arizona brought their own lawsuit in February, urging the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California to take action, with states emphasizing the harm in deaths and diseases from dropping vaccination rates, costs for public health, and implementation costs. Bloomberg Government

The plaintiffs’ lawyer, Richard Hughes, put it with appropriate precision: “This is a significant victory for public health, evidence-based medicine, the rule of law, and the American people.”

The Amateur Pilots

Among the most quotable passages in the ruling, and in the broader medical community’s response to Kennedy’s tenure, is an observation from Dr. Tina Hartert of the American Thoracic Society: “Allowing the panelists to make decisions about vaccines is just as reckless as letting a group of amateur pilots dictate how our airplanes should fly. The stakes in both scenarios are life and death.”

This analogy deserves to sit with the reader for a moment. When a commercial aircraft takes off from Pearson International Airport in Toronto — as many do daily, en route to destinations across the continent — no one on board is concerned that the flight deck is staffed by enthusiastic amateurs who simply feel very strongly that conventional aviation physics is overrated. The pilots have licences. They have training. They have logged thousands of hours. Their credentials have been reviewed by people whose own credentials have been reviewed by other people. The entire system is built on the recognition that certain decisions — decisions where the consequence of error is mass death — require rigorously verified expertise, not sincerely held conviction.

Kennedy dismantled the equivalent system for American vaccine policy and replaced it with sincerely held conviction. The courts have now intervened. The planes have been briefly grounded while the FAA sorts out who is actually qualified to fly them.

A Note From Canada

I am writing this from Toronto, Ontario, where we have had a functioning universal health care system since 1966 and where our equivalent of ACIP — the National Advisory Committee on Immunization — is staffed by immunologists, infectious disease specialists, epidemiologists, and public health scientists, none of whom have publicly expressed the view that polio vaccines should be optional because polio is not as dangerous as it used to be.

I will let the irony of that last clause stand without elaboration.

What I will note is that the countries with the highest life expectancy and the lowest rates of vaccine-preventable disease have, virtually without exception, strong public health institutions staffed by people with relevant scientific expertise, operating under legal frameworks that require evidence-based deliberation. This is not a coincidence. It is not a globalist conspiracy. It is the accumulated result of a century of trial, error, and hard scientific learning about how human immune systems work and how societies protect themselves from infectious disease.

Kennedy’s view — that this apparatus is itself the problem, that the experts are the enemy, that the toilet seat survivor’s instincts are a more reliable guide to public health than the peer-reviewed literature — is now, as of this morning, a view that a federal court has found to be arbitrary, capricious, and contrary to law.

We have been watching from Canada. We are relieved. We are not surprised.

What Happens Next

Photographer: Dwayne Senior/Bloomberg

The ruling will be appealed. The Trump administration has made clear throughout this process that it regards judicial oversight of executive health policy as an inconvenience rather than a constitutional feature, and there is no reason to expect it to accept this decision gracefully. The appeal will move through the First Circuit, and the Supreme Court — whose current composition has shown considerable appetite for executive authority arguments — remains the ultimate arbiter.

But the ruling matters regardless of its eventual fate on appeal, for two reasons.

First, it establishes a legal framework. Judge Murphy has now articulated, with considerable specificity, exactly where the limits of the HHS secretary’s authority lie. The “unreviewable authority” argument has been rejected not merely as unpersuasive but as so clearly wrong that the judge summarized his dismissal in five words. Every subsequent legal challenge to Kennedy’s vaccine agenda will cite this ruling. Every appellate judge who reviews the case will begin from a record that includes a detailed accounting of what the government did and why it was unlawful.

Second, it documents the record. This series has been building that record since August. The courts are now building it alongside us. The firing of seventeen experts. The replacement with ideological allies. The social media announcements substituting for scientific deliberation. The reduction of seventeen vaccines to eleven by memo. The chair who thinks polio vaccines might be optional. All of it is now in a federal court record, described in judicial prose, with findings of fact and conclusions of law.

History is taking notes.

The Conclusion This Series Has Been Building Toward

Six essays. Eight months. One consistent argument: that Kennedy’s dismantling of American public health infrastructure is not merely a healthcare policy dispute but a national security crisis, an institutional crisis, and a crisis of democratic accountability — made possible by a president who chose to enable it and a Congress that chose to watch.

The courts have now said, with considerable clarity, that the rule of law has not yet been entirely evacuated from this domain. That matters. It matters for the children whose parents will now receive accurate vaccine recommendations rather than the ideology of a man who established his immunity credentials on a podcast. It matters for the institutions that took generations to build and that Kennedy spent a year systematically dismantling. It matters for the proposition — not yet abandoned, apparently — that federal officials must follow the law even when they find it inconvenient.

Saturday Night Live got the laugh. Judge Murphy got the ruling.

Two days. Same target. Different weapons.

Both landed.
