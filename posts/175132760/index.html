The Baseline Problem

Part 5: “How Many Flashes of Insanity Should We Tolerate?"

October 2, 2025

From Expert Warnings to Governor’s Ultimatum

October 2, 2025

In June, inspired by Lawrence O’Donnell’s question “If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?”, I began documenting the baseline problem—how Trump’s abnormal cognitive starting point masks further decline. In August, I showed how that decline had escalated to institutional destruction: nuclear submarines deployed over tweets, statistical agencies gutted for reporting inconvenient data.

Two months later, we have our answer to a different question—the one O’Donnell posed October 1st: “How many flashes of insanity from a president should we tolerate?”

The answer came from an unexpected source: Illinois Governor JB Pritzker, standing in Chicago’s Englewood neighborhood on September 30, becoming the first sitting governor in American history to publicly demand invocation of the 25th Amendment. “There is something genuinely wrong with this man,” Pritzker declared, “and the 25th Amendment ought to be invoked.”

What prompted this unprecedented step? A 70-minute address Trump delivered to 800 generals and admirals hastily summoned to Quantico Marine Corps Base, where he proposed using American cities as military “training grounds” against what he called “the enemy within.”

We’re no longer in the realm of concerning speech patterns and temporal confusion. We’ve entered the territory where sitting governors use clinical language—”dementia”—and retired four-star generals describe presidential addresses as “incoherent” and “stupid.”

The Quantico Speech: When Military Leadership Goes Silent

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth summoned approximately 800 generals, admirals, and senior enlisted advisors from around the world on days’ notice for what sources described as the first-ever such “all-call.” What they witnessed wasn’t merely concerning—it was, in retired Gen. Barry McCaffrey’s assessment, “one of the most bizarre, unsettling events I’ve ever encountered.”

Trump’s proposal was explicit: “I told Pete, we should use some of these dangerous cities as training grounds for our military—National Guard, but military—because we’re going into Chicago very soon.” He identified the enemy: cities “run by radical left Democrats” where forces would operate against Americans engaged in “a war from within.”

His framing treated domestic political opposition as enemy combatants: “We’re under invasion from within. No different than a foreign enemy, but more difficult in many ways because they don’t wear uniforms. At least when they’re wearing a uniform, you can take them out.”

This rhetoric follows a historical pattern documented in authoritarian movements. The concept of an internal enemy—more dangerous than external threats because they “don’t wear uniforms”—echoes the fascist framing that justified purges and emergency powers throughout the 20th century. When leaders claim the nation faces an existential threat from within its own population, it creates justification for suspending normal legal constraints. The “enemy within” construct transforms political opposition into existential threat, ordinary citizens into potential traitors, and democratic disagreement into warfare requiring military response.

Pritzker, whose Illinois Holocaust Museum work involved studying such patterns, made this connection explicit: Trump is “copying tactics of Vladimir Putin.” He could have added that Putin’s playbook draws from an older authoritarian tradition—one that begins by identifying internal populations as enemies, frames cities as hostile territory within one’s own borders, and proposes military solutions to political problems.

The reaction from 800 of America’s top military leaders? Complete silence.

Trump himself remarked on it: “I’ve never walked into a room so silent before. This is very interesting.” He had explicitly invited applause, adding the threat: “if you don’t like what I’m saying, you can leave the room. Of course, there goes your rank, there goes your future.”

Three defense officials told The Intercept the speech was “embarrassing.” McCaffrey went further on MSNBC’s Morning Joe: The president “sounded incoherent, exhausted, rabidly partisan, at times stupid, meandering, couldn’t hold a thought together.”

Read that again. A retired four-star general—someone institutionally predisposed to respect the office—used the word “stupid” to describe a sitting commander-in-chief’s address to military leadership.

Pritzker’s Line in the Sand

Governor Pritzker’s response the next day wasn’t just political pushback. It was a medical and authoritarian alarm: “It appears that Donald Trump not only has dementia set in, but he’s copying tactics of Vladimir Putin. Sending troops into cities, thinking that that’s some sort of proving ground for war, or that indeed there’s some sort of internal war going on in the United States is just, frankly, inane and I’m concerned for his health.”

The Putin comparison was deliberate. Pritzker founded the Illinois Holocaust Museum and has studied authoritarian tactics for decades. Throughout September, he’d been escalating warnings: “Authoritarians want control. And every time they get someone to act in an appeasing fashion, they make one step toward control. This is the playbook, folks.”

Chicago Mayor Brandon Johnson joined him, calling Trump “unstable,” “unhinged,” and “double-minded.” Nineteen Democratic governors issued a coordinated statement. But here’s what’s telling: not a single Republican official addressed the 25th Amendment substance. They attacked Pritzker’s tone, but no one defended Trump’s cognitive fitness.

The silence speaks volumes.

O’Donnell’s Zero Tolerance Framework

Lawrence O’Donnell’s October 1 segment represented something I predicted in June would eventually happen: mainstream media abandoning euphemisms for clinical language.

His opening was stark: “How many flashes of insanity from a president should we tolerate? That sounds like something that deserves a zero tolerance policy, and we have never seen public flashes of insanity from any president other than Donald Trump. We now see them every day.”

He didn’t hedge: “The president has lost his mind. That is the clearest explanation for what Donald Trump is doing.”

O’Donnell connected the Quantico address to Trump’s simultaneous behavior during government shutdown negotiations—posting AI-generated videos of Hakeem Jeffries wearing a sombrero with fake mustache. When JD Vance defended this as Trump “having a little bit of fun while he is working,” O’Donnell’s response cut through: “JD Vance could not explain the joke. Jokes never have to be explained. JD Vance had to explain that one and couldn’t, because it’s not a joke.”

More significantly, O’Donnell noted Trump remained invisible on October 1—the day the government shut down—while Vance conducted the primary White House briefing. He called it potentially “25th Amendment day,” with Vance “deliberately” drawing “attention to Donald Trump’s public insanity by actively seeking out an opportunity to talk publicly about Donald Trump’s manifestations of outright insanity.”

This is the media evolution I documented in Parts 1-4. Eventually, the baseline problem breaks down when symptoms become too extreme to normalize. We’ve reached that point.

The Physical Evidence: September 11

Trump’s appearance at the Pentagon’s September 11 memorial provided visual documentation. Photographs showed the right side of his face appearing to droop—classic stroke symptom where left-brain blood flow compromise produces right-side facial changes.

Social media exploded. Ben Meiselas of MeidasTouch observed: “Trump looked absolutely awful... His face was very droopy, he seemed addled, like he didn’t even know where he was.” The White House called stroke speculation “ridiculous” but issued no medical statement. Trump appeared normal on Fox and Friends the next day, suggesting either temporary condition or favorable lighting.

More concerning was the chronic hand bruising documented since February. At the September 11 event, visible bruising appeared despite heavy makeup attempts. The White House attributes this to chronic venous insufficiency, but the progression and concealment efforts—hands “heavily caked in makeup” after absences, kept under tables at dinners—suggest awareness that optics are problematic.

Trump also forgot his own inauguration year during a September joint appearance with Benjamin Netanyahu, stating his presidency “officially began” in 2016. It began January 20, 2017. This is not a matter of interpretation—it’s on his resume.

The Expert Consensus Hardens

Four experts with relevant credentials issued assessments during this period that converge on alarming conclusions:

Dr. John Gartner (former Johns Hopkins psychiatry faculty): “There is absolutely no doubt Trump has dementia.” His September analysis detailed “major deterioration in functioning in language and thinking and psychomotor performance and impulse control,” predicting Trump will “fall off the cognitive cliff” before his term ends.

Harry Segal (Cornell psychology, Weill Cornell psychiatry): “What’s alarming is how the rate of Trump’s bizarre speech and political decisions have been increasing.” After viewing the September 11 Pentagon footage: “When you see someone with half their face drooping like that, that’s not just someone being tired, that’s not a normal face.”

Dr. Jeffrey Kuhlman (former White House physician for Clinton, Bush, Obama, Biden): “He exhibits cognitive decline in reasoning, memory, and processing speed, consistent with his advanced age.” Kuhlman emphasized that screening tests like the MoCA—on which Trump claims perfect scores—are insufficient for someone with “the most powerful position in the free world.”

Dr. Jamie Reilly (Temple University speech-language pathologist) takes a more cautious position, explicitly warning against remote diagnosis while acknowledging observable speech pattern changes.

The convergence is the story. These aren’t partisan talking heads—they’re credentialed experts willing to go on record with clinical assessments.

The Polling Shift: Americans Are Noticing

YouGov polling from September 2-4 shows 49% of Americans now believe Trump is experiencing cognitive decline—up from 40% just months earlier. More striking: Independent voters showed a 17-point increase, with 51% now perceiving decline.

Physical decline perceptions are even higher: 59% believe Trump is suffering physical decline. Crucially, 63% say Trump’s health and age affect his ability to perform duties—up from 56% pre-election.

This isn’t partisan noise. This is half the country—including the crucial Independent bloc—observing what experts have been documenting.

The Vance Elevation: Who’s Actually Governing?

Vice President Vance’s September-October visibility represents a marked departure from typical vice-presidential patterns. He led the September 29 shutdown meeting with congressional leaders. On October 1, he conducted the primary White House briefing—an unusual arrangement during crisis.

This coincided with Trump’s reduced visibility. From August 26 to September 2, Trump had no public appearances for a full week. His September 12 speech was widely described as rambling and incoherent. During the October 1 shutdown—traditionally a presidential moment—Trump remained invisible while Vance briefed the press.

Vance’s August statement that he was “ready to become president” now reads differently in context.

What’s Different Now

In June, I documented cognitive confusion about Fed Chair appointments and constitutional basics. In August, that confusion escalated to institutional destruction—BLS firings, nuclear deployments over tweets.

Now, in September-October, we have:

  • A sitting governor publicly demanding 25th Amendment invocation

  • A retired four-star general calling a presidential address “incoherent” and “stupid”

  • Mainstream media using “insanity” and “deranged” in official coverage

  • Half of Americans perceiving cognitive decline

  • The Vice President assuming primary crisis communication roles

  • Military leadership responding with silence to proposals for domestic deployment

The baseline problem was always about whether democratic institutions could recognize and address cognitive decline when traditional markers fail. We now have recognition—from governors, generals, media, and half the public.

What we don’t have is a mechanism for action. The 25th Amendment requires either presidential cooperation or two-thirds of Congress after the vice president and cabinet majority declare incapacity. With Republican control and cabinet loyalty, Pritzker’s call is a warning, not a remedy.

The Acceleration Question

The term both Gartner and Segal emphasize is “accelerating.” Not just declining—accelerating. The clustering of September-October incidents suggests increasing frequency: the Quantico address, the facial drooping, the forgotten inauguration year, Vance’s elevation, Pritzker’s ultimatum, O’Donnell’s clinical language, polling shifts—all within six weeks.

Trump turns 80 in June 2026. At that age, one in six Americans has dementia. His father had Alzheimer’s. The chronic venous insufficiency indicates cardiovascular issues affecting cerebral blood flow. The pattern isn’t subtle anymore.

O’Donnell’s question—”How many flashes of insanity should we tolerate?”—demands an answer. He suggests zero. Pritzker acted on that principle by calling for 25th Amendment invocation. Half of Americans are watching the same symptoms experts document.

The experts who warned in 2017 that cognitive decline “could only go in one direction” have been proven right. The question isn’t whether Trump displays concerning symptoms—49% of Americans believe he does. The question is whether institutional mechanisms exist to address cognitive decline when neither party nor administration will acknowledge the evidence.

We’re no longer managing potential decline. We’re watching active deterioration in real-time, documented by governors, generals, physicians, psychologists, pollsters, and our own eyes.

The timeline isn’t theoretical anymore. It’s operational. And it’s accelerating.
