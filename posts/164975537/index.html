The Myth of the Tariff Golden Age

A Historical and Economic Analysis

Episode 3: The Progressive Revolution - Why Income Taxes Replaced Tariffs

June 12, 2025

Welcome back to "Trump Tariff and Trade Thursdays." Over the past two weeks, we've deconstructed Trump's "golden age" mythology and examined the real costs of Gilded Age tariffs. This week, we're tackling the 1913 "mistake" that Trump claims burdened ordinary Americans: the introduction of federal income taxation.

Trump's narrative portrays the shift from tariffs to income taxes as a betrayal of the American people—a move from taxing foreigners to taxing citizens. This story is not just wrong; it's completely backwards. The 1913 tax reform was one of the most progressive policy changes in American history.

The Crisis of Tariff Dependence

By the early 20th century, the tariff-dependent revenue system was breaking down under multiple pressures:

Revenue Instability: Tariff revenues fluctuated wildly with economic cycles. During the Panic of 1893, tariff revenues fell by 20% just as unemployment soared and demands for government intervention increased. This pro-cyclical pattern made fiscal planning nearly impossible.

Increasing Government Responsibilities: The federal government was assuming new roles—food safety regulation after Upton Sinclair's "The Jungle," antitrust enforcement, conservation efforts, and infrastructure development. Tariff revenues couldn't fund these expanding responsibilities.

International Economic Integration: As America became more integrated into global markets, unilateral protectionism became increasingly counterproductive. Other nations developed sophisticated retaliatory capacities that could devastate American exporters.

Growing Inequality: The regressive nature of tariffs was becoming politically unsustainable as Progressive Era reformers highlighted how protection enriched industrial magnates while burdening working families.

The Progressive Reform Movement

The push for income taxation emerged from Progressive Era reformers who explicitly sought to create a more equitable tax system. This wasn't about expanding government power—it was about shifting tax burdens from working families to those with the greatest capacity to pay.

Leading reformers like Robert La Follette, William Jennings Bryan, and even some Republicans like Nelson Aldrich recognized that tariffs imposed higher effective tax rates on the poor than the rich. They argued that a modern industrial economy required a tax system based on ability to pay rather than consumption patterns.

The Sixteenth Amendment and Constitutional Authority

The Sixteenth Amendment, ratified in 1913, provided explicit constitutional authority for federal income taxation, overturning the Supreme Court's 1895 decision in Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co. This constitutional change wasn't a power grab—it was a response to legal obstacles that had prevented earlier tax reform efforts.

The amendment's ratification reflected broad popular support for progressive taxation. It passed Congress with overwhelming bipartisan majorities and was ratified by 42 of 48 states, including many agricultural states that bore disproportionate tariff burdens.

The 1913 Tax: Targeting the Wealthy, Not "The People"

The Underwood-Simmons Tariff Act of 1913, signed by President Woodrow Wilson, simultaneously lowered tariff rates and introduced a progressive income tax. The design explicitly contradicts Trump's claim that it burdened ordinary Americans:

Extremely Limited Scope: The income tax applied only to individuals earning more than $3,000 annually ($4,000 for married couples). In 1913 dollars, this threshold exempted approximately 97% of American households from any income taxation.

Progressive Rate Structure: Tax rates began at just 1% and increased gradually to 7% for the highest earners. The top rate applied only to incomes above $500,000—roughly equivalent to $15 million today.

Wealth Concentration: The tax specifically targeted the industrial elite who had benefited most from tariff protection. Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, and other magnates who had built fortunes behind tariff walls now faced modest taxes on their enormous incomes.

Revenue Replacement: The income tax initially raised only about 10% of federal revenue, supplementing rather than replacing other sources. This gradual transition allowed economic adjustment while maintaining fiscal stability.

Who Actually Paid?

Contemporary records reveal exactly who bore the burden of early income taxation:

  • Less than 3% of Americans filed income tax returns in 1913

  • The top 1% of earners paid over 80% of income tax revenue

  • Typical working families saw their tax burden decrease as tariff rates fell

  • Agricultural states particularly benefited from reduced tariff costs

This wasn't taxation of "the people"—it was the first systematic attempt to tax concentrated wealth in American history.

Economic Benefits of Tax Reform

The shift from tariffs to income taxes created multiple economic advantages:

Reduced Consumer Costs: Lower tariff rates decreased prices on imported goods, effectively cutting taxes on necessities for working families.

Economic Efficiency: Reduced trade barriers allowed resources to flow toward more productive uses, increasing overall economic welfare.

Revenue Stability: Income tax revenues proved more stable than tariff collections, providing reliable funding for expanding government services.

International Relations: Reduced tariffs improved trade relationships and reduced pressures for foreign retaliation against American exports.

The Real Mistake: Not Going Further

Rather than being a mistake, the 1913 reform's limitation was its modesty. Progressive reformers wanted even higher taxes on concentrated wealth and deeper tariff reductions. Political compromise limited the scope of change, allowing tariff protection to persist in many industries.

World War I would soon demonstrate the wisdom of income tax implementation. War financing required massive revenue increases that tariffs could never have provided. The progressive tax system established in 1913 proved essential for funding America's emergence as a global power.

Trump's Backward History

Trump's claim that 1913 marked a shift from taxing foreigners to taxing Americans reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of tariff economics. Tariffs were never paid by foreigners—they were consumption taxes imposed on American consumers. The 1913 reform shifted taxation from a regressive system that burdened working families to a progressive system that targeted concentrated wealth.

We'll see how protectionist overreach triggered international retaliation, collapsed global trade, and deepened the Great Depression—providing a clear preview of what happens when tariff mythology meets economic reality.
