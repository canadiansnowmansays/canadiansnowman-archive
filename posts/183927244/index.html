The Counterfactual President

The Justice Department

Part 3: Independence or Weaponization

April 3, 2026

On January 20, 2025, within hours of being sworn in, Donald Trump issued blanket pardons for over 1,500 January 6th defendants. Not case-by-case review. Not mercy for nonviolent first-time offenders. Blanket pardons — including for those who assaulted police officers, who planned the attack in advance, who came armed and organized.

Within weeks, the career prosecutors who had spent years building those cases — following evidence, applying law, doing exactly what prosecutors are supposed to do — were fired or reassigned. U.S. Attorneys across the country received a signal so clear it required no written memo: loyalty to Trump was now the criterion for keeping your job. Investigating Trump had been a career-ending move.

Within months, the Justice Department that generations of Americans had understood as serving the Constitution was openly serving the President. Personally. Politically. Vindictively.

This is what complete weaponization looks like. We’ve watched it for twelve months.

Here’s the question this essay examines: What does Justice Department independence actually look like when it’s allowed to exist — and what did we actually watch during Trump’s first year back?

There is an alternative reality we can examine alongside the one we lived. One where the Attorney General reported to the Constitution rather than to Mar-a-Lago. One where prosecutors followed evidence rather than presidential Truth Social posts. One where the principle that no one is above the law actually meant something.

That’s not fantasy. That’s how the Justice Department functioned under presidents of both parties for generations. It’s not innovation — it’s restoration of normalcy.

Let’s examine what that first year could have looked like.

The TACO Pattern: How DOJ Collapsed This Year

I’ve documented what I call the TACO framework — the four-stage pattern of institutional collapse under authoritarian pressure:

Threaten: The leader signals that loyalty matters more than law.

Accommodate: Officials bend rules, ignore violations, rationalize the unthinkable to keep their positions.

Capitulate: The pretense of independence collapses. The institution becomes a tool of personal power.

Obfuscate: The complicit rewrite history, deny reality, and gaslight anyone who remembers how things used to work.

The Justice Department completed this cycle during Trump’s first year back. But here’s what’s crucial: The TACO pattern requires a specific catalyst. It doesn’t activate spontaneously. It requires leadership that views institutional independence as betrayal rather than strength.

We watched the catalyst activate immediately upon Trump’s return to power. Within weeks, career prosecutors who had investigated Trump were reassigned or fired. Within months, U.S. Attorneys were replaced with loyalists. By the end of year one, the Justice Department had been reshaped into what Trump always wanted: his personal legal apparatus.

But remove that catalyst — remove leadership that demands personal loyalty over constitutional duty — and the TACO pattern doesn’t engage. Under President Harris, the signal from the top would have been fundamentally different: Do your job. Follow the law. Serve the Constitution, not me personally.

That’s not speculation. That’s documented pattern across her entire career as prosecutor, Attorney General of California, Senator, and Vice President.

What Trump Did in His First Year Back

Trump wasn’t subtle about his intentions, and his first year delivered on every threat:

Career prosecutors were systematically purged. Anyone who participated in investigations of Trump — the classified documents case, the January 6th investigation, election interference cases — was fired or reassigned within the first six months. The message became crystal clear: investigating Trump had been a career-ending move.

Loyalty oaths replaced legal standards. U.S. Attorneys were required to demonstrate personal loyalty to Trump, not commitment to law. Those who expressed any independence were removed. Those who remained understood what was expected.

DOGE dismantled oversight mechanisms. Beyond DOJ itself, the broader assault on institutional accountability accelerated throughout the year. Inspectors General were fired or sidelined. The oversight architecture that provided checks on executive power was systematically hollowed out — not through legislation, but through executive action and defunding, leaving career officials without the institutional backing to do their jobs.

The enemies list was operationalized. Trump’s threats weren’t idle. The Justice Department spent its first year investigating, harassing, and in some cases prosecuting people Trump viewed as enemies. The standard wasn’t evidence — it was presidential vendetta.

January 6th prosecutions were sabotaged. The over 1,000 cases against insurrectionists were systematically undermined throughout the year. Mass pardons came within weeks of inauguration. Evidence of coordination at higher levels was buried. The truth of that day was memory-holed through political interference.

This is what weaponization looked like across twelve months. Real weaponization is what we watched this past year: the Justice Department systematically redirected toward Trump’s personal and political interests.

And here’s the part that should terrify everyone: It was all legal. The Attorney General serves at the President’s pleasure. U.S. Attorneys can be replaced. Investigations can be opened or closed based on DOJ priorities. The law provides enormous discretion — discretion that has historically been constrained by norms, by institutional culture, by the understanding that weaponizing justice destroys democracy.

Trump spent a year proving he doesn’t care about those constraints. He views them as weakness to be exploited.

The Bondi Postscript: When Loyalty Isn’t Enough

As this essay is readied for publication, breaking news has delivered the perfect coda to everything written above.

Today, Donald Trump fired Attorney General Pam Bondi.

Not for being too independent. Not for prioritizing the Constitution over the President. Not for maintaining any institutional standards.

Bondi was fired for being insufficiently ruthless.

She was a loyalist from the start — defending Trump during his first impeachment, confirmed as Attorney General on a party-line Senate vote. She opened investigations into Trump’s political enemies, hollowed out the career workforce, gutted the Civil Rights Division, and dismantled the independence of the institution she led. By any traditional standard, she was a deeply compromised Attorney General.

It wasn’t enough.

Trump grew frustrated that the retribution campaign wasn’t delivering results. The Justice Department had secured indictments against former FBI Director James Comey and New York Attorney General Letitia James — but both cases were thrown out after a judge ruled the prosecutor was illegally serving. The Epstein files saga compounded the damage: Bondi had claimed a client list was “sitting on my desk right now to review,” only for the department to assert later that no such list existed. Even Trump’s own White House chief of staff publicly said she had “completely whiffed.”

Trump’s public statement called her “a Great American Patriot and a loyal friend.” His private assessment, per multiple sources: she hadn’t “executed on his vision.”

She will be replaced, at least temporarily, by Todd Blanche — Trump’s former personal criminal defense attorney. The man who defended Trump against felony charges will now lead the department responsible for bringing felony charges against others.

Read that again slowly.

This is what the TACO framework produces at its endpoint. Loyalty alone is no longer sufficient. The institution doesn’t just become a tool — it becomes a competition to see who can wield that tool most aggressively on the President’s behalf. Officials who accommodate fully but fail to deliver results are as disposable as those who resist.

Pam Bondi’s dismissal makes her the second cabinet member to lose her job in recent weeks.Credit...Eric Lee for The New York Times

One former DOJ attorney summarized Bondi’s tenure with devastating precision: “Pam Bondi took a sledgehammer to the Justice Department and its workforce. DOJ’s independence, integrity, and workforce have degraded more under her leadership than at any other time during the department’s 155-year history. What she destroyed in a year could take decades to rebuild. But we have a President who fired her because she didn’t go far enough.”

That last sentence is the one that should keep you up at night.

The Counterfactual: What Year One Under Harris Would Have Looked Like

Now imagine someone like Merrick Garland continued, Vanita Gupta, or Lisa Monaco as Attorney General throughout this first year under President Harris. Career prosecutors with institutional commitment, constitutional fidelity, and understanding that the Justice Department’s legitimacy depends on independence from political pressure.

What would that year have looked like?

January 6th prosecutions would have continued based on evidence. Not because Harris demanded them, but because DOJ would have followed facts. The over 1,000 prosecutions already initiated would have proceeded without political interference. If evidence had emerged of coordination at higher levels — among lawmakers, rally organizers, executive branch officials — career prosecutors would have investigated it.

No revenge prosecutions. Trump wouldn’t have been investigated because Harris wanted payback. If evidence from pre-existing investigations supported prosecution, those decisions would have been made by career prosecutors and a potentially continuing Special Counsel. The firewall between White House and DOJ that Trump demolished would have been rebuilt and reinforced.

Inspectors General could have inspected. The watchdogs Trump fired would have been restored or replaced with empowered successors. The oversight architecture that DOGE dismantled would have remained intact. Accountability mechanisms would have worked. You cannot have rule of law when the institutions designed to enforce it have been defunded and defanged.

But Harris would have faced intense political pressures throughout that first year: Republican accusations of weaponizing justice. Progressive pressure to prosecute Trump and allies aggressively. Media framing any prosecution as partisan revenge. Legal challenges at every stage. The line between accountability and perceived persecution would have been contested constantly.

This would have been rule of law — the principle that violent attempts to overturn democratic elections must face consequences.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, January 6th was forgotten and rewarded. Under Harris, it would have been prosecuted.

And we spent a year watching it forgotten and rewarded.

The Standard: Evidence, Not Loyalty

Here’s the fundamental difference we could measure after one year: What happened when credible allegations of misconduct emerged against administration officials?

Under Trump’s first year back: Depended entirely on loyalty. Officials loyal to Trump saw allegations ignored, buried, or investigated perfunctorily. Officials who showed any independence saw allegations become pretexts for persecution regardless of evidence.

Under Harris’s first year: Career prosecutors would have evaluated allegations based on evidence. If credible and supported by facts, investigations would have proceeded. If baseless, dismissed. The standard would have been evidence, consistently applied.

This isn’t about Harris officials being more ethical than Trump officials. It’s about having a functioning justice system that treats everyone equally under law.

The point isn’t ensuring Trump faced prosecution. The point is ensuring decisions were made based on law, not on what the President wanted.

That’s what independence means. And we spent a year watching its complete absence.

The Career Prosecutor’s Dilemma Over Twelve Months

Put yourself in the position of a career federal prosecutor during Trump’s first year back.

You spent 15 years building a career. You prosecuted gang violence, public corruption, financial fraud — ethically, by the book, based on evidence. You have a mortgage, kids’ college funds, retirement plans.

What you experienced this past year under Trump: You received signals — subtle at first, then explicit — that investigating certain people was forbidden and investigating other people was demanded. You watched colleagues fired for maintaining traditional standards. You saw promotions go to those who accommodated. You calculated whether keeping your integrity was worth losing your career.

Most people aren’t heroes. Most people have families to support. Most people accommodated this year. That’s not cowardice — it’s survival in a system designed to punish integrity.

What you would have experienced under Harris: A different signal: Do your job. Follow the evidence. Apply the law equally. Your career depends on competence, not political loyalty.

That’s why institutional leadership matters more than individual ethics. Good people made bad choices this year in Trump’s corrupt system. Decent people would have maintained integrity in Harris’s functional system.

The signal from the top determined which system we lived in this past year.

The Pardon Power: Accountability Erased

What Trump did in his first year back:

Mass pardons for January 6th defendants came within weeks of inauguration — including those who assaulted police officers. Pardons for anyone prosecuted for Trump-related crimes followed throughout the year. Loyalty to Trump guaranteed immunity.

This transformed pardon power from mercy into obstruction. It enabled future criminality by guaranteeing no consequences for Trump allies.

The counterfactual for Harris’s first year: Harris would have used pardon power traditionally — case-by-case review, consideration of rehabilitation and remorse, focus on nonviolent offenders serving excessive sentences. No pardons for political allies.

The difference: Under Trump’s year one, pardons obstructed justice. Under Harris’s year one, pardons would have served justice.

And we spent a year watching obstruction.

The Enemies List: A Year of Weaponized Prosecution

Trump maintained his extensive enemies list throughout year one: journalists, prosecutors, judges, election officials, former staff who testified against him, members of Congress who investigated him, whistleblowers.

What happened during that year: The Justice Department was directed to investigate Trump’s enemies — Jack Smith faced investigations, Liz Cheney was targeted, Adam Schiff faced harassment, journalists were investigated, judges faced threats, election officials were targeted.

These investigations weren’t based on probable cause or evidence. They were based on Trump’s desire for revenge — textbook authoritarian capture: using law enforcement to punish opposition and deter future accountability efforts.

The counterfactual: Under Harris, the enemies list wouldn’t have existed. DOJ would have followed evidence, not grudges. Only actual crimes would have been crimes.

And we spent a year watching opposition prosecuted.

The Accommodation Psychology Framework: A Year of Acceleration

When crimes at the highest levels face no consequences, everyone recalculates what’s possible. Officials who might have maintained boundaries now accommodate. Those who might have spoken up now stay silent.

What we watched during year one: The accommodation cascade accelerated across all twelve months. Every unpunished crime made the next one easier. Every pardoned crony emboldened others. The TACO pattern spread: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. The end state after one year was severe collapse of institutional integrity — not total, but enough to make the system functionally unreliable.

The Bondi firing today is the accommodation cascade reaching its logical conclusion: even total loyalists who hollow out the institution on the President’s behalf are disposable when results aren’t aggressive enough. The demand doesn’t plateau. It escalates.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, the opposite pattern would have emerged: accountability reinforcing boundaries. Officials maintaining standards because violations faced consequences. Whistleblowers speaking up because protection existed.

This is how systems heal rather than collapse.

We spent a year watching the opposite.

The International Dimension: What the World Watched

From my Canadian perspective, I watched American accountability erode over twelve months with dread.

When the world’s most powerful democracy demonstrated across a full year that crimes at the highest levels face no consequences, that violent coup attempts are pardoned and forgotten, that rule of law is negotiable based on who holds power — it sent a message to every authoritarian and every democrat worldwide.

To authoritarians after year one: Democracy is weak. Rule of law is pretense. Power is all that matters.

To democrats after year one: America can’t be counted on. Democratic norms are tissue paper. Constitutional systems collapse easily.

The counterfactual would have sent different signals: Democracy is resilient. Rule of law holds even under pressure. Accountability applies regardless of power.

Which set of signals did the world learn after the year we just lived through?

The Republican Rule-of-Law Question After Year One

I return to Republican readers who claimed to believe in law and order, in accountability, in constitutional governance.

Trump spent his first year back pardoning those who carried out the violence of January 6th, firing those who prosecuted the cases, punishing those who held him accountable — and now firing his own Attorney General for failing to punish his enemies aggressively enough.

If you actually believed in rule of law, this past year should have horrified you. We watched accountability disappear systematically across twelve months — and we watched the Justice Department become not just a political weapon, but a weapon that requires constant sharpening lest it be discarded.

If you can’t answer honestly after watching this past year, you never believed in rule of law.

And we spent a year confirming you never believed it.

The Exhaustion of Documenting a Year of Unpunished Crimes

I’ve documented thousands of crimes, corrupt acts, constitutional violations this past year.

And none of it mattered. The crimes continued. The perpetrators faced no consequences. The system produced no accountability across twelve months.

The counterfactual offers what we missed: functional accountability across a year. Investigations that follow evidence. Prosecutions that proceed based on law. Consequences that apply regardless of power. The basic principle that crimes have costs.

That’s not utopian. That’s baseline rule of law. It’s what functioning democracies require.

And we spent a year watching it disappear — and watching the person responsible for its disappearance fired today for not making it disappear fast enough.
