The Iran Crisis

“The Sixth”

Part XXXVII Day Eight: Saturday, March 7, 2026

March 7, 2026

I. Dover

President Donald Trump steps off Air Force One with Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth Saturday March , 2026 at Dover AF Base, Del. (AP Photo Mark Scheifelbein)

On Saturday afternoon, President Trump traveled to Dover Air Force Base in Delaware to receive the flag-draped transfer cases of the first six Americans killed in the war with Iran. Melania Trump was at his side. Pete Hegseth and Marco Rubio were in the room.

Trump has attended Dover before. In his first term, he described the ceremonies as “the hardest thing I have to do, by far.” He spoke at rallies about the guttural cries of parents upon seeing a transfer case emerge from a military cargo plane. He called it “a very tough experience.”

Those visits honored Americans who died in wars he inherited. Afghanistan. Yemen. The accumulated debris of twenty years of other men’s decisions. He could stand at Dover and mean it, because the gap between the commander-in-chief and the cause of death was wide enough to hold genuine grief.

Saturday was different. The six Americans who came home to Dover died in the port of Shuaiba, Kuwait, killed by an Iranian drone strike on a position they had been ordered to defend in a war that was eight days old. Their names: Maj. Jeffrey R. O'Brien, Capt. Cody A. Khork, Chief Warrant Officer 3 Robert M. Marzan, Sgt. 1st Class Nicole M. Amor, Sgt. 1st Class Noah L. Tietjens, and Sgt. Declan J. Coady. Trump launched the war. He signed the orders. He posted "MIGA" on Truth Social. He told reporters that casualties were inevitable — "That's the way it is. Likely be more."

He was right. And now he was standing at the base to receive them.

Since the first deaths, his public language has shifted. After the first three casualties he posted a video saying, “Sadly, there will likely be more before it ends. That’s the way it is.” The condolence had become a forecast. The grief had become a cost-benefit line item. On Thursday, Time magazine asked him whether Americans should worry about Iranian retaliation on the homeland. “I guess,” he said. “We expect some things. Like I said, some people will die. When you go to war, some people will die.”

He was right about that too.

II. The Five Presidencies

Kareem Sadjadpour has been tracking Iran’s relationship with American power for decades. On Friday evening, on Washington Week with The Atlantic, he offered a formulation that cuts through eight days of tactical noise to something structural.

By his count, Iran has consumed five American presidencies.

The hostage crisis ended Jimmy Carter’s. Iran-Contra tainted Ronald Reagan’s. Iran systematically sabotaged the American enterprise in Iraq, consuming George W. Bush’s second term in a war that was supposed to last six days. The JCPOA nuclear deal consumed the second half of Barack Obama’s presidency. And October 7, 2023 — executed by Hamas, an Iranian proxy — produced two years of escalating conflict that consumed Joe Biden, who ended his presidency without resolution of anything he had started.

“And now President Trump.”

The formulation is not a prediction. It is a pattern. Iran has an extraordinary record of converting American ambition into American exhaustion. The question at Dover on Saturday afternoon was whether Trump understood that he was not ending this pattern by launching a war, but entering it — and whether the six men in the transfer cases would be the last Americans Iran would take from him, or the first.

Trump himself has articulated the argument for action clearly enough: Iran has been a nuisance for 47 years, and he intends to be the president who ends it. Sadjadpour grants the ambition its due weight. “If you are able to change the leaders in Iran and empower the people into some kind of representative government — or at a minimum a government which represents its own national interests rather than this revolutionary ideology of 1979 — that would be a geopolitical game-changer for the United States and a huge victory for President Trump.”

The word Sadjadpour uses, deliberately, is gambit.

III. The Report They Ignored

The National Intelligence Council completed an assessment in late February finding that even a large-scale military assault on Iran would be unlikely to topple its theocratic government. The CIA had separately reached the same conclusion: killing Khamenei would not produce regime change. There was wide agreement across the intelligence community that the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps is too deeply entrenched — too embedded in the economy, the security apparatus, and the social fabric — for a popular uprising to dislodge it.

The NIC report is part of Tulsi Gabbard’s Office of National Intelligence. It was completed before the war began. The war began anyway.

Peter Baker, the Times’s chief White House correspondent, put the “why now” question directly on Washington Week. “He did nothing to lay the groundwork. He did nothing to convince the Americans this is necessary to do now.” Baker’s point is that even a 47-year argument — the Islamic Republic has been killing Americans and undermining US interests since 1979, and it is finally time to end it — might have persuaded a skeptical public given proper preparation. Trump didn’t try. He posted an eight-minute video in the middle of the night with shifting rationales and no coherent strategic architecture, then spent the week giving rapid-fire phone interviews that contradicted each other.

One moment he said the Iranian people should rise up. The next moment he said keep the same regime but with different people. One journalist was told: “We’ve got people I’m looking at who might be the next leader.” The next journalist call produced: “There were people we were looking at, but it turns out we killed them.”

Nancy Youssef named the structural problem precisely: tactical success does not produce strategic success. Iran’s air defense systems and missile launchers are being degraded. The strikes are effective by every military metric. “But those tactical successes don’t lead to a strategic success.” The NIC told them this before the first bomb fell.

IV. The Apology That Wasn’t — and the Deng Xiaoping Problem

Saturday morning produced a revealing sequence in the actual structure of Iranian power.

President Pezeshkian delivered a televised address apologizing to Gulf neighbors for Iranian missile and drone strikes, pledging to stop — conditional on those countries no longer hosting US bases being used against Iran. Since Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE all host those bases, the condition made the apology structurally meaningless. While he was still speaking, air-raid sirens went off in Qatar and Bahrain. Iran continued attacking.

Within hours, under pressure from Iranian hard-liners, Pezeshkian issued a second statement dropping the apology entirely, insisting Iran had only been targeting US military infrastructure and had never attacked “friendly and neighboring countries” — a claim demonstrably false on the evidence of the preceding eight days.

Trump immediately seized on the original apology, posting that Iran had “surrendered to its Middle East neighbors.” Iran then attacked a Warner Brothers facility in Dubai, a US naval warehouse at Bahrain’s Salman Port, and launched ballistic missiles at Haifa.

The sequence revealed the command structure with unusual clarity. Pezeshkian claimed that Iranian military forces had acted “on their own authority” after their commanders were killed — an extraordinary admission that the civilian president does not control the IRGC. Parliamentary speaker Ghalibaf directly contradicted him publicly. An IRGC commander said there was “nothing new” in Pezeshkian’s remarks. Hard-line judiciary head Mohseni-Ejei said “severe attacks on these targets will continue.” Ali Larijani scheduled a national address for Saturday night to project what state television described as a “more cohesive message” — the intervention required precisely because the fracture had become publicly visible.

Sadjadpour identifies Larijani as potentially the most significant figure not yet fully understood in Western analysis. He is not a reformer. He is a hard man who oversaw a massacre. But he may be one of the few figures who “believes that to save the regime, to save the revolution, we may need to compromise.” The model Larijani has in mind is not Gorbachev — who let the system collapse — but Deng Xiaoping: survive by prioritizing economic national interests over revolutionary ideology. Adapt in order to endure.

This is the only coherent off-ramp that has been articulated in eight days of coverage. It is not Trump’s Venezuela model — there is no Delcy Rodríguez, no one with both the authority and the will to make the deal, and as Ryan Hockstein noted, Israel has been killing anyone who might play that role. It is not regime collapse — the NIC has told us that won’t happen. It is Larijani concluding that the revolution survives by becoming something other than what Khamenei made it, in the same way China survived by becoming something other than what Mao made it.

Whether Larijani can execute this — or whether Israel, whose stated goal is regime dismantlement rather than a pliable successor, will allow him to try — is a different question. But it may be the only question that matters for how this ends.

V. What Russia Is Sending

Russia has been providing Iran with satellite imagery showing the locations of US warships and military personnel since the war began. American officials confirmed this Saturday. Some played it down — Russia has long shared such intelligence with Tehran, and it is not clear how much Iran has been able to use it given the degraded state of its missile launchers. Iran has not hit a US warship. But it has struck US military bases, killing six service members in Kuwait and damaging facilities in Bahrain. An Iranian drone struck the building housing the CIA station in Riyadh; no casualties, but the message was received.

Putin called Pezeshkian on Friday to express condolences for Khamenei and call for a return to diplomacy. The Kremlin said communication through “various channels” would continue.

The relationship has always run in both directions. Iran supplied Russia with Shahed drones for use in Ukraine — the same drones now flying against American positions across the Persian Gulf. Russia supplied Iran with technology and intelligence. That exchange is now active in a war between Iran and the United States.

Trump’s entire strategic posture toward Russia has been built on the premise that conciliation produces cooperation. He has spoken of a transformed US-Russia relationship, a grand bargain, an end to the Ukraine war. That posture is now directly in tension with a conflict in which Russia is providing updated satellite targeting data to a country engaged in killing Americans.

Trump has not addressed this. He has not been asked about it in terms that forced an answer. The White House spokeswoman said only that “the Iranian regime is being absolutely crushed.” The Russia question was left on the floor.

VI. Pakistan Unraveling

In October, Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif stood beside Trump in Egypt, praised him for “saving millions of lives,” and announced that Pakistan was nominating him for the Nobel Peace Prize.

Those videos are now circulating in Pakistan.

Twenty-six people have died in anti-US protests there in the past week. Eleven were shot dead at the US Consulate in Karachi. Neither the US Embassy nor Pakistani security services will confirm whose weapons fired the shots.

Pakistan is a nuclear-armed country sharing a 565-mile border with Iran, with a Shia population of 15 to 20 percent and a mutual defense pact with Saudi Arabia stipulating that an attack on one is an attack on both. Pakistan’s army chief flew to Riyadh on Saturday to discuss “measures needed to halt” Iranian attacks on the Kingdom. Nuclear Pakistan is now operationally aligned with Saudi Arabia against Iran, with its own border increasingly porous as the Balochistan separatist insurgency — which conducted more than 200 attacks last year — watches for any security vacuum on the Iranian side.

Pakistan’s crude oil reserves will last less than two weeks. Its LNG runs out at the end of the month. The government is weighing a four-day workweek and remote schooling. More than half of Pakistan’s $40 billion in annual remittances comes from the Gulf states now under Iranian drone attack.

Senior Pakistani officials have privately expressed concern that Trump is too mercurial for a durable partnership. The Nobel Peace Prize nomination has aged eight days.

VII. The War Comes Home

Colin Clarke published a detailed assessment Saturday morning of what happens next if the war continues: Iranian retaliation in Western cities.

He identifies three vectors. Inspired attacks — lone wolves radicalized by the assassination of Khamenei, the most revered figure in Shia Islam since the founder of the revolution. Directed attacks — the IRGC commissioning criminal organizations, as it did in 2011 when it sought to assassinate the Saudi ambassador in Washington using a man it believed was a member of Los Zetas, and in 2022 when it paid members of an Eastern European criminal syndicate to kill a dissident journalist in Brooklyn. And sleeper cells — elite IRGC or Hezbollah operatives placed in Western countries years ago for exactly this contingency. In 2019, a New Jersey resident was arrested for scouting Times Square, Wall Street, the New York Stock Exchange, and Rockefeller Center for a potential attack.

Rescue workers search through rubble after the bombing of a Jewish community center in Buenos Aires in 1994. (Enrique Marcarian / Reuters)

The historical benchmarks remain Buenos Aires in 1992 and 1994: 29 dead at the Israeli embassy, 85 dead at the Jewish community center. Hezbollah has maintained a regional footprint ever since. UK authorities dealt with 20 distinct Iranian-backed plots against British citizens and residents over a three-year span ending in 2024. In London this week, four men were arrested in connection with an alleged Iranian spy network.

The day after the war began, a man walked into a bar in Austin, Texas, wearing a sweatshirt reading “Property of Allah” and a shirt with a design resembling the Iranian flag, and killed three people. The incident is under investigation as potential terrorism. The motive remains officially unclear.

Clarke’s most sobering point is institutional: over the past five years, US authorities disrupted 17 Iranian plots targeting the American homeland. But the US government has shifted counterterrorism resources toward China, Russia, and immigration enforcement. The homeland is more vulnerable than it has been in years. And Iran’s intent, even as its conventional capabilities are degraded, is growing stronger.

Trump told Time magazine that Americans should probably worry about Iranian retaliation at home. “I guess,” he said. “We expect some things.” He launched the war that created the threat, then confirmed the threat exists, then attended Dover to receive the first six men the threat has claimed in uniform.

VIII. “Make the President Look Bad”

On Thursday, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth addressed the deaths of the six Americans whose transfer cases arrived at Dover on Saturday afternoon.

“This is what the fake news misses,” he said. “We’ve taken control of Iran’s airspace and waterways without boots on the ground. We control their fate. But when a few drones get through or tragic things happen, it’s front page news. I get it. The press only wants to make the president look bad.”

Susan Glasser, on Washington Week Friday evening: “I would wonder what he would say when he looked the families of those people in the eye.”

The White House also released a promotional video this week interweaving strike footage with pounding music and images of targets being destroyed. Glasser called it war as video game. Youssef, the Pentagon correspondent, noted the contrast with former Defense Secretary Robert Gates, who would watch news clips about the fallen on airplanes and cry. “That’s right,” she said. “Because the Secretary of Defense gave the order to send them into harm’s way.” Generals, she noted, still carry photographs in retirement of the service members they lost under their command.

The six men arrived at Dover on Saturday afternoon in flag-draped transfer cases. Trump stood and watched them come off the plane.

That is to his credit.

He built the war that put them there. That is also to the record.

Iran has consumed five American presidencies. At Dover on Saturday, with six transfer cases emerging from a military cargo hold and the families of the dead assembled on the tarmac, the sixth president stood watching, and the count was not yet final.

Day Eight closes with Brent crude at $92, US gas at $3.41 per gallon — up 14 percent in a week, the highest since 2024. Iran’s death toll above 1,300 by the UN’s count. Over 6,000 civilian buildings damaged: 5,535 residential units, 1,041 commercial buildings, 14 medical centers, 65 schools. UNICEF counts 190 children dead. The Houthis say they are ready to enter and it is “only a matter of time.” The Strait of Hormuz remains closed. CENTCOM is planning for at least 100 days. The supplemental funding request has not been submitted to Congress. The NIC report sits in a file somewhere. The war continues.
