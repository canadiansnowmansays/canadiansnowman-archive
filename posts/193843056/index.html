The Idealist’s Retreat

Part 23: The man who demanded oversight from Google now says governance boards don't work

April 16, 2026

Demis Hassabis imagined a singleton — one organization, with the right values and the right governance, developing powerful AI on behalf of all humanity. His colleagues said that if he told them to fly to a secret location in Morocco, they would have felt they had been given fair notice. The singleton did not survive contact with competitive reality. The idealist didn’t lose his values. He lost his belief that institutions could carry them / Grok AI

In 2014, Demis Hassabis sold his AI company to Google on conditions that Google had never heard before. He demanded that a for-profit corporation not have complete control over his technology. He insisted on an independent oversight board of outside sages. He required that military applications of AI be strictly prohibited. Google’s negotiators — representing one of the most powerful companies on earth — accepted.

In 2026, Hassabis says this: “Safety isn’t about governance structures. I mean, even if you have a governance board, it probably wouldn’t do the right thing when it came to the crunch.”

That distance — between the man who made Google agree to independent oversight and the man who now considers governance boards inadequate — is the distance this series has been measuring for twenty-two parts. Hassabis didn’t lose his values. He lost his belief that institutions could carry them. What happened in between is a story about why the Asimov Gap exists, told by the person who tried hardest to prevent it.

The Singleton and Its Collapse

Hassabis had a theory. He called it, in conversations with colleagues, the singleton scenario: a single scientific organization, with the right values and the right governance, would develop powerful AI on behalf of all humanity. The bunker in Morocco was not a metaphor. A researcher who joined DeepMind just after the Google acquisition told author Sebastian Mallaby that if Hassabis had told him to get on a flight to a secret location in Morocco, he “would have felt that he had been given fair notice.”

The singleton scenario had a logic. If one organization — the right organization — controlled the development of superintelligence, the governance problem was manageable. The organization itself was the governance. Hassabis believed he could be that organization.

Demis Hassabis won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2024 for the neural network research that underpins modern AI. He sold DeepMind to Google in 2014 on conditions Google had never heard before. By 2026, he says governance boards "probably wouldn't do the right thing when it came to the crunch. / Getty Images / Nobel Prize ceremony, Stockholm, October 2024

The scenario collapsed in 2015, at a meeting Hassabis convened at Elon Musk’s headquarters in Hawthorne, California. He had brought Musk in to lock him into the singleton vision — give him a stake in it, make him a partner. Musk listened to the presentations. Then he did the opposite of what they wanted. He founded OpenAI — explicitly anti-Google, explicitly anti-DeepMind — in partnership with Sam Altman.

Hassabis’s colleagues described this moment as “the fall.” The metaphor is apt in a way they may not have intended. The fall in Genesis is not a story about evil arriving from outside. It is a story about the conditions inside a closed system producing the outcome the system was designed to prevent. Musk didn’t betray the singleton. He revealed that the singleton was never stable — that concentrating the governance of a transformative technology in the good intentions of a few people is not a governance structure. It is a hope.

The Governance Experiments

Hassabis did not give up after 2015. He tried again, twice, in ways that illuminate exactly why private governance of AI cannot work.

The first attempt was Project Mario — a secret negotiation with Google to establish fresh governance safeguards over DeepMind’s technology. Hassabis spent months in private meetings with Google’s leadership, trying to recreate, from the inside, the oversight conditions he had extracted as a condition of sale. The effort failed. Commercial pressure, competitive dynamics, and the simple reality that Google had no incentive to constrain its own most valuable AI asset made the conditions Hassabis sought impossible to enforce.

The second attempt was the NHS partnership — an effort to demonstrate that DeepMind’s AI could be deployed in a public health context with appropriate governance, transparency, and accountability. The partnership produced important medical AI research. It also produced a data-sharing scandal: DeepMind received the medical records of 1.6 million NHS patients without their consent, raising questions about the gap between the governance framework Hassabis had described and the operational reality of working within Google’s data infrastructure.

The pattern across both experiments is the same. The governance intentions were genuine. The structural conditions — commercial incentives, competitive pressures, the difficulty of imposing accountability on proprietary systems operating within large corporations — made those intentions difficult to sustain. Private governance of powerful technology fails not because the people attempting it are bad, but because the structural forces against it are stronger than individual intentions.

The Seat at the Table

“My goal is to earn a seat at the table,” Demis Hassabis told Sebastian Mallaby, “so when a safety issue comes up, I can help decide the solutions.” A seat at the table is not a governance structure. It is not accountable to the public. It cannot be voted out. It does not survive a change of CEO, a merger, or a shift in corporate strategy. It lasts exactly as long as the influence of the person sitting in it. / Grok AI

By 2026, Hassabis has arrived at a position that is honest, pragmatic, and — from the perspective of what this series has been arguing — inadequate.

“My goal is to earn a seat at the table,” he told Mallaby, “so when a safety issue comes up, I can help decide the solutions.”

This is not nothing. A thoughtful, knowledgeable person at the table is better than no such person. Hassabis’s judgment on AI safety questions is likely better than that of most of the people who will be making those decisions.

But a seat at the table is not a governance structure. It is not accountable to the public. It cannot be voted out. It does not survive a change of CEO, a merger, an acquisition, or a shift in corporate strategy. It does not bind competitors. It does not set international standards. It is one person’s influence over decisions that will affect billions of people — and it lasts exactly as long as that person retains the influence.

Asimov understood this. His robots were governed by laws, not by the good intentions of their creators. The laws were inadequate — that was the point of fifty years of stories about how they failed. But Asimov at least understood that governance requires rules that exist independently of the people who made the technology. A seat at the table is the governance equivalent of hoping the people in the room are good.

Sometimes they are. The question is what happens when they’re not.

What the Idealist’s Retreat Tells Us

Hassabis is not a villain in this story. He is the most honest illustration of why private governance of transformative technology does not work — not because the people trying are bad, but because the structural conditions make it impossible.

He demanded governance safeguards as a condition of sale. They eroded within a decade under commercial pressure. He built an oversight board. It couldn’t prevent the competitive race when the race began. He pursued governance experiments with the NHS and with Google. Both failed for predictable structural reasons. He won a Nobel Prize for AI’s benefits while presiding over AI’s deployment at a scale that outran his governance frameworks.

His conclusion — “safety isn’t about governance structures” — is not cynicism. It is the honest assessment of a person who tried every available governance mechanism and watched each one fail. The idealist didn’t abandon his ideals. He abandoned his belief that the institutions available could carry them.

Which is exactly the argument this series has been making from the outside. The Asimov Gap is not a gap in good intentions. It is a gap in democratic institutions capable of governing technology at the speed and scale at which it is being deployed. Hassabis’s retreat is the inside confirmation.

The gap is real. And the people closest to it know it best.
