The Quiet War

“The Quantum Layer”

Part IV: Space-Earth Communication Networks and What They Enable. The Infrastructure That Makes Eavesdropping Impossible — and Who Is Building It.

March 31, 2026

Illustration of the three cooperating ground stations (Graz, Nanshan, and Xinglong). Listed are all paths used for key generation and the corresponding final key length. Credit: University of Science and Technology of China

I. The Network Nobody Can Hack

Every military communication system in operation today rests on the same foundation: mathematical encryption. Messages are scrambled using algorithms so complex that decrypting them without the key would take conventional computers millions of years. This is the basis of military command infrastructure, financial systems, diplomatic communications, and intelligence networks worldwide.

Quantum computers will break this foundation. Not theoretically. Not eventually. The mathematics is settled. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor’s algorithm can factor the large prime numbers that underpin RSA encryption in hours rather than millennia. The US-China Economic and Security Review Commission warned Congress in its 2025 annual report that China is likely racing to build quantum computers capable of breaking global cryptographic systems — and may be deliberately concealing the status of its most advanced projects.

The question is not whether current encryption will be broken. It is who builds the replacement first.

The 15th Five-Year Plan answers that question from the Chinese side: integrated space-Earth quantum communication networks, scalable quantum computers, and the infrastructure to connect them. This is not a research aspiration. It is a procurement directive backed by the full industrial capacity of the state.

II. What China Has Already Built

China’s quantum communication programme is the most advanced in the world. This is not a contested claim. It is an empirical description of deployed infrastructure.

The Micius satellite, launched in 2016, was the world’s first quantum communications satellite. It demonstrated quantum key distribution from space, entanglement-based communication across 1,200 kilometres, and the first quantum-encrypted intercontinental video conference, between Beijing and Vienna in 2017.

In March 2025, researchers used the Jinan-1 microsatellite to establish the world’s longest intercontinental quantum communication link — 12,900 kilometres, from China to South Africa. The microsatellite is drastically smaller and cheaper than Micius, demonstrating that the technology is moving from proof-of-concept toward scalable deployment. The experiment generated real-time quantum keys that enabled encrypted communication between Beijing and Stellenbosch — communication that is provably unbreakable by any computational method, classical or quantum.

China’s 2,000-kilometre terrestrial quantum communication network — the world’s only operational long-distance quantum network — connects 32 trusted nodes from Beijing to Shanghai. Source: Nature journal / Chinese Academy of Sciences (published with the 2021 network paper)

On the ground, China operates a 2,000-kilometre terrestrial fibre-based quantum network connecting 32 trusted nodes across major cities from Beijing to Shanghai. This is the world’s only operational long-distance quantum communication network.

The next phase is already scheduled. Two to three new quantum communication satellites were planned for low Earth orbit launch in 2025, with a further satellite targeting medium Earth orbit in 2027. The medium orbit satellite will carry a 600-millimetre telescope for photon transmission, enabling longer-duration quantum key distribution sessions and broader coverage. The Five-Year Plan directs the integration of these orbital assets with ground-based networks to create a wide-area quantum communication system — the space-Earth network referenced in the 141 pages.

Pan Jianwei, the physicist leading the programme, expects breakthroughs in quantum relay technologies within the next five to six years that would enable a full global quantum communications network. China has filed more quantum-related patents than any other country and invested an estimated $15 billion in quantum technologies — though the exact figure is not officially confirmed.

III. What the United States Has Not Built

The United States leads the world in quantum computing research. American companies — IBM, Google, IonQ, Quantinuum — are at or near the frontier of qubit technology, error correction, and algorithmic development. The research ecosystem is deep, well-funded by private capital, and producing genuine breakthroughs.

The United States does not lead in quantum communication. It has no operational quantum satellite. Boeing is targeting 2026 for a small quantum networking satellite — a decade after China’s Micius. The Department of Energy has invested $625 million over five years in quantum information science research centres. The proposed Quantum Leadership Act of 2025 offers $2.5 billion across fiscal years 2026 through 2030.

These are meaningful investments. They are also an order of magnitude smaller than China’s mobilisation. The trillion-yuan national venture capital fund announced in March 2025 — approximately $138 billion — targets quantum technology alongside AI and hydrogen energy. Even accounting for the fact that this fund covers multiple technology areas, the scale of the commitment dwarfs anything in the American federal pipeline.

More concerning than the funding gap is the strategic gap. The Trump administration’s FY2026 budget proposed no new quantum programmes, no growth plans, and no specific line items for workforce development or commercialisation. The White House budget document explicitly maintained quantum as a “priority area” while offering no increases. The U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission responded by urging Congress to adopt a “Quantum First” national goal by 2030, warning that quantum is not an isolated research agenda but a mission-essential national asset.

A draft executive order on quantum policy was reportedly being prepared in early 2026. But executive orders are not Five-Year Plans. They do not translate into ministerial budgets, provincial implementation targets, or state-owned enterprise capital allocations. They are statements of intent that require congressional action to become investments — and congressional attention is currently occupied by supplemental funding requests for the Iran war.

IV. What Unhackable Communications Enable

The military implications of a quantum communication network are straightforward: command and control infrastructure that cannot be intercepted, decoded, or disrupted by any adversary, regardless of their computational capabilities.

This is not a marginal improvement in communications security. It is a categorical change. Current military encryption can theoretically be broken by a sufficiently advanced computer. Quantum key distribution cannot — the laws of physics, not the difficulty of mathematics, guarantee the security. Any attempt to intercept a quantum-encrypted transmission disturbs the quantum state of the photons carrying the key, alerting both parties to the eavesdropping attempt instantly and rendering the intercepted key useless.

A country that deploys a global quantum communication network possesses command infrastructure that its adversaries cannot penetrate. Its diplomatic communications are secure against any signals intelligence capability. Its financial transactions are immune to interception. Its military orders cannot be decoded even if physically captured in transit.

China is building this network. The Five-Year Plan funds it. The satellites are being launched. The ground-based fibre network is operational. The relay technologies are advancing on a published timeline.

The United States is not building an equivalent network. It is funding research into quantum computing — which is essential and genuinely world-leading — but has not deployed quantum communication infrastructure at anything approaching the Chinese scale. The gap is not in capability. American physicists understand quantum communication as well as their Chinese counterparts. The gap is in deployment — in the translation of laboratory achievement into operational infrastructure.

The country that builds the quantum network first does not merely gain a communications advantage. It gains the ability to operate in a world where its adversary’s encryption has been broken by quantum computers while its own remains secure. That asymmetry — the ability to read everything while being read by no one — is the most consequential intelligence advantage since the breaking of Enigma.

V. The Encryption Clock

There is a timeline embedded in the quantum competition that most analysis overlooks.

Quantum computers capable of breaking current encryption may be years away. The precise timeline is uncertain — estimates range from five to fifteen years. But the data encrypted today will still be sensitive when those computers arrive. Intelligence agencies call this the “harvest now, decrypt later” threat: intercepting and storing encrypted communications today with the expectation that quantum computers will break the encryption in the future.

Pan Jianwei, the leading physicist for the invention of the world's first quantum computer which outpaces conventional computers. He compares quantum mechanics to Monkey King

China is almost certainly harvesting encrypted communications now. So is the United States. So is every major intelligence agency. The question is who will be able to decrypt the harvest first — and whose own communications will be protected when that moment arrives.

The space-Earth quantum communication network in the Five-Year Plan is not just a future capability. It is a present-tense response to the harvest-now-decrypt-later threat. Communications transmitted over quantum-encrypted channels today are secure against future quantum computers. Communications transmitted over conventional encryption today are not.

Every year of delay in deploying quantum communication infrastructure is a year of accumulated vulnerability. Every classified communication, every diplomatic exchange, every strategic planning document transmitted over conventional encryption today becomes readable the day a sufficiently powerful quantum computer comes online.

China is closing this window for itself. The United States, consumed by a war that dominates its attention, its budget, and its political bandwidth, has not yet begun to close it at equivalent scale.

The 141 pages allocate resources for quantum networks. The supplemental funding requests allocate resources for interceptors. Both are rational responses to perceived threats. Only one of them compounds.
