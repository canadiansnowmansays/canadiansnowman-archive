America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 7: “America’s Best-Kept Secret — We Already Have Single-Payer and Seniors Love It”

December 15, 2025

Every debate about single-payer healthcare in America eventually hits the same Republican talking point: “Government-run healthcare doesn’t work. Look at the VA! Look at the DMV! Government can’t run anything efficiently!”

Except for one inconvenient fact: America already runs the world’s largest single-payer healthcare system. It covers 67 million people. It operates more efficiently than any private insurance company. And Americans who have it are more satisfied than Americans with private insurance.

It’s called Medicare.

This is Part 7 of Code Blue, our investigation into America’s 80-year healthcare failure. We’ve documented the broken promises (Parts 1-2), followed the money (Part 3), examined international models (Part 4), described the bipartisan compromise nobody wants (Part 5), and exposed the lobbying apparatus that prevents reform (Part 6).

Now we examine the elephant in the room: America already proved single-payer works. We just arbitrarily restrict it to people over 65.

ONE BIRTHDAY. THAT’S THE ONLY DIFFERENCE.Let’s start with what makes Medicare different from private insurance:

Administrative overhead:

  • Medicare (Traditional): 2% of spending

  • Private insurance average: 12-18% of spending

  • Some private insurers: 20%+ of spending

Translation: For every $100 spent, Medicare spends $2 on administration (claims processing, billing, overhead). Private insurers spend $12-$20 on administration.

Where does that extra 10-18% go?

  • Marketing departments

  • Profit margins for shareholders

  • Executive compensation (UnitedHealth CEO: $23.5 million in 2022)

  • Complex network management

  • Claims denial departments

  • Prior authorization bureaucracy

Medicare doesn’t need any of that. You turn 65, you’re enrolled, you show your card, you get care. No networks. No prior authorizations for most services. No “surprise billing.” No fighting about coverage.

Satisfaction rates:

Every year, the American Customer Satisfaction Index measures satisfaction with health insurance:

2023 results:

  • Medicare (Traditional): 78/100

  • Private insurance average: 71/100

  • Medicare Advantage (private plans): 75/100

Medicare beneficiaries are more satisfied than people with private insurance. Not by a little — by a statistically significant margin that’s held steady for decades.

Why? Because Medicare delivers what it promises:

  • No claim denials for “pre-existing conditions”

  • No surprise out-of-network bills

  • No prior authorization for most care

  • No annual or lifetime limits

  • Coverage that continues regardless of employment status

Coverage comprehensiveness:

Medicare covers:

  • Hospital care (Part A)

  • Physician services (Part B)

  • Prescription drugs (Part D)

  • Preventive services (no cost-sharing)

What it doesn’t cover well:

  • Dental (limited)

  • Vision (limited)

  • Hearing (limited)

  • Long-term care (not covered)

But for core medical care — the difference between life and death, health and sickness — Medicare covers it. And it covers it more reliably than private insurance with its deductibles, co-insurance, and coverage denials.

The Political Third Rail

Here’s where it gets interesting: Try cutting Medicare benefits in a red state and watch the town hall revolt.

2017: The Republican Healthcare Debacle

Remember when Republicans tried to repeal Obamacare? The town halls were brutal. Angry constituents screaming at their representatives.

But notice what they weren’t angry about: Nobody was demanding Medicare cuts. Nobody was saying “please reduce my Medicare benefits.” Nobody was arguing that government-run healthcare doesn’t work.

In fact, the opposite happened. Many protesters were seniors on Medicare defending the ACA because their children and grandchildren needed coverage.

2011: Paul Ryan’s Medicare Voucher Proposal

Representative Paul Ryan (R-WI) proposed converting Medicare to a voucher system — giving seniors a fixed amount of money to buy private insurance instead of guaranteed government coverage.

Republican reaction: Panic. Congressional Republicans distanced themselves immediately.

Why? Because seniors vote. And seniors love Medicare. And telling seniors you’re taking away Medicare is political suicide.

Ryan’s proposal died before it could be seriously debated. Not because it wouldn’t save money (it probably would, by shifting costs to seniors). But because it was politically toxic.

The MAGA paradox:

Trump rallies featured seniors in MAGA hats chanting “government-run healthcare is socialism!” while receiving government-run healthcare through Medicare.

The cognitive dissonance is stunning. Seniors on Medicare — a single-payer, government-run, tax-funded healthcare system — oppose single-payer, government-run, tax-funded healthcare for everyone else.

Why? Because they have theirs. And they’re terrified that expanding it to younger people will somehow diminish what they receive.

What Medicare Actually Costs

2024 Medicare spending: $1.03 trillion 2024 Medicare enrollment: 67 million beneficiaries Per capita spending: $15,373

Wait — didn’t we say American healthcare costs $14,885 per capita? Why is Medicare more expensive?

Answer: Because Medicare covers the sickest, oldest Americans. People over 65 use more healthcare. That’s expected.

The miracle isn’t that Medicare spends $15,373 per beneficiary. The miracle is that it delivers comprehensive care to 67 million seniors at 2% administrative overhead — while private insurance struggles to cover younger, healthier people efficiently at 12-18% overhead.

Funding sources:

  • Payroll taxes: 2.9% (1.45% employer, 1.45% employee)

  • Premiums (Part B): $174.70/month for most beneficiaries (2024)

  • General revenue: Federal budget

  • Part D premiums: Varies by plan

Is Medicare sustainable? That’s the wrong question. The right question is: “Is it more sustainable than the current private insurance system?”

Answer: Yes.

Medicare spending growth has been slower than private insurance spending growth for the past 20 years. Medicare negotiates better rates with hospitals and physicians. Medicare has lower administrative costs. Medicare doesn’t extract profit margins.

The “Medicare is going bankrupt” scare is about the Hospital Insurance Trust Fund (Part A), which can be fixed by raising the payroll tax from 2.9% to 3.5% — a 0.6% increase that would fully fund Part A for decades.

Compare that to private insurance premiums rising 5-8% annually while covering less.

Medicare Advantage: The Privatization Experiment

Here’s where it gets complicated.

Medicare Advantage: Private insurance companies receive payments from Medicare to provide coverage to seniors instead of traditional Medicare.

Current enrollment: About 54% of Medicare beneficiaries choose Medicare Advantage over traditional Medicare.

Why? Marketing. Medicare Advantage plans often offer:

  • $0 premiums (Medicare pays the insurer)

  • Gym memberships

  • Dental and vision coverage

  • Other perks

Sounds great, right?

The catch:

  • Networks restrict which doctors you can see

  • Prior authorization required for many services

  • Higher out-of-pocket costs when you’re actually sick

  • Insurance companies deny 13-18% of claims that traditional Medicare would approve

The cost to taxpayers: Medicare pays insurance companies 4-6% MORE per beneficiary than it would cost to cover them in traditional Medicare. The federal government pays extra for seniors to get worse coverage through private insurers.

Why does this exist? Lobbying. Insurance companies lobbied for Medicare Advantage (originally called Medicare+Choice in 1997, rebranded in 2003). They argued “private competition will reduce costs.”

Result: Costs went up. Insurance company profits went up. Seniors got networks, prior authorizations, and claim denials.

But 54% of seniors still choose it because the marketing is better and the upfront costs are lower (until you get sick).

The Expansion Debate

If Medicare works so well, why not expand it?

Medicare for All proposals:

  • Bernie Sanders version: Expand Medicare to cover everyone, eliminate private insurance for core coverage

  • Public option version: Let people under 65 buy into Medicare, keep private insurance as an option

  • Medicare at 50 version: Lower eligibility age gradually (60, then 55, then 50)

Arguments for:

  • Medicare already works

  • Administrative costs would drop from 12-18% to 2%

  • Negotiating power of 330 million people vs. 67 million

  • Eliminates medical bankruptcy

  • Simplifies billing for doctors and hospitals

Arguments against:

  • Transition costs (estimates: $2-3 trillion over 10 years)

  • Physician payment rates might decrease

  • Some people would pay more in taxes than current premiums

  • Insurance industry would lose most of its business (2.9 million jobs)

  • Political difficulty of passing it

The honest answer: Medicare for All is achievable. Other countries run single-payer systems successfully. The question isn’t feasibility — it’s political will.

The German Alternative

Remember from Part 5: You don’t have to eliminate private insurance to achieve universal coverage.

Germany’s model:

  • Public option (statutory health insurance) covers 88% of Germans

  • Private insurance available for high earners (11%)

  • Both systems regulated for price and coverage

Why this matters for Medicare expansion: America could:

  1. Let anyone buy into Medicare (public option)

  2. Keep private insurance for those who want it

  3. Regulate both systems for price controls

  4. Achieve universal coverage without eliminating private insurance

This gives:

  • Republicans their “market choice”

  • Democrats their “universal coverage”

  • Insurance companies a smaller but still profitable market

  • Americans the coverage they need

But neither party will propose it (see Part 5).

What Seniors Actually Think

2023 Kaiser Family Foundation polling of Medicare beneficiaries:

Overall satisfaction with Medicare:

  • Very satisfied: 68%

  • Somewhat satisfied: 23%

  • Total satisfied: 91%

Would you recommend Medicare to friends/family?

  • Yes: 89%

  • No: 11%

Compared to private insurance you had before Medicare:

  • Medicare is better: 67%

  • Private insurance was better: 21%

  • About the same: 12%

Do you support expanding Medicare to people under 65?

  • Support: 58%

  • Oppose: 37%

  • Don’t know: 5%

Even seniors — who theoretically would worry about “diluting” their benefits — support expansion by 21 points.

The International Comparison

Largest single-payer systems in the world (by enrollment):

  1. NHS (United Kingdom): 67 million people

  2. Medicare (United States): 67 million people

  3. Canada (combined provinces): 40 million people

  4. Taiwan National Health Insurance: 24 million people

Administrative costs:

  1. Taiwan: 2% (tied with Medicare)

  2. Medicare: 2%

  3. Canada: 4% (provincial average)

  4. NHS: 5%

  5. US Private Insurance: 12-18%

America already runs one of the world’s most efficient single-payer systems. We just restrict it to 8% of the population.

The Absurdity Highlighted

Let’s say you’re 64 years old:

  • You might have diabetes, requiring insulin ($300/vial without insurance)

  • You might need a hip replacement ($40,364 without insurance)

  • You might have high blood pressure, requiring daily medication

  • Your private insurance has a $6,000 deductible and 20% coinsurance

  • Your premiums are $800/month

  • You live in fear of medical bankruptcy

You turn 65:

  • Medicare Part A and B premiums: $174.70/month

  • Insulin copay: $35/month (IRA cap)

  • Hip replacement: Covered after deductible (~$1,600 total)

  • Medications: $0-$50/month depending on plan

  • No more fear of medical bankruptcy

  • No more claim denials for pre-existing conditions

  • No more surprise bills

The difference? One birthday. One arbitrary age cutoff.

The healthcare didn’t change. The system changed. From private insurance designed to maximize profit to public insurance designed to deliver care.

The Question Nobody Wants to Answer

If Medicare works so well for seniors, why not for everyone?

Republicans say: “Medicare is different because seniors earned it.”

Response: Everyone who works pays Medicare taxes. Everyone over 65 gets Medicare regardless of how much they paid in. It’s already a universal system for its age group.

Democrats say: “We support Medicare for All!”

Response: Then why didn’t you pass it in 2021-2022 when you controlled Congress? (See Part 5.)

Insurance companies say: “Medicare only works because it piggybacks on private insurance pricing.”

Response: Medicare pays lower rates than private insurance. Hospitals accept Medicare rates. Doctors accept Medicare rates. The system works.

Hospital administrators say: “Medicare doesn’t pay enough to cover costs.”

Response: Then why do you accept Medicare patients? Because even Medicare’s “low” rates are profitable when you don’t have private insurance’s 12-18% administrative overhead.

The truth nobody wants to say:

America already proved single-payer works. Medicare delivers better coverage, higher satisfaction, and lower administrative costs than private insurance.

We just restrict it to people over 65 because apparently only seniors deserve decent healthcare.
