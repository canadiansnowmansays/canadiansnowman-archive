Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 13: The Crab and the Cardinal — Inside Cuba’s Endgame Negotiations

April 6, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Miguel Díaz-Canel, President of Cuba. Washington has told his government he must go — not as an ultimatum, but as the price of any meaningful deal. The man who inherited a revolution he didn't make is now being asked to absorb its failure.

On the morning of March 13, 2026, Miguel Díaz-Canel sat down in front of Cuban state television cameras and said something his government had never said before.

Cuba was talking to the United States.

The ninety-minute press conference was not, in the language of Cuban official communication, a declaration of surrender. Díaz-Canel was careful about that. The talks, he said, were based on “respect for the political systems of both countries, sovereignty and our government’s self-determination.” Cuba’s constitution, its socialist system, its right to govern itself as it chose — none of these were on the table. The negotiations were about finding a path through the crisis, not about the fundamental nature of the Cuban state.

But he was sitting in front of the cameras at all, which meant something the rhetoric could not entirely obscure. Cuba’s government, which has maintained for sixty-seven years that it will not negotiate under duress, was negotiating under duress. Two crucial power plants had exhausted their fuel supplies. The electrical grid was in what Díaz-Canel called “a very unstable situation.” Thirteen to fifteen hours of daily blackouts were making cooking, refrigeration, and water supply — the bare minimum requirements of ordinary life — unreliable or impossible for millions of Cubans. The country, he acknowledged, had imported no oil in three months.

“Workers are making every effort to overcome the impossible,” he said. Then, with the kind of sentence that can only be read as an appeal as much as a declaration: “It’s the fault of the energy blockade that has been imposed on us.”

That appeal was addressed to multiple audiences simultaneously: the Cuban people, who needed to be told their government was doing something; the international community, which needed to be reminded who was responsible for what was happening; and Washington, which was the only audience that actually mattered for the practical question of whether the lights would stay on.

The blockade was working. The question of what it was working toward was considerably less clear.

Three days later, on Monday March 16, it became marginally clearer — and considerably more alarming. The power grid failed entirely. The whole country went dark. And Donald Trump, asked about Cuba at the White House, said: “I do believe I’ll have the honor of taking Cuba.” When pressed on whether that meant diplomatically or militarily, he declined to specify. “I think I can do anything I want with it,” he said.

That sentence — applied to an island of ten million people ninety miles off the American coast — is worth sitting with for a moment before proceeding to the details of the negotiations.

The Crab

While Díaz-Canel was acknowledging talks publicly for the first time, the person actually conducting those talks from the Cuban side was not Díaz-Canel. It was a man most of the world had never heard of, known within Cuba’s intelligence apparatus by the nickname “El Cangrejo” — The Crab.

Raúl Guillermo Rodríguez Castro is in his early forties. He is a high-ranking official in Cuba’s state security apparatus — the institution that, along with GAESA, the military-run economic conglomerate, holds the real levers of power in Cuba. He is also the grandson of Raúl Castro, Fidel’s brother, who officially stepped down as president in 2018 and at ninety-four years old continues to exercise considerable influence over a system he spent decades building.

Raúl Guillermo Rodríguez Castro, a grandson of the former leader Raúl Castro, in January.Credit...Yamil Lage/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

The choice of Rodríguez Castro as the back-channel interlocutor with Marco Rubio reveals something important about how Cuba’s power structure actually works. Díaz-Canel is the president. He gives the speeches, chairs the meetings, and takes public responsibility for the government’s decisions. But the decision to engage with Washington — a decision of existential significance for the Cuban state — was not his alone to make. It required the blessing, and the active participation, of the Castro family network that still anchors the system’s deepest loyalties.

By sending a Castro grandson to the table, the Cuban government was signaling something to Rubio: we are serious enough about this to involve the family. And it was signaling something to itself: this negotiation, if it goes somewhere, will have the backing of the people whose backing actually matters.

Rubio, for his part, had publicly stated that the United States had struggled to find a Cuban equivalent to Delcy Rodríguez — the Venezuelan official who succeeded Maduro after his capture and who had been cooperating with Washington’s management of Venezuela’s transition. The Venezuelan template assumed a compliant successor waiting in the wings, ready to implement American preferences while maintaining the fiction of sovereign governance. William LeoGrande, an American University professor who has spent decades studying Cuba, called the search for a Cuban Delcy Rodríguez “a fool’s game.” The Cuban state is not the Venezuelan state. It does not have a Delcy Rodríguez, because it was not built to be taken over from within on American terms.

What it does have, apparently, is The Crab. And as of March 16, the shape of what The Crab has been negotiating has become somewhat clearer. According to four people familiar with the talks, the Trump administration has told Cuban negotiators that Díaz-Canel must go. Not as a formal ultimatum — the message was presented, in the careful language of diplomacy, as a positive step that would pave the way for productive agreements. But the meaning was unambiguous: no meaningful deal is possible with Díaz-Canel at the helm.

The demand is revealing in what it does and does not include. Washington is not pushing for action against Castro family members. The Crab — Raulito — would, sources say, continue to steer the government after Díaz-Canel’s departure, with another figure who does not bear the Castro name officially holding office. The pattern is familiar: a figurehead removed, the underlying power structure intact, a cosmetic reset that allows both sides to declare progress.

The Cubans involved in the talks, according to people familiar with them, agreed that Díaz-Canel’s presidency had been problematic — a remarkably candid internal assessment of the man their own system put in place — but were focused on how to make the change without appearing to do so on Washington’s instructions. The optics of sovereignty matter even when sovereignty is being negotiated away.

Meanwhile, Cuba appeared to be quietly auditioning potential successors — and the Castro family was anything but absent from the process. Oscar Pérez-Oliva Fraga — a grandnephew of both Fidel and Raúl Castro, appointed as deputy prime minister late last year — gave a rare interview to NBC News on Monday in which he spoke with unusual directness about opening Cuba to foreign investment. “Cuba is open to having a fluid commercial relationship with U.S. companies, also with Cubans residing in the United States and their descendants,” he said. He went further than anyone in the Cuban government had gone publicly, saying the opening would apply “not only to small investments, but large ones, especially in infrastructure.” His appointment to Cuba’s National Assembly was subsequently confirmed as calculated: under Cuban law, only National Assembly deputies can become president. Officials who normally work entirely behind the scenes were being given unusual public visibility. The next face of Cuban governance was being floated, carefully, to see how it landed.

Raúlito met Rubio’s team directly at a regional gathering of Caribbean nations in St. Kitts and Nevis — a detail that made his role as back-channel negotiator concrete rather than merely rumoured. And a second Castro family member has now surfaced in the talks: Alejandro Castro Espín, 60, Raúl’s only son, a brigadier general and former intelligence chief who led Cuba’s side in the secret Obama negotiations that produced the 2014 diplomatic opening. General Castro Espín, educated in Cuba and the Soviet Union, author of books critical of the United States, is now confirmed as taking a leading part in the current talks. The family that never really left power is making sure it is present at every table where its future is being decided.

What is emerging is what one historian called “de-Castrofication” — the creation of an illusion of change while real power remains with the family and the post-1959 elite. The faces change. The network holds.

The same Monday evening, Cuban state television had scheduled a program — Mesa Redonda, the government’s flagship political broadcast — to formally announce the new economic opening. The program did not air at the scheduled hour. The power was out.

Ricardo Zúniga, who conducted the secret negotiations for the Obama administration that produced the 2014 diplomatic opening, offered a verdict that was both analytically sharp and quietly brutal: “The captain goes down with the ship, and this ship is going down.” Díaz-Canel, he said, was never someone willing to pursue transformative policies — he was installed precisely because he wouldn’t — which made him the obvious candidate to absorb the blame for a crisis he did not entirely create and could not have prevented.

The Parallel Track: Indictments as Foreign Policy

While the diplomatic back-channel was being quietly established through the Vatican and other intermediaries, a different instrument was being prepared in South Florida — one that suggests the Trump administration was not exclusively committed to a negotiated outcome.

Jason Reding Quiñones, the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Florida, had convened a broad-ranging inquiry into Cuba’s leaders and Communist Party officials, with a goal of bringing fast indictments on drug, immigration, economic, and violent crime charges. The working group assembled for the purpose included prosecutors, FBI agents, and officials from the Treasury Department, the State Department, the DEA, and the ATF — an unusual cross-agency mobilization for what was being described as a law enforcement matter.

It was not, in any meaningful sense, primarily a law enforcement matter. The Maduro operation had been justified through pre-existing drug trafficking indictments that gave the administration a legal rationale for what was, operationally, a foreign policy action — the physical removal of a head of state from his country. The South Florida indictment effort was preparing the same scaffolding for Cuba. If Cuban leaders could be charged in American courts, those charges could then serve as the stated legal basis for the kind of operation that had worked against Maduro.

The combination of the diplomatic track and the indictment track is not necessarily contradictory. Administrations maintain multiple options simultaneously; the fact that Rubio was talking to The Crab did not mean he had abandoned the option of a more forceful approach if the talks failed. But it does reveal the structural dishonesty of the current American position — the gap between the stated objective of a “negotiated transition” and the parallel preparation of tools that could be used to bypass any negotiation entirely.

Trump himself was characteristically unsubtle about it. “Cuba is gonna fall pretty soon,” he told CNN in early March. “They want to make a deal so badly.” And, in a formulation that captured the essential arrogance of the approach: “Cuba’s at the end of the line. They have no money. They have no oil.”

He was not wrong about the material facts. He appeared entirely unconcerned with the human beings experiencing those facts.

The Cardinal

Cardinal Pietro Parolin celebrates a Mass marking the 50th anniversary of Italy's Movement for Life in St. Peter's Basilica at the Vatican March 8, 2025. (CNS/Pablo Esparza)

Between the two tracks — the diplomatic conversation and the legal preparation — stood a third party that neither Washington nor Havana had fully anticipated playing such a central role: the Vatican.

Cardinal Pietro Parolin, the Vatican’s Secretary of State, confirmed publicly that the Holy See had taken what he called “the necessary steps, always with a view to a dialogue-based solution.” Cuba’s foreign minister had met with Vatican officials. Mike Hammer, the top American diplomat in Havana, had done the same. The Vatican was providing the neutral ground — the institutional credibility that “neither Washington nor Havana currently trusts the other to uphold,” as one analyst put it — that made talks possible without either side having to publicly concede that it was talking.

The Vatican’s involvement was not accidental. Pope Francis had played a similar intermediary role in the Obama-era Cuba negotiations that led to the 2014 diplomatic opening, and the institutional memory of that success was available to both sides. The Catholic Church also has deep roots in Cuban society — it was, after all, the institution through which the Trump administration had chosen to channel its $6 million in humanitarian aid, specifically to bypass state channels. The Church was present on the ground in ways that neither Washington nor any international organization could replicate.

That the Vatican was needed as a guarantor of basic diplomatic good faith between two countries that maintain official diplomatic relations through Hammer’s presence in Havana is a measure of how thoroughly trust between them has been destroyed. They could not talk directly. They needed a Cardinal in the middle.

What the Cardinal would be able to deliver — what any deal brokered through his office could actually contain — was the question that Cuba experts were approaching with considerable skepticism. Ted Henken, a Cuba scholar at Baruch College, put it plainly: any meaningful deal would have to include the release of all political prisoners, an end to the criminalization of dissent, permission for independent political organizing, the legalization of political parties beyond the Communist Party, and a restoration of basic civil liberties including freedom of speech and press. “The main question for me,” Henken said, “is whether and what political, social and civic changes will also be included in any deal.”

Díaz-Canel’s framing of the talks — “respect for the political systems of both countries” — suggested that the Cuban government’s negotiating position began precisely where Henken’s minimum requirements ended.

What Cuba Is Offering

Amid the opacity of the back-channel negotiations, two concrete gestures had emerged by mid-March that suggested the contours of what Cuba was willing to put on the table.

The first was the announced release of 51 prisoners — a number that fell well short of the hundreds of political detainees imprisoned after the 2021 protests, many of whom had received sentences of up to thirty years for the act of demonstrating in the streets. That initial release was followed on April 3 by a far larger one: 2,010 prisoners, the biggest such release in years, framed by Havana around Holy Week but universally understood as a gesture toward Washington. The prisoner releases were calibrated to signal seriousness without conceding the principle that the government had a right to imprison people for political dissent in the first place.

The second was Díaz-Canel’s announcement that Cuba would soon unveil a policy allowing Cubans living abroad to invest in the island’s economy — a measure that the Cuban diaspora had been requesting for years and that would, if implemented substantively, create a new economic relationship between the government and the estimated 2.75 million Cubans who have left the country since 2020. “It is our responsibility as the government to embrace them, listen to them, tend to them and offer them a space,” Díaz-Canel said, in language that would have been inconceivable from the Cuban government of even five years ago.

Neither gesture addressed the fundamental political demands that American officials and Cuba experts identified as the minimum for a meaningful deal. But together they sketched the outline of what a Cuba-on-its-own-terms accommodation might look like: economic opening, some humanitarian concessions, enough movement to give Washington something to call a victory, while the political system remained intact and the Communist Party’s monopoly on power went unchallenged.

This was, as analysts noted with some irony, essentially the Obama framework — the approach that Rubio had spent a decade denouncing as naive and counterproductive. Rubio had called it “the lie and the illusion that more commerce and access to money and goods will translate to political freedom for the Cuban people.” He was now, in the assessment of most independent observers, heading toward precisely that outcome — possibly with less political liberalization than Obama had achieved, having spent several months causing extraordinary suffering to achieve it.

Morón: The Street Speaks

A screen grab from a social media post showing protesters outside the Communist Party headquarters in the Cuban city of Morón in March.Credit...via Reuters

While negotiators talked and the power failed and the political class calculated, something was happening in the streets that did not fit neatly into anyone’s framework.

Late on Friday March 14 — the night after Díaz-Canel’s press conference acknowledging the talks — protesters appeared in Morón, a city in central Cuba near the tourist resorts of Cayo Coco. What began as a gathering escalated when a group threw stones at the local Communist Party headquarters, broke into the building, dragged furniture outside, and set it alight. Videos verified by the New York Times showed people approaching the building with flaming objects. “Burn it all down!” one man shouted. “Freedom! Freedom!” others called back. In one video, what sounds like a gunshot is heard. A man collapses. People rush toward him calling for help.

The Cuban government reported five arrests. The Communist Party newspaper Granma acknowledged that other buildings were damaged, including a pharmacy and a state-run store. Officials held what they called a “revolutionary reaffirmation ceremony” the next day to criticize the protest. Díaz-Canel said he could understand people’s frustrations — but that violence against institutions would “never be understandable, justified or tolerated.”

Historians and analysts were careful but clear about what made Morón different from previous episodes of Cuban unrest. In 2021, when the largest protests since the Revolution swept the country, demonstrators attacked police stations and government stores. What happened in Morón was something else. “For the first time,” said José Raúl Gallego of the Cuban exile human rights group Cubalex, “people are going to the heart of political power and attacking it head-on.” The Communist Party building — not its enforcers, not its economic apparatus, but the institution itself — had become the target.

Andrés Pertierra, a Cuba historian at the University of Wisconsin, called it “exceptional.” He also said something that any honest reading of the current situation requires taking seriously: “The conditions that created this incident are still there, and will remain there indefinitely unless something changes drastically regarding energy and food supply.”

The numbers underneath Morón tell their own story. Protests and public expressions of dissent on the island numbered 30 in January 2026. In the first half of March alone, that figure had risen to 130. The trajectory is not ambiguous.

Cuba’s government has survived street pressure before — most recently and brutally in 2021, when more than 1,400 people were detained and many received sentences of up to thirty years. The apparatus of repression is intact. But the apparatus of repression runs on fuel too, and the conditions that produce one Morón will produce others. The negotiations happening in the offices and Vatican anterooms are racing against something happening in the streets that neither Washington nor Havana fully controls.

The Diesel Within the Diesel

People transit on a street without power during a nationwide blackout in Havana, March 21, 2026 [Yamil Lage/AFP]

While the blockade was producing its intended effects on Cuban society, it was also producing some unintended theatre that illuminated the absurdity of the situation with unusual clarity.

The U.S. Embassy in Havana, running low on diesel for its generators, asked the Cuban government for permission to import fuel. The Cuban government refused. The State Department, which had been weighing staff reductions at the embassy precisely because of the fuel shortage, now faced the prospect of reducing its diplomatic presence in Havana just as the most significant negotiations in years were underway — which would, under diplomatic convention, require a similar reduction in Cuban Embassy staff in Washington.

The Spanish Embassy in Havana found itself in an equally surreal position. It had a significant surplus of diesel — fuel originally imported for Spanish-owned hotels that were now sitting empty, closed for lack of tourists. The Spanish Embassy offered to share this surplus with other European embassies facing similar shortages. The Cuban government refused to allow the transfer.

Both refusals were tactically rational from Havana’s perspective. If the U.S. Embassy runs short of diesel, the blockade’s architects experience a small measure of the disruption they are imposing on the island. If the Spanish hotel fuel stays locked up, Cuba maintains control over every drop of petroleum on its territory — a leverage point in negotiations where fuel is literally the currency of survival.

But the image it produces is striking: a government so fuel-deprived that hospitals are suspending surgeries, simultaneously refusing to allow surplus fuel sitting in empty hotel basements to be shared with embassies across the street. The blockade had created a situation in which the Cuban government’s tactical interests and the Cuban people’s basic needs were pointing in opposite directions. That tension — the government managing the crisis in ways that serve its negotiating position while ordinary Cubans bear the cost — is not new in Cuban history. It is, by this point, the defining feature of it.

There was one break on the horizon that arrived: Russia’s Anatoly Kolodkin delivered 730,000 barrels of crude to Matanzas in late March — the first oil shipment in three months — after Trump waved it through saying “They have to survive.” The White House called it “not a policy change.” Cuba got enough fuel to keep the lights on for another few weeks. The negotiations continued.

The 1993 File

Somewhere in a government archive, there is a document that deserves to be read aloud in every meeting where American Cuba policy is being discussed.

It is a National Intelligence Estimate, since declassified, produced by the American intelligence community in 1993 — the year Cuba entered the Special Period, when the Soviet Union had collapsed and the island’s economy was in free fall, when the predictions of the regime’s imminent demise were universal and confident among the people paid to make such predictions.

“There is a better than even chance Fidel Castro’s government will fall within the next few years,” the document concluded.

That was thirty-three years ago. The government did not fall. The economy collapsed, recovered partially, collapsed again. The Soviet Union became Russia. Fidel died and Raúl took over and Raúl stepped aside and Díaz-Canel took office. Twelve American presidents — from Eisenhower through Biden — applied various combinations of pressure, incentive, embargo, and engagement, and the Cuban Communist Party is still governing the island.

The document also contained a warning that has aged considerably better than its prediction of collapse. Any change in power, it noted, would likely mean “substantial and possibly protracted instability,” including violent retribution and “large-scale emigration to the United States.” It predicted “demands for U.S. involvement” to manage the chaos that would follow.

The Trump administration, according to multiple analysts, appears to have given less thought to what happens after Díaz-Canel goes than to the mechanisms for making him go. The Venezuela template — remove the president, install a cooperative successor, control the resources — is being applied, but the fit is imperfect. Cuba has no oil to control. And where Venezuela had Delcy Rodríguez ready to step smoothly into Maduro’s place, Cuba’s succession picture is murkier: Raulito steering from behind the scenes, Fraga being tested in public, and a Communist Party apparatus that has spent sixty-seven years ensuring that no single individual’s removal can unlock the system. Zúniga is probably right that removing Díaz-Canel is the path of least resistance. Whether it produces anything beyond a symbolic reset is the question that experts across the political spectrum are answering with considerable skepticism. “Yes, they do have to get rid of him — along with the entire political bureau of the Communist Party and GAESA,” said Marlene Azor Hernández, a former University of Havana sociologist now in exile. “I think he has done a bad job, but he is a totally manipulated figurehead.”

“I don’t think it is in the DNA of a Cuban state to simply negotiate its exit,” Michael Bustamante, the chairman of Cuban studies at the University of Miami, said. That observation applies with equal force to the question of collapse: a state with sixty-seven years of experience surviving American pressure, built around a siege mentality that interprets every concession as an existential risk, does not simply stop existing because a blockade is applied. It adapts, calculates, and looks for the terms under which it can continue.

The Crab is at the table. The Cardinal is in the room. The indictments are being drafted in South Florida. And in Havana, ten million people are going fifteen hours a day without electricity, cooking on wood-fired ovens, paying thirty-five dollars a gallon for black market gasoline, waiting to find out what the men in the room will decide about their lives.

It is, as this series has argued from its first pages, entirely consistent with everything that has happened to Cuba for the past five hundred and thirty years. The people who bear the cost of the decisions made about Cuba have almost never been the people making them.

The pattern has not changed. Only the names in the room have.
