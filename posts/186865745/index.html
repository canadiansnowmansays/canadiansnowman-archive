The Asimov Gap

The Ultimate Question

Part 16: Democracy or Oligarchy

February 27, 2026

The Fork in the Road

We’ve spent fifteen parts examining different aspects of AI governance. Jobs. Surveillance. Weapons. Democracy. Children. Each part revealing another dimension of the same fundamental question.

Now we synthesize.

The ultimate question isn’t technical. It’s political:

Who controls AI? For whose benefit? Under what constraints?

Two answers possible:

Democratic control — AI developed with public input, regulated by democratic institutions, benefits broadly distributed, power constrained by law.

Oligarchic control — AI developed by billionaires, deployed for profit maximization, benefits concentrated at top, power unconstrained by democratic oversight.

We’re currently on the oligarchic path. We can still choose the democratic one. But the window is closing.

The Current State of Play

Let’s be clear about where we are:

Six companies control frontier AI: OpenAI (Microsoft-backed), Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta, xAI (Musk), Amazon. Combined market cap exceeds several trillion dollars.

These companies are:

  • Developing AGI without democratic input

  • Making decisions affecting billions without public oversight

  • Accumulating unprecedented power with minimal regulation

  • Lobbying aggressively against constraints

  • Moving faster than regulators can respond

The people running these companies:

  • Sam Altman (OpenAI): Net worth unknown, seeking trillions in investment

  • Elon Musk (xAI): $400+ billion, richest person in history

  • Mark Zuckerberg (Meta): $200+ billion

  • Sundar Pichai (Google): Runs company worth $2 trillion

  • Jeff Bezos (Amazon): $240+ billion

This is oligarchy by definition. Small group of ultra-wealthy individuals controlling transformative technology with civilizational implications.

They’re not elected. Not accountable to voters. Not constrained by democratic institutions in meaningful way.

And they’re building AGI.

Beyond Software: Embodied Oligarchic Control

Elon Musk calls it 'the infinite money glitch.' Not 'how do we share this.' Not 'how do we manage the transition.' Just: unlimited wealth extraction. 10 million units by 2027.

Oligarchic AI control isn’t limited to software.

Tesla’s Optimus represents oligarchy extending into physical labor. One company, controlled by one person, building humanoid robots at scale with minimal oversight.

Elon Musk calls it “the infinite money glitch.”

“If Optimus achieves five times the productivity of a person per year because it can operate 24/7, that’s why I call it the infinite money glitch.”

Think about that framing. AI-powered robots as “exploit in reality.” Break the rules. Generate unlimited wealth. Capture all gains.

Not “how do we ensure workers benefit?” Not “how do we manage this transition democratically?”

“This is my infinite money glitch.”

Manufacturing targets are extraordinary:

  • 1 million units per year by late 2026

  • 10 million annually by 2027

  • Dedicated factory in Texas already under construction

If achieved, this means:

  • 10 million humanoid workers under one company’s control

  • Operating 24/7 without breaks, benefits, or labor rights

  • Competing directly with human workers across industries

  • All productivity gains flowing to one corporation

This is oligarchic control of the means of production in its most literal form.

Not metaphorical. Not abstract. Actual control of robot workers displacing human workers, with zero democratic governance.

And it’s not just Tesla. Boston Dynamics. Figure AI. Multiple Chinese robotics firms. All racing to deploy humanoid robots at scale.

The pattern is identical to software AI:

  • Private development without public input

  • Race dynamics preventing caution

  • Deployment before governance established

  • Benefits captured by owners

  • Costs borne by workers

Except now it’s not just software replacing cognitive work. It’s machines replacing physical work.

This is the ultimate oligarchic capture: Control of both digital intelligence AND physical labor. All productivity gains flowing upward. Workers competing against tireless robots owned by billionaires.

And it’s happening right now, with virtually no democratic deliberation.

Sanders’ Core Warning

Return to December symposium. Sanders’ fundamental point:

“We have a choice. AI can benefit everyone or concentrate power among oligarchs. That choice will be made in next few years. If we don’t act, oligarchs win by default.”

Sanders is saying:

The technology is neutral. The outcome isn’t.

Same AI could:

Create abundance → Universal healthcare, education, housing through AI-driven productivity OR concentrate wealth → Oligarchs capture AI gains, everyone else fights for scraps

Enhance democracy → Better information, more participation, transparent governance OR enable authoritarianism → Surveillance, manipulation, algorithmic control

Protect workers → AI augments human labor, shares productivity gains OR destroy livelihoods → Mass unemployment, eliminated jobs, no social support

Outcome depends entirely on who controls AI and what constraints exist on that control.

The mindset is now explicit. At the India AI Impact Summit in February, Sam Altman told a packed pavilion that "it takes a lot of energy to train a human" — equating twenty years of raising a child with the compute cycles needed to produce ChatGPT. A week earlier, Dario Amodei made a nearly identical analogy, likening model training to human evolution. This isn't a gaffe. It's a worldview. When the people building AI see no meaningful distinction between organic life and algorithmic products, the question of "who controls AI and for whose benefit" answers itself. You don't ask a training dataset for consent. You don't owe it a living. You don't share profits with it. If humans are just inefficient models, the oligarchic logic is complete.

Currently: Oligarchs control. Few constraints exist. Default outcome is oligarchic.

The Oligarchic Playbook

We’ve seen this before. Oligarchs follow predictable pattern when new transformative technology emerges:

Step 1: Move Fast, Break Democracy

“Move fast and break things” — Facebook’s motto. Translated: Deploy before regulators can respond. Create facts on ground. Make regulation seem impossible.

This is happening with AI. Companies racing to deploy. Each breakthrough creating new capabilities before society can absorb last one. Regulators always behind.

Move fast. Capture the narrative. Shape the regulation. Normalize the new reality. It's the same playbook every time. It works every time — until it doesn't.

Step 2: Capture the Narrative

Control conversation about technology. Fund research that supports your interests. Sponsor conferences. Create industry-friendly frameworks. Make alternatives seem unrealistic.

AI industry doing this aggressively. Funding AI safety research that focuses on distant risks (paperclip maximizers) rather than immediate threats (oligarchic control). Creating “AI ethics” positions that provide cover without constraining deployment.

Step 3: Regulatory Arbitrage

When regulation comes, shape it. Lobby for industry-friendly rules. Threaten to move operations overseas. Claim regulation will hurt competitiveness. Offer “self-regulation” as alternative.

Already happening. AI companies lobbying heavily against EU AI Act. Threatening to leave Europe. Claiming China will win if we regulate. Classic oligarchic playbook.

Step 4: Normalize the New Reality

Make oligarchic control seem natural. Inevitable. The only way things could work. “Disruption is progress. Regulation stifles innovation. Trust the market.”

We’re here now. Many people assume AI must be developed by private companies. Can’t imagine democratic alternative. Accept oligarchic control as normal.

This normalization is dangerous. Makes alternatives seem unrealistic before they’re tried.

The political alignment is becoming explicit. OpenAI president Greg Brockman made a $25 million donation to the pro-Trump PAC MAGA Inc. The companies building AI aren't just failing to resist oligarchic capture — they're funding it.

The Democratic Alternative

What would democratic AI control actually look like?

Public Investment and Ownership

Instead of six companies controlling AI:

Public AI research institutions. Like CERN for particle physics. Funded by governments. Results publicly available. Governed democratically.

The Large Hadron Collider at CERN — 23 member nations, $5 billion in public investment, and the most complex machine ever built. No billionaire owns it. No CEO controls it. The results belong to humanity. We built this for particle physics. We could build one for AI. (Photo: CERN)

National AI services. Like public utilities. Basic AI capabilities available to everyone. Not controlled by profit-maximizing corporations.

Open source models. Funded publicly. Available freely. Can’t be weaponized for profit or control.

This isn’t radical. Internet was created by public research (DARPA). GPS is public service. Weather forecasting is public good. AI could be too.

Dash offers a practical checklist for what a democratic AI alternative looks like: environmentally responsible, trained on data with consent, open source so independent experts can evaluate it, responsible in its labor practices, and only implemented in apps users choose — never forced. "That doesn't feel like science fiction," he says. "That feels possible." He's right. It seemed insurmountable, once, that people would use a web browser that wasn't Microsoft's. Then Firefox happened. Then Chrome happened. Alternatives don't require the giants to fall. They just require the alternatives to exist.

Democratic Governance

Instead of CEOs making civilizational decisions:

Public deliberation. Citizens’ assemblies. Democratic input on AI development priorities. What problems should AI solve? What risks are acceptable?

Regulatory frameworks. Clear rules about AI use. Enforced by democratic institutions. Updated as technology evolves.

International cooperation. Like nuclear non-proliferation. Democratic nations cooperating to prevent AI arms race. Setting shared standards.

Shared Benefits

Instead of oligarchs capturing AI gains:

Universal basic income. Funded by AI productivity. Ensures everyone benefits from automation.

Reduced working hours. AI handles routine tasks. Humans work less. Productivity gains shared.

Public services. AI-enhanced healthcare, education, transportation. Available to all. Not just wealthy.

Worker protections. Job training. Income support during transitions. Bargaining power against AI displacement.

This is the abundance scenario. Possible if AI benefits are shared. Impossible if oligarchs hoard gains.

Asimov’s Ultimate Vision

Asimov spent 50 years exploring one question: Can humanity govern its own creations?

His answer evolved:

Early optimism (1940s-50s): Three Laws would work. Humans could build safe AI through clever engineering.

Growing concern (1960s-70s): Three Laws had loopholes. Power corrupts. Even good intentions can lead to control.

Mature wisdom (1980s-90s): Technology doesn’t determine outcomes. Human choices do. Democracy or oligarchy depends on which humans choose.

Foundation series ending (1993): Reveals that both psychohistory (algorithmic control) and robots (technological control) are insufficient. Humanity must govern itself through democratic means, or lose freedom entirely.

Asimov’s final message: Democratic self-governance is hard. Dangerous. Imperfect. But the only alternative to tyranny.

This applies directly to AI. Algorithmic governance seems efficient. Oligarchic control seems inevitable. But democracy remains only path to freedom.

Why Oligarchs Will Fight This

Democratic AI control threatens oligarchic power directly.

Currently, oligarchs have:

  • Control of AI development

  • Ability to deploy AI for profit

  • Power to shape AI’s impact

  • Minimal democratic constraints

Democratic AI governance would mean:

  • Public oversight of AI development

  • Regulation of AI deployment

  • Sharing of AI benefits

  • Accountability to voters

Oligarchs will fight this. Not because they’re evil. Because their interests conflict with democratic control.

They’ll argue:

“Regulation stifles innovation” — But innovation for whom? Innovation that benefits oligarchs at society’s expense isn’t progress.

“China will win AI race” — But China is authoritarian. Democratic nations can cooperate without becoming authoritarian. Nuclear non-proliferation proved this.

“Markets allocate resources efficiently” — But AI isn’t ordinary market good. It’s infrastructure. Like electricity. Water. Should be public utility.

“We can self-regulate” — But self-regulation has failed repeatedly. Tobacco. Finance. Social media. Oligarchs don’t constrain themselves voluntarily.

These arguments are predictable. We should expect them. And reject them.

The Asymmetry Problem

Here’s the difficulty:

Oligarchs are organized. They coordinate. Lobby together. Fund think tanks. Shape narrative. Move in unison when threatened.

Democratic forces are fragmented. Workers in different industries. Citizens in different countries. NGOs with different focuses. Movements with different priorities.

Oligarchs have resources. Billions to spend on lobbying. Ability to hire armies of lawyers. Control of platforms.

Democratic forces have limited resources. Grassroots funding. Volunteer time. Competing against concentrated wealth.

This asymmetry makes democratic organizing harder. But not impossible.

History shows: Concentrated power can be defeated by organized democratic movements. Labor unions defeated robber barons. Civil rights movements defeated Jim Crow. Environmental movements forced corporate accountability.

Pattern is consistent: Oligarchs have advantages. But democracy has legitimacy, numbers, persistence.

Democratic movements win when they organize effectively and maintain pressure.

The Window Is Closing

Here’s what makes AI different from previous technological challenges:

Speed. Earlier transformations took decades. Railroad age. Electrification. Computing. AI is transforming society in years, not decades.

By 2030:

  • AGI might exist

  • Millions of jobs might be automated

  • Surveillance infrastructure might be complete

  • Autonomous weapons might be deployed

  • Election manipulation might be normalized

Democratic governance must happen before these become entrenched realities.

After AGI exists, democratic control becomes much harder. Power concentrated. Systems locked in. Alternatives foreclosed.

This is the urgency Sanders is communicating. Not fear-mongering. Realism about closing window.

What Victory Looks Like

Imagine democratic AI future in 2040:

Economic: Universal basic income funded by AI productivity. 20-hour work week. High-quality public services. Shared prosperity.

Political: AI enhances democracy. Better information. More participation. Transparent governance. Accountable institutions.

Social: Education personalized but humanistic. Healthcare accessible to all. Environment protected. Creativity flourishing.

Technological: AI serves human values. Augments human capability. Remains under human control. Benefits distributed broadly.

This isn’t utopia. There would still be problems. Disagreements. Challenges. But problems would be democratically governed, not oligarchically imposed.

This future is possible. Technology exists. Wealth exists. What’s missing is political will to choose democracy over oligarchy.

What Defeat Looks Like

Imagine oligarchic AI future in 2040:

Economic: Extreme inequality. Oligarchs control AGI gains. Mass unemployment. Minimal social support. Permanent underclass.

Political: Managed democracy. Elections manipulated by AI. Surveillance ubiquitous. Dissent suppressed algorithmically.

Social: Education serves oligarchic interests. Healthcare for wealthy only. Environment sacrificed for profit. Creativity commodified.

Technological: AI optimizes for oligarchic power. Human agency diminished. Democratic control eliminated. Algorithmic authoritarianism.

This isn’t dystopian fiction. This is current trajectory. Default outcome if nothing changes.

We’re not there yet. But we’re heading there. Fast.

The Ultimate Choice

Synthesis of entire series:

AI is most powerful technology humans have created. More transformative than nuclear weapons. More impactful than internet. Could determine human future.

Two paths forward:

Democratic path: Public ownership. Democratic governance. Shared benefits. Human control. Requires political will. Organizing. Regulation. Public investment. Hard but possible.

Oligarchic path: Private control. Concentrated power. Hoarded gains. Algorithmic governance. Happens by default. Requires no effort. Easy but catastrophic.

We’re at fork in road. Must choose. Soon.

Asimov spent 50 years warning about this choice. Sanders is warning now. Hinton is warning. Countless others are warning.

The warning is clear:

Choose democracy, or lose it.

Not hyperbole. Reality.

AI in democratic hands could create unprecedented human flourishing.

AI in oligarchic hands could create permanent hierarchical control.

Same technology. Opposite outcomes.

Everything depends on who controls it.

What You Can Do

Individual actions matter:

  1. Get informed. Understand AI capabilities, risks, governance options. This series is start. Keep learning.

  2. Support democratic AI organizations. Groups fighting for public AI research, algorithmic accountability, democratic governance. Fund them. Amplify them.

  3. Demand political action. Contact representatives. Support candidates backing AI regulation. Make this electoral issue.

  4. Organize locally. Build coalitions. Workers. Citizens. Students. Shared interests in democratic AI control.

  5. Resist normalization. When oligarchs claim their control is inevitable, push back. Alternatives exist. Democracy is possible.

  6. Use your leverage. Workers can organize unions demanding AI oversight. Consumers can boycott oligarchic platforms. Voters can elect AI accountability candidates.

  7. Think long-term. This is generational fight. Like labor rights. Civil rights. Environmental protection. Requires sustained commitment.

Individual actions combine into movements. Movements create political pressure. Political pressure forces change.

This is how democracy works.

Closing: The Ultimate Question

Democracy or oligarchy?

That’s the question.

Not “Will AI be developed?” (It will.) Not “Can we stop AI?” (We can’t.) Not “Is AI dangerous?” (It can be.)

The question is: Who controls AI?

Current answer: Oligarchs.

Democratic answer: All of us.

We can still choose the democratic answer. But we must choose actively. Soon.

Because the ultimate question isn’t about AI at all.

It’s about what kind of society we want to be.

AI is mirror reflecting our choices. Will we choose shared prosperity or concentrated power? Democratic governance or algorithmic control? Human agency or oligarchic domination?

Asimov posed these questions 70 years ago.

Sanders is posing them now.

The answers will determine our future.

Choose wisely. Choose democratically. Choose soon.

The Asimov Gap isn’t technology gap.

It’s the gap between the thoughtful, democratic future we could build and the oligarchic, algorithmic present we’re accepting.

Close that gap. Before it’s too late.
