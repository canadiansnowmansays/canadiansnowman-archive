Part 1 The Foundation of Grift: How Fred Trump Built the Template

June 6, 2025

June 6, 2025

The $148 Million Dinner Party

Picture this: the President of the United States hosting a black-tie gala at his Virginia golf club for people who spent a combined $148 million on his cryptocurrency for the chance to eat dinner with him. The food was reportedly terrible, but the Richard Mille watches weren't rare—one attendee counted at least 16 people wearing the $200,000+ timepieces.

Meanwhile, about 764,000 people who bought the same cryptocurrency lost money. The typical buyer spent $100 and lost $62. Conservative radio host Rebecca Davis, who knew nothing about cryptocurrency but believed in Trump's business savvy, watched her $32 investment shrink to $11.

This isn't incompetence. It's the culmination of a business model perfected over three generations: extract maximum personal gain while transferring all risks and costs to others. To understand how we got to cryptocurrency dinner parties funded by the White House, you need to start with Donald Trump's father—a man who turned tax evasion into an art form.

Chapter 1: Fred Trump's Masterclass in Wealth Extraction

Fred Trump didn't just build apartments in Brooklyn—he built a template for generational wealth extraction that would make the robber barons blush. The New York Times investigation that exposed his schemes reads like a masterclass in creative accounting, revealing how Donald Trump received at least $413 million from his father over the decades, much of it through dubious tax dodges, including outright fraud.

But the devil is in the details, and those details show a level of sophistication that would influence generations of Trump family business practices.

The All County Scam: A Shell Game with Real Money

The centerpiece of Fred's operation was a company called All County Building Supply & Maintenance, set up in 1992 ostensibly as a purchasing agent to supply Fred Trump's buildings with boilers, cleaning supplies and other goods. But All County was actually owned by Fred's children—Donald, Robert, Maryanne, and Elizabeth.

Here's how the scam worked: Fred needed to buy supplies for his buildings anyway. Instead of buying them directly, he created All County as a middleman. All County bought the supplies at regular prices, then sold them to Fred at massive markups—often 20% to 50% higher than the original cost.

The inflated invoices became tax-deductible business expenses for Fred, while the markup became untaxed income for his children. It was brilliant in its simplicity: Fred was essentially paying his children through fake business expenses, avoiding both gift taxes and income taxes.

According to The New York Times, Fred and his wife Mary provided over $1 billion (in 2018 currency) to their children while avoiding over $500 million in gift taxes. The family that should have paid $550 million in taxes under the 55% gift tax rate paid just $52.2 million—about 5% of what they owed.

The Donald Loan Program: Gifts Disguised as Business

Fred's generosity to his son Donald was particularly creative. Documents show Donald received loans totaling nearly $5 million in 1979 alone. But these weren't ordinary loans—they were open-ended, meaning there was no set payment schedule, no real pressure or expectation to pay them back.

When you're getting $5 million with no repayment schedule, that's not a loan—that's a gift with paperwork. But calling it a loan avoided the 55% gift tax that would have applied to such a massive transfer of wealth.

The arrangement got even more creative as time went on. In 1987, when Donald's loan debt to his father exceeded $11 million, Fred invested $15.5 million in Trump Palace Condominiums. Four years later, in 1991, Fred sold these shares to his son for $10,000—evading millions of dollars in gift taxes by masking what was essentially a $15.5 million gift as a business transaction.

The Pattern Emerges

This pattern would define Donald's entire business career: someone else provides the money, he gets the upside, and when things go bad, the lenders eat the losses. But Fred's influence went deeper than just financial support—he was teaching Donald a worldview.

Fred's tax evasion schemes weren't just about saving money. They were about a fundamental approach to business: extract maximum value while minimizing personal risk and legal exposure. Use shell companies, inflate expenses, undervalue assets, and always find ways to make others bear the costs of your wealth accumulation.

Chapter 2: The Lessons Learned

The education Donald received from his father wasn't just about real estate—it was about a philosophy of wealth extraction. Fred showed him how to:

  • Use other people's money whenever possible

  • Structure deals so you profit regardless of outcomes

  • Transfer risk to partners, investors, and creditors

  • Exploit regulatory gaps and legal loopholes

  • Generate revenue from businesses you don't actually own or operate

Most importantly, Fred taught Donald that you could profit even from failure—as long as you structured the deals correctly from the beginning.

These weren't just business tactics; they were a comprehensive approach to extracting wealth from any system you could access. Fred had turned his position as a real estate developer into a money-printing machine that benefited his family while leaving others to absorb the risks and costs.

The Normalization of Cutting Corners

Growing up in this environment, Donald learned that everyone cuts corners, everyone looks for angles, and everyone tries to extract maximum value from every transaction. What might seem like fraud to outsiders was just smart business to the Trumps.

This mindset—that being clever enough to avoid taxes and shift risks makes you smart, not criminal—would become central to Donald's identity. He learned to see tax avoidance not as cheating the system, but as evidence of superior business acumen.

Fred's influence manifested in Donald's ventures throughout the 1970s and 1980s. Deals like the Commodore Hotel redevelopment involved mob-tied contractors, while his mentor Roy Cohn connected him to Genovese and Gambino figures. By the time Donald was ready to strike out on his own, he had internalized a business model that prioritized personal enrichment above all other considerations.

Chapter 3: The Template is Set

By the 1990s, the Trump family template was fully formed:

Step 1: Find ways to access other people's money (investors, lenders, taxpayers, customers)
Step 2: Structure deals to extract fees, payments, or value regardless of outcomes
Step 3: Use legal mechanisms (bankruptcy, shell companies, licensing deals) to limit personal exposure
Step 4: When things go wrong, let others absorb the losses while you move on to the next deal

This wasn't a business model—it was a wealth extraction system. And Fred had spent decades perfecting it, teaching Donald not just the tactics but the mindset that would make such extraction feel natural, even justified.

The genius of Fred's approach was that it was mostly legal. He found gaps in tax law, exploited regulatory blind spots, and used complex financial structures to achieve what simpler criminal schemes might accomplish through fraud. It was sophisticated fraud, but dressed up in legal language and executed by lawyers and accountants.

The Psychological Foundation

But perhaps Fred's most important contribution to the Trump empire wasn't financial—it was psychological. He taught Donald that wealth extraction was not only acceptable but admirable. That finding ways to avoid paying taxes or shifting costs to others wasn't cheating—it was being smart.

This psychological framework would prove crucial as Donald moved from his father's Brooklyn real estate business to Manhattan casinos, golf courses, and eventually the White House. The tactics would evolve and scale up, but the fundamental approach remained the same: find ways to get rich while making others pay the costs.

The Foundation is Laid

Fred Trump's legacy wasn't just the hundreds of millions he passed to his children—it was the business philosophy that wealth should flow to those clever enough to extract it, regardless of the consequences for others. He showed Donald that you could build an empire not by creating value, but by capturing value that others created.

This template—perfected in Brooklyn real estate deals and tax avoidance schemes—would soon be applied to Atlantic City casinos, Manhattan real estate, reality television, presidential campaigns, and eventually cryptocurrency schemes run from the White House.

The foundation was set. Fred had created not just a wealthy son, but a wealth extraction machine that would eventually capture the highest office in the land.
