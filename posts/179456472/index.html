The Rehabilitation of a Murderer

The Rehabilitation of a Murderer

Part 1: The Deal

November 20, 2025

On Tuesday evening, November 18, 2025, nearly 700 guests filed into the State Dining Room of the White House. Elon Musk was there. So were Tim Cook, Jensen Huang, Lisa Su, and Marc Benioff. Cristiano Ronaldo posed for selfies. The menu featured pistachio-crusted rack of lamb, followed by a couverture mousse pear for dessert. Outside, military bands had played and flag-carrying horsemen had paraded on the red carpet. F-35 fighter jets—the very aircraft that would soon be sold to the guest of honor—streaked overhead in formation.

The guest of honor was Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman of Saudi Arabia, making his first visit to the United States in seven years. The last time he was here, in 2018, he was feted as a reformer. Then came October 2, 2018, when Washington Post columnist Jamal Khashoggi walked into the Saudi consulate in Istanbul and never walked out.

Elsewhere that Tuesday evening, Hanan Elatr Khashoggi—Jamal’s widow—gave interviews describing what the past seven years have been like. “It destroyed my life,” she told CBS News. “It inverted my life.” She told CNN it was “very painful” to see Trump welcome MBS. “It would be better if Jamal was here and receiving the crown prince by himself and meet with him and share with him his vision and mission and all of his ideas.”

Hanan still doesn’t know where her husband’s body is. The Saudi government has never told her. She wrote on X: “Much like the families in Israel, I would like to recover the remains of my husband so that I may have an appropriate religious burial ceremony. And much like the 9-11 families, I am seeking financial compensation for the murder of my husband.”

The pistachio lamb was reportedly excellent.

“Things Happen”

Before the dinner, in the Oval Office, Trump and MBS sat for questions from reporters. ABC News Chief White House Correspondent Mary Bruce asked the crown prince about Khashoggi’s murder and the anger 9/11 families have expressed over the visit.

Trump’s response was immediate and furious. “You don’t have to embarrass our guest by asking a question like this,” he told Bruce. He called her a “terrible person” and “fake news,” one of “the worst in the business.” He threatened FCC action against ABC.

Then he defended MBS directly: “Things happen, but he knew nothing about it, and we can leave it at that.”

This directly contradicts the 2021 CIA assessment, declassified by the Biden administration, which concluded that MBS had approved the operation that killed Khashoggi. The assessment was based on the crown prince’s control of decision-making in the Kingdom, the involvement of his personal protective detail, and his previous support for using violent measures to silence dissidents abroad.

But Trump wasn’t finished. “You’re mentioning somebody that was extremely controversial,” he said of the murdered journalist. “A lot of people didn’t like that gentleman.”

Character assassination of the assassinated. To defend the murderer, Trump attacked the murdered man.

MBS, sitting beside Trump, called the killing “a huge mistake” and said it was “really painful to hear, anyone losing their life for no real purpose.” He referred to Khashoggi only as “the journalist”—never by name. He claimed Saudi Arabia “did all the right steps of investigation” and “improved our system.” He did not reveal where Khashoggi’s body is. He did not apologize to the widow.

And then Trump praised him: “He’s done something incredible in terms of human rights.”

Money and Power

Why do they get along so well?

The answer is not complicated. MBS has money. Trump wants money. MBS has unchecked power. Trump wants unchecked power. That’s it. That’s the whole relationship.

In a recording, Trump admitted he was fond of authoritarian leaders, telling journalist Bob Woodward the “tougher and meaner” they are, “the better I get along with them.”

MBS is tough. MBS is mean. MBS is rich beyond imagination. And MBS writes checks.

Money and power. The rest is theater.

Raghida Dergham, chair of the Beirut Institute think tank, told OPB: “There’s a blurred line between Trump’s family business and his administration. And they don’t hide it. They don’t make that a secret.”

Robert Weissman of Public Citizen was more blunt: “We’re seeing the complete merger of Trump’s business interests with US diplomacy and military policy. How are Americans supposed to think that Trump’s decision on F-35 sales, over internal objections, not to mention over human rights concerns, is unconnected to Trump’s business arrangements with Saudi Arabia?”

The Organization Jamal Built

Jamal Khashoggi was more than a columnist. He founded DAWN—Democracy for the Arab World Now—to promote democratic reform in the Middle East. The organization survives him, and it responded to Tuesday’s events with fury.

“President Trump has sold U.S. protection—backed by American soldiers whose lives will be on the line—to protect a reckless, impulsive dictator, all in the interests of personal and corporate gains,” said Sarah Leah Whitson, DAWN’s Executive Director. “While the defense industry and American billionaires will profit handsomely with the gifts Trump is doling out to MBS, the American people will be left holding the bill.”

Raed Jarrar, DAWN’s Advocacy Director, was more direct: “President Trump has Jamal Khashoggi’s blood on his hands. Seven years after ordering Jamal’s brutal murder and dismemberment, MBS still hasn’t revealed where his remains are so his family can bury him, and Trump won’t even ask. By defending MBS and lying about the intelligence community’s findings, Trump has made himself complicit in every execution and imprisonment MBS has ordered since.”

“Where Is Our White House Meeting?”

The timing of MBS’s visit carried additional weight. In August, a federal judge ruled that Saudi Arabia must stand trial for its role in the September 11 attacks, finding that Saudi government agents provided “essential support” for the hijackers.

Terry Strada, whose husband Tom died in the North Tower, chairs 9/11 Families United. Her statement on MBS’s visit was scathing: “The crown prince knows nothing of the pain of the 9/11 families. He is actively working to impede our efforts to ensure extensive evidence of Saudi government support for al-Qaeda and the terrorist hijackers are brought to light, harboring a former agent that produced a casing video of the U.S. Capitol building, and trying to rewrite history with investments.”

Brett Eagleson, whose father Bruce died in the attacks, asked the question that defines this administration’s priorities: “Where is our White House meeting? He will meet with anybody at the drop of a dime. He’ll meet with Putin. He’ll meet with MBS. But when it comes to the families, we are completely ignored.”

The Grift Timeline

To understand what happened Tuesday, you need to understand what happened in the days leading up to it. The sequence is remarkable for its lack of subtlety:

Saturday, November 15: Jerry Inzerillo, CEO of Diriyah—the $63 billion Saudi government development project that has been courting the Trump Organization—told reporters that a deal with Trump was “just a matter of time.” The New York Times reported that Eric Trump and Jared Kushner have both visited Diriyah in the past, and that Inzerillo has specifically framed the pitch to “appeal to him as a developer.” MBS chairs the sovereign wealth fund that controls Diriyah.

Sunday, November 16: The Trump Organization announced a new project with Dar Global—their Saudi-linked development partners—in the Maldives. The Trump International Hotel Maldives will cost $300 million and feature 80 ultra-luxury beach and overwater villas. It will also be the world’s first “tokenized” hotel development project. The announcement came on the eve of MBS’s arrival.

Monday, November 17: Reports confirmed that Trump had approved the sale of F-35 fighter jets to Saudi Arabia—the first sale of these advanced aircraft to an Arab country. This came over internal objections and without the Israel normalization requirement that had been a condition for such sales.

Tuesday, November 18: The state dinner. Trump designated Saudi Arabia a “major non-NATO ally”—a strategic designation providing expedited access to American military hardware and intelligence. MBS announced he would increase Saudi investment commitments to the United States from $600 billion to $1 trillion.

Wednesday, November 19: At the Kennedy Center, Trump and MBS—joined by Elon Musk and Jensen Huang—presided over the signing of $270 billion in business deals at the US-Saudi Investment Forum.

Throughout all of this, Dar Global’s payments to the Trump Organization continue. Last year alone, they paid the Trump Organization $21.9 million in licensing fees—a portion of which goes directly to the president.

As Robert Weissman noted: “Licensing deals are a mechanism that allows the Saudi government to transfer money straight to the Trump family in exchange for no services or work at all.”

What MBS Actually Bought

The state dinner was not merely ceremonial. MBS left Washington with an extraordinary package of military and diplomatic concessions:

Military: “Major non-NATO ally” designation, providing expedited access to U.S. military hardware. F-35 fighter jets—the first sale to an Arab country. Nearly 300 Abrams tanks. A Strategic Defense Agreement that “fortifies deterrence across the Middle East.”

Nuclear: A Joint Declaration on Civil Nuclear Energy Cooperation that “builds the legal foundation for a decades-long, multi-billion-dollar nuclear energy partnership.”

Other Agreements: Critical Minerals Framework. AI Memorandum of Understanding.

Rehabilitation: Complete transformation from international pariah to honored guest. The CIA assessment contradicted by the president himself. Seven years of isolation ended with a red carpet, military flyover, and pistachio lamb.

When asked if Israel would be concerned about the F-35 sale—Saudi Arabia has not normalized relations with Israel, and the sale eliminates Israel’s exclusive regional military edge—Trump said he didn’t think so. He said he wouldn’t downgrade the planes for Israel’s sake.

Multiple Saudi sources told CNN that Riyadh had successfully separated the two issues: they got the F-35s without having to normalize with Israel first. Yair Golan, former deputy chief of the Israeli army, warned that the move risked opening “an arms race in the Middle East.”

Mohammed bin Salman walked into the White House seven years after the CIA assessed he ordered the murder and dismemberment of a Washington Post journalist. He received everything he wanted. And he left without ever revealing where Jamal Khashoggi’s body is.

That’s what a trillion dollars buys.

But there is another ledger—one that doesn’t appear in the $270 billion in signed agreements or the $1 trillion in investment commitments.

It’s the ledger of human lives.

Jamal Khashoggi is not the only person MBS has killed. He is not even the only person killed this month. And the pattern of expendable lives extends far beyond Saudi Arabia—to the Caribbean, where American bombs are falling on fishing boats, and to the coast of Massachusetts, where the fishermen who voted for Trump are watching their livelihoods disappear.

Even he knows.
