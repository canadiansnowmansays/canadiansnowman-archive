The Enablers Then and Now

What a 10,000-Word NYT Investigation Reveals About DOJ's Silence

December 16, 2025

Pattern recognition: Jeffrey Epstein at Bear Stearns, late 1970s, and Attorney General Pam Bondi in 2025. The New York Times documents how institutions enabled Epstein for decades. December 19 will show whether DOJ continues that pattern or ends it.


CanadianSnowmanSays
December 16, 2025

This morning, The New York Times published a 10,000-word investigation into how Jeffrey Epstein built his fortune. The reporters spent months interviewing dozens of former colleagues, tracking down unpublished recordings, reviewing private archives, and combing through thousands of pages of records.

The investigation reveals a pattern that started in 1976 and continued for decades: institutions and powerful people repeatedly gave Epstein second chances, protected him from consequences, and enabled his rise despite clear evidence of fraud, manipulation, and criminality.

At Bear Stearns in 1976, Epstein was caught lying about his college degrees. His supervisor asked the CEO what to do. The response: treat him like a normal employee—which made clear he was not a normal employee. When violations continued—expense fraud, securities violations, illegal loans—the firm conducted an internal investigation. Epstein resigned. Bear Stearns let him walk away with credentials intact and continued to enable him for decades.

Ace Greenberg, a top Bear Stearns executive, hired Epstein and then introduced him to his daughter.Credit...Fred R. Conrad/The New York Times


The Times investigation documents how this pattern repeated itself across forty years. Douglas Leese, a British defense contractor, mentored Epstein and gave him an expense account—until he discovered Epstein was charging personal Concorde flights and luxury hotels. Leese cut ties, but the damage was done. Epstein had learned he could abuse trust and walk away.

Michael Stroll gave Epstein $450,000 to invest. Most of it vanished. Epstein dodged calls and sent Stroll a quart of oil to pretend a deal existed. The dispute went to court. Epstein won on technical grounds and never paid back the money. Stroll would later say: “I unknowingly seeded his growth.”

The pattern continued. Epstein and partners manipulated Pennwalt Corporation’s stock price by announcing a fake buyout offer, driving shares up 40 percent. But Epstein’s scheme went further: he had recruited outside investors to fund the deal, used their money to book profits for himself, and then refused to return their funds. It was stock manipulation combined with outright theft. No consequences.

Les Wexner gave Epstein power of attorney over his finances. Epstein “seemed to be taking whatever he thought he deserved,” often measured in tens of millions. Wexner would later claim Epstein “had misappropriated vast sums of money.” By then, Epstein had bought mansions, private islands, and aircraft with funds that appeared to flow from Wexner.

At every stage, institutions and individuals saw warning signs—and chose to look away, give second chances, or actively enable him.

Now read that history and ask yourself: What’s happening at the Department of Justice in December 2025?

THE PARALLEL PATTERN

The Times investigation reveals how Epstein built his fortune through a specific pattern:

  1. Show promise, gain access - Get hired at Bear Stearns, impress executives, date the CEO’s daughter

  2. Test boundaries, commit violations - Lie about credentials, abuse expense accounts, break securities rules

  3. Get caught, face consequences - Internal investigations, documented violations, clear evidence

  4. Avoid punishment through connections - Protected status, powerful defenders, institutional reluctance

  5. Walk away and repeat - Use credentials from previous institution to gain access to the next

This isn’t just Epstein’s pattern. It’s the pattern of accommodation psychology we’ve documented across thirteen episodes.

Compare:

Bear Stearns 1976-1981:

  • Epstein caught lying about college degrees → Kept his job because of relationship with CEO’s daughter

  • Caught abusing expense accounts → No consequences because of protected status

  • Caught violating securities rules → Internal investigation but allowed to resign with credentials intact

  • Continued using Bear Stearns affiliation for decades → Firm never publicly disavowed him

DOJ 2025:

  • Bondi claims “client list” exists in February → Claims it doesn’t exist in July → No accountability for false statements

  • FBI sanitizes files in March, flagging Trump’s name → Bloomberg reports operation → No investigation of who ordered it

  • DOJ closes investigation in July claiming “nothing there” → Reopens in November with “new information” → Never explains what changed

  • Congress demands briefing by December 5 → Bondi ignores deadline → No consequences

  • Statutory deadline December 19 approaching → No indication of comprehensive release → Pattern suggests sanitized version coming

The institutions are different. The decades are different. But the pattern is identical: evidence of wrongdoing, institutional protection, avoidance of accountability, continuation of the behavior.

WHAT THE TIMES COULDN’T INVESTIGATE

The Times investigation ends essentially in 1999, when Epstein applied for tax breaks in the U.S. Virgin Islands. The reporters note: “Epstein’s pace of raping, abusing and trafficking girls and young women was accelerating. Ultimately, he would face accusations from hundreds of women.”

But the investigation doesn’t—and couldn’t—examine what else might have been happening during those years. The reporters focused on financial crimes, not intelligence operations. They documented scams and manipulation, not kompromat.

This is where the Epstein Files become critical.

Lev Parnas has stated the files contain intelligence assessments documenting a 40-year Russian operation. He’s described materials showing which foreign intelligence services ran collection operations through Epstein’s network. He’s claimed the files prove foreign intelligence compromise of American officials.

The Times investigation shows Epstein had access to:

  • Presidents (Bill Clinton, with “get well soon” notes and White House visits)

  • Senators (George Mitchell, John Glenn, hosting them on private planes)

  • Billionaires (Les Wexner, with power of attorney over finances; Leon Black, as later client)

  • Intelligence officials (through his “bounty hunting” work and international connections)

  • Academics (Harvard donations, Alan Dershowitz cultivation)

  • Foreign dignitaries (Benjamin Netanyahu, Shimon Peres, Prince Andrew, Saudi crown prince)

If you’re a foreign intelligence service looking to compromise American leadership, Epstein’s network—documented in meticulous detail by the Times—would be the perfect vehicle.

The financial crimes the Times documents—the scams, the theft, the manipulation—could be leverage. The sex trafficking operation that was “accelerating” by 1999 could be leverage. The connections to power could be access.

This is what the Epstein Files might contain. This is what DOJ is preparing to withhold in three days.

THE INSTITUTIONAL ENABLERS

The Times investigation is devastating in its documentation of who enabled Epstein:

Bear Stearns - Hired him despite no credentials, protected him despite violations, continued relationship after he left Douglas Leese - Mentored him, gave him expense account and access to British elites Clark Schubach - His former manager, who told the Times he has “fond memories” of Epstein and admitted to sending multiple young female assistants to his apartment Les Wexner - Gave him power of attorney, ignored warnings from colleagues and investigators JPMorgan Chase - Provided services enabling his sex trafficking operation until 2013 U.S. Virgin Islands - Granted tax breaks after hearing his “fantastical version of his life story” Alan Dershowitz - Defended him after Epstein bailed him out of investment losses Harvard University - Accepted his donations, gave him academic connections Rockefeller University - Put him on the board after donations Bill Clinton - Made him regular White House visitor, wrote personal notes

And dozens more—colleagues who maintained friendships, investors who funded schemes, institutions that granted legitimacy, officials who granted access.

Now add another name to that list:

Department of Justice, 2025 - Ignores statutory deadline, refuses briefings to Congress, prepares sanitized release.

The institution charged with prosecuting Epstein’s crimes is now exhibiting the same pattern as the institutions that enabled them.

THREE DAYS FROM JUDGMENT

The Times investigation published today because journalists spent months tracking down sources, reviewing archives, and documenting patterns. They did this work because it matters—because understanding how Epstein built his network helps us understand the scope of his crimes and the breadth of his enablers.

But there’s work journalists can’t do. They can’t compel DOJ to release classified materials. They can’t force disclosure of FBI investigative memos. They can’t access the intelligence assessments that might prove foreign intelligence operations.

That’s what the Epstein Files Transparency Act was supposed to accomplish. That’s what December 19 is supposed to deliver.

But based on everything we’ve documented—and everything the Times investigation reveals about institutional enabling—what’s likely to arrive?

The Times shows us Epstein’s pattern: test boundaries, get caught, avoid consequences, escalate.

DOJ is following the same pattern: claim transparency, get exposed, deny and delay, sanitize release.

THE CHOICE FACING INSTITUTIONS NOW

Michael Tennenbaum, Epstein’s manager at Bear Stearns, regrets that he didn’t end Epstein’s career when he had the chance.Credit...Elizabeth Bick for The New York Times

When Michael Tennenbaum, Epstein’s supervisor at Bear Stearns, was interviewed by the Times, he said something striking:

“I didn’t realize that I was creating one of the monsters of Wall Street.”

Tennenbaum gave Epstein a second chance in 1976 when he was caught lying about college degrees. That decision—treating him like a “normal employee” despite clear evidence he wasn’t—helped launch everything that followed.

Tennenbaum told the Times he still regrets it more than four decades later.

The question for DOJ—for Pam Bondi, Kash Patel, Jay Clayton, and everyone else involved—is whether they want to be Tennenbaum in forty years.

Do they want to be the people who, when historians examine the cover-up of potential foreign intelligence compromise, gave the powerful figure second chances despite clear evidence?

Do they want to be the institution that, when the full files eventually emerge—through leaks, through the next administration, through FOIA lawsuits—is shown to have sanitized, delayed, and obstructed?

The Times investigation shows that Epstein’s enablers have names. Their decisions are documented. Their choices are recorded. History remembers them—not kindly.

December 19 is three days away. DOJ will release something. The question is whether it will be comprehensive transparency or another chapter in the accommodation psychology.

WHAT THE TIMES INVESTIGATION MEANS FOR DECEMBER 19

The publication timing is not coincidental. This creates several important dynamics:

1. It raises public attention

The Times investigation will be widely read and discussed. Epstein is back in headlines. Public attention turns to the question of what’s in the government files. This makes it harder for DOJ to release a sanitized version and hope no one notices.

2. It provides comparison baseline

The Times documented Epstein’s network through 1999. We know from the investigation that by then he had connections to presidents, senators, billionaires, foreign leaders, and intelligence-adjacent figures. The December 19 release should show what happened in the 25 years after—when, as the Times notes, his sex trafficking operation was “accelerating.” If the DOJ files show less detail than what journalists found through months of archive-diving, that’s suspicious.

3. It demonstrates what transparency looks like

The Times didn’t just report “Epstein built wealth through legitimate means.” They documented specific scams, named specific enablers, showed specific patterns. They let Tennenbaum express regret. They let Stroll call Epstein “a despicable prick.” They published the uncomfortable details. That’s what accountability looks like. DOJ’s release should meet that standard.

4. It exposes the gap between journalism and justice

Journalists spent months investigating decades-old financial crimes that are largely beyond prosecution. DOJ has spent months preparing to release files about recent federal investigations into sex trafficking and potential intelligence operations—crimes very much within statute of limitations. Yet journalists seem more committed to transparency than law enforcement.

This gap is the story. The Times investigation makes it harder to ignore.

THE QUESTION FOR CITIZENS

When December 19 arrives, you’ll have a choice.

You can accept DOJ’s framing: “We’ve released thousands of pages, protected victims, maintained national security, maximum transparency achieved.”

Or you can compare what arrives to what the Times investigation shows should exist:

  • Detailed documentation of Epstein’s network (the Times found it in archives; FBI should have it in investigative files)

  • Specific names of enablers and participants (the Times named dozens; DOJ files should name hundreds)

  • Evidence of financial crimes and manipulation (the Times documented decades of scams; FBI investigated recent operations)

  • Intelligence about foreign actors (the Times couldn’t access classified materials; DOJ can and should declassify)

The Times investigation provides a benchmark. If DOJ’s release falls short of what journalists achieved through archive research and interviews, that tells you something.

CONCLUSION: THE PATTERN MUST END

Across forty-eight years, institutions repeatedly enabled Jeffrey Epstein:

  • 1976: Bear Stearns gave him a job despite no credentials

  • 1980: Bear Stearns ignored expense fraud

  • 1981: Bear Stearns let him resign with credentials intact

  • 1982: Stroll gave him money that vanished

  • 1984: Leese’s enablement ended but damage done

  • 1987: Wexner gave him power of attorney

  • 1999: Virgin Islands gave him tax breaks

  • 2008: Federal prosecutors gave him a sweetheart deal

  • 2025: DOJ preparing to give him—and his network—a sanitized historical record

The Times investigation documents the first two decades of that pattern. Our thirteen-episode series documents the most recent chapter.

December 19 is the test of whether that pattern continues or ends.

The Times has shown us what investigative transparency looks like. They named names, documented patterns, showed institutional failure, and let those involved express regret or defend themselves on the record.

DOJ should meet that standard. Congress should demand it. Citizens should accept nothing less.

The accommodation psychology doesn’t end until institutions decide it ends.

December 19 will show us whether DOJ is another enabler in the long line the Times documented—or whether, finally, an institution chooses accountability over protection.

Based on everything we’ve seen, documented, and analyzed across thirteen episodes, the answer seems clear.

But it doesn’t have to be. The choice is DOJ’s—and if they choose wrong, the choice becomes yours.
