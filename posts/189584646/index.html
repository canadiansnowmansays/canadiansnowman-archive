The Iran Crisis

The Axis Alone

Part XXVI: The Proxy Network That Wasn’t There

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

Large mourning crowds in Tehran after Khamenei’s death (Newsweek, March 1, 2026)—Iranians gathered in squares/mosques, with no visible proxy mobilization, illustrating the “axis alone” reality (source: Newsweek/AP).

For nearly four decades, the Islamic Republic of Iran built its regional strategy on a single foundational insight: it could not fight its enemies directly, so it would make them bleed through others.

The result was the “axis of resistance” — a network of armed groups stretching from Lebanon to Gaza to Yemen to Iraq to Syria, all funded, armed, trained, and to varying degrees directed by Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps. Hezbollah in Lebanon was the crown jewel: a military force more capable than most national armies, equipped with 150,000 rockets aimed at Israel, battle-hardened through decades of conflict, ideologically coherent, and deeply loyal to Khamenei’s vision of the resistance.

The theory of deterrence was explicit. Any Israeli or American attack on Iran would trigger a multi-front response: Hezbollah rockets from Lebanon, Hamas rockets from Gaza, Houthi missiles from Yemen, Iraqi militia strikes on American bases. The cost of attacking Iran would be paid not only in Iranian blood but in the blood of Iran’s enemies across the entire region. That cost, in theory, was prohibitive.

On Saturday morning, the United States and Israel attacked Iran anyway.

And the axis was not there.

What the Proxy Network Looked Like Before

The inventory, as it existed before October 7, 2023, was formidable.

Hezbollah: an estimated 150,000 rockets and missiles, 45,000 fighters, a proven track record in the 2006 Lebanon war and in Syria’s civil war, and financial and military backing that made it the most powerful non-state military actor in the world. Iran’s strategic trump card.

Hamas: the governing authority in Gaza, with a military wing capable of the October 7 attack that killed 1,200 Israelis and took 250 hostages — demonstrating both offensive capability and the willingness to use it regardless of consequences.

The Houthis: a Yemeni rebel movement that had survived years of Saudi-led war, acquired ballistic missile capability, and demonstrated in 2023-2024 that it could disrupt global shipping through the Red Sea with drones and anti-ship missiles.

Iraqi militias: the Popular Mobilization Forces, a constellation of Iran-backed groups embedded in the Iraqi security structure, with reach across Iraq and into Syria, capable of striking American bases with rockets and drones.

Syria under Assad: the land bridge connecting Iran to Hezbollah — the corridor through which weapons, money, and personnel flowed from Tehran to Beirut for decades.

Together, these forces constituted what Iranian strategists called “strategic depth” — the ability to project power and impose costs without exposing the Iranian homeland directly. The axis was Iran’s insurance policy against the day when its enemies decided the benefits of attacking Iran outweighed the costs.

That day arrived on Saturday.

What the Proxy Network Looks Like Now

Alan Eyre, diplomatic fellow at the Middle East Institute and a man who has spent his career studying Iran’s regional strategy: “They’re alone and they know it.”

Five words. The entire analysis.

Hezbollah is not capable of a significant response. The devastating war with Israel that followed October 7 — culminating in the killing of Hassan Nasrallah in September 2024 — broke the organization as an offensive military force. The ceasefire that ended the conflict stipulated that Hezbollah must be disarmed. The Lebanese military completed the first phase of that disarmament last month. Naim Qassem, Nasrallah’s successor, issued a statement on Sunday calling Khamenei’s killing “the height of crime” and promising Hezbollah would “fulfill its duty to confront the aggression.” He did not specify what that meant. Analysts say he cannot: Hezbollah is “not in a position to get involved.” The crown jewel of the axis is a disarmed and depleted organization operating under a ceasefire it cannot afford to violate.

Hamas released two statements. The first condemned the airstrikes on Iran. The second mourned Khamenei. Neither contained a call for action or a pledge to respond militarily. Two years of war with Israel have killed thousands of Hamas commanders and fighters and depleted its arsenal to the point where operational military action is not a realistic option. Hamas is mourning, not mobilizing.

The Houthis called for mass protests. Their leader Abdul Malik al-Houthi said his group was “preparing for all outcomes.” As of Sunday evening, there had been no retaliatory attacks from Yemen. Maersk suspended Red Sea shipping in anticipation of potential Houthi action — meaning the financial markets are pricing Houthi risk even though the Houthis have not yet acted. The threat is doing work the capability may no longer be able to back up.

Iraqi militias — the Popular Mobilization Forces — suffered four killed in a bombing in Diyala Province on Sunday. The message is clear: the US and Israel are not waiting for Iraqi militias to mobilize. They are being struck preemptively. Iranian-backed forces in Iraq are being managed as a threat to be suppressed, not a force to be feared.

Syria is the most striking development of all. Under Assad, Syria was the land bridge — the corridor that made the entire axis function logistically. Without Syria, Hezbollah’s weapons resupply from Iran becomes an air bridge that Israel controls. The rebel government that replaced Assad in late 2024 not only declined to condemn the initial US-Israeli strikes on Iran — it condemned Iran’s retaliatory strikes on Gulf states. Some Syrians celebrated Khamenei’s death in the streets. The land bridge is gone, and the country that hosted it is cheering its former patron’s demise.

How the Axis Was Dismantled

This did not happen overnight. It happened in sequence, across two years, in a series of Israeli military operations that — viewed in retrospect — look less like a series of separate conflicts and more like a systematic campaign to strip Iran’s strategic insurance policy before the final confrontation.

October 7, 2023: Hamas attacks Israel. The response is the Gaza war — which, whatever its humanitarian costs, functionally destroys Hamas as a military organization capable of opening a second front against Israel.

September 2024: Nasrallah killed. Hezbollah’s leadership decapitated. The Lebanon war that follows strips Hezbollah of its offensive rocket arsenal, forces a ceasefire, and initiates disarmament. The crown jewel is neutralized.

Late 2024: Assad falls. Syria transitions. The land bridge closes.

Throughout 2024-2025: Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping are partially suppressed by US and British naval operations. The Houthis retain capability but have demonstrated its limits.

June 2025: US and Israel strike Iranian nuclear facilities in “Operation Rising Lion” — the formal name for the 12-day air attack that this series documented as the first phase of the current campaign. But Rising Lion was not only a nuclear operation. Israeli intelligence had determined that Iran was seeking to increase its ballistic missile stockpile from approximately 3,000 missiles to as many as 8,000 by 2027 — “posing a real, direct and existential threat to the State of Israel and the Middle East,” according to Israeli intelligence reports. Rising Lion targeted missile launchers, the central explosives manufacturing facility supplying warheads and weapons programs, and specialized research sites. It established the operational template for what followed.

February 28-March 1, 2026: The final phase. The US operation is designated “Operation Epic Fury.” Israel’s operation is designated “Roaring Lion” — a successor to June’s “Rising Lion” in name as in operational logic. Iran is attacked directly, with its supreme leader killed, its military leadership decimated, its naval assets struck, its ballistic missile program targeted with stealth bombers and 2,000-pound bombs.

Each step stripped one layer of Iran’s strategic insurance. By the time the decisive operation came, the insurance had expired. The axis that was supposed to make attacking Iran prohibitively costly was a Hezbollah under ceasefire, a Hamas in ruins, a Houthi movement calling for protests, an Iraqi militia getting bombed in Diyala Province, and a Syria celebrating in the streets. And the Arab states that Iran’s missile barrages are now striking — the UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, Saudi Arabia — were not neutral bystanders. According to Atlantic reporting, Saudi Arabia privately urged Trump to order the attack on Iran. The coalition against the axis was broader than the visible one.

The Deterrence Failure

Destroyed buildings at Konarak drone base (Newsweek satellite, March 1, 2026)—wreckage from U.S./Israeli strikes, emphasizing Iran’s isolation as proxies like Hezbollah and Hamas offer no meaningful response (source: Newsweek/Vantor).

This is, analytically, one of the most significant strategic developments of the 21st century — regardless of how the war ends.

Iran built its regional network over 40 years at enormous cost: financial, diplomatic, moral. The Quds Force under Soleimani and then Vahidi spent decades cultivating, funding, arming, and directing these groups. Iran accepted international isolation, sanctions, and pariah status partly because the network was its deterrent — the thing that made attacking Iran too costly to contemplate.

The deterrent failed. Not because the network didn’t exist. Not because Iran didn’t invest in it. But because Israel, across two years of sustained military operations, methodically dismantled it before the decisive moment arrived.

Before examining the proxy network failure, it is worth noting what the Israeli military is explicitly saying about its own objectives — because the language is revealing. The stated goal of the strikes, per an Israeli military official, is to delay Iran’s weapons development “by several years.” Not to destroy it permanently. Not to eliminate the threat. To delay it.

That is attrition language, not victory language. Israel is describing a campaign designed to push Iran’s missile production capacity — which had been accelerating toward 8,000 missiles by 2027 — back behind a horizon. The combined Rising Lion and current strikes have destroyed approximately 200 ballistic missile launchers, rendering roughly half of Iran’s current inventory inoperable. The central explosives manufacturing facility supplying warheads for missiles, drones, and cruise missiles has been struck. Hezbollah anti-tank weapon factories have been specifically targeted.

“By several years” tells you that Israel expects Iran to survive, reconstitute, and eventually rebuild. The military operation is buying time, not ending the threat. That framing matters for every analysis of what comes next.

The strategic lesson for every state that relies on proxy networks as deterrence is uncomfortable: proxies can be degraded. The investment Iran made in Hezbollah over 30 years was neutralized in a matter of months. The crown jewel turned out to be a wasting asset — one that could be depleted faster than it could be replaced.

Eyre’s framework captures this precisely: the proxies “have their own interests, their own agendas and their own timing.” They were never fully under Iranian command. They were aligned with Iran’s interests when those interests coincided with their own. When their own survival became the priority — as it has for all of them now — the alignment fractured. Hezbollah did not sacrifice itself to defend the regime that armed it. Hamas did not mobilize to avenge the leader who funded it. The axis of resistance, under real pressure, revealed itself as an axis of interests. And the interests, in the end, did not align.

What Iran Has Left

Overview of destroyed storage bunkers at Konarak airbase (Newsweek gallery, March 1, 2026)—wide view of scorched facilities, underscoring the proxy network’s absence leaving Iran to absorb bombardment alone (source: Newsweek/Vantor).

Strip away the proxy network and what remains is Iran itself: its own military, its own missile arsenal (being systematically destroyed by stealth bombers), its own naval capability (one warship confirmed sinking, others targeted), its own IRGC (Joint Headquarters confirmed destroyed by CENTCOM).

And its nuclear program — the ultimate deterrent that the proxy network was always, at some level, buying time to complete. The hardened ballistic missile facilities being struck by 2,000-pound bombs from stealth aircraft are directly connected to the delivery systems for a weapon Iran has not yet built. The race between Iran’s nuclear timeline and Israel’s willingness to tolerate it has now reached its terminal phase.

Iran is fighting this war alone, with a degraded military, under a bombardment that has killed its supreme leader and most of its senior command, with an IRGC now commanded by a man wanted by Interpol, with a proxy network that is mourning rather than mobilizing, with a Strait of Hormuz closed and a state broadcaster that was struck on Sunday evening while it was still broadcasting.

“They’re alone and they know it.”

The question that follows — the question Part XXVIII will take up — is what a state in that position does when the other side says “they want to talk.” Does it deal from weakness? Does it fight from desperation? Does it fracture into competing factions making incompatible calculations?

The axis is gone. What happens next is up to the Iranian state itself — the 80% opportunists and the 20% true believers, in their bunkers, under bombardment, calculating.
