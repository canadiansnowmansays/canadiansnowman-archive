Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 14: Chapter 11 — The Business of Taking Cuba

April 8, 2026

Yamil Lage / AFP / Getty

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

In October 2008, Donald Trump filed a trademark application in Cuba. It was approved in March 2010 and remained active until its expiration in 2018. During his first presidency, he held discussions with administration officials and Trump Organization staff about financial opportunities on the island. According to a person who was present at those discussions, Trump had been most excited about the prospect of Trump-branded hotels or condominiums along Cuba’s coastline.

“He’s interested in Cuba as a market for him,” that person told The Atlantic this week, “and completely agnostic about the politics. He didn’t care.”

That sentence, sourced to someone who was in the room, reframes everything about the Cuba crisis of 2026. It does not mean the geopolitical logic — eliminate a hostile government, project American power in the hemisphere, satisfy Marco Rubio’s political debts to the exile community — is absent. It means that for the man at the center of the decisions, the geopolitical logic is secondary to a simpler calculation: Cuba has underdeveloped cities and beaches, and there are, as one administration official put it to The Atlantic, “billions of dollars to be made there.”

John Kavulich, president of the U.S.-Cuba Trade and Economic Council, offered the most precise analytical frame anyone has produced for what the Trump administration actually wants. “The Trump administration is going to put Cuba into Chapter 11,” he said, invoking the bankruptcy code section that companies use for financial reorganization while still operating. “It’s not going to be Chapter 7 liquidation. It’s going to be Chapter 11 — a country reorganization. But the whole focus is business.”

Chapter 11. Not the destruction of Cuba. Its acquisition.

What “Regime Change Is Lined Up” Actually Means

The Atlantic’s reporting, published March 22, 2026, is the most significant piece of journalism about the Cuba crisis yet produced — not because it reveals that American pressure is intense, which has been documented extensively, but because it documents, with named sources and specific detail, the architecture of what is being planned and the sequence in which it is expected to unfold.

The key sentence, attributed to an administration official: “Regime change is lined up.” Awaiting the president’s signal.

Officials told The Atlantic that the U.S. approach to Cuba would likely replicate the course of events in Venezuela — and that several of them described the Caracas operation as a “dry run” for Havana. The word “dry run” is not an analyst’s metaphor. It is an operational term used by people involved in planning. The Venezuela operation — the naval buildup, the indictments, the capture, the installation of a cooperative successor — was a rehearsal. Cuba is the performance.

The South Florida indictment effort, which this series has tracked since it was first reported, is now confirmed as targeting not just Cuban officials generally but specifically members of the Castro family — on charges related to alleged violent crime, drug trafficking, immigration, and espionage. The legal mechanism that justified Maduro’s capture is being prepared for Cuba. U.S. Attorney Jason Reding Quiñones is leading the multi-agency effort.

The timeline depends entirely on Trump’s bandwidth. He is still prosecuting a war in Iran. The USS Gerald R. Ford carrier group moved from Croatia to the Caribbean for the Venezuela raid, then steamed to the Middle East, extending its deployment by months. A Cuban operation would require a similar buildup — which has not yet happened near Cuban shores, the reporting makes clear, “in part because the Navy has been stretched by those other conflicts.”

But preparations on several fronts are, in the words of The Atlantic, “well advanced.”

The Subterfuge Question

The most alarming detail in The Atlantic’s reporting is a single sentence about the negotiations themselves.

The discussions between Washington and Havana — the talks that Díaz-Canel acknowledged publicly on March 13, the back-channel that runs through the Crab and the Cardinal — could turn out to be, one person familiar with them told The Atlantic, “in part a form of subterfuge.” The U.S. could claim that Cuba has refused to budge on some key condition as a predicate for military-backed law enforcement action. “Much as they were with Venezuela,” the source added.

This is not, it should be noted, a confirmed fact. It is one person’s characterization of how the administration might use the talks. But it is a characterization offered by someone described as familiar with the discussions, and it is consistent with the Venezuela template: talks that proceeded in parallel with military and legal preparation, terminated at the moment of Washington’s choosing, justified by a claim that the other side refused to cooperate.

If accurate, it means that the negotiations Díaz-Canel acknowledged last week — the talks that Cuba’s government entered as its only viable path to relief — may simultaneously be the mechanism by which Washington builds a case for abandoning them. Cuba would be negotiating in good faith against an interlocutor that has already determined the outcome and is constructing the legal scaffolding to justify it.

Cuba’s diplomats appear to be at least partially aware of this risk. According to The Atlantic, Cuban intermediaries have been seeking meetings with American think tanks, academics, and journalists in an effort to influence public opinion and buy time. The phrase “buy time” is revealing: time for what, exactly, is unclear, since the oil reserves are nearly gone and the structural pressure shows no sign of easing. Perhaps time for the negotiations to produce something genuine. Perhaps time for the world to pay enough attention that unilateral military action becomes politically costly. Perhaps simply time.

Who Would Run Cuba

Beyond Raúl Guillermo Rodríguez Castro — the Crab — and Oscar Pérez-Oliva Fraga, the Castro grandnephew who gave the NBC interview last week, The Atlantic adds a third name to the list of potential successors Washington could work with: Manuel Marrero Cruz, Cuba’s current prime minister.

Marrero Cruz is a Communist Party loyalist who is nonetheless viewed by some in Washington as a pragmatic technocrat — the kind of official who can manage a system’s transition without dismantling its bones too quickly, which is precisely the profile the Chapter 11 model requires. You need someone who can keep order while the reorganization proceeds, who has enough institutional credibility to maintain the loyalty of the security apparatus, and who is sufficiently pragmatic to sign the agreements that open the economy to American capital.

The Atlantic also reports that the Trump administration is discussing which wealthy Republican donors with Cuban ancestry could be considered for future transition or leadership roles in Havana. The sentence is almost too brazen to parse. American political donors — people who wrote checks to Republican campaigns — are being discussed as candidates for governing roles in a sovereign country. The colonial logic is so naked that it barely requires commentary. But it is worth noting that this is consistent with the Chapter 11 frame: in a corporate reorganization, the creditors often have significant say in who runs the restructured entity.

Marco Rubio, meanwhile, has been focused privately on something specific: dismantling GAESA, the military-run conglomerate that controls between 40 and 70 percent of the Cuban economy. The financial scale of what he is targeting is staggering: government documents leaked to the Miami Herald suggested GAESA holds as much as $18 billion in assets. Its tourism subsidiary earned a 42 percent profit margin in the first quarter of 2024. GAESA pays no taxes, transfers no dividends to the state budget, and is legally exempt from audits. It is, in the most precise sense of the term, a kleptocracy — a ruthlessly extractive state monopoly run by generals and Castro family members that no longer even pretends to act in the public interest. Dismantling it — or opening it to foreign investment — is the structural change that would make the Chapter 11 reorganization economically meaningful rather than cosmetically political.

The analyst Quico Toro has offered the sharpest frame for what happens when this kleptocracy meets the Trump administration: two systems that operate by the same logic, haggling over who extracts the most. “The new reality is tawdry,” Toro wrote in The Atlantic, “but it might put a resolution within reach. Officials who operate like mob bosses don’t need to agree on transcendent values to make a bargain.” What Trump seems to object to in Cuba’s economic system isn’t how corrupt and extractive it is — it’s that he’s not getting a share of the take. The generals keep their cut. The Republican donors get their roles. The president gets his deal, and maybe a hotel. And the Cubans who believed the slogans — from either side — get nothing.

The Tanker

Anatoly Kolodkin Photo © CiberCuba / Sora

As this essay was written on March 22, 2026, a Russian state-owned oil tanker called the Anatoly Kolodkin was somewhere in the Atlantic, closing on Cuba. It departed Primorsk, Russia on March 8, carrying 730,000 barrels of crude oil — roughly a month’s supply for the island after refining, though American military officials estimated closer to two weeks. Its declared destination on its tracking system read, with evident sardonic intent: “Atlantis, USA.”

The Royal Navy dispatched HMS Mersey with a Wildcat helicopter to track it through the English Channel. The U.S. Southern Command confirmed it was tracking the vessel. Two U.S. Coast Guard cutters were positioned off Cuba’s northeast coast. The Treasury Department’s OFAC explicitly added Cuba to the list of countries blocked from receiving Russian oil, closing a loophole that had briefly existed when sanctions were temporarily eased to stabilize energy markets during the Iran war.

A second Russian tanker, the Sea Horse, had been heading toward Cuba before changing its destination to Trinidad and Tobago when the sanctions terms were updated. The Anatoly Kolodkin appeared to do the same — maritime tracking data on March 24 showed it altering course toward the central Caribbean, with the U.S. Navy destroyer USS Nitze moving on an intersecting trajectory near the Bahamas. It appeared the blockade had held. It had not.

The Kolodkin delivered its oil to the port of Matanzas. Trump, asked about it, said: “They have to survive.” The White House afterward clarified this was “not a formal change in sanction policy” and that decisions on future shipments would be made “case by case.” The blockade that had been absolute for three months had, with a presidential shrug and four words, admitted its first exception.

The episode reveals something important about the nature of the pressure being applied. This was never a pure strategic embargo with fixed rules enforced by law. It was a tool of coercion managed by presidential whim — tightened, loosened, and reframed as conditions shifted. A blockade whose architect can wave a tanker through with “they have to survive” is a negotiating instrument, not a siege. Which means it will be lifted, partially or fully, when the negotiation produces what Washington wants — not when Cuba has been brought to its knees, but when the deal is close enough to closing that the pressure can be eased without losing the leverage.

That calculation — the tanker as signal rather than exception — is consistent with everything else this essay has argued about the Chapter 11 model. You don’t liquidate a company you’re trying to reorganize. You keep it barely functional while the terms are worked out. Cuba got just enough oil to keep the lights on for another few weeks. The negotiations continue.

“We Don’t Believe in Diplomacy”

The Atlantic’s most quotable line does not come from a Cuba expert or a geopolitical analyst. It comes from an unnamed person familiar with the administration’s planning, describing the fundamental operating philosophy of the Trump foreign policy apparatus:

“We control our hemisphere, and we have the ability to do this. We want these hostile regimes out of our hemisphere, and we’re going to set up the business community, because we don’t believe in diplomacy.”

We don’t believe in diplomacy. It is a statement of such startling directness that it functions almost as a confession. The negotiations with Cuba are not, on this account, an expression of diplomatic intent. They are a mechanism — for buying time, for constructing legal scaffolding, for managing the optics of what is, at its core, a campaign of economic warfare aimed at creating the conditions for acquisition.

The Monroe Doctrine, which declared the Western Hemisphere off-limits to European powers in 1823, has been variously invoked, ignored, and reinterpreted across two centuries of American foreign policy. What the current administration is constructing is something that goes beyond the Monroe Doctrine’s defensive framing — not merely “keep other powers out” but “we will determine who governs, and on what terms, and for whose benefit.” It is the Doctrine’s logic completed: not just a hemisphere free of rivals, but a hemisphere organized for American commercial advantage.

Cuba has been the sharpest test case of that logic for sixty-seven years. It is about to become the sharpest test case again — not of whether the United States can break a small island’s government, which it probably can, but of what it intends to do with the pieces, and whether the people who live on the island have any say in the arrangement.

History suggests they will not. History has been consistent on this point across five hundred and thirty years.
