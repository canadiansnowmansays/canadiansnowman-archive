The Iran Crisis

“The Statement”

Part XLIII: Day Thirteen: A New Supreme Leader Speaks, a Secretary of War Defines His Doctrine, and the Global Economy Issues Its Own Verdict

March 13, 2026

Mojtaba Khamenei, Iran’s third Supreme Leader, who issued his first defiant statement vowing to keep pressure on the Strait of Hormuz and avenge martyrs—including the children killed at Minab—while remaining out of public view due to reported injuries.Source: WUNC News / Getty Images.

I. The Statement

He has not appeared on video. He has not been photographed since the strikes. He is believed to be injured — a fractured foot, bruising around his left eye, cuts on his face — and in hiding somewhere his enemies cannot find. His inauguration featured a cardboard cutout in his image. His first written statement was issued Thursday morning, thirteen days after the bombs that killed his father, his mother, his wife, one of his sons, and his sister.

Mojtaba Khamenei, the third Supreme Leader of the Islamic Republic of Iran, chose his words carefully.

“Certainly, the lever of closing the Strait of Hormuz must continue to be used.”

“We will not refrain from avenging the blood of your martyrs.”

He named the Minab girls’ school specifically — the Shajarah Tayyebeh elementary school, where an ongoing US military investigation has determined that a Tomahawk missile, launched by the United States, killed at least 175 people, most of them children. The man who lost his own family in the opening hours of this war named the children of Minab in his first statement as supreme leader. He was not speaking abstractly about martyrdom. He was making a list.

He added that Iran had studied the possibility of “opening other fronts in areas where the enemy has little experience and would be highly vulnerable.” He did not specify what he meant. Western officials, according to reporting in this paper’s previous coverage, are concerned about sleeper cells in Europe. He told Iran’s neighbors to close American military bases “as soon as possible, because by now they must have realized that America’s claim of establishing security and peace has been nothing more than a lie.”

Hamidreza Azizi, a visiting fellow at the German Institute for International and Security Affairs who specializes in Iranian security, observed that the statement was even more defiant than his father’s typical utterances. “He gets right to the point,” Azizi said. “It tells a lot, I think, about the direction of the country.” He raised the possibility that the statement was written by others in Mojtaba’s name — that Iran’s system rather than Iran’s new leader was speaking. “What matters is that this is the reflection of the tone of the system. Whoever is making decisions is not going to show any sign of compromise or appeasement.”

Beni Sabti of Israel’s Institute for National Security Studies went further. “I don’t think he is actually running Iran,” he said. “I believe the senior officials who remained from his father’s era are managing the country.” Even Mojtaba’s voice has not been heard. His absence from public view, Sabti argued, suggests he is not yet functioning as leader in any meaningful sense.

Within an hour of the statement’s publication, Brent crude crossed $100 again.

Benjamin Netanyahu held his first press conference since February 28 on Thursday evening. Asked whether Israel intended to target the new supreme leader, he did not pause long. “I wouldn’t sign off on life insurance,” he said, “for him or for the leaders of Iranian-backed militant groups.” He also told reporters he could not say “for certain that the Iranian people would topple” the regime, scaling back the regime-change rhetoric that has animated public statements from both governments since Day One. “A regime is toppled from within,” he said. He and Trump speak “almost every day — exchanging ideas and advice, and making decisions together.” He claimed Israel had just killed additional senior Iranian officials and a top nuclear scientist, without providing names or details.

Two men issuing statements across the rubble. One vowing vengeance for his dead. The other refusing to sign off on the other’s life insurance. The global economy making its own statement, in a language that requires no translation.

II. The Secretary of War

Pete Hegseth, who signed off on “Operation Epic Fury,” framing the conflict as retribution with “death and destruction from the sky all day long.” Source: DVIDS

In the summer of 2005, Pete Hegseth was working on Wall Street when he read an article about an insurgent who blew himself up, killing 18 Iraqi children. “To me, that was the face of evil,” he told The Princeton Alumni Weekly. “That sent me a signal that I need to do my part not to let that ideology win in Iraq.” He deployed to the war-torn city of Samarra a short time later.

He was, by accounts of those who knew him then, genuine. Philip Shishkin, a Wall Street Journal reporter who embedded with Hegseth’s unit in 2006, described him as one of the midlevel officers who “very competent and actually cared despite being thrust into an almost impossible situation not of their making.” He pored over reconstruction contract spreadsheets and visited half-finished sites. He concluded the money was funding the insurgency, redirected it to a local Iraqi leader who used it to build a security force, and spent the night at that Iraqi leader’s home as a gesture of solidarity — his own idea. He came home and campaigned for more troops, for counterinsurgency, for the strategy that prioritized protecting Iraqi citizens over killing insurgents.

That man is not who signed off on “Epic Fury.”

Today, twenty years and a Fox News career later, Pete Hegseth — who prefers, he has said, to be called the “secretary of war” — describes the moral purpose of American military force in Iran as follows: “death and destruction from the sky all day long.” The goal, he has explained, is not liberation. It is not democracy. It is not the protection of civilians. “Their war on Americans has become our retribution.” In place of “Operation Enduring Freedom” or “Operation Unified Protector” — names that connoted, however imperfectly, something being defended — he signed off on “Epic Fury.” Retribution and rage. The name as doctrine.

His diagnosis is not without internal logic. He concluded, watching Iraq, that the lofty moral framing caused the military to lose focus on what he believed was its primary function — killing the enemy — and that this loss of focus produced defeat. “Maximum lethality, not tepid legality,” he has said this year. “Violent effect, not politically correct.” Anything that distracts from killing is weakness. The military’s strength, in his view, lies not in its ideals but in its capacity to punish.

Three people who have thought carefully about war pushed back on this framework with varying degrees of force.

Phil Klay, a novelist and Marine Corps veteran of Iraq, offered the historical pattern. “There’s always someone who thinks that if only we were crueler, if only we’d killed another million Vietnamese, then we would have won this war. If you reduce war to the satisfied feeling you get when you kill the enemy, it makes it a lot simpler and more satisfying.” The simplification, he implied, is the problem — not the solution.

Michael Valdovinos, a former Air Force psychologist and author of the forthcoming book “Moral Injuries,” addressed the institutional consequence. “Moral language acts as a psychological scaffolding for service members. When that disappears, it can leave troops carrying the moral burden alone.” The question Valdovinos is raising is about what happens after — when the pilots and sailors and soldiers who have been told this war is purely about punishment come home and discover that the country they served does not share the moral certainty their commander projected.

Elliot Ackerman, who led Marines in the second battle of Falluja and has spent years writing about what war does to the people who fight it, was most direct. “Some might say at least they’re being honest about the fact that it’s just sheer brute force. But it’s also very dangerous. You’re asking people to die for the ambitions of a president and a moral calculus that’s no greater than might makes right.” And then, from experience: “I can tell you from experience on the back end, it doesn’t feel very good to have participated in a war that everybody thinks was a disaster.”

The administration’s response to these concerns is that the rhetoric “projects strength, resolve and confidence” to enemies and allies in “an increasingly dangerous world.” This is the Pentagon spokeswoman’s formulation, and it may well be true as a tactical matter. The question being raised is whether it remains true as a strategic and human one, and whether the troops being asked to fight a war framed as pure vengeance will be carrying a weight that has no scaffolding when the shooting stops.

III. The Infrastructure War

A commercial oil tanker engulfed in flames in the Persian Gulf after Iranian attacks, emblematic of the infrastructure war that has sunk dozens of vessels and slashed daily Strait transits to 1-2 ships.Source: ABC News / AP imagery.

The military picture on Day Thirteen is no longer a campaign against Iran’s nuclear and military facilities. It is a war against the infrastructure of global energy.

United States Central Command confirmed on Thursday that American forces have struck approximately 6,000 targets in Iran since February 28 — up from the 5,500 figure used in previous reporting. More than 90 vessels have been destroyed: over 60 Iranian ships and more than 30 mine-laying boats. Iran has attacked 16 oil tankers, cargo ships and other commercial vessels in and near the Persian Gulf since the war began — a pace that accelerated sharply, with 10 ships struck in a single day on March 11.

A new and significant development: Iran has begun laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. US officials confirmed the operation Thursday. Iran destroyed its larger mine-laying vessels but has shifted to small boats — the same Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps fast boats that have harassed the US Navy for decades. The calculation is explicit: Iran is betting it can lay mines faster than the United States can clear them, creating a further deterrent to shipping that is independent of its remaining naval capacity. As recently as Tuesday, Iran’s deputy foreign minister denied mining the strait. On Monday, Trump posted that the United States would hit Iran “twenty times harder” if it blocked oil. On Tuesday he posted that any mines should be removed “IMMEDIATELY!” — unaware, apparently, that the operation was already underway.

Before the war, approximately 80 oil and gas tankers passed through the Strait daily, carrying around 20 million barrels of oil. The current figure is 1 to 2 ships per day.

S&P Global Energy calculated on Thursday that the war has reduced oil and fuel supplies from the Persian Gulf by approximately 17 million barrels per day. “No other historical episode comes close,” the firm wrote. If the Strait remains closed for a couple of months, prices would “most likely hit new records.” Brent settled above $100 on Thursday, the highest settlement in nearly four years, having climbed from below $73 when the war began — an increase of nearly 40 percent.

Iraq and Oman closed oil terminals Thursday after two tankers were struck and left burning off Iraq’s coast. Qatar intercepted two ballistic missiles, one cruise missile, and multiple drones. Israel struck Basij militia checkpoints across Tehran with drones, killing several people and generating fear among Iranian residents about traveling through the city. Israeli military chief of staff Zamir said the fight against Hezbollah in Lebanon “will not be short.” Israel struck central Beirut — the Bachoura neighborhood, filled with restaurants and bars, a few blocks from the Lebanese government’s headquarters — spreading the evacuation orders well beyond the southern suburbs that Hezbollah has long controlled. PM Nawaf Salam gave a televised speech while the strikes fell: “I address you today while Beirut is under bombardment. We cannot, under any circumstances, accept that Lebanon once again becomes an open arena for the wars of others.”

Lebanon death toll: more than 680 killed, over 800,000 displaced. Nearly 100 children killed. Roughly one in ten children in Lebanon is among the displaced.

One more development warrants a separate paragraph. The United States Central Command confirmed Thursday the loss of a KC-135 refueling aircraft over western Iraq — a region of isolated desert. The incident involved two aircraft in friendly airspace. One went down. The other landed safely. Investigators suspect a midair collision, though the inquiry is ongoing and details remain murky. Rescue efforts were underway Thursday night. The KC-135 carries at least a three-person crew. Air-to-air refueling mishaps are rare. This one may have been caused by the collision of two American aircraft, in friendly airspace, with no enemy involvement. The war’s accounting does not require an enemy to grow.

IV. The Statement the Global Economy Is Making

Satellite analysis showing the dramatic drop in maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz (January vs. March 2026), with Iran’s mining and attacks creating a near-total blockade and triggering global energy chaos.Source: Barron’s / AFP Sentinel-1 satellite data.

The economic damage from this war is no longer abstract. It is arriving in kitchens, on farms, at gas pumps, and in mortgage applications across every inhabited continent. This is what the closure of the world’s most important oil chokepoint looks like at ground level, and the picture it produces is not a military briefing slide. It is a gas station in Hanoi with a “sold out” sign. It is a family in Western India arriving at a crematory to bury their dead and being turned away because the gas-fired furnaces have been shut down. It is a tea grower in Kenya watching the exports that were supposed to reach Iran rot on a dock. It is a farmer in South Carolina calculating whether he can afford to plant this spring.

David Goldwyn, a former US diplomat and Energy Department official, did not reach for bureaucratic language when asked to characterize the Strait closure. “This really is the big one,” he said. “It is the emergency scenario everyone feared.” Saudi Aramco CEO Amin Nasser warned this week of “catastrophic consequences” for the global economy if the war drags on. Neil Shearing, group chief economist at Capital Economics, identified the structural shift underneath the price movement: Washington is now actively directing “the flow of goods, services and money around the globe.” That matters for the economy in ways that cannot be unwound by a ceasefire.

Mortgage rates in the United States crossed 6 percent this week. The 30-year fixed rate reached 6.11 percent Thursday — the second consecutive week of increases — having slipped below 6 percent for the first time in years in late February, just days before the bombs fell. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.25 percent, up from below 4 percent before the war. Federal Reserve rate cut expectations have faded. The housing market, which had offered the first real signs of relief in years, is frozen again. Hannah Jones of Realtor.com put it precisely: “This definitely feels like we’re back where we were a year ago: tariff uncertainty, economic uncertainty, geopolitical uncertainty.”

Historian Meg Jacobs, author of a study of the 1970s energy crisis, offered the longer view. After the 1973–74 Arab oil embargo, prices did not go back down. They remained elevated for the rest of the decade. The embargo set off a chain of consequences the Arab nations had not foreseen: it drove the United States toward energy conservation, fuel efficiency, and domestic oil development. It ultimately broke the monopoly it was designed to protect, and oil prices collapsed in 1986. Today’s actions, Jacobs observed, may have similarly far-reaching and similarly unintended consequences. She pointed specifically to an emboldened Vladimir Putin — whose economy benefits directly from $100 oil, and who is watching from a position of considerable satisfaction as the United States removes the energy sanctions it had used to pressure him over Ukraine.

Europe, which spent years and enormous political capital extracting itself from Russian energy dependency, now faces a new energy squeeze on top of tariff damage on top of existing industrial weakness. Germany’s chemical, pharmaceutical and auto sectors are energy-hungry. European gas storage is low. Goldwyn warned of likely “panic refilling” over the next six months that will keep prices elevated even if the military situation improves. Asian economies are more exposed still, dependent on imports and subject to currency volatility that makes every dollar-denominated commodity more expensive when their currencies weaken.

Carsten Brzeski of the Dutch bank ING identified the hidden fragility at the center of the US economy: tech companies, especially AI firms, are highly sensitive to interest rate movements. A small number of these companies have been the primary drivers of US growth and stock valuations. “It could lead to the sharp correction in stock markets,” he said. On Thursday it began. The S&P 500 posted its worst single-day performance since the war began, falling 1.52 percent. Brent crude settled at $100.46 — up 10.1 percent on the day, the highest settlement since August 2022. WTI settled at $96.40, up 10.5 percent. Asian markets opened Friday lower: the Nikkei down 1.2 percent, the Kospi in South Korea down 1.7 percent. European stocks followed. The correction Brzeski warned about is no longer hypothetical.

V. The Farmers

Even before the war, American farmers were in trouble. A January survey by Farm Journal found nearly three-quarters of farmers believed the crop sector was in recession. Two-thirds named the cost of farming inputs as their primary obstacle to profitability. For most crop growers, 2026 projects to be their third or fourth consecutive year of losses. Harry Ott, president of the South Carolina Farm Bureau, said that “even before any of this happened, we were already at a break-even or lose proposition on most row crops.”

The war has made this materially worse in a matter of days.

Diesel — which powers the tractors that plant the fields, and the trucks that haul the inputs in and the harvest out — is up nearly a dollar per gallon in the past week alone. Urea, the key nitrogen-based fertilizer, was selling Thursday for $585 per ton at the port of New Orleans, up from $470 before the war. One third of the world’s fertilizer trade passes through the Strait of Hormuz — Qatar, Iran, and Saudi Arabia are among the world’s largest nitrogen fertilizer exporters, and all ship through the Strait. Israel has stopped sending natural gas to Egypt, a major fertilizer producer. India, unable to import sufficient natural gas, may not produce enough fertilizer domestically and will enter world markets to buy it, pushing prices higher everywhere.

“The longer this goes on,” said Samuel Taylor of Rabobank’s farm inputs division, “the more the intermediate supply chains break down and it takes longer to put back together.” The Farm Bureau has written to Trump calling for the Navy to escort ships carrying fertilizer through the Strait. This is where the war finds the people growing the food.

John Newton, chief economist at the American Farm Bureau Federation, identified the timing problem: “Because of the poor farm conditions we were already in, a lot of people didn’t pre-buy.” Farmers who locked in nitrogen prices last fall are protected. Those who waited — hoping prices would drop, unable to absorb another upfront cost in a multi-year losing streak — are now facing a market that has moved sharply against them during the planting window.

The administration that waged this war has called itself the most pro-farmer in decades. It launched a $12 billion bailout, of which roughly $6 billion has been distributed. It has made little difference to farmer sentiment. Hours after every major agricultural organization in the country issued statements calling on Trump to end the trade war, he announced new worldwide tariffs. The Agriculture Department offered Thursday that the president “is utilizing all the tools available to ensure farmers have what they need to continue their farming operations — emphasizing the long-term gains will far outweigh any short-term disruptions.”

Short-term disruptions. Spring planting begins now.

VI. Day Thirteen

Iran’s UN representative told the Security Council that 1,348 civilians have been killed in Iran. Iranian officials say the number is approaching 2,000. The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees estimates 3.2 million people are displaced inside Iran — 600,000 to one million households — most of them moving north and toward rural areas from Tehran and the major cities absorbing the heaviest strikes. Among the most vulnerable, the UNHCR noted, are Afghan refugees who were already living precariously inside Iran before the bombs fell.

Russia sent 13 metric tons of medicine to Iranian civilians by airlift to Azerbaijan, handing the supplies to Iranian officials for transport by land. It was Moscow’s first announced aid shipment since February 28.

More than 120 House Democrats joined Senate colleagues in demanding that Pete Hegseth disclose whether the United States was responsible for the Minab school strike. Senate Majority Leader Thune said: “We don’t target schools.” The investigation that found the United States responsible has been described as preliminary. No public disclosure has been made.

Mojtaba Khamenei’s statement named Minab specifically. He is aware of the investigation’s findings. He has made clear that the families of 175 dead, most of them children, are part of his personal accounting of what this war has cost him and his country. The men who launched the missile that killed them have not yet said so publicly.

Senator Kennedy of Louisiana remains the only Republican to have voted against the war.

The KC-135 crew is still being searched for.

Next: Part XLIV — Day Fourteen. The mines are in the water. The ships are not moving. Spring planting begins across the American heartland without the fertilizer it needs. And the man who cannot be photographed, who lost his father, his mother, his wife, his son, and his sister in the opening hours of this war, has issued his instructions.

They are obeying them.
