The Asimov Gap

The Preparedness Gap

Part 13: Nobody's Driving

February 17, 2026

"On the other hand, I've never been more productive." The CEOs warned us the ice was cracking — then kept stomping on it. (Illustration by Stephan Dybus / The Atlantic)

In 1869, Massachusetts created the nation’s first Bureau of Statistics of Labor. Not because politicians suddenly cared about workers. Because factory machines were tearing children’s arms off, and wealthy citizens were funding private armories, and someone decided that counting might accomplish what conscience could not.

That’s the origin story of American economic measurement. Born from crisis. Built to prevent revolution.

157 years later, the Bureau of Labor Statistics — direct descendant of that Massachusetts experiment — can tell you that 44,119 Americans work in food trucks, that pet grooming employment is up 513 percent since 2000, and that Napa, California has five times the national concentration of massage therapists.

What it cannot tell you is whether artificial intelligence is about to do to the American workforce what the asteroid did to the dinosaurs.

Not because the data doesn’t matter. Because nobody is funding the effort to collect it.

The Silence

Something strange happened in early 2025.

The CEOs started talking.

Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, said AI could drive unemployment up 10 to 20 percent in one to five years and “wipe out half of all entry-level white-collar jobs.”

Jim Farley, CEO of Ford, estimated it would eliminate “literally half of all white-collar workers” in a decade.

Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, revealed that his “little group chat with my tech-CEO friends” has a bet about the inevitable date when a billion-dollar company is staffed by just one person.

Meta. Amazon. UnitedHealth. Walmart. JPMorgan Chase. UPS. All announced layoffs, framing them in sunny investor reports about “automation” and “head count trending down.”

As Josh Tyrangiel wrote in The Atlantic this month: “The owners of capital warning workers that the ice beneath them is about to crack — while continuing to stomp on it.”

And then they stopped talking.

All of them. At roughly the same time. As if someone had sent a memo.

Anyone who has seen a shark fin break the water and then disappear knows this is not reassuring.

The Three Groups

Reid Hoffman — LinkedIn co-founder, Microsoft board member, Silicon Valley’s favorite neutral sounding board — told The Atlantic that AI has sorted CEOs into three groups.

The dabblers. Latecomers finally having their first real conversation with their chief technology officers.

The posturers. Rushing to declare themselves AI leaders for vanity or stock price. “They’re like, Look at me! I’m important!“ Hoffman said. “But they’re not actually doing anything yet.”

The planners. Executives quietly making transformational decisions. Some genuinely trying to help their workforce transition. Most convinced that investors — after years of hearing about AI’s promise — have lost patience with dreaming. This year, they expect results.

And the fastest way for a CEO to produce results is to cut head count.

“A lot of them have convinced themselves this only ends one way,” Hoffman said. “Which I think is a failure of the imagination.”

Gina Raimondo, former Commerce Secretary, called it “a fever.” She attended the Allen & Company conference in Sun Valley last summer — “summer camp for billionaires” — and asked every CEO the same two questions: How are you using AI? And what happens to your workers when you do?

A number admitted they felt trapped. Wall Street expects them to replace human labor with AI. If they don’t, they’ll be out of a job themselves. But if they all order mass eliminations simultaneously, the consequences will be enormous.

Raimondo’s response: “It’s the responsibility of the country’s most powerful CEOs to help figure this out.”

Their response: silence.

The Ghost Ship

Congress let the Trade Adjustment Assistance program expire in 2022. The AI industry spent $100 million making sure nobody grabs the wheel. The branch is democracy's safety net. The chainsaw is bipartisan. (Illustration by Stephan Dybus / The Atlantic)

So to Washington. Where surely someone is preparing for the largest disruption to the American workforce since industrialization.

“At least from my interactions here in the Senate, not many people are talking about it,” said Gary Peters, the senior senator from Michigan. “There’s a general attitude among my colleagues like, We don’t need to do anything. It’s going to be fine. The government should just stay out of it.“

Here is what “staying out of it” looks like.

Since 1974, the Trade Adjustment Assistance program has helped more than 5 million workers displaced by economic shocks with retraining, wage insurance, and relocation grants. Cost: roughly half a billion dollars annually. In 2018, Peters co-sponsored the TAA for Automation Act, extending the same benefits to workers displaced by AI.

It died quietly.

In 2022, authorization for the TAA itself expired.

Peters’s efforts to revive it have gone nowhere.

Meanwhile, the AI industry is spending millions to make sure nobody grabs the wheel. Leading the Future, a super PAC backed by $50 million from Andreessen Horowitz and $50 million from OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman, plans to “aggressively oppose” candidates from either party who threaten the industry’s priorities.

Those priorities boil down to two words: Go faster.

The 119th Congress, as Tyrangiel put it, “is a ghost ship, steered by ennui and the desire to evade hard choices.”

And the person overseeing AI for the executive branch? David Sacks — venture capitalist, podcast host, and holder of at least 449 investments in companies with ties to artificial intelligence. He co-wrote the Trump administration’s “America’s AI Action Plan.”

The fox isn’t just guarding the henhouse. He’s livestreaming the feast.

The Economists in the Rearview Mirror

Meanwhile, the economists are doing what economists do: looking backward.

Austan Goolsbee, president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago, was asked whether any conclusive data shows AI eating into the labor market. His answer: “It’s just too early.”

The “Canaries Paper” from Stanford — showing a 13 percent decline in employment for workers aged 22 to 25 since late 2022 — generated excitement for several days before other economists punched holes in it. Youth employment is cyclical. Interest rates spiked in the same period. The data is ambiguous.

David Autor and Daron Acemoglu at MIT insist that speed is what matters. If AI displaces workers slowly enough, the economy adjusts. Elevator operators and tollbooth attendants faded without catastrophe. But when change comes fast — as it did with the China shock that eliminated 2 million manufacturing jobs in six years — communities are devastated, workers never recover, and the political consequences are severe.

“Many of the workers in those places still haven’t recovered,” Autor said. “And we’re obviously living with the political consequences.”

Acemoglu, who won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2024, argues that tech CEOs are hyping the speed of transformation. Legacy systems, mainframe computers, integration challenges — all of these create friction that slows AI deployment. “History tells us it’s actually going to happen much slower.”

This is the standard economist’s playbook. Find the historical analogy. Trust the rearview mirror.

But what if the road is about to turn?

The Man Who’s Not Looking Backward

Anton Korinek is “super worried.”

A professor at the University of Virginia and faculty director of the Economics of Transformative AI Initiative, Korinek grew up in an Austrian mountain village writing machine code in 0s and 1s. He understands where systems bottleneck, where they jam, and what breaks first when pushed too hard.

When he got his first demo of a large language model in September 2022, it took “about five seconds” before he considered the consequences for the future of work.

His argument is simple: His colleagues aren’t misreading the data. They’re misreading the technology.

“We can’t quite conceptualize having very smart machines,” Korinek told The Atlantic. “Machines have always been dumb, and that’s why we don’t trust them and it’s always taken time to roll them out. But if they’re smarter than us, in many ways they can roll themselves out.”

This is already happening. AI tools are accelerating the integration of other AI tools. Rollout times shrinking by 50 percent. Each successful deployment intensifying pressure for more.

Consider consulting firms. They’ve always charged premium fees for junior associates doing research and drafting reports. If one firm uses AI to deliver the same work faster and cheaper, competitors face a stark choice: adopt or die. Competition doesn’t just reward AI adoption. It makes delay indefensible.

Korinek thinks America will see major job losses — “a very noticeable labor-market effect” — as soon as this year. “And then those economists you’ve been talking to, they’re going to say, ‘I see that in the data!’”

He paused. “Let’s not joke about it, because it’s too serious.”

Mass job loss doesn’t just mean unemployment. It means missed loan payments. Cascading defaults. Shrinking consumer demand. The kind of self-reinforcing downturn that transforms a shock into a crisis, and a crisis into the decline of an empire.

The Horseshoe

Bannon wants partial nationalization. Sanders wants a robot tax. The workers are all on one side of the seesaw. The other side — where governance, measurement, and preparation should sit — is empty. (Illustration by Stephan Dybus / The Atlantic)

Here’s where it gets interesting for anyone who thought AI was a partisan issue.

From the left: Bernie Sanders issued a 95-theses-style report in October proposing a shorter workweek, worker protections, profit sharing, and an unspecified “robot tax on large corporations.” It reads, as The Atlantic noted, “as though Sanders typed it with his fists.”

From the right: Steve Bannon — yes, that Steve Bannon — is calling for the federal government to take a 50 percent stake in AI companies. Partial nationalization. From the man who built his career on deconstructing the administrative state.

“I’m for the deconstruction of the administrative state, but I’m not an anarchist,” Bannon told The Atlantic. “You do have to have a regulatory apparatus. If you don’t have a regulatory apparatus for this, then fucking take the whole thing down, right? Because this is what the thing was built for.”

Bannon and Glenn Beck signed a letter demanding a ban on superintelligent AI development. They were joined by eminent academics and former Obama-administration officials — people who, as Bannon put it, “would rather spit on the floor than say Steve Bannon is with them on anything.”

This is a horseshoe issue. The far right and the far left touching. It happens in American politics when something highly technical alchemizes into something emotional. The gold standard in 1896. The subprime crisis in 2008. Populism.

The threat of pitchforks has occasionally made American capitalism more humane. The eight-hour workday. Weekends. The minimum wage. All emerged from the space between reform and revolution.

But pitchforks don’t come with a governance plan.

The Counting Crisis

The BLS can't measure what's happening, nobody's funding the effort, and the cubicles are already emptying. Visual proof of the vacuum

Which brings us back to where we started. Massachusetts. 1869. The radical idea that democracy’s first duty is to know what is happening to its people.

Erika McEntarfer was commissioner of the Bureau of Labor Statistics until President Trump fired her in August 2025 for producing a jobs report he didn’t like. She’s seen no evidence of political interference in the data.

But “independence is not the only threat facing economic data,” McEntarfer told The Atlantic. “Inadequate funding and staffing are also a danger.”

The BLS’s Current Population Survey — the foundational tool for measuring the labor market — surveys only 60,000 households. That sample hasn’t increased in 20 years. Response rates have declined. The budget has been shrinking for decades.

An obvious first step toward understanding what AI is doing to American workers: expand the sample size and add a supplement on AI usage at work. Cost: a few million dollars. A rounding error in a federal budget.

Nobody has funded it.

We are flying into the most consequential transformation of the workforce since industrialization, and we can’t be bothered to look out the windshield. The economists are driving by looking in the rearview mirror. The CEOs have gone silent. Congress is a ghost ship. The executive branch handed the wheel to a venture capitalist with 449 AI investments. And the one agency designed to tell us what’s actually happening — the BLS — is being simultaneously attacked for political reasons and starved of the resources it needs to do its job.

Nobody’s driving.

A View from the 49th Parallel

I watch all of this from Toronto, which gives me the advantage of distance and the disadvantage of consequence.

Canada isn’t immune to any of this. Our economy is inextricably linked to America’s. When American workers lose jobs, Canadian supply chains fracture. When American institutions lose credibility, the democratic norms we share lose their foundation.

But I’ll tell you what we don’t have up here: a government that fires statistical agency heads for producing inconvenient data, an AI policy written by a venture capitalist with hundreds of conflicting investments, and a Congress that lets workforce adjustment programs expire while spending billions subsidizing the companies doing the displacing.

And the problem isn’t just American.

Macron and Modi raise their hands in triumph at the AI Impact Summit in New Delhi. The summit was called "Safety" in 2023, "Action" in 2025, and "Impact" in 2026. The ambition shrinks with each handshake. (AP Photo)

As this essay publishes, world leaders are gathering in New Delhi for that summit. The summit’s name tells you everything: it was “Safety” in 2023, “Action” in 2025, and now “Impact” in 2026. They’ve given up on governing it and moved on to measuring how fast it hits. India, the host country, has 72 million daily ChatGPT users and an investment bank predicting its call centres will lose half their revenue to AI by 2030. A report released days before the opening ceremony is titled “AI Governance at the Edge of Democratic Backsliding.” Even the country convening the world to discuss AI governance can’t govern AI.

Nick Clegg, the former British deputy prime minister who spent seven years running global affairs at Meta, told The Atlantic that the societies best positioned for AI are either small homogeneous democracies like the Scandinavians — or large authoritarian states like China. “If democratic governments drift into this period,” he warned, “then democracy is not going to pass this test with flying colors.”

That warning should haunt every essay in this series.

That warning should haunt every essay in this series.

We’ve spent twelve parts examining the threats: to jobs, privacy, elections, children, warfare, the very fabric of democratic governance. Now we know the most dangerous threat of all.

It’s not the technology.

It’s the vacuum.

Nobody is preparing. Nobody is measuring. Nobody is driving.

And the road is about to turn.
