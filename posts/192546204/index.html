Still Not 3.5% — But the Curve Doesn’t Lie

No Kings, March 28th: A Follow-Up

March 30, 2026

Two Americas Series — CanadianSnowmanSays

When I wrote The 3.5% Question before Saturday’s No Kings rallies, I framed it around a deceptively simple premise borrowed from Harvard political scientist Erica Chenoweth: no government in modern history has survived a sustained nonviolent movement in which 3.5% of the population actively participated. For the United States, that threshold is roughly 11.6 million people.

Eight million showed up on March 28th.

They are not there yet. But let’s be precise about what “not there yet” actually means — because the trajectory of this movement is telling a story that the raw number alone does not.

The Progression

The No Kings movement now has three data points:

  • June 2025: approximately 5 million participants

  • October 2025: approximately 7 million participants

  • March 28, 2026: approximately 8 million participants, across more than 3,300 events in all 50 states

Each round has been larger than the last. The June-to-October jump was 2 million. The October-to-March jump was 1 million — smaller in absolute terms, but consider what happened in between: winter set in, the Iran war began, the country absorbed the shock of Renée Good and Alex Pretti being shot dead by federal agents in Minnesota. Protest fatigue was a reasonable expectation. Instead, participation grew.

At 8 million in a country of approximately 335 million, Saturday’s turnout represents roughly 2.4% of the U.S. population — meaningfully short of the Chenoweth threshold, but meaningfully larger than anything the United States has seen in a single day of civic action. National organizers called it potentially the single largest nonviolent day of action in American history. Given what we know about the June and Women’s March numbers, that claim is defensible.

What Changed on March 28th

Two things distinguished Saturday from the previous rounds, and both matter.

The war. Exactly one month before the protests, President Trump ordered the first U.S. strikes against Iran, setting off a conflict that has sent gasoline prices soaring and rattled Republicans who thought they had signed up for tariffs and immigration enforcement, not another Middle Eastern war. Dana R. Fisher, a professor at American University who studies civic engagement and surveyed protesters in Washington, found that the share citing war and peace as their primary motivator jumped dramatically compared with previous rounds. It was roughly comparable to the share citing immigration — meaning March 28th drew from two distinct wells of anger simultaneously.

At the University of Iowa, organizer Katy Gates, 22, described a crowd that was “a lot younger, more diverse and more energetic” than previous No Kings demonstrations. She attributed it to the war. “Our generation has grown up with this idea of endless war in the Middle East,” she said. “And the idea of getting into yet another is something that people are rightfully really angry about.”

People march through downtown Dallas in protest against the Trump administration on March 28, 2026. Brandon Bell / Getty

Red-state penetration. Two-thirds of the 3,300+ events were held outside major urban centres. Texas had over 100 events. So did Florida and Ohio. Idaho, Wyoming, and Utah each had protests in the double digits. One of the most distant demonstrations took place in Kotzebue, Alaska. Rural communities participated for the first time — East Glacier Park, Montana; Seward, Alaska; Rocky Ford, Colorado. When the resistance moves beyond the predictable blue-city precincts and begins organizing in counties where Trump won by 26 points, you are no longer looking at a partisan rally. You are looking at something structurally different.

Minnesota: Where the Nation Focused

Bruce Springsteen performs during a “No Kings” protest outside the Minnesota State Capitol building in St. Paul on March 28, 2026 Stephen Maturen / Getty

The flagship rally was held in St. Paul — a deliberate choice. Minnesota became the symbolic heart of this movement after Operation Metro Surge, the Trump administration’s mass immigration enforcement operation that left two American citizens dead. The administration ultimately withdrew the surge after mounting public criticism, but the wound did not close.

At the State Capitol lawn, the crowd heard from Governor Tim Walz, Senator Bernie Sanders, Jane Fonda, and Joan Baez. Bruce Springsteen performed a song about Renée Good and Alex Pretti. “This is still America,” he told the crowd. “And this reactionary nightmare and these invasions of American cities will not stand.”

Organizers claimed 200,000 in attendance. The Minnesota State Patrol put the figure at 100,000. The gap between organizer estimates and official counts is a recurring feature of protest coverage — what’s notable is that even the conservative official number for a single city rally is historically significant.

The Monarchical Fuel

Credit: Canva / Arman Aisultan / Qazinform

Two days before the protests, the Treasury Department announced it would begin printing U.S. dollars bearing President Trump’s signature — a first for a sitting president. The move landed with the symbolic precision of a gift to the No Kings organizers. When your opposition movement is literally named after the concept of democratic non-monarchy, and the administration responds by putting the president’s name on the currency, the narrative writes itself.

Senator Kirsten Gillibrand of New York announced on Saturday that she plans to introduce legislation barring presidents from putting their name, likeness, or signature on federal property or money. “In America,” she said, “we do not bow to kings.”

The White House, for its part, dismissed the entire day as the product of “leftist funding networks” with little real public support. Spokesperson Abigail Jackson called the protests “Trump Derangement Therapy Sessions.” Eight million people declined to take the note.

The Midterm Dimension

Something important happened alongside the protest counts: the 2026 midterm elections arrived on the same street corners.

In Maine, Democratic gubernatorial incumbent Janet Mills attended three No Kings protests in and around Portland — she’s running for Senate against Republican Susan Collins, one of her party’s most vulnerable incumbents. Her primary challenger, Graham Platner, an oyster farmer positioning himself as a political outsider, spoke at a rally in Aroostook County, where Trump won by 26 points in 2024. Both Democrats appear to have concluded that showing up matters.

In Michigan, Senate primary candidates Mallory McMorrow and Haley Stevens attended rallies in the Detroit suburbs and downtown respectively. In Massachusetts, both Ed Markey and his challenger Seth Moulton were out. Pete Buttigieg attended a rally in Traverse City, Michigan. Cory Booker joined one in St. Louis.

Then there was Emily Gregory in Florida — a Democrat who won a state legislative seat this past week in a district Trump carried by 11 points in 2024. She was greeted at the Palm Beach County rally near Mar-a-Lago like a returning hero. “There were people asking if that was really her,” said one organizer. The crowd played Springsteen, Bob Marley, and Tracy Chapman.

A counterprotest unfurled a large Trump flag nearby. The two sides chanted at each other. Democracy, performing itself.

The Arithmetic of Change

Let me return to where I began.

3.5% of the United States is approximately 11.6 million people. Saturday’s rallies, at roughly 8 million, represent 69% of that threshold. Three rounds of protests — June, October, March — have drawn 5 million, 7 million, and 8 million respectively.

The Chenoweth framework is not a guarantee. It is a historical observation: movements that reach 3.5% active participation have, in the cases studied, succeeded in forcing significant political change. It says nothing about the quality of that change, the timeline, or the specific mechanism. What it does say is that sustained, growing, nonviolent civic participation at sufficient scale has historically been impossible for governments to simply absorb and ignore.

The No Kings movement is not yet at 3.5%. But it is growing in the right direction, reaching into geography previously considered safe for the administration, drawing new demographics energized by a war no one asked for, and increasingly intersecting with electoral politics in ways that suggest the streets and the ballot box are beginning to speak the same language.

From Toronto, I keep watching the numbers. Not out of detachment — the drift toward authoritarianism in the United States matters enormously to every country that shares a border, a language, an economy, and a democratic inheritance with it. I watch because the numbers are the most honest thing available. Crowd size estimates are contested. Media coverage is filtered. Political spin is omnipresent.

But 5 million, then 7 million, then 8 million — that sequence does not lie.

The 3.5% question remains open. The answer, it seems, is still being written in the streets.
