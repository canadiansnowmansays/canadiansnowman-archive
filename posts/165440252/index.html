The American Contradiction

How Anti-"Woke" Politics Betrays the Nation's Foundational Promise

Week 7: Contemporary LGBTQ+ Backlash - History Repeating

July 16, 2025

The Acceleration of Progress and Backlash

The contemporary backlash against LGBTQ+ rights, particularly targeting transgender individuals, represents one of the most striking examples of the cyclical pattern of American exclusion and inclusion. What makes this case particularly revealing is the speed with which attitudes toward gay and lesbian Americans changed, followed by intensified resistance to transgender inclusion.

From a Canadian perspective, where LGBTQ+ rights are constitutionally protected under the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and generally less politically controversial, the American fixation on excluding LGBTQ+ individuals appears to be creating precisely the social division that opponents claim to prevent.

The Recent Success Story: Marriage Equality

The rapidity of change regarding same-sex marriage demonstrates how quickly American attitudes can shift once inclusion occurs, making current resistance to transgender rights appear historically inevitable to overcome.

The Transformation Timeline

  • 2004: Same-sex marriage was illegal in all fifty states and opposed by most Americans

  • 2008: California's Proposition 8 banned same-sex marriage with 52% support

  • 2012: Obama became the first sitting president to support marriage equality

  • 2015: Obergefell v. Hodges established constitutional right to same-sex marriage

  • 2023: Over 70% of Americans support marriage equality

This transformation occurred in less than two decades—remarkable speed for such a fundamental social change.

Outside the Supreme Court on the morning of June 26, 2015, James Obergefell (foreground, center) and attorney Al Gerhardstein (foreground, left) react to its historic decision.

The Arguments That Failed

Opponents of marriage equality made identical arguments to those now used against transgender rights:

  • Traditional marriage was essential to social stability

  • Children would be harmed by non-traditional family structures

  • Religious freedom required the right to discriminate

  • Courts were imposing values on unwilling populations

  • Society was changing too rapidly and should slow down

Each of these arguments was proven wrong by subsequent experience. States with marriage equality did not experience social breakdown. Children of same-sex couples performed as well as those of different-sex couples. Religious freedom continued to be protected. Public opinion shifted to support rather than oppose equality.

The Business Case for Inclusion

Corporate America played a crucial role in marriage equality by recognizing the business benefits of inclusive policies. Companies found that:

  • LGBTQ+ inclusive policies attracted better talent

  • Diverse workforces were more innovative and productive

  • LGBTQ+ consumers responded positively to inclusive companies

  • International business required inclusive policies in global markets

This business support for LGBTQ+ rights represented practical recognition that inclusion strengthened rather than weakened organizational effectiveness.

The Current Target: Transgender Americans

The success of marriage equality has shifted opposition focus to transgender Americans, who now face the same arguments and exclusionary policies that were previously applied to gay and lesbian Americans.

The Scale of Current Attacks

Since 2021, state legislatures have introduced over 500 bills targeting transgender Americans, particularly transgender youth. These bills seek to:

  • Ban gender-affirming medical care for transgender minors

  • Prohibit transgender students from participating in school sports

  • Restrict transgender individuals' use of public restrooms

  • Limit discussion of gender identity in schools

  • Allow medical professionals to refuse treatment to transgender patients

  • Permit adoption agencies to discriminate against LGBTQ+ families

The "Protecting Children" Argument

The justification for anti-transgender legislation centers on "protecting children"—the same argument used throughout American history to justify excluding marginalized groups. This argument was used to:

  • Justify segregated schools (protecting white children from racial mixing)

  • Restrict immigration (protecting American children from foreign influences)

  • Limit women's rights (protecting children from maternal neglect)

  • Maintain religious discrimination (protecting children from false beliefs)

The "protecting children" framework allows discriminatory policies to be presented as caring and protective rather than exclusionary and harmful.

Medical Care Restrictions

Anti-transgender legislation often focuses on restricting medical care for transgender youth, despite opposition from major medical associations. The American Academy of Pediatrics, American Medical Association, American Psychological Association, and other medical organizations support gender-affirming care as medically necessary and effective.

The legislative overruling of medical expertise represents an unprecedented intrusion of politics into healthcare decisions. The same politicians who oppose government healthcare regulation are mandating specific medical treatments based on political rather than medical considerations.

Sports Participation Bans

Transgender sports bans represent a disproportionate response to a minimal issue. Despite intense political attention, transgender student athletes represent a tiny percentage of student athletes overall. Many states with transgender sports bans have few or no known transgender student athletes.

The focus on sports reveals how symbolic politics can override practical considerations. The energy devoted to restricting transgender sports participation far exceeds any actual problem requiring solution.

The Merit vs. Diversity False Dichotomy

One of the most persistent and pernicious arguments against DEI programs claims that diversity initiatives force employers to hire less qualified candidates based on race or gender rather than merit. This argument represents perhaps the most sophisticated form of contemporary exclusionary politics because it appears to support fairness while actually perpetuating discrimination.

The "Merit" Myth

The anti-DEI argument assumes that the previous hiring system was purely merit-based and that DEI programs corrupted this merit-based approach. This assumption is fundamentally flawed for several reasons:

Networking and Nepotism: Much traditional hiring occurred through personal networks, family connections, and informal referrals that systematically excluded minorities and women. The "old boys' network" was never merit-based—it was relationship-based and inherently discriminatory.

Educational Access Barriers: Historical exclusion from elite universities and professional schools meant that qualified minorities and women were systematically prevented from obtaining credentials that would demonstrate their merit. How can merit be fairly assessed when access to merit-building opportunities is restricted?

Implicit Bias Documentation: Extensive research demonstrates that identical resumes receive different responses based on applicant names. "Emily" and "Greg" receive 50% more callbacks than "Lakisha" and "Jamal" for identical qualifications. This proves the hiring system was never purely merit-based.

Legacy and Geographic Preferences: Elite institutions maintained legacy admissions (favoring less qualified children of alumni) and geographic preferences (favoring less qualified rural applicants) without criticism, suggesting "merit" concerns only arise when minorities and women benefit.

How DEI Actually Works

Critics of DEI programs fundamentally misunderstand how most diversity initiatives actually operate:

Expanding Candidate Pools: Most DEI programs focus on recruiting from historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs), women's organizations, and minority professional associations to ensure qualified diverse candidates know about opportunities.

Removing Structural Barriers: DEI initiatives identify and eliminate unnecessary requirements that disproportionately exclude qualified minorities and women, such as requirements for unpaid internships that exclude those who cannot afford to work without pay.

Bias Training and Awareness: Programs train hiring managers to recognize and counteract implicit biases that lead to unfair evaluation of qualified diverse candidates.

Holistic Evaluation: DEI programs often advocate for considering a broader range of qualifications and experiences rather than relying solely on traditional metrics that may not predict actual job performance.

The Historical Pattern of "Merit" Arguments

The merit-versus-diversity argument has been used throughout American history to justify exclusion:

Irish Americans: Critics claimed Irish immigrants were inherently less capable and would lower workplace standards. "No Irish Need Apply" was justified as maintaining quality rather than as discrimination.

Jewish Americans: University quotas were justified as maintaining academic standards and preventing the "domination" of Jewish students who were actually outperforming non-Jewish students academically.

Women: Exclusion from professional schools and workplaces was justified by claims that women were intellectually inferior or that their presence would distract male colleagues and reduce productivity.

Asian Americans: Current legal challenges to university admissions claim Asian Americans are harmed by diversity programs, using the same arguments previously used to exclude Asian Americans entirely.

In each case, "merit" arguments were used to justify exclusion of groups that often outperformed the majority once given equal opportunities.

The Canadian Contrast: Employment Equity

Canada's experience with employment equity provides useful comparison to American DEI debates. Canadian employment equity legislation requires federally regulated employers to achieve representation of designated groups (women, Indigenous peoples, persons with disabilities, and visible minorities) in their workforce.

Critics made identical arguments to those now heard in America:

  • Merit would be compromised by diversity requirements

  • Qualified majority candidates would be unfairly excluded

  • Workplace efficiency would decline due to "forced" diversity

The results contradicted these predictions:

  • Workplace productivity increased as diverse perspectives improved problem-solving

  • Innovation improved as teams included broader ranges of experience and viewpoint

  • Employee satisfaction increased as workplaces became more inclusive

  • Canadian businesses became more competitive in global markets

The Research Evidence

Extensive research contradicts claims that diversity programs compromise merit or effectiveness:

McKinsey Studies: Companies with diverse executive teams consistently outperform less diverse companies in profitability and innovation.

Boston Consulting Group Research: Diverse teams solve problems faster and more creatively than homogeneous teams.

Academic Performance: Universities with diverse student bodies produce better educational outcomes for all students, including improved critical thinking and reduced prejudice.

Military Effectiveness: Integrated military units consistently outperform segregated units in combat effectiveness and unit cohesion.

The Real Impact on Black Americans

As noted, Black Americans will disproportionately bear the costs of DEI elimination. Historical and contemporary evidence demonstrates that when diversity programs are eliminated:

University Admissions: Black and Hispanic enrollment drops dramatically when affirmative action is banned, while white enrollment increases.

Corporate Hiring: Companies that eliminate diversity programs typically see reduced hiring of underrepresented minorities.

Leadership Representation: Black representation in management and executive positions declines when diversity initiatives are ended.

Economic Mobility: Reduced access to education and employment opportunities limits Black economic advancement and perpetuates wealth gaps.

The argument that DEI programs harm merit is particularly harmful to Black Americans because it suggests their achievements are undeserved rather than recognizing the additional barriers they overcame to achieve success.

The Broader LGBTQ+ Rollback

Transgender targeting represents part of a broader rollback of LGBTQ+ rights that extends beyond transgender issues to challenge previously settled questions about LGBTQ+ inclusion.

Marriage Equality Under Threat

Justice Clarence Thomas's concurring opinion in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization explicitly called for reconsidering Obergefell v. Hodges and other privacy-based rights decisions. This suggests that marriage equality, despite its current popularity, may face renewed legal challenges.

Religious Exemption Expansion

Religious exemption claims are being expanded to allow discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals in employment, housing, adoption, and public accommodations. The argument that religious freedom requires the right to discriminate contradicts the principle that civil rights protections apply equally to all citizens.

Educational Restrictions

"Don't Say Gay" laws in multiple states prohibit discussion of LGBTQ+ topics in schools, effectively erasing LGBTQ+ people from educational curricula. These laws create hostile environments for LGBTQ+ students and families while limiting educational content based on political considerations.

Healthcare Discrimination

Healthcare discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals is being legally protected through religious exemption laws. Healthcare providers are gaining the right to refuse treatment to LGBTQ+ patients, contradicting medical ethics and public health principles.

The Economic Impact of LGBTQ+ Exclusion

LGBTQ+ exclusionary policies impose significant economic costs that contradict claims about protecting American interests.

Corporate Responses

Major corporations have opposed anti-LGBTQ+ legislation because:

  • It creates hostile environments that reduce talent attraction and retention

  • It complicates business operations in states with discriminatory laws

  • It damages corporate reputations in increasingly inclusive markets

  • It creates legal compliance issues for companies with inclusive policies

Tourism and Convention Losses

States with anti-LGBTQ+ legislation have lost tourism revenue and convention business. North Carolina's HB2 transgender bathroom ban cost the state over $3.76 billion in lost business before its partial repeal.

Brain Drain Effects

LGBTQ+ professionals and allies may avoid states with discriminatory laws, creating brain drain effects that reduce economic competitiveness. Technology companies, universities, and other knowledge-based industries depend on diverse talent pools that discriminatory policies discourage.

The Generational Divide

LGBTQ+ attitudes show sharp generational differences that suggest current exclusionary policies are fighting against demographic inevitability.

Younger Americans Support Inclusion

  • 84% of Gen Z Americans support LGBTQ+ rights

  • 77% of Millennials support LGBTQ+ equality

  • Support decreases with age but remains majority even among older Americans

  • Younger Americans are more likely to identify as LGBTQ+ themselves

Political Implications

The generational divide suggests that anti-LGBTQ+ politics may provide short-term political benefits while creating long-term political problems. As younger Americans become a larger share of the electorate, LGBTQ+ inclusive policies will likely become politically necessary rather than optional.

Educational Impact

Younger Americans have grown up with greater LGBTQ+ visibility and inclusion in schools and media. Anti-LGBTQ+ policies conflict with their lived experience of LGBTQ+ people as friends, family members, and classmates.

The Historical Pattern: Inevitable Progress

The historical pattern of American inclusion suggests that current anti-LGBTQ+ policies will eventually be recognized as discriminatory and counterproductive, but at considerable cost to individual lives and American competitiveness.

Previous Reversals

Every previous American experiment with systematic exclusion was eventually reversed:

  • Racial segregation was ended through civil rights legislation

  • Gender discrimination was reduced through feminist movements

  • Religious discrimination was largely eliminated through constitutional protections

  • Immigration restrictions were relaxed through civil rights reforms

The Costs of Delay

Each delay in recognizing inclusion as strengthening rather than weakening American society imposed costs:

  • Individual lives damaged by discriminatory policies

  • Economic opportunities lost through talent exclusion

  • International credibility harmed by contradictory values

  • Social division increased through political conflict

The Inevitable Resolution

The historical pattern suggests that LGBTQ+ inclusion will eventually be recognized as strengthening American society, but the question is how much damage will be done before this recognition occurs.

The current LGBTQ+ backlash, particularly the focus on transgender Americans, represents the latest iteration of America's recurring pattern of exclusion and inclusion. The arguments are identical, the fears are the same, and the predicted disasters mirror those forecast for every previous group that eventually became accepted as integral to American society. The rapid transformation on marriage equality demonstrates how quickly American attitudes can change once inclusion occurs, suggesting that current transgender exclusion will eventually be recognized as discriminatory and counterproductive.

From a Canadian perspective, where LGBTQ+ rights are constitutionally protected and generally celebrated as strengthening Canadian society, American resistance appears self-defeating and historically doomed to failure.

The question is not whether America will eventually embrace LGBTQ+ inclusion—the historical pattern suggests this is inevitable. The question is how much damage will be done to individual lives, American competitiveness, and democratic credibility before America learns from its own successful experience with inclusion.
