Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 9: America Invades: From 9/11 To Withdrawal (2001-2021)

April 15, 2026

Osama bin Laden

Opening: Twenty Years to Learn Nothing

On September 11, 2001, nineteen men hijacked four planes and killed nearly 3,000 Americans.

Fifteen were Saudi. Two were Emiratis

One Egyptian. One Lebanese.

Zero were Afghan.

The attacks were planned in Hamburg and Florida, financed from Gulf States, organized by al-Qaeda leadership in Afghanistan.

Afghanistan - which had nothing to do with planning or executing 9/11 - was about to experience its fourth major invasion in 200 years.

And America - with access to complete historical records of Persian, Macedonian, Mongol, British, and Soviet failures - would spend twenty years and over $2 trillion to repeat every single mistake.

That elderly woman in Badgor, that three-month-old Zakia dying from malnutrition, that closed health clinic in Nalej - they exist because America invaded Afghanistan, stayed for twenty years, created economic dependency, and then cut aid.

This is the story of how American exceptionalism proved no match for 2,500 years of historical precedent.

The Taliban and al-Qaeda (1996-2001)

To understand the American disaster, we need to know how al-Qaeda ended up in Afghanistan.

Osama bin Laden’s Return (1996)

Osama bin Laden, a Saudi who’d fought against the Soviets in the 1980s, returned to Afghanistan in 1996 after being expelled from Sudan.

The Taliban provided sanctuary. He was their guest. Pashtunwali - the ancient code requiring protection of guests - meant more than international pressure.

From Afghan bases, al-Qaeda:

  • Trained terrorists from around the world

  • Planned attacks on American targets

  • Built infrastructure for global jihad

  • Drew on the narrative that Afghanistan had defeated a superpower

The Clinton administration launched cruise missiles at al-Qaeda camps in 1998 (after embassy bombings in Africa). Bin Laden survived.

The Taliban refused to hand him over.

9/11 and the Ultimatum

After 9/11, President Bush demanded the Taliban:

  • Hand over Osama bin Laden

  • Close al-Qaeda training camps

  • Give U.S. full access to terrorist facilities

The Taliban offered to try bin Laden in Islamic court or hand him to a third country, but not to the United States.

Bush rejected the offers. On October 7, 2001, American bombs began falling.

Operation Enduring Freedom: The Initial Success (2001)

The U.S. invasion strategy was initially clever:

  • Massive air power destroyed Taliban positions

  • Special Forces coordinated with anti-Taliban Northern Alliance

  • CIA brought suitcases of cash to bribe warlords

  • No large-scale U.S. ground invasion initially

U.S. Special Forces and Northern Alliance fighters advancing on horseback in late 2001. The rapid collapse of the Taliban seemed like a stunning success with only 12 U.S. combat deaths in the initial phase

The Taliban government collapsed in weeks. By mid-November, the Northern Alliance entered Kabul. By early December, Taliban had fled to Pakistan or melted into countryside.

Total U.S. combat deaths during initial invasion: 12.

It seemed like stunning success. Afghanistan “liberated” with minimal American casualties.

Then came the occupation. And mission creep. And everything that 2,500 years of history said was impossible.

Mission Creep: From Counterterrorism to Nation-Building

The initial mission was clear: Destroy al-Qaeda, topple Taliban, prevent Afghanistan from being terrorist sanctuary.

But once Taliban fell, new questions emerged: What next?

The Bonn Conference (December 2001)

In December 2001, international diplomats met in Bonn to decide Afghanistan’s future. They selected Hamid Karzai to head an interim government.

Karzai was:

  • Pashtun (dominant ethnic group)

  • Pro-Western, English-speaking

  • Acceptable to various factions (seemingly)

But the Bonn Conference committed what UN envoy Lakhdar Brahimi later called the “original sin”: Complete exclusion of the Taliban.

This meant many Pashtuns were excluded from the new government. Moderate Taliban had no path to reintegration.

Seeds of renewed insurgency were planted immediately.

The Expansion of Mission (2002-2009)

The U.S. mission expanded dramatically:

From: Counterterrorism (destroy al-Qaeda) To: Counterinsurgency (defeat Taliban) + Nation-building (create democratic state)

This meant:

  • Building national Afghan army from scratch

  • Creating democratic institutions

  • Promoting women’s rights and education

  • Infrastructure development

  • Judicial reform

  • Anti-corruption efforts

  • Economic development

In other words: Everything the British failed at. Everything the Soviets failed at. America would now attempt.

The Occupation: The Fundamental Problems (2001-2021)

The American occupation faced the same problems every invader faced, plus new ones:

1. Geography (Unchanged for 2,500 Years)

  • Mountains negate technological advantages

  • Valleys remain isolated

  • Taliban could retreat to Pakistan and return

2. Tribal Structure (Unchanged)

  • Loyalty to family, tribe, valley - not Kabul government

  • Centralized government never controlled countryside

  • Local warlords more important than distant politicians

3. Legitimacy Crisis

  • Karzai seen as American puppet

  • Government riddled with corruption

  • Rural Afghans saw Americans as occupiers, not liberators

4. The Manipulation Problem

British Army Captain Mike Martin (quoted in BBC’s ‘Bitter Lake’) described how local conflicts were presented to Americans:

“They understood how we saw the conflict - they presented their local group conflict as Taliban. So they came to us and said ‘Those people over there are Taliban.’ And we went off and dealt with them. But actually, we were dealing with their previous enemies, so we were just creating more enemies for ourselves.”

Translation: Local warlords used American firepower to settle scores. Americans bombed their rivals. Survivors became actual Taliban.

5. The Opium Economy

U.S. Marine moving through an Afghan poppy field. Opium production financed the Taliban, warlords, and corruption, while eradication efforts often alienated local farmers.

Afghanistan produces ~90% of world’s opium. The trade finances:

  • Taliban operations

  • Local warlords

  • Corrupt officials

  • Pakistani intelligence

Martin again: “The currency of power in Helmand is opium. That’s largely what the conflict’s about.”

American eradication efforts alienated farmers while enriching those controlling remaining trade.

6. The Pakistan Sanctuary

Taliban leadership sheltered in Pakistan. Pakistani ISI supported Taliban as hedge against Indian influence.

America couldn’t invade Pakistan (nuclear-armed nominal ally). So Taliban had sanctuary - could regroup, rearm, return at will.

The Afghanistan Papers: The Lies Revealed (2019)

In 2019, The Washington Post published “The Afghanistan Papers” - thousands of pages revealing senior officials knew the war was unwinnable but kept lying publicly.

Key quotes:

“We were devoid of a fundamental understanding of Afghanistan - we didn’t know what we were doing.”

“We didn’t have the foggiest notion of what we were undertaking.”

“If the American people knew the magnitude of this dysfunction...”

For eighteen years, officials privately admitted failure while publicly claiming progress.

The British did the same thing for 80 years. The Soviets did it for 10 years. Now America.

The pattern holds.

The Surge and Drawdown (2009-2014)

Obama’s Surge (2009)

President Obama inherited the war and decided to “surge” - temporarily increase troops to ~100,000 (from ~30,000) to:

  • Secure population centers

  • Degrade Taliban

  • Train Afghan forces

  • Create conditions for withdrawal

The surge lasted 2009-2012. It reduced Taliban control in some areas, at high cost:

  • Over 1,000 U.S. soldiers killed

  • Massive spending increase

  • Temporary gains that evaporated when troops withdrew

The Endless War

By 2014, it was clear:

  • Afghan army couldn’t defeat Taliban alone

  • Government remained corrupt

  • Taliban controlled significant territory

  • No military victory possible

  • But no political settlement achievable either

So the war continued. Year after year. With no clear purpose except avoiding the embarrassment of withdrawal.

Trump’s Deal with the Taliban (February 2020)

In February 2020, the Trump administration signed an agreement with the Taliban:

  • U.S. would withdraw all troops by May 2021

  • Taliban would prevent terrorist attacks from Afghanistan

  • Taliban would negotiate with Afghan government

The agreement:

  • Excluded the Afghan government

  • Gave Taliban legitimacy

  • Set timeline for withdrawal regardless of conditions

The Withdrawal and Collapse (August 2021)

Taliban fighters inside the presidential palace in Kabul on August 15, 2021, after the Afghan government collapsed without significant resistance.

President Biden delayed withdrawal to August 2021 but stuck to the plan.

The Collapse

Desperate Afghans swarm a U.S. Air Force plane at Kabul airport during the chaotic August 2021 evacuation, creating images that echoed the retreats of previous empires.

As U.S. troops withdrew, Taliban swept through Afghanistan with shocking speed.

The Afghan army - trained for 20 years at cost of ~$80 billion - collapsed in days. Some units fought. Most melted away. Many were never paid.

President Ghani fled with cash.

On August 15, 2021, Taliban entered Kabul without firing a shot.

The Images That Haunted

The withdrawal echoed 1842 and 1989:

  • Desperate Afghans clinging to departing aircraft

  • Some falling to their deaths

  • Chaos at Kabul airport

  • Taliban fighters posing in presidential palace

  • The last U.S. military flight leaving

What America Achieved

After twenty years:

  • Over $2 trillion spent

  • 2,400+ U.S. soldiers dead

  • Over 100,000 Afghan soldiers, police, civilians dead

  • Taliban back in power

  • Ruling the same way as 2001

The only significant change: The generation who grew up during American occupation - educated, exposed to relative freedom, different expectations - now faced Taliban rule.

But the fundamental outcome: Taliban won. America left. Afghanistan worse than before.

The Pattern Repeated: Every Mistake

America repeated every mistake previous empires made:

1. Underestimating Resistance ✓ Assumed Taliban were defeated in 2001. They regrouped and returned.

2. Overestimating Technology ✓ Drones, precision weapons, body armor - none overcame geography or lack of legitimacy.

3. Installing Unpopular Leaders ✓ Karzai seen as puppet. Government seen as corrupt.

4. Confusing City Control with Country Control ✓ Controlled Kabul, major cities. Never controlled countryside.

5. Creating Dependency ✓ Afghan economy, military, government dependent on American support.

6. Lying About Progress ✓ The Afghanistan Papers proved 18 years of lies.

7. Eventually Withdrawing ✓ And when America left, Afghans were still there. Taliban were still there. Mountains were still there.

Why America Failed

1. Mission Impossible

Nation-building in Afghanistan was always impossible:

  • No history of centralized state

  • Tribal structure resistant to central authority

  • Geographic isolation preventing unity

  • No industrial economy to sustain state apparatus

The golden age (1919-1978) succeeded because it didn’t try to change fundamental structures. America tried to remake Afghanistan. It couldn’t be done.

2. The Legitimacy Problem

No government seen as American client could have legitimacy. This is the lesson every empire learned. America learned it too, after 20 years.

3. The Pakistan Problem

Taliban sanctuary in Pakistan meant America fought an enemy that could always retreat, regroup, and return. Without ability to strike Pakistan directly, war was unwinnable.

4. The Corruption Problem

American aid flooded into corrupt system. Warlords and officials enriched themselves. Afghan people saw little benefit. This undermined any legitimacy the government might have had.

5. The Time Problem

Americans measured progress in months and years. Afghans measured endurance in generations. Taliban had one advantage America couldn’t match: They lived there. They could wait.

6. The Knowledge Problem

Captain Martin’s observation is devastating: Americans didn’t understand they were being manipulated. They saw the conflict through their own framework (good vs. evil, government vs. Taliban) while Afghans were fighting tribal conflicts that predated American arrival by decades.

What Afghanistan Cost America

Financial:

  • Over $2 trillion in direct costs

  • Trillions more in long-term obligations (veteran care, etc.)

Human:

  • 2,400+ U.S. soldiers killed

  • 20,000+ U.S. soldiers wounded

  • Psychological trauma affecting hundreds of thousands

  • Over 100,000 Afghans killed (soldiers, police, civilians)

Strategic:

  • 20 years of attention diverted from other priorities

  • American credibility damaged

  • Alliances strained

  • Demonstrated limits of American power

Moral:

  • Promises to Afghan women and children broken

  • Allies abandoned

  • Those who helped America left behind or endangered

The Historical Count: America’s Twenty Years

From 2001 to 2021: 20 years of American occupation.

In that time:

  • Controlled major cities: Yes

  • Controlled countryside: No

  • Built sustainable institutions: No

  • Defeated Taliban: No

  • Created legitimate government: No

  • Left Afghanistan better than found it: No

The result: Same as every previous empire. Temporary occupation. Ultimate failure. Afghans still there.

What America’s Failure Teaches

1. Technology Doesn’t Matter

America had the most advanced military in history. Didn’t matter. Geography and legitimacy matter more.

2. Good Intentions Don’t Matter

America genuinely wanted to help Afghan women and children (alongside counterterrorism). Results were same as British “civilizing mission” or Soviet “socialist liberation.”

3. Money Doesn’t Matter

$2 trillion bought temporary compliance, massive corruption, and dependency. Didn’t buy loyalty or lasting change.

4. Time Doesn’t Matter

British tried 80 years. Soviets lasted 10. America spent 20. The pattern held every time.

5. Historical Knowledge Requires Actually Learning

America had complete records of every previous failure. Repeated every mistake anyway.

6. Empires Don’t Learn from Other Empires

They only learn from their own failures - if they learn at all.

The Bridge to Abandonment

The American withdrawal in 2021 was arguably inevitable. The occupation was never sustainable. The mission was impossible from the start.

But what came next was not inevitable. It was a choice.

Having spent 20 years in Afghanistan, having created an economy dependent on American aid, having promised to support Afghan women and children - America could have continued humanitarian assistance even after military withdrawal.

Instead, in 2024, the Trump administration cut nearly all aid.

Those children dying in the New York Times article are dying because of that choice.

In Part 10, the final part of this series, we’ll examine the unique cruelty of creating dependency and then cutting aid - leaving children to starve not from bombs or bullets, but from preventable malnutrition.
