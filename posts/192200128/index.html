The Iran Crisis

“The Audit”

Part LVIII Day 30: What One Month of War Has Revealed About the American Way of Fighting, the Republican Way of Governing, and the Iranian Way of Winning by Not Losing

March 26, 2026

I. Thirty Days

The war is one month old.

In thirty days: 10,000+ US strikes. 140+ Iranian naval vessels damaged or sunk. More than two-thirds of Iran’s missile, drone, and naval production facilities damaged, per US Central Command. Thirteen American service members dead. At least 1,443 Iranian civilians dead including 217 children, per HRANA — a toll the UN ambassador has not updated since March 11. More than 1,100 dead in Lebanon. Gas at $3.98 nationally, diesel at $5.38, both rising daily until Thursday when gas held flat for the first time since the war began — the first day without an increase since February 28. Brent crude back to $106 Thursday after the killing of the IRGC Naval Commander briefly sent it down Wednesday.

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. Approximately 800 tankers idle at either end. Iran has offered “non-hostile” ships conditional passage it cannot guarantee and that shipping insurers will not accept. The 15-point US peace plan has met Iran’s five-point counter-proposal — reparations, Hormuz sovereignty recognition, sanctions removal, Hezbollah protection. The distance between those positions is not negotiating distance. It is the distance between a country that believes it is winning and a country that needs to believe it has won.

Thirty days in, the breaking news and the analysis have arrived at the same moment — and they say the same thing.

II. The Man Who Closed the Strait

Alireza Tangsiri, Commander of the IRGC Navy and the man most directly responsible for closing the Strait of Hormuz. His strategy inflicted more strategic damage on the U.S. than any direct Iranian military operation in the first thirty days. Source: Official Iranian military and state media portraits

At some point overnight Wednesday into Thursday, in an apartment hideout in the port city of Bandar Abbas on Iran’s southern coast, Israeli forces killed Alireza Tangsiri — the commander of the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps Navy and the man most directly responsible for the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Israel’s Defense Minister Israel Katz confirmed the strike, calling it “precise and deadly.” Iran did not immediately comment on Tangsiri’s status.

Yeganeh Torbati, writing in the Times, documented what Tangsiri represented. He had been chosen to lead the IRGC’s naval force by Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in 2018 — the same Khamenei killed on the first day of this war. He oversaw the navy’s testing of drones and cruise missiles, chaired the board of a company that manufactured attack drones, and had become in recent weeks a vocal social media presence — posting updates on ships Iran refused to allow through the strait, threatening US-linked oil facilities, and warning against any attack on Kharg Island.

In the weeks since February 28, the Hormuz blockade has done more strategic damage to the United States than any military operation Iran has conducted. The 11 million barrels per day removed from global supply — worse than 1973 and 1979 combined, per the IEA — the $106 Brent crude price, the $3.98 American gas average, the 800 tankers idling at either end of the strait: Tangsiri did that. He did it with Shaheds costing $35,000 each against interceptors costing millions. He did it by making shipping insurance prohibitively expensive. He did it without a surface navy.

It is not clear what effect his death will have on Iran’s Hormuz strategy. The drone production problem is unchanged — the weapons are decentralized, the technology is democratized, and the factories that make them can be rebuilt faster than they can be destroyed. Tangsiri was the commander. The strategy he implemented does not die with him. But the institutional knowledge, the networks, and the operational continuity he provided are gone — and Iran’s ability to replace him while its leadership structure is decapitated, its communications are impaired, and its remaining officials fear meeting in person is genuinely uncertain.

Trump posted on Truth Social Thursday morning: “They better get serious soon, before it is too late, because once that happens, there is NO TURNING BACK, and it won’t be pretty!” Brent crude rebounded to $106 — up 4 percent from Wednesday’s close — as investors recalibrated whether Tangsiri’s death made peace more or less likely.

III. Netanyahu Races the Clock

Netanyahu races the clock: ordering a 48-hour maximum push against Iran’s arms industry while Trump pursues a five-day diplomatic pause — exposing the explicit fracture in U.S.-Israeli war objectives. Source: Israeli government and international news coverage of Netanyahu’s briefings/press conferences during the Iran conflict (C-SPAN footage stills)

While Trump pursues diplomacy and Pakistan shuttles a 15-point plan, Netanyahu has issued different orders.

The Times reported Thursday that Netanyahu ordered the Israeli military to attack as much of Iran’s arms industry as possible this week — a 48-hour push timed specifically to the diplomatic window. Two senior Israeli officials and two people briefed on the matter confirmed the instructions. The reason is explicit: Israel is concerned that Trump might end the war before its key military aims are achieved.

This is the coalition fracture in its clearest form. The United States and Israel entered this war together. They have conducted 10,000+ strikes together. But the war’s objectives have never been fully synchronized. Trump wants the Strait of Hormuz open, gas prices down, and a deal he can call a victory before the midterms. Netanyahu wants Iran’s military capacity permanently destroyed, its nuclear program eliminated, and the regime — if not overthrown — rendered incapable of reconstituting. Those objectives require different timelines. Trump’s is measured in weeks. Netanyahu’s is measured in months or years.

The 48-hour push is Netanyahu’s answer to the five-day pause. While Trump extends deadlines and Pakistan carries proposals, Israel is using the window to destroy as much as possible before a deal locks in a ceasefire that leaves Iran’s remaining industrial capacity intact.

The USS Abraham Lincoln aircraft carrier was struck by Iranian cruise missiles Thursday — the first successful Iranian strike on an American carrier in this war. Details on damage were not immediately available.

The USS Abraham Lincoln struck by Iranian cruise missiles on Day 29 — the first successful hit on a U.S. carrier in this conflict, underscoring the vulnerabilities in America’s naval posture amid the broader military audit. Source: Official U.S. Navy photography

Israel also struck targets in Isfahan Thursday morning. Iran’s Tasnim news agency reported strikes hitting warehouses in Alborz province west of Tehran, and a residential building in Karaj injured 18 people. Seven waves of incoming missiles from Iran toward Israel were reported. One impact was confirmed in Kafr Qassem, northeast of Tel Aviv. Six people were treated for blast injuries.

IV. Iran’s Counter-Terms

The 15-point US plan has met its answer. Iran said Wednesday, in comments carried on state television, that it would not end its attacks unless the United States paid war reparations, recognized Iranian control over the Strait of Hormuz, ended all sanctions, and agreed to a wider ceasefire protecting Hezbollah.

These are not the terms of a country close to defeat. These are the terms of a country that has read the bond market, watched the TACO sequence play out twice, noted that the United States is simultaneously lifting sanctions on its oil while bombing its military, and concluded that survival is winning.

Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf — Parliament Speaker, former IRGC commander, and the most senior Iranian official still publicly functional — is the man the Times profiles as the potential Iranian contact point in any Pakistan-hosted negotiations. He survived the wave of killings. He was designated by Khamenei as de facto deputy for wartime before the strikes. He is close to Mojtaba Khamenei, the injured new Supreme Leader still not seen in public. He is 64, a former pilot, former police chief, former mayor of Tehran who once replaced 1,500 city billboards with works by Western artists. He is not a moderate — he ordered live ammunition against student protesters in 1999 and called January’s uprising “sedition.” But he is functional, communicating, and reachable through Pakistani channels.

An Iranian official said Wednesday that Iran would not allow Trump “to dictate the timing of the war’s end.” The gap between the US position and Iran’s is not fifteen points. It is a chasm. The Pakistani facilitators are not bridging a negotiating gap. They are determining whether the parties are willing to be in the same room.

V. The Congressional Fracture

For twenty-nine days, Congressional Republicans gave Trump unlimited latitude to wage war with no authorization, no public testimony, no cost accounting, and no timeline. On Wednesday, that accommodation began to crack.

Robert Jimison, Megan Mineiro, and Eric Schmitt, writing in the Times, documented what happened when Republicans emerged from classified Pentagon briefings on Capitol Hill and got nothing.

Representative Mike Rogers of Alabama, Republican chairman of the Armed Services Committee, was “uncharacteristically irritated.” “We want to know more about what’s going on. We’re just not getting enough answers.” Senator Roger Wicker of Mississippi, Republican chairman of the Senate Armed Services panel: “I can see why he might have said that.” Republican Senators McConnell, Moran, and Murkowski all complained in the classified session about the limits of information provided. Senator Hoeven said officials “don’t have an official number at this point” for the cost of the operation — as the Pentagon prepares a $200 billion funding request.

President Donald Trump disembarking from Air Force One in Maryland on Monday.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

Representative Nancy Mace of South Carolina said what no Republican had yet said publicly: “The justifications presented to the American public for the war in Iran were not the same military objectives we were briefed on today in the House Armed Services Committee. This gap is deeply troubling.” She went further: “We will not sacrifice American lives for the same failed foreign policies. The war machine may be willing to give the lives of your sons and daughters for the price of oil, but we are not.”

Bipartisan backlash erupted simultaneously on the sanctions relief. Republicans Grassley, Wicker, and Bacon joined Democrats in condemning the lifting of Russian and Iranian oil sanctions. Senator Moran, in a floor speech: “It makes no sense to provide financial relief to a country that we are currently fighting.” His warning deserves to be read as the clearest Republican statement yet of what Iran’s strategy has achieved: “The waivers signal desperation to the Iranian regime and reinforce that their strategy of taking the Strait of Hormuz hostage is working.”

The White House’s response was revealing. Press Secretary Leavitt described the classified briefings as provided “out of courtesy and out of respect” and declined to call the conflict a war — describing it instead as “major combat operations.”

VI. The European Double Bind

Mark Landler, writing in the Times from Paris, documented the trap Trump has set for European leaders — and why they cannot escape it in either direction.

European voters are furious about energy prices. German gas averages $9.48 per gallon. Germany passed emergency legislation Thursday limiting gas stations to one price change per day to prevent gouging. France’s far-left France Unbowed scored gains in recent mayoral elections, partly on Muslim anger at the war. Italy’s Giorgia Meloni lost a judicial reform referendum — damaged, analysts said, by her perceived proximity to a Trump who did not bother to call her before starting the war.

The mechanism for European participation in a Hormuz operation exists. The coalition statement from last week expressed “readiness to contribute to appropriate efforts.” But as retired French General Yakovleff told Landler, assembling a functioning multinational coalition requires “at least two months.” Trump has given himself a five-day pause.

The deeper problem is Trump himself. He did not consult allies before the strikes. He has insulted Starmer publicly. He told Europeans their military contribution was not needed — then demanded they take the political risk of joining. Former NATO Ambassador Nicholas Burns: “All of that has contributed to the political problems that European countries have, and they’re all democracies.”

Former French Ambassador Gérard Araud’s summary is the most precise: “We could play the role of go-between, but Trump would rather have the Pakistanis. The Iranians don’t trust us either; they think we are in the pocket of the Americans.” Europe is watching from the expensive seats.

VII. The Audit of War

Eliot A. Cohen, writing in The Atlantic, has published the military audit that thirty days of fighting demands.

The good news first, because Cohen is precise. No country could have projected force from its homeland at this scale and sustained it. Not a single F-35 has been lost. The intelligence performance has been extraordinary. The professionalism of the military from top to bottom is not in question.

The bad news is structural and accumulated.

Stockpiles of advanced munitions are “radically inadequate.” Nearly a quarter of the Army’s THAAD interceptor missiles were consumed in Middle East conflicts in 2025. A comparable number may have been expended in the current war. Many high-end precision-strike systems are being consumed faster than the yearly replenishment rate scheduled for fiscal year 2026.

The naval situation is worse. In the 1987 conflict with Iran, the US Navy deployed approximately 30 warships in the Gulf. Today it has scarcely a dozen just outside it. In 1986, the Navy had 214 surface combatants. In 2026, it has half that. The frigate program that was supposed to replace aging ships collapsed because modifications made the proposed vessel prohibitively expensive. The Navy is now considering modifying Coast Guard cutters that lack the vertical launch systems required for modern naval warfare.

The mine-hunting capability is the most alarming specific. The Navy had 21 mine-warfare ships in 1986. It now has four aging Avenger-class minesweepers — due for retirement, currently in Japan, not the Middle East. The Navy first encountered Iranian mines in the Persian Gulf nearly 40 years ago.

Cohen identifies the underlying flaw: a deep-rooted bias toward quality over quantity. The same bias that produced 214 surface combatants in 1986 and 107 in 2026. The same bias that produced exquisite F-35s and almost no hardened shelters to protect them — planes “lined up wingtip to wingtip” at Al Udeid, as they were at Clark Air Base on December 7, 1941.

“The Trump administration has yet to prove that it understands just how perilous this situation is,” Cohen writes. “It has talked of a $200 billion supplemental appropriation for the military, but has yet to spell out what this will buy. Rather than bluster and braggadocio, the Department of Defense needs a well-conceived and thoughtfully presented multiyear program to build a military fit for large-scale and sustained war.”

VIII. The Asymmetric War

Iran’s asymmetric edge: Shahed drones costing ~$35,000 each against U.S. interceptors costing millions. Decentralized production and swarm tactics have sustained — and even increased — Iran’s attack rate through thirty days of war. Source: Defense analysis imagery and public exhibition photos (Getty Images archives, Open Source Munitions Portal (OSMP)

Nancy Youssef and Missy Ryan, writing in The Atlantic, have documented what the military audit means in operational terms: the United States and Iran are not fighting the same war, and Iran’s version is designed to outlast.

The strategic architecture is insurgency, not conventional warfare. Low-cost mobile weapons that can be concealed — Shaheds at $35,000 each against interceptors costing millions, floating mines against destroyers — impose costs that far exceed their production price. Iran’s attack rate has not declined. It has increased: 18-27 attacks per week in week one, 24-39 in week two, 25-41 in week three.

Iran has shifted from ballistic missiles — conserved for high-value symbolic targets like Tel Aviv and now the Abraham Lincoln — to drones for frequent shorter-range attacks against Gulf infrastructure. The drone production problem, as documented in Part LVII, is structural: decentralized manufacturing, off-the-shelf components, no specialized facilities required.

“Iran is using local weapons to generate global impact,” Kelly Grieco of the Stimson Center told The Atlantic. Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak warned on Israeli television: reopening the strait would require “two American divisions there and prepare to be there for the long haul” — at least 20,000 troops, sustained. The retired rear admiral Mark Montgomery told The Atlantic what concerns him most about the Hormuz operation: “I’m not sweating those,” he said of the drones. “I’m sweating the mines.”

Youssef and Ryan draw the parallel to Iraq directly. The United States never defeated the IED threat — it built better protection around its troops and absorbed the cost. “No matter how many strikes the U.S. launches against insurgents, America’s foes always find a way to build one more explosive. And sometimes, that is enough.”

IX. The Orphan Who Killed His Parents

David Graham, writing in The Atlantic, located the political logic of Trump’s war in an 1850s joke.

A man convicted of murdering his parents throws himself at the judge’s feet and begs for mercy on a poor orphan.

Trump did not ask Congress to declare war. He did not receive or request authorization. He briefed the Gang of Eight just before strikes but, per the Times, misled them about the scope of the attack. He did not build public support. He did not assemble a coalition other than Israel.

Now that the operation has hit difficulty, Trump wants exactly the people he ignored to bail him out. Congress is asked for $200 billion to fund a war the president also sporadically claims is over. The American public is asked to accept higher gas prices in service of a war whose aims the president cannot articulate. European allies are alternately pleaded with and raged at for refusing to take the political risk of joining a military campaign they were not consulted about.

Graham closes with the lawyer’s observation from the White House ballroom lawsuit — brought after Trump demolished the East Wing without consultation and then pleaded for mercy from the court: “They have forgotten the proverbial first law of holes. When you find yourself in one, stop digging.”

The administration has yet to stop digging. It has simply hired Pakistan to hand it a different shovel.

X. What Happens If the Hawks Lose

Jonathan Chait, writing in The Atlantic, has identified the political consequence no Republican hawk has publicly acknowledged: if the Iran war fails, the anti-Semitic isolationist wing of the Republican Party inherits it.

The GOP contains two warring factions. The interventionist hawks — pro-Israel, pro-Ukraine, anti-Russia. And the MAGA populists — anti-interventionist, Russophilic, anti-Israel, and at least tolerant of the party’s anti-Semitic currents. The Iran war has temporarily elevated the hawks. Trump sided with them decisively. They are, for now, the fair-haired child of the coalition.

But the hawks’ position depends entirely on the war going well. “Implicit in this praise,” Chait writes, “is a concession that Trump’s decision might go badly.” If the war ends in failure — undefined endpoint, Hormuz still contested, Iran still standing, gas still high, Americans still dying — the anti-interventionist faction that predicted failure will have been vindicated. And its most prominent voices are Tucker Carlson, Nick Fuentes, and Joe Kent — figures who have made anti-Semitism and anti-Israel conspiracy theories central to their politics.

“If Kent’s criticism of the war proves prescient,” Chait writes, “his other beliefs, especially those concerning Jews, will ascend in stature along with it.”

The mechanism by which the anti-interventionist, anti-Semitic wing of MAGA takes over the Republican Party after a failed Iran war is not hypothetical. It is the same mechanism by which Trump took over the Republican Party after Iraq. “Today’s god becomes tomorrow’s false prophet.” The hawks have, Chait concludes, “compounded a moral failing with a geopolitical blunder.”

XI. Bushehr

Russia’s Rosatom begins evacuating personnel from Bushehr after projectiles struck the grounds twice. Source: Satellite imagery from Planet Labs PBC (via Reuters, AP, and open news reports)

A detail buried in Wednesday’s live updates deserves its own paragraph.

Alexei Likhachev, head of Rosatom — Russia’s state atomic energy corporation — announced that Russia planned to carry out “several rounds of personnel evacuations” from Iran’s Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant, which Russia helps operate. A projectile struck the plant’s grounds on Tuesday; a similar incident occurred the week before.

Russia is evacuating its nuclear workers from an Iranian nuclear power plant during an active war in which both sides have struck nuclear infrastructure, in which the United States has threatened to strike Iran’s largest power plant, and in which Iran has promised to retaliate against Gulf energy infrastructure if its power grid is attacked. The IAEA has called for maximum military restraint regarding nuclear facilities. The signal that Russia’s evacuation sends about its assessment of whether those restraints will hold is not reassuring.
