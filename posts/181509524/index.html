The Masculinity Con

Part 2: The Evidence: What 30+ Women and Three Countries Say

December 15, 2025

On his streaming show in 2023, Tucker Carlson told Andrew Tate and 112 million viewers:

This was false. Comprehensively, demonstrably false.

The 360-page Romanian indictment obtained by The New York Times explicitly charged Andrew Tate with rape. It detailed allegations involving seven women trafficked through what Romanian prosecutors call “Lover Boy” methods - romance fraud used to coerce women into pornography. It described systematic coercion, surveillance, movement restrictions, and violence. Court records showed evidence of 30+ additional women coerced since 2014, including a 17-year-old. Andrew was also accused of having sex with a 15-year-old, beating her with a belt, choking her until she passed out, and trying to bribe her to stay quiet.

British authorities had authorized new trafficking and rape charges against both brothers based on allegations from three additional women. American Homeland Security anti-trafficking agents had been investigating the Tates for years.

But 112 million people heard Tucker’s version: no sex crimes, no violence, not actually human trafficking.

This is how you launder alleged criminals into culture war martyrs: you simply lie about what they’re accused of. And when you have Tucker Carlson’s platform, 112 million people believe the lie before they ever see the evidence.

Let’s look at what the evidence actually says.

The Romanian Indictment: What Prosecutors Actually Allege

Romania has expanded its prosecution of human trafficking in recent years to include “Lover Boy” cases - operations that use romance and psychological coercion rather than kidnapping and physical force. This reflects growing understanding of how modern trafficking actually works: manipulating trust and emotion to create situations of control.

The 360-page indictment against the Tates provides a detailed picture of the alleged operation:

The Recruitment: The brothers would romance attractive women, creating the appearance of genuine long-term relationships. Women believed they were dating someone who loved them and saw a future with them.

The Transition: Once emotionally invested, women were gradually introduced to the webcam business. The relationship provided leverage: you do this because you love me, because we’re building a future together, because this is temporary until we make enough money to start our real life.

The Control: Once involved, women were placed under surveillance, had their movements restricted, were subject to rule systems with financial penalties, were isolated from outside support networks, and were kept in a state of psychological dependence on the relationship.

The Extraction: The brothers took most of the profits. Women received minority shares, further reduced for “violations” like crying on camera. The relationship that got them in now kept them compliant - leaving meant admitting the love was a lie, meant losing their “investment,” meant facing the shame of having been deceived.

The Enforcement: When psychological control wasn’t enough, the indictment alleges, the brothers used violence.

The most damning evidence comes from Andrew Tate’s own text messages, which prosecutors cite throughout the indictment. In one exchange with a woman about what would happen when she came to Romania, the dynamic becomes crystal clear:

Woman: “do you want me to be your slave or your wife?”

Andrew Tate: “you’ll do whatever I tell you / which is the same thing / if I choose a woman to be my wife, she’ll love me enough to let me tell her what I feel like / I WILL DECIDE if she’s a slave”

She’s asking him to clarify the nature of their relationship. He’s telling her the relationship is control. “Which is the same thing” - he’s not even hiding it. Wife or slave, the label doesn’t matter. What matters is “you’ll do whatever I tell you.”

For someone who claims Romanian charges are political persecution, Andrew Tate provided prosecutors with remarkably clear evidence of his own philosophy: relationships are about control, control means obedience, and whether you call it marriage or slavery is just semantics.

The indictment describes what happened after she arrived: He raped her twice, on one occasion forcing her into group sex.

Another woman was choked so hard that blood vessels in her eye burst, prosecutors said.

The indictment includes a text message chat titled “PIMPS,” in which Tristan Tate and two associates discussed how to punish a woman who said she would no longer work for them:

Tristan Tate: “The move now is as follows.”

Associate 1: “ok”

Tristan Tate: “both of you go, get her out of the house”

Associate 2: “Ok”

Tristan Tate: “hit her if you have to, I don’t care / don’t let her take her things”

Associate 2: “thanks / Ok”

The woman was pushed down and dragged out of the house, she and witnesses told prosecutors.

This wasn’t aberrant behavior. This was business management through violence, documented in writing, with clear instructions: “hit her if you have to, I don’t care.”

The indictment names seven women as victims in the original case. But court records show evidence of 30+ additional women coerced since 2014, including minors.

The indictment acknowledges that two of the seven women initially identified as victims said they didn’t see themselves that way. This is common in coercive control situations - the psychological manipulation is designed specifically to prevent victims from recognizing their victimization. The relationship was real to them, even as it was systematically exploiting them. Prosecutors said evidence showed they were victims regardless of their self-perception.

Tucker Carlson told 112 million people Andrew Tate wasn’t accused of sex crimes or violence. The indictment describes rape, forced group sex, choking until blood vessels burst, and explicit instructions to “hit her if you have to.”

The British Pattern: A Decade of Allegations

The Romanian case isn’t isolated. It’s part of a pattern stretching back over a decade, starting in Britain where the brothers first built their webcam operation.

2014: Tristan Tate was arrested after a woman reported he had assaulted her. No charges were filed.

2015: Andrew Tate was arrested three times in Britain. Three different women made allegations:

  • One woman who had worked for him said in court documents that he strangled her during sex

  • Another said he had raped, beaten, and pulled a gun on her

  • A third woman said he had raped and strangled her while they were dating

British authorities did not charge Andrew. But he said the scrutiny prompted him to leave Britain.

His words on a podcast years later reveal his thinking: “I will not live under a government that will do this to me when I’ve done nothing wrong.”

He and Tristan moved to Romania. Andrew explained the choice on another show: “you can get away with shit you can’t get away with in the West.”

This is criminal forum shopping stated plainly. He’s not saying Romania is more free or less corrupt. He’s saying you can “get away with shit” there that would get you prosecuted in Britain. The jurisdiction choice wasn’t about opportunity - it was about evading scrutiny.

By 2015, the brothers had women in apartments and a hotel room in Britain doing webcam work. They expanded operations to Romania as their home base, running the business across both countries.

2024: British authorities authorized new trafficking and rape charges against both brothers based on allegations from three additional women - separate from the 2015 allegations. These charges are awaiting the conclusion of Romanian proceedings before moving forward.

So in Britain alone: three women made allegations in 2015, three more made allegations that led to 2024 charges. Six women across nearly a decade, leading to active criminal charges.

The American Investigation: Following the Money

In the United States, Department of Homeland Security anti-trafficking agents have been investigating the Tates for years, according to people familiar with the confidential investigation. These agents work with federal prosecutors who won the conviction of Sean “Diddy” Combs on sex trafficking charges.

The investigation remains active and confidential. But a new allegation emerged when the brothers returned to the United States in February 2025 after their release from Romanian travel restrictions.

Brianna Stern, one of Andrew’s then-girlfriends, told police in Beverly Hills he had assaulted her before he returned to Romania for a court appearance in March. On social media, she shared text messages from Andrew:

Andrew Tate: “i really love hitting you its very good for me / and you / its relaxing dont you think?”

Brianna Stern: “pookie no i cry”

Beverly Hills prosecutors declined to bring charges, citing insufficient evidence. But Stern filed a civil lawsuit against Andrew, and a court granted her a restraining order. Andrew has countersued, claiming defamation.

The text messages reveal the pattern again: violence characterized as beneficial (”very good for me / and you”), dismissed despite the victim’s clear distress (”pookie no i cry”). The framing is telling - “its relaxing dont you think?” suggests this is normal, mutual, even therapeutic. Her response - that she cries when he hits her - is treated as irrelevant to whether the violence continues.

The brothers are also suing a woman in Florida who is cited as a victim in the Romanian investigation, an apparent intimidation tactic against someone who cooperated with prosecutors.

The Youngest Victims: Minors in the Pattern

Among the most serious allegations are those involving minors.

Court records in Romania show that a 17-year-old was among the women coerced into the brothers’ pornography operation.

Andrew Tate is also accused of having sex with a 15-year-old. She told investigators he had beaten her with a belt and choked her until she passed out. He then tried to bribe her to stay quiet about what happened.

These aren’t allegations of statutory rape involving teens close in age navigating consent. Andrew Tate was in his 30s. The alleged victims were 15 and 17. The allegations include violence - beating with a belt, choking until unconscious - and attempted obstruction through bribery.

When Tucker Carlson told his audience that Andrew Tate wasn’t accused of sex crimes, 112 million people didn’t hear about the 15-year-old. They didn’t hear about the belt, the choking, the bribery to cover it up.

This is the pattern Tucker Carlson ignored. Charlie Kirk downplayed. Candace Owens dismissed.

Three countries. Multiple allegations spanning over a decade. Over 30 women. Including minors. Leading to active criminal charges in two countries and ongoing investigation in a third.

How The Media Obscured The Evidence

Tucker Carlson’s misrepresentation wasn’t an accident - it was a choice. The Romanian indictment was public record. The charges were explicit. Yet Carlson told 112 million people the opposite.

When The Times asked him about it recently, Carlson said: “If I misstated their indictment or got the facts wrong, I sincerely apologize.” Then he added: “It doesn’t change my view that the Tates, whatever their personal behavior, had a message worth hearing.”

“Whatever their personal behavior” does extraordinary work in that sentence when the “personal behavior” includes allegations of raping and trafficking over 30 women, including minors.

But Carlson’s interview created a template others followed:

Charlie Kirk watched and concluded: “Now I see why he’s popular.” He expressed disgust at the pornography business but embraced the masculinity message anyway. The criminal allegations became background noise to the culture war signal.

Candace Owens flew to Romania for a sympathetic interview. She told her audience not to judge Tate for past comments. “This is how guys have fun,” she said, “the same way that girls sit around and talk about ‘Real Housewives.’”

The genius of this media strategy: don’t defend the allegations directly. Just ignore them. Focus entirely on censorship, masculinity under siege, political persecution. Make the story about whether he should be allowed to speak, not about what he’s accused of doing.

Each media appearance moved the conversation further from the evidence:

  • Carlson: misrepresented what he’s charged with

  • Kirk: acknowledged problems but focused on message

  • Owens: normalized the entire package as “how guys have fun”

By the time casual viewers encountered the story, the allegations had been buried under layers of culture war rhetoric. The Tates weren’t alleged traffickers - they were free speech martyrs. The 30+ women weren’t victims - they were part of a persecution narrative.

This is how you launder reputation: give someone a platform, let them tell their story, don’t press on the evidence, and create enough alternative narrative that people can choose what to believe based on political affiliation rather than facts.

At What Point Does Pattern Become Undeniable?

One allegation can be disputed. Two might be coincidence. Three starts looking like pattern. But thirty?

Thirty-plus women across three countries over more than a decade, including minors, leading to:

  • Active criminal charges in Britain

  • 360-page indictment in Romania

  • Ongoing federal investigation in the United States

  • Text messages from the accused discussing slavery, hitting women, and giving explicit instructions for violence

  • £21 million in documented earnings from the operation

At what point does a pattern become impossible to dismiss as coincidence or persecution?

The Tates’ defenders focus on “innocent until proven guilty” - a crucial principle. But this isn’t about guilt or innocence in a legal sense. It’s about whether conservative media figures should be platforming, defending, and misrepresenting allegations involving three dozen women across multiple countries, including minors.

“Innocent until proven guilty” means the government must prove its case beyond reasonable doubt before punishment. It doesn’t mean media figures must pretend the allegations don’t exist or that the pattern doesn’t matter.

Tucker Carlson chose to misrepresent the charges to 112 million people. That’s not about presumption of innocence - it’s about active deception.

Conservative influencers chose to focus on “masculinity under siege” while ignoring allegations involving a 15-year-old. That’s not about fair treatment - it’s about prioritizing political messaging over accountability.

Trump officials chose to intervene diplomatically despite the pattern across multiple countries. That’s not about justice - it’s about political utility overriding prosecutorial independence.

Conclusion: The Evidence Speaks

When a woman texts “do you want me to be your slave or your wife?” and the response is “you’ll do whatever I tell you / which is the same thing,” that’s not ambiguous.

When a chat titled “PIMPS” includes the instruction “hit her if you have to, I don’t care,” that’s not unclear.

When someone texts “i really love hitting you its very good for me” and the response is “pookie no i cry,” that’s not consent.

When three dozen women across three countries over a decade tell similar stories - romance that became coercion, relationships that became exploitation, psychological control that became violence - that’s not conspiracy. That’s pattern.

When someone leaves Britain saying they won’t live under a government that investigates them and explicitly states they moved to Romania because “you can get away with shit you can’t get away with in the West,” that’s not political persecution. That’s forum shopping for less accountability.

The evidence isn’t subtle. The pattern isn’t hidden. The text messages are explicit. The indictments are detailed. The allegations span multiple countries and include minors.

Tucker Carlson told 112 million people that Andrew Tate wasn’t accused of sex crimes or violence. The evidence says otherwise. Comprehensively. Repeatedly. Across multiple jurisdictions.

The question isn’t whether there’s evidence. The question isn’t whether the pattern is clear. The question isn’t even whether we should believe victims over the accused.

The question is whether we’re willing to look at the evidence, or whether we’ll let culture war rhetoric obscure criminal allegations involving three dozen women because the accused deliver a useful voting demographic and claim to be defending masculinity.

Thirty-plus women across three countries say they were exploited, coerced, and in many cases, assaulted. Their allegations are documented in indictments, witnessed by others, and in some cases supported by the accused’s own text messages.

At some point, the pattern speaks louder than the denials.

We’re well past that point.
