The Annotated Kennedy — MAHAhahaha

A polite Canadian watches seven congressional hearings so you don’t have to

April 26, 2026

Stephen’s Sunday Satire Series

The Secretary of Health explains pharmaceutical pricing to the United States Senate. Illustration: Grok / CanadianSnowmanSays

Editor’s note: Earlier this week, the RFK Files published “Seven Hearings, Zero Accountability” a full accountability assessment of Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s four days of congressional testimony. What follows is the same testimony, rendered for those who prefer their devastation with footnotes.

This week, Robert F. Kennedy Jr. appeared before seven congressional committees over four days to defend his department's budget, his department's record, and, periodically, his department's relationship with mathematics. By the end of day one, a new acronym had suggested itself: MASA — Make America Smart Again. It has not, one feels, been trademarked. It has not, one also feels, been considered.

I watched from Toronto, where our parliamentary committees are considerably less dramatic and our Health Ministers have not, to my knowledge, body-slammed anyone in a Twinkie costume. I took notes. What follows is a lightly annotated transcript of the highlights, presented in the spirit of public service and the tradition of a nation that has always found American politics simultaneously baffling and riveting, like a car accident involving a very expensive vehicle and a mathematics textbook.

Part One: The Numbers

SENATOR WARREN: Secretary Kennedy, can you explain why Americans using TrumpRx are paying several hundred dollars for drugs available in generic form at Costco for $16?

KENNEDY: I’d like to address the President’s historic achievement on drug pricing. If you have a $600 drug and you reduce it to $10, that’s a 600 percent reduction.

[Note: It is not. A reduction from $600 to $10 is a reduction of approximately 98 percent. A discount cannot exceed 100 percent. At 100 percent, the drug is free. Beyond that, the company is paying you to take the medication, which would represent an extraordinary business model but is not what is happening here.]

WARREN: That’s not how percentages work.

KENNEDY: The President has a different way of calculating.

[Note: The President has said, on the record, “You could say whatever you want.” The Secretary of Health has chosen to say a number that does not exist in the discipline of mathematics, before the United States Senate, in defense of a drug discount website Senator Warren called “scam discounts.” This concludes the mathematics portion of the hearing.]

Part Two: The Measles

SENATOR BENNET: Secretary Kennedy, do you agree with your own CDC director that the measles vaccine is vital for children?

KENNEDY: We promote the measles vaccine.

[Note: Promote. The word chosen by the Secretary of Health, when asked about a vaccine preventing a disease that has killed three Americans this year including two children, is promote. Not “endorse.” Not “recommend.” Not “absolutely vital,” which is what Acting CDC Director Bhattacharya said last month in front of CDC staff and which represented the first time anyone in this administration said something medically coherent about measles. Promote. As one might promote a mattress sale.]

BENNET: Do you personally endorse it?

KENNEDY: We promote the measles vaccine.

[Note: We have received our answer.]

REPRESENTATIVE SCHRIER: Secretary Kennedy, your department’s vaccine skepticism has caused a spillover effect. Mothers are now refusing vitamin K injections for their newborns — injections that prevent brain bleeding.

KENNEDY: I’ve never said anything about vitamin K.

SCHRIER: That’s exactly the point.

[Note: Representative Schrier’s “that’s exactly the point” deserves a moment of appreciation. It is the cleanest possible response to a man who has spent a decade dismantling trust in medical institutions and is now surprised that the damage is not limited to the specific institutions he aimed at. The Secretary appeared not to grasp the distinction. The Representative did not elaborate further. She did not need to.]

Part Three: The Memory

SENATOR ALSOBROOKS: Secretary Kennedy, in 2024 you said — and I am reading directly from the transcript — “Every Black kid is now just standard put on Adderall, on SSRIs, benzos, which are known to induce violence, and those kids are going to have a chance to go somewhere and get re-parented.”

[Note: Senator Alsobrooks brought a large placard to the hearing bearing this exact quote. The placard was visible to the Secretary. The Secretary was looking at the placard.]

KENNEDY: I don’t believe I said that.

ALSOBROOKS: I have the transcript.

KENNEDY: I don’t remember saying that.

ALSOBROOKS: I have a placard.

KENNEDY: If I said it, I apologize, but I have to see the transcript.

[Note: The Secretary is looking at a placard of his own words and offering a conditional apology contingent on transcript verification. The transcript exists. The placard exists. The Secretary is looking at the placard. This is what philosophers call an epistemological problem and what the rest of us call a Wednesday.]

The Secretary reviews the evidence. Illustration: Grok / CanadianSnowmanSays

Part Four: The Budget

KENNEDY: There are no cuts to Medicaid!

[Note: The budget being defended proposes to reduce Medicaid spending by between $665 billion and nearly $1 trillion over the next decade relative to projected levels. The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities estimates 7.5 million Americans will lose coverage by 2034. The Secretary said this loudly.]

DEMOCRATS: There are $665 billion to $1 trillion in cuts to Medicaid.

KENNEDY: There are no cuts to Medicaid!

[Note: The Secretary’s position is that because Medicaid spending will still nominally increase in absolute terms — just by far less than it would have without the legislation — there are no cuts. This is the fiscal equivalent of arguing that because you are still moving forward, you have not slowed down. Georgetown University professor Edwin Park noted that the federal government is spending nearly a trillion dollars less than it otherwise would have. The Secretary continued shouting.]

Part Five: The Vanity Projects

SENATOR HASSAN: Secretary Kennedy, while your department has done relatively little to promote the measles vaccine, you have used official government channels to produce videos of yourself shirtless, wrestling a Twinkie, working out with Kid Rock, and appearing as a video game character. Can you characterize these as anything other than vanity projects?

[Note: The Senator is referring to content produced by the Department of Health and Human Services, the agency responsible for the health of 335 million Americans, including the ones currently catching measles. The Twinkie video was set to Limp Bizkit. The action figure had waterproof jeans. The birthday greeting from the official HHS account was a photograph of the Secretary cutting into a steak with birthday candles in it. A steak. With birthday candles. From the Department of Health.]

KENNEDY: [moved on]

[Note: The Secretary moved on.]

Part Six: The Hire and Fire

KENNEDY: I want to address our workforce strategy. After reducing our headcount by approximately 20,000 employees, we are now hiring 12,000 new workers to make sure we have people to do every job.

[Note: The Secretary fired 20,000 people including scientists, epidemiologists, and public health experts with decades of institutional knowledge, then concluded he needed people to do the jobs the fired people were doing, and is now hiring 12,000 replacements. This is described as a strategy. Elizabeth Jacobs, professor emeritus of epidemiology at the University of Arizona, said the institutional knowledge lost “is absolutely devastating” and estimated it will take “a decade or a generation to rebuild.” The Secretary described his approach as “painful but necessary.” He did not specify which of his historic wins required the elimination of two decades of epidemiological expertise to achieve. The Twinkie video remains unaffected.]

Part Seven: The MAHA Reckoning

[Interlude — from the Canadian commentary booth]

While the Secretary was testifying, two things were happening simultaneously outside the hearing room.

The MAHA PAC, which exists to elect MAHA-aligned Republicans and has announced a $100 million fundraising target, was revealed by campaign finance filings to have raised $1.2 million through February. This leaves $98.8 million still to be raised, which in the mathematics of this administration is probably a 9,800 percent shortfall — though the President has noted you can say whatever you want.

And Tricia Busch, a two-time cancer survivor and former Democrat from Iowa who met Kennedy from a wheelchair during the 2024 campaign and believed, truly believed, that he was speaking for people like her — people who felt slowly poisoned — described herself this week as “a political orphan.” Trump’s glyphosate executive order was the breaking point. “I’ve never felt so betrayed,” she said.

Alex Clark, one of MAHA’s most prominent leaders, said her voters “have nowhere to go. They feel like their vote is useless. They have lost the energy.”

Across the hearing room, the Secretary was shouting about Medicaid.

Closing Notes from the Commentary Booth

Senator Bill Cassidy, the Louisiana Republican who cast the pivotal vote to confirm Kennedy after extracting promises now visible mainly by their absence, was asked by reporters for his assessment of four days of testimony.

“Things were illuminating,” he said.

He then left.

I have watched a great deal of American political theatre from this comfortable northern distance. I have never heard three words carry more weight. Things were illuminating. The measles is back. The math doesn’t work. The action figure has waterproof jeans. The Secretary cannot remember his own quotes while looking at a placard of his own quotes. The PAC is $98.8 million short. The Twinkies are still on the shelf.

I was thirteen years old when they shot Uncle Jack. I remember thinking, in the way that a thirteen-year-old processes something that large, that American leadership was serious business conducted by serious people at serious cost. That the performance and the substance were the same thing.

His nephew promotes the measles vaccine.

Things were illuminating.
