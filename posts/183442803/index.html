The Next Targets

Cuba and Nicaragua: “Their Days Are Counted”

January 4, 2026

January 4, 2026

Hours after announcing the capture of Nicolás Maduro, Florida Republicans gathered in Doral to deliver a message: Venezuela was just the beginning. Rep. Mario Díaz-Balart stood before cameras and declared that two more “tyrannies” in Latin America would not survive Trump’s presidency. The domino strategy is no longer theoretical. It’s operational.

INTRODUCTION: THE PUBLIC THREAT

At 2:00 PM on Saturday, January 3, 2026, as smoke still rose from Caracas and Maduro sat handcuffed aboard the USS Iwo Jima, three Cuban-American Republican congressmen held a press conference in Doral, Florida.

Rep. Mario Díaz-Balart, the longest-serving member of Florida’s congressional delegation, stood flanked by Reps. Carlos Giménez and María Elvira Salazar. All three praised Trump’s Venezuela operation. But Díaz-Balart had come to deliver a different message entirely.

When asked about the futures of Cuba and Nicaragua, he didn’t hedge. He didn’t qualify. He issued a direct threat.

“The first of those tyrannies has not survived President Trump,” Díaz-Balart said, referring to Venezuela. “The next two, their days are also counted.”

He paused for emphasis.

“I am convinced that the two remaining ones will not survive the last three years of the President Trump administration.”

This wasn’t speculation. This wasn’t analysis. This was a timeline.

Cuba and Nicaragua: Three years or less.

The Trump administration, Díaz-Balart said, has placed “very strong pressure” on the dictatorship in Havana. “You’re going to see more,” he promised.

The domino strategy that analysts predicted after the Venezuela invasion isn’t coming. It’s here. And Congressional Republicans are publicly telegraphing the targets, the timeline, and the method.

CUBA: THE COLLAPSING DOMINO

The Economic Vise

Energy crisis: Venezuela sends ~35,000 barrels/day oil to Cuba. Power grid depends on it. 2025: Blackouts and water cuts worse than ever.

Economic collapse: Cuban peso down 95% since 2020. Tourism down 20% in 2025 (lowest in 20 years). Sugar harvest 2025: ~160,000 tons.

That sugar statistic matters: In 1898, under Spanish rule, Cuba produced 8 million tons of sugar. After 60+ years of Castro communism: 160,000 tons. 98% decline.

Population exodus: 25% of Cuba’s population fled since 2020 (matches Venezuela).

The Venezuelan Lifeline—Cut

Elliott Abrams (CFR, January 1, 2026): “The end of the Maduro regime in Venezuela and its oil subsidies of Cuba will bring a final collapse of the Cuban economy.”

Before Maduro’s capture: Venezuela sends 35,000 barrels/day, Cuba barely functions.

After Maduro’s capture: Venezuelan oil flow uncertain, U.S. controls Venezuela’s oil infrastructure, Cuba’s energy crisis becomes catastrophic.

Cuba’s government faces a choice: Reform (refuses), collapse and face uprising, seek another benefactor (Russia/China exhausted), or face U.S. intervention.

The Venezuela Playbook Applied to Cuba

The Venezuela invasion eliminated every obstacle:

Constitutional: Bypassed (no Congressional approval needed)
International law: Ignored (UN Charter violations meaningless)
Framing: “Law enforcement” not war
Occupation: Indefinite, no consequences
Resources: Extraction normalized as objective

The application to Cuba:

  1. Economic strangulation (cut Venezuelan oil—already happening)

  2. Designate Díaz-Canel as “narco-terror” leader

  3. Issue arrest warrants through DOJ

  4. Frame as “humanitarian intervention” (90%+ poverty, 25% fled)

  5. Strike when regime weakest (economy collapsed)

  6. Announce U.S. will “run the country”

The economic trigger is already pulled. The military is in position. The precedent is set.

The Cuba Difference

Cuba is not Venezuela in several critical ways:

Proximity:

  • 90 miles from Florida

  • U.S. already has military base (Guantanamo Bay)

  • No need for carrier strike groups—Air Force can operate from Florida

  • Easier logistics than Venezuela

U.S. history:

  • Bay of Pigs (1961 failed invasion)

  • Cuban Missile Crisis (1962 near-nuclear war)

  • 60+ years of embargo

  • Deep emotional resonance for Cuban-American community in Florida

Political support:

  • Florida’s Cuban-American Republicans unanimous

  • Larger exile community than Venezuelan-Americans

  • Political pressure enormous

  • 2026 midterms: Florida Republicans need Miami/Hialeah turnout

Military capability:

  • Cuban military weaker than Venezuela’s

  • Economy completely collapsed

  • Less Russian/Chinese support than Venezuela had

  • Regime survival depends entirely on avoiding popular uprising

But also:

  • Cuba-Russia relationship (though Russia stretched thin in Ukraine)

  • Potential Russian response (military advisers? Equipment? Threats?)

  • China’s Caribbean interests

  • International backlash might be greater (Cold War legacy)

Díaz-Balart’s Conviction

The Hialeah Republican didn’t just predict Cuba’s fall. He promised it.

“I was convinced that these terrorist regimes would not have survived another four years of Donald Trump,” Díaz-Balart said. “Let me say it now in a different way, I am convinced that the two remaining ones will not survive.”

He used past tense (”I was convinced”) then shifted to present tense (”I am convinced”).

Translation: Venezuela’s capture changed the calculation. What seemed possible before is now certain.

The press conference included a warning for anyone who might help Cuba’s government: “We live in another era now.”

The era of U.S. restraint is over. The era of consequences for opposing Trump is here.

NICARAGUA: THE FORGOTTEN DOMINO

Daniel Ortega delivers a speech in Managua, Nicaragua, on Sept. 28, 2022

While Venezuela dominated headlines and Cuba represents the emotional target, Nicaragua may be the easiest domino to topple.

Daniel Ortega and wife Rosario Murillo have “intensified repression” (Human Rights Watch 2025): Catholic Church persecution, political prisoners, dynastic power consolidation.

Nicaragua is the weakest target: Smaller economy/military than Venezuela or Cuba, less international support, more isolated diplomatically, canal route strategic value, borders Honduras/Costa Rica (U.S.-friendly).

Abrams’ assessment: “And that would leave one repressive, brutal, failed anti-American regime in the Caribbean area: that of Daniel Ortega in Nicaragua. Could it survive alone, without Cuba and Venezuela?” Answer implicit: No.

Trump’s Venezuela Playbook → Nicaragua

Nicaragua is the weakest target—smaller economy, weaker military, more isolated. The application is straightforward:

  1. Designate Ortega-Murillo as “narco-terror dynasty”

  2. Humanitarian justification (Catholic Church persecution, political prisoners)

  3. Regional support from Honduras/Costa Rica

  4. Quick military operation (smaller than Venezuela)

  5. Strategic objectives: Canal control, eliminate Russia/China presence

After Cuba falls, Nicaragua is isolated and indefensible.

The Three-Year Timeline

Díaz-Balart: “I am convinced that the two remaining ones will not survive the last three years of the President Trump administration.”

That’s not speculation. It’s a schedule:

  • 2026: Venezuela done, Cuba economy collapses → U.S. invasion

  • 2027: Cuba completed, Nicaragua isolated → U.S. invasion

  • By 2029: All three under U.S. control, “Donroe Doctrine” complete

The compressed timeline: If Trump moves quickly, both Cuba and Nicaragua could fall within 18 months. Cuba’s economy is collapsing now. Nicaragua has no support without Venezuela and Cuba. The dominoes are already tipping.

THE STRATEGIC VISION: TRUMP’S WESTERN HEMISPHERE

“The Donroe Doctrine”

Trump at Saturday’s press conference: “The Monroe Doctrine is a big deal, but we’ve superseded it by a lot. They now call it the Donroe Doctrine. American dominance in the Western Hemisphere will never be questioned again.”

The Monroe Doctrine (1823) warned European powers away from the hemisphere. Trump’s “Donroe Doctrine”: American dominance through direct control.

The Target List

Elliott Abrams (CFR, January 1):

Primary targets: Venezuela ✓ (captured Jan 3), Cuba (collapsing), Nicaragua (isolated/weak)

Strategic objectives: End anti-American regimes, stop refugee flows (8M Venezuelans, 25% Cuba fled), cut drug trafficking, eliminate Russia/China/Iran footholds.

Abrams’ logic: These regimes harm U.S. interests, removing them benefits U.S., therefore remove them. International law irrelevant. Sovereignty only for nations strong enough to defend it.

The Domino Effect—Confirmed

In Part 8, I predicted the cascading effect: Trump’s Venezuela invasion would create permission structure for autocrats worldwide.

What I didn’t predict: Trump himself would be the first to use it.

The cascading effect isn’t China invading Taiwan or Russia citing Venezuela for Ukraine.

The cascading effect is Trump invading Cuba and Nicaragua.

Venezuela proved the playbook works:

  • Bypass Congress ✓

  • Ignore international law ✓

  • Frame as “law enforcement” ✓

  • Announce indefinite occupation ✓

  • Extract resources ✓

  • Face no consequences ✓

Now apply to Cuba. Then Nicaragua. Then... who’s next?

Who’s Next After Nicaragua?

If Trump successfully removes three governments in three years, what stops him from continuing?

Potential targets (all mentioned by Trump or advisers at various times):

  • Haiti: Failed state, humanitarian crisis, refugee flows

  • Bolivia: Lithium reserves, leftist government

  • Colombia: Gustavo Petro deployed forces to Venezuela border, Trump warned him to “watch his ass”

  • Mexico: Drug cartels, fentanyl production, Trump repeatedly threatened

The limiting factors:

  • Mexico too large, too integrated with U.S. economy

  • Colombia has U.S. military cooperation, complicated

  • Bolivia landlocked, difficult logistics

  • Haiti already partially occupied by international forces

But the principle is established: Any government Trump deems “narco-terror state” or “criminal organization” or “threat to U.S. interests” can be invaded, leadership captured, country occupied.

The only limit is military feasibility and political will.

After Venezuela, Cuba, and Nicaragua... the limit may be gone.

THE FLORIDA FACTOR: ELECTORAL POLITICS DRIVE FOREIGN POLICY

The Cuban-American Caucus

Saturday’s Doral press conference wasn’t just about foreign policy. It was about Florida politics.

The three congressmen:

  • Mario Díaz-Balart (R-Hialeah): Longest-serving Florida delegation member, Cuban-American

  • Carlos Giménez (R-Miami): Cuban-American, former Miami-Dade mayor

  • María Elvira Salazar (R-Miami): Cuban-American, former Telemundo journalist

All three represent districts with large Cuban-American populations.

All three face 2026 midterm elections.

All three need Cuban-American turnout.

The Electoral Calculation

Cuban-Americans in Florida have waited 65 years for the fall of Castro’s regime. Venezuelan-Americans celebrated in the streets when Maduro was captured. If Trump delivers Cuba within two years—before the 2026 midterms—Florida Republicans win in landslides.

The political incentive structure:

For Trump:

  • Florida delivered Electoral College victory

  • Cuban-American community intensely loyal

  • Success in Cuba = Florida locked up for 2028 (if Trump runs) or Vance 2028

  • Legacy: “The president who ended Castro’s communism”

For Florida Republicans:

  • 2026 midterms approaching

  • Delivering Cuba/Nicaragua = hero status in Miami/Hialeah

  • Fundraising bonanza from exile community

  • Electoral dominance in South Florida for generation

For Rubio:

  • Cuban-American Secretary of State

  • Personal stake in ending regime that drove his family from Cuba

  • Legacy defining moment

  • Potentially positions him for post-Trump GOP leadership

The alignment of electoral incentives and military capability is dangerous.

When domestic politics rewards foreign military intervention, constraints disappear. The 2026 midterms aren’t just elections—they’re a timeline for the next invasion.

The Whisperers Redux

In Part 6, I documented how Rubio, Stephen Miller, and Pam Bondi whispered Venezuela into Trump’s ear.

The same whisperers are now whispering Cuba:

Marco Rubio:

  • Cuban-American heritage

  • Personal connection to anti-Castro cause

  • Political incentive to deliver Cuba

  • Already positioned as architect of Venezuela operation

  • Technique: Frame Cuba as “narco-terror state” + economic collapse = opportunity + military success in Venezuela = confidence

Stephen Miller:

  • Already articulated justification for Venezuela (”American toil created oil industry”)

  • Can apply same logic to Cuba (”American investment built pre-Castro economy”)

  • Immigration hardliner: Cuba falling stops refugee flows

  • Ideological motivation: Ending communism in hemisphere

Florida Congressional delegation:

  • Díaz-Balart, Giménez, Salazar constantly pressing Trump

  • Direct access through Mar-a-Lago proximity

  • Electoral pressure: Deliver Cuba or face primary challenges

  • Unified message: “Now is the time”

The whisper campaign: “Mr. President, Venezuela proved it works. Cuba is weaker. The economy is collapsing. You could be the president who ended Castro’s regime. Think about Florida in 2026. Think about your legacy. The Cuban people are ready. Our military is ready. What are we waiting for?”

Trump already proved he listens to these whispers. Venezuela happened. Cuba is next.

INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE: THE SILENCE IS DEAFENING

Where Are the Consequences?

After Venezuela: UN meetings (vetoed), ICC consideration (U.S. non-member), condemnation from allies (ignored).

If there are no consequences for Venezuela, there will be none for Cuba.

Russia’s dilemma: Military bogged down in Ukraine (year 4), economy sanctioned, can’t project Caribbean power like USSR in 1962. Will condemn loudly (hypocritically given Ukraine), threaten vaguely, deliver nothing.

China’s calculation: Taiwan far more important than Cuba. Economic ties to U.S. dwarf Caribbean interests. Can’t defend Cuba militarily. Will cut losses, cite Venezuela precedent when invading Taiwan.

Neither Russia nor China will meaningfully oppose Cuba or Nicaragua invasions. The international order that might have constrained Trump died with Venezuela.

THE RESOURCE PROBLEM: WHY TRUMP MIGHT NOT FOLLOW THROUGH

Venezuela Had What Trump Wanted

Trump invaded Venezuela for oil. He said it explicitly, repeatedly, without shame:

“They stole our oil.”

“We’re going to have our very large United States oil companies... fix the badly broken infrastructure, the oil infrastructure, and start making money for the country.”

“It’s going to make a lot of money.”

Venezuela has the world’s largest proven oil reserves: ~300 billion barrels.

That’s the motive. That’s why Trump invaded. Not democracy. Not humanitarianism. Not even eliminating a “narco-terror” threat.

Oil.

Cuba Has Nothing Trump Wants

Cuba’s oil production: Nearly zero. Cuba imports 100% of its oil—primarily from Venezuela (now cut off).

Cuba’s strategic resources: None that Trump can extract and monetize.

Cuba’s economy: Collapsed. GDP per capita ~$9,500. Nothing to extract.

What does Trump gain by invading Cuba?

Electoral gratitude from Cuban-Americans? Maybe. But Trump already has Florida locked. Cuban-Americans voted overwhelmingly for him in 2024. He doesn’t need to invade Cuba to secure their votes in 2026 or 2028.

Legacy points for “ending Castro communism”? Trump doesn’t care about ideology. He cares about money, ego, and what benefits him personally.

Cuba offers none of these.

Nicaragua Has Even Less

Nicaragua’s oil production: Essentially zero.

Nicaragua’s GDP: $15 billion (smaller than Vermont).

Nicaragua’s strategic value: Canal route (but the U.S. doesn’t need it—Panama Canal exists and U.S. has enormous influence there).

What does Trump gain by invading Nicaragua?

Eliminating a weak dictator who poses no threat to U.S. interests? Trump doesn’t invade countries to promote democracy. He just proved that by rejecting Machado.

Completing the “domino effect”? That’s a narrative, not a motive.

Nicaragua offers nothing Trump wants to extract.

Trump Doesn’t Do Ideology—He Does Money

Review Trump’s foreign policy decisions:

Afghanistan withdrawal: “Endless war” cost money, no resources to extract, got out

Syria oil fields: Trump kept troops there explicitly to “take the oil”

Greenland purchase attempt: Rare earth minerals, strategic resources

Venezuela invasion: Explicitly about oil, repeatedly stated

Sunday morning (January 4), Marco Rubio confirmed the pattern: On CBS “Face the Nation,” Rubio said the U.S. naval force will maintain a “quarantine” around Venezuela “until we see changes” and focused on “opportunities for American companies in Venezuela’s oil sector.”

Michael Shifter, Inter-American Dialogue: “For Trump, democracy is not a concern — it is about money, power, and protecting the homeland from drugs and criminals.”

Pattern: Trump uses military force when there’s something to gain—resources, money, strategic assets he can monetize or control.

Cuba and Nicaragua don’t fit this pattern.

The Florida Republicans Want It—But Trump’s Calculus Is Different

Díaz-Balart, Giménez, and Salazar desperately want Cuba invaded. Their reasons:

  1. Electoral: Cuban-American voters reward Cuba regime change

  2. Personal: Their families fled Cuba, personal vendetta against regime

  3. Ideological: End Castro communism (legacy project)

  4. Political: 2026 midterms approaching, need wins

These are their motives. Not Trump’s.

Trump invaded Venezuela because he benefits (oil extraction, “making money”). The Florida Republicans supported it because they benefit (Venezuelan-American voters, anti-socialism credentials).

But Cuba and Nicaragua only benefit the Florida Republicans—not Trump.

Unless Trump sees a way to personally profit from invading Cuba (he doesn’t—there’s no oil), why would he do it?

The Whisper Campaign Might Fail

Part 6 documented how Rubio, Miller, and Bondi whispered Trump into invading Venezuela. The Florida delegation is trying the same thing with Cuba.

The Venezuela whispers worked because:

  • Oil (Trump’s personal interest aligned)

  • Rubio’s expertise (Venezuela policy, knew the angles)

  • Easy military operation (quick win)

  • All motives aligned (Trump gets oil, Florida Republicans get votes)

The Cuba whispers might fail because:

  • No oil (Trump’s personal interest not aligned)

  • Harder military operation (larger than Venezuela, closer to U.S., more complex)

  • Only Florida Republicans benefit (Trump gains nothing material)

  • Trump’s attention may shift elsewhere (Middle East, China, domestic issues)

Rubio can whisper all he wants. But if Trump doesn’t see profit, he won’t invade.

Díaz-Balart’s Confidence May Be Wishful Thinking

“I am convinced that the two remaining ones will not survive the last three years of the President Trump administration.”

Díaz-Balart sounded certain. He gave a timeline. He framed it as inevitable.

But consider:

Díaz-Balart wants Cuba to fall. He’s wanted it his entire political career. His family fled Cuba. Ending the regime is personal for him.

When you want something badly enough, prediction becomes hope.

Díaz-Balart may be projecting his desires onto Trump’s intentions. He may believe that because Venezuela worked, Cuba is next—without recognizing that Venezuela had oil and Cuba doesn’t.

He may be reading Trump’s Venezuela success as ideological (anti-socialism, pro-democracy) when it was actually mercenary (oil extraction, resource control).

If Trump’s motive was oil, not ideology, then Cuba doesn’t follow logically from Venezuela.

The Alternative Scenario: Cuba Collapses on Its Own

Here’s what might actually happen:

2026: Venezuelan oil cut off, Cuba’s economy collapses further, blackouts worsen, protests increase

2027: Cuban government weakens, military fractures, popular uprising possible

2028: Regime falls internally (like USSR 1991, not U.S. invasion 1989 Panama)

Result: Cuba transitions without U.S. invasion, Trump claims credit anyway (”My pressure brought freedom”), Florida Republicans celebrate, no U.S. troops needed

This costs Trump nothing, risks nothing, and he still gets to claim victory.

Why invade when you can just wait for collapse and take credit?

What This Means for Part 9’s Predictions

Cuba invasion is possible if:

  • Trump sees a way to profit (currently doesn’t exist)

  • Florida Republican pressure becomes overwhelming (possible but not certain)

  • Cuba does something that personally offends Trump (wildcard)

  • Trump wants “legacy” of liberating Cuba (unlikely—he’s transactional, not ideological)

Cuba invasion is unlikely because:

  • No oil or extractable resources

  • Trump’s personal interest not aligned

  • Harder military operation than Venezuela

  • Cuba might collapse on its own (Trump gets credit without risk)

Nicaragua invasion even less likely:

  • Smaller, weaker, less important to Florida Republicans

  • No resources, no strategic value Trump cares about

  • Could fall after Cuba without U.S. invasion

The honest assessment: Díaz-Balart’s prediction may reflect what he wants, not what Trump will do.

Trump invaded Venezuela for oil. Unless Cuba or Nicaragua suddenly discover oil reserves, Trump’s calculus doesn’t support invasion.

The Florida Republicans can whisper. But Trump invades for profit, not ideology. And Cuba has no profit to extract.

THE QUESTION: WHEN, NOT IF

Díaz-Balart didn’t say “if.” He said “will not survive.” He gave a timeline: Three years.

Cuba: Economy collapses without Venezuelan oil (2026) → Pressure campaign and “narco-terror” designation → Mass protests trigger “humanitarian intervention” → Invasion (late 2026/early 2027) → Indefinite U.S. occupation.

Nicaragua: Isolated after Cuba/Venezuela fall → Designated “narco-terror dynasty” → Quick military operation (2027) → U.S.-installed government.

By January 2029: Venezuela, Cuba, Nicaragua all under U.S. control. Caribbean/Central America dominated. Russia and China expelled. Trump’s legacy: Western Hemisphere conquest.

This isn’t speculation. This is the schedule Congressional Republicans just announced.

CONCLUSION: PREDICTION VS. REALITY

On December 10, I published “The Threat Matrix,” predicting Trump would invade Venezuela.

On January 3, Trump invaded Venezuela.

On January 3, Díaz-Balart announced Cuba and Nicaragua are next.

The pattern isn’t theoretical. It’s operational—according to Florida Republicans.

But here’s the critical question: Will Trump actually follow through?

Venezuela had oil—300 billion barrels. Trump said it explicitly, repeatedly: “They stole our oil,” “U.S. companies will make money,” “largest oil reserves.” That was the motive.

Cuba has no oil. Zero extractable resources Trump wants. Nicaragua has even less.

Trump invaded Venezuela using legal theories that bypass every constitutional constraint. The playbook works. The precedent is set. The targets are named. The timeline announced: Three years or less.

But Trump invades for profit, not ideology. Venezuela proved it.

Díaz-Balart’s confidence—”I am convinced that the two remaining ones will not survive”—may reflect what he wants (Cuba’s regime ended, his family’s homeland free) rather than what Trump will do (invade only when profitable).

Two scenarios:

Scenario 1 (Florida Republicans right): Trump invades Cuba (late 2026) and Nicaragua (2027) to complete “Donroe Doctrine,” secure Florida votes, eliminate China/Russia footholds. Electoral politics and momentum overcome lack of resource motive.

Scenario 2 (Realist view): Trump doesn’t invade without resources to extract. Cuba collapses internally from Venezuelan oil cutoff. Trump claims credit without invasion. Nicaragua follows. Florida Republicans disappointed but can’t force Trump’s hand.

The honest assessment: Díaz-Balart predicted invasions. Trump’s actual behavior suggests he needs profit motive. Venezuela had oil. Cuba doesn’t.

Every obstacle to invasion is gone (Congressional approval, international law, diplomatic immunity, occupation authority). But the obstacle that remains might be the only one that matters: Trump’s personal interest.

After Cuba and Nicaragua... who’s next? That question may be premature. The real question: Will Trump invade countries that offer him nothing to extract?

The cascading effect I predicted in Part 8 is happening—but will Trump be the one cascading, or will he stop after Venezuela because the profit motive disappears?

Cuba’s days may be numbered—but by internal collapse, not U.S. invasion.

Unless Trump finds a way to profit from “liberating” Cuba, Díaz-Balart’s three-year timeline may prove to be wishful thinking.

Díaz-Balart isn’t predicting the future. He’s describing what he wants. Whether Trump delivers is another question entirely.

POSTSCRIPT: COLOMBIA EMERGES (Sunday Night, January 4)

Hours after this essay was written, President Trump named a different next target.

Sunday night, Air Force One: Asked about Colombia’s President Gustavo Petro, Trump said: “Colombia is run by a sick man who likes making cocaine and selling it to the United States. He’s not going to be doing it for very long. He has cocaine mills and cocaine factories.”

Asked if the U.S. would conduct an operation against Colombia: “It sounds good to me.”

The pattern: Same “narco” framing as Venezuela. Same explicit threat. Same presidential authority claiming he can invade to stop drugs.

But Colombia has key differences from Cuba/Nicaragua:

  • Larger economy (GDP $380 billion vs Cuba $107B, Nicaragua $15B)

  • More strategic location (Pacific and Caribbean coasts, borders Panama Canal)

  • U.S. has military presence already (bases, counter-drug operations)

  • Petro is leftist who criticized Trump repeatedly

  • And critically: Colombia produces oil (~800,000 barrels/day)

The resource calculus: Cuba has no oil. Nicaragua has no oil. Colombia has oil—not Venezuela-level, but significant.

Trump’s actual next target may not be the one Florida Republicans predict. Díaz-Balart wants Cuba for electoral politics. Trump wants countries with resources to extract.

Colombia has resources. Colombia’s president criticized Trump. Colombia fits the playbook.

The question isn’t whether Trump will invade more countries. The question is which ones have what Trump wants to take.

Cuba may fall on its own from economic collapse. Nicaragua may follow. But Colombia—with its oil, its critical location, its leftist president who insults Trump—fits Trump’s pattern better than either.

Florida Republicans predicted Cuba and Nicaragua. Trump threatened Colombia.

Pay attention to what Trump does, not what his supporters want him to do.

The view from the 49th parallel: I predicted the cascading effect after Venezuela. Congressional Republicans are publicly announcing Cuba and Nicaragua are next. The playbook works. The precedent is set. But here’s what gives me pause: Trump invaded Venezuela for oil. He said it explicitly, without shame. Cuba has no oil. Nicaragua has no oil. Trump is transactional, not ideological. The Florida Republicans want these invasions desperately—for votes, for family, for legacy. But what does Trump want? Unless he sees profit, why invade? Díaz-Balart’s confidence may be hope disguised as prediction. The dominoes might not fall—because Trump only pushes them when there’s money to be made.
