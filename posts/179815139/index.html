The Charlie Kirk Debates

#11: Seven Mountains Dominionism - Part II: The Challenge

December 3, 2025

Charlie Kirk challenged others to “prove me wrong” while embracing a theology that mainstream Christianity condemns as heretical, that advocates theocratic dominion over democratic institutions, and that has been linked to political violence. The evidence proves Seven Mountains Dominionism wrong on biblical, theological, historical, and democratic grounds.

I. The Biblical Case Against Seven Mountains

Eisegesis, Not Exegesis

Seven Mountains Dominionism distorts Scripture through eisegesis—reading meanings into the text that aren’t there—rather than exegesis—drawing meaning out of what the text actually says.

The “Occupy” Misinterpretation: Kirk’s use of Luke 19:13 (”Occupy until I come”) exemplifies this. In context, Jesus’s Parable of the Ten Minas teaches faithful stewardship of what God has entrusted to us—not political conquest of earthly institutions.

The parable addresses individuals’ spiritual accountability, not corporate Christian dominion over secular power structures. Jesus never commanded His followers to seize control of Roman government, Jewish religious authorities, or Greco-Roman culture. He told them to make disciples, not capture institutions.

The Genesis 1:28 Distortion: Seven Mountains teachers cite God’s command to Adam and Eve—”Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth, and subdue it; and rule over... every living thing”—as justification for Christian dominion over society.

But this “dominion” concerned humanity’s stewardship of creation, not Christians’ political control over non-Christians. God gave this mandate to all humanity, not exclusively to Christians. And humanity’s fall broke this dominion—Seven Mountains theology tries to reclaim through political power what can only be restored through Christ’s redemption.

The Deuteronomy 7 Conflation: Seven Mountains teachers correlate the seven mountains to the seven Canaanite nations God commanded Israel to drive out (Deuteronomy 7:1). They apply Old Testament conquest narratives to modern culture war.

This confuses Israel’s unique theocratic role in redemptive history with the church’s missionary calling. Israel was a nation-state with borders, military, and civil government. The church is a spiritual kingdom without ethnic boundaries or earthly armies. Applying Israel’s military conquest commands to Christian political action fundamentally misunderstands both testaments.

Jesus’s Kingdom Is Not of This World

The clearest biblical refutation of Seven Mountains Dominionism comes from Jesus Himself.

When Pontius Pilate questioned Him about kingship, Jesus replied:

“My kingdom is not of this world. If My kingdom were of this world, then My servants would be fighting so that I would not be handed over to the Jews; but as it is, My kingdom is not of this realm.” (John 18:36)

Jesus explicitly rejected earthly political power. When crowds wanted to make Him king by force, He withdrew. When Satan offered Him all the kingdoms of the world, He refused. When disciples expected Him to restore Israel’s political kingdom, He redirected them to spiritual mission.

Jesus’s entire ministry demonstrated that God’s kingdom advances through sacrificial love, not political dominion—through service, not control—through the cross, not the sword.

The Apostles Never Pursued Political Power

Early Christians lived under Roman persecution. They had every incentive to “take the mountain” of government if that was their calling. Yet not once do we see apostles organizing to capture Roman political power, educational institutions, or entertainment venues.

Paul told believers to pray for authorities and submit to government (Romans 13:1-7), not to conquer and control it. Peter called Christians “aliens and strangers” in this world (1 Peter 2:11), not occupying forces. James warned against worldly ambition and power-seeking (James 4:1-4).

The apostles planted churches and made disciples. They never attempted to Christianize Roman culture by capturing its power centers.

II. The Theological Condemnation by Mainstream Christianity

Assemblies of God Denounced the Predecessor Movement

Seven Mountains Dominionism derives from the Latter Rain Movement, a heretical Pentecostal movement from the 1940s. The Assemblies of God—one of the largest Pentecostal denominations—officially denounced Latter Rain teachings in September 1949.

The theological problems that led to that denunciation persist in Seven Mountains theology: extra-biblical revelation (apostles and prophets receiving new doctrine), authoritarian leadership structures, and dominion theology contrary to Scripture.

Mainstream Evangelical Critiques

Roger E. Olson (Baptist theologian): Examining NAR and Seven Mountains theology, Olson concluded that these movements have departed from mainstream Christianity so significantly that they no longer follow the three ecumenical creeds.

Holly Pivec and R. Douglas Geivett (Biola University): Their three books on NAR document how the movement’s promises—working mighty miracles, becoming part of an end-time army, calling down biblical judgments on opponents—contradict biblical teaching about the church’s mission and Christ’s return.

CARM (Christian Apologetics & Research Ministry): Their analysis concludes: “The Seven Mountain mandate is a hyperfocus on Christian dominion theology that does not seem to have the centrality of Christ’s atoning sacrifice as a central theme... making disciples of unbelievers is not the central issue. Instead, it’s taking dominion.”

ETHOS Institute for Public Christianity: Their detailed examination argues: “Christians should reject this doctrine because it finds no support whatsoever in Scripture and because it distorts the Church’s understanding of its relationship to the State and the eschatological kingdom of God.”

The “Dominion Now” Heresy

Albert James Dager succinctly summarized dominion theology’s three heretical beliefs:

  1. Satan usurped man’s dominion over earth through Adam and Eve’s temptation

  2. The Church is God’s instrument to take dominion back from Satan

  3. Jesus cannot or will not return until the Church has taken dominion by controlling earth’s governmental and social institutions

This theology makes Christ’s return contingent on human political success—a fundamental distortion of biblical eschatology. Scripture teaches Christ returns sovereignly, not after Christians first establish earthly dominion.

III. The Democratic Threat

From Evangelism to Theocratic Blueprint

The shift from Cunningham’s original vision (spheres for evangelism) to Wallnau’s dominionism (mountains for conquest) transformed a missionary strategy into a theocratic blueprint.

When Kirk embraced Seven Mountains, he wasn’t advocating for Christians to be active citizens. He was promoting systematic capture of democratic institutions by religious ideology—the same pattern that produces theocracy in Iran, Saudi Arabia, and Afghanistan.

Comparison to Islamic Extremism

In 2012, sociologist Margaret Poloma described NAR’s spiritual warfare rhetoric:

“The way some of the leaders talk, you’d think they were an army planning to take over the world... It sounds to me like radical Islam.”

In 2013, Paul Rosenberg called NAR “America’s Own Taliban,” highlighting how its dominionist language resembles Islamic extremism’s approach to state power.

Both movements share core features:

  • Belief that religious law should govern society

  • Framing of political opposition as demonic/satanic

  • Use of spiritual warfare language to justify earthly conquest

  • Training followers for confrontation and potential violence

  • Rejection of pluralism and religious freedom for non-believers

Links to Political Violence

Seven Mountains theology has been connected to actual political violence:

January 6, 2021: The “Appeal to Heaven” flags waved by Capitol rioters were Seven Mountains banners. NAR prophets had declared Trump God’s chosen leader; his election loss became framed as demonic opposition requiring forceful resistance.

Minnesota State Representative Shooting (2025): Vance Boelter, who allegedly shot Democratic State Representative Melissa Hortman and her husband, studied at Christ for the Nations Institute—a Bible school linked to NAR.

Religious scholar Matthew Taylor’s book “The Violent Take It By Force” documents how NAR leaders train followers that Christians should reclaim mountains “through influence, leadership and even, if necessary, the use of force.”

Project 2025 Connection

Scholar analysis reveals coordination between Seven Mountains theology and Project 2025 (Heritage Foundation’s plan for conservative governance):

  • Seven Mountains provides the theological justification: Christians must rule every sphere

  • Project 2025 provides the operational manual: how to capture and remake government

As one analyst put it: “7M says Christians must rule every sphere of life. Project 2025 hands them the how-to manual for seizing government and gutting democracy.”

IV. How Kirk Failed His Own Standards

The Evidence-Based Reasoning Test

Kirk’s claim: Seven Mountains is biblical Christianity.

The evidence: Mainstream Christian theologians across denominations—Baptist, Reformed, Evangelical—condemn Seven Mountains as unbiblical. The Assemblies of God denounced its predecessor movement. Biblical scholars show its prooftexts are eisegesis, not exegesis.

Kirk ignored this theological consensus while claiming to represent authentic Christianity.

The Christian Values Test

Jesus’s model: “My kingdom is not of this world” / rejected earthly political power / chose the cross over the sword

Kirk’s embrace: Political conquest of earthly institutions / spiritual warfare to vanquish demonic forces / “occupying” power centers until Christ returns

Kirk claimed to follow Christ while advocating a path Christ explicitly rejected.

The Constitutional Values Test

Madison’s warnings: Government must remain separate from church / religious establishment corrupts both faith and democracy / freedom requires protecting conscience from state power

Kirk’s embrace: Christians must control government / Seven Mountains adherents should “occupy” the governmental mountain / democracy can be “destroyed” if necessary to establish Christian dominion

Kirk claimed to honor the Founders while promoting a vision they fought against.

The “Prove Me Wrong” Test

Kirk never debated Seven Mountains theology openly. He never defended its biblical basis against scholarly critique. He never explained how Christian dominion over democratic institutions differs from theocracy.

When NBC News exposed his Seven Mountains connections, Turning Point issued a denial claiming Kirk “probably couldn’t tell you what the seven mountains are”—despite his repeated public endorsements.

Kirk demanded others defend their positions while hiding his most radical theological commitments behind plausible deniability.

V. The Deeper Contradictions

The “Religious Freedom” Hypocrisy

Kirk claimed to champion religious freedom while embracing theology that demands Christian control over all societal institutions.

Seven Mountains Dominionism doesn’t protect religious liberty—it establishes religious supremacy. When Christians “occupy” the government mountain, what happens to Muslims, Jews, Hindus, atheists, and non-dominionionist Christians? In Seven Mountains theology, they’re under demonic influence requiring spiritual warfare to defeat.

This isn’t pluralism—it’s theocratic dominion dressed as religious engagement.

The Democracy Destruction Admission

Scholar Matthew Boedy titled his book on Seven Mountains: “Exposing the Dangerous Plan to Christianize America and Destroy Democracy.” Kirk’s embrace of this theology represented exactly what Boedy warned against.

Democracy requires accepting that citizens with different religious views (or no religious views) share equal standing under law. Seven Mountains explicitly rejects this—it demands Christian dominion over secular institutions.

Kirk couldn’t simultaneously champion democracy and Seven Mountains Dominionism. He chose dominion.

The “Spiritual Warfare” Escalation

When Kirk described Democrats as demons, elections as spiritual warfare, and political opponents as satanic forces, he wasn’t using metaphor. This was Seven Mountains theology’s literal framework.

NAR prophet Cindy Jacobs describes the need for “spiritual warfare” in schools to combat “demonic ideologies” like sex education and LGBTQ inclusion. Lance Wallnau says Democrats are under the “Kingdom of Darkness.”

This language doesn’t invite democratic discourse—it demonizes half the country and frames politics as cosmic battle requiring any means necessary to defeat evil.

VI. What Mainstream Christianity Says

Evangelical, Reformed, Baptist, and traditional Pentecostal theologians agree: Seven Mountains Dominionism distorts Scripture, contradicts Jesus’s teaching, and threatens both religious liberty and democratic governance.

Christianity advances through evangelism, discipleship, and sacrificial love—not through capturing political power to impose Christian law on pluralistic society.

When Christians make disciples through sharing the Gospel, some will enter government, education, media, and other fields. That’s natural. But Seven Mountains reverses the process—it makes capturing institutional power the primary goal and treats spiritual transformation as the means to that political end.

Conclusion: Dominion Is Not Discipleship

Charlie Kirk challenged others to “prove me wrong” while embracing a theology that:

  1. Distorts Scripture through eisegesis, applying Old Testament conquest to New Testament church

  2. Contradicts Jesus who explicitly rejected earthly political power

  3. Ignores apostolic example of planting churches, not capturing institutions

  4. Departs from mainstream Christianity across denominational lines

  5. Threatens democracy by demanding Christian dominion over pluralistic society

  6. Links to violence through spiritual warfare rhetoric and demonization of opponents

  7. Operates through deception by using code words while denying radical implications

The evidence proves Seven Mountains Dominionism wrong—biblically, theologically, historically, and democratically.

Kirk never defended this theology openly because it can’t withstand scrutiny. He promoted it through dog whistles, partnerships with its leaders, and organizational transformation while maintaining plausible deniability about its ultimate goals.

Seven Mountains Dominionism isn’t Christianity engaging culture—it’s Christian supremacy seeking theocratic control. Kirk didn’t champion religious freedom—he promoted religious dominion.

When Kirk told Christians to “occupy until I come,” he wasn’t quoting Jesus’s call to faithful stewardship. He was invoking a dominionist battle cry that mainstream Christianity condemns, that threatens democratic governance, and that has been linked to actual political violence.

Charlie Kirk challenged others to “prove me wrong.” On Seven Mountains Dominionism, the Bible, church history, mainstream theology, and American constitutional principles all prove him catastrophically wrong.
