When Democracy Fights Back

Eight Days in April

Part 18: When Democracy Fights Back - An Ongoing Series

April 14, 2026

One week from now, Virginia votes.

That is the whole sentence. It contains everything. One week from now, voters in the Commonwealth of Virginia will be asked whether they want to reclaim, temporarily and deliberately, the power to draw their own congressional map — a power that was handed to a bipartisan commission in 2020, by a two-thirds majority of the same voters who are now being asked to take it back.

It is, on its face, a paradox. Virginia built a model. Virginia got it right. And now Virginia is proposing to dismantle it, not because the model failed, but because the game around it changed.

That is the story of these eight days. It is worth telling carefully.

Let’s start with the man who finds himself on the wrong side of his own convictions.

Glenn Youngkin served as Governor of Virginia from 2022 to 2026. He campaigned, in part, on the integrity of the electoral process. He was present and broadly supportive when Virginia’s bipartisan redistricting commission — approved by voters in 2020, enacted before his governorship began — became a national model for what sensible process could look like. Partisan politicians would not draw their own maps. Citizens and legislators, split evenly between parties, would do it instead. It was, at the time, hailed as exactly the kind of structural reform that made democracy more durable.

Glenn Youngkin is now on the campaign trail opposing the April 21 referendum.

Former Virginia Governor Glenn Youngkin, who benefited from the bipartisan redistricting commission he now campaigns to circumvent. The man who praised the model is working to dismantle it.

He is calling it a Democratic power grab. He is not wrong that the map Democrats have proposed would be favorable to their party — it would favour Democrats in ten of Virginia’s eleven congressional districts. But he is conspicuously silent on the question of why Virginia Democrats propose to do this, which is that Republican-controlled states, at Donald Trump’s explicit direction, have been redrawing congressional maps across the country to engineer Republican House majorities before the November midterms.

Youngkin knows this. He is an intelligent man. He is choosing to argue the symptom while ignoring the cause, which is a political choice, not an analytical failure.

It is worth noticing. It will not be the last time this week we notice something like it.

On the other side of the ledger, Eric Holder is now publicly supporting the Virginia referendum.

This is the same Eric Holder who served as Attorney General under Barack Obama. He is not a man typically described as the foremost champion of gerrymandering. He has spent years, through the National Redistricting Foundation, arguing for independent commissions and fair maps. He arrived at his support for the Virginia referendum the same way most serious people have arrived at it: reluctantly, logically, and with a clear-eyed understanding that you cannot play cricket in a rugby match and expect to win.

“We have to do something,” he said.

Former U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder, who spent years championing independent redistricting commissions, now supports the Virginia referendum. His position in three words: "We have to do something.

That is not the slogan of a man who wanted this fight. It is the slogan of a man who looked at what was happening and decided that the alternative to doing something was allowing it to happen. The redistricting war was started in Texas, encouraged from the White House, and extended into state after state. Virginia’s referendum is a response, not an opening move.

Here is what $9 million buys you when you’d rather not say who you are.

The primary Republican-aligned group opposing the April 21 referendum — Virginians for Fair Maps — had raised approximately $9 million by April 6th. The majority came from a group of the same name that has declined to disclose its donors. Peter Thiel’s Justice for Democracy PAC added another $5 million. The advertising paid for by these undisclosed millions has included imagery drawn from the civil rights movement. It has included imagery of the Ku Klux Klan. The implied message — that a Democratic redistricting effort is the racial threat — was condemned as misinformation by the NAACP and by civil rights leaders who know precisely what that imagery means and who it has historically served.

You do not run that advertising if you are confident in your argument. You run it when you need to confuse voters before they can think clearly. Confusion is the strategy when the facts are inconvenient.

The facts, in this case, are as follows: Virginia Democrats drew a map that favours their party. They did so transparently. They put it on the ballot. They invited voters to approve or reject it. The proposed map exists and can be examined by anyone who wants to look. Every eligible voter in Virginia can read it before they decide.

That is not the behaviour of a party trying to steal something. That is the behaviour of a party that read the room and concluded that asking voters directly was the most defensible thing they could do.

The opposition, by contrast, is funded in significant part by people who will not say who they are.

Draw your own conclusions about who is afraid of transparency.

The polls, as of April 8th, show 53 percent in favour and 44 percent opposed, with over 60 percent of registered voters saying they expect to participate. For a special election — historically the quietest, lowest-turnout genre of American voting — that number is remarkable. Early voting began March 6th and runs through April 18th. The surge in early ballots has been striking enough to merit its own tracking by VPAP, Virginia’s nonpartisan political analysis organization.

Democracy, it turns out, is not a passive institution. When people believe something is at stake, they show up. When they believe their vote matters, they cast it.

This has been, consistently, the thing that the architects of voter suppression underestimate. They treat the electorate as static, as a pool of water that can be managed by controlling the pipe. What they discover, repeatedly, is that the pool has its own weather.

Now for the part that has received less attention than it deserves.

The Florida special session begins April 20th. That is the day before Virginia votes. Governor Ron DeSantis — who needs no encouragement in this direction — will preside over a legislative session whose primary purpose is to redraw Florida’s congressional map in ways that are expected to produce additional Republican House seats. The Florida session runs through April 24th.

This is not coincidence in the sense of cosmic randomness. It is coincidence in the sense that two things happening simultaneously illuminate each other. On the day Virginia voters go to the polls to decide whether to answer gerrymandering with gerrymandering, Ron DeSantis will be signing gerrymandered maps in Tallahassee.

That is the context. That is the world in which the April 21 referendum exists. Virginia did not create this situation. Virginia is responding to it. The response may be imperfect. The redistricting commission model Virginia pioneered in 2020 was genuinely good, and it is genuinely unfortunate that circumstances have forced this choice. But the alternative — maintaining a principled posture while the map is being redrawn around you — is not principle. It is unilateral disarmament dressed in the language of virtue.

And while Virginia’s early voters were making their way to polling stations, twenty-three state attorneys general filed a federal lawsuit challenging Donald Trump’s March 31st executive order on mail voting.

This is the same order I wrote about in Part XVII of this series. The order that instructs the Department of Homeland Security and the Social Security Administration to compile federal citizen lists. The order that would give the U.S. Postal Service authority over who receives an absentee ballot. The order whose constitutional defects were visible from across the Canadian border on the day it was signed.

By April 6th — six days after signing — the order was buried under four separate legal challenges. A coalition of 23 Democratic attorneys general. National Democratic organizations. Civil rights groups. All making the same foundational argument: the Constitution does not give the president authority over state elections. Congress can legislate on elections. States administer them. The president gets no seat at that table. This was true when Adam Kinzinger said it on camera at 6:30 at night. It remains true in federal court filings at 11 points in twelve jurisdictions.

The courts have heard this argument before. They have agreed with it before. The Brennan Center, which has been tracking the litigation on the 2025 executive order for over a year, notes that three federal courts have already blocked key provisions of that earlier order, establishing a clear precedent. The judicial immune response, as I have observed in this series, has been building its antibodies for some time now.

The slot is still there. The sign doesn't say who's buying.

One week. One state referendum, one Florida special session, four federal lawsuits, 23 attorneys general, and a former Republican governor on the campaign trail against the very redistricting model he once praised.

This is not the news cycle moving fast. This is democracy moving fast. It is moving fast because it has to. The window between now and November 3rd is not infinite. The window between now and the end of early voting in Virginia is five days. The window between the Florida session and the Virginia vote is, effectively, hours.

The immune system is not winning every battle. But it is fighting every one. The attorneys general are in court. The early voters are in line. The poll numbers are moving in the right direction, if you believe the polls. The courts have their roadmap and their precedents and their history of stopping exactly this kind of overreach.

Eight days from now, Virginia votes.

What happens after that — win or lose, yes or no — we will examine in Part XIX, the Virginia Postmortem. The Callais ruling will come in June or July, and Part XX will be waiting for it.

For now, eight days.

Every one of them matters.
