The Davos Rupture

Mark Carney’s Davos Diagnosis

Part 2: How Canada’s Prime Minister used a Czech dissident’s Cold War essay to diagnose the collapse of the rules-based international order

January 21, 2026

Part 2 of the Davos Rupture Series

CanadianSnowmanSays


On Tuesday, January 20, 2026, Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney stood before the assembled global elite at the World Economic Forum in Davos, Switzerland, and told them it was time to remove their signs from the window.

The metaphor required explanation. And for fifteen minutes, Carney delivered what observers would call the most consequential speech on global affairs ever given by a Canadian Prime Minister—a philosophical and strategic break with seven decades of how middle powers navigated great power politics.

To understand what Carney was saying, you need to understand the greengrocer.

The Greengrocer’s Sign

Václav Havel portrait

Carney began not with economics or geopolitics, but with a Czech dissident’s meditation on how totalitarian systems sustain themselves.

In 1978, Václav Havel—who would later become president of Czechoslovakia—wrote an essay called “The Power of the Powerless.” It asked a simple question: How did the Communist system persist?

Carney explained Havel’s answer:

“A greengrocer places a sign in his window: ‘Workers of the world, unite!’ He doesn’t believe it, no one does, but he places the sign anyway to avoid trouble, to signal compliance, to get along. And because every shopkeeper on every street does the same, the system persists. Not through violence alone, but through the participation of ordinary people in rituals they privately know to be false.”

Havel called this “living within the lie.” The system’s power comes not from its truth, but from everyone’s willingness to perform as if it were true. Its fragility comes from the same source: when even one person stops performing, when the greengrocer removes his sign, the illusion begins to crack.

Then came Carney’s pivot—the line that would define his entire address:

“Friends, it is time for companies and countries to take their signs down.”

The Fiction We Maintained

What sign had middle powers been displaying in their windows?

Carney was unflinching in his diagnosis:

“For decades, countries like Canada prospered under what we called the rules-based international order. We joined its institutions. We praised its principles. We benefited from its predictability. Because of that, we could pursue values-based foreign policies under its protection.”

But here’s what middle powers always knew:

“We knew the story of the international rules-based order was partially false. That the strongest would exempt themselves when convenient. That trade rules were enforced asymmetrically. And we knew that international law applied with varying rigour, depending on the identity of the accused or the victim.”

So what did they do?

“This fiction was useful, and American hegemony, in particular, helped provide public goods: open sea lanes, a stable financial system, collective security and support for frameworks for resolving disputes. So we placed the sign in the window. We participated in the rituals and we largely avoided calling out the gaps between rhetoric and reality.”

The Havel metaphor is perfect. Countries weren’t duped—they knew the rules-based order was selective, that the powerful exempted themselves. But the fiction provided enough stability, enough predictability, enough actual public goods that maintaining the pretense was worthwhile.

Like the greengrocer, they didn’t believe the sign. But they displayed it anyway because the system, for all its flaws, was preferable to the alternatives.

Then came the pivot:

“This bargain no longer works.”

The Rupture

“Let me be direct,” Carney declared. “We are in the midst of a rupture, not a transition.”

He identified the progression:

“Over the past two decades, a series of crises in finance, health, energy and geopolitics have laid bare the risks of extreme global integration. But more recently, great powers have begun using economic integration as weapons, tariffs as leverage, financial infrastructure as coercion, supply chains as vulnerabilities to be exploited.”

Then came the line that directly indicts the Trump approach without naming him:

“You cannot live within the lie of mutual benefit through integration when integration becomes the source of your subordination.”

This is the diagnostic statement that explains everything we’re witnessing. The fiction was that economic integration created mutual benefit—that trade made everyone prosperous, that interconnection prevented conflict.

But when great powers weaponize that integration—when they use tariffs to extort territorial concessions, when they threaten wine industries to force submission to vanity “peace boards,” when they demand “complete and total control” as the price of alliance membership—the mutual benefit becomes one-sided subordination.

You can’t maintain the greengrocer’s sign when the system has stopped even pretending to serve any purpose beyond naked coercion.

As Carney put it:

“The multilateral institutions on which the middle powers have relied—the WTO, the UN, COP, the architecture, the very architecture of collective problem solving—are under threat.”

The Cost of Building Fortresses

Carney warned against the natural response to this rupture:

“Many countries are drawing the same conclusions: that they must develop greater strategic autonomy in energy, food, critical minerals, in finance and supply chains. This impulse is understandable. A country that can’t feed itself, fuel itself, or defend itself has few options when the rules no longer protect you. You must protect yourself.”

But the consequence is grim:

“Let’s be clear about where this leads. A world of fortresses will be poorer, more fragile, and less sustainable.”

And there’s a deeper truth about what happens when hegemons abandon even the pretense of rules:

“If great powers abandon even the pretence of rules and values for the unhindered pursuit of their power and interest, the gains from transactionalism will become harder to replicate. Hegemons cannot continually monetize their relationships. Allies will diversify to hedge against uncertainty, buy insurance, increase options in order to rebuild sovereignty—sovereignty that was once grounded in rules but will be increasingly anchored in the ability to withstand pressure.”

This is risk management. And risk management comes at a price.

But here’s Carney’s key insight: “The cost of strategic autonomy and sovereignty can be shared.”

Collectively, investments in resilience are cheaper than everyone building their own fortresses. Shared standards reduce fragmentation. Complementarities create positive-sum outcomes.

The question isn’t whether to adapt to the new reality—middle powers must. The question is whether they adapt by simply building higher walls, or whether they can do something more ambitious.

Values-Based Realism

Carney outlined Canada’s response, which he called “values-based realism”—borrowing the phrase from Finnish President Alexander Stubb. The approach is both “principled and pragmatic.”

Principled in commitment to fundamental values: sovereignty, territorial integrity, prohibition of the use of force except when consistent with the UN Charter, respect for human rights.

Pragmatic in “recognizing that progress is often incremental, that interests diverge, that not every partner will share all of our values.”

The result: “We actively take on the world as it is, not wait around for a world we wish to be.”

Carney detailed concrete actions:

  • Cutting taxes on incomes, capital gains, business investment

  • Removing all federal barriers to interprovincial trade

  • Fast-tracking a trillion dollars of investment in energy, AI, critical minerals

  • Doubling defense spending by decade’s end

  • Signing 12 trade and security deals on four continents in six months

  • Concluding strategic partnerships with China and Qatar just in the past few days

This wasn’t abstract philosophy. This was a middle power systematically reducing its vulnerability to coercion by diversifying relationships and building domestic strength.

Variable Geometry: The Alternative to the Board

Then came a concept that represents the practical alternative to the collapsing rules-based order—and to Trump’s performative “Board of Peace.”

Carney called it “variable geometry.”

Different coalitions for different issues based on common values and interests.

On Ukraine: Canada is “a core member of the coalition of the willing” and one of the largest per capita contributors to its defense.

On Arctic sovereignty: “We stand firmly with Greenland and Denmark and fully support their unique right to determine Greenland’s future.” Canada is investing in submarines, aircraft, and boots on the ground. And critically: “Canada strongly opposes tariffs over Greenland.”

On plurilateral trade: Building a bridge between the Trans-Pacific Partnership and the European Union to create a trading block of 1.5 billion people.

On critical minerals: Forming buyers’ clubs to diversify away from concentrated supply.

This is the alternative to Trump’s Board of Peace—actual coalitions built on actual shared interests, not performative structures designed to showcase a chairman’s dominance.

Variable geometry isn’t naive multilateralism. It isn’t relying on diminished institutions. It’s building coalitions that work, issue by issue, with partners who share enough common ground to act together.

The Performance of Sovereignty

Carney’s most devastating line came when he addressed the core delusion that bilateral negotiations with a hegemon can preserve sovereignty:

“Great powers can afford for now to go it alone. They have the market size, the military capacity and the leverage to dictate terms. Middle powers do not. But when we only negotiate bilaterally with a hegemon, we negotiate from weakness. We accept what’s offered. We compete with each other to be the most accommodating. This is not sovereignty. It’s the performance of sovereignty while accepting subordination.“

There it is again—the Havel framework. Just as the greengrocer performs belief by placing the sign in the window, countries can perform sovereignty by engaging in bilateral negotiations—even as they accept whatever terms are dictated to them.

Real sovereignty requires something else:

“Middle powers must act together because if we’re not at the table, we’re on the menu.”

The metaphor is perfect. Trump isn’t interested in sitting at the table for sustained diplomatic work—he’s bored of that. What he wants is to decide who’s on the menu, who gets devoured, who gets threatened into submission.

Carney was telling the assembled leaders: band together or be consumed one by one.

Living the Truth

Carney returned to his Havel framework to outline what it means for middle powers to “live the truth”:

First: “Stop invoking the rules-based international order as though it still functions as advertised. Call it what it is: a system of intensifying great-power rivalry where the most powerful pursue their interests using economic integration as coercion.”

Second: “Act consistently, applying the same standards to allies and rivals. When middle powers criticize economic intimidation from one direction but stay silent when it comes from another, we are keeping the sign in the window.”

Third: “Build what we claim to believe in rather than waiting for the old order to be restored. Create institutions and agreements that function as described.”

Fourth: “Reduce the leverage that enables coercion. Building a strong domestic economy should be every government’s immediate priority. Diversification internationally is not just economic prudence—it’s the material foundation for honest foreign policy. Because countries earn the right to principled stances by reducing their vulnerability to retaliation.”

We Are Taking Down the Sign

Carney closed with a declaration that directly contradicts the nostalgia Trump traffics in:

“We know the old order is not coming back. We shouldn’t mourn it. Nostalgia is not a strategy. But we believe that from the fracture, we can build something bigger, better, stronger, more just.”

And then the final return to Havel’s greengrocer:

“We are taking the sign out of the window.”

No more pretending. No more participating in rituals everyone knows are false. No more maintaining fictions that no longer serve their purpose.

The Carney Doctrine

Jeremy Kinsman, former Canadian ambassador to Russia, the UK, Italy and the EU, called it “the most consequential speech on global affairs I have ever heard from a Canadian PM.”

Colin Robertson of the Canadian Global Affairs Institute described it as “the Carney doctrine”—a framework other middle powers will likely adopt.

This wasn’t diplomacy. This was a philosophical and strategic break with seven decades of how middle powers navigated great power politics.

The speech had everything:

  • Intellectual depth (the Havel framework)

  • Strategic clarity (variable geometry)

  • Moral authority (living the truth)

  • Practical implementation (the Canadian pivot)

  • A memorable metaphor (the greengrocer’s sign)

And it was delivered one day before Trump arrived to defend his Board of Peace and his Greenland conquest fantasies.

The serious world was taking down its signs.
