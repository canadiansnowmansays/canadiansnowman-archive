Trump 2025-2026: Scorecards State of the Union and Reality Checks

A First-Year Scorecard

Part 2 of 3: Security, Economy, and Diplomacy

January 1, 2026

An Unofficial State of the Union: Annual Review and Resolutions for 2026

Recap from Part 1

Part 1 examined the foundational domestic policy areas: immigration enforcement that achieved border security goals through legally questionable means; federal workforce reductions that crippled government capacity; USAID elimination causing 500,000-1,000,000 preventable deaths; and chaotic trade policy that failed to achieve stated manufacturing objectives.

As established in Part 1, this assessment comes from outside America’s partisan trenches—a Canadian perspective that brings neither defensive patriotism nor partisan animosity, just rigorous analysis of governance that affects us all.

Part 2 examines economic outcomes, the expansion of drug interdiction into extrajudicial killings, diplomatic achievements and exaggerations, and the normalization of federal troops operating in American cities.

5. Affordability: Promises Unfulfilled

The Promise: “Rapidly drive prices down and make America affordable again.”

The Reality: Trump failed to deliver meaningful relief. Inflation persisted, with most prices higher than a year ago. While the administration claimed credit for gas prices around $3/gallon, inflation remained around 3%—near January levels—with the Consumer Price Index showing persistent elevation.

Grocery prices remained stubbornly high, contradicting administration messaging. The economy showed resilience through September with 4.3% third-quarter GDP growth, but this was driven by wealthy consumers while lower-income households struggled. This “K-shaped economy” saw prosperity for the affluent while most Americans faced rising costs and stagnant wages.

Voters increasingly expressed dissatisfaction, with only 36% approving Trump’s economic handling in recent polling—his worst mark since such tracking began. His pressure on the Federal Reserve to sharply lower interest rates despite wrong-direction inflation showed economic illiteracy. Mortgage rates remained high, and Trump’s proposal for 50-year mortgages drew bipartisan criticism for failing to address the fundamental housing shortage.

The Conference Board’s consumer confidence index declined for five consecutive months by year’s end, with Americans worried about inflation, political environment, and labor market prospects. Despite third-quarter strength, disposable personal income remained flat as inflation ate away at wages.

Verdict: Complete failure to deliver on central campaign promise. Economic gains flowed to wealthy Americans while working families saw continued affordability challenges.

2026 Resolution: Address the K-shaped economy through policies that help working families, not just the wealthy. Target housing shortage with supply-side solutions. Stop pressuring Fed for inappropriate rate cuts. Focus on wage growth and job creation in sectors serving middle-income Americans.

6. Drug Policy: Extrajudicial Killings at Sea

The Promise: Take aggressive action against drug cartels and reduce overdose deaths.

The Reality: Trump authorized the most legally dubious military operations since the Vietnam War’s secret bombing campaigns. Beginning September 2, the U.S. military conducted at least 29 strikes on boats in the Caribbean and Pacific, killing 105 people by year’s end—all without due process, identification, or legal justification.

The administration claimed these were “narco-terrorists” from designated groups, but provided no evidence of identities, cargo, or affiliation. The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights, Human Rights Watch, and multiple special rapporteurs characterized the strikes as “extrajudicial killings” violating international law.

Most damning, the administration conducted “double-tap” strikes—following up initial attacks to kill survivors. CNN reported that after the September 2 strike left survivors in the water, a second strike deliberately killed them rather than rescue them. Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth allegedly gave verbal orders to SEAL Team Six to “leave no survivors,” though the Pentagon denied this. Military lawyers and Republican senators expressed alarm over potential violations of the federal murder statute.

The strikes targeted cocaine smuggling from South America, yet the overdose crisis stems primarily from fentanyl manufactured in Mexican labs using Chinese chemicals—making the entire operation strategically pointless. The real purpose appeared to be laying groundwork for regime change in oil-rich Venezuela, with extensive naval deployments and administration discussions about forcibly removing President Maduro.

The hypocrisy proved stunning: Trump indicted Venezuelan President Maduro for cocaine trafficking, then pardoned former Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernández, who was convicted in federal court for identical charges of conspiring to export cocaine to the United States.

Verdict: Flagrant violations of domestic and international law producing zero drug interdiction benefits. Establishes precedent for extrajudicial execution that threatens fundamental legal norms.

2026 Resolution: Immediately halt extrajudicial killings. Return to traditional Coast Guard interdiction with arrest and prosecution. Focus fentanyl efforts on actual source: Mexican labs using Chinese precursors. If regime change in Venezuela is the goal, make the case to Congress rather than disguise it as drug policy.

7. Wars and Diplomacy: Mixed Results Through Questionable Means

The Promise: End wars quickly through personal diplomacy and “peace through strength.”

The Reality: Trump’s foreign policy record combined legitimate diplomatic engagement with exaggerated claims and contradictory actions. His most significant achievement was brokering a Gaza ceasefire in October 2025, though the framework was largely negotiated under Biden.

The October 10 ceasefire achieved the release of hostages and reduced violence, but remained fragile. By December, Israel had killed at least 406 Palestinians during the truce while continuing to block full aid delivery. Phase two negotiations stalled over Hamas disarmament and governance questions. Critics, including historian Rashid Khalidi, argued Trump’s plan constituted temporary pacification rather than genuine peace, lacking provisions for Palestinian rights.

Ukraine negotiations failed completely. Trump’s promise to end the war within 24 hours proved hollow, with Russia unwilling to accept terms despite Ukrainian concessions. His special envoys continued working with limited progress.

Trump’s claims of solving “eight wars in eight months” lacked substance. The Thailand-Cambodia peace deal he brokered in July collapsed by December. Fighting persisted between Rwanda and the Democratic Republic of Congo despite claimed mediation. India and Pakistan noted they directly negotiated their conflict resolution, with minimal U.S. involvement.

Most troubling, Trump angered his own supporters by greenlighting strikes on Iran’s nuclear sites, raising fears of regional war before announcing a mediated ceasefire days later. This contradicted his campaign promises to avoid new wars.

Verdict: Gaza ceasefire represents genuine achievement despite fragility and flaws. Other claims proved exaggerated or false. Iran strikes violated non-interventionist pledges.

2026 Resolution: Build on Gaza ceasefire success by ensuring phase two implementation with genuine Palestinian governance participation. Stop exaggerating diplomatic achievements—credibility matters. Engage seriously on Ukraine with realistic goals. Avoid strikes that contradict stated non-intervention principles.

8. Domestic Military Deployment: Normalizing Federal Troops in American Streets

The Promise: Use military troops domestically for immigration enforcement, urban crime fighting, and protest suppression.

The Reality: Trump shattered post-Reconstruction norms against domestic military use through novel legal interpretations and aggressive federalization of National Guard units. In April, he deployed troops to the Mexican border, treating a 60-foot strip of federal land as a military base to empower soldiers as Border Patrol agents.

In Los Angeles in June, Trump took federal control of California’s National Guard over Governor Newsom’s objections—the first such action without gubernatorial consent since the civil rights movement. He deployed 4,000 Guard troops and 700 active-duty Marines into city streets during immigration protests.

Later deployments in Washington D.C. and Memphis followed. While a federal judge ruled in December that the Los Angeles deployment lasted too long and ordered it ended—a decision upheld on appeal—Trump had succeeded in normalizing the concept of federal troops patrolling American cities.

The Supreme Court stymied attempts to send troops into Chicago, casting doubt on deployments in Portland and elsewhere. But the precedent was set: Americans grew accustomed to seeing military forces on their streets conducting law enforcement functions that traditionally belonged to civilian authorities.

Verdict: Successfully shattered democratic norms and established precedents that future presidents can exploit. Legal limits remain contested but may prove inadequate to prevent escalation.

2026 Resolution: Return to traditional limits on domestic military use. Work with governors rather than federalize Guard units over their objections. Invest in civilian law enforcement capacity instead of militarizing policing. Acknowledge that normalizing troops in American streets threatens civil-military balance.

Part 2 Conclusion: Security Theater and Diplomatic Mixed Results

The four policy areas in Part 2 reveal a troubling pattern: security and economic policies that failed to achieve stated objectives while creating new problems and establishing dangerous precedents.

The affordability crisis persisted despite campaign promises, with a K-shaped economy benefiting wealthy Americans while working families struggled. Drug interdiction descended into extrajudicial killings that violated both domestic and international law while doing nothing to address the actual fentanyl crisis. Foreign policy achieved one genuine success (Gaza ceasefire) while exaggerating other accomplishments and contradicting non-interventionist promises through Iran strikes. Domestic military deployment normalized troops in American streets, shattering post-Reconstruction democratic norms.

Each area presents clear paths for 2026 improvement: economic policies targeting working families rather than the wealthy; return to lawful drug interdiction; building on Gaza success while maintaining diplomatic credibility; and restoring traditional civil-military separation.

Part 3 will examine justice system weaponization, civil rights policy reversals, constitutional questions about executive power, achievements worth acknowledging, and comprehensive resolutions for moving forward in 2026.
