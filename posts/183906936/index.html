The Deterrent Effect

How Venezuela’s Invasion Brought Colombia to Heel

January 8, 2026

A Leftist President Who Called Trump “Senile” Capitulated in 72 Hours

January 8, 2026

Wednesday morning, Cartagena, Colombia: President Gustavo Petro sat down with The New York Times, visibly strained. “Well, we are in danger,” he said. “Because the threat is real. It was made by Trump.” He planned to sleep in the presidential palace that night next to the sword of Simón Bolívar—the sword he once stole as an urban guerrilla fighter. He feared he might be “extracted like Venezuela’s Nicolás Maduro.” By Wednesday evening, everything changed. Trump called. The conversation lasted an hour—unusually long for Trump and another head of state. When Petro emerged from the call, he looked “better than he had in days,” according to an aide. His message to supporters waiting at a rally: “Well, looks like we’re going to Washington.” From “senile brain” on Tuesday to “Great Honor” on Wednesday. From sleeping with a sword to scheduling a White House visit. Venezuela’s invasion wasn’t about Venezuela alone. It was a demonstration. And Colombia—watching Delta Force extract Maduro from his bedroom—got the message.

SUNDAY: THE THREAT BECOMES EXPLICIT

January 4, three days after Maduro’s capture, President Trump addressed Colombia:

He called President Gustavo Petro a “sick man” who makes cocaine to send to the United States.

Asked about military action against Colombia, Trump said: “Sounds good.”

Not: “We have no plans for that.”

Not: “Colombia is an ally.”

Just: “Sounds good.”

The threat was explicit. Venezuela Saturday. Colombia next?

PETRO’S HISTORY: NOT A NATURAL TARGET FOR CAPITULATION

Gustavo Petro is not a man who scares easily.

At 65, he’s a former urban guerrilla—member of the M-19 movement that stole Simón Bolívar’s sword from a museum in 1974 as a symbolic act. He spent time in military prison. He’s Colombia’s first leftist president, elected in 2022.

Since Trump took office a year ago, their relationship has been openly hostile:

February 2025: Petro briefly blocked military deportation flights carrying migrants from the United States. Backed down only after Trump threatened “crushing tariffs.”

Throughout 2025: Petro denounced Trump’s support for Israel, called on American soldiers to disobey Trump at a pro-Palestinian rally in New York (State Department revoked his visa for this).

December 2025-January 2026: Petro accused the U.S. of committing “murder” in boat strikes off South American coast, said strikes were to “distract from the Epstein scandal.”

Tuesday, January 6: Petro social media post referred to Trump’s “senile brain.”

This is not a president who accommodates easily.

And yet, by Wednesday night, he was thanking Trump for the “great honor” of their phone call.

What changed?

Saturday morning. Caracas. Delta Force.

MONDAY-WEDNESDAY: WATCHING VENEZUELA, FEARING COLOMBIA

Petro watched what happened to Nicolás Maduro.

Saturday: Delta Force breached Maduro’s heavily-fortified compound. Killed 80+ defenders. Extracted Maduro and his wife within hours.

Sunday: Trump announced Maduro in U.S. custody, facing trial in New York.

Monday: Venezuela’s National Assembly—277 of 285 members chavista—made defiant gestures but took no action. Delcy Rodríguez, Maduro’s former vice president, emerged as interim leader. She accommodated within 12 hours of Trump’s explicit threat.

The message to Latin America: No compound is too fortified. No leader is safe. Accommodate or face extraction.

And Trump, on Sunday, made clear who might be next:

“Colombia,” he said. Military action “sounds good.”

WEDNESDAY MORNING: “WE ARE IN DANGER”

The New York Times interviewed Petro Wednesday morning in Cartagena. The interview lasted two hours.

Petro’s opening: “Well, we are in danger. Because the threat is real. It was made by Trump.”

He said he feared Trump would try to justify extraction either by linking him to Maduro or by labeling him a drug trafficker—claims he “forcefully denied.”

“He’s completely mistaken about that,” Petro said. “I live humbly off my salary, even if it is a relatively high one for Colombia.”

But the justification didn’t matter. Trump labeled Petro a “sick man” who makes cocaine. That was enough.

Petro’s plan for Wednesday night: Sleep in the presidential palace next to Simón Bolívar’s sword.

The symbolism was clear: A former guerrilla, facing potential American invasion, seeking protection from the icon of South American independence.

THE MOBILIZATION: “DEFEND ME”

Petro didn’t just prepare to defend himself. He called on Colombians to defend him.

Wednesday, he asked supporters to come to Plaza de Bolívar in Bogotá—”draped in their national flag”—to protect him and Colombia’s sovereignty.

His foreign minister stressed diplomacy but added: Colombia would not rule out a military response if the U.S. attacked.

Thousands showed up. Signs read: “Respect Colombia” and “Gringos Go Home.”

Petro’s social media storm: He said Trump’s incursion in Venezuela and threats toward Colombia and Mexico had awakened the “jaguar”—referring to anti-imperialist sentiments across Latin America.

The rally was scheduled for 6 PM Wednesday.

Petro never made it on time.

WEDNESDAY AFTERNOON: SCHUMER’S WARNING

While Petro prepared for his rally, Capitol Hill received classified briefings on Venezuela.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, CIA Director John Ratcliffe, and Attorney General Pam Bondi briefed senators.

Senator Chuck Schumer, Democratic minority leader, asked for assurances: Was the United States planning operations in other countries, including Colombia?

Schumer afterward: “I was very, very disappointed in their answer.”

Translation: The Trump administration would not rule out military action against Colombia.

At the same time, White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt held a briefing.

Reporter: Should Colombia’s President Petro expect a visit from Delta Force?

Leavitt: “That would be a very unwise question for me to answer.”

Not: “Of course not.”

Not: “Colombia is an ally.”

Instead: Refusing to rule it out.

The threat was real. Petro was right to be in danger.

THE CALL: TRUMP REACHES OUT

Wednesday evening, while traveling from Cartagena back to Bogotá, Petro learned Trump wanted to speak with him.

The call took place once Petro arrived at the presidential palace—the same moment thousands of supporters waited for him at the rally.

A U.S. official confirmed: The call lasted about an hour, unusually long for Trump and another head of state.

What was said? The conversation was off-the-record.

But Trump’s social media post afterward told the story:

“It was a Great Honor to speak with President Petro of Colombia, who called to explain the situation of drugs and other disagreements that we have had. I appreciated his call and tone.”

Notice the framing: Trump claimed Petro “called to explain.”

Actually: Trump called Petro. But the narrative became: Petro sought out Trump to apologize, explain, accommodate.

Trump continued: “I look forward to meeting with him, and a trip to the White House is now being set up!”

THE REVERSAL: FROM “SENILE” TO “GREAT HONOR”

Tuesday: Petro posted about Trump’s “senile brain.”

Wednesday morning: Petro feared extraction, planned to sleep with Bolívar’s sword.

Wednesday evening: “Well, looks like we’re going to Washington.”

The aide who recounted Petro’s words after the call: He looked “better than he had in days.”

The threat worked. One hour on the phone with Trump, and Colombia’s leftist president—who spent decades fighting American influence in Latin America—capitulated completely.

WEDNESDAY NIGHT: THE RALLY

Petro finally emerged onto the stage around 6:30 PM, delayed by Trump’s call.

Thousands chanting his name. Signs reading “Respect Colombia” and “Gringos Go Home.”

Petro informed the crowd he had just spoken with Trump. He read part of Trump’s Truth Social post aloud.

The crowd broke into applause.

“Talking is one thing, being partners is another,” Petro said. “I talk so we can have fewer deaths, fewer children being recruited into war, fewer coca plantations.”

The defiance was muted. The tone conciliatory.

A few hours later, Petro posted on X: A picture of a bald eagle and a jaguar nuzzling each other.

The jaguar—symbol of Latin American resistance—nuzzling the American eagle.

The visual said everything.

WHY PETRO CAPITULATED: THE VENEZUELA LESSON

Petro is constitutionally barred from seeking a consecutive second term. Colombia’s presidential election is in May.

Rubio, last month, dismissed Petro: “Colombia, despite its unusual president, has institutions in that country that work very closely with us...We will not allow the actions of a president whose term will end soon to damage the relationship between the United States and Colombia.”

Translation: We can work around Petro. Wait him out.

But Trump didn’t wait. He threatened. And the threat worked.

Why did Petro fold?

Because he watched what happened Saturday in Caracas.

Delta Force breached a compound defended by presidential guards, Cuban special forces, intelligence agents. They killed 80+. They extracted Maduro within hours.

Maduro’s compound was more fortified than Petro’s palace.

If Delta Force could get Maduro, they could get Petro.

And Trump made clear: The justification didn’t need to be true. “Sick man who makes cocaine” was enough.

Petro did the math:

Option 1: Continue defying Trump. Risk extraction. Spend remainder of presidency (4 months until May) fighting American occupation or in American custody.

Option 2: Capitulate. Take the phone call. Agree to White House visit. Survive until May. Let successor deal with Trump.

Petro chose survival.

THE DETERRENT EFFECT: VENEZUELA AS DEMONSTRATION

This is why Trump invaded Venezuela.

Not to liberate Venezuelans. Maduro’s capture achieved nothing for Venezuelan democracy—Trump rejected María Corina Machado, chose Maduro’s vice president instead, and announced years of U.S. control.

Not to stop drug trafficking. Venezuela isn’t a major cocaine producer. Colombia is. But Trump invaded Venezuela, threatened Colombia.

Not for oil alone. Yes, Trump is seizing Venezuelan oil. But the invasion cost billions, killed 80+, deployed 15,000 troops. There were cheaper ways to get oil.

Trump invaded Venezuela to demonstrate American power.

To send a message to every leftist government in Latin America:

Accommodate, or face Delta Force.

And it worked.

Sunday: Trump threatens Colombia.

Wednesday: Petro capitulates.

72 hours. That’s how long it took for Venezuela’s invasion to bring Colombia to heel.

WHO’S WATCHING: THE REST OF LATIN AMERICA

Nicaragua’s Daniel Ortega: Watching. His relationship with Trump is worse than Petro’s. He’s defied U.S. for decades. But he watched Maduro extracted. He watched Petro fold.

Bolivia’s Luis Arce: Watching. Leftist. Close ties to Venezuela. Now calculating: Accommodate or resist?

Cuba’s Miguel Díaz-Canel: Watching. Already facing economic collapse (14-15 hour blackouts, 28% of population fled). Venezuela was Cuba’s lifeline—oil subsidies, economic support. Now cut off. And Trump said Wednesday: “Cuba looks like it is ready to fall.”

Mexico’s Claudia Sheinbaum: Watching. Leftist. Took office October 2024. Condemned Venezuela invasion at OAS. But how long before Trump threatens Mexico? And when he does, will she fold like Petro?

Brazil’s Lula da Silva: Watching. Most powerful leftist in hemisphere. Condemned invasion. But Brazil needs U.S. economically. How much defiance can Lula afford?

Every leader in Latin America is doing the same calculation Petro did:

Resist and risk extraction? Or accommodate and survive?

Petro chose accommodation. Others are watching.

RUBIO’S DISMISSAL: “HIS TERM WILL END SOON”

Rubio’s comment last month deserves revisiting:

“We will not allow the actions of a president whose term will end soon to damage the relationship that exists between the United States and Colombia.”

This revealed Trump’s strategy:

For leaders with limited time remaining (like Petro, 4 months): Apply pressure. Force capitulation. Wait them out.

For leaders with years remaining (like Ortega, Arce): The threat is extraction, not waiting.

Petro was the test case: Could Trump intimidate a leftist president into submission in days?

Answer: Yes.

And the lesson for other leaders: If Petro—former guerrilla, defiant for years—folded in 72 hours, what chance do they have?

THE VISA PROBLEM: HOW DOES PETRO VISIT DC?

There’s one complication with Petro’s White House visit: The State Department revoked his visa last fall after he called on American soldiers to disobey Trump.

So Trump just invited a person without a U.S. visa to visit the White House.

The Times noted this “will require some finessing.”

Translation: State Department will issue special diplomatic visa, or waive requirement, or find some workaround.

Because the point isn’t legal process. The point is the photo op.

Trump wants the image: Petro, who called him “senile,” sitting across from him in the Oval Office. Accommodating. Explaining. Thanking Trump for the “great honor.”

That image—broadcast across Latin America—is worth more than any statement.

It says: Even the guerrilla fighter bends the knee.

COLOMBIA’S INSTITUTIONS: RUBIO WAS RIGHT ABOUT THIS

Rubio said U.S. works closely with Colombian institutions despite Petro.

He’s right. Tens of billions of dollars have flowed to Colombia in U.S. training and equipment over decades. The U.S. helped Colombia track down and kill Pablo Escobar in 1993.

Counternarcotics cooperation continued even as Petro criticized Trump. Colombian military, police, intelligence agencies maintain close ties with U.S. counterparts.

This is the model for Venezuela: Work around the leader. Control the institutions. Wait for the next election.

But Venezuela’s next election has no timeline. Trump said Wednesday: “Much longer” than a year of U.S. control. “Only time will tell.”

Colombia’s next election is in May. Petro’s successor—likely more conservative, less confrontational—will have to navigate the relationship Trump just redefined.

But the lesson is clear: Colombia’s institutions cooperate with the U.S. regardless of who’s president. And if the president doesn’t cooperate, Trump can threaten extraction until he does.

THE PATTERN: THREATEN, CALL, CLAIM VICTORY

Trump has now used this pattern twice in one week:

Venezuela/Delcy Rodríguez:

  • Saturday: Invade, capture Maduro

  • Sunday morning: Rodríguez calls invasion “illegitimate military aggression,” demands Maduro’s return

  • Sunday evening: Trump threatens continued blockade until “changes”

  • Sunday night: Rodríguez offers “cooperative agenda” and “peaceful coexistence”

Colombia/Gustavo Petro:

  • Sunday: Trump calls Petro “sick man,” military action “sounds good”

  • Monday-Wednesday: Petro mobilizes, fears extraction

  • Wednesday evening: Trump calls, talks for an hour

  • Afterward: Trump says Petro “called to explain,” praises “his tone,” invites to White House

The pattern:

  1. Apply overwhelming threat (military action, economic strangulation)

  2. Wait for target to capitulate (call, accommodate, explain)

  3. Frame capitulation as target “calling to explain” or “offering cooperation”

  4. Claim victory, move to next target

It works because:

  • Venezuela demonstrated Trump’s willingness to act

  • Targets know threat is credible

  • Capitulation is framed as their choice (”they called me”)

  • Trump looks strong, target looks weak

And it’s efficient: Petro folded in 72 hours. No invasion needed. Just the threat, backed by Venezuela’s example.

WHAT TRUMP ACHIEVED: HEMISPHERE-WIDE INTIMIDATION

Saturday’s Venezuela invasion wasn’t just about Venezuela.

It was about redefining America’s relationship with Latin America:

From partnership to domination.

From alliance to subordination.

From cooperation to compliance.

Trump demonstrated: No compound is too secure. No leader is safe. Accommodate or face extraction.

And then he tested it: Threatened Colombia. Waited to see if Petro would fold.

Petro folded.

Now every leftist leader in the hemisphere knows: Defying Trump isn’t courageous. It’s suicidal.

Trump didn’t need to invade Colombia. He just needed to threaten. And point at Venezuela.

That’s the strategic genius of the Venezuela operation: One demonstration, infinite deterrent effect.

Maduro extracted Saturday. Petro capitulates Wednesday. Who’s next?

Nicaragua? Bolivia? Cuba?

Or maybe no one needs to be next. Because the message is clear:

Cross Trump, and you end up like Maduro. Capitulate like Petro, and you get a White House visit.

The choice, Trump is saying, is theirs.

But watching Venezuela, watching Colombia, watching 72 hours of fear and capitulation, every leader in Latin America knows:

It’s not really a choice at all.

CONCLUSION: THE DETERRENT WORKS

Wednesday morning, Gustavo Petro told The New York Times he was in danger. The threat was real. He planned to sleep with Bolívar’s sword.

Wednesday night, he told supporters: “Looks like we’re going to Washington.”

What changed in 12 hours? A phone call. One hour with Trump. And the calculation that resisting means risking extraction.

Venezuela’s invasion wasn’t about Venezuela. It was about this moment. Petro, the former guerrilla, the leftist president, the man who called Trump “senile”—capitulating in 72 hours.

That’s the deterrent effect.

Trump invaded Venezuela to terrorize Latin America. And it worked.

Colombia got the message. Others are watching.

And Trump, having demonstrated his willingness to act and his ability to intimidate, is moving on to the next target.

Greenland. Canada. Mexico. Cuba.

The pattern accelerates.

And every leader who watched Venezuela, who watched Colombia, who saw Maduro extracted and Petro fold, is doing the same calculation:

Accommodate, or face Delta Force.

Most will choose accommodation.

Which is exactly what Trump intended.
