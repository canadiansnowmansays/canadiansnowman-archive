The Great Canadian Trade War

Part X - The USMCA Endgame

How Trump went from “100% tariffs” to “half inclined to leave” in 26 days—and why Canada is already planning for the breakup

February 20, 2026

If President Trump were to scrap the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, the results could be catastrophic for companies that have structured their business around the flow of goods among the three countries. NYT

By CanadianSnowmanSays | February 20, 2026

Remember when I wrote that Trump’s 100% tariff threat made USMCA renewal “nearly impossible”?

That was 26 days ago.

Today, the New York Times confirms what officials are saying privately: USMCA renewal is all but impossible.

US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer: “The president’s been quite clear. He’s half inclined to leave. So we’ll see how that goes.”

Canadian officials (anonymous): Expectations in Ottawa for full USMCA renewal are “very low.” Canada “questions whether it could trust any fresh trade agreements with Mr. Trump.”

In 26 days, we went from my prediction to official confirmation. Let me show you how the math proved me right—and why the path to renewal is vanishing.

The 26-Day Validation

Here’s the timeline:

January 24, 2026 (Part VIII): I wrote that Trump’s 100% tariff threat over the China deal “just made USMCA renewal virtually impossible.”

My reasoning:

  • Any Canadian concession looks like caving to threat

  • Why negotiate when Trump changes positions based on ego?

  • USMCA failure becomes politically beneficial to Carney

February 19, 2026 (Today): NYT reports what Canadian and US officials are saying privately:

Jamieson Greer: “He’s half inclined to leave... We’re not wedded to any particular agreement or format.”

Canadian officials: “Very low” expectations for renewal. “Questions whether it could trust” new agreements with Trump. Preparing for “breakup of the U.S.M.C.A.”

Timeline from threat to death sentence: 26 days.

This isn’t me being proven right. This is math being proven inevitable. Once Trump threatened 100% tariffs over a speech, the negotiating environment became toxic. Canadian officials did the same calculation I did and reached the same conclusion.

The prediction wasn’t bold. It was obvious—to anyone doing the math.

The Apology That Didn’t Work

But here’s something I missed in Part V, and the NYT just revealed:

After Doug Ford’s Reagan ad triggered Trump’s October tantrum and trade talk termination, Mark Carney apologized.

Let me say that again: The Prime Minister of Canada apologized to Donald Trump for an advertisement accurately quoting Ronald Reagan about tariffs.

And what happened after the apology?

Nothing.

Talks didn’t resume. The relationship didn’t improve. Trump still called it “terminated.”

The apology bought Canada exactly zero goodwill. And four months later, Carney won election by promising to stand up to Trump.

This is the most important revelation in the NYT piece.

It proves accommodation doesn’t work. Carney tried the soft approach. He literally apologized for someone else airing factual Reagan quotes. Trump pocketed the apology and gave nothing back.

That’s why Carney can’t—and won’t—compromise on USMCA now. He has empirical proof that giving Trump what he wants doesn’t change Trump’s behavior.

The TACO cycle broke down at Stage 2. Accommodation failed. And when accommodation fails, you can’t proceed to capitulation. You have to exit the cycle entirely.

The Davos Grudge

But there’s another piece of psychology that explains why USMCA renewal is dying: Trump is still nursing his Davos humiliation.

Remember the sequence:

  • Jan 22: Carney’s Davos speech gets standing ovation

  • Jan 23: Trump responds: “Canada lives because of the United States. Remember that, Mark, the next time you make your statements.“

That “remember that” wasn’t just posturing. It was a promise of consequences.

And here we are, 26 days later, with US officials saying Trump is “half inclined to leave” USMCA.

The NYT reveals Trump’s specific USMCA demands - dairy, liquor boards, digital tax, “full compensation.” But there’s an unstated demand: Make Carney pay for the Davos embarrassment.

Carney got a standing ovation for saying the US-led order is “over” and great powers “weaponize tariffs.” Trump got... criticized for proving Carney right.

The rational US position would be: negotiate USMCA renewal, secure economic wins, move on.

The Trump position is: punish Carney for making me look bad in Davos.

That’s not game theory. That’s ego theory. And it’s why Canadian officials are preparing for breakup—they know they’re not negotiating with someone pursuing rational objectives.

The 2018 Playbook Returns

The NYT reveals Trump’s strategy, and it’s identical to 2017-2018:

Then:

  • Greer secretly met with Mexico in early 2018

  • Negotiated bilateral deal without Canada’s knowledge

  • Told Canada: “Get on board or be left out”

  • Canada squeezed in “one minute before deadline”

Now:

  • US already meeting with Mexico (started January 2026)

  • Mexican officials report they’re “optimistic”

  • Canada being excluded from talks

  • Greer: No “natural reason” USMCA needs to be one pact

The playbook is: negotiate with Mexico, pressure Canada, force last-minute concessions.

But here’s what changed: Mark Carney.

Canada’s Prime Minister Mark Carney speaks during a news conference at Rideau Hall for after a swearing in ceremony on March 14, 2025 in Ottawa. Dave Chan/AFP via Getty Images

Trudeau caved “one minute before deadline” in 2018. That’s how USMCA got signed.

Carney was elected in April 2025 specifically by promising NOT to cave. His entire political mandate is “stand up to Trump.” The voters who gave him that mandate are the same voters who:

  • Dropped liquor imports 85%

  • Reduced US purchases 71%

  • View America negatively at 20-year highs

  • Want less US reliance at 91%

Trudeau’s political calculation in 2018: Cave at deadline, claim victory, move on.

Carney’s political calculation in 2026: Caving destroys his government. Walking away validates his China strategy.

The 2018 playbook assumes Canada will blink. But Carney can’t blink—even if he wanted to—without losing his job.

The Bridge as USMCA Leverage

Aerial view of the Gordie Howe International Bridge under construction, highlighting the cable-stayed design and connections to highways on both sides.

Now connect Part IX (the Gordie Howe Bridge threat) to today’s revelations.

NYT today: “Earlier this month, President Trump found the latest pressure point to extend his leverage over Canada: a new bridge... What excited him, officials familiar with his thinking said, was the opportunity to use the bridge to force Canada to make trade concessions.”

Read that again. Trump didn’t care about the bridge. He cared about leverage for USMCA.

The bridge threat wasn’t random. It was calculated USMCA pressure.

Here’s Trump’s list of USMCA demands (per NYT):

  • Dairy market access (longstanding grievance)

  • Provincial liquor boards restocking US alcohol

  • Digital services tax reversal

  • “Full compensation” for... something

The bridge threat (Feb 9) came one week before today’s NYT story (Feb 19) confirming USMCA talks are failing.

The progression:

  1. Bridge threat for “compensation” (Feb 9)

  2. House votes against Canada tariffs anyway (Feb 11)

  3. Officials confirm USMCA nearly dead (Feb 19)

Even Trump’s own party voted against his leverage play. The bridge threat failed to move Canada. And now officials are leaking that the whole agreement is doomed.

That’s not successful negotiation. That’s failing to secure the cooperation needed for renewal—and accelerating Canada’s exit strategy instead.

What Changed (And What Didn’t)

Let me show you what’s different from 2018—and what’s exactly the same.

What’s the same:

  • ✓ Greer running the strategy

  • ✓ Divide-and-conquer with Mexico

  • ✓ Last-minute deadline pressure

  • ✓ Demands for Canadian concessions

What’s different:

20182026Trudeau needed a winCarney needs to stand firmNo alternative markets readyChina deal signed and operationalCanadian public wanted deal91% want less US reliancePre-tariff relationshipPost-85% boycott relationshipTrump’s first term credibilityArt of No Deal track recordNo apology historyCarney apologized, got nothing

The fundamentals flipped. In 2018, Canada was negotiating from weakness and needed the deal more than Trump did. In 2026, Carney is negotiating from a position where USMCA failure helps him politically.

The Trust Problem

Here’s the quote that matters most:

Canadian officials: Canada “questions whether it could trust any fresh trade agreements with Mr. Trump.”

This isn’t rhetoric. This is game theory.

Why would Canada agree to new terms when:

  • Trump praised China deal (Jan 16) → threatened 100% tariffs over it (Jan 24)

  • Trump endorsed bridge (2017) → threatened to block it (Feb 9)

  • Trump signed USMCA (2020) → imposed tariffs anyway despite it (2025)

  • Carney apologized (October) → relationship didn’t improve

Every time Canada has accommodated Trump, he’s escalated anyway.

From a pure game theory perspective, the rational Canadian strategy is: assume Trump will violate any new agreement, plan accordingly.

And that’s exactly what they’re doing. The NYT reports Canada is “preparing for long, bumpy and dramatic talks with a hostile U.S. administration, and even for a breakup of the U.S.M.C.A.“

Not “preparing for tough negotiations.” Preparing for breakup.

That’s not pessimism. That’s realism based on observed behavior.

Updated Scenarios: The Math Gets Worse

Back in Part VIII, I gave three scenarios. Let’s update them with new information.

Scenario 1: The Grand Bargain (20%, down from 35%)

What it requires:

  • Trump moderates demands

  • Canada makes strategic concessions

  • 16-year extension signed by July 1

Why probability dropped:

  • “Half inclined to leave” means no appetite for compromise

  • Carney apology precedent: accommodation doesn’t work

  • Canadian officials already planning for breakup

  • House GOP breaking ranks shows weak political support

What would need to change: Trump’s entire approach to negotiation

Verdict: Increasingly unlikely

Scenario 2: The Bilateral Endgame (65%, up from 50%)

What it looks like:

  • US-Mexico bilateral deal announced

  • Canada offered separate deal with worse terms

  • USMCA enters 10-year sunset (annual review until 2036)

  • Gradual North American integration unraveling

Why probability increased:

  • Greer explicitly floated bilateral option: “No natural reason” for one pact

  • Mexico already in talks, “optimistic”

  • Canada being excluded (2018 playbook)

  • This gives Trump “win” with Mexico, blame Canada for failure

What this means:

  • Different rules for Mexico vs Canada

  • Higher tariffs on Canadian goods

  • Accelerated diversification to China/Europe

  • Doug Ford’s electricity leverage goes live

Verdict: Most likely outcome

Scenario 3: The Full Break (15%, unchanged)

What it looks like:

  • No agreement by July 1, 2026

  • USMCA terminates immediately or begins countdown

  • Return to WTO rules (MFN tariffs)

  • Complete North American trade rupture

Why probability stayed same:

  • Business pressure will prevent immediate termination

  • Courts could block unilateral withdrawal

  • Economic damage too severe for both sides

  • Even Trump’s chaos has limits

But note: The gap between Scenario 2 and 3 is narrowing. A bilateral deal that excludes Canada is functionally similar to termination for Canadian purposes.

Verdict: Still possible, not probable

Why Canada Can’t Blink

Even if Carney wanted to make major concessions (he doesn’t), the political math won’t allow it.

The mandate:

  • Won election by standing up to Trump

  • Voters already boycotting at 85% (liquor)

  • 91% want less US reliance

  • China deal popular domestically

The precedent:

  • Apologized after Reagan ad → got nothing

  • Briefed US on China deal → got 100% threat

  • Complied with USMCA → got tariffs anyway

The alternative:

  • China deal operational (canola access, EV market)

  • India trip planned (more diversification)

  • Europe relationships strengthening

  • Domestic “Buy Canadian” momentum

The calculation: If Carney caves on dairy, liquor, digital tax = looks weak, loses government

If USMCA fails because Trump is unreasonable = “See? This is why we needed China”

Trump made USMCA failure the politically safer option for Carney.

That’s not Canadian stubbornness. That’s Trump creating an environment where accommodation costs more than resistance.

The Greer Factor

Jamieson Greer, U.S. Trade Representative, testifies during his Senate Finance Committee hearing.

One final observation: Jamieson Greer negotiated USMCA in 2018 as Lighthizer’s chief of staff. He knows the agreement intimately. He helped pass it through Congress.

And now he’s the one saying Trump is “half inclined to leave” it.

NYT: “Mr. Greer last summer began urging a more disruptive approach to U.S.M.C.A. negotiations, saying its current form wasn’t working for the United States.”

Translation: The guy who negotiated it thinks it failed.

But look at the actual trade data (from NYT):

  • Trade deficit with Canada: $62B (2024) → $46.4B (2025)

  • That’s a $15.6B improvement

The deficit shrank because both imports and exports fell—the US bought less from Canada AND sold less to Canada. That’s mutual economic harm, though Greer appears to measure success solely by deficit reduction regardless of trade volume.

This is the mentality negotiating for the US: measure success by deficit reduction, not trade growth. Which means the goal isn’t a better deal—it’s less trade.

The Bottom Line

January 24: Trump threatened 100% tariffs over a speech. I said it made USMCA renewal “nearly impossible.”

February 19: Officials confirm USMCA renewal IS nearly impossible. “Half inclined to leave.” “Very low” expectations. “Breakup.”

Timeline: 26 days from prediction to confirmation.

The math was always obvious:

  • Trump creates crisis over ego (100% threat, bridge threat)

  • Canada can’t trust promises (apology didn’t work)

  • Accommodation costs more than resistance (political mandate)

  • Alternatives exist (China, India, Europe)

  • Trump’s own party voting against him (House vote)

Greer’s strategy: Divide Canada and Mexico, force last-minute cave

Why it won’t work: Carney was elected NOT to cave, and has alternatives Trump didn’t account for

Most likely outcome: Bilateral deals, with Canada getting worse terms than Mexico, triggering exactly the diversification Trump claimed to oppose

USMCA isn’t dead yet. The review is still months away. Business pressure will mount. Last-minute deals might emerge.

But the path to renewal is vanishing. Trust is dead. The idea that Canada and the US are “partners” rather than adversaries is dead.

You can’t threaten 100% tariffs over a speech, block a bridge you endorsed, let nuclear treaties expire, and expect your neighbor to trust you with their economic future.

Canada is preparing for the breakup. Mexico is optimistic about bilateral terms. And Trump is “half inclined to leave” an agreement he spent his first term bragging about.

The Art of No Deal isn’t just a pattern anymore. It’s the official US negotiating position.

And Canada’s response?

Go TACO yourself, Donald. We’ve got alternatives now.
