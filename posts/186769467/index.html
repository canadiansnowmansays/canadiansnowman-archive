The Epstein Files

Three Million Pages of Strategic Incompetence

Part 20 of The Epstein Files: When “Transparency” Becomes the Ultimate Accommodation

February 3, 2026

A document that was included in the U.S. Department of Justice release of the Jeffrey Epstein files shows Epstein's Florida sexual predator/offender registration form from July 2018. (Jon Elswick/AP)

February 3, 2026

Put your hand up if you’re tired of hearing about the Epstein files.

Even President Trump says it’s a waste of time. “Frankly, the DOJ, I think, should just say we have other things to do,” he told reporters on Monday, February 2.

And you know what? He might be right.

It IS becoming a waste of time—but not for the reasons he claims. It’s a waste of time because the Justice Department has spent a year finding ways to impede the investigation, hide facts, expose victims while protecting co-conspirators, and ensure that the end result reveals exactly what Trump could call a “nothing burger.”

The obstruction IS the point. Making it a waste of time IS the strategy. Creating so much confusion, so many “technical errors,” so many millions of pages with strategic redactions that the public becomes exhausted and stops paying attention—that’s the goal.

And when the President says the DOJ should “just say we have other things to do,” he’s not complaining about the investigation. He’s describing what they’ve already been doing for a year. He’s saying the quiet part out loud: mission accomplished, time to declare victory and move on.

But wait a minute.

In the twenty years since Jeffrey Epstein’s crimes first came to light, in the seventeen years since his sweetheart plea deal, in the six years since his death in federal custody, exactly two people have been held accountable: Epstein himself and Ghislaine Maxwell.

Not the co-conspirators named in the 2007 draft federal indictment—their names remain redacted. Not the “three others who were his personal assistants and were suspected of participating in a conspiracy to recruit underage girls,” according to that same indictment. Not the wealthy and powerful men who visited his homes, flew on his planes, and maintained friendships with him for years—sometimes decades—after his 2008 conviction for soliciting a minor for prostitution.

Two people. In twenty years.

So no, this isn’t a waste of time. Two people held accountable while everyone else walks free—that’s the entire problem. That’s exactly why we can’t stop now. That’s why Trump wants us to believe it’s pointless.

And on January 30, 2026, when Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche released what he called “more than 3 million pages of documents” and declared “this review is over,” we got to see exactly how the Justice Department ensures that only two people are ever held accountable.

The answer is strategic incompetence so consistent, so patterned, so precisely calibrated to protect power while exposing victims, that calling it incompetence insults the concept of actual mistakes.

The Numbers That Tell the Story

Documents that were included in a previous Justice Department release of the Jeffrey Epstein files, seen in early January. (Jon Elswick/AP)

The Justice Department reviewed more than 6 million potentially responsive files. It released 3.5 million pages, along with 2,000 videos and 180,000 images.

That means 2.5 million pages remain withheld.

The Epstein Files Transparency Act—passed by Congress with overwhelming support and signed into law by Trump himself—required the release of substantially all Epstein-related documents. It allowed redactions to protect victims’ identities but explicitly stated that “no document should be withheld or redacted on the basis of embarrassment, reputational harm, or political sensitivity.”

The law also allowed withholding of classified materials and documents that could compromise ongoing criminal investigations. But Blanche’s Friday press conference made clear there are no ongoing criminal investigations stemming from the Epstein files.

“There was nothing in there that allowed us to prosecute anybody,” Blanche told CNN on Sunday.

So what’s in the 2.5 million pages still being withheld?

The Justice Department hasn’t said. But a clue lies in what was released—and how it was released.

When “Technical Errors” Only Go One Way

Within 48 hours of the January 30 release, lawyers for Epstein’s victims filed an emergency motion with the court describing catastrophic failures in redacting victim-identifying information.

The exposed materials included:

  • Nude photographs showing victims’ faces that should have been completely redacted

  • Names, email addresses, and other identifying information either unredacted or insufficiently obscured

  • Banking information for at least one victim that resulted in death threats

  • An organizational chart created by federal investigators that clearly showed one alleged underage victim’s face

Eight women submitted statements to the court. One wrote that the release was “life threatening.” Another said she’d received death threats after 51 entries included her private banking information, forcing her to shut down credit cards and accounts.

Lawyers Brittany Henderson and Brad Edwards wrote that the scale of the failures suggested something beyond mere incompetence: “There is no conceivable degree of institutional incompetence sufficient to explain the scale, consistency, and persistence of the failures that occurred.”

The Justice Department blamed “technical or human error” and quickly began pulling down flagged documents. By Monday, February 2, the department had withdrawn “several thousand documents and media” for review and re-redaction.

But here’s what makes the pattern unmistakable: While victims’ faces appeared in nude photos, while their names and banking information were exposed, while their identities were revealed in organizational charts—the names of co-conspirators in the draft 2007 federal indictment remained heavily redacted.

Representative Thomas Massie, one of the architects of the Epstein Files Transparency Act, put it bluntly: “They, through incompetence, failed to redact the victims’ names, while at the same time intentionally withholding documents and redacting documents that contain names of people that should probably be investigated.”

The draft indictment from 2007—one of the most anticipated documents in the release—had described allegations of sex crimes federal prosecutors compiled before Epstein reached his sweetheart plea deal. It identified not just Epstein but “three others who were his personal assistants and were suspected of participating in a conspiracy to recruit underage girls.”

Those names are redacted. But the victims’ banking information was left exposed.

The Casualties Who Weren’t Prosecuted

The 3 million pages released January 30 included devastating revelations about numerous prominent figures who maintained relationships with Epstein—some before his 2008 conviction, many after.

Casey Wasserman, chairman of the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics, had to apologize for “flirtatious” emails with Ghislaine Maxwell from 2002. While he denied any direct relationship with Epstein, the emails were suggestive enough to overshadow the Winter Olympics opening ceremonies in Milan, where Wasserman was scheduled to promote the L.A. Games.

Peter Attia, a wellness influencer with a popular podcast and best-selling book, appeared in the files more than 1,700 times. His emails with Epstein between 2015-2018 included the memorable line about “pussy” being “low carb” and references to “JE withdrawal” when they didn’t see each other. Epstein wrote about women not needing to live past reproductive age. CBS dropped Attia as a contributor after the revelations.

Peter Mandelson, former UK ambassador to the United States under previous Labour governments, resigned from the Labour Party, quit the House of Lords, and now faces a police investigation. The files showed $75,000 in payments from Epstein in 2003-2004, continued friendship after Epstein’s 2008 conviction, and evidence that Mandelson may have shared market-sensitive government information about the 2008 financial crisis.

Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor (formerly Prince Andrew) appeared in photographs kneeling over an unidentified woman lying on the floor. Emails between “The Duke” and Epstein included an invitation to dine at Buckingham Palace “and lots of privacy” and Epstein offering to introduce him to a “26, russian, clevere beautiful” woman. Andrew’s ex-wife Sarah Ferguson wrote warm emails addressing Epstein as “My dear spectacular and special friend Jeffrey.”

Elon Musk emailed Epstein in 2012 asking “What day/night will be the wildest party on our island?” and inquired again in 2013 about visiting. Musk has maintained he repeatedly refused Epstein’s invitations, posting on X that “Epstein tried to get me to go to his island and I REFUSED.”

Howard Lutnick, now Trump’s Commerce Secretary, received an invitation from Epstein to his private island in December 2012. Lutnick’s wife accepted enthusiastically and said they would arrive on a yacht with their children. They also had drinks together in 2011.

Steve Tisch, New York Giants co-owner, appeared at least 440 times in the documents. Emails showed Epstein connecting Tisch with women, with crude discussions of physical features. The NFL announced it would investigate.

Steve Bannon, former Trump adviser, exchanged hundreds of texts with Epstein in 2019, discussed politics and travel, and in March 2019 asked if Epstein could supply his plane to pick him up in Rome.

The files revealed communications between Epstein and numerous other prominent figures across politics, business, entertainment, and academia. In every case documented, the Justice Department’s conclusion was the same: no evidence sufficient to prosecute.

“There’s a lot of correspondence. There’s a lot of emails. There’s a lot of photographs. There’s a lot of horrible photographs that appear to be taken by Mr. Epstein or people around him,” Blanche said Sunday on CNN. “But that doesn’t allow us necessarily to prosecute somebody.”

Which raises the question: What would allow them to prosecute somebody? And why are the names of people suspected of recruiting underage girls still redacted in the 2007 draft indictment?

The Trump Material No One Will Investigate

The New York Times, using a proprietary search tool, identified more than 5,300 files containing over 38,000 references to President Trump, his wife, his Mar-a-Lago club, and related terms.

Many references were news articles and publicly available materials. But the files also included:

Victim testimony placing Trump at the scene: Handwritten notes from a September 2019 interview with a victim (name redacted) who recalled being transported to Mar-a-Lago to meet Trump. The notes quote Epstein saying to Trump: “This is a good one, huh?” The notes don’t suggest misconduct by Trump.

FBI tips alleging sexual abuse: A spreadsheet created in August 2025 summarizing calls to the FBI’s National Threat Operation Center included “uncorroborated stories” and “somewhat fantastical scenarios” about Trump. The spreadsheet doesn’t include corroborating information, and The Times chose not to describe the details of unverified claims.

2002 email from “Melania”: A woman named Melania wrote a warm email to Ghislaine Maxwell shortly after New York Magazine published a profile of Epstein that included Trump’s now-famous quote calling him a “terrific guy” who “likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side.” The email praised the article and Maxwell’s photo. It’s unclear if the sender is the future first lady.

Juan Alessi testimony: Epstein’s former employee told investigators that Trump—along with other well-known individuals—had visited Epstein’s home.

Epstein’s 2011 consideration of contacting Trump: An email to a private investigator shows Epstein contemplating whether to contact Trump about Virginia Giuffre, who had been trafficked from Mar-a-Lago. It’s unclear if Epstein tried to reach Trump.

When Blanche was asked on CNN about Trump’s appearance in the files, he emphasized that there were “a ton of people” named besides Trump and that the FBI had fielded “hundreds of calls” about prominent individuals where allegations were “quickly determined to not be credible.”

A White House spokesman declined to comment on specific documents and referred to Trump’s Saturday claim that the files “absolved me” of wrongdoing.

But the files don’t absolve anyone. They document years of associations, parties, flights, and business dealings between Epstein and numerous powerful men. What they don’t include—because Blanche says it doesn’t exist—is evidence sufficient to prosecute anyone else beyond Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell.

Which means either:

  1. Epstein operated his trafficking network for decades essentially alone except for Maxwell, or

  2. The bar for “sufficient evidence to prosecute” is set impossibly high, or

  3. The documents that might provide that evidence are in the 2.5 million pages still being withheld

What Congress Is Finally Forcing

The Clintons spent months resisting the House Oversight Committee’s subpoenas for depositions. They argued the subpoenas were “legally invalid,” submitted sworn statements instead, and twice rescheduled their appearances.

On January 27, the Oversight Committee voted to hold both Bill and Hillary Clinton in contempt of Congress in a bipartisan vote.

On February 2, facing a full House contempt vote, the Clintons agreed to depositions. But even then, clarity remained elusive. Chairman James Comer gave them until noon Tuesday, February 3, to sign an agreement confirming they would be deposed before the end of February under the committee’s parameters: transcribed, filmed, no time limits, no restrictions on topics.

The committee also scheduled Ghislaine Maxwell for a deposition on February 9—a remarkable development given that she’s currently serving a 20-year prison sentence for sex trafficking.

Representatives Massie and Khanna, meanwhile, have demanded meetings with Blanche about documents they believe were improperly withheld or redacted. Both have stated publicly that victims have told them their statements to law enforcement have not been released.

“They, through incompetence, failed to redact the victims’ names,” Massie said, “while at the same time intentionally withholding documents and redacting documents that contain names of people that should probably be investigated.”

Representative Robert Garcia, the top Democrat on the House Oversight Committee, put it more directly: “They are in violation of the law. We are demanding the names of Epstein’s co-conspirators and the men and pedophiles who abused women and girls.”

Blanche told ABC’s “This Week” on Sunday that “our doors are open if they want to come and review any of the materials that we produced.”

“We have nothing to hide,” he said.

But 2.5 million pages remain hidden. And the President says the Justice Department should “just say we have other things to do.”

The Pattern Complete

Parts 14 through 19 of this series documented the accommodation psychology that characterized the Justice Department’s handling of the Epstein files from February 2025 through January 2026:

  • Information manipulation: Non-functional search tools, deleted files, improper redactions

  • Strategic investigations as shields: Trump-ordered Clinton probe while withholding files

  • Weaponizing DOJ: Investigating opponents, surveilling journalists

  • Threatening Congress: Exploiting lack of enforcement mechanisms

  • Strategic retreat: Partial compliance, delete-restore cycles

  • Running out the clock: Rolling releases, missed deadlines

The January 30 release represents accommodation’s final form: declare transparency while protecting power.

The Justice Department released 3.5 million pages—enough to create the appearance of disclosure, enough to generate weeks of media coverage about prominent men’s associations with Epstein, enough to claim compliance with the law.

But it withheld 2.5 million more pages. It redacted co-conspirators’ names from the draft 2007 indictment. It declared no evidence exists to prosecute anyone else. And it blamed “technical errors” for exposing victims while protecting the powerful.

When Henderson and Edwards wrote that “there is no conceivable degree of institutional incompetence sufficient to explain” the pattern of failures, they were documenting what this series has shown for months: This isn’t incompetence. This is strategy.

Expose victims through “errors” while redacting co-conspirators through “law.” Release millions of pages of emails and photos showing associations while withholding documents about recruitment and conspiracy. Declare the review “over” while Congress demands answers. Have the President publicly wish it would all just go away.

The Test That Continues

Lawrence O’Donnell compared this moment to Watergate on his MSNBC program in December. The analogy holds.

Nixon could fire Archibald Cox. He could refuse to turn over tapes. He could claim executive privilege and national security. But he couldn’t erase the documentary record. And when that record finally came out, the lies collapsed.

Trump can order investigations of Bill Clinton. He can declare himself “absolved.” He can have his Deputy Attorney General announce “this review is over.” He can say the Justice Department should “just say we have other things to do.”

But he cannot erase the documentary record.

The prosecutor’s 2020 email confirming Trump flew on Epstein’s plane eight times still exists. The New York Times investigation showing Epstein recruited from Mar-a-Lago still exists. The flight logs still exist. Virginia Giuffre’s employment records still exist. The victim’s handwritten notes about being brought to Mar-a-Lago for Trump still exist. The draft 2007 indictment naming co-conspirators still exists—even if those names remain redacted.

Trump’s own Christmas 2025 social media posts demanding selective release to “embarrass” Democrats still exist. His admission aboard Air Force One that spa employees “were taken to work for Epstein” still exists.

And 2.5 million pages of additional Epstein files exist somewhere in Justice Department storage.

Congress reconvenes this week with new tools. The Clintons will be deposed. Maxwell will be deposed. Massie and Khanna are demanding access to withheld files. Garcia has made clear the Oversight Committee considers DOJ in violation of law.

Survivors continue to speak. Eight women submitted statements to the court about being re-traumatized by DOJ’s “errors.” Their lawyers are demanding “immediate judicial intervention.”

The files matter because they document not just Epstein’s crimes but the systems that enabled them. The accommodations by law enforcement. The protections extended to wealth and power. The recruitment pipelines at clubs like Mar-a-Lago. The warnings ignored. The victims dismissed.

Blanche says “this review is over.” Trump says DOJ should “just say we have other things to do.”

But the documentary record cannot be erased. The survivors will not stop. Congress has enforcement mechanisms it hasn’t yet used. And the 2.5 million pages still exist.

The test continues.
