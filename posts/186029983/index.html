The Iran Crisis

The Massacre

The Largest Mass Killing of Protesters in Modern History

January 28, 2026

Part 1 of 2 January 28, 2026

On January 8, 2026, as Iranian protesters gathered in response to exiled Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi’s call for nationwide demonstrations at 8 p.m., the Islamic Republic made its decision.

At approximately 6 p.m., two hours before the scheduled protests, Iran shut down the internet. Mobile service went dead. International phone lines stopped working. Within minutes, 90 million people disappeared from the global communications network in what would become the longest and most complete digital blackout in Iran’s history.

What happened next, in the darkness behind that curtain, may be the bloodiest massacre in modern history.

The Numbers: A Regime Counting Its Own Victims

More than 36,500 Iranians were killed by security forces during the January 8-9 crackdown, according to classified documents obtained by Iran International.

Iran International’s Editorial Board reported on January 25 that it had reviewed newly obtained classified documents from Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) Intelligence Organization, field reports from hospitals, and accounts from medical staff, witnesses, and victims’ families.

The death toll progression in these classified government reports reveals a regime struggling to count its own atrocity:

January 11 (two days after the massacre): IRGC Intelligence Organization submitted a report to the Supreme National Security Council and Presidential Office documenting at least 12,000 deaths.

January 20: Sources within Iran’s Interior Ministry told Iran International that consolidation of figures from provincial security councils showed the death toll had exceeded 30,000.

January 21: A report presented to Iran’s parliament’s National Security and Foreign Policy Committee, seen by Iran International, listed at least 27,500 killed.

January 22: An IRGC Intelligence Organization report to the Supreme National Security Council listed more than 33,000 killed.

January 24: The most recent IRGC Intelligence Organization report listed more than 36,500 killed.

Iran’s government has publicly acknowledged only 3,117 deaths. That figure, announced January 21, split casualties into 2,427 “civilians and security forces” and 690 labeled “terrorists”—a designation used to criminalize protesters.

According to Iran International, even security agencies themselves do not yet know the precise final death toll due to “deliberate concealment” and “intentional disorder in the registration and transfer of bodies,” along with pressure on families and quiet burials of victims.

Other sources paint a similarly horrific picture. The U.S.-based Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA)—which verifies each death through activists inside Iran cross-checking public records and witness statements—has documented 6,159 deaths as of January 27, with verification ongoing for another 9,387 suspected fatalities. More than 42,200 people have been arrested.

UN Special Rapporteur on Human Rights in Iran Mai Sato said this week that civilian deaths were estimated at 5,000 or more, adding that medical reports from doctors inside Iran suggest the toll “could be far higher, at about 20,000 or more.”

Interior Ministry reports reviewed by Iran International show security forces confronted demonstrators in more than 400 cities and towns, with more than 4,000 clash locations reported nationwide.

Conservative assessments by medical sources, based on bodies delivered to hospitals, estimate:

  • More than 2,500 killed in Rasht

  • At least 1,800 in Mashhad

  • More than 2,000 in Isfahan, Najafabad, and Khorasgan

  • At least 3,000 in Karaj, Shahriar, and Andisheh

  • 700 in Kermanshah

  • 400 in Gorgan

No clear aggregate figure has been obtained for Tehran. However, images from Kahrizak morgue and hospitals across the capital indicate thousands were killed, with a significant proportion of deaths occurring in southern Tehran.

If the IRGC’s own classified documents are accurate, what occurred on January 8-9, 2026 constitutes the largest mass killing of protesters in a 48-hour period in modern history.

The New York Times Confirms the Massacre

A visual investigation published by The New York Times on January 27 confirmed the scale and brutality of the crackdown through verified videos, photographs, and witness testimony.

The Times learned from Iranian officials familiar with security matters that on January 9, Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei ordered the Supreme National Security Council to crush the protests “by any means necessary.”

The Times obtained photographs of more than 300 bodies taken to Tehran’s main morgue. A majority of the bodies showed severe head trauma and collapsed eye sockets.

Security forces opened fire on protesters in at least 19 cities, according to videos verified by The Times. Hospitals were overwhelmed by protesters with gunshot and pellet wounds to the torso, head and eyes—an injury pattern consistent with lethal force, not crowd control.

One doctor told The Times that injuries had changed from “limb fractures and tear gas inhalation” to “skull fractures and gunshot wounds.”

Within four days, the protests had been crushed.

Rasht: “Massacred Before My Eyes”

The Caspian Sea city of Rasht, 200 miles northwest of Tehran, experienced some of the worst violence. The Washington Post reconstructed what happened there through witness testimonies, videos, and medical records—a rare detailed account of how Iran’s security forces operated across dozens of similar cities that night.

By January 7, merchants in Rasht’s historic bazaar had joined the protests by going on strike. Intelligence officers called shop owners, pressuring them to reopen. When merchants refused, members of the Basij paramilitary force and IRGC threatened them. The merchants would “badly regret” their defiance, security forces warned.

Protests in the Rasht Bazaar on Jan. 7 showing merchants in the bazaar striking in solidarity with the ongoing demonstrations. (Video still: Etelaf10/X)

On the evening of January 8, hundreds of protesters marched toward the bazaar, their numbers swelling as they moved through the city. “There was a flood of people coming from every street that connected to the main road,” one participant recalled. “The crowd was so massive that at every street or alley we passed, more people kept joining us.”

Security forces initially tried tear gas. The crowd kept coming.

Then they opened fire.

“The crowd approached the bazaar, security forces initially tried to disperse people with tear gas but the demonstrators were determined to keep going,” one protester recounted in testimony provided to the Abdorrahman Boroumand Center, a human rights organization. “That’s when security forces began shooting directly at the crowd. People fell to the ground.”

Around 9 p.m., fire erupted in the bazaar—the exact cause remains unclear, though witnesses reported strong winds that night. Some protesters had taken refuge from gunfire in the market’s maze of alleys. Now they faced a choice: stay and burn, or flee into the guns waiting outside.

They fled.

“Security forces were shooting with Kalashnikovs into the bazaar,” said Saman, a witness who participated in the protests. “It was terrifying. They killed so many people, even people escaping.”

Another protester described the scene: “Forced by the fire to flee the bazaar, protesters emerged into the direct sights of the armed security forces, some riding motorcycles. My contemporaries were massacred before my eyes.”

Security forces blocked fire trucks from responding until after 1 a.m., witnesses said—four hours after the blaze began. By morning, Shariati Street was covered in blood and littered with debris.

Data collected by medical staff at two hospitals in Rasht showed more than 80 fatalities during the two days of peak violence. HRANA has documented 392 deaths in Rasht alone. Iran International’s medical sources estimate more than 2,500 killed in the city.

The Weapons: Live Fire and Chemical Agents

Iranian security forces in hazmat suits, Sabzevar, January 2026. Witnesses reported chemical weapons deployment causing protesters to collapse before they could flee

Videos and witness accounts reveal the evolution of force used by Iranian security forces—and raise disturbing questions about the deployment of chemical weapons.

On January 8, riot police carried shotguns and pellet guns. By January 10, they were armed with AK-47 assault rifles and heavy machine guns.

Security forces fired from rooftops. They shot birdshot into crowds—corpses showed wounds consistent with metal pellets, and scores suffered blinding eye wounds. Witnesses described plainclothes officers on motorcycles, Basij volunteers with long guns and batons, anti-riot police in helmets and body armor carrying submachine guns.

But witnesses in multiple cities told Iran International they were exposed to chemical substances that went far beyond conventional tear gas.

“What was fired was not tear gas,” one protester said. “People collapsed.”

According to eyewitness accounts reviewed by Iran International, the substances caused intense burning of eyes, skin and lungs, along with acute respiratory distress, repeated coughing, dizziness, loss of balance and, in some cases, vomiting or coughing up blood.

Witnesses said the severity and persistence of symptoms differed dramatically from past experiences with tear gas. In some cases, people who had been mobile seconds earlier suddenly lost the ability to walk or run and fell to the ground.

“Seconds later, the street was full of smoke and vapor,” witnesses from Isfahan said, describing the smell as resembling ammonia, drain cleaner and, in some areas, mustard.

Video footage from Sabzevar in northeastern Iran, reviewed by Iran International, showed security personnel wearing specialized protective clothing and masks designed for hazardous chemical materials, positioned on military-style vehicles in city streets. Warning symbols associated with hazardous substances were visible on the vehicles.

Alan Fotouhi, a physician and professor of clinical pharmacology based in Sweden, told Iran International that symptoms described by witnesses did not match those typically associated with standard tear gas. The pattern of symptoms, severity of harm and persistence of effects differed from what is normally observed with conventional tear gas exposure, he said.

Fotouhi said the reported effects could result from a combination of high-dose tear gas and other highly irritating chemical substances, but identifying the exact materials would require laboratory analysis.

Iran is a signatory to the Chemical Weapons Convention, which restricts the use of chemical agents against civilians.

Hospital Executions: “Finishing Shots”

Iran International received reports and evidence indicating the extrajudicial execution of detainees in Tehran and other cities—including the systematic killing of wounded protesters while they were hospitalized and receiving medical treatment.

Images released from morgues, according to Iran International, leave little doubt that some wounded citizens were shot in the head while hospitalized. The outlet noted that had these individuals sustained fatal head wounds on the streets, there would have been no reason to admit them to hospital or begin treatment.

The images show that in some cases, medical tubes and patient-monitoring equipment remained attached to bodies. In other cases, cardiac monitoring electrodes are visible on the chest, suggesting these individuals were under medical care before being shot in the head.

Three doctors and four nurses in Tehran who spoke to Iran International said security forces entered hospitals and took away wounded patients who were undergoing treatment.

A number of doctors and nurses told Iran International that so-called “finishing shots” were fired at wounded patients.

Two nurses described to Iran International how, after a wounded young man was transferred to an ambulance in a clash area in western Tehran, a security agent suddenly entered the vehicle and, in front of them, killed him by firing two consecutive shots. The nurses said the man had been severely beaten before being moved and was semi-conscious. A trusted specialist physician at a Tehran hospital confirmed their account.

Amnesty International’s report on January 23 documented that security forces removed wounded protesters from hospitals in several provinces and threatened medical staff with prosecution for treating injured demonstrators without notifying authorities.

Behesht-e Zahra: The Cemetery of Horrors

This satellite photo shows Iran paving over Lot 41, where some of those executed in the chaos after Iran’s 1979 Islamic Revolution were buried, at Behesht-e Zahra cemetery outside of Tehran on Aug. 18, 2025. (Planet Labs PBC / Associated Press)

The families came to Behesht-e Zahra, Tehran’s largest cemetery, to bury their dead.

Instead, they found themselves searching through piles of corpses.

Kiarash—not his real name—witnessed the scene on January 10 when he joined his brother to bury a family friend, a 41-year-old mother of two who had been shot in the neck at a protest the night before.

At the cemetery’s reception area, mourners received a number matching the tag on their loved one’s body. Then they made their way toward the mortuary, where workers perform Muslim burial rites.

But as families waited outside, they noticed something: piles of bodies stacked in nearby warehouses.

“It was rows upon rows” of corpses, Kiarash told The New York Times.

Panic spread as people broke past security forces to search the piles, frantically looking for their relatives among the dead. Then refrigerated trucks arrived—40-foot-long vehicles more commonly used for commercial transport—and began unloading more corpses.

“The body bags landed with sickening thuds in front of onlookers who had come to bury their children, siblings, fathers, mothers,” The Times reported.

“That moment — it broke people,” Kiarash said. “People couldn’t just watch them throwing the bodies out like that. There was a mother lying on her child’s body, begging for help so they wouldn’t throw him somewhere.”

The crowds surged into the mortuary hallway, cursing Ayatollah Khamenei—a criminal offense in Iran—as security forces looked on.

“Mothers were crying, shouting,” Kiarash said. “And all the people were shouting things like, ‘Death to Khamenei.’”

What unnerved Kiarash most were the small body bags—children, he guessed, no more than 10 or 12 years old—being carelessly thrown onto piles of adult corpses.

“Throwing bodies on top of each other, throwing the children in small bags — they’d get crushed beneath them,” he said. “Every time I say this, my heart breaks.”

Even young conscripts assigned to control the crowds appeared stunned. “Some of them were crying,” Kiarash said.

Sources told Iran International on January 22 that hospitals and morgues are facing shortages of body bags, forcing authorities to store bodies in corridors and other areas.

In Mashhad, Iran’s second-largest city, families were forced to pay up to $6,000 to retrieve bodies—sums impossible for most Iranians to afford. “These people cannot pick up their loved ones’ bodies because they can’t pay that kind of money,” said Hadi Ghaemi, director of the Center for Human Rights in Iran. “So the bodies are just being dumped and buried in mass graves.”

The practice of charging families for bodies has precedent. During the brutal crackdown on opponents in the 1980s, authorities required payment to return the bodies of those executed—a practice known as the “bullet price.”

Iran’s Supreme National Security Council instructed newspaper editors and online media managers to stop publishing independent reporting on protest deaths and to avoid interviewing bereaved families, according to information shared with Iran International.

Three Days Among the Dead

Perhaps the most haunting testimony came from a teenage protester who survived by hiding among corpses.

The Iran Human Rights Documentation Center reported on January 22 that a wounded Iranian protester, under 18 years old, played dead inside a plastic body bag for three days at the Kahrizak Forensic Center south of Tehran to hide from security forces.

The youth was in critical condition after suffering gunshot wounds.

“During the three days he was held among the bodies transferred to Kahrizak, he heard the ringing of cell phones among the corpses and smelled the intense stench of decay,” the IHRDC said, citing the teenager’s account.

The young man described hearing gunfire after sounds from wounded detainees, suggesting they were summarily executed.

“The witness reported that whenever groans from the wounded were heard, they were shortly followed by the sound of gunfire and the cessation of the groaning, strongly indicating that security forces delivered fatal shots to wounded individuals who were still alive,” IHRDC said.

The teenager stayed motionless until his family eventually found him alive.

“These details raise serious concerns regarding the treatment of the wounded, violations of the right to life, and extrajudicial killings, including of minors,” the rights group said.

Khamenei’s Orders: “Victory Through Terror”

According to information received by Iran International, following a speech by Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on January 9, phrases such as “al-nasr bil-ru’b” (victory through terror) and “fight them until there is no sedition” were used in briefings and discussions among senior IRGC commanders. The same phrases appeared on January 9 in Telegram channels affiliated with hardliners.

The organized killings across Iran, Iran International reported, indicate the brutal crackdown was carried out with the agreement and cooperation of state institutions and on the orders of the highest authorities of the Islamic Republic.

While most killings were carried out by IRGC and Basij forces, Iran International received reports indicating that proxy forces from Iraq and Syria were also used in the crackdown. The deployment of non-local forces suggests a decision to expand repression capacity as quickly as possible.

Two sources close to the Iranian government told Iran International on January 24 that Khamenei has moved into a special underground shelter in Tehran after senior military and security officials assessed an increased risk of a potential U.S. attack. The facility was described as a fortified site with interconnected tunnels.

Siavash Shirzad

Among the tens of thousands dead is one whose story captures the tragedy of false hope.

Siavash Shirzad, 38, lived with his family in Bukan in western Iran. On January 2, he decided to join the protests despite his family’s attempts to dissuade him.

“This is a celebration of a revolution,” he told them, according to family members. “Trump said he supports us. I’m going.”

That faith cost him his life.

His cousin, Arina Moradi, who works for the Norway-based rights group Hengaw, said authorities forced her family to pay money to retrieve Siavash’s body. But they denied the family the right to bury him in his birth city of Bukan.

The family was forced to bury him in a remote ancestral village. “They paid the money, then they had to agree they would not allow any demonstrations or anything, that they would bury him in silence,” Moradi said.

His story would later become central to the debate over foreign intervention in Iran—a debate about promises made and promises broken, about hope offered and hope betrayed.

But on January 2, Siavash Shirzad believed help was coming.

More Faces of the Dead

The New York Times pieced together stories of other victims through interviews with family members outside Iran and reports by human rights groups.

Sorena Golgoon, 18, hoped to one day work for a major automaker, his cousin said. Credit...via the Golgoon family

Sorena Golgoon, 18, dreamed of working for a major international automaker. He was studying mechanical engineering and hoped to move to Tehran to pursue his career. The Hengaw Organization for Human Rights reported that Golgoon was “killed by direct fire from Iranian government forces” during protests in his hometown, the coastal city of Tonekabon.

His family, according to Hengaw, came “under intense pressure from government authorities to falsely claim that he was killed by protesters.” His cousin, Ensieh Raoufi, who lives in France, said the loss left the family in profound sorrow. “Their grief is endless.”

Robina Aminian, right, with her aunt, Hali Nouri, who lives in Norway. “She wanted to be free, to live unrestrained,” Ms. Nouri said of Ms. Aminian.Credit...via the Aminian family

Robina Aminian, 23, had just finished a college exam in early January when she decided to join the protests in Tehran. A fashion student known for weaving traditional Iranian elements into her designs, she was an avid guitarist with a creative spirit.

Her aunt, Hali Nouri, who lives in Norway, was told by family members that Aminian was shot in the head on the night of January 8 after leaving the Shariati Technical and Vocational College for Girls. “She knew what she wanted in life,” Nouri said. “She wanted to be free, to live unrestrained.”

Authorities in Marivan, western Iran, disrupted Aminian’s memorial service. Security officers stationed outside a family member’s home prevented relatives from paying their respects.

Zahra Bani-Amerian and Bijan Mostafavi at the tomb of the Persian poet Hafez in the city of Shiraz. The family had considered leaving Iran at one point, a relative said, but decided to stay.Credit...via the Mostafavi family

Zahra Bani-Amerian, 48, her husband Bijan Mostafavi, 56, and their son Danial, 19, were shot in their car by government forces during protests in Karaj, west of Tehran, according to Hengaw.

Bani-Amerian had sent a text message to her cousin just days before her death, saying the government was killing people “one by one.” She felt other countries were failing to help Iranians amid the crackdown. She was a retired employee of Iran’s Social Security Organization, curious and humorous, who loved singing.

Her husband had been a beloved teacher for years in Sonqor in western Iran. Their son Danial was cheerful and loved video games. At one point, the family considered emigrating to Turkey, but Mostafavi hesitated, saying starting over at his age wasn’t easy.

The authorities imposed restrictions on their funeral, compelling the family to bury their loved ones before sunrise under tight security measures.

“I will not let them be forgotten in silence,” said Michael Askari, Bani-Amerian’s cousin who lives in Turkey.

A History of Violence

There is only one moment in Iran’s post-revolutionary history that compares to the January 8-9 massacre, according to analysts interviewed by The New York Times.

In 1988, as the newly founded Islamic Republic consolidated power while facing internal opposition and the Iran-Iraq war, Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini ordered the mass execution of what is estimated to be thousands of political prisoners behind closed doors.

The January 2026 massacre differs in one critical respect: it occurred in public, broadcast in real-time through smuggled Starlink connections, with the world watching.

“If it hadn’t been as serious, they wouldn’t have cracked down the way they did,” said Sanam Vakil, director of the Middle East and North Africa program at Chatham House, speaking to The Times. The protests “showcased that more people than ever are at a breaking point.”

The Islamic Republic now faces what The Times characterized as “its most fragile geopolitical moment in years, and perhaps ever”—confronting a legitimacy crisis at home while its regional proxies weaken abroad and the prospect of more attacks from the United States and Israel looms.

History and the January crackdown reveal what the Iranian regime is willing to do when its survival is threatened.

As The Times concluded: “When it feels weak, it seems to grow more ferocious.”

The Silence

By January 16, Tehran residents told The New York Times that the streets had mostly gone quiet. Security forces deployed in large numbers to nearly every neighborhood. Shops and restaurants closed after 6 p.m. The typically bustling, traffic-clogged capital felt as if it were under martial law.

“There is massive disappointment and disillusionment,” one resident said.

On January 21, Iran’s prosecutor general, Mohammad Movahedi, declared victory.

“The sedition is over now,” he said, according to the judiciary’s Mizan News agency. “And we must be grateful, as always, to the people who extinguished this sedition by being in the field in a timely manner.”

The government vowed to restore internet access “in the coming days,” though officials noted foreign sites might remain blocked. Text messages from Iran’s intelligence services warned families not to let children into the streets, urged citizens to report “suspicious activities,” and encouraged attendance at pro-government rallies.

Fear had done its work.

“Now everyone knows someone who was killed,” a doctor who escaped Iran told The Times. “The silence imposed on people is not normal — it’s a silence that carries the dust of death.”

The Cost

In this photo obtained by The Associated Press, Iranians attend an anti-government protest in Tehran, Iran, Jan. 9, 2026. (UGC via AP, File) (Uncredited/UGC)

Cities and towns across Iran smell of smoke. Fire-damaged mosques and government offices line streets. Banks have been torched, their ATMs smashed. Officials estimate at least $125 million in damage across more than 400 cities.

Today, as the death toll continued to rise, Iran’s currency collapsed to a record low. Exchange shops in Tehran offered 1.5 million rials to the dollar—a rate unthinkable just a decade ago when the exchange was 32,000 to $1.

“The city smells of death,” wrote Elaheh Mohammadi, a journalist at Tehran’s pro-reform newspaper Ham Mihan, which authorities shut down. “Hard days have passed and everyone is stunned; a whole country is in mourning, a whole country is holding back tears, a whole country has a lump in its throat.”

For journalist Masih Alinejad, who has survived three assassination attempts by Iran on U.S. soil, the aftermath is clear.

“Inaction has a body count,” she wrote in The New York Times.

“When the world doesn’t choose one of the available options, we are left with graveyards.”

Today, three weeks after the massacre began, classified IRGC documents show more than 36,500 killed. Iran’s currency collapsed to a record low. And the USS Abraham Lincoln carrier strike group arrived in the Middle East, bringing with it the military capability Trump promised—but three weeks too late for those already dead.
