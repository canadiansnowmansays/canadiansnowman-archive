The Trump Grift

Part 2 The Bankruptcy King: How Donald Turned Failure Into Fortune

June 13, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
June 13, 2025

From Brooklyn to Billions: The Student Becomes the Master

Armed with his father's money and methods, Donald Trump set out to conquer Manhattan and then Atlantic City. What followed was a masterpiece of heads-I-win, tails-you-lose capitalism that would define his business career for decades.

Remember the template Fred Trump created: access other people's money, extract maximum value regardless of outcomes, use legal mechanisms to limit personal exposure, and let others absorb the losses. Donald was about to apply this formula on a scale that would dwarf his father's Brooklyn operations.

The result? Six corporate bankruptcies that cost investors and creditors billions while making Donald Trump personally wealthy. This wasn't business failure—it was business genius, if your only measure of success is personal enrichment at others' expense.

Chapter 1: The Taj Mahal - A $1.2 Billion Masterclass in Extraction

In April 1990, Donald Trump opened what he called "the eighth wonder of the world"—the Trump Taj Mahal Casino Resort in Atlantic City. At $1.2 billion, it was the most expensive casino ever built, covering 4.2 million square feet on 17 acres. More than 60,000 people visited on opening day.

One year later, the Taj Mahal filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection.

But here's the thing: the failure of the Taj Mahal wasn't a failure for Trump. It was exactly what his father had taught him—a way to extract wealth while transferring risk to others.

The Setup: Building with Other People's Money

Trump financed the Taj Mahal primarily with junk bonds carrying a crushing 14% interest rate. The project was so overleveraged that it would have needed to capture an impossible share of Atlantic City's gambling market just to service its debt. Many analysts predicted failure before the doors even opened.

But Trump didn't care if the casino succeeded long-term. He cared about extracting maximum value during construction and early operation. The New York Times investigation found that Trump "put up little of his own money, shifted personal debts to the casinos and collected millions of dollars in salary, bonuses, and other payments."

While the Taj Mahal was hemorrhaging money, Trump was collecting:

  • Management fees for overseeing the construction

  • Consulting fees for his "expertise"

  • Salary and bonuses as the casino's leader

  • Payments for services provided by other Trump companies

The Payoff: Profit from "Failure"

When the inevitable bankruptcy came, Trump used his father's playbook. He negotiated to keep 50% ownership of the casino in exchange for giving bondholders the other half and accepting lower interest rates. The bondholders—pension funds, insurance companies, and individual investors—lost hundreds of millions. Trump kept collecting paychecks.

"The burden of his failures," The New York Times reported, "fell on investors and others who had bet on his business acumen."

This became the Trump template: use other people's money to build something, extract maximum management fees and compensation, then let the creditors fight over the scraps when it inevitably collapses under its debt load.

Chapter 2: The Bankruptcy Assembly Line

The Taj Mahal was just the beginning. Trump turned bankruptcy into a business model, filing Chapter 11 six times between 1991 and 2009:

Trump Taj Mahal (1991): The flagship disaster that set the template

Trump Plaza Hotel and Casino (1992): Filed bankruptcy the same year, with an 80% decline in cash flow

Trump Castle Hotel and Casino (1992): His "weakest gambling hall" that faced competition from his own Taj Mahal

Plaza Hotel in New York (1992): The Manhattan hotel that entered bankruptcy due to crushing debt service

Trump Hotels and Casino Resorts (2004): A holding company that restructured $1.8 billion in debt

Trump Entertainment Resorts (2009): The final casino bankruptcy during the 2008 recession

Each bankruptcy followed the same script: Trump would acquire properties with massive debt, extract fees and compensation during operation, then declare bankruptcy when the debt service became unsustainable.

Why Lenders Kept Funding Him

You might wonder: why did lenders keep giving Trump money after multiple bankruptcies? The answer reveals the sophistication of his father's template.

One former chairman of New Jersey's casino commission described Trump as "too big to fail" in Atlantic City. His casino closures would have devastated the local economy, so lenders and gaming officials found ways to keep Trump in business while reducing his control.

Trump's lenders lost hundreds of millions of dollars but often wrote off the losses and extended him more credit because liquidation would have been even worse. They were essentially subsidizing Trump's lifestyle while absorbing his business losses.

As Trump himself explained: "I have used the laws of this country just like the greatest people that you read about every day in business have used the laws of this country, the chapter laws, to do a great job for my company, my employees, myself and my family."

Notice the order: company, employees, myself, family. But the reality was always: myself and family first, everything else second.

Chapter 3: The Licensing Pivot - Getting Rich Off Other People's Failures

After his casino disasters, Trump evolved his father's template into something even more elegant: licensing his name while avoiding any real financial exposure. Why risk your own money when you can profit from other people's investments—and their failures?

Trump University: The $25 Million Education in Fraud

The most notorious example was Trump University, which operated from 2005 to 2010. Students paid up to $35,000 for Trump's "real estate secrets," but what they got was something entirely different.

"They claim that the promised Donald Trump investment techniques were mostly stuff that you could find on the Internet, some of which was illegal in some of the states where the seminars took place," NPR reported. "They say that the promised mentoring was worthless or nonexistent, that the instructors were unqualified and that they were definitely not handpicked by Donald Trump, as he claimed."

Student Bob Guillo, who paid $35,000, told NPR: "The more and more I got involved in Trump University, the more and more I found out that I had truly been scammed. At first it was embarrassing. Then I became very, very angry that the man that scammed me out of all that money had the audacity to run for president."

The lawsuits mounted until Trump agreed to a $25 million settlement. But here's the kicker: the settlement was paid not by Trump but by his Las Vegas hotel business partner, billionaire Phil Ruffin. Even when forced to pay for his fraud, Trump found a way to make someone else pick up the tab.

The Licensing Pyramid

Trump University was just one spoke in Trump's licensing wheel, which included:

Trump Steaks (2007): Sold through The Sharper Image, pulled after barely two months when they "literally sold almost no steaks"

Trump Vodka (2006-2011): Netted Trump $1 million while Drinks Americas lost $2 million on unsold stock

Trump Ice (2004-2010): Earned Trump $600,000 in licensing fees while bottlers lost $1 million

Trump Magazine (2007-2009): Trump collected $400,000 in fees while publishers lost $1.5 million

GoTrump.com (2006-2007): Trump earned $300,000 in licensing while developers lost $800,000

Trump Mortgage (2006-2008): Generated $2 million for Trump while investors lost $10 million

In each case, Trump collected licensing fees upfront while partners and investors absorbed the losses when the ventures inevitably failed. The pattern was perfect: maximum extraction, zero risk.

Chapter 4: The Criminal Conviction - When the Template Finally Catches Up

Trump's business practices eventually caught up with him in criminal court. In December 2022, a Manhattan jury found the Trump Organization guilty on all 17 counts of criminal tax fraud and falsifying business records.

The scheme was classic Trump: top executives reduced their reported salaries by the amount of company-issued fringe benefits to avoid paying taxes. CFO Allen Weisselberg admitted to avoiding taxes on $200,000 in annual compensation, including a luxury Manhattan apartment, two Mercedes Benz leases, parking, utilities, and private school tuition for his grandchildren.

The company was literally stealing from taxpayers by hiding executive compensation as business expenses—a sophisticated version of Fred Trump's All County scam.

Even the criminal conviction followed the family pattern: Trump built his political brand on being an "aggressive and successful businessman," and the maximum penalty was just $1.6 million for a billion-dollar organization. A $1.6 million fine isn't punishment—it's a cost of doing business.

Chapter 5: The Psychology of Profitable Failure

What made Trump's bankruptcy strategy so effective wasn't just the financial engineering—it was the psychological framework he inherited from his father. Fred had taught Donald that avoiding losses while others absorbed them wasn't cheating—it was being smart.

This mindset allowed Trump to file for bankruptcy six times while maintaining his image as a successful businessman. He reframed each failure as evidence of his sophistication: "I have used the laws of this country just like the greatest people."

The bankruptcies weren't business failures—they were business successes, measured by the only metric that mattered to Trump: how much money he personally extracted from each deal.

The Ultimate Pivot

By the 2000s, Trump had perfected the model: why own risky businesses when you can license your name to other people's risky businesses? Why invest your own money when you can profit from other people's investments? Why take responsibility for failures when you can collect fees regardless of outcomes?

This evolution set the stage for Trump's next chapter: monetizing fame itself through reality television, political campaigns, and eventually the presidency. The bankruptcy king was about to become something even more powerful—a brand that could extract value from any system it touched.

The Template Perfected

Donald Trump's casino and licensing years weren't about building successful businesses—they were about perfecting his father's wealth extraction template on a national scale. He learned to profit from failure, extract value from other people's investments, and use legal mechanisms to avoid personal consequences.

The bankruptcies taught him that he could fail spectacularly and still come out ahead, as long as he structured the deals correctly from the beginning. The licensing deals showed him that he could profit from other people's businesses without risking his own money.

Most importantly, he learned that the American business and legal system would enable this behavior, as long as he had good lawyers and understood the loopholes.

The bankruptcy king was ready for his next evolution: turning politics itself into the ultimate licensing deal.
