America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 5: “The Bipartisan Path Nobody Wants to Talk About”

December 11, 2025

Here Is the Plan — And It’s Not a Concept

Nine years after promising healthcare reform, Donald Trump offered America “concepts of a plan.” No details. No timeline. No solution.

So let me do what he wouldn’t: Present an actual plan. Not a concept. Not a promise. Not a theoretical framework.

A tested, proven, working system that delivers universal coverage while preserving private insurance and market competition. A system that would cut American healthcare costs roughly in half while improving outcomes. A system that gives Republicans what they say they want and Democrats what they say they need.

The plan exists. Switzerland has been running it since 1996. Germany since 1883. Both countries achieve universal coverage at approximately half of American per-capita costs.

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: Nobody in American politics wants this plan.

Not because it wouldn’t work — it would. Not because it’s untested — it’s been proven across multiple countries for decades. Not because Americans would reject it — polling shows strong support for the core elements.

But because admitting this compromise exists would require both parties to acknowledge they’ve been lying about what’s actually possible.

This is Part 5 of Code Blue, our investigation into America’s 80-year healthcare failure. We’ve documented the broken promises (Parts 1-2), followed the money (Part 3), and examined successful international models (Part 4). Now comes the hardest part: describing the solution that could actually pass — and explaining why neither party will embrace it.

Welcome to the politics of healthcare reform, where ideology trumps outcomes and special interests buy silence.

The Obvious Compromise

Take Switzerland’s model of competing private insurers with mandatory coverage. Add Germany’s price controls and public option. Include premium subsidies for affordability. Mandate coverage for pre-existing conditions. Allow insurers to profit on supplemental plans but not basic coverage.

The result:

What Republicans Get:

  • Private insurance companies compete for customers

  • Market-based system with consumer choice

  • No government-run hospitals or government-employed doctors

  • Preservation of employer-based insurance for those who want it

  • HSAs and high-deductible plans remain available

  • Innovation incentives through supplemental plan competition

What Democrats Get:

  • Universal coverage (everyone insured)

  • Price controls preventing gouging

  • Subsidies ensuring affordability

  • Pre-existing condition protections

  • Possible public option competing with private plans

  • Administrative simplification through standardized coverage

What Americans Get:

  • Coverage for 100% of population

  • Costs cut roughly in half (from $14,885 to ~$7,500 per capita)

  • No medical bankruptcy

  • Freedom to choose insurance company

  • Simplified billing and claims

  • Better health outcomes

This isn’t theoretical. Switzerland has been doing this since 1996. Germany since 1883. Both countries have conservative and progressive parties. Both countries have powerful insurance industries. Both countries have citizens who value personal freedom and market choice.

Both countries achieve universal coverage at half of American costs.

Why Republicans Won’t Embrace It

The Republican Party claims to support market-based healthcare solutions. They denounce “government-run healthcare” and “socialized medicine.” They praise competition, consumer choice, and private enterprise.

The Swiss-German model gives them all of that. So why don’t they embrace it?

Reason 1: It requires mandates

Universal coverage requires everyone to have insurance. Switzerland fines residents who don’t buy coverage. Germany makes coverage automatic through payroll deductions.

Republicans spent years fighting the ACA’s individual mandate — even though the mandate was originally a Republican idea, proposed by the Heritage Foundation in 1989 and implemented by Mitt Romney in Massachusetts in 2006.

Why the reversal? Because once Democrats embraced the mandate, Republicans had to oppose it. Tribal politics trumped policy consistency.

Reason 2: It requires price controls

Market competition only works when consumers have actual choice and price transparency. Healthcare fails both tests — patients in medical emergencies can’t shop around, and prices are deliberately obscured through complex billing.

Switzerland and Germany solve this through government price regulation. The government sets maximum prices or negotiates fee schedules with providers. Hospitals can’t charge $283 for a $20 X-ray because the government won’t allow it.

American Republicans call this “government interference in free markets.” But healthcare isn’t a free market — it’s a captured market where providers have monopoly pricing power and consumers have no choice.

Price controls aren’t socialism. They’re the only mechanism that makes market competition functional in healthcare. But Republicans can’t admit this without acknowledging that unregulated healthcare markets don’t work.

Reason 3: It requires subsidies

If you mandate that everyone buy insurance, you must subsidize those who can’t afford it. Switzerland spends heavily on premium subsidies — about 30% of residents receive government assistance.

Republicans fought ACA subsidies as “welfare” and “government handouts.” Never mind that subsidies are the only way to make universal coverage work in a market-based system. Without subsidies, mandates become regressive taxes on the poor.

Reason 4: Special interests

The insurance industry likes the current American system. High administrative complexity creates barriers to entry. Claim denials generate profit. Confusing networks and surprise billing create revenue opportunities.

A Swiss-German system would simplify administration, prohibit denials for pre-existing conditions, standardize coverage, and cap profits on basic plans. Insurance companies could still profit on supplemental coverage, but their basic plan margins would shrink.

Pharmaceutical companies, hospital chains, and device manufacturers also benefit from the current system’s lack of price controls. A Swiss-German model would cut their revenues significantly.

These industries donate heavily to Republican campaigns. Embracing real reform would mean losing that funding.

Reason 5: Ideological purity

The Republican Party has painted itself into a corner where any government involvement in healthcare equals “socialism.” Mandates = socialism. Price controls = socialism. Subsidies = socialism. Public option = socialism.

Never mind that Switzerland — one of the world’s most capitalist countries — uses all of these mechanisms. Never mind that Germany — Europe’s economic powerhouse — has combined public and private insurance for 142 years.

Republican ideology now treats healthcare as a religious issue rather than a practical problem. Compromise equals heresy.

Why Democrats Won’t Embrace It

Democrats claim to support universal healthcare. They point to international models. They promise that “healthcare is a human right.”

The Swiss-German model delivers everything they say they want. So why don’t they embrace it?

Reason 1: It preserves private insurance

The progressive wing of the Democratic Party wants Medicare for All — a fully government-run, single-payer system that eliminates private insurance for basic coverage.

The Swiss-German model keeps private insurers in business. They’re heavily regulated, forced to operate as near-utilities on basic coverage, but they survive.

For many progressives, preserving private insurance companies is morally unacceptable. Insurance companies profit from denying care — therefore they must be eliminated, not reformed.

This is emotionally satisfying but politically unrealistic. The private insurance industry employs 2.9 million Americans. Eliminating it would require transitioning millions of workers, unwinding trillions in assets, and overcoming massive political resistance.

Switzerland proves you can achieve universal coverage, control costs, and deliver excellent outcomes while keeping private insurers — IF you regulate them effectively. But that compromise offends progressive purity.

Reason 2: It doesn’t go far enough

Democrats spent decades fighting for incremental healthcare reforms. The ACA was a compromise — keeping private insurance while expanding coverage and regulating markets.

Many progressives see incremental reform as capitulation. They want a complete system overhaul — Medicare for All, immediately, no compromises.

The Swiss-German model is incremental. It reforms the existing system rather than replacing it. It regulates private insurers rather than eliminating them. It achieves universal coverage through mandates and subsidies rather than government-run insurance.

For progressives who view healthcare as a moral issue, incremental reform feels like settling for injustice.

Reason 3: It requires admitting the ACA’s limitations

The Affordable Care Act was modeled partially on Switzerland’s system — but without the teeth. The ACA included:

  • Individual mandate (later eliminated)

  • Subsidies (inadequate for many middle-income families)

  • Pre-existing condition protections (effective)

  • Standardized coverage tiers (Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum)

But the ACA lacked:

  • Serious price controls (except for Medicare negotiation, added much later)

  • Sufficient subsidy funding

  • Effective mandate enforcement

  • Administrative simplification

Embracing a true Swiss-German model would require Democrats to admit the ACA was a half-measure — politically difficult after defending it for years.

Reason 4: Special interests

Yes, Democrats also receive healthcare industry donations. Hospital associations, pharmaceutical companies, and insurance companies donate to both parties.

Many Democrats rely on union support, and healthcare unions (nurses, hospital workers) have complex positions on reform. Some support Medicare for All; others worry about job losses from system disruption.

Trial lawyers — a major Democratic donor base — oppose tort reform and malpractice caps, even though these are part of cost control in countries like Germany.

Reason 5: The 2024 landscape

With Republicans controlling Congress and the presidency, Democrats have zero incentive to propose compromise. They can blame Republicans for inaction, campaign on Medicare for All, and avoid the hard work of building bipartisan consensus.

Compromise makes you vulnerable to primary challenges. Bernie Sanders’ 2016 and 2020 campaigns proved that Democratic primary voters reward ideological purity over pragmatic compromise.

Why embrace a Swiss-German model that might actually pass when you can champion Medicare for All, lose the legislative fight, and use the defeat for campaign fundraising?

The Democratic Failure: 2021-2022

Let’s talk about what Democrats did when they actually had power.

January 2021: Democrats controlled the White House, Senate (50-50 with VP Harris tiebreaker), and House. For the first time since 2010, Democrats had the votes to pass healthcare reform through budget reconciliation — requiring only 50 Senate votes, no Republican support needed.

They had two years. Here’s what they accomplished on healthcare:

What they did:

  • Included temporary ACA subsidy expansion in American Rescue Plan (expired December 2025)

  • Passed limited Medicare drug price negotiation in Inflation Reduction Act (covering 10 drugs initially, expanding slowly to 20 by 2029)

  • Extended COBRA subsidies temporarily

What they didn’t do:

  • Make ACA subsidies permanent

  • Lower Medicare eligibility age to 60 (or 55, or 50)

  • Create a public option (despite Biden’s campaign promise)

  • Allow Medicare to negotiate prices for all drugs (not just 10)

  • Fix the ACA’s subsidy cliff for middle-income families

  • Address surprise billing comprehensively

  • Simplify insurance networks and claims processing

  • Expand Medicaid in holdout states through federal incentives

Why not? Because Democrats spent 2021-2022 fighting each other over Build Back Better — a massive social spending bill that eventually collapsed under its own weight. Healthcare reform was sacrificed for climate provisions, childcare funding, and intra-party warfare between progressives and moderates.

Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer could have prioritized a clean ACA expansion bill in early 2021 when political capital was highest. He didn’t.

President Biden could have used executive authority to expand ACA enrollment, increase subsidy generosity within existing statute, and pressure holdout states on Medicaid expansion. He did some of this, but not aggressively.

Senator Bernie Sanders (as Budget Committee Chair) pushed for Medicare expansion but couldn’t unite his caucus behind a specific proposal.

The result? Democrats squandered their best opportunity for healthcare reform in a decade fighting over legislation that ultimately passed without most healthcare provisions.

This matters because it proves Democrats aren’t actually willing to fight for healthcare reform when it requires political capital and internal compromise. They’ll campaign on it. They’ll blame Republicans for blocking it. But when they have power, they choose other priorities or collapse into infighting.

Republicans deserve blame for 80 years of obstruction. But Democrats deserve blame for repeated failures to deliver when they control government. Both parties prefer healthcare as a permanent campaign issue rather than a solved problem.

The 2009-2010 lesson, repeated:

In 2009-2010, Democrats had a larger majority than 2021-2022. They passed the ACA — a significant achievement — but only after months of negotiations with Joe Lieberman, repeated compromises that weakened the bill, and abandonment of the public option.

The result was a law that expanded coverage but didn’t control costs effectively. Insurance companies remained profitable. Drug prices continued rising. Administrative complexity persisted.

Democrats defended the ACA against Republican attacks for a decade. But they never built on it, never improved it, never addressed its weaknesses — even when they had opportunities.

Why not? Because improving the ACA would require admitting it was incomplete. Because fixing its problems would anger insurance company donors. Because meaningful cost control would require confronting physicians, hospitals, and pharmaceutical companies — all of whom donate to Democratic campaigns.

So here we are: both parties failing, for different reasons, while Americans pay the price.

The Stakeholders Who Profit From Failure

Both parties avoid the obvious compromise because powerful interests benefit from the status quo:

Insurance companies profit from administrative complexity and claim denials. A regulated Swiss system would cut their margins on basic coverage (though they could still profit on supplemental plans).

Pharmaceutical companies profit from lack of price negotiation. Swiss and German price controls would slash their American revenues by 40-60%.

Hospital chains profit from opaque pricing and regional monopolies. Price transparency and fee schedules would end surprise billing and price gouging.

Medical device manufacturers profit from lack of price controls. Germany pays 30-50% less for the same devices.

PBMs (Pharmacy Benefit Managers) profit from their middleman position between insurers, pharmacies, and drug makers. Administrative simplification would reduce their role.

Political consultants and campaign operatives profit from healthcare as a permanent campaign issue. Solving it would eliminate a major fundraising and messaging tool.

Media companies profit from healthcare industry advertising. Pharmaceutical companies alone spend $6+ billion annually on TV advertising. Health insurers spend billions more.

Every one of these stakeholders donates to political campaigns. Every one lobbies Congress. Every one runs advertising campaigns.

And every one benefits more from a broken system than a Swiss-German compromise.

What Polling Actually Shows

Here’s what makes this even more frustrating: Americans support the core elements of a Swiss-German compromise.

Kaiser Family Foundation polling (2024):

  • 88% support allowing Medicare to negotiate drug prices (price controls)

  • 70% support a public option competing with private insurance

  • 62% support requiring everyone to have health insurance (mandate)

  • 75% support increased subsidies for private insurance

  • 71% support standardized coverage requirements

  • 67% support banning surprise medical billing

Americans aren’t demanding Medicare for All (support hovers around 55% with high volatility). They’re not defending the current system (only 35% satisfaction).

They support regulated private insurance with universal coverage — exactly what Switzerland and Germany deliver.

But neither party will give them that option.

The Implementation Path Nobody Wants

Here’s how America could implement a Swiss-German compromise:

Phase 1 (Years 1-2): Build the foundation

  • Establish federal coverage standards (standardized basic plan)

  • Create income-based subsidy structure

  • Implement strict price controls through negotiated fee schedules

  • Require price transparency for all procedures and drugs

  • Strengthen individual mandate with real enforcement

  • Create federal reinsurance program to stabilize markets

Phase 2 (Years 3-4): Expand coverage

  • Auto-enroll uninsured in subsidized private plans

  • Expand subsidy eligibility to cover middle-income families

  • Allow public option to compete with private insurers

  • Simplify administrative requirements and billing codes

  • Implement nationwide claim standardization

Phase 3 (Years 5-6): Optimize the system

  • Fine-tune subsidy levels based on real-world experience

  • Adjust price controls for specialty care and rare conditions

  • Evaluate public option performance and market share

  • Address remaining coverage gaps and affordability issues

  • Transition Medicare Advantage to new regulated framework

Cost estimate: $200-300 billion annually in additional federal spending (mostly for subsidies), offset by $600-800 billion in systemic savings from price controls and administrative simplification. Net result: $300-500 billion in annual savings while covering 100% of Americans.

This is achievable. This is tested. This works.

Nobody will propose it.

Why This Essay Will Change Nothing

I’ve now written 2,800 words describing a bipartisan healthcare compromise that would:

  • Achieve universal coverage

  • Cut costs by 40-50%

  • Preserve private insurance and market competition

  • Satisfy both party’s stated principles

  • Have strong public support

And it will change exactly nothing.

Why? Because American healthcare reform isn’t actually about healthcare. It’s about:

  • Campaign fundraising from industry stakeholders

  • Tribal political identity

  • Ideological purity over pragmatic solutions

  • Preserving profitable complexity

  • Maintaining permanent campaign issues

Republicans won’t embrace it because it requires admitting that unregulated markets fail in healthcare. Democrats won’t embrace it because it requires admitting that private insurance can work with proper regulation. Industry stakeholders won’t embrace it because it threatens profits.

And so Americans will continue paying $14,885 per capita for healthcare that costs $7,500 elsewhere. They’ll continue going bankrupt from medical bills. They’ll continue with 30 million uninsured and millions more underinsured.

Not because the solution is unknown. Not because it’s untested. Not because Americans oppose it.

But because solving the problem would require both parties to choose governance over politics — and that’s apparently too much to ask.
