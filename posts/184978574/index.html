The Weekend Greenland Said No

How Mass Protests, Trade War Threats, and European Defiance Transformed Diplomatic Crisis Into Active Confrontation

January 19, 2026

January 19, 2026

Three days ago, I published an introduction warning that Greenland represented a test of American empire—that Trump’s threats against a NATO ally could end the alliance itself.

This weekend, theory became reality.

On Saturday, January 17, Greenlanders took to the streets in coordinated protests across the Arctic island. In Nuuk, the capital, several hundred people marched through freezing temperatures—a remarkable turnout in a city of fewer than 20,000 residents. To grasp the scale: proportionally, that would be 90,000 protesters in Toronto, 450,000 in New York.

Prime Minister Jens-Frederik Nielsen suddenly appeared. The crowd roared as he climbed onto a snowbank to raise the Greenlandic flag.

“We said it last year, and we will keep saying it: We are not for sale,” declared Isak Berthelsen, a 43-year-old electrician from Nuuk. “It shows that we are strong enough to say no, and that we have our own voice.”

Hand-painted signs read “No means no,” “Greenland Is Already Great,” and “Yankee, go home!” Others displayed harsher—and cruder—messages aimed at the Trump administration. Simultaneous demonstrations erupted in Aasiaat, Qaqortoq, and Ilulissat, with solidarity rallies held in Copenhagen and other Danish cities.

Many protesters said they had reached a breaking point after Trump repeatedly suggested the United States would take Greenland “one way or the other” and that it would happen “whether they like it or not.”

“There is a lot of unrest inside people right now,” said Kristian Johansen, 67, a retired carpenter who helped organize the demonstration. “Every new statement makes it worse.”

The Numbers That Matter

A January 2026 poll revealed what the protests confirmed:

  • 85% of Greenlanders oppose becoming part of the United States

  • Only 6% support American control

  • 56% support eventual independence from Denmark—but as a sovereign nation, not to join the U.S.

Prime Minister Nielsen has been unequivocal: “Greenland does not want to be owned by the United States. Greenland does not want to be governed by the United States. Greenland does not want to be part of the United States.”

Naaja Nathanielsen, Greenland’s minister for business and mineral resources, testified before British Parliament on Tuesday: “People are not sleeping, children are afraid, and it just fills everything these days. For others this might be a piece of land, but for us it’s home.”

She added: “My deepest dream or hope is that the people of Greenland will get a say no matter what.”

The crowd in Nuuk included teachers, children, fishermen, and retirees, many wrapped in Greenlandic flags. Angu Kristensen, 63, expressed the frustration many felt: “I don’t know how else you talk to someone who doesn’t understand anything at all.”

In Copenhagen, protesters packed City Hall Square before marching to the U.S. Embassy. Hanne Dueholm, 61, had traveled from her horse farm in Lejre with her husband and daughter, sporting hats reading “Make America Go Away.”

“When Trump began making threats, I thought: He is just like Putin—he needs another country, so he takes it,” she said. “But you can’t have that mind-set in a democratic world.”

Saturday Night: The Tariff Ultimatum

Hours after the protests, Trump responded—not with conciliation, but with economic warfare.

In a Truth Social post Saturday night, Trump demanded a deal to buy Greenland, threatening tariffs on European nations that have expressed solidarity with Denmark: 10 percent in February, rising to 25 percent in June.

The targeted countries: Denmark, Norway, Sweden, France, Germany, and Britain.

It appeared to leave little room for Europe to maneuver or negotiate. European leaders had spent months hoping appeasement and dialogue would work—that they could satisfy Trump with willingness to increase defense spending on Greenland and enhanced Arctic cooperation.

Trump’s fixation on ownership crushed those hopes.

Sunday Morning: “Leaders Will Come Around”

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent doubled down on NBC’s “Meet the Press” Sunday morning.

American ownership of Greenland would be “best for Greenland, best for Europe and best for the United States,” Bessent said—adding this would be true even if Greenland were taken by military force.

“The European leaders will come around,” he added.

There is little sign of that.

Europe’s Response: The Trade Bazooka

Within hours of Trump’s post, members of the European Parliament announced they would freeze ratification of the trade deal that Trump and Ursula von der Leyen struck last summer.

By Sunday afternoon, Emmanuel Macron, president of France, requested activation of the EU’s “anti-coercion instrument”—officially a tool to defend against political coercion, unofficially called Europe’s trade “bazooka.”

The instrument could impose limitations on major American technology companies or other service providers doing business on the continent. Using it would sharply escalate trans-Atlantic tensions—but European leaders increasingly argue this is the moment to wield it.

Ambassadors from across the 27-nation European Union gathered for an emergency meeting at 5 p.m. Sunday in Brussels.

Sweden’s Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson wrote that “we will not let ourselves be blackmailed.” Macron declared on social media that “no intimidation nor threat will influence us.”

Even Britain’s Prime Minister Keir Starmer—who has carefully cultivated positive relations with the White House—called Trump’s tariff move “completely wrong.”

Wolfgang Ischinger, former German ambassador to Washington and chairman of the Munich Security Conference, published an essay in the New York Times on Sunday warning that Trump’s threats would “boomerang on America.”

Ischinger’s analysis was stark: “An attack on Greenland would be the end of NATO. That would rob the United States of an unbeatable web of allies, offer rifts for our common enemies to exploit and eradicate the alliance’s collective moral high ground that has helped project American soft power across Europe and the world for decades. In short, it would make America weaker, not safer.”

Why This Weekend Changed Everything

For weeks, many European officials dismissed Trump’s Greenland threats as negotiating tactics—bluster designed to extract concessions on defense spending or Arctic cooperation.

This weekend ended that illusion.

The protests demonstrated that Greenlanders overwhelmingly reject American control—85% opposed, only 6% in favor. The legal reality remains: Denmark cannot sell Greenland without Greenlandic consent. International law in 2026 recognizes indigenous self-determination. This isn’t property to be transferred. These are people with rights.

Trump’s tariff threats proved he won’t accept diplomatic compromise. He doesn’t want enhanced cooperation. He wants ownership. And he’s willing to trigger trade war with America’s closest allies to get it.

Europe’s response—freezing the trade deal, activating the anti-coercion instrument, emergency meetings—shows European leaders finally understand appeasement won’t work.

This is no longer diplomatic disagreement. This is active confrontation.

The Question That Haunts Everything

In Nuuk on Saturday, demonstrators gathered near a darkened U.S. consulate, sealed off by police barricades, playing traditional drums and chanting “Kalaallit Nunaat”—the name for the island in Greenlandic.

“The president has nothing to do here,” said Parnuna Olsen, 25, a high school student in Nuuk.

If Trump were to come to Greenland, she said, he would not be welcomed. Her response, she added, would not be polite.

Maria Meier Brun, a social worker whose father was Greenlandic, joined protests in Copenhagen. She spoke of the complex history between Greenland and Denmark—”many wounds and a lot of pain between us.”

But she also spoke of unity: “We are not American, and we will never be American. Some time ago, we wanted to be partners. But right now, I don’t really want to.”

That loss—the transformation from partner to threat—captures what’s at stake.

Senator Mark Kelly’s question runs through everything that follows: “Aren’t we supposed to be the good guys here?”

This weekend, Greenland answered for us.
