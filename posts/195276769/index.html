Seven Hearings, Zero Accountability

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. goes to Congress and discovers that shouting is not the same as governing

April 24, 2026

Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s vaccine agenda — his insistence that vaccines are somehow linked to autism, despite a lack of evidence — has always been a bit of an Achilles’ heel.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Last month, in the Sunday Satire Series, I argued that Kennedy’s meme machine was papering over a collapsing policy record. Last month, The Atlantic confirmed it. This week, Kennedy walked into seven consecutive congressional hearings and confirmed it himself.

There is a certain kind of politician who mistakes volume for argument. Who, when confronted with an uncomfortable fact, responds not with evidence but with escalation — louder, faster, more indignant, as if the sheer force of the performance might dislodge the question from the room.

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. had seven opportunities this week to demonstrate that he was not that kind of politician.

He did not take them.

The mathematics of Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

Let us begin with the numbers, because the numbers are instructive.

When Senator Elizabeth Warren asked Kennedy about prescription drug pricing — specifically about TrumpRx, the administration’s flagship drug discount website, and why Americans using it were still paying vastly more than the price of generic equivalents — Kennedy responded by defending the President’s claim that he had secured discounts of 400 to 1,500 percent on pharmaceutical drugs.

“If you have a $600 drug, and you reduce it to $10,” Kennedy explained to the Senate Finance Committee, “that’s a 600 percent reduction.”

It is not. A reduction from $600 to $10 is a discount of approximately 98 percent. A discount cannot exceed 100 percent, because at 100 percent the drug costs nothing, and beyond that the company would be paying you to take it. The President has been making this mathematically impossible claim since TrumpRx launched. When pressed on it, Trump himself said: “You could say whatever you want.” Kennedy, appearing before the United States Senate, chose to say the same thing — and to dress it in the language of a calculation.

Eric Thayer/Getty Images

As the New York Times reported this week, when Democrats came out swinging, Kennedy became more defiant, even at times screaming his rebuttals — though some of them didn’t align with the facts.

This is the RFK Files record on the mathematics of this administration: the Secretary of Health and Human Services, before a congressional committee, defended a percentage that does not exist in the discipline of mathematics, on behalf of a program that Senator Warren demonstrated was charging Americans several hundred dollars for drugs available in generic form for $16.

“Scam discounts,” Warren called them. Kennedy moved on.

The about-face on vaccines: a man in full retreat

The most significant story of this week’s hearings was not what Kennedy said. It was what he could no longer bring himself to say — and what he said instead.

Kennedy built his public career on vaccine skepticism. For more than a decade, he was the most prominent anti-vaccine voice in America. In 2021, he urged people to “resist” CDC guidelines on when children should receive vaccines. He called COVID shots “the deadliest vaccine ever made.” He built a legal organization, a movement, and ultimately a Cabinet appointment on the proposition that America’s vaccine infrastructure was corrupt, captured by pharmaceutical companies, and actively harming children.

This week, before seven congressional committees, that man was nowhere to be found.

In his place was a Secretary who said “We promote the measles vaccine” and cited its 97 percent effectiveness. Who made his strongest statement yet, telling lawmakers that his department “has advised every child” to get the shot — though notably declining to attribute that advice to himself personally. Who, when Senator Michael Bennet pressed him directly on whether he agreed with his own CDC director that the measles vaccine is vital to keeping American children healthy, replied: “We promote the measles vaccine.”

Promote. Not endorse. Not recommend. Not, as Acting CDC Director Bhattacharya said plainly last month, “absolutely vital.” Promote — the language of a communications strategy, not a public health position.

The retreat is real, but it is incomplete, and the incompleteness is telling. Even as Kennedy softened his public rhetoric on vaccines, he was simultaneously performing a second retreat on glyphosate. He initially defended Trump’s executive order protecting Roundup’s manufacturers on social media, writing that while herbicides were “toxic by design,” the food supply depended on them. Only later did he say publicly that he was “not happy” about the order — after his own base had already registered their fury. Two about-faces running in parallel, both incomplete, both calibrated to the news cycle rather than to principle.

Back on vaccines: even as Kennedy softened his public rhetoric, he could not sustain the new position under scrutiny. He insisted, under persistent questioning, that hygiene and sanitation — not vaccination — were primarily responsible for the decline in deaths from infectious diseases during the 20th century. Senator Bill Cassidy, the Republican committee chairman and a physician, pushed back, telling Kennedy he had cited the supporting study out of context.

And when asked whether he would commit to accepting the vaccine decisions of his newly nominated CDC director, Dr. Erica Schwartz — whose supportive views on vaccines diverge sharply from his own — Kennedy was unambiguous: “I’m not going to make that kind of commitment.”

Read that sentence carefully. The Secretary of Health has nominated a CDC director whose vaccine views he will not commit to accepting. The about-face on vaccines is a performance for the cameras. The actual position, stated plainly to Congress, is that he reserves the right to override his own agency’s science.

The New York Times captured the full arc of Kennedy’s Capitol Hill performance this week. Trust in federal health agencies, surveys show, has continued to decline during his tenure.

“It has nothing to do with me”

Kennedy’s refrain on the measles outbreak was consistent across all seven hearings: “It has nothing to do with me.” He noted there is a global rise in measles cases, including in countries like Canada, Mexico, and the United Kingdom.

For the record: the man who fired all 17 members of the CDC’s independent vaccine advisory committee, replaced them with allies whose qualifications ranged from thin to non-existent, had his vaccine schedule changes struck down by a federal judge as probably illegal, and — as The Atlantic reported last month — privately told the father of a girl who died of measles that “you don’t know what’s in the vaccine anymore” — that man told Congress this week that the ongoing outbreak has nothing to do with him.

Mark Peterson / Redux

Representative Kim Schrier, a Democrat from Washington, argued that Kennedy’s vaccine views have caused a “spillover effect” that led to mothers not giving their babies vitamin K injections common at birth to prevent brain bleeding. “I’ve never said anything about vitamin K,” Kennedy said. “That’s exactly the point,” Schrier replied.

That exchange is worth sitting with. Kennedy’s defence was that he had not specifically targeted vitamin K. Schrier’s point was that the damage runs wider than his specific statements — that an administration which has systematically eroded trust in medical guidance produces consequences its Secretary cannot individually disavow. Kennedy did not appear to grasp the distinction.

The broader policy contradiction was documented by The Atlantic last month — an administration that simultaneously erodes confidence in the medical establishment while instructing Americans to consult their doctors on vaccines is not pursuing a coherent public health policy. It is pursuing a coherent political one.

The vanity projects question

Senator Maggie Hassan of New Hampshire used her time to raise a matter that readers of this series will recognize immediately.

“I have noticed that while HHS has done relatively little promotion of the life-saving measles vaccine,” Hassan told Kennedy, “you have been doing a lot of your own self-promotion using official government channels and resources.” She specifically cited the HHS promotional videos — Kennedy with Kid Rock drinking milk in a hot tub, Kennedy shirtless as a WWE fighter, Kennedy as a video game hero — which she termed “vanity projects.”

Kennedy did not deny that the videos exist, that they were produced using government resources, or that his department has been considerably more prolific in generating shirtless content than measles vaccination guidance. He moved on.

The Sunday Satire Series called this a masterwork of distraction. Senator Hassan, using the decorum appropriate to a Senate hearing, called it a vanity project. The underlying observation is the same.

The firing and the hiring

Kennedy revealed during the hearings that after firing or laying off close to 20,000 employees at the Department of Health and Human Services, he was now hiring 12,000 new workers to replace them. He told lawmakers he needed to “make sure that we have people to do every job.”

He presented this as management. Others saw it differently.

“We have lost so many exceptional scientists and other employees of HHS,” said Elizabeth Jacobs, a professor emeritus of epidemiology and founding member of Defend Public Health. “The decades of institutional knowledge that we have lost is absolutely devastating. The estimates are it’s going to take a decade or a generation to rebuild the massive medical science juggernaut that we once had.”

A decade or a generation. Kennedy’s response, across seven hearings, was to describe the cuts as “painful but necessary” to address the federal deficit — while simultaneously claiming to have delivered “historic wins” for American health. The historic wins referenced included new dietary guidelines, a push to phase out petroleum-based food dyes, and the Rural Health Transformation Fund. He did not specify which of these initiatives required dismantling twenty years of institutional epidemiological expertise to achieve.

Medicaid: a vocabulary lesson

This week, Kennedy shouted at the Senate Finance Committee: “There are no cuts to Medicaid!”

The budget Kennedy was defending proposes to reduce Medicaid spending by between $665 billion and nearly $1 trillion over the next decade relative to projected levels. The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities estimates this results in 7.5 million people losing coverage by 2034. Kennedy’s defence is that Medicaid spending will still nominally increase in absolute terms — just far less than it would have without the legislation.

“This is an old, sort of tired argument that’s been used by conservatives to justify spending cuts by saying, well, if spending is still growing in nominal terms, somehow there wasn’t a cut,” said Edwin Park, a research professor at Georgetown University. “The federal government is spending nearly a trillion dollars less than it otherwise would have in the absence of the legislation.”

There are no cuts to Medicaid, Kennedy told the Finance Committee of the United States Senate. He said it loudly.

The re-parenting of Black children

The hearings also revived a 2024 interview in which Kennedy, then running for president, proposed sending American children — specifically citing Black children — to “wellness farms” where they would be weaned off psychiatric medications, free of cellphones, and, in his word, “re-parented.”

Black Democratic lawmakers, including Representative Terri Sewell of Alabama and Senator Angela Alsobrooks of Maryland, upbraided Kennedy for the remarks. He insisted he had not made them, then said he did not remember making them, then edged toward an apology. “If I said it, I apologize, but I have to see the transcript,” he told Alsobrooks, who brought a large placard bearing the direct quote to the hearing.

If I said it. The man is looking at a placard of his own words, in a United States Senate hearing room, and offering a conditional apology contingent on transcript verification.

The MAHA coalition: ambition, betrayal, and a $98.8 million gap

Oliver Contreras / AFP / Getty

This week, The Atlantic published MAHA Swing Voters Are an Illusion and the New York Times published The ‘Make America Healthy Again’ Movement Is Cooling on Trump and Republicans — two pieces that together document a coalition fraying faster than its leaders can manage.

Start with the money. The MAHA PAC, run by Kennedy ally Tony Lyons, announced an initiative in March to raise $100 million to elect MAHA-aligned Republicans. Campaign finance filings through the end of February show the group had raised $1.2 million. That is not a rounding error. That is a $98.8 million gap between the ambition and the reality.

Then there is the base itself. Meet Tricia Busch, a former elementary schoolteacher in a Des Moines suburb, a two-time cancer survivor currently in remission from non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma — the same blood cancer at the centre of this week’s Supreme Court case against Roundup’s manufacturer. She blames glyphosate exposure in Iowa for her illness. She met Kennedy during the Iowa campaign while she was in a wheelchair, still on morphine, temporarily paralysed from the waist down after a stem cell transplant. She was a registered Democrat. She believed. “I really had faith in him and this whole MAHA,” she told the Times. “I thought he was speaking to people like me, who feel like we’re slowly being poisoned.”

Trump’s glyphosate executive order changed her mind. “I’ve never felt so betrayed,” she said.

Ms. Busch now describes herself as “a political orphan.” She is pouring her energy into a state governor’s race instead of federal politics. She is one voter — but she is not alone, and her trajectory is exactly what MAHA leaders are warning about privately.

Alex Clark, one of the movement’s most prominent voices, put it with unusual bluntness: “They have nowhere to go. They feel like their vote is useless. They have lost the energy. They have lost the enthusiasm. They feel like the Democrats don’t care about them. They feel like the Republicans lied to them, and they’re not planning on voting.”

That is a MAHA leader, with millions of social media followers, describing her own coalition as demobilized.

“Republicans would be stupid, moronic,” Clark added, “to let these voters just slip through our fingers.”

The most prominent MAHA moms tend to be swing voters rather than Trump loyalists, and rank-and-file MAHA supporters were largely MAGA before they were MAHA. Virtually none said they would realistically consider voting blue in November. But in close races, voters who crossed over because of Kennedy are precisely the margin. “MAHA is not going to affect the aggregate, but it could affect various districts which are very close,” concluded Bob Blendon, a Harvard professor who studies public opinion on health.

This matters for Kennedy’s position within the administration. The political case for tolerating his vaccine heterodoxy has always rested on the assumption that MAHA moms represent a genuine electoral constituency that could be lost. If that constituency is smaller, less swing-prone, and actively demobilizing, the White House’s calculation changes further. Kennedy was not mentioned once in the State of the Union address — a conspicuous silence for a man who featured prominently in the previous year’s address to Congress.

What Cassidy said when it was over

Sen. Bill Cassidy, a Louisiana Republican (Eric Harkleroad/KFF Health News)

Senator Bill Cassidy, the Republican who cast the pivotal confirmation vote for Kennedy after extracting promises the Secretary has systematically failed to keep, gave reporters his assessment of the testimony when this week’s final hearing concluded.

“Things were illuminating,” he said.

Three words. No elaboration. Cassidy, who is facing a primary challenge backed by the MAHA PAC for the crime of occasionally telling Kennedy that his science is wrong, chose those three words carefully and left the room.

In fifteen months of covering this tenure, I have not encountered a more compressed expression of Republican exhaustion with Robert F. Kennedy Jr. Things were illuminating. The measles is back. The math doesn’t work. The Secretary is shouting. The about-face on vaccines is incomplete and everyone in the room knows it. The MAHA base is demobilizing. The PAC is $98.8 million short of its target. The Twinkies are still on the shelf.

Things were illuminating.
