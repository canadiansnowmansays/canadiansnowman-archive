The Rehabilitation of a Murderer

The Rehabilitation of a Murderer

Part 2: The Cost

November 20, 2025

President Trump posing next to Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman of Saudi Arabia for a group photo at the U.S.-Saudi Investment Forum at the Kennedy Center in Washington. Credit...Haiyun Jiang/The New York Times

“Incredible in Terms of Human Rights”

At the Kennedy Center forum on Wednesday, Trump was asked about Saudi Arabia’s human rights record. His answer was revealing: “I think he’s been incredible in terms of human rights.”

The facts tell a different story.

As of MBS’s visit to Washington, Saudi Arabia had executed at least 322 people in 2025, according to DAWN. In 2024, the kingdom executed 345 people—double the rate of the past few years. Saudi Arabia now executes more people than almost any other country on earth, with the exception of Iran and China.

Seventy percent of those executed in Saudi Arabia are killed for non-lethal offenses, primarily drug-related. Foreign nationals—Egyptian, Somali, Ethiopian migrant workers—comprise 75% of drug-related executions.

On October 20, 2025—less than a month before MBS arrived in Washington—Abdullah al-Derazi became the 300th person executed in Saudi Arabia this year. He was a child at the time of his alleged crimes.

Saudi Arabia has executed at least 13 child defendants since 2015, according to DAWN.

Human Rights Watch’s Joey Shea said: “Saudi authorities have weaponized the country’s justice system to carry out a terrifying number of executions in 2025. The surge in executions is just the latest evidence of the brutally autocratic rule of Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman.”

The State Department’s own 2024 report on Saudi Arabia documents arbitrary and unlawful killings, disappearances, torture, serious restrictions on freedom of expression, and restrictions on religious freedom.

This is who Trump says has done “something incredible in terms of human rights.”

Expendable Lives

“Things happen.”

With those two words, Trump dismissed the murder of Jamal Khashoggi. But Khashoggi is not alone. A pattern emerges when you look at who this president considers expendable—whose lives can be dismissed with a shrug, whose deaths are acceptable costs of doing business.

The Fishermen of the Red Sea

Three days before MBS arrived in Washington, CNN published an investigation into Saudi Arabia’s execution of migrant workers. The story centered on Mohamed Saad, an Egyptian fisherman.

On October 21, 2025, the Saudi state killed Mohamed Saad, eight years after he was detained. His family learned of his death through a cellmate. The official Saudi news agency said a court had judged him guilty of smuggling amphetamine pills. His family says he was a fisherman.

As of now, Saudi officials have not notified Saad’s family of his killing, nor told them where he is buried.

Sound familiar? Hanan Elatr doesn’t know where Jamal’s body is. The Saad family doesn’t know where Mohamed’s body is. Both killed by MBS’s regime. Both dismissed.

Another Egyptian fisherman, Essam al-Shazly, 27 years old, sits on death row in the same prison where Saad was held. He was accused of smuggling amphetamines and 1.8 grams of a substance “thought to be heroin.” A source close to his family told CNN that he wasn’t aware of what was on the boat when he boarded. “The family had been searching for two months for him. It wasn’t until they received a phone call from him in prison they knew what happened.”

Madawi al-Rasheed, a Saudi scholar based in London, told CNN: “Saudi society has been silenced, especially with Trump’s return. Saudi Arabia thinks it has carte blanche.”

The Fishermen of the Caribbean

But the expendable fishermen are not only in Saudi Arabia. They are in the Caribbean, too—and there, it is the United States doing the killing.

Since September 2, 2025, the Trump administration has conducted military strikes on boats in the Caribbean and Eastern Pacific that it claims are trafficking drugs. As of this writing, at least 76 people have been killed in these strikes.

The Guardian reported on November 6 that “governments and families of those killed in the US strikes on alleged drug boats have said many of the dead were civilians—primarily fishers.”

Colombian President Gustavo Petro identified one of the dead as Alejandro Carranza. “Fisherman Alejandro Carranza had no ties to drug trafficking and his daily activity was fishing,” Petro wrote. Trump responded on Truth Social by calling Petro an “illegal drug leader” and cutting funds to Colombia to battle narcotics trafficking.

Christine Clement still doesn’t know what happened to her son, 38-year-old Maurice Clement, a Trinidadian fisherman who hasn’t been heard from since a U.S. strike in early September. “All I’m asking for is closure,” she told the media. “If I know he’s dead, if they find his body, I can bury him and put my mind at rest.”

Trump was direct about his intentions. “We’ve taken a very hard stand on drugs… the water drugs—the drugs that come in through water they’re not coming—there are no boats anymore, frankly there are no fishing boats, there’s no boats out there period.”

No fishing boats. Just targets.

The Fishermen of New Bedford

And then there are the American fishermen—not killed, but expendable all the same.

In January 2025, Trump signed an executive order halting offshore wind development. The order revoked existing leases and declared a moratorium on new wind energy production in federal waters.

The fishermen of New Bedford, Massachusetts—many of whom voted for Trump—had been promised that offshore wind would destroy their livelihoods. What they got instead was a president who destroyed the emerging industry that was creating jobs in their communities while doing nothing to address their actual concerns about fishing grounds.

“It’s like having the rug pulled out from under you,” said one offshore worker. “Nobody understands why Trump did it. I don’t know what Trump’s agenda is.”

Many of the fishermen who work through Sea Services voted for Trump. And if the president fulfills his promise to halt the industry, it would be devastating not only for the Northeast’s climate goals and grid reliability—but for thousands of workers in the region.

The cruel irony: In New Bedford, Massachusetts, home to the country’s most profitable fishing port, “TRUMP 2024” flags fly from dozens of boats docked in the harbor. A few of those crews work for the offshore wind companies, and at least one captain lowers his MAGA flag before setting out to the wind farms.

The Egyptian fishermen are invisible collateral of the regime Trump defends. The Caribbean fishermen are acceptable casualties of Trump’s war on drugs. The American fishermen are background noise in Trump’s war on renewable energy.

Things happen.

Render Unto Caesar

When Jesus walked along the Sea of Galilee, he saw two brothers—Simon, who would be called Peter, and Andrew—casting nets into the water. They were fishermen. Jesus called to them: “Follow me, and I will make you fishers of men.”

This is one of the most famous passages in the New Testament—Matthew 4:19. Jesus chose fishermen as his first disciples. He valued their lives so much that he called them to a higher purpose: saving souls, valuing human life, bringing people from darkness into light.

Trump claims to be a Christian. He claims God saved him from an assassin’s bullet in Butler, Pennsylvania. He has urged Americans to “bring God back” into their lives. He sells Bibles with his name on them. He created a White House task force to “eradicate anti-Christian bias.” He has characterized his campaign as a “righteous crusade” against “atheists, globalists and the Marxists.”

And yet he wonders aloud whether he’ll get to heaven.

“I want to try and get to heaven, if possible. I’m hearing I’m not doing well,” Trump said at the Al Smith Dinner. “I am really at the bottom of the totem pole.”

Jesus called fishermen to be fishers of men—to value human life and save it.

Trump treats fishermen as expendable. Mohamed Saad, executed by a regime Trump celebrates. Alejandro Carranza, blown apart by American bombs without trial. Maurice Clement, still missing, his mother begging for closure. The fishermen of New Bedford, their concerns dismissed as inconvenient.

The contrast could not be starker. Jesus saw fishermen and saw disciples. Trump sees fishermen and sees collateral.

Andrew Whitehead, a professor at Indiana University-Purdue University Indianapolis who has written extensively about Christian nationalism, told USA Today that “Christian nationalism is not a synonym for being a devout Christian.” Rather, it is about cultural identity and power. “Christian nationalism is about social control, about policing boundaries around who is an American and who is not.” Whitehead cautioned: “When a majority begins to claim persecution, that is often a license for attacks on minorities.”

Professor Robert Tuttle, who studies the relationship between constitutional law and religion at George Washington University, put it most clearly: “It’s about the relationship between Christianity and its perceived mistreatment by the government and the elites that has long been a staple of the Protestant evangelical right.” This persecution narrative, Tuttle says, leads to the false idea that Christians are an embattled minority when, in reality, about two-thirds of the U.S. identifies as Christian. “What’s really going on here is that the loss of Christian hegemony feels like persecution to these evangelicals.”

And so the man who claims to defend Christianity defends a murderer. The man who says God saved him says “things happen” to the murdered. The man who wonders if he’ll get to heaven orders drone strikes on fishermen and watches a regime execute them while he serves pistachio lamb at their leader’s state dinner.

Jesus made fishermen into fishers of men.

Trump makes fishermen into targets.

The Price of Rehabilitation

Sarah Leah Whitson, the executive director of DAWN—the organization Jamal Khashoggi founded—described what happened this week as “an extraordinary political feat.”

Mohammed bin Salman walked into the White House seven years after ordering the murder and dismemberment of a Washington Post journalist. He sat in the Oval Office while the President of the United States defended him, attacked the reporter who asked about it, and character-assassinated the man he killed. He received F-35 fighter jets, major non-NATO ally status, a nuclear cooperation agreement, a strategic defense pact, and a $1 trillion investment commitment. He attended a state dinner with the most powerful executives in America. And he left without ever revealing where Jamal Khashoggi’s body is.

“Things happen,” Trump said.

Meanwhile, Hanan Elatr Khashoggi waits for answers she will never receive. Christine Clement waits for a phone call that will never come. The Saad family waits to learn where their son is buried. The fishermen of New Bedford wait to see if the president they voted for will destroy their livelihoods.

And Trump waits to see if he’ll get to heaven.

“I’m hearing I’m not doing well,” he said. “I am really at the bottom of the totem pole.”

Even he knows.
