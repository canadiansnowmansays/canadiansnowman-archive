Democrats Draw The Line

No More Money For An Agency That Kills Citizens

While Democrats debated whether to say “Abolish ICE,” some decided to actually do it. Now the government may shut down Friday because Senate Democrats refuse to fund an agency that murders Americans.

January 26, 2026

While Democratic strategists agonized over whether “Abolish ICE” would play well in focus groups, Representative Robin Kelly was filing articles of impeachment.

While think tanks warned that the slogan was “politically lethal,” Representative Delia Ramirez was drafting legislation to defund the Department of Homeland Security entirely.

While moderates begged progressives not to say “abolish,” Senate Democrats were preparing to shut down the government rather than give ICE another dollar.

The debate over slogans became irrelevant on Saturday, January 24, 2026, when Border Patrol agents shot Alex Jeffrey Pretti ten times in five seconds. By Saturday night, Democrats had stopped arguing about messaging and started using actual power.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer announced he would block any government funding package that includes money for DHS. Senator Patty Murray, who’d been negotiating the funding deal for months, reversed course. Senator Catherine Cortez Masto, who’d voted to end last year’s shutdown, drew the line.

The deadline is Friday, January 31, 2026. In five days, we’ll know if Democrats meant what they said.

Robin Kelly: “There Will Be Consequences”

Congresswoman Robin Kelly

On January 7, 2026—the day ICE Officer Jonathan Ross killed Renee Nicole Good—Representative Robin Kelly announced she would file articles of impeachment against DHS Secretary Kristi Noem.

“I’ve had enough,” Kelly said. “[Noem] has turned ICE into a rogue force, violating the Constitution, tearing families apart, and leaving death in her wake. From Chicago to Minneapolis, her recklessness cost lives, including Renee Nicole Good. This isn’t just dangerous—it’s impeachable.”

On January 14, Kelly formally introduced three articles of impeachment. Within seven days, 100 House Democrats had cosponsored the resolution—nearly half the Democratic caucus.

“Secretary Noem has called my impeachment effort ‘silly,’” Kelly said at her press conference introducing the articles. “I want to tell her right now: Secretary Noem, you have violated your oath of office, and there will be consequences. I am watching you. Members of Congress are watching you. The American people are watching you. And most of all, we are not liking what we are seeing. If you believe impeachment is ‘silly,’ then you are not taking your job or our Constitution seriously.”

The three articles charge:

Article I: Obstruction of Congress

Noem violated federal law by denying Kelly and other members of Congress oversight access to ICE detention facilities. Kelly attempted to enter the ICE facility in Broadview, Illinois, following Noem’s own seven-day notice requirement, but was denied entry. Six other members of Congress were similarly denied access to facilities in Texas, Colorado, California, New York, Virginia, and Washington.

The Constitution gives Congress explicit oversight authority over executive agencies. Noem blocked it.

Article II: Violation of Public Trust

Noem “repeatedly violated the Immigration and Nationality Act, the First and Fourth Amendments of the United States Constitution.” She directed DHS agents to arrest people without warrants, authorized the use of tear gas against peaceful protesters in violation of a federal court order, and compromised the due process rights of American citizens.

“Renee Nicole Good is dead because Secretary Noem allowed her DHS agents to run amok,” Kelly said. “Families are forever torn apart.”

Kelly noted that Noem frequently claims she’s “taking murderers and rapists off our streets, but none of the 614 people arrested during Operation Midway Blitz in Chicago has been charged or convicted of murder or rape.”

Article III: Self-Dealing

Noem abused her office for personal benefit by steering a $200 million ICE recruitment advertising contract to Strategy Group, a firm formed just days before the contract award. Strategy Group is run by the husband of Tricia McLaughlin, a senior DHS official and close friend of Secretary Noem.

Noem bypassed the normal competitive bidding process to award the contract.

The 100 cosponsors represent districts from California to New York, Minnesota to Texas. They include Congressional Progressive Caucus Chair Adriano Espaillat, Representatives Ilhan Omar, Jasmine Crockett, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, and dozens of others.

The impeachment has no chance of passing the Republican-controlled House. Even if it did, the Republican Senate would never convict. Kelly knows this.

But that’s not the point. The point is establishing a public record. The point is forcing Republicans to vote to defend an agency that kills American citizens. The point is accountability, even if justice is impossible under current political conditions.

“Secretary Noem and her rogue agency have caused destruction and devastation across the country,” Kelly said when the resolution reached 100 cosponsors. “One hundred House Democrats from California to New York, Minnesota to Texas support my impeachment resolution because our communities have seen ICE’s Gestapo-like tactics firsthand.”

DHS responded by calling Kelly’s effort “silly” and claiming she’s “more focused on showmanship and fundraising clicks than actually cleaning up her crime-ridden Chicago district.”

Kelly is also running for Senate in Illinois. Her impeachment push has become central to her campaign.

Delia Ramirez: “Criminals In Masks”

Congresswoman Delia Ramirez (IL-3) addressed a large crowd yesterday in Chicago

Representative Delia Ramirez has been even more direct.

“Fine! Abolish ICE and DHS,” Ramirez wrote on X on January 13, responding to Third Way’s memo warning Democrats away from the slogan.

On January 21, Ramirez introduced the MELT ICE Act—legislation that would bar DHS from using detention centers or contracting with new ones, then redirect that funding to health care, housing, and social services in communities affected by immigration enforcement.

“We need to prosecute the criminals in masks and to cut and claw back ICE’s funding,” Ramirez told reporters. She described ICE agents as “criminals in masks” multiple times.

Ramirez, a member of the Homeland Security Committee, is also pushing for the committee and the House Judiciary Committee to open investigations into Noem as the first step toward impeachment.

“There’s a deep level of outrage, even from colleagues that I have not heard directly talk about [constraining ICE] before, and we need to seize this moment,” Ramirez told The Bulwark. “You hear the desperation of our people over and over. What do they say: ‘What the hell is Congress doing? Why won’t they stand up for us? Who is fighting for us? Who will defend us? How could this be legal?’”

“To me, it’s really important for us to demonstrate a sense of urgency, especially right now,” Ramirez said.

The MELT ICE Act has no chance of passing. Ramirez knows this. But like Kelly’s impeachment articles, that’s not the point. The point is demonstrating to constituents that their representatives are fighting back.

“People keep arguing, ‘Is it a bad hashtag? Is it going to lose elections? Is it going to kill us in 2026?’” Ramirez told The 19th. “People, regardless of whatever you call it, are saying we need serious accountability.”

Ramirez represents Illinois’s 3rd Congressional District, which includes parts of Chicago heavily affected by ICE operations during Operation Midway Blitz. Her district watched federal agents conduct raids, saw the shootings, experienced the occupation. She’s responding to what her constituents witnessed firsthand.

The House Vote: Seven Democrats Break Ranks

On January 22, the House voted on the DHS funding bill. The vote was 220-207.

Seven Democrats joined nearly all Republicans to pass the bill. The seven were:

  • Henry Cuellar (Texas)

  • Vicente Gonzalez (Texas)

  • Jared Golden (Maine)

  • Marie Gluesenkamp Perez (Washington)

  • Don Davis (North Carolina)

  • Laura Gillen (New York)

  • Tom Suozzi (New York)

All seven represent swing districts. All seven cited the potential damaging effects of a government shutdown. All seven will face brutal attack ads from both sides—Republicans calling them soft on immigration, progressives calling them complicit in state violence.

Representative Thomas Massie of Kentucky was the only Republican to vote no.

The bill provides $64.4 billion for DHS, including $10 billion for ICE—matching 2025 levels. But that’s on top of $75 billion already allocated to DHS in Trump’s reconciliation bill passed last summer, $14 billion of which was earmarked specifically for deportation efforts.

Even without new appropriations, ICE has massive resources to continue operations. The fight over the funding bill is largely symbolic—but symbols matter.

House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries made the decision to allow Democrats to vote their conscience rather than whip votes against the bill. When asked why, Jeffries said: “You have to ask every individual member who’s going to vote the best interest of their district why they’ve chosen to vote one way or the other.”

The decision reflected Democratic division: Leadership couldn’t unite the caucus even if they wanted to. Some Democrats in blue districts face primary challenges if they fund ICE. Others in purple districts face general election losses if they don’t.

Before the House vote, Democrats had looked divided and weak on immigration. After the House vote, seven Democrats had voted to fund an agency most of their caucus considers irredeemable, while 200 Democrats had voted to shut down the government over ICE.

Then Alex Pretti was killed on Saturday. And everything changed.

Saturday Night: Democrats Draw The Line

On Saturday morning, January 24, 2026, Border Patrol agents shot Alex Jeffrey Pretti ten times in five seconds on a Minneapolis sidewalk.

Video showed Pretti approaching agents with his phone to record their actions. An agent pepper-sprayed the group. Seven agents wrestled Pretti to the ground and fired ten shots while he lay motionless.

DHS claimed Pretti “approached US Border Patrol officers with a 9mm semi-automatic handgun” and “wanted to massacre law enforcement.”

Video showed he had his phone out.

It was the second American citizen DHS had killed in Minneapolis in seventeen days. Both were 37 years old. Both were lied about immediately. Both video contradicted the official narrative.

By Saturday night, the political calculus had completely changed.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer announced he would oppose any government spending package that includes funding for DHS—a move that could derail the entire six-bill package covering agencies that haven’t yet been funded.

“What’s happening in Minnesota is appalling—and unacceptable in any American city,” Schumer said in a statement. “Democrats sought common sense reforms in the Department of Homeland Security spending bill, but because of Republicans’ refusal to stand up to President Trump, the DHS bill is woefully inadequate to rein in the abuses of ICE.”

Senator Patty Murray, the top Democrat on the Senate Appropriations Committee and key negotiator on the funding package, had spent months working on the deal. She’d been pushing colleagues to vote for the homeland security bill, arguing Democrats had successfully fought off major increases to the ICE budget.

But after Pretti’s killing, Murray reversed course.

“I will NOT support the DHS bill as it stands,” Murray wrote on X. “Federal agents cannot murder people in broad daylight and face zero consequences.”

Senator Catherine Cortez Masto, who voted with GOP senators more than a dozen times to end last year’s 43-day shutdown, also drew the line:

“The Trump Administration and Kristi Noem are putting undertrained, combative federal agents on the streets with no accountability. They are oppressing Americans and are at odds with local law enforcement. This is clearly not about keeping Americans safe, it’s brutalizing U.S. citizens and law-abiding immigrants. I will not support the current Homeland Security funding bill.”

Senator Amy Klobuchar appeared on “Meet the Press”: “No, I am not voting for this funding. Our Republican colleagues have to stand up and stop this.”

Senators Chris Van Hollen and Chris Murphy have also indicated they won’t vote for DHS funding without restraints on immigration enforcement operations.

Republicans need at least seven Democrats to join their 53 senators to break the 60-vote filibuster threshold and advance the funding package. Without them, the government faces partial shutdown by Friday, January 31.

They won’t get them.

Trump’s Response: Deploy Homan, Investigate Omar

As Senate Democrats drew their line, President Trump escalated.

On Sunday, Trump posted on Truth Social:

“I am sending Tom Homan to Minnesota tonight. He has not been involved in that area, but knows and likes many of the people there. Tom is tough but fair, and will report directly to me. Separately, a major investigation is going on with respect to the massive 20 Billion Dollar, Plus, Welfare Fraud that has taken place in Minnesota, and is at least partially responsible for the violent organized protests going on in the streets. Additionally, the DOJ and Congress are looking at ‘Congresswoman’ Illhan Omar, who left Somalia with NOTHING, and is now reportedly worth more than 44 Million Dollars. Time will tell all. Thank you for your attention to this matter! President DJT”

The deployment of Tom Homan—Trump’s “border czar” and former acting ICE director—signals that Trump views Minneapolis not as a city requiring de-escalation but as enemy territory requiring subjugation.

The bizarre pairing of Homan’s deployment with fraud allegations is revealing. Trump mentions “welfare fraud” in the same breath as deploying his most aggressive immigration enforcer—as if investigating fraud requires sending the man who designed family separation and mass deportation operations.

Homan has no investigative authority over welfare fraud. That’s not his portfolio. His expertise is immigration enforcement—specifically, the aggressive, unrestrained kind that Trump favors.

The message is clear: Trump is responding to Democratic resistance not by constraining ICE operations but by doubling down. If Minnesota’s governor and senators won’t cooperate, Trump will send Homan to run the operation personally, reporting “directly to me.”

The simultaneous attack on Ilhan Omar—who led 100 Congressional Progressive Caucus members in pledging to vote against ICE funding—is equally transparent. Trump isn’t investigating Omar for fraud. He’s targeting her for opposing his immigration agenda.

The “left Somalia with NOTHING” language is particularly revealing. Trump is explicitly connecting Omar’s immigrant background to allegations of corruption—the same playbook he used against other progressive women of color in Congress. The subtext: Immigrants who criticize Trump’s immigration enforcement must be corrupt.

The fact that Trump announced both Homan’s deployment and Omar’s investigation in the same post is the point. He’s telling Democrats: Oppose my immigration agenda, face investigation. Refuse to fund ICE, face federal occupation.

This is authoritarianism operating in plain sight. The president deploys federal law enforcement not to investigate actual crimes but to intimidate political opponents. He announces investigations not because evidence exists but because opposition exists.

And he does it in a post that mentions “welfare fraud” while deploying an immigration enforcer—as if the association between welfare, fraud, and immigration is so obvious it doesn’t require explanation.

Democrats now face not just a funding fight but an explicit threat: Vote against DHS funding, and the president will send Homan to your state and investigate your colleagues.

The question is whether that threat makes Democrats more or less likely to hold their line.

The Shutdown Mechanics

The Senate is not scheduled to return to session until Tuesday due to the massive winter storm that hit the East Coast. The House plans to be out of session this week and would have to pass the funding package again if it’s changed.

Six of the 12 annual spending bills for the current budget year have been signed into law. Six more await Senate action. The package includes funding for around $1.3 trillion in annual spending. Its failure would mean some parts of the government would have to shut down.

Senate Republicans are scrambling. Maine Republican Senator Susan Collins told the New York Times she was “exploring all options,” including potentially stripping DHS funding from the package and passing the other five bills separately.

But doing that would require the House to return from recess and pass a revised bill—a difficult lift with little time to spare and a massive winter storm already shortening the legislative week.

“We have five other bills that are really vital, and I’m relatively confident they would pass,” Collins said.

The path ahead is uncertain. Republicans could try to pressure Democrats by arguing they’re holding vital government functions hostage over one agency. But that framing is difficult when the agency in question just killed two American citizens in seventeen days.

Democrats are also demanding policy changes be added to the homeland security spending bill:

  • Require ICE agents to use warrants for immigration arrests

  • Mandate strengthened training

  • Require agents to identify themselves clearly

  • Keep Border Patrol agents on the border rather than assisting ICE with immigration raids in the interior

These reforms wouldn’t abolish ICE, but they would significantly constrain its operations. Republicans have refused all of them.

What “Abolishing ICE” Actually Means

Part of the 2018 problem was definitional chaos. Advocates couldn’t agree what “Abolish ICE” meant. Some wanted to eliminate the agency entirely. Others wanted to dismantle it and create something new with different authorities and mission. Still others used “abolish” as shorthand for dramatic reform while keeping some enforcement capacity.

The ambiguity let opponents define the term. Republicans said it meant “no immigration enforcement” and “open borders.” Advocates couldn’t respond coherently because they disagreed among themselves.

If the slogan returns in 2026, this problem persists. ICE is responsible for both immigration enforcement and customs enforcement (the “CE” in ICE). Customs investigations include counterterrorism, human trafficking, child exploitation, and other law enforcement functions separate from immigration.

“Abolish ICE” sounds like eliminating all of that. Proponents need to be clear: Are you eliminating immigration enforcement entirely? Creating a new agency with narrower mission? Keeping customs enforcement but ending deportations? What specifically does “abolish” mean?

Delia Ramirez’s MELT ICE Act provides one answer: End detention and monitoring, redirect funds to social services. That’s not eliminating immigration enforcement entirely—it’s eliminating the enforcement mechanisms ICE currently uses.

Robin Kelly’s impeachment articles provide another answer: Hold leadership accountable for constitutional violations. Remove Noem. Reform the agency from the top.

Senate Democrats’ funding blockade provides a third answer: No new money until abuses stop. Force reforms through appropriations pressure.

These are three different strategies that all get labeled “Abolish ICE” but propose different outcomes. The lack of clarity creates political vulnerability Republicans will exploit.

But progressives counter: ICE has become so corrupted, so captured by authoritarianism, so systemically abusive that “reform” is inadequate. You don’t reform the Gestapo. You dismantle it.

That’s the fundamental disagreement: Is ICE reformable, or is it so thoroughly corrupted that only elimination works?

The Test Case: What Happens Next

Friday, January 31, 2026, is the deadline. We’re five days away as I write this.

Either Senate Democrats hold the line and refuse to fund DHS, triggering a partial government shutdown. Or they cave, fund DHS, and look weak after threatening consequences they couldn’t deliver.

If Democrats hold the line and the government shuts down, Republicans will spend hundreds of millions of dollars blaming Democrats for the shutdown. “Democrats care more about protecting criminals than funding your government,” the ads will say.

If Democrats cave and fund DHS anyway, progressives will spend the next nine months primarying any Democrat who voted yes. “You funded an agency that murders citizens,” the challenge ads will say.

There’s no good option. Every choice carries enormous political risk.

But here’s what’s different from the “Abolish ICE” slogan debate: This is actual power being used. This isn’t messaging or branding or focus-grouped language. This is the appropriations process—the Constitution’s check on executive overreach.

Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution: “No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law.”

Congress has the power—and the responsibility—to decide what gets funded. When an agency kills American citizens and faces no accountability, Congress has a constitutional duty to withhold funding until accountability is restored.

That’s not “Abolish ICE” as slogan. That’s abolition—or at least constraint—as constitutional process.

Robin Kelly understood this when she filed impeachment articles. Delia Ramirez understood this when she drafted the MELT ICE Act. Senate Democrats understood this when they refused to vote for funding.

They stopped debating slogans and started using the power they actually have.

Conclusion: Actions vs. Rhetoric

For weeks, Democrats have agonized over whether to say “Abolish ICE.” Think tanks warned it was “politically lethal.” Strategists begged progressives to say “reform” instead. Moderates worried about swing district vulnerabilities.

While they debated, ICE killed Renee Nicole Good and called her a terrorist. While they focused on messaging, ICE killed Alex Jeffrey Pretti and claimed he wanted to massacre law enforcement.

Then some Democrats stopped debating and started acting.

Robin Kelly filed articles of impeachment charging obstruction, constitutional violations, and corruption. One hundred Democrats cosponsored within seven days.

Delia Ramirez introduced legislation to defund DHS detention operations entirely and redirect the money to social services.

Ilhan Omar led nearly 100 Progressive Caucus members in pledging to vote against all ICE funding.

Chuck Schumer, Patty Murray, Catherine Cortez Masto, and Amy Klobuchar drew the line: Not one more dollar for an agency that murders citizens.

By Friday, we’ll know if they meant it.

If Democrats hold the line, the government shuts down. Republicans will blame Democrats. Democrats will argue they’re holding an agency accountable for killing Americans. Voters will decide who’s right.

If Democrats cave, progressives will primary them. Republicans will call them weak. And ICE will continue killing people with impunity because Democrats proved they’ll threaten consequences but never deliver them.

Either outcome is politically dangerous. But only one is morally defensible.

The question isn’t whether “Abolish ICE” polls well. The question is whether Democrats have the courage to actually constrain an agency that kills American citizens—not just talk about constraining it, not just debate the messaging, but actually use the constitutional power they have to withhold funding until the killing stops.

Some Democrats have already answered that question. Robin Kelly answered it with impeachment articles. Delia Ramirez answered it with defunding legislation. Senate Democrats answered it by refusing to vote for the funding bill.

But the test isn’t filing articles or introducing bills or making statements. The test is what happens Friday when the government funding expires and Democrats have to decide: Do we actually mean what we said, or was it just talk?

ICE has killed two American citizens in seventeen days. Both were 37 years old. Both were Minneapolis residents. Both were lied about immediately. Both video proved the lies.

The Department of Homeland Security was created to protect Americans. It now kills them.

Either Democrats use their constitutional power to stop funding an agency that murders citizens, or they admit they’re unwilling to use power even when Americans are dying.

We’ll find out Friday which Democrats they are.
