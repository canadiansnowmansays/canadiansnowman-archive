The Democratic Promise

The Betrayers

Part 4: Zuckerberg and Surveillance Capitalism

March 30, 2026

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon - March 2026

Mark Zuckerberg in his Harvard dorm room in the early days of Facebook, 2004 – the origin of the promise to make the world more open and connected. Source: Historical press photo (Business Insider archives)

The Pitch

Every con begins with a pitch that sounds too good to be true. Facebook’s pitch was this: connect with your friends, for free, forever.

Mark Zuckerberg launched Facebook from his Harvard dorm room in February 2004. The origin story is well known — the social network that spread from Harvard to the Ivy League to every university to the entire world. Less well remembered is the language Zuckerberg used to describe his mission. “Making the world more open and connected.” It could have been lifted directly from Jobs’s Aspen speech. Distribute. Connect. Empower.

For a few years, it even looked real. Facebook gave people the ability to find old friends, organize events, share photographs, form communities around shared interests. The platform was vibrant, personal, human-scaled. In the developing world, Facebook became synonymous with the internet itself — in many countries, Facebook was the internet, the first and only way millions of people connected to the digital world.

This is the part of the story that makes what followed not merely a business failure but a moral betrayal. Facebook did not fail to deliver on the democratic promise. It delivered on it — brilliantly, globally, at unprecedented scale — and then it weaponized the result.

The Business Model

The Age of Surveillance Capitalism by Shoshana Zuboff – the foundational critique of the model that turned users into raw material for behavioral prediction and advertising. Source: Book cover (Public domain promotional image)

The betrayal was not a single decision. It was a business model.

Shoshana Zuboff, the Harvard professor who coined the term “surveillance capitalism” in her landmark 2019 book, described the model with clinical precision: extract behavioral data from users, process that data into predictions about future behavior, and sell those predictions to advertisers. The user is not the customer. The user is the raw material. The product is the prediction. The customer is the advertiser.

This model required two things that were fundamentally incompatible with the democratic promise. First, it required maximizing the amount of time users spent on the platform, because more time meant more data. Second, it required making users as predictable as possible, because more predictable users were worth more to advertisers.

The algorithms that resulted from these incentives were not designed to inform, empower, or connect. They were designed to engage. And the most engaging content, as Facebook’s own internal research repeatedly documented, was content that provoked outrage, fear, and tribal identity. The algorithm did not distribute intelligence. It distributed rage. Not because anyone at Facebook wanted rage, but because rage kept people scrolling, and scrolling generated data, and data generated revenue.

Jobs had imagined a citizen sitting at home, filtering congressional testimony, writing informed letters to representatives. Facebook produced a citizen sitting at home, algorithmically fed a diet of increasingly extreme content, radicalized by a system designed to exploit psychological vulnerabilities for advertising revenue.

The tool of democratic empowerment became the tool of democratic erosion.

The Evidence

Frances Haugen, former Facebook data scientist, testifying before Congress in 2021 after leaking internal documents exposing the platform’s amplification of misinformation, hate speech, and harm to users. Source: Official congressional hearing photo (MIT Technology Review / Reuters)

This is not speculation. Facebook’s own documents confirm it.

In 2021, Frances Haugen, a former Facebook data scientist, leaked thousands of internal documents to the Wall Street Journal and to Congress. The documents revealed that Facebook knew its algorithm amplified misinformation and hate speech. It knew Instagram was harmful to teenage girls’ mental health. It knew its platform was being used to incite ethnic violence in Myanmar, where Facebook-fueled hatred contributed to a genocide against the Rohingya people. It knew all of this, documented all of this, and chose to do nothing meaningful about any of it — because the fixes would have reduced engagement, and reduced engagement would have reduced revenue.

The internal research was explicit. One internal presentation stated that Facebook’s algorithmic changes had made the platform more divisive, and that reversing those changes would cost the company engagement metrics. The company chose the metrics.

Rohingya refugees fleeing violence in Myanmar, where Facebook’s algorithms amplified hate speech that contributed to ethnic cleansing and genocide. Source: Documentary and news coverage of the Rohingya crisis (Reuters / BBC)

Cambridge Analytica. The 2016 election. The Myanmar genocide. The January 6th insurrection, organized in significant part on Facebook. The anti-vaccine misinformation that contributed to hundreds of thousands of preventable deaths during the COVID-19 pandemic. Each of these was not a bug in the system. Each was the system working as designed — maximizing engagement by amplifying the content most likely to provoke emotional reaction, regardless of its truth, its consequences, or its effect on democratic institutions.

The Pivot

And then, in 2024 and 2025, Zuckerberg completed the betrayal by abandoning even the pretense of democratic values.

When the political winds shifted, Zuckerberg shifted with them. He dismantled Facebook’s fact-checking programs. He relaxed content moderation policies. He traveled to Mar-a-Lago. He made a one-million-dollar donation to the inaugural fund. He restructured Meta’s content policies to align with the preferences of the incoming administration.

Mark Zuckerberg meeting with Donald Trump – the 2024–2025 political realignment that saw Meta dismantle fact-checking and moderation policies to align with shifting power. Source: News photography from the Mar-a-Lago meetings (Hindustan Times / pool images)

The man who had positioned himself as the champion of global connectivity revealed himself as something simpler: a businessman who would align with whatever power structure maximized his company’s position. The democratic rhetoric had always been marketing. When democracy stopped being good for business, Zuckerberg dropped it without hesitation.

This is what distinguishes Zuckerberg from the complicated cases in Part 3. Gates and Jobs struggled with the tension between their democratic instincts and the logic of business. Zuckerberg never struggled. There was never a tension because there was never a genuine commitment. “Making the world more open and connected” was a slogan, not a mission. When openness and connection became liabilities, they were discarded like last quarter’s earnings guidance.

The Invention of Surveillance Capitalism

Zuckerberg did not invent surveillance capitalism alone. Google, under Larry Page and Sergey Brin, pioneered the model — discovering that the behavioral data generated by search queries could be processed into advertising predictions of extraordinary value. Google’s motto was “Don’t be evil.” Its business model was built on the most comprehensive surveillance apparatus in human history.

But Facebook perfected the model by adding something Google lacked: the social graph. Google knew what you searched for. Facebook knew who you were — your real name, your real friends, your real family, your real interests, your real emotions, your real political beliefs. The data was not anonymous search queries. It was identity. And identity, processed through algorithms optimized for engagement, became the most valuable commodity in the history of advertising.

Jobs had described a world in which personal computers would give individuals the power to process information for themselves. Surveillance capitalism inverted this completely. The personal computer became the instrument through which information about the individual was processed by corporations for their own purposes. The user held the device. The corporation held the data. The power flowed upward, not outward.

The fractional horsepower motor, it turned out, had an exhaust pipe. And what it exhausted was personal data, collected without meaningful consent, processed without transparency, and sold without the user’s knowledge or benefit.

The Democratic Cost

The cost of surveillance capitalism to democratic governance has been documented extensively and is, by now, difficult to dispute. Algorithmic amplification of misinformation has degraded the shared factual foundation that democratic deliberation requires. Filter bubbles have sorted citizens into information silos where they encounter only views that confirm their existing beliefs. Microtargeted political advertising has enabled campaigns to deliver different — and sometimes contradictory — messages to different voters without public scrutiny.

Jobs imagined technology making citizens more informed, more engaged, more capable of holding their representatives accountable. Surveillance capitalism made citizens more manipulated, more polarized, and more vulnerable to demagogues who understood how to exploit the algorithmic incentives.

The democratic promise was not merely unfulfilled. It was inverted. The tools of empowerment became the tools of manipulation. The platforms of connection became the platforms of division. The networks of communication became the networks of radicalization.

And the man who built the largest of those platforms — the man who said he wanted to make the world more open and connected — made himself one of the richest people on Earth by doing exactly the opposite.

That is not a complicated case. That is a betrayal.
