The Regulatory Heist

Part 30: When Trump Sold Consumer Protection to Industry (2025-2026)

March 19, 2026

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) headquarters in Washington, D.C., US, on Sunday, May 22, 2022. Joshua Roberts/Bloomberg/Getty Images

In Part 29, we documented how Trump’s grant of corporate immunity cost American lives—workers dying on the job, miners suffocating from black lung, Americans killed by contaminated food, and elderly nursing home residents dying of neglect after industry executives donated $4.8 million to Trump’s super PAC.

But Trump’s corporate capture campaign extended beyond immediate deaths. He systematically dismantled the regulatory agencies designed to protect Americans from financial predation, infrastructure failure, and corporate fraud. And in every case, the pattern was identical: place industry insiders in charge of agencies regulating their former employers, gut enforcement, slash budgets, and watch as Americans lose billions while corporations profit.

This is the story of how Trump sold every regulatory agency to the industries they regulate—and how Americans will pay the price in stolen money, preventable accidents, and systemic corruption for decades to come.

Consumer Financial Protection Destroyed: $19 Billion Lost

The most brazen corporate giveaway came at the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau—the agency created after the 2008 financial crisis to protect Americans from predatory lending and financial fraud.

The CFPB had returned $21 billion to consumers since its creation. It oversaw an $18 trillion consumer debt market—credit cards, auto loans, student debt, mortgages, payday lending. It was, by design, the only federal agency with the power and mandate to hold banks and financial companies accountable when they cheated consumers.

Trump’s team set out to destroy it.

Stop-Work Orders: “Do Not Perform Any Work Tasks”

Russell Vought, the Acting Director of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and Director of the Office of Management and Budget, speaks outside of the White House in July 2025 in Washington, DC. Anna Moneymaker via Getty Images

In February 2025, Trump named Russell Vought—his budget director and architect of Project 2025—as acting CFPB director.

Vought had publicly advocated for abolishing the agency. In an October 2024 interview with The Charlie Kirk Show, he said of CFPB staffers: “All they want to do is weaponize the tools of financial law against basically small mom-and-pop lenders and other small financial institutions.”

On February 10, 2025, Vought emailed all CFPB staff with a directive: “do not perform any work tasks.”

Staff were locked out of buildings, phones, and laptops. Elon Musk’s Department of Government Efficiency accessed CFPB’s internal computer systems and deleted the agency’s social media accounts. All supervision, investigations, enforcement, rulemaking, and stakeholder activities ceased.

The National Treasury Employees Union sued. A federal judge issued a temporary restraining order on February 14, forbidding Trump from:

  • Deleting or destroying CFPB data

  • Terminating employees except for cause

  • Reducing or relinquishing the agency’s reserve funding

But the damage was done. Even with court orders blocking the most extreme actions, the CFPB has been effectively neutered.

Attempted Mass Firing and Funding Cutoff

Trump attempted to slash the CFPB workforce from 1,700 employees to fewer than 200. Courts blocked the mass firings, but the threat remains in ongoing litigation.

The administration then tried a different approach: refusing to request funding from the Federal Reserve. Unlike most federal agencies funded through Congressional appropriations, the CFPB is funded by the Fed. Vought claimed the agency was legally barred from receiving funding because the Fed was technically operating at a loss.

Federal judges repeatedly rejected this argument. On December 30, 2025, Judge Amy Berman Jackson ruled that the administration was “actively and unabashedly trying to shut the agency down” and that the CFPB “is hanging by a thread.”

She noted that Vought had openly predicted in October that his efforts to “close down” the agency would be “successful probably within the next two, three months.”

In January 2026, facing court orders, Vought requested $145 million to keep the agency operating through March. But Congress had already slashed the CFPB’s maximum allowable funding by roughly 50%.

The agency survived—barely. But its enforcement capacity has been decimated.

Enforcement Collapse: 37% Fewer Actions, 95% Fewer Resolutions

The numbers tell the story:

Enforcement actions dropped 37% Monetary penalties fell 32% Complaint resolution plummeted from 50% to less than 5%

Under the Biden administration, about half of consumer complaints resulted in relief. Under Vought’s leadership, fewer than one in twenty complaints are resolved.

Two senior enforcement officials resigned in protest. Cara Petersen, the acting head of enforcement, wrote in her farewell email: “It is clear that the bureau’s current leadership has no intention to enforce the law in any meaningful way.”

The Cost: $19 Billion and Counting

Senator Elizabeth Warren’s office released a report in February 2026 estimating that Trump’s changes to the CFPB have cost Americans at least $19 billion in lost financial protections and relief.

The specific harms are measurable:

Credit Card Fraud and Billing Disputes: Complaint resolution dropped from 50% to under 5%. Thousands of Americans facing fraudulent charges now get form letters instead of relief.

Debt Collection Abuse: Enforcement actions against abusive debt collectors have ceased. Companies that violated collection laws—threatening consumers, calling outside legal hours, pursuing debts already paid—face no federal consequences.

Payday Lending Predation: Rules protecting consumers from predatory payday loans were under development. Those protections died with Vought’s stop-work order. Companies charging 400% interest rates operate unchecked.

Mortgage Servicing Violations: Enforcement of mortgage servicing rules—protections against wrongful foreclosures, improper fees, and misleading statements—has collapsed. Homeowners facing servicer abuse have lost their federal watchdog.

Student Loan Relief: The CFPB had been investigating student loan servicers for misleading borrowers about repayment options and loan forgiveness programs. Those investigations stopped. Borrowers lost an advocate.

Auto Loan Discrimination: Cases involving auto lenders charging higher interest rates to Black and Latino borrowers—markup discrimination that cost consumers millions—were dropped or deprioritized.

The corporations benefiting from the CFPB’s destruction—banks, payday lenders, debt collectors, credit card companies, mortgage servicers—face no accountability. The Americans being cheated by these companies have lost their federal watchdog. Trump faces no consequences for attempting to eliminate consumer financial protection.

A separate Government Accountability Office investigation found it could not fully track the CFPB’s restructuring because the agency and White House refused to cooperate with the review.

Transportation Safety Gutted: When Industry Designs the Henhouse

If the CFPB destruction showed Trump’s willingness to dismantle consumer protection, the Department of Transportation revealed his strategy: place industry insiders in charge of agencies regulating their former employers, then watch them gut safety enforcement.

ProPublica’s investigation found that Trump’s DOT has moved to cut, soften, or delay dozens of safety regulations for cars, trucks, planes, trains, and oil pipelines. The agency’s own data shows enforcement has collapsed:

Vehicle safety defect investigations: down 50% Enforcement cases against trucking/bus companies: down 83% Pipeline enforcement cases: down 58%

Overseeing these changes are 32 DOT political appointees who previously worked for industries regulated by the department. The agency’s top posts are now occupied by:

  • Lobbyists and consultants

  • Former airline and railroad CEOs

  • Alumni of autonomous vehicle technology startups

  • Executives from shipping and infrastructure firms

  • Ex-lawyers for pipeline and trucking companies

Bill Caram, executive director of the Pipeline Safety Trust, captured the situation perfectly: “You hear of the phrase ‘the fox guarding the henhouse.’ What we’re worrying about in this situation is the fox designing the henhouse.”

Pipeline Safety: Waivers Without Oversight

In July 2025, the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration proposed changes to its process for issuing regulatory waivers. The changes appeared technical and bureaucratic.

Between the lines, agency watchers saw something more consequential: a rule that would curtail the power of agency experts to impose conditions aimed at preventing catastrophic pipeline failures.

The rule was signed by Trump appointee Alan Kochman, who previously worked as a consultant to the pipeline industry. Under the new rule, companies could more easily obtain waivers from safety requirements—with less oversight from career staff who might impose conditions to protect public safety.

Rail Safety: East Palestine and the Consequences of Deregulation

The Canadian Press, Paul Chiasson / AP

The most devastating example of Trump’s transportation deregulation came on February 3, 2023, in East Palestine, Ohio.

A Norfolk Southern freight train carrying vinyl chloride and other toxic chemicals derailed, spilling a “stew of hazardous materials” that forced evacuations, contaminated water supplies, and left residents fearing long-term health consequences. In a desperate attempt to prevent a catastrophic explosion, officials conducted a “controlled burn” of vinyl chloride—releasing a massive plume of black smoke containing phosgene and hydrogen chloride, both deadly chemicals.

The disaster was preventable. And Trump’s deregulation helped make it inevitable.

In 2015, after multiple deadly derailments—including a 2013 Quebec explosion that killed 47 people—the Obama administration issued a rule requiring electronically controlled pneumatic (ECP) brakes on trains carrying hazardous flammable materials. ECP brakes work simultaneously on all cars rather than sequentially, reducing stopping distance and the number of cars that derail in crashes.

Industry hated the rule. It would cost money. Norfolk Southern lobbied aggressively against it, arguing the expense outweighed safety benefits.

In 2018, Trump’s Department of Transportation repealed the brake requirement. The agency claimed costs exceeded benefits—relying on an analysis that, according to the Associated Press, underestimated future derailment damages by more than $100 million.

The East Palestine train wasn’t technically classified as “high hazard” under the narrow definition in the repealed rule—it carried multiple toxic chemicals but in configurations that fell outside the specific regulatory category. However, experts noted that if the broader Obama-era rule had remained in place, the train would likely have been equipped with ECP brakes, potentially preventing the derailment or reducing its severity.

Five months after the disaster, Trump visited East Palestine. He criticized the Biden administration’s response, blamed regulations for being too burdensome on railroads, and promised to champion the industry.

He didn’t mention that his own administration had eliminated the safety rule that might have prevented the disaster.

The White House responded to Trump’s visit with a pointed statement: “There is only one way [Republicans] can prove that they are finally disowning their long history of giveaways to rail industry management at the expense of communities like East Palestine: work across the aisle with us to put Obama-Biden protections back in place.”

They didn’t. Trump’s second term has continued the rollbacks. The DOT has proposed allowing companies subject to enforcement actions to bypass career staff and appeal directly to Trump appointees—ensuring industry-friendly political appointees, not safety experts, make final decisions.

The Pattern: Self-Regulation by Industry

The DOT rollbacks reveal a consistent strategy:

  1. Appoint industry insiders to top regulatory posts

  2. Reduce enforcement dramatically (down 50-83% across categories)

  3. Weaken oversight by allowing companies to appeal to political appointees

  4. Grant waivers more easily with less expert scrutiny

  5. Eliminate safety rules industry claims are too costly

The result is a de facto system of industry self-regulation. Companies police themselves. When violations occur, enforcement is rare and penalties are light. When accidents happen—derailments, pipeline explosions, trucking crashes—the regulatory system designed to prevent them has been dismantled by the very industries it was meant to regulate.

Transportation industry representatives and shareholders benefit from reduced costs and lighter enforcement. Americans who will die in preventable accidents have no mechanism to hold anyone accountable. Trump faces no consequences for handing transportation safety to industry insiders.

The Pattern Across Government: Every Agency Captured

The CFPB and DOT represent just two examples of a pattern that extends across the entire federal regulatory apparatus.

The Systematic Approach to Regulatory Capture

Across agencies, Trump’s approach follows the same playbook:

1. Appoint Industry Insiders

  • Russell Vought (budget director) running CFPB

  • 32 industry veterans at DOT

  • Wayne Palmer (mining industry veteran) at MSHA

  • David Keeling (Amazon/UPS safety director) at OSHA

2. Issue Stop-Work Orders or Hiring Freezes

  • CFPB: “do not perform any work tasks”

  • FDA: spending freeze disrupting inspections

  • All agencies: hiring freezes preventing replacement of retiring staff

3. Slash Budgets and Cut Staff

  • CFPB: budget cut 50%

  • OSHA: budget cut 8%, workforce cut 223 positions

  • NIOSH: budget cut 80%, workforce cut 90%

  • FDA: state funding cut 30%

4. Gut Enforcement

  • CFPB: enforcement actions down 37%

  • DOT: vehicle investigations down 50%, trucking enforcement down 83%, pipeline cases down 58%

  • OSHA: inspections down 20%

  • FDA: foreign inspections down 50%

5. Eliminate or Delay Regulations

  • FDA: FSMA 204 delayed 30 months

  • OSHA: 60+ safety rules under review

  • MSHA: silica rule eliminated

  • Nursing homes: staffing requirements eliminated

6. Grant Industry Requests

  • Glyphosate: explicit corporate immunity via Defense Production Act

  • Nursing homes: $4.8M donation = staffing rule elimination

  • Pipelines: easier waivers with less expert oversight

  • Climate: deregulation website for corporate exemption requests

7. Remove Public Access to Information

  • CFPB: Social media accounts deleted

  • DOT: Corporate appeals bypass expert review

8. Prevent Future Reversal

  • Supreme Court stacked with conservatives sympathetic to limiting agency authority

  • Loper Bright decision eliminated Chevron deference

  • Legal arguments designed to make reversals difficult for future administrations

  • Congressional moratoria (nursing homes: 10-year ban on staffing rules)

The Accountability Void: No Mechanism to Stop Corporate Capture

Trump faces no consequences for systematically handing regulatory agencies to the industries they regulate:

No Congressional Action: Republicans control Congress and have shown no interest in restoring regulatory protections.

No Judicial Relief: Courts have blocked some extreme actions (CFPB mass firings) but litigation takes years. Meanwhile, enforcement collapses, regulations are eliminated, and Americans lose protections.

No Electoral Consequences: Trump won re-election despite promising to “drain the swamp.” His base sees regulatory capture as “draining government,” not recognizing that he’s replaced government oversight with corporate control.

No Public Shame: Trump proudly announces these changes as victories. Industry groups openly celebrate. The corporations benefiting from deregulation donate to his campaigns and super PACs.

No Criminal Liability: Appointing industry insiders to regulatory posts isn’t illegal. Neither is gutting enforcement, eliminating regulations, or accepting campaign donations from benefiting industries—even when the connection is explicit ($4.8M = nursing home deregulation).

The Americans being cheated by unregulated financial companies can’t restore CFPB enforcement. The Americans who will die in transportation accidents can’t force DOT to rehire safety inspectors. The consumers losing billions to corporate fraud have no recourse.

The system has no mechanism to stop a president from systematically handing every regulatory agency to the industries they regulate.

What It Means: The Price Americans Will Pay

The consequences of regulatory capture are both immediate and long-term:

Immediate Financial Harm

  • $19 billion lost in CFPB consumer protections (and counting)

  • Predatory lending unchecked across $18 trillion debt market

  • Credit card fraud, debt collection abuse, payday loan scams face minimal enforcement

  • Consumers cheated have no federal watchdog

Transportation Deaths

  • Vehicle defects go uninvestigated (down 50%)

  • Trucking company violations go unenforced (down 83%)

  • Pipeline safety violations ignored (down 58%)

  • Rail derailments will increase as brake requirements remain eliminated

  • Americans will die in preventable accidents

Systemic Corruption

  • Industry designs its own regulation via appointed insiders

  • Self-regulation replaces oversight as enforcement collapses

  • Corporate profits prioritized explicitly over public safety

  • Future administrations face structural barriers to restoring protections

  • Legal precedents (Loper Bright) make agency authority harder to justify

Long-Term Infrastructure Decay

  • Safety standards deteriorate as rules are eliminated

  • Inspection capacity declines as budgets shrink and staff retire

  • Institutional knowledge lost as experts are fired or quit

  • Corporate corners cut with minimal oversight

  • Catastrophic failures inevitable as maintenance and oversight collapse

Conclusion: The Complete Regulatory Heist

In Part 29, we documented how Trump’s corporate immunity campaign cost American lives—workers, miners, food consumers, nursing home residents dying because corporations needed protection from accountability.

This part shows the other half of the pattern: Trump systematically dismantled the regulatory agencies designed to protect Americans from financial fraud, infrastructure failure, and corporate abuse.

He attempted to eliminate the CFPB entirely, costing consumers $19 billion. He placed 32 industry insiders atop the DOT and watched enforcement collapse by 50-83%. He appointed industry veterans to OSHA and MSHA and watched them gut workplace safety.

In every case, the approach was identical: appoint insiders, slash budgets, eliminate enforcement, repeal regulations, grant industry requests, remove public information, and prevent future reversal.

This is not deregulation. This is regulatory capture—the complete takeover of government oversight by the industries being overseen.

Trump will face no accountability for any of it. The corporations benefiting from the heist are more powerful than the Americans being robbed. The system designed to prevent regulatory capture has been captured itself.

Americans will lose billions to financial fraud. Americans will die in transportation accidents. And the industries responsible will face no consequences—because the agencies that would hold them accountable no longer exist in any meaningful form.

When government becomes a subsidiary of industry, Americans become expendable.

The regulatory capture documented here—the systematic handover of agencies to the industries they regulate—represents one dimension of Trump’s assault on accountability. He used executive power to protect corporations from worker deaths and consumer losses, appointing industry insiders and gutting enforcement.

But the pattern extends beyond workplace safety and financial protection into an even more fundamental domain: the air we breathe, the water we drink, and the land our children play on.

While handing the Department of Transportation to railroad executives and eliminating the CFPB to protect predatory lenders, Trump simultaneously moved to poison communities across America—weakening drinking water standards that protected 73 million people, delaying cleanup of toxic waste sites, and eliminating the legal framework that gave communities a voice when polluters threatened their health.

The woman who exposed one of America’s most infamous corporate contamination cases warned this would happen. And when it did, she watched as the President of the United States protected the polluters she had spent her career fighting.
