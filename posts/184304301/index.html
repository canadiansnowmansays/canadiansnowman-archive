Uninvestable

Why Big Oil Is Rejecting Trump’s Venezuela Dream

January 12, 2026

When the President’s 1980s Fantasy Meets 2026 Reality

January 11, 2026

On Friday afternoon, President Trump convened America’s most powerful oil executives at the White House with a simple pitch: Venezuela has vast oil reserves, Maduro is gone, American companies should “go in” and “spend billions of dollars” to rebuild the country’s petroleum industry. The executives listened politely. Then Exxon Mobil CEO Darren Woods delivered the verdict that should end Trump’s imperial fantasy: “Today, it’s uninvestable.” Two words that expose the fundamental flaw in Trump’s Venezuela adventure: you can invade a country with Delta Force commandos, but you can’t extract oil with military threats. The economic reality of modern energy markets just killed Trump’s 1980s fever dream—and the oil executives know what the president apparently doesn’t.

THE FRIDAY PITCH THAT FELL FLAT

The scene at the White House on Friday captured the collision between Trump’s vision and reality.

Trump, flanked by Vice President JD Vance and Secretary of State Marco Rubio, addressed executives from Exxon Mobil, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, and other major oil companies. He promised they would “rapidly rebuild Venezuela’s dilapidated oil industry and bring millions of barrels of oil production to benefit the United States, the people of Venezuela and the entire world.”

He announced that U.S. oil companies would invest “at least $100 billion in Venezuela.”

The problem: Nobody in the room had actually committed to that figure.

Two sources close to the companies told The New York Times they were “not aware of any such commitment” to the $100 billion Trump announced.

Then came the uncomfortable reality check from the man running America’s largest oil company.

Darren Woods, Exxon Mobil CEO: “We’ve had our assets seized there twice, and so you can imagine to re-enter a third time would require some pretty significant changes. Today it’s uninvestable.”

Uninvestable.

Not “challenging.” Not “requires careful consideration.” Not “we’re evaluating options.”

Uninvestable.

Woods said Exxon might send an “exploratory team” to Venezuela “within the next few weeks if it received security guarantees”—but stopped far short of committing actual investment.

Even Harold Hamm, an Oklahoma oil tycoon and one of Trump’s closest industry allies, hedged. “It excites me as an explorationist,” Hamm said carefully. “Everybody has that in their blood.” Then the qualifier: “But, he added, Venezuela has ‘got its challenges.’”

Translation: Thanks, but no thanks.

When pressed by Trump, ConocoPhillips CEO Ryan Lance noted that his company has been pursuing $12 billion in claims against Venezuela for assets seized during Hugo Chávez’s 2007 nationalization wave.

Trump’s response: “We’re not going to look at what people lost in the past, because that was their fault.”

His suggestion: Write it off as a tax loss.

Lance’s reply: “It’s already been written off.”

But as Lance knows, companies don’t write something off because they’ve given up recovering it—they write it off for tax purposes while continuing to pursue recovery through courts and arbitration for years. ConocoPhillips isn’t abandoning $12 billion because Trump says it’s their “fault.”

The only executives showing real interest were smaller players eager to grab market share. The industry giants—the ones with actual capacity to invest $100 billion—remain deeply skeptical.

TRUMP’S 1980S FANTASY VS. 2026 REALITY

Trump’s obsession with Venezuelan oil would have made sense half a century ago.

In the 1970s and 1980s, the United States depended heavily on foreign oil, much of it from the Middle East. The Arab Oil Embargo quadrupled prices, created gas station lines, and triggered a recession. In that environment, securing Venezuelan oil was strategic—diversifying supply sources mattered when OPEC could choke the American economy at will.

But that world no longer exists.

As energy lawyer Arnab Datta told The Atlantic: “Trump seems locked in the world of the 1980s and ‘90s, when the U.S. imported most of its oil from a handful of foreign sources. But today, America is the world’s biggest oil producer. The play for Venezuelan oil doesn’t make a whole lot of sense for the new world we’re in.”

The numbers tell the story:

Then (1980s): U.S. imported majority of oil, vulnerable to foreign supply disruptions

Now (2026):

  • U.S. is world’s largest oil producer

  • Texas alone produces nearly 6 million barrels/day (almost 2x Venezuela’s peak)

  • Canada (4th largest producer) exports most oil to America

  • Argentina, Brazil, Guyana have discovered massive new reserves

  • Global oil production nearly doubled since 1980s

The strategic calculation has inverted: America no longer needs Venezuelan oil for energy security. In fact, flooding global markets with Venezuelan oil could hurt American producers.

THE MATH DOESN’T WORK: COST, TIMELINE, RISK

Even if Trump could convince oil companies to invest, the economics are brutal.

THE DECLINE

Venezuela was once an oil powerhouse:

  • 1990s: Pumping 3+ million barrels per day

  • Early 2000s: Hugo Chávez forced out Western companies, seized assets, handed operations to dysfunctional state company PDVSA

  • Today: Production collapsed by more than two-thirds

Trump sees opportunity: Maduro’s gone, U.S. companies can restore production, hundreds of millions of new barrels flood markets.

Every expert says this is delusional.

THE TIMELINE

Ben Nussdorf, senior adviser for Department of Energy’s Office of Oil and Natural Gas (2014-2021), told The Atlantic: “I really can’t overstate just how hard this would be to actually pull off. Don’t even think about making a dollar for at least a decade.”

Optimistic estimates: 10 years, $100 billion investment to restore production to previous peak

Conservative estimates: 15 years, $200 billion investment

What’s required:

  • Train army of workers with no industry experience

  • Rebuild decrepit processing facilities

  • Replace miles of crumbling pipelines

  • Amass private security force to protect investments from cartels/militias

  • Establish stable legal framework

  • Navigate endemic corruption

THE PRICE PROBLEM

Current oil prices: ~$60/barrel

Venezuelan extraction cost: ~$80/barrel

Venezuelan crude is “heavy sour”—thick, low-quality petroleum requiring extensive processing. It’s expensive to extract and expensive to refine.

At current prices, every barrel loses money.

Oil prices are expected to remain near current levels (or fall further) through 2030 as:

  • Demand growth slows in developed countries

  • New oil projects elsewhere come online

  • Global production capacity increases

Rory Johnston, veteran oil-market analyst: “It’s hard to imagine that companies are going to be willing to take such big, risky bets in the current price environment. It just doesn’t make financial sense.”

THE IMPACT ILLUSION

Even if Venezuela restored 1990s production levels, the global impact would be minimal.

Johnston’s estimate: Restoring Venezuelan oil would lower global prices by $2-4 per barrel over a decade—about 5-10 cents per gallon at the pump.

That’s the reward for $100-200 billion invested over 10-15 years in an unstable country with a history of seizing foreign assets.

The math simply doesn’t work.

BURNED TWICE, NOT COMING BACK FOR ROUND THREE

The oil executives sitting in the White House on Friday remember what happened last time they invested in Venezuela.

2007: Hugo Chávez nationalizes oil industry

  • Confiscates assets from Exxon Mobil, ConocoPhillips, and other Western companies

  • Hands operations to state-run PDVSA

  • Companies lose billions in investments

Exxon and ConocoPhillips are still pursuing claims through international arbitration. ConocoPhillips alone seeks $12 billion in compensation—not because they’ve given up, but because recovery takes years of legal battles.

Woods made this crystal clear: “We’ve had our assets seized there twice“—emphasizing that Exxon’s Venezuelan pain didn’t end in 2007. “To re-enter a third time would require some pretty significant changes.”

What changes would be needed?

  • Legal protections that survived government transitions

  • Durable investment guarantees

  • Security for personnel and infrastructure

  • Confidence that contracts won’t be torn up

  • Assurance the next Venezuelan government won’t nationalize again

None of these exist today.

S&P Global’s 2024 report ranked Venezuela last out of 111 countries for oil investment attractiveness, citing:

  • Weak legal protections for companies

  • Confiscatory corporate tax levels

  • High corruption

  • Political instability

Other energy investment reports reach similar conclusions: Venezuela is where oil investments go to die.

THE POLITICAL INSTABILITY PROBLEM

Every oil industry veteran emphasized the same fundamental requirement: stable government.

Oil investments are measured in decades. Companies need confidence that a country won’t descend into:

  • Civil unrest

  • Military conflict

  • Armed revolution

  • Government collapse

  • Asset seizure

Venezuela today offers none of this confidence.

Trump has declared the U.S. will “run the country,” which so far means:

  • Issuing commands to interim leader Delcy Rodríguez

  • Backing demands with military force threats

  • Occupying the country indefinitely (”could last years”)

Rodríguez has mostly complied—under duress. But she’s also declared Venezuela is “ready to defend our natural resources” and “shall never be a colony ever again.”

The questions oil executives are asking:

How long will Venezuela’s government tolerate foreign control?

How long will the military accept taking orders from Washington?

How long will 30+ million Venezuelans accept occupation?

What happens when Rodríguez stops complying?

What happens when protests escalate?

What happens when Trump leaves office and the next administration changes policy?

An oil industry lobbyist told The Atlantic (requesting anonymity): “Venezuela has 120,000 troops and a 300,000-person militia. Want to run that country forever? It will make Iraq look like a cakewalk.”

Nobody knows what Venezuela’s political situation will be in a week, let alone a decade.

And oil companies aren’t betting $100 billion on “maybe it works out.”

THE U.S. OIL INDUSTRY PROBLEM: VENEZUELAN OIL WOULD HURT AMERICA

Here’s the twist Trump apparently hasn’t considered: Flooding global markets with Venezuelan oil would devastate American oil producers.

Current oil price (~$60/barrel) roughly matches production costs for most U.S. companies. If prices drop significantly:

American oil companies would:

  • Scale back investment

  • Shut down least profitable domestic wells

  • Face wave of bankruptcies across sector (especially smaller companies)

  • Struggle to repay loans

  • Cut jobs

A top executive at a leading U.S. shale company told the Financial Times: “We’re talking about this administration screwing us over again. If the US government starts providing guarantees to oil companies to produce or grow oil production in Venezuela I’m going to be … pissed.”

The cycle repeats: Low prices → bankruptcies → investment dries up → sector less able to respond to future price spikes

As Arnab Datta noted: “When oil prices get too low, you get bankruptcies. You get investment drying up. And every time, the sector is less able to respond to higher prices in the future.”

Trump’s Venezuelan oil gambit could trigger:

  1. Short-term consumer benefit (slightly lower gas prices)

  2. Collapse in American oil production

  3. Future vulnerability to price spikes

  4. Loss of U.S. energy independence

  5. Bankruptcy wave across domestic industry

The irony: Trump’s attempt to secure foreign oil would destroy American energy dominance—the exact opposite of his stated goal.

THE DESPERATION: RODRÍGUEZ ENLISTS U.S. MILITARY AGAINST RIVALS

With oil companies refusing to invest, a remarkable story emerged Friday that exposes the desperation of Trump’s Venezuela project.

The Olina tanker incident:

Late last weekend, a tanker called the Olina (also known as Minerva M) left a Venezuelan port without authorization from port authorities or state oil company PDVSA. The ship carried 500,000 barrels of oil belonging to a company controlled by Alex Saab, a billionaire Colombian businessman.

Who is Alex Saab?

  • Indicted by U.S. in 2019 for laundering money for Maduro’s government

  • Spent 2 years in U.S. prison before prisoner swap release

  • Dominated Venezuela’s oil trade until 2020 capture

  • Still has contracts exporting oil to China

  • Different faction from Rodríguez in Venezuela’s fractured ruling coalition

  • “Tense personal relationship” with Rodríguez

The internal power struggle:

  • Rodríguez + brother Jorge = control economic policy and legislature

  • Saab = owed business empire to Maduro, his wife Cilia Flores, and her relatives (most under U.S. sanctions)

  • Tanker leaving = Saab’s faction trying to preserve assets during chaotic power transition

What Rodríguez did: She enlisted U.S. military support to force the tanker back.

U.S. Coast Guard and Navy boarded the Olina in the Caribbean Sea on Friday and forced the ship to return to Venezuelan port.

Trump confirmed: The tanker “departed Venezuela without our approval” and was returning “in coordination” with Rodríguez.

The stunning accommodation:

Just one week earlier, the U.S. military attacked Caracas, killing at least 100 civilians, soldiers, and security officers according to Venezuelan officials.

In September, Rodríguez told The New York Times: “The Pentagon always had a strategic objective of obtaining Venezuelan reserves” of oil. “There’s no doubt that one of the strategic objectives is what is called regime change.”

Now: U.S. soldiers are helping Rodríguez assert control of Venezuela’s oil against internal rivals.

The occupation isn’t rhetorical—it’s operational.

What this reveals:

Without oil companies willing to invest, Trump’s Venezuela project depends entirely on:

  • Military force to maintain control

  • Cooperation from Rodríguez (who speaks “all the time” with Marco Rubio)

  • U.S. forces protecting Venezuelan government assets from Venezuelan factions

  • Indefinite occupation to prevent chaos

This isn’t oil development. It’s armed babysitting of a failed state.

THE NATION-BUILDING TRUMP CLAIMS TO REJECT

The Atlantic’s analysis cuts to the core contradiction: “Addressing all of these problems sufficiently to unlock Venezuelan oil may require the exact kind of nation building that Trump and his allies now decry.”

What “unlocking Venezuelan oil” actually requires:

Legal framework:

  • Property rights enforcement

  • Contract sanctity across government transitions

  • Judicial independence

  • Anti-corruption measures

  • Corporate tax reform

Security infrastructure:

  • Disarm/defeat cartels and militias

  • Establish rule of law

  • Protect oil infrastructure from sabotage

  • Secure supply chains

  • Train/equip professional police force

Economic institutions:

  • Functioning central bank

  • Currency stabilization

  • Banking system restoration

  • Regulatory frameworks

  • Labor market development

Political stability:

  • Legitimate government (not U.S.-imposed)

  • Peaceful transition mechanisms

  • Popular legitimacy

  • Military subordinated to civilian control

  • Functioning democratic institutions

This is textbook nation-building. The exact quagmire Trump spent the 2016 and 2024 campaigns condemning.

Iraq and Afghanistan taught painful lessons: You can’t impose stability through military force. You can’t create functioning institutions through occupation. You can’t extract resources from countries that don’t have basic governance.

Trump’s Venezuela project requires:

  • Years of military occupation

  • Billions in reconstruction spending

  • Training Venezuelan institutions

  • Building infrastructure

  • Defeating armed groups

  • Establishing legitimate government

All while pretending you’re just “letting American companies drill.”

The oil executives know better. They’ve seen this movie before—in Iraq, in Libya, in countless other places where “just get the oil” fantasies collided with messy reality.

That’s why Exxon’s CEO said “uninvestable.”

ENERGY SECRETARY SUGGESTS FEDERAL GUARANTEES: SOCIALIZING RISK, PRIVATIZING PROFIT

With companies balking, Energy Secretary Chris Wright floated a solution Friday on Fox News: The U.S. Export-Import Bank might provide “credit support” for companies making large investments in Venezuela.

Translation: Taxpayers would insure private oil companies’ Venezuelan investments.

The Export-Import Bank offers financing “when private capital is not available”—meaning when investments are too risky for companies to make with their own money.

The proposal:

  • Oil companies invest in Venezuela

  • If investments fail (assets seized, government collapses, war erupts), U.S. taxpayers eat the losses

  • If investments succeed, oil companies keep the profits

This is the opposite of Trump’s promise that companies would invest “their money, not the government’s money.”

Trump claimed Friday: “Our giant oil companies will be spending at least $100 billion of their money, not the government’s money. They don’t need government money. But they need government protection.”

But “government protection” for investments that are “uninvestable” means government assuming the risk.

Some oil executives have privately discussed seeking federal guarantees before agreeing to Venezuela operations, according to sources familiar with their thinking.

The pattern: Companies won’t invest their own capital because the risks are too high. But they’ll invest taxpayer capital if the government insures them against loss.

Socialize the risk. Privatize the profit. Use military occupation to protect investments. Call it “letting American companies do what they do best.”

This isn’t free market capitalism. It’s corporate welfare backed by military force.

CONCLUSION: YOU CAN INVADE A COUNTRY, BUT YOU CAN’T EXTRACT ITS RESOURCES

The Friday White House meeting exposed the fundamental flaw in Trump’s Venezuela adventure.

Military phase: SUCCESS

  • Delta Force captured Maduro

  • U.S. jets bombed Venezuelan positions

  • American forces killed 80+ people

  • Installed compliant interim leader

  • Declared “we’re in charge”

Economic phase: FAILURE

  • Oil companies say “uninvestable”

  • $100 billion investment has zero commitments

  • Math doesn’t work (cost > price, 10-15 year timeline)

  • Companies burned twice before won’t return

  • Political instability makes investment impossible

  • Would hurt U.S. oil industry

  • Requires nation-building Trump claims to reject

The gap between military capability and economic reality is unbridgeable.

You can invade a country with Special Forces. You can capture its president with Delta operators. You can bomb its military installations with F-35s.

But you cannot extract oil profitably from a country where:

  • Assets have been seized twice before

  • Legal protections don’t exist

  • Political stability is non-existent

  • Extraction costs exceed oil prices

  • Investment timeline spans 10-15 years

  • Government is U.S.-imposed and lacks legitimacy

  • Military occupation required indefinitely

  • Companies would need taxpayer guarantees

Exxon’s CEO understood this immediately: “Today it’s uninvestable.”

Trump apparently still doesn’t.

He invaded Venezuela expecting oil companies would rush in, spend $100 billion, restore production, flood markets with cheap oil, and somehow this would benefit America despite hurting American oil producers.

Reality: Companies won’t touch Venezuela without guarantees they know won’t materialize. The occupation will drag on. The costs will mount. The oil won’t flow.

And years from now, when the adventure collapses in failure, Trump will blame the oil companies for not having enough “vision.”

But the oil companies saw clearly from the beginning what Trump couldn’t: You can conquer a country. You can’t conquer economics.

The invasion succeeded. The economic extraction is failing before it begins.

That’s the difference between military power and market reality.

And no amount of bombing will change the math that makes Venezuelan oil uninvestable.
