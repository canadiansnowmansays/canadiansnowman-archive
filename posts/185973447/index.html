The Year That Dragged Itself Out

January 27, 2026

For those of you who wish Trump’s first year would have drifted along rather than dragged itself out, you are not alone. Rather than drift, it was more like a long grift.

And I do mean long. Every week brought another scheme, another “coincidental” announcement, another perfectly timed deal between Trump family business and a foreign government seeking presidential favors. What felt like exhaustion wasn’t just political fatigue—it was the cognitive load of tracking systematic corruption happening in real time.

For those of us who have been documenting this systematic wealth extraction since May 2025—across nine essays in this series—every one of those schemes is now backed by receipts. Every transaction has a dollar amount. Every “coincidental” announcement has a date stamp showing the quid pro quo.

On January 20, 2026, exactly one year after Donald Trump’s second inauguration, The New York Times editorial board published the accounting that validates everything we’ve tracked.

Their conclusion: $1,408,500,000.

For those of us documenting the grift? The Times piece isn’t news—it’s validation. Every scheme we’ve tracked across this series appears in their accounting. The drift we wished for became a grift we can now measure.

But here’s what makes the Times piece significant: they’re not just documenting corruption. They’re providing the mathematical framework that reveals its scale.

$1.408 billion equals 16,822 times the median U.S. household income of $83,730.

Read that again. In one year as president, Donald Trump has pocketed the equivalent of what 16,822 average American families earn in an entire year.

The Breakdown: Where The Money Came From

Let me show you how the Times’ accounting maps onto the schemes we’ve documented in this series:

Cryptocurrency: $867 million

(Covered in Parts 7, 7.5, and 8)

This is the largest single category—and the most brazen. The $867 million includes:

  • $TRUMP meme coin: Approximately $427 million in fees and trading profits from a cryptocurrency that exists solely to enrich the Trump family

  • The UAE Deal: $2 billion investment in Trump’s USD1 stablecoin by Abu Dhabi’s state-backed MGX, announced two weeks before Trump approved hundreds of thousands of advanced AI chips for the UAE

As Reuters documented in October 2025, this represents a 17-fold increase from the previous year. Trump’s crypto income in six months exceeded what most Fortune 500 companies make in a year.

The Times notes this is likely an underestimate—Eric Trump himself told the Financial Times their crypto gains were “probably even larger” than reported.

Tech and Media Settlements: $90.5 million

(This essay’s new material)

The Times calls these settlements what they are: none “were justified on the merits.” They represent corporate America’s capitulation to presidential intimidation:

  • YouTube/Alphabet: $24 million to settle Trump’s lawsuit over his account suspension

  • Meta: $25 million for the same

  • Paramount/CBS: $16 million after Trump sued over “60 Minutes” editing of a Kamala Harris interview—with the FCC approving Paramount’s $8 billion Skydance merger three weeks later

As constitutional law expert RonNell Andersen Jones noted: “First Amendment cases that Trump has settled are now probably the primary contributors to his presidential library and museum.”

Translation: Trump is funding his post-presidential legacy by shaking down the free press.

Amazon and Melania: $28 million

(This essay’s new material)

Jeff Bezos’s Amazon paid $40 million for the rights to a documentary about Melania Trump—the highest price ever paid for a documentary. Melania receives $28 million directly.

For context:

  • Disney bid $14 million

  • Paramount bid $4 million

  • Netflix and Apple declined to bid

Amazon paid nearly three times the next highest offer for a vanity documentary that cost $40 million to make, $35 million to market, and is projected to earn only $1-5 million on opening weekend.

The film will lose tens of millions of dollars. But Bezos isn’t buying a documentary—he’s buying favorable treatment for Amazon’s antitrust issues, defense contracts, and his space company’s federal contracts.

Overseas Licensing: $23 million

(Covered in Part 9)

Since Trump’s re-election, the Trump Organization has made at least $23 million licensing Trump’s name on foreign projects:

  • A hotel in Oman

These deals often require cooperation with foreign governments—the same governments Trump sets U.S. policy toward.

Qatar Boeing 747: $400 million

(Covered in Part 4)

The most audacious scheme: a “gift” from Qatar’s royal family—a $400 million Boeing 747-8 that Trump will use as Air Force One during his presidency and transfer to his presidential library afterward.

Trump asked for the plane, called it a “gift,” stuck taxpayers with the $150 million conversion cost, and will keep it when he leaves office.

Shortly after receiving it, Trump pledged to “protect” Qatar and announced lucrative new military and economic partnerships with the country.

Total documented by The New York Times: $1,408,500,000

What’s Missing: The Hidden Billions

But here’s the critical point the Times acknowledges: this $1.408 billion is a minimum, not a complete accounting.

Their editorial board notes explicitly: “We know this number to be an underestimate because some of his profits remain hidden from public view. And they continue to grow.”

What’s not included in the $1.408 billion that we’ve documented in this series?

(Covered in Part 9)

The donations aren’t in the $1.4 billion because they’re going to the ballroom construction, not directly to Trump. But the quid pro quo is identical: pay Trump, get favorable treatment.

Truth Predict Betting Platform

(Covered in Part 9)

In October 2025, Trump Media announced a partnership with Crypto.com to launch prediction markets on Truth Social where users can bet on:

  • Political elections

  • Interest rate changes

  • Federal policy decisions

  • Tariff announcements

  • Commodity prices

Think about the conflict: Trump controls or heavily influences every category users can bet on. He announces he’s “considering” new tariffs, Truth Social users bet on the outcome, Trump sees the betting patterns, then announces his decision. His company takes a percentage of every bet.

This is ongoing revenue not captured in the $1.4 billion annual accounting.

Vietnam Golf Resort

(Covered in previous essays)

The $1.5 billion golf complex outside Hanoi that received Vietnamese government approval one week before Eric Trump’s groundbreaking ceremony—while Vietnam was negotiating to avoid 46% tariffs.

Vietnamese officials offered to fast-track the project in exchange for tariff relief. They ignored their own environmental laws to expedite approval.

This deal was announced in 2025 but won’t generate licensing revenue until construction progresses, so it’s not in the annual tally.

Hidden Crypto Transactions

The Times notes: “It is probable that Trump has collected several hundred million dollars in additional profits from his cryptocurrency ventures over the past year. The Trumps have acknowledged as much.”

When the Financial Times asked Eric Trump about their estimated value of the family’s crypto gains, he said they were “probably even larger” than estimated.

How much larger? We don’t know—and that’s the point. Crypto’s anonymity allows unlimited foreign money to flow directly into Trump family wallets with zero transparency.

Saudi Deals Not Yet Monetized

The $63 billion Diriyah licensing deal with Saudi Arabia was announced in November 2025—just 48 hours before MBS’s state dinner.

But it hasn’t generated revenue yet. The licensing fees will flow over years as construction progresses. None of that future income appears in the $1.4 billion.

Same with the Maldives hotel, the additional UAE towers, the Oman resort expansions—all announced, all generating future revenue, none in the current tally.

Conservative estimate of what’s missing: Another $500 million to $1 billion in hidden or future revenue.

The real number isn’t $1.4 billion. It’s closer to $2 billion—and counting.

The Constitutional Crisis Aristotle Warned About

The Times editorial board ends with something profound—a warning from 2,300 years ago that explains exactly what we’re witnessing:

“Aristotle, writing more than 2,000 years ago, saw clearly and warned that a government whose leaders worked to enrich themselves might still call itself a republic, and might still go through the motions, but when the aim of government shifts from public good to private gain, its constitution becomes an empty shell. The government is no longer for the people.”

This isn’t hyperbole. It’s the clinical diagnosis of democratic collapse.

When leaders use public office for private enrichment, Aristotle observed, three things happen:

First, the government’s legitimacy erodes. Citizens stop believing their representatives act in their interests. Why follow laws written by the highest bidder?

Second, corruption becomes self-reinforcing. Officials facilitate personal wealth accumulation, which funds more corruption, which normalizes more extraction.

Third, the people become corrupted. They learn that society rewards those who cheat, which makes them less likely to participate in democracy—speaking, voting, paying taxes, obeying laws.

“The United States risks falling into this cynical spiral,” the Times warns, “as Mr. Trump hollows out the institutions of government for personal gain.”
