America’s Crisis of Political Violence

Part II: Butler, Pennsylvania

April 29, 2026

Republican presidential candidate former President Donald Trump is surrounded by U.S. Secret Service agents at a campaign rally, Saturday, July 13, 2024, in Butler, Pa. (AP Photo/Evan Vucci)

Before the Washington Hilton, there was a farm show grounds in western Pennsylvania and a twenty-year-old on a roof.

It is worth going back to July 13, 2024 — not because the facts are unfamiliar, but because the speed at which that event was absorbed into American political mythology tells you nearly everything you need to know about what followed.

The rally was in Butler, Pennsylvania. Population roughly fourteen thousand. Farm country. Trump country. The kind of place where a political rally doubles as a social event and the crowd arrives early with lawn chairs and coolers. It was a Saturday evening, warm, the light still golden at 6:11 p.m. when the first shot was fired.

Thomas Matthew Crooks, twenty years old, had positioned himself on the roof of a manufacturing building roughly 130 metres from the stage. He had a clear line of sight. He fired several rounds. One grazed the president’s right ear. One killed Corey Comperatore, a former fire chief who threw himself over his family. Two other rallygoers were critically injured.

The Secret Service counter-sniper returned fire and killed Crooks with a single shot. Trump went down behind the podium, surrounded by agents. Then he stood up.

And here is where the event stopped being a shooting and became something else entirely.

Trump, blood streaming from his ear, raised his fist in the air and mouthed — or shouted — the word “fight.” Three times. The crowd, which moments earlier had been diving for cover, began chanting “USA.” A photographer captured the image: Trump, fist raised, blood on his face, the American flag billowing behind him against a blue sky.

Republican presidential candidate and former President Donald Trump is rushed offstage during a rally on July 13, 2024 in Butler, Pennsylvania. (Photo by Anna Moneymaker/Getty Images via JNS)

Within hours, that photograph was everywhere. Within days, it was on T-shirts, campaign merchandise, and the kind of hagiographic artwork that typically adorns the walls of authoritarian regimes. It became, almost instantaneously, the most iconic political image of the twenty-first century. Not because it documented a tragedy, but because it documented a performance so perfectly calibrated to the American myth of rugged defiance that it barely mattered whether the calibration was instinctive or deliberate.

I want to be precise here, because precision matters when the subject is a bullet that nearly killed a president.

What happened at Butler was real. A man fired a rifle at the president of the United States from 130 metres. The bullet was real. The blood was real. Corey Comperatore’s death was real. The grief of the injured rallygoers was real. There is nothing manufactured about any of that, and anyone who suggests otherwise is trafficking in conspiracy, not analysis.

But what happened to Butler — the transformation of Butler from a crime scene into a brand — that is something different, and it is fair game for examination.

The fist-pump photograph became the centrepiece of the 2024 campaign’s closing argument. Not policy. Not platform. An image of survival. The message was not “here is what I will do for you” but “here is what I endured for you.” The near-assassination was folded into a narrative of providential purpose — Trump had been spared by God, the argument went, because his work was not finished.

That is powerful rhetoric. It is also, if you step back far enough, a profoundly strange foundation for democratic governance. The idea that surviving an assassination attempt constitutes a qualification for office has no precedent in democratic theory. It has considerable precedent in strongman mythology. The leader who cannot be killed. The leader who bleeds but does not fall. The leader whose body, marked by violence, becomes proof of his indispensability.

From Canada, watching this unfold, the dissonance was acute. In our political tradition, which is admittedly less dramatic in every conceivable way, a near-assassination would be followed by a period of genuine reflection, a security review, and an unspoken agreement that the event should not be exploited for electoral advantage. The Canadian instinct — and I acknowledge it can shade into false modesty — is to treat political violence as an institutional failure, not a personal triumph.

The American instinct, or at least the instinct that prevailed in the summer of 2024, was the opposite. Butler was not treated as a failure of security, of rhetoric, of a culture saturated in guns and grievance. It was treated as a coronation. The blood on the ear was not a wound. It was an anointing.

Police snipers return fire after shots were fired while Republican presidential candidate former President Donald Trump was speaking at a campaign event in Butler, Pa., on Saturday, July 13, 2024. (AP Photo/Gene J. Puskar)

The Secret Service failures at Butler were, by any professional standard, catastrophic. A twenty-year-old with no military training, no sophisticated planning, and a commercially available rifle was able to position himself on an elevated rooftop within direct line of sight of the president. Rally attendees had spotted him and tried to alert authorities before the shooting. Local law enforcement had been aware of a suspicious person in the area. The counter-sniper who eventually killed Crooks did so brilliantly, but the fact that Crooks was able to fire at all represented a systemic failure that should have been career-ending for multiple officials.

The director of the Secret Service resigned. Congressional hearings were held. Reports were written. The usual apparatus of American accountability — loud, thorough in appearance, structurally inconsequential — whirred into motion and produced its customary output: a narrative of reform that left the underlying conditions untouched.

Because the underlying condition is not a security gap at a particular rally. It is a country in which political violence has become so deeply woven into the culture that the question is never whether it will happen but where and when. The Secret Service can close every rooftop and screen every magnetometer and it will not change the fundamental equation, which is that the United States is a nation of 330 million people, roughly 400 million firearms, a political culture organized around tribal hatred, and a media ecosystem that converts every act of violence into a content opportunity before the shell casings have cooled.

Thomas Matthew Crooks was twenty years old. He was a registered Republican who had once donated fifteen dollars to a Democratic-aligned political action committee. His motive was never definitively established because he was killed on that rooftop. The absence of a clear motive became, predictably, an invitation for everyone to project their preferred narrative. He was a troubled loner. He was radicalized. He was a false flag. He was a symptom. He was an anomaly.

Thomas Matthew Crooks in an undated picture from his time at Bethel Park High School. He graduated in 2022. Bethel Park High School

He was, in the end, a dead twenty-year-old on a roof in Pennsylvania. And within seventy-two hours, the country had moved on — not from the event, which was preserved and amplified endlessly, but from the dead boy who precipitated it. Crooks disappeared into the footnotes. The fist pump became the story.

There is a pattern here, and it is the pattern this series is designed to trace.

An act of political violence occurs. For a brief, disorienting moment, the country confronts something that should be intolerable. Then the machinery of absorption activates. The event is claimed by competing narratives. The victim — if the victim survives — becomes either a hero or a symbol, depending on the viewer’s allegiance. The perpetrator becomes either a monster or a martyr or, most commonly, a ghost who fades from the story entirely. The structural conditions that produced the violence are acknowledged in the abstract and ignored in the particular. And the baseline resets, just slightly higher than before.

After Butler, the new baseline was this: a former president and active presidential candidate was nearly killed at a public rally by a shooter who exploited elementary security failures, and the dominant political response was not horror but mythology.

That baseline held for exactly two months before it was tested again.
