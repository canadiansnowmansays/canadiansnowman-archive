Trump is MIA

Part 23 of The Epstein Files: What the DOJ Didn’t Release

February 24, 2026

February 24, 2026

On February 24, 2026, MSNBC senior legal reporter Lisa Rubin broke a story that should have been impossible under the Epstein Files Transparency Act: The Justice Department appears to have withheld specific documents that contain accusations against President Donald Trump.

Not vague tips called into a hotline. Not unverified claims from anonymous sources. Detailed FBI interview memoranda from a woman who was interviewed four times in 2019 about Jeffrey Epstein—and who, according to an FBI presentation compiled in 2025, accused Donald Trump of sexually assaulting her when she was between 13 and 15 years old.

Of those four FBI interview memos from 2019, only one appears in the publicly released Epstein files.

The other three are missing.

And the one interview memo that does appear? It doesn’t contain her accusations against Trump. Instead, it shows a woman so afraid of the person in a photograph with Epstein that she was reluctant to show it to FBI agents “lest she incriminate someone she feared could retaliate against her.”

That photograph was of Jeffrey Epstein and Donald Trump together.

How We Know the Files Are Missing

Lisa Rubin’s reporting is based on a document that the Justice Department itself released: an index of witness statements from people who were not expected to testify at Ghislaine Maxwell’s 2021 trial, but whose statements were nonetheless produced to her defense counsel.

That index assigns a unique numerical identifier to more than 500 witnesses and lists, in numerical order, every document associated with each witness—FBI interview memos, Palm Beach police incident reports, handwritten notes, emails, everything.

For the woman at the center of this story, the index lists 15 documents reflecting her statements to law enforcement.

Rubin and other journalists, including NPR reporters, have searched the released Epstein files for all 15 of those documents.

They have found only seven.

Eight documents are missing from the public release. Among them: three FBI interview memoranda from 2019.

NPR went further. By examining the consecutive page numbering system used in the files (each page has a unique identifier starting with “EFTA” for Epstein Files Transparency Act), they calculated that approximately 50 pages are missing that correspond to these three interview memos.

As Rubin put it: “If these were important enough to give to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense, it’s unclear to me what the rationale is for withholding them from the American public, particularly given the text of the Epstein Files Transparency Act.”

What We Know About This Woman

MSNBC confirmed, through a human source, that this woman is the same person identified in a 2025 FBI presentation as having accused Donald Trump of sexual assault.

According to that presentation, her accusations include:

  • Trump sexually assaulting her when she was between 13 and 15 years old

  • Trump forcing her to perform oral sex

  • Trump physically hitting her during the same episode

The FBI interviewed this woman four times in 2019. Three of those interviews are missing from the public files. The fourth—the only one available—makes no mention of these accusations against Trump.

But the abuse by Epstein that she reported to the FBI didn’t occur in Florida or New York, the locations most associated with Epstein’s crimes. It happened “somewhere else entirely,” according to Rubin’s reporting, though the specific location hasn’t been disclosed.

The One Interview We Can See

The single FBI interview memo from 2019 that does appear in the public files tells us what investigators thought was important enough to document—and what this woman was willing to share with them.

She told FBI agents she had met Trump on one occasion, but didn’t detail the circumstances.

When asked how she understood that Epstein was a person who committed abuse against her, she showed FBI agents a photograph that a friend had sent her.

But she was reluctant to show them the full image.

Why? Because there was somebody else in the photograph, and she feared showing it might “incriminate someone she feared could retaliate against her.”

That photograph, according to the FBI interview memo, was “a well-known photograph of Epstein and Trump together.”

Think about what that means. This woman, in 2019, was so afraid of implicating Donald Trump—then a sitting president—that she hesitated to show FBI agents a publicly available photograph of him with Epstein. A photograph that had appeared in magazines and newspapers. A photograph that proved nothing except that the two men knew each other, which Trump himself had already admitted.

She wasn’t afraid the photograph would prove anything. She was afraid it would anger someone powerful enough to retaliate against her.

And now three of her FBI interviews—the ones that allegedly contain her accusations against that same powerful person—are missing from the files that the Justice Department released to the public.

The Legal Standard: What the Law Requires

The Epstein Files Transparency Act, passed with overwhelming bipartisan support and signed into law by President Trump, is explicit about what can and cannot be withheld.

The law allows redactions to protect:

  • Victims’ identities

  • Classified information

  • Ongoing criminal investigations

The law explicitly prohibits withholding or redacting documents based on:

  • Embarrassment

  • Reputational harm

  • Political sensitivity

As MSNBC legal analyst Catherine Christian explained: “It is a violation if the purpose of withholding it was for reputational harm, meaning of the President of the United States, because that is an explicitly prohibited ground for withholding a document.”

The question is simple: Why are three FBI interview memos from 2019 missing from the public release when they were important enough to be produced to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense team?

The Justice Department has not provided an answer.

The White House Response

MSNBC requested a statement from the Justice Department about the missing documents. As of Rubin’s February 24 report, they had not received one.

NPR, which was first to report the missing pages based on consecutive numbering, did receive a statement—but not from DOJ. The White House responded instead.

The White House did not address the missing interview memos. They did not explain why documents given to Maxwell’s lawyers were withheld from the public. They did not justify the absence of 50 pages from a witness’s file.

Instead, they fell back on a familiar defense: Donald Trump is “the most transparent president in history” and has “fully committed to producing documents in compliance with the Epstein Files Transparency Act.”

As Rubin noted: “That’s a defense that rings hollow to many of us who have been through these files and have seen not only over-redaction with respect to some individuals, under-redaction with respect to survivors, and now inexplicable withholding of documents.”

The Pattern of Missing Documents

This isn’t an isolated case.

Rubin emphasized that based on the witness indices, “there are many other instances in which witnesses, many of whom were alleged victims, don’t have all of their statements produced.”

The index she examined covers more than 500 witnesses. By comparing what the index lists against what appears in the public files, journalists can identify what’s missing.

“Going through those methodically,” Rubin explained, “you can see as a layperson or as a reporter what has been produced in the files and what hasn’t.”

And what they’re finding is systematic gaps—particularly in documents that might contain accusations against powerful men.

Catherine Christian raised the obvious question: “Were these documents even produced? Were they taken out by someone in the FBI or DOJ? You know, we know that hundreds of AUSAs and other members of DOJ were looking at these files and redacting. Were they even given those documents?”

It’s a question that goes back to the original Epstein prosecution in Florida—a prosecution so corrupt that federal prosecutors crafted a secret non-prosecution agreement giving Epstein immunity without informing his victims, in violation of federal law.

Are we seeing the same pattern now? Documents withheld to protect powerful men while victims’ names and faces were exposed through “technical errors”?

What Trump Tried to Stop

This reporting takes on additional significance when you remember what happened before the files were released.

Marjorie Taylor Greene, then a Republican congresswoman and Trump ally, was instrumental in forcing the release of the Epstein files through a discharge petition. Trump called her a “traitor” for her role in that effort.

According to Greene, Trump personally tried to pressure her not to push for the release of the files because, in her words, they would “hurt some of his friends.”

Greene is no longer in Congress. Trump primary-challenged her, and she lost. Her advocacy for Epstein file transparency cost her the support of the president she had loyally defended for years.

And now we know that files containing accusations against Trump himself are missing from the public release.

The DOJ’s Contradictory Statements

On January 30, 2026, when the Justice Department released the initial batch of Epstein files, they included an official press release addressing claims against President Trump.

The statement read: “Some of the documents contain untrue and sensationalist claims against President Trump that were submitted to the FBI right before the 2020 election. To be clear, the claims are unfounded and false. And if they had a shred of credibility, they certainly would have been weaponized against President Trump already.”

But the woman at the center of Lisa Rubin’s reporting was interviewed by the FBI in 2019—more than a year before the 2020 election.

And as MSNBC’s Vaughn Hilliard noted: “There’s been no indication, and I’ll let Lisa correct me, that this particular witness was ever deemed by the FBI or the Department of Justice to be a witness that was not credible.”

So which is it? Were the claims against Trump “untrue and sensationalist” submissions from right before the election? Or were they detailed accounts from a woman interviewed multiple times by the FBI in 2019 who was credible enough that her statements were produced to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense team?

The answer appears to be: different claims, different witnesses, different stories. But the DOJ’s blanket dismissal of “untrue and sensationalist claims” against Trump obscures the fact that there are multiple accusers with multiple accounts, some of which were deemed serious enough for repeated FBI interviews and inclusion in Maxwell’s discovery materials.

And the most detailed interviews with at least one of those accusers are now missing from the files the public was promised.

What Trump Has Never Said

MSNBC’s Vaughn Hilliard made a point that deserves emphasis: “There has not been a single time in which President Trump has expressed sympathy or regret on behalf of the victims of Jeffrey Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell.”

Not once.

Not when the files were first released in December 2025. Not during any of the subsequent releases in January 2026. Not during his State of the Union address on February 13, 2026, when Epstein survivors and their families sat in the audience.

President Trump has talked about the Epstein files extensively. He’s called the investigation a “witch hunt.” He’s claimed the files “absolved” him. He’s said the Justice Department should “just say we have other things to do” and move on.

But he has never expressed sympathy for the victims.

And now we know that FBI interview memos containing accusations against him from a woman who was 13 to 15 years old at the time of the alleged assault are missing from the files his administration released.

The State of the Union Silence

Earlier in February, President Trump delivered his State of the Union address to a joint session of Congress.

In the audience sat survivors of Jeffrey Epstein’s abuse and family members of victims who had died, including representatives of Virginia Giuffre’s family. This was less than two weeks after Attorney General Bondi refused to turn around and look at survivors during her February 11 testimony before the House Judiciary Committee.

Trump did not acknowledge them. He did not mention the Epstein files. He did not address the Justice Department’s handling of the release, the missing documents, or the victims whose identities had been exposed while powerful men’s names were redacted.

The preview materials released by the White House before the speech gave no indication Trump would address the Epstein survivors or the files.

And he didn’t.

Instead, Epstein survivors sat in the House chamber and watched the President of the United States—a man whose administration had withheld documents containing accusations against him—talk about everything except what had brought them there.

What This Means

Nearly two weeks after Attorney General Bondi refused to look at survivors, and weeks after the Justice Department declared its review “over,” Lisa Rubin’s February 24 reporting reveals that the accommodation wasn’t just strategic incompetence or bureaucratic failure.

It was active concealment.

Part 20 of this series documented how the Justice Department released 3.5 million pages while withholding 2.5 million more, and how they exposed victims while redacting co-conspirators.

Part 21 showed Attorney General Pam Bondi refusing to even turn around and look at survivors who stood behind her during her congressional testimony.

Part 22 explained that the files revealed elite impunity rather than a trafficking ring, and that only two people—Epstein and Maxwell—have been held accountable in twenty years.

Now we know something even more specific: Documents containing accusations against the President of the United States appear to have been systematically withheld from the public release, in potential violation of the law that required their disclosure.

This isn’t about vague conspiracy theories or unverified claims. This is about FBI interview memos that exist, that were important enough to give to Ghislaine Maxwell’s lawyers, that can be identified by index number and consecutive page numbering—and that are simply not in the files the Justice Department claims represent full compliance with the law.

Lisa Rubin’s reporting provides the roadmap for what’s missing. NPR has calculated the pages. Multiple journalists have confirmed the gaps.

The question is whether Congress will force the Justice Department to explain why documents protected by federal law are missing from the public record.

And whether the President of the United States will explain why FBI interviews with a woman who says he sexually assaulted her as a child are nowhere to be found in the “most transparent” release in history.

Nixon’s Ghost

Donald Trump hates being compared to Richard Nixon.

He hates the association with disgrace, with resignation, with the fall from grace that ended Nixon’s presidency. He hates Watergate being invoked as a parallel to his own scandals.

But here we are.

Nixon tried to cover up a break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters. Trump’s Justice Department appears to be covering up FBI interviews about the sexual assault of a child.

Nixon had 18.5 minutes of missing tape. Trump has 50-plus pages of missing FBI interview memos.

Nixon used the Justice Department to go after his enemies. Trump’s DOJ has prosecuted James Comey, Letitia James, Jerome Powell, and six members of Congress—while withholding documents about accusations against the President himself.

Nixon resigned before he could be impeached and removed from office. Trump is still president, still withholding documents, still refusing to acknowledge Epstein’s victims.

The Epstein cover-up is many orders of magnitude bigger than Watergate.

Watergate was about political power and political espionage. The Epstein cover-up is about child sex trafficking, systematic abuse, and a justice system that has held exactly two people accountable in twenty years while protecting everyone else.

Watergate brought down a president.

The Epstein files haven’t even forced an apology.

Nixon understood, eventually, that the cover-up was worse than the crime. That the missing tapes, the obstruction, the lies to the American people would be his legacy.

Trump apparently believes he can withhold FBI interviews about child sexual assault accusations, declare the investigation “over,” and move on.

History will remember which calculation was correct.
