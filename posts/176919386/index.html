The Ministry of Truth

Now This is Fake News

Part 2: Why this is unconstitutional, historically dangerous, and what we lose when propaganda replaces journalism

October 23, 2025

The First Amendment Crisis Hiding in Plain Sight

What’s happening at the Pentagon isn’t just bad policy—it may be unconstitutional.

Legal experts have pointed to several established First Amendment principles that Hegseth’s policy appears to violate. First, there’s the doctrine of prior restraint, famously established in the 1971 Pentagon Papers case. The Supreme Court ruled then that the government cannot require journalists to submit their reporting for approval before publication. Yet the Pentagon’s new policy does exactly that, requiring all information to be “approved for public release by an appropriate authorizing official before it is released, even if it is unclassified.”

Then there’s the public forum doctrine. As First Amendment lawyer Carey Shenkman explained, “Once the government opens its doors to some kind of journalistic access, then it’s in effect creating a form of public forum, even if that’s limited in some way. And what the First Amendment offers as a protection is that once a government invites the press or the public in, they then can’t discriminate and start to exclude either the press or individuals based on their political viewpoints, their expressive conduct, or their reporting.”

That’s precisely what’s happened here. The Pentagon hasn’t closed its press operation entirely—it’s simply replaced journalists who ask tough questions with those who won’t. It’s viewpoint discrimination in its purest form: maintaining access for friendly outlets while revoking it for adversarial ones.

Media law professor Jane Kirtley at the University of Minnesota was even more direct: “The idea that it is somehow improper for journalists to seek out information from the agency that they’re covering is very problematic. If you’re going to ask a source for material, then it’s on that source to make a judgment about whether they’re allowed to distribute it or not.” The Pentagon’s policy reverses this, making it the journalist’s responsibility—and potential crime—to even ask the question.

Could this be challenged in court? Possibly. The Associated Press sued White House officials in February after the administration restricted AP reporters’ access when the agency refused to call the Gulf of Mexico by Trump’s preferred name, the “Gulf of America.” A judge initially ruled for the AP, finding First Amendment violations, though an appeals court issued a stay. The legal battle continues, but it establishes that courts recognize limits on the government’s ability to punish press outlets for their coverage through access restrictions.

The Pentagon situation is even more egregious because it’s not about retaliation for one story—it’s about establishing a permanent system where only pre-approved information can be reported.

The Stakes

Barbara Starr, who covered the Pentagon for CNN for years, put it plainly: “Military families who have their sons and daughters serving, they want to know everything and they want to know it fast.”

But now they won’t get it. Or they’ll only get what Pete Hegseth wants them to know.

This matters right now, today, in concrete ways. The Trump administration is conducting military operations in South America that legal experts say may be illegal—but good luck getting real reporting on that from outlets that can’t ask unauthorized questions. Russia’s war on Ukraine continues, including recent attacks on kindergartens with children present, while Putin ignores Trump’s peace overtures—but the press corps that owes its access to the administration isn’t going to aggressively question policy failures. The Pentagon just issued a new memo restricting military personnel from contacting Congress without going through official channels first—part of what reporters have described as Hegseth’s increasing paranoia about leaks revealing chaos and incompetence within the department.

Without independent oversight, there’s no accountability when things go wrong. No investigation of potential war crimes, as when Seymour Hersh exposed the My Lai massacre. No scrutiny of defense spending and contractor fraud. No reporting on military failures, sexual assault epidemics, or leadership misconduct.

James Risen, a Pulitzer Prize-winning national security reporter, noted that “I covered the CIA for a long time, and the CIA does not give reporters press passes. And in a way, I think it forced reporters who cover the CIA to be more adversarial.” He sees the current situation as potentially forcing better, tougher coverage from outside the building—if news organizations are willing to invest the resources.

But that’s a big if. Reporting from outside is harder, slower, and more expensive. Not every outlet has the resources to pursue it aggressively. And that’s what Hegseth is counting on: that over time, coverage will soften, investigations will become rarer, and the American public will simply know less about what their military does.

A Playbook We’ve Seen Before

The irony is almost too perfect. For years, figures like James O’Keefe have appropriated the term “Pravda” - Soviet state propaganda - to attack mainstream journalism. His book “American Pravda” claims to expose “fake news” in traditional media.

But now we’re witnessing the creation of actual American Pravda: government-approved outlets producing government-approved coverage, with the Pentagon’s stamp of legitimacy.

This isn’t new. We’ve watched this exact sequence play out in authoritarian regimes around the world.

Step one: Discredit the independent press as biased, corrupt, or treasonous. Call them “enemies of the people.” Accuse them of spreading “fake news.” Convince the public that journalists can’t be trusted.

Step two: Create competing “official” media outlets that present themselves as alternatives to the “biased” mainstream. In Soviet Russia, it was Pravda—literally “Truth”—which printed whatever the Communist Party wanted citizens to believe. The irony of calling state propaganda “Truth” would be funny if it weren’t so chilling.

Step three: Control access to information. Make it increasingly difficult or dangerous for independent journalists to do their jobs. Restrict physical access, criminalize the solicitation of information, threaten sources with prosecution.

Step four: Gradually normalize this new reality. Over time, citizens forget what independent journalism looked like. The “official” version of events becomes the only version readily available.

What made America different—what was supposed to make us different—was the First Amendment. The principle that government cannot control the press, cannot dictate coverage, cannot punish journalists for doing their jobs. The Pentagon Papers case in 1971 was supposed to settle this: the government tried to stop the New York Times and Washington Post from publishing classified documents about the Vietnam War, and the Supreme Court said no. Prior restraint is unconstitutional. The public’s right to know trumps the government’s desire for secrecy.

That precedent stood for 54 years. Until last month.

The erosion didn’t happen overnight. In February, Hegseth removed the New York Times and NPR from their dedicated office spaces, replacing them with the New York Post, Breitbart, and OAN. In May, reporters were told they needed Pentagon escorts in more areas of the building. Each restriction was presented as minor, reasonable, no big deal.

But restrictions compound. Each small step makes the next one easier to justify. And now we’re at a point where the Pentagon can announce a press corps full of conspiracy theorists and Russian propaganda beneficiaries, and roughly half the country will think that’s just fine—because those outlets tell them what they want to hear.

The Broader Pattern: Press Freedom Under Siege

The Pentagon purge isn’t happening in isolation. It’s part of a coordinated assault on press freedom across the entire Trump administration.

The AP lawsuit over “Gulf of America” is instructive. When the Associated Press refused to adopt President Trump’s preferred name for the Gulf of Mexico, the administration restricted AP reporters’ access to White House events. The AP sued, arguing this was viewpoint discrimination in violation of the First Amendment. They won initially, with a judge ruling that the government cannot punish news organizations for their editorial decisions by restricting access. But the battle continues on appeal, and in the meantime, the restrictions remain.

The White House has simultaneously expanded access for friendly outlets and influencers. Special briefings are held for pro-Trump podcasters and “new media” personalities who can be counted on for favorable coverage. A “new media seat” in the briefing room sometimes goes to mainstream outlets like Semafor, but often to partisan Trump supporters.

Cabinet officials have followed suit, limiting traditional press access while embracing friendly media. When Hegseth traveled to Europe earlier this year, he invited Jack Posobiec from Human Events—though Posobiec ultimately didn’t attend. The message was clear: access is a reward for friendly coverage.

President Trump himself has sued the New York Times and Wall Street Journal over their coverage, attempting to use the legal system to punish outlets that report critically on him. These lawsuits face long odds in court—First Amendment protections for the press are strong when it comes to reporting on public figures—but they serve another purpose: intimidation. Legal battles are expensive and time-consuming. The threat of litigation can make editors more cautious, reporters more hesitant.

And now the Pentagon has taken the next logical step: simply replacing the press corps entirely with outlets that won’t cause problems.

This is a pattern. This is a strategy. And it’s working.

The Orwellian Inversion

What makes this moment so darkly perfect is the complete inversion of language. The outlets that practice actual journalism—verification, fact-checking, seeking multiple sources, maintaining editorial independence—are dismissed as “fake news.” Meanwhile, outlets that have agreed to function as government mouthpieces are celebrated as “real news” that “circumvents mainstream media lies.”

Jordan Conradson’s declaration that he’s “restoring honest journalism” by agreeing not to pursue unauthorized information is Orwell’s Ministry of Truth made manifest. War is Peace. Freedom is Slavery. Prior Restraint is Honest Journalism.

Pentagon spokesperson Sean Parnell’s celebration of journalists who “self-deported” deserves special mention. Barbara Starr responded perfectly: “’Self deport’? Naw. Too busy working!” Indeed, the reporters who walked out haven’t stopped covering the Pentagon. They’re just doing it the hard way now—without the access that made it easier, but with their integrity intact. They’re still investigating, still reporting, still asking the questions no one in the new press corps will ask.

The outlets with Pentagon credentials are the ones who’ve surrendered their independence in exchange for access. They’re the ones who’ve agreed to let the government define the boundaries of acceptable journalism. They’re the ones producing actual propaganda while calling it truth.

And yet they’re held up as the model of “honest journalism” while the AP, Reuters, and the New York Times are cast as partisan hacks.

What Fox News Knew

The most telling detail in this entire saga is Fox News’s decision.

Pete Hegseth worked at Fox. He was a Fox & Friends host. Fox News is the most-watched cable news network among Trump supporters. It has built its brand on being friendly to Republican administrations. And even Fox said no.

When the outlet most sympathetic to your administration looks at your press policy and says “this threatens core journalistic protections,” you’ve crossed a line that should frighten everyone. Fox’s refusal to sign strips away any pretense that this is about “balancing” media coverage or reaching new audiences. This is about replacing journalism with propaganda, period.

Fox understands something the new Pentagon press corps apparently doesn’t: access without independence isn’t journalism. It’s public relations. And once you cross that line—once you agree that the government can dictate what questions you ask and what information you pursue—you’re not a news organization anymore. You’re a marketing department.

Even Newsmax, hardly a bastion of aggressive investigative reporting, refused to sign. So did the Washington Times and Washington Examiner. These are outlets with clear conservative perspectives, outlets that generally support Republican administrations, outlets that have every incentive to maintain good relationships with Pentagon leadership.

And they still said no.

That should tell you everything about how extreme this policy is.

Conclusion: When Words Lose All Meaning

We now have Pentagon press credentials held by an outlet that received Russian propaganda payments, a bankruptcy-filing conspiracy site that settled defamation lawsuits, and a streaming service run by an election denier—while the Associated Press, Reuters, and The New York Times cover the military from outside the building.

And we’re told this represents “honest journalism” replacing “fake news.”

If this is what “real news” looks like—pre-approved, unauthorized-question-free stenography—then perhaps we need to revisit what “fake news” meant all along. Perhaps it was never about accuracy versus inaccuracy. Perhaps it was always about control versus independence. Approved versus adversarial. Propaganda versus journalism.

The outlets now holding Pentagon credentials aren’t there to report the news. They’re there to ratify it. And in doing so, they’ve become the very thing they claimed to oppose: fake news, in its purest form—information shaped by power, for power, distributed to maintain power.

The projection is complete. For years we heard about “fake news” from mainstream media. Now we have the real thing: government-approved outlets producing government-approved coverage, calling it independent journalism while attacking anyone who practices actual reporting as liars and propagandists.

The journalists who walked out are still working. They’re still reporting. They’re still asking the hard questions. The only difference is that now, Pete Hegseth doesn’t have to listen to them inside his building. Which was, perhaps, the point all along. Not to improve journalism, but to eliminate it. Not to reach new audiences, but to control the message. Not to fight fake news, but to create it.

Welcome to the new Pentagon press corps. Now this is fake news.
