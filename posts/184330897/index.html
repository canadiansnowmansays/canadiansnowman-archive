The Iran Crisis

Negotiation or War

Iran’s Impossible Choice

January 14, 2026

The impossible choice: Trump cancels all meetings with Iran, promising protesters "help is on its way." Foreign Minister Araghchi says Iran is ready for "war or negotiations." With death toll reaching 2,000-3,000, both sides prepare for violence while claiming willingness to talk.

Part 6 of The Iran Crisis Series

Monday, January 12, 2026. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi stood before assembled foreign ambassadors in Tehran and delivered a message of calculated ambiguity:

“We are not looking for war, but we are prepared for war—even more prepared than the previous war,” he said, referring to the June 2025 12-day conflict with Israel and the United States. “We are also ready for negotiations, but negotiations that are fair, with equal rights and mutual respect.”

Hours later, President Trump told reporters aboard Air Force One that Iran’s leaders had called him Saturday, wanting to negotiate. “I think they are tired of being beat up by the United States,” he said. “A meeting is being set up, but we may have to act because of what’s happening.”

So which is it? War or negotiations?

The answer is both—and that ambiguity itself has become a weapon. Iran signals readiness for dialogue while promising military resistance. Trump threatens strikes while claiming diplomatic progress. Each side jockeys for advantage, using the other’s uncertainty as leverage.

But beneath the tactical maneuvering lies a strategic reality: Iran faces an impossible choice between accommodation that threatens regime survival and resistance that might trigger regime destruction. And Trump, despite his bluster, faces equally uncomfortable options—none of which guarantee the outcome he wants.

This is the high-stakes poker game playing out as hundreds of Iranians lie dead in morgues and thousands more sit in prisons facing execution.

The Pattern We’ve Seen Before

Trump’s approach to Iran follows a familiar playbook he’s deployed repeatedly in his second term:

Step 1: Threaten overwhelming military force Step 2: Target government signals willingness to negotiate
Step 3: Trump claims victory, suggests meeting imminent Step 4: Negotiations stall or produce minimal results Step 5: Return to Step 1

We saw this with North Korea in Trump’s first term. Threats of “fire and fury” gave way to summit meetings, which produced photo opportunities but no denuclearization. The pattern ended in failure—North Korea still has nuclear weapons and expanded its arsenal during Trump’s presidency.

We’re seeing it now with Colombia. Trump threatened President Petro with tariffs and military action. Petro initially defied him, then capitulated within 72 hours after a phone call “partly brokered by Rand Paul.” They “agreed to soon meet” but meaningful policy changes remain unclear.

And we’re seeing it with Iran. Trump threatens strikes. Iran signals diplomatic openness. Both sides claim they’re negotiating from strength. Neither side trusts the other. Both prepare for violence while talking about peace.

The question is whether Iran follows the Colombia model (capitulate, accommodate, survive) or becomes the next Venezuela (resist, get invaded, lose everything).

What Iran Is Actually Offering

Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei clarified Monday that “communication channels were open” between Araghchi and Steve Witkoff, Trump’s envoy to the Middle East.

“Iran has never left the negotiating table,” Baghaei said, “but it will not engage in one-sided negotiations.”

That last phrase—”one-sided negotiations”—is critical. It reveals Iran’s fundamental problem: What does Iran have to negotiate with?

What the U.S. wants from Iran (per State Department):

  • End uranium enrichment completely

  • End support for terrorist proxies (Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis, Iraqi militias)

  • Massively limit ballistic missile program

  • Stop threatening Israel

  • Allow international inspections

  • Change regime behavior fundamentally

What Iran wants from the U.S.:

  • Lift sanctions

  • Unfreeze assets

  • Allow oil exports

  • Stop military threats

  • Recognize Iran’s regional influence

  • Respect sovereignty

The asymmetry is stark. The U.S. demands Iran abandon its nuclear program, its missile program, its regional allies, and its foreign policy—essentially demanding regime transformation. Iran wants sanctions relief and to be left alone.

As Vali Nasr observed: “The Iranian definition of what negotiations mean is very, very different from the American conception. The Venezuelan example shows that the U.S. is not interested in nickel-and-diming; the U.S. is basically asking [Iran] to bend the knee.”

This is not a negotiation between equals. This is a demand for surrender dressed up as diplomacy.

The Venezuela Model: Accommodate or Be Destroyed

Destruction in Venezuela

Iran’s leadership has studied Venezuela intensively. As documented in Part 2 of this series, the Venezuela operation was “intensely covered on state television and mentioned by Iranian officials in online posts and articles.”

What lessons did they learn?

Lesson 1: Military invasion is real. Trump actually did it. Not just threats—actual Delta Force raid, actual president in Miami jail, actual U.S. control of Venezuelan oil.

Lesson 2: Russia and China won’t help. Moscow and Beijing did nothing when the U.S. seized Maduro. If they won’t protect Venezuela, they won’t protect Iran.

Lesson 3: Accommodation works—to a point. Delcy Rodríguez went from defiant rhetoric about “illegitimate military aggression” to signing over control of Venezuela’s oil within 72 hours. She survived by accommodating. Maduro resisted and sits in a Miami jail.

Lesson 4: But accommodation means losing everything. Venezuela now has U.S. forces controlling its oil sales “indefinitely.” The government survives, but sovereignty is gone.

So Iran’s choice is:

Option A - Accommodate: Abandon nuclear program, end proxy support, limit missiles, accept intrusive inspections, become a U.S. client state Result: Regime might survive, but Islamic Republic’s entire purpose—resistance to U.S. hegemony—disappears

Option B - Resist: Maintain nuclear threshold status, support remaining proxies, build missiles, reject U.S. demands Result: Risk U.S. military strikes, possible regime change, potential invasion

Neither option is acceptable. Accommodation destroys the regime ideologically. Resistance risks destroying it physically.

This is the definition of an impossible choice.

What Trump’s Threats Actually Mean

When Trump says he’s been briefed on “military strike options” and is “looking at some very strong options,” what does he actually mean?

According to reporting from The New York Times and Washington Post, Trump’s options include:

Lethal Force Options:

  • Strikes on Iranian military targets

  • Strikes on nuclear facilities (again—they were already bombed in June 2025)

  • Strikes on Revolutionary Guards installations

  • Targeted strikes on leadership (potentially including Khamenei)

  • Support for Israeli strikes

  • Naval operations (seizing more tankers, blockading oil exports)

Nonlethal Options:

  • Cyberattacks on Iranian military systems

  • Cyberattacks to restore internet access for protesters

  • Additional economic sanctions (though Iran is already maximally sanctioned)

  • Covert support for opposition groups

  • Diplomatic isolation campaigns

The constraints Trump faces:

1. No carrier strike group in region. Victoria Taylor of the Atlantic Council noted: “There are still ways to strike targets, but we are not positioned as we normally would be to undertake strikes of this significance in Iran.” The U.S. can strike, but not with the overwhelming force Trump might want.

2. Risk of retaliation. Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf warned that if attacked, Iran “could target the U.S., Israel and international shipping lanes.” Iran has missiles that can reach U.S. bases in the region, can strike Israel, and can close the Strait of Hormuz (through which 20% of global oil flows).

3. No guarantee of regime change. Victoria Taylor again: “Even if you take out the supreme leader, you could still wind up with another regime figure assuming leadership. There is no guarantee that prevents the [Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps] or other regime leaders from assuming control.”

4. Venezuela isn’t going well. As documented in my “Uninvestable” essay, Trump’s Venezuela operation is hitting serious problems. Oil companies say Venezuela is “uninvestable.” The Senate voted 52-47 against him. If Venezuela is proving harder than expected, why try something even more difficult in Iran?

5. Strategic overextension. As one person close to Secretary of State Rubio noted: The U.S. “can’t be everywhere at once.” Trump is dealing with Venezuela, Gaza, Ukraine, and now Iran. Military and diplomatic resources have limits.

So Trump’s threats carry weight—he might actually strike—but they’re not unlimited. He faces real constraints that Iran’s leadership understands.

The Oman Channel: Where Negotiations Actually Happen

While public rhetoric escalates, actual negotiations happen through quiet channels.

Araghchi met Saturday with his Omani counterpart, Badr Albusaidi, who traveled to Tehran for talks. Oman has historically served as intermediary between Tehran and Washington—it’s where much of the 2015 nuclear deal negotiation happened.

This is how serious diplomacy works: Public threats establish leverage. Private channels explore actual deals. Each side uses public pressure to improve its private negotiating position.

But skepticism about Iranian sincerity is warranted. Western intelligence analysts and regional diplomats privately question whether Iran’s negotiation signals represent genuine willingness to compromise or tactical delay while the regime crushes protests.

The pattern is familiar from previous crises: Iran signals openness to talks when under pressure, negotiations drag on for months, Iran uses the time to consolidate position domestically or advance nuclear program, talks eventually collapse. Rinse and repeat.

“Iran has perfected the art of negotiating while not negotiating,” one former U.S. diplomat told researchers. “They’ll talk endlessly about talks, establish channels, send envoys—all while doing nothing substantial. It’s delay tactic dressed as diplomacy.”

Current signals could mean:

  • Genuine desire to avoid war - Regime recognizes military strikes could be devastating

  • Buying time to crush protests - Keep Trump talking while massacring demonstrators

  • Splitting international community - Show “reasonableness” to European allies, undermine U.S. position

  • Internal political cover - Hardliners can tell moderates “we tried diplomacy” before escalating

The gap between positions remains enormous regardless of intent:

What the U.S. State Department says it needs: “As President Trump has repeatedly said, the United States has kept the door open for a serious and direct dialogue. Any deal would require the Iranian regime to end uranium enrichment, end its support for terrorist proxies and massively limit its ballistic missile program.”

What Iran says it will accept: “Fair negotiations, with equal rights and mutual respect.”

These positions don’t converge. “End uranium enrichment” is non-negotiable for the U.S. Maintaining nuclear threshold capability is non-negotiable for Iran. “End support for terrorist proxies” means abandoning Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis—Iran’s entire regional strategy. “Massively limit ballistic missile program” means giving up Iran’s primary deterrent against U.S./Israeli attack.

Nasr’s assessment is blunt: “I don’t see Khamenei and the Revolutionary Guard giving up on missiles, let alone the nuclear program.”

So what are they actually negotiating about?

The North Korea Precedent: Talks That Lead Nowhere

Trump’s Iran approach mirrors his North Korea strategy from 2017-2019, which ultimately failed:

2017: “Fire and fury” threats, military buildup, talk of preventive war

2018: Sudden pivot to diplomacy, Singapore summit, Trump/Kim photo op, declarations of friendship

2019: Hanoi summit collapses, no deal reached, North Korea continues weapons development

2020-2024: Stalemate continues, North Korea advances nuclear/missile programs

Result: Complete failure. North Korea has more nuclear weapons and better missiles than when Trump took office.

The pattern: Trump threatens war, accepts diplomatic opening, oversells progress, negotiations stall on substance, nothing changes.

Will Iran follow this pattern?

The risk for Trump is that Iran learns from North Korea: Talk to buy time, make minimal concessions, wait out Trump’s attention span, preserve core capabilities.

The risk for Iran is that Trump learns from North Korea: Don’t accept talks as substitute for action, don’t oversell diplomatic progress, strike if negotiations aren’t producing results.

Both sides are trying to avoid their worst outcomes while the other side does the same. This creates a negotiating dynamic where neither side can trust the other’s signals.

What “Bending the Knee” Actually Requires

Nasr’s phrase—”the U.S. is basically asking [Iran] to bend the knee”—captures the fundamental issue.

The United States isn’t asking for arms control or conflict management or crisis stabilization. The U.S. is demanding regime transformation:

Ideologically: Give up resistance to U.S./Israeli regional dominance Militarily: Abandon nuclear deterrent and ballistic missiles
Strategically: Cut ties to proxies, accept isolation Economically: Open to Western investment/influence on U.S. terms Politically: Accept intrusive inspections that undermine sovereignty

This isn’t negotiation. This is capitulation.

For the Islamic Republic to accept these terms would be to admit its entire 46-year project failed. Revolutionary Iran’s purpose was resisting Western hegemony, supporting oppressed Muslims, maintaining independence. To “bend the knee” means abandoning everything the revolution stood for.

Could Khamenei do this? Politically, probably not. He’d face internal revolt from hardliners, Revolutionary Guards, clerical establishment. He’d be seen as betraying Khomeini’s legacy. He might not survive politically even if he tried.

Could a successor do this? Maybe. If the regime is collapsing anyway, a new leader might accept humiliating terms to survive. But that requires concluding the regime is doomed—which brings us back to the question of security force defections analyzed in Part 5.

As long as the IRGC thinks it can win through violence, Iran won’t bend the knee. Only if commanders conclude the regime is doomed would Tehran accept transformational concessions.

The Protest Wildcard: What If Demonstrations Continue?

Reported protests in Iran between Dec. 29, 2025, and Jan. 13, 2026

Everything discussed above assumes relatively stable conditions for negotiation. But what if protests continue? What if more hundreds or thousands die? What if bazaar strikes spread? What if oil workers actually strike?

Then both Iran and the U.S. face complicating factors:

For Iran:

  • Can’t negotiate from strength if losing control domestically

  • But can’t crush protests without more massacres

  • Which might trigger U.S. intervention

  • Which makes negotiations impossible

For Trump:

  • Can’t ignore mass protests while negotiating with regime

  • But can’t intervene militarily without major commitment

  • Can’t threaten intervention without following through eventually

  • Can’t follow through without accepting enormous risks

The protests create a three-way dynamic: protesters, regime, and Trump. Each affects the others:

  • If protests succeed → regime falls, no one to negotiate with

  • If regime crushes protests → Trump’s credibility damaged if he doesn’t intervene

  • If Trump intervenes → negotiations end, military conflict begins

  • If Trump doesn’t intervene → protesters feel betrayed, Pahlavi discredited

There’s no stable equilibrium. The situation is inherently unstable, which makes negotiations nearly impossible.

The Venezuela Warning: Even “Success” Might Fail

Even if Trump strikes Iran’s leadership, even if the Islamic Republic falls, even if a new government emerges—Venezuela suggests this might not produce the outcome Trump wants.

Remember: Trump invaded Venezuela, captured Maduro, announced U.S. control. One week later:

  • Oil companies said Venezuela was “uninvestable”

  • Senate voted 52-47 against him

  • Delcy Rodríguez (Maduro’s VP) now runs Venezuela

  • U.S. controls oil sales but can’t make companies invest

  • No clear path to democracy or stability

Apply this to Iran:

Scenario: Trump strikes Iran, kills Khamenei, destabilizes regime

Then what?

  • IRGC commanders seize control (they have guns, organization, legitimacy with some Iranians)

  • Or Iran fragments into regional conflicts (ethnic minorities, different factions)

  • Or Pahlavi tries to take power but lacks sufficient support

  • Or reformist factions emerge but can’t consolidate authority

  • Or chaos reigns, creating vacuum worse than current regime

Victoria Taylor’s warning applies: “Even if you take out the supreme leader, you could still wind up with another regime figure assuming leadership.”

The Iraq lesson looms: Removing Saddam was militarily easy. Building a stable, democratic Iraq proved impossible. Twenty years later, Iraq remains fragile, violent, and authoritarian.

Does Trump want to own Iran’s political reconstruction? Does he want responsibility for 80 million people’s governance? Does he want to manage ethnic conflicts, religious tensions, and economic collapse?

Venezuela suggests no—Trump wants quick military victory, not long-term nation-building. But you can’t have one without the other.

The Venezuela Model: Regime Transformation Without Regime Change

But there’s a third path that analysts are now discussing: What if Trump doesn’t want full regime change? What if he wants what he got in Venezuela—same structure, different head?

Multiple experts examining the Iran crisis have noticed something: The Venezuela operation didn’t produce democracy or complete regime change. It removed Maduro but left Delcy Rodríguez running the same authoritarian system. Trump got what he wanted (control over oil exports) without what he didn’t want (responsibility for democratic transition).

Could Iran follow the same model?

What the Venezuela Model would look like in Iran:

Step 1: Remove Khamenei specifically. Strike him, force his resignation, or marginalize him through internal pressure. The 86-year-old supreme leader is “aging and eminently expendable,” as Suzanne Maloney of the Brookings Institution noted.

Step 2: Cut a deal with remaining leadership—IRGC commanders, clerical establishment, technocratic officials. They offer major concessions in exchange for survival.

Step 3: Iran agrees to:

  • Stop uranium enrichment under international monitoring

  • Dismantle ballistic missile program

  • End support for regional proxies (especially Hezbollah)

  • Open oil sector to U.S. companies

Step 4: U.S. lifts key sanctions, providing economic relief. Regime can tell Iranians: “We negotiated peace, saved the economy, protected Iran from invasion.”

Step 5: Trump declares victory. Removed hostile leader, secured nuclear concessions, gained oil access, avoided occupation. No American troops committed long-term.

Ali Alfoneh of the Arab Gulf States Institute described exactly this scenario: “Iran’s collective leadership could marginalize or remove Khamenei, open negotiations with Trump, invite U.S. oil companies back into Iran, and secure sanctions relief sufficient to stabilize the economy.”

Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group noted that Venezuela demonstrates Trump’s flexibility: “The odds of regime transformation are much higher than of regime change.” He pointed out that the Maduro precedent shows “Washington is willing to live with the same structure if it has a different head.”

Why Trump might prefer this:

Trump gets to claim he:

  • Removed Iran’s supreme leader (matching the Maduro achievement)

  • Ended Iran’s nuclear threat permanently

  • Secured American oil access

  • Protected Israel and Arab allies

  • Avoided another Iraq/Afghanistan occupation

He avoids:

  • Long-term military commitment

  • Responsibility for Iranian democratic transition

  • Managing ethnic conflicts and civil war

  • Congressional opposition to nation-building

  • Base backlash over Middle East entanglement

Why Iran’s leadership might accept this:

The regime gets:

  • Survival of institutions (IRGC, clerical establishment, government structure)

  • Economic sanctions relief to address protests’ root cause

  • Khamenei as scapegoat (blame him for Iran’s problems, present his removal as reform)

  • Avoidance of full regime collapse

  • Time to reconsolidate power domestically

According to European officials speaking to The Washington Post, Iran’s leaders “are keenly aware that Trump faces potential backlash from his MAGA movement if he uses military force, and are offering negotiations to stall for time.”

Steven Erlanger of The New York Times reported that despite the crackdown, “there is also the question of the future of Ayatollah Khamenei, who has been unwilling to make tough decisions for change.” If protests continue pressuring the system while Trump threatens intervention, removing Khamenei could provide both sides with an off-ramp.

The problems with this model:

For protesters: This leaves them betrayed. They risked death demanding regime change, got regime survival with cosmetic changes. The IRGC remains in power. The system that killed 2,000-3,000 protesters remains intact. Only the supreme leader changed.

For Trump’s credibility: How does “Take over your institutions” and “Help is on its way” lead to negotiated accommodation with regime figures? Trump would be accused of selling out Iranian democrats exactly as critics accused him of selling out Venezuelan democrats.

For Iran’s hardliners: Can the IRGC and clerical establishment really give up everything the revolution stood for (nuclear program, regional proxies, anti-American stance) just to survive? Or would hardliners see this as betrayal and revolt internally?

For regional stability: Iran without nuclear program but with IRGC still in power might be more stable short-term but leaves fundamental problems unresolved. The authoritarian structure that prompted protests remains.

The Venezuela comparison has limitations:

Suzanne Maloney argued that unlike Venezuela, Iran’s system is “trapped by its complicity and its foundational reliance on anti-Americanism.” The entire regime—not just Khamenei—is implicated in the crackdown, the nuclear program, the regional proxy wars. “All of the ayatollah’s apparatchiks understand that they, too, will go down with the ship,” she wrote.

Alan Eyre of the Middle East Institute called the Venezuela scenario “not plausible in the short term”: “I don’t think this regime is currently willing to make the changes necessary in its power structure necessary to induce President Trump to seek accommodation.”

But the situation is fluid. As the protests continue, as the death toll rises, as economic conditions worsen, what seems implausible today might become necessary tomorrow.

The Venezuela Model represents a path between full regime change (which Trump may not want to commit to) and accepting the current regime (which Trump has ruled out). It’s regime transformation—keeping structure while changing leadership and extracting major concessions.

Whether Trump pursues this path depends on:

  • Whether protests can be crushed or continue indefinitely

  • Whether Iran’s leadership concludes Khamenei is expendable

  • Whether Trump decides regime transformation satisfies his objectives

  • Whether either side can sell accommodation to their respective audiences after so much bloodshed

For now, this remains one of three possible paths: (1) negotiated transformation, (2) military intervention and regime collapse, or (3) continued standoff with ongoing protests and crackdowns.

But Venezuela opened the door to a fourth option that didn’t exist before: Remove the leader, keep the system, declare victory, move on.

Monday’s Simultaneous Messages

Monday, January 12, crystallized the ambiguity perfectly:

Tehran: Araghchi says prepared for war OR negotiations Washington: Trump says Iran wants to talk, but “we may have to act” Oman: Facilitating quiet talks between sides Streets: Protests continuing despite massacre Prisons: 10,000+ arrested, death penalty threats Morgues: Hundreds dead, possibly thousands

War and negotiations happening simultaneously. Crackdown and diplomacy coexisting. Threats and talks proceeding in parallel.

This is not contradiction—this is strategy. Both sides position for optimal outcome while preparing for worst case.

But the underlying reality hasn’t changed:

Iran cannot accept terms that destroy the regime ideologically. The U.S. won’t accept terms that leave Iran’s nuclear/missile programs intact. Protesters want regime change, not reformed regime. Trump wants victory without commitment.

These positions cannot all be satisfied. Something has to give.

Trump’s Internal Doubt: The “Coin Flip” Decision (January 13 Evening Update)

While Trump publicly promises “help is on its way,” reporting Tuesday evening reveals significant uncertainty behind the scenes.

According to The Washington Post (5:18 PM ET), people close to the White House describe Trump as “less enthusiastic than before the bombings in June.” Another called the strike decision “a coin flip.”

Why Trump is hesitant:

Venezuela’s complications: Trump’s supposedly successful January 3 operation is already showing problems. Oil companies say Venezuela is “uninvestable.” The Senate voted 52-47 against him. Delcy Rodríguez is playing both sides. As one person close to the White House noted: “Trump’s open-ended commitment in Venezuela hangs over the National Security Council as officials consider another risky military action that could leave a country with an unclear future.”

Fear of failure: The Maduro raid “avoided the loss of any American lives or equipment but could have ended in disaster when one of the attack helicopters was hit.” One person close to the White House said bluntly: “He hasn’t had a blemish on the military side yet. That’s going to come if you keep pressing this stuff.”

Political opposition from his base: Stephen K. Bannon, longtime Trump adviser, publicly argued against strikes on his popular online talk show Monday: “We don’t care about making Iran great again. Israel and the United States in there, you’re just going to prolong the problem.”

Advisers less hawkish: Unlike Trump’s first term, when advisers came from hawkish Republican foreign policy tradition, current advisers are “less inclined to intervene in the Middle East.” Vice President JD Vance “supported Trump’s decision in June to bomb Iran’s main nuclear facilities but is wary of being dragged into a deeper Middle East entanglement,” according to two people close to him.

Tucker Carlson’s influence: Former Fox News host Tucker Carlson—who loudly criticized the June strikes—met with Trump for lunch at the White House on Friday. While the meeting “was not primarily about Iran,” Carlson’s opposition to Middle East intervention resonates with parts of Trump’s base.

This internal uncertainty creates a contradiction: Trump committed publicly (”help is on its way”), canceling all diplomatic meetings. But privately, he’s described as hesitant, uncertain, conducting what amounts to a “coin flip” deliberation.

The National Security Council met Tuesday without Trump to prepare options. Vice President Vance, Secretary of State Rubio, and other officials are “presenting options to Trump without preference.”

Options being considered:

  • Military strikes on leadership of security forces (NOT nuclear facilities)

  • Cyberattacks on government infrastructure

  • Measures to counter Iran’s communications blocking

  • Increased economic pressure (Trump announced 25% tariffs Monday on countries doing business with Iran)

  • Stepped-up support for protest movement

European officials told The Post their countries were asked by the Trump administration Monday to share intelligence on possible targets within Iran. “We don’t have any indications that President Trump will target nuclear facilities,” one official said. “It is more likely that he will go after the leadership of organizations and forces that are responsible and involved in the killings of protesters.”

So Trump has committed publicly but remains privately uncertain. He’s cancelled negotiations but hasn’t ordered strikes. He’s promised help but hasn’t defined what help means.

This is not the decisive leadership Trump projects. This is genuine uncertainty about whether intervention will succeed or become his “blemish on the military side.”

Conclusion: The Bluff That Became Real (Updated January 13, 2026)

We were watching two sides play a dangerous game of chicken.

Iran signaled: We’ll negotiate, but we won’t surrender. Attack us and we’ll retaliate. We’re stronger than you think.

Trump signaled: I’ll strike if you keep killing protesters. But I’m willing to deal if you comply. I’m stronger than you are.

Both sides hoped the other would blink first. Both sides prepared for violence while talking peace. Both sides claimed strength while harboring doubts.

Then, on Tuesday morning, January 13, Trump blinked—not toward accommodation, but toward confrontation.

Posted on Truth Social at 10:29 AM ET:

“Iranian Patriots, KEEP PROTESTING — TAKE OVER YOUR INSTITUTIONS!!! Save the names of the killers and abusers. They will pay a big price. I have cancelled all meetings with Iranian Officials until the senseless killing of protesters STOPS. HELP IS ON ITS WAY. MIGA!!!”

The negotiation track is dead. Trump cancelled “all meetings with Iranian Officials”—ending the Oman channel, the quiet diplomacy, the ambiguity about whether talks might produce results. The door isn’t just closed. It’s been publicly slammed shut.

What this means:

The impossible choice just became impossible in a different way. Iran can no longer hope that negotiation might provide an off-ramp. Trump has committed publicly to the protesters’ side. He’s promised “help is on its way”—not talks, not compromise, but help.

For Iran’s leadership: You can’t accommodate your way out of this. Trump won’t meet with you. He’s cancelled diplomacy. Your only options now are crush the protests completely before American intervention arrives, or face regime change.

For Iranian protesters: Trump just validated your strategy. Keep protesting, take over institutions, document crimes. American help is coming. This is the commitment Pahlavi solicited. The question is whether it arrives in time.

For the world: We’re no longer watching negotiations parallel to threats. We’re watching the prelude to military action. Trump doesn’t cancel all meetings, promise help, and tell protesters to “take over institutions” if he’s planning to negotiate.

The bluff was never a bluff. Trump meant it. And now hundreds or thousands more will die before we learn what “help is on its way” actually means.

The impossible choice between negotiation and war has been resolved. Trump chose war—or at minimum, chose to end any possibility of negotiated resolution until after the immediate crisis is decided by force.

Iran’s dead are piling up. Trump’s promised help is coming. And neither side can turn back now.
