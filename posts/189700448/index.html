The Department of War Got Its War

Part 1 of 2: From Branding to Bombs in Five Months

March 2, 2026

Photo Illustration by Damon Winter/The New York Times

Trump’s Foreign Policy Series CanadianSnowmanSays — March 2, 2026

For a guy who calls himself the Secretary of War, Pete Hegseth sure is defensive.

That’s how New York Times opinion writer W.J. Hennigan opened his column today, and it’s the right place to start this essay. Because the most important word in that sentence isn’t “defensive.” It’s “War.” Hegseth chose that title. Trump gave it to him. And now, four days into the largest American military operation in the Middle East since 2003, the man who branded himself the Secretary of War is refusing to explain what the war is for.

This essay isn’t about Operation Epic Fury itself — the strikes, the casualties, the retaliation. Others are covering that in real time. This essay is about the road that got us here, and specifically about a question that sounds simple but isn’t: Did the September 2025 renaming of the Department of Defense to the “Department of War” signal a premeditated trajectory toward this moment?

The short answer is yes — but not in the way you might think. The rename didn’t cause the war. The rename was part of the war. Not the military operation, but the narrative operation that preceded it. The DOW branding was one node in a constellation of actions that, taken together, constituted the most publicly telegraphed march to war in modern American history.

Let me show you the timeline.

The Promise

To understand where we are, you have to remember where we started.

Donald Trump ran for president in 2024 as the anti-war candidate. This wasn’t a minor talking point — it was a central pillar of his campaign identity. At rally after rally, he told supporters: “We will demolish the deep state. We will expel the warmongers, those horrible warmongers from our government — those stupid, stupid people. They love seeing people die.” He repeated variations of this line at hundreds of campaign stops.

The people around him reinforced the message with striking specificity. Vice President JD Vance wrote a Wall Street Journal op-ed in 2023 making the case for Trump’s candidacy. The headline: “Trump’s Best Foreign Policy? Not Starting Any Wars.” Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard sold “No War with Iran” shirts in 2020 and declared in October 2024: “A vote for Donald Trump is a vote for a man who wants to end wars, not start them.” Senior adviser Stephen Miller posted: “Liz Cheney is Kamala’s top advisor. Liz wants to invade the whole Middle East. Kamala = WWIII. Trump = Peace.”

At his inauguration on January 20, 2025, Trump declared: “My proudest legacy will be that of a peacemaker and unifier.”

Remember that sentence. We’ll need it later.

The Branding

By March 2025 — barely two months into the new administration — Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ran a social media poll asking followers about renaming the Department of Defense to the Department of War. This was not idle musing. It was a trial balloon for a cultural project that had been discussed for months.

On September 5, 2025, Trump signed Executive Order 14347, authorizing “Department of War” as the secondary title for the Department of Defense. It was his 200th executive order — a milestone number deliberately chosen for this particular action. Within minutes of the signing, Pentagon staff began removing DOD signage. The department’s website URL and social media handles were updated in real time. Hegseth wasted no time adopting the title “Secretary of War.”

At the signing ceremony, Trump explained his reasoning: “We won the First World War. We won the Second World War. We won everything before that and in between. And then we decided to go woke and we changed the name to Department of Defense. So we’re going Department of War.”

This framing was historically illiterate — the 1947 rename had nothing to do with “woke” ideology; it was a deliberate post-WWII signal that America’s military existed to prevent war in the nuclear age — but the illiteracy was the point. Trump wasn’t making a historical argument. He was making a cultural one. As historians noted at the time, the original name change was meant to signal an emphasis on preventing war amid the threat of nuclear destruction. Reversing it was an ideological statement: deterrence was out, dominance was in.

The rename didn’t happen in isolation. It was part of a comprehensive cultural transformation of the military: the purging of “woke” elements, the removal of all transgender troops, the restoration of Confederate-era base names, and Hegseth’s relentless emphasis on “warrior ethos.” This wasn’t bureaucratic housekeeping. It was the reconstruction of the American military as a war-fighting institution rather than a deterrence institution — rebranded, restructured, and renamed for the purpose its new leaders intended.

The Escalation

To understand the DOW rename as a signal rather than a cause, you need to see it in the context of what was already happening.

By the time the DOW executive order was signed in September 2025, Trump had already authorized military action on a scale that dwarfed his predecessors. According to Axios, Trump attacked seven nations in his second term, three of which — Iran, Nigeria, and Venezuela — had never been targeted by U.S. military strikes before. He authorized more individual air strikes in 2025 than President Biden did in four years.

The escalation followed a clear pattern, with each step larger than the last and each conducted without congressional authorization:

The Caribbean drug boat strikes began early in the administration and killed over 80 people by December 2025, including a September 2 incident where survivors of an initial strike were hit again — footage that left members of Congress “profoundly shaken.”

On Christmas Day 2025, the U.S. struck Nigeria, an American ally, targeting Islamic insurgents.

In January 2026, U.S. special forces captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro in an overnight raid and brought him to New York to face drug trafficking charges — again, without congressional authorization.

And in June 2025 — three months before the DOW rename — the U.S. had already struck Iran’s nuclear facilities at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan in Operation Midnight Hammer. At that time, Hegseth explicitly promised: “This mission was not and has not been about regime change.” The administration claimed Iran’s nuclear program had been “obliterated.”

The DOW rename, in other words, didn’t precede the escalation. It branded it.

The Reagan Speech

On December 6, 2025, Hegseth delivered a keynote address at the Reagan National Defense Forum that deserves careful attention, because it is the single most comprehensive statement of what the administration said it would do — and the single most comprehensive contradiction of what it then did.

Standing in the Air Force One Pavilion at the Reagan Presidential Library, the Secretary of War told an audience of lawmakers, military officials, and defense industry executives: “The War Department will not be distracted by democracy building, interventionism, undefined wars, regime change, climate change, woke moralizing and feckless nation building. We will instead put our nation’s practical, concrete interests first. We will deter war. We will advance our interests. We will defend our people. Peace is our goal.”

A War Department official confirmed to The Federalist that the speech was a preview of the forthcoming National Defense Strategy.

Less than a month later, the U.S. launched the Venezuela raid to capture Maduro. Less than three months later, it launched Operation Epic Fury — a regime-change war in Iran.

Every single thing Hegseth promised the DOW would not do at the Reagan Forum — democracy building, interventionism, undefined wars, regime change, nation building — is precisely what it is now doing.

The Nobel Letter

If the DOW rename was the brand and the Reagan speech was the mission statement, then the Nobel Peace Prize letter was the confession.

On January 18, 2026 — forty days before Operation Epic Fury — Trump texted Norwegian Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre: “Considering your Country decided not to give me the Nobel Peace Prize for having stopped 8 Wars PLUS, I no longer feel an obligation to think purely of Peace, although it will always be predominant, but can now think about what is good and proper for the United States of America.”

Let that sink in. The President of the United States, in writing, told a foreign head of state that he considered himself unbound by any obligation toward peace because he didn’t receive a prize he wanted. A prize, it should be noted, that is awarded by an independent Norwegian committee, not by the Norwegian government — a distinction Trump either didn’t understand or didn’t care about.

The letter continued with Trump’s demand for “Complete and Total Control of Greenland” and his claim that Denmark couldn’t protect the territory from Russia or China. It was petulant, historically illiterate, and diplomatically reckless. But it was also, in hindsight, something more: a written declaration that the “peacemaker and unifier” of the inaugural address was gone, replaced by someone who explicitly viewed peace as a conditional posture dependent on personal recognition.

The Daily Beast reported that Trump’s “growing, campaign-promise-busting appetite for military engagement also comes amid his bitter disappointment at being passed over for last year’s Nobel Peace Prize.” In a letter to Norway, Trump wrote that since the committee chose not to honor him, he could “now think about what is good and proper for the United States of America” — framing military action as what became proper once the peace incentive was removed.

Forty days later, bombs fell on Tehran.

The Pattern

Here is what the timeline shows when you lay it out:

January 2025: “My proudest legacy will be that of a peacemaker and unifier.”

March 2025: Hegseth polls followers on renaming DOD to DOW.

June 2025: U.S. strikes Iranian nuclear facilities. Claims nuclear program “obliterated.” Promises this is “not about regime change.”

September 2025: DOD renamed to DOW. 200th executive order. Signs changed within minutes.

December 2025: Hegseth pledges DOW will not pursue “regime change” or “undefined wars.” Peace is the goal.

January 18, 2026: Trump tells Norway he no longer feels bound by obligations of peace.

January 2026: Venezuela raid. No congressional authorization.

Early February 2026: Largest military buildup since 2003 Iraq invasion deployed to the Gulf. Carrier groups take months to plan.

February 13, 2026: Trump says regime change in Iran would be “the best thing that could happen.”

February 20, 2026: Trump gives Iran a 10-day deadline.

February 24, 2026: Trump claims Iran pursuing nuclear ambitions. His own intelligence agencies disagree.

February 26, 2026: Omani mediator announces unprecedented diplomatic breakthrough. Iran agrees to zero stockpiling, full IAEA verification.

February 27, 2026: Trump says he’s “not exactly happy” with negotiations.

February 28, 2026, 2:00 AM: Operation Epic Fury launched via Truth Social video. No congressional authorization. No press conference. No public legal justification.

The question was never whether the DOW rename incentivized war. The rename and the war were concurrent outputs of the same operating system. The name didn’t push Trump toward conflict — it was Trump telling the world he was already heading there. It was a declaration of intent dressed up as a historical restoration.

When you rename the Department of Defense to the Department of War, you aren’t changing a bureaucratic label. You are telling the institution — and the nation — what you expect it to do.

Five months later, it did it.
