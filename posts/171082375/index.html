Why We Need a Bell Bylaw (And Maybe Bike Licensing Too)

A cyclist's 6,500-kilometer journey through Toronto's trails

September 2, 2025

The Problem Hiding in Plain Sight

I've ridden 6,500 kilometers on Toronto's trails over the past two years, side by side with my trusty and energetic husky. To put that in perspective, the distance from St. John's, Newfoundland to Victoria, BC via the Trans Canada Highway is 7,775 km. Every single day, I see the same dangerous pattern: cyclists silently swooping past pedestrians, families, seniors, and other trail users without any warning.

Here's what's wild: Every cyclist in Ontario is legally required to have a bell on their bike. It's right there in the Highway Traffic Act, Section 75(5). The fine? $110 if you don't have one.

But here's the kicker: There's no law requiring you to actually use that bell when passing people.

Think about that for a second. We mandate the safety equipment but not its use in the exact situation it's designed for. It's like requiring seat belts but not requiring you to wear them.

What I See Every Day

Let me paint you a picture of my typical morning ride. I'm on my e-bike with my husky tethered alongside (she loves trail running). The trail is busy with the usual mix: commuter cyclists, recreational riders, joggers, dog walkers, families with strollers, seniors out for their morning exercise.

The Headphone Problem: At least half the pedestrians and runners are wearing earbuds or headphones. I ring my bell multiple times – nothing. I call out "passing on your left" – nothing. Sometimes I have to practically yell to get their attention. When that fails, I slow down and give them a wide berth, which often means waiting for oncoming traffic to clear.

The Courtesy Vacuum: But here's what really gets me – the number of cyclists who just... don't. No bell, no voice, no warning whatsoever. They ghost past people like silent missiles, often startling pedestrians who had no idea they were coming.

The Accidents Waiting to Happen (And the Ones I Witnessed)

I've seen this story play out too many times, but one incident really stuck with me:

I was sitting on a bench when I watched a cyclist approach a senior woman from behind, completely silent. Just as he was passing, she stepped to her left – probably checking her phone or adjusting her path. Boom. Both went down hard on the paved trail.

The woman was clearly shaken up and hurt. But here's what really got me: the cyclist immediately started blaming her for "stepping in front of him."

Think about that logic for a second. That's like rear-ending someone with your car and then blaming them for being in your way. When you're approaching someone from behind – whether you're driving, cycling, or running – it's your responsibility to make your presence known safely.

The whole thing could have been prevented with a simple bell ring five seconds earlier. Instead, we had an injured senior and a cyclist who couldn't understand that silent passing puts the responsibility on everyone else to magically know he's there.

But here's an incident that really shows how broken our trail etiquette has become:

I was approaching a mother pushing a stroller when I noticed an oncoming pedestrian. The gap was too tight for a safe pass, so I stopped and waited – basic common sense, right?

Two cyclists I'd previously passed came up behind me and just... squeezed through anyway. No bell, no warning, no "excuse me." They forced themselves through a gap I'd determined was unsafe, with a mother and baby in the middle of it.

I was so shocked I didn't even call out to them. These weren't teenagers being reckless – these were adult cyclists who saw the same situation I did and chose to prioritize their speed over everyone's safety.

The Complex Reality of Trail Dynamics

The research revealed multiple scenarios where predictable user movements create collision potential:

Families with Strollers: Unpredictable lateral movements, children on foot or bikes Dog Walkers: Leash entanglement risks, sudden directional changes Group Runners/Cyclists: Formation changes, varying speeds within groups Mobility Device Users: Consistent travel patterns but requiring advance notice for safe passing

Tethered Animal Considerations

Operating with a tethered husky on a 180-degree swing radius demonstrated how dynamic trail width requirements necessitate advance communication. The effective trail width occupied varies from 1.5 to 4+ meters depending on the dog's position, making audible approach signals critical for other trail users to make informed positioning decisions.

Wildlife Encounters and Situational Judgment

Wildlife encounters such as this one are common on Toronto's trails - deer can react unpredictably to audible signals, demonstrating why proposed bylaws need exception provisions for situational judgment

Toronto's trail system runs through diverse natural environments where wildlife encounters add complexity to passing protocols. During 6,500 kilometers of observation, regular sightings of deer, raccoons, coyotes, and various bird species demonstrate why the proposed bylaw includes exception language for situations where audible signals "would be reasonably likely to startle or endanger the person being passed." Wildlife can react unpredictably to bells - deer may freeze or bound suddenly into other trail users' paths, requiring cyclists to exercise situational judgment about when to modify standard communication protocols.

Winter Trail Conditions and Year-Round Safety

Toronto's trail system experiences dramatically different usage patterns and safety challenges during winter months. While some trails receive snow clearing, many remain unplowed, creating narrow, icy corridors where passing becomes significantly more hazardous. Winter trail users include fat-tire cyclists, cross-country skiers, snowshoers, and dog walkers, each with different speed capabilities and maneuvering limitations.

Reduced Visibility and Hearing: Winter clothing, hoods, and wind noise reduce users' ability to hear approaching cyclists. Snow-covered ground muffles bell sounds, making audible signals even more critical for safe winter passing. The combination of limited trail width due to snow accumulation and reduced sensory awareness creates heightened collision risk.

Equipment Considerations: Winter cycling requires additional safety equipment including lights and reflective gear, reinforcing the argument for systematic equipment verification through licensing. Year-round trail safety demands that cyclists maintain functional bells and communication devices regardless of seasonal conditions.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

Toronto's trail network is exploding. The 2012 Bikeway Trails Implementation Plan called for 77 kilometers of new trails. We're investing millions in this infrastructure, and it's becoming critical transportation infrastructure, not just recreation.

But we're setting ourselves up for a safety crisis. Canadian data shows 7,500 cyclists are seriously injured every year. While most cycling fatalities involve cars, trail incidents are a growing and completely preventable category.

Research on multi-use trail intersections found collision rates of 38% compared to 23% at regular road intersections, with higher injury rates too. When cyclists and pedestrians collide, it's often the pedestrian who gets hurt worse.

The Solution Is Simpler Than You Think

Option 1: Just Fix the Bylaw

Add one line to Toronto's parks bylaw: cyclists must signal audibly when passing other trail users. Done. Calgary already does this successfully.

Option 2: The Bigger Fix – Bike Licensing

But here's where it gets interesting. Toronto has rejected bike licensing three times since the 1990s, always citing administrative nightmares and database management issues. But Calgary has already proven the behavioral requirements work - they require bell usage when passing and have comprehensive equipment standards (bells, brakes, lights, reflectors).

Toronto could be the Canadian leader by taking Calgary's proven model and adding systematic verification through licensing. We wouldn't be experimenting with unproven rules - we'd be enforcing standards that Calgary has already shown are practical and effective.

But what if we kept it focused? What if bike licensing was about ensuring basic safety compliance that's already required by law but never systematically verified?

This would make Toronto a Canadian leader in systematic bicycle safety compliance. No major Canadian city has successfully implemented comprehensive bicycle licensing with safety verification, despite most provinces having detailed equipment requirements.

Think about it:

  • 10-15 minute safety inspection: Does your bike have a working bell? Working brakes? Required reflectors? Proper lights if you ride at night?

  • Existing legal standards: We're not creating new rules - just checking compliance with current Highway Traffic Act requirements

  • Modern technology: Digital licensing and QR codes solve the database problems that killed previous attempts

  • Community-based: Bike shops, community centers, even cycling clubs could do inspections

  • Revenue-positive: License fees ($40-75?) fund trail maintenance and safety programs

  • Educational opportunity: Everyone learns proper passing etiquette and equipment requirements as part of licensing

  • Renewal cycle: Annual or bi-annual checks ensure equipment stays functional

This isn't about turning the city into bike mechanics. It's about enforcing laws that already exist but are never checked. Right now, you could ride a bike with no bell, no brakes, and no lights, and unless you're in an accident, no one would ever know.

Hybrid Implementation Strategy

Implement immediate safety improvements while developing comprehensive framework:

Phase 1 (0-12 months): Direct bylaw amendment requiring audible passing signals Phase 2 (12-24 months): Pilot voluntary licensing program focusing on bell compliance Phase 3 (24+ months): Evaluate mandatory licensing based on pilot results and public acceptance

Why Now?

Toronto has Vision Zero – the goal of eliminating traffic deaths and serious injuries. But that vision has focused mainly on cars and bikes, not on what happens when bikes and pedestrians share space.

With our aging population, more families using trails, and the explosion in trail usage post-COVID, we can't keep pretending courtesy will solve this. If people won't do the obviously right thing voluntarily, sometimes you need a rule.

We've done this before. Helmet laws for kids, smoking bans, accessible design requirements – sometimes society needs a nudge to do what's obviously right.

The Path Forward

Immediate: Pilot mandatory bell usage on high-conflict trail segments. See how it works.

Medium-term: Study modern bike licensing focused on safety equipment compliance. Partner with bike shops and community groups.

Long-term: Integrate this with Toronto's broader transportation safety framework.

The licensing piece is interesting because it could generate ongoing revenue for trail maintenance and improvements. Imagine if every trail user contributed $40-75 annually toward keeping our trail system safe and well-maintained.

What You Can Do

If you use Toronto's trails – whether you walk, run, cycle, or roll – you've probably experienced this problem firsthand.

For cyclists: Start using your bell. Yes, even if people are wearing headphones. Yes, even if they don't seem to hear you. It's about establishing the habit and the cultural norm.

For everyone: Contact your city councillor. Share your own trail experiences. Ask them why we require safety equipment but not its use.

For city officials reading this: The data is clear, the solution is straightforward, and the technology exists to implement it efficiently. Calgary has proven the behavioral requirements work. Toronto can lead by adding systematic verification.

The Bottom Line

I love Toronto's trails. They're part of what makes this city great. But love means wanting something to be its best, safest version.

We're one preventable accident away from a tragedy that will make everyone question whether our trails are safe for families, seniors, and casual users. We can fix this now, with simple solutions that respect both cyclists' mobility and everyone else's safety.

The bell is already required. The courtesy should be obvious. Let's just make it official.
