The Day Confabulation Went Public

Part 14: The President Who Could Not Remember Which War He Was In

May 2, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays May 1, 2026

In Part 13, I documented the night of April 12: seven hours of compulsive posting, the attack on Pope Leo XIV, the AI-generated image of Trump as Jesus Christ, the naval blockade announced at 12:43 AM between Newsmax clips. I wrote that the disinhibition was complete and that the intervals between incidents were disappearing.

Seventeen days later, a new category of incident appeared.

Not disinhibition. Not compulsion. Not grandiosity.

Confabulation.

April 29: The Phone Call and What Followed

On April 29, President Trump spoke by telephone with Vladimir Putin. The Kremlin confirmed the call covered both the Iran and Ukraine conflicts. By all accounts, it was a wide-ranging conversation — two wars, two theatres, one phone call.

What happened at the press gaggle immediately afterward is now confirmed by multiple international news organizations including The Independent, IBTimes UK, NBC News, and international wire services.

A reporter asked the President a simple question: which war would end first — Iran or Ukraine?

Trump answered about Ukraine.

Or rather: he answered about Iran, while believing he was answering about Ukraine.

The Record

The president’s exact words, on camera, in front of the press corps:

“Ukraine militarily they’re defeated, OK, you wouldn’t know that by reading the fake news, but militarily look there, maybe, so they had 159 ships. Every ship is right now underwater.”

One hundred and fifty-nine ships. Underwater. Ukraine.

Ukraine’s navy was effectively scuttled or seized in 2014. It has operated since then primarily with sea drones and small vessels. It does not have, and has never had during this conflict, 159 ships.

One hundred and fifty-nine ships is the number the President’s own Pentagon spent six weeks citing as the Iranian naval fleet destroyed during Operation Epic Fury. Those are Iran’s ships. That is the Iran damage assessment. Trump recited it as though it described Ukraine.

He continued:

“OK, now they have an Air Force. Every one of their airplanes has been shut down or has been decimated. They have missiles about 82% are gone, and they have drones and most of them are gone. Most of the factories are mostly gone.”

Every airplane decimated. Missiles 82% gone. Drone factories destroyed. This is, again, not a description of Ukraine’s military situation. It is a verbatim recitation of the Pentagon’s Iran battle damage assessment — the same figures, the same categories, the same percentages used in formal briefings about Operation Epic Fury.

He then added:

“Their economy is crashing. They uh, as you know, their money, they’re, they go with their money, they don’t have a name for it. Their money is valueless.”

They don’t have a name for their money.

Ukraine’s currency is the hryvnia. It has been the hryvnia since 1996. It appears on every banknote, in every international financial wire, in every news article about the Ukrainian economy for thirty years.

Iran’s currency is the rial. The President did not appear to know that either.

He has confused two currencies in two different wars simultaneously.

What Confabulation Is

confabulation (n.) /kənˌfabjʊˈleɪʃ(ə)n/ A neurological symptom in which a person unconsciously fills gaps in memory with fabricated, distorted, or misattributed information — without any intent to deceive. The person experiencing confabulation believes the information they are producing to be accurate. It is not lying. It is the brain generating plausible-seeming content to cover what it can no longer reliably retrieve. Most commonly associated with damage to the frontal lobes and conditions including frontotemporal dementia.

This series has tracked disinhibition — the progressive removal of the internal governor that separates thought from speech and impulse from action.

April 29 introduced something distinct.

Confabulation is not lying. It is not a rhetorical trick or deliberate misdirection. Confabulation is the neurological phenomenon in which the brain, presented with a gap in memory or executive function, fills that gap automatically and unconsciously with available material — and presents the filled-in version as confident fact.

The person confabulating does not experience themselves as confused. They experience themselves as remembering.

What the president appears to have done on April 29 is take two wars that had just been discussed in a single phone call, allow them to merge in the retrieval process, and then recite Iran’s military statistics — the ones most recently in his working memory from the Pentagon briefings — in response to a question about Ukraine. With complete confidence. On camera. In front of the press corps.

No one in the room corrected him.

The Putin Dimension

Trump and Putin discuss Iran war and float temporary Ukraine ceasefire in Wednesday call

The context of the phone call matters and cannot be separated from what followed.

Putin rang. They discussed both wars. Trump emerged and immediately described the wrong war’s destruction. He then added, regarding Putin:

“I’ve known him a long time. I think he was ready to make a deal a while ago. I think some people made it difficult for him to make a deal.”

“Some people made it difficult.” Within minutes of getting off the phone with Putin, the President of the United States characterized those who opposed Russia’s invasion of a sovereign democratic nation as obstacles to peace.

This is Kremlin framing. Delivered live. Immediately after a private call with the Kremlin’s principal.

Putin, whose country is Iran’s largest strategic partner, whose military has coordinated with the IRGC, whose drone supply chain runs through Tehran, reportedly offered to “be of help” with Iran. Trump appeared to accept.

Whether Putin sold out Iran for a diplomatic concession, or whether he is playing a longer game with a counterpart whose ability to track two simultaneous conflicts has now publicly failed — that is a question for analysts. What is observable is that Trump walked out of that call, recited Iranian war statistics as Ukrainian ones, parroted Kremlin talking points about “some people” blocking peace, and appears to have received a policy offer from the strategic partner of the country he is currently bombing.

This Was Not the First Time

The April 29 incident did not arrive without warning. It was the largest and most consequential in a documented pattern of geographic and factual confabulation that has been building for months — each instance explained away as a slip, a joke, or a deliberate provocation. Taken together, they describe something else.

September 18, 2025 — Azerbaijan and Albania. At a joint press conference with UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer, Trump boasted about ending a conflict between “Azerbaijan and Albania.” There is no such conflict. Albania is a Balkan nation with no involvement in the Caucasus. He had merged Armenia — one of the actual parties to the Azerbaijan ceasefire he brokered in August — with Albania, a country on a different continent. He also struggled to pronounce Azerbaijan, pausing visibly before getting it out.

September 20, 2025 — Cambodia and Armenia. Two days later, at the American Cornerstone Institute’s Founders’ Dinner at Mount Vernon, Trump again listed his peace achievements. This time he had “stopped the war between Cambodia and Armenia.” There is no such war. Cambodia and Armenia are 4,150 miles apart and have no territorial dispute. He had fused together two separate actual conflicts: Armenia’s ceasefire with Azerbaijan, and Cambodia’s border clashes with Thailand, into a single invented war that he alone had ended. European leaders were caught on camera at the Copenhagen summit mocking the error, with Albanian Prime Minister Edi Rama joking to Emmanuel Macron and Azerbaijani President Aliyev: “You should congratulate us — Trump has ended the conflict between Albania and Azerbaijan.” Aliyev was seen laughing.

September 2025 — Zelenskyy called “President of Russia.” Also during this period, Trump referred to Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy as “the President of Russia” during a briefing. He corrected himself, but the slip was recorded.

January 21, 2026 — Greenland and Iceland, four times. At the World Economic Forum in Davos — the most watched economic forum on earth — Trump delivered a speech about his stated intention to acquire Greenland from Denmark. He referred to Greenland as Iceland four separate times in a 90-second span, including: “until the last few days when I told them about Iceland, they loved me” and “our stock market took the first dip yesterday because of Iceland.” The WEF’s own official transcript captured the errors. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt told reporters they had not heard what the video plainly showed. Greenland and Iceland are separate North Atlantic nations separated by roughly 1,100 kilometres. At the time, Greenland was the single most prominent topic in American foreign policy.

March 16, 2026 — Calls Newsom “the president.” As documented in Part 12’s acceleration timeline, Trump referred to California Governor Gavin Newsom as “the president of the United States” during an exchange, forgetting his own title mid-sentence.

What emerges from these incidents is not a list of isolated slips. It is a pattern with a specific signature: two simultaneous facts competing for the same retrieval slot, with one displacing the other. Azerbaijan becomes Albania. Cambodia replaces Azerbaijan. Two wars merge into one narrative. A territory becomes a neighbouring country. In each case, the substitution is not random — it reflects partial, genuine knowledge imperfectly retrieved. Trump knows there is an Armenia conflict and a Cambodia conflict. He knows there is a Greenland and an Iceland. He knows there is a Ukraine war and an Iran war. The confabulation does not erase what he knows. It scrambles the retrieval.

That is the clinical distinction. This is not ignorance. It is something more concerning than ignorance.

The Pattern of Acceleration: Updated

September 18, 2025: “Azerbaijan and Albania” — Starmer press conference September 20, 2025: “Cambodia and Armenia” — invents war between nations 4,150 miles apart; European leaders mock him at Copenhagen summit September 2025: Calls Zelenskyy “the President of Russia” January 1, 2026: “Nice, thin blood” — WSJ interview January 19: “Dear Jonas” letter — demanding Greenland January 21: Greenland/Iceland — four times in 90 seconds, on the world stage at Davos March 16: Forgets he’s president — calls Newsom “the president of the United States” April 6: 93-minute press conference — contradictions, “I don’t know” responses April 7 (Easter): Genocide threat at 8:03 AM April 10: 72-hour reversal — business partnership with Iran April 11: MAGA coalition breaks — Carlson, Kelly, Owens, Jones, Greene condemn him April 12: Attacks Pope, posts himself as Jesus, announces blockade at 12:43 AM, posts until 4:10 AM April 29: Confuses Ukraine and Iran on camera — recites Iran’s military statistics as Ukraine’s, states Ukraine’s currency “has no name”

From the first documented confabulation (September 2025) to merging two active wars: approximately 7 months. From Greenland/Iceland at Davos to Ukraine/Iran on camera: 98 days. From attacking the Pope to confusing two wars: 17 days. From “does not know Ukraine’s currency” to “does not know Iran’s currency either”: simultaneous.

The intervals are not just disappearing. The categories are expanding.

The Baseline Question

This series does not diagnose. It observes patterns and asks whether they represent a departure from baseline.

Disinhibition was the first category: saying what should not be said. Compulsion was the second: unable to stop, posting until 4 AM. Grandiosity was the third: claiming to have motivated the Holy Spirit.

April 29 introduced a fourth category: confabulation — the confident, public, unconscious substitution of one reality for another.

The president has now, on camera, merged two active wars into a single narrative and recited the wrong country’s military destruction. He has described a currency that does not exist in the country he named. He has done this not in a private setting, not in a rambling aside, but in a direct answer to a direct question from the press corps immediately following a call with a foreign head of state.

Dr. Frank George’s framework, documented in Part 9, described what frontotemporal dementia does: it does not diminish the person; it removes the inhibitions progressively. “His decisions will become more erratic, more vengeful. It’s just getting expressed with no guard rails.”

The guard rails that once kept two wars separate have failed.

Conclusion

Tom Nichols wrote in Part 13 that Americans “must not shrug off the president’s latest implosion” and must demand that elected representatives “ask questions about the course of the war and whether Trump still has the capacity to fulfill his constitutional duty as commander in chief.”

On April 29, the president demonstrated on camera that he cannot reliably distinguish between two active military conflicts he is simultaneously commanding. He cannot recall the names of either country’s currency. He accepted what appears to be a policy offer from the strategic partner of the country he is bombing.

The question this series has asked since Part 1 — does this represent Trump being Trump, or has something shifted beyond that baseline — has now been answered in a new register.

Trump has always been provocative, impulsive, and given to exaggeration. He has not, until now, confused which war he was in.

From “nice, thin blood” to confabulating two wars on live television: 119 days.

The acceleration is not a metaphor.
