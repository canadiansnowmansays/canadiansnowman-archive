Part 13: January 6th - The Indictment (2021-2024)

January 20, 2026

On December 19, 2020, at 1:42 a.m., Donald Trump sent a tweet that would eventually lead to his federal indictment:

“Big protest in D.C. on January 6th. Be there, will be wild!”

Six weeks later, a mob of Trump supporters stormed the United States Capitol, attacking police officers, hunting for lawmakers, and disrupting the constitutional process of certifying electoral votes. Five people died. Over 140 police officers were injured. The peaceful transfer of power—a bedrock of American democracy—was interrupted for the first time in the nation’s history.

Special counsel Jack Smith’s investigation concluded that Trump had orchestrated a criminal conspiracy to overturn the 2020 election, culminating in the January 6th attack. Smith charged Trump with four federal crimes related to his efforts to remain in power despite losing the election.

The evidence was extensive. The witnesses were numerous. The criminal intent was documented in real time.

And Trump never faced trial.

Through a masterful strategy of delay, appeals on presidential immunity, and ultimate electoral victory, Trump ensured the case would never reach a jury. On November 25, 2024, Judge Tanya Chutkan dismissed the case at Jack Smith’s request.

This is the story of the most important criminal case in American history—and how it died without ever testing the evidence in court.

The Four Charges: A Criminal Scheme to Subvert Democracy

On August 1, 2023, a federal grand jury in Washington, D.C., returned a four-count indictment against Donald Trump:

Count 1: Conspiracy to Defraud the United States (18 U.S.C. § 371)
Trump conspired to impair, obstruct, and defeat the lawful function of collecting, counting, and certifying presidential election results.

Count 2: Conspiracy to Obstruct an Official Proceeding (18 U.S.C. § 1512(k))
Trump conspired to corruptly obstruct the January 6th congressional proceeding to certify the Electoral College vote.

Count 3: Obstruction of and Attempt to Obstruct an Official Proceeding (18 U.S.C. § 1512(c)(2))
Trump attempted to corruptly obstruct the January 6th certification.

Count 4: Conspiracy Against Rights (18 U.S.C. § 241)
Trump conspired to injure, oppress, threaten, and intimidate people in their right to vote and have their votes counted.

Trump pleaded not guilty on August 3, 2023. His legal team immediately began a campaign to delay trial indefinitely.

The Evidence: “Unsupported, Objectively Unreasonable, and Ever-Changing”

The indictment laid out Trump’s scheme in meticulous detail, drawing on testimony from dozens of witnesses including Trump’s own advisors, attorneys, and White House staff.

The Big Lie: Trump Knew He Lost

Despite knowing he had lost the election, Trump repeatedly made false claims of widespread voter fraud. The indictment documented that Trump’s fraud claims were “unsupported, objectively unreasonable, and ever-changing.”

Trump was told repeatedly by his own advisors that the fraud claims were false:

  • Attorney General Bill Barr told Trump the claims were “bullshit”

  • Acting Attorney General Jeffrey Rosen said DOJ found no fraud that would change the outcome

  • White House Counsel Pat Cipollone told Trump there was no evidence

  • Campaign advisors told Trump he’d lost

  • Data experts showed Trump he’d lost

  • State officials confirmed there was no fraud

Trump continued making false claims anyway.

The Fake Electors Scheme

An image in the final report compares documents for real and fake electors. (via the U.S. House Select Committee to Investigate the January 6th Attack on the United States Capitol

Trump and his co-conspirators organized slates of fake electors in seven states Biden won: Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Nevada, New Mexico, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin.

These fake electors signed fraudulent certificates claiming Trump had won their states. The scheme was designed to create confusion on January 6th and give Vice President Pence a pretext to reject legitimate electoral votes.

When state officials refused to go along, Trump’s team proceeded with the fake certificates anyway.

Pressuring State Officials

Trump personally called state officials trying to get them to “find” votes or overturn results:

  • Trump called Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger on January 2, 2021, demanding he “find 11,780 votes”—exactly the number needed to flip Georgia

  • Trump called Michigan legislators trying to overturn Biden’s victory

  • Trump called Pennsylvania legislators with similar pressure

  • Trump’s team called Arizona officials repeatedly

All of these calls were documented. Many were recorded. The pressure campaign was systematic and relentless.

Pressuring Mike Pence

Trump launched a weeks-long campaign to pressure Vice President Pence to refuse to certify the electoral votes on January 6th:

  • Trump told Pence he had the power to reject electoral votes (he didn’t)

  • Trump had lawyer John Eastman brief Pence on illegal schemes

  • Trump publicly attacked Pence for refusing

  • On January 6th, after Pence refused, Trump tweeted: “Mike Pence didn’t have the courage to do what should have been done”

This tweet came as the mob was already attacking the Capitol, hunting for Pence.

Exploiting the Capitol Attack

Even as the violent attack unfolded, Trump:

  • Waited 187 minutes before telling rioters to leave

  • Resisted calls from advisors to condemn the violence

  • Told advisors the rioters were “doing what they should be doing”

  • Attempted to further delay certification by calling senators during the attack

The indictment quoted Trump telling his chief of staff: “So what?” in response to being told Pence had been evacuated for his safety.

The Witnesses: Trump’s Own People

The strength of Smith’s case was that the key witnesses were Trump’s own appointees, advisors, and staff:

Mike Pence: Trump’s Vice President would testify about the pressure campaign and Trump’s reaction to the Capitol attack.

White House Staff: Chief of Staff Mark Meadows, White House Counsel Pat Cipollone, deputy counsel Patrick Philbin witnessed Trump’s conduct firsthand.

Campaign Officials: Campaign manager Bill Stepien, senior advisor Jason Miller, and others told Trump he’d lost.

State Officials: Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger, Michigan officials, Pennsylvania officials Trump pressured.

Justice Department Officials: Attorney General Bill Barr, Acting AG Jeffrey Rosen, Acting Deputy AG Richard Donoghue refused Trump’s demands to declare fraud.

The Fake Electors: People who participated in the scheme could describe its organization.

Capitol Police: Officers who defended the Capitol could describe the violence Trump’s lies unleashed.

These weren’t Democratic partisans or Trump enemies. They were Republicans. Trump appointees. People who worked for and supported Trump. And they would testify that Trump knew he lost, lied anyway, and orchestrated a criminal conspiracy.

Trump’s Defense Strategy: Delay Until Victory

Trump’s legal strategy was simple and brilliant:

Step 1: Claim presidential immunity from criminal prosecution
Step 2: Appeal every adverse immunity ruling
Step 3: Let appeals consume months, preventing trial
Step 4: Run for president again
Step 5: If you win, use presidential power to end prosecution

The strategy required keeping the case tied up in appeals until November 2024. If Trump won the election, DOJ policy would prevent prosecuting a sitting president.

The immunity claim was central. Trump argued that everything he did to overturn the election was an “official act” of the presidency, and presidents cannot be criminally prosecuted for official acts—even after leaving office.

The Immunity Fight: Round One

On December 1, 2023, Trump’s attorneys filed a motion to dismiss on immunity grounds. Judge Chutkan denied the motion the same day, ruling that former presidents are not immune from criminal prosecution.

Trump immediately appealed to the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals. The appeal automatically stayed all proceedings in the trial court—exactly the delay Trump wanted.

On February 6, 2024, a three-judge panel heard oral arguments. Trump’s attorney D. John Sauer made an extraordinary argument: a president can only be criminally prosecuted if first impeached and convicted by Congress.

Special counsel attorney James Pearce highlighted the absurdity: “If I understood my friend on the other side to say here, a president orders a SEAL team to assassinate a political rival and resigned, for example before an impeachment, it’s not a criminal act. I think that is extraordinarily frightening future.”

On February 6, 2024, the D.C. Circuit unanimously rejected Trump’s immunity claim in a decisive opinion.

But Trump wasn’t done. He appealed to the Supreme Court—and that’s where his delay strategy really paid off.

The Supreme Court: Delay Becomes Victory

On February 28, 2024, Trump asked the Supreme Court to hear his immunity appeal. The Court could have:

  1. Denied the appeal (allowing immediate trial)

  2. Granted the appeal and scheduled quick argument (allowing trial before election)

  3. Granted the appeal and delayed argument (making pre-election trial unlikely)

The Court chose option 3—and went even further.

The Supreme Court granted Trump’s appeal and scheduled oral argument for April 25, 2024—two months away. The Court didn’t rule on whether to pause lower court proceedings; taking the case effectively paused everything.

The delay was devastating. The case scheduled for trial in March 2024 was now stalled indefinitely.

On April 25, 2024, the Supreme Court heard oral arguments. Several conservative justices seemed sympathetic to some form of presidential immunity.

Then the Court sat on the case for two more months.

The delay ensured there would be no trial before the election. Trump’s strategy was working perfectly.

The Pattern Repeats: Technical Escapes

The January 6th case followed Trump’s lifelong pattern:

Strong Evidence of Wrongdoing: Extensive testimony from Trump’s own staff, documented pressure campaigns, recorded phone calls, the fake elector certificates

Competent Prosecution: Jack Smith built a comprehensive case with multiple witnesses ready to testify

Technical/Procedural Defense: Presidential immunity—a novel claim that required extensive appellate review

Extensive Litigation: Appeals consuming critical months, preventing trial before election

Changed Circumstances: Trump wins presidency, making prosecution impossible

Case Dismissed: Not on merits, but because DOJ policy bars prosecuting sitting presidents

Claims of Vindication: Trump declares “total exoneration” and “witch hunt” exposed

This is the same pattern as the bone spurs (medical deferment), housing discrimination (consent decree without admission), Atlantic City (bankruptcy protections), Trump University (settlement without admission), Trump Foundation (settlement without admission), and classified documents (dismissed on technical grounds).

Trump doesn’t win on the facts. He wins on procedure, delay, and changed circumstances.

And in the January 6th case, the Supreme Court provided exactly the delay Trump needed to win the presidency and escape accountability.

Jack Smith’s Congressional Testimony: “Proof Beyond a Reasonable Doubt” (December 17, 2024)

Jack Smith tells lawmakers his team developed ‘proof beyond a reasonable doubt’ against Trump

On December 17, 2024, Special Counsel Jack Smith testified for nearly eight hours before the House Judiciary Committee in a closed-door deposition. On December 31, 2024, Congress released the full 255-page transcript along with video of his testimony—the most comprehensive public accounting yet of Trump’s attempted coup.

Smith’s opening statement provided the most powerful confirmation that Trump committed crimes but escaped prosecution through electoral victory:

“Our investigation developed proof beyond a reasonable doubt that President Trump engaged in a criminal scheme to overturn the results of the 2020 presidential election and to prevent the lawful transfer of power.”

Those words—”proof beyond a reasonable doubt”—carry extraordinary weight. This is the highest standard of proof in American criminal law. It’s the standard required for conviction. When a prosecutor says they had “proof beyond a reasonable doubt,” they’re saying they could have proven guilt to a jury.

Jack Smith wasn’t expressing an opinion. He wasn’t making an allegation. He was stating under oath that his investigation developed evidence sufficient to convict Donald Trump of attempting to overturn a presidential election.

How Smith Built the Case: Call Records and Witness Testimony

The transcript reveals the methodical investigation that proved Trump’s guilt. Smith testified extensively about how his team used call records to piece together the White House’s communications with lawmakers before and on January 6th.

The call records were “critical to finding out how the White House communicated with lawmakers” during the crucial period when Trump was pressuring Congress to overturn the election.

Smith explained that the records didn’t include content—just metadata: “when the call started, when the call stopped ... and who the call is from.” This allowed investigators to establish which members of Trump’s inner circle contacted which lawmakers, and which senators Trump allegedly tried to reach during the Capitol attack.

Combined with witness interviews, these records allowed Smith’s team to reconstruct Trump’s pressure campaign in detail.

Trump’s Own Team Told Him His Claims Were False

Perhaps most damning, Smith testified about what the evidence showed regarding Trump’s knowledge that his election fraud claims were lies:

“Sometimes the communications were relevant to us to show that people were telling Donald Trump that the things he was saying were not true.”

This goes to criminal intent. Trump wasn’t deluded—he was lying. His own advisors, his own campaign staff, his own attorneys told him the election fraud claims were false. And Trump continued making those claims anyway, using them to pressure officials and incite the mob.

The Four Key Findings

Smith’s testimony establishes for the historical record:

The Indictment Was Proven: Smith confirms the four-count indictment wasn’t speculative—it was backed by overwhelming evidence meeting the conviction standard.

Trump’s Guilt Established: “Proof beyond a reasonable doubt” means the evidence showed Trump was guilty of engaging in a criminal scheme.

Trump Knew He Was Lying: Communications records and witness testimony proved people repeatedly told Trump his claims were false.

No Trial Needed to Know: While Trump never faced a jury, Smith’s testimony establishes that the prosecution had the evidence to convict.

Electoral Victory Ended Justice: Smith’s testimony makes explicit what the dismissal obscured: Trump avoided accountability not because the evidence was insufficient, but because he won an election.

This testimony will stand as the permanent congressional record. The Special Counsel confirmed under oath that Donald Trump committed federal crimes attempting to overturn a presidential election, that the investigation proved it beyond a reasonable doubt, that Trump knew his fraud claims were lies, but that Trump escaped prosecution by winning the presidency.

The evidence was there. The crimes were serious. The proof met the conviction standard. Trump’s own people told him the truth.

But there was no trial. No jury. No accountability.

Because Trump won an election and used presidential power to end the prosecution.
