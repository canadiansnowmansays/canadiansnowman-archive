The Charlie Kirk Debates

#16: Birth Control “Screws Up Female Brains” - Part I: The Case

January 6, 2026

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to your claim that birth control “screws up female brains.” At a 2024 TPUSA Faith event, you declared: “Birth control like really screws up female brains... It increases depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation. It creates very angry and bitter young ladies.” You blamed women’s Democratic voting on contraception while telling followers to control what medications women take. Prove me wrong that this isn’t pseudoscience serving patriarchal control.

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to scriptural light. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Kirk’s Birth Control Conspiracy

At a Turning Point USA Faith event in March 2024 (later widely circulated in April), Charlie Kirk unveiled a novel theory explaining why young women vote Democrat: birth control causes brain damage.

The Full Statement

Kirk’s birth control attack came during a broader rant about unmarried women and political affiliation:

“A lot of them are on birth control, too. And birth control like really screws up female brains, by the way. Every single one of you need to make sure that your loved ones are not on birth control. It increases depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation. Birth control is the number one prescribed medication for young ladies under the age of 25. They will give young ladies birth control for pimples, for acne, to control their moods, their period. It is awful, it’s terrible, and it creates very angry and bitter young ladies and young women.”

Kirk continued:

“Then that bitterness then manifests into a political party that is the bitter party. I mean, the Democrat Party is all about ‘bring us your bitterness and, you know, we’ll give you free stuff.’”

The Theory: Birth Control Makes Democrats

Kirk’s argument followed this logic:

  1. Birth control “screws up female brains”

  2. This creates “angry and bitter young ladies”

  3. Bitterness manifests as voting Democrat

  4. Therefore, controlling women’s contraception will change their politics

He presented this medical conspiracy theory as fact, instructing his audience—”Every single one of you”—to “make sure that your loved ones are not on birth control.”

The Broader Context: Women Past Their “Prime”

Kirk’s birth control attack came within a larger monologue about women’s life choices. He described his ideal political constituency:

“If you are a 32-year-old young lady who went to college, who has a nice apartment, a corporate job, and cats, you know, you’re a Democrat voter, right? And so, how do we win them over?”

His answer: marriage and motherhood transform women’s politics.

“What’s amazing is once they get married, they become 50-50. Once they have kids, they become like right-wing by 20 points.”

But he lamented that women delay marriage, creating political problems:

“We basically told a great generation of young women ‘don’t get married, don’t have kids, go get a corporate job,’ and it’s created mass political hysteria. And then in their early 30s they get really upset because they say ‘you know, the boys don’t want to date me anymore,’ because they’re not at their prime and people get mad when I say that — well, it’s just true. You’re in your early 30s, I’m sorry, you’re not as attractive in the dating pool as you were in your early 20s, but again, you have your corporate job and cats.”

Kirk positioned birth control as enabling women to delay traditional roles, pursue careers, and vote Democrat—all problems requiring male intervention.

The Call for Male Control

Kirk’s “make sure your loved ones are not on birth control” wasn’t medical advice—it was a call for men to control women’s reproductive autonomy. He framed contraception as something men should prevent their wives, daughters, and girlfriends from accessing.

This aligned with his broader messaging that young women have been “sold a lie” by culture and media about careers, independence, and equality. The solution: male authority reasserted through controlling women’s medical decisions.

Kirk’s Supposed Evidence

When challenged on his claims, Kirk pointed to:

  1. Side effect inserts - claiming birth control warnings prove his case

  2. Selected studies - cherry-picking research on hormonal effects

  3. Anecdotal emails - “They email me all the time” about regret

He cited articles with alarming headlines but ignored:

  • The actual prevalence rates in rigorous studies

  • The net benefits of contraception documented in research

  • Medical consensus on contraceptive safety

  • Women’s own reported experiences

The Political Project

Kirk’s birth control attack served clear political goals:

Explain the gender gap: Rather than address why women reject conservative politics, blame contraception for “brain damage”

Undermine reproductive autonomy: Frame contraception as dangerous, requiring male oversight

Promote traditional roles: Argue women’s political views will “fix” themselves through marriage and motherhood

Attack women’s independence: Link careers, education, and contraception to political and personal failure

Normalize male control: Instruct men to “make sure” women don’t access contraception

The Broader Anti-Contraception Campaign

Kirk’s statement reflected a coordinated MAGA campaign against birth control access. As Amanda Marcotte observed in Salon, the attack represents conditioning Republicans to accept contraception restrictions or bans.

Conservative figures increasingly invoke the dormant Comstock Act—an 1873 law that criminalized contraception distribution—as justification for federal birth control restrictions. Kirk’s medical misinformation laid groundwork for policy restrictions by framing contraception as harmful rather than beneficial.

The Historical Echo

Kirk’s rhetoric echoed Anthony Comstock himself, the 19th-century anti-vice crusader who:

  • Bragged about driving women to suicide through prosecution

  • Targeted Margaret Sanger for teaching contraception

  • Framed reproductive autonomy as threat to social order

Comstock’s crusade was explicitly about male control over women’s bodies and sexuality. Kirk’s 21st-century version updated the rhetoric—now it’s about “protecting” women from “brain damage”—but served the same function.

The Case Established: Kirk claimed birth control “screws up female brains,” creates “angry and bitter” women who vote Democrat, and instructed men to control women’s access to contraception. He provided cherry-picked studies while ignoring medical consensus, framed reproductive autonomy as political problem, and promoted male authority over women’s medical decisions.

Now let’s test Kirk’s medical claims, examine actual contraception research, explore what medical organizations say, and expose how controlling women’s bodies has always been about power, not health.
