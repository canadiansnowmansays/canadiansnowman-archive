The Charlie Kirk Debates

#8: “Black Women ‘Lack Brain Processing Power’” - Part I: The Case

November 11, 2025

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to scriptural light. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to your claim that prominent Black women leaders “do not have the brain processing power to otherwise be taken really seriously.” You named Joy Reid, Michelle Obama, Sheila Jackson Lee, and Ketanji Brown Jackson, saying they had to “steal a white person’s slot” to succeed. Prove me wrong.

Kirk’s Statements on Black Women’s Intelligence

On July 13, 2023, during an episode of The Charlie Kirk Show responding to the Supreme Court’s affirmative action ruling, Charlie Kirk targeted four prominent Black women by name, questioning their intelligence and legitimacy.

The “Brain Processing Power” Attack

Kirk’s words left no room for interpretation:

“If we would have said three weeks ago that Joy Reid and Michelle Obama and Sheila Jackson Lee and Ketanji Brown Jackson were affirmative-action picks, we would have been called racist. But now they’re coming out and they’re saying it for us! They’re coming out and they’re saying, ‘I’m only here because of affirmative action.’ Yeah, we know. You do not have the brain processing power to otherwise be taken really seriously. You had to go steal a white person’s slot to go be taken somewhat seriously.”

This wasn’t a policy critique about affirmative action’s effectiveness. Kirk made a declaration about these women’s inherent intellectual capacity—a deeply personal attack that revealed his fundamental beliefs about race, gender, and intelligence. The violence of these words cannot be understated: telling accomplished women that their minds are inferior strikes at the core of human dignity.

The Women Kirk Targeted

Kirk specifically named four accomplished Black women:

  • Joy Reid: MSNBC host and Harvard graduate

  • Michelle Obama: Former First Lady, Princeton and Harvard Law graduate

  • Sheila Jackson Lee: Late Congresswoman, Yale and University of Virginia Law graduate

  • Ketanji Brown Jackson: Supreme Court Justice, Harvard and Harvard Law graduate

Each represents excellence in their field, yet Kirk reduced them to thieves of white opportunity.

The “Stealing Slots” Narrative

Kirk’s language revealed a zero-sum worldview where opportunities inherently belong to white people unless “stolen” by others. This framing assumes that white people have a natural claim to positions of power and prestige, and that any Black person in such positions must have obtained them illegitimately. He portrayed their achievements not as earned through merit, but as theft from more deserving white candidates.

Kirk mocked Rep. Jackson Lee’s pronunciation and dismissed Joy Reid’s influence: “If people like me that are where we are because of affirmative action, how could they survive?” The contempt was palpable.

Kirk’s Pattern of Racist Comments

This attack fit into Kirk’s broader pattern of questioning Black competence:

  • On January 23, 2024 (The Charlie Kirk Show): “If I see a Black pilot, I’m going to be like, ‘Boy, I hope he’s qualified.’”

  • In a 2023 episode: “If I’m dealing with somebody in customer service who’s a moronic Black woman, I wonder is she there because of her excellence, or is she there because of affirmative action?”

The Academic Irony

The deepest irony: Kirk, who attended Harper College for one semester before dropping out, presumed to judge the intellectual capacity of women with degrees from Yale, Harvard, Princeton, and University of Virginia Law School. A man who couldn’t complete freshman year questioned the intelligence of Harvard Law graduates.

The Historical Echo

Kirk’s “brain processing power” language directly echoed centuries of racist pseudoscience that claimed Black people possessed inferior cognitive abilities. As Ibram X. Kendi documents in Stamped from the Beginning (2016), these theories included:

  • Phrenology: The false science of skull measurements to “prove” racial hierarchy

  • IQ testing: Weaponized to justify forced sterilizations and immigration bans

  • “Scientific racism”: Used to rationalize slavery and Jim Crow laws

These weren’t abstract theories—they shaped policies that destroyed lives. Forced sterilizations, educational segregation, and employment discrimination all flowed from the same poisonous well Kirk was drawing from. He simply updated 19th-century racism for a 21st-century audience. A stark recent reminder came with the death of James Watson, co-discoverer of DNA’s structure, on November 6, 2025. In 2007, Watson claimed Africans have inherently lower intelligence than Europeans, declaring it a “fact” underpinning social policies and suggesting genetic intervention as the path to equality. Even after retraction and professional fallout, Watson doubled down, underscoring how such “genius”-backed bigotry endures, lending pseudoscience a lab coat Kirk’s rhetoric gratefully borrows.

Kirk’s Framework

Kirk’s attack revealed his operating premises:

  • Intellectual Hierarchy: White people possess inherently superior cognitive capacity

  • Illegitimate Success: Black women’s achievements are automatically suspect

  • Zero-Sum Thinking: Their gains necessarily mean white losses

  • Credential Irrelevance: No degree can overcome racial cognitive deficits

  • Natural Order: Power and prestige naturally belong to white people

The Stakes & Public Reaction

Kirk wasn’t making a nuanced policy argument—he was promoting the most fundamental form of racism: the belief that Black women are intellectually inferior by nature. No achievement—Harvard degree, Supreme Court seat, First Lady of the United States—could overcome this supposed biological deficit.

The backlash was immediate and fierce. Critics across the political spectrum called it what it was: naked racism. Conservative defenders claimed Kirk’s words were taken “out of context,” though the full video only made it worse. Kirk himself responded dismissively: “Discussing policy isn’t hate” (X, July 2023).

The controversy gained new life after 2025’s events. Washington Post columnist Karen Attiah was fired for quoting Kirk’s words in her social media posts, highlighting how even repeating his racism verbatim was deemed too controversial. This suppression of legitimate criticism showed how certain figures become untouchable even in death. The divisions persist, with some rallying to “protect” Kirk’s legacy while others document his actual words and their impact.
