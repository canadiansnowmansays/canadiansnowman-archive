How America Built the World Order and Chose to Destroy It

Introduction: A twelve-part history from the Canadian side of the table

April 28, 2026

The Witness

My great-uncle Hugh Keenleyside was in the room when it began.

Not metaphorically. Literally. In the conference rooms and diplomatic corridors of the 1940s, Hugh Llewellyn Keenleyside — Canadian diplomat, internationalist, one of the most consequential civil servants his country ever produced — was present at the creation of the world that is now being unmade. He attended the conferences, drafted the frameworks, argued the positions, and shook the hands of the men who built the post-war order. He was there when the Americans, somewhat to their own surprise, became responsible for the world.

I am his country’s neighbor’s most persistent critic, writing from Toronto in the spring of 2026, watching B-52 bombers take off from a British airfield to strike a Persian city while the Strait of Hormuz charges tolls in Iranian rials and the United States Secretary of Defense tells reporters that America negotiates with bombs. I am trying to understand how the country that Hugh Keenleyside watched build the world order came to be the primary threat to it.

This series is my attempt at that understanding.

Hugh L Keenleyside at home in Victoria BC, 1984. Harry Palmer Photographs by Harry Palmer

Hugh Keenleyside was born in 1898 in Toronto — the same city where I am writing this — and died in 1992, having outlived most of the world he helped construct. He joined the Canadian Department of External Affairs in 1928 and spent the next four decades at the intersection of Canadian-American relations, multilateral institution-building, and the slow, difficult work of creating a world that might not destroy itself again.

His career traced the arc of the American century almost perfectly. In the 1930s he served in Japan and Washington, watching the old order crack. During the Second World War he was one of the principal architects of the wartime Canadian-American relationship — the Permanent Joint Board on Defence, the continental security arrangements, the Alaska Highway, the extraordinary cooperation between two countries that had to figure out, in real time, how to be allies without one absorbing the other. After the war he worked within the early United Nations system on development and technical assistance, helping to build the frameworks that would become the Colombo Plan and eventually the entire architecture of international development cooperation.

He was, in other words, a builder. Of institutions, of partnerships, of the unglamorous but essential infrastructure of international order. He understood something that the men who came after him — and the men who are now dismantling what he built — have consistently failed to grasp: that power is only durable when it is legitimate, and legitimacy requires consent.

The Americans he worked with in the 1940s understood this. Not all of them, and not always. But enough of them, enough of the time, to construct something unprecedented: a global order that other nations joined voluntarily, that they defended as their own, that survived the inevitable American blunders and selfishness and occasional imperial overreach because its foundations were solid enough to absorb the shocks.

Hugh Keenleyside watched that order being built. I am watching it being destroyed.

Hugh Keenleyside calling on President Miguel Alemán of Mexico, 1947.

This is a Canadian’s history of American power.

That positioning is deliberate and, I think, necessary. Americans cannot easily write this history because they are inside it — too close to the achievements to see the contradictions, too invested in the mythology to examine the mechanisms. Europeans can write it but they write it from the position of supplicants, which distorts the analysis in the other direction. The Chinese and Russians write it as adversaries, which is its own distortion.

Canadians occupy a singular vantage point. We share the longest undefended border in the world with the United States. We watch American television, read American newspapers, consume American culture, and depend on American military power for our security — a dependence we are comfortable enough with to forget most of the time, and anxious enough about to remember the rest. We are not Americans, but we are not foreign to America. We understand it from the inside without being of it.

Hugh Keenleyside understood this instinctively. Throughout his career he operated in the space between Canadian sovereignty and American power — close enough to be useful, independent enough to be honest. That is the space I am trying to occupy in this series.

Canada joined the Permanent Joint Board on Defence in 1940 not because it had no choice but because it calculated, correctly, that continental defence cooperation served Canadian interests. Canada did not simply defer to American leadership after 1945 — it actively shaped the multilateral institutions that constrained and legitimized that leadership. Canada’s diplomats, including Keenleyside, understood that the best way to live next to a superpower was to embed it in rules it had helped write and institutions it had helped build. An America bound by its own commitments was a manageable America. An America that had repudiated those commitments was something else entirely.

We are now finding out what that something else looks like.

The argument of this series, stated plainly:

The United States became the most powerful nation in history not primarily because of its military force — though that was formidable — but because it built an international order that other nations chose to join, maintain, and defend as their own. The genius of the post-war American settlement was not domination but legitimacy: the creation of institutions, alliances, and norms that distributed the benefits of American power widely enough that the recipients had a stake in its continuation.

This was not altruism. It was enlightened self-interest of the highest order. An America embedded in legitimate multilateral institutions was an America whose power was amplified, sustained, and protected by the cooperation of others. The Marshall Plan was not charity — it was the most effective strategic investment in American history, creating the prosperous allies whose markets and military contributions underpinned American dominance for eight decades.

The system had failures, contradictions, and hypocrisies. Vietnam was a catastrophe. Iraq 2003 was a self-inflicted wound from which American credibility has never fully recovered. The financial crisis of 2008 was made in America and exported to the world. American support for authoritarian regimes in the name of anti-communism undermined the liberal values the order was supposed to embody.

But the system survived all of these because its foundations were sound. Nations that disagreed with specific American decisions continued to support the overall order because the alternative — a world without American leadership — seemed worse. Even America’s critics, through the Cold War and after it, quietly depended on the stability that American power provided.

What is different now is not that America has made mistakes. It always has. What is different is that America has chosen to repudiate the order itself — the institutions, the alliances, the norms, the commitments that made its power legitimate and sustainable. The Trump administration has not merely pursued controversial policies within the existing framework. It has systematically dismantled the framework.

The result is what Robert Kagan — writing in The Atlantic in March 2026, as B-52s flew over Tehran and the Strait of Hormuz charged shipping tolls — called “the rogue American superpower.” A nation that is simultaneously the world’s strongest military force and its primary source of instability. A power that has more aircraft carriers than any other nation and fewer reliable allies than at any point since the 1930s.

Smoke rises over a Tehran highway on March 5. (Atta Kenare / AFP / Getty)

Hugh Keenleyside’s generation built the architecture to prevent exactly this outcome. They had watched what happened when the world’s leading power retreated from international responsibility — when American isolationism in the 1930s left the democracies to face fascism without the world’s largest economy at their side. They built the post-war order specifically to make that retreat impossible — to embed the United States so deeply in international institutions, alliances, and commitments that no future administration could simply walk away.

They did not anticipate an administration that would walk away anyway, and call it strength.

This series traces the arc from Keenleyside’s world to Hegseth’s. From the conference rooms of the 1940s where the order was being built to the Pentagon briefing room of March 31, 2026, where the Secretary of Defense told reporters that America negotiates with bombs.

Twelve essays. Eighty years. One question: how did the reluctant empire that Hugh Keenleyside watched being built become the rogue superpower that is now dismantling it?

The answer is long, complicated, and not entirely America’s fault. But it is mostly America’s fault. And understanding it is not an academic exercise. It is the most urgent analytical task of our moment — because the order that is being destroyed was the closest thing the world has ever constructed to a framework for avoiding catastrophe, and we are now learning what its absence looks like, in real time, from the shores of the Persian Gulf.

The tankers are waiting. The toll booth is open. The B-52s are overhead.

Let us go back to the beginning.
