The Reluctant Empire

The Architecture

Part II: NATO, the United Nations, Bretton Woods, and the Institutions That Turned Power Into Leadership

April 30, 2026

Secretary of State Dean Acheson signs the Washington Treaty, April 4, 1949. President Harry Truman and Vice President Alben Barkley are standing next to him. Records of the Office of the Chief Signal Officer.

In the spring of 1949, the United States did something it had never done in peacetime and had explicitly promised itself it would never do: it signed a permanent military alliance with European nations, committing itself to go to war if any of them was attacked.

The Senate ratified the North Atlantic Treaty by 82 votes to 13. Senator Arthur Vandenberg of Michigan, who had been one of America’s most influential isolationists until Pearl Harbor, rose to explain his vote in favour. He had changed his mind, he said, not because he had abandoned his belief in American independence, but because he had come to understand that in the world that existed in 1949, independence was not achievable through isolation. The only way for the United States to remain free was to ensure that its neighbours in the democratic world remained free as well.

It was the most consequential vote in the history of the United States Senate. And it rested on an architectural insight that the Trump administration has spent eight years systematically dismantling: that American power is most durable when it is least arbitrary.

Why Architecture Matters

The word “architecture” is used deliberately in discussions of the post-war international order. Architecture implies design — the intentional arrangement of elements to achieve specific purposes. But it also implies constraint. A well-designed building channels movement, shapes behaviour, limits what can be done in which spaces. The post-war international architecture was designed not merely to project American power but to shape and constrain it — to create a system in which American dominance was exercised through rules that applied to everyone, including America.

This was a radical departure from how great powers had previously organized international relations. The Concert of Europe — the nineteenth-century system that managed relations between the great powers after the Napoleonic Wars — was explicitly a system for the management of power by the powerful. The strong negotiated among themselves. Smaller nations had no formal role. The rules were whatever the great powers agreed on this week, and they changed when the great powers’ interests changed.

The post-war American architecture was different in kind, not just degree. The United Nations gave every sovereign state — including the smallest and weakest — a formal voice in the General Assembly. The International Monetary Fund and World Bank had governance structures that gave developing nations representation, however imperfect. The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade bound the United States to the same rules on international commerce that it expected of others. NATO committed the United States to the defence of countries it could have simply dominated.

None of these constraints was absolute. The United States retained enormous latitude to pursue its interests, and it exercised that latitude frequently and not always benevolently. The Security Council veto meant that the UN could not act against American interests. The IMF and World Bank, in practice, often served as instruments of American economic policy in the developing world. NATO was always asymmetric — American military capability and political weight vastly exceeded that of its allies.

But the framework mattered. The constraints were real even when they were imperfect. The requirement to justify American actions within multilateral frameworks — to make arguments, build coalitions, accept some scrutiny — produced a different kind of American behaviour than raw unilateralism would have. And crucially, it produced a different perception of American power among the nations that lived under it. An America embedded in rules it had helped write was an America that could be criticized, negotiated with, and sometimes constrained. That was tolerable. An America that operated purely on the basis of its own judgment, answerable to no framework except its own interests — that was something else.

That something else is what the world is now discovering.

The United Nations: The Imperfect Masterpiece

A delegate signs the United Nations Charter at the closing ceremony of the San Francisco Conference, June 26, 1945. Fifty nations signed the Charter that day — the largest gathering of sovereign states in history to that point. The ceremony's staging told the story: one table, one document, fifty flags. Every signatory nation, regardless of size or power, affixed its name to the same text. The UN was not designed to enforce world peace through collective action. It was designed to create a forum for the management of international disputes and a set of norms that defined acceptable state behaviour — functions imperfect in execution but transformative in effect. Canada's delegation, which included officials from Hugh Keenleyside's Department of External Affairs, helped shape provisions on human rights and development that would define the organization's broader mandate.

The United Nations is the most ambitious institutional creation in human history and the most consistently misunderstood.

Its critics on the right have always dismissed it as a talking shop — a forum for anti-American rhetoric that accomplishes nothing while consuming American resources. Its admirers on the left have always expected more from it than it was designed to deliver, and been perpetually disappointed. Both camps mistake the instrument for the intention.

The UN was not designed to enforce world peace through collective action. The architects of San Francisco — and Hugh Keenleyside was among the Canadian diplomats who helped shape its provisions — understood perfectly well that collective enforcement required great power consensus that would rarely exist. The Security Council veto, which frustrates idealists endlessly, was not a design flaw. It was a deliberate recognition that an organization the great powers could not veto would be an organization the great powers would not join. Better an imperfect institution that included the powerful than a perfect institution they would ignore.

What the UN was designed to do — and what it has done, with genuine if uneven success — was create a forum for the management of international disputes, a set of norms that defined acceptable and unacceptable state behaviour, and a mechanism for collective legitimization of actions that required international support to be sustainable.

That last function is the one most commonly overlooked and most important. The United States intervened in Korea in 1950 with UN authorization. It did not intervene in Hungary in 1956, in part because Soviet veto power made UN authorization impossible and American unilateralism would have looked identical to Soviet behaviour. The distinction shaped American choices in ways that mattered for the order’s legitimacy. When the United States invaded Iraq in 2003 without UN authorization, the absence of that legitimization was not merely a procedural inconvenience. It was a signal to the world — a signal that proved correct — that American power was becoming less constrained, less predictable, and therefore less safe to align with.

The UN’s specialised agencies — WHO, UNICEF, UNHCR, UNESCO, the World Food Programme — have delivered genuine and measurable improvements in human welfare across the developing world. The UN Development Programme, within which Keenleyside did some of his most significant work, built the institutional frameworks for international development cooperation that — however inadequately — addressed the consequences of colonialism and poverty that the great powers had created and then left behind.

None of this is to romanticize the UN. It has been captured by dictatorships, paralyzed by great power competition, and used for cynical purposes by every member state including the United States. The General Assembly has passed resolutions of breathtaking hypocrisy. The Human Rights Council has been chaired by some of the world’s most egregious human rights violators.

But the alternative to a flawed institution is not a better institution. It is no institution. And no institution means no framework, no norms, no forum for the management of disputes except the raw exercise of power by the most powerful. The Trump administration’s contempt for the United Nations — its refusal to fund it adequately, its willingness to ignore its proceedings, its preference for bilateral transactions over multilateral frameworks — is not a critique of the UN’s imperfections. It is a preference for the world the UN was designed to supersede.

Bretton Woods: The Dollar’s Bargain

Harry Dexter White (left) and John Maynard Keynes (right) at the Bretton Woods Conference, New Hampshire, July 1944. They disagreed sharply on the details but together designed the architecture that made the US dollar the world's reserve currency. The "exorbitant privilege" it gave the United States rests entirely on the world's continued willingness to hold dollars. That willingness is not unconditional.

The most technically complex and economically consequential element of the post-war architecture is also the least understood by the general public: the international monetary system established at Bretton Woods in July 1944.

The 730 delegates from 44 nations who gathered at the Mount Washington Hotel in the White Mountains of New Hampshire for three weeks in the summer of 1944 were trying to solve a specific problem: how to organize international trade and finance in the post-war world in a way that would prevent the competitive devaluations, trade wars, and financial instability that had contributed to the Great Depression and, by extension, to the Second World War.

The solution they constructed — largely designed by the American economist Harry Dexter White and the British economist John Maynard Keynes, who disagreed sharply on the details — was a system of fixed but adjustable exchange rates anchored to the US dollar, which was itself fixed to gold at $35 per ounce. The International Monetary Fund would provide short-term credit to countries facing balance of payments difficulties. The World Bank — originally called the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development — would provide long-term financing for reconstruction and development.

For the United States, Bretton Woods was a remarkable bargain. By making the dollar the world’s reserve currency — the currency in which international trade was denominated, in which central banks held their reserves, in which commodity prices were set — the United States gained an “exorbitant privilege,” as French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d’Estaing would later call it: the ability to run persistent deficits, to print the currency the world needed, to finance its foreign policy and military commitments with money that the rest of the world was, in effect, obligated to accept.

In exchange, the United States accepted certain obligations. It had to maintain the dollar’s convertibility to gold. It had to operate the IMF and World Bank as genuinely multilateral institutions, not simply as extensions of American Treasury policy. It had to accept that other nations’ monetary policies were their own business, not subject to American veto.

These constraints were real, and they occasionally chafed. But they were also what made the dollar’s privilege sustainable. The world accepted the dollar as the reserve currency not because it had no choice — the Europeans could, in theory, have constructed an alternative — but because the system it anchored delivered genuine benefits: stable exchange rates, accessible financing, a predictable framework for international trade. The dollar’s privilege rested on the dollar’s trustworthiness, and the dollar’s trustworthiness rested on the United States’ willingness to honour its obligations.

When Nixon broke the link between the dollar and gold in August 1971 — the subject of Part V of this series — he demonstrated that American commitments were not unconditional. The world adapted. The dollar survived as the reserve currency even without the gold anchor because no alternative had been developed. But the seed of doubt was planted: the anchor that held the system together had been revealed as something the United States would abandon when it became inconvenient.

The Bretton Woods insight — that American financial power was most sustainable when embedded in institutional frameworks that constrained American behaviour — remains relevant in 2026, even though the specific system of 1944 has long since evolved. The dollar’s reserve currency status, which gives the United States the ability to impose sanctions, to finance deficits that would bankrupt any other country, to conduct foreign policy through financial instruments — all of this rests on the world’s continued willingness to hold dollars. And that willingness is not unconditional. It is a judgment, constantly renewed, that the benefits of the dollar system outweigh the costs of American financial power.

Every time the United States uses that financial power arbitrarily — to punish allies as well as adversaries, to impose sanctions that serve domestic political purposes rather than strategic ones, to weaponize the dollar system in ways that make other nations worry they might be next — it erodes the judgment that the system is worth tolerating. China’s patient construction of alternative financial architecture — the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, the digital yuan, the bilateral currency swap agreements — is not irrational aggression. It is the rational response of a nation that has watched the United States use the dollar system as a weapon and has decided to invest in alternatives.

NATO: The Commitment That Changed Everything

In 1950, senior British and French army officers observe a North Atlantic Treaty Organisation (NATO) exercise in West Germany. Lieutenant Colonel F. Stephens, Commanding Officer 1st Battalion The Rifle Brigade, explains the exercise to a group of officers including General Jean de Lattre de Tassigny, Major General Robert Harry Bertram Arkwright, General Officer Commanding 7th Armoured Division and Lieutenant General Sir Charles F. Keightley, Commander-in-Chief British Army of the Rhine.

Of all the architectural achievements of the post-war order, the North Atlantic Treaty Organization is the most consequential and the most misunderstood.

It is misunderstood primarily as a military alliance — a collective defence arrangement that pools the military resources of its members to deter Soviet aggression. That is what it is. But that description misses the more fundamental transformation it represented: the permanent entanglement of the United States in European security, the end of American isolationism as a viable strategic option, and the creation of an institutional framework that would shape American foreign policy for eight decades.

The genius of NATO was Article 5 — the collective defence clause that declared an attack on one member an attack on all. The clause did not commit the United States to any specific response. It did not require automatic war. What it did was create an expectation — a credible commitment — that the United States would respond to aggression against its allies. That expectation was the deterrent. The actual military capability was secondary to the political commitment.

This is why Trump’s repeated questioning of the Article 5 commitment — his suggestions that America might not defend NATO allies who do not meet defence spending targets, his intimation at the Saudi sovereign wealth fund conference in March 2026 that America might not need to honour its NATO commitments — is so strategically destructive. The deterrent value of NATO rests entirely on the credibility of the commitment. An Article 5 that might or might not be honoured depending on the current American president’s mood is not a deterrent. It is a suggestion. And suggestions do not deter.

NATO also served a function that its architects understood but rarely discussed publicly: it kept the Germans down, the Americans in, and the Russians out, as Lord Ismay, NATO’s first Secretary General, famously said. Germany — the nation whose aggressive nationalism had twice in thirty years produced catastrophic European wars — was rearmed and integrated into the Western alliance in a framework that guaranteed its defence while embedding it in institutional constraints that made aggressive unilateralism impossible. France and Britain — exhausted former great powers that would have spent the next decades competing with each other if left to their own devices — were locked into a framework that channelled their remaining energy toward collective defence rather than bilateral rivalry.

This is the aspect of NATO’s legacy that is most directly relevant to the current crisis. The alliance was not merely a military arrangement. It was a political technology for managing the relationships between nations that, left unmanaged, would compete destructively. The institutions it created — the regular consultations, the integrated military commands, the shared intelligence, the common doctrine — produced a degree of political intimacy between the United States and its European allies that had no historical precedent. European leaders could pick up the phone and reach American counterparts. American military planners coordinated daily with European counterparts. The relationship was not equal — it was never equal — but it was genuinely collaborative in ways that changed how the parties thought about each other and about the problems they faced together.

That intimacy is what Trump’s treatment of the allies has destroyed. The Europeans who were told they were not doing enough, threatened with tariffs, watched their intelligence sharing curtailed, and then invited to join a war they had not been consulted about and that threatened their energy supplies — those Europeans are not going to rebuild the intimacy of the post-war relationship simply because a future American administration changes its tone. Trust, once broken, is rebuilt slowly and incompletely. The institutional damage inflicted since 2017 will take decades to repair, if it is ever repaired.

The Lesson of the Architecture

NATO HQ Brussels: The angular glass-and-steel building completed in 2018 photographs beautifully. The SOM architectural shots in particular capture the modernist severity of the structure.

The post-war international architecture — imperfect, contradictory, often inadequate to the challenges it faced — represented a genuinely new approach to the organization of international relations. Not utopian. Not idealistic. Practical, hard-headed, and grounded in the specific lesson that the architects had learned from the catastrophe they had just survived.

That lesson was: unconstrained great power competition leads to war. The only alternative is a system of rules and institutions that gives great powers reasons to cooperate rather than conflict. And those reasons must be genuine — rooted in real interests, not rhetorical commitments — or the system will not hold.

The architects got this right. The system they built held, imperfectly but consequentially, for eight decades. It held through the Cuban Missile Crisis. It held through Vietnam. It held through the financial crisis of 2008. It held through the first Trump term.

It is not holding through the second.

What is being dismantled is not merely a collection of policies. It is the architecture itself — the institutional framework, the alliance commitments, the norms of consultation and collective decision-making that gave American power its legitimacy. When Hegseth says “we negotiate with bombs,” he is not just describing a tactic. He is announcing the abandonment of the architectural principle that made American leadership sustainable.

Hugh Keenleyside’s generation would have understood what that means. They built the architecture specifically because they knew what the alternative looked like. It looked like 1939.
