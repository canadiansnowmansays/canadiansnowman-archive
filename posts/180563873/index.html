Moscow Redux - When Five Hours Yields Nothing But Validation

Part 12 of the Trump Foreign Policy Series

December 2, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays December 2, 2025

The Pattern Completes: From 28 Points to 19, From Capitulation to Theater

Two weeks ago, I documented how Trump’s “Week of Weakness” revealed his foreign policy as psychology masquerading as strategy—the Epstein files humiliation triggering death threats against Democratic lawmakers while producing a 28-point Ukraine peace plan that read like Putin’s wish list. Today, that analysis gained five more hours of evidence.

Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner spent nearly five hours with Vladimir Putin at the Kremlin on December 2nd. The marathon session produced no specific compromises, no agreement on territorial control, and no scheduled Putin-Trump summit—but it did provide something far more valuable to Putin: American validation of Russian positions without European interference.

The TACO pattern I identified in September—Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate—hasn’t broken. It’s evolved into performative diplomacy that mistakes activity for achievement while systematically excluding America’s actual allies.

The Moscow Meeting: Five Hours of “Constructive” Stalemate

Kremlin aide Yuri Ushakov described the talks as “very useful, constructive and very substantive,” lasting “not five minutes but five hours.” “We could agree on some things,” Ushakov said, while other proposals “provoked criticism” and Putin made “no secret of our critical and even negative attitude toward a number of proposals.”

Translation: Putin spent five hours explaining why Trump should offer more.

This was Witkoff’s sixth meeting with Putin in Russia but the first in-person meeting between Putin and Trump’s team since the Alaska summit in August, and Kushner’s first direct participation in the talks Axios. Kirill Dmitriev—head of the Russian Direct Investment Fund—joined Putin during talks that stretched past midnight.

Dmitriev’s presence is telling. As I documented in November, Witkoff and Dmitriev spent “three days huddled” together in Miami drafting the original plan, with Dmitriev expressing “optimism because ‘we feel the Russian position is really being heard.’”

When Russia’s chief negotiator is optimistic about how well “the Russian position is really being heard,” you’re not negotiating—you’re transcribing.

From 28 Points to 19: Trimming the Wish List, Not Changing It

The U.S. envoys presented Putin with a revised peace plan “whittled from 28 points down to 19 during talks with the Ukrainians” Axios. The initial plan was “seen by Ukraine and its backers as heavily tilted toward Russia,” and “as it became more palatable to the Ukrainians with subsequent revisions, it likely became less attractive to the Russians” Axios.

The original 28-point plan demanded Ukraine withdraw from territories it currently controls in Donetsk, recognize Crimea as “de facto Russian,” cap its military, renounce NATO membership, and provide “full amnesty” for war crimes. Reducing Putin’s wish list from 28 demands to 19 doesn’t transform capitulation into compromise. It just makes the surrender document shorter.

Ushakov said some American formulations on territory “look more or less acceptable but need to be discussed,” while others “don’t work for us.” Notably, the U.S. presented “a document containing 27 points” along with “four additional documents” NBC News, suggesting the plan continues morphing to accommodate Russian objections.

Translation: Putin will continue negotiating with himself, mediated by American envoys, while Europe watches from the sidelines.

Europe Excluded, Putin Empowered: The Alliance Abandonment Accelerates

The most damning aspect wasn’t what happened in the Kremlin—it’s who wasn’t there.

European officials were not involved in the Florida or Moscow dialogues, and U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio plans to skip a NATO foreign ministers meeting in Brussels on Wednesday Foreign Policy. America’s top diplomat is skipping a NATO meeting to avoid defending a peace plan developed without European input.

Before the Moscow meeting, Putin accused Kyiv’s European allies of being “on the side of war,” claiming European changes to proposals were “aimed at only one thing: to completely block this peace process.”

Putin then threatened Ukraine’s European allies directly: “We are not planning to fight with Europe, but if Europe suddenly starts a war with us, we are ready right now.” He suggested Russia would use “even greater violence in such a conflict than it has in Ukraine”—a likely reference to Moscow’s nuclear arsenal.

Putin continued: “If Europe suddenly starts a war with us, I think it will end very quickly. If Europe starts a war with Russia, then Moscow will soon have ‘no one to negotiate with’” Foreign Policy.

This is Trump’s diplomatic environment: Putin threatening nuclear annihilation of NATO allies while Trump’s envoys spend five hours in the Kremlin without a single European representative present. Meanwhile, EU foreign affairs chief Kaja Kallas stated: “It is clear that Russia does not want peace, and therefore we need to make Ukraine as strong as possible” NBC News.

Europe sees the threat clearly. Trump sees a deal-making opportunity.

Battlefield Posturing: Putin’s Pre-Negotiation Flex

Putin made public appearances ahead of talks promoting Russia’s battlefield progress, underscoring Russia’s upper hand to convince his interlocutors that Kyiv must settle on his terms. He claimed to have captured Pokrovsk, though Kyiv denied Moscow has full control. Ukrainian official Andriy Kovalenko said “the Russians will make many attempts to exert pressure on the front in the coming weeks,” noting “all this is done exclusively for the Western audience and to raise the stakes in diplomacy.”

Putin understands Trump’s psychology: claim victories, threaten escalation, watch Trump pressure Ukraine to accept worse terms.

The Witkoff Problem: When Your Envoy Coaches the Other Side

Bloomberg previously published a phone transcript in which Witkoff reportedly advised Yuri Ushakov, a top Putin official, on how Putin should approach Trump in peace negotiations—a transcript the White House did not dispute CBS News.

Witkoff “is viewed with suspicion by Kyiv and its allies after a leaked phone call suggested he was advising Moscow on how to deal with Trump” and has been “long criticized for being too accommodating to Russia” Putin meets Witkoff and Kushner for five-hour Ukraine talks but no immediate breakthrough +2.

When your special envoy coaches the opposing side on negotiation tactics with your own president, you’re not conducting diplomacy—you’re performing theater where the script is written in Moscow.

Ukraine’s Perspective: “There Will Be No Easy Decisions”

Witkoff and Kushner are expected to meet Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky on Wednesday in Europe to brief him on the Putin talks Axios. The Moscow meeting came two days after American and Ukrainian delegations met in Miami for talks both sides called constructive.

Zelensky said Tuesday he expected to speak with Witkoff and Kushner “right after their meeting with Putin,” stating “the chances now to end the war are better than ever,” while cautioning “there will be no easy decisions” Axios.

Zelensky warned any deal must ensure Russia doesn’t attack again after Moscow’s invasions in 2014 and 2022: “We must end this war in a way that prevents Russia from coming back a year later with a third invasion. They did not achieve their goal of occupying our country. But I am not sure their objectives have changed.”

Putin has demanded limits on Ukraine’s military while Russia remains free to build up its forces—an asymmetry Trump refuses to address. That’s not a peace plan. It’s a reloading period.

The Kushner Wild Card: Real Estate Thinking Applied to Existential Conflicts

Kushner’s involvement began months ago as he worked alongside Witkoff to negotiate the October Israel-Hamas ceasefire, with Trump stating “we always bring Jared when we want to get that deal closed” CBC News. But Kushner has no official White House adviser title, and in a 2021 Wall Street Journal op-ed, wrote that the Israel-Hamas war was “nothing more than a real-estate dispute between Israelis and Palestinians” CBC News.

If Kushner views existential conflicts as “real-estate disputes,” his approach to Ukraine becomes predictable: territorial swaps, property settlements, deal-making divorced from strategic implications. Complex geopolitical struggles reduced to transactional negotiations where everyone gets something, consequences be damned.

The Credibility Chasm

My September and November analyses documented Trump’s pattern of empty ultimatums throughout 2025—threats Putin systematically ignored, deadlines that evaporated, each making the next less credible.

But this peace plan is different. It’s not a threat Putin needs to outlast—it’s a gift Putin can accept whenever convenient, having spent five hours explaining how America should sweeten the terms.

Russia has demanded Ukraine cede remaining Donbas territory, drop NATO aspirations, and secure status for the Russian language and Russian Orthodox Church—demands Ukraine has refused. In November, Russian forces almost doubled their September battlefield gains, highlighting increasing strain on Ukraine’s military.

Putin’s calculation: Keep grinding forward militarily, wait for Ukrainian exhaustion and American attention deficit, then accept whatever territorial concessions Trump offers while rejecting meaningful constraints on Russian rearmament.

Conclusion: Accommodation Without End, Alliance Without Trust

The five-hour Moscow meeting reveals the bankruptcy of Trump’s approach: maximum process, minimum progress; extensive engagement with Russia, systematic exclusion of Europe; territorial discussions without security guarantees; American mediation that Russians view as “really hearing” their position.

My November analysis asked whether Trump’s “paper tiger” declaration represented “strategic awakening or tactical theater.” The answer is definitive: it was theater. The accommodation pattern never broke.

The details tell the story:

  • Europe excluded from negotiations about European security

  • Rubio skipping NATO meeting rather than defending the indefensible

  • Putin threatening nuclear escalation against NATO allies

  • American envoy coaching Russian officials on Trump management

  • Territorial concessions without military restrictions

  • Peace plan Russians view as “really hearing” their position

This isn’t diplomacy. It’s capitulation with extra steps.

From a Canadian perspective, watching America’s foreign policy deteriorate from strategic doctrine to psychological theater is genuinely alarming. We share a continent with a superpower whose president excludes actual allies from security negotiations while spending five hours hearing out an adversary’s objections to surrender terms that weren’t onerous enough.

The Alaska summit was predictable accommodation. September’s reversal was surprising resistance. November’s 28-point plan was crude capitulation. December’s five-hour Kremlin session? Putin explaining to Trump’s envoys why the surrender document needs more Russian-friendly revisions.

My September analysis concluded: “Trump’s foreign policy is not strategy. It’s psychology. It’s not doctrine. It’s disorder.”

Today added five more hours of evidence. Putin got American validation, European exclusion, and Ukrainian isolation—all wrapped in “constructive” language that mistakes engagement for achievement.

The pattern never broke. It evolved. And Europe is taking notes.
