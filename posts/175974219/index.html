The Comey Indictment

What Really Happened Here?

How Trump's Enemies List Became Federal Indictments

October 12, 2025

James Comey got indicted on September 25th. If you’re scratching your head wondering how we got here, you’re not alone. After digging through the court documents, legal precedents, and—perhaps most importantly—the extraordinary sequence of events that led to these charges, the picture that emerges is both clearer and more troubling than you might expect.

Let’s break down what actually happened, what the law says about it, and why this case might matter more than just another political prosecution.

What They’re Saying Comey Did

Here’s the core allegation: back in September 2020, Comey testified before the Senate Judiciary Committee about the FBI’s Russia investigation. Senator Ted Cruz asked him about a 2016 leak to The Wall Street Journal concerning Hillary Clinton’s email investigation.

The problem? Comey’s former deputy, Andrew McCabe, had already told investigators that he leaked the information and that Comey knew about it and approved it. But when Cruz pressed Comey on this contradiction, Comey stood by his previous testimony that he never authorized any leaks.

Someone’s lying under oath—and prosecutors decided it was Comey.

The Legal Case: Stronger Than It Looks... Or Is It?

On paper, this seems straightforward. Federal law is pretty clear about lying to Congress—do it knowingly about something material, and you can face up to five years in prison. The government doesn’t even need to prove the lie actually influenced anything, just that it could have.

And this stuff definitely mattered. We’re talking about FBI leaks during the final weeks of the 2016 presidential election, involving an investigation of a presidential candidate. Hard to argue that’s not material to congressional oversight.

But here’s where it gets interesting: the career prosecutors working this case wrote a memo explaining why they didn’t think there was enough evidence to even meet the probable cause standard for an indictment. That’s a pretty low bar—much lower than what you’d need for conviction.

Why the weak case? A few reasons stand out:

McCabe’s credibility problem: The guy whose testimony contradicts Comey? The Justice Department’s own Inspector General found he made “multiple false or misleading statements” during investigations. Not exactly your star witness.

The IG’s independent finding: When the Inspector General investigated this whole mess independently, they concluded that Comey didn’t authorize the leak. They found McCabe had the authority to speak to media on his own.

Memory vs. intent: The events happened nearly four years before Comey’s 2020 testimony. Courts generally recognize that honest mistakes or memory lapses aren’t the same as willful perjury.

DOJ’s own standards: The Justice Department’s guidelines say you shouldn’t bring a case unless prosecutors think it’s “more likely than not” they can get a conviction. The career prosecutors clearly didn’t think this met that standard.

But Here’s Where It Gets Really Wild

If this case was so weak that the career prosecutors said “don’t bring it,” how did we end up with an indictment anyway? That’s where this story gets a lot more interesting—and troubling.

Trump’s Public Pressure Campaign

This wasn’t subtle. On September 20th, Trump posted directly to Attorney General Pam Bondi on Truth Social:

He continued: “We can’t delay any longer, it’s killing our reputation and credibility. They impeached me twice, and indicted me (5 times!), OVER NOTHING.”

Read that again. The President of the United States publicly declared specific individuals “guilty as hell” and demanded his Attorney General prosecute them.

Prosecutor Shopping

US attorney Erik Siebert tasked with inquiring into Trump critics resigns after president says ‘I want him out’

When U.S. Attorney Erik Siebert wouldn’t bring the charges Trump wanted, Trump didn’t just fire him—he publicly trashed him. Trump claimed Siebert was “a Democrat supported U.S. Attorney, in Virginia, with a really bad Republican past. A Woke RINO, who was never going to do his job.”

Then Trump did something remarkable: he claimed Siebert “lied to the media and said he quit, and that we had no case. No, I fired him, and there is a GREAT CASE.”

Installing a Loyalist

Lindsey Halligan was sworn in as acting U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia in September despite having almost no experience as a federal prosecutor (AP)

Enter Lindsey Halligan. Her main qualification for taking over this prosecution? She was one of Trump’s personal defense attorneys during his classified documents case. Trump specifically praised her to Bondi, saying she “likes you, a lot.”

Within days of taking over, Halligan brought the indictment that career prosecutors had said lacked probable cause.

The White House Dinner

The timeline is remarkable: On September 24th, Trump had dinner at the White House Rose Garden with Attorney General Bondi and others. The next day, the Comey indictment was announced.

Even after the indictment, Trump couldn’t help himself: “I think I’d be allowed to get involved if I want, but I don’t really choose to do so. I can only say that Comey is a bad person. He’s a sick person.”

Think about that statement for a moment. A president saying he could “get involved” in criminal prosecutions if he wanted to.

Why This Matters (And Why It Should Worry You)

Look, prosecuting lying to Congress isn’t new. People get charged with perjury. But this case breaks every norm we’ve had about prosecutorial independence since Watergate.

Here’s what’s different:

The pressure was systematic: This wasn’t just a president expressing frustration. Trump publicly named targets, demanded prosecutions, fired prosecutors who wouldn’t comply, and installed loyalists who would.

Career prosecutors said no: When the people whose job it is to evaluate evidence professionally said there wasn’t a case, political appointees overruled them to please the president.

The precedent is dangerous: If this becomes normal, every future president—Republican or Democrat—can point to this case and say “Trump did it too” when they want to prosecute their political enemies.

We’re watching the potential end of prosecutorial independence. Since Nixon, both parties have generally maintained that presidents don’t get to pick and choose who gets prosecuted. That norm just got blown up.

Some Historical Context

Congressional perjury prosecutions are actually pretty rare, despite all the accusations you hear. When they do happen, they usually involve much clearer evidence than this case presents.

More importantly, this level of direct presidential involvement in specific prosecutions hasn’t happened since Nixon. Even Trump’s first term didn’t feature this kind of systematic pressure campaign complete with prosecutor shopping.

The post-Watergate consensus was simple: presidents can set broad law enforcement priorities, but they don’t get to order specific prosecutions of specific people. That consensus just died.

The Bottom Line

So what’s the justification for this indictment? Legally, there’s a technical case to be made—contradictory testimony under oath about material matters. But practically?

  • The evidence is weak: Career prosecutors said it didn’t meet basic standards for probable cause.

  • The key witness has credibility problems: McCabe’s own history of false statements makes him a problematic foundation for a perjury case.

  • Independent investigators disagreed: The Inspector General found that Comey’s version was correct.

  • The pressure was extraordinary: This prosecution happened only after systematic presidential intervention that included firing prosecutors and installing loyalists.

Will Comey get convicted? That’s for a jury to decide. But the decision to bring charges appears driven more by political pressure than legal merit.

The Case for Prosecution: What DOJ and Trump Allies Would Say

The Department of Justice and Trump allies would argue this prosecution is legitimate accountability, not political persecution. Their case would rest on several points: Comey’s testimony to Congress contradicted his deputy’s account under oath—someone was lying. The FBI Inspector General found that leaks did occur during Comey’s tenure, creating a cloud of impropriety. Comey has a documented history of controversial decisions (the Clinton email announcement, memos about Trump conversations leaked to the press) that suggests poor judgment at minimum. And importantly, career prosecutors at DOJ ultimately moved forward with the indictment, suggesting professional merit beyond political pressure.

Here’s why that explanation doesn’t hold up: The career prosecutors didn’t just have concerns—they wrote a formal memo stating the evidence didn’t meet even the low bar of probable cause for indictment. The key witness (McCabe) has been found by DOJ’s own Inspector General to have made “multiple false or misleading statements”—hardly a credible foundation for a perjury case. The IG independently investigated and sided with Comey’s version. Most tellingly, this indictment came only after systematic presidential intervention: Trump publicly named Comey as “guilty as hell,” fired the prosecutor who wouldn’t bring charges, installed his personal defense attorney to take over, and the indictment followed within days. The process—not just the charges—reveals political motivation. Even if prosecutors can articulate legal theories for the case, the manner in which it was brought demonstrates weaponization of prosecutorial power.

Trump’s Enemies List: From Threat to Reality

The Pattern Emerges

In the two weeks since Comey’s indictment, two more Trump critics have been charged—and a third indictment is reportedly imminent. The timeline is remarkable:

Three critics. Three indictments. Less than three weeks. All after direct presidential demands.

Letitia James: The $19,000 “Crime”

New York Attorney General Letitia James was indicted on October 9th on charges of bank fraud and making false statements to a financial institution. The alleged crime? She’s accused of claiming a Norfolk, Virginia property would be her secondary residence on a 2020 mortgage application, when prosecutors say she actually rented it out.

The total alleged “ill-gotten gains”? About $19,000 over the life of a 30-year loan—roughly $50 per month in interest savings.

Let that sink in. The Attorney General of New York, who successfully sued Trump and his company for a $454 million fraud judgment, is now facing up to 60 years in prison for allegedly saving $50 a month on a mortgage.

The same pattern repeated: Career prosecutors in Virginia determined there wasn’t enough evidence to bring charges. The U.S. Attorney who wouldn’t prosecute (Erik Siebert) was forced out. Trump’s personal attorney Lindsey Halligan took over and immediately brought the indictment.

In her statement, James was direct: “This is nothing more than a continuation of the president’s desperate weaponization of our justice system. These charges are baseless, and the president’s own public statements make clear that his only goal is political retribution at any cost.”

Ed Martin, Trump’s “weaponization czar” at DOJ, wasn’t subtle either. After the charges were announced, he posted: “Promises made, Promises kept.” Martin had previously traveled to Brooklyn to stand outside James’ home and demand her resignation.

John Bolton: Coming Soon

Former National Security Adviser John Bolton is reportedly next. According to two federal officials, Bolton could be indicted as early as next week on charges related to handling classified documents—the same type of documents Trump himself was indicted for hoarding at Mar-a-Lago (charges that were dismissed by Judge Aileen Cannon, a Trump appointee).

The investigation centers on Bolton’s 2020 tell-all book about his time in the Trump administration. Bolton maintained he had clearance from the National Security Council that the book contained no classified material. But now, five years later, he’s facing potential indictment.

The timing is telling: Trump canceled Bolton’s Secret Service protection earlier this year, even though Bolton was the target of an alleged Iranian murder-for-hire plot. Bolton has been one of Trump’s most vocal critics, particularly on foreign policy.

Federal prosecutors met over the weekend to finalize potential charges. If indicted, Bolton would be the third Trump critic charged in three weeks.

What We’re Watching

This isn’t speculation anymore. It’s a documented pattern. Trump publicly names enemies. The Justice Department investigates them. Career prosecutors say there’s insufficient evidence. Trump removes the prosecutors. Trump loyalists take over and bring indictments.

The Washington Post reported on October 11 that Justice Department officials are “pushing ahead with a list of additional cases.” Others reportedly under investigation include:

  • Senator Adam Schiff (California Democrat, led Trump’s first impeachment)

  • Former CIA Director John Brennan

  • Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook

The Nixon Comparison

People keep invoking Nixon’s “enemies list,” but this is different—and worse. Nixon compiled a list and tried to use the IRS to harass his enemies. But he didn’t successfully prosecute them through the Justice Department. That norm held, even during Watergate.

What we’re seeing now is the complete collapse of that post-Watergate consensus. Trump isn’t just maintaining an enemies list. He’s converting it into federal indictments through a systematic process:

  • Public demands for prosecution

  • Removal of resistant prosecutors

  • Installation of loyalists

  • Rushed indictments despite career prosecutors’ objections

  • Public celebration of “promises kept”

Why This Matters More Than Any Individual Case

The legal merits of each case will be debated in court. Comey might be acquitted. James might beat the charges. Bolton might never be indicted.

But that misses the point.

The precedent is set: A president can publicly demand prosecution of political enemies, fire prosecutors who refuse, install loyalists who comply, and face no consequences for doing so.

The chilling effect is real: Anyone who crosses Trump—prosecutors, attorneys general, former officials, politicians—now knows they could face federal indictment. The process itself is the punishment.

The damage is permanent: Even if every defendant is acquitted, the system has been fundamentally altered. Future presidents, of either party, can point to Trump’s playbook and say “he did it too.”

The Bigger Picture

This isn’t just about James Comey or Letitia James or John Bolton. It’s about whether presidents can successfully weaponize criminal prosecution against political enemies despite weak evidence and professional opposition from career prosecutors.

Based on the last three weeks, the answer appears to be yes—as long as they’re willing to fire prosecutors until they find one who will comply, and as long as there’s no meaningful pushback from Congress, the courts, or the public.

That’s not justice. That’s something much more dangerous. And unlike the individual prosecutions, which might fail in court, the precedent is already established.

The enemies list isn’t a threat anymore. It’s operational.

The enemies list is operational. But it’s not inevitable. Institutional resistance, public awareness, and sustained pressure can still matter—if we recognize what’s happening while there’s still time to push back.

The precedent being set in the Comey case will define whether future presidents—of either party—can use the Justice Department as a weapon against political opponents.

That’s not about James Comey’s guilt or innocence. It’s about whether American democracy can survive the systematic weaponization of prosecutorial power.
