What We Owe the Conqueror

The Greatest and Most Beautiful Triumphal Arch in the World is Coming. We Should Be Grateful.

April 12, 2026

A rendering released by the U.S. Commission of Fine Arts of plans for a 250-foot triumphal arch in Washington.Credit...U.S. Commission of Fine Arts

A note to readers: This Sunday you are receiving two satirical essays for the price of one — which, in the spirit of the administration we are writing about, we will describe as an unprecedented efficiency saving. The first, “The Auditors Who Couldn’t Find a Mirror,” examined the Department of Government Efficiency’s remarkable achievement of finding waste, fraud, and abuse everywhere except directly in front of them. This second piece turns to an announcement made this week that the first piece, in all its careful accounting, could not have anticipated — because no careful accountant would have seen it coming. Consider this your bonus. The management apologizes for nothing.

There is a long and honourable tradition of building triumphal arches, and it is a tradition that, if we are being honest with ourselves, has been shamefully neglected in Washington for far too long.

The Romans understood this. Augustus Caesar built the Arch of Augustus in 29 BC to commemorate the defeat of Antony and Cleopatra at the Battle of Actium — a naval engagement that decided the fate of the Mediterranean world, ended a generation of civil war, and established the Roman Empire. The Arch of Titus, erected in 82 AD, marked the conquest of Jerusalem. The Arch of Constantine, the grandest of them all, celebrated a victory at the Milvian Bridge that changed the religious history of Western civilization.

These were, by any reasonable measure, significant events.

The Arc de Triomphe in Paris — currently the world’s largest triumphal arch, and therefore soon to be demoted to second place — was commissioned by Napoleon Bonaparte following the Battle of Austerlitz, widely considered the greatest tactical victory in the history of warfare, a battle in which Napoleon lured the combined armies of Austria and Russia into a trap, shattered their formation in four hours, and brought the Holy Roman Empire to its knees.

And now, on April 14th, 2026, the White House filed plans with the Commission of Fine Arts for what it describes — in a social media post, naturally, because this is how history is made now — as the GREATEST and MOST BEAUTIFUL Triumphal Arch, anywhere in the World.

It will stand 250 feet tall. It will be the largest triumphal arch ever constructed by any civilization in recorded history. It will feature, atop its crown, a golden eagle of the kind you normally encounter at the entrance to a Las Vegas resort, which is either a bold aesthetic choice or the most accidentally self-revealing architectural decision since Nero built himself a palace with a 100-foot bronze statue of his own face in the vestibule.

The question that a small, ungenerous, deeply unpatriotic person might raise — the kind of person who asks tedious questions about things like “precedent” and “what is this commemorating, exactly” — is what specific triumph this arch is being built to honour.

I am glad you asked. Let us count the victories.

There is, first and most obviously, the Great Tariff War of 2025, in which the President of the United States imposed sweeping levies on the entire planet simultaneously, triggering the largest single-day stock market collapse since the 1987 crash, briefly wiping out $6.6 trillion in market value, and causing the Canadian Prime Minister — a man representing a country that produces more than a third of America’s lumber and steel — to announce that Canada would no longer consider itself America’s friend. This was subsequently described by the White House as a “bold negotiating strategy.” The markets, after a brief pause for cardiac arrest, partially recovered. Arch-worthy? The President seems to think so.

There is the matter of the Iran War — forty days of US-Israeli strikes on Iranian infrastructure, at an estimated cost of $11.3 billion in the first week alone, six American service members killed in a Kuwaiti base attack (whose surviving colleagues have since told CBS News that their unit was “left dangerously exposed”), and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, through which approximately 20 percent of the world’s oil normally flows. The conflict concluded — for now, for fourteen days, pending further negotiation — with a ceasefire brokered not by American diplomacy but by Pakistan. Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announced the truce. Pakistani intermediaries hosted the talks in Islamabad. The President, for his part, posted on social media that “a whole civilization will die tonight” and was publicly rebuked by the Pope. The ceasefire holds, as of this writing, for another ten days. One imagines the inscription on the arch will require careful drafting.

There is the historic diplomatic achievement of securing a $400 million Boeing 747 from the government of Qatar — a gesture of such breathtaking generosity that it will require approximately $600 million to $1 billion in taxpayer-funded renovations before it can be flown, and which, under the terms of the arrangement, becomes the personal property of the Trump Presidential Library Foundation when the President leaves office. Critics have called this a constitutional violation, a foreign emoluments catastrophe, and a national security risk. The President has called it “a very nice plane.” These are not mutually exclusive positions.

There is the triumphant reopening of Alcatraz — the island federal penitentiary that closed in 1963 because it cost three times as much to operate as an ordinary prison, could not hold enough inmates to justify its logistical footprint, and famously failed to contain, among others, the criminal organization run by Al Capone — which the President has proposed reopening at an estimated cost of $152 million as a high-security facility. This is being presented as a law-and-order initiative rather than what it more closely resembles, which is a theme park concept that got loose from its moorings.

And there is, perhaps most triumphantly of all, the complete and total elimination of waste, fraud, and abuse from the federal government, achieved by the Department of Government Efficiency at a cost of somewhere between $7 billion and $40 billion in documented savings, against a backdrop of $1.8 trillion in deficit spending, $301 billion in increased outlays, and a national debt that grew by $2.25 trillion in year one. The efficiency dividend, by the most optimistic accounting, covered roughly forty-eight hours of federal operating costs. The arch, which will be paid for by — the White House has not yet specified, but one assumes not Elon Musk — will stand for the ages.

Now. I anticipate objections from the usual quarters.

Someone will point out that Rome built its arches after the victories, not during the ongoing negotiation, active litigation, and pending Congressional investigation into whether the victory constituted a crime. Someone will note that the Arc de Triomphe took thirty years to complete and Napoleon didn’t live to see it finished, which might serve as something of a cautionary tale about the relationship between announced grandeur and actual legacy. Someone will observe that Washington already has a surfeit of monuments to people who accomplished specific, nameable, historically documented things, and that adding a 250-foot arch to commemorate a presidency that is, at this writing, not yet finished seems — architecturally, historically, constitutionally — a touch premature.

These people are small. They are haters. They are, almost certainly, reading newspapers from other countries.

The great triumphal arches of history were not built by timid men who waited for posterity’s verdict. They were built by emperors, conquerors, and men of vision who understood that history is written not by the victors but by whoever files the paperwork with the Commission of Fine Arts first. Augustus didn’t wait for Antony to write his side of the story. Napoleon didn’t convene a blue-ribbon panel to assess whether Austerlitz had been worth the casualties. They built. They inscribed. They put a golden eagle on top and dared anyone to complain.

And so here we are.

"The GREATEST and MOST BEAUTIFUL Triumphal Arch, anywhere in the World" will rise 250 feet above Washington, DC — taller than the Capitol dome, taller than the Washington Monument’s shoulder, taller than any arch any civilization has ever dared to erect — and it will stand as a permanent testament to everything this presidency has achieved: the tariffs that shook the planet, the ceasefire that Pakistan brokered, the plane Qatar gave us, the prison island we’re reopening, and the waste, fraud, and abuse that we decisively identified and attributed to everyone else.

Beneath the golden eagle, if there is justice in the world, the inscription will read what all great imperial monuments have always read, in one form or another:

I was here. It was tremendous. Nobody had ever seen anything like it.

That, at least, is undeniably true.
