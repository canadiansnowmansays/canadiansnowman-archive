The Trump Variety Hour

A Critical Review

Two hours. One host. Zero intermissions. Our television critic weighs in.

March 1, 2026

by CanadianSnowmanSays March 1, 2026

All claims in this review are backed by receipts — see the companion fact-check: SOTU 2026: The Speech and the Silence

THE TRUMP VARIETY HOUR Season 2, Episode 1 Network: C-SPAN (simulcast on all major networks, whether they liked it or not) Runtime: 1 hour, 48 minutes (felt longer) Rating: TV-MA (Mature Audiences — for misleading content, graphic immigration anecdotes, and scenes of Congressional discomfort)

★★½ out of ★★★★★

THE REVIEW

As a Canadian who has now watched both seasons of this particular program, I can report that Season 2 of The Trump Variety Hour opened Tuesday night with all the restraint and subtlety of a monster truck rally at a cathedral.

The host was in fine form — energetic, combative, occasionally tender, and seemingly unaware that the show had a runtime. At 108 minutes, this was the longest State of the Union in modern history — breaking the record the host himself set last season — which is saying something for a format that already tests the human bladder. Ronald Reagan once did the whole thing in 31 minutes. But Reagan could land a joke without explaining it, repeat it, then poll the audience on whether they’d laughed. As Jimmy Kimmel observed from his studio, where he’d been waiting as long as possible for the speech to end before giving up and taping his show while the president was still talking: “When you ramble mostly incoherently for two hours, is that technically still a speech or does it at some point become a conniption fit?” A fair question. Kimmel also suggested they “hire an orchestra to play him off like the Oscars. At the stroke of 90 minutes, go straight into YMCA and end it.” As a Canadian, I can confirm: our Throne Speech equivalent rarely exceeds 45 minutes. We have things to do.

The production values were high. The set — the House chamber — was its usual impressive self, though the blocking was chaotic. The host kept gesturing toward audience members like a game show contestant pointing at doors, and the camera operators struggled to keep up. At one point, the host appeared to be simultaneously awarding a Medal of Honor, insulting the Democratic caucus, and teasing a healthcare announcement, which is the legislative equivalent of juggling flaming chainsaws while riding a unicycle. Dan Pfeiffer’s observation that Trump was “kind of like fascist Oprah out there — ‘You get a medal, you get a medal!’” was unkind but not inaccurate.

The pacing was, to put it diplomatically, self-indulgent. The first thirty minutes covered immigration with the intensity of a man who had been told he had five minutes. The next thirty covered the economy with the confidence of someone who had not read the AAA gas price report. The final forty-eight minutes covered everything else — foreign policy, healthcare, crime, hockey, the Medal of Honor, IVF, Venezuela, and a fishing joke about dead people — with the organizational structure of a man emptying his pockets onto a table and narrating each item. By the time the host reached his peroration, the chamber had the glazed look of a lecture hall at 4:55 p.m. on a Friday.

The Guest Stars were the real story. This wasn’t a policy address — it was a casting call.

Olympic goalie Connor Hellebuyck received a Presidential Medal of Freedom, an honor typically reserved for lifetime achievement but here awarded with the energy of a new car reveal on The Price Is Right. “Connor, come on down!” One does not wish to diminish Mr. Hellebuyck’s athletic achievements — he is an excellent goaltender — but one notes that the Medal of Freedom has previously been awarded to Nelson Mandela, Mother Teresa, and Martin Luther King Jr. The president appears to have recalibrated the criteria. If winning Olympic gold now qualifies, Canada would like to submit approximately 400 nominations, beginning with every member of our women’s hockey program.

Chief Warrant Officer Eric Slover’s story was genuinely heroic and deserved every second of recognition, though one suspects the hero himself might have preferred a quieter ceremony to being a prop in a variety show. The camera lingered on his face while the host described his service. Slover’s expression suggested a man who understood exactly what was happening and had decided to endure it with the same discipline he’d brought to combat. It was the evening’s most dignified moment, and it belonged entirely to Slover, not to the man narrating it.

Erika Kirk’s presence was somber and appropriate. The freed Venezuelan political prisoner’s reunion with his niece was moving — perhaps the evening’s only moment that would have worked in any State of the Union, regardless of who was delivering it. Katherine Rener’s IVF story was compelling and timely, though one noticed the host did not dwell on the members of his own party who had voted against IVF protections. The camera did not pan to them. An oversight, presumably.

But by the eighth guest introduction, the format began to feel less like a State of the Union and more like a particularly aggressive episode of This Is Your Life — if This Is Your Life included segments where the host accused half the studio audience of treason.

Notably absent from the guest list: the U.S. women’s Olympic hockey team. The host had extended an invitation. They declined. As Kimmel reported, the women’s team subsequently received “an even better offer from none other than Flavor Flav.” The host responded during his speech by claiming the women’s team “would be coming to the White House soon, whether they like it or not” — which, as a sentence uttered by a president about female athletes, perhaps deserved more scrutiny than it received. The women’s team issued no further comment. They didn’t need to. The refusal was the comment.

The men’s team, which the host celebrated for twenty-two minutes, was present and willing. Twenty-two minutes. For hockey. In a State of the Union address. As a Canadian whose nation has won Olympic hockey gold nine times, I note this with the quiet dignity the occasion requires. We don’t devote twenty-two minutes to hockey during the Stanley Cup parade. We don’t devote twenty-two minutes to hockey during Hockey Night in Canada. The president of the United States devoted twenty-two minutes to hockey during a constitutional address to a joint session of Congress while the Department of Homeland Security was unfunded, two American citizens lay dead from federal bullets, and six hundred thousand people had perished from the elimination of foreign aid. But Connor Hellebuyck got a medal. So there’s that.

The Audience Participation Segments were, from a pure entertainment standpoint, the highlight.

The host’s attempt to force Democrats to physically stand if they agreed that government should “protect American citizens, not illegal aliens” produced the longest, most uncomfortable silence since my last attempt at karaoke. The Democrats sat in granite-faced solidarity while the host stared them down like a substitute teacher who’d lost control of the class but hadn’t realized it yet.

“You should be ashamed of yourself, not standing up!” the host admonished, before adding: “These people are crazy, I’m telling you, they’re crazy.”

The Democrats’ refusal to participate was not, as the host seemed to believe, evidence of insanity. It was a calculated response to a statement deliberately designed to be impossible to stand for without endorsing its framing — particularly given that the speech omitted any mention of the two American citizens, Renee Good and Alex Pretti, killed by the host’s own federal immigration agents in Minnesota. Standing for the protection of American citizens while the man asking you to stand had failed to mention the American citizens his agents had killed required a level of cognitive dissonance even Congress couldn’t manage.

The Republicans, for their part, performed their roles with the practiced enthusiasm of a studio audience that knows the “APPLAUSE” sign is also a surveillance camera. They rose and sat with metronomic regularity — up, clap, sit, repeat — executing the choreography with the precision of a dance troupe that had been rehearsing for months. Which, in a sense, they had. By the 90-minute mark, even the most committed among them had taken on the mechanical quality of a morning aerobics class. Several members appeared to be performing the movements from muscle memory alone. One imagines their Fitbits registered it as a moderate cardio workout.

Representative Ilhan Omar broke ranks to shout something back, which the host seized upon with visible delight — the only moment all evening where an audience member gave him what he wanted. The remaining Democrats appeared to have attended a pre-show workshop titled “How to Sit Very Still for Two Hours While Someone Insults You on National Television.”

Representative Al Green did not attend the workshop. He was ejected from the chamber for protesting an AI-generated video depicting the Obamas as apes — which had apparently circulated among some attendees. Green’s removal was the evening’s most honest moment: one man refusing to sit politely while the theater of civility continued around him. It was also barely covered, because by that point in the broadcast, the audience had entered a fugue state from which recovery required caffeine and distance.

The Writing was, charitably, uneven.

The first act — immigration and the economy — recycled extensively from Season 1, with updated numbers that frequently contradicted available data. The writers appear to have a complicated relationship with statistics, treating them less as facts and more as suggestions. The “$18 trillion in investment” line was particularly bold, given that the host’s own website says $9.7 trillion — itself generous. The claim that gasoline is “below $2.30 a gallon in most states” was even bolder, given that AAA data shows no state averaging below $2.37 and the national average sits at $2.95. In Iowa, a member of the president’s own crowd once shouted “No, $2.63!” — a moment of audience participation that the writers surely did not intend and have presumably not recovered from. One admires the commitment to the bit. As Kimmel summarized the evening’s thesis: “The theme of tonight’s speech was all foreigners are murderers.”

The host claimed “zero illegal aliens have been admitted” on his watch — a dramatic reduction presented as absolute elimination, the statistical equivalent of claiming you’ve stopped eating because you skipped breakfast. Kimmel’s response captured the absurdity better than any fact-checker could: “The door is always open to those who come in legally to be his next wife. So that’s something.”

He bragged about ending DEI. He bragged, as Kimmel noted, about kicking two million people off food stamps — during a cost-of-living crisis his own tariffs had worsened. As Kimmel put it: “It was like a Christmas message from the Grinch.” One notes that the Grinch eventually felt bad about it. We’re still waiting.

The second act — foreign policy and military operations — was more compelling television, if constitutionally troubling drama. The Venezuela sequence was genuinely gripping. The Iran segment raised questions the writers clearly hope the audience won’t Google. The host’s claim to have “ended eight wars” was the evening’s most ambitious fiction — a point we examine in detail in our companion piece, And the Envelope Please...

The third act — healthcare, crime, and the extended finale — is where the runtime became punishing. The healthcare promises were delivered with the confidence of a man making New Year’s resolutions he has no intention of keeping. The crime statistics were presented with the selectivity of a man showing you only the photos from his good side. The finale — a crescendo of patriotic assertion, military tribute, and implied threats — built toward a conclusion that arrived like a relief rather than an inspiration.

Perhaps the evening’s most haunting moment came when the host applauded a World War II veteran who helped liberate an internment camp — during a speech defending the administration’s own detention facilities. As Kimmel later observed: the president was celebrating liberation and construction simultaneously, without any apparent awareness that these are opposite things. The camera lingered on the veteran’s face. He did not appear to notice the irony. The host certainly did not. History, watching from the gallery, took notes.

What Was Missing was more interesting than what was included.

The name “Elon Musk” — arguably the show’s most controversial recurring character from Season 1 — was never spoken. DOGE, the signature initiative of the first season, vanished from the script entirely. It was as if the writers had killed off a main character between seasons and hoped nobody would notice. The 317,000 federal workers who left under DOGE’s watch were neither mentioned nor mourned. Their absence from the speech mirrored their absence from their desks — thorough, deliberate, and designed to avoid uncomfortable questions.

Also absent: any mention of the USAID elimination and its humanitarian consequences, the extrajudicial boat strikes that have killed at least 115 people since September, the two American citizens killed by ICE agents (whose deaths caused the very shutdown the host blamed on Democrats), the Epstein files (which the host’s own DOJ is selectively releasing while withholding 2.5 million pages), or the Supreme Court ruling that invalidated his tariff regime four days earlier — reduced to a passing reference to an “unfortunate ruling,” delivered with the tone of a man discussing a parking ticket.

The host celebrated record deportation numbers. He did not mention the bodies. He praised law enforcement. He did not name the Americans law enforcement had killed. He invoked God. He did not mention the Bible he sells for $99.99, made in China, with his name on it.

One hundred and eight minutes. And still not enough time for the truth.

The Competition — or what passed for it — offered a study in asymmetric warfare.

Governor Spanberger’s Democratic Response was a competent if workmanlike rebuttal that suffered primarily from the impossible task of following a 108-minute variety show with a ten-minute policy brief. It was like scheduling a TED Talk after Cirque du Soleil. She made her points on affordability and healthcare. Nobody will remember them tomorrow. This is not a reflection on Spanberger, who was articulate and focused. It is a reflection on a format that pits a ten-minute prepared statement against a two-hour spectacle and expects equilibrium. The Democrats continue to bring a policy paper to a knife fight.

Kimmel’s monologue, by contrast, was the evening’s actual rebuttal — sharper, angrier, and more efficient than anything the Democratic Party produced. His “real State of the Union” — delivered in under a minute — catalogued what the president’s 108 minutes had avoided: censoring dissent, killing citizens, gutting cancer research, protecting pedophiles, grifting billions, and doing it all “in the name of Jesus, by the way, who you could read all about in a Donald Trump edition of the God Bless the USA Bible that is made in China and available for $99.99.”

It was the most devastating sixty seconds of political commentary delivered on American television last night. It was also, notably, delivered by a man the president tried to have fired five months ago — the same man whose show was suspended by the FCC chairman, pulled from sixty affiliates, and celebrated by the president as “Great News for America.” Kimmel’s contract was extended through 2027 in December. His comeback monologue drew 21 million views. The president’s attempt to silence him produced the most-watched late-night episode in a decade. There is a lesson here about the Streisand Effect, though it is unlikely to be learned by the man who most needs to learn it.

Stephen Colbert, going live after the speech for one of his final broadcasts before The Late Show ends its run, noted that the official theme was “America at 250: Strong, Prosperous, and Respected.” He then observed that he’d “dropped an edible and strapped in” to watch. It was unclear whether this was a joke or a survival strategy. Both seem reasonable. Colbert’s departure from late night will leave Kimmel as the last standing host of a network late-night show willing to directly confront the president — a fact that is simultaneously a testament to Kimmel’s stubbornness and an indictment of the industry’s courage.

Overall Assessment:

As policy, it was thin. As entertainment, it was overlong but sporadically effective. As constitutional governance, it was concerning. As a vehicle for the host’s particular talents — commanding a room, creating spectacle, forcing emotional moments — it was exactly what the audience has come to expect.

The show has been renewed for at least two more seasons.

God help us all.
