The Response Charlie Kirk Still Deserves

Addendum: When Evidence Gets Complicated — The Ballistic Question and the Sheriff Who Quit

April 10, 2026

Sheriff Nate Brooksby speaking in Hurricane, Utah, following the shooting of Charlie Kirk in September 2025. | Ty ONeil/AP

Two significant developments broke simultaneously on March 31, 2026 — developments that deserve careful, honest analysis rather than the conspiratorial fog already descending on them. An April 17 hearing on courtroom cameras and a May 18 preliminary hearing make this a critical moment to understand exactly where the evidence stands.

On the same day — March 31, 2026, more than six months after Charlie Kirk was shot dead at Utah Valley University — two things happened within hours of each other that sent social media into predictable overdrive.

Washington County Sheriff Nate Brooksby resigned after thirty years in law enforcement, citing undisclosed complaints about office operations. And Tyler Robinson’s defense attorneys filed a motion to postpone the May 18 preliminary hearing, citing an ATF report indicating that toolmark analysis on a bullet jacket recovered during Kirk’s autopsy was inconclusive — due to extensive damage to the bullet itself.

By nightfall, Marjorie Taylor Greene had already composed her verdict: “The ATF could not match the bullet to Tyler Robinson’s gun and when the news breaks the sheriff resigns.” Candace Owens, Kirk’s former communications director at Turning Point USA, added claims about multiple fingerprints on the weapon and bomb dogs that allegedly missed the rifle entirely.

Let me be the voice in the room that pauses before lighting the fuse.

What the ATF Actually Said — And What It Didn’t

The defense filing quotes the ATF’s finding precisely: the agency was “unable to identify the bullet recovered at autopsy to the rifle allegedly tied to Mr. Robinson.”

That sentence is being read by much of the internet as exculpatory. It is not. Not yet. Possibly not ever.

“Unable to identify” in forensic ballistics means the analysis was inconclusive — not that the bullet came from a different weapon. The filing itself states the inconclusiveness was due to extensive damage to the bullet jacket. A severely deformed bullet that has passed through a human body at high velocity and struck bone can lose the toolmarks that forensic analysts use to match it to a specific barrel. The ATF couldn’t say it was Robinson’s rifle. They also couldn’t say it wasn’t.

PolitiFact consulted forensic science and criminology experts who confirmed that an inconclusive finding on a bullet fragment of this kind is not uncommon, and does not rule out that the weapon was the same one linked to Robinson. The prosecution put it plainly: “When the results of a bullet fragment analysis come back as ‘inconclusive,’ that does not mean that the rifle did not fire the bullet.” The FBI, notably, is running additional tests. The ATF report is not the last word — it is one inconclusive chapter in a much larger evidentiary story.

This is a legitimate gap in the forensic picture. It is not a bombshell exoneration. The defense is doing exactly what defense attorneys are supposed to do: identifying every weakness in the prosecution’s case and pressing on it hard. That is how the adversarial legal system is supposed to work. The motion to delay the preliminary hearing also reflects the sheer scale of the case — the defense received more than 600,000 files during a March 12 discovery meeting, with additional materials still outstanding. The ballistics argument is real, but it is not the only reason they want more time.

What the ballistic gap is not is proof of a frame-up, a conspiracy, or an innocent man in chains.

The Sheriff: Timing Is Not Evidence

Sheriff Nate Brooksby’s resignation on the same day is, I will admit, a striking coincidence in optics. He was the man who took the call that led to Robinson surrendering. He arranged the handoff. His sudden departure — after thirty years of service, following a meeting with county commissioners about unnamed complaints, with the complainants then asking that no further action be taken — is genuinely unusual.

But correlation is not causation. “Unusual” is not “connected.” The complaints against Brooksby have not been made public. The county has offered no suggestion that they relate to the Kirk case in any way. The deputy county attorney stated explicitly that the complainants requested the matter not be pursued — hardly the posture of whistleblowers trying to expose a conspiracy.

What we have is a long-serving sheriff who resigned abruptly under murky circumstances on an awkward day. That warrants scrutiny. It does not warrant the leap to “the case is falling apart” or “someone is covering something up.”

What the Conspiracy Ecosystem Is Choosing to Ignore

I have written in this series about the danger of weaponizing tragedy for political ends. That danger cuts in multiple directions.

The Trump administration weaponized Kirk’s death immediately — targeting liberal groups, threatening antifa designation, issuing memoranda about “anti-traditional American views” as precursors to violence. I documented that in Addendum II. It was shameful, and I stand by that assessment.

But there is a mirror-image temptation on the other side: the desire to see Robinson as a martyr, to build a conspiracy from circumstantial timing, to treat inconclusive ballistic evidence as proof that the whole prosecution is rotten.

Here is what MTG and Owens are not posting about.

Robinson’s DNA was found on the rifle’s trigger, other parts of the rifle, the fired cartridge casing, two of the three unfired cartridges, and the towel used to wrap the weapon. Investigators also found in Robinson’s home the tools believed to have been used to engrave bullet casings — matching the engraved casings recovered near the crime scene. Beneath his keyboard, a handwritten note: “I had the opportunity to take out Charlie Kirk, and I’m going to take it.” When his roommate and romantic partner Lance Twiggs asked why he did it, Robinson allegedly replied: “I had enough of his hatred. Some hate can’t be negotiated out.”

Robinson allegedly confessed in a Discord group chat within an hour of the shooting. He allegedly told his father he did it. His father told a youth pastor connected to the U.S. Marshals. Robinson surrendered himself at Brooksby’s sheriff’s office. These facts have not changed. They are not addressed by the defense filing. They are not being discussed by the people posting fire emojis about inconclusive bullet fragments.

The ATF’s report may matter at trial. It may narrow the evidentiary picture prosecutors can paint. A good defense attorney will make the most of it — as they should. But it does not erase the confession, the DNA, the note under the keyboard, or the surrender.

What the Courts Have Already Decided

One other development deserves mention. The judge has already rejected the defense motion to disqualify the prosecutors handling the case. Robinson’s attorneys had argued a conflict of interest because a deputy attorney’s 18-year-old child was in the crowd when Kirk was shot. The motion was denied. The prosecution team remains in place, death penalty notice and all.

Erika Kirk, meanwhile, has been fighting for cameras in the courtroom throughout this process. “There were cameras all over my husband when he was murdered,” she told Fox News. “We deserve to have cameras in there. There’s nothing to hide. I know there’s not because I’ve seen what the case is built on.”

That is the statement of a widow who has read the evidence file. It is worth weighing.

What Honest Journalism Requires Right Now

Charlie Kirk believed, whatever one thought of his politics, that you should be willing to defend your positions with evidence. Prove me wrong was not just a brand — it was a methodological commitment to the adversarial exchange of ideas.

The honest application of that standard here means acknowledging several things simultaneously.

The ballistic gap is real and deserves serious attention. The defense has a legitimate argument for delay. Independent forensic review is appropriate and should proceed. The FBI’s additional testing should be made public when complete. These are reasonable positions that have nothing to do with conspiracy.

And: Robinson’s own words, as preserved in handwritten notes, chat logs, and statements to family, remain the most powerful evidence in this case. A bullet that cannot be matched to a specific rifle does not unmatch a man from his own confession, his own DNA, and his own written declaration of intent.

The sheriff’s resignation is worth watching. Was it unrelated? Possibly. Was it connected to pressure related to the case? Unknown. Does it deserve a reporter’s sustained attention rather than a tweet? Absolutely.

The April 17 hearing on cameras and the May 18 preliminary hearing — if it proceeds on schedule — will tell us considerably more. Watch those closely.

A Personal Note from the Canadian Watching All This

Seven months ago I wrote that Kirk’s death offered America a choice: honest reckoning or partisan weaponization. The administration chose the latter almost immediately. Now the conspiracy ecosystem is making the same error from the opposite direction, substituting outrage for analysis and coincidence for evidence.

Evidence that is inconclusive belongs in a courtroom, not in a Marjorie Taylor Greene tweet. A sheriff’s unexplained resignation belongs in an investigative journalist’s notebook, not in a Candace Owens post about bomb dogs and fingerprints.

Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” standard demands better than this from all of us — including those who disagreed with everything he ever said.

The case against Tyler Robinson may strengthen, weaken, or fracture further as more evidence emerges. We should watch it carefully, honestly, and without the partisan lens that has distorted every development since September 10, 2025.

That is the least we owe the man who built his entire public identity around the idea that the truth, properly examined, should be able to stand on its own.
