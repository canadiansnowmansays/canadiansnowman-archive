The Kash Patel Files

The Purge

Part III: Hundreds of Years of Experience in One Brutal Swoop

April 22, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

On January 30, 2025 — the day after Kash Patel assured senators under oath that “no one will be terminated for case assignments” and that “all FBI employees will be protected against political retribution” — Trump administration officials forced out the FBI’s top six executives and the heads of the bureau’s field offices in Washington and Miami.

The action was coordinated by Emil Bove, Trump’s former defence attorney, now serving as acting deputy attorney general. The FBI workforce, according to multiple accounts in The New York Times, experienced Bove as “halfway between a mob enforcer and the grim reaper.”

“It was hundreds of years of experience in one brutal swoop,” a senior executive told reporters. “It made it easier to bring their people in, yes, but there are no people to bring in with that level of experience, because it’s so specialized.”

The swoop was not the end. It was the beginning.

The List

Before Patel was even confirmed, Bove ordered the acting FBI leadership to create a list of all employees who had been assigned to January 6 investigations — “to determine whether additional personnel actions are necessary.” The January 6 case was the largest investigation in the history of the FBI. It had involved thousands of agents, analysts, surveillance teams, negotiators, and SWAT operators. “It’s hard not to have touched one of those cases in some way,” one agent told The New York Times. “Maybe even more so than 9/11.”

The acting director, Brian Driscoll, and acting deputy director, Rob Kissane, were faced with an impossible demand. Comply and hand over names that would be used to fire agents for doing their jobs. Refuse and be fired themselves.

They found a third path. They submitted a list of thousands of employees identified only by bureau ID numbers — flooding the zone to protect individuals. When Bove demanded actual names, they refused.

Bove sent a bureau-wide email describing Driscoll and Kissane’s response as “insubordination.” The email was bizarre — a political appointee publicly castigating career law enforcement officials in front of 38,000 employees for protecting their workforce from political retaliation.

Driscoll became, in the words of multiple FBI sources, “a folk hero.”

He was later fired.

The Polygraph Machine

Once installed, Patel turned to a tool that the FBI’s own experts regard with professional scepticism: the polygraph.

Polygraph machines do not detect lies. They detect stress. This is why honest but nervous people sometimes fail their examinations, while smooth, sociopathic liars pass them. Their results are generally not admissible as evidence in American courts. But they have one property that makes them invaluable to an authoritarian: they are frightening. Being strapped into a chair that measures your heartbeat, your breathing, and your biological responses, while being told that your answers could end your career — that produces stress in virtually everyone, regardless of what they have or haven’t done.

Patel weaponized them.

Under previous directors, polygraphs were used for security clearances and to investigate specific allegations of misconduct. Under Patel, they became loyalty tests. The New York Times reported that FBI agents were being asked, under polygraph, whether they had said anything negative about the director. Not about national security. Not about corruption. About him.

“They were polygraphing their own senior leadership team because they were so mad about media leaks,” said Tonya Ugoretz, former assistant director of the directorate of intelligence. Each division head was ordered to identify two people to be polygraphed. Many, including Ugoretz, volunteered themselves rather than impose the ordeal on their subordinates.

As Tom Nichols wrote in The Atlantic, the spectacle bore less resemblance to McCarthyism than to something from The Death of Stalin: “All the FBI needs now to complete the scene is for Patel to run down the hallways shouting, ‘I have documents on all of you!’”

One former official told The Atlantic‘s Fitzpatrick that in these sessions, agents had been asked for opinions about Patel’s perceived “enemies” — as well as whether they had ever said anything disparaging about the director or the president. This is not counterintelligence. This is not internal affairs. This is the personal insecurity of a man with a badge and a lie detector, hunting for people who have been insufficiently reverential.

The Grassley Pipeline

Perhaps the most insidious mechanism of the purge was the partnership between Patel’s FBI and Senator Chuck Grassley, the Republican chairman of the Senate Judiciary Committee.

Throughout 2025 and into 2026, Grassley released documents naming individual street-level FBI agents who had worked on investigations into Trump — including the Arctic Frost investigation into Trump’s efforts to overturn the 2020 election. The names came from materials provided by the FBI’s own office of congressional affairs, headed by Marshall Yates.

The pipeline worked like this: Yates and his team combed through FBI files to identify agents they considered biased. Those names were provided to Grassley. Grassley read them aloud on the Senate floor or released them in public letters. Patel then fired the named agents.

The result was a targeting mechanism that used congressional privilege as a shield. Grassley could name agents without consequence — congressional speech is protected — while Patel could fire them, citing the senator’s disclosures as justification. The FBI’s traditional practice of protecting case agents by asking Congress to redact their names from sensitive disclosures was abandoned.

Walter Giardina was one of the agents named.

Walter Giardina

Giardina was a veteran agent on CR-15, the FBI’s elite public corruption squad in Washington. Grassley’s letter to Patel accused him of having corroborated the Steele dossier — a set of discredited memos about Trump’s ties to Russia in 2016.

Giardina had never had access to the Steele dossier. The FBI’s own case access history proved this. He spent weeks trying to meet with Marshall Yates and the office of congressional affairs to correct the record.

During this period, his wife was dying of cancer. She was in and out of the hospital from June 5 to July 15. On June 19, Giardina had a meeting scheduled at the Hoover Building to address Grassley’s allegations. His wife had just started a new chemotherapy medication and suffered a terrible adverse reaction. He asked to do the meeting via video instead. They cancelled it.

He reserved a conference room at the hospital, next to his wife’s room, so the congressional affairs office could interview him there. They didn’t show up.

His wife died the next day.

Two days after her funeral, at his insistence, Giardina was interviewed. He brought a prepared statement with all the facts. They didn’t ask any questions. He sent all the records they requested. “They’d already decided to fire me,” he said, “so no one was taking the interview seriously except me.”

He was fired. He had three children.

“Walter was one of the most heartbreaking ones,” said Maria Ricci, a former assistant special agent in charge of the Washington field office. “Because it was the week after he buried his wife.”

David Maltinsky

Maltinsky was an FBI agent in training, three weeks from graduating from Quantico. His aunt was a retired special agent. He had spent years working toward the bureau as a tactical specialist in Los Angeles. Being accepted at Quantico was, he said, “accomplishing my life’s goal.”

He was terminated for displaying a Pride flag at his desk.

The flag had been given to him by FBI leadership in 2021, after it flew in front of the L.A. field office during Pride month. It had been on his desk for years. After Trump’s inauguration, an employee on another floor complained that the flag violated the president’s executive order banning D.E.I. Maltinsky’s supervisor told him it didn’t violate anything and to keep it up.

Somehow, word reached Patel.

The termination letter said Maltinsky had “exercised poor judgment with an inappropriate display of political signage.”

When the executives delivered the news, one asked where Maltinsky would go that night. He said he had nowhere to go. “You could see in their faces that they thought this was awful,” he recalled. “They said, ‘You could stay another night if you need to plan and pack.’”

Three weeks from graduation. A life’s goal. A flag that his own bureau had given him.

The Kneelers

In the summer of 2020, during protests following the killing of George Floyd, a group of FBI agents were photographed kneeling during a Black Lives Matter demonstration. They later explained — and an internal investigation confirmed — that a crowd had backed them against a wall and was throwing objects. They knelt to de-escalate a volatile situation. They were cleared.

Then the photograph went viral. The internet dubbed them “Kneel Team Six.” Right-wing media had its outrage.

Patel reopened the investigation. The agents were cleared again.

He fired them anyway.

“We’re not cops, despite what Kash Patel says,” said Maria Ricci. “We’re investigators. We gather evidence and build cases. That summer, we were put on the street to handle riots. And we were never, ever trained to do that. Cops have batons. They have pepper spray. They have all sorts of other tools for crowd control. What we have are our voices and our guns. These agents were surrounded and made a decision to de-escalate the situation. And that was, in my opinion, the right choice.”

Michael Feinberg

Feinberg was a leader of the FBI’s field office in Norfolk, Virginia. He was forced out after Dan Bongino, the deputy director, discovered he was friends with Peter Strzok — the former agent fired in 2018 for sending text messages critical of Trump during the Russia investigation.

Feinberg and Strzok had worked together in the counterintelligence division years before any scandal was associated with Strzok’s name. They discovered they liked the same bands. They went to see Nick Cave and Warren Ellis together.

That was the offence. Attending a concert with the wrong person.

Bongino ordered an investigation. Feinberg was told he would be demoted, polygraphed, and investigated about his “relationship” with Strzok. His wife was in the middle of a high-risk pregnancy.

He resigned within 30 seconds of hanging up the phone.

“I took an oath to the Constitution, as idealistic as that sounds,” he said, “and the point of an oath is that you follow it regardless of personal consequences. I was being targeted for blatantly political reasons.”

CR-15

The final blow to the FBI’s public corruption capability came when Patel disbanded CR-15 entirely — the storied squad that had handled some of the bureau’s most sensitive investigations into government officials, including the Arctic Frost case.

After Grassley released documents showing that agents working on Arctic Frost had subpoenaed the January 6 call logs of nine Republican members of Congress — records showing only metadata, not the content of conversations — Patel fired two more agents and shut the squad down.

“You’re darn right I fired those agents,” Patel said on Fox News that night. “You’re darn right I blew up CR-15.”

Blaire Toleman, the supervisory special agent who ran the squad, was fired separately. Her termination letter said she was being dismissed for “weaponization of the government.” In reality, she said, “they were firing us because they didn’t like the case we worked.”

The firing itself was a bureaucratic farce. Toleman was told she was fired. Then told she wasn’t. Then, two hours later, told she wasn’t fired. Then, the next day, fired again.

“A lot of people in the bureau are pissed about what’s happening,” she said on her way out, surrounded by roughly 40 colleagues who had walked her to the door. “But they will step up to try to make sure the American people remain safe, because that’s our job.”

The Message

The purge was not reform. Patel did not study the bureau, identify systemic failures, and restructure accordingly. He did not audit performance, evaluate leadership, or propose institutional improvements. He arrived with a list of perceived enemies and a framework for punishment, and he executed both with the blunt instrument of Article II — claiming the constitutional authority to fire anyone he chose, without cause.

The message to the remaining 38,000 employees was not complicated. It did not require a briefing or a memo. It was delivered through the examples themselves:

You can be fired for the case your supervisor assigned you to work.

You can be fired for a flag your bureau gave you to display.

You can be fired for attending a concert with a friend.

You can be fired for kneeling when a crowd throws objects at you.

You can be fired the week after you bury your wife, on allegations the records disprove.

And if you report any of this — through whistleblower channels, through the press, through the congressional oversight mechanisms that are supposed to exist for exactly this purpose — you will be polygraphed, investigated, and added to the next list.

“The downside of having folks scared about losing their jobs,” said John Sullivan, a former section chief in the intelligence division, “is that you go from being an organization where people come in and do the work to spending time keeping their head down or on LinkedIn looking for jobs.”

He added: “I want to sound the alarm about how this affects Americans’ safety. I’ve seen it up close, where we have stopped stuff Americans will never know about.”

The institutional damage of the purge is not merely current. It is generational. The expertise that walked out the door — or was pushed — represents decades of accumulated knowledge about how the FBI works, how investigations are built, how threats are assessed, how allies are managed, how constitutional boundaries are maintained.

That expertise cannot be replaced by executive order. It cannot be reconstituted by hiring. It can only be rebuilt over years, by people willing to invest their careers in an institution that has just demonstrated, with brutal clarity, that it will not protect them.

The question is not whether anyone will be willing to do that.

The question is whether the country can afford to wait.

But while the agents were packing their offices and the polygraph machines were humming, the director was busy with other matters. There was a girlfriend who needed a SWAT team. There was an Olympics that needed attending. There was a Five Eyes conference where the MI5 director wanted to talk about counterterrorism and the FBI director wanted to go jet skiing. And there was a challenge coin to design — one with a Punisher skull and spider eyes and the letters K$H, because even the destruction of an institution requires branding.
