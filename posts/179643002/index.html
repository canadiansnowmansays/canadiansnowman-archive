The Rise and Fall of Marjorie Taylor Greene

The Rise and Fall of Marjorie Taylor Greene

Part 1: From Facebook Conspiracist to Congressional Power Broker

November 22, 2025

A CanadianSnowmanSays Investigation

On November 21, 2025, Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene (R-GA) posted a 22-minute video and four-page letter to X announcing her resignation from Congress, effective January 5, 2026. The woman who entered Washington as perhaps the most controversial freshman representative in modern American history was leaving as a self-declared martyr, betrayed by the very president who once called her a “future Republican star.”

Her departure wasn’t graceful. In characteristic MTG fashion, it combined grievance, defiance, and apocalyptic rhetoric about a “Political Industrial Complex” destroying America. But unlike her countless previous controversies, this time she wasn’t fighting Democrats, the mainstream media, or establishment Republicans. She was fighting Donald Trump himself—and losing.

The arc from Trump’s “future star” to “Marjorie ‘Traitor’ Greene” took just five years. It’s a story that reveals something fundamental about the nature of MAGA politics: loyalty to the movement matters less than loyalty to the man. And in that collision, even one of Trump’s most fervent defenders discovered there are limits to disruption.

The Construction Company Owner Goes Viral

Marjorie Taylor Greene didn’t emerge from political obscurity through traditional channels. She built no local political base, held no lower office, and demonstrated no particular policy expertise. What she had was a construction company (Taylor Commercial), a gym habit that would become central to her brand, and a Facebook account.

Between 2018 and 2019, Greene went down the QAnon rabbit hole publicly and enthusiastically. She embraced the conspiracy theory that a cabal of Satan-worshipping pedophiles ran the Democratic Party and that Trump was waging a secret war against them. She shared videos suggesting that Hillary Clinton had murdered a child and drank their blood. She called the 2018 Parkland school shooting a “false flag” operation and harassed survivor David Hogg. Most notoriously, she speculated about “Jewish space lasers” causing California wildfires

The transformation: Greene at a 2020 gun rights rally (left) surrounded by armed militia members, versus her official Congressional portrait (right). From Facebook conspiracist to congressional power broker in six years.

This wasn’t typical political positioning. It was algorithmic radicalization in real-time, and Greene leaned into it. The Facebook posts, the shares, the increasingly unhinged theories—they weren’t bugs in her political strategy. They were features. In Georgia’s deep-red 14th congressional district, where Trump had won by 50 points in 2016, being too extreme for the Republican establishment was an asset, not a liability.

The 2020 Primary: Outsider Insurgency

When longtime incumbent Tom Graves announced his retirement in 2019, Greene jumped into a crowded primary. The establishment favorite was Dr. John Cowan, a respected neurosurgeon with actual medical credentials—a contrast that would have mattered in any previous Republican era.

It didn’t matter in 2020. Greene raised money nationally from small-dollar donors who loved her combative style. She traveled to Washington to speak at far-right rallies. She made national headlines for all the wrong reasons, which turned out to be all the right reasons in GA-14.

When no candidate cleared 50% in the June primary, Greene and Cowan headed to an August runoff. Trump’s endorsement arrived at the perfect moment: “Congratulations to future Republican Star Marjorie Taylor Greene on a big Congressional primary win in Georgia against a very tough opponent,” he tweeted. “Marjorie is strong on everything and never gives up - a real WINNER!”

She crushed Cowan by 15 points in the runoff. In the general election, the Democratic nominee withdrew after reported death threats and harassment, leaving Greene to win with 75% of the vote in a walkover against a write-in candidate.

She was heading to Congress, and everyone knew exactly what kind of representative she would be.

Freshman Year: From Pariah to Profit

Greene arrived in Washington in January 2021 with two clear goals: make noise and refuse to apologize for anything. She succeeded spectacularly at both.

Within weeks, the House voted 230-199 (with 11 Republicans joining Democrats) to strip her of committee assignments. The proximate cause was her past statements about violence against Democrats and her QAnon embrace. For any normal representative, losing committee seats would be a career-killing humiliation. For Greene, it was rocket fuel

She immediately spun the punishment into a fundraising bonanza, casting herself as a victim of the “radical left” and the “weak” Republican establishment. The money poured in—she raised over $3.2 million in the first quarter of 2021, more than all but a handful of House members.

Without committee work, Greene became a full-time media personality and grenade-thrower. She filed articles of impeachment against Joe Biden on his first full day in office. She compared vaccine requirements to the Holocaust (later apologizing after a visit to the Holocaust Museum). She screamed “Liar!” at Biden during his 2022 State of the Union address.

But here’s what the early narratives missed: Greene wasn’t just performing for clicks. She was building real political infrastructure. She became a fixture on Steve Bannon’s “War Room” podcast, reaching millions of MAGA hardliners daily. She cultivated relationships with the House Freedom Caucus firebrands. She worked her district relentlessly, holding town halls and staying visible.

And crucially, she remained absolutely loyal to Donald Trump through everything—the January 6 investigations, the Mar-a-Lago documents case, all of it. While other Republicans hedged or distanced themselves, Greene was all in.

2022: Surviving and Thriving

The 2022 midterms tested whether Greene’s brand of chaos could survive a serious primary challenge. She faced moderate Republican Jennifer Strahan, who had establishment backing and painted Greene as an embarrassment to the district.

Greene won the primary with 70% of the vote. In the general, she defeated Democrat Marcus Flowers (who raised a stunning $15 million) by nearly 30 points, improving on her 2020 margin.

The message was clear: in GA-14, Marjorie Taylor Greene wasn’t a liability. She was exactly what Republican voters wanted.

The Speaker Fight: From Backbencher to Kingmaker

Everything changed in January 2023. Republicans had narrowly retaken the House majority, and Kevin McCarthy needed 218 votes to become Speaker. He had a problem: about 20 Freedom Caucus hardliners, including Greene’s allies Matt Gaetz and Andy Biggs, refused to support him.

This is where Greene revealed something surprising: she could count votes, cut deals, and play inside ball when it suited her purposes

.She negotiated with McCarthy early, extracting promises about committee assignments, rules changes giving individual members more power, and commitments on spending fights. Then she became one of McCarthy’s most effective advocates to the holdouts, working the phones and the House floor to bring rebels into line.

After 15 ballots and four days of chaos, McCarthy won. Greene got her reward: assignments to the Homeland Security and Oversight committees. More importantly, she’d demonstrated that she wasn’t just a bomb-thrower—she could be a power broker.

For about nine months, this arrangement held. Greene supported McCarthy’s legislative agenda, defended him in the media, and played the role of loyal ally. She even moderated slightly, pulling back from some of her most inflammatory rhetoric.

October 2023: The Snake Strikes

Then came the motion to vacate.

Matt Gaetz, frustrated with McCarthy’s deal to avoid a government shutdown, filed the motion to remove him as Speaker. Only eight Republicans needed to join Democrats to oust McCarthy. Greene had to make a choice: loyalty to the man who’d empowered her, or loyalty to the chaos caucus that had made her famous.

She chose chaos. Greene was one of the eight Republicans who voted to remove McCarthy, sending the House into three weeks of paralysis before Mike Johnson finally won the speakership.

Her explanation was pure MTG: McCarthy had betrayed conservative principles, sold out to Democrats, and broken his promises. Whether those charges were fair was almost beside the point. What mattered was that Greene had demonstrated she would burn down the very structures she’d helped build if she felt betrayed.

It was a preview of what would come 14 months later, when Trump would do the same to her.

2024: Peak Power

By 2024, Greene had achieved something remarkable: normalization. Yes, she was still controversial. Yes, she still said outrageous things. But she was also a known quantity, a reliable vote for Republican leadership (except when she wasn’t), and a formidable fundraiser and campaigner.

When Trump secured the 2024 Republican nomination, Greene was again all-in. She campaigned for him across the country, appeared at his rallies, and defended him through the multiple criminal indictments. She was rewarded with prime speaking slots and continued presidential support.

The relationship had come a long way from its rocky start. In October 2022, according to New York Times reporter Maggie Haberman, Trump had “repeatedly” discussed choosing Greene as his 2024 running mate in conversations with advisers. The idea never materialized—Trump ultimately chose JD Vance—but the very fact that Greene was in the conversation spoke volumes. From QAnon pariah stripped of committee assignments to potential vice president in less than two years. It was the ultimate MAGA success story.

In November 2024, Trump won. Republicans held the House by a razor-thin margin. And Greene, now in her third term, seemed positioned for even greater influence in a second Trump administration.

She’d survived scandals that would have destroyed most politicians. She’d built a national brand. She’d demonstrated both the ability to disrupt and the capacity to deal. From Facebook conspiracist to congressional power broker in six years—it was an improbable American success story.

What could possibly go wrong?
