The Battery Revolution the United States Is Choosing to Miss

Essay #18: While the World Builds the Future, Washington Hands Out Coal Trophies and Bans the Word “Clean”

February 16, 2026

The Changan Nevo A06, powered by CATL's Naxtra sodium-ion battery, undergoes cold-weather testing in Inner Mongolia. The battery retains over 90% capacity at -40°C. Feb 2026.

On February 5, 2026, in Yakeshi, Inner Mongolia — a city where winter temperatures routinely plunge to minus 40 — CATL and Changan Automobile unveiled the world’s first mass-production passenger vehicle powered by a sodium-ion battery. The car, a Changan Nevo A06, will reach the market by mid-year. The battery retains over 90% of its capacity at minus 40 degrees Celsius. It charges fast, it doesn’t catch fire, and it is made from one of the most abundant elements on Earth — sodium, which is literally table salt.

One week later, on February 12, at the White House, the Washington Coal Club presented Donald Trump with a trophy inscribed “Undisputed Champion of Beautiful Clean Coal.” Trump signed an executive order directing the Pentagon to purchase more coal-fired electricity. The Department of Energy allocated $175 million to upgrade coal plants. And the Environmental Protection Agency formally rescinded the 2009 endangerment finding — the legal and scientific determination that greenhouse gases threaten public health — eliminating the federal government’s authority to regulate the emissions that are warming the planet.

“We are driving a dagger straight into the heart of the climate change religion,” said EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin.

While Zeldin was driving daggers, China was driving sodium-ion cars. In minus 40 weather. Powered by batteries made from salt.

This essay is about that gap — the distance between where battery technology is going and where American energy policy is being dragged. It is about what happened in the world of energy storage in the four weeks since I last wrote about batteries in this series, and it is about the staggering velocity of innovation that the rest of the world is embracing and the United States is choosing — consciously, deliberately, with trophies and ceremonies — to ignore.

A Précis on the Demolition in Washington

I will not dwell long on the policy catastrophe. In forthcoming installments of The Trump Accountability Project — Parts 27 and 28, publishing in March — I document in comprehensive detail the 16-year secret campaign by four individuals to destroy the endangerment finding, and the intergenerational consequences that destruction guarantees. The short version is this:

Between 2005 and 2026, Russell Vought, Jeffrey Clark, Mandy Gunasekara, and Jonathan Brightbill — operating with Heritage Foundation funding and deliberate secrecy — built the legal, scientific, and regulatory architecture to dismantle every federal climate protection in the United States. They drafted executive orders. They solicited white papers from scientists willing to dispute the physics of atmospheric warming. They sought $2 million in covert funding. And they operated, by their own written admission, in secrecy “to prevent media and other conflicted sources from shaming participants and undercutting the work before it is done.”

Two of them — Vought and Clark — now hold senior positions in the Trump White House. The plan they wrote is the plan they are executing. The endangerment finding is gone. Vehicle emission standards are gone. The EPA has banned the phrases “energy transition,” “sustainability,” and “clean energy” from federal communications. The DOE has saved 17 gigawatts of coal-fired generation that the market was ready to retire. And the administration has blocked billions in clean energy funding that Congress had already appropriated.

Myron Ebell, who has spent three decades attacking climate science, described the situation with unusual candour: “We are pretty close to total victory.”

I will let those words stand for now. The full reckoning is coming in March. This essay is about something else entirely.

It is about what the rest of the world was building while Washington was demolishing — and I don’t mean the East Wing.

Sodium-Ion: From Laboratory Curiosity to the Road in Record Time

When I wrote Essay #15 in this series on January 18, sodium-ion batteries were a promising technology with clear theoretical advantages — abundant materials, lower cost potential, superior cold-weather performance — but limited commercial deployment. That was four weeks ago. In those four weeks, the landscape shifted so dramatically that MIT Technology Review named sodium-ion batteries one of its 10 Breakthrough Technologies for 2026. Not for some future year. For right now.

Here is what happened.

CATL, the world’s largest battery manufacturer, launched its Naxtra sodium-ion battery platform commercially. The technology achieves an energy density of 175 watt-hours per kilogram, which puts it on par with lithium iron phosphate — the chemistry that currently powers most of China’s electric vehicles. The company deployed sodium-ion batteries first in light commercial vehicles, then announced the passenger vehicle partnership with Changan that produced the Nevo A06 unveiling in Inner Mongolia.

The cold-weather numbers deserve emphasis, because they matter for anyone who lives above the 45th parallel — which includes all of Canada, Scandinavia, and large portions of Europe, Russia, and the northern United States. CATL’s sodium-ion batteries retain over 90% of their usable capacity at minus 40°C. At that same temperature, they deliver nearly triple the discharge power of equivalent lithium iron phosphate batteries. They charge reliably at minus 30°C. And at minus 50°C — a temperature that would render most lithium-ion cells effectively useless — they still deliver stable power.

For a Canadian writer covering battery technology, these are not incremental improvements. They are a potential paradigm shift for cold-climate electrification — the thing that skeptics have always said couldn’t be done.

And CATL is not alone. BYD is building a massive sodium-ion production facility. Sodium-ion battery shipments reached 9 gigawatt-hours in 2025, up 150% from the previous year. Industry projections expect that number to exceed 1,000 GWh within four years. CATL itself has declared 2026 the beginning of what it calls a “dual-chemistry era” in which sodium-ion and lithium-ion batteries coexist, with each deployed where its strengths are greatest — sodium for grid storage and cold-climate applications, lithium for high-energy-density uses like long-range vehicles.

Meanwhile, researchers solved one of the fundamental obstacles to fast-charging sodium-ion cells. A study published at the end of 2025 identified the mechanism — essentially a molecular traffic jam in how sodium ions enter hard carbon particles — and demonstrated that mixing hard carbon with aluminum oxide eliminates the bottleneck entirely. The theoretical fast-charging capability of sodium-ion batteries, which had never been achievable in practice, is now within reach.

And then there is the economics. A new study published in the Journal of Energy Storage projects that by 2050, sodium-ion batteries with reasonable learning rates could deliver grid-scale storage at 11 to 14 euros per megawatt-hour — significantly cheaper than lithium-ion at 16 to 22 euros. But the more immediate economic argument is simpler: sodium is everywhere. It does not require mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo. It does not depend on lithium deposits concentrated in a handful of countries. It does not create the supply chain vulnerabilities that have made battery manufacturing a geopolitical chess match. You can make sodium-ion batteries from salt, iron, and manganese — materials so abundant that scarcity is essentially a non-issue.

This is what the United States is choosing to ignore while its president accepts coal trophies.

Solid-State: The Decade of “Almost Ready” May Actually Be Over

Solid-state batteries have been the perpetual next big thing in energy storage for so long that even optimists had learned to hedge their projections. The promise has always been extraordinary: replace the flammable liquid electrolyte in conventional lithium-ion batteries with a solid material, and you get a cell that is safer, more energy-dense, faster-charging, and longer-lasting. The problem has always been engineering: making solid electrolytes that conduct ions efficiently, don’t crack under pressure, and can be manufactured at scale.

In the four weeks since Essay #15, the engineering problems took several significant steps toward resolution — and the first solid-state batteries arrived in production vehicles.

The Verge TS, now powered by Donut Lab's all-solid-state battery — the first production vehicle on public roads running solid-state technology. Charges in under 10 minutes, 600 km range. Q1 2026.

At CES 2026, in the first week of January, a company called Donut Lab announced that its all-solid-state batteries were powering Verge Motorcycles’ current lineup — the TS Pro and Ultra — making them, by their account, the first production vehicles on public roads running on solid-state battery technology. The motorcycles charge in under 10 minutes, with the long-range version delivering 600 kilometres on a single charge.

This is a motorcycle, not a sedan. The batteries are smaller and the engineering challenges are more manageable at that scale. But the symbolic importance is real: solid-state has left the laboratory.

FAW Group's test vehicle equipped with what the company calls the industry's first lithium-rich manganese semi-solid-state battery — energy density exceeding 500 Wh/kg, range over 1,000 km. February 2026.

On the automotive side, the developments are coming almost too fast to catalogue. China’s FAW Group installed what it called the industry’s first lithium-rich manganese semi-solid-state battery in a vehicle in February. The cell energy density exceeds 500 watt-hours per kilogram — roughly double what today’s best lithium-ion cells achieve — with a total pack capacity of 142 kilowatt-hours and a range exceeding 1,000 kilometres.

GAC Group completed China’s first production line for automotive-grade all-solid-state batteries with more than 60 ampere-hour capacity. The cells have nearly double the energy density of conventional lithium-ion. Dongfeng Motors began cold-weather testing of solid-state battery prototypes claiming 350 Wh/kg and over 1,000 kilometres of range. BYD, Geely, and several other Chinese automakers are all on parallel tracks.

China is now formalizing this progress into industrial policy. In December 2025, China’s National Automotive Standardization Technical Committee released the first draft of a national standard for solid-state EV batteries — defining terminology, classification, and performance benchmarks. The comment period closes in late February. If approved, the standard takes effect in July 2026.

Think about what that means. China is not just building solid-state batteries. It is writing the rules for how they will be defined, tested, and traded globally. Standards-setting is where industrial dominance is locked in. The country that writes the standard often controls the market.

Outside China, the trajectory is similar if slightly slower. Stellantis and Factorial Energy validated automotive-sized solid-state battery cells with 375 Wh/kg energy density and charging from 15% to 90% in 18 minutes at room temperature. Stellantis plans a demonstration fleet using these cells in 2026. Mercedes-Benz drove a modified EQS equipped with Factorial’s solid-state batteries over 1,200 kilometres on a single charge. Toyota has committed to solid-state EVs by 2028. BMW, Nissan, Volkswagen, Hyundai — all targeting the 2027–2030 window.

And on the fundamental science side, Stanford researchers published a breakthrough on January 16 — two days before my last battery essay — demonstrating that an ultrathin silver coating on solid electrolytes makes them five times more resistant to cracking, addressing one of the most persistent failure modes in solid-state cells. Separately, researchers at KAIST in South Korea showed that divalent anion doping can dramatically improve lithium-ion transport in solid electrolytes using inexpensive materials, eliminating the need for costly metals.

The industry consensus — from IDTechEx, TrendForce, CAS, and the major battery manufacturers themselves — is converging: semi-solid batteries are in vehicles today at 300–350 Wh/kg. Small-series fully solid-state EVs will arrive around 2027. Mass production begins circa 2030. The projected market is $10 billion by 2036.

These are not the timelines of laboratory speculation. These are the timelines of industrial mobilization. And they are being executed primarily by companies in China, South Korea, Japan, and Europe.

Grid-Scale Storage: The Inflection Point Has Arrived

While the headlines focus on vehicle batteries, an equally consequential revolution is happening in stationary energy storage — the grid-scale battery systems that store electricity from solar and wind farms for use when the sun isn’t shining and the wind isn’t blowing. This is the technology that critics once said made renewable energy fatally unreliable. That argument is now obsolete.

The global battery energy storage system fleet surpassed 250 gigawatts of installed capacity in early 2026, overtaking pumped hydroelectric storage for the first time in history. Pumped hydro — the technology of damming rivers to store gravitational energy — had dominated grid-scale storage for over a century. It has just been surpassed by batteries that did not meaningfully exist at grid scale a decade ago.

Rystad Energy projects that BESS additions will exceed 130 GW and 350 gigawatt-hours in 2026 alone. China, the United States, the United Kingdom, Australia, and Germany are leading deployments. In China, the world’s largest AI-powered battery storage cluster — 12.8 GWh across multiple projects — came online recently, including a single 4 GWh facility. In Belgium, Europe’s largest battery storage project — 600 megawatts, 2,400 megawatt-hours — just received its final permit. The Netherlands allocated 100 million euros for battery storage subsidies alongside solar installations.

The U.S. Solar Energy Industries Association projects that American demand for battery storage will grow sixfold by 2030 — a projection that was made before the Trump administration began systematically dismantling the incentive structures that would make that growth possible.

Peak Energy's grid-scale sodium-ion storage system. The U.S. startup is already deploying the technology — even as the federal government bans the phrase 'clean energy' from official communications.

And here is where sodium-ion re-enters the picture. The technology’s greatest immediate impact may not be on roads but on grids. Sodium-ion batteries’ combination of low cost, long cycle life (some formats exceed 10,000 cycles), wide temperature tolerance, and stable supply chains makes them ideal for the stationary storage applications that are the backbone of the renewable energy transition. Peak Energy, a U.S. startup, is already deploying grid-scale sodium-ion storage. The U.S. Department of Energy — the same DOE that is now celebrating coal — has Argonne National Laboratory supporting sodium-ion research.

The irony is suffocating. The federal government’s own national laboratory is advancing the technology that the administration’s own policies are designed to suppress.

The Coal Trophy and the Sodium Car: A Tale of Two Civilizations

Let me put the timeline in sharp relief, because the juxtaposition is so precise it barely requires commentary.

February 5, 2026: CATL and Changan unveil the world’s first mass-production sodium-ion passenger vehicle in Inner Mongolia. The battery works at minus 40. It’s made from salt.

February 11, 2026: Donald Trump hosts a “Clean, Beautiful Coal” event at the White House. The Washington Coal Club gives him a miner-shaped trophy. He orders the Pentagon to buy more coal electricity.

February 12, 2026: The EPA rescinds the endangerment finding, eliminating the legal basis for all federal greenhouse gas regulation. Lee Zeldin describes this as driving a dagger into the heart of “the climate change religion.”

February 13, 2026: The Department of Energy publishes a “one year, promises kept” press release celebrating that 17 gigawatts of coal-fired generation have been “saved” from retirement. And at the White House, construction cranes hover over the demolished East Wing — razed in October without congressional approval, environmental review, or public notice — as the administration files its most detailed plans yet for a 89,000-square-foot ballroom designed to resemble the gilded interiors of Trump’s private clubs. Estimated cost: $400 million. Capacity: 999 guests. Funded by Apple, Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, Lockheed Martin, and others.

February 14, 2026: The ballroom renderings are briefly posted on the National Capital Planning Commission’s website, then swiftly taken down. They show a structure that dwarfs the demolished East Wing and matches the height of the historic White House mansion. The National Trust for Historic Preservation has sued to halt construction. A federal judge has expressed deep scepticism about the president’s authority to build it. The administration has invoked national security to defend the project.

February 13, 2026. The construction site where the East Wing of the White House stood for 84 years. It is being replaced by an 89,000-square-foot ballroom, estimated at $400 million, funded by Apple, Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Lockheed Martin. Two days earlier, the same administration ordered the Pentagon to purchase coal-fired electricity and allocated $175 million to upgrade coal plants. The same week, global battery storage capacity surpassed 250 GW for the first time in history, overtaking pumped hydro. The priorities are not hidden. They are architectural. AP Photo.

Let me hold those two weeks in frame for a moment. In the span of ten days, the President of the United States received a coal trophy, rescinded the scientific basis for climate regulation, ordered the military to buy coal-fired electricity, allocated $175 million in federal funds to upgrade coal plants, and continued construction on a $400 million personal ballroom on the grounds of the White House. In those same ten days, China unveiled the world’s first sodium-ion passenger vehicle, Stanford published a breakthrough in solid-state electrolyte engineering, and the global battery storage fleet surpassed pumped hydro for the first time in history.

The administration found $400 million in private donations for a ballroom and $175 million in public funds for coal plants. It is simultaneously clawing back billions that Congress appropriated for clean energy. The priorities are not hidden. They are architectural.

Here is what 17 gigawatts of “saved” coal means in economic terms. Grid Strategies, the energy consulting firm, estimates that keeping those coal plants open will cost U.S. utility customers between $3 billion and $6 billion by the end of 2028. This is not an environmental argument. This is a math argument. In the United States today, 99% of coal plants are more expensive to operate than it would be to replace them with new wind or solar generation. The administration is not choosing the cheaper option. It is not choosing the more reliable option. It is not choosing the option that creates more jobs. It is subsidizing the most expensive electricity source available and forcing ratepayers to cover the difference — while simultaneously cutting the incentives for the cheapest sources.

Julie McNamara of the Union of Concerned Scientists put it concisely: “The country has real solutions at hand — yet instead of pushing ahead with investments in the fastest, cheapest, cleanest resources available, the Trump administration is actively doing everything it can to stop the deployment of new solar and wind projects, to stop investments in energy efficiency, and to stop the buildout of modern grid infrastructure.”

The art of the deal: for the president, a coal trophy and a ballroom. For the American people, higher electricity bills and a vanishing share of the global clean energy economy.

The Word Ban

There is one detail from the administration’s climate rollback that I have saved for this section because it belongs here, among the battery innovations and the grid-scale deployments and the cold-weather breakthroughs. It is small, almost bureaucratic, and it is the detail that makes me angriest.

The Trump administration has compiled a list of words and phrases that federal agencies are discouraged or prohibited from using in official communications. The list includes: “energy transition.” “Sustainability.” “Sustainable.” “’Clean’ or ‘dirty’ energy.” “Carbon footprint.” “Tax breaks, tax credits, subsidies.”

They are banning the vocabulary of the future.Let me hold those two weeks.

While CATL announces a “dual-chemistry era” of sodium and lithium batteries shining together, the U.S. government is ensuring its own employees cannot use the phrase “clean energy” in a memorandum. While Stanford publishes breakthroughs in solid electrolyte engineering, federal scientists are being told that “sustainability” is a prohibited concept. While the rest of the world builds the lexicon of a post-carbon economy, the United States is erasing the words.

This is not censorship in the dramatic, book-burning sense. It is something more insidious — the bureaucratic suffocation of ideas through the elimination of the language needed to express them. George Orwell understood this instinct better than anyone: “If thought corrupts language, language can also corrupt thought.” Control the vocabulary and you control the frame. Ban the word “transition” and you make it harder to discuss the thing itself. Ban “clean energy” and the concept begins to fade from institutional memory — not because it stopped existing, but because the people paid to think about it can no longer name it in their reports.

Sodium-ion batteries do not care what the Department of Energy calls them. Solid-state electrolytes conduct lithium ions regardless of whether a federal employee in Washington is permitted to type “clean” in an email. Physics does not require government approval. But policy does. Investment signals do. Workforce development does. And when the government of the world’s largest economy bans the words that describe the largest industrial transformation since electrification, it is not making a linguistic choice. It is making an economic one.

It is choosing to lose — and making sure nobody on the federal payroll is allowed to say so.

What the United States Is Actually Forfeiting

Let me be specific about the cost of what is happening, because “losing the clean energy race” sounds abstract and the bill is anything but.

Battery manufacturing is the oil industry of the 21st century. The country that controls battery production controls the energy supply chain for electric vehicles, grid storage, consumer electronics, military applications, and — critically for this series — the AI infrastructure buildout that is the single largest new source of electricity demand in a generation.

As of early 2026, China controls approximately 80% of global lithium-ion battery cell manufacturing. It dominates the processing of critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, nickel, graphite — even when those minerals are mined elsewhere. CATL alone commands roughly a third of the global EV battery market. BYD is both the world’s largest EV manufacturer and its second-largest battery producer. The supply chain is overwhelmingly Chinese, and the sodium-ion and solid-state pipelines are being built on the same foundation.

This did not happen by accident. It happened because the Chinese government decided, fifteen years ago, that battery technology was a strategic priority and invested accordingly — in research, manufacturing capacity, workforce training, and standards-setting. It happened because Europe followed with its own battery alliance and industrial strategy. It happened because South Korea and Japan poured resources into next-generation chemistries.

And it happened because the United States — which invented lithium-ion battery technology, which houses the research universities producing the breakthroughs, which has the venture capital ecosystem and the consumer market to scale these technologies faster than anyone — chose, under two separate Trump administrations, to treat clean energy as a culture war issue rather than an industrial strategy.

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, whatever its political liabilities, was the first serious attempt to reverse this trajectory. It offered tax credits for domestic battery manufacturing, incentives for mining and processing critical minerals on American soil, and consumer subsidies for electric vehicles assembled in North America. It was, in effect, an industrial policy designed to compete with China.

The Trump administration is dismantling it. The clean energy tax credits face accelerated expiration in July 2026 under the reconciliation bill. The Treasury Department has issued guidance designed to limit eligibility for wind and solar projects. Billions in appropriated clean energy funding have been frozen or clawed back. The signal to investors, manufacturers, and trading partners is unmistakable: the United States government does not want this industry.

Consider the math of misplaced priorities. The $400 million being spent on the White House ballroom — a gilded event space for 999 guests, funded by the same technology companies whose AI data centres are desperate for clean electricity — would fund a significant battery manufacturing facility. CATL’s sodium-ion production lines, BYD’s gigafactories, Europe’s battery alliance investments — these are the projects that create thousands of permanent manufacturing jobs, generate export revenue, and build strategic industrial capacity. A ballroom creates a nice room. The fact that Apple, Amazon, Google, Meta, and Microsoft are writing cheques for a ballroom instead of being pressed to invest those dollars in domestic battery production tells you everything about where the administration’s priorities actually lie. The companies that need clean energy most are funding a party venue for the man dismantling clean energy policy.

And the rest of the world is listening. Every dollar of investment that flows to a Chinese sodium-ion factory instead of an American one, every solid-state patent filed in Shenzhen instead of Silicon Valley, every grid-scale storage project built in Europe instead of the American Southwest — these are not temporary setbacks that the next administration can reverse with a new executive order. They are structural advantages that compound over time. Supply chains, once established, do not relocate easily. Manufacturing expertise, once lost, takes a generation to rebuild. Market share, once ceded, does not return voluntarily.

The United States is not losing a race. It is withdrawing from the race, tearing up the track, and demanding that the spectators pretend the race isn’t happening — all while the other runners accelerate.

The View from Canada

I write this from Toronto, and I confess to a particular frustration with the battery developments of the past month, because they solve a problem that is especially Canadian.

The single most common objection I hear to electric vehicles in this country — from cab drivers, from neighbours, from people at dinner parties — is cold weather performance. “They don’t work in winter.” This has been, historically, a legitimate concern. Lithium-ion batteries lose capacity in extreme cold. Range drops. Charging slows. In a country where minus 20 is a routine January morning and minus 40 is not unheard of, this matters.

Sodium-ion batteries change that equation. CATL’s Naxtra cells retain 90% capacity at minus 40°C. They charge at minus 30°C. They deliver stable power at minus 50°C. If these numbers hold at commercial scale — and CATL is deploying them commercially right now, not in a laboratory — the cold-weather objection to electrification effectively disappears for most Canadian use cases.

And solid-state batteries, with their wider operating temperature range and higher energy density, push the envelope further still. Stellantis reports that its Factorial-supplied solid-state cells operate from minus 30°C to plus 45°C. As the technology matures and temperature ranges expand, the viability of electrified transport in northern climates becomes not a question of if but when.

Canada should be paying very close attention to these developments. We have the critical minerals. We have the cold-weather testing environment. We have the automotive manufacturing base in Ontario. We have a trade relationship with the United States that, however battered, still moves more goods across the border than any bilateral trade corridor on Earth. And we have every incentive to position ourselves as a battery supply chain partner for whichever market — American, European, or Asian — is willing to invest in the technology.

What we should NOT do is follow the American example. What we should NOT do is pretend that batteries made from salt don’t work at minus 40 because acknowledging them would inconvenience an ideology. What we should NOT do is hand out coal trophies while the rest of the world hands out car keys.

The Bottom Line

In January, when I wrote Essay #15, I described energy storage as the missing link in the clean energy revolution. One month later, the links are being forged at a pace that exceeds even the most optimistic scenarios.

Sodium-ion batteries have gone from laboratory promise to the first mass-production passenger vehicle in commercial deployment. Solid-state batteries have gone from “maybe by 2030” to production motorcycles on the road in Q1 2026, with automotive-grade cells validated at energy densities that would have seemed fantastical five years ago. Grid-scale storage has surpassed pumped hydro — a century-old technology — for the first time in history. The research pipeline is delivering breakthroughs in charging speed, crack resistance, temperature tolerance, and cost reduction on a near-weekly basis.

And the United States — the country whose scientists produced many of these breakthroughs, whose universities trained the researchers, whose venture capital funded the startups — is choosing this precise moment to rescind its climate regulations, ban the words “clean energy” from government communications, order the military to buy coal, and give the president a trophy for it.

The bad guys are not winning. The bad guys are choosing to lose — and they are choosing to take 330 million Americans with them. The rest of the world is not waiting. China is not waiting. Europe is not waiting. South Korea, Japan, India — none of them are waiting. They are building the industries of the 21st century while the United States builds monuments to the 19th — and, in the case of the White House ballroom, a gilded monument to one man’s vanity on the rubble of the East Wing.

There is a word for what the Trump administration is doing to America’s energy future. It is not “dominance.” It is not “independence.” It is not even “deregulation,” though that is the label they prefer.

The word is “forfeiture.” And the receipts are everywhere — a coal trophy inscribed “Undisputed Champion,” a $400 million ballroom where the East Wing used to stand, and construction cranes visible from Pennsylvania Avenue while the rest of the world builds battery factories visible from orbit.

The batteries do not care. The batteries are getting built. The only question is where — and the United States, under the leadership of a man who thinks wind turbines cause cancer, that coal is beautiful, and that what the White House really needed was a bigger ballroom, has answered that question for the rest of the world.

Build them somewhere else. We’re busy redecorating.
