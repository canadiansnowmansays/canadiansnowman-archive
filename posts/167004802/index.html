Why Trump Is Wrong About Canada's Digital Tax (And Why It's Actually Fair)

Big Tech has been getting away with paying almost nothing in taxes for years

June 27, 2025

Trump just ended all trade talks with Canada over their 3% digital services tax, calling it "egregious." But the real story? Big Tech has been getting away with paying almost nothing in taxes for years. Here's why Canada is right.

The Real Story Trump Doesn't Want You to Know

Trump's latest tantrum about Canada's digital services tax is classic misdirection. While he's screaming about "unfair" treatment of American tech companies, these same companies have been systematically avoiding billions in taxes for over a decade.

The numbers don't lie:

  • Facebook paid just 2.6% tax on its non-US profits in 2016, while paying 30.9% in the US

  • Adobe made 86% of its profits outside the US and paid only 7.2% tax on them

  • Amazon's effective federal tax rate in 2021? Just 6.1% despite $35.1 billion in earnings

  • Google paid 7.6% tax outside the US versus 28.7% domestically

Meanwhile, Amazon pays 11 times less corporation tax than traditional bookstores in the UK. If that's not rigged, what is?

What Canada's Tax Actually Does

Canada's Digital Services Tax is simple: if you're a massive tech company making over $15 million from Canadian users, you pay 3% on that revenue. That's it.

This isn't some crazy new concept. France, the UK, Italy, and Spain already have these taxes. Fifteen of 37 OECD countries have implemented or proposed similar measures. Canada is just catching up.

The "Fairness" Trump Claims to Care About

Let's talk about what's actually unfair:

Traditional businesses pay their full tax rate where they operate. They can't magically shift their profits to Ireland or Singapore to avoid taxes.

Tech giants operate everywhere but pay taxes nowhere. They use:

  • Our infrastructure and internet networks

  • Our educated workforce

  • Our legal systems that protect their intellectual property

  • Our consumer protection laws that build trust

  • Our stable currencies and financial systems

Then they pay almost nothing back.

The Scale of This Tax Dodge

This isn't small money we're talking about:

  • Half of all large US corporations paid zero federal taxes from 2014-2018

  • Tech companies pay an average of just 9.6% on foreign profits

  • The EU's proposed digital tax would raise €5 billion annually

  • Canada's tax could generate $2.3 billion from US companies alone

That's billions in lost revenue that could fund healthcare, education, and infrastructure while these companies get richer by the day.

Why the "Discrimination" Argument Is Nonsense

Trump claims Canada is "discriminating" against American companies. This is backwards.

The discrimination is in the current system that lets digital companies avoid taxes while traditional businesses can't. Canada's tax applies to any company over the threshold—it just happens that most of them are American because the US dominates digital services.

If American companies don't want to pay Canadian taxes on Canadian revenue, they're free to stop serving Canadian customers. But they won't, because they're making too much money.

What About "Double Taxation"?

Critics worry about companies being taxed twice. But here's what's actually happening:

Current reality: Companies like Nvidia paid zero net taxes to the US Treasury from 2016-2022 despite massive profits.

With DSTs: Companies pay a minimum amount where they actually do business, with credits available to prevent genuine double taxation.

The choice isn't between fair taxation and double taxation—it's between some taxation and zero taxation.

Why International "Solutions" Have Failed

Trump supporters love to say "wait for international agreements" instead of acting unilaterally. But:

  • The OECD has been negotiating for 6+ years with no real progress

  • Trump himself withdrew the US from these negotiations in January 2025

  • Developing countries oppose the OECD deal because it would cost them billions in lost revenue

Waiting for Trump to voluntarily agree to tax his Big Tech donors more? Good luck with that!

The Real Numbers on Corporate Tax Avoidance

From 2014-2018, profitable large corporations saw their effective tax rates fall from 16% to 9%. Some specific examples:

  • 19 Fortune 100 companies paid single-digit tax rates or nothing at all in 2021

  • Microsoft, Amazon, ExxonMobil, JPMorgan Chase, and others all paid less than 10%

  • This while the official corporate tax rate was 21%

Meanwhile, regular Americans pay their full tax rate with no fancy offshore schemes available.

What This Means for Regular People

When massive corporations don't pay their fair share:

  • Your taxes have to be higher to fund the same services

  • Small businesses can't compete with tax-dodging giants

  • Public services get underfunded while billionaires get richer

  • Infrastructure that everyone depends on deteriorates

Digital services taxes help fix this by making sure the companies that profit most from our society actually contribute to it.

The Trump Hypocrisy

The same Trump who claims to support American workers is now threatening trade wars to protect the tax avoidance schemes of America's richest companies.

Think about it: He's willing to harm trade relationships and hurt working families to ensure that tech billionaires can keep avoiding taxes. Does that sound like "America First" to you?

Canada Is Right to Stand Firm

Canada waited years for international cooperation that never came. They gave the OECD process a chance. They held off implementing their tax hoping for a global solution.

But at some point, you stop being the sucker in the room.

Every day Canada waits is another day that Google, Amazon, Facebook, and others extract wealth from Canadian users while contributing almost nothing to the Canadian treasury. That's not sustainable, and it's not fair.

What Other Countries Should Do

Canada isn't alone in this fight. Countries around the world are waking up to the reality that:

  1. Big Tech won't voluntarily pay fair taxes

  2. International negotiations are stalled by US obstruction

  3. Unilateral action is the only option left

More countries should follow Canada's lead and implement their own digital services taxes. The alternative is letting tech giants continue their decade-long tax holiday while everyone else pays the bills.

The Bottom Line

Trump can throw all the tantrums he wants. He can threaten trade wars and tariffs. But he can't change the basic math:

American tech companies are undertaxed everywhere except America.

Canada's 3% digital services tax doesn't fix this completely, but it's a start. It's a reasonable response to an unreasonable situation. And it's a lot fairer than the current system where tech billionaires pay lower effective tax rates than their own employees.

If Trump really cared about fairness, he'd be asking why American companies pay 45% tax at home but under 10% everywhere else. Instead, he's protecting a system that lets his wealthiest donors avoid the taxes that fund the military, infrastructure, and education systems that made their success possible.

Canada is fighting for tax fairness. Trump is fighting for tax avoidance.

The choice of which side you're on should be pretty clear. #elbowsup

UPDATE: Canada Just Blinked (And That's Exactly The Problem)

June 30, 2025 - Just hours after I published this piece

Well, that didn't take long.

Canada announced last night that they're rescinding their Digital Services Tax to "advance broader trade negotiations" with Trump. Finance Minister François-Philippe Champagne says they'll resume negotiations "with a view towards agreeing on a deal by July 21, 2025."

This is exactly why Big Tech keeps winning.

Think about what just happened here: Trump threw a tantrum, threatened tariffs, and within 48 hours, Canada folded. Not because the policy was wrong. Not because the tax was unfair. But because one man's Twitter meltdown was enough to scare them off.

The Message This Sends

Canada's capitulation sends a crystal-clear message to every other country considering digital services taxes: Don't bother. Trump will just threaten you until you back down.

This is exactly how we got into this mess in the first place. Countries keep waiting for "the right time" or "international cooperation" while tech giants laugh all the way to their tax-haven banks.

What "Negotiations" Really Mean

Let's be honest about what these "broader trade negotiations" will look like:

  • Trump's position: No digital taxes, ever, period

  • Canada's position: Please don't hurt us with tariffs

  • Big Tech's position: [counting money while this plays out]

The July 21st deadline? That's just kicking the can down the road while Facebook, Google, Amazon, and the rest continue their tax holiday.

The Real Winners and Losers

Winners:

  • Big Tech (obviously)

  • Tax avoidance schemes

  • The broken international tax system

Losers:

  • Canadian taxpayers who'll keep subsidizing tech infrastructure while getting nothing back

  • Every other country that might have followed Canada's lead

  • The basic principle that profitable companies should pay their fair share

Why This Matters Beyond Canada

Canada wasn't just implementing a tax policy—they were testing whether individual countries could stand up to US tech giants. The answer, apparently, is no.

This sets a precedent that will echo around the world. Why would France, the UK, or anyone else stick their necks out when Canada—America's closest ally—couldn't handle the pressure?

The Bigger Picture

Remember, this is the same Trump who claims to put "America First" but just used the full weight of the US government to protect tax avoidance schemes that cost the US Treasury billions.

Think about that irony: American companies pay 45% tax in America but 2-7% everywhere else, and Trump is fighting to keep it that way. How exactly does that help American workers or the American tax base?

What Should Happen Next

Other countries watching this should learn the opposite lesson Canada did. Don't negotiate with economic hostage-takers. France, the UK, Italy, and Spain still have their digital services taxes in place. They should strengthen them, not weaken them.

Because here's the thing: Trump can't tariff everyone. The more countries that stand together on this, the less credible his threats become.

The Bottom Line

Canada's reversal proves everything I wrote in the original piece. The system is rigged in favor of tax-avoiding tech giants, and they'll use every tool—including presidential tantrums—to keep it that way.

The real test isn't whether digital services taxes are fair (they obviously are). It's whether democratic governments have the backbone to implement fair policies when billionaires start screaming.

Canada just failed that test. Let's hope other countries do better.

The fight for tax fairness continues—just not in Canada, apparently.
