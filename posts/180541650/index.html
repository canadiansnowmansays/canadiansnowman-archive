Part 5

Trump’s First Term: The Loyalty Test

December 23, 2025

Roger Stone (left), Paul Manafort (center), and Charles Kushner

Donald Trump’s first term (2017-2021) established patterns that would explode into systematic corruption in his second. While the numbers were relatively modest—144 pardons and 94 commutations—the whom and why revealed something disturbing: clemency as a tool for protecting those who protected him.

Trump pardoned his campaign chairman, his political advisor, his national security advisor, and his son-in-law’s father—all convicted in investigations touching his administration. He commuted the sentence of a close associate days before he was to report to prison. He bypassed normal Justice Department processes. And he sent an unmistakable message: Stay loyal, stay silent, and you’ll be protected.

This wasn’t criminal justice reform. It was obstruction through clemency.

The Numbers in Context

Trump’s first term: 144 pardons, 94 commutations = 238 total clemency actions over four years.

This is comparable to Bush’s 200 over eight years. On volume alone, Trump’s first term wasn’t remarkable. But volume isn’t the issue—connection is.

Of Bush’s 200 clemency actions, only one (Libby) involved someone connected to his administration, and Bush refused to grant the full pardon Cheney wanted.

Of Obama’s 1,927 clemency actions, only 2-3 involved anyone with connections to his administration, and none involved people who could implicate Obama personally.

Of Biden’s 4,245 clemency actions, the vast majority went to strangers—but six went to family members.

Trump’s first term? At least 15-20 of his 238 clemency actions went to political allies, campaign associates, or people connected to investigations of his conduct.

That ratio tells the story.

The Russia Investigation Pardons

The most significant pattern in Trump’s first-term clemency: systematic protection of figures convicted in Special Counsel Robert Mueller’s investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election and possible coordination with the Trump campaign.

Paul Manafort: The Campaign Chairman

The Crimes: Paul Manafort served as Trump’s campaign chairman during a critical period in 2016 before being ousted over his ties to Ukraine. Manafort was convicted in August 2018 on eight counts including bank fraud and tax fraud, and later pleaded guilty to conspiracy charges in a separate case.

Prosecutors proved Manafort had:

  • Hidden millions earned working for pro-Russian Ukrainian politicians

  • Failed to report foreign bank accounts

  • Committed bank fraud to maintain his lavish lifestyle

  • Engaged in witness tampering

He was sentenced to more than seven years in prison—the toughest sentence of any Trump associate in the Mueller probe.

The Pardon: On December 23, 2020—just weeks before leaving office—Trump granted Manafort a full pardon.

The White House statement claimed: “As a result of blatant prosecutorial overreach, Mr. Manafort has endured years of unfair treatment and is one of the most prominent victims of what has been revealed to be perhaps the greatest witch hunt in American history.”

Manafort had been released to home confinement in May 2020 due to COVID concerns. The pardon erased his conviction entirely.

Applying Our Framework:

  1. Connection: Extremely strong—Trump’s campaign chairman during crucial 2016 period

  2. Nature of Crime: Financial fraud, tax evasion, witness tampering, conspiracy

  3. Victim Impact: Taxpayers defrauded, witnesses intimidated, justice system obstructed

  4. Stage: After serving less than two years of 7+ year sentence

  5. Process: Presidential initiative, bypassing DOJ review

  6. Timing: Final weeks of presidency, after losing 2020 election

  7. Justification: Attack on prosecution as “witch hunt”—no acknowledgment of jury verdict

The Message: Manafort’s pardon sent a clear signal: if you’re convicted in an investigation touching Trump, and you don’t cooperate against him, you’ll be protected. Notably, Manafort’s deputy Rick Gates, who extensively cooperated with prosecutors, received no pardon.

Roger Stone: The Political Advisor

The Crimes: Roger Stone, Trump’s longtime political advisor and confidant, was convicted in November 2019 on seven felony counts:

  • Obstruction of justice

  • Five counts of making false statements to Congress

  • Witness tampering

Stone lied to Congress about his efforts to contact WikiLeaks regarding emails stolen from Hillary Clinton’s campaign. He threatened a witness to prevent truthful testimony, telling him to “do a Frank Pentangeli” (a Godfather reference to lying under oath).

Stone was sentenced to 40 months in prison.

The Commutation and Pardon: In July 2020—just days before Stone was to report to prison—Trump commuted his sentence. Then in December 2020, Trump granted Stone a full pardon.

The White House claimed Stone was a “victim of the Russia Hoax” and faced “juror misconduct” and “potential political bias.”

Applying Our Framework:

  1. Connection: Extremely strong—longtime Trump political advisor and friend

  2. Nature of Crime: Lying to Congress, obstruction, witness tampering in investigation of Trump campaign

  3. Victim Impact: Congressional oversight obstructed, witness intimidated, justice system corrupted

  4. Stage: Commutation before prison, full pardon months later

  5. Process: Presidential initiative

  6. Timing: Commutation days before prison report date; pardon in final weeks of presidency

  7. Justification: Attack on prosecution, jury, and investigation

The Pattern: Like Manafort, Stone was convicted of obstructing investigations into Trump’s campaign. Like Manafort, Stone refused to cooperate against Trump. And like Manafort, Stone was pardoned.

Michael Flynn: The National Security Advisor

The Crime: Michael Flynn served as Trump’s first National Security Advisor for just 24 days before resigning. Flynn pleaded guilty in December 2017 to lying to the FBI about his contacts with Russian Ambassador Sergey Kislyak during the transition.

Flynn admitted lying about asking Kislyak to refrain from escalating tensions in response to Obama administration sanctions.

The Pardon: In November 2020, Trump granted Flynn a full pardon.

The White House statement called Flynn an “innocent man” who had been “targeted” in a “partisan witch hunt.”

The Complication: Flynn’s case was unusual because he had pleaded guilty—twice—and cooperated extensively with Mueller’s investigation. However, Flynn later sought to withdraw his guilty plea, hired new lawyers, and became a vocal Trump supporter and conspiracy theorist.

The pardon came after the Justice Department, under Attorney General William Barr, had moved to dismiss the case—a move that Judge Emmet Sullivan refused to automatically grant, leading to protracted legal battles.

Applying Our Framework:

  1. Connection: Very strong—National Security Advisor, though only briefly

  2. Nature of Crime: Lying to FBI about contacts with Russian officials

  3. Victim Impact: FBI investigation obstructed, though Flynn eventually cooperated

  4. Stage: After guilty plea but before sentencing completed

  5. Process: Presidential initiative

  6. Timing: Shortly after Trump lost 2020 election

  7. Justification: Claim of innocence despite Flynn’s own guilty pleas

The Distinction: Flynn is a more complicated case than Manafort or Stone because he initially cooperated. But his later recantation, attacks on the prosecution, and embrace of Trump’s narrative transformed him into a Trump ally worthy of protection.

George Papadopoulos: The Campaign Advisor

Trump also pardoned George Papadopoulos, a former campaign advisor who pleaded guilty to lying to FBI investigators about his contacts with Russian intermediaries. Papadopoulos served 12 days in prison.

The pardon came in December 2020, in the same wave as Manafort and Stone.

The Russia Investigation Pattern

Four people convicted in Mueller’s investigation received clemency from Trump:

  • Paul Manafort (full pardon)

  • Roger Stone (commutation then full pardon)

  • Michael Flynn (full pardon)

  • George Papadopoulos (full pardon)

Two people convicted in Mueller’s investigation who cooperated extensively against Trump associates did NOT receive pardons:

  • Rick Gates (Manafort’s deputy)

  • Michael Cohen (Trump’s personal lawyer)

The message was explicit: Loyalty is rewarded. Cooperation is punished.

This is obstruction through clemency. Trump used the pardon power to protect those who protected him, and to signal to others that maintaining loyalty would bring protection.

Charles Kushner: The Family Connection

In May 2025, the U.S. Senate confirmed Charles Kushner as the U.S. ambassador to France

Perhaps no first-term pardon better exemplifies Trump’s approach than Charles Kushner.

The Crimes: Charles Kushner, father of Trump’s son-in-law and senior advisor Jared Kushner, pleaded guilty in 2005 to 18 counts including:

  • Tax evasion

  • Witness tampering

  • Making illegal campaign contributions

The witness tampering was particularly egregious: Charles Kushner hired a prostitute to seduce his brother-in-law (whose sister was cooperating with prosecutors), recorded the encounter, and sent the tape to his sister to intimidate her into silence.

Kushner served 14 months of a two-year sentence and was released in 2006. The prosecution was led by then-U.S. Attorney Chris Christie.

The Pardon: On December 23, 2020—the same day as Manafort’s pardon—Trump granted Charles Kushner a full pardon.

The White House statement emphasized Kushner’s philanthropy and argued that “this record of reform and charity overshadows Mr. Kushner’s conviction.”

Applying Our Framework:

  1. Connection: Very strong—father of son-in-law who served as senior White House advisor

  2. Nature of Crime: Tax evasion, witness tampering (hiring prostitute to blackmail family member), illegal campaign contributions

  3. Victim Impact: Taxpayers, family members intimidated, campaign finance system corrupted

  4. Stage: 14 years after release from prison

  5. Process: Presidential initiative

  6. Timing: Final weeks of presidency

  7. Justification: Rehabilitation and philanthropy

The Significance: The Kushner pardon is notable because the crime was so personally sordid—using a prostitute and hidden camera to blackmail your own family member. It’s also notable because Kushner had long since completed his sentence, suggesting the pardon was about restoring his reputation and civil rights rather than shortening punishment.

But most significantly: Trump later appointed Charles Kushner as U.S. Ambassador to France in his second term. The pardon wasn’t just forgiveness—it was restoration to public service and diplomatic posting.

Other Notable First-Term Pardons

Blackwater Contractors: Trump pardoned four Blackwater security contractors convicted in the 2007 Nisour Square massacre in Baghdad that killed 17 Iraqi civilians. The pardons were widely condemned by military officials and sparked international outrage. They established Trump’s willingness to pardon those convicted of war crimes.

Republican Politicians: Trump pardoned several Republican lawmakers:

  • Duncan Hunter (California congressman, campaign finance violations)

  • Chris Collins (New York congressman, insider trading)

Both had been Trump supporters. Both received pardons after pleading guilty.

Celebrity-Driven Pardons: Trump granted several pardons championed by celebrities:

  • Alice Marie Johnson (life sentence for non-violent drug offense, advocated by Kim Kardashian)—a legitimate use of clemency

  • Various others supported by celebrity advocates

These celebrity pardons were often legitimate—Johnson’s case was genuinely unjust. But they established the precedent that access to Trump (through celebrities, political allies, or personal connections) was the path to clemency, not the traditional Justice Department review process.

What Trump’s First Term Reveals

Several disturbing patterns emerged:

1. Systematic Protection of Russia Investigation Figures: Four convicted associates pardoned, while cooperators received nothing. This is obstruction through clemency—using presidential power to protect those who refuse to implicate the president.

2. Family Favoritism: Charles Kushner’s pardon for his son-in-law’s father, followed by appointment as ambassador.

3. Loyalty Rewards: Republican politicians who supported Trump received pardons. Those who didn’t, didn’t.

4. Process Corruption: Trump largely bypassed the Office of the Pardon Attorney, making decisions based on personal relationships, political loyalty, and celebrity advocacy rather than DOJ review.

5. No Proportionality Analysis: Unlike Obama’s systematic review of drug sentences for proportionality, Trump’s pardons followed no coherent policy. The common thread was connection to Trump, not injustice of sentence.

6. Aggressive Use of Clemency Power: Trump pardoned people convicted of obstructing investigations into his own conduct—using clemency not just to show mercy but to obstruct accountability.

Comparing Trump’s First Term

Let’s apply our framework:

Versus Bush:

  • Bush: 1 controversial commutation (Libby), refused full pardon despite VP pressure

  • Trump: Multiple pardons for campaign associates, embraced rather than resisted

Versus Obama:

  • Obama: 1,927 clemency actions, ~2 with any connection to administration

  • Trump: 238 clemency actions, 15-20+ with direct connections

Versus Biden:

  • Biden: Massive clemency for strangers + indefensible family pardons

  • Trump: Less systematic reform but more protection of political associates

The Key Distinction: Biden’s family pardons were nepotism—protecting his own relatives. Trump’s first-term pardons were obstruction—protecting people who could implicate him and rewarding those who stayed loyal.

Both are corrupt, but Trump’s corruption serves a different function: maintaining omertà (code of silence) among associates.

The Warning Signs

Trump’s first term established several precedents that would explode in his second:

1. Clemency as Loyalty Reward: The Russia investigation pardons signaled that protecting Trump brings protection in return.

2. Process Corruption: Bypassing DOJ review became standard, opening door to lobbying and cash-for-clemency.

3. No Victim Consideration: Pardons erased accountability without regard for victims.

4. Family Access: The Kushner pardon showed family connections provide clemency access.

5. Messaging Through Clemency: Trump used pardons to attack investigations, prosecutors, and the justice system itself.

These patterns, relatively contained in the first term (238 total clemency actions), would metastasize into systematic corruption in the second term.

The question wasn’t whether Trump would continue these patterns—it was how far they would go.
