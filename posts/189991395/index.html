The Contribution Index

Costa Rica

Part VII: The Nation That Chose to Help

March 31, 2026

Explore the Unique Rainforests of Costa Rica / Javier Oreamuno

Top Performer #7

Seven essays into this countdown, we have moved through nations with strong institutions, robust economies, and impressive per-capita metrics. The top performers profiled so far each bring considerable resources to their contribution scores. They are wealthy, well-educated, well-governed.

Costa Rica is none of these things — at least not by the standards of the nations ranked around it. Its GDP per capita is roughly one-fifth that of the country directly above it on this list. Its total economy is smaller than that of a single European metropolitan area. It has no military, no nuclear deterrent, no permanent seat on any global council.

And yet it outperforms every nation we have profiled so far in this countdown.

This essay is, I believe, the most important one in this series. Not because Costa Rica’s score is the highest — it is not. The nations still ahead of it on the countdown all outperform it. But because Costa Rica is the proof of concept for the entire Contribution Index framework. It is the living, breathing demonstration that contribution is a choice, not a consequence of wealth.

I called it the Costa Rica Principle in the Introduction. Now let me show you why.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 38 out of 50

On December 1, 1948, President José Figueres Ferrer stood in the courtyard of the Cuartel Bellavista — the military headquarters in San José — and took a sledgehammer to the wall. Then he abolished the army.

Not reduced it. Not reformed it. Abolished it entirely. Costa Rica redirected its military budget to education, healthcare, and environmental protection, and it has not maintained a standing military force since. In a hemisphere defined by military coups, authoritarian strongmen, and Cold War proxy conflicts, Costa Rica simply opted out.

Life expectancy stands at approximately 80 years — higher than the United States, which spends roughly four times as much per capita on healthcare. Costa Rica achieved universal healthcare coverage through its Caja Costarricense de Seguro Social, established in 1941. The system is imperfect — wait times can be long, and the wealthy sometimes opt for private care — but the foundational principle that every person deserves access to medical treatment regardless of ability to pay has produced outcomes that embarrass far wealthier nations.

Education scores a 7. Costa Rica invests roughly 7 percent of its GDP in education — among the highest rates in the world. Literacy rates exceed 97 percent.

Democratic governance earns an 8. While much of Central and South America cycled through authoritarian regimes, Costa Rica maintained continuous democratic governance with regular peaceful transfers of power, a functioning independent judiciary, and press freedoms that rank among the strongest in Latin America. The trajectory is what matters: in a region trending toward democratic erosion, Costa Rica is trending toward consolidation.

The scores are not perfect. Poverty remains a persistent challenge — roughly 20 percent of the population lives below the national poverty line. Gender equality has improved markedly, with Costa Rica becoming a regional leader on LGBTQ+ rights, but economic participation by women still lags behind the legal frameworks. These are the scores of a middle-income nation doing extraordinarily well with limited resources.

Environmental Stewardship: 44 out of 50

Costa Rica uses a mix of hydro, wind and geothermal energy to power the homes of its 4.9 million people. Photograph: Ezequiel Becerra/AFP/Getty Images

Ninety-eight percent of Costa Rica’s electricity comes from renewable sources. This is not an aspiration or a target for 2050. It is the current reality. Costa Rica has run its entire electrical grid on renewable energy for stretches exceeding 300 consecutive days. The only score available is a 10.

Forest cover has roughly doubled from its low point, rising from approximately 21 percent in 1987 to over 50 percent today, driven by a pioneering Payments for Environmental Services programme. Costa Rica is one of the only tropical nations in the world to have reversed deforestation at scale.

Costa Rica contains roughly 6 percent of the world’s biodiversity on just 0.03 percent of the Earth’s surface. Over 25 percent of its territory is under active environmental protection — not “paper park” protection but actively managed, patrolled, and integrated into a national ecotourism economy.

Tico Times / Ileana Fernandez

The 44 out of 50 environmental score is the highest on the entire top performers list, exceeding every Nordic nation. It demonstrates that environmental excellence does not require enormous wealth. It requires political will, long-term planning, and the courage to make choices that prioritize the planet over short-term economic extraction.

Systemic Behaviour: 35 out of 50

The arms export score is essentially a freebie — Costa Rica has no military and therefore no arms industry. It contributes zero to the global arms trade, zero to international conflict escalation, and zero to the militarisation that the Global Peace Index documents as accelerating worldwide.

Adherence to international law earns an 8. Costa Rica is home to the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, hosts the University for Peace established by the United Nations, and has used its moral authority to punch well above its weight in international forums.

The scores dip on refugee and humanitarian contribution (6) and on investment in developing nations (5) — not because Costa Rica is unwilling, but because it lacks the resources to make large-scale contributions. Costa Rica has absorbed significant Nicaraguan migration in recent years — measured against its GDP and population, that effort is substantial.

The Neighbour Test: 36 out of 50

Costa Rica’s Neighbour Test scores illuminate something important about the framework: a nation with no military, no economic leverage, and no geopolitical weight can still score strongly on global citizenship through sheer consistency of disposition.

Multilateral orientation earns an 8. Treatment of allies earns an 8 — Costa Rica does not exploit its neighbours, weaponize trade, or use its strategic position to extract concessions. In a region where these behaviours are routine, Costa Rica’s restraint is itself a contribution.

Consistency of values versus interests earns an 8 — the highest score for this parameter among all twenty-one nations assessed. Costa Rica abolished its army and has maintained that choice for nearly eighty years. It committed to renewable energy and achieved 98 percent. It established universal healthcare and sustained it through economic pressures. The gap between stated values and observed behaviour is, in Costa Rica, remarkably narrow. This is what consistency looks like.

Crisis response and stewardship score lower — 6 each — reflecting the capacity constraints of a small, middle-income nation. The disposition is limited by the resources, not by the will.

The Editorial Conclusion

Costa Rica scores 153 out of 200 — seventh in the top performers. No nation on this entire list has a wider gap between its resources and its achievement in the positive direction.

In 1948, José Figueres Ferrer abolished Costa Rica’s army with a sledgehammer and built a society dedicated to human wellbeing and environmental stewardship. Every year since, the nation has proven that contribution is not a luxury that only wealthy nations can afford. It is a choice available to everyone. The question is not whether you can afford to help. The question is whether you choose to.

As we continue climbing, keep Costa Rica in mind. And as we continue descending through the underperformer countdown, keep it in mind for a different reason. When you encounter nations with trillion-dollar economies that cannot provide universal healthcare — remember that a country of five million people with no army figured it out decades ago.

They just chose to.
