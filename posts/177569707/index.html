Now This is Fake News

Part 5: Memos and Missiles: What Gets Announced When Nobody’s Watching

October 30, 2025

October 30, 2025

Two bombshells have dropped this month. You might have missed them.

The first came via leaked memo: The Pentagon is building a “quick reaction force” of 23,500 National Guard troops trained in crowd control and civil disturbance, ready to deploy to U.S. cities by January 1, 2026 Task & PurposeThe Hill.

The second came via Truth Social: President Trump announced the United States will resume nuclear weapons testing “on an equal basis” with Russia and China CNN, ending a 33-year moratorium.

No Pentagon briefing. No press conference. No questions asked and damn, we wish it was fake news.

Welcome to military policy in the post-press era, where decisions that could reshape America get the same treatment as your uncle’s Facebook rants.

The Quick Reaction Force Nobody Questioned

Let’s start with what we know from the October 8 memo, signed by National Guard Bureau Director of Operations Maj. Gen. Ronald Burkett.

All 50 states, Puerto Rico and Guam will have their own quick reaction force, or QRF. Most states will have 500 troops assigned to these units, except for those with smaller populations like Delaware, which will have 250 troops in its QRF, Alaska with 350, and Guam with 100 troops Task & Purpose.

The training is specific: courses on de-escalation of force techniques, crowd control, handheld radio communications, proper use of body shields, batons and tasers, pepper spray, and public safety Task & Purpose. You know, the kind of skills every democracy needs 23,500 soldiers to master. For peace.

Remember how well the last deployment went? Trump mobilized the D.C. National Guard to the nation’s capital claiming the city’s crime was out of control, despite data showing that rates had dropped over the past two years. In their more than two months in the city, guard members have largely been helping with “beautification” efforts such as picking up trash and laying mulch The Hill.

That’s right. Military deployment to fight imaginary crime ended with soldiers doing landscaping. Your tax dollars at work, folks.

Here’s what makes this unprecedented. A Guard member told Task & Purpose: “This is different because we’re essentially establishing a unit for space to respond to civilian activities. We are ready to go when we’re called upon. We’re not asked to stand up an entire unit ready to quell dissent at any moment” Task & PurposeThe Hill.

Until now, apparently. stand up an entire unit ready to quell dissent at any moment” Task & PurposeThe Hill.

Questions That Would Have Been Asked (If Anyone Was There)

At a functioning Pentagon press briefing, reporters would have demanded answers:

Legal Authority: Under what statute can the federal government create permanent crowd control forces? The Posse Comitatus Act generally prohibits military forces from domestic law enforcement. The Act originally applied only to the United States Army, but subsequent amendments expanded its scope to the United States Air Force and, in 2021, to cover the United States Navy, Marine Corps, and Space Force Brennan Center for Justice.

Deployment Triggers: Who decides when these forces deploy? What constitutes a situation requiring military crowd control? The rationale for prohibiting federal armed forces from enforcing civilian law is increasingly applicable to National Guard forces even when they are operating entirely under state control CNAS.

State Sovereignty: Can these forces be deployed against a governor’s wishes? A federal judge already ruled that Trump’s deployment of the National Guard to Los Angeles violated federal laws, specifically the Posse Comitatus Act CalMatters.

Historical Precedent: The creation of national QRFs is not unprecedented, but they usually occur after serious emergencies such as when Hurricane Katrina hit New Orleans in 2005 The Hill. Why create them now, absent any emergency?

But hey, why let little things like legal authority or historical precedent get in the way of creating your own personal army?

There was no briefing. No questions. Just a leaked memo and silence—which is apparently how we do military policy now.

Nuclear Testing Via Truth Social

The nuclear announcement was even more brazen. Trump made the announcement on social media minutes before he met with Chinese leader Xi Jinping on Thursday in South Korea NPR.

“Because of other countries’ testing programs, I have instructed the Department of War to start testing our Nuclear Weapons on an equal basis. That process will begin immediately Fox News,” the President wrote.

Think about that. A decision to end 33 years of nuclear testing moratorium—announced on social media. The White House did not immediately respond to questions seeking more details NPR. Pentagon officials didn’t immediately respond to questions about the announcement NPR.

The Questions Nobody’s Asking

Donald, who the hell in your administration are you listening to these days who’s telling you it’s a good idea to test nuclear weapons? We know it’s not the perennial hawk John Bolton since word is you two aren’t on speaking terms. So who is it?

Wait a minute—there’s no press corps at the Pentagon to ask.

The American people do NOT want you to drag them down some hawkish rabbit hole to forever be buried beneath the excrement you so vividly illustrated in your recent AI video. If we wanted to wander down a rabbit hole, we’d re-read Alice in Wonderland.

But here we are, watching nuclear policy get made on Truth Social with all the gravitas of a midnight burger order.

Beyond the absurdity of the announcement method, the actual substance raises questions that should be keeping every American up at night—questions that would dominate any normal Pentagon briefing:

Strategic Rationale: It was unclear what he was referring to, but it evoked Cold War-era escalations NPR. Russia hasn’t tested nuclear weapons since 1990. China last tested nuclear weapons in 1996 Al Jazeera. So why now?

Treaty Obligations: The Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty, which was adopted in 1996 and bans all nuclear explosions anywhere in the world, was signed by President Bill Clinton but never ratified by the Senate NPR. What are the international legal implications?

Practical Reality: Daryl Kimball, the executive director of the Arms Control Association, said the U.S. has no reason to resume nuclear explosive testing and it would take at least 36 months to resume testing at the former test site in Nevada NPR.

Global Consequences: Tilman Ruff, a board member of International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War, wrote that a resumption of US nuclear weapon testing “would be extremely dangerous for humanity. It would almost inevitably be followed by tit-for-tat reciprocal announcements by other nuclear-armed states” Common Dreams.

The Kremlin warned Thursday that it would “act accordingly” if a Cold War-era moratorium on nuclear weapons’ testing was broken CNBC.

The Pattern Becomes Clear

When Secretary Hegseth was asked about the QRF plans, he said he would not comment on “particulars,” but said the U.S. has “multiple layers of National Guard response forces” Task & Purpose.

That’s it. That’s the extent of public accountability for creating a 23,500-person domestic crowd control force. “Multiple layers” sounds so much nicer than “we’re training an army to deploy against American cities,” doesn’t it?

Meanwhile, during a trip to Japan earlier this week, Trump told U.S. troops he was prepared to send “more than the National Guard” into U.S. cities if needed U.S. News & World Report.

More than the National Guard? What does that mean? Active duty military? Under what authority?

These are the questions a Pentagon press corps would ask. Would demand answers to. Would follow up on, day after day, until the American people understood what their military was being ordered to do.

Beyond Truth Social

The nuclear testing announcement is particularly striking for what it reveals about decision-making in the absence of press scrutiny. Beatrice Fihn, former executive director of the International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons, warned that jumpstarting nuke tests would be “incredibly stupid” Common Dreams.

But who’s there to ask why? To demand the strategic assessment? To question the timing, coming moments before meeting Xi Jinping? To investigate whether military leadership was consulted?

Trump claimed that China was rapidly building its nuclear stockpile, citing that as among the reasons to justify his decision Al Jazeera. But China’s count was 410 nuclear weapons in 2023, while the US had 3,708 Al Jazeera. Who’s there to fact-check?

“Trump mentioned in his statement that other countries were allegedly testing nuclear weapons. Until now, we were not aware that anyone was testing anything,” Kremlin Spokesperson Dmitry Peskov told journalists CNBC.

The Constitutional Questions

Both announcements raise profound constitutional issues that would normally dominate Pentagon press briefings for weeks. You remember those, right? Back when we pretended to care about checks and balances?

For the QRF: Sending one state’s National Guard into another state without the consent of BOTH governors would violate the U.S. Constitution and federal law Publicrightsproject. When Guard forces perform a federal mission, even if legally under the command and control of their governor, they may of necessity be following federal direction CNAS.

For nuclear testing: Does the President have unilateral authority to resume testing? What about Congressional oversight? What about treaty obligations?

But without a press corps to ask these questions daily, to demand briefings, to follow up relentlessly—they simply go unasked.

What Happens Next

Each state must report monthly on its progress via an online defense readiness reporting system Task & Purpose for the QRF. But report to whom? And who sees these reports? Is there a spreadsheet somewhere tracking how well Idaho is learning to “quell dissent”?

For nuclear testing, Trump told reporters “It’ll be announced. We have test sites” NPR. Announced when? Where? Under what safety protocols?

These aren’t abstract questions. “This would have real, devastating impact for Americans,” Fihn said of explosive nuclear testing. “It will harm, kill, and poison people” Common Dreams.

The Silence is Deafening

Nine months ago, these announcements would have triggered a frenzy at the Pentagon. Reporters would have flooded the briefing room. Questions would have dominated every interaction with military officials. Investigative pieces would have dug into the implications, the legality, the strategic rationale.

Now? A leaked memo here. A social media post there. And silence.

This is what happens when nobody’s watching. When there’s no one with a press pass to ask uncomfortable questions. When military policy gets made in the dark—or worse, in the harsh light of a phone screen at 3 AM.

The body count, covered in Part 4, was just the beginning. Now we’re seeing the broader implications: fundamental changes to America’s military posture, both domestic and global, announced without scrutiny, implemented without accountability.

A 23,500-person force explicitly trained to “quell dissent.” A return to nuclear testing after 33 years. And not a single Pentagon press briefing to explain why.

Welcome to the new normal. Where military policy happens on Truth Social. Where leaked memos replace press conferences. Where “no comment” isn’t even necessary anymore—because nobody’s there to ask for one.

The Ministry of Truth doesn’t need to control information when there’s no one left to question it.

And we’re all left wondering: if these are the policies they’re comfortable announcing via leak and social media, what are they doing that we don’t even know about?
