The Destruction Machine

Volume 2: From Atlantic City to the 2016 Primary - How Trump Perfected the Art of Annihilation (1986–2016)

March 9, 2026

Roy Cohn died in August 1986, five weeks after being disbarred. His protégé was forty years old. Donald Trump had absorbed thirteen years of instruction in the art of retribution — attack, deny, declare victory — and he was about to spend the next three decades proving he’d learned every lesson.

What followed wasn’t random cruelty. It was systematic. From the wreckage of Atlantic City to the birther campaign against Barack Obama to the destruction of sixteen Republican presidential candidates in 2016, Trump refined Cohn’s playbook into something more powerful than his mentor ever achieved. Cohn was feared in New York courtrooms. Trump would become feared across an entire country.

This volume traces the evolution of the Destruction Machine — from the small contractors who built Trump’s casinos and were rewarded with bankruptcy, to the sitting president who was rewarded for a night of jokes with a political movement dedicated to delegitimizing his presidency, to the fellow Republicans who were rewarded for running against Trump with nicknames that would follow them for the rest of their careers.

The weapons changed. The instinct never did.

Part I: Atlantic City — The Laboratory (1986–1996)

The iconic exterior of the Trump Taj Mahal casino in Atlantic City, one of Trump’s three casinos that went bankrupt multiple times, symbolizing the “laboratory” where his retribution playbook scaled against contractors and investors. tripadvisor.com

In the decade after Cohn’s death, Trump didn’t need a mentor anymore. He had internalized the curriculum. And Atlantic City became the laboratory where he tested it at scale.

The numbers tell part of the story. Trump opened three casinos in Atlantic City — Trump Plaza, Trump Castle, and the Taj Mahal. All three eventually filed for bankruptcy. Collectively, his casino companies went through bankruptcy proceedings four times. Stockholders lost $1.5 billion. Bondholders were devastated. Small businesses were destroyed.

But Trump personally made a fortune. He collected millions in salary, bonuses, and management fees even as his companies collapsed around their investors. He shifted personal debts onto the casino corporations. When his lenders put him on a budget during the 1990 crisis, that budget was $450,000 per month. “The money I took out of there was incredible,” he told the New York Times. He said it repeatedly, with evident pride.

The people who actually built his casinos didn’t share in the windfall.

Volume 1 introduced the Friel family and Forest Jenkins — the cabinet maker who lost a decades-old family business and the contractor who received thirty cents on the dollar after years of bankruptcy court. But they were just two entries in a catalog of destruction that dwarfs anything a single volume can contain.

On the Taj Mahal alone, casino regulators documented that at least 253 subcontractors were not paid in full or on time. Across all Trump projects, USA Today found more than 200 mechanic’s liens filed since the 1980s — by curtain makers, chandelier shops, cabinet makers, air conditioning companies, plumbing firms, and even Trump’s own lawyers. At least 60 lawsuits were filed by contractors claiming they were stiffed. Trump’s companies were cited 24 times for Fair Labor Standards Act violations since 2005, including failure to pay overtime and minimum wages.

The pattern was consistent: contractors completed their work, submitted their bills, and were told the work was unsatisfactory — regardless of whether it had been approved by general contractors. Trump would offer a fraction of the agreed amount. If they refused, the choice was stark: sue a billionaire, absorb the cost of litigation that could stretch for years, and risk being blacklisted from future Atlantic City work — or accept the loss and move on.

Most accepted the loss. Some went bankrupt. A few sued and won — including a Florida paint shop owner who secured a court order to foreclose on Trump’s Doral resort to collect $30,000. Even Trump’s own lawyers experienced the pattern: Morrison Cohen LLP, which had represented Trump in lawsuits against contractors, ended up suing Trump for $94,511 in unpaid legal fees. When Trump’s deputies attacked the firm’s quality of work in the press, the firm added defamation claims.

New Jersey’s former casino regulator Steven Perskie described the atmosphere Trump left behind: “He put a number of local contractors and suppliers out of business when he didn’t pay them. So when he left Atlantic City, it wasn’t, ‘Sorry to see you go.’ It was, ‘How fast can you get the hell out of here?’”

One small business owner captured the essence of the experience. Nat Hyman, who spent fourteen years litigating with Trump after being strong-armed out of his jewelry kiosk in Trump Tower, put it simply: “To him, it’s a sport. To him, it’s fun.”

This is the critical insight. For Trump, retribution against the powerless wasn’t a byproduct of business. It was the business. The process of fighting — the intimidation, the delay, the asymmetry of resources — was itself the point. Roy Cohn had taught him that the process is the punishment. Atlantic City proved the lesson could scale.

Part II: The Art of the 3,500 Lawsuits

USA Today's investigation revealed that Trump and his companies were involved in more than 3,500 lawsuits over three decades — a figure so extraordinary that it requires context. Major real estate developers litigate. Construction disputes are common in the industry. But 3,500 lawsuits isn't business as usual. It's litigation as a lifestyle. The number was so staggering that it became the subject of an entire book — James D. Zirin's Plaintiff in Chief: A Portrait of Donald Trump in 3,504 Lawsuits — and was featured in People magazine's coverage of Trump's unprecedented use of the courts as a personal weapon. When a single individual's litigation history warrants its own book-length portrait and mainstream magazine coverage, that individual has not been conducting business. He has been conducting warfare.

The lawsuits served multiple functions, all of them consistent with Cohn’s teachings.

Litigation as intimidation. Every lawsuit filed against a contractor, critic, or competitor sent a message to the next person who might consider challenging Trump: you will spend years in court, you will spend money you can’t afford, and you will face a legal team that bills more per hour than your company earns in a week. The lawsuits weren’t always about winning — they were about deterrence.

Litigation as retribution. When Trump was crossed, the lawsuit was personal. It wasn’t about recovering damages; it was about punishing the person who had the audacity to challenge him. The chandelier company that was sued to avoid paying half of a $34,000 bill wasn’t being sued for business reasons. It was being taught a lesson.

Litigation as narrative control. Cohn had taught Trump that the court of public opinion was often more important than the court of law. A countersuit, no matter how frivolous, changed the story. The defendant became the aggressor. The accuser became the accused. The $100 million countersuit against the Justice Department in 1973 was the prototype; thousands of subsequent filings followed the same logic.

Litigation as revenue. In a twist that Fortune magazine uncovered in 2016, the unpaid bills to contractors weren’t just a savings on construction costs — they were converted into tax deductions. When Trump’s casino went bankrupt owing $70 million to contractors, those unpaid obligations became part of the $916 million loss he claimed on his 1995 tax return — a loss that may have allowed him to avoid federal income taxes for nearly two decades. Trump didn’t just stiff the people who built his casinos. He made money on their losses.

The 3,500 lawsuits weren’t aberrations. They were the system working as designed. Roy Cohn had shown Trump that legal processes could be weaponized. Atlantic City proved it could be done at industrial scale. And the lesson Trump drew wasn’t that he had gone too far. It was that he hadn’t gone far enough.

Part III: The Night That Changed Everything (April 30, 2011)

Trump (center, stone-faced) in the audience as President Obama roasts him over the birther claims on April 30, 2011—the night widely seen as redirecting Trump’s “Destruction Machine” toward national politics.

For two decades after Cohn’s death, Trump’s retribution machine operated primarily in the business arena — lawsuits, contractors, competitors, disloyal associates. Then, on a warm spring evening in Washington, Donald Trump experienced something that would redirect the machine toward an entirely new target: the American political system.

The 2011 White House Correspondents’ Dinner has become one of the most analyzed evenings in modern political history. What happened was straightforward: President Barack Obama roasted Donald Trump to his face while a room full of Washington’s elite laughed.

The context matters. For weeks, Trump had been the loudest voice in the “birther” movement — the conspiracy theory that Obama was not born in the United States and was therefore an illegitimate president. Trump had turned it into a media spectacle, demanding Obama produce his birth certificate, questioning his religious identity, and implying that the first Black president had no right to hold the office. Three days before the dinner, Obama had released his long-form birth certificate, dismissing the controversy as a distraction created by “sideshows and carnival barkers.”

Then came the dinner.

Obama walked out to Rick Derringer’s “Real American.” He “revealed” his “long-form birth video” — a clip from The Lion King. He mocked Trump’s qualifications: “In an episode of Celebrity Apprentice, at the steakhouse, the men’s cooking team did not impress the judges from Omaha Steaks. And there was a lot of blame to go around, but you, Mr. Trump, recognized that the real problem was a lack of leadership and so, ultimately, you didn’t blame Little John or Meatloaf — you fired Gary Busey. And these are the kinds of decisions that would keep me up at night.”

Comedian Seth Meyers followed with minutes more of jokes at Trump’s expense. The room roared with laughter. The cameras panned to Trump, who sat stiffly, stone-faced, lips pursed, eyes fixed ahead.

What no one in the room knew: while Obama was delivering jokes about Trump’s reality television career, he had already authorized Operation Neptune’s Spear — the mission that would kill Osama bin Laden the following day. The President of the United States was simultaneously mocking a reality TV star and conducting the most consequential national security operation in a decade.

Trump later insisted he “had a great time” and blamed the “dishonest media” for reporting that he felt humiliated. Rule Two: deny everything.

But Roger Stone, Trump’s longtime political adviser, told PBS Frontline the truth: “I think that is the night he resolves to run for president. I think that he is kind of motivated by it.”

Author Michael D’Antonio went further: “Donald dreads humiliation and he dreads shame, and this is why he often attempts to humiliate and shame other people. This is a burning, personal need that he has to redeem himself from being humiliated by the first Black president.”

The White House Correspondents’ Dinner didn’t create Trump’s retribution instinct — that was forged decades earlier with Roy Cohn. But it gave the instinct a new and vastly larger target. After April 30, 2011, the Destruction Machine wasn’t just aimed at contractors and business rivals. It was aimed at the presidency itself.

Part IV: The Birther Campaign — Retribution Disguised as Politics

President Obama displays his long-form birth certificate in the White House on April 27, 2011, amid Trump’s birther campaign attacks that continued as personal retribution.

The birther movement is usually analyzed as a political strategy or a racial provocation. It was both. But within the framework of this series, it was also something else: retribution.

Trump didn’t invent birtherism. The conspiracy theory circulated on the internet’s margins before Trump amplified it. But he transformed it from a fringe obsession into a mainstream political weapon — and the timing was not coincidental. Obama had humiliated him. Trump’s response, consistent with everything Cohn had taught him, was disproportionate attack.

The birther campaign lasted years. Even after Obama released his long-form birth certificate in April 2011, Trump continued to raise questions. In 2012, he offered $5 million to a charity of Obama’s choice if the president would release his passport and college transcripts. In television appearance after television appearance, he cast doubt on Obama’s legitimacy as president.

The campaign accomplished several things simultaneously. It delegitimized the first Black president in the eyes of millions of Americans. It established Trump as a political figure willing to say what establishment Republicans wouldn’t. It built a constituency of voters who shared Trump’s grievance. And it served as personal retribution against the man who had laughed at him in front of the entire Washington establishment.

Members of the Congressional Black Caucus identified the deeper function. As one member said: “This is not just about degrading the reputation of Barack Obama. It’s about degrading the American dream for all African-Americans.”

Whether Trump was motivated primarily by racial animus, political calculation, or personal revenge is ultimately unknowable — and may be a false choice. What matters for this series is the pattern: Trump was humiliated. Trump attacked. The attack was disproportionate, personal, and sustained over years. It followed the Cohn playbook precisely.

And it worked. Not in the narrow sense — Obama was reelected in 2012. But the birther campaign established Trump as a political force, built the base that would carry him to the presidency, and demonstrated that the Destruction Machine could operate on a national stage.

Obama, reflecting later in his memoir, captured the paradox: “What I knew was that he was a spectacle, and in the United States of America in 2011, that was a form of power. Trump trafficked in a currency that, however shallow, seemed to gain more purchase with each passing day.”

Part V: The 2016 Primary — Sixteen Opponents, Sixteen Destructions

Donald Trump (center) on stage with key 2016 primary opponents including Marco Rubio (left), Ted Cruz (second from right), and others—where Trump deployed nicknames like “Little Marco,” “Lyin’ Ted,” and “Low Energy Jeb” to dismantle rivals. cnn.com

If the White House Correspondents’ Dinner redirected the Destruction Machine toward politics, the 2016 Republican primary is where it achieved its fullest expression. In the space of a single campaign, Trump destroyed sixteen opponents — not just by winning more votes, but by branding them with identities they have never fully escaped.

The nicknames were the most visible weapon. “Low Energy Jeb.” “Little Marco.” “Lyin’ Ted.” “Crooked Hillary.” But the nicknames were just the surface. Beneath them lay a comprehensive destruction strategy built on every lesson Cohn had taught.

Jeb Bush: The Prototype Destruction

Bush entered the 2016 race as the establishment favorite — the son and brother of presidents, backed by over $100 million in super PAC money, endorsed by the Republican donor class. Trump dismantled him with two words: “Low Energy.”

The genius of the attack — and it was, in its dark way, a form of genius — was its simplicity and its irrefutability. How does a candidate prove they have “energy”? Every attempt to demonstrate vigor only drew attention to the charge. Bush’s mild-mannered temperament, which in any other context would have been considered presidential composure, became evidence for Trump’s prosecution.

“I defined him,” Trump later told Jimmy Kimmel. “I gave him this term, ‘Low energy.’ We do not need in this country low energy.”

Bush spent more than $130 million and won four delegates. He dropped out in February 2016. The destruction was so complete that Trump later gloated: “Low energy Jeb Bush just endorsed a man he truly hates, Lyin’ Ted Cruz. Honestly, I can’t blame Jeb in that I drove him into oblivion!”

“Drove him into oblivion.” That’s not a political opponent describing a campaign victory. That’s a predator describing prey.

Ted Cruz: The Total War

Cruz and Trump didn’t start as enemies. In the early months of the campaign, Cruz carefully avoided criticizing Trump, positioning himself to inherit Trump’s supporters if Trump’s candidacy collapsed. Trump noticed the strategy and initially played along.

Then the competition got real, and Trump went to war — not just against Cruz, but against his family.

He branded Cruz “Lyin’ Ted” and used the nickname relentlessly. He retweeted an unflattering photo of Cruz’s wife Heidi next to a glamour shot of Melania Trump with the caption “The images are worth 1,000 words.” He tweeted: “Be careful, Lyin’ Ted, or I will spill the beans on your wife!” Cruz responded by calling Trump “a sniveling coward” and demanded he “leave Heidi the hell alone.”

Then Trump went further. On the morning of the Indiana primary — the contest that would effectively end Cruz’s campaign — Trump told Fox News that Cruz’s father Rafael had been photographed with Lee Harvey Oswald before JFK’s assassination. The claim was sourced from the National Enquirer and had been corroborated by no other outlet. Rafael Cruz denied it. Trump pressed it anyway. “I don’t think anybody denied it,” Trump told ABC News. He refused to apologize.

Cruz’s response that day was extraordinary — a candidate, on the verge of elimination, delivering a raw and unfiltered assessment of his destroyer. He called Trump “utterly amoral,” “a pathological liar,” and “a narcissist at a level I don’t think this country has ever seen.” He said Trump was “a bully” who “is not going to stop.”

Cruz lost Indiana that night and suspended his campaign.

What followed is one of the most remarkable submissions in modern political history. Two months later, at the Republican National Convention, Cruz delivered a speech in which he conspicuously declined to endorse Trump, instead telling the audience to “vote your conscience.” The arena erupted in boos. Cruz was escorted out by security.

Two months after that, Cruz endorsed Trump. He later brought Heidi to dinner with the Trumps at the White House. In 2018, Trump campaigned for Cruz in Houston, rebranding him: “To me, he’s not Lyin’ Ted anymore. He’s Beautiful Ted.” Asked whether he regretted the accusation about Cruz’s father and JFK, Trump said: “I regret nothing.”

When asked on The View whether he was “lying then or lying now,” Cruz said he and Trump had “beat the living crap out of each other” and that his wife and father had “laughed” about the attacks. “In the end,” he said, Trump “won.”

The submission was complete. And the lesson was clear for every Republican watching: even calling your wife ugly and your father a co-conspirator in a presidential assassination could be forgiven — if you bend the knee.

Marco Rubio: Diminished to Nothing

“Little Marco” was deployed after Rubio made a late-campaign surge in early 2016. The nickname worked because it conveyed not just physical smallness but political insignificance — the suggestion that Rubio was simply too minor a figure to compete at Trump’s level.

Rubio tried to fight fire with fire, making jokes about Trump’s “small hands” and their anatomical implications. It was a disaster. Rubio looked desperate and undignified — exactly what Trump’s branding had predicted. The Florida senator who had been celebrated as the future of the Republican Party was reduced to anatomical insults on a debate stage.

Rubio dropped out after losing his home state of Florida. Three years later, he was one of Trump’s most reliable Senate allies. Today he serves as Trump’s Secretary of State — the man Trump once called “a con artist” elevated to the nation’s top diplomatic position precisely because his submission was so total.

Lindsey Graham: The Most Spectacular Reversal

Perhaps no destruction-to-submission arc is more dramatic than Lindsey Graham’s. In 2015, Trump called Graham “an idiot” and read Graham’s personal cell phone number aloud at a campaign rally, inviting supporters to call. Graham had called Trump “a jackass.” Trump described Graham as a “total lightweight” who “couldn’t get a job” in the private sector.

Graham, in turn, called Trump “a race-baiting, xenophobic, religious bigot” and said he didn’t think Trump was “a reliable Republican conservative.” He warned that nominating Trump would lead to the destruction of the party.

Today, Graham and Trump golf together regularly. Graham has become one of Trump’s most vocal defenders. The man who publicly declared Trump a threat to the Republican Party became the man who publicly declared Trump a great president. The reversal required no explanation from either party. The submission was its own explanation.

Part VI: The Pattern That Explains Everything

The 2016 primary established a pattern that the rest of this series will document in exhaustive detail: the Three Paths.

Path One: Never Oppose. Those who never challenged Trump were rewarded from the start. Throughout the primary, figures who rallied early — Chris Christie, Jeff Sessions — were given prominent roles. The cost of early loyalty was steep (Christie’s presidential ambitions were permanently ended when Trump made him hold his coat at a press conference), but the alternative was worse.

Path Two: Oppose, Then Submit. This is the path that Cruz, Rubio, and Graham took — and it is the path Trump values most. The submission must be public and complete. It must involve abandoning the very principles the person claimed to hold. And paradoxically, the more extreme the original opposition, the more valuable the submission becomes. When Marco Rubio becomes Secretary of State after being called “a con artist,” the hypocrisy isn’t a bug — it’s the feature. It proves that Rubio has chosen Trump over his own stated convictions. Every time Rubio appears on camera as Secretary of State, the subtext is: this man abandoned everything he claimed to believe, and he did it for me.

Path Three: Oppose and Stay Principled. This is the path of destruction. As we’ll document in Volume 4, the Republicans who refused to submit — Liz Cheney, Adam Kinzinger, Mitt Romney, and the others who voted for impeachment or conviction — were systematically eliminated. The message was unambiguous: you can oppose and survive only if you eventually submit. Oppose and refuse to submit, and you will be destroyed.

These three paths didn’t emerge in Trump’s presidency. They were established in the 2016 primary. Every Republican candidate who challenged Trump was forced to choose: submit or be destroyed. Most submitted. Those who didn’t were, as Trump said of Jeb Bush, driven “into oblivion.”

The Machine Evolves

Between 1986 and 2016 — from Cohn’s death to Trump’s nomination — the Destruction Machine underwent a fundamental transformation.

In Atlantic City, it operated against powerless individuals — small contractors, working-class tradesmen, local business owners who had no resources to fight a billionaire. The weapons were litigation, delay, and financial asymmetry.

With the birther campaign, it scaled to national politics — targeting the sitting President of the United States with a conspiracy theory that delegitimized his presidency and built a political movement from racial grievance and personal revenge.

In the 2016 primary, it became a comprehensive system — branding, humiliation, family attacks, conspiracy theories, and the systematic destruction of anyone who stood in Trump’s path, followed by the ritualized submission of the survivors.

Each stage was an evolution, not a departure. The contractor who was stiffed in 1988 and the senator who was branded “Lyin’ Ted” in 2016 experienced the same fundamental dynamic: challenge Trump, and the response will be disproportionate, personal, and sustained until you are either destroyed or submit.

Roy Cohn taught Trump to counterattack. Atlantic City taught him to scale it. The birther campaign taught him to nationalize it. And the 2016 primary taught him that it could be used to seize the most powerful office on earth.

In the next volume, we’ll examine what happened when Trump gained the power to pardon — and how the clemency process itself became another weapon in the retribution arsenal. Who got pardons and who didn’t tells you everything about how the Destruction Machine operates when its operator controls the machinery of government.
