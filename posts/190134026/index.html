The Contribution Index

Canada

Part 1: The Well-Meaning Hypocrite

March 10, 2026

Top Performer #10

I said the countdown would begin at home. Here we are.

I am a 74-year-old Torontonian. I have lived in this country my entire life. I have watched it evolve from a quiet middle power with an outsized reputation for decency into something more complicated — a nation that still talks like a global citizen but increasingly acts like one that has stopped paying attention to its own contradictions.

Canada makes the top ten. Barely. And the reasons it barely makes the top ten are, I think, more instructive than the reasons it makes the list at all.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 42 out of 50

This is where Canada looks most like the country it believes itself to be.

Universal healthcare — the defining feature of Canadian social identity — is real. Every Canadian has access to publicly funded medical care. Life expectancy is strong at roughly 82 years. The system works, mostly, for most people. The 8 rather than the 9 reflects the cracks that Canadians know intimately: emergency room wait times that have become a national embarrassment, a family doctor shortage that leaves millions relying on walk-in clinics, and mental health services so inadequate that the system essentially does not cover them.

Education scores a 9. Canada consistently ranks among the top nations globally on PISA assessments, and — unlike many wealthy nations — it achieves this across socioeconomic strata. The gap between the highest and lowest performing students is narrower in Canada than in virtually any comparable nation. Post-secondary attainment is among the highest in the world.

Democratic governance earns a 9. Canada is a full democracy on the EIU’s Democracy Index, with strong press freedom, judicial independence, and peaceful transfers of power. The system is imperfect — First Nations governance is a structural failure, and the first-past-the-post electoral system regularly produces majority governments from minority vote shares — but it functions with an integrity that much of the world would envy.

Gender equality and minority rights earn an 8. Canada has been a global leader on LGBTQ+ rights, has made significant legislative progress on gender equity, and maintains a broadly inclusive civic culture. The score is held back by the lived reality of Indigenous Canadians — a population that experiences poverty rates, incarceration rates, and health outcomes that would be scandalous in any nation and are inexcusable in one this wealthy. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission documented the failures. The remediation remains tragically incomplete.

Environmental Stewardship: 33 out of 50

And here is where the hypocrisy begins.

Canada’s environmental stewardship score is not just the lowest in the top ten — it is lower by a significant margin than the next lowest. It is the anchor dragging Canada to the bottom of this list, and the reason is no mystery to anyone who has driven through northern Alberta.

Emissions per capita earn a 4. Canada produces roughly 15 metric tons of CO2 equivalent per person per year — among the highest in the developed world, comparable to the United States and dramatically higher than any European nation. For a country that signed the Paris Agreement, that hosted COP conferences, that lectures the world on climate responsibility, this number is an indictment.

The tar sands are the centrepiece of the failure, but not its totality. Canada’s economy remains deeply entangled with fossil fuel extraction. The Trans Mountain pipeline expansion — publicly purchased by the federal government — was justified as an economic necessity while the same government committed to net-zero emissions by 2050. You cannot simultaneously expand pipeline capacity and claim climate leadership. Canada tries. The scorecard does not reward the attempt.

The gap between Canadian environmental rhetoric and environmental action is visible not just in government policy but in where private capital flows. The Trottier Family Foundation has invested heavily in climate research and clean energy advocacy — doing the analytical work that the federal government has declined to fund at scale. The McConnell Foundation pushes social innovation. The Chagnon Foundation invests in child development. These are meaningful contributions, but they also reveal a pattern familiar across this series: when private philanthropy fills gaps that public policy should have closed, the giving is evidence of the failure as much as of the generosity.

Renewable energy share scores a 7, buoyed by substantial hydroelectric capacity — particularly in Quebec and British Columbia. But hydro is legacy infrastructure, not evidence of a transition. Outside hydroelectric, Canada’s renewable energy adoption has been modest relative to its capacity and its rhetoric.

Biodiversity and habitat protection score a 7. Canada has vast wilderness areas and has made commitments to the 30x30 biodiversity framework. But the protection of these areas is uneven, and resource extraction continues to encroach on critical habitats. Boreal forest management, while better than tropical deforestation analogues, faces persistent pressure from logging and mining interests.

Systemic Behaviour: 35 out of 50

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earns an 8. Canada resettled more refugees than any country in the world in several recent years, and public support for refugee acceptance, while not universal, has been remarkably resilient compared to European and American backlash politics. The Syrian refugee programme, the Afghan resettlement effort, and the private sponsorship model are genuine contributions to global humanitarian infrastructure.

Adherence to international law earns an 8. Canada participates constructively in multilateral institutions, contributes to UN peacekeeping (though far less than its historical reputation suggests), and generally respects international legal frameworks.

Arms exports score a 6 — the most uncomfortable number on Canada’s systemic behaviour card. The $15 billion contract to supply light armoured vehicles to Saudi Arabia, signed under one government and upheld by the next, is a stain on Canada’s international reputation that no amount of peacekeeping rhetoric can wash away. A nation that sells weapons to a regime waging war in Yemen cannot score higher than a 6, and some would argue the score is generous.

Tax haven and corruption facilitation earns a 7. Canada is not a significant offshore financial centre, and its domestic governance — the RCMP, the CRA, the Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions — maintains reasonable accountability. The 7 rather than an 8 or 9 reflects a persistent blind spot: Canadian real estate, particularly in Vancouver and Toronto, has been systematically used for money laundering by foreign actors, a phenomenon so well documented that it acquired its own name — the Vancouver Model — and prompted a formal public inquiry. The Cullen Commission found that billions of dollars in illicit funds had moved through British Columbia’s real estate market, casinos, and luxury goods sector. Canada knew. Canada moved slowly. The gap between a nation that prides itself on clean governance and one that allowed its housing market to become a laundromat for foreign criminal wealth is not enormous — but it is real, and it is scored.

Exploitation versus investment in developing nations earns a 6. Canada’s mining sector operates extensively in Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia — and its record is genuinely mixed. Canadian mining companies have been implicated in environmental damage, community displacement, and labour violations in host countries, often with limited legal accountability because Canadian law has historically not required Canadian companies to meet Canadian standards abroad. The 2023 Fighting Against Forced Labour and Child Labour in Supply Chains Act was a meaningful step forward. The 6 reflects improvement in the right direction — Canada has invested in development finance and trade capacity in developing nations — alongside a mining sector whose global footprint is more complicated than the country’s self-image typically acknowledges.

The Neighbour Test: 33 out of 50

Canada’s multilateral orientation is genuine — earning a 7. This is a nation that instinctively looks outward, that values international cooperation, that sees itself as a bridge-builder. It is a constructive participant in NATO, the UN, the G7, and international climate frameworks. Response to global crises earns a 7 — Canada has shown up for refugee emergencies and pandemic cooperation, if sometimes slowly.

Canadian Multiculturalism Day. A nation that celebrates diversity in its Senate imagery while scoring 5 out of 10 on consistency of values versus interests. The aspiration is real. The gap between the poster and the policy is what makes Canada the well-meaning hypocrite.

Treatment of allies earns a 7. Canada does not bully its neighbours, threaten sovereignty, or weaponize trade for political leverage — a distinction that feels increasingly significant when your only land neighbour is doing all three simultaneously.

But consistency of values versus interests earns a 5, and this is the score that defines the well-meaning hypocrite. Canada champions human rights while selling armoured vehicles to Saudi Arabia. It promotes environmental stewardship while extracting tar sands. It celebrates Indigenous reconciliation while failing to deliver clean drinking water to reserves. The gap between what Canada says and what Canada does is not hypocrisy in the malicious sense — it is hypocrisy in the Canadian sense, which is to say: sincere in intention, inadequate in execution, and seemingly unaware of how visible the contradiction is from the outside.

Long-term stewardship earns a 7 — Canada’s investment in future generations through education, social programmes, and institutional stability is real. But the tar sands represent the opposite of stewardship: the extraction of short-term economic value at the cost of long-term environmental health. The 7 reflects both realities.

The Editorial Conclusion

Canada scores 143 out of 200 — tenth in the top performers, the last nation to make the cut.

The number both flatters and damns. It flatters because Canada is, by global standards, an exceptional place. Its democratic institutions work. Its citizens are well-educated, well-cared-for, and broadly committed to a civic culture of inclusion. Its multilateral orientation is genuine. It does more, in absolute and per-capita terms, for refugees and humanitarian aid than most nations its size.

It damns because Canada’s environmental stewardship score is an embarrassment that no amount of democratic virtue can offset, and its consistency score on the Neighbour Test — a 5, the lowest among the top ten — reveals the fundamental gap between aspiration and action. A nation that produces 15 metric tons of CO2 per person while lecturing others on climate responsibility is not a global leader. It is a global hypocrite. And hypocrisy, in the Contribution Index framework, is a specific kind of failure — the failure of a nation that knows better, has the resources to do better, and chooses not to.

The well-meaning hypocrite. It is an uncomfortable title. It is, I believe, an accurate one.

If I sound harsh, it is because I love this country, and love without honesty is sentimentality. Canada can be better. It has everything it needs to be better. What it lacks is the political will to close the gap between what it says and what it does — on climate, on Indigenous reconciliation, on arms exports, on the persistent distance between Canadian values as articulated and Canadian values as practiced.

The countdown begins with this discomfort. It will not be the last time in this series that a nation’s score reveals the distance between aspiration and action.

But it is the first time it has been my own country on the scorecard. And I can tell you: it stings exactly as much as it should.
