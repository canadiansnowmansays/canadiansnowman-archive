The Asimov Gap

The Confidence Theft

Part 19: When AI Doesn’t Just Replace Human Judgment — It Erodes It

March 26, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 25, 2026

Image by Freepik

She wanted an amicable divorce. No lawyers, no adversarial process. So she typed her question into ChatGPT and received a calm, confident, step-by-step answer: draft the settlement agreement, get it notarized, file it yourselves, done in a month.

What ChatGPT didn’t mention — what a mortgage bank had to explain later, after the condo she’d fallen in love with was already gone — is that a signed settlement agreement is not a court-finalized divorce decree. That requires a judge. That takes at least six months in California.

She went back to ChatGPT anyway. Kept going back. For medical questions. For legal questions. She knows it can be wrong. That’s not why she uses it.

She uses it for the confidence.

The First Crisis Nobody Talked About

For eighteen essays in this series, I’ve written about the Asimov Gap in terms of governance: the absence of democratic frameworks for controlling AI at the institutional level. The governance gap is real and urgent and I stand by every word.

But this week, two Atlantic pieces — published two days apart — forced me to reckon with a dimension of the gap I’ve underweighted.

The first, by Nick Dothée, is titled “The First AI Crisis Is Psychological.” The second is a Galaxy Brain podcast transcript by Charlie Warzel, sitting down with Will Knight of Wired to discuss autonomous weapons and the Anthropic/Pentagon fallout.

Read separately, each is valuable. Read together, they reveal something that cuts deeper than I expected.

The gap isn’t only in the institutions. It’s in us.

The Machine That Sounds Like It Knows

Dothée names two mechanisms that explain the divorce story — and ten thousand stories like it.

The first is the “confidence heuristic.” Decades of psychological research show that people use confidence as a shortcut for credibility, especially when accuracy is hard to judge independently. When someone sounds certain, we tend to trust them. The effect persists even when we consciously know the speaker can be wrong.

The second is the “machine heuristic.” Penn State researcher S. Shyam Sundar identified this more than a decade ago: we automatically attribute objectivity and expertise to machine-generated answers, especially when delivered fluently and without hesitation. Sundar named the effect before most of us had daily contact with AI. Now it governs millions of interactions every day.

Stack these two mechanisms together and you get the problem. AI sounds confident. AI sounds like a machine. Both shortcuts push toward trust. Neither is calibrated to accuracy.

And AI has no reason not to be confident. If it gives you wrong advice, nothing happens to it. No social cost. No reputational damage. No hesitation the next time it speaks. The tone stays identical whether the answer is accurate, speculative, or completely fabricated.

What We Spent a Lifetime Earning

Here is where Dothée’s argument becomes genuinely uncomfortable.

When a human expert sounds certain, that certainty carries weight because it was earned. Years of training. A reputation staked. The memory of being wrong in front of people who remembered. The credibility isn’t just a signal — it’s the residue of real cost.

When AI sounds certain, the certainty is free. It hasn’t done the reading in any way that corresponds to human learning. It hasn’t failed publicly and recovered. It hasn’t built anything slowly. And yet it uses exactly the same vocal register — the same tone of authority — that you’ve spent your whole life associating with earned expertise.

Which means the cue you’ve used your entire life to sort the credible from the noise — suddenly stops working.

And if you can’t tell the difference between real authority and a very good impression of it: what does that say about your own judgment? What was all that experience for?

This is the confidence theft. AI doesn’t just give wrong answers. It quietly devalues the human capacity for judgment that would catch the wrong answers.

The Soldier Who Gives Brilliant Answers Until He Doesn’t

Galaxy Brain with Charlie Warzel and Will Knight

Will Knight, speaking to Charlie Warzel, puts his finger on the same problem from the military side — and the stakes there are existential rather than personal.

Knight describes what makes AI failure different from human failure in a combat chain of command. Humans make mistakes too, he says. Military systems are designed to absorb and correct human error through hierarchy, oversight, and redundancy.

But AI fails differently.

In Knight’s framing: imagine you’re talking to an infantryman who gives you the right answer again and again. Impressive. Reliable. You trust him. And then suddenly — a completely absurd, catastrophic answer. Not a human error. A spectacular system failure with no warning.

The machine heuristic means you’ve been building inappropriate trust through every correct answer. You’ve been calibrating your confidence in the system upward. And then it fails in a way that doesn’t look like any failure you’ve seen before.

Knight isn’t speculating. This is what the AI companies themselves understand about their systems. It’s part of why Anthropic refused to remove safety guardrails from Claude Gov. The company knows how and why these models fail. They believe those failure modes matter enough to stake their Pentagon contract on.

The Pentagon disagreed. OpenAI stepped in. The machines are now in the chain, with fewer guardrails, in a shooting war.

The Epistemic Surrender

Dothée describes what happens at the far end of this process, when individuals have absorbed enough AI-confidence-as-authority that their own epistemic footing gives way entirely.

A Facebook post circulated on his feed declaring that every emotional story posted by strangers — the heroic teacher, the animal rescue, the celebrity speaking out — was fake. All of it AI-generated. Don’t bother trying to sort it out.

He identifies the appeal beneath the fury: if everything is fake, you don’t have to carry the burden of discernment anymore. You don’t have to weigh credibility or sit with uncertainty. You can reject it all.

This is epistemic surrender. Not skepticism — skepticism is active. This is exhaustion. The decision to stop trying to know.

Psychologists studying misinformation have documented what happens when people lose confidence in perceptual judgment. They don’t become more analytical. They simplify. They defer to the most decisive available source — or disengage entirely.

In other words: the very people most damaged by AI’s unearned confidence are most likely to end up trusting it more, not less. The alternative — continuing to bear the weight of judgment in an environment where everything can be generated — is simply too costly.

What Asimov Never Imagined

Isaac Asimov built his Three Laws of Robotics on an implicit assumption that has gone almost entirely unexamined in the decades since: that humans would remain capable arbiters of their own judgment.

The Three Laws are a governance framework. They exist to constrain machine behavior in ways that protect human interests. The whole architecture depends on humans being able to assess whether those laws are being followed — to notice when something has gone wrong, to intervene, to override.

Asimov never imagined a scenario in which the machines were operating within their constraints and the humans had stopped trusting themselves to evaluate the outputs.

That is the gap that neither the Three Laws nor any of our current governance frameworks addresses. We’ve been building rules for AI behavior. We haven’t been building defenses for human epistemic capacity.

Asimov worried about robots that would harm humans. The more immediate risk is subtler: AI systems that are functioning exactly as designed, delivering confident answers with machine-smooth authority, while the humans on the receiving end quietly lose confidence in their own ability to question.

The Democratic Dimension

Democracy is, at its core, an epistemic project. It rests on the belief that citizens — not just experts, not just rulers — can assess evidence, weigh competing claims, and make judgments about their own governance.

That belief has always been contested. Every authoritarian movement in modern history has exploited the gap between the ideal of informed citizens and the reality of confused, overwhelmed ones. The playbook is consistent: flood the zone, discredit all sources equally, make the burden of discernment feel too heavy to bear. Then offer certainty. Then offer a strongman who embodies it.

AI didn’t invent this playbook. But it industrializes it.

The confidence heuristic and the machine heuristic together are a precision instrument for exactly the kind of epistemic softening that authoritarianism requires. Not through disinformation alone — though that too — but through the slower, quieter process of making people doubt their own ability to know.

When Knight asks “whose values does the AI reflect?” he’s asking a governance question. But underneath it is an epistemic one: when citizens can no longer trust their own judgment about what’s real, who benefits?

Not democracy.

What the Gap Actually Requires

For eighteen essays I’ve argued that the Asimov Gap requires democratic governance frameworks: independent oversight, international standards, accountability mechanisms that don’t depend on the goodwill of the companies building the systems.

I still believe that. But after reading Dothée’s piece alongside Warzel and Knight, I’m adding something.

The Asimov Gap also requires epistemic infrastructure — social, educational, and institutional — that protects human judgment capacity in an environment saturated with authoritative-sounding machine outputs.

This isn’t a new idea. Media literacy has been a policy conversation for years. Critical thinking is on school curricula everywhere. But the speed of AI deployment has outrun every institutional response, and the specific mechanism — the confidence heuristic, the machine heuristic, the slow erosion of earned authority — is not yet part of any serious policy framework I’m aware of.

It needs to be.

Because Dothée is right about the stakes. If we give up on knowing what’s real, we don’t just lose facts. We lose contact — with the world, with each other, and most dangerously, with the governing capacity of citizens who can no longer trust their own ability to know.

Asimov gave us the Three Laws. He forgot to account for the humans who would eventually stop being sure enough to enforce them.
