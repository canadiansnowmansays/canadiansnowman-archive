The Day Trump Tried to Own the Ballot Box

Part 17: When Democracy Fights Back - An Ongoing Series

April 1, 2026

There’s a man named Adam Kinzinger you should know about, if you don’t already.

He served nine terms in Congress as a Republican from Illinois. He was a combat veteran, an Air Force pilot, a reliable conservative vote — until January 6th, 2021, when he watched a mob incited by the President of the United States storm the Capitol, and decided that some things matter more than a party label. He became one of only two Republicans to sit on the House Select Committee investigating January 6th, alongside Liz Cheney. He voted to impeach Donald Trump. He paid for it with his career — redistricted out, primaried into irrelevance, cast out of the caucus he had served for over a decade.

He knows these people. He served with them. He watched them, one by one, make their calculations and choose the man over the Constitution. He is not an outsider commenting on a party he never understood. He is an insider who understood it perfectly, saw what it was becoming, and said no — loudly, publicly, at considerable personal cost.

These days he does short, unscripted videos. No notes. No teleprompter. Usually looking like a man who’d rather be having dinner but can’t quite let something go. Last night, at around 6:30, he sat down in front of a camera because he felt he had no choice.

“Donald Trump,” he said, “signed an executive order which is clearly illegal. And he knows it.”

That’s the whole story, right there, in seventeen words. But it deserves the full telling.

Let’s be precise about what was signed on March 31, 2026, because precision matters when democracy is on the table.

Trump’s new executive order instructs the Department of Homeland Security — working alongside the Social Security Administration — to compile a verified list of American citizens eligible to vote in each state. The U.S. Postal Service is then directed to send absentee ballots only to voters on that federally approved list. States must receive their revised citizen rosters no fewer than 60 days before each federal election. Ballots will require specific secure envelopes with unique barcodes. And states that decline to comply face the loss of federal funding.

This is not a nudge. This is not a suggestion. This is a federal takeover of the machinery of American elections, accomplished by the stroke of a pen, in a nation whose Constitution explicitly places that machinery in the hands of the states.

Article I, Section 4. Look it up. It’s not ambiguous. The “Times, Places and Manner” of federal elections belong to individual states, with Congress — Congress, not the president — authorized to enact changes. The president gets no seat at that table. None.

Trump himself, signing the order, acknowledged it would face legal challenges. Then he called it “foolproof.”

A man who bankrupted a casino — an institution whose entire business model is separating customers from their money — once claimed the slot machines were too popular on opening day. This is the same man. The same instinct. Never the failure. Always someone else’s fault. Always a story to tell instead of a truth to face.

Here’s what makes Adam Kizzenger effective, and why his voice matters alongside the legal analysis: he remembers things.

He remembers when the Republican Party spent eight years in performative agony every time Barack Obama reached for an executive order. Governing by pen. Governing by phone. Where’s the Congress? Where’s the process? This is not how America works. They weren’t wrong, incidentally. It isn’t how America works. The problem is they were never actually principled about it. They were tactical. And tactics have no memory.

Now the same party watches Donald Trump issue executive orders the way other men issue tweets — reflexively, compulsively, constitutionally — and every one of them has gone quiet. Not because they believe it’s right. Kizzenger is clear on this: “Everyone of them in there right now knows that this is wrong, knows that this violates the law, and won’t say a damn word. Because they’re scared.”

Scared of the primary. Scared of the post-Congress lobbying career. Scared of the man who has made cowardice the price of admission.

The party of states’ rights has become the party of federal overreach, on a dime, without a backward glance, because the man at the top demanded it. That is not a political party. That is a protection racket.

Now for the question that actually keeps election law scholars up at night: how much time does democracy have?

The 2026 midterms are November 3rd. That sounds far away. It is not far away. Many states begin early and absentee voting in September. Primaries — which directly determine who appears on November ballots — are running from March through September across the country. The window for courts to act is not measured in months. It is measured in weeks, and some of those weeks are already gone.

The good news — and there is good news — is that the courts have been here before. Trump’s 2025 executive order on elections was largely blocked by federal judges who used the same constitutional logic: the president doesn’t get to rewrite election law. Rick Hasen, the election law expert at UCLA, was direct when asked whether DHS and the Postal Service could realistically implement these changes before November. “No way,” he said. And if the administration tried anyway? “Courts would stop it.”

Votebeat reported that legal experts described the prior court rulings as providing “a clear roadmap” for challenging this one. Multiple attorneys general — Arizona’s Kris Mayes, Colorado’s Phil Weiser, Massachusetts’s Andrea Joy Campbell — announced legal action within hours of the signing. The Brennan Center, which has been tracking and litigating this battle for over a year, was ready before the ink was dry.

Democracy’s immune system, it turns out, has been building antibodies.

But here is where I want to slow down, because the headline fight is not the only fight.

The executive order is almost certainly going to be blocked. Trump likely knows this. Hasen suspects he knows it. The order may function less as a governing document than as a loyalty test — a mechanism for identifying which Republicans will stand up and which will sit down, which state officials will comply and which will resist, and which voters can be frightened into disengaging before a single ballot is cast.

That is voter suppression without implementation. Confusion as policy. Chaos as strategy.

And beneath that — quieter, less visible, and considerably more dangerous — is the apparatus already in motion. The DOJ is currently waging more than two dozen lawsuits to extract sensitive voter data from states. A DOJ official admitted in court that this data is slated to be shared with DHS and run through the so-called SAVE system to search for noncitizens. The SAVE system, according to NPR, has already erroneously flagged American citizens. The Brennan Center estimates that a “show your papers” policy, if fully implemented, could disenfranchise 21 million American citizens — citizens, not illegal voters, citizens — who simply lack the documentation the administration wants to require.

And then there is the tabulation end. The back end. The part the cameras don’t cover. Hasen has noted that if you want to interfere with an election and minimize the public reaction, you do it when the votes are being counted, not when they’re being cast. CISA — the agency responsible for protecting election infrastructure — has been effectively sidelined by this administration. State and local officials are increasingly on their own.

Courts can stop an executive order. Courts have a harder time stopping a bureaucratic system designed to quietly fail the right people at the right moment.

Adam Kizzenger ended his video with a note about the polls. CNN reported that Trump’s approval rating has dropped to the lowest ever recorded for any president — lower than George W. Bush at the depths of the Iraq War.

“He’s not coming back from that,” Kizzenger said.

Maybe. Maybe not. What history teaches us is that leaders who cannot accept democratic outcomes tend not to accept declining poll numbers either. The response to failure is never accountability. It’s escalation.

We have seen this before, in other countries, in other centuries, in the long catalog of men who decided that losing wasn’t something that happened to them. Those stories rarely end the way the protagonist imagined. But they can do tremendous damage on the way to their conclusion.

Democracy fights back. It is fighting back. The state attorneys general are in court. The voting rights organizations are filing briefs. The secretaries of state are at their podiums calling it what it is. The constitutional scholars are writing their analyses. A former Republican operative named Kizzenger is sitting in front of a camera at 6:30 at night because someone has to say the plain thing plainly.

The question — the only question that matters between now and November 3rd — is whether the immune system is fast enough.

So far, it has been.

We need it to keep being.

Thank you, Adam Kinzinger. You lost a party. You kept your integrity. In the current climate, that is not a small thing. It may, in fact, be everything.
