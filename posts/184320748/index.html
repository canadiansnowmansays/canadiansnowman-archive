Behind the Blackout: The Massacre Iran Tried to Hide

Part 3 of The Iran Crisis Series

Thursday, January 9, 8:47 PM Tehran Time: Iran’s internet went dark.

NetBlocks, the global internet monitoring organization, recorded “an abrupt and near-total drop in connection levels.” The Georgia Institute of Technology’s monitoring systems showed the country “almost completely offline.” Within minutes, 80 million Iranians were cut off from the world—and the world was cut off from what was about to happen in Iran.

By Sunday, January 11, human rights organizations were reporting a death toll in the hundreds. By Monday, credible estimates suggested “hundreds and possibly thousands” killed. Bodies were stacked in forensics centers. Hospitals overflowed with gunshot wounds. Families desperately searched for missing loved ones.

The Center for Human Rights in Iran issued a stark warning: “A massacre is unfolding.”

This is what happened behind Iran’s internet blackout—and why the regime was so desperate to hide it.

Thursday Night: When the Lights Went Out

The timing was not coincidental.

Thursday, January 8, had seen the largest protests since demonstrations began on December 28. Multiple opposition groups had coordinated calls for nationwide demonstrations at 8 PM. Reza Pahlavi (the Shah’s exiled son), Kurdish groups, and Nobel laureate Narges Mohammadi (from detention) all urged Iranians to take to the streets.

They did. In massive numbers.

Tehran, Mashhad, Bushehr, Shiraz, Isfahan, Karaj, Tabriz, Kerman, Gonabad—protests erupted across Iran’s major cities and impoverished rural towns simultaneously. Affluent neighborhoods like Shahrak Gharb in Tehran, which had stayed quiet during previous protests, joined for the first time. The demonstrations crossed every demographic line: young and old, men and women, rich and poor, urban and rural.

The slogans had evolved beyond economic grievances:

  • “Death to Khamenei!”

  • “Long live the Shah!”

  • “The street will prevail!”

  • “Clerics get lost, the shah is coming back!”

Government buildings burned. A seminary in Gonabad was stormed, clerics beaten. Security forces fired weapons and tear gas. The regime faced something it hadn’t seen in decades: a genuine mass uprising threatening its existence.

And so, at 8:47 PM, Iran’s government made a decision: If the world couldn’t see what happened next, maybe they could get away with it.

The internet went dark.

Why Regimes Black Out the Internet

“The Iranian government uses internet shutdowns as a tool of repression,” explained Omid Memarian, an Iranian human rights expert. “Whenever protests reach a critical point, authorities sever the country’s connection to the global internet.”

But Thursday’s blackout was different in scope and implication.

Previous Iranian internet disruptions were partial—slowing connection speeds, blocking specific platforms, throttling bandwidth. This was total. Not just social media. Not just messaging apps. Everything. International phone calls were blocked. Iranians in the diaspora reported being unable to reach loved ones inside the country for days.

The Center for Human Rights in Iran issued an urgent statement Friday: “The regime typically does this as a preface to the mass slaughter of protesters.”

They weren’t being hyperbolic. The pattern is well-documented globally:

Myanmar (February 2021): Internet blackout preceded military coup and massacre of protesters. Thousands killed.

Ethiopia (November 2020): Communications blackout in Tigray region preceded mass atrocities. Estimates suggest tens of thousands killed.

Kashmir (August 2019): India imposed months-long internet shutdown during crackdown. Exact casualties remain unknown.

Iran itself (November 2019): Internet blackout during protests. Amnesty International later confirmed at least 304 killed, likely many more.

The logic is brutal but simple:

  1. Prevents coordination. Protesters can’t organize without internet.

  2. Hides evidence. No video of crackdown reaches the world.

  3. Isolates victims. Families can’t report missing persons.

  4. Enables impunity. Security forces operate without accountability.

  5. Delays response. By the time the world learns what happened, it’s over.

When Iran cut the internet Thursday night, human rights groups knew what was coming.

The Death Toll: “Shoot to Kill” - 3,000 Feared Dead (Updated January 13)

Even with the blackout, information leaked out. Some Iranians had smuggled Starlink satellite internet devices into the country over the past few years. Brief connections were possible. Videos made it to the outside world, though verification took hours or days.

What emerged was horrifying—and far worse than initially estimated.

Thursday-Sunday (January 8-11): Initial reports and verification efforts:

  • Iran Human Rights (Oslo): 192 killed

  • HRANA (Washington): 490 protesters + 50 security forces = 540 total

  • Center for Human Rights in Iran (New York): “Hundreds and possibly thousands of deaths”

Monday, January 12: The numbers continued rising as more information broke through the blackout.

Tuesday, January 13 - 12:01 PM ET: The New York Times published updated reporting with dramatically higher death toll estimates based on Iranian government sources and U.S. intelligence:

Iranian Government Officials (speaking anonymously):

A senior Iranian health ministry official told The Times that approximately 3,000 people had been killed across the country. The official sought to shift blame to “terrorists” fomenting unrest. The figure included hundreds of security officers, he said.

Another government official, also speaking anonymously, said he had seen an internal report that referred to at least 3,000 dead, and added that the toll could climb.

The New York Times noted: “If confirmed, the death toll would be among the worst in recent Iranian history.”

U.S. Intelligence Assessment:

Multiple American officials told The Times that U.S. intelligence agencies have conservatively estimated that more than 600 protesters have been killed so far. The agencies noted that “both the current protests and the crackdown are far more violent than those in 2022 or other recent uprisings against the government.”

Updated Human Rights Organization Counts (Monday, January 12):

HRANA (Washington) - updated toll: 572 protesters + 69 security forces = 641 total killed. The organization noted it had 500 additional cases awaiting verification, suggesting the true toll could exceed 1,100 based on their methodology alone.

Skylar Thompson, HRANA’s deputy director, explained the death toll “has jumped in fits and starts over the past two days, as people sporadically managed to get online and share what they had learned.” On Saturday, the group counted 70 dead. By Monday morning: 641. “There will most likely be another spike,” Thompson said, “once the group works through verifying 500 other cases.”

Center for Human Rights in Iran (New York) - Executive Director Hadi Ghaemi told The Times his organization chose not to publish a specific toll because “it has been unable to connect with enough people to follow its typical procedures of corroborating the accounts of local rights activists with those of victims’ families.”

But Ghaemi provided an estimate: “We can’t confirm detailed numbers, but all indications so far point to large-scale killings over the past few days. We estimate at least 1,000 deaths nationwide and potentially higher. But the current information is just the tip of the iceberg.”

Understanding the methodology differences: These organizations use different verification standards, explaining the wide range:

Iran Human Rights (Oslo) employs the most conservative methodology—only counts deaths verified through multiple sources including family confirmation, medical records, or video evidence. This produces lower numbers but higher confidence.

HRANA (Washington) uses broader verification—includes deaths reported by single credible sources (hospital staff, witnesses, local activists) even without family confirmation. This captures more deaths but includes some uncertainty.

Center for Human Rights in Iran (New York) aggregates reports from multiple sources and Iranian civil society, using “at least 1,000 and potentially higher” to reflect the range of credible but unverified reports emerging from the blackout.

U.S. Intelligence uses signals intelligence, satellite imagery, hospital records access, and other classified methods to produce “conservative estimate” of 600+ protesters killed—not including security forces.

Iranian Government Officials citing internal reports of 3,000 represents the highest estimate and, crucially, comes from regime sources who have every incentive to minimize rather than exaggerate casualties. That they’re acknowledging 3,000 dead—even while blaming “terrorists”—suggests the true toll may be at or above this figure.

What “Shoot to Kill” Means:

The Center for Human Rights in Iran released testimony Monday from a doctor who has been treating patients in Tehran and Isfahan since the crackdown began. The group withheld his name for safety.

Earlier in the protests, the doctor said, protesters were being treated for tear gas exposure and pellet gun wounds. Then on Thursday, the doctor said, he started hearing heavy machine gunfire from the hospital.

“This was a mass-casualty situation,” the doctor said in his account. “Our facilities, space and personnel were far below the number of injured people arriving. The trauma cases I saw were brutal, shoot to kill.”

The truth likely lies between HRANA’s verified 641, U.S. intelligence’s 600+, CHRI’s estimated 1,000+, and Iranian officials’ internal reports of 3,000. Even the most conservative verified count represents a massacre by any standard. If the 3,000 figure is accurate, this is one of the deadliest government crackdowns in modern Iranian history.

Hadi Ghaemi, director of the Center for Human Rights in Iran, explained the verification challenge: “The violence has certainly skyrocketed in the past several days as the internet shutdown has been implemented. There are hundreds and possibly thousands of deaths. It will only become clear when the internet shutdown ends.“

BBC Persian, drawing on “informed sources” at hospitals, reported 110 bodies had been transferred to facilities in Tehran and the northern city of Rasht. Doctors told the outlet people were arriving with bullet wounds to the head, neck, and eyes.

The Videos That Escaped: Evidence of Massacre (Updated January 13)

Despite the blackout, several videos made it out and were verified by major news organizations. The Times also managed to reach witnesses by phone who provided firsthand accounts:

Video 1 - Kahrizak Forensics Center (Sunday, January 11): The New York Times verified footage showing “dozens of what appeared to be black body bags, lined up on the ground or on stretchers outside the Forensic Diagnostic and Laboratory Centre in Kahrizak,” a city on Tehran’s outskirts.

The video showed large crowds of people around the body bags. Some unzipped them, trying to identify loved ones. Others crouched or lay on the ground, weeping. Still others offered comfort to families who’d just learned their child, spouse, or parent was dead.

This wasn’t a normal morgue procedure. This was mass casualty processing. Bodies stacked outside because the facility couldn’t handle the volume.

Witness Testimony - Yasi (Friday night, Tehran):

Yasi, who is in her 30s and works for a publishing company, told The Times she was marching along Andarzgoo Boulevard in Tehran on Friday night with friends when security forces stormed in and shot a teenage boy in the leg as his mother looked on.

“My son! My son! They shot my son!” the woman cried, Yasi said.

Witness Testimony - Saeed (Tehran businessman):

“I managed to get connected for a few minutes just to say it’s a blood bath here,” Saeed, a businessman in Tehran, told The Times via Starlink internet connection late on Sunday.

“I personally saw a young man get shot in the head,” he told The Times in recorded audio messages. “I witnessed someone get shot with a bullet to the knee. The person fell to the ground unconscious, and then security forces gathered over him.”

Saeed, who had participated in protests in 2022 and before, said this crackdown is “unlike any of the protests that came before.”

“People are not afraid anymore,” he said.

Video 2 - Tehran Protests (Friday-Saturday): BBC Persian and Radio Farda, Persian-language outlets based abroad, verified videos showing huge crowds in Tehran, Mashhad, and Yazd. Chants calling for an end to the Islamic Republic. Praise for Iran’s deposed monarchy. Buildings set on fire.

Video 3 - Tehranpars Police Station (January 9): A six-minute video verified by The Times showed security forces firing “a hail of bullets at protesters in eastern Tehran.” The video “captures the sound of hundreds of gunshots and shows the muzzle blasts of weapons atop the police station in Tehranpars. At one point in the video, a person is dragged inside the courtyard of the police station.”

Video 4 - Monday Night Protests: Videos posted to social media Monday night and verified by The Times “showed a large crowd of protesters in Tehran. The sound of gunfire could be heard, and the cry: ‘Death to the dictator!’”

Video 5 - Gunfire Evidence: Radio Farda published audio from an eyewitness in Kerman province who reported “hundreds of instances of gunfire” Friday evening.

The verification process was painstaking—checking timestamps, geolocating landmarks, cross-referencing multiple sources. But the conclusion was unavoidable: Security forces were killing protesters at scale.

How They Killed: The Methods of Repression (Updated January 13)

According to human rights organizations and witnesses who managed to communicate despite the blackout, Iran’s security forces employed escalating violence. The New York Times spoke with nine residents of Tehran, along with two doctors and a nurse working at government hospitals, who described the regime’s tactics firsthand.

Snipers: Protesters reported being shot from rooftops and high buildings. Multiple witnesses told The Times they had seen snipers firing down at crowds in the Sattarkhan and Pasvaran neighborhoods of Tehran. Precision targeting of protest leaders and vocal demonstrators.

Military Rifles and Machine Guns: Not just riot police with less-lethal weapons. Military-grade firearms used against civilians. A Tehran resident who went to Sattarkhan with his wife on Saturday night witnessed “security forces open a barrage of gunfire with machine guns into a crowd of young men and women. They dropped to the ground on top of one another,” he said.

Videos verified by The Times showed security forces firing “a hail of bullets at protesters in eastern Tehran.” One six-minute video dated January 9 captured “the sound of hundreds of gunshots” and showed “muzzle blasts of weapons atop the police station in Tehranpars.”

Indiscriminate Shooting: One witness recounted “a security agent in the Aghdasieh neighborhood indiscriminately shooting at the crowd as he drove by.”

Close-Range Execution-Style Killings: From The Times reporting: “Four security agents swarmed a middle-aged man and his teenage son who had been standing at a corner, cheering on the crowd and joining in the antigovernment chants. The officers opened fire, killing the father,” said Parisa, a 35-year-old Tehran resident who witnessed the Friday night violence. “Perhaps the security forces had believed he was reaching for a weapon. But she saw none.”

At Shohada Hospital in the Tajrish neighborhood of Tehran, a doctor told The Times that “many protesters taken there were declared dead upon their arrival at the hospital, and that many had been shot at a close range in the head, neck, lungs and heart.”

Surveillance Drones: Used to identify protest organizers and track movements.

Metal Pellets and Rubber Bullets Aimed at Eyes: A particularly cruel tactic documented in previous Iranian protests. Security forces specifically target protesters’ eyes to blind and terrorize.

Gunfire on Empty Streets: Videos verified by The Times “show armed men shooting down empty streets in two other Iranian cities, an apparent effort to intimidate residents and would-be protesters.”

Revolutionary Guards Deployment: The regime moved beyond regular police to Iran’s ideological military force. Revolutionary Guards plus paramilitary forces “roaming streets in large numbers” with machine guns, according to reports.

Stopping Cars and Pedestrians: Systematic checkpoints. Anyone suspected of protest involvement detained or worse.

Power Cuts and Highway Lights Turned Off: Saturday night, cities went dark. Made it harder to document violence. Made protesters more vulnerable.

Tear Gas at Mosques: “Security forces also fired tear gas at worshipers exiting a mosque,” The Times reported.

Iran’s police chief, Brig. Gen. Ahmadreza Radan, acknowledged deaths but implied they were caused by agitators, not security forces. His evidence: “A significant portion of those killed died from bladed weapons and knife wounds. In cases involving gunfire, the shooting distance was very close, indicating that these actions were not carried out by security forces, but by trained and directed elements.”

This explanation strains credulity. Hundreds killed by mysterious knife-wielding assassins working at close range while security forces just happened to be present with military weapons? It’s the regime’s familiar playbook: Acknowledge the dead, blame foreign agents, deny responsibility.

The Hospital Crisis: Where the Injured Couldn’t Go (Updated January 13)

Perhaps most chilling were reports about hospitals, documented in detail by The Times’ Tuesday reporting.

The Center for Human Rights in Iran received “credible firsthand reports of hospitals in Tehran, Mashhad and Karaj filled with injured protesters.” But injured protesters faced a terrible choice:

Go to hospital and receive treatment → Get arrested Stay away from hospital and risk death → Remain free

The Scale of Injuries:

At Nikan Hospital in northern Tehran, a nurse told The Times that “medical workers had been overwhelmed when 19 gunshot victims came in almost at once.”

At Shohada Hospital in the Tajrish neighborhood of Tehran, a doctor said “many protesters taken there were declared dead upon their arrival at the hospital, and that many had been shot at a close range in the head, neck, lungs and heart.”

A doctor treating patients in Tehran and Isfahan described the progression: Earlier in the protests, protesters were being treated for tear gas exposure and pellet gun wounds. Then on Thursday, “he started hearing heavy machine gunfire from the hospital.”

“This was a mass-casualty situation,” the doctor said. “Our facilities, space and personnel were far below the number of injured people arriving. The trauma cases I saw were brutal, shoot to kill.”

Hospital Occupations:

Hadi Ghaemi reported that his team had “pieced together accounts from Karaj, west of Tehran, and Kermanshah, in western Iran, indicating that hospitals and clinics had been occupied by security forces who were searching for wounded protesters and collecting their personal information.”

Saeed, a Tehran businessman who managed brief Starlink connection, described similar experiences in the capital:

“They take the injured protesters to the hospital and if they recover, they arrest them,” he told The Times. “If their families arrive first, they try to somehow help them escape. The families who come to receive the bodies of those killed are forced into humiliating confessions: They have to say that the ‘terrorists’ have killed them.”

Skylar Thompson, HRANA’s deputy director, confirmed cases of authorities “removing injured protesters from hospitals.”

“We have hospital documents that show individuals have been impacted with tear gas, we have similar instances of people being hit with bullets—rubber bullets and live ammunition—and being transferred to detention facilities without proper care,” Thompson said.

This created a secondary death toll beyond those killed in streets. People with treatable gunshot wounds died because they couldn’t risk hospitalization. People with head injuries from beatings suffered permanent damage because they couldn’t access care.

The hospitals were “overwhelmed, blood supplies critically low,” according to CHRI. Bodies were “being piled up.” Emergency rooms couldn’t handle the volume even before factoring in protesters who stayed away out of fear.

This is how authoritarian regimes compound massacre: Kill openly in streets, then prevent treatment for survivors.

The Legal Threats: Death Penalty for Protest

Even as bodies accumulated, Iran’s legal system mobilized to threaten survivors.

Friday, January 9: Prosecutor General Mohammad Movahedi Azad announced prosecutors would act “without leniency, mercy or appeasement.”

The charge for protesters: “Enemy of God” (moharebeh in Persian).

This charge carries the death penalty in Iran.

It’s typically reserved for those who “wage war against God” through armed rebellion or acts deemed to threaten the Islamic Republic’s existence. But Iranian authorities have historically expanded its application to peaceful protesters, journalists, and activists.

The message was clear: We’re not just shooting you in the streets. If you survive, we’ll hang you legally.

Iran’s judiciary head, Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Eje’i, had warned earlier: “This time it’s different. This time there are no excuses left. I tell the people and the families that this time no one will be spared.”

According to HRANA, more than 10,000 people had been arrested since protests began December 28. These weren’t just overnight detentions. In Iran’s judicial system, arrest often means months or years in prison, torture during interrogation, and show trials with predetermined guilty verdicts.

Mass arrests plus death penalty threats plus hospital occupations plus internet blackout equals a comprehensive strategy to crush dissent through overwhelming force and fear.

The Government’s Narrative: Blaming “Terrorists”

As the death toll mounted, the Iranian government doubled down on its foreign conspiracy theory.

President Masoud Pezeshkian (Sunday, January 11): “The enemy has brought trained terrorists into the country. Those causing disturbances and riots are not protesting citizens.”

Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi (Monday, January 12): “A large number of the people who have unfortunately died in these incidents have been shot in the back by these people.”

Ali Larijani (Iran’s top security official): An “organized, violent group hijacked... legitimate economic protests to carry out extreme atrocities, killings, burnings, and [Islamic State]-like terrorist acts [that are] clearly unrelated to genuine public protests.”

Iranian diplomat (anonymous, to Washington Post): “It was actually the follow-up of the 12-day war,” referring to the June 2025 Israel-U.S. conflict. Israel intelligence officers were infiltrating protests and fomenting violence. “There was no other option for the Iranian government [but] to shut down internet to disrupt the communication of terrorist cells.”

The logic of the regime’s narrative deserves examination: Iranian state media (Tasnim, Fars, Press TV) constructed a detailed counter-story to Western accounts. Their version:

  1. Legitimate protests existed - Citizens had real economic grievances

  2. But protests were hijacked - Foreign-trained operatives infiltrated crowds

  3. These operatives committed violence - Set fires, attacked security forces, shot protesters from behind to blame regime

  4. Evidence: weapons seized - State TV showed stockpiles of firearms, Molotov cocktails allegedly found

  5. Mossad/CIA coordination - Linked to June 2025 war preparation, designed to destabilize during vulnerable period

  6. Internet shutdown necessary - Only way to disrupt terrorist communication networks

This narrative serves multiple purposes:

  1. Delegitimizes protesters - They’re not citizens with grievances, they’re terrorists

  2. Justifies violence - Killing terrorists is defense, not murder

  3. Rallies nationalist support - Foreign enemies attacking Iran requires unity

  4. Precludes negotiation - You don’t negotiate with terrorists

  5. Prevents accountability - Wartime actions aren’t subject to normal legal standards

The narrative’s weakness: Scale. Massive protests across all 31 provinces, involving hundreds of thousands of Iranians from every demographic, cannot be explained by foreign infiltration. Economic collapse, seven years of inflation, currency crash—these were real grievances driving real Iranians into streets. No amount of Mossad operatives could create that scale of mobilization.

But for regime supporters—particularly Revolutionary Guards who must justify massacring their countrymen—the terrorist narrative provides psychological cover. They’re not killing protesters. They’re eliminating foreign-backed terrorists. This framing allows violence while maintaining self-image as defenders of nation.

Three Days of National Mourning: The Regime’s Counter-Mobilization

Sunday, January 11, Iran’s government announced a three-day period of national mourning for “those killed by urban terrorist criminals”—meaning security forces who died fighting protesters.

President Pezeshkian called on the population to join a “National Resistance March” on Monday, January 13.

This was the regime’s attempt at counter-mobilization. If they could get pro-government supporters into streets, they could claim the “real” Iranian people supported the Islamic Republic. The protesters would be exposed as a small, foreign-backed minority.

Whether this counter-mobilization would succeed remained unclear. But the attempt revealed the regime’s recognition that it faced a legitimacy crisis. Violence alone might not be enough. They needed to demonstrate popular support, even if they had to manufacture it through coercion.

What the Senior Western Official Said: Inside the Regime’s Deliberations

Perhaps the most revealing information came from a senior Western official who briefed journalists on condition of anonymity about internal Iranian decision-making.

According to this official, who had access to intelligence about regime deliberations:

“Iranian authorities initially appeared reluctant to use lethal force, but once the protests threatened the regime, authorities shifted course.”

Supreme Leader Khamenei personally ordered the crackdown after protesters “who had started by voicing economic grievances began to call for regime change and demonstrations grew more violent.”

The official continued: “This is now unequivocally about regime change for many of the protesters, hence also the regime’s escalation into violence.”

The regime was “holding it together for now,” but only with “massive violence.”

Regarding potential regime defections—always the critical question in revolutionary situations—the official noted: “Some Iranian officials with whom he is in contact appear to be preparing for ‘the day after,’” but he “hasn’t seen credible reports of increased regime defections.”

This is crucial intelligence. The regime concluded:

  1. Protests genuinely threaten their survival

  2. Violence is necessary to maintain power

  3. No significant elite defections yet, but some hedging

  4. Internet blackout enables massive violence without immediate international response

The decision tree was simple: Use overwhelming force now, or risk collapse later.

They chose overwhelming force.

Trump’s Threats and the Red Line Question

Throughout the escalating violence, President Trump had repeatedly threatened intervention if Iran killed peaceful protesters.

January 6: “Locked and loaded” if Iran kills protesters January 9 (Friday): “If they start killing people like they have in the past, I think they’re going to get hit very hard” January 10 (Saturday): Briefed on “new options for military strikes” January 11 (Sunday): Asked if Iran crossed a red line, Trump replied: “It looks like it. There seems to be some people killed who weren’t supposed to be killed.”

Trump said he was receiving “hourly reports” on the crackdown and was “looking at it very seriously, the military’s looking at it. And we’re looking at some very strong options.”

Multiple U.S. officials confirmed to The New York Times and Washington Post that Trump had been briefed on military strike options, ranging from lethal force to nonlethal cyberattacks aimed at restoring internet access.

But as the death toll climbed into the hundreds, Trump’s response remained limited to threats. No strikes materialized. No cyberattacks restored internet. The Venezuelan precedent that had emboldened Iranian protesters—the expectation that Trump might actually intervene—was not repeating itself in Tehran.

Whether Trump was bluffing, whether he faced military/political constraints, or whether he was still deciding remained unclear. But with each passing day that American intervention didn’t come, Iranian protesters who’d taken to streets partly based on hope of U.S. support faced massacre without protection.

Why the Blackout Matters: Information as Weapon

The internet blackout wasn’t incidental to the massacre—it was central to enabling it.

Consider the counterfactual: What if Iran’s internet had remained online Thursday through Sunday?

  • Videos of security forces shooting protesters would have gone viral globally in real-time

  • International pressure would have intensified immediately

  • Trump’s threats would have required more immediate response

  • Iranian diaspora could have maintained contact with families, providing real-time death toll estimates

  • Protesters could have coordinated more effectively

  • Hospitals could have called for blood donations and medical supplies

  • Journalists could have reported deaths as they happened rather than days later

The fog of war favors the regime. When the world doesn’t know what’s happening, authoritarian governments can kill with relative impunity. By the time comprehensive information emerges, the political moment has often passed.

The blackout bought Iran’s regime time—time to kill protesters without immediate consequence, time to arrest thousands before they could flee, time to establish a narrative before contrary evidence accumulated.

As Hadi Ghaemi noted, we won’t know the true death toll “until the internet shutdown ends.” And the regime has every incentive to keep it offline as long as possible.

Conclusion: A Massacre by Any Definition

When historians eventually catalog what happened in Iran between January 8-12, 2026, they will use the word massacre.

Not because the regime admitted to mass killings—they blamed “terrorists.” Not because exact death tolls are confirmed—verification remains difficult. But because the evidence is overwhelming:

  • Sudden internet blackout coinciding with largest protests

  • Death toll estimates ranging from 192 (Iran Human Rights) to “hundreds and possibly thousands” (CHRI) within 72 hours

  • Videos of body bags stacked at forensics centers

  • Hospitals overwhelmed with gunshot wounds to head, neck, eyes

  • Security forces using military weapons against civilians

  • Deliberate targeting of protest leaders with snipers

  • Hospital occupations preventing treatment for injured

  • Death penalty threats for survivors

  • More than 10,000 arrested

This meets any reasonable definition of massacre: The deliberate killing of a large number of relatively defenseless people.

The internet blackout was designed to hide this reality. It partially succeeded—we don’t know if 200 or 2,000 died. But it couldn’t fully succeed. Some videos escaped. Some witnesses connected briefly via Starlink. Some information leaked through.

Enough to know that behind Iran’s internet blackout, the regime chose overwhelming violence over political reform. Enough to know that hundreds of Iranians—probably more—paid with their lives for demanding bread, dignity, and freedom.

The blackout may end. The internet may return. But the families mourning loved ones at Kahrizak forensics center, the injured protesters hiding from hospital arrest, the thousands detained facing death penalty charges—they’ll carry the cost of this massacre for the rest of their lives.

Iran’s government tried to hide what they did. They failed.

A massacre is unfolding. The world is watching. And the dead are being counted.

Horrifying Witness Accounts:

Photos emerge from protests, veiled by government blackout

In a separate incident, the same woman witnessed security forces raiding a small clinic where injured protesters were receiving treatment: "They went in, we heard shootings and they left. Then there were women and men shouting and crying. I went inside; they had killed the two injured protesters who had come to the clinic."

Starlink Jamming:

Iran has been using "military-grade jamming tools" to disrupt Starlink satellite internet—the primary way protesters have been communicating with the outside world and sharing evidence of the massacre. The jamming specifically targets users' ability to upload data, preventing videos and images from leaving the country.

With death toll estimates ranging from HRANA's verified 2,000+ to Iranian officials' acknowledgment of 3,000, and Trump's promise of imminent help, the crisis has reached a scale and urgency that represents the escalation many protesters hoped for and the regime feared most.
