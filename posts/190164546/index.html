The Iran Crisis

“The Collision”

Part XXXVI Day Seven: The Economics, the Alliance, and the 1953 Question

March 6, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 7, 2026

Illustration by The Atlantic. Source: Joe Raedle / Getty.

The photograph that illustrates Thomas Wright’s Atlantic analysis published Friday shows Donald Trump and Benjamin Netanyahu standing back to back, a red line bisecting the frame between them. Each man has his hand on the other’s shoulder. They are facing opposite directions.

It was chosen for the piece titled “America’s and Israel’s Goals Are Already Colliding.” It deserves to be the image that defines the week, because it captures the structural problem beneath everything else: two countries that launched this war together are now pursuing incompatible objectives within it, and neither has a mechanism to reconcile the difference.

This essay is about that collision — and about the economic catastrophe accumulating around it, and about the historical question that Vivian Salama and Jonathan Lemire raised in The Atlantic and that has haunted this entire series: the 1953 question. Whether Donald Trump, a man who has never demonstrated sustained interest in history, knows what Dwight Eisenhower knew he had done.

I. Two Wars Inside One War

Thomas Wright’s analysis is the clearest account yet of the divergence between American and Israeli objectives. It is worth working through methodically.

Trump’s preferred outcome in Tehran, Wright establishes, is a strongman who will cooperate with him on a peace deal and perhaps offer the United States a share of Iran’s oil industry. He has shown little interest in democracy or in cultivating exiled opposition figures such as former Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi. Instead, as Trump said on Tuesday, installing a new leader “from within” the regime “might be more appropriate.” The Venezuela model — Maduro removed, his second-in-command left in place as a pliable partner — is Trump’s explicit template. “It’s going to work very easily. It’s going to work like in Venezuela,” he told CNN Friday.

Israel is seeking something categorically different: the complete dismantlement of the Islamic Republic, not just a change of its leadership. Amos Hochstein, who served as Biden’s senior adviser for energy security and was deeply involved in Middle Eastern diplomacy, told Wright that Israeli officials fear any surviving version of the regime will simply rebuild Iran’s missile, drone, and nuclear programs once international attention moves on. For Israel, a functional successor regime — even a cooperative one — leaves the existential threat intact at one remove.

President Trump with Mr. Netanyahu after their meeting at the White House. Political analysts say Mr. Trump’s return to power has unleashed Mr. Netanyahu, removing guardrails that might have constrained his actions in Gaza, Lebanon and Syria.Credit...Haiyun Jiang for The New York Times

The divergence became visible almost immediately after the first strikes. The opening air campaign was so effective that it “knocked out most of the candidates” to replace Khamenei, Trump told ABC’s Jonathan Karl on Sunday. “It’s not going to be anybody that we were thinking of because they are all dead. Second or third place is dead.” Israel’s strategy produced this outcome deliberately. The Israelis are continuing to kill as many senior regime officials as they can, not because doing so advances Trump’s Venezuela model, but because it advances Israel’s goal of making any regime continuity impossible.

Trump needs a Delcy Rodríguez. Israel is killing everyone who could play that role.

The tension could resolve in one of two ways, and both are dangerous. If Trump declares victory and forces a ceasefire — which Hochstein suggested as a plausible near-term scenario, pointing to Iran’s degraded capabilities as the declared basis — Israel would almost certainly resist. Trump strong-armed Israel into accepting a ceasefire last June. He may attempt it again, this time in a context where Israel’s investment in the outcome is far greater and where the Israeli public has been told this is an existential moment. The political cost of Netanyahu accepting a Trump-imposed ceasefire before the regime is destroyed could be terminal for his government.

If the war continues until the regime actually fractures, Wright identifies what follows: civil war, refugee flows flooding into Gulf states already straining under Iranian bombardment, terrorism and insurgents crossing a Persian Gulf narrow enough to traverse in speedboats, and competing factions drawing in outside powers — Turkey, Russia, China — each with their own interests in who controls the ruins. “Israel couldn’t care less about the future or the stability of Iran,” Danny Citrinowicz of Tel Aviv’s Institute for National Security Studies told the Financial Times. “If we can have a coup, great. If we can have people on the streets, great. If we can have a civil war, great.”

From America’s perspective, Iranian civil war is the Iraq scenario Trump explicitly promised to avoid — the nation-building entanglement Hegseth dismissed Wednesday. Hegseth’s dismissal is already being contradicted by the CENTCOM 100-day planning horizon. The Venezuela model was never a model for Iran at all. It was a template borrowed from a 36-hour operation against a country of 30 million people with a single empowered interlocutor, applied to a country of 92 million governed by an interlocking system of clerical authority and Revolutionary Guard commanders who have spent decades preparing for exactly this moment.

The Kurdish dimension adds another layer of instability. The Trump administration is reportedly planning to arm Kurdish fighters and encourage attacks on the Iranian regime — a way to pressure Tehran without US ground troops. Wright notes that Ankara is currently engaged in a peace process with the PKK, a Kurdish militant group affiliated with the fighters Trump may now arm. If the US moves forward, Turkey could find itself facing another American-sponsored Kurdish enclave along its border, not unlike the Syrian precedent. Erdoğan might attack Kurdish forces inside Iran, or quietly coordinate with Tehran to contain them. Either course disrupts the fragile PKK peace process. The Trump administration, according to Wright’s Brookings colleague Aslı Aydıntaşbaş, initially reassured Ankara the war would last only four days.

From America’s perspective, Iranian civil war is the Iraq scenario Trump explicitly promised to avoid. It means indefinite entanglement. It means the CENTCOM 100-day planning horizon becomes a three-year planning horizon. It means the Venezuela model was never a model for Iran at all — it was a fantasy borrowed from a different operation in a different country with a different history, applied by a president who, as The Atlantic’s Missy Ryan put it, is “feeling his oats” in a second term without the Jim Mattises and Mark Espers asking about second- and third-order effects.

II. $92 and the Stagflation Trap

On the same morning Trump posted UNCONDITIONAL SURRENDER, the Labor Department released the February jobs report.

The US economy lost 92,000 jobs in February. The unemployment rate rose to 4.4 percent. The two previous months were revised downward: January showed fewer job gains than initially estimated, December showed net job losses. Rogé Karma in The Atlantic assembled the cumulative picture: 2025 appears to have had the most months with negative job growth since 2010, the depth of the Great Recession. GDP growth slowed from 4.4 percent in the third quarter of 2025 to 1.4 percent in the fourth, the lowest since COVID. Inflation was running at 3 percent in December, the highest since April 2024. The native-born unemployment rate has risen by half a percentage point since Trump took office, undercutting the administration’s deportation explanation for weak numbers.

Now add the oil.

Brent crude settled Friday at $92 a barrel — up nearly 30 percent in one week, the biggest weekly jump since the start of the COVID pandemic in April 2020. WTI crossed $90 as well, the highest in over two years. United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby said jet fuel costs have risen 58 percent since the war began; ticket prices will follow. The S&P 500 opened down 1.3 percent Friday, hammered by the simultaneous pressure of weak jobs data — which would normally call for rate cuts — and surging oil inflation, which makes rate cuts dangerous. The Federal Reserve cannot cut without worsening inflation. It cannot raise without worsening the already-weakening economy. The stagflation trap has teeth.

Todays Oil Price

Qatar’s energy minister Saad al-Kaabi told the Financial Times that oil could reach $150 a barrel within weeks. That translates to roughly $6 US gas. The current average is $3.32. The Karma calculation from The Atlantic’s morning piece: Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022 spiked oil to over $100 a barrel. The Iran war could produce the same result after a few more weeks, simply from inventory drawdown. If the Strait closure and energy facility attacks continue past that threshold, prices could go to $200.

“It’s hard to overstate just how big of a deal this would be,” oil analyst Rory Johnston told Karma. “It’s the mother of all tail risks — the boogeyman that keeps oil traders up at night.”

The boogeyman is awake.

Robert Worth’s Atlantic analysis of the Gulf states adds the structural vulnerability that makes this worse. The Arabian peninsula has no rivers or lakes. Every Gulf country depends entirely on enormous desalination plants on the coast — facilities that account for half the planet’s total desalination capacity, typically conjoined with power plants, making the complexes doubly valuable as targets and doubly catastrophic to lose. The UAE’s president was filmed calmly strolling through a Dubai mall during the opening days of the war, influencers pushing the message that Dubai was statistically safer than European cities even with missiles in the air. The reassurance campaign reflects a genuine vulnerability: Dubai’s economy rests entirely on its reputation as a haven for investment, tourism, and global commerce. That reputation is incompatible with sustained war.

Striking desalination infrastructure would be a war crime under Geneva, but not unprecedented: Saddam Hussein destroyed Kuwait’s desalination plants in 1991 as his forces retreated; Houthi rebels struck Saudi desalination facilities in 2022. The Gulf network has not yet been directly targeted. Worth’s sources in Gulf foreign ministries describe this as the scenario they fear most. Iran appears to understand this pressure point: its pattern of targeting UAE hotels, ports, and commercial hubs rather than military installations appears designed to squeeze Gulf states economically until they pressure Washington to end the war — the same logic Iran is applying with the Strait closure. Not military victory, but economic attrition until the Americans decide the cost exceeds the objective.

Qatar’s energy minister has already said that all Gulf oil and gas producers could be forced to stop production entirely within days. That is not a prediction. It is the energy minister of a major LNG exporter describing what he can see from where he is standing.

Karma’s 1970s parallel is precise and deserves to be stated plainly. At the beginning of the 1970s, the US economy was already struggling — inflation ticking up, growth slowing, unemployment rising from recent lows. Then the 1973 Arab oil embargo hit. Oil prices quadrupled. Everything priced in energy — which is everything — followed. Inflation hit double digits. Consumers pulled back. Businesses laid off workers. The Federal Reserve hesitated, making it worse. The crisis resolved only when a new Fed chair jacked interest rates to record levels, producing unemployment of 11 percent and a recession that lasted most of the decade.

“The current situation is not yet 1973 all over again,” Karma writes, “and it doesn’t have to be. The biggest difference is that this time the pain is mostly self-inflicted.”

GDP at its lowest since COVID. Jobs at their worst since the Great Recession. Inflation at its highest in nearly two years. Brent at $92 and rising. An energy minister warning about $150 oil and economies brought down. The Federal Reserve trapped between two directions it cannot move simultaneously.

The White House has reportedly considered a temporary gas tax holiday. It has considered deploying the military to defend energy infrastructure in the Gulf. Neither of these ideas addresses the structural cause, which is that the United States launched a war against the country that controls the world’s most important energy chokepoint, and now the chokepoint is closed, and the mathematics of what follows do not care what is posted to Truth Social.

III. The Oil Question Trump Won’t Answer

Salama and Lemire’s Atlantic investigation raised a question that, once you see it, you cannot unsee: why hasn’t Trump mentioned Iran’s oil?

Fatemeh Bahrami / Anadolu / Getty

He has treated every other intervention as a resource extraction opportunity. Iraqi oil. Syrian oil. Venezuelan oil — explicitly and repeatedly, including in the Mar-a-Lago press conference after Maduro’s capture. Seizing natural resources as repayment for American military action is not an incidental feature of Trump’s foreign policy worldview. It is a central one, long predating his presidency.

Iran has 209 billion barrels of proven crude-oil reserves — approximately 12 percent of the world’s total. Seizing control of Iranian oil, and combining it with Venezuela’s fields, would represent the most dramatic single expansion of American energy dominance imaginable. It would deprive China of a vital supply source. It would, by Trump’s own logic, be exactly the kind of deal he should be announcing with characteristic fanfare.

He has said nothing about it. Publicly.

The silence is more telling than the usual noise. Senior Arab officials told Salama and Lemire that Trump has been advised to focus his public comments on the military mission, given the gravity of a conflict that has already produced US casualties. “He knows it’s all very sobering,” one Arab official said. “It’s bigger than Venezuela.” White House aides, watching gas prices rise, are reportedly considering offsetting measures. Trump himself told Reuters “if they rise, they rise,” but those around him are not so casual about the midterm math.

The oil question Trump won’t answer publicly is the question Eisenhower eventually answered privately. In a diary entry dated October 8, 1953, Eisenhower wrote that the restoration of the Shah to power was “a development that we helped bring about,” noting the actions were “covert” and that the US would be “embarrassed” if the plot ever became public. The CIA operation — TPAJAX, or Operation Ajax — had been framed publicly as a Cold War necessity, containing Soviet influence. The actual driver was oil: Iran’s nationalist Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh had moved to nationalize the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, threatening Western petroleum interests. The US and Britain arranged a coup. The Shah was installed. American oil companies received significant access to Iran’s oil wealth for the next 26 years, until the 1979 Islamic Revolution swept everything away.

The revolution was fueled, in significant part, by Iranian rage at the US role in imposing the Shah — a role the US government did not officially acknowledge until Madeleine Albright’s statement in 2000.

Seventy-three years later, Trump is publicly announcing that he must be involved in selecting Iran’s next leader, bombing the country from the air, and maintaining public silence on 209 billion barrels of oil. The structural logic of 1953 has returned, stripped of its Cold War justification and its covert cover, conducted in broad daylight with an eleven-post rationale buffet and a Truth Social sign-off.

Operation Ajax was embarrassing only because it became known.

Operation Epic Fury cannot be hidden. The question of what it is actually for — the question Trump won’t answer — will be answered eventually, not by Trump but by the shape of whatever agreement ends it, by the contracts that follow, by who controls the faucet when the Strait reopens.

IV. “I Don’t Know How the War Is Going”

Illustration by The Atlantic. Source: Ata Kenare / AFP / Getty.

Eliot Cohen published a piece in The Atlantic on Friday titled precisely that: “I Don’t Know How the War Is Going.” It is the most honest thing written about this conflict by someone with genuine strategic expertise, and it deserves a hearing precisely because it resists the temptation to know more than anyone currently can.

Cohen’s core argument: the Iraq analogy, constantly invoked, is probably wrong in both directions. This war will not become a protracted ground campaign because neither the Trump administration nor any plausible logic supports sending an expeditionary force to Iran. But the confident-victory narrative is equally suspect, because air campaigns have brought down regimes before — Yugoslavia, Libya — without ground forces, and whether this one will produce the same result cannot be assessed from a week of bombing.

The questions he poses for proper assessment are more useful than any of the conclusions being offered: Have the strikes wiped out multiple levels of Iranian leadership or just one? Is Iranian command-and-control coherent or merely executing pre-laid plans? Are members of the Iranian regime reaching out to US allies through channels that don’t appear in press briefings?

His sharpest observation: “Bungling and competence can exist side by side to a frightening degree.” The Trump administration’s demonstrable incompetence in presenting a consistent rationale for the war — eleven rationales in seven days — does not necessarily spill over into the US military’s operational execution. Two things can be true simultaneously. The political leadership can be chaotic while the military campaign is effective. Or both can be chaotic. Or the military campaign can be generating its own complications that won’t be visible until later.

“Because the truth is,” Cohen writes at the close, “we do not” know how this war is going.

The MIGA post, Rationale Eleven, the bunker strike, the Qom evacuation warning, Brent at $92, the 100-day CENTCOM horizon, the Lebanon death toll at 200 and climbing, the man who cannot be named still unnamed — none of this resolves into a clear picture of how this ends. Hochstein thinks Trump may declare victory in a week or two and force a ceasefire. Wright thinks neither side can control how it ends. Sadjadpour thinks Tehran is betting on outlasting American public patience. Karma thinks the oil math will force Trump’s hand before public patience has time to become the variable.

All of them might be right about different pieces of it. All of them might be wrong.

“Every war is rich in unique episodes,” Clausewitz wrote. “Each is an uncharted sea, full of reefs.”

The reefs are accumulating. The charts are out of date.
