Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Introduction: The Blockade That Dare Not Speak Its Name

February 20, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

The oil tanker Ocean Mariner at the port of Havana last month. Cuba is facing the United States’ first effective blockade since the Cuban Missile Crisis.Credit...Yamil Lage/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

On February 11, 2026, a tanker called the Ocean Mariner was sailing through the Caribbean Sea, loaded with 84,579 barrels of fuel oil, headed for Havana. It had been a circuitous journey — the ship had loaded in Barranquilla, Colombia, then loitered off the Dominican coast for days, broadcasting a false destination while its operators weighed their options. Cuba was running out of fuel. The tanker’s cargo could keep hospitals open, water pumps running, schools lit.

Sixty-five miles from the Cuban coast, the Ocean Mariner made a U-turn.

It had spotted a U.S. Coast Guard vessel in pursuit. The next day, the Coast Guard pulled alongside and asked the tanker where it was headed. The Dominican Republic, the captain reported — an answer that fooled no one, given the ship was pointed the wrong direction entirely. The Coast Guard then sailed alongside the Ocean Mariner for nearly two days, escorting it away from Cuba, its cargo intact, its mission failed. An island nation of ten million people, 65 miles away, would go without.

A U.S. official, speaking anonymously to the New York Times, confirmed what the ship-tracking data already suggested: this was a blockade. Not the kind Washington was willing to name publicly — the word carries uncomfortable legal implications, potentially constituting an act of war under international law — but a blockade in every operational sense. U.S. military vessels policing Caribbean waters. Executive orders threatening tariffs against any nation that dares supply Cuba with petroleum. Mexico, Cuba’s last major supplier, standing down under American economic pressure. Venezuela, once Cuba’s lifeline, now effectively under U.S. control following the capture of Nicolás Maduro. Russia promising oil shipments that never materialize.

“Among us longtime Cuba watchers,” said Fulton Armstrong, former lead Latin America analyst for the CIA, “we’ve always resisted people using the word blockade. But it is indeed a blockade.”

Cuba’s fuel reserves, analysts estimate, could be fully depleted by mid-March 2026. Hospitals are already suspending surgeries. Schools are cancelling classes. Garbage is piling up on Havana’s streets. Trash collectors have no diesel. Water pumps are failing. Cubans are living through prolonged blackouts in the tropical heat, cooking gas is scarce, and food prices are soaring as the distribution network collapses.

Not the geopolitics. Not the chess match between Washington and Havana that has been running since 1960. Not the argument about whether the embargo is justified, or what Castro’s legacy amounts to, or whether the Cuban government deserves to survive.

The image that triggered this series is simpler and older than all of that: a people suffering. A people who have been suffering, in one form or another, for a very long time.

I am a Canadian. That matters here, and I want to be direct about why.

Canada has maintained unbroken diplomatic relations with Cuba since 1945 — through the Revolution, through the Missile Crisis, through the Cold War, through every American administration that pressed its allies to join the embargo and treat Cuba as a pariah. We declined. Not because Canadians have been blind to the Cuban government’s authoritarian character, but because Canada has generally believed that engagement serves people better than isolation, and that a small island nation 90 miles from a superpower deserves the dignity of being treated as a country, not a problem to be solved.

In January 1976, Prime Minister Pierre Elliott Trudeau became the first NATO leader to visit Cuba since the Revolution. He brought his wife Margaret and their four-month-old son Michel. Fidel Castro held that infant in his arms before the cameras, and the two leaders — both cerebral, both aloof, both quietly convinced of their own exceptionalism — discovered they genuinely liked each other. Trudeau addressed a crowd of 250,000 Havana residents and shouted “Viva Castro!” to the horror of Canada’s allies. Washington fumed. Trudeau was unmoved.

Fidel Castro was immediately taken with four-month-old Michel when he arrived with his parents in 1976, Margaret Trudeau remembers. The Ottawa Citizen

That friendship lasted until Trudeau’s death in 2000. Castro flew to Montreal for the state funeral, served as an honorary pallbearer, and was photographed embracing a grieving Justin Trudeau — then 29 years old, brother of the same baby who had been held in Castro's arms at the start of it all. When Justin became Prime Minister and visited Havana in November 2016, Castro was too ill to receive him. The old revolutionary died ten days later. Justin’s statement praising Castro as “a larger than life leader” drew considerable criticism internationally, but it reflected something genuine about the relationship between these two countries — a relationship built on the premise that you can disagree with a government and still respect its people.

Canadians have long since flocked to Cuba as a sun destination in numbers that would astonish most Americans. At the peak, Canadians accounted for more than 40 percent of all tourist visits to the island — 1.3 million Canadians per year choosing Varadero over Miami. It was, for a generation of Canadians, where you went for winter warmth: inexpensive, beautiful, friendly, and carrying the mild frisson of doing something the Americans couldn’t.

That calculus has changed. Cuba’s tourism industry has been devastated by a decade of compounding crises — hurricanes, the collapse of Venezuelan subsidies, COVID, the accelerating economic deterioration and now the blockade. The lights literally go out. And on the other side of the equation, Canadians are now staying away from the United States in growing numbers, as Trump’s economic aggression, hostile rhetoric, and the general atmosphere of his administration have made what was once the world’s most comfortable border feel unwelcoming. The irony would be almost funny if the stakes weren’t so serious: Canadians who once chose Cuba over Florida are now avoiding both.

We watch this from the outside, and what we see is not a foreign policy debate. What we see is people.

This series is about those people.

Not about the embargo as an abstraction. Not about the Cold War as geopolitical theatre. Not primarily about the Castro brothers or the Communist Party or the litany of American presidents who have each contributed their chapter to this particular standoff. Those things will appear — they must, because context matters — but they are not the subject.

The subject is what it has cost ordinary Cubans to live on this island across five and a half centuries.

Because the suffering did not begin with the Revolution, or with the embargo, or with the Missile Crisis. It did not begin with Batista or with the Platt Amendment or with the Spanish-American War. It began, as best we can trace it, in 1492, when Columbus made landfall and within a few decades the Taíno people who had lived on this island for centuries had been effectively annihilated — killed by disease, by forced labour, by the catastrophic violence of Spanish colonization. Cuba has been administered by outside powers for the near entirety of its recorded history. Spain for nearly four hundred years. The United States as colonial substitute for another sixty. And then the long standoff of the past six and a half decades, in which Cuba has tried to govern itself and has been punished economically for the attempt in ways that have fallen most heavily not on its leaders but on its people.

Honesty requires adding one thing. The Cuban government itself bears responsibility for a portion of its people’s suffering — a portion that has grown over the decades as the Revolution’s early idealism calcified into institutional rigidity, economic mismanagement, and the suppression of the very entrepreneurial energy that might have helped Cuba adapt. Centralized control, resistance to market reform, and the persecution of dissent have compounded the damage done by outside pressure in ways that cannot be attributed to Washington alone. This series will not pretend otherwise. But there is a difference between a government that has failed its people in certain ways, and a foreign power that has deliberately engineered civilian deprivation as a political weapon for sixty-five years. Both things can be true. In Cuba, both things are true.

The question animating this series is not whether the embargo is right or wrong, or whether the Cuban government deserves American punishment, or what Marco Rubio — whose family left Cuba in 1956, before the Revolution, and who is almost certainly the voice whispering Cuba policy into the current administration’s ear — hopes to achieve by tightening the vise until the fuel runs out.

Those questions will be addressed. The endgame matters. When a president who ordered the capture of Venezuela’s leader and has publicly discussed regime change in Iran orchestrates what an American intelligence official quietly confirms is a blockade, it is reasonable to ask what he expects to happen next. Cubans, historically, have not responded to strangulation by surrendering. But the people who bear the cost of that defiance are not the ones making the decision.

Single turquoise convertible leading the way at dusk, crowds on the wall, Havana glowing behind—personal, human-scale, and full of that Caribbean warmth amid struggle. lonelyplanet.com

The question at the heart of this series is older and simpler: Who are the Cuban people, what have they already survived, and what does it tell us about the nature of power that the most formidable nation in human history has spent sixty-five years trying to break an island of ten million people ninety miles off its coast?

We begin, as all Cuban stories must eventually begin, before Columbus. Before the Spanish. Before anyone who is still here.

We begin with the people who were here first, and with what was done to them, and with the pattern that was established then that has never quite stopped repeating.
