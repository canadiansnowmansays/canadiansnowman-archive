The Myth of the Tariff Golden Age

A Historical and Economic Analysis

Episode 5: Modern Evidence - Why Recent Tariffs Failed (And Are Failing Again)

June 26, 2025

Stephen Lawton
Jun 26, 2025

Welcome back to "Trump Tariff and Trade Thursdays." Last week we examined the Smoot-Hawley disaster of 1930. This week, we're analyzing the most recent large-scale tariff experiments: the 2018-2020 trade wars and the even more dramatic 2025 escalation currently unfolding. The results provide compelling modern evidence that validates historical lessons about protectionism's limitations—and show how those same patterns are repeating in real-time.

If Smoot-Hawley seems like ancient history, recent tariff experiences offer contemporary proof that the fundamental economics of trade protection haven't changed. Despite technological advances and global economic integration, tariffs continue to fail in remarkably similar ways to their historical predecessors. What's particularly striking is how the 2025 tariff escalation is following the exact same playbook as 2018-2020, just faster and at greater scale.

The Scale and Scope of Recent Tariffs (2018-2020)

The 2018-2020 tariff increases were substantial by modern standards, though smaller than what we're witnessing today:

  • Covered approximately $380 billion in imports

  • Average tariff rates increased from 1.5% to 7.4% across affected goods

  • Specific sectors faced much higher rates: steel (25%), aluminum (10%), washing machines (20-50%)

  • China-specific tariffs reached 15-25% on hundreds of billions in goods

Politicians promised these tariffs would bring manufacturing jobs back to America, reduce the trade deficit, and generate revenue to fund tax cuts. They claimed foreign countries and companies would bear the costs, not American consumers.

The 2025 Escalation: History Repeating at Unprecedented Scale

The current Trump administration has implemented the most aggressive tariff regime in over a century. From January to April 2025, average effective US tariff rates rose from 2.5% to an estimated 27%—levels not seen since the 1930s. Following economic turmoil that forced policy rollbacks, rates stood at 15.6% as of June 2025.

Key 2025 developments include:

  • Steel and aluminum tariffs increased to 50%

  • Introduction of 25% tariffs on imported cars

  • Unprecedented use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose broad, universal tariffs

  • "Reciprocal tariffs" targeting specific countries based on trade deficits

  • Federal courts ruling the IEEPA use unconstitutional, though decisions stayed pending appeal

Peter Navarro, recently released from federal prison for contempt of Congress, has returned as senior counselor for trade and manufacturing and is the key architect behind the "reciprocal tariff" policy.

Empirical Results: The Data Doesn't Lie (2018-2020 Evidence)

Comprehensive academic studies and government data provide clear evidence of tariff impacts:

Consumer Cost Pass-Through: Research by the National Bureau of Economic Research found that tariff costs were passed through to American consumers at rates of 95-100%. A 25% tariff translated directly into 25% higher prices for affected goods, confirming theoretical predictions about tariff incidence.

Total Consumer Costs: Studies consistently calculated that tariffs imposed costs of $40-50 billion annually on American consumers. This represented approximately $150-200 per household annually, with lower-income families bearing disproportionate burdens.

Revenue vs. Costs: While tariffs generated $30-40 billion in government revenue annually, they imposed $60-80 billion in economic costs, creating a negative return that undermined their attractiveness as a fiscal policy tool.

Early Evidence from 2025: Same Patterns, Amplified Scale

The 2025 tariff escalation is already producing identical results to the 2018-2020 experience:

  • Consumer Price Impacts: Toy prices are rising faster than ever due to China tariffs affecting 75% of imports in that sector

  • Corporate Earnings: Companies like General Mills are projecting lower annual profits due to weak demand amid tariff-induced economic uncertainty

  • Economic Warnings: JP Morgan analysts warn of a 40% chance of recession in the second half of 2025 due to current trade policies

  • Market Volatility: Financial markets are experiencing the most acute volatility since the pandemic

The Reshoring Mirage (Then and Now)

One of the most striking findings from 2018-2020 was how little production actually returned to the United States despite substantial tariff protection—a pattern already evident in 2025:

Supply Chain Redirection: Instead of reshoring to America, companies diversified supply chains to other low-cost countries including Vietnam, Mexico, Malaysia, and Bangladesh. This "trade diversion" maintained most cost advantages while avoiding tariff penalties.

Limited Manufacturing Returns: Manufacturing employment in protected industries showed mixed results. Steel and aluminum production added approximately 5,000-8,000 jobs over two years. However, steel-using industries—automotive, construction, machinery—lost an estimated 15,000-20,000 jobs due to higher input costs.

Investment Patterns: Rather than spurring domestic investment, tariffs often discouraged it by creating uncertainty about future trade policies and increasing input costs for American manufacturers.

Industry-Specific Case Studies (2018-2020)

Washing Machines: The washing machine tariff provides a perfect controlled experiment in tariff economics. Tariffs led to price increases of $100-150 per unit, costing consumers an estimated $1.5 billion annually while supporting approximately 1,500 domestic jobs—a cost per job of roughly $1 million annually.

Automotive Sector: Auto manufacturers faced higher steel and aluminum costs while benefiting minimally from reduced import competition. The sector experienced net job losses as higher input costs reduced competitiveness and profitability.

Agriculture: Farmers bore the brunt of retaliatory measures. Soybean exports to China fell by 95% during peak trade tensions, corn exports declined by 75%, and wheat exports dropped by 50%. These losses often exceeded any benefits to protected agricultural sectors.

International Retaliation: History Repeats (And Escalates)

Foreign governments responded to American tariffs with sophisticated retaliatory strategies that targeted politically sensitive exports—patterns now repeating with greater intensity:

2018-2020 Retaliation:

  • China's Strategic Response: Chinese retaliation focused on agricultural products from states that had supported tariff policies

  • European Union Precision: EU retaliation targeted iconic American products including Harley-Davidson motorcycles, bourbon whiskey, and blue jeans

2025 Escalation:

  • EU Threats: The European Union has vowed to hit US imports, including Boeing planes, with retaliatory tariffs if Trump maintains baseline 10% levies, with potential escalation to 50% duties

  • Negotiation Pressure: Trade talks are struggling with just weeks before major deadlines, as countries refuse to accept "asymmetrical deals"

  • Global Coordination: Trading partners are demonstrating greater capacity for sustained, coordinated retaliation than in previous episodes

Economic Modeling vs. Political Promises

Economic analyses consistently predicted the problems that materialized in 2018-2020 and are materializing again in 2025:

Trade Balance Effects: Despite promises to reduce trade deficits, overall American trade balances showed minimal improvement. Trade diversion meant that imports from targeted countries fell while imports from other sources increased.

Employment Effects: Economic models correctly predicted that job losses in export industries and downstream manufacturing would exceed gains in protected sectors. The net employment effect was negative, contradicting political promises of job creation.

Consumer Welfare: Studies accurately forecast that consumer costs would far exceed any benefits to protected industries, creating net welfare losses for the American economy.

Why Modern Tariffs Face Even Greater Challenges

Contemporary global economic integration creates additional vulnerabilities that didn't exist during historical tariff episodes:

Supply Chain Complexity: Modern manufacturing relies on intricate international supply chains that tariffs disrupt. American companies often import components for further processing, making tariffs taxes on domestic production rather than protection from foreign competition.

Retaliatory Capacity: Modern economies have sophisticated tools for targeted retaliation that can inflict political damage while minimizing costs to their own consumers.

Financial Market Integration: Uncertainty about trade policies affects investment decisions and financial markets in ways that can amplify economic costs beyond direct trade effects.

Legal Challenges: Unlike historical periods, current tariff policies face immediate constitutional challenges, with federal courts ruling some measures unconstitutional, creating additional uncertainty.

The Political Economy of Failure

Despite clear evidence of economic failure, tariff policies often persist due to political dynamics:

Concentrated Benefits vs. Dispersed Costs: Protected industries have strong incentives to lobby for continuation, while consumers face small individual costs that don't generate equivalent political pressure.

Visibility Bias: Job losses in export industries receive less media attention than job gains in protected sectors, creating asymmetric political narratives.

Sunk Cost Fallacy: Once implemented, tariffs create political pressure to persist rather than admit failure, leading to escalation rather than correction.

Ideological Commitment: Key figures like Peter Navarro represent true believers in protectionism who interpret any negative evidence as temporary adjustment costs rather than fundamental policy failures.

Lessons from Repetition

The 2025 tariff escalation represents a real-time natural experiment that validates every prediction from economic theory and historical experience. What makes this episode particularly valuable for analysis is the speed and scale of implementation, providing almost immediate empirical feedback on tariff impacts.

The patterns are unmistakable:

  • Consumer cost pass-through remains near 100%

  • Supply chain diversion continues to dominate reshoring

  • Retaliation remains precisely targeted for maximum political impact

  • Economic disruption scales with tariff magnitude

Most tellingly, even when policymakers have access to detailed studies of recent tariff failures, they repeat identical mistakes while expecting different results.

We'll explore judicial oversight, congressional authority, and institutional constraints that can limit executive overreach—and why these checks and balances remain essential for sound economic policy, especially given current legal challenges to unprecedented tariff expansions.

The good news: even when political rhetoric promises golden ages, institutional design can prevent copper-plated disasters.
