Unconditional Discharge

Part 21: Zero Punishment After Election Victory (2025)

February 17, 2026

Judge Merchan warned Trump: 'If you continue to violate my orders, I will have no choice but to impose an incarceratory punishment.' Then Trump won the election. Then Merchan gave him an unconditional discharge—admitting it was only because Trump was president.

Trump was convicted on May 30, 2024, on 34 felony counts of falsifying business records to conceal hush money payments to Stormy Daniels during the 2016 election—a scheme to defraud voters and interfere with the election. Judge Merchan scheduled sentencing for July 11—just four days before the Republican National Convention where Trump would be formally nominated for president.

That sentencing would never happen.

Instead, Trump’s legal team deployed every possible tactic to delay justice. What followed was a masterclass in how wealth, power, and political positioning can be used to evade accountability even after conviction.

Trump would ultimately be sentenced—but not until January 10, 2025, just 10 days before his inauguration for a second term. And when that sentencing finally came, he received no punishment whatsoever.

Delay #1: Presidential Immunity (July to September)

Trump’s lawyers immediately filed motions seeking dismissal or delay based on a Supreme Court decision issued in July 2024 that granted presidents broad immunity for official acts.

They claimed some evidence presented at trial—particularly Hope Hicks’ testimony about conversations she had with Trump while he was president—should have been excluded under presidential immunity. The fact that Trump was a private citizen when he committed the crimes apparently didn’t matter.

Judge Merchan postponed sentencing from July to September 18 to allow time for this argument to be resolved.

Delay #2: Avoiding Pre-Election Sentencing (September to November)

On September 6, 2024, Merchan further delayed sentencing to November 26—after the presidential election. The judge cited a need to avoid any appearance of political interference as Trump was actively campaigning as the Republican nominee.

So Trump, a convicted felon facing 34 felony counts, ran for president without being sentenced. Voters went to the polls not knowing what punishment, if any, he would face.

Delay #3: Post-Election Victory (November to January)

Trump won the November 2024 election. Suddenly everything changed.

His lawyers filed new motions seeking dismissal, arguing that sentencing would interfere with the presidential transition and his ability to govern. They leveraged his electoral victory as a reason to avoid accountability for his crimes.

On November 22, Merchan postponed sentencing again, pending additional filings on the motion to dismiss.

December Decision: Immunity Doesn’t Apply

On December 17, 2024, Merchan ruled that the Supreme Court’s immunity decision did not nullify Trump’s conviction. The trial evidence about Trump’s official acts as president was properly admitted because the criminal conduct—the falsification of business records—occurred while he was a private citizen during the campaign.

Trump’s immunity argument failed. But he still wasn’t sentenced.

The Final Sentencing Date: No Punishment Guaranteed

On January 3, 2025, with Trump just 17 days from inauguration, Merchan set a final sentencing date: January 10, 2025.

But in his order, Merchan made something extraordinary clear: he would impose an “unconditional discharge,” meaning Trump would receive no jail time, no probation, and no fines.

Merchan wrote he was seeking “finality” while respecting the “extraordinary” protections Trump would receive as president-elect.

Trump had successfully delayed justice until he could reclaim the presidency. At that point, the judge had no choice but to impose no punishment out of respect for the office.

Trump’s Last-Ditch Efforts

Even with a no-penalty sentencing guaranteed, Trump’s lawyers launched a final flurry of efforts to block it entirely.

On January 9, 2025, the Supreme Court declined to intervene in a 5-4 decision. The Court noted that “the burden that sentencing will impose on the President-Elect’s responsibilities is relatively insubstantial” given Merchan’s stated intent to impose an “unconditional discharge” after a brief virtual hearing.

Trump had run out of options. The sentencing would proceed.

The “Sentencing”: January 10, 2025

On January 10, 2025—10 days before Trump’s inauguration—Judge Merchan held the sentencing hearing via video conference. Trump appeared remotely.

Prosecutor Joshua Steinglass noted that each of Trump’s 34 convictions carried a potential sentence of up to four years in prison, but recommended unconditional discharge “out of respect for the office of the presidency.”

However, Steinglass didn’t hold back about Trump’s conduct. He called out Trump’s “coordinated campaign to undermine” the legitimacy of the trial, his calls for retaliation against those involved (including demands that the judge be disbarred), and the “enduring damage to public perception of the criminal justice system” Trump had caused.

Trump was given the opportunity to speak. He doubled down: “I was treated very, very unfairly,” he told the court. “The fact is, I’m totally innocent.”

He repeated his false claims that the trial was politically motivated and that the business records were legitimate legal expenses—claims the jury had already rejected.

The Sentence: Nothing

Judge Merchan then pronounced sentence. He acknowledged the “unique set of circumstances” and said “the only lawful sentence that does not encroach on the office of the president is that of an unconditional discharge on all counts.”

The protection of the presidential office, Merchan said, “is a factor that overrides all others.”

The judge was blunt about what this meant: “Donald Trump, the ordinary citizen, Donald Trump the criminal defendant, would not be entitled to such considerable protections.”

In other words: Trump received no punishment not because he deserved no punishment, but because he had successfully delayed justice until he could reclaim the presidency, and the office now shielded him.

Merchan noted that despite the “extraordinary breadth” of presidential protections, “one power they do not provide is the power to erase a jury verdict.”

As the judge wished Trump “godspeed in your second term in office,” Trump sat with his lips pursed, frowning slightly.

The hearing lasted just minutes. Trump was now officially a convicted felon with 34 felonies on his record. But he faced:

  • No jail time

  • No probation

  • No fines

  • No restrictions

  • Nothing

Trump’s “Victory” Declaration

January 10, 2025: Convicted felon, zero punishment. The expression says it all.

After the hearing, Trump declared victory on Truth Social: “The Radical Democrats have lost another pathetic, unAmerican Witch Hunt.”

His campaign characterized the unconditional discharge as proof the entire case was a “hoax.”

Trump filed his formal appeal on October 28, 2025, calling it “the most politically charged prosecution in our Nation’s history.” His lawyers argued the charges were “manufactured,” the DA was a Democrat targeting him during an election, and the case “should never have seen the inside of a courtroom.”

What “Unconditional Discharge” Means

An unconditional discharge is rare for felony convictions. It means:

  • The conviction remains on Trump’s record

  • He is officially a convicted felon under New York law

  • But no penalties, fines, probation, or conditions are imposed

  • He faces no restrictions on his activities

  • He cannot own a firearm under federal law, but as president he commands the nuclear arsenal

  • He can still vote in Florida (since his sentence is complete)

According to New York law, a court may impose unconditional discharge “if the court, having regard to the nature and circumstances of the offense and to the history, character and condition of the defendant, is of the opinion that neither the public interest nor the ends of justice would be served by a sentence of imprisonment and that probation supervision is not appropriate.”

In Trump’s case, Merchan made clear the sentence was based not on the nature of his crimes (which were serious) or his character (which showed no remorse), but purely on the fact that he would soon be president.

The Other Criminal Cases: All Gone

The hush money case was one of four criminal cases Trump faced at the beginning of 2024. It was the only one to actually go to trial. The others:

Federal Election Interference: Special Counsel Jack Smith charged Trump with conspiring to overturn the 2020 election. After Trump won the 2024 election, Smith closed the case, citing DOJ policy against prosecuting sitting presidents.

Federal Classified Documents: Smith also charged Trump with willfully retaining classified documents and obstructing justice. This case was also dropped after Trump’s election.

Georgia Election Interference: Fulton County DA Fani Willis charged Trump with trying to overturn Georgia’s 2020 results. The case has been in “legal limbo” and is effectively dead.

Four criminal cases. One trial. One conviction on 34 counts. Zero punishment.

The other three cases simply evaporated when Trump won re-election.

The Two-Tiered Justice System

The hush money case exposes a fundamental truth: America has two justice systems.

For ordinary citizens: Conviction on 34 felony counts means years in prison, permanent criminal record, loss of rights, inability to find employment, lifelong stigma.

For Trump: Conviction on 34 felony counts means... a delay, an appeal, no punishment, and another term as president.

As Judge Merchan explicitly acknowledged: “Donald Trump, the ordinary citizen, Donald Trump the criminal defendant, would not be entitled to such considerable protections.”

The Ultimate Accountability Evasion

The hush money case should be studied as a textbook example of how wealthy, powerful defendants can evade accountability even after conviction:

  1. Commit the crime (2016-2017): Falsify business records to cover up election fraud

  2. Delay investigation (2019-2023): Fight subpoenas, refuse cooperation, claim immunity

  3. Delay trial (2023-2024): File endless motions, change lawyers, seek dismissal

  4. Get convicted (May 2024): Lose unanimously on all 34 counts

  5. Delay sentencing (May 2024-January 2025): Use presidential immunity arguments, leverage election victory

  6. Avoid punishment (January 2025): Win presidency, receive unconditional discharge

  7. Continue appealing: Keep the case alive indefinitely while serving as president

This isn’t justice. This is justice deferred until it becomes justice denied.

The Voters Knew Argument

Trump and his defenders argue that voters knew about his conviction and elected him anyway, making the case moot. “The real verdict,” Trump said the day of his conviction, would come from voters.

But this fundamentally misunderstands criminal justice. Elections aren’t substitutes for trials. Popularity isn’t a defense. Winning an election doesn’t erase crimes.

The fact that American voters elected a convicted felon makes this more troubling, not less. It suggests criminality is no longer disqualifying—at least not for the politically powerful.

What Remains

While Trump escaped punishment, the historical record is clear:

  • Donald J. Trump was convicted by a jury on 34 felony counts

  • He is the first and only president—former, sitting, or future—to be convicted of criminal charges

  • He serves as president as a convicted felon

  • His conviction was for defrauding voters in the 2016 election

No appeal, no election victory, no unconditional discharge can erase these facts.

But Trump faces no consequences. No jail. No probation. No fines. He simply... governs.

This is accountability in Trump’s America: technically convicted but functionally immune, historically disgraced but politically empowered, legally guilty but practically untouchable.

The hush money conviction proved that even being found guilty on 34 felony counts doesn’t guarantee accountability—not if you’re wealthy enough, powerful enough, and shameless enough to run out the clock until you can reclaim the presidency.

The hush money conviction proved Trump could delay justice until the presidency itself became his shield from punishment. But the criminal case wasn't the only major legal judgment Trump faced in 2024.
