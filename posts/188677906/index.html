Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 12: What Cuba Tells Us — Empire, Endurance, and the Courage to Survive

April 1, 2026

The Ocean Mariner / Willem Oldenburg

We began this series with a tanker.

The Ocean Mariner, loaded with 84,579 barrels of fuel oil, making its U-turn sixty-five miles from the coast of Cuba on February 11, 2026. A U.S. Coast Guard vessel alongside. An American official, speaking without attribution, confirming what the ship-tracking data already showed: this was a blockade. The word nobody in Washington would say out loud, because saying it would require acknowledging that the United States was, for the second time in its history, encircling a ten-million-person island ninety miles off its coast and cutting off its supplies.

The first time was the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962. That one lasted thirteen days and was resolved by negotiation between the two superpowers who had engineered it. This one has no negotiated resolution in sight, and the Cuban people — who had nothing to do with the decisions that created either crisis — are once again bearing the cost.

We have traveled a long way since that opening image. Five hundred and thirty years, to be precise. From the Taíno greeting Columbus with gifts and curiosity in 1492 to the tanker captain who spent twelve days trying to find a way to deliver fuel to a starving island in 2026. It is time to ask what that journey tells us.

The Pattern That Doesn’t Change

17th century map of Cuba

The most striking thing about Cuban history, viewed across its full span, is the consistency of the pattern that runs through it. An outside power arrives with its own interests and its own certainty that what it is doing is justified — by God, by commerce, by civilization, by freedom, by strategic necessity. The people of Cuba are incorporated into the new arrangement as subjects, as labor, as geopolitical pawns, as collateral. Their welfare is not the organizing principle of the decisions being made about them. It is, at most, a secondary consideration, often invoked rhetorically while being ignored practically.

Spain came to Cuba to extract gold, then sugar, then trade revenue. It did not come to serve the Taíno, or the enslaved Africans, or the Cuban creoles who wanted self-governance. The United States came to Cuba in 1898 to establish strategic dominance over the Caribbean and protect American commercial interests. It did not come to honor the mambises who had spent thirty years fighting for independence, or to implement José Martí’s vision of genuine Cuban self-determination. The Soviet Union came to Cuba in the 1960s to establish a forward position in its competition with the United States. It did not come to serve Cuban socialism or the Cuban people’s aspirations. When the Soviet Union’s interests changed in 1991, the subsidies stopped — overnight, without warning, leaving Cuba to manage the catastrophic consequences of the Special Period alone.

The United States has spent sixty-five years trying to destroy the Cuban government through economic strangulation. It is not doing this to liberate the Cuban people. The evidence for this claim is simple: the policies that most directly harm ordinary Cubans — the embargo’s restrictions on food and medicine, the terrorism designation that cuts Cuba off from the international financial system, the current blockade that is causing hospitals to suspend surgeries — are the policies that have been most consistently maintained and periodically tightened. Policies that might actually improve Cuban living standards — normalized trade, unrestricted remittances, people-to-people contact, the kind of economic engagement that Obama briefly allowed — have been the ones most vigorously opposed by the hardliners who drive American Cuba policy.

The people who benefit from the current policy, on the American side, are not the Cuban people. They are the politicians who collect Cuban-American votes in Florida. They are the exile organizations whose identity and fundraising depend on maintaining a state of permanent confrontation with Havana. They are the ideological entrepreneurs for whom Cuba functions as a perpetual demonstration of communism’s failures, regardless of the degree to which American policy has contributed to producing those failures. The Cuban people are the instrument of a political argument, not its beneficiary.

What Cuba Has Taught the World

Against this history of external exploitation and manipulation, the Cuban people’s endurance is remarkable enough to deserve acknowledgment on its own terms — separate from any assessment of the political system they live under.

The Taíno were gone within a generation of Spanish contact. The enslaved Africans brought to Cuba’s sugar plantations survived by preserving, in secret and at great risk, the languages, religions, and cultural practices that gave their lives coherence and dignity. The mambises fought for thirty years against a colonial power that had defeated every previous armed challenge, and they nearly won before the Americans arrived to claim the victory. The Cuban people of the Special Period lost twenty pounds and kept functioning, invented urban agriculture from necessity, and maintained a health system that most countries with their resources could not have sustained through such a crisis.

This endurance is not the product of the Cuban government. The government deserves credit for the health and education systems that have given Cubans the tools to survive adversity, but the resilience itself is a cultural achievement that predates the Revolution and would survive it. It is the product of five hundred years of a people learning, generation by generation, what is required to persist when outside powers keep deciding your fate without consulting you.

Cuba has also taught the world something about the limits of what economic pressure can achieve against a population with strong cultural identity and genuine grievances against the power applying that pressure. The embargo has been the longest-running experiment in coercive economic isolation in modern history, and its results are unambiguous: it has not produced democratic transition, it has not produced regime change, it has not improved Cuban political freedoms, and it has not served the interests of ordinary Cubans. It has demonstrated, repeatedly and at enormous cost to innocent people, that you cannot starve a population into freedom.

The Cuban Government’s Responsibility

This series has tried to be honest about the Cuban government’s own contribution to Cuban suffering, and that honesty requires repeating it in the concluding installment.

The Revolution’s early achievements — in literacy, health care, and the elimination of the extreme rural poverty that characterized pre-revolutionary Cuba — were genuine and significant. They explain the base of popular support that the government has been able to draw on through decades of hardship that would have destroyed a purely repressive regime. A government that only coerced would not have lasted sixty-five years against the combination of American pressure and its own economic failures.

But the political repression has been real, sustained, and severe. Tens of thousands of Cubans have been imprisoned for political dissent over the past six decades. Freedom of expression, freedom of assembly, and freedom of the press do not exist. The 2021 protesters were given sentences of up to thirty years — sentences that were not an aberration but a demonstration of the government’s fundamental unwillingness to permit organized political challenge.

The economic mismanagement has been real and costly. The centrally planned economy’s inefficiencies, the dual currency system’s perverse incentives, the resistance to market reforms that might have improved material conditions, the corruption that has enriched party officials while ordinary Cubans queued for necessities — these are not the products of American pressure. They are the products of a political system that prioritizes control over competence and loyalty over expertise.

The Cuban government has used the American embargo as a shield behind which to hide its own failures. This is not speculation — Cuban officials have, at various points, effectively admitted it. The embargo is both a genuine cause of Cuban suffering and a convenient excuse for suffering whose origins are domestic. Separating the two is analytically difficult but necessary for honest assessment.

Both things are true: the embargo has caused enormous harm to ordinary Cubans, and the Cuban government has caused enormous harm to ordinary Cubans. The people who have paid the price for both are the same people. That is the essence of the tragedy.

The Canadian Reflection

Writing this series as a Canadian has required a particular kind of honesty about my own country’s position.

Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau is greeted by Fidel Castro after arriving in Cuba on Jan. 26, 1976. The Canadian Press/Fred Chartrand

Canada has done the right thing on Cuba for seventy years, by the measure of maintaining diplomatic relations and refusing to join an embargo that has been a documented failure as foreign policy. Canadian tourists have had genuine relationships with ordinary Cubans over four decades of Varadero summers, relationships that have treated Cuban people as fully human rather than as geopolitical abstractions. Pierre Trudeau’s Havana speech in 1976 was a genuine act of diplomatic courage that cost him something politically and that was, on balance, correct.

But Canada has not said what needs to be said about the 2026 blockade with the directness that the situation requires. The current Canadian government has not found, or perhaps not sought, the words that Mexico’s president found: you cannot suffocate a people like this.

There is a tradition in Canadian foreign policy — the tradition of Lester Pearson, of Pierre Trudeau, of Lloyd Axworthy’s human security agenda — that defines Canada’s international role partly through its willingness to say things that powerful friends prefer not to hear. That tradition has been honored inconsistently and has always been in tension with the economic and strategic dependence on the United States that constrains Canadian foreign policy freedom. The Trump era has made that constraint more acute: a Canada already locked in a damaging trade dispute with Washington has less latitude to antagonize the administration over Cuba.

But silence is a choice, and it has consequences. When Canada and the rest of Cuba’s friends in the international community allow a naval blockade designed to exhaust a civilian population’s fuel supply to proceed without clear and consistent condemnation, they are making a statement about the limits of international norms — about what powerful states can do to small ones without triggering the kind of response that might actually constrain them.

The United Nations has voted to condemn the embargo for more than thirty consecutive years, typically by votes of 180-something to two or three. Those votes have been entirely without practical consequence. The international community has, in effect, agreed that the American Cuba policy is wrong and agreed to do nothing about it. That combination of moral clarity and practical passivity is perhaps the most accurate description of the current state of international order.

Cuba’s Future

Predicting what happens next in Cuba is genuinely difficult, because the situation has so many variables in play simultaneously that reasonable analysts reach entirely different conclusions.

The optimistic scenario is some version of the following: the blockade creates enough pressure that the Cuban government enters into negotiations with Washington, makes sufficient concessions on political prisoners and economic reform to give the Trump administration something to call a victory, and the blockade is partially lifted. Cuba begins a gradual transition to a mixed economy under continued one-party governance — the China or Vietnam model — that improves material conditions while maintaining political control. The Cuban Communist Party survives but transforms. Ordinary Cubans see their living standards improve.

The pessimistic scenario is the following: the blockade causes fuel reserves to be exhausted, triggering a social and political crisis that the government manages through increased repression. The crisis produces mass emigration rather than organized political opposition. The economy contracts severely. Cuba enters a second Special Period worse than the first, from which recovery is slower and more difficult. A million more Cubans leave for the United States, Mexico, Spain, and wherever else will take them. The government survives, diminished and more repressive, because the population capable of organized opposition has left.

The catastrophic scenario is regime collapse followed by chaos — a power vacuum into which various factions compete, potentially including American military intervention to manage the transition, an outcome that historically has not served the populations it supposedly rescues.

None of these scenarios puts the Cuban people’s welfare at the center. That is not an accident. It reflects the actual structure of the situation, in which the Cuban people’s welfare has never been the organizing principle of the decisions being made about them.

The Last Word

José Martí portrait

The Cuban poet and national hero José Martí wrote, in 1895, the night before he landed in Cuba for the last time, of his fear that the United States would use Cuba’s independence struggle for its own purposes. He had spent his adult life inside the monster, as he called it, and he knew what it was capable of.

He died six weeks after landing, in his first real battle, before he could see whether his fears would be confirmed. They were.

Five hundred and thirty years of Cuban history is a story of extraordinary suffering and extraordinary endurance, of a people who have been subjected to the decisions of others since before anyone alive can remember, who have survived things that should not have been survivable, who have maintained their cultural vitality and their human dignity under conditions specifically designed to extinguish both.

The Ocean Mariner is still out there somewhere, full of fuel, being escorted from port to port by a Coast Guard vessel, unable to deliver what it is carrying to the people who need it.

The island it cannot reach has refused to kneel before every power that has tried to bring it to its knees. Spain could not do it. The United States could not do it across sixty-five years of embargo. The Special Period could not do it.

Whether the 2026 blockade will succeed where everything else has failed remains to be seen. What is not in doubt is who will pay the price while the world waits to find out.

It will be, as it has always been, the people of Cuba.

They deserve better than that. They always have.
