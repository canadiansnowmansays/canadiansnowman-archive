The Epstein Files

"I’m Not a Pedophile”

Part XXXIII: Trump’s 60 Minutes Meltdown

April 28, 2026

I. The Unprompted Confession

There is a concept in criminal interrogation called the “unprompted denial.” It is the moment when a suspect, responding to a question that does not accuse them of anything specific, volunteers a denial so precise, so targeted, that it becomes its own kind of evidence — not of guilt, necessarily, but of preoccupation. Of a mind that has rehearsed the accusation so thoroughly that it produces the rebuttal before the question arrives.

Sunday night. 60 Minutes. The Washington Hilton still smelling of spent ammunition and institutional panic. Norah O’Donnell sits across from the president of the United States and reads from the manifesto of Cole Tomas Allen, the 31-year-old Caltech graduate from Torrance, California, who the night before had charged a Secret Service checkpoint armed with a shotgun, a handgun, and knives.

“Administration officials, they are targets,” Allen had written. And: “I am no longer willing to permit a pedophile, rapist, and traitor to coat my hands with his crimes.”

O’Donnell asked for the president’s reaction.

Note what she did not do. She did not say these words referred to Trump. She did not say the manifesto named him. It did not, in fact, name him. The word “pedophile” appeared in a document about “administration officials.” There were dozens of administration officials at the dinner.

Watch what Trump did next.

“I’m not a rapist,” he said. “I didn’t rape anybody.”

O’Donnell, to her credit, let a beat pass. “Oh, you think — do you think he was referring to you?”

“I’m not a pedophile.”

Let that settle. A journalist reads from a manifesto. The manifesto does not name the president. The journalist asks for a reaction. The president immediately identifies himself as the person being described as a pedophile and a rapist.

Nobody made him do that.

II. “I Was Totally Exonerated”

The president continued. “You read that crap from some sick person? I got associated with all — stuff that has nothing to do with me. I was totally exonerated. Your friends on the other side of the plate are the ones that were involved with, let’s say, Epstein or other things.”

Let us take these claims in order, because precision is the enemy of bluster.

“I was totally exonerated.”

Exonerated by whom? The files that could exonerate or implicate him remain, in significant part, unreleased. The Department of Justice identified over six million pages as potentially responsive to the Epstein Files Transparency Act. It released approximately half. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche declared the January 30 release to be the final one — a position that Rep. Ro Khanna immediately disputed and that Rep. Thomas Massie, the Republican co-author of the act, has never endorsed.

More damningly: on February 24, NPR published an investigation finding that the DOJ had specifically removed or withheld Epstein files related to allegations of sexual abuse against the president. These included what appear to be more than fifty pages of FBI interviews with a woman who accused Trump of abuse when she was a minor — a woman the FBI interviewed four times. When NPR asked the White House for comment, spokesperson Abigail Jackson replied that Trump “has been totally exonerated on anything relating to Epstein.”

The phrase “totally exonerated” is doing extraordinary work here. It is being applied to a situation in which the man claiming exoneration controls the department withholding the documents that might prove or disprove the claim. It is like a defendant burning the discovery file and then telling the jury there is insufficient evidence to convict.

“Your friends on the other side of the plate are the ones that were involved with, let’s say, Epstein or other things.”

This is the deflection that has anchored Trump’s Epstein strategy since the files first became a political liability. It functions as a kind of universal solvent: any question about his own connections to Epstein can be dissolved by gesturing vaguely toward Bill Clinton, or Democrats generally, or “the other side.” That prominent figures across party lines appear in the Epstein files is well documented. That this fact constitutes exoneration of any individual named in those files is a logical fallacy so elementary it should come with a textbook citation.

“I’m not a rapist.”

As to this claim: in 2023, a federal jury found that Donald Trump sexually abused E. Jean Carroll. When Trump’s lawyers argued this finding did not constitute “rape,” Judge Lewis Kaplan clarified in writing that the jury’s finding — under the specific statutory definitions of New York’s penal code — did in fact describe what most people would commonly understand as rape. The jury found, the judge wrote, that Trump “in fact did exactly that.”

Trump has never been criminally charged with rape. He has been found civilly liable for sexual abuse in a proceeding in which his own lawyers participated, in a courtroom with a federal judge presiding, by a jury of his peers. Whether one calls that “exoneration” depends entirely on one’s definition of the word.

III. The Week That Converged

Now pull back. Because the 60 Minutes interview did not occur in isolation. It occurred at the end of a week in which the machinery of Epstein accountability was being systematically dismantled on multiple fronts simultaneously.

April 22: The Roundtable Gambit. The House Oversight Committee, which had been conducting the most consequential congressional investigation into the Epstein files, quietly replaced its formal hearings with informal “roundtables.” Rep. Jasmine Crockett, who sits on the committee, said it plainly: “They paused Oversight Committee hearings the minute we started getting answers about the Epstein files. Now it’s ‘roundtables’: no rules, no oaths, no subpoenas.”

The distinction matters. In a formal hearing, witnesses testify under oath. Subpoenas can be issued. Perjury is a crime. In a “roundtable,” witnesses can say whatever they like, or nothing at all, with no legal consequence. It is the investigative equivalent of replacing a courtroom with a cocktail party.

April 22–23: The Maxwell Pardon Trial Balloon. Chairman Comer told Politico that “a lot of people” on his committee were open to granting a presidential pardon to Ghislaine Maxwell — a woman convicted of sex trafficking minors — in exchange for her testimony. “My committee’s split on that,” he said. He added, without evident irony, that he personally opposed the idea because “it looks bad.”

Maxwell’s attorney, David Oscar Markus, told Politico there was “a good chance” of a pardon, even though no formal request had been filed. He had previously offered a curious formulation: “Both President Trump and President Clinton are innocent of any wrongdoing. Ms. Maxwell alone can explain why.”

Maxwell’s Lawyer David Oscar Markus

Read that sentence again. A convicted sex trafficker’s lawyer is offering, on her behalf, to absolve the president — in exchange for the president freeing the sex trafficker. This is not a legal strategy. It is a mutual insurance policy dressed in legal clothing.

Rep. Robert Garcia, the top Democrat on the panel, said committee Democrats were “united in opposing any pardon.” Epstein survivor Annie Farmer wrote to committee leaders: “Before you consider a pardon of a convicted sex offender and trafficker, imagine explaining it to your wife, sister, or daughter.”

Even Marjorie Taylor Greene — Marjorie Taylor Greene — called the proposal toxic, warning it would set up “a very potential quid pro quo.”

When Marjorie Taylor Greene is the voice of restraint on your committee, something has gone structurally wrong.

April 23: The Inspector General Opens an Investigation. Acting Inspector General William Blier announced an audit of the DOJ’s compliance with the Epstein Files Transparency Act. The probe will examine how records were identified, redacted, and released — and how the department handled complaints about the selective protection of powerful names while victim identities were exposed.

This sounds encouraging until you learn that Trump nominated his own candidate to replace Blier as permanent Inspector General just days before the audit was announced. Whether the audit will be completed — or continued — under new leadership is an open question. Former Inspector General Michael Bromwich noted publicly that an “audit” is a weaker instrument than a full investigation, calling it “an insufficient response to the Epstein mess.”

April 25: The Correspondents’ Dinner. And then a man with a shotgun ran at a security checkpoint, and everything else disappeared from the news.

IV. The Manifesto and the Files

Cole Tomas Allen

Cole Tomas Allen was a 31-year-old mechanical engineer turned schoolteacher. He had earned his degree at Caltech, completed a master’s in computer science, and spent six years teaching test prep to aspiring college students. His colleagues described him as smart and quiet. His employer once named him teacher of the month.

His manifesto, emailed to family members minutes before the attack, was addressed with the casual tone of a man who had already departed from the world of consequences. “Hello everybody! So I may have given a lot of people a surprise today.” He apologized to his parents for telling them he had an interview — “without specifying it was for ‘Most Wanted.’” He apologized to his students for the personal emergency that was not a personal emergency.

And then he laid out his reasoning. He wanted to target administration officials, he wrote, “prioritized from highest-ranking to lowest” — but “not including Mr. Patel,” apparently sparing the FBI director. He said he planned to use buckshot rather than slugs to minimize collateral casualties. He addressed hypothetical objections to his own actions and supplied rebuttals, like a man workshopping a debate case with himself.

He described the administration’s “crimes” as the catalyst. He did not elaborate on which crimes, beyond the words that O’Donnell would later read on national television.

Let me be clear about what this essay is not. It is not a justification. It is not a rationalization. The act of political violence is indefensible regardless of motive, regardless of target, regardless of whatever words a man puts in an email before picking up a gun. Full stop.

But the manifesto is relevant to this series for a specific reason: the accusations Allen referenced — pedophilia, rape, complicity in Epstein’s crimes — are not fringe inventions. They are the precise accusations documented in the FBI files that the DOJ has been withholding. They are the subject of the NPR investigation that found missing pages. They are the reason Pam Bondi was subpoenaed, and the reason Congress has been fighting for months to pry answers out of a department that keeps finding new ways to avoid giving them.

A disturbed man read the same files that the rest of the country has been reading and reached a monstrous conclusion. The correct response to that is not to stop reading the files. It is to answer the questions the files raise, so that people do not have to fill the silence with their own worst interpretations.

Silence is not a containment strategy. It is an incubation chamber.

V. The Media Curve

One more thing requires noting, because it speaks to the environment in which all of this is unfolding.

After the shooting, CNN’s coverage included a correspondent noting that Trump had shown admirable restraint: “I’m thankful that President Trump talked about that unity last night and did not try to heighten the tensions or escalate the situation. He could have very easily gone out there and bashed the press last night and blamed the media.”

PBS described the president as “somberly contemplative and unusually conciliatory.”

The bar, apparently, is now located in the sub-basement. The president of the United States did not blame journalists for attracting a mass shooter to a dinner celebrating the First Amendment, and for this he is to receive praise. He managed not to accuse reporters of complicity in their own near-assassination, and this is treated as statesmanship.

Then he went on 60 Minutes the next day and called the correspondent a “disgrace” for reading the shooter’s manifesto aloud. So much for the sombre contemplation.

The pattern is familiar to readers of this series. A moment of performed restraint, followed almost immediately by the reassertion of dominance and grievance. The legacy media, trained to reward any deviation from the baseline, treats the first part as news and the second part as reversion to form — as though the reversion is the anomaly rather than the other way around.

VI. What the Week Tells Us

Stand back far enough and the shape becomes clear.

In a single week, the Trump administration:

Replaced sworn Oversight Committee hearings with informal roundtables carrying no legal authority.

Floated the possibility of pardoning a convicted child sex trafficker in exchange for testimony — testimony that her own lawyer has previewed as exculpatory for the president.

Had its Inspector General announce an audit of the Epstein files process — while the president moved to install his own nominee to lead the office conducting that audit.

And then, when a violent attack briefly united the political world, the president went on national television and, unprompted, denied being a pedophile and a rapist, claimed total exoneration, and attacked the journalist who asked the question.

These are not separate stories. They are the same story, observed from different angles in the same seven days. The through-line is control: control of the files, control of the investigation, control of the testimony, control of the narrative.

The Epstein Files Transparency Act was supposed to end the era of strategic concealment. Instead, it has inaugurated a new one — in which the documents are technically released but selectively redacted, the investigations are technically ongoing but procedurally neutered, and the president claims exoneration on national television while his own government hides the evidence that could prove or disprove the claim.

VII. The Scorecard, Updated

Files released: ~3.5 million pages. Presented as full compliance.

Files identified as responsive but not released: ~2.5 million pages. Presented as unnecessary.

FBI interviews with a Trump accuser removed from the public database: 50+ pages. Discovered by NPR.

Inspector General audits launched: 1. Inspector General nominees waiting in the wings: also 1.

House Oversight hearings conducted under oath since March: 0. Roundtables with no rules substituted: ongoing.

Committee members openly discussing pardoning a convicted sex trafficker: “A lot,” per the chairman.

Attorneys general who oversaw the Epstein process: 2. Depositions completed under oath: 0.

Unprompted presidential denials of being a pedophile on national television: 1.

Number of powerful individuals prosecuted as a result of the files’ release: 0.

Survivors still waiting: All of them.

The Epstein Files series documents the longest-running exercise in strategic transparency in American legal history — in which everything is released, nothing is revealed, and the cover-up is the press release.
