Poisoned Water, Silenced Communities

Part 31: Trump’s Environmental Destruction (2025-2026)

March 24, 2026

The Woman Who Won, Then Warned, Then Watched It Happen Again

Erin Brockovich addresses a rally on Capitol Hill, April 23, 2014, protesting the federal government’s support for polluters in a landmark Supreme Court case. Two decades after defeating Pacific Gas & Electric, she continues fighting to protect communities from contaminated water—the same fight she would watch Trump systematically dismantle in 2025-2026. Getty Images

In 1996, a legal clerk named Erin Brockovich exposed Pacific Gas & Electric’s contamination of drinking water in Hinkley, California, with hexavalent chromium—a carcinogen that had poisoned an entire community for decades. Her investigation led to a $333 million settlement, the largest direct-action lawsuit in U.S. history at the time. The case became legendary. Julia Roberts won an Oscar playing Brockovich in the film that bore her name.

Map showing the spreading hexavalent chromium plume in Hinkley—site of Brockovich’s 1996 landmark victory—highlighting the long-term dangers of unaddressed pollution, now echoed nationwide under deregulated protections.
Source: San Francisco Chronicle / Lahontan Regional Water Quality Control Board

Twenty-eight years later, on July 30, 2024, Brockovich published an essay in The New York Times with a warning: the Supreme Court’s decision in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo—overturning the Chevron doctrine that had empowered federal agencies to regulate pollution—”weakens the ability of regulatory agencies to do their jobs protecting the public’s health from problems such as per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances.”

If Donald Trump won the 2024 election, she wrote, “Americans’ health will be at risk” as corporations challenged or ignored weakly enforced environmental protections.

Trump won.

And in 2025 and 2026, exactly as Brockovich predicted, his administration systematically dismantled the protections that had been built to prevent another Hinkley—protections against PFAS “forever chemicals,” toxic coal ash, and industrial contamination. His EPA gutted the very rules that gave communities a voice when polluters threatened their water, their health, and their children.

The woman who defeated Pacific Gas & Electric watched it happen all over again. But this time, the polluters weren’t hiding in the shadows—they were being protected by the President of the United States.

This is the story of how Trump used executive power to poison communities, silence public input, and grant immunity to the corporations contaminating America’s drinking water.

And the story of how, once again, no one was held accountable.

PFAS: The Forever Chemicals in Everyone’s Blood

Water droplets marked with “PFAS” labels show the persistent, accumulating nature of these forever chemicals in water—now left more vulnerable after Trump’s EPA rescinded standards for several compounds in 2025. Source: Shutterstock

PFAS—per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances—are called “forever chemicals” because they never break down. Introduced in the 1940s, they’ve been used in thousands of consumer products: nonstick cookware, stain-resistant furniture, food packaging, firefighting foam, waterproof clothing.

They accumulate in human blood. Studies show nearly all Americans now have PFAS in their bodies.

And they cause serious harm. Research links PFAS exposure to cancer (kidney, breast, pancreatic, prostate, testicular), liver damage, decreased birth weight and birth defects, decreased vaccine response, high cholesterol, thyroid disease, and infertility. The International Agency for Research on Cancer classified certain PFAS as “probably carcinogenic to humans” in 2015.

For decades, manufacturers like 3M and DuPont hid the health dangers from regulators, workers, and neighboring communities. When the truth emerged—through lawsuits, investigative journalism, and scientific research—the contamination crisis was already massive. Environmental Working Group estimates nearly 30,000 industrial polluters have released PFAS into the environment, contaminating drinking water for an estimated 158 million Americans.

Communities discovered they’d been drinking poisoned water for years.

And Erin Brockovich, who had spent her career fighting corporate contamination, recognized the pattern immediately. This was Hinkley all over again—corporate profits prioritized over public health, regulators failing to act, communities left to suffer.

But in 2024, under the Biden administration, something changed: the EPA finally set enforceable drinking water standards for six PFAS chemicals, including maximum contaminant levels for PFOA, PFOS, PFNA, PFHxS, GenX, and PFBS. The rule, finalized in April 2024, required public water systems to monitor for these chemicals and reduce contamination to safe levels by 2029.

It was one of the most significant public health victories in a generation—decades in the making, protecting millions of people from known carcinogens in their tap water.

Then Trump won the election.

And his EPA moved immediately to dismantle the protections.

September 2025: The Rollback Begins

On September 16, 2025, Trump’s Environmental Protection Agency announced it would keep drinking water standards for only two PFAS chemicals—PFOA and PFOS—while eliminating the standards for four others: PFNA, PFHxS, GenX, and PFBS.

The announcement came just days after the EPA had delayed a key PFAS reporting rule and barely a week after it had pledged to “address the most significant compliance challenges” with drinking water standards.

Instead of strengthening protections, the administration shifted the burden of PFAS contamination onto the American public.

Over 73 million people in the United States are served by water systems that have detected PFAS levels above the limits the EPA now seeks to rescind or delay. Those people would continue drinking contaminated water—not because the science changed, but because the chemical industry lobbied and the Trump administration capitulated.

Ken Cook, president of the Environmental Working Group, called it a “betrayal of public health at the highest level.”

Polluted, foamy effluent gushes from industrial pipes into a canal/river, representing the unchecked corporate contamination enabled by the gutting of NEPA reviews and public participation in 2025-2026. Source: Dreamstime

“You can’t make America healthy while allowing toxic chemicals to flow freely from our taps,” he said. “The EPA is caving to chemical industry lobbyists and pressure by the water utilities, and in doing so, it’s sentencing millions of Americans to drink contaminated water for years to come.”

The EPA also extended the compliance deadline for the two remaining standards—PFOA and PFOS—pushing it from 2029 to 2031. Two more years of contaminated drinking water.

Laurene Allen, founder of Merrimack Citizens for Clean Water and a 2025 Goldman Environmental Prize winner, was stunned.

“We are exposed as a class,” she said. “Keeping the focus on two legacy PFAS ignores the immense body of toxicological evidence the EPA has accrued. In an administration that claims they will ‘Make America Healthy Again,’ today’s announcement is an insulting action that shows how reality is being manipulated for profits.”

The rollback likely violates the Safe Drinking Water Act’s anti-backsliding provision, which requires that any revision to a drinking water standard “maintain, or provide for greater, protection of the health of persons.” Legal experts say the administration’s move—weakening protections based on industry pressure rather than new science—appears to violate federal law.

Trump faces no consequences for poisoning 73 million Americans’ drinking water.

The 97% Blackout: Hiding PFAS from the Public

The drinking water rollback was devastating. But Trump’s EPA went further: it moved to eliminate transparency about PFAS contamination entirely.

In 2019, Congress mandated that manufacturers and importers of PFAS report what they know about the chemicals in their products—information about identities and amounts of PFAS manufactured, known health and environmental effects, and how many workers are exposed. The reporting requirement, finalized by the EPA in October 2023, was scheduled to begin in April 2026.

It would finally force industry to disclose the PFAS contaminating our homes, workplaces, and environment.

But in 2025, Trump’s EPA proposed gutting the rule—creating exemptions that would shield over 98% of entities expected to have relevant information from having to report anything.

The proposed exemptions include:

  • De minimis exemption for articles with PFAS concentrations below 0.1%

  • Imported articles exemption

  • Exemption for PFAS manufactured as byproducts

  • Exemption for PFAS manufactured as impurities

  • Exemption for PFAS manufactured as non-isolated intermediates

  • Research and development exemption

Attorney General William Tong of Connecticut led a coalition of 15 attorneys general opposing the rollback.

“PFAS forever chemicals are a toxic menace to human health and our environment,” Tong said. “Trump’s latest effort to gut PFAS regulation is an insult to the families, workers, and especially the firefighters who have been disproportionately exposed to these dangerous chemicals.”

By slashing reporting requirements by 97%, the administration ensures that consumers, communities, and regulators remain in the dark about which products contain PFAS, which companies manufacture them, and which workers are exposed. Without data, there can be no accountability.

That’s the point.

Trump also terminated over $15 million in PFAS research grants—including a grant to Michigan State University that was canceled on April 29, 2025, one day after the EPA issued a press release saying it was “committed to combating PFAS contamination” by “strengthening the science.”

The administration claimed it would focus on PFAS. Then it eliminated the research funding needed to understand the chemicals and the reporting requirements needed to track them.

When contamination is invisible, polluters are untouchable.

Coal Ash: 300 Sites, Indefinite Delays

Vast coal ash disposal site with toxic sludge and leachate near waterways—Trump’s indefinite 2025 delay on cleanup reporting keeps these hazardous ponds endangering groundwater and nearby communities. Source: Inside Climate News

While gutting PFAS protections, Trump’s EPA simultaneously rolled back rules requiring cleanup of toxic coal ash—the waste left behind when coal is burned for electricity.

Coal ash contains arsenic, mercury, lead, selenium, and other heavy metals. When stored in unlined pits, berms, or landfills, it leaches into groundwater and contaminates drinking water supplies.

There are more than 700 coal ash disposal sites at over 300 power plants nationwide. Initial coal ash rules took effect in 2015, but those rules didn’t cover coal ash used to fill in earth, build up berms, or scattered about. Updated rules, finalized under Biden, required utilities to report by February 2026 whether they had coal ash in repositories that would be newly subject to regulation—and then to monitor groundwater and develop cleanup plans.

On July 22, 2025, Trump’s EPA delayed the reporting deadline indefinitely.

The delay affects sites like the Michigan City coal plant near Lake Michigan, where decades of coal ash accumulation threatens both the lake and groundwater. NIPSCO, the plant operator, should have been required to file reports and cleanup plans. Now it faces no deadline.

“Having the delay in some of those requirements is pretty devastating to hear,” said Ben Inskeep, program director of the Citizens Action Coalition in Indiana. “These are coal ash waste dumps that have been there for decades. For all this time, they are just leaching really nasty things into our water supplies, putting us in grave danger of a catastrophic failure of the coal ash, all that coal ash getting into our waterways or drinking water supplies.”

Lisa Evans, senior counsel at Earthjustice, noted that utilities had already been given years to comply. “The utilities were given years, and now they’re coming back for more, thinking this EPA will grant them more time.”

The EPA’s press release about the delays referenced “March 12, 2025, the greatest and most consequential day of deregulation in the history of the U.S.”—touting the rollback as an accomplishment.

Contaminated groundwater threatening drinking water near 300 coal plants. Communities at risk of catastrophic coal ash failures. And Trump’s EPA celebrates delaying cleanup as a historic achievement.

No one is held accountable for the contamination. The companies that created the toxic waste face no deadlines. The communities drinking poisoned water get no relief.

NEPA: Silencing Communities, Fast-Tracking Polluters

The National Environmental Policy Act—signed into law by Richard Nixon in 1970—required federal agencies to assess the environmental impact of major projects before approval. If the federal government wanted to build a highway, approve a drilling permit, authorize a new factory, or site a toxic waste incinerator, NEPA required an environmental review and public comment period.

For 50 years, it gave communities a fighting chance against projects that would harm their health, contaminate their water, or destroy their environment.

Trump eliminated it.

In July 2020, during his first term, Trump began dismantling NEPA requirements. His second term completed the destruction. Under the 2025 rollbacks, federal agencies are no longer required to:

  • Assess climate impacts of proposed projects

  • Assess cumulative pollution—the compounding harm of multiple pollution sources close together

  • Conduct environmental justice assessments of harm to marginalized communities

  • Provide meaningful public participation

  • Conduct thorough scientific analysis

Public participation has been limited. Review timelines have been rushed. Scientific analysis has been made optional rather than essential.

The administration frames this as “cutting red tape” and “modernizing” environmental review. In reality, it strips communities of their only legal tool to challenge projects that will poison them.

If a company wants to site a toxic waste facility in a low-income neighborhood, NEPA used to require environmental review and community input. Now the project can be fast-tracked with minimal assessment, rushed timelines, and token public participation.

If multiple polluters want to operate in the same community—creating cumulative exposure that individually permissible emissions don’t capture—NEPA used to require assessment of combined harm. Now each project is approved in isolation, and the community that suffers the compounded pollution has no recourse.

Erin Brockovich built her career on uncovering contamination that regulators had failed to prevent. NEPA was the legal framework that, after Hinkley, was supposed to prevent the next Hinkley. It required transparency, assessment, and community voice before projects moved forward.

Trump eliminated those requirements. Polluters can now operate with minimal oversight, rushed approvals, and silenced communities.

And when contamination inevitably occurs, there will be no accountability—because the system designed to prevent it no longer exists.

The Pattern: Poison, Silence, Protect

Across every category of environmental protection, Trump’s approach follows the same pattern:

Eliminate standards. PFAS drinking water limits for four chemicals rescinded. Coal ash cleanup deadlines eliminated. NEPA environmental assessments made optional.

Hide information. PFAS reporting gutted by 97%. Research grants terminated. Public participation limited.

Delay compliance. PFAS compliance pushed from 2029 to 2031. Coal ash reporting delayed indefinitely. Projects fast-tracked with rushed review.

Shift burden to victims. Communities drink contaminated water. Taxpayers fund cleanup when polluters aren’t forced to act. Victims bear health costs.

Protect polluters. Chemical industry gets reporting exemptions. Coal plant operators get indefinite delays. Corporations get fast-tracked approvals with minimal review.

The result is a wholesale transfer of environmental risk from corporations to communities. The companies that profit from contamination face no accountability. The people who suffer the health consequences have no recourse. The government that is supposed to protect public health instead protects corporate profits.

Erin Brockovich spent decades fighting this pattern. She defeated PG&E in 1996. She warned about Trump in 2024. And in 2025-2026, she watched as Trump systematically dismantled every protection that had been built to prevent another corporate poisoning disaster.

“There is a level of anger and frustration like I’ve never witnessed before,” Brockovich said in February 2026, responding to Trump’s environmental rollbacks.

Ken Cook, whose Environmental Working Group has fought PFAS contamination for years, was even blunter: “I can’t envision a bigger middle finger to every MAHA mom than this.”

Trump promised to “Make America Healthy Again.” Then he allowed 73 million Americans to continue drinking water contaminated with cancer-causing chemicals. He hid PFAS from public disclosure. He delayed cleanup of toxic coal ash threatening drinking water. And he eliminated the legal framework that gave communities a voice when polluters threatened their health.

The Accountability Void

No one has been held accountable for these actions.

Trump faces no legal consequences for weakening drinking water protections in violation of the Safe Drinking Water Act’s anti-backsliding provision. The EPA administrators who gutted PFAS reporting and delayed coal ash cleanup face no discipline. The chemical companies that lobbied for these rollbacks—whose products are causing cancer in millions of Americans—face no penalties.

Congress has conducted no oversight. Republicans celebrate the “deregulation” as economic progress. Democrats issue statements expressing concern, but file no articles of impeachment, pass no corrective legislation, hold no administrators in contempt.

The courts move slowly. Lawsuits challenging the PFAS rollbacks will take years. By then, millions more Americans will have been exposed. Communities will have suffered. Children will have been poisoned.

The public has normalized it. Environmental rollbacks barely make headlines. The poisoning of 73 million people’s drinking water is treated as a routine policy dispute rather than a public health catastrophe.

Erin Brockovich built her career on the principle that communities deserve clean water, that corporations must be held accountable for contamination, and that government exists to protect public health.

Trump has systematically destroyed each of those principles.

The companies that contaminated Hinkley’s water paid a $333 million settlement after Brockovich’s investigation. The companies contaminating America’s water in 2026 face no accountability at all. They’ve been granted immunity by the President of the United States, who celebrates weakening their regulations as a historic achievement while claiming to care about American health.

Nearly 158 million Americans drink water contaminated with PFAS. Over 73 million are served by systems exceeding the safe limits Trump just weakened. Hundreds of communities near coal plants face threats from toxic ash that Trump delayed cleanup for. And every community in America has lost the legal framework—NEPA—that gave them a voice when pollution threatened their health.

Erin Brockovich defeated one polluter. Trump protected them all.

When poisoning communities becomes presidential policy, and the President faces no consequences for it, what protection do ordinary Americans have left?
