Greenland When Allies Become Targets

When Allies Become Targets

Part 1: The Myth of Seward's Vindication

January 20, 2026

January 20, 2026

EDITOR’S NOTE: This is Part 1 of a 12-part investigative series examining President Trump’s campaign to seize Greenland. For context on this weekend’s mass protests and tariff threats, see “The Weekend Greenland Said No.”

The myth has seductive power. In 1867, Secretary of State William Seward purchased Alaska from Russia for $7.2 million and was mocked mercilessly. Critics called it “Seward’s Folly,” “Seward’s Icebox,” and “Andrew Johnson’s Polar Bear Garden.” The New York World declared it a “sucked orange.” The New York Tribune dismissed it as “a barren, worthless, God-forsaken region.”

History proved the critics spectacularly wrong. Alaska yielded gold, oil, strategic military positioning invaluable during World War II and the Cold War. Today, invoking Alaska has become central to justifying President Donald Trump’s campaign to seize Greenland. If shortsighted critics mocked Seward’s vision in 1867, the argument goes, won’t history similarly vindicate Trump’s “genius” in 2026?

“I think it’s a big opportunity and a new beginning for Greenland, with Trump’s interest,” Jørgen Boassen, Trump’s most prominent Greenlandic supporter, told The Atlantic. Trump himself framed it explicitly in transactional terms in 2021: “It’s like a real estate deal except it’s a lot lot bigger, obviously. I looked on a map. I love maps. I’m a real estate guy. It’s just like a corner store.”

But the Alaska precedent, examined honestly, doesn’t justify Trump’s Greenland ambitions. It exposes them. Every meaningful difference between the 1867 Alaska Purchase and the 2026 Greenland crisis undermines rather than supports the case for American acquisition.

In 1867, Russia sold Alaska to the United States for $7.2 million without consulting indigenous peoples, who had no recognized rights under international law. Territorial transfers through purchase were accepted practice.

In 2026, Greenland operates under Denmark’s 2009 Self-Government Act, with recognized autonomy, parliamentary democracy, and protected indigenous rights. The UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples requires “free, prior and informed consent” for any status change. 85% of Greenlanders oppose U.S. control.

The map shows the same Arctic region. The law governing it has fundamentally changed.

I. The Willing Seller Problem

The Alaska Purchase succeeded because Russia desperately wanted to sell.

By the 1860s, Russian America had become a liability. The Crimean War (1853-1856) devastated Russian finances and exposed military vulnerabilities. Alaska sat thousands of miles from Moscow, accessible only by arduous voyage around South America or overland across Siberia. The Russian-American Company that administered Alaska was failing financially, its fur trade declining as sea otter populations collapsed from overharvesting.

Most critically, Russia feared British seizure. Britain’s Royal Navy dominated the seas, and British Canada bordered Alaska directly. Russian officials calculated: better to sell to the Americans than lose it to the British for nothing.

Russia actively sought a buyer. When Seward expressed interest, Russian diplomat Baron Eduard de Stoeckl negotiated quickly and accepted Seward’s offer with minimal haggling. The entire negotiation took place over a single night session, with the treaty signed at 4 a.m. on March 30, 1867.

This was a motivated seller transaction.

Compare that to Denmark in 2026.

Denmark doesn’t want to sell. Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen has stated unequivocally: “Greenland is not for sale and will never be for sale.” This isn’t negotiating posture—it’s Danish constitutional reality and political consensus across the entire Danish political spectrum.

More fundamentally, Denmark legally cannot sell Greenland without Greenlandic consent. Under Greenland’s 2009 Self-Government Act, any change in Greenland’s status requires a referendum approved by Greenlanders themselves. The Danish constitution bars selling Danish territory without inhabitants’ approval.

And Greenlanders have spoken clearly. A January 2026 poll found 85% oppose becoming part of the United States. Only 6% support American control. Last Saturday, hundreds marched through Nuuk’s freezing streets—proportionally equivalent to 450,000 protesters in New York—carrying signs reading “Yankee, go home” and “No means no.”

Prime Minister Jens-Frederik Nielsen declared at the protests: “If we have to choose between the United States and Denmark here and now, we choose Denmark. We choose NATO, the Kingdom of Denmark and the European Union.”

The willing seller that made Alaska possible doesn’t exist for Greenland. This transforms the entire transaction from negotiation into coercion.

II. The Legal Framework Shift

In 1867, international law recognized territorial acquisition through purchase as legitimate sovereign right. Empires bought and sold territories routinely. The Alaska treaty required only Russian and American signatures—no consultation with Alaska Natives, no international approval, no consideration of indigenous rights.

The treaty’s Article VI explicitly transferred Alaska’s indigenous peoples as property: “The cession hereby made conveys all the rights, franchises, and privileges now belonging to Russia in the said territory or dominions, and appurtenances thereto.” Alaska Natives weren’t consulted. They weren’t citizens. Under 19th-century legal frameworks, they were effectively transferable assets.

That world no longer exists—and shouldn’t exist.

By 2026, international law has evolved fundamentally. The United Nations Charter (1945) established principles of territorial integrity and sovereign equality. The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (1966) recognized self-determination as a fundamental right. The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007) affirmed that indigenous peoples have “the right to self-determination” and that states must obtain “free, prior and informed consent before adopting and implementing measures that may affect them.”

These aren’t abstract principles. They’re binding international law that the United States helped create and repeatedly endorsed.

Crucially, the United States itself formally recognized Danish sovereignty over Greenland in the 1916 Treaty of the Danish West Indies. When America purchased what are now the U.S. Virgin Islands from Denmark for $25 million, the treaty explicitly stated that the United States recognized Danish sovereignty over Greenland. This wasn’t incidental—it was a negotiated term ensuring Denmark’s northern possession was secure.

The United States reaffirmed this recognition in the 1951 Defense Agreement, which grants America extensive military rights in Greenland while explicitly acknowledging Danish sovereignty. The agreement’s preamble recognizes “the sovereignty of the Kingdom of Denmark over Greenland.”

Trump’s Greenland campaign doesn’t just violate modern international law. It violates treaties the United States itself negotiated, signed, and has honored for over a century.

III. The Indigenous Voice That Now Matters

Perhaps no difference between 1867 and 2026 matters more than this: indigenous peoples now have recognized legal rights and political voice.

Alaska Natives in 1867 numbered approximately 30,000. They had no representation in negotiations. No vote on the transfer. No legal standing to object. The treaty transferred them along with the land—explicitly treating human beings as transferable property.

This was standard practice in the 19th century. It’s a war crime in the 21st.

Greenland’s 57,000 residents have self-government rights established through democratic process. The 2009 Self-Government Act grants Greenland authority over most domestic affairs, with foreign policy and defense remaining with Denmark. Greenlanders elect their own parliament, control education and health care, and possess the right to hold a referendum on independence.

Most significantly, Greenlanders have spoken—and they’ve said no.

The January 2026 poll showing 85% opposition isn’t close. It’s overwhelming. And it tracks with 2019 polling after Trump’s first Greenland purchase attempt: 67% of Greenlanders opposed then, with similar percentages rejecting American control while supporting eventual independence from Denmark.

Greenlanders want independence from Denmark—not subordination to America.

Naaja Nathanielsen, Greenland’s minister for business and mineral resources, testified before British Parliament last Tuesday: “People are not sleeping, children are afraid, and it just fills everything these days. For others this might be a piece of land, but for us it’s home.”

She added: “My deepest dream or hope is that the people of Greenland will get a say no matter what.”

International law in 2026 recognizes what it didn’t in 1867: Indigenous peoples have rights. Their consent matters. Their voices count.

Trump’s invocation of Alaska as precedent ignores that the very elements that made Alaska “legal” in 1867—treating indigenous peoples as property, ignoring their consent, transferring sovereignty without consultation—are precisely what makes Greenland seizure illegal in 2026.

IV. The Alliance Dimension

In 1867, purchasing Alaska from Russia involved no alliance betrayal. Russia wasn’t America’s ally. No mutual defense treaty bound them. No collective security arrangement was violated. The transaction occurred between two sovereign powers with no obligation to each other beyond the purchase terms.

Greenland in 2026 is fundamentally different. Denmark is a founding NATO member. Article 5 of the NATO treaty—the mutual defense commitment that defines the alliance—explicitly covers Greenland: “The Parties agree that an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or North America shall be considered an attack against them all.”

This clause has been invoked exactly once in NATO’s 77-year history: after 9/11, when European allies declared America’s fight was their fight. Forty-three Danish soldiers died in Afghanistan supporting that commitment. Seven more died in Iraq.

A photo of Sophia Bruun in military dress sits in her mother’s home west of Copenhagen. (Taby Cheng for The Atlantic)

Now the ally Denmark bled for threatens to seize Danish territory.

This isn’t just unprecedented in NATO history—it’s the alliance-destroying scenario NATO’s founders never imagined needing to address. Article 5 has no mechanism for one member attacking another. The alliance’s entire legal and practical framework assumes members don’t threaten each other’s sovereignty.

Wolfgang Ischinger, former German ambassador to Washington and chairman of the Munich Security Conference, wrote in Sunday’s New York Times: “An attack on Greenland would be the end of NATO. That would rob the United States of an unbeatable web of allies, offer rifts for our common enemies to exploit and eradicate the alliance’s collective moral high ground that has helped project American soft power across Europe and the world for decades.”

Alaska’s purchase involved no such alliance destruction. It threatened no collective security arrangement. It violated no mutual defense commitment.

Greenland does all three.

V. The Strategic Reality

Trump claims ownership of Greenland is necessary for American security—particularly to counter Chinese and Russian Arctic ambitions. “NATO becomes far more formidable and effective with Greenland in the hands of the UNITED STATES,” he posted Wednesday.

But here’s what Trump doesn’t mention: The United States already has everything Alaska’s purchase provided—and more.

The 1951 Defense Agreement grants America essentially unlimited military access to Greenland. Pituffik Space Base (formerly Thule Air Base) hosts critical early warning systems, space surveillance equipment, and strategic air operations. The agreement allows the United States to establish additional installations as needed, permits American military flights and naval operations, and provides the strategic positioning Trump claims requires ownership.

Denmark has accommodated every American request for expanded military presence. In 2023, Denmark invested $2.3 billion in Arctic defense capabilities specifically supporting American strategic interests: three new Arctic patrol vessels, two long-range surveillance drones, enhanced satellite capacity, 27 F-35A fighter aircraft compatible with American systems.

Pituffik Space Base

Denmark reached 3.22% of GDP in defense spending by 2025—far exceeding NATO’s 2% target—and committed to 5% by the early 2030s.

What would ownership add to this arrangement? Nothing strategic. Everything is already provided through cooperative agreement with a willing, capable ally spending heavily to support American interests.

The security argument collapses under scrutiny. America already has the strategic position, the military access, the allied partnership, and the defense investments Trump claims require ownership.

What Alaska’s purchase actually provided in 1867—sovereign control enabling unilateral military development—is exactly what the 1951 agreement already grants America in Greenland without the political cost of seizing allied territory.

VI. The 19th Century vs. the 21st

Seward operated in a 19th-century world of empires, colonialism, and territorial expansion. Russia controlled Alaska through conquest. The United States acquired it through purchase. Both actions were consistent with the international order of that era.

That world produced catastrophic results: World War I, World War II, colonialism’s horrors, indigenous peoples’ systematic oppression. The casualties of that 19th-century world order numbered in the tens of millions.

Those catastrophes taught lessons that shaped the post-1945 international order: territorial integrity matters, indigenous rights must be respected, alliances must be built on mutual consent rather than coercion, sovereignty cannot be seized simply because stronger nations desire territory.

The United States led the creation of that order—not out of altruism, but out of recognition that a world of predatory empires produces endless war. America championed the UN Charter, helped draft the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, built NATO as a collective security alliance where members don’t threaten each other.

Trump’s invocation of Alaska reveals fundamental misunderstanding. He sees Alaska’s eventual success and wants to replicate it. But Alaska succeeded because the circumstances of 1867 made it possible—circumstances that no longer exist and shouldn’t exist in a world that learned from colonialism’s horrors.

When White House Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller declared Monday on CNN that “we live in a world that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power,” he wasn’t describing the world America built after 1945. He was describing the world that existed before 1945—the world that produced two world wars, the world the United States spent 80 years trying to transcend.

Alaska’s ghost isn’t encouraging Trump to seize Greenland. It’s haunting him—warning that historical parallels can be historical warnings of what not to do.

Conclusion: The Precedent That Condemns

Every element that made the Alaska Purchase legitimate and successful is absent from the Greenland situation:

  • The willing seller? Denmark refuses, and legally cannot sell without Greenlandic consent.

  • The era when territorial conquest was acceptable? We built an international order specifically to prevent it.

  • The indigenous peoples who had no voice? Greenlanders explicitly reject American control with 85% opposition.

  • The strategic necessity? The United States already has everything Alaska provided through cooperative agreement.

  • The absence of alliance obligations? Denmark is a founding NATO ally that has bled for America.

When Trump looks at a map and sees Greenland as “like a corner store” he wants to own, he’s not channeling William Seward’s strategic vision. He’s revealing a real estate developer’s mindset utterly disconnected from 21st-century international law, alliance obligations, and indigenous rights.

Seward bought Alaska through legitimate transaction between willing parties in an era when such transactions were acceptable international practice. Trump wants to coerce Greenland through threats against an unwilling ally in an era when such actions violate every principle of the international order America built.

The Alaska precedent doesn’t validate Trump’s vision. It exposes how profoundly different—and profoundly wrong—his Greenland campaign actually is.

As those hundreds of Greenlanders marched through Nuuk’s frozen streets Saturday, carrying their flags and their signs reading “No means no,” they represented something Alaska Natives in 1867 never had: recognized rights, political voice, international law protection.

The world has changed. The precedent that justified Alaska in 1867 is precisely what condemns Greenland seizure in 2026.
