Part 14: January 6th — The Dismissal (2024)

January 22, 2026

Breaking: Mr. Smith’s testimony on Thursday January 22, 2026 was the summation he was not allowed to make in a courtroom. Credit...Kenny Holston/The New York Times

In a historic ruling, the Supreme Court said for the first time former presidents can be shielded from prosecution for at least some of what they do in the Oval Office.

On July 1, 2024—almost exactly one month before the Republican convention—the Supreme Court issued its decision in Trump v. United States.

The ruling was a massive victory for Trump:

Absolute Immunity for Core Constitutional Powers: Presidents have absolute immunity from prosecution for actions within their “core constitutional powers” (like directing the Justice Department).

Presumptive Immunity for Official Acts: Presidents have at least presumptive immunity for all “official acts” within the “outer perimeter” of presidential responsibility.

No Immunity for Unofficial Acts: Presidents can be prosecuted for purely private conduct.

Evidence Restrictions: Even unofficial acts cannot be proven using evidence of official acts.

The decision didn’t dismiss Trump’s case outright, but it required Judge Chutkan to conduct extensive proceedings to determine which of Trump’s actions were “official” (and thus immune) versus “unofficial” (and prosecutable).

This guaranteed there would be no trial before the November 2024 election.

Trump had won by delay.

The Timing: Perfect for Trump

The Supreme Court’s timeline was devastating for the prosecution:

February 28: Trump appeals to Supreme Court
April 25: Oral arguments (two months later)
July 1: Decision issued (another two months)

Four months consumed by the Supreme Court’s deliberation. The trial that could have happened in March or April 2024 was now impossible before the election.

And the decision itself created months more work: Judge Chutkan would have to hold hearings to determine which acts were official versus unofficial. Trump’s lawyers would file new motions. The litigation would continue for months.

Legal experts noted the Court could have decided much faster. It could have denied Trump’s appeal in February. It could have issued a narrower ruling more quickly.

Instead, the Court’s conservative majority gave Trump exactly what he needed: enough delay to reach election day without facing trial.

The Superseding Indictment: Starting Over

On August 27, 2024, Jack Smith filed a superseding indictment charging Trump with the same four crimes but adjusted to respect the Supreme Court’s immunity ruling.

The new indictment:

  • Removed allegations about Trump’s interactions with Justice Department officials (core constitutional powers = immune)

  • Emphasized Trump acting as a candidate, not president

  • Focused on private actors Trump enlisted in his scheme

  • Clarified that Trump’s conduct was unofficial

Trump pleaded not guilty to the superseding indictment on August 27, 2024.

But the damage was done. Judge Chutkan now had to hold hearings on immunity questions. Trump’s lawyers filed new motions. The case that had been scheduled for March 2024 trial was now mired in immunity litigation that could last months or years.

The trial would not happen before the election. Trump’s delay strategy had succeeded completely.

November 5, 2024: Trump’s Ultimate Escape

On November 5, 2024, Donald Trump won the presidential election, defeating Kamala Harris.

Trump’s victory transformed a difficult legal situation into an impossible one. Under longstanding Department of Justice policy, a sitting president cannot be indicted or prosecuted.

The policy is based on Office of Legal Counsel opinions from 1973 and 2000. Special counsel Robert Mueller had cited the same policy in declining to charge Trump for obstruction during the Russia investigation: “Charging Trump was ‘not an option we could consider,’” Mueller explained in 2019.

Now Jack Smith faced the same constraint.

Trump’s delay strategy had worked perfectly:

  1. Delay through immunity appeals

  2. Let Supreme Court consume months

  3. Prevent trial before election

  4. Win presidency

  5. Become immune from prosecution

November 25, 2024: The Dismissal

On November 25, 2024—exactly three weeks after Trump’s electoral victory—Smith filed motions to dismiss both the January 6th case and his appeal in the classified documents case.

“After careful consideration, the Department has determined that OLC’s prior opinions concerning the Constitution’s prohibition on federal indictment and prosecution of a sitting President apply to this situation and that as a result this prosecution must be dismissed before the defendant is inaugurated,” Smith’s team wrote.

Smith emphasized: “That prohibition is categorical and does not turn on the gravity of the crimes charged, the strength of the Government’s proof, or the merits of the prosecution, which the Government stands fully behind.”

Hours later, Judge Chutkan granted the motion and dismissed the case without prejudice—meaning Trump could theoretically be prosecuted again after leaving office in 2029.

But everyone understood this was the end. Trump would be president until January 2029, when he’d be 82 years old. By then, witnesses would have scattered, memories would have faded, and the political will to prosecute a former president would be gone.

Trump spokesman Steven Cheung declared: “Today’s decision by the DOJ ends the unconstitutional federal cases against President Trump, and is a major victory for the rule of law.”

The Orwellian framing—calling the end of prosecution a “victory for the rule of law”—captured Trump’s complete success.

Smith’s Final Report: The Case That Should Have Been

On January 7, 2025, Jack Smith filed his final report on his Trump investigations.

Volume I, covering the January 6th case, was released publicly. In it, Smith laid out his case against Trump in devastating detail.

According to legal scholars who reviewed the report, Smith concluded:

  • The evidence of Trump’s guilt was overwhelming

  • Trump would have been convicted had the case gone to trial

  • Trump’s intimidation tactics were systematic

  • The investigation amassed sufficient evidence to convict on multiple felonies

Smith addressed First Amendment concerns directly, explaining why he didn’t seek to prosecute Trump for his January 6th Ellipse speech (which may have incited the Capitol attack) but focused instead on Trump’s criminal conduct: organizing fake electors, pressuring state officials, attempting to block certification.

One legal scholar summarized Smith’s position: “Smith clearly believes that Trump was guilty of a number of serious crimes related to election interference and that he would have been convicted had Trump been tried for these offenses prior to his reelection. If Smith is correct, then Trump’s success in delaying the implementation of justice resulted in justice being denied to the American public.”

The Trial That Never Was: What a Jury Would Have Heard

If Trump had not successfully delayed trial, what would the evidence have been?

The Plot: A comprehensive scheme using fake electors, pressure on state officials, pressure on the Vice President, and exploitation of the Capitol attack to overturn election results.

Trump’s State of Mind: Extensive testimony that Trump knew he had lost but claimed fraud anyway. His own Attorney General, White House Counsel, campaign staff, and data experts all told him there was no fraud.

The Key Witnesses:

  • Mike Pence on Trump’s pressure campaign

  • White House staff who witnessed Trump’s conduct

  • Campaign officials who told Trump he’d lost

  • State officials Trump pressured

  • Justice Department officials who refused Trump’s demands

  • The fake electors who participated

  • Capitol Police who defended against the mob

The Documentary Evidence:

  • Trump’s tweets and social media posts

  • The January 2, 2021 Raffensperger call (recorded)

  • White House call logs

  • Text messages between conspirators

  • The fake elector certificates

  • Video of the January 6th attack

  • Trump’s public statements

The Timeline: November 2020 through January 6, 2021—a day-by-day reconstruction of Trump’s efforts to overturn the election.

Legal experts believed the case was strong. The main defenses (presidential immunity, First Amendment protection, “good faith” belief in fraud) had significant weaknesses.

Most importantly: a jury would have heard the evidence and made a decision. That’s how the justice system is supposed to work.

Instead, the American people never got to see the evidence tested in court.

The January 6th Defendants: Trump’s Followers Pay While Trump Walks

While Trump escaped prosecution, over 1,500 of his supporters have been charged with crimes related to the January 6th attack:

  • Assaulting police officers

  • Obstructing an official proceeding

  • Trespassing

  • Destruction of property

  • Conspiracy

Many January 6th defendants told judges they were misled by Trump. They expressed regret that they were “gullible” enough to fall for Trump’s falsehoods about a stolen election.

So Trump’s followers faced years in federal prison for acting on Trump’s lies, while Trump—who told the lies and organized the scheme—faced no consequences.

On January 20, 2025, President Trump pardoned approximately 1,500 people charged with participating in the January 6th attack.

The rioters who attacked police, broke into the Capitol, and hunted for lawmakers are now free. The president who allegedly orchestrated the scheme was never tried.

The Systemic Failures: How the System Failed

The collapse of the January 6th prosecution reveals multiple systemic failures:

1. The Slow Investigation

The Justice Department didn’t appoint a special counsel until November 2022—nearly two years after January 6th. This delay meant Trump was only indicted in August 2023, giving him time to run out the clock before the 2024 election.

2. The Supreme Court’s Delay

The Court could have resolved the immunity question quickly. Instead, it took four months from Trump’s appeal to its decision—ensuring no trial before the election. The Court’s conservative majority gave Trump exactly what he needed: delay.

3. The Impossibility of Prosecuting a Candidate

Trump announced his 2024 campaign in November 2022—shortly before the special counsel was appointed. From that moment, Trump could argue any prosecution was election interference.

But not prosecuting Trump meant letting him run while under indictment for trying to overturn the previous election—itself absurd.

4. The Sitting President Policy

The DOJ policy against prosecuting sitting presidents is defensible as constitutional prudence. But combined with Trump’s delay tactics, it created an escape route: delay until election, win election, become immune.

5. Presidential Immunity Doctrine

The Supreme Court’s July 2024 immunity decision was broader than anyone expected. By creating extensive immunity for “official acts,” the Court made it nearly impossible to prosecute former presidents for conduct related to their office.

What Should Have Happened vs. What Did

What Should Have Happened:

2023: Indictment announced
Early 2024: Trial begins
Mid-2024: Jury hears evidence, deliberates, reaches verdict
2024-2025: If convicted, Trump faces sentencing and appeals

What Actually Happened:

2023: Indictment announced
2024: Immunity appeals consume months, Supreme Court delays for months, decision requires new proceedings, Trump wins presidency
2025: Case dismissed under DOJ policy, evidence never tested

The evidence was extensive. The witnesses were ready. The charges were serious.

But Trump never faced a jury. Not because he was innocent, but because he successfully delayed until he won the presidency.

Conclusion: The Crime That Worked

Donald Trump allegedly committed the most serious crime a president can commit: attempting to overturn a democratic election through fraud, pressure, and violence.

The evidence of his conduct was extensive. Dozens of witnesses were prepared to testify. The documentary proof was overwhelming. The criminal intent was documented in real time.

And Trump will never face a jury.

His strategy was brilliant:

  1. Delay through pretrial motions

  2. Appeal on immunity grounds

  3. Let appeals consume months

  4. Run for president again

  5. Win the presidency

  6. Become immune from prosecution

The strategy worked perfectly. Trump wasn’t convicted—he wasn’t even tried. He went from defendant to president-elect, from facing prison to controlling the Justice Department.

The case’s dismissal sends a message to future presidents: if you attempt a coup, work hard to delay prosecution, and win the next election, you’ll never face accountability.

This isn’t the rule of law. It’s the rule of power and timing.

Trump committed the crime, organized the conspiracy, pressured the officials, exploited the violence, and escaped justice.

The crime worked. And the criminal won.

Postscript: Smith’s Confirmation for the Historical Record (December 17, 2024)

Two months after the case’s dismissal, Jack Smith testified for nearly eight hours before the House Judiciary Committee. On December 31, 2024, Congress released the full 255-page transcript. His testimony confirmed what the dismissal prevented a jury from ever hearing:

“Our investigation developed proof beyond a reasonable doubt that President Trump engaged in a criminal scheme to overturn the results of the 2020 presidential election and to prevent the lawful transfer of power.”

Proof beyond a reasonable doubt. The highest standard in criminal law. The standard required for conviction. Smith stated under oath that his investigation proved Trump’s guilt to that standard.

But Trump never faced trial. The Supreme Court delayed the case with its immunity ruling. Trump won the election. The prosecution ended.

Breaking: Public Testimony Under Fire (January 22, 2026)

One month later, Smith appeared publicly before the same committee—this time on camera, under intense Republican scrutiny, and facing real-time presidential attack.

Smith delivered the statement he was never allowed to make in court:

“Our investigation revealed that Donald Trump is the person who caused Jan. 6, that it was foreseeable to him and that he sought to exploit the violence.”

This went beyond his closed-door testimony. Smith wasn’t just stating Trump committed crimes—he was directly accusing Trump of causing and exploiting the Capitol attack.

As Smith testified, Trump posted on Truth Social calling for Attorney General Pam Bondi to investigate him and demanding his disbarment. The intimidation was immediate and public.

Smith’s response was equally direct: “I believe that they will do everything in their power to do that because they’ve been ordered to by the president. The statements are meant to intimidate me. I will not be intimidated. I think these statements are also made as a warning to others what will happen if they stand up.”

Before testifying, Smith greeted four Capitol Police officers who defended the Capitol on January 6: Michael Fanone, Daniel Hodges, Aquilino Gonell, and Harry Dunn. The gesture was symbolic—standing with those who protected democracy against the violence Smith says Trump caused.

Smith’s testimony also revealed the FBI, now run by Trump ally Kash Patel, is “scouring its vast holdings to root out negative information” about those who once investigated Trump—what critics call an opposition research operation using federal law enforcement resources.

And Judge Cannon’s protection of Trump continues: Smith’s detailed report on the classified documents case remains sealed by her order. That order expires February 24, 2026, but Trump’s lawyers filed this week to permanently seal it, arguing its release would “irreparably harm President Trump.”

Then Trump Took Revenge

After returning to office, Trump fired Jack Smith and his entire staff—punishing prosecutors for doing their jobs. Asked about Trump seeking retribution during his testimony, Smith was blunt:

“I have no doubt that the President wants to seek retribution against me.”

He added that the Justice Department under Trump now “wants to seek retribution against anybody who worked on cases against President Trump.”

Smith wasn’t speculating. The terminations proved it. Trump used presidential power not just to end his prosecution, but to punish those who had sought to hold him accountable.

The Ultimate Vindication of the Crimes

As if to underscore everything the January 6th prosecution had documented, Trump then embarked on a systematic pardon campaign that rewarded everyone involved in his attempted coup.

First came the riot defendants: over 1,500 Capitol riot defendants pardoned—including those convicted of assaulting police officers during the attack. Trump called January 6th “a day of love.” He claimed there had been “nothing done wrong at all.” He granted clemency to the very people whose violence his own pressure campaign had incited.

Then came the coup plotters themselves: 77 pardons for those involved in the fake elector scheme. These pardons reached all the way up to Trump’s former attorney Rudy Giuliani and his former chief of staff Mark Meadows—the architects of the plot to overturn the election.

The most revealing aspect? Most of these 77 people hadn’t even faced federal charges yet. These were preemptive pardons—Trump protecting his co-conspirators before prosecutors could even bring cases.

As retired federal judge Mark L. Wolf observed, the message was unmistakable: “Stick with Donald Trump and get your own get out of jail free card.”

The pardons served multiple purposes: rewarding loyalty, signaling to future supporters that crimes on his behalf carry no consequences, protecting co-conspirators from prosecution, and obscuring the historical record by treating the coup attempt as legitimate political activity.

Dismantling the Corruption-Fighting Infrastructure

But Trump didn’t stop at pardons and firing prosecutors. He systematically dismantled the very mechanisms designed to hold powerful people accountable for corruption.

Since Trump took office, 5,449 people have left the Department of Justice—an exodus of experienced prosecutors and investigators that gutted institutional knowledge and expertise.

More deliberately destructive:

The FBI’s Public Corruption Unit: Abolished. The unit responsible for investigating corruption by government officials—eliminated entirely.

DOJ’s Public Integrity Section: Decimated. Reduced from 30 lawyers to just 5, and stripped of all authority over election-related cases—the very cases where Trump and his allies posed the greatest legal exposure.

This wasn’t bureaucratic reorganization. It was systematic destruction of the infrastructure designed to investigate and prosecute political corruption. Trump eliminated the units that could investigate whether he, his family, his donors, or his allies were engaged in corrupt conduct.

Judge Wolf, who served as a federal prosecutor during Watergate and spent 40 years on the federal bench, put it starkly: “What Nixon did episodically and covertly, knowing it was illegal or improper, Trump now does routinely and overtly.”

Nixon understood he was breaking the law and tried to hide it. Trump breaks the law openly and then dismantles the mechanisms that would normally hold him accountable.

The Permanent Record

Smith’s testimony now stands as the permanent record: Trump committed federal crimes attempting to overturn a presidential election, the Special Counsel had proof beyond reasonable doubt, Trump knew his fraud claims were lies because his own people told him so, but Trump escaped prosecution by winning the presidency.

Then Trump fired the prosecutors, pardoned the rioters, pardoned the coup plotters, and systematically destroyed the DOJ’s corruption-fighting capabilities.

This wasn’t acquittal. It wasn’t vindication. It was the ultimate demonstration that in America, you can attempt a coup, delay justice long enough to win another election, face zero consequences, punish your prosecutors, pardon your co-conspirators, and dismantle the institutions designed to prevent future corruption.

Smith proved the crime. The courts delayed the trial. Trump regained power. The case died. The prosecutors were fired. The rioters were pardoned. The coup plotters were pardoned. The corruption-fighting units were abolished.

Not because Trump was innocent—Smith confirmed the evidence proved guilt beyond reasonable doubt—but because Trump successfully ran out the clock until electoral victory made him immune from prosecution and gave him the power to rewrite history while ensuring future crimes would go uninvestigated.
