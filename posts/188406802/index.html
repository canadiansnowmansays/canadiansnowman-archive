The Number That Should Terrify the GOP

Part 1: The Fort Worth special election that sent Trump into damage control

February 18, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays · Let’s Talk Texas · February 2026

Thirty-one points.

That’s the swing. In January, a 33-year-old machinist named Taylor Rehmet won a special election to the Texas State Senate in a Fort Worth-area district that Donald Trump carried by 17 points in 2024. Rehmet didn’t just win. He won by 14. Do the math: that’s a 31-point shift from presidential to special election in a district the GOP had held since the early 1990s.

And here’s the part that should keep Republican strategists awake at night: it wasn’t turnout. It was persuasion.

Voting analyses show Rehmet won swing voters by an overwhelming margin. He drew in soft Republicans. Among Latino voters, he improved on Kamala Harris’s vote share in his district by roughly 26 percent, according to VoteHub. He didn’t do it with a national brand or celebrity endorsements or a war chest from coastal donors. He talked about housing prices, health insurance costs, and the mess in local school districts. He kept it local. He kept it normal. And he won by a margin that nobody — Democrat or Republican — saw coming.

The day after Rehmet’s victory, Donald Trump said he would give “very serious thought” to endorsing in the Texas Republican Senate primary. That’s not confidence. That’s damage control.

Why This Canadian Is Paying Attention

I’ve been writing about American redistricting for weeks now — tracking the mid-decade gerrymanders, the SAVE Act, the coordinated assault on voting rights. Texas keeps showing up in every thread. It’s where Trump launched his scheme to redistrict Democratic members of Congress out of their seats. It’s where the maps from my redistricting scorecard carved TX-15, TX-34, and TX-35 into shapes that would make Elbridge Gerry blush. It’s where the Republican trifecta has spent years building what was supposed to be an impregnable fortress.

And now a machinist from Fort Worth just kicked a hole in the wall.

Texas deserves its own conversation — not a footnote in a redistricting series, but a sustained look at what’s happening in a state that both parties have treated as either a foregone conclusion or a fever dream for decades. Everything’s bigger in Texas, including the stakes. So let’s talk about it.

The Convergence

What makes 2026 different from every other cycle when Democrats got excited about Texas, raised a fortune, and went home empty-handed?

Three things are converging simultaneously.

First, the national environment is toxic for Republicans. Trump’s approval ratings are dragging down candidates everywhere. Democrats have overperformed in every special election since November 2024. They dominated the off-year races. Rehmet’s win wasn’t an isolated fluke — it was the Texas data point in a national pattern. When the political tide is running this hard, it reaches places the mapmakers thought were safely above the waterline.

Second, the overreach is visible. This is the part the redistricting story feeds directly. Texas voters aren’t abstract — they can see what their state government is doing. They can see the mid-decade gerrymander designed to eliminate competitive districts. They can see DOGE slashing federal jobs that sustain military communities across the state. They can see the immigration raids and the DHS shutdown. Trump won Texas by 14 points in 2024, and now he’s governing like he won it by 40. That gap between mandate and behaviour is where backlash breeds.

Third — and this is the one that actually matters — someone is finally building infrastructure. A coalition of Texas Democratic operatives, led in part by former state senator Wendy Davis, has launched the Blue Texas project to do what Democrats have never done in the state: build a permanent campaign operation that survives between election cycles. They’ve secured multi-year funding commitments. And for the first time since at least 1994, Democrats have fielded candidates in every federal race and every state legislative, statewide judicial, and State Board of Education race on the ballot.

That last detail sounds bureaucratic. It’s not. You can’t win races you don’t contest. And for thirty years, Texas Democrats have been handing Republicans dozens of uncontested seats every cycle — seats that become the farm team for higher office, the bench strength that builds a party from the ground up. Filling every line on the ballot isn’t glamorous. It’s the single most important structural change Texas Democrats have made in a generation.

The Rehmet Model

What did Rehmet actually do right? The answer is almost comically simple, and it’s the thing national Democrats keep refusing to learn.

He fit his district.

He didn’t run as a progressive champion. He didn’t run against Trump by name in every sentence. He didn’t import messaging from Brooklyn or Berkeley. He ran as a working-class Texan talking about working-class problems. Housing. Healthcare. Schools. The fundamentals.

Matt Angle, who runs the Lone Star Project PAC, told the New York Times that even after national money and attention poured in during the runoff, nobody tried to impose outside priorities on Rehmet’s campaign. That restraint, Angle suggested, was the exception rather than the rule. Usually, he said, national groups show up, issue a press release about flipping Texas, and “undermine every penny they’re going to spend in the state.”

Republican and Democratic pollsters agree on the critical finding: Rehmet didn’t win on differential turnout. He won on persuasion. He changed minds. In a polarized electorate where most races are decided by who shows up, Rehmet actually convinced people to cross over. That’s not a fluke. That’s a template.

The question is whether anyone will follow it.

What’s Coming

Texas has an open U.S. Senate seat on the ballot this November. The Republican primary is expected to be a bruising, expensive affair — exactly the kind of intraparty warfare that leaves the winner wounded for the general election. On the Democratic side, the primary features state representative James Talarico and U.S. Representative Jasmine Crockett, and the choice between them will say a lot about whether Democrats have learned anything from thirty years of losing in Texas.

There’s also the matter of the Colbert ban — the CBS decision this week to pull Talarico’s interview from broadcast television under pressure from Trump’s FCC, which backfired so spectacularly that the YouTube-only interview became a bigger story than the original booking ever would have been. That episode deserves its own piece, because it sits at the intersection of press freedom, political censorship, and the Streisand effect in ways that illuminate everything about this moment.

And underneath all of it, the redistricting gerrymander is ticking like a time bomb. The maps that were supposed to lock in Republican dominance for a decade are now the maps that a wave election will crash against. If the wave is big enough — and Rehmet’s 31-point swing suggests it might be — gerrymandered districts become liabilities. They spread Republican voters thin across too many seats, and when the tide turns, they all become vulnerable at once.

Texas isn’t a foregone conclusion. It isn’t a fever dream. It’s a test — of whether Democrats can build something durable, whether Republicans can hold a fortress they’ve been taking for granted, and whether the machinery of gerrymandering and voter suppression can withstand the backlash it was designed to prevent.

Let’s talk about it.
