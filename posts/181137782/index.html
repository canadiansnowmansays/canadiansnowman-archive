From Insults to Elimination

The Escalating War on the Fourth Estate

If Trump hasn’t called you a ‘terrible reporter,’ you’re not doing your job.

December 9, 2025

If Trump hasn’t called you a ‘terrible reporter,’ you’re not doing your job. But Pete Hegseth learned something more efficient at Fox News: why insult reporters when you can simply eliminate them?

I. The Standard

MSNBC’s Lawrence O’Donnell set a standard last night that every member of the White House press corps should memorize:

“If you are a member of the White House press corps and Donald Trump has not told you that you are a terrible reporter, then you are not doing your job. If you are a member of the White House press corps and Donald Trump has not told you you are the most obnoxious reporter in the whole place, then you are not doing your job. You are failing.”

This is not hyperbole. This is mathematics.

When a man the Washington Post documented telling 30,573 lies during his first presidency calls you “terrible,” he is actually confirming you are telling the truth. When a pathological liar—and I mean that as careful observation, not political insult—attacks your credibility, he is inadvertently validating it.

The paradox is perfect: Being insulted by someone who lies as reflexively as breathing is not a professional liability. It’s a credential.

And ABC News correspondent Rachel Scott earned hers yesterday.

II. The Master Class

Watch what happened when Rachel Scott asked Donald Trump about the video of the second missile strike that killed two men clinging to wreckage in the Caribbean—a strike that former Pentagon lawyer and NYU law professor Ryan Goodman has described as “just murder.”

Last week, Trump said: “Whatever they have, we’d certainly release. No problem.”

Those were his exact words.

Yesterday, when confronted with those exact words, here’s what happened:

RACHEL SCOTT: “Mr. President, you said that you would have no problem with releasing the full video of that strike on September second off the coast of Venezuela.”

TRUMP: “I didn’t say that. This is ABC fake news.”

SCOTT: “You said that you would have no problem releasing the full video.”

TRUMP: “Whatever Heath wants to do is okay with me.”

Let’s analyze what just happened, because this is a clinic in how to handle Trump’s lies:

  1. Scott presents the facts

  2. Trump lies (”I didn’t say that”)

  3. Scott doesn’t let the lie breathe (”You said that you would have no problem releasing the full video”)

  4. Trump surrenders immediately and changes his story

This is critical: She didn’t move on. She didn’t let him spin. She didn’t accept “I didn’t say that” and then ask a follow-up question about something else. She held the line.

You said that.

Three words that stopped a lie in its tracks.

But Scott wasn’t finished:

SCOTT: “He now says it’s under review. Are you ordering the secretary to release that full video?”

TRUMP: “Whatever he decides is okay with me.”

SCOTT: “Are you committing to releasing the full video?”

TRUMP: “Didn’t I just tell you that you are the most obnoxious reporter in the whole place. Let me just tell you, you are an obnoxious, a terrible, actually a terrible reporter and it’s always the same thing with you. I told you whatever Pete Heath wants to do is okay with me.”

There it is.

Rachel Scott earned her badge. She is a terrible, obnoxious reporter. Which is to say: she’s excellent at her job. Last year she won an award from the Washington Association of Black Journalists, which is precisely why Trump hates her. She asks questions he can’t answer, and she doesn’t let him lie his way out of them.

For 57 years, Trump has evaded accountability through intimidation, litigation, and simply wearing people down. Rachel Scott didn’t wear down. She held the line for exactly as long as it took for Trump to reveal himself: one round of lying, then surrender, then insults.

The insults are the tell. They’re what he does when he’s lost.

III. The Hegseth Innovation: Why Insult When You Can Eliminate?

But here’s what Lawrence O’Donnell’s standard doesn’t capture, because it’s new: Pete Hegseth learned something more efficient during his years at Fox News.

At Fox, Hegseth learned that the solution to uncomfortable questions isn’t better answers. The solution is eliminating the questions entirely.

Why fight with reporters like Rachel Scott when you can simply ensure reporters like Rachel Scott aren’t in the room?

Consider the evolution:

Phase 1 (Traditional): Government official faces tough question, provides evasive answer, press follows up, public gets some information even if incomplete.

Phase 2 (Trump): Government official faces tough question, attacks reporter’s credibility, provides evasive answer, but press is still in room to follow up.

Phase 3 (Hegseth): Tough questions disappear because questioners are eliminated from access.

Hegseth didn’t learn journalism at Fox News. He learned how to avoid it. Fox’s entire model is predicated on the careful curation of which questions get asked and by whom. Hostile questions are scripted out. Uncomfortable follow-ups don’t happen. The appearance of accountability without any actual accountability.

Now Hegseth has brought that model to the Pentagon, implementing systematic press restrictions that I’ve been documenting in The Ministry of Truth series. The video of the second missile strike? The one that killed two men Senator Mark Warner says were “incapacitated”? That video stays hidden behind Pentagon restrictions that Hegseth controls.

The logic is airtight: No press access = no uncomfortable questions = no accountability.

Trump insults reporters. Hegseth eliminates them. Guess which approach is more effective?

IV. The Strategic Diversion

Lawrence O’Donnell explained why this matters right now: These Caribbean missile strikes serve one purpose—diversion.

Diversion from the Epstein files Trump desperately doesn’t want discussed. (The accommodation psychology Trump used with Epstein is documented in The Epstein Files.) Diversion from the healthcare crisis hitting 22 million Americans whose Affordable Care Act subsidies expire—a crisis Trump has no plan to address. Yesterday he confirmed again: there is no Republican healthcare plan. There never has been, as I detail in Code Blue: America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare.

The pattern: create spectacle to hide scandal.

But the diversion only works if press can’t ask about what’s being diverted from. When Rachel Scott is in the room, she can ask about the video, healthcare, Epstein, accountability. When she’s excluded, Trump controls the narrative completely.

The video cover-up is the perfect case study: They’ve shown us plenty of videos of killing people in small boats. But this one video—showing two incapacitated men killed by a second missile—stays hidden. Because even they know it looks like murder.

And if reporters can’t ask about it, it effectively didn’t happen.

V. The Pattern of Attack

Trump’s attacks follow a pattern that reveals what he fears:

Rachel Scott, ABC—Crime: Asking about his own words. Jim Acosta, CNN—Had credentials revoked for follow-up questions. Yamiche Alcindor, PBS—Called “threatening” for asking direct questions. April Ryan, Urban Radio—Threatened for asking about accountability.

Notice the pattern? Women. Journalists of color. People who follow up. People who don’t let lies breathe. This pattern isn’t coincidental—it reflects both Trump’s personal bigotries and a broader authoritarian strategy of targeting those historically excluded from power who now dare to question it. These are the reporters who earn their place on what I’ve been calling The Enemies List.

Now consider: Which reporters hasn’t Trump attacked? They know who they are. What questions aren’t they asking? What lies are they letting slide? If Trump hasn’t called you terrible after months of covering him, what does that tell you about your work?

This is the cost of access journalism: You get to stay in the room, but only if you never ask the questions that would get you thrown out.

VI. The Two-Pronged Strategy

Here’s how Trump and Hegseth work together:

Trump’s Role: Insult, intimidate, and delegitimize reporters who are present. Call them terrible, obnoxious, fake news. Plant the idea in his supporters’ minds that these reporters can’t be trusted.

Hegseth’s Role: Use Trump’s attacks as justification for restricting access. “See how terrible they are? That’s why we can’t have them in sensitive briefings.”

The strategies reinforce each other: Trump attacks reporters → Public questions their credibility → Hegseth restricts access “for security” → Fewer questions → Less accountability → More lies go unchallenged → The cycle continues.

This is the systematic dismantling of every accountability mechanism—fire inspectors general, pardon co-conspirators, attack prosecutors, intimidate witnesses, and now eliminate press access. It’s Chapter 58 in a 57-year project of evading consequences, which I’ve been tracking in The Trump Accountability Project.

The authoritarian dream: government by press release, where the only information available is what they choose to share. And they’re doing it while using Republican rhetoric about “government transparency”—the hypocrisy documented in The Hypocrisy Chronicles.

VII. The Constitutional Crisis

This is about the First Amendment under assault from two directions simultaneously.

The video of the second missile strike should be classified if releasing it would genuinely compromise military operations. But we know that’s not why it’s hidden. It’s hidden because it looks bad. That’s not a classification decision. That’s a political decision.

Consider the military’s claim: These two men, clinging to wreckage, could have been “trying to signal drug traffickers to rescue them.”

The military knows exactly what aircraft and vessels were in that area. They have radar, satellite tracking, real-time monitoring that exceeds anything in the civilian sector. The New York Times reports “no other unknown aircraft or boats in visual range.” These boats don’t have the fuel capacity to travel 1,500 miles from southern Caribbean to the United States. The only aircraft that could retrieve men from ocean wreckage is a Coast Guard helicopter.

The military’s claim depends on the public knowing nothing about boats, radar, fuel capacity, or maritime distances. They’re depending on people not thinking.

And without reporters in the room asking these obvious questions, the military can claim anything. This is why press access matters. This is why Rachel Scott being called “terrible” matters.

VIII. What We Do

For Journalists: Rachel Scott showed you how. Come prepared with exact quotes. Don’t let lies breathe—when he says “I didn’t say that,” respond “You said that” as a statement of fact. Follow up relentlessly. Accept the insults as confirmation you’ve hit the target. Build solidarity with other reporters. Make accountability personal—ask “Are YOU committing” not “Will it be done.”

For Readers: Support attacked reporters—subscribe, share their work, defend them. Demand access through representatives and FOIA requests. Recognize accommodation—notice which outlets get friendly treatment and ask why. Follow the receipts—when Trump says “I didn’t say that,” look it up. He said it.

For Democracy: Every Rachel Scott moment is democracy fighting back, as I document in [When Democracy Fights Back]. Every FOIA lawsuit demanding that video is democracy fighting back. Every reader who supports accountability journalism over access journalism is democracy fighting back.

The question is whether we fight back loudly enough, persistently enough, and collectively enough to matter.

IX. The Fox News Model at the Pentagon

At Fox, Hegseth learned that the most effective way to handle hostile questions is to ensure they never get asked. Now he’s applying that lesson at the Pentagon, where the stakes are infinitely higher—military operations, American lives, constitutional oversight. He’s treating it all like a Fox News booking strategy: friendly outlets get access, skeptical reporters get excluded.

Pentagon press access has been systematically restricted. Briefings canceled. Reporters excluded. Information classified that has no business being classified. All while Trump provides political cover by attacking the reporters Hegseth is excluding.

It’s beautifully coordinated. It’s also completely antithetical to democratic accountability.

X. The New Standard

Let’s update Lawrence O’Donnell’s standard for the Trump-Hegseth era:

If Trump hasn’t called you a terrible reporter, you’re not doing your job.

If Hegseth hasn’t tried to exclude you from a briefing, you’re not asking hard enough questions.

If you can sit through a press conference without being insulted, you’re accommodating rather than reporting.

This is Chapter 58: Eliminating Accountability Mechanisms. For 57 years, Trump has evaded consequences through litigation, intimidation, and attrition. Now he’s added elimination—not just of accountability, but of the people who would hold him accountable.

They want to eliminate questions by eliminating questioners.

Rachel Scott earned her insults yesterday. She earned her badge. And in Trump’s America, that’s the highest credential a journalist can hold.

The badge of honor is being called terrible by a liar. The badge of courage is being excluded by people who fear your questions.

The only question that remains: Are you in the room to earn yours?

Because if they succeed in keeping you out, there won’t be a room anymore. There will only be the press releases they choose to issue, the videos they choose to show, and the lies they choose to tell.

And no one left to say: “You said that.”
