The Domestic Chaos - De Minimis, Shutdowns, and the November Reckoning

How Trump’s Trade War Creates Cascading Crises at Home

October 11, 2025

October 11, 2025

Bottom Line Up Front: While courts threaten Trump’s tariff authority and China retaliates with rare earth controls (covered in Part 1), the domestic impact of his trade war has accelerated dramatically. The August 29 closure of the de minimis exemption imposed up to $71 billion in new costs on businesses while suspending international shipments. Trump continued announcing sector-specific tariffs through September—lumber, furniture, pharmaceuticals, trucks—even as the legal foundation crumbled. Most bizarrely, the government shut down October 1 despite $190 billion in tariff revenue flowing into Treasury, which remains frozen and inaccessible. Trump used the shutdown to fire over 4,000 federal workers in an unprecedented purge. As all these crises converge with Supreme Court arguments and China deadlines in early November, the chaos Trump created threatens to consume his presidency.

This is Part 2 of a two-part update covering August 7 - October 11, 2025. Part 1 examined the existential legal and diplomatic threats facing Trump’s tariff regime.

De Minimis: The $71 Billion Disruption

On August 29, Trump closed the de minimis exemption for all countries, extending to the world what he’d done to China in May. Any commercial shipments valued at $800 or less became subject to full tariff treatment—affecting 92% of all cargo entering the United States, up to 4 million packages daily.

The scale is staggering. De minimis shipments grew from 140 million in 2014 to 1.36 billion in 2024 after Congress raised the threshold from $200 to $800 in 2016. What was originally intended as a minor administrative convenience for travelers bringing home souvenirs had morphed into the dominant pathway for international e-commerce, with companies like Shein and Temu building entire business models around it.

The administrative burden shifted immediately to shippers and customs brokers. Previously, these packages bypassed formal entry procedures. Now they require customs processing, duties, fees, and formal entry documentation. For packages sent through international postal networks, the order established flat duty rates: $80, $160, or $200 per item depending on the country’s IEEPA tariff rate.

Immediate Fallout: Suspended Shipments and Business Chaos

In the week before August 29, major shipping companies across Europe and Asia suspended deliveries to the United States, citing inadequate time to revamp their systems. DHL, FedEx, and other carriers added surcharges and warned of longer transit times. European exporters faced not just tariffs but added paperwork and shipping costs.

For small-to-medium businesses relying on de minimis—whether importing components or using drop-shipping models—the impact was severe. Customs experts estimate the change created up to $71 billion in new costs. “Tariffs are now universal, regardless of value,” said one customs broker. “Every package counts. That’s a major change in how global e-commerce has operated for the last decade.”

Even major retailers suffered. Lululemon shares plunged in September after reporting its e-commerce strategy was no longer viable due to the exemption’s closure.

Justification vs. Reality

The administration cited three justifications: combating fentanyl trafficking, preventing counterfeits, and protecting American businesses. According to Homeland Security, de minimis shipments accounted for 90% of cargo seizures in fiscal 2024, with 98% of narcotics and 97% of counterfeits falling under the exemption.

These concerns have merit—de minimis did facilitate customs evasion. But the solution imposes enormous costs on legitimate commerce while doing nothing to address fentanyl smuggling through other channels, including the U.S. Postal Service.

Meanwhile, a select group of U.S. Customs-designated “qualified parties”—customs brokers and fulfillment services—experienced a windfall as businesses scrambled for help navigating the new requirements. Closing de minimis didn’t eliminate international e-commerce; it just made it more expensive and complex, enriching intermediaries while squeezing businesses and consumers.

More Tariffs: September-October Announcements

Even as courts challenged his legal authority and businesses struggled with de minimis, Trump continued announcing new tariffs:

September 29 - Construction Materials: 10% tariffs on softwood lumber effective October 14, plus 25% on kitchen cabinets, bathroom vanities, and upholstered furniture (increasing to 50% and 30% respectively on January 1, 2026). These build on existing price pressures—furniture prices were already up 4.7% year-over-year in August, with living room and dining room furniture surging 9.5%.

The lumber tariffs particularly target Canada, which provides over 70% of U.S. softwood lumber imports. Despite USMCA covering most Canada-U.S. trade, Trump selectively attacks sectors outside the agreement.

September-October - Pharmaceuticals, Trucks, Films: Trump announced but hasn’t implemented:

  • 100% tariff on imported pharmaceuticals (would make life-saving medications unaffordable)

  • 25% tariff on heavy trucks, delayed from October 1 to November 1 (primarily targets Mexico’s 78% share of heavy truck imports)

  • 100% tariff on foreign-made films (enforcement mechanism unclear)

These follow a pattern: dramatic Truth Social announcements without immediate executive orders, creating uncertainty while using tariff threats to pressure foreign governments.

What Happens If Trump Loses at the Supreme Court?

The looming Supreme Court decision raises a critical question: if the justices strike down IEEPA tariffs (60-80% odds according to legal experts), what can Trump do?

The Alternative Authorities—and Their Limitations

Even if the Supreme Court strikes down IEEPA tariffs, Trump retains other legal mechanisms, though none provide the sweeping, immediate authority he claimed under IEEPA:

Section 301 and 232 Tariffs Remain Untouched: The existing Section 301 tariffs on China (7.5-25% on ~$370 billion in goods) and Section 232 “national security” tariffs on steel (25%), aluminum (25%), copper (50%), and automobiles (25%) are unaffected by court challenges. These would stay in place regardless of the Supreme Court ruling.

New Section 301 Investigations: Trump could direct the U.S. Trade Representative to launch Section 301 investigations against trading partners for “unreasonable or discriminatory” practices. However, these require formal investigations taking 7-12 months to complete—far slower than Trump’s preferred approach of announcing tariffs by tweet.

Section 338 of the Trade Act of 1930: This never-before-used provision allows the president to impose tariffs up to 50% on imports from countries that discriminate against U.S. commerce. However, it requires demonstrating actual discrimination, not just trade deficits.

Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974: This could authorize tariffs up to 15% to address balance-of-payments problems, but they would be limited to 150 days unless reauthorized by Congress—and getting congressional authorization in this political environment seems unlikely.

The Reality: None of these alternatives provide what Trump wants: the ability to impose unlimited tariffs on any country instantly based solely on his declaration of an emergency. If the Court rules against him, Trump faces a stark choice: rebuild tariffs slowly using legitimate authorities that require investigations and Congressional oversight, or defy the Supreme Court and trigger a full-blown constitutional crisis.

The framework agreements Trump negotiated with the EU, Japan, and others were all predicated on IEEPA tariffs. If those tariffs are struck down, those agreements collapse—and rebuilding them under different legal authorities would require entirely new negotiations.

The Government Shutdown: Tariff Revenue Frozen

October 1: The Irony of Abundance

At 12:01 a.m. EDT on October 1, the federal government shut down after Congress failed to pass appropriations for fiscal year 2026. The shutdown resulted from partisan disagreements over spending levels, with Trump’s unprecedented use of rescissions (cutting already-appropriated funds) creating a major negotiating obstacle that Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer called an insurmountable barrier to any deal.

The shutdown furloughed approximately 900,000 federal employees and left 700,000 working without pay. Essential services continue, but agencies like the National Institutes of Health, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and WIC program (providing nutrition assistance to 6.6 million women, infants, and children) face partial or full suspensions.

The irony is stark: even as government shut down for “lack of funding,” tariff revenue flowed at record levels. In September alone, Treasury collected $31.7 billion in tariffs, bringing year-to-date totals to $190 billion—a 160% increase over 2024. On October 1, the first shutdown day, government collected $315 million from tariffs.

But that revenue sits frozen in Treasury’s general fund, legally unavailable without congressional appropriations. Daily compensation for 750,000 furloughed workers is roughly $400 million—meaning daily tariff revenue could cover most salaries, but cannot be accessed without legislation.

The Math Exposes the Absurdity: Trump collected more in tariff revenue in the first nine months of 2025 ($190 billion) than the federal government spent on:

  • The entire Department of Education’s annual budget ($238 billion requested for FY 2026, but $190B exceeds most agency budgets)

  • NASA’s annual budget ($25.2 billion)

  • EPA’s annual budget ($9.2 billion)

  • Combined budgets of multiple agencies Trump claims to want to cut

Yet somehow, with this river of revenue flowing in, the government is shut down and workers aren’t being paid. The White House could theoretically ask Congress to pass narrow legislation designating tariff revenue for specific uses like paying furloughed workers, but neither party appears interested. Democrats view tariff revenue as coming “at the expense of many Americans who will be or are already paying higher prices,” while Republicans want to use the shutdown to force deeper spending cuts.

The Unprecedented Workforce Purge

Trump weaponized the shutdown for federal workforce destruction. On October 10, the administration sent “reduction in force” (RIF) notices to over 4,000 workers amid the shutdown—an action unions called “disgraceful” and “illegal.”

“In AFGE’s 93 years of existence—including during Trump’s first term—no president has ever fired thousands of furloughed workers during a government shutdown,” the union president stated.

This follows earlier cuts of over 200,000 federal employees since January, with numbers expected to reach 300,000 by year’s end. Trump explicitly framed the shutdown as opportunity for further cuts: “I have a meeting today with Russ Vought, he of PROJECT 2025 Fame, to determine which of the many Democrat Agencies, most of which are a political SCAM, he recommends to be cut.”

The cuts degrade government capacity precisely when needed most. The shutdown forced the Forest Service to pause prescribed burns reducing wildfire risk, contributed to air traffic control short staffing, and left military families uncertain about October 15 paychecks.

States Left Holding the Bag

States face profound uncertainty about reimbursement for covering federal program costs during the shutdown. Nevada State Treasurer Zach Conine noted the administration “has not made good on its word to states in recent months—freezing congressionally approved funding and cutting already awarded grants.”

Maryland Governor Wes Moore’s office said it had “received no indication that the federal government would deviate from past practice” of reimbursing states, “but we are monitoring closely.” The hedging language reflects eroded trust.

“This shutdown feels different,” multiple state officials noted. Unlike previous shutdowns where states could reasonably expect reimbursement for covering federal costs, Trump’s systematic cuts to previously-approved funding and aggressive use of rescissions have created genuine doubt about whether states will be made whole.

States are continuing to operate federally funded programs during the shutdown—programs like SNAP (food stamps), Medicaid administrative costs, and highway maintenance. By doing so, they’re essentially providing short-term loans to the federal government. But states are simultaneously facing record municipal bond issuance (projected at $600 billion by end of 2025) and higher borrowing costs.

As William Glasgall of the Volcker Alliance noted, “States and localities are kind of getting the message they really need to fend for themselves much more than they ever had.”

The WIC program illustrates the stakes. Federal officials told states there were enough funds to cover October benefits, but the program may run out of money sooner for the special food program for women, infants and children. States would either need to cover those costs or see families lose nutrition assistance—and states have no certainty they’ll be reimbursed if they step in.

The November Perfect Storm: Everything Converges

Multiple crisis points collide in early November:

November 1:

  • Trump’s additional 100% China tariffs supposedly take effect

  • 25% tariffs on heavy trucks (delayed from October 1)

  • Various pharmaceutical and product tariffs potentially implemented

Early November (First Week):

  • Supreme Court oral arguments on IEEPA tariff authority

  • Legal experts assess 60-80% odds Court rules against Trump

  • Court signals could affect ongoing trade negotiations

November 10:

  • China tariff truce expires for the second time

  • Without renewal, IEEPA tariffs could revert to 145%/125%

  • But Section 301 tariffs (7.5-25%) and Section 232 tariffs (50% on steel/aluminum) would remain regardless of court ruling

  • December 1 deadline for China’s rare earth restrictions looms

The Tariff Math If Everything Goes Wrong: If Trump implements the November 1 tariffs and the November 10 truce expires, Chinese goods would face a nightmarish stack:

  • 100% new IEEPA tariff (announced October 10)

  • 145% IEEPA reciprocal tariff (if truce expires)

  • 20% IEEPA fentanyl tariff

  • 7.5-25% Section 301 tariffs (survive regardless of IEEPA ruling)

  • 50% Section 232 steel/aluminum tariffs (where applicable)

Total: Up to 340%+ on some goods—making imports from China essentially impossible and constituting a de facto trade embargo.

But this assumes the Supreme Court doesn’t strike down IEEPA authority. If the Court rules against Trump in early November, the IEEPA tariffs (the 100%, 145%, and 20% components) would disappear, leaving “only” the Section 301 and Section 232 tariffs—still substantial at 57.5-75% on many goods, but not an embargo.

The confluence creates maximum uncertainty. Companies don’t know whether tariffs they’re paying will be ruled illegal and refunded. China doesn’t know whether to negotiate with an administration whose authority may be struck down. Allies don’t know whether framework agreements built on IEEPA tariffs will survive judicial review.

Even if Trump loses at the Supreme Court, substantial tariffs would remain—all Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, and automobiles, plus Section 301 tariffs on China. But the sweeping worldwide “reciprocal tariffs” that formed his trade policy’s centerpiece would collapse, along with the additional “fentanyl” tariffs and the leverage they provided.

Trump would then face the choice outlined earlier: rebuild tariffs slowly using Section 301 and Section 232 authorities that require investigations and evidence, or defy the Supreme Court and trigger a constitutional crisis that would make the current chaos look manageable by comparison.

Meanwhile, the government shutdown continues, workforce purges accelerate, and Trump shows no sign of moderating despite mounting legal, economic, and diplomatic costs.

Conclusion: The Endgame Approaches

The three months since August 7 have transformed Trump’s tariff regime from economic policy disaster into comprehensive institutional crisis. Courts have rejected his constitutional theories. China has escalated with sophisticated rare earth controls mirroring U.S. semiconductor warfare. The de minimis closure imposed tens of billions in new business costs. More tariffs keep getting announced as existing ones face judicial annihilation. The government has shut down while sitting on $190 billion in frozen tariff revenue that may need refunding.

Every element of Trump’s trade policy now stands exposed: constitutional overreach, economic damage, diplomatic destruction, administrative chaos. The question is no longer whether this approach works—it manifestly doesn’t. The question is how much additional damage will be inflicted before the Supreme Court, economic reality, or political backlash finally forces a reckoning.

The November deadline cascade will provide the answer. One way or another, Trump’s tariff war built on constitutional quicksand cannot survive much longer in its current form. What replaces it—whether return to rule of law and rational trade policy using legitimate authorities like Section 301 and Section 232, or an even more destructive constitutional crisis as Trump defies court rulings—will define America’s economic future for years to come.

China’s rare earth controls demonstrate that other countries have learned to fight back using their own leverage, with sophisticated measures designed to prevent third-country workarounds. The United States’ technological and manufacturing dependencies—built over decades of globalization—cannot be unwound through tariff threats alone. And the legal system continues functioning as a check on executive overreach, even when the president claims unlimited emergency powers.

The courts will soon have their say. Whether Trump listens—and whether the damage he’s inflicted on American trade relationships, business confidence, and constitutional governance can ever be fully repaired—remains to be seen.

The trade war Trump promised would be “easy to win” has become a multi-front catastrophe: losing in court, losing leverage to China, losing business support at home, and losing the ability to govern effectively as his own shutdown paralyzes the federal government. November will reveal just how much further this can unravel.
