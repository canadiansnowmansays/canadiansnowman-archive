ABBA Against the Odds

Against the Odds

Part 6: The Mamma Mia! Machine How One Vision Created a Billion-Dollar Empire

February 13, 2026

West End & Stage Production

In the early 1990s, ABBA hadn’t released new material in a decade. The band had split in 1982 and showed no signs of reuniting. The four members were pursuing separate projects—Björn and Benny writing musicals together, Agnetha and Frida pursuing solo work or stepping back from public life entirely.

Their catalog continued generating steady revenue through radio play, catalog sales, and licensing. But culturally, ABBA occupied an ambiguous space. They were too recent to be “classic” but too dated to be contemporary. They were respected for their hits but not taken entirely seriously as artists. They were successful but somewhat embarrassing—the sequined jumpsuits and platform boots had become punch lines rather than iconic fashion statements.

Then a British producer named Judy Craymer had an idea that would transform ABBA’s legacy, generate over $5 billion in revenue, and prove that their music could anchor the fifth-longest-running show in West End history.

She wanted to create a stage musical using ABBA’s catalog.

The idea wasn’t obviously brilliant. Jukebox musicals—shows built around existing popular music rather than original scores—had a spotty track record. Most failed to find the narrative logic connecting disparate songs. They felt forced, artificial, like elaborate karaoke rather than coherent theater.

But Craymer persisted. She convinced Björn and Benny to give her a chance. She found the perfect writer and director. She created a show that worked on its own terms while celebrating ABBA’s music.

And when Mamma Mia! opened in London’s West End on April 6, 1999, it became a phenomenon that would eventually generate more wealth than ABBA earned during their entire active career as a band.

This wasn’t luck. This was strategic vision, creative collaboration, and—crucially—ABBA’s ownership structure allowing them to capture the value they’d created decades earlier.

Judy Craymer’s Vision

Judy Craymer with Björn Ulvaeus & Benny Andersson

The story of Mamma Mia!’s creation reveals how important individual vision and persistence can be in unlocking latent value.

Judy Craymer was a theater producer who’d worked with Tim Rice and Andrew Lloyd Webber. She’d been involved with Chess, the musical Björn and Benny wrote with Rice that premiered in 1984. Through that collaboration, she got to know Björn and Benny personally and developed appreciation for ABBA’s catalog.

In the early 1990s, she approached them with a proposal: create a stage musical using ABBA’s songs.

Their initial response was skeptical. As one account describes: “She said you should have a musical of your songs. And they said, no, there’s no plot. And she said, well, what if I can convince you? And they said, yeah, we’ll be fine anyway.”

That exchange—”there’s no plot”—captures the fundamental challenge of jukebox musicals. ABBA’s songs weren’t written as integrated narrative. They were discrete pop songs about various topics: love, heartbreak, dancing, relationships, resilience. How do you create a coherent story from material never intended to tell one?

Craymer’s response—”what if I can convince you?”—reveals her confidence. She believed it could work if the right creative team assembled it properly.

Björn and Benny’s response—”yeah, we’ll be fine”—is equally telling. They weren’t opposed. They just needed convincing. And because they owned their catalog (the ownership strategy we discussed in Part 4), they could make this decision themselves. No record label executives had to approve. No complex rights negotiations across multiple parties were required. Craymer convinced them, they agreed, and the project moved forward.

That ownership flexibility—the ability to say yes to experimental ideas without corporate gatekeepers—proved crucial to Mamma Mia!’s existence.

Assembling the Creative Team

Craymer’s vision required the right collaborators. She found them in writer Catherine Johnson and director Phyllida Lloyd.

Catherine Johnson’s contribution can’t be overstated. She had to solve an impossible puzzle: create a narrative that made ABBA’s songs feel inevitable rather than forced.

The solution she found was brilliant: a mother-daughter story set on a Greek island where a young bride-to-be invites three men to her wedding, any one of whom might be her father. The mother, Donna, hasn’t seen any of them in decades. Their arrival reopens old memories, unresolved feelings, and questions about choices made long ago.

This structure accomplished several things simultaneously:

It justified the emotional range. The story needed joy (wedding celebration), nostalgia (rekindled memories), heartbreak (old relationships), confusion (paternity mystery), resilience (single motherhood), and triumph (ultimate self-acceptance). ABBA’s catalog contained all these emotions. The story provided context making them coherent.

It targeted the right demographic. Middle-aged women—who remembered ABBA fondly from their youth and now dealt with their own children becoming adults—were the sweet spot. The story spoke directly to their experiences while the music triggered nostalgia.

It made the songs feel organic. When Donna sings “The Winner Takes It All” confronting an ex-lover who abandoned her, the song’s devastating lyrics about loss and dignity feel perfectly placed. When the ensemble celebrates with “Dancing Queen,” it’s an actual party scene, not a random musical number.

It avoided mocking ABBA. Some jukebox musicals treat their source material ironically, winking at the audience about how dated or silly the songs are. Johnson’s book took ABBA seriously—these were real emotions expressed through excellent pop songs, and the story honored that.

Phyllida Lloyd’s direction complemented Johnson’s book. Lloyd had directed both the London and Broadway productions of the original stage Chess, so she’d worked with Björn and Benny before. She understood their music and respected their artistic vision.

Lloyd brought theatrical sophistication without pretension. Mamma Mia! wasn’t trying to be Sondheim or Lloyd Webber. It was unpretentious, joyful, emotionally direct pop theater—and Lloyd ensured it achieved excellence within that framework rather than apologizing for not being something else.

The West End Opening

Björn Ulvaeus and Benny Andersson on stage with the London cast (including former “Dynamos”) at the 20th anniversary finale (2019)

Mamma Mia! premiered at the Prince Edward Theatre in London’s West End on April 6, 1999.

The critical reception was mixed. Theater critics—who often valued complexity and artistic ambition over popular appeal—found it lightweight. The Guardian called it “cheesy.” Others questioned whether ABBA’s catalog could sustain a full evening.

The audience reception was ecstatic.

The show sold out. Word of mouth was phenomenal. People who’d seen it told friends they had to see it. Those friends brought their friends. The buzz built organically because the show delivered something people hadn’t realized they wanted: pure, joyful, emotionally satisfying theatrical entertainment that didn’t require cultural capital or dramatic knowledge to appreciate.

Within months, Mamma Mia! established itself as a West End fixture. It would eventually run for over 8,000 performances in London, be seen by over 9 million people, and become the third-longest-running show in West End history.

The financial scale was extraordinary. The stage musical has now grossed over £4.5 billion globally across all productions—West End, Broadway, touring companies, and international productions. It’s been produced in over 440 cities across 50 productions in 16 different languages.

To put that in perspective: £4.5 billion from the stage musical alone. Before the films. Before the merchandising. Before the cast albums. Just ticket sales from theatrical productions.

Remember ABBA’s peak earning year was 1979, when they generated $15 million in net profits (about $68 million in today’s money) and were the second-most profitable company in Sweden. The Mamma Mia! stage musical has generated roughly 66 times that annual peak in total gross revenue.

One theatrical franchise built on their catalog generated orders of magnitude more wealth than they earned during their entire active career as a band.

Broadway and Beyond

The Broadway production opened in 2001 at the Winter Garden Theatre. It ran for 14 years—5,773 performances—making it the ninth-longest-running show in Broadway history.

But the real genius of Mamma Mia!’s business model was its replicability. Once the show was proven, it could be reproduced globally. Touring companies could perform it in cities that couldn’t sustain long-run productions. International productions could adapt it for local audiences while maintaining the core structure.

This created multiple simultaneous revenue streams:

  • West End production (ongoing)

  • Broadway production (2001-2015, then returned 2017-2021)

  • First North American tour (2001-2009)

  • Second North American tour (2009-2012)

  • Third North American tour (2014-2016)

  • UK touring productions

  • Australian productions

  • European productions in multiple languages

  • Asian productions

  • South American productions

Each production generated revenue independently. The same intellectual property—Johnson’s book, Lloyd’s staging, ABBA’s songs—could be monetized repeatedly across different markets simultaneously.

This is the franchise model that Disney perfected for films applied to theater. Create excellent core content once, then replicate it globally with local casts and production teams.

The Film Franchise

In 2008, Universal Pictures released Mamma Mia! The Movie, directed by Phyllida Lloyd from Catherine Johnson’s screenplay, produced by Judy Craymer.

The creative team that built the stage success transferred to film, maintaining continuity and vision. They assembled an extraordinary cast: Meryl Streep as Donna, Pierce Brosnan, Colin Firth, Stellan Skarsgård as the three potential fathers, Amanda Seyfried as Sophie, and Christine Baranski and Julie Walters as Donna’s friends.

The film faced obvious challenges. Most of the cast weren’t professional singers. Brosnan in particular struggled vocally—critics compared his singing to “a water buffalo” and “a wounded raccoon.” Streep was game but not a trained vocalist. The stage show’s theatrical energy might not translate to screen.

None of it mattered.

The film grossed $610 million worldwide on a $52 million budget. It became the highest-grossing live-action musical film of all time (later surpassed by Beauty and the Beast in 2017). In the UK, it became one of the highest-grossing films ever.

The DVD sales were extraordinary. It sold 1.7 million copies on its first day of release in Britain—the fastest and biggest-selling film on DVD at that time.

The film’s success proved something crucial: Mamma Mia! worked across mediums. Stage, screen, different casts, different contexts—the combination of Johnson’s story and ABBA’s music created something resilient enough to survive varied executions.

Ten years later, in 2018, Mamma Mia! Here We Go Again—a sequel/prequel showing Donna’s youth in parallel with Sophie’s present—grossed another $395 million worldwide.

Combined, the two films generated over $1 billion in theatrical revenue, plus substantial additional revenue from home video, streaming rights, television broadcasts, and soundtrack sales.

Add that to the £4.5 billion from stage productions, and you’re approaching $6 billion in total revenue from the Mamma Mia! franchise—all built on ABBA’s catalog.

Why It Worked

Most jukebox musicals fail. They feel forced, artificial, like watching a playlist rather than a story. So why did Mamma Mia! succeed where so many others failed?

The catalog had range. ABBA wrote songs about joy, heartbreak, resilience, confusion, triumph, loss, celebration, reflection—the full spectrum of human emotion. This gave Johnson material to build a complete emotional journey rather than variations on a single theme.

The songs were genuinely excellent. Many jukebox musicals use songs people tolerate. ABBA’s songs are songs people love. The baseline quality was extraordinarily high, which meant even weaker story moments were carried by strong music.

The story respected the music. Johnson didn’t force songs into inappropriate contexts or use them ironically. She found narrative moments where the songs felt organic.

The emotional honesty matched ABBA’s authenticity. Remember from Part 5—ABBA’s later work channeled genuine pain and mature emotion. Mamma Mia!’s story about complicated relationships, single motherhood, aging, and self-acceptance matched that emotional maturity.

It targeted the right demographic. Middle-aged women had disposable income, nostalgia for ABBA, and appreciation for stories about their life experiences. They brought daughters, friends, partners. They saw it multiple times. They bought the merchandise.

It was unironically joyful. In an era of irony and cynicism, Mamma Mia! offered sincere celebration of life, love, friendship, and resilience. People starved for genuine positive emotion embraced it.

The finale was participatory. The curtain call featured the cast performing “Mamma Mia,” “Dancing Queen,” and “Waterloo” in full ABBA costume, with audience members dancing in the aisles. This transformed theater from passive observation to communal celebration—exactly what ABBA’s music had always inspired.

It worked across cultures. The Greek island setting was exotic but not culturally specific. The mother-daughter relationship was universal. The music translated across languages. The show could be adapted globally without losing essential elements.

Capturing the Value

Here’s where ABBA’s ownership strategy (from Part 4) proved its long-term value.

Because Björn and Benny owned the music publishing, they controlled licensing rights for theatrical use. When Judy Craymer wanted to create Mamma Mia!, she negotiated directly with them.

This meant:

They could approve or reject the project. No label executive could force them to license songs for a project they didn’t believe in.

They negotiated favorable financial terms. Rather than accepting standard licensing rates, they could structure deals capturing significant value from the show’s success.

They participated in subsidiary rights. When the stage show spawned film adaptations, touring companies, international productions, and cast albums, they participated in those revenue streams.

They protected artistic integrity. They could ensure the show treated their music respectfully and maintained quality standards.

The financial result: Mamma Mia! generated more wealth for ABBA than their entire active career as a band. As one observer noted: “They haven’t made more money off of Mamma Mia than from being ABBA. When you consider all of the stage shows, the movies, the whole thing, Mamma Mia made more money than ABBA.”

This validates everything discussed in Part 4 about ownership and long-term value creation. Artists who sold their catalogs in the 1970s for what seemed like good money watched others profit from subsequent exploitation. ABBA retained ownership and captured the value themselves 20-30 years later.

The Longevity Connection

Mamma Mia! contributed to ABBA’s longevity in several crucial ways:

Introduced ABBA to new generations. Millennials who saw the film as teenagers or young adults discovered ABBA through Mamma Mia! They explored the catalog beyond the musical’s tracks. Many became fans.

Changed cultural perception. Pre-Mamma Mia!, ABBA was somewhat embarrassing nostalgia. Post-Mamma Mia!, they were cultural icons. The musical’s success granted them legitimacy and respect.

Sustained catalog relevance. The show kept ABBA’s music in active circulation. Radio played the songs. Streaming services recommended them. They remained present in popular culture.

Demonstrated catalog versatility. The songs could anchor theater, film, multiple languages, varied contexts. This versatility suggested ongoing exploitation opportunities.

Generated ongoing revenue. Mamma Mia! productions continue running globally. Each performance generates revenue for ABBA decades after they stopped creating new material.

Validated the ownership strategy. The spectacular financial success proved that retaining ownership and waiting for the right opportunity generated far more wealth than quick licensing deals ever could.

Created franchise potential. If Mamma Mia! worked, other ABBA-based projects might work too. This opened doors to future exploitation like ABBA Voyage.

Proved intergenerational appeal. Grandmothers brought daughters who brought granddaughters. Three generations enjoyed ABBA together. This demonstrated the music’s timeless quality.

The Irony

There’s beautiful irony in Mamma Mia!’s success.

ABBA stopped performing in 1982 partly because the emotional complexity of working together after two divorces became unsustainable. They couldn’t continue as a band.

But 17 years later, a musical celebrating love, resilience, female friendship, and complicated relationships—built entirely on their catalog—became one of the most successful theatrical properties in history.

The music they created while struggling with relationship failure generated a show about navigating relationships with grace, humor, and joy.

The catalog they built while dealing with personal pain became the foundation for communal celebration.

The songs written when they couldn’t stay together created a franchise that brought millions of people together in theaters globally.

ABBA didn’t need to reunite or tour or create new material. Their existing work, properly recontextualized, generated more value than continued performance ever could.

That’s the ultimate validation of their strategic choices across decades: minimizing touring to preserve relationships, investing in ownership to capture long-term value, creating music of sufficient quality and emotional authenticity to work across contexts, and building a business structure that could monetize their catalog long after they stopped performing.
