The Noem Files

The Noem Files Are Now Closed

She wasn't fired for what she did to Renee Good and Alex Pretti. She was fired for what she said about Donald Trump.

March 5, 2026

Brendon Smialowski / AFP / Getty

On March 5, 2026, Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that Kristi Noem “has served us well” and would be moving to a new role as Envoy for the Shield of the Americas. He thanked her for her service. He offered no explanation.

That is how the most consequential domestic policy tenure in the Trump second term ended — not with a reckoning, not with accountability, but with a press release dressed up as a promotion.

But we know what happened. Two days of congressional testimony under oath had destroyed her. And the question worth asking — the question that gets to the heart of how this administration actually works — is not why she failed. It is what, specifically, she said that made Trump pull the trigger.

The answer tells you everything.

What She Did

Kristi Noem became Secretary of Homeland Security on January 25, 2025, confirmed 59-34 by a Senate that included seven Democrats who would later regret their votes. She played “Hot Mama” as her walk-up song at her formal DHS introduction. She inherited the most powerful domestic enforcement apparatus in American history and immediately weaponized it.

Over the following thirteen months, she oversaw militarized immigration sweeps in American cities. She bottlenecked FEMA disaster relief for Hurricane Helene survivors by requiring her personal sign-off on expenditures over $100,000. She cycled through three acting FEMA administrators. She presided over mass workforce reductions at the agency tasked with keeping Americans safe from natural disasters. She acquired a $70 million government jet equipped with a bedroom. She starred in a $220 million taxpayer-funded advertising campaign featuring herself.

And then came Minnesota.

On January 7, 2026, Renee Nicole Good — a 37-year-old poet and mother of three — was shot and killed by an ICE officer in Minneapolis during Operation Metro Surge. Within hours, before any investigation, Noem declared the shooting an act of domestic terrorism. Seventeen days later, Alex Jeffrey Pretti, a nurse, was shot and killed by federal agents. Again, Noem reached for the same label.

Video evidence and witness accounts contradicted her narrative in both cases. Republican senators called for her resignation. Democrats blocked DHS funding entirely. Tom Homan was dispatched to Minneapolis to take control. The operation was wound down by February 12.

That should have been the end. It wasn’t. Noem fought to hold on — and made it worse. Nine days after Pretti’s shooting, she unilaterally announced that all federal agents in Minneapolis would begin wearing body cameras, with the policy to expand nationwide as funding allowed. Body cameras were a central Democratic demand in the DHS funding negotiations. The White House was furious: she had given away their leverage without getting anything in return. It was one more reason Trump’s team had begun to see her as a liability who could not be controlled.

Then came the hearings.

Two Days Under Oath

Kristi Noem, the homeland security secretary, testifying before lawmakers this week on Capitol Hill.Credit...Eric Lee for The New York Times

On March 3, Noem appeared before the Senate Judiciary Committee. On March 4, the House Judiciary Committee. Forty-eight hours that ended her career.

The hearings had everything: the Lewandowski affair allegations, the luxury jet, the inspector general obstruction, the deployment of ICE agents to polling places that she refused to rule out. Democrats were relentless. Even Republicans broke ranks.

On the Lewandowski question, Democratic Rep. Sydney Kamlager-Dove of California asked Noem directly, under oath, whether she had ever had sexual relations with her top adviser. Noem called it “tabloid garbage” and refused to answer directly — neither confirming nor denying. Seated directly behind her as she spoke was her husband.

Sen. Thom Tillis of North Carolina — a Republican — called her tenure “a disaster,” vowed to block all Senate nominations until she answered his questions, and accused her of turning public opinion against deportation itself. “We’re beginning to get the American people to think that deporting people is wrong,” Tillis said. “It’s the exact opposite. The way you’re going about deporting them is wrong.”

Sen. John Kennedy of Louisiana — another Republican — pressed her on the $200 million advertising campaign in which she prominently featured herself. It was his question that drew the answer that likely sealed her fate.

Under oath, Noem testified that the ad campaign had been approved by the president himself. That Trump knew. Kennedy’s response was pointed: spending $220 million on ads that boosted her name recognition “puts the president in a terribly awkward spot.” He added, on the record, that her claim Trump approved the budget was “hard for me to believe.”

After the hearing, Kennedy told reporters he had spoken with Trump directly. His assessment was surgical: “Her version of the truth and the president’s version of the truth are decidedly different.”

Trump then told Reuters: “I never knew anything about it.”

A sitting Cabinet secretary had contradicted the president under oath. The president had contradicted her back, publicly, within 24 hours. That was the end.

There it is.

The Real Reason

Trump does not fire people for killing American citizens. He does not fire people for lying to the public. He does not fire people for mismanaging disasters or running vanity projects or obstructing inspectors general.

He fires people who embarrass him. More precisely: he fires people who create evidence of his own involvement in their failures.

Noem’s testimony did something unforgivable. Under oath — in a setting where lying has consequences — she drew a direct line between a $220 million self-promotional advertising campaign and the president who authorized it. She put his fingerprints on her vanity project in a legally significant forum. She made him a co-owner of the disaster.

Sen. Kennedy understood this immediately. The ads “put the president in a terribly awkward spot.” That phrase is the tell. Not you are in an awkward spot. The president.

Within 24 hours, Trump told Reuters he had nothing to do with the ad campaign. He announced her replacement the same day.

He did not fire her for Renee Good. He did not fire her for Alex Pretti. He did not fire her for FEMA, or for the luxury jet, or for the obstruction, or for the $220 million she spent on herself. He fired her because under oath, in front of the cameras, she told the truth about him — and he could not let that stand.

The Domestic Terrorism Lie, One Last Time

There is one more moment from the hearings that deserves its own accounting.

Rep. Jamie Raskin, the ranking Democrat on the House Judiciary Committee, asked Noem more than six times whether she stood by her characterization of Good and Pretti as domestic terrorists. Six times, she refused to retract.

She tried to split a hair: she had not called them domestic terrorists, she said. She had said their actions appeared to be an act of domestic terrorism. The distinction was one she appeared to have invented on the spot.

Sen. Amy Klobuchar asked Noem if she had anything to say to Alex Pretti’s parents.

“I can not even imagine what they have gone through in the loss of their son,” Noem said, declining for the second time Tuesday when asked to apologize to Pretti’s family.

She could imagine it enough to offer condolences. She could not bring herself to admit she was wrong. The video evidence contradicted her. The witness accounts contradicted her. Her own Republican colleagues contradicted her. And she sat before the cameras and the cameras carried it live and she still would not say she was wrong.

This was not defiance. This was a person who had no other move. To admit error was to hand the families of Renee Good and Alex Pretti a weapon they could use in court. To admit error was to validate the Democratic case for blocking DHS funding. To admit error, above all, was to admit that the domestic terrorism framing — the framing that came from the White House, from Tom Homan, from Donald Trump himself — was a lie.

She held the line. She held it for everyone above her, right to the end.

And they fired her anyway.

File Closed

The Noem Files began in January 2026 with the death of Renee Nicole Good — a mother, a poet, an American citizen shot in the street by her own government. It continued through the death of Alex Jeffrey Pretti, through the shutdown of DHS, through the Democratic funding standoff, through Operation Metro Surge’s humiliating end on February 12, through two days of congressional testimony.

Now it ends here. Not with justice. Not with accountability. With a Truth Social post and a made-up title: Envoy for the Shield of the Americas. A promotion-shaped firing. A graceful exit for a woman who called two dead Americans terrorists and never apologized to their families.

Her replacement, Sen. Markwayne Mullin of Oklahoma, takes over March 31. He is expected to be “spectacular,” Trump said. The mass deportation agenda continues. The investigations into the Minneapolis shootings continue. The DHS remains partially shuttered as Democrats continue to negotiate for the accountability reforms they have demanded since January.

Note the date: March 31 is also the candidate filing deadline in South Dakota. Some Republicans had urged Trump to wait until after that date before firing Noem, worried she might gather signatures to run for Senate or House and create a messy primary. Trump couldn’t wait — but he buried the filing deadline into his own Truth Social announcement, ensuring she would be mid-transition when the window closed. Even the firing was engineered to contain her.

What does not continue is Kristi Noem’s presence at the head of the Department of Homeland Security. That much, at least, is resolved.

But note carefully how it resolved. Not because Republicans found their conscience. Not because the rule of law prevailed. Not because two families received justice for their murdered children. Kristi Noem fell because she said something inconvenient about Donald Trump in a room where lying had consequences.

In the Trump administration, that is the only sin that cannot be forgiven.

Inside DHS, the reaction was unambiguous. As one official told The Atlantic: “Lots of happy people here today.”

A Note From the Canadian Side of the Border

From up here, we watch American democracy test itself in real time. This is one of those tests.

Two United States citizens are dead. They were shot by their own government. The official who branded them terrorists without evidence served thirteen months, acquired a government jet with a bedroom, spent $200 million on ads featuring herself, and departed to a diplomatic sinecure with the president’s warm regards.

The families of Renee Good and Alex Pretti are still waiting for an apology that will not come from this administration.

The file is closed. The case is not.
