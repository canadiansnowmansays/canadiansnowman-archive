The Pledge

Essay #19: How AI’s Energy Crisis Became America’s Midterm Problem

March 5, 2026

President Trump hosts tech CEOs for the signing of the Ratepayer Protection Pledge at the White House, March 4, 2026. The event promised that data-center operators would ‘build, bring, or buy’ their own power. Official White House pool photo (via Reuters coverage)

So let me get this straight.

On March 4, 2026 — today, as I write this — Donald Trump summoned the chief executives of Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, Oracle, OpenAI, and xAI to the White House to sign something called the “Ratepayer Protection Pledge.” The ceremony had all the theatrical precision this administration has perfected: the titans of American technology, seated before cameras, pledging to “build, bring, or buy” their own power for AI data centers so that ordinary Americans won’t foot the bill.

This is the same president who promised during his 2024 campaign to cut electricity prices in half during his first year. Instead, residential electricity prices increased 6.9 percent in 2025 — more than double the headline inflation rate. This is the same president who halted the Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind project, a 2.6 gigawatt project that was months from powering the largest cluster of data centers on earth. And now he’s asking the companies that need that power to pinky-swear they’ll find their own.

Ari Peskoe, Director of the Harvard Law School Electricity Law Initiative, called it what it is: “an unenforceable corporate ESG commitment intended to trick the public into thinking that the White House is doing something about rising energy costs.” The White House, he noted, “has no real authority here aside from the bully pulpit.”

From Toronto, I confess I’m marveling at the audacity. But I’m not laughing. Because the numbers underneath this ceremony are genuinely frightening, and the political consequences are heading straight for the November midterms.

Here’s What’s Actually Happening to Your Electricity Bill

Before 2019, American electricity prices had been essentially flat for more than a decade — about 13 cents per kilowatt-hour. By the end of 2025, the national average hit 19 cents. That’s a 27 percent increase in six years, and the Energy Information Administration says another four percent is coming in 2026. Goldman Sachs projects the pain will persist through the end of the decade, with lower-income households hit hardest because electricity eats a bigger share of their budgets.

Now here’s the number that should stop you cold. In 2025, utilities received data center interconnection requests totaling at least 700 gigawatts of power. The entire United States consumed 477 gigawatts in 2023. Read that again. The data center industry is requesting more electricity than America currently uses. Yes, many of these projects will never be built. But the requests alone are driving a massive infrastructure ramp-up. Utilities requested more than $29 billion in rate increases in the first half of 2025 — double the previous year. Guess who pays?

You do.

The Brookings Institution pegs data center electricity consumption at 4.4 percent of national power in 2023, rising to as much as 12 percent by 2028. Globally, AI could account for 21 percent of all electricity consumption by 2030.

And here’s the part that should make you furious. While residential prices rose 25 percent between 2020 and 2024, data centers — the entities actually driving the demand — were scoring handsome discounts on their rates. Yale Climate Connections documented this in painstaking detail: everyday people using their toasters and laptops and electric heating are paying more and more, while the companies consuming ever more power pay less. If you wanted to design a formula for political backlash, you couldn’t do better.

Data Center Alley in Virginia—home to the densest cluster on the planet—illustrates why PJM’s grid is under unprecedented strain. The map shows hyperscale demand hotspots across the U.S. Visual Capitalist (data-center demand capacity map)

Ground Zero: 65 Million People, One Broken Grid

If you want to understand why this issue is about to eat the midterm elections alive, you need to understand PJM Interconnection.

PJM operates the largest wholesale electricity market in the United States — more than 65 million people across 13 states in the Mid-Atlantic and Midwest. That footprint includes Pennsylvania and Virginia. Swing states. Remember that.

PJM holds capacity auctions to ensure enough power generation exists to meet future demand. The results tell a story of a market in freefall. Auction pricing went from $34 per megawatt-day in 2023-24 to $329 for 2026-27. Nearly a tenfold increase. In the December 2025 auction alone, data centers accounted for $6.5 billion — 40 percent — of the $16.4 billion in total costs. Across the last three auctions combined, data center forecasts drove 45 percent of $47.2 billion in capacity costs.

The independent market monitor calls this a “massive wealth transfer.” I’d call it something less polite.

It gets worse. That December auction came up 6,600 megawatts short of PJM’s reliability target. The grid operator cannot guarantee enough power to prevent more than one unexpected outage every ten years. Without a temporary price cap negotiated with Pennsylvania’s governor, capacity prices would have hit $530 per megawatt-day — 60 percent higher. Joe Bowring, president of the market monitor, told CNBC that “PJM has never been this short” and called it “a crisis stage.”

PJM capacity-auction clearing prices exploded from $34/MW-day (2023-24) to $329/MW-day (2026-27), with data centers driving 40–45% of recent costs. The December 2025 auction fell 6,600 MW short of reliability targets. TPI Efficiency analysis of PJM Base Residual Auction results

So what did the administration do? In January, Trump and a bipartisan group of governors asked PJM to hold an emergency auction where tech companies would bid on 15-year contracts for new power. Analysts at Capstone immediately noted the proposal “lacks binding authority, reinforcing that this is policy signaling, not an imminent market reform.” PJM wasn’t even invited to the White House event announcing it. As of this writing, PJM has filed proposals with FERC to fast-track a handful of interconnection requests and extend a price collar on its next two auctions. Incremental band-aids on a hemorrhaging wound.

Why the Pledge Can’t Do What It Promises

Here’s the thing about today’s ceremony that nobody on that stage wants to talk about: the federal government doesn’t control the levers that would make this pledge work.

Electricity regulation is decentralized across 50 states, each with its own public utility commission and its own laws. Rob Gramlich, president of Grid Strategies and a former FERC economic advisor, put it simply: “The White House can’t do that on its own. It doesn’t have any jurisdiction there, and of course the technology companies can’t do that on their own either.” You’d need a new federal law. Good luck getting that through this Congress.

But the deeper problem is structural. Say the tech companies actually build their own power plants. Wonderful. Except now they’re buying less electricity from the grid, which means less revenue for utilities — but the aging grid still needs upgrading, and those costs still land on residential ratepayers. Data centers also need backup grid access to run 24/7, so upgrades may be necessary anyway. Under old rules, data centers could offset their grid charges by the full amount of their on-site generation — effectively dodging their share of infrastructure costs that everyone else paid. PJM is now trying to cap that offset at 50 megawatts. The fact that this cap didn’t exist before tells you everything about who’s been subsidizing whom.

Jon Gordon, a director at Advanced Energy United, offered what I think is the most devastating critique: the pledge doesn’t get new power online any faster. And by pushing fossil fuel generation over solar and wind — which deploy far more quickly — the administration may actually be delaying the relief consumers need most.

Senator Mark Kelly of Arizona nailed it: “A handshake agreement with Big Tech over data center costs isn’t good enough. Americans need a guarantee that energy prices won’t soar and communities have a say.”

A handshake agreement. That’s exactly what this is. To be fair, the pledge represents at least an explicit acknowledgment that ratepayers shouldn’t be subsidizing hyperscale load — which is more than we’ve heard from any previous administration. But here’s the thing: several of these CEOs had already made similar commitments independently. Microsoft pledged in January not to let its data centers drive up prices. Anthropic committed to covering 100 percent of consumer price increases from its facilities. So what, exactly, is the White House adding beyond the photo opportunity?

Meanwhile, the States Are Slamming the Brakes

While the White House stages its ceremony, something far more consequential is happening in state legislatures across the country. I’ve been tracking this, and the scale is remarkable.

As of late February 2026, at least eleven states have filed data center moratorium bills. More than 300 data center–related bills have been introduced across 30-plus states in just six weeks. Good Jobs First, a nonprofit that tracks economic development subsidies, calls it a signal that “the political system is starting to acknowledge the obvious: hyperscale data centers are huge, fast-moving, and highly subsidized.”

Moratorium momentum: Protesters and legislators in multiple states (New York, Michigan, Oklahoma, Georgia, etc.) are demanding pauses on new hyperscale projects above 20–100 MW while impacts on rates, water, and land are studied.
Source: Wisconsin Examiner / MPR News (2026 moratorium protest coverage)

New York’s proposal is the most aggressive — a three-year moratorium on new facilities above 20 megawatts. Oklahoma wants a ban above 100 megawatts until November 2029. Vermont’s bill would freeze construction until July 2030. Georgia, Maryland, Michigan, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, South Dakota, and Virginia all have moratorium bills in play. On March 3 — yesterday — the Ypsilanti, Michigan city council passed both an emergency 60-day moratorium and a 365-day resolution. Ann Arbor Township already passed its own 12-month freeze.

Here’s what’s important: the moratorium movement is not anti-technology. It’s anti-subsidy. For years, states treated hyperscale data centers as a race — bigger tax breaks, faster approvals, fewer questions asked. At least 36 states offer data center subsidies, but only 11 publicly disclose the recipients. Virginia’s data center tax subsidy alone has reached nearly a billion dollars a year. The moratoriums are the market correction — communities demanding time to figure out what these facilities actually cost in electricity, water, land use, and public money before approving more of them.

For a retired businessman from Toronto, this feels familiar. It’s what happens when the incentives get so far out of alignment that the political system has to impose a hard stop. And in fairness, this is American federalism doing what it’s designed to do — when federal leadership fails, states and municipalities step into the vacuum, messy and slow and responsive to local voters. That’s not a bug. But it does mean you get eleven different brakes applied eleven different ways with no coordinated plan for what comes after the pause.

The Offshore Wind Contradiction (or: You Can’t Make This Up)

I’ve saved the most absurd part for here, because it deserves its own moment.

In December 2025, the Trump administration halted five East Coast offshore wind projects under construction, citing national security concerns — specifically, that turbine blades and towers could interfere with military radar systems. The concern isn’t fabricated; offshore turbines are as tall as the Eiffel Tower and radar does see them. But CVOW’s two pilot turbines had been operating for five years without a single security incident, the project passed both FAA and Department of Defense review during permitting, and it enjoyed bipartisan support including from Republican House Speaker Mike Johnson. Among the halted projects was the Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind project — 176 turbines, $11.2 billion, 60 percent complete, months from generating power. It would have delivered 2.6 gigawatts to Virginia — home to the largest data center cluster on the planet. Enough electricity for more than 600,000 homes.

Dominion Energy sued. Their filing was unusually blunt for a major utility: “Stopping CVOW for any length of time will threaten grid reliability for some of the nation’s most important war fighting, AI, and civilian assets. It will also lead to energy inflation and threaten thousands of jobs.” A federal court agreed, and construction resumed in January. But the interruption cost $228 million. Tariffs added another $137 million. Total price tag: $11.5 billion, up from $11.2 billion. Virginia ratepayers will absorb the difference.

I want to make sure you’re following the logic here. The president halted the largest single source of new electricity generation heading to the region where AI data centers are most concentrated. He blocked 2.6 gigawatts of clean energy from reaching Data Center Alley. Then — three months later — he invited the companies building those data centers to the White House to pledge they’d find their own power.

Abe Silverman, former general counsel for New Jersey’s public utility board, didn’t mince words: “By stopping a project that had line of sight to coming online in the very near future, you are directly increasing the prices that we all pay for electricity, and not by a little bit. That’s a huge, huge additional hole that we now have to dig out of.”

As a Canadian who has spent many essays documenting the energy technologies that could solve this crisis — solar, wind, nuclear, fusion, batteries, even space-based solutions — watching America actively sabotage one of the fastest paths to new generation while staging a pledge ceremony about energy costs is like watching someone set fire to the kitchen and then announce a new fire safety policy.

The Electoral Reckoning Is Already Here

A hyperscale data center campus in Virginia. Utilities received interconnection requests for at least 700 GW in 2025—more than the entire U.S. grid consumed in 2023. Virginia Mercury (aerial of Northern Virginia data-center park)

The political consequences arrived in November 2025. They weren’t subtle.

In Virginia, Democrat Abigail Spanberger won the governor’s race by more than 14 points after promising to make data centers “pay their own way and their fair share.” In New Jersey, where ratepayers had been hit with a 20 percent utility bill increase, Democrat Mikie Sherrill won on a promise to declare a state of emergency over electricity costs on her first day in office. The League of Conservation Voters called the results proof that “electricity bills are the kitchen table issue driving Democratic wins in 2025 and beyond.”

Note what these candidates did not campaign on. They didn’t campaign on climate change. They campaigned on affordability. Spanberger pushed renewable energy not to save the planet but to lower bills. Sherrill promised a rate freeze, new nuclear capacity, and an end to PJM’s “mismanagement.” The lesson for any politician paying attention: whoever is seen as responsible for rising electricity prices will pay at the ballot box.

Now multiply this by the 2026 midterms. PJM’s 13-state footprint includes Pennsylvania and Virginia — the swing states that decide congressional majorities. Every candidate in those regions will be asked what they intend to do about electricity prices. As Gramlich told CNBC: “Every politician is going to be saying that they have the answer to affordability and their opponents’ policies would raise rates.”

Energy Secretary Chris Wright warned the tech companies that if they “are perceived to drive up electricity prices, they will reap the backlash.” But perception is already reality. Wholesale electricity costs have increased as much as 267 percent over five years in areas near data centers. One Maryland ratepayer commenting on today’s pledge said his utility costs jumped 37 percent last year. “Pledges from companies are nice,” he wrote, “but they haven’t lowered my utility bills.”

No, they haven’t. And a ceremony at the White House won’t change that.

The Pledge That Matters

From Toronto, the picture is clarifying with uncomfortable speed.

The United States has an AI energy crisis that is real, measurable, and heading for the ballot box. The administration’s response consists of a voluntary pledge with no enforcement mechanism, an emergency auction proposal with no binding authority, and an energy policy that simultaneously promotes data centers while blocking the clean energy projects that could most quickly power them.

The moratorium wave sweeping eleven states isn’t a solution. It’s a symptom — communities slamming the brakes because nobody in Washington built the road. The states are doing what federalism allows them to do, and good for them. But eleven separate pauses without a coordinating federal framework isn’t a plan. It’s a holding pattern.

Throughout this series, I’ve documented the technologies that could address this crisis. Every one of them requires political will, regulatory coherence, and investment horizons measured in years, not news cycles. What we’re watching instead is an administration that treats energy policy as a series of performative moments — a pledge here, an executive order there — while the underlying crisis deepens month by month.

The AI revolution will not wait for American politics to catch up. The question is whether the political costs of inaction will force a reckoning before the grid itself forces one.

Right now, the answer is being written in utility bills arriving in 65 million mailboxes across PJM’s 13-state footprint every single month. Those bills are the pledge that matters. And unlike the one signed at the White House today, they are not voluntary.
