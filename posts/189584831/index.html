The Iran Crisis

The Price of Everything

Part XXVII: The Market Reaction

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

The attack on Iran has brought disruption to oil markets, shipping and supply chains.Credit...Karsten Moran for The New York Times

At some point every war becomes a number.

Not a body count, though those matter. Not a missile count, though those are being tracked. A price. The price the world assigns — in real time, with real money, with real consequences for everyone who heats a home or fills a tank or runs a factory — to the question: how bad is this, and how long does it last?

Sunday night the number arrived.

WTI crude opened at $72.52 at 6 PM ET — up $7.31, or 11.2%, from Friday’s close of $65.21. Brent hit $79.41 in early trading, up roughly 9%, with the OTC market having already touched $80 during the afternoon. By 7:30 PM ET the picture was deteriorating for equities, spiking for energy, and remarkably contained for gold. S&P 500 futures dropped 1.08%. Nasdaq futures fell 1.11%. Dow futures shed over 500 points, down 1.18%. Oil is pricing a war. Equities are pricing a short one.

This essay is about what those numbers mean — and what they reveal about how the world is actually pricing a war that has closed the Strait of Hormuz, killed a supreme leader, killed three Americans, killed 115 children in a schoolyard, and produced a back-channel diplomatic signal all in the same 36 hours.

The Baseline: What We Already Knew

Before Sunday night’s open, three data points had already established the market’s early posture.

Brent crude closed Friday at $73 per barrel — already up approximately 20% on the year before a single bomb fell. The war arrived into a market that was already pricing geopolitical risk into energy. The question Sunday night was not whether oil would rise. It was how far, and what that distance means.

Bitcoin offered the first sentiment read. Day One saw it drop 4% on the war news, recover, and close up 1.15%. As of Sunday morning it was trading at $66,970. The crypto market was not pricing civilizational collapse. It was pricing uncertainty — which is different from pricing catastrophe.

Middle Eastern equity markets that trade on Sunday gave the first preview of what Monday morning would look like everywhere else. Egypt and Saudi Arabia closed down more than 2%. Kuwait’s stock exchange suspended trading indefinitely, concluding its announcement: “May God protect Kuwait and its people from all harm.” The one exception: Saudi Aramco gained more than 3%. The company that pumps the oil that will now be needed to replace what isn’t moving through Hormuz was the first winner of the war’s economic phase.

OPEC+ moved preemptively, announcing a production increase of 206,000 barrels per day beginning in April. The move was almost universally assessed as insufficient relative to the scale of the disruption. It was nonetheless a signal: the cartel was not prepared to let the war burn unchecked through the global energy system without at least gesturing toward stabilization.

Against that baseline, two analyst projections framed Sunday night’s open:

Eurasia Group: $5-10 per barrel increase. The managed scenario — significant but containable, pricing a short conflict with a negotiated off-ramp.

Barclays analyst Amarpreet Singh: above $100 per barrel. The fear scenario. “How this ends is extremely uncertain at this point, but in the meantime oil markets will have to face their worst fears.” Brent has not traded at $100 since the post-Ukraine invasion inflation surge of 2022 — the surge that contributed materially to the inflation that defined the political landscape of the years that followed.

Sunday night’s number falls somewhere in that range. Where it falls tells you which scenario the world’s capital allocators believe they are in.

The Hormuz Premium

The Strait of Hormuz is not merely a shipping lane. It is the single most consequential geographic chokepoint in the global energy system.

One-fifth of the world’s daily oil supply — approximately 20 million barrels — moves through a passage 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE are all significantly or entirely dependent on Hormuz for their oil and gas exports. Saudi Arabia can route some volume through the East-West Pipeline to the Red Sea, but not enough to compensate for a sustained closure. Iran sits on the northern shore. The US Fifth Fleet operates from Bahrain.

The numbers from the Strait tell the story more starkly than any analyst forecast.

On Friday — two days ago — 65 tankers carrying energy products transited the Strait of Hormuz, according to S&P Global Energy’s Commodities at Sea. By Sunday afternoon Iran time, that figure had fallen to 6. Not 70% down. Not “effectively closed.” A 91% collapse in two days.

Six tankers. Down from sixty-five.

Kpler analyst Dimitris Ampatzidis confirmed only Iranian tankers were transiting. Ships that had been in the waterway were either heading to Iranian ports or had not disclosed their destinations. Every other flag had stood down.

The oil tanker Skylight — struck 5 miles north of Khasab Port in Oman, four sailors injured, photographed burning by witnesses — is the physical embodiment of the risk premium that every energy trader in the world is now pricing. A second vessel was struck. A projectile exploded near a third. The naval dimension of this war is fully operational and the shipping industry has drawn its own conclusions without waiting for official guidance.

There is one mitigating factor the bulls are pointing to: as of late Sunday, no major oil production installations in the region had been struck. Amy Myers Jaffe, director of the Energy, Climate Justice and Sustainability Lab at NYU: “The biggest question is what, if any, oil installations get damaged. If the answer to that is none, my opinion is the price of oil will come back down.” That conditional is still open.

The transmission to the consumer pump is not instantaneous but it is reliable: every $10 per barrel increase in oil translates to approximately 20-30 cents per gallon at the pump. The AAA national average for regular gasoline on Sunday was $2.98 per gallon — a number that has defined Trump’s economic messaging and approval ratings. A $20 oil shock puts gas at roughly $3.38-3.58. A $30 shock approaches $3.58-3.88. The post-Ukraine peak was $5.02 in June 2022. The political damage from that spike was generational — and Trump rode the backlash back to the White House.

At 6:41 PM ET: WTI at $72.52, up 11.2% from Friday’s $65.21 close. Brent at $79.41, up 8.8% from $73.00. Day range for WTI: $71.11-$74.99 — meaning even the low of the session is 9% above Friday’s close. The market opened hard and stayed there. No early spike-and-fade. This is a sustained bid on energy disruption risk.

The gap between Brent and WTI — normally a reliable spread — will be especially instructive Sunday night. A widening Brent premium over WTI signals that the market is specifically pricing the Middle Eastern supply disruption, since Brent is more directly tied to international seaborne crude while WTI reflects North American production. American energy independence means the US is somewhat insulated from a Hormuz closure in ways that Europe, China, Japan, and South Korea are not. If the spread widens, the market is telling you the pain is being allocated unevenly — and that the economies most exposed are not the ones that launched the war.

What Each Price Scenario Means

Below $85: Markets are pricing the Trump back-channel. They believe “they want to talk, and I have agreed to talk” is a real signal toward a short conflict. The Eurasia Group’s $5-10 scenario. Disruption but not catastrophe. The global economy absorbs it. OPEC+’s 206,000 barrel increase looks sufficient as a confidence signal. The war stays a Middle East story rather than becoming a global economic story.

$85-100: Markets are pricing an extended conflict with uncertain resolution — weeks of bombing, a leadership transition in Iran that takes time, the Strait remaining partially or wholly disrupted through March. The Barclays threshold approaches but hasn’t broken. This is the scenario in which the economic war begins to arrive in the daily lives of ordinary people in Europe, Asia, and eventually the United States — not catastrophically, but noticeably. Gas prices. Heating bills. Freight costs feeding through to consumer goods. The midterm election math starts shifting.

Above $100: The Barclays scenario. Not seen since post-Ukraine 2022. The Strait is being priced as a sustained disruption, not a temporary crisis. This scenario prices the possibility that the war outlasts its initial phase without a negotiated pause — that Vahidi’s IRGC fights rather than deals, that the back-channel fails or stalls, that the 80% opportunists calculating their futures cannot produce a unified decision fast enough to stop the economic hemorrhage. At $100+, this is no longer primarily a Middle East geopolitical story. It is a global inflation story. It is a Chinese industrial slowdown story. It is a European recession risk story. It is the China energy thesis activating in the worst possible way — American energy leverage over China demonstrably real, but at a cost that ricochets through every economy on earth including the American one.

Jason Bordoff, founding director of the Center on Global Energy Policy at Columbia University, offered the most grounded pre-open assessment: “Americans will see some impact at the gasoline pump. But even with a massive strike on Iran that killed the leader of the country, at this point we’re still talking about oil prices that are well within historical norms — and much less than one would have ever expected with a strike of this magnitude.” That is the credentialed bull case. It depends on no oil installations being struck, on the back-channel being real, and on the conflict being short.

The verdict at 7:30 PM ET: WTI at $72.52. Brent at ~$79.41. This puts us in the below-$85 scenario — the Eurasia Group call confirmed almost exactly. Markets are pricing a short conflict with a diplomatic off-ramp. They believe the back-channel is real. The Barclays $100 scenario is not being priced. The UBS $120 scenario is not being priced. What is being priced is disruption — serious, real, sustained — but not catastrophe. The distinction between those two words is worth about $20 per barrel and the difference between a market correction and a market crash.

The China Number

Part XXV documented the China energy thesis: that the Iran operation is, at its strategic core, an energy leverage play against China. Sunday night’s market open provides the first empirical data point on whether that leverage is actually working.

China imports over 10 million barrels of oil per day. It holds strategic petroleum reserves — estimates vary, but most analysts place Chinese SPR holdings at 60-90 days of import coverage at normal consumption rates. A short Hormuz disruption China can absorb from reserves. A disruption measured in weeks begins to stress that buffer. A disruption measured in months becomes a structural economic problem.

China’s Sunday night posture: Beijing has been notably restrained — calling for de-escalation, requesting a UN Security Council meeting alongside Russia, but taking no action. Bloomberg attributes this restraint to the Xi-Trump March summit and the desire to preserve the trade truce. Chinese markets don’t open until Monday morning Beijing time. The first real Chinese market verdict — yuan movement, Shanghai Composite, Chinese energy futures — comes overnight. Watch for it before publication if time allows. The absence of any Chinese economic countermove through Sunday night is itself a data point the Newsweek analysts predicted: a power that watches its energy jugular threatened and responds with press releases may be calculating that the cost of defending Iran exceeds the benefit.

China’s public response has been a foreign ministry statement condemning the strikes. Its market response — when Chinese futures open — will be more informative than any statement. Capital does not lie in the way that diplomats do.

The China energy thesis predicts that China’s energy vulnerability will translate into strategic restraint — that Beijing, watching its oil supply threatened, will calculate that the cost of defending Iran exceeds the benefit and will effectively stand aside. Sunday night’s numbers begin the long process of confirming or refuting that prediction.

The Inflation Ghost

The number that should be haunting every Western political leader Sunday night is not the oil price itself. It is the inflation transmission mechanism.

Oil at $100 per barrel is not just a number at the pump. It is a cost input for every manufactured good that moves by truck, train, or ship. It is a heating cost for every household in Europe that depends on natural gas priced in relation to oil. It is a production cost for every factory in Germany, Japan, South Korea, and India that buys petrochemical feedstocks. It is a logistics cost for every supply chain that has not yet fully recovered from the 2021-2022 inflation surge.

The post-Ukraine inflation surge — triggered in significant part by energy price shocks — defined the political landscape of the following three years in every Western democracy. Gas peaked at $5.02 per gallon in June 2022, several months after the initial invasion price shock. The political damage lasted years. Trump rode it back to the White House.

The Ukraine comparison is instructive on timing. In the week after Russia invaded, oil prices climbed approximately 20%. But the average price of a gallon of regular gasoline rose only about 3% in that same week — because the pump lags the futures market. It was not until the following week that drivers started seeing significantly higher prices. The record came months later.

Ken Medlock, energy fellow at Rice University’s Baker Institute, named the variable that controls the timeline: “The pace will really depend on how severe the supply constraint reveals itself to be.”

There is a dimension beyond crude oil that is not yet in most headlines. A significant amount of liquefied natural gas — the fuel that powers electricity generation and heavy industry across Europe and Asia — also moves through the waters around Iran. A sustained disruption to LNG flows would, over time, raise electricity prices in Europe, increase industrial production costs globally, and compound the inflationary pressure from crude oil alone. The crude oil price is the visible number. The LNG dimension is the slow-building second wave. It produced electoral upheaval across Europe. It contributed to the conditions that returned Trump to power. Voters who experienced that inflation have not forgotten it. The political leaders who are now watching oil approach or breach $100 are looking at an electorate that has been pre-sensitized to energy price pain in a way it was not before 2022.

If Sunday night’s number trends toward $100 and holds there, the war’s domestic political consequences in the United States, Europe, and beyond will not wait for the battlefield outcome. They will arrive at the pump, on the heating bill, at the grocery store — before any peace deal, before any successor government in Tehran, before the Assembly of Experts names a new supreme leader.

Trump launched this war in part because he was convinced Iran’s breaking point was near. The economic war that Sunday night’s market open inaugurates has its own breaking points — and some of them are domestic.

What Analysts Said Before the Bell

The New York Times canvassed Wall Street analysts on Sunday afternoon — before a single futures contract had traded. Their collective positioning tells you something important about how professional money managers are thinking about this war.

The consensus view is cautious but not catastrophic. Investors have been rewarded over the past year by buying the dip whenever markets declined and quickly recovered. The last two US military interventions — Venezuela in January and the June nuclear facility strikes — had negligible effects on financial markets. Wall Street has largely shrugged off geopolitical upheaval in recent years.

But analysts are flagging two reasons this shock is different.

First, the market structure has shifted. Technology company dominance has waned in 2026 as AI concerns have mounted. The new drivers are industrial and energy companies — sectors more directly tied to the global economy and world events. Some analysts say this leaves the market more vulnerable to the current shock than it would have been twelve months ago.

Second, the off-ramp problem. The path to de-escalation from the June nuclear facility strikes was clearly laid out — defined objective, contained scope, known endpoint. This operation has no equivalent clarity. “The path to de-escalation in Iran is not as clearly laid out,” the NYT noted. Prolonged uncertainty is a different market environment than a defined shock.

Ajay Rajadhyaksha, global chairman of research at Barclays — the same firm that put the $100/barrel warning into the series in the first place — pulled back from the buy-the-dip instinct on Sunday: “We would recommend not buying any immediate dip — the risk-reward doesn’t seem compelling.” He added: “There is likely to come a time to buy. But not yet.”

The contrarian call comes from Peter Tchir, head of macro strategy at Academy Securities: “I would not be surprised to see stocks close up on Monday and oil down.” That is the bull scenario — short shock, back-channel deal, markets look through the noise. It is not the consensus, but it is on the table.

Michael Purves at Tallbacken Capital offered the cleanest framing: “To me, the most obvious thing here is if you are overweight oil stocks then you are happy.” Saudi Aramco’s 3%+ gain on Sunday confirmed this. The war has a winner in the energy sector. Everything else is a question mark.

One more variable: the Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Kevin Book of ClearView Energy Partners flagged that the Trump administration can tap the SPR — currently holding approximately 415 million barrels — to cap oil prices if they spike. “But we’ll say it again: in supply crises, duration matters. Scale does, too. A full Hormuz crisis could outstrip offsets provided by strategic stocks.”

Duration is the word every analyst is circling. A short shock the market can absorb. A prolonged closure it cannot.

Markets Tonight — Snapshot

Two columns matter here. The Friday baseline is the last price before the war began. The Sunday night read is the first price after markets reopened with bombs falling — the war premium expressed in dollars.

Reading the table:

Oil futures and gold futures both spiking = inflation ghost and fear premium arriving simultaneously — the worst combined scenario for Western economies still carrying post-2022 inflation sensitivity.

Brent premium widening sharply over WTI = market allocating energy pain specifically to import-dependent economies (Europe, China, Japan, South Korea) rather than energy-independent US — the China energy thesis activating in real time.

S&P/Nasdaq/Dow futures down hard while oil spikes = war premium entering equity valuations; watch for the spread between energy sector gains (Aramco +3% already) and broad market losses.

VIX above 25 = fear entering the system seriously. VIX above 30 = institutional hedging in full swing.

Bitcoin diverging from gold = crypto pricing uncertainty while gold prices catastrophe, or vice versa. Convergence upward in both = flight from all paper assets.

Any of these combinations tells a different story about which scenario — short conflict with diplomatic off-ramp, extended attrition, or failed state chaos — the world’s capital allocators believe they are actually in.

The Number That Matters Most

Brent at ~$79.41 as of 7:30 PM ET, ahead of ICE official open at 8 PM. The OTC market had already touched $80 during the afternoon. Reuters headline from earlier today: “Brent crude jumped 10% to about $80 a barrel over the counter.” The official ICE open will confirm or extend this move. Watch for whether Brent breaks decisively through $80 — that is the line between Eurasia Group’s confirmed call and the beginning of the Barclays scenario territory.

The Brent-WTI spread: Brent at $79.41, WTI at $72.52. Spread: $6.89. Normal spread is $3-5. A widening spread confirms the market is specifically pricing Middle Eastern supply disruption and allocating that pain to import-dependent economies rather than the energy-independent US. At $6.89, the spread is already signaling that Europe, China, Japan, and South Korea are bearing the cost of a war they did not start and were not consulted about.

By the time this essay is published, the market has spoken its first word on the Iran war. Not its final word — oil markets will trade every scenario the coming days produce, every diplomatic signal, every missile launch, every statement from Larijani or Arafi or Trump or Netanyahu.

But the first word is in. And in a conflict where so much is happening in the dark — the succession in Tehran, the back-channel diplomacy, the IRGC’s internal calculations, the Assembly of Experts deliberating under bombardment — the market’s first word is the only number that has been produced in full transparency, by thousands of actors with real stakes, in real time.

It is not a perfect signal. Markets are not omniscient. They priced the 2008 financial crisis incorrectly until they couldn’t. They priced the post-COVID recovery incorrectly until supply chains forced a correction. They are pricing this war with incomplete information, like everyone else.

But they are the best available aggregation of what informed people with money at stake believe about how this ends. And that belief, expressed in a dollar figure per barrel on a Sunday night while bombs are still falling on Tehran, is the most honest assessment available of the question this series has been asking since Saturday morning:

How bad is this? And how long does it last?

The number that matters most, at 7:30 PM ET on the first Sunday night of the war:

WTI: $72.52. Brent: ~$79.41. S&P futures: -1.08%. Gold: +1.4%. Bitcoin: -2.0%.

Read together, these numbers say one thing: the world’s capital allocators believe this war will be short, the back-channel is real, and the damage will be contained to the energy sector. They are pricing disruption, not catastrophe. They are buying oil stocks and selling everything else modestly. They are not running for gold. They are not collapsing equities. They are not pricing the $100 Barclays scenario or the $120 UBS scenario.

They may be right. Trump confirmed the back-channel. Iran confirmed it. The White House confirmed it. A senior official said the military assault “would continue unabated” — and in the same breath confirmed Iran had “indicated willingness to talk.” Bombs and back-channels. That combination historically ends wars rather than extending them.

Or the market may be wrong. The Strait has gone from 65 tankers to 6 in two days. No major oil installations have been struck — yet. Vahidi commands the IRGC. The Houthis have not yet acted. The axis of resistance is depleted but not dead. And Barclays’ Rajadhyaksha said it plainly: “There is likely to come a time to buy. But not yet.”

One war. Two readings. The market has placed its bet.

By the time you read this, one of them will be closer to right.
