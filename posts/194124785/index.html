The Contribution Index

Saudi Arabia

Part XIV: The Kingdom of Contradiction

April 24, 2026

The Line, NEOM — a $500 billion mirror in the desert. Saudi Arabia's capacity-to-contribution gap rendered in glass and steel. (Source: NEOM promotional rendering)

Underperformer #4

Saudi Arabia possesses approximately 17 percent of the world’s proven petroleum reserves. Its sovereign wealth fund — the Public Investment Fund — holds assets approaching $1 trillion. It is building NEOM, a planned city in the desert projected to cost $500 billion. It hosts the two holiest sites in Islam, making it the spiritual centre of a faith practised by nearly two billion people worldwide.

It also executes people for sorcery.

The distance between Saudi Arabia’s capacity and its contribution to humanity is not merely a gap. It is an abyss — and it is an abyss by design, maintained by a ruling family that has calculated, correctly so far, that the world will overlook virtually any human rights failure as long as the oil keeps flowing.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 23 out of 50

Saudi Arabia’s human development story is one of genuine material progress coexisting with profound political and social repression. Healthcare has improved significantly over the past three decades — Saudi citizens have access to a well-funded public healthcare system, and life expectancy has risen to approximately 78 years. The infrastructure is modern, the investment substantial. The 7 reflects outcomes that are, for citizens, legitimately good.

Education investment has expanded significantly, particularly for women, who now outnumber men in Saudi universities — a remarkable trajectory for a nation that, within living memory, debated whether women should be educated at all. The 6 reflects both the progress and the persistent limitations: curriculum quality is uneven, critical thinking is not encouraged in a system shaped by religious orthodoxy, and academic freedom is nonexistent.

But democratic governance earns a 1. Saudi Arabia is an absolute monarchy. There are no elections, no political parties, no independent judiciary, no freedom of assembly, and no freedom of expression. Dissidents are imprisoned, tortured, and — as the murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi demonstrated to the world in devastating detail — assassinated. The Khashoggi killing was not an aberration. It was the logical endpoint of a system in which the state tolerates no dissent and faces no accountability.

The score of 1 has no further to fall. In the entire series, only three nations earn a 1 on democratic governance: Saudi Arabia, China, and Russia. The company tells the story.

Saudi women behind the wheel — a right granted by royal decree in 2018, not won through democratic process. What the crown prince gives, the crown prince can take away. (Source: Reuters / AP)

Gender equality earns a 2. The reforms under Vision 2030 are real — women can now drive, attend sporting events, travel without a male guardian’s permission, and participate in parts of the workforce previously closed to them. These changes matter to the women who benefit from them, and dismissing them would be dishonest.

But these reforms are granted by royal decree, not protected by rights. What the crown prince gives, the crown prince can take away. The guardianship system, while weakened, persists in practice. Women’s legal status in matters of marriage, divorce, child custody, and inheritance remains fundamentally unequal. LGBTQ+ rights are nonexistent — homosexuality is punishable by imprisonment, flogging, or death. And the pace of reform is calibrated to the crown prince’s political calculus, not to any principle of human dignity. Reform as political strategy is not the same as reform as moral commitment.

Environmental Stewardship: 18 out of 50

Emissions per capita earn a 2 — Saudi Arabia has one of the highest carbon footprints per person on Earth. Domestic energy consumption is enormous, driven by subsidized petroleum products, energy-intensive desalination (Saudi Arabia is the world’s largest desalinator), and air conditioning in extreme heat. The national economy is built on extracting and selling the fuel that is driving the climate crisis. This is not a secondary feature of the Saudi economy. It is the economy.

Saudi Arabia's solar potential is among the highest on Earth — and among the least urgently pursued. (Source: EcoMENA / ACWA Power)

Renewable energy earns a 3. Saudi Arabia has announced ambitious solar targets — the potential is obvious given the kingdom’s extraordinary solar irradiance and vast uninhabited desert. But execution has been slow relative to the rhetoric, and the fundamental business model remains oil. The Saudi government has stated its intention to diversify the economy through Vision 2030, but as long as petroleum revenues constitute the overwhelming majority of government income, the transition is aspirational rather than operational. A nation that derives its wealth from selling fossil fuels cannot score well on environmental stewardship regardless of how many solar panels it installs alongside the wellheads.

Biodiversity and habitat protection earn a 4. The Arabian Peninsula is not typically associated with ecological richness, but Saudi Arabia possesses significant marine ecosystems in the Red Sea — coral reefs of genuine global importance — and has made some investment in protected areas. The NEOM development on the Red Sea coast raises concerns about the impact of mega-construction on these fragile marine environments.

Red Sea coral reefs — among the world's most resilient marine ecosystems, now adjacent to NEOM's mega-construction footprint. (Source: Saudi Marine Sciences / About Her)

Water and air quality earn a 4. Desalination provides clean drinking water but at enormous energy cost. Air quality in urban areas is affected by dust, industrial emissions, and the petroleum economy’s pervasive footprint.

Systemic Behaviour: 12 out of 50

Arms and conflict contribution earn a 2. Saudi Arabia has been a primary belligerent in the Yemen war — a conflict that has produced one of the world’s worst humanitarian crises, with hundreds of thousands of civilian casualties, millions displaced, and famine conditions affecting a nation of 30 million people. The Saudi-led coalition’s bombing campaign has been documented by the UN, Human Rights Watch, and Amnesty International as causing massive civilian harm, including strikes on hospitals, schools, weddings, and funeral processions. Saudi Arabia is also one of the world’s largest arms importers, channelling billions into weapons systems that sustain the global arms trade.

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earns a 2. Despite its proximity to multiple refugee-producing crises — Syria, Yemen, Iraq, Somalia — and its extraordinary wealth, Saudi Arabia has accepted negligible numbers of refugees. The kingdom’s position is that it has hosted millions of foreign workers, which it characterises as a form of humanitarian contribution. The Contribution Index does not accept this framing: foreign workers under the kafala system are labourers whose legal status is tied to their employers, not refugees offered protection and a path to stability.

Adherence to international law earns a 2. Saudi Arabia’s conduct in Yemen violates multiple provisions of international humanitarian law. Its domestic legal system — based on an interpretation of Sharia that permits execution for apostasy, sorcery, and homosexuality — is fundamentally at odds with international human rights frameworks that the kingdom has nominally endorsed.

Saudi Arabia channels substantial funds through state and royal charitable entities — the King Salman Humanitarian Aid and Relief Centre, the Islamic Development Bank, various royal foundations. The dollar figures are real, and some of the programmes deliver genuine aid. But when the crown prince is the philanthropic sector — when the same individual who ordered the murder of Jamal Khashoggi also directs the charitable spending — the line between contribution and reputation laundering is not merely blurred. It does not exist. Saudi philanthropy is governance by another name, and governance in Saudi Arabia serves one purpose: the perpetuation of royal power.

The Neighbour Test: 9 out of 50

Saudi Arabia’s Neighbour Test scores are uniformly low — 2s across four parameters and a 1 on consistency. This is the score of a kingdom that views every international relationship as a transaction, every alliance as leverage, and every global institution as a venue for reputation management.

Multilateral orientation earns a 2. Saudi Arabia participates in international institutions — OPEC, the G20, the Arab League, the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation — but instrumentalises each one for strategic advantage rather than collective benefit. Its role in OPEC is defined by production quota manipulation for geopolitical and economic leverage. Its G20 participation is primarily a status symbol.

Response to global crises earns a 2. Despite possessing the financial resources to be among the world’s leading humanitarian responders, Saudi Arabia’s contributions are modest relative to its capacity and overwhelmingly channelled through state-directed entities that serve diplomatic objectives as much as humanitarian ones.

Treatment of allies earns a 2. Saudi Arabia’s foreign policy is defined by regional dominance and sectarian competition with Iran — a rivalry that has fuelled proxy conflicts across the Middle East, from Yemen to Lebanon to Iraq. The blockade of Qatar (2017-2021), imposed against a fellow Gulf Cooperation Council member over political disagreements, demonstrated that Saudi Arabia treats even its closest regional partners as subordinates to be disciplined rather than allies to be respected. The recent rapprochement with Iran, brokered by China, was driven by strategic calculation rather than any change in disposition.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 1 — the lowest possible score. Saudi Arabia promotes Vision 2030 as a modernization programme while maintaining a legal system that executes people for sorcery. It brands itself as a reforming nation while the man who ordered the murder of Jamal Khashoggi directs the reforms. It hosts interfaith dialogue while criminalizing non-Islamic worship on its own territory. It pledges climate leadership while its economy depends entirely on selling the fuel that causes the crisis. It champions women’s advancement while women remain legally subordinate in matters of marriage, divorce, and inheritance.

This is not inconsistency in the sense of a nation struggling to align its values with its actions. This is a kingdom in which the gap between branding and reality is not a flaw in the strategy — it is the strategy. Vision 2030 is not a programme of reform. It is a programme of reputational management designed to attract foreign investment and international legitimacy while changing as little as possible about the fundamental distribution of power.

Long-term stewardship earns a 2. A nation sitting on $1 trillion in sovereign wealth that builds $500 billion vanity cities in the desert while its neighbours burn — while Yemen starves, while Lebanon collapses, while the climate crisis that Saudi oil exports accelerate threatens the entire region — is not practising stewardship. It is practising narcissism at civilisational scale.

The Editorial Conclusion

Saudi Arabia scores 62 out of 200. The fourth-ranked underperformer.

The kingdom of contradiction: a nation with the wealth to end poverty across the entire Middle East that instead builds vanity cities in the desert. A nation with the solar potential to power half of Africa that instead exports the oil that is destroying the climate. A nation that reforms women’s rights by royal decree and murders journalists by royal order. A nation that hosts the holiest sites of a faith that teaches charity and compassion while prosecuting a war in Yemen that has produced famine.

Saudi Arabia’s capacity ranking of 12th globally — driven by its vast petroleum wealth, its sovereign fund, and its strategic position — means its Capacity-to-Contribution Gap is enormous. The resources are there. The geographic advantages are permanent. The question is not whether Saudi Arabia can contribute more to humanity. It is whether the ruling family that controls every dimension of national life will ever choose to.

The evidence of this series — scored across twenty parameters, measured against twenty other nations, evaluated by every credible international index available — suggests that the answer, so far, is no.
