The Rise and Disappearance of Tulsi Gabbard

The Director of National Intelligence was not in the room when America decided to go to war. Nobody noticed

April 29, 2026

While America wages war with Iran, the DNI catches a wave. Photo: Tulsi Gabbard's FB

This series documents what happens when the blind lead the blind — when political loyalty becomes the sole qualification for positions that demand expertise. Tulsi Gabbard may be the purest example yet. She wasn't fired. She wasn't disgraced. She simply disappeared. The nation's top intelligence official became invisible during the biggest American military engagement in twenty years, and the only people who noticed were the ones writing about it.

I. The Rise

Give her this much: the original product was interesting.

Tulsi Gabbard arrived in Congress in 2013 as something genuinely rare — a combat veteran, a Hindu, a Democrat from Hawaii who opposed regime-change wars with the kind of moral clarity that made the party establishment deeply uncomfortable. She went to Syria. She met Assad. She called the Iraq War what it was. She resigned as vice chair of the DNC to endorse Bernie Sanders in 2016, torching her relationship with the Clinton wing of the party in a single afternoon.

Agree with her or not, that took something. The old Tulsi Gabbard had a spine, a worldview, and a willingness to pay the price for both. She ran for president in 2020 on an anti-interventionist platform. She was polling in the low single digits, but she was consistent. She sold T-shirts opposing a war with Iran. Not as a fashion statement — as a declaration of principle.

She was, in short, exactly the kind of politician who should never have ended up where she ended up.

II. The Bargain

The bargain is always the same in this series. The details differ, but the transaction is identical: you give up everything that made you interesting, and in exchange you receive a title, an office, and proximity to a man who will discard you the moment you become inconvenient.

Gabbard left the Democratic Party in October 2022, calling it an “elitist cabal of warmongers.” By 2024, she had endorsed Donald Trump. By January 2025, she was the Director of National Intelligence — technically the most senior intelligence official in the United States, the person charged with overseeing the CIA, the NSA, the DIA, and more than a dozen other agencies, and with delivering the President’s Daily Brief to the commander in chief.

The anti-war Democrat from Hawaii was now running the intelligence apparatus of a nation that, within weeks of her confirmation, would be at war.

Let’s pause on the price of admission. To get through her confirmation, Gabbard had to renounce or simply stop mentioning virtually every position that had defined her political identity. The Syria visit? An error of judgment. The scepticism of intelligence agencies? Evolved. The anti-interventionism? Nuanced. The T-shirts? Presumably donated to Goodwill.

What she received in return was a corner office at Liberty Crossing, a title that put her nominally above the CIA director on the org chart, and an institution — the Office of the Director of National Intelligence — that was created in a post-9/11 panic and has been searching for a reason to exist ever since.

III. The Seat at the Kids’ Table

Study this image carefully. Dozens of officials crowd the Situation Room — generals, advisors, aides, the President himself at the centre of the table. Maps are spread. Screens are glowing. Everyone is engaged, animated, essential. Now find the Director of National Intelligence.

Take your time. She’s in there somewhere. Maybe.

In mid-February 2026, the real version of this scene played out. The President of the United States made the decision to go to war with Iran. This was, by any measure, the most consequential national security decision of the administration — and one of the most consequential in a generation.

Present in the room with the President and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu: the White House Chief of Staff, the Secretary of State, the Secretary of Defence, and the CIA Director.

Not present: the Vice President.

Also not present: the Director of National Intelligence. The person who is, on paper, in charge of the entire U.S. intelligence community. The person who is, on the organizational chart, the CIA Director’s boss.

Tulsi Gabbard was not in the room when America decided to go to war.

Read that sentence again, because it is the entire essay in fourteen words. The Director of National Intelligence was excluded from the meeting where intelligence mattered most. She wasn’t overruled. She wasn’t outvoted. She wasn’t even given the dignity of being told she was wrong. She simply wasn’t there.

When the war began, the White House released a photograph of Gabbard and Vice President Vance in the Situation Room with a few Cabinet officials, looking — as Tom Nichols put it with characteristic precision — like they’d been sent to the kids’ table at a wedding.

The kids’ table. The Director of National Intelligence of the United States of America, the woman who commands the attention of seventeen intelligence agencies and holds the highest security clearances in the land, was seated at the kids’ table during the most significant military engagement in twenty years.

In the Rise and Fall series, this is usually where the fall begins. But Gabbard’s story doesn’t have a fall. A fall requires gravity, and gravity requires mass. What happened to Tulsi Gabbard was something more unsettling.

She disappeared.

IV. The Vanishing

The chair is empty now. Not pushed back, as though someone just stepped away. Not pulled out, as though someone is about to sit down. Simply vacant. The classified folders are still on the table. The screens are still glowing. The room is full of people doing the work of national security. Nobody is looking at the chair. Nobody seems to notice it’s there.

Here is what the Director of National Intelligence has been doing while America wages war with Iran.

She has been investigating election security. In Georgia. In late January — weeks before the war — Gabbard travelled to Atlanta to inspect voting sites, claiming that the possibility of foreign interference gave her jurisdiction over domestic elections. Domestic elections are, to be clear, absolutely nowhere near the remit of the DNI. When Congress asked about the trip, Gabbard said the President had directed her to go. The White House, apparently unaware of this directive, distanced itself from her field trip.

Think about what this means. The DNI was so desperate to demonstrate relevance — so eager to find something that would keep her in Trump’s good graces — that she invented a mission for herself. She went cosplaying as an election investigator, wandering around Georgia polling sites like a lost tourist with a security clearance, hoping that performing the President’s 2020 obsession would earn her a place at the grown-ups’ table.

It didn’t work. The war came, and she wasn’t invited.

Meanwhile, her own handpicked man did the thing she couldn’t. Joe Kent — former head of the National Counterterrorism Center, Gabbard’s chief of staff, her protégé — resigned to protest the war with Iran. He went public with blistering criticisms of the administration. He did exactly what the old Tulsi Gabbard would have done — the Tulsi who went to Syria, who sold the T-shirts, who called the warmongers what they were.

Kent resigned after less than eight months. The President reportedly believes that Gabbard was shielding him from the White House, which may be the only interesting thing Gabbard has done since taking office.

And now the Washington rumour mill — that perpetual motion machine of informed gossip — has Gabbard high on the list of Cabinet officials likely to be fired next, especially after Pam Bondi’s departure. When The Atlantic contacted the Office of the Director of National Intelligence to ask about Gabbard’s recent activities, ODNI responded by asking the journalist for more information about his story.

Then they stopped responding entirely.

The office of the nation’s top intelligence official could not produce intelligence about what its own director has been doing.

V. The Ghost in the Machine

In previous series, we’ve tracked recognizable trajectories. Matt Gaetz rose on provocation and fell on scandal. Pam Bondi rose on loyalty and fell on the sword of her own compromises. Kristi Noem rose on ambition and fell when the mask slipped. Marjorie Taylor Greene rose on outrage and fell when outrage stopped being enough.

Each of them, in their way, burned brightly enough to cast a shadow when they fell.

Tulsi Gabbard casts no shadow. She traded every principle she ever held for a title that turned out to be decorative. She sits atop an intelligence community that doesn’t report to her, in an office that may not need to exist, during a war she wasn’t consulted about, in an administration that has already replaced her in practice — if not yet on paper — with the CIA Director, John Ratcliffe.

She is the Director of National Intelligence in the same way that a scarecrow is a farmer. The title is accurate. The function is absent.

And here is what makes her story different from every other entry in this series — and, in its way, more damning. Gaetz was destroyed. Bondi was fired. Noem was dismissed. Greene was sidelined. Each of them, at least, was considered consequential enough to be dealt with.

Gabbard wasn’t fired. She wasn’t confronted. She wasn’t even publicly humiliated in the way that Trump usually humiliates the people he discards. She was simply... not invited. Not consulted. Not included. The administration looked at the most important decision it would ever make and decided that the Director of National Intelligence was not essential personnel.

That is not a fall. That is a disappearance.

VI. The Lesson

From up here in Toronto, where we watch American political careers the way birdwatchers track migratory patterns, Gabbard’s arc offers something the others in this series don’t: a cautionary tale about the specific price of selling out your principles for a position that was never real.

Gaetz sold out for power and got caught. Bondi sold out for influence and got fired. Gabbard sold out for a title — and got the title. That’s all she got. A parking space at Liberty Crossing, a seat at the kids’ table, and the unique distinction of being the nation’s top intelligence official during a war in which nobody asked for her intelligence.

She could have been interesting. She could have been the anti-war voice that forced uncomfortable conversations. She could have resigned, the way Joe Kent did — the way the old Tulsi Gabbard would have, the Tulsi who stormed out of the DNC and endorsed Bernie Sanders and flew to Damascus because she believed that seeing a war zone with your own eyes was worth the political cost.

Instead, she is nowhere. Not fired. Not resigned. Not protesting. Not visible. The woman who once went to a war zone to oppose war is now invisible during a war she was supposed to help direct.

Somewhere in a Goodwill bin in Honolulu, there is a T-shirt that reads “No War With Iran.”

Its original owner could not be reached for comment.
