The Pardon Loyalty Test

Volume 3: How Silence Bought Freedom and Truth Bought Prison (2017–2021)

March 4, 2026

In my nine-part Clemency Chronicles, I examined presidential pardons across four administrations — from George W. Bush’s restraint to Obama’s reform agenda to Biden’s family corruption to Trump’s systematic weaponization. That series documented the mechanics. This essay places those mechanics within the larger retribution architecture.

Because the pardon power is where the Destruction Machine becomes most nakedly transactional. In Atlantic City, the equation was money: do the work, get stiffed, absorb the loss or spend years in court. In the 2016 primary, the equation was political survival: submit or be branded into oblivion. But with the pardon power, the equation became something far more primal: keep your mouth shut and walk free, or tell the truth and rot in prison.

The story of who Trump pardoned — and who he didn’t — is the Rosetta Stone of his entire retribution system.

The Two Fixers

To understand the pardon loyalty test, start with two men who served the same purpose.

Michael Cohen was Donald Trump’s personal attorney and self-described “fixer” for over a decade. He once said he would “take a bullet” for Trump. He arranged hush money payments to women who claimed sexual relationships with Trump. He lied to Congress about a Trump real estate project in Moscow. He was, by his own subsequent admission, a willing participant in Trump’s shadow operations — the man who solved problems through whatever means necessary, morality and legality beside the point.

Roger Stone was Trump’s longest-serving political operative, a relationship stretching back to the Roy Cohn era. Not a lawyer like Cohen, but a strategist, provocateur, and self-described “dirty trickster” who served as Trump’s connection to the darker currents of American politics for decades. Stone was convicted by a jury on seven felony counts, including witness tampering, obstructing congressional proceedings, and making false statements — all related to his efforts to protect Trump during the Russia investigation. Before his trial, Stone had a tattoo of Richard Nixon on his back. After his conviction, he had something more valuable: Donald Trump’s gratitude.

Both men committed crimes connected to Trump. Both were convicted. Both faced prison.

One cooperated with investigators. One didn’t.

One was pardoned. One wasn’t.

That’s the entire pardon loyalty test, distilled to its essentials.

Michael Cohen: The Man Who Talked

Michael Cohen, Trump’s former fixer, testifies publicly before Congress February 2019, after cooperating with investigators—calling out Trump’s “rat” mentality for demanding silence, resulting in no pardon and prison as the price of truth-telling.

Cohen’s journey from Trump’s fiercest protector to his most dangerous accuser is a case study in what happens when the loyalty equation breaks.

For years, Cohen operated in Trump’s shadow. He made threats on Trump’s behalf. He arranged payments. He lied to protect Trump’s interests. When the FBI raided his office and home in April 2018, the initial expectation — certainly Trump’s expectation — was that Cohen would maintain omertà. He would stay silent, endure whatever legal consequences followed, and trust that Trump would take care of him.

Cohen made a different calculation. He cooperated with federal investigators. He sat for over fifty hours of interviews with Robert Mueller’s team. He testified before Congress, delivering a devastating public assessment: “Mr. Trump called me a ‘rat’ for choosing to tell the truth, much like a mobster would do when one of his men decides to cooperate with the government.”

The word “rat” — Trump’s own word — is telling. It comes directly from the organized crime lexicon that Roy Cohn inhabited and that Trump absorbed during his formative years. In the mob world, the worst thing you can be is a cooperator. Not a criminal, not a liar, not a cheat — a cooperator. Someone who tells the truth to the authorities.

Cohen pleaded guilty to tax evasion, campaign finance violations, and lying to Congress. He was sentenced to three years in prison. Before sentencing, he told the judge: “I am ashamed of my weakness and misplaced loyalty, of the things I did for Mr. Trump in an effort to protect and promote him.”

He was not pardoned.

He served his sentence. At one point in July 2020, Cohen was placed in solitary confinement by the Federal Bureau of Prisons for more than two weeks — something Cohen claims was direct retaliation by Trump for his cooperation. A federal judge later found that Cohen’s re-arrest during COVID-related home confinement was retaliatory, connected to his plans to publish a book critical of Trump.

Cohen told the truth and went to prison. The process was the punishment — and even after the sentence was imposed, the punishment continued.

Roger Stone: The Man Who Didn’t

Roger Stone, post pardon December 2020, who stayed silent and refused to cooperate, receives Trump’s full pardon—rewarded for loyalty with freedom, contrasting sharply with Cohen’s fate.

Roger Stone’s case followed the opposite trajectory.

Convicted on seven felony counts, Stone was sentenced to three years and four months in prison. Throughout the legal process, Stone maintained his silence about Trump. He did not cooperate with Mueller’s investigation. He did not testify against Trump. He did not write a book. He kept the code.

Before Stone was scheduled to report to prison in July 2020, Trump commuted his sentence — allowing him to avoid incarceration entirely. In December 2020, in the final weeks of his presidency, Trump issued Stone a full pardon.

The message was unambiguous. Stone himself seemed to understand the transaction. In the months leading up to his commutation, he made public statements widely interpreted as appeals for clemency — and widely interpreted as implicit reminders that he had kept quiet despite what he knew.

Andrew Weissmann, a member of Mueller’s prosecution team, captured the dynamic: “The pardons from this President are what you would expect to get if you gave the pardon power to a mob boss.”

The mob boss metaphor — the same framework I’ve explored in my Enforcers series — isn’t rhetorical excess. It’s structural description. In organized crime, the pardon equivalent is simple: keep your mouth shut and we’ll take care of you. Talk and you’re dead to us. Trump didn’t have people killed. He had something almost as powerful in the context of federal criminal prosecution: the constitutional authority to make their convictions disappear.

The Full Ledger

Cohen and Stone were the starkest contrast, but they weren’t the only entries in the pardon loyalty test.

Michael Flynn, who initially cooperated but later submitted fully to Trump’s narrative, receives redemption via pardon—showing the system’s “redemption clause” for dramatic loyalty shifts

Michael Flynn, Trump’s first National Security Adviser, pleaded guilty twice to lying to the FBI about communications with Russian officials. Flynn initially cooperated with investigators — a dangerous deviation from the loyalty code. But he subsequently attempted to withdraw his guilty plea, hired new lawyers who challenged the prosecution’s legitimacy, and aligned himself publicly with Trump. He became an avowed QAnon supporter. Trump issued Flynn a “wide-ranging” pardon in November 2020, shortly after losing the presidential election.

Flynn’s case is instructive because it shows that the loyalty test has a redemption clause. Initial cooperation can be forgiven — if the subsequent submission is sufficiently total and public. Flynn didn’t just stop cooperating; he became a leading figure in the movement to overturn the 2020 election results. The extremity of his subsequent loyalty compensated for his earlier deviation.

Paul Manafort, who defied investigators and maintained loyalty through non-cooperation, is pardoned—exemplifying silence as the path to clemency.

Paul Manafort, Trump’s former campaign chairman, was sentenced to over seven years in prison for financial crimes related to his political consulting work in Ukraine. Manafort did not cooperate meaningfully with investigators. Judges in his cases noted that he appeared to be “thumbing his nose” at the justice system — a posture that, in Trump’s framework, was a feature, not a bug. Defiance of investigators was itself an act of loyalty to Trump. Manafort was pardoned in December 2020.

Steve Bannon, granted a preemptive pardon before trial due to his loyalty—illustrating how allegiance alone can secure freedom in Trump’s transactional system.

Steve Bannon, Trump’s former White House chief strategist, was indicted for allegedly defrauding donors to a private border wall fundraising campaign. Trump pardoned Bannon before he could even be tried — a preemptive pardon that sent an extraordinary message: you don’t even need to be convicted. Loyalty alone is sufficient.

Rick Gates, Manafort’s deputy, cooperated extensively with prosecutors. He was sentenced to 45 days in prison. He was not pardoned.

George Papadopoulos, a campaign adviser who pleaded guilty to lying to the FBI but later aligned himself with Trump’s narrative that the investigation was a “witch hunt,” was pardoned.

Charles Kushner, Jared Kushner’s father, who pleaded guilty years earlier to tax evasion and illegal campaign donations, was pardoned — an act that had nothing to do with the Russia investigation and everything to do with family loyalty.

The pattern is unmistakable. Across every case connected to Trump, the pardon decision tracked a single variable: loyalty. Not the severity of the crime. Not the strength of the evidence. Not the recommendation of the Justice Department’s pardon office, which Trump largely bypassed. The only question that mattered was whether the person had maintained their loyalty to Donald Trump.

The Code, Stated Plainly

The pardon loyalty test operates on principles that Roy Cohn would have recognized instantly — because they’re the same principles that govern organized crime families.

Principle One: Silence is rewarded. Stone kept quiet. Manafort kept quiet. Both were pardoned. The message to every future Trump associate is clear: if you face legal jeopardy connected to Trump, your freedom depends on one thing — keeping your mouth shut.

Principle Two: Cooperation is punished. Cohen cooperated. Gates cooperated. Neither was pardoned. The message is equally clear: if you tell investigators the truth about Trump’s conduct, you will not only fail to receive clemency — you will be actively punished.

Principle Three: Redemption through submission. Flynn initially cooperated, then reversed course and became more loyal than ever. He was pardoned. The system allows for apostasy if the subsequent submission is sufficiently dramatic — a principle identical to Path Two from the 2016 primary, where opponents who submitted were rewarded in proportion to the extremity of their original opposition.

Principle Four: The pardon is the lesson. The point of pardoning Stone and not pardoning Cohen isn’t just about those two individuals. It’s about every future person who might consider cooperating with investigators against Trump. The pardon ledger functions as a public menu of consequences: this is what happens if you stay loyal, and this is what happens if you don’t.

Trump made this explicit when he called Cohen a “rat.” The terminology was precise. In the world Cohn introduced Trump to — the world of Gotti and Salerno and Galante — calling someone a rat wasn’t just an insult. It was a declaration that the person had violated the foundational code and would be treated accordingly.

What the Clemency Chronicles Revealed

My nine-part Clemency Chronicles series examined pardons across four presidencies. The comparison is devastating.

George W. Bush used the pardon power sparingly — 200 pardons and 11 commutations over eight years. His clemency decisions were generally processed through the Justice Department’s pardon office and followed traditional criteria: the severity of the offense, the time served, evidence of rehabilitation, community standing.

Barack Obama used the pardon power more aggressively, but with a reformist agenda — 1,927 commutations, many for nonviolent drug offenders serving sentences later recognized as disproportionate. His clemency program was the largest in modern history, driven by a philosophy that the criminal justice system itself needed correction.

Joe Biden pardoned his own son, Hunter — an act I documented as family corruption that damaged the institution of the pardon regardless of its legal legitimacy.

Trump’s pardons were none of these things. They were not processed through the pardon office. They did not follow traditional criteria. They were not motivated by reform or mercy or even family favoritism in the conventional sense. They were business transactions in which the currency was loyalty and the commodity was freedom.

When Andrew Weissmann compared Trump’s pardon behavior to a mob boss’s, he was identifying something that my Enforcers series has explored through satire but that the Clemency Chronicles documents through evidence: the pardon power, in Trump’s hands, became another weapon in the retribution arsenal. It rewarded those who maintained the code and punished those who broke it — exactly as the contractor payments in Atlantic City rewarded those who accepted losses quietly and punished those who dared to sue.

The weapons change. The Atlantic City tool was money. The 2016 primary tool was branding. The pardon tool was freedom itself. But the underlying principle is identical in every case: loyalty is the only currency Trump recognizes, and disloyalty is the only sin he never forgives.

The Second Term: Pardons Unleashed

In Trump’s second term, the pardon power has been deployed on an even larger scale — most dramatically in the pardoning of January 6th defendants, which I’ve covered in other series. But within the retribution framework, the January 6th pardons serve the same function as the Mueller-era pardons: they tell every future Trump supporter that loyalty to Trump will be rewarded, and they tell every potential whistleblower or cooperator that the system protects those who protect Trump.

The pardon has become something it was never designed to be: a loyalty enforcement mechanism. The Founders included the pardon power in the Constitution as an instrument of mercy — a safety valve against the rigidity of law. Alexander Hamilton, in Federalist No. 74, argued that “the criminal code of every country partakes so much of necessary severity, that without an easy access to exceptions in favor of unfortunate guilt, justice would wear a countenance too sanguinary and cruel.”

Hamilton imagined pardons tempering justice with mercy. Trump has made them transactional instruments of personal loyalty — the constitutional equivalent of the envelope left at the table of a mob associate who kept his mouth shut during a federal investigation.

Roy Cohn would have appreciated the efficiency. He spent his career exploiting legal mechanisms for personal advantage. His student found the ultimate legal mechanism — one written into the Constitution itself, with no judicial review and no congressional override — and turned it into the most powerful tool in the Destruction Machine’s arsenal.

What Comes Next

The pardon loyalty test establishes a principle that extends far beyond clemency: in Trump’s system, every instrument of government power is evaluated for its retribution potential.

Pardons reward loyalty. Investigations punish disloyalty. Endorsements reward submission. Primary challenges punish independence. Federal funding rewards compliant states. Funding cuts punish defiant ones. Research grants reward compliant institutions. Grant cancellations punish dissent.

In the next volume, we examine the most dramatic application of this principle within Trump’s own party: the systematic purge of Republicans who voted for impeachment or conviction, the destruction of Marjorie Taylor Greene after five years of total loyalty, and the ongoing siege of Thomas Massie for the sin of voting his conscience.

The pardon loyalty test proved that Trump would use the most sacred instruments of presidential power as retribution tools. The Republican purge proved he would use them against his own allies.
