Part 11: Classified Documents — The Evidence (2021-2024)

January 13, 2026

Jan 13, 2026

On August 8, 2022, FBI agents executed a search warrant at Mar-a-Lago, Donald Trump’s Palm Beach estate and private club. What they found shocked even seasoned national security officials: boxes upon boxes of classified documents, some marked “TOP SECRET/SCI” (Sensitive Compartmented Information)—the highest classification level—stored haphazardly throughout the property.

Documents were found in:

  • Trump’s office

  • A storage room

  • Trump’s bedroom

  • A bathroom and shower

  • A ballroom used for events and parties

This image contained in a court filing by the Department of Justice on Aug. 30, 2022, and partially redacted by the source, shows a photo of documents seized during the Aug. 8 FBI search of former President Donald Trump’s Mar-a-Lago estate.

Photos released by the Justice Department showed boxes stacked in a bathroom, classified cover sheets spread across carpeted floors, and documents containing America’s most sensitive national security secrets stored alongside newspaper clippings and personal items.

This wasn’t a paperwork mix-up. According to the 37-count federal indictment, Trump had willfully retained national defense information, showed classified materials to people without security clearances, made false statements to investigators, and orchestrated an elaborate scheme to obstruct the government’s efforts to retrieve the documents.

Legal experts called it the most straightforward, evidence-heavy case against Trump. The documentary evidence was overwhelming. The obstruction was brazen. The national security implications were staggering.

And on July 15, 2024, Judge Aileen Cannon dismissed it all.

The Road to Mar-a-Lago: A Year of Obstruction

The story begins not with the FBI search, but with Trump’s refusal to comply with basic presidential records law.

January 2021: Trump leaves the White House, taking thousands of documents with him to Mar-a-Lago. Under the Presidential Records Act, these belong to the National Archives, not Trump.

Spring 2021: The National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) realizes presidential records are missing and begins requesting their return.

January 2022: After a year of requests, Trump returns 15 boxes. Archivists discover the boxes contain 184 documents with classification markings, including 25 marked “TOP SECRET.”

May 2022: A federal grand jury issues a subpoena demanding all remaining documents with classification markings.

June 2022: Trump’s lawyer Evan Corcoran searches Mar-a-Lago and finds 38 more classified documents. He provides them to the FBI and signs a certification stating that a “diligent search” had been conducted and all responsive documents had been produced.

This was false. Hundreds of classified documents remained at Mar-a-Lago. And according to the indictment, Trump knew it.

The Obstruction: A Criminal Cover-Up Caught on Camera

The special counsel’s investigation revealed systematic obstruction documented in security footage, text messages, and witness testimony:

Trump Showed Classified Documents to Unauthorized Persons

In July 2021, Trump showed a classified “Plan of Attack” against Iran to a writer, a publisher, and two staff members—none of whom had security clearances. The indictment quotes Trump acknowledging: “As president I could have declassified it... Now I can’t, you know, but this is still a secret.”

Trump knew he couldn’t declassify documents after leaving office. He showed them anyway.

Trump Directed Subordinates to Hide Documents

According to the indictment, Trump told his aide Walt Nauta to move boxes before Corcoran’s search. Security camera footage showed Nauta removing boxes from the storage room where they were supposed to be located.

Trump Suggested Destruction of Evidence

The indictment alleges Trump suggested to his lawyer that documents should be hidden or destroyed, saying “wouldn’t it be better if we just told them we don’t have anything here?”

Trump Ordered Deletion of Security Footage

After the FBI subpoenaed Mar-a-Lago surveillance video, Trump allegedly directed property manager Carlos De Oliveira to have IT director Yuscil Taveras delete the footage. When Taveras refused, De Oliveira allegedly told him “the boss” wanted it done.

Trump Made False Statements

Trump repeatedly claimed he had returned everything, that he had automatically declassified everything, and that the documents were his personal property. All false.

The obstruction wasn’t subtle. It was brazen, systematic, and documented in security footage, text messages, and testimony from Trump’s own employees.

What Secrets Were at Risk?

The documents Trump took reportedly included:

  • Intelligence on foreign nuclear capabilities

  • Military contingency plans

  • Communications intercepts

  • Information about U.S. intelligence sources and methods

  • War plans

  • Defense assessments

These weren’t just “classified”—some were TOP SECRET/SCI, the highest level. Unauthorized disclosure could:

  • Reveal intelligence sources

  • Compromise ongoing operations

  • Damage relationships with allies

  • Put American lives at risk

Trump stored these secrets in a bathroom and ballroom at an active social club where:

  • Foreign nationals attended events

  • Members and guests wandered freely

  • Security was minimal

  • Hundreds of people had access

The national security implications were staggering.

Special Counsel Jack Smith: Building an Iron-Clad Case

In November 2022, Attorney General Merrick Garland appointed Jack Smith as special counsel to investigate Trump’s retention of classified documents and his efforts to overturn the 2020 election.

Smith’s team built an overwhelming case:

Documentary Evidence:

  • Photographs showing classified documents in bathrooms, ballrooms, storage rooms

  • Security footage of boxes being moved

  • Text messages coordinating obstruction

  • The false certification Corcoran signed

Witness Testimony:

  • Mar-a-Lago employees who moved boxes

  • Trump’s lawyers

  • National Archives officials

  • FBI agents who conducted the search

  • IT staff who refused to delete video

Trump’s Own Words:

  • The recorded conversation where Trump admits he can’t declassify documents

  • False public statements about returning everything

  • Instructions to hide or destroy evidence

Physical Evidence:

  • Over 300 classified documents recovered from Mar-a-Lago

  • Boxes stored in bathroom, ballroom, bedroom, office

  • The documents themselves—tangible, classified, clearly marked

On June 8, 2023, a federal grand jury in Miami returned a 37-count indictment charging:

  • Trump (31 counts of willful retention of national defense information, plus obstruction charges)

  • Walt Nauta (Trump’s valet, charged with obstruction)

A superseding indictment in July 2023 added Trump property manager Carlos De Oliveira as a defendant.

Legal analysts across the political spectrum called it the strongest case against Trump. Unlike the January 6th case’s complex questions about presidential power, the documents case was straightforward: Trump took classified materials, refused to return them, lied about having them, and orchestrated a cover-up.

“If the government can prove what’s alleged here, this is a very strong case,” said former federal prosecutor Renato Mariotti.

Enter Judge Aileen Cannon: Trump’s Lucky Draw

When Trump was indicted, the case was randomly assigned to U.S. District Judge Aileen Cannon in the Southern District of Florida.

This was spectacularly bad luck for the prosecution—and spectacularly good luck for Trump.

Cannon, 43, had been nominated by Trump in 2020 and confirmed by the Senate just weeks before Trump left office. She had limited trial experience and no background in national security law.

More troubling: Cannon had already intervened in the documents investigation once, in ways legal experts found deeply problematic.

In September 2022—before Trump was even indicted—Cannon granted Trump’s motion to appoint a “special master” to review the seized documents, temporarily halting the FBI’s criminal investigation. She ordered that materials with classification markings be included in the review, suggesting Trump might have some ownership interest in classified government documents.

The ruling was legally bizarre. The 11th Circuit Court of Appeals swiftly overturned it in a scathing opinion that accused Cannon of trying to “carve out an unprecedented exception in our law for former presidents” and giving Trump “special treatment.”

Now Cannon was back, presiding over Trump’s criminal trial. Prosecutors and legal observers worried immediately.

They were right to worry.

A Year of Delays: Cannon’s “Meandering Approach”

From the moment she took control of the criminal case, Cannon embarked on what CNN described as a “meandering approach”:

Indefinite Postponements: Cannon initially set trial for May 20, 2024. Then she postponed it indefinitely, citing “myriad” pending motions. The trial never happened.

Endless Hearings: Cannon held hearing after hearing on defense motions that most judges would have resolved on the papers. She allowed Trump’s lawyers to make far-fetched arguments that consumed court time without advancing the case.

Slow-Walking Decisions: Routine evidentiary matters that typically take days or weeks stretched into months under Cannon’s management.

Sympathy for Defense Theories: Cannon repeatedly entertained legally dubious defense arguments, giving them serious consideration even when they contradicted settled law.

NBC News reported: “She has repeatedly come under criticism from legal experts for her meandering approach to the case.”

The effect was clear: ensure Trump would not face trial before the 2024 election. Every delay brought Trump closer to potential electoral victory—and immunity from prosecution.

The Appointments Clause: A Radical Theory Gets a Hearing

In February 2024, Trump’s attorneys filed a motion to dismiss on the grounds that Special Counsel Jack Smith had been unconstitutionally appointed.

The argument was radical: Attorney General Garland lacked authority to appoint a special counsel; Congress needed to create the position; Smith’s office was unconstitutionally funded.

Legal experts across the ideological spectrum rejected this theory. Special counsels had been appointed under identical authority for decades:

  • Robert Mueller (Russia investigation)

  • John Durham (origins of Russia investigation)

  • David Weiss (Hunter Biden investigation)

Multiple judges—including Trump appointees—had rejected similar challenges. The legal consensus was overwhelming.

Even Trump’s lawyers didn’t make this argument in his other federal case (January 6th in D.C.). It was considered a “long-shot” with minimal prospects.

But Cannon took it seriously. She held detailed hearings, asking pointed questions about Smith’s funding and authority. She seemed genuinely interested in an argument virtually every other judge had rejected.

Then came Justice Clarence Thomas.

Justice Thomas’s Roadmap

On July 1, 2024, the Supreme Court issued its landmark immunity decision in Trump v. United States.

The decision was a massive victory for Trump in his January 6th case. But buried in a concurrence was something even more valuable: Justice Clarence Thomas, writing alone, raised questions about Jack Smith’s appointment.

Thomas suggested special counsel appointments might violate the Appointments Clause. He wrote that “if this unprecedented prosecution is to proceed, it must be conducted by someone duly authorized to do so by the American people.”

Thomas’s concurrence wasn’t precedent. It had no binding effect. It was one justice’s musings on an issue not before the Court.

But it was a roadmap for Judge Cannon—and two weeks later, she followed it.

The Pattern Continues: Technical Escapes

The classified documents case followed Trump’s lifelong pattern:

Strong Evidence of Wrongdoing: Classified documents in bathrooms, obstruction on video, Trump’s own admissions on tape

Competent Prosecution: Jack Smith built an overwhelming case with documentary proof and witness testimony

Technical/Procedural Defense: The appointments clause challenge—rejected by every other court but accepted by a Trump-appointed judge

Extensive Litigation: Months of hearings consuming time, preventing trial

Changed Circumstances: Delay until Trump wins election

Case Dismissed: Not on the merits, but on technical grounds

Claims of Vindication: Trump declares “total exoneration”

This pattern has repeated for 57 years, from the 1968 bone spurs to today. Trump doesn’t win on the facts. He wins on delay, procedure, and changing circumstances.

And in the classified documents case, Judge Cannon provided exactly the delay Trump needed.

Jack Smith’s Congressional Testimony: “Powerful Evidence” (December 17, 2024)

Former special counsel Jack Smith speaks during a deposition on 17 December 2025 at the US Capitol in Washington DC. Photograph: AP

On December 17, 2024, Special Counsel Jack Smith testified for nearly eight hours before the House Judiciary Committee in a closed-door deposition. On December 31, 2024, Congress released the full 255-page transcript along with video of his testimony—creating the most detailed public record yet of Trump’s classified documents crimes.

Under oath, Smith stated:

“Our investigation also developed powerful evidence that showed President Trump willfully retained highly classified documents after he left office in January 2021, storing them at his social club, including in a bathroom and a ballroom where events and gatherings took place. He then repeatedly tried to obstruct justice to conceal his continued retention of those documents.”

Smith emphasized that the investigation was his decision, but the crimes were Trump’s: “The decision to bring charges against President Trump was mine, but the basis for those charges rests entirely with President Trump and his actions.”

The Testimony’s Significance

Smith’s testimony was extraordinary for several reasons:

“Powerful Evidence”: Smith, known for careful and precise language, described the evidence as “powerful”—not merely sufficient, but compelling.

“Willfully Retained”: Smith confirmed Trump’s retention was willful, not inadvertent. This establishes criminal intent.

Specific Locations: Smith referenced the bathroom and ballroom storage, emphasizing the absurdity and danger of how Trump kept America’s most sensitive secrets.

“Repeatedly Tried to Obstruct Justice”: Smith explicitly confirmed obstruction—multiple attempts to conceal the documents.

The Retribution Warning

Smith’s testimony also revealed what happened after Trump’s electoral victory. Asked about Trump seeking revenge, Smith was blunt:

“I have no doubt that the President wants to seek retribution against me.”

He added that the Justice Department under Trump now “wants to seek retribution against anybody who worked on cases against President Trump.”

This wasn’t speculation. After returning to office, Trump fired Smith and his entire staff—punishing prosecutors for doing their jobs.

The Permanent Record

The 255-page transcript creates a permanent congressional record: the Special Counsel confirmed under oath that Trump committed serious federal crimes involving national security secrets and obstruction of justice. The investigation developed powerful evidence proving Trump’s guilt.

But there was no trial. No conviction. No accountability.

Because Trump won the 2024 election, ordered the case dismissed, and then fired the prosecutors who had investigated him.

Smith’s testimony validates everything documented in this essay: the evidence was overwhelming, the crimes were serious, and Trump escaped consequences not because the evidence was insufficient, but because he regained presidential power and used it to end the prosecution—then retaliated against those who had sought to hold him accountable.
