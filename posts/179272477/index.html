"Concepts" Finally Revealed—and They're a Death Spiral

"Working Around the Clock on What, Exactly" has been answered

November 18, 2025

November 18, 2025

Six days after asking Mike Johnson “ Working Around the Clock on What, Exactly?”, we finally know what Trump’s “concepts of a plan” looks like.

It’s not a plan. It’s an ultimatum delivered via social media.

On November 18th, Trump posted in all caps:

“THE ONLY HEALTHCARE I WILL SUPPORT OR APPROVE IS SENDING THE MONEY DIRECTLY BACK TO THE PEOPLE, WITH NOTHING GOING TO THE BIG, FAT, RICH INSURANCE COMPANIES... THE PEOPLE WILL BE ALLOWED TO NEGOTIATE AND BUY THEIR OWN, MUCH BETTER, INSURANCE. POWER TO THE PEOPLE! Congress, do not waste your time and energy on anything else. This is the only way to have great Healthcare in America!!! GET IT DONE, NOW.”

Read that again. After 15 years of promising a healthcare plan. After seven years of “two weeks” promises. After admitting at the 2024 debate he only had “concepts.” The best Trump can offer is a social media post telling Congress to “GET IT DONE, NOW” on a proposal that his own Treasury Secretary admits doesn’t exist as formal policy.

When asked why Trump was posting about healthcare ideas online instead of submitting actual legislation, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told ABC News bluntly: the administration “does not have a formal proposal before the Senate.”

No formal proposal. Just a Truth Social post demanding Congress figure it out.

That’s what “working around the clock” produced. A concept of a concept.

Good News, Speaker Johnson: You Can Stop Now

Remember when Mike Johnson looked into that camera on October 6th and solemnly declared Republicans were “working around the clock” on healthcare?

Good news, Mr. Speaker: You can clock out.

Your handler has spoken.

After five weeks of Johnson’s supposed around-the-clock labor—five weeks of claiming Republicans were doing the hard work of healthcare policy—Trump solved the whole thing with a Truth Social post. No legislation required. No committee hearings. No CBO scores. No actual policy details. Just caps lock and righteous indignation about “BIG, FAT, RICH INSURANCE COMPANIES.”

All that working around the clock, and it turns out Johnson just needed to wait for a tweet.

Of course, this raises an uncomfortable question: What exactly was Johnson working on? If the President’s healthcare “plan” is a social media post demanding Congress “GET IT DONE, NOW” on a policy that doesn’t exist as formal legislation, what was the Speaker doing all those nights?

Perhaps “working around the clock” meant refreshing Truth Social, waiting for instructions.

Because that’s the reality of Republican healthcare policy in 2025. The Speaker of the House claims to be laboring tirelessly on solutions, then the President posts a concept on social media with no details, no formal proposal, and demands Congress implement it immediately. Johnson’s “work” was never about developing policy. It was about stalling until Trump told him what to say.

And now Trump has spoken. The concept has been revealed. And it’s worse than nothing.

The Death Spiral

Here’s what makes Trump’s “concept” worse than no plan at all: health policy experts say it would collapse the entire individual insurance market.

Larry Levitt, executive vice president for health policy at KFF, didn’t mince words: “This would in a big way pull healthy people out of the ACA risk pool and likely lead to a premium death spiral and collapse of the ACA marketplaces.”

A death spiral. That’s the technical term for what happens when healthy people flee a risk pool, leaving only sick people who cost more to cover, driving premiums higher, pushing out more healthy people, repeat until collapse.

Professor Leighton Ku at George Washington University called Trump’s idea “a poorly fleshed out idea” that suffers from “structural deficiency” because it doesn’t consider how insurance markets actually work.

Healthcare researcher Gerard Anderson pointed out the obvious flaw: if you give people money directly, many won’t buy insurance—they’ll pay rent, buy groceries, make car payments. “The question is, would they use it to buy health insurance, or would they just keep it because they have to make a car payment?”

And when those people who skipped insurance get a cancer diagnosis? Senator Chris Murphy put it plainly: “Is he suggesting eliminating health insurance and giving people a few thousand dollars instead? And then when they get a cancer diagnosis they just go bankrupt?”

So after five weeks of Johnson’s around-the-clock labor, waiting patiently for his handler’s instructions, this is what the President delivered: a policy that would cause market collapse.

The 15-Year Pattern Confirmed

Trump’s “concept” reveals everything about Republican healthcare “work”:

It’s destruction dressed up as populism. Trump attacks “BIG, FAT, RICH INSURANCE COMPANIES” while proposing a policy that would eliminate protections for pre-existing conditions, create a race to the bottom on coverage, and ultimately leave Americans with junk insurance that doesn’t cover them when they actually get sick.

There’s still no actual plan. Ten months into his second term, after nine years of promises, Trump’s healthcare policy is a Truth Social post with no legislative text, no CBO score, no details on implementation, and his own Treasury Secretary admitting there’s no formal proposal.

It makes the crisis worse, not better. With 43 days until 24 million Americans face 114% premium increases, kff.org, Trump’s response is to demand Congress pursue a policy that would cause an insurance market “death spiral.” That’s not solving the crisis. That’s accelerating it.

It proves Johnson was always waiting for orders. The Speaker wasn’t working around the clock on healthcare solutions. He was running out the clock until Trump posted something—anything—that he could point to as a “plan.” Now he has his marching orders: implement a policy that doesn’t exist as legislation and would destroy the insurance market if it did.

“Negotiate and Buy Their Own”

Perhaps the most revealing phrase in Trump’s post: “THE PEOPLE WILL BE ALLOWED TO NEGOTIATE AND BUY THEIR OWN, MUCH BETTER, INSURANCE.”

Negotiate with whom? Insurance companies whose entire business model is denying claims?

Buy what, exactly? Before the ACA, individual insurance was often worthless—policies with lifetime caps, pre-existing condition exclusions, and fine print designed to deny coverage when you actually needed it.

This isn’t “power to the people.” It’s abandoning people to navigate a market designed to extract maximum premiums while providing minimum coverage.

But it sounds good in all caps on Truth Social, and that’s apparently all that matters.

The Question Remains

So here we are. Fifteen years of Republican healthcare “work” has produced:

  • 70+ votes to destroy the ACA

  • Zero alternative plans

  • Seven years of “two weeks” promises

  • “Concepts of a plan” at a presidential debate

  • A Speaker “working around the clock” on nothing

  • A social media ultimatum with no formal proposal

And now, with 43 days until premiums explode for 24 million Americans, the President of the United States is demanding Congress implement a policy that health experts say would cause a “death spiral” and collapse the individual insurance market.

That’s not a healthcare plan. That’s healthcare nihilism.

At least Mike Johnson can finally stop pretending to work. His handler has spoken. The concept has been revealed. And it would make everything worse.

When Trump said “don’t forget this,” he was more right than he knew. Americans shouldn’t forget that when finally pressed after 15 years, the best Republicans could offer was a tweet that would destroy the insurance market—and a Speaker who was just waiting to be told what to do.

Because if that’s what “working around the clock” produces, American families are better off with no work at all.
