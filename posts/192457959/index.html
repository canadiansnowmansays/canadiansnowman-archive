Putin: The Ultimate Whisperer

Addendum

The Asset Belongs to Everyone

March 29, 2026

The asset assumed it had one handler. The handlers assumed they were alone.

When the Mueller investigation framed the central question of the Trump presidency as “is he a Russian asset?”, it made an assumption so obvious it went unexamined: that assets have a single handler.

Intelligence tradecraft doesn’t actually work that way. And neither, it turns out, does Donald Trump.

A post on X this week — 3.1 million views and counting — captured something analysts have been slow to articulate. Iran, the account observed, has no interest in assassinating Trump, because no weapon in its arsenal, including nuclear weapons, could inflict more damage on the United States than its current president is already doing. Tehran, the post concluded, views Trump as a major asset.

The joke landed because it is also, structurally, true.

The Hierarchy of Benefit

Let us be precise about what “asset” means in this context, because the word carries legal and moral weight that deserves careful handling.

An asset, in intelligence terminology, is a person whose actions produce value for a foreign power. The relationship can be witting — cultivated, compensated, directed — or unwitting, where the subject simply behaves in ways that serve another nation’s interests without awareness or coordination. The tradecraft distinction matters enormously for culpability. The strategic outcome is identical.

With that framework established, consider the three-tier structure of who benefits from the Trump presidency.

Tier One: Active Cultivation — Russia

The Russian relationship has been examined exhaustively, and this addendum will not relitigate it. What the Mueller investigation established — and what subsequent reporting, the Helsinki summit performance, and Trump’s consistent deference on Ukraine have confirmed — is a pattern of behaviour that serves Russian strategic interests with a consistency that defies innocent explanation. Whether the mechanism is leverage, ideological alignment, financial entanglement, or some combination of all three, Russia invested in this relationship and Russia has collected returns.

Putin wanted NATO weakened. It is weaker. He wanted European unity fractured. It is fractured. He wanted American credibility as a security guarantor undermined. Consider it undermined.

Russia is the handler in this story. But handlers rarely own their assets exclusively.

Tier Two: Passive Beneficiary — Iran

The original question was: who is the handler? The better question was: how many?

Iran and the United States have been in a state of structured hostility since 1979. In that time, Iran has survived sanctions, assassinations, cyberattacks, proxy wars, and the occasional American president who genuinely frightened them.

Donald Trump frightens them less than he delights them strategically.

The JCPOA withdrawal that Trump celebrated as a triumph handed Iran exactly what it needed: the moral high ground in negotiations, the fracturing of the P5+1 coalition, and the domestic political ammunition to accelerate its nuclear programme while pointing at American bad faith. Maximum pressure, as a policy, produced maximum Iranian enrichment capacity. This is not a subtle outcome.

Meanwhile, Trump’s assault on the institutional architecture of American power — the State Department hollowed out, USAID dismantled, alliances transactional, intelligence community politically compromised — removes the instruments Iran has most feared. It is difficult to project soft power without a functioning diplomatic corps. It is difficult to build coalitions against Iran when your allies no longer trust your word. It is difficult to enforce sanctions regimes when your Treasury Department is consumed by domestic political warfare.

Iran did not cultivate this. Iran does not need to. The damage accrues automatically, like interest on a debt America is paying to its own chaos.

Within the IRGC’s strategic planning apparatus, there is almost certainly a faction making precisely this argument: that the optimal Iranian posture is patience. Let the Americans continue. Do not provoke a rally-around-the-flag moment. Do not give Washington a unifying external enemy. Simply wait, enrich, and collect the strategic dividend that American self-destruction is generating on Tehran’s behalf.

The tweet, in other words, was not satire. It was an accurate summary of a policy position.

Tier Three: Strategic Patience — China

China’s relationship to the Trump chaos is the most sophisticated of the three, because China is playing the longest game.

Beijing does not need Trump to hand it specific policy victories, though it has collected several — the withdrawal from the Trans-Pacific Partnership alone was a strategic gift of the first order, ceding American economic architecture in the Pacific to Chinese alternatives. What China needs is time. Time to build out the Belt and Road network. Time to develop semiconductor independence. Time to expand naval capacity in the South China Sea. Time to make the yuan a credible alternative reserve currency. Time to demonstrate to the Global South that the American model of governance is less stable, less reliable, and less attractive than advertised.

Every month that American institutions are visibly degraded, every week that democratic allies express open dismay at Washington’s behaviour, every day that the rule of law in America is treated as optional — all of it serves China’s patient accumulation of the conditions it needs to complete its own rise.

Xi Jinping has not cultivated Donald Trump. He has simply identified that the man is doing China’s work for it, and declined to interfere.

Three flags. One dividend. No coordination required.

The Asset Nobody Had to Recruit

The original six parts of this series documented the Russian cultivation with care. That relationship involved effort, investment, and probable coordination. It has the character of a deliberate intelligence operation.

What this addendum describes is something more unsettling: a president whose behaviour pattern is so reliably destructive to American interests that multiple adversarial powers can run him as an asset without ever having to recruit him.

Russia built the relationship. Iran and China simply subscribed to the feed.

The Mueller question — is he a Russian asset? — was always the wrong frame. The right question was: whose interests does he serve? The answer, it turns out, is not singular. The damage Donald Trump is doing to the United States is ecumenical in its distribution. Every revisionist power with a grievance against the American-led order is collecting its share.

The asset, in the end, belongs to everyone.

Which is, of course, the most expensive kind.
