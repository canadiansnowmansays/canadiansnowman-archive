Let's Talk Texas

Talarico vs. Crockett

Part 5: The Primary That Defines a Direction

March 2, 2026

State Rep. James Talarico, left, and U.S. Rep. Jasmine Crockett, Democratic primary candidates for U.S. Senate, shake hands prior to a debate at the Texas AFL-CIO COPE Convention in Georgetown on Jan. 24, 2026. Bob Daemmrich for The Texas Tribune

CanadianSnowmanSays · Let’s Talk Texas · March 2026

Texas Democrats have a choice to make, and it’s not just about candidates. It’s about theory.

The March 3 Democratic Senate primary between state representative James Talarico and U.S. representative Jasmine Crockett is a referendum on how Democrats think they can win in a state they haven’t won statewide in over thirty years. Both candidates are talented. Both are compelling on a stage. But they represent fundamentally different strategies — and the choice between them will shape not just the general election, but the direction of the Texas Democratic Party for years to come.

Two Theories of Victory

Jasmine Crockett is a national figure. The U.S. representative from Dallas has built a formidable media profile since entering Congress, becoming one of the party’s most visible communicators — sharp, confrontational, and unapologetically progressive. She’s a lawyer. She’s a fighter. And her theory of victory is straightforward: energize the Democratic base, turn out Black and Latino voters in urban centres, and ride the anti-Trump wave to a historic upset.

This is the mobilization theory. It says the votes to win Texas already exist — they’re just not showing up. If you can drive turnout in Houston’s Harris County, Dallas County, Bexar County around San Antonio, and the Rio Grande Valley, the math works. You don’t need to persuade Republicans. You need to give Democrats a reason to vote.

James Talarico is a state legislator. He’s not a national brand — or wasn’t, until the FCC and CBS made him one. His theory of victory is different: persuasion. Win the base, yes, but also reach into the moderate suburbs, the mainline Protestant communities, the soft Republican voters who are uncomfortable with Trump but haven’t been given permission to cross over. His faith argument, his “top versus bottom” framing, his deliberate avoidance of culture-war language — all of it is designed to expand the map rather than deepen the turnout in places Democrats already win.

This is the Rehmet model scaled statewide. Taylor Rehmet didn’t flip Fort Worth by turning out more Democrats. He won swing voters by an overwhelming margin. He improved among Latino voters by 26 percent through persuasion, not mobilization. Talarico is betting that what worked in one Fort Worth Senate district can work across Texas.

What the Republicans Think

Michelle Cottle’s reporting in the New York Times surfaced a telling detail: the Texas Republicans she spoke with are more concerned about facing Talarico than Crockett in the general election.

The logic isn’t complicated. Crockett is a known quantity. Republicans have a playbook for running against a progressive, nationally prominent Democrat in Texas — they’ve been doing it for years, and they keep winning. The attack lines write themselves: too liberal, too coastal in her appeal, too focused on national media and not enough on Texas voters.

Talarico is harder to attack. A Presbyterian seminarian running on Matthew 25, quoting his Baptist grandfather, talking about housing costs and health insurance? That doesn’t fit in the “coastal liberal” box. The faith argument in particular neutralizes the single most powerful weapon in the Texas Republican arsenal — the claim that Democrats are hostile to Christian values.

None of the Republicans Cottle spoke with seemed especially worried about either Democrat. Texas is still Texas. But “more worried about Talarico” is a data point worth noting, because Republican strategists in Texas haven’t been wrong about their state very often.

The Primary Problem

Here’s the tension: the candidate who might be stronger in a general election isn’t necessarily the candidate who wins a Democratic primary.

Primary electorates are smaller, more partisan, and more ideologically motivated than general election electorates. Democratic primary voters in Texas are disproportionately urban, disproportionately voters of colour, and disproportionately interested in a candidate who will fight — visibly, loudly, and without apology. Crockett’s national profile, her media savvy, and her combative style are assets in a primary in ways that Talarico’s bridge-building centrism may not be.

And Crockett has the numbers to back it up. A University of Houston poll conducted in late January showed her leading Talarico 47% to 39% among likely Democratic primary voters, with 12% undecided. Her favourability rating among those voters was 84% — extraordinary for a primary field. She has Colin Allred’s endorsement, which carries weight with Black voters across the state and keeps the Talarico quote controversy alive as a background issue. “We are living in unprecedented times,” she told Newsweek as early voting opened, “and I think an unprecedented candidate is exactly what it’s going to take to flip this seat.” She cast her ballot at a Dallas library, surrounded by family and volunteers — the kind of visual that wins primaries.

But other polls complicate the picture considerably. Texas Public Opinion Research found Crockett leading by just a single point — 38% to 37% — with a massive 21% undecided. And an Emerson College poll flipped the script entirely, putting Talarico ahead 47% to 38%. Three polls, three different stories. The only consistent finding: somewhere between 12% and 21% of Democratic primary voters haven’t made up their minds. In a race this close, that undecided bloc is the election.

Talarico’s counter-argument is grassroots scale. He claims 14,000 volunteers and record grassroots fundraising without corporate PAC money. If those numbers are real — and not every campaign’s volunteer count survives close scrutiny — they suggest an organizing capacity that could offset Crockett’s media advantage in a low-turnout primary. Special elections and primaries are won by the campaigns that knock on doors, not the ones that dominate cable news.

The Allred Shadow

There’s an uncomfortable subplot hanging over this primary, and it’s worth addressing directly.

A TikTok personality who met Talarico in person has attributed to him a remark about former congressman Colin Allred — reportedly that Talarico “signed up originally to run against a mediocre Black man, not a formidable and intelligent Black woman.” Talarico has denied making the statement. He told Colbert that he did critique Allred’s campaigning as mediocre but would never attack someone on the basis of race.

Allred has endorsed Crockett and continued to reference the alleged remark, amplifying what Newsweek called the contest’s “already heated atmosphere.” Talarico says he called Allred privately to reiterate his denial and his respect, and declined to share details of the conversation.

I’m a Canadian journalist. I wasn’t in the room. I don’t know what was said. What I can observe is this: the allegation has not been corroborated by anyone other than the original source. Talarico’s public record in the Texas House — including his fights against the Ten Commandments bill and the furries hoax — does not suggest a pattern of racial insensitivity, though public records are not private conversations. And the political dynamics of the situation are obvious: the allegation serves Crockett’s campaign whether or not it’s true, and denying it keeps it in the news cycle.

This is where primaries get ugly. Talarico’s instinct — “lower the temperature, we’re all on the same team” — is strategically sound for the general election but may not satisfy primary voters who want the question answered more forcefully. The March 3 results will tell us whether it mattered.

What I’m Watching

From the 49th parallel, without a horse in this race, here’s what I think matters:

Turnout composition. If the primary electorate looks like Texas’s urban core — heavily Black and Latino, concentrated in Houston and Dallas — that favours Crockett. If it extends into the suburbs, smaller cities, and the kind of voters Rehmet reached in Fort Worth, Talarico has a path.

The Latino vote. Both candidates need it. Talarico’s statewide tour and grassroots operation are partly designed to reach Latino communities that have been drifting away from Democrats. Crockett’s Dallas base gives her strong support among Black voters but her Latino outreach is less well documented in the material I’ve reviewed.

The tone of the final two weeks. If the primary turns nasty — if the Allred allegation becomes the dominant story, if the candidates spend more time attacking each other than making their case — the winner will emerge damaged for the general. Republicans are watching and taking notes.

Whether anyone follows the Rehmet model. The biggest lesson from Fort Worth is that Texas voters respond to candidates who feel local, who talk about local problems, and who don’t import their messaging from Washington. Which candidate sounds more like a Texan talking to Texans? Which one sounds like a national brand looking for a Texas-sized platform? Primary voters may not articulate it that way, but they feel the difference.

The Other Primary

There’s a reason this Senate seat is in play that has nothing to do with Democrats: the Republican primary is a bloodbath.

Senator John Cornyn, a four-term incumbent and conservative stalwart, is facing a serious challenge from Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton — the same Ken Paxton who survived a high-profile impeachment trial and emerged as a hero to the MAGA wing of the party. U.S. Representative Wesley Hunt, another Trump-aligned candidate, is also in the mix, offering Republicans an alternative for those wary of both the establishment incumbent and the impeachment-scarred attorney general.

This is exactly the kind of intraparty warfare that Michelle Cottle flagged in her Times column — a “nasty primary runoff” that leaves the winner wounded, broke, and vulnerable in November. Trump said he’d give “very serious thought” to endorsing in the Republican primary specifically to prevent this scenario. The fact that he felt compelled to intervene tells you how worried Republicans are about what a bloody primary does to their general election prospects.

For Democrats, the Republican primary is a gift. Whoever emerges — an establishment senator weakened by a MAGA challenger, or a MAGA champion carrying impeachment baggage — will have spent months and millions attacking fellow Republicans instead of building a general election operation. And the tone of that primary will alienate some portion of the losing side’s voters, creating exactly the kind of soft-Republican universe that Talarico’s persuasion theory is designed to reach — or that Crockett’s mobilization theory counts on staying home.

The Stakes

Let me be blunt about what’s at stake beyond Texas.

If Democrats win this Senate seat, they are within striking distance of retaking the Senate. If they retake the Senate, they have a check on executive power, judicial appointments, and the legislative agenda. If they don’t, the second half of Trump’s term proceeds without meaningful congressional oversight.

Texas isn’t a sideshow. It’s arguably the most consequential battleground in the country — more important than any single House race, because a Senate seat changes the mathematics of national power.

The primary is the first gate. And the choice Texas Democrats make on March 3 isn’t just about who they like. It’s about who they think can win in November — and whether they’ve finally learned the difference between falling in love and building something that lasts.
