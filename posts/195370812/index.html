The TACO Arrived on Schedule

Part XX: The List Does Not End Here

April 24, 2026

April 24, 2026

Federal Reserve Board Chair Jerome H. Powell, right, and President Donald Trump look over cost figures during a visit to the Fed in July. (Julia Demaree Nikhinson/AP)

Nine days ago, in Part XIX of this series, I wrote the following:

“The most likely scenario in which Donald Trump does not fire Jerome Powell is not a SCOTUS ruling, though that may come. It is not a Senate revolt, though Tillis has laid the groundwork. It is a morning when the bond market opens badly, the dollar slides, the phones start ringing in Palm Beach, and someone whose net worth Trump respects says, plainly: this is going to cost you more than Powell is worth.”

I closed with this: “The marble was always a lie. The TACO may be the ending.”

This morning, the Justice Department dropped its criminal probe of Jerome Powell.

The marble is gone. The TACO has arrived. The ending was exactly as described — with one caveat that tells you everything you need to know about the people who executed it.

What Actually Happened

Jeanine Pirro, the U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia, announced Friday that she had directed her office to close the investigation into the Federal Reserve and its chair. The decision came two days after Pirro had appeared defiant at a news conference, vowing to appeal Chief Judge Boasberg’s ruling quashing the subpoenas and accusing him of standing at “the door of a grand jury” blocking prosecutors from entering. The May 3 deadline for that appeal was approaching. There was no new evidence. The math had changed.

The political logic, stripped of ornament, was simple: Trump wanted Kevin Warsh confirmed as the next Fed Chair. Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina had publicly declared he would block that nomination until the probe was dropped. The probe was blocking the confirmation. The confirmation was the goal. The probe was dropped.

But here is the caveat, delivered in Pirro’s own announcement: she would “not hesitate to restart a criminal investigation should the facts warrant doing so.”

No exoneration. No acknowledgment of wrongdoing by the prosecution. No reckoning with what Boasberg called the government’s “zero evidence” of a crime. Just a quiet shelving, with a loaded weapon left on the table, pointed at Jerome Powell, in case he proves insufficiently cooperative with whatever comes next.

Senator Elizabeth Warren, the top Democrat on the Senate Banking Committee, read it correctly: the announcement, she said, showed the administration was not done pursuing baseless allegations against the central bank. And she named what the whole operation was actually about — clearing the path to install what she called Trump’s “sock puppet” at the Fed.

That is not a polite description. It is, however, an accurate one.

The Warsh Question

Kevin Warsh’s Congressional testimony

Kevin Warsh’s confirmation hearing before the Senate Banking Committee took place Tuesday. His path to the chairmanship became considerably clearer Friday, with the probe’s dismissal removing the primary obstacle Tillis had identified. As of this writing, Tillis had not yet formally commented on Pirro’s announcement — his next move will tell us whether he considers the conditional dismissal sufficient, or whether Pirro’s “I can restart this anytime” caveat gives him grounds to maintain his hold.

The Warsh confirmation matters well beyond the politics of the Powell feud. He has signalled an intention to significantly reduce the Federal Reserve’s $6 trillion balance sheet — a project that even sympathetic economists describe as extraordinarily complex, given a 2019 episode in which a previous attempt to reduce holdings caused short-term interest rates to spike in what one former Fed official called a “near heart attack” for markets. He has also floated closer coordination between the Fed and Treasury — a proposal that critics warn could represent the first step toward what economists call “fiscal dominance,” in which the central bank begins prioritizing the government’s financing needs over controlling inflation.

In other words: Trump may not have broken the Fed through criminal prosecution. He may instead achieve through confirmation what the marble gambit could not — a Fed chair who arrives already committed to a different theory of monetary independence.

Warren’s “sock puppet” charge, intemperate as it sounds, is the concern in plain language.

The IG Gambit

One more element of Pirro’s announcement deserves scrutiny.

In closing the probe, Pirro pointed to the Fed’s inspector general as the body now tasked with reviewing the renovation cost overruns — framing this as a responsible handoff to an appropriate watchdog.

What she did not mention: Powell had already directed the Fed’s own inspector general to review the project last summer. The IG confirmed Friday that its evaluation had been ongoing since July. A previous IG investigation conducted five years ago found no evidence of a crime.

So the criminal investigation that Pirro opened, that consumed months of prosecutorial resources, that generated grand jury subpoenas Boasberg found politically motivated, that delayed Warsh’s confirmation and fractured the Republican Senate coalition — that investigation was dropped in favour of a watchdog review that was already underway before Pirro issued her first subpoena.

The marble investigation ended where a competent inquiry would have begun.

The TACO, Defined and Confirmed

For readers new to this series, TACO is the framework I have used across nineteen previous installments to describe the Trump escalation and retreat cycle: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate.

The pattern is consistent across trade policy, foreign policy, and now — with today’s confirmation — institutional policy. The threat is issued loudly and publicly. It fails to produce the desired result, or produces consequences that outweigh the benefit. The accommodation comes quietly. The capitulation is complete but never acknowledged. The obfuscation follows: the retreat is reframed as responsible governance, the original threat forgotten, the president taking credit for the outcome his pressure supposedly produced.

Watch for the obfuscation in the coming days. The White House has already opened that account. Kush Desai, a White House spokesman, said Friday the administration remained “confident” the Senate would “swiftly confirm Kevin Warsh as the next Federal Reserve Chairman to finally restore competence and confidence in Fed decision-making.”

Restore competence and confidence. In the institution the White House spent a year trying to criminalize.

That is the obfuscation phase, arriving on schedule.

What Tillis Actually Did

It is worth pausing to note what Senator Thom Tillis accomplished here, because it tends to disappear into the larger drama.

Tillis used the one tool available to him — confirmation leverage — and used it with unusual clarity and discipline. In January, he announced he would block any Fed nominee until the probe was dropped. He held that position through Boasberg’s ruling, through Trump’s firing threats, through nine days of escalating pressure following Part XIX. He described the investigation publicly as “bogus” and called on Pirro to save herself “further embarrassment.”

The result: the Justice Department dropped a probe that three federal court rulings had found to be politically motivated and evidentially empty. A single Republican senator, using entirely legitimate parliamentary tools, accomplished what the courts had been working toward for months.

This is not dramatic. It does not involve a constitutional confrontation or a market crisis. It involves one person with leverage deciding to use it, and holding.

The List Does Not End Here

Todd Blanche, the acting attorney general, has made it clear to senior White House officials that he plans to move more efficiently than his predecessor, Pam Bondi.Credit...Chip Somodevilla/Getty Images

Twenty parts. Nineteen targets. One through-line.

But this morning’s other major story — overshadowed by the Powell announcement — is a reminder that the through-line continues.

Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, according to reporting published today, is accelerating investigations into a roster of new targets: John Brennan, the former CIA director; Cassidy Hutchinson, the former White House aide who testified against Trump on January 6th; the Democratic fundraising organization ActBlue; the Southern Poverty Law Center, already indicted this week; and a potential revival of charges against James Comey, after the judge threw out the previous case for illegal prosecution. Prosecutors are also expected to subpoena bodyguards who protected Fani Willis.

The career prosecutor leading the Brennan inquiry was removed after raising concerns about its validity. He was replaced by Joseph diGenova, a Trump ally, who was put in charge last week.

The marble investigation is closed. The list is open.

The pattern that began with Comey — a target identified, a pretext constructed, machinery deployed, institution resists, escalation follows — did not end today. It paused, in one case, because the financial and political logic of a single confirmation blocked the path.

Jerome Powell held. The courts held. Thom Tillis held. The TACO came.

But Blanche has been “unleashed,” as the president put it at a private White House event earlier this month.

The Enemies List continues.
