The Counterfactual President

Fiscal Policy

Part 6: Who Pays, Who Benefits, and the Debt Hypocrisy

April 24, 2026

MORE jobs. FAIRER taxes. BIGGER paychecks. The sign said it all — none of it true. December 2017." Source: Associated Press / Getty Images

In December 2017, Republicans passed the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act on a party-line vote. The law slashed corporate tax rates from 35% to 21%, reduced taxes on high earners, provided minimal benefits to middle-class families, and added an estimated $1.9 trillion to the national debt over a decade.

Republicans who had spent eight years screaming about deficits under Obama suddenly discovered that deficits don’t matter when cutting taxes for the wealthy and corporations.

Now, after Trump’s first year back in power, we can assess what his fiscal policy produced. He extended and expanded those tax cuts. Corporate tax rates were reduced further. Wealth concentrated more at the top. The deficit exploded again. And then, predictably, Republicans began demanding cuts to Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid to “address the deficit crisis” they had just made worse.

This is the fiscal policy pattern we watched play out over twelve months: Cut revenue through tax breaks for the wealthy, explode the deficit, then demand cuts to social programs serving everyone else to “restore fiscal responsibility.”

The counterfactual isn’t whether President Harris would have eliminated the deficit in her first year—neither party has seriously addressed long-term fiscal sustainability. The counterfactual is who paid and who benefited during that year, and whether fiscal responsibility was actual principle or political weapon.

Let’s examine what that first year actually produced versus what it could have been.

The Tax Cut Expansion: What Year One Produced

Trump promised more tax cuts for his return, and his first year delivered:

What we watched over twelve months:

  • Extended tax cuts for corporations and the wealthy made permanent

  • Additional reductions in corporate tax rates

  • Estate tax eliminations benefiting only multimillionaires

  • Capital gains tax cuts primarily benefiting the top 1%

The promised benefits never materialized during that year. Corporations used the windfall for stock buybacks, not investment or wages. Wage growth remained stagnant throughout those twelve months. The deficit exploded further.

Where the tax cut 'windfall' actually went: not to workers.

But here’s what did happen over that year: Wealth concentrated even more at the top. Corporate executives received massive bonuses. Billionaires got richer. And the deficit grew by hundreds of billions.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have reversed this approach during her first year:

  • Increased taxes on corporations and high earners to pre-2017 levels

  • Closed loopholes allowing tax avoidance

  • Implemented minimum taxes on billionaires and corporations

  • Made permanent the expanded child tax credits

  • Funded social investments through progressive taxation

But implementing this would have faced enormous challenges throughout that first year: Capital flight concerns as corporations threatened to relocate. Business opposition and lobbying spending millions. Republican obstruction in Congress. Moderate Democratic resistance from business-friendly members. Implementation complexity with sophisticated tax avoidance strategies. Enforcement difficulties as wealthy hired armies of lawyers. Harris would likely have achieved only partial revenue increases during year one, falling short of what full progressive agenda required while still facing fierce backlash.

This isn’t radical redistribution—it’s returning to rates that existed under Republican presidents like Eisenhower and Nixon.

The question isn’t whether government should tax and spend—both parties do. The question is who paid and who benefited during that year.

And we spent twelve months watching the wealthy benefit while everyone else paid.

The Deficit Hawk Fraud: What Year One Exposed

Here’s the pattern we watched play out over twelve months:

When Democrats proposed social spending during the previous administration: “How will you pay for it?” “We can’t afford it!” “Think of the deficit!”

When Republicans expanded tax cuts during Trump’s first year back: “Tax cuts pay for themselves!” “Economic growth will offset losses!” “Deficits don’t matter!”

This isn’t principle. It’s political theater. And we watched it throughout the year.

The numbers from that first year are stark:

The deficit trajectory Republicans created — then blamed on social spending they wanted to cut

Trump’s expanded tax cuts added approximately $500 billion to the deficit in year one alone. Yet Republicans who supported these revenue cuts began demanding cuts to:

  • Universal healthcare proposals

  • Climate investment

  • Education expansion

  • Infrastructure maintenance

  • Social safety net programs

The claimed justification throughout that year: fiscal responsibility. The actual reason: ideological opposition to government serving public interest.

What the counterfactual would have exposed:

Harris would have been honest about fiscal tradeoffs during her first year. Social investments cost money. They require revenue. Progressive taxation provides that revenue while addressing inequality. The deficit implications would have been discussed openly rather than being weaponized selectively.

The asymmetry we watched over twelve months was stark. Republicans blew up deficits through tax cuts for the wealthy, then demanded spending cuts affecting everyone else.

That’s the difference between actual fiscal policy and political theater disguised as fiscal responsibility.

And we spent a year watching political theater.

Spending Priorities: What Twelve Months Revealed

Federal spending reveals values. Where money went during Trump’s first year back showed what his government prioritized.

Trump’s spending priorities over those twelve months:

Defense: Massive increases beyond Pentagon requests. Political theater showing “strength.” Much spending went to contractors and weapons systems the military didn’t need.

Border wall: Billions diverted to build ineffective barrier. Performance spending serving political narrative.

Tax enforcement cuts: IRS funding slashed throughout the year, making it harder to catch wealthy tax cheats. This cost revenue—every dollar cut from IRS enforcement costs approximately $6 in uncollected taxes.

Corporate subsidies: Continued support for fossil fuel companies, agricultural corporations, defense contractors throughout the year. Welfare for the wealthy while opposing welfare for the poor.

What got cut during that year:

Everything serving broad public benefit: education, healthcare, environmental protection, scientific research, infrastructure maintenance, social services, foreign aid.

The counterfactual for Harris’s first year:

Her spending priorities would have differed:

Healthcare expansion: Universal coverage, drug affordability, reproductive healthcare access, mental health services.

Climate investment: Clean energy deployment, infrastructure resilience, research and development.

Education: Universal pre-K, community college affordability, teacher pay, research funding.

Infrastructure: Roads, bridges, water systems, broadband, electric grid modernization.

Scientific research: Basic research, pandemic preparedness, technological innovation.

Child poverty elimination: Enhanced tax credits, nutrition programs, housing assistance.

The pattern was clear over those twelve months: Trump prioritized military theater and benefits for corporations. Harris would have prioritized social investment and broad public benefit.

Both cost money. The difference is who benefits.

And we spent a year watching narrow benefits for the wealthy and corporations.

The Social Security and Medicare Threat: What Year One Produced

Republicans have targeted Social Security and Medicare for cuts for decades. Trump promised not to touch these programs.

What happened during his first year back:

His budget proposed cuts to Medicare and Social Security disability. Republicans began openly discussing throughout the year:

  • Raising retirement age (effectively a benefit cut)

  • Means-testing (converting universal programs into welfare)

  • Privatization schemes

  • Benefit cuts justified by deficit concerns they created

The justification throughout that year was always “fiscal necessity” or “saving programs from bankruptcy.” The reality: These programs are solvent with minor adjustments, and solvency could be ensured through progressive revenue increases.

But Republicans opposed asking the wealthy to pay more throughout that year. They’d rather cut benefits for everyone than increase taxes on the top 1%.

Americans who understood the threat before the budget was even released.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have strengthened Social Security and Medicare during her first year:

  • Eliminated cap on Social Security taxes

  • Increased benefits for lowest earners

  • Expanded Medicare coverage to include vision, dental, hearing

  • Negotiated drug prices to reduce costs

  • Ensured long-term solvency through progressive revenue increases

This isn’t about creating new entitlements—it’s about protecting and strengthening the most popular and successful government programs in American history.

The difference: Trump’s year one threatened these programs. Harris would have protected and strengthened them.

And we spent a year watching threats.

Infrastructure: What a Year of Neglect Produced

Trump promised “Infrastructure Week” so often it became a punchline in his first term. No comprehensive infrastructure bill ever materialized.

What happened during his first year back:

Infrastructure continued decaying throughout those twelve months while tax cuts for the wealthy took priority. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act that Biden passed was systematically undermined—funding redirected, projects cancelled, maintenance deferred.

The pattern over that year: prioritize immediate benefits for wealthy over long-term national interests. Let bridges collapse, water systems fail, roads deteriorate—as long as corporate tax rates stay low.

Infrastructure Week, every week, forever. The bridge that never gets fixed while tax cuts get made permanent

The counterfactual:

Harris would have continued and expanded infrastructure investment during her first year:

  • Electric grid modernization

  • High-speed rail development

  • Universal broadband access

  • Water system upgrades

  • Climate resilience infrastructure

  • Public transit expansion

This isn’t wasteful spending—it’s investment in economic foundation. Every dollar spent on infrastructure generates economic multiplier effects. Deferred maintenance costs more than timely investment.

The difference: Trump spent year one seeing infrastructure as cost to be minimized. Harris would have seen it as investment in national capability.

And we spent a year watching infrastructure decay.

Defense Spending: What Twelve Months Produced

Defense spending deserves particular scrutiny because it’s where fiscal responsibility allegedly disappears—but with important distinctions.

What Trump’s first year produced:

He increased defense spending beyond strategic necessity throughout those twelve months:

  • Weapons systems the Pentagon didn’t request

  • Bases the military wanted to close but Congress kept open

  • Contractors building equipment that sat unused

  • Military parades and displays serving ego

This wasn’t about national security over that year—it was about defense contractor profits and congressional district jobs.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have maintained robust defense spending during her first year but with strategic priorities:

  • Focus on actual threats (China, Russia, cyber capabilities)

  • Reduce Cold War weapons systems

  • Close unnecessary bases

  • Invest in personnel readiness and care

  • Integrate climate security into planning

This isn’t cutting defense—it’s spending defense dollars strategically rather than treating the Pentagon budget as a jobs program.

The difference: Trump spent year one using defense spending for political theater. Harris would have used it for actual defense needs.

And we spent a year watching political theater.

The Investment Framework: What Year One Revealed

Here’s the fundamental philosophical divergence we watched play out:

Republican framework demonstrated over those twelve months: Government spending is cost to be minimized. Taxes are theft. Social programs create dependency. Market solutions are always superior.

Democratic framework Harris would have demonstrated: Government spending is investment producing returns. Taxes are how we collectively provide for shared needs. Social programs prevent market failures from producing catastrophic outcomes.

Consider education spending over that year:

Republican approach we watched: Minimize spending, privatize where possible, oppose federal role, treat as expense without return.

Democratic approach Harris would have taken: Every dollar spent produces returns through higher productivity, reduced crime, better health outcomes, more innovation.

The same logic applies to healthcare, infrastructure, climate policy, scientific research—all produce returns exceeding costs, but only if you measure returns beyond immediate fiscal year.

The counterfactual asks: Which framework produced better outcomes during year one? Evidence from other developed nations suggests public investment correlates with better economic and social outcomes than American underinvestment.

And we spent a year demonstrating underinvestment.

Earlier Work: Both Parties Contributed

My summer series on national debt contributions documented that Republicans and Democrats both added to national debt—Republicans through tax cuts reducing revenue, Democrats through spending increases.

That honest accounting matters because it prevents this from becoming partisan cheerleading.

The distinction I made:

Republicans cut revenue to benefit narrow elite interests, then demand spending cuts affecting broad public benefit.

Democrats increase spending for broad public benefit, generally with revenue increases to fund it (though not always fully).

What Trump’s first year confirmed:

This pattern played out exactly as predicted. Revenue cuts for the wealthy during year one. Then demands for spending cuts affecting everyone else. The distributional implications were clear over those twelve months: benefits concentrated upward.

In the counterfactual:

Harris wouldn’t have eliminated deficits during year one—neither party seriously proposes fiscal measures achieving that. But her fiscal approach would have been:

  • Honest about costs

  • Progressive in distribution

  • Investment-oriented

  • Willing to increase revenue to fund priorities

Versus Trump’s approach we watched:

  • Dishonest about costs

  • Regressive in distribution

  • Consumption-oriented

  • Refusing revenue increases while exploding deficits

And we spent a year watching regressive distribution.

The Canadian Comparison: What Different Tradeoffs Produce

From my Canadian vantage point, I see different fiscal tradeoffs producing different outcomes.

Canada has higher taxes than the US—particularly on high earners and through value-added taxes. Those higher taxes fund:

  • Universal healthcare (eliminating medical bankruptcies)

  • Stronger social safety net (reducing extreme poverty)

  • Better infrastructure maintenance

  • More affordable higher education

Is Canada perfect? No. But the fiscal tradeoff—higher taxes funding universal programs—produces measurably better outcomes on health, poverty, social mobility, and infrastructure quality.

Americans pay less in taxes but more in total for healthcare, education, and other necessities because they’re paying privately for things Canadians fund collectively. The US spends more per capita on healthcare than any nation while covering fewer people and producing worse outcomes.

What Trump’s first year demonstrated: Low taxation leaving individuals to purchase necessities in expensive, inefficient private markets.

What Harris’s first year would have demonstrated: Progressive taxation funding social investment can produce better outcomes.

And we spent a year watching the former.

The Generational Theft Argument: What Year One Exposed

Republicans love claiming deficit spending is “generational theft”—burdening children with debt to fund current consumption.

What we watched during Trump’s first year:

This argument was applied selectively throughout those twelve months:

Applied to: Any social spending proposal.

Not applied to: Tax cuts for the wealthy or defense increases.

The honest version we didn’t hear:

Some deficit spending during that year was generational theft: tax cuts for current wealthy that future generations must pay for.

Some deficit spending would have been generational investment: infrastructure, climate mitigation, education, research producing returns for future generations.

The difference isn’t spending versus not spending—it’s what the spending produces.

In the counterfactual:

Harris would have been honest during her first year: Social investment requires deficit spending in the short term but produces long-term returns. Climate investment costs money now but prevents catastrophic costs later. Education spending increases productivity and reduces social costs.

Trump’s approach we watched: Cut taxes for wealthy (pure consumption benefiting narrow elite), then claim fiscal responsibility requires cutting social investment.

Which actually served future generations during that year?

And we spent twelve months watching generational theft disguised as fiscal responsibility.

The Austerity Trap: What We Avoided by Luck

After the 2008 financial crisis, Europe imposed austerity—spending cuts justified by deficit concerns. The result: prolonged stagnation, increased poverty, delayed recovery, social unrest.

What Trump’s approach during year one threatened:

Selective austerity: cuts to social programs while maintaining tax cuts for wealthy and defense spending increases. This is the worst possible combination—reducing support for vulnerable populations while maintaining benefits for those least affected.

Fortunately, the economy remained relatively stable during that year, so the full catastrophic potential wasn’t realized. But the framework was established for disaster during any economic crisis.

The counterfactual:

Harris would have maintained or expanded social support during year one, funded through progressive taxation. This follows Keynesian logic: government provides support when private sector can’t, preventing economic spirals.

The difference: Trump’s selective austerity framework risked increasing suffering without fiscal benefit. Harris’s social investment approach would have prevented catastrophe.

And we spent a year establishing the framework for future disaster.

The Billionaire Question: What Year One Ignored

Here’s a question neither party adequately addressed during Trump’s first year: Is extreme wealth concentration compatible with democratic governance?

When individuals have wealth exceeding GDP of small nations, when they can buy political influence at scale, when they face no meaningful resource constraints while millions struggle—is that democracy or oligarchy?

Republican answer demonstrated over that year: Wealth is earned through merit. Billionaires deserve their fortunes. Taxing wealth is theft.

Democratic answer Harris would have given: Some extreme wealth results from market failures, rent-seeking, inheritance, and regulatory capture. Progressive taxation can address inequality without eliminating incentives.

What year one demonstrated: Wealth concentration accelerated. Billionaires got richer. Inequality grew. And no meaningful policy response emerged.

Trump spent the year celebrating wealth accumulation and viewing taxes as punishment. Harris would have at least recognized inequality as challenge requiring response.

And we spent a year watching inequality celebrated.

Why Fiscal Policy Mattered Internationally This Year

Fiscal policy isn’t just domestic concern. It affects:

Global economic stability: US deficits impact global interest rates and capital flows.

Development assistance: US foreign aid depends on budget priorities. Trump cut it throughout year one.

Climate investment: US fiscal commitment affects global emissions and technological development. Trump defunded it throughout year one.

Social model demonstration: US fiscal choices inform global debates about what works.

From my Canadian perspective, American fiscal dysfunction during that year was frightening because global stability depends partially on American fiscal stability.

The counterfactual mattered internationally because billions were affected by whether America chose responsible fiscal policy or accelerating dysfunction.

And we spent a year watching accelerating dysfunction.

The Exhaustion of Watching a Year of Lies

I need to acknowledge: Writing about fiscal policy after this year is exhausting because it requires debunking lies repeated so often they’re treated as truth.

“Tax cuts pay for themselves.” We watched them not pay for themselves over twelve months.

“Social spending creates dependency.” We watched social spending cuts increase poverty and suffering over twelve months.

“We can’t afford universal healthcare.” We watched more spending per capita with worse outcomes over twelve months.

“Deficits will bankrupt future generations.” Only the deficits incurred for tax cuts benefiting current wealthy, which we watched over twelve months.

These aren’t good-faith economic disagreements. They’re propaganda protecting wealth concentration.

Billions of people saw through it during that year. They were asking: What would fiscal policy look like if it served broad prosperity rather than narrow elite benefit? What would taxation look like if it was actually progressive? What would spending look like if it invested in long-term capabilities?

That’s not radical. That’s fiscal sanity.

The counterfactual reminds us this past year didn’t have to be this way.

And we spent a year watching it be this way anyway.
