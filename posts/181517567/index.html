The Masculinity Con

The Masculinity Con

Part 5: When ‘Free Speech’ Meets Human Trafficking: The Conservative Dilemma

December 18, 2025

“Florida is not a place where you’re welcome with that type of conduct in the air.”

Ron DeSantis drew this line in March 2025, shortly after the Tate brothers arrived in the United States. It shouldn’t have been controversial. The “conduct” included a 360-page Romanian indictment alleging rape and trafficking of seven women, evidence of 30+ additional women coerced since 2014 including minors, and pending charges in Britain for trafficking and rape of three more women.

Yet DeSantis’s statement fractured the conservative movement. Some praised him for moral clarity. Others accused him of embracing cancel culture and abandoning free speech principles. The question became: At what point do criminal allegations - specifically, human trafficking and sexual violence against over 30 women - outweigh concerns about censorship and political persecution?

The Tate case exposed a fault line in modern conservatism that reveals deeper tensions about where principles end and complicity begins, about what conservatives will tolerate in pursuit of political allies, and about whether the movement has any limiting principles at all.

The Free Speech Defense: Why It Resonated

The Tates positioned themselves brilliantly. They weren’t pimps facing criminal charges - they were free speech martyrs being persecuted for telling uncomfortable truths about masculinity.

The Censorship Narrative:

Andrew Tate was kicked off Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, and YouTube in 2022 before being reinstated on Twitter/X by Elon Musk. The platforms cited violations of policies against harmful content. The Tates framed it as silencing masculine voices.

Greta thunberg / Andrew Tate

When Romanian authorities arrested them in December 2022, the timing seemed suspicious to supporters. Days earlier, Andrew had gotten into a Twitter spat with climate activist Greta Thunberg. Some supporters saw the arrest as retaliation for his social media presence, not response to a criminal investigation that had been underway for months.

This created a powerful narrative: powerful forces were so threatened by what the Tates were saying that they used criminal charges to silence them.

The Trump Parallel:

The Tates explicitly drew parallels to Trump. Both kicked off social media. Both criminally prosecuted. Both targets of what they characterized as political warfare. In September 2023, Trump shared a video of Tristan defending him: “Thank you!”

For many conservatives, Trump’s experience with what they saw as politically motivated prosecutions created a permission structure. If criminal charges could be weaponized against a former president, why not against controversial influencers?

This wasn’t unreasonable skepticism. Trump did face charges that many conservatives believe were politically motivated. The question is whether that possibility justifies dismissing allegations involving 30+ women across multiple countries.

The Masculinity Under Siege Angle:

The Tates’ core message resonated: masculinity is under attack, men are being feminized, traditional male virtues are denigrated. This tapped into real frustrations among young men about changing gender roles, economic displacement, and cultural shifts.

Tucker Carlson’s interview focused almost entirely on this shared ground. Charlie Kirk acknowledged it: “He is hitting on something that you’re not allowed to say, where there’s a lot of truth to it.” The message was valuable enough that the criminal allegations became secondary.

This is where the free speech defense gained power: if you believe the message is important and true, and you believe powerful forces want to silence it, then criminal charges look like the weapon, not the response.

The Legitimate Conservative Concerns

Conservatives weren’t wrong to have concerns about censorship and prosecutorial overreach. These are real issues:

Anti-Censorship Principles:

Social media companies shouldn’t ban people solely for viewpoints. De-platforming can be ideologically motivated. Preserving space for controversial speech matters in a free society. These are sound principles.

Skepticism of Prosecutions:

Criminal justice systems can be weaponized. Trump’s legal battles demonstrate how charges can have political dimensions. Foreign prosecutions may be even less trustworthy given different legal standards and potential corruption. “Innocent until proven guilty” is a fundamental principle.

Concerns About “Lover Boy” Prosecutions:

Romania’s expansion of trafficking prosecutions to include psychological coercion and romance fraud raises legitimate questions. How do you distinguish coercion from regret? What if alleged victims don’t see themselves as victims? Where’s the line between manipulation and criminality?

These aren’t bad-faith concerns. They’re real questions about how far trafficking laws should extend and whether convictions can be secured fairly.

The problem isn’t that conservatives raised these concerns. The problem is what they did with them.

Where The Principles Break Down

The free speech defense and prosecutorial skepticism become harder to maintain when you examine the specifics:

This Wasn’t One Allegation:

One woman’s accusation can be disputed. Two might be coincidence. But 30+ women across three countries over a decade, including minors? At some point, pattern becomes dispositive.

The evidence includes:

  • Seven women in Romanian indictment

  • 30+ additional women with evidence of coercion since 2014

  • Three women in British assault allegations (2015)

  • Three more women in new British trafficking charges (2024)

  • 15-year-old alleging sex, beating with belt, choking until unconscious, bribery

  • New allegation in Beverly Hills (2025) with text messages shared publicly

This isn’t about one questionable prosecution. It’s about systematic allegations across multiple jurisdictions.

The Tates’ Own Words:

Andrew Tate on his business model (2019): “It’s not just about picking up girls. It’s about converting them into really loving you enough to moving in with you and working for you and giving you all the money.”

Text message to woman (in indictment): “do you want me to be your slave or your wife?” / “you’ll do whatever I tell you / which is the same thing”

“PIMPS” chat - Tristan to associates about woman who won’t work: “hit her if you have to, I don’t care / don’t let her take her things”

Text to girlfriend (2025): “i really love hitting you its very good for me / and you / its relaxing dont you think?” Response: “pookie no i cry”

The “PHD” course: “pimping hoes degree.”

They’re not hiding their philosophy. They taught others to replicate it. When someone explicitly tells you relationships are about control and commerce, and prosecutors allege they built a business around that philosophy, maybe believe both the words and the charges.

The “Message” and The Methods Are Inseparable:

Tucker Carlson said the Tates, “whatever their personal behavior, had a message worth hearing.”

But can you separate their message about women from how they allegedly treated women? Their philosophy - that women “aren’t built to be completely independent creatures,” that they need male “direction” - isn’t incidental to the charges. It’s foundational to them.

If someone’s message is that women need controlling and their business allegedly involved controlling women through coercion and violence, those aren’t separate issues. The worldview and the alleged conduct are consistent, not contradictory.

The Conservative Split: Who Drew Lines and Why

When the Tates arrived in America after their release, some conservatives finally broke ranks:

Those Who Opposed:

Megyn Kelly: “This actually is toxic masculinity, people. This is the real version of it.” Kelly recognized that “defending masculinity” can’t mean defending alleged violence against women.

Ben Shapiro: “America does not need more self-proclaimed pimps.” Shapiro, who built his brand on traditional values and religious conservatism, couldn’t reconcile supporting the Tates with those principles.

Ron DeSantis: “Florida is not a place where you’re welcome with that type of conduct in the air.” DeSantis drew a bright line, and Florida’s AG announced a criminal investigation.

Josh Hawley: “I certainly don’t think that we should be using any influence in our government to try to get him out of what seemed to be extremely serious charges in Romania.” Hawley opposed diplomatic intervention specifically.

Many traditional conservatives compared the Tates to Jeffrey Epstein - another wealthy man with international connections, allegations of systematic exploitation, political allies who initially looked the other way, and eventual disgrace.

Those Who Maintained Support:

Tucker Carlson: Apologized for misstatements but doubled down that the message was “worth hearing.” Never rescinded the platform or expressed concern about the pattern of allegations.

Charlie Kirk: Called out “war on masculinity” while expressing disgust at the porn business but never fully addressed evidence of coercion and violence.

Candace Owens: Flew to Romania for sympathetic interview, dismissed concerns about past comments as judgment.

Nick Fuentes: Exception who continued public defense. In October 2024, told Tucker Carlson: “Men are going with him because he’s putting women in their place.”

Various MAGA activists continued positioning Tates as political prisoners, focusing exclusively on censorship angle.

What Explains The Split:

The divide wasn’t random. Those who opposed tended to have:

  • Stronger attachment to traditional moral frameworks

  • Less investment in the “masculinity crisis” narrative

  • More concern about movement credibility

  • Recognition that some alliances cost too much

Those who maintained support tended to:

  • Prioritize the political utility of the Tates’ young male following

  • View “war on masculinity” as existential fight justifying uncomfortable alliances

  • Be more invested in anti-censorship absolutism

  • Calculate that defending the Tates was worth the cost

The Epstein Comparison: Why Some Made It

Multiple conservatives invoked Jeffrey Epstein when discussing the Tates. The comparison is instructive:

Similarities:

  • Wealthy men with international connections and mobility

  • Allegations of systematic exploitation of women/girls

  • Evidence of coordination and planning (organized operation)

  • Multiple victims across years

  • Pattern of using wealth and influence to avoid accountability

  • Political and social connections across ideological spectrum

Differences:

  • Epstein’s operation was covert; Tates’ was brazenly public

  • Epstein’s allies largely abandoned him; Tates retained significant support

  • Epstein faced bipartisan condemnation; Tates got partisan split

  • Epstein never positioned himself as political martyr; Tates made it central

The uncomfortable question: Would Epstein have retained support if he’d positioned himself as a free speech martyr, wrapped his operation in culture war rhetoric, and delivered a useful voting demographic?

The Tates’ success suggests the answer might be yes. What distinguished them from Epstein wasn’t the nature of the allegations - it was the political packaging.

The “Message Worth Hearing” Problem

Tucker Carlson’s formulation - “whatever their personal behavior, had a message worth hearing” - deserves scrutiny.

Where’s the line? At what point do allegations become serious enough that the “message” is compromised by the “personal behavior”?

If someone’s message is about proper treatment of women, but they’re accused of trafficking women, does the message have credibility?

If someone teaches “how to convert girls into really loving you enough to moving in with you and working for you and giving you all the money,” is that a message worth amplifying?

If someone sells courses called “pimping hoes degree” while facing trafficking charges, should platforms provide them legitimacy?

The “message worth hearing” defense assumes you can firewall the message from the messenger. But when the message is explicitly about male-female relationships and power dynamics, and the allegations involve systematically exploiting women through those exact dynamics, the firewall doesn’t hold.

What This Reveals About The Movement

The conservative response to the Tates reveals deeper truths about the current state of the movement:

Audience Capture:

The Tates had millions of young male followers. Conservative influencers wanted access to that audience. Political calculations entered the moral equation. When your movement is trying to win young male voters, do you really want to alienate their influencers over criminal allegations?

The Persecution Complex:

Anyone facing left-wing criticism gets defended reflexively. The enemy of my enemy becomes my friend, regardless of what that friend is accused of. Opposition to “woke culture” overrides other considerations.

The Gender Politics:

“War on masculinity” is politically useful. It mobilizes young men, creates grievance energy, and provides justification for uncomfortable alliances. The Tates became useful soldiers in the culture war, which meant overlooking allegations involving 30+ women.

The Power Question:

Will conservatives use political power to help allies escape accountability? If so, what distinguishes them from the corruption they criticize? The Tates case provided an answer: yes, and diplomatic intervention.

Limiting Principles:

Does the movement have any? If alleged trafficking of 30+ women isn’t disqualifying, what is? If providing platforms to self-described pimps is acceptable because they oppose feminism, where’s the line?

The Precedent Problem

If conservatives defended the Tates successfully, what’s the limiting principle for future cases?

The slippery slope is real:

  • “Free speech martyr” becomes shield for any allegation

  • Political utility justifies overlooking criminal conduct

  • Pattern doesn’t matter if you frame it as persecution

  • Movement defines itself by enemies rather than principles

  • Anyone aligned with “our side” gets defended regardless of allegations

But the counter-example matters: Many conservatives drew the line. DeSantis, Hawley, Kelly, Shapiro recognized that some things matter more than political alliances. They demonstrated that conservatism can maintain standards even when politically inconvenient.

The question is whether that faction or the Carlson-Kirk-Owens faction defines the movement going forward.

Conclusion: Where Principles Meet Reality

The question isn’t whether conservatives should support free speech - of course they should. It’s whether “free speech” means platforming alleged traffickers and misrepresenting their charges to millions.

The question isn’t whether criminal charges can be political - they can be, and Trump’s experience proves it. It’s whether that possibility justifies ignoring a decade of allegations from 30+ women across multiple countries.

The question isn’t whether the Tates deserve due process - they do, and they’re getting it in Romania, Britain, and potentially America. It’s whether due process requires conservative media to sanitize their record and conservative politicians to pressure foreign governments for their release.

Ron DeSantis drew a line: Florida isn’t a place where alleged traffickers are welcome, regardless of their politics. Megyn Kelly drew a line: toxic masculinity is still toxic when wrapped in anti-woke rhetoric. Ben Shapiro drew a line: America doesn’t need self-proclaimed pimps, even if they criticize feminism. Josh Hawley drew a line: government shouldn’t intervene to free people facing serious charges abroad.

They recognized that defending masculinity can’t mean defending alleged violence against women, that free speech doesn’t require providing platforms to alleged predators, and that political alliances have limits.

The conservatives who defended the Tates chose differently. They chose audience access over accountability, political utility over moral standards, and culture war positioning over the allegations of 30+ women.

That choice will define them.

The Tate case asked conservatives a simple question: Where’s the line? At what point do allegations become too serious, patterns too clear, evidence too damning to continue supporting someone because they’re politically useful?

Some conservatives answered: right here. This is the line.

Others answered: there is no line. Or more precisely: the line is whatever harms our political enemies. If the Tates oppose feminism, deliver young male voters, and position themselves as victims of the same forces we oppose, then the allegations don’t matter.

That’s not conservatism. That’s tribalism.

And it’s exactly what will allow the next Tate-like figure to exploit the same playbook, knowing that political utility provides immunity from accountability.
