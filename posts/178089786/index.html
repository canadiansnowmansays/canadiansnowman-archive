When Democracy Fights Back

Breaking the Cycle

Part 3: Can You Save Democracy from the Mapmakers?

November 5, 2025

Cartoon, “The Gerry-Mander”, 1813

Three Decisions That Broke Democracy

If you want to understand how American democracy reached the point where both parties openly gerrymander with impunity, you need to understand three Supreme Court decisions that dismantled voting rights protections and gave politicians the green light to choose their voters.

Shelby County v. Holder (2013): Opening Pandora’s Box

The Voting Rights Act’s Section 5 required states with histories of discrimination to get federal “preclearance” before changing voting laws or redrawing districts. For 50 years, it worked, stopping thousands of discriminatory changes.

In 2013, Chief Justice John Roberts led a 5-4 conservative majority in gutting Section 5, arguing the formula was outdated and “our country has changed.” Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg’s dissent was prophetic: “Throwing out preclearance when it has worked and is continuing to work is like throwing away your umbrella in a rainstorm because you are not getting wet.”

Within 24 hours, Texas announced voter ID laws previously blocked under Section 5. Within months, Southern states began redrawing maps without federal oversight. The 2025 gerrymandering free-for-all has its roots here.

Rucho v. Common Cause (2019): The Partisan Green Light

When extreme partisan gerrymanders in North Carolina and Maryland reached the Supreme Court, Roberts again led a 5-4 majority ruling that partisan gerrymandering was a “political question” beyond federal court jurisdiction. Even if gerrymanders were extreme, even if they entrenched minority rule—federal courts couldn’t intervene.

Justice Elena Kagan’s dissent pulled no punches: “The partisan gerrymanders here debased and dishonored our democracy, turning upside-down the core American idea that all governmental power derives from the people.”

The message to politicians: Gerrymander as aggressively as you want. Federal courts won’t stop you.

Callais v. Louisiana (2025): The Potential Death Blow

After Shelby gutted Section 5 and Rucho removed federal oversight of partisan gerrymandering, only Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act remains to challenge racial vote dilution. Now the Supreme Court is considering whether to kill that too.

Louisiana drew maps that give its 32% Black population just one of six congressional districts. When civil rights groups sued under Section 2, Louisiana argued that the law only prohibits intentional discrimination, not discriminatory effects. If legislators don’t explicitly say “we’re doing this because of race,” they can draw maps that dilute minority voting power as long as they claim partisan motives.

This distinction sounds technical but is revolutionary. Proving discriminatory intent is nearly impossible. Modern data analytics make it trivial to achieve racial discrimination while claiming partisan purposes.

Oral arguments in November 2025 suggested at least five justices are sympathetic to Louisiana’s argument. A decision is expected in spring 2026.

If Callais goes badly: The Brennan Center projects elimination of 8-12 majority-minority districts across the South and Midwest, further entrenching white minority rule in states with large and growing communities of color. Combined with 2025’s aggressive gerrymandering, it would be devastating.

The Doom Loop: How Gerrymandering Kills Democracy from Within

The consequences of gerrymandering extend far beyond partisan seat counts. What’s emerging is a self-reinforcing cycle of democratic decay—a doom loop that accelerates with each election:

Safe districts breed extremism: When 80% of House seats are decided by 10+ points, politicians don’t fear general election voters—they fear primary challenges from their party’s most ideological base. A Republican in a R+20 district has zero incentive to compromise with Democrats. A Democrat in a D+25 district faces the same dynamic on the left. The result: moderation becomes political suicide.

Extremism produces gridlock: When Congress is filled with members who can’t compromise without risking their seats, basic governance becomes impossible. Budgets turn into crises. Debt ceiling votes become hostage situations. Bipartisan legislation dies not because ideas are bad, but because cooperation itself is career-ending.

Gridlock erodes trust: When Americans watch Congress fail repeatedly at basic functions—passing budgets, fixing infrastructure, addressing healthcare—they lose faith in democratic institutions. The November 2025 Brennan Center poll showing 62% of Americans believe elections are “rigged” isn’t just about gerrymandering. It’s about a system that appears fundamentally broken.

Distrust lowers turnout: When voters believe the system is rigged and their votes don’t matter, they stop participating. Turnout in safely gerrymandered districts is consistently 15-20 percentage points lower than in competitive districts. Why bother voting when the outcome is predetermined?

Lower turnout enables more aggressive gerrymandering: When fewer people pay attention and participate, mapmakers face less resistance. They can draw more extreme lines, pack and crack more aggressively, and face fewer consequences because the apathetic public isn’t watching.

The cycle accelerates. Each iteration makes the next worse. And breaking out requires intervention before cynicism becomes permanent.

Why Reform Seems Impossible (But Isn’t)

The bleak picture suggests reform is hopeless. Politicians benefit from gerrymandering, so they won’t voluntarily give it up. The Supreme Court is hostile to voting rights. Both parties are now committed to cartographic warfare.

But history says otherwise. We know what works because we’ve seen it succeed.

Solution 1: Independent Redistricting Commissions (Proven Success)

Michigan provides the proof of concept. In 2018, Michigan voters approved a constitutional amendment creating a 13-member independent commission (4 Democrats, 4 Republicans, 5 independents). Members are randomly selected, can’t be politicians or lobbyists, and must reach supermajority agreement.

The results: Michigan’s 2021 maps earned an ‘A’ from Princeton’s Gerrymandering Project. The state went from one of the worst Republican gerrymanders to genuinely competitive districts. In 2024, Michigan’s delegation split 7D-6R in a state Biden won by 2.8 points—almost perfectly proportional.

Arizona’s commission (established 2000) and Colorado’s (2018) show similar success. The Supreme Court upheld these commissions in Moore v. Harper (2023), confirming states can take redistricting from legislators

The challenge: Commissions require constitutional amendments or legislation. Turkeys don’t vote for Thanksgiving. But voters consistently support them when given direct choice through ballot initiatives—even in red states like Utah and Oklahoma where proposals are gaining momentum.

Solution 2: National Standards Through Congress

State-by-state reform isn’t enough because it creates advantage for states that gerrymander. Congress has constitutional power to set national standards.

The For the People Act includes comprehensive redistricting reform:

  • Ban mid-decade redistricting except by court order

  • Require independent commissions with transparent criteria

  • Mandate algorithmic transparency (all data/formulas public)

  • Prohibit packing and cracking that dilutes minority voting

  • Create private right of action for citizens to sue

The reality: Requires either Democrats controlling Senate/House/Presidency and eliminating filibuster, bipartisan agreement (currently impossible), or a crisis severe enough to force action.

Solution 3: Restore the Voting Rights Act

The John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act would restore Section 5 preclearance with a modern formula based on current discrimination data:

  • Recent court findings of voting discrimination

  • High numbers of VRA violations

  • Significant racial turnout disparities

  • History of voter intimidation

This would force states like Texas, Georgia, and North Carolina to justify redistricting changes before implementation, catching discriminatory maps before they’re used.

The California Paradox: Democracy Enabling Its Own Erosion

California’s Proposition 50 exposes an uncomfortable truth: Voters, through transparent democratic process, can choose to undermine democracy itself.

Californians weren’t deceived. They knew Prop 50 would eliminate competition, pack Republican voters, and predetermine outcomes. They voted for it anyway—64% approval—explicitly as retaliation against Texas.

How do you reconcile this? A democratic choice that undermines democracy?

The answer: Democracy isn’t just about majority rule. It’s also about protecting minority rights, ensuring competition, and maintaining conditions that make future democratic choices possible.

When a temporary majority entrenches permanent advantages, they’re pulling up the ladder behind them. They’re using democratic processes to make future democracy less possible.

California’s Prop 50 is simultaneously:

  • Legitimate (voters chose it with full information)

  • Understandable (responding to Republican gerrymandering)

  • Necessary (within the current broken system)

  • Corrosive (undermines democratic competition)

  • Temporary (sunsets in 2030—the crucial difference)

That sunset clause may be most important. It suggests that even as Californians voted to fight fire with fire, they recognized the fire must eventually be extinguished, not fought forever.

The Most Likely Path to Reform

Here’s a realistic assessment of how change might happen:

2026-2027: If the proposed redistricting proceeds as expected, the midterms could be a disaster for democracy—low turnout, extreme polarization, obvious predetermination. Public backlash could build when voters realize their votes truly didn’t matter in 84% of House races.

2027-2028: Ballot initiatives for independent commissions could pass in swing states (Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Nevada, Arizona), inspired by Michigan’s success. These aren’t partisan—they pass with bipartisan voter support even when opposed by legislators.

2028-2030: By the next census cycle, 15-20 states could have independent commissions. The difference in electoral quality would become stark. States with commissions would see higher turnout, more competitive races, better governance.

2030+: Pressure could build for national standards. After a particularly egregious gerrymander or Supreme Court decision, Congress might act with bipartisan support driven by constituent demands.

This isn’t quick. It isn’t easy. But it’s possible—if the 2026 backlash is severe enough and reformers seize the moment.

What You Can Actually Do

Reform requires citizen action. Here’s how:

  • Support independent redistricting commissions in your state through ballot initiatives

  • Demand For the People Act and John Lewis VRA from federal representatives

  • Vote in state legislature races—they control redistricting even more than Congress

  • Back candidates who prioritize democracy reform over partisan advantage

  • Show up to redistricting hearings—public pressure matters

  • Educate others—most people don’t understand how gerrymandering works

Most importantly: Don’t let cynicism win. When voters believe “both sides do it, so why bother,” democracy dies. The difference between inevitable decline and possible reform is whether enough citizens demand change.

Conclusion: Government of the People Must Not Perish

Abraham Lincoln spoke at Gettysburg of ensuring “government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth.”

The gerrymandering wars of 2025 test whether that government can survive its own mechanisms—whether people can prevent politicians from choosing voters instead of voters choosing politicians.

Texas Republicans and California Democrats both claim they’re defending democracy. Both are right by their own lights within a broken system. And both are wrong in what matters most.

Elbridge Gerry’s 1812 salamander has become a digital Frankenstein devouring democracy district by district. You can kill it, but only if you stop fighting each other long enough to recognize the real enemy: a system that allows politicians to choose voters instead of the other way around.

The 2026 midterms could be decided by mapmakers, not voters—if current proposals proceed. But mapmakers answer to someone: Americans.

You need to demand:

  • Independent redistricting commissions in every state

  • A restored Voting Rights Act with teeth

  • National standards that end mid-decade games

  • Transparency requirements that expose discriminatory maps

Most critically, you need to reject the false choice between partisan advantage and democratic principle. You need to tell Republicans their Texas power grab was wrong. Tell Democrats that California’s retaliation, while understandable, perpetuates the cycle. And tell both parties you’re done being pawns.

Democracy doesn’t die in darkness. It dies in daylight, with full transparency, when people make rational choices within broken systems and collectively destroy what they’re individually trying to protect.

California voters made a choice on November 4, 2025—to fight fire with fire, but with a sunset provision recognizing this can’t continue forever.

The question for America: Will you follow the path of escalation? Or will you follow Michigan and Arizona, choosing fairness even when politically costly?

The choice is yours. The time is now. The stakes couldn’t be higher.

Government of the people, by the people, for the people must not perish from the earth—even when the people, acting democratically, are tempted to let it go.
