The Charlie Kirk Debates

#12: Using Faith to Justify Exclusion - Part I: The Case

December 9, 2025

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to scriptural light. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to how you weaponized Christianity. You claimed to follow Christ while denigrating Black people, immigrants, Muslims, women, and LGBTQ+ people. You cited Leviticus on stoning while ignoring Jesus’s “love thy neighbor.” You invoked God while promoting exclusion and hate. Black pastors called this “white nationalism wrapped in talk of Jesus.” Prove me wrong.

Kirk’s Selective Christianity

Charlie Kirk presented himself as a champion of Christian values while systematically ignoring or inverting core Christian teachings. His version of Christianity affirmed traditional stances on masculinity and sexuality while rejecting biblical commands about empathy, humble speech, love for all people, and concern for “the least of these.”

The Pattern: Weaponizing Faith for Political Exclusion

As Rev. Jacqui Lewis of Middle Collegiate Church in New York observed after Kirk’s death, powerful voices have long “controlled the microphone and used it to reshape Christianity to serve power, exclusion and hate.” She called Kirk’s version “white nationalism wrapped in talk of Jesus” and concluded: “It’s not Christian. It’s just not.”

Many Black pastors in the largest African American Christian denominations linked Kirk’s use of faith to justify exclusion to Christianity’s historical weaponization to support colonialism, enslavement, and bigotry. As Rev. Lewis noted: “Christianity told itself that Black people were inferior and therefore enslaved us.”

Kirk’s Christianity: What He Emphasized

Kirk’s Christian messaging focused intensely on certain issues:

Traditional Gender Roles:

  • Women should prioritize their “fertile window” over careers

  • Taylor Swift should “submit to your husband”

  • Birth control “screws up female brains”

  • Masculinity required dominance and strength

Sexual Purity:

  • LGBTQ+ identity as “an error”

  • Transgender people cause inflation (yes, really)

  • Pride flags should be burned

  • Biblical stoning laws represent “God’s perfect law”

Christian Nationalism:

  • “No liberty without Christian population”

  • “No separation of church and state”

  • Seven Mountains Dominionism

  • America must be ruled by Christians

Law and Order:

  • Gun deaths are “worth it” for Second Amendment

  • Support for Paul Pelosi’s attacker

  • Framing political opponents as demonic

Kirk’s Christianity: What He Ignored

Kirk systematically avoided or contradicted core Christian teachings:

On Loving Enemies:

  • Jesus: “Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you” (Matthew 5:44)

  • Kirk: Called George Floyd a “scumbag,” described Democrats as demons, demonized political opponents

On Welcoming Strangers:

  • Jesus: “I was a stranger and you welcomed me” (Matthew 25:35)

  • Leviticus: “You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you” (Leviticus 19:34)

  • Kirk: Promoted “America is full,” Great Replacement conspiracy, screening immigrants based on religion

On Humility:

  • Jesus: “Blessed are the meek” (Matthew 5:5) / “The greatest among you shall be your servant” (Matthew 23:11)

  • Kirk: Performative hyper-masculinity, dominance theology, conquest rhetoric

On Caring for the Poor:

  • Jesus: “Whatever you did for the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me” (Matthew 25:40)

  • Kirk: Focused on free markets and limited government, not compassion for vulnerable

On Judging Others:

  • Jesus: “Judge not, that you be not judged” (Matthew 7:1)

  • Kirk: Declared Black women lack “brain processing power,” questioned Black pilot qualifications

On Empathy:

  • Jesus wept, felt compassion, identified with human suffering

  • Kirk: Called empathy “made-up” and “damaging”

The Ms. Rachel Incident: Selective Scripture Par Excellence

On June 8, 2024, Kirk demonstrated his selective Scripture use perfectly. YouTuber Ms. Rachel posted celebrating Pride Month by quoting the Bible verse “love thy neighbor.”

Kirk responded:

“You might want to crack open that Bible of yours, in a lesser reference—part of the same part of scripture is in Leviticus 18, is that thou shall lay with another man shall be stoned to death. Just saying. So, Ms. Rachel, you quote Leviticus 19, love your neighbor as yourself. The chapter before affirms God’s perfect law when it comes to sexual matters.”

Kirk’s response revealed his methodology:

  1. He cherry-picked Old Testament law while ignoring Jesus’s fulfillment and transformation of that law

  2. He cited punishment verses while dismissing love commands

  3. He called stoning “God’s perfect law” despite Jesus stopping a stoning (John 8:1-11)

  4. He ignored context - Leviticus 19 connects loving your neighbor to treating strangers as natives

The same Leviticus 19 Kirk invoked for “love thy neighbor” also commands: “When a stranger sojourns with you in your land, you shall not do him wrong. You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself” (Leviticus 19:33-34).

Kirk quoted one verse while systematically violating the others.

Kirk on Race: Christianity Weaponized

Kirk’s statements on race consistently contradicted Christian teaching while claiming Christian authority:

On George Floyd: Kirk called Floyd a “scumbag”—dehumanizing language that ignores Christianity’s teaching that every person bears God’s image (Genesis 1:27).

On Black Women: He claimed Black women leaders “do not have the brain processing power to otherwise be taken really seriously”—directly contradicting biblical teaching that “there is neither... male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus” (Galatians 3:28).

On “Prowling Blacks”: In November 2023, Kirk said: “Prowling Blacks go around for fun to go target white people”—racist fearmongering that contradicts Christ’s command to love all neighbors.

On Black Pilots: “If I see a Black pilot, I hope he’s qualified”—presuming incompetence based on race, violating Christian teaching about judging by character not appearance.

Kirk on Immigration: Ignoring Biblical Commands

Despite Scripture’s repeated commands to welcome strangers, Kirk promoted:

“America is Full”: He advocated refusing immigrants, particularly from India, despite biblical commands to welcome foreigners.

Great Replacement Theory: Kirk promoted conspiracy theories that “liberals are bringing non-white people in as immigrants to replace white people”—fear-based politics contradicting biblical hospitality.

Religious Screening: He supported Trump’s promise to screen immigrants based on faith and reject those who “don’t like our religion”—directly contradicting religious freedom principles and Christian universalism.

Biblical Commands He Ignored:

  • “You shall not oppress a sojourner” (Exodus 23:9)

  • “The Lord watches over the sojourners” (Psalm 146:9)

  • “Do not neglect to show hospitality to strangers” (Hebrews 13:2)

Kirk on LGBTQ+ People: Law Over Love

Kirk’s approach to LGBTQ+ issues exemplified his selective Christianity:

Emphasizing Punishment:

  • Cited Leviticus stoning laws as “God’s perfect law”

  • Called being gay “an error”

  • Compared LGBTQ+ pride to “encouraging drug addicts”

  • Advocated “Nuremberg trials” for doctors providing gender-affirming care

Ignoring Jesus’s Example:

  • Jesus dined with outcasts and “sinners” (Luke 15:1-2)

  • Jesus stopped a stoning despite the law commanding it (John 8:1-11)

  • Jesus said “Love your neighbor” was second only to loving God (Mark 12:31)

Dehumanizing Language:

  • Called LGBTQ+ community “alphabet mafia”

  • Described transgender issues as “a throbbing middle finger to God”

  • Framed acceptance as part of “LGBTQ agenda” harming children

The “Christian-esque” Problem

Baptist News Global described the core issue: “Charlie Kirk built his platform presenting a version of Christianity that was less about following Christ and more about enforcing a partisan agenda.”

Kirk’s Christianity was what scholar Jay Michaelson called “selective Christianity, affirming some traditional Christian teachings (on masculinity, sexuality, and so on) while rejecting others, such as the importance of empathy, humble speech, love for all people, and concern for the ‘least of these.’ In contemporary Christian Nationalism, these virtues became vices.”

The Inversion

Kirk performed an extraordinary theological inversion:

Biblical Christianity says:

  • Love enemies

  • Welcome strangers

  • Practice humility

  • Show empathy

  • Care for the vulnerable

  • Judge not

Kirk’s Christianity said:

  • Demonize opponents

  • Close borders

  • Dominate culture

  • Reject empathy

  • Prioritize power

  • Judge ruthlessly

He kept Christianity’s label while inverting its content.

The “How You Lived” Question

Rev. Howard-John Wesley of Alexandria, Virginia, preached after Kirk’s death: “How you die does not redeem how you lived.”

This challenged the narrative that Kirk’s assassination made him a martyr for Christianity. Wesley and other Black pastors argued that Kirk’s rhetoric—targeting immigrants, LGBTQ+ people, Muslims, women, and Black people—ran counter to Jesus Christ’s teachings and the Gospel.

Rev. Joel Bowman of Temple of Faith Baptist Church in Louisville observed: “The conflation of Christian symbolism and right-wing conservativism has really been a hallmark of the brand of Christian nationalism we have seen in the last eight, nine, 10 years.”

Kirk’s memorial service exemplified this—part memorial, part political rally, with Christianity used to sanctify partisan politics rather than transform it.

The Case Complete

This is the pattern: Kirk claimed Christian authority while systematically contradicting Christian teaching. He cited Scripture selectively—emphasizing punishment verses while ignoring love commands, invoking Old Testament law while disregarding Jesus’s fulfillment of that law, promoting exclusion while ignoring explicit biblical commands to welcome strangers. He built a version of Christianity that served his political agenda rather than transforming it. Black pastors, mainstream theologians, and Christian ethicists recognized this immediately as something other than authentic Christianity.

Now let’s test Kirk’s “prove me wrong” challenge against the faith he claimed to represent—using Jesus’s actual words, biblical commands Kirk ignored, and the standards of mainstream Christianity itself.
