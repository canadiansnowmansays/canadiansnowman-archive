When Loyalty Trumps Competence in the Justice System (Part 2)

How Fabricated Grand Jury Indictments Are Tomorrow's Career Credentials: The Lindsey Halligan Story

December 10, 2025

December 10, 2025

BREAKING: She’s Not Leaving

As this essay was being written, federal Judge Leonie M. Brinkema became the third jurist to challenge Lindsey Halligan’s continued claim to be U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia—despite a November ruling declaring her appointment unlawful.

During a hearing Tuesday, Judge Brinkema pressed federal prosecutors to explain why Halligan’s name still appears on court filings. The judge specifically noted that Alina Habba—whose unlawful appointment we examined in Part 1—had “properly” resigned on Monday.

“That’s the proper position, in my view,” Judge Brinkema said of Habba’s resignation.

But Halligan isn’t leaving. According to The New York Times, Justice Department officials consulted the Office of Legal Counsel, which advised that Halligan could remain in position because the court’s ruling “did not require a specific measure to be taken, like her removal.”

This is the advisory role workaround in real-time: when formal authority is blocked, find a technicality. When technicalities run out, shift to advisory positions. The pattern adapts, but never stops.

Halligan’s refusal to resign makes her case even more important than Habba’s. Where Habba accepted the unlawful ruling and stepped down, Halligan is exploiting a loophole to continue wielding prosecutorial power she was never lawfully entitled to exercise.

In Part 1 of this series, we examined Alina Habba’s trajectory from losing an $83 million case for Donald Trump to being appointed New Jersey’s U.S. Attorney—only to have that appointment ruled unlawful. We explored how loyalty trumped competence as the selection criteria, and how Habba might circumvent accountability by serving as an advisor to Attorney General Pam Bondi.

The Habba case revealed the pattern. The Lindsey Halligan case reveals what happens when that pattern is fully implemented—and it’s far worse than the Habba precedent suggested.

Where Habba’s unlawful appointment was caught before she could bring cases, Halligan actually weaponized her position to indict Trump’s perceived enemies. And the manner in which she secured those indictments—admitting in court that she submitted grand jury indictments the grand jury never voted on—represents prosecutorial misconduct of a breathtaking degree.

This is the story of how an insurance lawyer with zero prosecutorial experience became a federal prosecutor in 48 hours, secured indictments against a former FBI Director and a state Attorney General, and had everything collapse when federal judges discovered she’d been serving unlawfully and engaging in extraordinary misconduct.

It’s the blind leading the blind, weaponized.

The Halligan Background: From Insurance Claims to Trump’s Legal Team

Lindsey Halligan graduated from the University of Miami School of Law in 2013 and spent nine years as an insurance defense attorney at a Fort Lauderdale firm, handling property damage claims. She made partner in 2018. She was competent in her narrow specialty.

The problem? Insurance defense work bears no resemblance to federal criminal prosecution. Being good at fighting property damage claims doesn’t qualify you to indict former FBI Directors.

Meeting Trump: The Career Pivot

In November 2021, Halligan met Trump at his West Palm Beach golf club. Several months later, he added her to his legal team for the classified documents case.

Her federal court debut was rocky. Even Judge Aileen Cannon—a Trump appointee—rejected Halligan’s filings twice for failing to comply with basic court rules, directing her to the court’s website for instructions.

Court records show Halligan appeared in exactly three federal cases total—all as Trump’s personal attorney, all civil cases. One was the CNN defamation suit (dismissed). Zero criminal cases. Zero prosecutorial experience.

But she had something more valuable: absolute loyalty to Trump.

The White House Years: From Lawyer to Museum Curator

When Trump returned to the White House in January 2025, Halligan joined as senior associate staff secretary. Her most visible work? Leading Trump’s review of Smithsonian museums to “remove divisive narratives.”

She toured museums questioning why there was “so much emphasis” on slavery. She told Fox News there should be “more of an overemphasis on how far we’ve come since slavery.” She sought removal of art depicting transgender and Black subjects.

This was Halligan’s preparation for becoming a federal prosecutor: deciding which museum exhibits were insufficiently patriotic.

The Setup: When Career Prosecutors Won’t Prosecute

Trump wanted prosecutions of former FBI Director James Comey and New York Attorney General Letitia James. Erik Siebert, the interim U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, refused because the evidence was insufficient.

On September 19, 2025, Trump posted on Truth Social to Attorney General Pam Bondi:

“We can’t delay any longer, it’s killing our reputation and credibility. They impeached me twice, and indicted me (5 times!) OVER NOTHING. JUSTICE MUST BE SERVED, NOW!!! ... Lindsey Halligan is a really good lawyer, and likes you, a lot.”

Within hours, Siebert resigned under pressure. Two days later, Halligan—currently reviewing museum exhibits—was sworn in as interim U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia. She had zero prosecutorial experience and 48 hours to learn, because the statute of limitations on Comey was about to expire.

The Crash Course: Learning to Prosecute in 48 Hours

The statute of limitations on the alleged Comey perjury expired September 30, 2025. Halligan had eight days from her swearing-in to secure an indictment.

According to multiple sources, DOJ headquarters declined to provide attorneys to assist Halligan. Career prosecutors in her own office declined to accompany her. The FBI agents preparing the case were denied even a paralegal.

Halligan was on her own.

Over two days, she underwent a “crash course” with FBI attorneys and agents. On September 25, 2025, she entered a grand jury room for the first time in her life—alone, operating the exhibits herself.

She presented three counts. The grand jury rejected one but approved two counts with 14 of 23 votes.

Two days later, using the same process, she secured an indictment against Letitia James.

There was just one problem—actually, several problems.

The Misconduct: An Indictment the Grand Jury Never Saw

What came out in subsequent court proceedings was stunning: Halligan submitted to the court an indictment that the full grand jury had never actually seen or voted on.

This isn’t just one error. It’s a cascade of incompetence so severe that judges used words like “disturbing,” “profound,” and “fundamental” to describe it.

The Specifics Matter:

Federal indictments work this way, per the Fifth Amendment: “No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a grand jury.” The grand jury must see and vote on the actual charges.

Halligan’s process was:

  1. Present three-count indictment to grand jury

  2. Grand jury rejects one count, approves two

  3. Seven minutes pass

  4. Halligan appears in magistrate’s courtroom with a revised two-count indictment

  5. Only the grand jury foreperson signs it—the full grand jury never saw this version

U.S. Magistrate Judge William Fitzpatrick did the math: “The short time span between the moment the prosecutor learned that the grand jury rejected one count in the original indictment and the time the prosecutor appeared in court to return the second indictment could not have been sufficient to draft the second indictment, sign the second indictment, present it to the grand jury, provide legal instructions to the grand jury, and give them an opportunity to deliberate and render a decision on the new indictment.”

In other words: It’s physically impossible for what Halligan claimed to have happened.

But It Gets Worse:

Judge Fitzpatrick identified multiple “fundamental misstatements of law” Halligan made to the grand jury:

  • She told jurors they didn’t need to rely on the evidence before them because the government had “better” evidence for trial. This is an “egregious violation” of basic grand jury rules—the jury must decide based on what they see, not promises of better evidence later.

  • She wrongly suggested Comey had no right to remain silent. Every first-year law student knows about Fifth Amendment rights. Every person who’s watched “Law & Order” knows this. Halligan—a practicing attorney—apparently didn’t.

  • She used evidence tainted by attorney-client privilege. The FBI agent who testified had access to privileged communications between Comey and his lawyer. Using privileged material before a grand jury is a Fourth Amendment violation.

  • That FBI agent was the only witness she called. The entire case rested on one compromised witness.

  • She presented the case alone. No other prosecutors joined her. According to sources, career prosecutors in her office refused to participate.

Judge Fitzpatrick’s assessment was devastating: he found a “disturbing pattern of profound investigative missteps” and questioned whether the grand jury transcripts were even complete.

When Halligan appeared before U.S. District Judge Michael Nachmanoff to explain all this, the questioning was brutal:

Judge: “The second indictment was never shown to the entire grand jury?”
Prosecutor: “That is correct.”
Comey’s attorney: “The Justice Department has just acknowledged the indictment was never presented to the grand jury and never returned.”

Halligan’s admission sent shockwaves through the courtroom.

The Collapse: Two Federal Judges Dismantle the Scheme

On November 25, 2024, U.S. District Judge Cameron McGowan Currie issued dual opinions that brought the entire scheme crashing down.

The Unlawful Appointment Ruling

Judge Currie’s first ruling addressed Halligan’s appointment itself. The legal issue was straightforward but consequential: the statute governing interim U.S. Attorney appointments, 28 U.S.C. § 546, permits the Attorney General to appoint an interim U.S. Attorney for 120 days. After that period expires, only a district court can make the appointment.

Why this limitation? To prevent exactly what Trump and Bondi attempted: installing political loyalists in prosecutorial positions without oversight.

Here’s how the statute was supposed to work in Virginia:

  1. Bondi appointed Siebert as interim U.S. Attorney for the initial 120 days (proper)

  2. When that 120-day period expired, the district court reappointed Siebert (proper)

  3. By the time Trump forced Siebert out, the AG’s appointment authority had expired

  4. Only the district court could make further appointments

When Bondi swore in Halligan on September 22, she had no legal authority to do so. The 120-day window had long since closed. Halligan’s appointment was unlawful from day one.

Judge Currie’s analysis was thorough and damning. She examined:

  • The plain text of the statute

  • Legislative history showing Congress specifically intended to limit AG appointment power

  • The 2006-2007 amendments where Congress briefly allowed what Trump attempted, then immediately reversed course

  • A 1986 memo from Justice Samuel Alito (then in Reagan’s DOJ) taking the same view

The conclusion was inescapable: Halligan had no legal authority to serve as U.S. Attorney. Therefore, she had no authority to bring the Comey and James indictments.

Both cases were dismissed.

The Grand Jury Misconduct Exposed

But Judge Currie went further. She didn’t just invalidate Halligan’s appointment—she exposed the misconduct in how the indictments were secured.

Even if Halligan had been properly appointed, the indictments were defective because they hadn’t been properly approved by the grand jury. The Comey indictment submitted to the court wasn’t the same document the grand jury voted on. This violated the Fifth Amendment’s core requirement that indictments must be approved by an actual grand jury vote.

A separate magistrate judge had already noted the problem. Now Judge Currie made it explicit: these indictments were fundamentally invalid, quite apart from Halligan’s lack of authority to bring them.

The 63-Day Tenure: A Complete Failure

Let’s take stock of what Lindsey Halligan accomplished in her 63 days as U.S. Attorney:

What She Did:

  • Secured two high-profile indictments (Comey and James)

  • Became the first person in modern times to admit submitting grand jury indictments the grand jury never voted on

  • Set precedent on the 120-day limitation for interim appointments

  • Demonstrated Trump’s willingness to weaponize DOJ regardless of legal constraints

What Happened:

  • Both indictments thrown out as unlawful

  • Her appointment declared unlawful by a federal judge

  • Exposed extraordinary prosecutorial misconduct

  • Damaged the credibility of the Eastern District of Virginia U.S. Attorney’s Office

  • Made herself liable for potential professional discipline (disbarment)

The Result:

  • Comey and James face no valid charges

  • The cases cannot easily be re-brought (even with a valid prosecutor)

  • Trump’s weaponization attempt failed

  • But the damage to DOJ norms and public trust succeeded

From Trump’s perspective, Halligan accomplished exactly what she was hired to do: she brought the charges he wanted, regardless of evidence, procedures, or legal constraints. That the charges were ultimately dismissed is less important than having demonstrated his power to force the Justice Department to indict his enemies.

The message was sent: cross Trump, and federal prosecution will come for you—even if those prosecutions are ultimately illegitimate.

The Pattern Confirmed: Incompetence as Strategy

As detailed in Part 1, the Habba-Halligan cases reveal a deliberate selection process: career prosecutors have professional standards that constrain what they’ll do. Unqualified loyalists don’t know what prosecutors aren’t supposed to do—and don’t care. Their ignorance makes them compliant.

Consider what actually qualified these women for positions wielding federal prosecutorial power:

Habba’s resume highlights:

  • Didn’t know how to authenticate evidence

  • Required “Evidence 101” instruction from judge

  • Threatened with jail for talking out of turn

  • Lost $83.3 million for her client

  • Remained loyal anyway

Halligan’s resume highlights:

  • Judge Cannon rejected her filings twice for not knowing basic federal court procedures

  • Zero prosecutorial experience

  • Zero criminal law experience

  • Demonstrated loyalty through classified documents case

In any rational system, these would be disqualifications. In Trump’s Justice Department, they’re the qualifications.

Halligan’s lack of experience wasn’t a bug. It was the feature that made her willing to do what Siebert wouldn’t: indict Trump’s enemies regardless of evidence, submit grand jury indictments the grand jury never voted on, tell jurors they didn’t need to rely on the evidence, violate attorney-client privilege, and commit what judges called “fundamental” errors and “profound investigative missteps.”

Someone with actual prosecutorial experience would have known not to do these things. That’s precisely why Trump didn’t want someone with prosecutorial experience.

When Courts Must Save Democracy

Both appointments were stopped by federal judges, not by internal DOJ safeguards or Senate oversight. When you systematically replace professionals with loyalists, internal safeguards disappear. The institution becomes hollow—retaining the appearance of legitimacy while serving political purposes.

Judge Currie’s ruling laid out how the statutory framework was specifically designed to prevent exactly what Trump attempted. The 120-day limitation exists to prevent presidents from installing loyalists without oversight. Congress debated this explicitly in 2007, closing a loophole that could be abused.

Trump found that loophole, tried to exploit it, and judges had to stop it. But that’s fragile protection. What happens when enough Trump-appointed judges prioritize loyalty over law?

And here’s the truly insidious adaptation: when courts block formal appointments, the same people simply move into advisory roles that face no statutory constraints, no judicial review, no Senate confirmation. The incompetence and loyalty remain—only the organizational chart changes.

The Advisory Role Workaround

The lesson learned isn’t “don’t appoint unqualified loyalists.” It’s “appoint them as advisors instead.”

Both women proved they would prioritize Trump’s desires over legal constraints. When direct appointments face statutory limits and judicial review, advisory roles provide the perfect workaround: same influence, zero accountability.

Bondi advised by Habba can pursue the same cases. Any U.S. Attorney advised by Halligan benefits from her willingness to abuse procedures without bearing formal responsibility. It’s completely legal—presidents can have advisors, and courts can’t stop informal influence.

The Habba-Halligan “failures” were actually market research, revealing that unqualified loyalists exist and face no real consequences. The next phase is moving them where legal obstacles don’t apply.

Institutional Corruption with a Workaround

Traditional corruption involves bad people in functional institutions. This is different: systematic replacement of functional structures with loyalty-based patronage.

The Justice Department retains its formal structure and authority, but operates as a tool of personal vendetta. This is harder to fix than simple corruption—you can prosecute a corrupt official, but not an entire corrupted system. And when courts block unlawful appointments, the system adapts by moving loyalists to advisory roles.

It’s self-perpetuating: loyalists select more loyalists. If Halligan had lasted, she’d have hired assistants selected for loyalty, not competence. They’d make future hiring decisions. The rot spreads—through authority when possible, through influence when necessary.

The Accountability Gap

Habba resigned on Monday after her unlawful appointment ruling. But Halligan? She’s still there.

Despite a federal court ruling declaring her appointment unlawful, despite three judges now challenging her continued claim to the position, despite admitting to submitting grand jury indictments the grand jury never voted on—Halligan’s name still appears on court filings as U.S. Attorney.

The Justice Department’s Office of Legal Counsel found a loophole: since the court didn’t explicitly order her removal, she can stay. It’s technically correct—and completely subverts the ruling’s intent.

What actual consequences has either woman faced?

Habba: No investigation, no bar discipline, resigned under pressure, free to resurface as Bondi’s advisor.

Halligan: Admitted to submitting fake grand jury indictments—misconduct warranting disbarment or criminal charges—yet remains in position via loophole. As of this writing: no investigations, no charges, still signing federal court documents.

This is worse than no accountability. It’s a demonstration that accountability is optional. When you abuse power for Trump, even court rulings can be gamed through technicalities. And when technicalities expire, advisory roles await.

This is how institutions die: not with a bang, but with a loophole. Each unpunished abuse makes the next more likely. Each blocked formal position expands informal advisory influence.

Conclusion: When Loopholes Replace Resignations

The Habba and Halligan cases reveal a systematic attempt to transform the Justice Department from law enforcement institution into political weapon. Courts intervened and ruled both appointments unlawful—but the attempt itself is the scandal.

And now we’re learning that even court rulings can be gamed. Habba resigned. Halligan found a loophole and stayed.

The damage remains regardless: Comey and James defended against illegitimate indictments, U.S. Attorney offices lost credibility, career prosecutors saw professional standards overridden, the grand jury system was abused. And now we know that federal judges saying “this is unlawful” isn’t enough—because the Justice Department can simply declare that since the judge didn’t explicitly order removal, the unlawfully appointed official can remain.

Most importantly, a precedent was set: if you’re loyal enough to Trump, incompetence becomes qualification. Lose his cases, get promoted. Submit fake indictments, and when caught, find a loophole. When loopholes expire, shift to advisory roles.

This is the blind leading the blind, weaponized and systematized—with workarounds built in.

The question isn’t whether Trump will try again. It’s whether courts will catch it in time, whether judges will enforce law over loyalty, and whether court rulings will actually matter when the Justice Department can simply ignore their intent through technicalities.

We’re depending on federal judges to save us from a President willing to corrupt the Justice Department for personal vendettas. And we’re learning that even when judges rule against corruption, the corrupted institution can game the system to stay corrupted.

That’s not a system of government. That’s a system of loopholes.

And loopholes are not a democracy.
