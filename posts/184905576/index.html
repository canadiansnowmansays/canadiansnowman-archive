The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 4: Woman, Life, Freedom

January 30, 2026

The 2022-2023 Uprising That Challenged the Regime’s Identity

Woman, Life, Freedom: Global solidarity — A protester holds images of Mahsa (Jina) Amini at an international demonstration following her death in Iranian morality police custody, September 2022. The bloodied mask symbolizes the regime's violent response to women who dared to remove their hijabs and demand freedom.

On September 13, 2022, Mahsa (Zhina) Amini, a 22-year-old Kurdish woman visiting Tehran with her family, was arrested by Iran’s morality police for allegedly wearing her hijab “improperly.” Three days later, she was dead.

The government claimed natural causes—a heart attack. Her family and eyewitnesses said she’d been beaten severely in the police van and at the detention center. Medical evidence supported the latter: a brain injury consistent with blunt force trauma.

Within hours of her death becoming public, protests erupted at her funeral in Saqqez, her hometown in Kurdistan province. Within days, demonstrations spread nationwide. Within weeks, Iran faced its most sustained and significant uprising since 1979—not demanding reform, but revolutionary change.

The movement’s slogan—”Woman, Life, Freedom” (Zan, Zendegi, Azadi)—challenged not just policies but the Islamic Republic’s fundamental identity. For 43 years, mandatory hijab had been enforced as proof of the revolution’s Islamic character. Women’s bodies became battleground for ideology.

When women removed their hijabs in public, burned them in streets, cut their hair in defiance, they weren’t just protesting a law. They were rejecting the entire system’s claim to represent Islam, to protect morality, to serve justice.

Ayatollah Khamenei’s response would be characteristically brutal: immediate violence, rushed executions, systematic torture—all justified as defending Islam itself.

By the Guardian’s own standards—protecting the Iranian people, upholding Islamic values of mercy and compassion, serving justice—the “Woman, Life, Freedom” movement and his response exposed the hollowness of every claim he’d made for four decades.

I. THE DEATH THAT BROKE THE SILENCE

Mahsa Amini wasn’t the first woman killed by morality police. She wasn’t the hundredth. For decades, Iran’s Gasht-e Ershad (Guidance Patrol) had harassed, arrested, beaten women over hijab “violations.”

But something about her death—perhaps the medical evidence contradicting government lies, perhaps the video of her family’s grief, perhaps simply accumulated rage—ignited something different.

Her funeral in Saqqez on September 17 drew thousands. Women removed their hijabs. Mourners chanted: “Death to the dictator!” Security forces attacked the funeral procession.

Videos spread on social media despite government efforts at control. Within 48 hours, protests erupted in Tehran, Isfahan, Mashhad, Tabriz, Shiraz—major cities across Iran. Within a week, demonstrations reached smaller towns, villages, university campuses nationwide.

Women at the Forefront

The slogan that united the world — "Woman, Life, Freedom" displayed in both English and Persian at an international solidarity demonstration. The movement that began with Mahsa Amini's death became a global demand for Iranian women's basic rights and bodily autonomy.

What made this revolutionary: women led. Not supporting male protesters. Not auxiliary to student movements. Leading.

Women stood in public squares, removed their hijabs, waved them in the air, sometimes burned them. They cut their hair—a profound symbolic act in Iranian culture. They danced in streets. They confronted security forces directly.

Young schoolgirls removed headscarves in class, chanting anti-government slogans. University women marched unveiled through campuses. Working women removed hijabs on buses, in shops, in parks.

The message was visceral: We reject your authority over our bodies. We reject your interpretation of Islam. We reject your entire system.

Iranian men joined—not to lead, but to protect. Videos showed men forming human shields around protesting women, fighting off security forces, chanting: “Women, Life, Freedom!”

The Slogans’ Evolution

Within days, the movement expanded beyond hijab:

“Woman, Life, Freedom!” (the core slogan)

“Death to the Dictator!” (Khamenei)

“Death to Khamenei!”

“We don’t want the Islamic Republic!”

“This is the year of blood, Seyyed Ali will be overthrown!”

Unlike 2009 (”Where is my vote?”) or 2019 (economic grievances), these weren’t demands for reform. These were calls for revolution—explicit rejection of the system itself.

One chant captured the totality: “From Kurdistan to Tehran, I will sacrifice my life for Iran!”—invoking national identity over Islamic revolutionary identity, unity across ethnic divisions, willingness to die for change.

Victory to the People's Revolution in Iran" — Older Iranian diaspora women join solidarity protests, showing the movement transcended age and geography. The poster's silhouette of a woman holding her removed hijab aloft became an iconic symbol of defiance against mandatory veiling laws.

II. THE VIOLENCE BEGINS IMMEDIATELY

Unlike 2009, when violence escalated gradually, security forces used deadly force from day one.

Reports documented live ammunition, beatings, mass arrests within the first week. No waiting for Khamenei’s signal. No gradual escalation. Immediate brutality.

This suggested two possibilities: either local commanders had standing orders to crush any protest immediately, or the regime understood this movement threatened its core identity and required maximum force from the start.

Targeting the Young

Security forces deliberately targeted teenagers and young adults. Schoolgirls arrested for removing hijabs in class. University students beaten on campuses. Seventeen-year-olds shot in the street.

Human rights monitors documented cases of:

  • Protesters shot in the face, eyes, genitals—targeting meant to maim and terrorize

  • Teenage girls arrested, sexually assaulted in detention

  • Young men beaten to death under interrogation

  • Children as young as 15 facing criminal charges

The regime was waging war on Iran’s future—literally killing and torturing the next generation for demanding the revolution’s promises be kept.

Three Weeks of Silence

Khamenei made no public statement for three weeks while protests grew and death toll mounted. His silence spoke volumes—this wasn’t confusion about how to respond. This was calculation.

On October 3, 2022, he finally spoke: “Her death deeply broke my heart. It was a bitter incident.”

But he immediately pivoted: The protests were “riots” orchestrated by “America and the Zionist regime.” He gave security forces “full support” to crush demonstrators. Protesters were “foreign agents” and “enemies of Islam.”

The pattern held: Khamenei speaks, violence escalates.

III. THE EXECUTIONS BEGIN

The regime's response to "Woman, Life, Freedom" — As Iranian women removed hijabs, Khamenei's security forces responded with disappearances, torture, and killing. International protesters documented the systematic violence, with discarded scarves symbolizing both defiance and the regime's victims.

On November 26, 2022, Khamenei addressed Basij members: “Each rioter, each terrorist, must be punished.”

Less than two weeks later, December 8, Iran executed its first protester: Mohsen Shekari, 23 years old, hanged after a rushed trial for allegedly blocking a road and wounding a Basij member with a machete.

The execution served as message: We will kill you legally. We will call it justice. We will do it publicly.

Iran Human Rights director Mahmood Amiry-Moghaddam called it what it was: “A show trial without any due process.”

More executions followed:

  • Majidreza Rahnavard, 23, hanged publicly from a crane in Mashhad

  • Mohammad Mehdi Karami, 22, executed

  • Seyed Mohammad Hosseini, 39, executed

  • Mohammad Boroughani, 19, executed

  • Mohammad Ghobadlou, 22, executed

  • Javad Rouhi, 35, executed

As of spring 2023, at least seven protesters had been executed. Dozens more remained on death row. Thousands faced trial on charges carrying death penalty.

The Charge: “Moharebeh”

The regime prosecuted protesters under moharebeh—”enmity against God.” The charge traditionally applied to armed rebellion against the Islamic state. Now it encompassed blocking roads, attending protests, organizing demonstrations.

If protesting hijab enforcement = enmity against God, then God’s representatives (Khamenei’s regime) can kill you “Islamically.”

This was theological perversion. Islam’s emphasis on mercy, forgiveness, proportionality disappeared. In their place: rushed trials, tortured confessions, public hangings to terrorize.

His Islamic Standard Violated

In countless speeches, Khamenei proclaimed: “Islam is mercy. The Prophet was sent as mercy to the worlds. Islamic governance means compassionate justice.”

The Prophet Muhammad said: “God is gentle and loves gentleness in all affairs.”

Islamic jurisprudence emphasizes: even in cases requiring capital punishment, every doubt should favor the accused, trials must meet rigorous standards, confessions under duress are invalid.

Test Khamenei’s actions:

  • Rushed trials lasting hours

  • Confessions obtained through torture

  • Charges of “enmity against God” for attending protests

  • Execution of 19-year-olds

  • Public hangings to terrorize

This isn’t Islamic justice. This is authoritarian brutality wrapped in religious language.

IV. THE STATISTICS OF REPRESSION

By spring 2023, when sustained street protests had largely subsided (though defiance continued), human rights organizations compiled grim totals:

Deaths: More than 500 protesters killed (some monitors documented over 550), including at least 70 children

Arrests: Over 20,000 detained, with thousands remaining imprisoned

Executions: 7 confirmed executed, dozens more sentenced to death

Torture: Systematic and widespread, documented by UN investigators

Sexual Violence: Used deliberately against women and girls in detention

In November 2024, a UN fact-finding mission concluded Iran had committed crimes against humanity during the 2022 crackdown, citing murder, torture, sexual violence, and persecution.

Crimes against humanity. Not by Western standards. By international law. By standards Iran as UN member is bound to uphold.

The Geographic and Social Spread

Like 2019, this uprising wasn’t limited to Tehran or middle class:

  • Over 100 cities involved

  • Kurdish regions (Mahsa’s homeland) particularly active

  • Baloch areas joined (Sunni minority showing solidarity)

  • University campuses nationwide

  • High schools across country

  • Working-class neighborhoods

  • Small towns and villages

Every ethnic group, every social class, every region—united in rejection.

When Kurdish, Persian, Baloch, Arab, and Azeri Iranians unite across ethnic lines that the regime usually exploits, the system faces existential threat.

V. THE REGIME’S IDENTITY CRISIS

Mandatory hijab isn’t peripheral to the Islamic Republic—it’s foundational. When Ayatollah Khomeini established the requirement in 1983, it symbolized the revolution’s rejection of Western influence and embrace of Islamic identity.

For 40 years, enforcing hijab served as daily demonstration of revolutionary control. Women’s bodies became the visible proof of the revolution’s success—every covered head a testament to Islamic governance.

When millions of women removed hijabs, they weren’t just breaking a law. They were declaring: This regime doesn’t represent Islam. Your Islam is patriarchal control, not divine guidance. We reject your authority to define faith.

The Revolutionary Contradiction

The 1979 revolution saw significant female participation. Women marched against the Shah. Many wore headscarves voluntarily as symbol of opposition to Western-backed monarchy.

But post-revolution, choice disappeared. What had been voluntary religious expression became mandatory state enforcement. What had been personal faith became political control.

Forty-three years later, women who had never lived without mandatory hijab were saying: This isn’t Islam. This is oppression.

Khamenei’s Theological Bind

If mandatory hijab is essential to Islamic governance (as regime claims), then millions of Iranian women rejecting it means either:

  1. Iranian women have lost faith in Islam (regime’s explanation)

  2. The regime’s version of Islam lacks legitimacy (protesters’ explanation)

The simpler truth: Khamenei and his system confused control with faith, conflated obedience with piety, mistook fear for belief.

When enforcement requires beating women to death over clothing, when maintaining “Islamic modesty” demands torture and execution, the system has perverted the religion it claims to serve.

VI. THE QUIET REVOLUTION CONTINUES

By spring 2023, massive street protests had subsided—not through resolution but exhaustion and brutal repression. Hundreds dead. Thousands imprisoned. Seven executed. Tens of thousands terrorized.

But something unprecedented happened: the defiance didn’t stop.

Throughout 2023, 2024, and into 2025, millions of Iranian women simply stopped wearing hijab in public. Not protesting. Not demonstrating. Just living unveiled.

In Tehran’s upscale neighborhoods, hijab compliance became rare. In middle-class areas, enforcement collapsed. Even in conservative cities, increasing numbers of women went unveiled or wore token scarves that met no standard of “proper” hijab.

The government responded inconsistently: sometimes harassment, sometimes arrests, sometimes ignoring. Morality police appeared, disappeared, reappeared. New surveillance systems to identify unveiled women were announced, implemented sporadically, overwhelmed by scale.

The Irreversible Shift

By 2025, the Islamic Republic faced reality: it couldn’t arrest millions. It couldn’t beat compliance back into existence. The social contract over hijab—never truly consensual—had shattered irreparably.

Women had reclaimed their bodies and, in doing so, exposed the regime’s powerlessness. The system could kill hundreds, imprison thousands, execute individuals—but it couldn’t restore the pre-September 2022 status quo.

December 2025-January 2026: They’re Back

When economic protests erupted in December 2025, women were again at the forefront. Not just unveiled—but burning images of Khamenei, filming themselves, posting videos globally.

The “Woman, Life, Freedom” movement hadn’t ended. It had evolved into permanent resistance.

VII. HIS OWN WORDS CONDEMN HIM

Throughout his rule, Khamenei positioned himself as protector of Islamic values and Iranian women’s dignity.

In a 2018 speech: “Islam honors women. The hijab protects women from exploitation and objectification. Western critics who attack our hijab laws are hypocrites who treat women as commodities.”

In 2021: “Iranian women are free—free to pursue education, careers, spiritual development. The hijab doesn’t limit freedom; it enables true liberation from materialistic culture.”

Test these claims against September 2022 onward:

A 22-year-old woman died in custody for “improper” hijab. Her death sparked revolution because millions of Iranian women rejected the notion that fabric positioning equals divine law. When the regime responded with bullets, torture, and executions, it proved the hijab was never about honor or protection—it was about control.

The Islamic Standard

The Quran states: “There is no compulsion in religion” (2:256).

Islam emphasizes free will in matters of faith. Forced compliance isn’t piety—it’s tyranny.

The Prophet Muhammad said: “Beware! Whoever is cruel and hard on a non-Muslim minority, curtails their rights, burdens them with more than they can bear, or takes anything from them against their free will; I will complain against that person on the Day of Judgment.”

Apply that standard to Iranian women: Khamenei’s regime curtailed their rights, burdened them with laws they reject, took their freedom against their will—and beat them to death when they resisted.

By Islamic principles, by the Prophet’s own words, the Guardian violated everything he claimed to protect.

CLOSING: THE WOMEN WHO WON

By January 2026, the Islamic Republic still stands. Khamenei still rules. Security forces still patrol streets. New protests face same brutal response.

But walk through Tehran, Isfahan, Shiraz—millions of women live unveiled. The regime cannot restore what “Woman, Life, Freedom” destroyed: the illusion of consent, the facade of religious legitimacy, the pretense that mandatory hijab represents Islam rather than authoritarian control.

Mahsa Amini’s death accomplished what decades of activism couldn’t: it exposed the system’s fundamental lie. When government beats women to death over clothing while claiming to honor them, when execution follows protests while claiming Islamic justice, when torture enforces faith while claiming religious truth—the hypocrisy becomes undeniable.

The revolution promised Islamic governance. Khamenei delivered theocratic brutality.

The revolution promised women’s dignity. Khamenei delivered morality police killing 22-year-olds.

The revolution promised justice. Khamenei delivered rushed trials and public hangings.

By revolutionary standards, by Islamic principles, by his own proclaimed values—Mahsa Amini’s death and his response stand as final proof of the Guardian’s failure.

The women of Iran issued their verdict: “Woman, Life, Freedom.”

Not “Reform, Patience, Compliance.”

Not “Dialogue, Negotiation, Compromise.”

Freedom. The revolutionary promise Khamenei spent 43 years denying.

And they’re still fighting for it—unveiled, unafraid, unbroken—in the streets of 108 cities as of January 2026.

The bunker awaits. The women won’t stop until he’s in it permanently.
