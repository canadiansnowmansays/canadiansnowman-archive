The $37 Trillion Question

Episode 2: The Democratic Spending Legacy - Building America's Safety Net

June 3, 2025

A weekly series examining how America broke its postwar promise of fiscal responsibility. Each week, we trace the bipartisan policy choices that created today's debt crisis and explore evidence-based solutions that could restore fiscal sanity. This analysis is updated regularly with the latest economic and political developments.

The Democratic Party's postwar legacy reads like a tale of noble ambition colliding with fiscal reality—building a nation's backbone while often bending its fiscal spine to the breaking point. From Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal recovery programs to Joe Biden's COVID response plans, Democrats have wielded federal spending as both sword and shield: lifting the vulnerable, protecting the disadvantaged, and projecting American power across the globe.

Their triumphs anchor modern American life—Social Security prevents elderly poverty, Medicare saves countless lives, and the interstate highway system connects a continent. Their missteps—Vietnam's bloody quagmire, Libya's chaos, unchecked deficit spending—fuel today's fiscal crisis. As a Canadian observer watching from across our shared border, I see a party that delivered tangible good for ordinary Americans yet sowed seeds of debt and division that compound annually.

This isn't a one-sided ledger. Democratic spending drove growth, reduced inequality, and built infrastructure that remains America's foundation. But the price—$37 trillion and counting—demands an honest reckoning with both achievements and excesses.

Roosevelt to Truman: Building with Purpose (1933-1953)

It began with Franklin Roosevelt facing economic collapse. The New Deal poured $50 billion into jobs, dams, and relief programs—massive sums for the era, driving deficits to $4 billion yearly by 1940. Yet this spending sparked 9% annual GDP growth, pulling America from depression's depths while building infrastructure that lasted generations.

Post-1945, Harry Truman's Fair Deal extended this philosophy. His administration added $10 billion in housing programs and Social Security expansions by 1950, while the Korean War (1950-1953) cost $30 billion—doubling deficits to $6 billion by 1952. The results justified the expense: growth averaged 4% annually, unemployment fell to 4%, and America emerged as the unchallenged global leader.

From Canada's perspective, this spending paid dividends for us too. Our trade with a robust United States soared—exports jumped from $10 billion in 1945 to $20 billion by 1950 as American prosperity lifted all boats. This was spending with clear purpose: modest deficits, vast outcomes, and a growing economy that could service its debts.

Yet even then, appetites grew. The pattern was set—Democrats would spend first, worry about revenue later.

Kennedy: Ambition and Early Excess (1961-1963)

John F. Kennedy's brief presidency packed extraordinary ambition into just 1,000 days. His signature achievement—the 1962 tax cut that dropped the top rate from 91% to 70% by 1964—cost $11 billion yearly but sparked magnificent 5.8% growth in 1964. Here was proof that strategic spending could yield spectacular returns.

But Kennedy's appetites extended far beyond tax policy. The Apollo program, launched in 1961 after Soviet cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin's orbital flight, hit $3 billion by 1963—part of a $25 billion moonshot by 1969 that would employ 400,000 Americans and birth entire industries. The Cuban Missile Crisis cost $1 billion in naval operations—war averted, American prestige secured, yet an expensive flex of superpower muscle.

Kennedy's Alliance for Progress pledged $20 billion over a decade to Latin America, with $2 billion spent by 1963. Results proved mixed—the Bay of Pigs invasion cost $46 million and failed spectacularly, while Vietnam's early involvement hit $1 billion by 1963, planting seeds for a war that would consume a generation.

Deficits rose to $5 billion by 1963, but growth soared and America's global leadership seemed unshakeable. Kennedy's ambition stretched the fiscal framework, but within bounds that a growing economy could manage.

Johnson: The Great Society and Vietnam's Devastating Toll (1963-1969)

Lyndon Johnson transformed Kennedy's ambitions into the most sweeping domestic program since the New Deal. The Great Society—Medicare, Medicaid, the War on Poverty, federal education funding—launched in 1965 with programs costing $20 billion annually by 1968.

The achievements were monumental. Medicare and Medicaid cost $10 billion yearly by 1970 but cut elderly poverty from 35% to 15% while reducing elderly mortality by 20%. The War on Poverty added $5 billion in food stamps and Head Start programs that lifted millions from destitution. Economic growth held at 5% annually, and inequality actually decreased as the Gini coefficient improved from 0.40 to 0.38.

But Vietnam devoured everything. Military spending ballooned to $25 billion yearly by 1968—killing 58,000 Americans and millions of Vietnamese while draining resources from domestic priorities. Total spending hit 20% of GDP while revenue lagged at 17.6%—a gap Johnson ignored as he pursued both "guns and butter."

Deficits exploded to $25 billion by 1968. For the first time in the postwar era, spending clearly outpaced the economy's ability to fund it sustainably. From Canada, we watched our neighbor bleed both cash and credibility in Southeast Asian jungles—our quieter stance on Vietnam reflecting wisdom Johnson ignored.

Analysis: This period reveals the pattern that would plague Democratic fiscal policy for decades—excellent programs funded through borrowing rather than progressive taxation that could have sustained them.

Carter: Modest Spending Amid Crisis (1977-1981)

Jimmy Carter inherited economic chaos—stagflation combining 13.5% inflation with sluggish growth—and responded with unusual Democratic restraint. Unlike his predecessors' grand ambitions, Carter approached fiscal policy with surgical precision, understanding that economic malaise required careful intervention rather than massive government programs.

His modest spending initiatives included $10 billion for airline deregulation in 1978 and $5 billion for energy programs in 1979—both successful policies that increased competition and reduced long-term costs. Deficits grew from $53 billion in 1977 to $74 billion by 1980, but this increase paled beside previous Democratic spending explosions.

Carter's Camp David Accords cost roughly $1.5 billion annually in aid to stabilize Egypt-Israel relations—a diplomatic triumph that brought Middle East peace. But the Iran hostage crisis ($200 million in operations) and the 1979 oil shock (driving prices from $40 to $100 per barrel) demonstrated America's vulnerability to external shocks.

Growth faltered to 0.2% by 1980, yet Carter's deregulation sparked competition and efficiency gains that would influence policy across party lines. His energy crisis also highlighted economic interdependence—Canadian oil exports to the United States surged as Americans scrambled for alternatives, showing how fiscal crises become globally contagious.

Carter represented a Democratic pivot toward fiscal discipline, even as political pressures mounted for expansive solutions to mounting economic woes.

Clinton to Obama: Balancing and Bailing Out (1993-2017)

The late 20th and early 21st centuries tested Democratic fiscal restraint. Bill Clinton achieved something remarkable—balanced budgets from 1998-2001 yielding $236 billion in surpluses. He accomplished this through $20 billion in welfare reforms (1996) combined with 39.6% top tax rates that actually funded government operations.

Growth averaged 4% under Clinton—proof that higher taxes on the wealthy could coexist with prosperity. Revenue finally matched spending, and debt-to-GDP stabilized for the first time since the 1970s.

But Barack Obama faced the 2008 financial collapse—the worst crisis since the Great Depression. His $831 billion American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) added 3 million jobs according to Congressional Budget Office analysis, preventing economic catastrophe. The Affordable Care Act cost $940 billion over a decade but insured 20 million Americans while slowing healthcare cost growth.

Deficits soared to $1.4 trillion in 2009 as tax revenues collapsed and spending surged to prevent depression. Growth averaged just 2% through Obama's presidency—reflecting the severity of the inherited crisis rather than policy failures. Obama's foreign interventions—Libya cost $1 billion, drone programs $5 billion yearly—toppled dictators but stoked regional chaos.

Obama's spending was largely defensive—responding to Republican-created crises rather than pursuing ambitious new programs. Yet deficits persisted throughout his presidency, even as the economy recovered.

Biden: Recovery at a Monumental Cost (2021-2025)

Joe Biden's presidency caps this Democratic spending arc with the largest peacetime fiscal expansion in American history. The $2.1 trillion American Rescue Plan (2021) provided $1,400 direct payments and state aid that added 3 million jobs according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The $1.2 trillion Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021) and $740 billion Inflation Reduction Act (2022) added roughly $689 billion to debt while funding roads, broadband, and climate initiatives.

Economic results were mixed. Growth ticked up to 2.5% by 2022, but inflation hit 9%—the highest in four decades. Deficits actually fell from $1.4 trillion (2022) to $1 trillion (2024) as temporary pandemic aid expired and revenue increased to 18% of GDP. Biden's blocked $400 billion student loan forgiveness program and $2 trillion in new regulatory costs offset these gains.

Republicans, including Trump, claim Biden created a recession—ignoring that Biden inherited a COVID-devastated economy from Trump's $1 trillion deficit (2019) and $2.2 trillion CARES Act (2020). Biden's policies drove job gains despite global inflation affecting every developed nation.

From Canada, where our $400 billion in exports faced trade strains, Biden's policies appeared necessary if expensive responses to genuine crises.

This chart shows the devastating compound effect of debt accumulation. Interest payments consumed just 2-5% of federal revenue through the 1970s, but Reagan's debt explosion drove it to 15% by 1990. Despite Clinton's brief respite, the trend resumed upward, reaching a crisis point of 25% by 2025—meaning one dollar in four collected by the federal government now goes to service past debt rather than current needs.

Weighing the Democratic Ledger: Triumphs and Costs

The Democratic spending legacy reveals both profound achievements and troubling patterns:

Undeniable Successes:

  • Social Security lifts 22 million Americans from poverty annually

  • Medicare reduced elderly mortality by 20% since 1965

  • Apollo program spurred technological revolutions that persist today

  • Clinton's surplus proved fiscal discipline was possible

  • Obama and Biden prevented depressions through countercyclical spending

  • Infrastructure investments from highways to broadband connect the nation

Persistent Problems:

  • Vietnam's $150 billion cost bought tragedy, not victory

  • Revenue-to-GDP rarely exceeded 18% even during Democratic administrations

  • Spending consistently hit 20-24% of GDP during crises

  • Wars from Korea to Libya cost hundreds of billions with mixed results

  • Structural deficits persisted even during recovery periods

The key insight: Democratic spending created lasting institutions—Social Security, Medicare, interstate highways—that form America's foundation. Yet without matching revenue increases, this noble construction project was built on borrowed time and borrowed money.

The Pattern Emerges

Democrats didn't create the debt crisis alone—Republican tax cuts widened the revenue hole significantly. But their spending philosophy established a dangerous precedent: build first, fund later. Growth slowed from 4% in the postwar era to 2% post-2008, while trust in government eroded from 73% in 1964 to 17% by 2024.

From Canada, with our $2 trillion debt funding universal healthcare and social programs sustainably, the scale appears both impressive and troubling. Your safety nets surpass ours in many ways, but so does your red ink.

The Democratic vision—government as protector and builder—remains compelling. Social Security prevents elderly destitution, Medicare saves lives, infrastructure connects communities. These achievements justify Democratic pride and explain persistent public support for activist government.

But the fiscal foundation cracked. Without progressive taxation to fund their ambitions, Democrats built a magnificent structure on an increasingly unstable base. The $37 trillion debt represents both their greatest achievement—a nation that cares for its vulnerable—and their greatest failure to secure sustainable funding for that vision.

Looking Ahead

Next week, we'll examine how Republicans responded to Democratic spending with their own fiscal philosophy: tax cuts for the wealthy that promised growth but delivered deficits. Their approach—slashing revenue while maintaining spending—created structural holes that dwarf even the most ambitious Democratic programs.

The tragedy is that both parties possessed pieces of the solution. Democrats understood that government investment could drive growth and equity. Republicans grasped that excessive taxation could stifle enterprise. Neither party sustained the postwar balance that funded prosperity through progressive taxation while maintaining growth incentives.

The $37 trillion question isn't whether government should spend—it's whether America can rediscover the fiscal discipline that once made such spending sustainable.
