The Iran Crisis

It Begins

Part XVII: Operation Epic Fury

February 28, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | February 28, 2026

The author of this series hoped to be wrong.

At approximately 1:33 a.m. Eastern time on Saturday, February 28, 2026, three massive explosions shook central Tehran. Within minutes, the United States confirmed that military strikes on Iran were underway. Israel announced it had joined the attack. The Department of Defense named the operation: Epic Fury.

This series has documented, across sixteen parts, every signal that pointed here. The 30-day buildup. The carrier deployments. The allied evacuations. The Geneva silence. Trump’s “not happy” statement Friday afternoon. Ambassador Huckabee’s email telling embassy staff to leave Israel today, not tomorrow.

The author hoped to be wrong.

What Trump Said

At 2:30 a.m. Eastern, President Trump posted an eight-minute video to Truth Social. He was standing behind a lectern, wearing a white USA hat, speaking from Mar-a-Lago where he had arrived hours earlier after a speech in Corpus Christi, Texas.

“Our objective is to defend the American people by eliminating imminent threats from the Iranian regime, a vicious group of very hard, terrible people.”

He announced that the United States would “raze their missile industry to the ground” and “annihilate their navy.” He called on members of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps to “lay down your weapons” with the promise of “total immunity” — or “face certain death.”

Then he addressed the Iranian people directly:

“For many years, you have asked for America’s help, but you never got it. No president was willing to do what I am willing to do tonight. Now you have a president who is giving you what you want, so let’s see how you respond.”

“This will be probably your only chance for generations.”

“When we are finished, take over your government.”

Three things are worth noting about this statement.

First, Trump acknowledged the possibility of American casualties. “The lives of courageous American heroes may be lost and we may have casualties. That often happens in war.” General Caine, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, had warned him privately that American troops could be killed. Trump acknowledged this warning — and proceeded.

Second, Trump made almost no effort to argue that an immediate threat had prompted the strikes. As David Sanger reported for the Times: he “recited a litany of Iranian attacks on American interests back to the hostage crisis during the Carter administration” but “made little effort to argue that any immediate threat had prompted the latest U.S. and Israeli strikes.” The intelligence community had not concluded that Iran was close to a nuclear weapon. The Defense Intelligence Agency concluded in 2025 that no decision had been made to pursue an intercontinental ballistic missile.

Third, the war aim Trump stated is not the destruction of the nuclear program. It is regime change.

“When we are finished, take over your government.”

What Is Actually True About the Justifications

The New York Times published a fact-check alongside the live updates that deserves prominent placement in this record.

Trump and his aides made three public claims in the days before the strikes: that Iran had restarted its nuclear program, that it had enough material to build a bomb within days, and that it was developing missiles that could soon hit the United States.

All three claims are either false or unproven.

Iran has taken steps to dig out facilities hit in last June’s strikes and resumed work at some sites. But officials said there is no evidence Iran has made active efforts to resume enriching uranium or building a bomb mechanism. The stockpiles of enriched uranium Iran already holds remain buried after June’s strikes — making it nearly impossible to build a bomb “within days.” American intelligence agencies believe Iran is probably years away from missiles capable of hitting the United States.

The war began on a false premise. Sound familiar? The United States launched the Iraq War on fabricated intelligence about weapons of mass destruction. It is now 23 years later, the weapons were never found, and the region has never recovered. That is now on the record.

Senator Jack Reed, the top Democrat on the Senate Armed Services Committee, had not been notified in advance. He had warned on Friday: “Congress has received no real briefings or intelligence” on a possible attack. The Constitution grants Congress alone the power to declare war. Trump did not consult Congress.

Senator Ruben Gallego, Democrat of Arizona and a former Marine who served in Iraq, said the United States “can support the democracy movement and the Iranian people without sending our troops to die.” He added that Americans should not “pay the ultimate price for regime change and a war that hasn’t been explained or justified to the American people.”

Representative Thomas Massie, Republican of Kentucky, criticized the strikes immediately. He had been part of a group of lawmakers planning to force votes next week to prohibit Trump from authorizing strikes without consulting Congress — a move the Times noted may be “considered moot by many given U.S. involvement in military action today.”

The Constitution grants Congress alone the power to formally declare war. Trump did not seek that declaration. He did not consult congressional leadership in advance. Senator Reed, the top Democrat on the Armed Services Committee, was not notified before the strikes began. Trump launched this war unilaterally, from Mar-a-Lago, in the middle of the night, while most members of Congress were asleep.

In his video, Trump acknowledged this was a war — “that often happens in war,” he said of potential casualties. A president who calls something a war while bypassing the branch of government the Constitution assigns war powers to is not a legal anomaly. It is a pattern. Venezuela was the same. No declaration, no authorization, no congressional consultation.

The difference is that Iran is not Venezuela. The scale of what has been launched — and the scale of what Iran can launch in return — makes the constitutional bypass not just a legal question but a practical one. Congress was not briefed on the risks. Congress was not asked to weigh them. The American people’s elected representatives learned about this war the same way everyone else did: from a video posted to Truth Social at 2:30 in the morning.

The Scale of What Has Been Launched

A plume of smoke rises following an explosion in Tehran on Saturday.Credit...Atta Kenare/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

The initial wave involved dozens of US strikes carried out by attack planes launched from bases across the Middle East and from at least one aircraft carrier. This is the largest US military deployment since the Iraq War in 2003: two aircraft carriers, naval destroyers, more than 50 fighter planes.

The targets in this operation are fundamentally different from last June’s strikes on nuclear facilities deep in the mountains. These strikes have hit the heart of Tehran.

Videos verified by the Times show strikes on the area housing the presidential palace and Iran’s National Security Council. A strike near the Ministry of Intelligence. Thick smoke rising from the compound where Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei normally resides. Satellite imagery shows collapsed buildings at Khamenei’s secure compound — though his whereabouts remain unclear.

The strikes also hit Qom, Kermanshah, Isfahan, Karaj, Shiraz, Isfahan, and Urmia near the Turkish border. Dozens of people were killed in a strike on a girls’ school in Minab, in Hormozgan province along the Strait of Hormuz.

Sources: Iranian state news agency, verified satellite images and video. The New York Times

The strikes hit an area of Tehran known to be home to former President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad — the same Ahmadinejad whose former vice president, Jaber Rajabi told Graeme Wood just days ago, was “the one figure with enough domestic support to lead Iran.” His whereabouts at the time of the blast were unknown.

A central goal of the first joint wave, according to three Israeli security officials, was to hit as many leaders as possible in the opening minutes — before Iranian leadership could take precautions.

Iran’s Response

Iran’s response was immediate and broad.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps launched multiple waves of ballistic missiles and drones at Israel. Air raid sirens sounded across the country. Israelis raced to bomb shelters — many of them, on Sabbath morning, still wearing prayer shawls and carrying prayer books.

Iran simultaneously launched missile strikes targeting US military bases across the region: Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait, Al Dhafra Air Base in the UAE, and the US Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain. The Fifth Fleet headquarters was hit. The UAE intercepted Iranian missiles but reported one civilian death from falling debris.

Qatar’s defense ministry said it had “successfully thwarted the third wave of attacks.” Jordan intercepted two ballistic missiles. Syria closed its southern air corridors. Kurdish Iraq ordered schools closed through Wednesday — the last US military base in Iraq and a massive US consulate are in that region.

Kata’ib Hezbollah, the most powerful Iranian proxy militia in Iraq, announced it would begin attacking American bases. “We will soon begin attacking American bases in response to their aggression,” a group leader told the Times.

Iran’s Supreme National Security Council stated: “The enemy thinks that the innocent nation of Iran will surrender to their despicable demands with these cowardly actions. The armed forces of the Islamic Republic of Iran have launched a crushing response to their vicious actions.”

Internet access in Iran plummeted to 4 percent of normal levels.

Iranian authorities warned civilians that Tehran and other cities would continue to be struck and told them to flee to safer areas. The deputy mayor of Tehran made public transportation free to help residents escape. Highways out of the city choked with traffic.

What Iranians Said

Highways leading out of the city were backed up for miles, residents said.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

The Times reached ordinary Iranians in the first hours. Their voices belong in this record.

Ali Zeinalipoor, a Tehran resident: “I rushed to school to get my daughter from middle school. The girls were hiding under the stairs and crying. The principal did not know what had happened — everyone was so scared.”

Golshan Fathi, from her rooftop in northern Tehran: “People are standing on the roof looking at the sky, pointing down. You can hear women screaming. Some of my neighbors are running to their cars. It feels like we are in a movie.”

Esfandiar, an engineer in the Pasdaran area: “My children are crying and scared, we are huddling in the bathroom, we don’t know what to do. This is terrifying.”

Laleh, a lawyer and mother of two, on Trump’s call for Iranians to rise up once the bombing ends: “The only thing on our mind right now is to get to safety. Nobody is thinking of protesting right now.”

Not all Iranians were opposed. Arian, a resident west of Tehran: “Some of his relatives were cheering the strikes.” Voices outside his building chanting “Long live the shah.”

A country in terror. A country divided. Exactly as this series documented — a population that largely wants the regime to fall, trapped inside an attack they did not ask for and cannot control.

A photo released by Iranian state media on Saturday shows people reacting after an explosion in Tehran.Credit...Majid Asgaripour/WANA News Agency, via Reuters

The World Reacts

The most significant international reaction came from the man who was inside the room trying to prevent this war.

Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi — who on Friday said a peace deal was “within our reach” and that Iran had made meaningful concessions on its nuclear program — broke his silence within hours of the strikes. “Active and serious negotiations have yet again been undermined,” he wrote on social media. “I urge the United States not to get sucked in further. This is not your war.”

That statement is worth sitting with. Albusaidi was the mediator. He flew to Washington on Friday to argue that progress was being made. He met with Vice President Vance. He was optimistic in public. He is now publicly condemning the decision he was trying to prevent. If the man running the diplomacy says the diplomacy was working, that is not a minor data point.

France’s President Macron called the war one with “grave consequences for international peace and security,” demanded an end to “the ongoing escalation,” and called for an emergency UN Security Council meeting.

Britain did not participate and said it does “not want to see further escalation into a wider regional conflict.” It did not express support.

Germany was informed by Israel in advance. Chancellor Merz is scheduled to meet Trump in Washington next week. The statement did not express support or opposition.

The European Union called preventing “any actions that could further escalate tensions or undermine the global nonproliferation regime” of critical importance.

Canada and Australia both backed the strikes. Prime Minister Mark Carney and Foreign Minister Anita Anand issued a joint statement: “Canada supports the United States acting to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon and to prevent its regime from further threatening international peace and security.” Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese echoed nearly identical language, citing two Iranian-directed terrorist attacks on Australian soil in 2024 as context for his government’s position.

As a Canadian journalist, I note my own government’s support without endorsing or condemning it here. That is a conversation Canada will need to have with itself.

Saudi Arabia condemned Iranian retaliatory strikes on Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait and the UAE as “a blatant violation of national sovereignty” and pledged “full solidarity” with those nations.

Iran asked the UN Security Council to take “immediate action.”

Turkey’s largest airlines cancelled all regional flights. Dubai and Doha — two of the world’s largest aviation hubs — suspended service.

The Talks Were Theatre

Oman's Foreign Affairs Minister Badr al-Busaidi, right, is shown Thursday with White House special envoys Steve Witkoff, centre, and Jared Kushner, left. Oman is mediating the talks between the U.S. and Iran in Geneva, Switzerland. (Foreign Ministry of Oman/The Associated Press)

This series stated weeks ago that Trump had already made up his mind — that the Geneva negotiations were not a genuine search for a diplomatic solution but public cover for a decision already taken. That assessment is now confirmed by the evidence on the ground.

Consider the timeline.

On February 20, Trump gave Iran a self-imposed deadline of 10 to 15 days to reach a deal. That window expired on March 6 or 7 — before Ramadan ends on March 19. The timeline never left room for a real negotiation. A genuine diplomatic process does not come with a countdown clock set before the first session begins.

Steve Witkoff, Trump’s envoy, told Fox News that Iran was “probably a week away from industrial-grade bomb-making material.” The IAEA directly contradicted this — assessing that Iran’s program had been set back years by last June’s strikes. You do not make factually false claims about your adversary’s capabilities during genuine negotiations. You make them to build the public case for strikes you have already decided to launch.

When the second round of Geneva talks concluded, Iran and Oman said there had been “good progress.” Within 24 hours, JD Vance said the two sides were “still very far apart.” Two parties cannot both be accurately describing the same negotiation. One of them was performing for a different audience.

Iran’s foreign ministry noted this morning — in its statement responding to the strikes — that this was the second time the United States had attacked while negotiations were underway. “Just as they had before the June war,” they said. The pattern is not ambiguous.

And now the Omani mediator himself has closed the case. Badr Albusaidi — who was inside the room, who flew to Washington on Friday to argue that a deal was possible, who said Iran had made meaningful concessions — stated this morning: “Active and serious negotiations have yet again been undermined.”

The man running the diplomacy says the diplomacy was real. Trump struck anyway.

The Geneva talks were theatre. The military buildup was already 40 to 50 percent of all deployable US airpower before the first negotiating session. The evacuation of embassy staff was authorized the day after the final round of talks. The decision had been made. The negotiations were the public performance that preceded an announcement already written.

This matters not just as a historical record but as a live question: if Trump was not negotiating in good faith with Iran, what does that mean for every other diplomatic process he is currently conducting? What does it mean for the countries — Canada among them — that endorsed the strikes on the basis that Iran refused to negotiate?

They refused to capitulate. That is not the same thing.

What This Series Documented

This is the seventeenth part of a series that began tracking the signals of this war weeks before it started.

Part XIII documented the initial 30-day buildup — 40-50 percent of all deployable US airpower flowing to the region.

Part XIV documented why Iran was unlikely to capitulate to US demands, and why the off-ramps were narrowing.

Part XV documented the evacuation signal — the coordinated allied departures, the Huckabee email, the Geneva silence — and concluded that the evidence suggested the US appeared to be in the final hours or days before military action.

Part XVI, written yesterday and published earlier this morning, asked the question nobody in Washington was publicly asking: what happens after? Who governs Iran the morning after a regime falls? What is the plan if it doesn’t fall but is radicalized? The governance question. The succession question. The Russia-China question.

Those questions remain unanswered. The strikes are now underway.

David Sanger of the Times noted that just hours before the attacks began, “senior officials from Arab states and European allies were saying that the White House was still not telling them what the objective of the strikes would be.”

Trump left no doubt in his video: regime change.

There are almost no successful examples in modern history of regime change through an air campaign alone. Administration officials have made clear they envision a swift campaign with no US troops on the ground.

Two American military officials told the Times before the strikes that it was unclear “whether Mr. Trump and senior officials who were pushing for the sweeping military operation understood the depth of many Islamic Revolutionary Guards’ distaste for the United States, or the complexities of the forces that this military campaign may unleash.”

What Comes Now

The strikes are expected to last several days, possibly weeks. Iran has vowed “crushing retaliation.” Its missile stockpile — estimated at 2,000 to 6,000 weapons — is the primary threat. Iranian proxies across the region are activating.

The governance question this series raised in Part XVI is now the central question of the war.

If Khamenei is killed or incapacitated in the strikes, who governs Iran? What coalition is ready to take over? What prevents civil war? What prevents Mojtaba Khamenei — the supreme leader’s son, described by Jaber Rajabi as more dangerous and more ideologically extreme than his father — from using the chaos of succession to consolidate power?

None of these questions appear to have public answers.

Trump told Iranians: “When we are finished, take over your government. This will be probably your only chance for generations.”

A lawyer in Tehran sheltering with her children said: “The only thing on our mind right now is to get to safety.”

The gap between those two statements is where this war will be won or lost.
