Country-Specific Tariffs: Alienating Allies and Destroying Relationships

Trump's country-by-country tariff assault is systematically destroying America's relationships with its most important partners, creating a cascade of economic and diplomatic damage that will take decades to repair.

Constitutional Crisis: The Emergency Powers Fraud

Trump's entire tariff regime rests on a fraudulent interpretation of emergency powers that courts are systematically rejecting.

The Legal Framework Trump is Destroying

The Constitution explicitly grants Congress—not the president—the power to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises" and to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations." Congress has created specific, limited authorities for presidential tariffs:

Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act of 1962): Allows tariffs only when Commerce Secretary finds imports threaten national security

Section 301 (Trade Act of 1974): Permits retaliation against specific unfair trade practices after investigation

Section 201 (Trade Act of 1974): Provides temporary relief for industries seriously injured by imports, with strict time limits and congressional oversight

Antidumping and Countervailing Duty Laws: Target specific unfair pricing practices through detailed legal procedures

The IEEPA Abuse

Instead of using established authorities, Trump weaponized the 1977 International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)—a sanctions and embargo law that doesn't even mention tariffs. No president invoked IEEPA for tariffs for 48 years until Trump did so this year.

IEEPA can only be used during an emergency, defined as "a sudden, unexpected crisis." Trump's rationale—bilateral trade deficits—fails this test completely: "They are not sudden or unexpected or a crisis. They've existed for decades with many countries, and so they're definitely not an emergency."

Judicial Rejection

The U.S. Court of International Trade ruled unanimously that Trump's use of IEEPA to institute sweeping tariffs is illegal, writing that "Because of the Constitution's express allocation of the tariff power to Congress … we do not read IEEPA to delegate an unbounded tariff authority to the President."

At least seven cases have been filed in American federal courts challenging Trump's authority to impose tariffs under IEEPA, with each case arguing that tariff imposition without clear congressional authorization constitutes an overextension of executive power into areas traditionally reserved for the legislative branch.

Court arguments were held July 31, 2025, with the appeals panel seeming hesitant to support the government's position.

Major Questions Doctrine

Legal expert Ilya Somin argues that "if starting the biggest trade war since the Great Depression is not a major question, then I do not know what is," noting that the Supreme Court requires clear congressional delegation for major economic decisions. Courts can apply the major questions doctrine, which requires that "when the executive branch claims the power to decide some major economic or social issue, they must show that Congress clearly delegated that authority to them."

Economic Evidence: Devastating Impact on American Families

Multiple authoritative studies demonstrate that Trump's tariffs function as a massive regressive tax, with working families bearing the heaviest burden.

Household Tax Burden

The Tax Foundation calculates that Trump tariffs amount to "an average tax increase of nearly $1,300 per US household in 2025." The Budget Lab at Yale estimates consumer losses of $2,700-$3,400 per household on average, noting that "tariffs are a regressive tax, especially in the short-run" with the burden falling heaviest on low-income families.

For households in the second lowest income decile, the tariff proposal leads to consumer loss of just under $1,300 per household on average when countries retaliate. For households in the middle, the burden rises to $2,100 per household on average, and for those in the top tenth, it averages $5,400 per household.

GDP and Wage Destruction

The Penn Wharton Budget Model projects Trump's tariffs would reduce GDP by about 8% and wages by 7%, with a middle-income household facing a $58,000 lifetime loss. These losses are twice as large as would result from raising corporate taxes from 21% to 36%.

The illustrative policy would permanently reduce US output by $100-$175 billion annually, with additional dynamic reductions in tax revenue of $200-$400 billion. That's the equivalent of the US economy being permanently smaller by this massive amount in 2024 dollars.

Manufacturing Job Myth Debunked

Federal Reserve economists found a net decrease in manufacturing employment due to tariffs, with benefits from increased production in protected industries outweighed by consequences of rising input costs and retaliatory tariffs.

A Federal Reserve Board study found that rising input costs from US tariff hikes in 2018-19 led to job losses in American manufacturing, with damage from higher expenses compounded by retaliatory taxes on US exports, more than offsetting a small boost to manufacturing employment from US tariffs.
