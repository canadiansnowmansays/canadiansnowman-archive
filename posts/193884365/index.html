To the Moon, Alice

Part IV: The Real Estate Agent Has Left the Building

April 15, 2026

April 15, 2026

On April 10th, 2026, four astronauts splashed down in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego, concluding the most watched space mission in a generation. Humanity cheered. The world exhaled.

On April 9th, 2026 — the day before splashdown, while the world was watching Integrity begin its fireball descent — China quietly transported the Chang’e 7 lunar probe to its launch site at the Wenchang Space Launch Center on Hainan Island.

No fanfare. No press conference. No livestream.

Just a spacecraft, a rocket, and a destination: the lunar south pole. Launch target: August 2026.

If you want to understand what the next chapter of lunar history actually looks like, forget the poetry of Part II and the mission wrap of Part III. Start with that image — Artemis II coming home on one side of the planet, Chang’e 7 arriving at its launch pad on the other. Two missions. Two programs. Two governance frameworks. One prize.

A Note on What I’ve Already Written

Before going further, I need to point regular readers toward my Great Energy Challenge series, Parts 9 through 14, published in December 2025. In those essays I covered in considerable technical and economic detail: the orbital data centre race, the Kessler Syndrome debris problem, the case for lunar infrastructure, the Starcloud-1 satellite that trained an AI model in space, Google’s radiation testing of commercial chips, Musk’s lunar factory and mass driver vision, and the Antarctic Treaty as a governance model.

I am not going to repeat that material here. If you haven’t read it, the links are at the bottom of this essay. Consider it the technical foundation. This essay is about something different.

This essay is about the real estate.

Before We Get to the Real Estate

A moment for what was just left behind.

Writing in The Atlantic on April 14th, journalist Lynn Sherr — who covered NASA in the early 1980s and once stood next to Gene Cernan at Kennedy Space Center while he pointed at the night sky and said quietly, “that’s where I landed” — asked the question that haunts the transition from Part III of this series to Part IV:

“Can that glow ever reflect back on Earth?”

Christina Koch, on return, was asked to define the word crew. She didn’t hesitate: “a group that is in it all the time, no matter what, stroking together every minute, for the same purpose.” Then she extended the metaphor to the rest of us. Looking back at Earth from a quarter million miles away, she said: “Planet Earth, you are a crew.”

NASA associate administrator Amit Kshatriya, speaking at a press conference — a NASA official, on the record — said this: “If you can’t take love to the stars, what are we doing? Like, why are we even going? That’s why we send humans instead of robots sometimes.”

Hold all of that. Because what follows is the story of how Planet Earth, as a crew, is doing rather badly.

What Is Actually at Stake

The lunar south pole contains something that has not existed on the moon’s surface for billions of years: water.

Not liquid water — the moon has no atmosphere to sustain it. But frozen water, locked in the permanently shadowed craters near the pole, craters that have not seen sunlight in geological time and may not for billions of years more. The temperatures in these craters drop to minus 240 degrees Celsius — colder than Pluto, among the coldest places in the solar system. Cold enough to preserve ice that arrived via comet and asteroid impacts across deep time, accumulating in darkness, waiting.

NASA’s instruments have confirmed it is there. The question is how much, and how accessible.

The answer to that question will determine everything that follows. Because water on the moon is not just water. Split it with electricity — electrolysis, the same process a high school chemistry class demonstrates — and you get hydrogen and oxygen. Oxygen to breathe. Hydrogen and oxygen combined to make rocket fuel. In other words: the moon’s south pole is potentially a gas station, a life support system, and a construction material depot, all in one frozen package.

The regolith — the loose surface material — contains helium-3, deposited by the solar wind over billions of years, theoretically the ideal fuel for fusion reactors. It contains silicon, aluminium, titanium, iron. The permanently shadowed craters offer natural cooling for computing infrastructure at temperatures that would cost enormous energy to replicate on Earth.

As China’s chief lunar designer Wu Weiren put it plainly: “The permanently shadowed craters on the south pole may harbor reservoirs of ice and other volatile compounds, and they will be valuable resources for manned explorations.”

He is being diplomatic. What he means is: whoever gets there first and establishes working infrastructure controls the economics of everything that follows. The south pole is not just a scientific destination. It is the high ground of the cislunar economy.

The Map Nobody Has Drawn

Here is the problem that keeps space lawyers awake at night.

The permanently shadowed craters at the lunar south pole are finite. Shackleton Crater is the most studied candidate — its rim receives near-constant sunlight while its floor sits in permanent darkness, making it simultaneously a power source and a cold storage facility. There are others: Haworth, Nobile, Faustini, de Gerlache. Each one is a potential industrial site. Each one is a fixed, irreplaceable, non-renewable location.

There are not many of them. And multiple nations, multiple billionaires, and multiple commercial operators are all planning to be there within the same decade.

The 1967 Outer Space Treaty — the foundational document of international space law, signed by 118 countries including the United States, Russia, and China — explicitly prohibits any nation from claiming sovereignty over the moon or any celestial body. You cannot own the moon. This is settled law.

But here is what the treaty does not say: it says nothing about what happens to the resources you extract from the moon. Can you own the water ice once you have drilled it out of a crater? Can you fence off a crater as a “safety zone” while you are using it? Can you build infrastructure that effectively controls access to a resource without formally claiming sovereignty over the territory?

The United States decided unilaterally in 2015 that yes, you can own extracted resources. The Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act granted American companies property rights over materials they mine in space. Luxembourg, Japan, China, India, and Russia have all subsequently passed similar national legislation. Every major space power has now written its own rules. None of them agree with each other. None of them are binding on anyone else.

Into this legal vacuum, the United States inserted the Artemis Accords in 2020 — a framework now signed by 61 countries that codifies the American interpretation: extraction is not appropriation, safety zones around operations are legitimate, first-come-first-served is the operating principle.

China’s state media called this “akin to European colonial enclosure land-taking methods.”

Russia called it “a blatant attempt to create international space law that favors the United States.”

Both countries declined to sign. Both countries launched their own counter-framework — the International Lunar Research Station, targeting the same south pole, with its own coalition of partners including Pakistan, South Africa, Belarus, Venezuela, Egypt, Turkey, and Azerbaijan.

The world has now formally divided into two space blocs with incompatible legal frameworks, competing for the same finite resource, with no agreed rules, no enforcement mechanism, and no neutral arbiter.

Chang’e 7: The Mission Worth Understanding

The spacecraft that arrived at Wenchang on April 9th is not a simple lander. It is a system — orbiter, lander, rover, and a remarkable miniature hopping probe that will do something no spacecraft has done before: jump from the sunlit crater rim into the permanently shadowed interior, spending precious minutes in the extreme cold, analysing what it finds, then hopping back out.

The hopper carries a water molecule analyser. Its entire purpose is to confirm what orbital data strongly suggests: that there is ice in those craters, and to determine where, how much, and in what form.

Chang’e 7 is targeting the rim of Shackleton Crater — the same location that NASA’s Artemis programme has identified as its preferred landing zone for future crewed missions. The same crater. The same rim. The same permanently shadowed interior.

Beijing has not done this by accident.

China’s lunar programme has been methodical, patient, and increasingly impressive. Chang’e 1 and 2 orbited. Chang’e 3 landed. Chang’e 4 landed on the far side — the first nation ever to do so. Chang’e 5 returned samples. Chang’e 6 returned samples from the far side. Each mission built on the last. Each mission demonstrated a capability the next one required.

Chang’e 7 is the reconnaissance mission for what follows: Chang’e 8 in 2028, which will test in-situ resource utilisation — the technical phrase for learning to build things from what you find on the moon. And then, in the 2030s, the International Lunar Research Station itself, a permanent presence at the south pole, built in partnership with an expanding coalition of nations.

China is not racing to be first in the symbolic sense that Apollo was first. China is racing to be established — to have working infrastructure, confirmed resources, and operational precedent before anyone else does. As one analysis put it: the competition is no longer about reaching the moon. It is about who can stay, operate, and extract value from it.

The Safety Zone Problem

The Artemis Accords contain a provision that has received insufficient attention: the concept of “safety zones” around lunar operations.

The logic is straightforward. If you are operating a habitat, a mine, or a power system on the lunar surface, you do not want another nation’s rover driving through your infrastructure, kicking up dust that coats your solar panels, or conducting operations that interfere with yours. Safety zones are operationally sensible.

The problem is that nobody has defined how large they can be, how long they last, or what happens when two operators’ safety zones overlap.

A safety zone around a permanently shadowed crater rich in water ice is, in practice, an exclusion zone around that crater. If you establish infrastructure first and declare a safety zone, you have achieved effective control of that resource without formally claiming sovereignty — which the treaty prohibits. You have found the loophole. First mover wins. Treaty technically honoured. Resource effectively controlled.

One legal scholar described the Artemis Accords as a “diplomatic effort to paper over flaws” in the Outer Space Treaty. The accords, he wrote, work “only so long as neither China nor Russia decide to renounce the 1967 Outer Space Treaty by annexing their own slices of lunar territory.”

That is the nightmare scenario. Not a war on the moon — the logistics are too difficult and the prize too remote for that. But a situation in which two or more incompatible safety zones overlap the same crater, both sides claim legitimacy under their own legal framework, and there is no court, no treaty body, and no enforcement mechanism to resolve it.

The moon becoming the South China Sea. Limited territory, overlapping claims, inadequate governance, and great powers who have already decided they will not be bound by the other side’s rules.

The Billionaires and the Gas Station

My Energy series covered the commercial dimension in detail. But it is worth restating the stakes in plain language here.

Elon Musk has described the moon as a staging point for Mars — a refuelling depot, a construction yard, eventually a satellite factory with a mass driver to launch payloads into deep space without rocket fuel. Jeff Bezos has spoken of moving heavy industry off Earth entirely, preserving the planet while expanding human economic activity into space. Both men have the launch infrastructure, the capital ambitions, and the stated intentions to pursue lunar operations at industrial scale.

The companies pursuing orbital data centres — Starcloud, Blue Origin’s Project Sunrise, SpaceX’s orbital AI constellation — all point toward the moon as the long-term sustainable solution for computing infrastructure, for the reasons I laid out in Parts 10 and 11 of the Energy series: free cooling in permanently shadowed craters, continuous solar power on crater rims, no orbital debris problem, unlimited expansion capacity.

All of that requires water ice. Water ice for life support, for rocket fuel, for industrial processes, for the hydrogen economy of cislunar space. The water ice in those craters is not just water. It is the economic foundation of everything that follows.

Which is why the question of who gets there first, who establishes infrastructure, and who writes the safety zone rules is not an abstract legal question. It is the central economic question of the next fifty years of space development.

The Governance Gap — And the Precedent

I argued in Part 11 of the Energy series that the Antarctic Treaty provides the historical proof that “impossible” international cooperation becomes inevitable when crisis creates necessity. Sworn enemies signed a treaty in 1959. It has held for sixty-five years.

I still believe that. But I want to be honest about the timeline.

The Antarctic Treaty took the entire Cold War to mature. The governance framework for the lunar south pole will not emerge from goodwill or diplomatic inspiration. It will emerge — if it emerges — from the convergence of three things: a forcing event serious enough to make non-cooperation obviously costly, a shift in leadership that creates diplomatic space, and commercial pressure from operators who cannot get insurance or financing without legal certainty.

None of those three conditions currently exist. The forcing event has not happened. Leadership in Washington, Beijing, and Moscow is not inclined toward new agreements. And commercial operators are still in early enough stages that the legal vacuum has not yet cost them enough money to mobilise serious lobbying pressure.

We are, in other words, in the competitive sprint phase I described in the Energy series. Both blocs are racing to establish facts on the ground — or rather, facts in the regolith — before anyone sits down to negotiate rules.

That is not necessarily catastrophic. Facts on the ground can be negotiated around. The Antarctic Treaty froze territorial claims rather than resolving them. A lunar framework could do the same: acknowledge existing infrastructure, establish operational protocols, leave the sovereignty questions formally unresolved while creating rules everyone can live with.

But that requires both sides to want a framework more than they want to win. And right now, both sides think they can win.

What Happens Next

Chang’e 7 launches in August 2026. Its hopper drops into a permanently shadowed crater near Shackleton. Its water molecule analyser transmits data back to Earth.

If the data confirms accessible water ice in quantity — and most scientists expect it will — the race accelerates from a jog to a sprint. Every timeline compresses. Every investment calculus shifts. Every governance negotiation becomes more urgent and more difficult.

NASA’s VIPER rover, designed to do similar water ice prospecting, was cancelled in 2024 due to budget pressures. The United States will be watching China’s data from the sidelines. Artemis IV and V, targeting 2028, aim to put astronauts near the south pole. If those missions succeed, the US establishes human presence. If they slip — and NASA administrator Isaacman has acknowledged they might — China’s robotic infrastructure will have years of operational head start.

The moon is not going anywhere. It has been there for four and a half billion years and will be there for billions more. The craters are patient.

The nations racing toward them are not.
