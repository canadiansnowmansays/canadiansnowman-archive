The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 10: The Moon Alternative - Why Lunar Data Centers Solve the Technical Problem

December 22, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 2025

Last week, a Chinese rocket stage came dangerously close to a SpaceX satellite in low Earth orbit. The incident received minimal coverage, treated as just another near-miss in an increasingly crowded orbital environment.

But it shouldn’t be dismissed so easily. That near-collision is a warning shot.

In Part 9, I documented how the first AI data center is already operational in orbit, how Google proved modern chips can survive space radiation, and why tech giants are racing to escape Earth’s energy constraints. The hardware works. The economics are approaching viability. Multiple billionaires are betting billions on orbital infrastructure.

But there’s a problem nobody wants to talk about: we’re about to turn low Earth orbit into a debris field that could make space unusable for generations.

The projections call for millions of satellites to achieve the computing capacity the AI revolution demands. Current orbital population: roughly 6,000 Starlink satellites, with astronomers already complaining. Proposed scale: increase that by three orders of magnitude.

Every satellite is a potential collision. Every collision creates fragments. Those fragments collide with other satellites, creating more fragments. It’s called Kessler Syndrome—a cascading debris catastrophe that could trap humanity on Earth for decades.

The Kessler syndrome could be one step closer to reality (Getty Stock Images)

So here’s the question everyone’s avoiding: What if we’re solving the wrong problem?

What if, instead of putting millions of satellites in orbit, we should be building data centers on the Moon?

The Latency Objection (And Why It Doesn’t Matter)

The immediate response to lunar data centers is always the same: “But the latency!”

The physics are unforgiving. The Moon sits roughly 384,400 kilometers from Earth. Light travels at 299,792 kilometers per second. That means:

  • One-way transmission: ~1.28 seconds

  • Round-trip communication: ~2.56 seconds

Compare that to low Earth orbit satellites at 500-2,000 km altitude, where round-trip latency is 3-13 milliseconds. The Moon is 200 to 750 times slower.

For many applications, that’s a dealbreaker. You can’t do real-time video conferencing with a 2.5-second delay. Autonomous vehicles can’t wait that long for processing. High-frequency trading algorithms would be worthless. Interactive chatbots would feel broken.

But here’s what everyone misses: most of the AI energy crisis isn’t about those applications.

The massive energy demand comes from training large language models—runs that take hours, days, or weeks. GPT-4’s training reportedly took months. Whether a training iteration takes 10 milliseconds or 2.5 seconds to communicate back to Earth is completely irrelevant when the overall training run spans thousands of hours.

Batch processing workloads—scientific computing, climate modeling, drug discovery simulations, video rendering—similarly don’t care about sub-second latency. These jobs run for hours or days and check in periodically. An extra 2.5 seconds per checkpoint is noise in the overall timeline.

Data storage and disaster recovery—already being deployed on the Moon—obviously don’t require real-time access.

When you actually break down the workloads, 70-80% of the AI energy demand is latency-insensitive.

That means the Moon could handle the bulk of the computing load that’s driving the energy crisis, while minimal orbital infrastructure handles the 20-30% that genuinely needs millisecond response times.

Instead of millions of satellites creating a debris nightmare, you’d have thousands handling real-time tasks, with the heavy lifting done safely on the lunar surface.

The Data Center Already on the Moon

This isn’t theoretical. There’s already a data center operating on the lunar surface.

In February 2024, a Florida-based startup called Lonestar Data Holdings launched its “Independence” mission aboard an Intuitive Machines lunar lander. It was the world’s first commercially operated lunar software-defined data center—receiving data transmissions from Earth and storing them on the Moon.

In March 2025, they followed up with “Freedom”—the first lunar hardware data center. This mission carried actual storage hardware: Phison Pascari enterprise-grade SSDs specially modified to survive lunar conditions, along with FPGA processors for edge computing.

The Freedom data center is currently operational on the lunar surface, powered by solar panels and naturally cooled by the lunar environment. Its initial capacity is 8 terabytes—modest by terrestrial standards, but revolutionary in demonstrating that enterprise-grade storage can survive and function in the lunar environment.

Freedom Payload On Board Athena

The hardware is impressive: Phison Pascari D205V enterprise SSDs, modified to handle lunar thermal extremes, radiation exposure, and the mechanical stress of launch and landing. These aren’t consumer-grade components. They’re the same drives used in hyperscale data centers on Earth, proven reliable for mission-critical applications.

Who’s using it? The State of Florida has contracted for disaster recovery services—protecting state records against hurricanes, cyberattacks, and infrastructure failures. Six different government entities booked space on the Freedom mission. The use case isn’t science fiction—it’s pragmatic disaster recovery and data sovereignty.

“The idea of using Earth’s largest satellite as an anchor point, it’s far enough that we can have security on communications,” CEO Chris Stott told Reuters. The Moon provides what Earth can’t: complete isolation from terrestrial disasters, political instability, and network vulnerabilities.

Think about what that means operationally. A hurricane wipes out Florida’s data centers? The backups are safely 384,000 kilometers away. A cyberattack compromises every server on Earth? The lunar facility is physically unreachable. A geopolitical crisis shuts down cloud providers? The Moon continues operating.

Lonestar isn’t stopping at surface infrastructure. They’ve already contracted with satellite manufacturer Sidus Space to build six orbital data center satellites that will operate around the Moon between now and 2030. They’re planning multi-petabyte facilities at the Earth-Moon L1 Lagrange point by 2027—a gravitationally stable location where satellites can maintain position with minimal fuel expenditure.

The partnership with Flexential—a major US data center operator—adds commercial credibility. This isn’t a science experiment. It’s infrastructure deployment with paying customers and multi-year expansion plans.

This isn’t a distant vision. It’s operational infrastructure expanding right now.

And it completely sidesteps the orbital debris problem.

The Permanently Shadowed Crater Advantage

Astronauts survey a shadowed crater floor, searching for water deposits, in this illustration. Research has shown that permanently shadowed regions near the north and south lunar poles could hold water ice as well as scientific riches. Credit: Michael Carroll

On November 2, 2025—the same day Starcloud-1 launched with its Nvidia H100 to orbit—Elon Musk posted something revealing:

“Quantum computing is best done in the permanently shadowed craters on the Moon.”

He was describing a genuine physical advantage the Moon offers that Earth orbit cannot match.

Near the lunar poles, some craters never receive sunlight. The Moon’s axial tilt means these permanently shadowed regions have remained dark for billions of years. With no atmosphere to carry heat, they’ve become some of the coldest places in the solar system: -200°C to -240°C (20 to 40 Kelvin, colder than Pluto).

For computing infrastructure, this is transformative. On Earth, data centers spend roughly 40% of their energy budget on cooling. Quantum computers require dilution refrigerators consuming megawatts to reach millikelvin temperatures.

On the Moon, nature does the cooling for free. Estimates suggest lunar facilities could reduce cooling energy requirements by 85% compared to terrestrial equivalents. Unlike orbital radiators—which need enormous surface areas to reject heat into vacuum—lunar facilities can use the Moon’s thermal mass as a heat sink.

And the geometry that creates permanent shadow at crater floors means crater rims are exposed to near-constant sunlight. Solar panels on those rims generate power around the clock, transmitting it to shadowed data centers below via microwave or laser.

Continuous solar power generation paired with natural cryogenic cooling—exactly what AI infrastructure needs most.

The Moon also offers Helium-3, a rare isotope critical for ultra-low-temperature cooling systems. Earth’s entire annual supply is only a few thousand liters. The Moon has an estimated one million tonnes embedded in its regolith. Companies like Interlune have already signed agreements to deliver “thousands of liters per year” of lunar Helium-3 once extraction begins.

Blue Origin’s “Blue Alchemist” program is developing techniques to manufacture solar cells directly from lunar regolith, eliminating the need to launch panels from Earth.

The Lava Tube Solution

Billions of years ago, volcanic activity created vast underground tunnels on the Moon as lava flowed beneath the surface. When volcanic activity ceased, these tubes remained—stable, protected caverns that can span kilometers.

Some lunar lava tubes are estimated to be hundreds of meters wide—large enough to fit entire data center complexes inside.

The advantages:

  • Radiation protection: Meters of rock block cosmic rays and solar radiation that cause bit-flips and hardware degradation

  • Thermal stability: Constant temperatures year-round, unlike surface swings from -173°C to +127°C

  • Micrometeorite protection: Underground facilities inherently protected from constant bombardment

  • Expansion capacity: Unlimited space for infrastructure growth

NASA has already identified hundreds of potential lava tube locations through orbital imaging. Some are located near permanently shadowed craters—offering both natural cooling and protected environments for computing hardware.

The Realistic Hybrid Architecture

The most likely outcome isn’t “orbital wins” or “lunar wins”—it’s an intelligent division of labor:

Low Earth Orbit (Minimal satellite count):

  • Real-time AI inference for latency-critical applications

  • Interactive services (video, gaming, communications)

  • Essential low-latency tasks only

  • Thousands of satellites, not millions

  • Carefully managed debris protocols

Lunar Surface (Heavy lifting):

  • All AI training (the energy-intensive workload)

  • Batch processing and scientific computing

  • Data storage and disaster recovery

  • Quantum computing in permanently shadowed craters

  • Long-term sustainable infrastructure

This architecture solves the energy crisis without creating a debris catastrophe.

It accepts the latency trade-off for sustainability. It puts the bulk of computing where unlimited solar power and free cooling naturally exist. And it leverages the Moon’s unique advantages—resources, protection, expansion capacity—that orbit cannot match.

The strategic advantages of this division:

For orbital infrastructure, keeping the satellite count to thousands rather than millions means:

  • Manageable debris risk with current tracking and avoidance systems

  • Economically viable replacement cycles (losing 1% of 10,000 satellites annually is manageable; losing 1% of 10 million is catastrophic)

  • Sustainable long-term use of near-Earth space

  • Continued viability of orbital slots for communications, Earth observation, and scientific missions

For lunar infrastructure, concentrating energy-intensive computing on the surface means:

  • No consumption of Earth’s limited power grid capacity

  • No environmental impact from terrestrial data center construction

  • No water usage for cooling (a massive issue in water-stressed regions)

  • Infrastructure that improves with scale rather than degrading

  • Resource extraction that benefits rather than costs energy

The hybrid architecture also creates redundancy. If lunar operations face unexpected challenges, orbital capacity handles critical real-time workloads. If orbital debris becomes unmanageable, the Moon handles the bulk of computing needs. Neither system is single-point-of-failure.

It’s the difference between putting all your infrastructure in one fragile environment versus diversifying across complementary systems that each play to their strengths.

Why This Matters for Earth’s Energy Crisis

If the Moon can absorb 70-80% of AI computing demand, the pressure on terrestrial power grids drops dramatically.

Instead of needing to double or triple Earth’s electricity generation to feed AI data centers—an impossible timeline for building nuclear plants or renewable capacity—we offload the growth to lunar infrastructure.

This doesn’t eliminate the need for clean energy expansion on Earth. We still need massive buildouts of solar, wind, and nuclear for transportation electrification, industrial processes, and residential use. (That’s what Parts 2-8 of this series covered.)

But for AI specifically—the fastest-growing and most energy-intensive computing demand—the Moon offers a pressure-release valve that terrestrial infrastructure cannot match at the required scale and timeline.

It also changes the geopolitical dynamics. Energy independence becomes less critical for AI leadership if the infrastructure exists off-planet, accessible to whoever can reach it.

The Economic Case Strengthens Over Time

The initial establishment costs for lunar infrastructure are higher than orbital deployment—no question. Getting equipment to the lunar surface costs more than launching to low Earth orbit. Building the first base requires enormous investment.

But the economics flip dramatically once you’re operational:

Orbital satellites:

  • 5-6 year operational lifespan

  • Constant replacement cycle (launch new satellites, deorbit old ones)

  • Increasing collision risk as density grows

  • Insurance costs rise with debris risk

  • No infrastructure reuse (each satellite is self-contained)

  • Energy costs for thermal management remain high

Lunar facilities:

  • Multi-decade operational lifespan

  • Infrastructure investment amortizes over 30-50 years

  • Marginal cost of expansion drops dramatically (shared power, cooling, landing infrastructure)

  • Zero collision risk

  • Resource extraction reduces launch costs for local manufacturing

  • Free cooling eliminates 40% of operational expenses

The business case follows the same pattern as terrestrial infrastructure: high capital expenditure, low operational expenditure, improving economics with scale.

The hundredth lunar data center will cost a fraction of what the first one costs, because you’re leveraging shared infrastructure. The millionth orbital satellite still costs nearly as much as the first, because each one is independent.

For companies planning 20-30 year investments, the Moon becomes obviously superior once the first wave of infrastructure is in place. And Lonestar is proving that the first wave is achievable now.

The Technical Case Is Clear

When I started researching space-based AI infrastructure, I expected orbital to be the obvious answer. It’s closer, faster to deploy, and already has massive industry momentum.

But the technical case for the Moon is overwhelming:

Orbital offers:

  • Fast deployment (2025-2030)

  • Low latency (3-13 milliseconds)

  • Proven launch infrastructure

But creates:

  • Kessler Syndrome risk at scale

  • Constant replacement cycles (5-6 year lifespan)

  • Enormous cooling challenges

  • Limited expansion without debris catastrophe

The Moon offers:

  • Free cooling (85% energy savings)

  • Natural radiation shielding

  • Unlimited expansion capacity

  • Zero debris risk

  • Resource availability (He-3, regolith for manufacturing)

  • Infrastructure that lasts decades

With acceptable trade-offs:

  • Higher latency (2.5 seconds)—but 70-80% of workloads don’t care

  • Slower initial deployment—but sustainable at scale

  • Higher establishment costs—but lower marginal costs long-term

The latency objection is real but overstated. The sustainability advantages are overwhelming. And Lonestar proves it’s already working.

Orbital is the sprint—fast, immediate, necessary for proving the concept. It will happen, it’s happening now, and it matters for the next 5-10 years.

But the Moon is the marathon. If we want computing infrastructure that operates at 100 gigawatts, 300 gigawatts, or the terawatt-scale systems the industry demands—without turning near-Earth space into an unusable debris field—the Moon is where the real infrastructure will be built.

And Elon Musk has described exactly how it scales beyond anything orbital can achieve.

“The level beyond that,” Musk wrote in November 2025, “is constructing satellite factories on the Moon and using a mass driver (electromagnetic railgun) to accelerate AI satellites to lunar escape velocity without the need for rockets. That scales to >100 terawatts/year of AI.”

Think about what that means. Not 100 gigawatts like the orbital plan. Not even 1 terawatt. 100 terawatts per year—two hundred times total current U.S. electricity generation—being manufactured on the Moon and launched into optimal orbits using electromagnetic launchers that don’t require rocket fuel.

The Moon’s low gravity (1/6th of Earth’s) makes electromagnetic launch feasible in ways it never could be on Earth. A mass driver—essentially a giant electromagnetic rail gun—accelerates payloads to lunar escape velocity (2.38 km/s) using only electricity. No propellant. No rockets. Just solar power and magnetic acceleration.

Build the satellite factories in lava tubes near permanently shadowed craters. Mine lunar regolith for silicon, aluminum, and titanium. Extract water ice from craters for hydrogen fuel cells. Manufacture solar panels, computing hardware, and structural components on-site. Launch completed satellites using the mass driver. Close the entire industrial loop without depending on Earth-based supply chains.

That’s not science fiction. That’s industrial engineering applied to a low-gravity, high-resource environment with unlimited solar energy.

And Now It’s Official U.S. Policy

On December 18, 2025—just weeks after Musk described the 100 terawatt lunar manufacturing vision—President Trump signed an executive order titled “Ensuring American Space Superiority” that commits the United States to:

  • Return astronauts to the Moon by 2028 through the Artemis program

  • Establish initial elements of a permanent lunar outpost by 2030

  • Deploy nuclear reactors on the Moon and in orbit, with a lunar surface reactor ready for launch by 2030

  • Attract at least $50 billion of additional investment in American space markets by 2028

The order explicitly states the goal is to “lay the foundations for lunar economic development” and establish “a sustained American presence in space.” This isn’t exploration for exploration’s sake. It’s industrial infrastructure.

Nuclear power on the lunar surface solves one of the biggest technical challenges: providing continuous energy during the 14-day lunar night when solar panels are useless. NASA and the Department of Energy have been developing kilopower fission reactors specifically for lunar deployment, capable of generating kilowatts continuously regardless of sunlight.

The 2028-2030 timeline is aggressive—many experts consider it unlikely given current Starship delays and lander development challenges. Former NASA Administrator Jim Bridenstine told the Senate Commerce Committee in September 2025: “It is highly unlikely that we will land on the moon before China. We don’t have a landing system for the moon.”

But the executive order transforms lunar infrastructure from “interesting research project” to national policy priority with specific deadlines and accountability.

The timing is remarkable. Within the same month, we saw:

  • Lonestar’s Freedom mission proving lunar data operations work

  • Musk describing the 100 terawatt manufacturing vision

  • Trump committing the U.S. government to permanent lunar infrastructure by 2030

Whether or not the 2028 landing happens on schedule, the direction is clear: the Moon is no longer science fiction. It’s now official policy for industrial development.

The technical case is clear. The question isn’t whether the Moon is technically superior—it obviously is for 70-80% of AI workloads.

The question is whether we can overcome the geopolitical obstacles to make it happen.
