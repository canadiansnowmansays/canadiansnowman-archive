Trump's Foreign Policy

Episode 3: Alliance Fractures in Real Time

June 19, 2025

Stephen Lawton June 19, 2025

The image that will define Trump's second presidency isn't him standing victoriously at a podium, but sitting alone at the Resolute Desk, staring at a globe he no longer understands—while allies build new relationships without him. The Ukraine-Iran whiplash hasn't just damaged American credibility; it's accelerated a quiet realignment as traditional partners conclude they can no longer depend on American consistency or judgment.

Bottom Line Up Front: Traditional allies are quietly building alternative arrangements as Trump's erratic pivoting from Ukraine abandonment to Iran escalation confirms their worst fears about American reliability. These "hedging strategies" may outlast Trump's presidency, fundamentally altering the global order America once led.

The G7 Debacle: When Allies Start Eye-Rolling

The June 2025 G7 summit in Alberta provided a perfect microcosm of alliance deterioration under Trump's leadership. Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni was caught on camera dramatically rolling her eyes during a whispered conversation with French President Emmanuel Macron, while Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney cut off questions to Trump ahead of scheduled meetings. These aren't diplomatic niceties—they're allies losing patience with American unreliability.

Most telling was Trump's abrupt departure from the summit early to focus on the Israel-Iran conflict, leaving Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese's first in-person meeting with Trump canceled. When the leader of the free world abandons multilateral commitments for crisis management, it signals that alliance structures have become secondary to reactive decision-making.

Trump's bizarre suggestion that Canada become America's 51st state initially seemed like typical Trumpian bombast. But the response revealed something deeper: "this led to a wave of nationalist pride in the northern neighbour and an abrupt shift from the Conservative Party to the Liberal Party." When your closest ally and largest trading partner responds to your "joke" by moving away from your preferred political party, you've crossed a line from eccentric to dangerous.

The G7 incidents weren't isolated diplomatic moments—they were visible manifestations of deeper frustration with American decision-making. Meloni's "eye roll of the century" occurred as world leaders gathered specifically to help defuse global pressure points, only to watch Trump abandon the summit for crisis management elsewhere. Carney cutting off questions to Trump with "I'm going to exercise my role if you will as G7 chair" represented the kind of diplomatic intervention necessary when American leadership becomes unreliable.

This matters because Canada represents the easiest relationship in American foreign policy—shared values, integrated economies, common security interests. If Trump can't maintain stable relations with Canada, what does that signal to allies facing genuine security threats?

European Hedging: Building Bridges Away from America

European responses have been more subtle but potentially more consequential. While publicly maintaining diplomatic courtesy, European capitals are accelerating plans for "strategic autonomy" that were previously theoretical. The Ukraine abandonment provided the catalyst; the Iran escalation threat provided the confirmation that America under Trump operates on emotional rather than strategic logic.

French President Macron's recent comments about European defense capabilities "independent of American guarantees" weren't anti-American posturing—they were insurance policies. German officials speak privately about developing "Plan B" frameworks for scenarios where American security guarantees prove unreliable. Even traditionally Atlanticist Britain is exploring deeper defense cooperation with European partners as a hedge against American fickleness.

The European Union's announcement of expanded defense procurement cooperation, accelerated after Trump's return, represents more than bureaucratic efficiency. It's the infrastructure for a world where European security doesn't depend on American reliability. These institutional changes will survive Trump's presidency, creating permanent alternatives to American leadership.

The Pacific Pivot That Isn't: Asian Allies Read the Room

The most consequential alliance shifts may be occurring in the Pacific, where Trump's Ukraine abandonment and Iran escalation threats have terrified allies facing the China challenge. If America will pressure Ukraine to surrender to Russia and potentially stumble into a Middle East war, what does that mean for Taiwan, South Korea, or Japan?

Japanese officials privately express concern that Trump's transactional approach treats alliance commitments as negotiable rather than foundational. The speed of his pivot from Ukraine to Iran suggests decisions driven by domestic political calculations rather than strategic consistency—a terrifying prospect for allies who need reliable security guarantees against Chinese pressure.

South Korea faces a particularly acute dilemma: Trump's willingness to abandon Ukraine while threatening Iran suggests American commitments vary based on Trump's personal interests rather than objective strategic importance. This uncertainty is driving quiet conversations about developing independent deterrent capabilities that don't require American approval or support.

Taiwan watches the Ukraine precedent with obvious alarm. If Trump will pressure a democratic ally under attack to negotiate with its aggressor, what happens when China increases pressure on Taiwan? The Iran escalation threat only compounds these concerns by suggesting American attention can be diverted from the Pacific theater by Middle East drama.

The Middle East Calculation: When American Support Becomes Liability

Even Israel, ostensibly the beneficiary of Trump's Iran threats, faces complicated alliance dynamics. Trump's maximalist rhetoric followed by equivocation ("nobody knows what I'm going to do") creates uncertainty about American follow-through that complicates Israeli strategic planning. If Trump backs down from his Iran threats after escalating rhetoric, Israel faces the consequences of American inconsistency.

Arab allies who watched Trump abandon Ukraine wonder whether American security guarantees have any meaning beyond Trump's personal whims. The speed of his pivot to potential Iran military action, without apparent consultation with regional partners, suggests decisions made in Washington without regard for broader regional consequences.

This pattern of American unreliability is driving Middle Eastern allies toward alternative arrangements. Saudi Arabia's recent defense cooperation agreements with European partners represent hedging against American fickleness. Even UAE officials speak privately about developing security relationships that don't depend on American consistency.

The Institutional Damage: Brain Drain and Credibility Collapse

Ambassador Bridget Brink's resignation represents more than individual conscience—it's symptomatic of institutional damage that will take decades to repair. As I noted in my original analysis, Brink "is one of the highest-ranking career diplomats to leave the State Department since President Donald Trump took office" and "joins other departing veteran officials with decades of experience."

This brain drain matters because American diplomacy depends on relationships built over years by professional diplomats who understand cultural nuances and historical contexts. When career professionals resign rather than implement policies they consider destructive, America loses institutional memory and relationship capital that can't be quickly replaced.

Foreign counterparts notice when America's most experienced diplomats abandon their posts. They understand that such resignations signal fundamental policy dysfunction. This reputational damage extends beyond Trump's presidency because foreign officials will remember how American institutions collapsed under political pressure.

The China Opportunity: Strategic Patience Pays Dividends

Xi Jinping must be amazed at his good fortune. America's abandonment of Ukraine removed a major irritant in China-Russia relations while demonstrating Western inconsistency. Trump's threat to escalate against Iran provides perfect distraction from Chinese activities in the South China Sea and Taiwan Strait.

Chinese officials privately express satisfaction that America has returned to the Middle East focus that previously enabled Chinese expansion in the Pacific. Every American dollar and attention span devoted to potential Iran conflict is a dollar and attention span not focused on Chinese challenges to the regional order.

More strategically, Trump's alliance management validates Chinese arguments about American unreliability. When China tells Asian partners they can't depend on distant American guarantees, they can point to Ukraine abandonment and Iran escalation threats as evidence of American fickleness and poor judgment.

The Putin Masterclass: Dividing Western Unity

Vladimir Putin has achieved something remarkable: he's watching America voluntarily abandon the alliance structures that contained Russian expansion for decades. The Ukraine abandonment removed immediate military pressure; the Iran escalation threat divides American attention and potentially fractures Middle East relationships.

Putin's strategic patience has been rewarded beyond his wildest expectations. Rather than confronting united Western opposition, he watches America stumble from crisis to crisis while allies quietly build alternatives to American leadership. Every day Trump remains focused on Iran rather than Ukraine is another day for Russian consolidation of territorial gains.

The recent U.S.-Russia talks in Saudi Arabia, excluding Ukraine despite the war being fought on Ukrainian territory, represent Putin's diplomatic victory. Russia has been rehabilitated as a negotiating partner while Ukraine has been marginalized as an obstacle to peace—exactly the outcome Putin sought through three years of warfare.

Economic Dimensions: Trade Wars and Alliance Erosion

Trump's simultaneous pursuit of trade wars, alliance abandonment, and military escalation creates compounding economic pressures that allies notice. His tariff policies that "were a significant factor" in the U.S. economy shrinking "0.3% in the first three months of this year" signal economic weakness at precisely the moment when allies need American strength.

European officials privately worry that American economic decline, combined with strategic unreliability, creates a vacuum that authoritarian powers will fill. The combination of trade wars with allies and potential military escalation in Iran suggests American leadership that maximizes costs while minimizing benefits for partnership.

This economic dimension matters because alliance structures depend on mutual benefit. When America simultaneously imposes economic costs through tariffs and security risks through erratic military policy, the value proposition of American alliance becomes questionable.

The Authoritarian Axis: Cooperation Through American Weakness

The most concerning development may be how American unreliability enables deeper cooperation between authoritarian powers. Russia and China, despite historical tensions, find common ground in exploiting American strategic confusion. Iran, despite sectarian differences with Russia and ideological differences with China, benefits from American distraction and alliance fractures.

This emerging axis doesn't require formal alliance structures—it simply requires continued American strategic dysfunction. Every day America focuses on Iran rather than Ukraine helps Russia. Every day America threatens allies with trade wars while threatening enemies with military action helps China. Every day American attention fragments across multiple crisis theaters without strategic coherence helps all three.

Looking Forward: The Permanent Damage

The tragedy of current alliance deterioration is its permanence. Institutional changes Europeans are making for "strategic autonomy" won't be reversed when Trump leaves office. Asian allies developing independent capabilities won't abandon them when American leadership theoretically returns. Middle Eastern partners building alternative security arrangements won't automatically revert to American dependence.

UC Berkeley's M. Steven Fish warned that "it's hard to see the United States ever recovering its power and prestige" because alliance structures took decades to build but can be destroyed much more quickly. The Ukraine-Iran sequence accelerates this destruction by confirming allied fears about American judgment and reliability.

Future American presidents will inherit a world where allies have alternatives to American leadership and adversaries have learned to exploit American inconsistency. The question isn't whether Trump's approach serves American interests—the evidence is overwhelming that it doesn't. The question is whether the damage can ever be fully repaired.
