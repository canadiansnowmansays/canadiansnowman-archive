The Sham Grand Jury

When “Indict First, Investigate Second” Becomes DOJ Policy

December 5, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays
December 5, 2025

Before Judge Cameron McGowan Currie dismissed the Comey and James prosecutions for illegal appointments, another federal judge had already documented something even more disturbing: the grand jury process itself was corrupt.

The smoking gun wasn’t just that Lindsey Halligan was illegally appointed. It was what she did once she got the job.

The Admission That Changes Everything

Michael S. Nachmanoff, a U.S. District Court judge for the Eastern District of Virginia

November 19, 2025. U.S. District Judge Michael Nachmanoff is hearing motions in the Comey case. He asks a simple question: Did the grand jury see the final indictment before it was filed?

Assistant U.S. Attorney Tyler Lemons hesitates. He confers with Halligan. Then the admission:

“The full grand jury never reviewed the indictment it handed up against former FBI Director James Comey.”

Let that sink in. The document that accused a former FBI Director of federal crimes—the indictment that triggered months of legal proceedings and national headlines—was never actually approved by the grand jury that supposedly issued it.

Instead, Halligan simply brought an altered version to a magistrate’s courtroom for the grand jury foreperson to sign. Just two people saw the final document: the foreperson and one other grand juror.

As Judge Nachmanoff pressed for clarity, Lemons confirmed: “That is my understanding.”

Comey’s attorney, Michael Dreeben, immediately seized on the revelation: “There is no indictment.”

How It Happened: The Shell Game

Here’s what we now know about the grand jury process:

September 25, 2025: Halligan presents three proposed counts to the grand jury. The grand jurors hear the evidence and vote.

The Result: The grand jury rejects one count. They approve only two charges.

What Should Have Happened: Halligan should have brought the rejected indictment back to the full grand jury with the count removed, so they could review and approve the modified version.

What Actually Happened: Halligan simply edited the document herself, removed the rejected count, and took the altered indictment to the magistrate’s courtroom where only the foreperson and one other juror signed it.

The full grand jury—the 23 citizens empowered to decide whether criminal charges are warranted—never saw what they supposedly approved.

Think about this procedure. It’s like a jury returning a verdict of “guilty on counts 1 and 3, but not guilty on count 2,” and then the prosecutor rewriting the verdict form to say “guilty on all counts” without telling the jury.

“Indict First, Investigate Second”

The grand jury revelation was shocking, but it wasn’t the only problem. Days before this admission, U.S. Magistrate Judge William Fitzpatrick had already issued a blistering order documenting what he called “a disturbing pattern of profound investigative missteps.”

On November 6, Judge Fitzpatrick took the extraordinary step of ordering prosecutors to produce all grand jury materials and evidence seized during the investigation. His reason? He was concerned about “prosecutorial misconduct.”

In court, Fitzpatrick captured the problem in one devastating phrase:

“We’re in a bit of a feeling of indict first, investigate second.”

Let’s unpack what he found:

The Stale Warrants

The evidence against Comey came from four search warrants executed in 2019 and 2020—targeting Daniel Richman, a Columbia Law School professor and Comey’s friend. Those warrants were obtained more than five years before Halligan used them to indict Comey.

In federal law, warrants can become “stale” if too much time passes between when they’re executed and when the evidence is used. Why? Because the justification for the original search may no longer be valid.

Judge Fitzpatrick raised exactly this concern. If the government obtained Richman’s communications in 2019 to investigate one matter, can they use those same materials in 2025 to indict someone else for something different?

More troubling: The government had possessed these materials for over five years but never resolved questions about attorney-client privilege. Richman had served as Comey’s attorney. Some communications were clearly privileged. Yet prosecutors never sorted through them properly until after the indictment.

The Compromised Witness

Fitzpatrick also documented concerns about whether prosecutors used “a compromised witness” in their presentation to the grand jury. While the details remain under seal, the implication is clear: the witness’s credibility was questionable, and the grand jurors may not have been told about the problems.

This matters enormously. Grand juries rely on prosecutors to present evidence fairly. If prosecutors knowingly put on a witness with credibility issues without disclosing those problems, it’s prosecutorial misconduct.

The Missing Context

Perhaps most damningly, there’s evidence that Halligan misled the grand jury about the law and the evidence.

The indictment accuses Comey of lying during a September 30, 2020 Senate Judiciary Committee hearing when he denied authorizing FBI leaks. The government claims he actually did authorize leaks through Daniel Richman.

But here’s the problem: When Senator Ted Cruz asked Comey about “authorizing FBI personnel to be an anonymous source,” Cruz appeared to be asking about Andrew McCabe—the FBI Deputy Director who was known to have leaked to the press.

Comey’s denial was about McCabe, not Richman. And Richman wasn’t “FBI personnel”—he was an outside attorney who also happened to be a “special government employee” on a separate matter.

Did Halligan explain this context to the grand jury? Did she tell them the question was ambiguous? Did she present Comey’s full testimony, or just the snippet that sounded like a lie?

We don’t know. But Judge Fitzpatrick’s order suggests he has serious doubts about whether the grand jury got the full picture.

Why Grand Juries Matter

The Fifth Amendment to the Constitution requires that “no person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury.”

This isn’t a technicality. The grand jury is supposed to be a check on prosecutorial power—a panel of ordinary citizens who can reject charges if the evidence is insufficient or the prosecution is abusive.

But that check only works if prosecutors present evidence fairly and follow proper procedures.

What happened here? The grand jury rejected one of Halligan’s charges. That’s the system working—citizens saying “no, that doesn’t meet the standard.”

Halligan’s response? Bypass them. Edit the indictment. Get just two jurors to sign off instead of all 23.

This isn’t how the American justice system is supposed to work.

The Inevitable Collapse

Once these procedural violations came to light, dismissal was inevitable—even before the illegal appointment issue.

Defense attorney Michael Dreeben told Judge Nachmanoff the grand jury problems were “grounds for dismissing the case.” He argued that without a valid indictment approved by the grand jury, “there is no indictment.”

Even if Halligan had been properly appointed, the case was likely doomed by the procedural irregularities.

But she wasn’t properly appointed. And Judge Currie didn’t need to reach the grand jury issues because the appointments problem was dispositive.

Still, Judge Fitzpatrick’s findings matter enormously. They validate everything Comey’s defense team argued about this being a political prosecution:

  • The rush: Halligan indicted Comey four days after being appointed, just before the statute of limitations expired. There wasn’t time for proper investigation.

  • The end-run around career prosecutors: When the prosecutors who knew the case said there wasn’t enough evidence, Halligan was brought in to indict anyway.

  • The result-oriented process: The investigation wasn’t designed to find truth. It was designed to indict a target before time ran out.

“Indict first, investigate second” isn’t hyperbole. It’s a precise description of what happened.

The Letitia James Case: Same Pattern

While the grand jury revelations focused on Comey, similar problems plagued the James prosecution.

In October, ABC News reported that prosecutors who investigated James found evidence that “would appear to undercut some of the allegations in the indictment.” Career prosecutors concluded any financial benefit James received from the allegedly false mortgage statements amounted to roughly $800—not exactly bank fraud of the century.

Those prosecutors recommended against charges. They were overruled.

An internal DOJ memo summarizing the case—which has never been officially released—documented the weakness of the evidence. Yet Halligan indicted anyway.

And like Comey, the timing was suspicious. The mortgage transaction in question occurred in 2005—twenty years ago. The alleged false statements were from 2020. Why investigate this now? Why bring charges in October 2025?

Because Donald Trump posted on Truth Social on September 20 demanding prosecution, and nine days later, she was indicted.

The Message to Prosecutors

For career federal prosecutors watching this unfold, the message is chilling:

Do your job professionally, and you’ll be fired. Lindsey Halligan replaced Erik Siebert after Siebert refused to bring charges. Elizabeth Yusi, another prosecutor who found no probable cause against James, was also pushed out—and accused of improperly sharing case files after she tried to document the political pressure.

Bring cases you know are weak, and you’ll be rewarded. Halligan had no prosecutorial experience but got appointed U.S. Attorney because she was willing to indict Trump’s enemies on thin evidence.

Cut corners, and you’ll be protected. Even after judges documented profound misconduct, the Justice Department defended Halligan’s actions and promised to appeal.

This is how you corrupt an institution. Not all at once, but case by case. Prosecutor by prosecutor. Until the professionals are gone and only the loyalists remain.

The Evidence of Political Motivation

The timeline remains the most damning evidence:

September 20: Trump posts demanding prosecutions
September 21: Siebert forced out
September 22: Halligan appointed
September 25: Comey indicted (4 days later)
October 9: James indicted (17 days later)

Career prosecutors spent months investigating and recommended against charges in both cases.

Halligan spent days and obtained indictments.

The difference? Career prosecutors follow the evidence. Political prosecutors follow orders.

As Judge Fitzpatrick observed, the process was backwards. In a normal prosecution, you investigate first, then indict. Here, the decision to indict came first, and the investigation was scrambled together to justify it.

What This Means for Bolton

John Bolton is still facing 18 Espionage Act counts for sharing diary entries with family members. His case wasn’t brought by Halligan—it’s being prosecuted in Maryland by different U.S. Attorneys.

But are those prosecutors watching what happened in Virginia?

They just saw two political prosecutions collapse due to procedural irregularities and illegal appointments. They saw judges document “profound investigative missteps” and question whether the cases were brought vindictively.

Will they reconsider? Or will they press forward, hoping Maryland judges are more accommodating?

Bolton’s attorneys are surely preparing motions highlighting the Comey and James cases as evidence of vindictive prosecution. They’ll point to Trump’s public statements demanding Bolton be prosecuted. They’ll note that Biden’s DOJ investigated and closed the case, only to have Trump reopen it.

The question is whether Maryland judges will follow Virginia’s lead.

The Broader Implications

The Comey and James cases reveal something profound about this administration’s approach to law enforcement:

They care more about punishing enemies than following the law.

A legitimate prosecution might stumble on technical issues, but it wouldn’t have this many fundamental problems:

  • Illegally appointed prosecutor

  • Grand jury never seeing the final indictment

  • Stale warrants

  • Compromised witnesses

  • Misleading presentations to the grand jury

  • Rushed timeline

  • Career prosecutors overruled

  • President publicly demanding the prosecutions

Any one of these might be explained. All of them together? That’s not a prosecution. It’s a hit job that wasn’t even competently executed.

Conclusion: The System Found Them Out

Critics said I was being alarmist when I began documenting the enemies list. They said our institutions would catch any improper prosecutions.

They were right—but only partially.

Yes, judges caught the problems. Yes, the cases were dismissed. Yes, the system worked.

But it took two federal judges issuing extraordinary orders documenting profound misconduct. It took defense attorneys with the resources to fight back. It took a special visiting judge willing to rule against the Trump administration.

Most defendants don’t get that. Most targets of prosecutorial misconduct don’t have Patrick Fitzgerald and Michael Dreeben as their attorneys. Most cases don’t get this level of judicial scrutiny.

The system caught these cases because they were so high-profile and so blatantly improper that judges couldn’t ignore the problems.

What about the next case? The one that’s slightly more careful? The one where the prosecutor follows procedures just closely enough to avoid reversal?

The grand jury revelations show us what happens when political prosecution becomes DOJ policy: corners get cut, procedures get bypassed, and the rush to indict overrides the duty to seek justice.

Comey and James got lucky. The prosecutors were sloppy enough to get caught.

But the Trump administration is learning. Next time, they might be more careful. Next time, they might appoint prosecutors properly and make sure the grand jury signs off on the final indictment.

And then what stops them?
