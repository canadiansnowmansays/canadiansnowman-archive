Working Around the Clock on What, Exactly?

The GOP’s 15-Year Healthcare Lie, From “Two Weeks” to “Concepts of a Plan”

November 13, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays

On October 6th, 2025, as the government shutdown dragged into its second week, Speaker Mike Johnson looked directly into the camera with the practiced sincerity of a man who knows he’s lying:

“Let me look right into the camera and tell you very clearly. Republicans are the ones concerned about health care. Republicans are the party working around the clock every day to fix health care. We’re not - it’s not talking points for us. We’ve done it.”

Five weeks later, on November 12th - 26 minutes after the House voted to end the shutdown - that same Mike Johnson told reporters that before Republicans would vote on extending ACA subsidies, they would “demand a lot of reforms before anything like that was ever possible.”

So which is it, Speaker Johnson? Are Republicans “working around the clock” to fix healthcare? Or are they demanding “reforms” as preconditions to prevent 24 million Americans from facing 114% premium increases in 50 days?

Let’s look at the actual record of Republicans “working around the clock” on healthcare.

The Scorecard: 15 Years of GOP Healthcare “Work”

Legislation passed to improve healthcare: Zero

Times voted to destroy the Affordable Care Act: 70+

Alternative healthcare plans presented: Zero

“Two weeks” until Trump’s plan arrives: Still waiting (going on 7+ years)

Trump’s actual plan: “Concepts of a plan”

That’s what Republicans “working around the clock” looks like. Seventy attempts to destroy. Zero attempts to build. And when pressed for an alternative after 15 years, the best the Republican presidential nominee could offer was “concepts.”

The “Two Weeks” Theory of Republican Healthcare

Trump loves the phrase “two weeks.” It’s his go-to timeline for things that will never happen.

Healthcare plan? “Two weeks.”

Ukraine peace deal? “Two weeks” (said repeatedly since 2025).

Deal with Putin? “Two weeks.”

Infrastructure plan? “Two weeks.”

Tax returns? “Two weeks” (spoiler: took years and a Supreme Court case).

It’s the perfect timeline - far enough away to sound credible, close enough that people might wait, and vague enough that when two weeks pass with nothing, he can just say “two weeks” again.

On healthcare specifically, Trump has been promising a plan in “two weeks” since 2017. That’s over 200 “two weeks” cycles. Still waiting.

“Concepts of a Plan”

But the moment that truly captured the GOP’s healthcare strategy came during the September 2024 presidential debate. Moderator Linsey Davis asked Trump directly about his healthcare plan.

His answer: “I have concepts of a plan.”

Not “a plan.” Not “we’re working on the details.” Not even “two weeks.”

Concepts. Of. A. Plan.

After nine years of promising to repeal and replace Obamacare. After claiming he had “something terrific” ready to go. After years of “two weeks” promises. When finally pressed on national television for specifics, the best he could offer was that he had... concepts.

Think about what that means. In 2017, Republicans controlled the House, Senate, and White House. They held 70+ votes to repeal the ACA. They passed a tax cut adding trillions to the deficit. But they couldn’t pass a healthcare plan.

Why? Because they never had one. They had “concepts.”

What Republicans Actually Did on Healthcare

While Johnson claims they’re “working around the clock,” here’s the actual record:

1. Tried to destroy the ACA 70+ times Between 2011 and 2017, Republicans held over 70 votes to repeal or undermine the Affordable Care Act. Not to improve it. Not to fix its problems. To destroy it.

With no replacement plan.

2. Passed exactly zero healthcare improvements In the entire modern history of the Republican Party, they have not passed a single piece of legislation to expand healthcare coverage or make it more affordable.

The one exception: Medicare Part D in 2003, which was a giveaway to pharmaceutical companies that prohibited Medicare from negotiating drug prices. That’s not improving healthcare - that’s protecting corporate profits.

3. Blocked Democrats’ removal of medical debt from credit reports In January 2025, the Biden administration finalized a rule that would have removed $49 billion in medical debt from 15 million Americans’ credit reports. Medical debt isn’t a choice - it’s what happens when you get sick.

The Trump administration declined to defend the rule in court. A federal judge vacated it in July 2025. Then Trump’s administration issued guidance threatening state laws that tried to protect consumers from medical debt reporting.

Democrats tried to protect consumers by recognizing that your ability to pay outrageous medical bills isn’t an indicator of creditworthiness - it’s an indicator of whether you got sick.

Republicans blocked it.

Why? Because their donors asked them to. Banks and credit agencies wanted that data. So Republicans gave it to them, making it harder for Americans who faced medical emergencies to get loans, rent apartments, or buy cars.

That’s what Republicans “working around the clock” looks like - blocking efforts to make healthcare affordability better, not making it better themselves.

4. Blocked every Democratic healthcare expansion

  • Opposed Medicaid expansion (costing millions of Americans coverage)

  • Opposed ACA subsidies (setting up the current crisis)

  • Opposed drug price negotiations

  • Opposed insulin price caps

  • Opposed every attempt to make healthcare more affordable

The “Skinny Repeal” Debacle

In July 2017, Republicans had their chance. They controlled everything. They’d been promising “repeal and replace” for seven years.

What did they offer? The “skinny repeal” - a bill that would:

  • Strip coverage from 16 million Americans

  • Increase premiums by 20%

  • Eliminate protections for pre-existing conditions

  • Provide... nothing in return

No replacement. No alternative. Just destruction.

It failed when John McCain walked onto the Senate floor at 1:30 AM and gave a thumbs down. Not because he loved Obamacare, but because Republicans literally had no plan for what came after.

Trump responded by attacking McCain for years, even after his death. But McCain was right: You can’t “repeal and replace” when you only have concepts of a plan.

Johnson’s “Reforms”

So when Mike Johnson says Republicans would “demand a lot of reforms before anything like that was ever possible,” what does he mean?

Based on 15 years of history, “reforms” means:

  • Requirements that make coverage harder to get

  • Work requirements that kick people off Medicaid

  • Cuts to benefits that save money by denying care

  • “Free market solutions” that mean higher costs and less coverage

“Reforms” is Republican-speak for “ways to kill this without technically voting against it.”

It’s the same strategy they used on the ACA repeal. They couldn’t admit they had no alternative, so they claimed they were “working on reforms.” Those reforms never materialized, because reforms require actual policy work, and Republicans have spent 15 years proving they have no interest in that work.

The October Lie Exposed by the November Admission

On October 6th, during the shutdown, Johnson looked into the camera and declared Republicans were “working around the clock” on healthcare. “We’ve done it,” he said.

On November 12th - 26 minutes after the House voted to end that shutdown - he admitted Republicans would demand “reforms” before any ACA vote, making it impossible.

Those five weeks between October 6th and November 12th? That’s what “working around the clock” produced: An admission that any vote would be impossible.

Johnson Doubles Down on the Lie

Hours after the shutdown ended, Johnson posted on X:

“Democrats destroyed American health care. The so-called ‘Affordable Care Act’ was never affordable... Republicans are fixing it — restoring choice, lowering costs, and protecting care for those who truly need it.”

Read that again. “Republicans are fixing it.”

This is the same Johnson who admitted 26 minutes after the House vote that any ACA action would require “reforms” making it “impossible.”

The same Johnson who claimed in October they were “working around the clock,” then five weeks later proved they did nothing.

The same Johnson who now claims Republicans are “lowering costs” while enabling 24 million Americans to face 114% premium increases.

“Fixing it” with what, exactly? Trump’s “concepts of a plan”? The 70+ votes to destroy with zero alternatives? The medical debt protections you blocked?

Johnson’s tweet isn’t leadership. It’s gaslighting. He’s blaming Democrats for healthcare costs while actively making them worse, claiming Republicans are fixing what they spent 15 years trying to destroy, and pretending “impossible” reforms equal a solution.

The Premium Time Bomb

In 50 days, 24 million Americans will face premium increases averaging 114%. That’s $816 more per year for families already struggling with affordability.

Republicans knew this was coming. Democrats warned them repeatedly. The Congressional Budget Office documented it. Healthcare policy experts explained it.

Trump’s response during the shutdown? Attend football games and post about his gold ballroom while claiming the Walmart Thanksgiving basket proved affordability was solved.

Johnson’s response in October? Claim Republicans are “working around the clock” on healthcare.

Johnson’s response in November? Admit any healthcare vote would require “reforms” making it impossible.

Republican leadership’s response? Nothing. Just let 24 million Americans face the catastrophe while claiming they’re “working around the clock.”

What “Working Around the Clock” Actually Means

If Republicans are truly “working around the clock” on healthcare, here’s what that work has produced:

Year 1 (2011): Vote to repeal ACA. No alternative presented.

Year 2 (2012): Vote to repeal ACA. No alternative presented.

Year 3 (2013): Vote to repeal ACA. No alternative presented.

Year 4 (2014): Vote to repeal ACA. No alternative presented.

Year 5 (2015): Vote to repeal ACA. No alternative presented.

Year 6 (2016): Vote to repeal ACA. Promise replacement is coming. Campaign on “repeal and replace.”

Year 7 (2017): Control government. Fail to repeal ACA. Claim they’re still working on replacement. Trump promises plan in “two weeks.”

Year 8 (2018): Trump promises plan in “two weeks.” Multiple times. Plan never arrives.

Year 9 (2019): Trump promises plan in “two weeks.” Multiple times. Plan never arrives.

Year 10 (2020): Trump promises plan in “two weeks.” Multiple times. Plan never arrives. COVID hits. Republicans oppose expanded healthcare coverage during a pandemic.

Year 11 (2021): Democrats expand ACA subsidies. Republicans oppose it.

Year 12 (2022): Democrats cap insulin at $35. Republicans oppose it.

Year 13 (2023): Democrats allow Medicare to negotiate drug prices. Republicans oppose it.

Year 14 (2024): Trump admits he has “concepts of a plan” after nine years of promises.

Year 15 (2025): Johnson claims Republicans are “working around the clock.” Five weeks later, admits any vote would require impossible “reforms.” 24 million Americans face 114% premium increases.

That’s 15 years of “work.” Seventy votes to destroy. Zero alternatives. And when pressed, “concepts of a plan.”

Trump’s “Concepts” Come Home to Roost

When Trump admitted he had “concepts of a plan” in that debate, he accidentally told the truth about Republican healthcare policy. Because “concepts” is all they’ve ever had.

Concepts of letting the free market solve it. (It didn’t.)

Concepts of health savings accounts. (They benefit the wealthy, not working families.)

Concepts of selling insurance across state lines. (Would eliminate consumer protections.)

Concepts of tax credits. (Far less effective than subsidies.)

But concepts don’t cover pre-existing conditions. Concepts don’t lower premiums. Concepts don’t keep rural hospitals open. Concepts don’t help diabetics afford insulin.

And in 50 days, 24 million Americans will learn the hard way that you can’t pay insurance premiums with concepts.

The Question That Matters

Mike Johnson said in October that Republicans are “working around the clock” on healthcare.

In November, he admitted any vote would require “reforms” making it impossible.

So here’s my question: Working on what, exactly?

What bill have you introduced? What reforms have you proposed? What alternative plan have you presented after 15 years of promising one?

You claimed you were “working around the clock” but in 50 days, 24 million Americans face 114% premium increases and you’ve done nothing to stop it.

You claimed you were “working around the clock” but Trump still only has “concepts of a plan” after nearly a decade of promises.

You claimed you were “working around the clock” but your only healthcare legislation in 15 years was blocking protections that would have removed medical debt from credit reports.

You claimed you were “working around the clock” in October, then admitted in November that any vote would require “reforms” making it “impossible.”

That’s not working around the clock. That’s running out the clock.

Democrats Put It On the GOP’s Record

House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries made it clear where responsibility now lies. On the House floor Wednesday, he laid out the stakes:

“There are only two ways this fight will end. Either Republicans finally decide to extend the Affordable Care Act tax credits this year. Or the American people will throw Republicans out of their jobs next year and end the speakership of Donald J. Trump once and for all.”

That’s not a threat. That’s a promise to make healthcare the defining issue of 2026.

Jeffries rejected the idea that Democrats would accept a promise of a future vote: “I don’t think that the House Democratic Caucus is prepared to support a promise, a wink and a prayer from folks who have been devastating the health care of the American people for years.”

He called out what everyone knows: Republicans “aren’t operating in good faith as it relates to doing anything to actually make health care more affordable.”

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer amplified the message. After Republicans rejected every Democratic proposal during the shutdown, Schumer made the choice crystal clear: “Leader Thune just needs to add a clean, one-year extension of the [Obamacare] tax credits to the CR so that we can immediately address rising healthcare costs.”

Republicans refused. Now they own what comes next.

Representative Jim McGovern summed up the Democratic fury: “What the hell is wrong with these people? How can you not extend these tax benefits for people who are in such desperate need?”

The answer? Because Republicans have spent 15 years proving they have no interest in making healthcare affordable. They only know how to destroy, not build. They only have “concepts,” not plans.

And given their track record - 70+ votes to destroy the ACA, zero alternatives, zero improvements, blocking medical debt protections, admitting any future vote would be “impossible” - Americans should be very, very worried about what Republicans do with the healthcare crisis they just enabled.

Don’t Forget What They’ve Done

At the November 12th bill signing, Trump said: “I just want to tell the American people, you should not forget this.”

He’s right about one thing. Americans shouldn’t forget.

But while Trump begs you not to forget his version of the shutdown “victory,”

  • Don’t forget how desperately he wants you to forget the real affordability crisis—dismissing skyrocketing costs with tales of bargain Thanksgiving baskets at Walmart, as if $816 extra a year per family on premiums alone is just a rounding error.

  • Don’t forget that Republicans held 70+ votes to destroy healthcare with zero alternatives.

  • Don’t forget that Trump promised a plan in “two weeks” for seven years and delivered “concepts.”

  • Don’t forget that Republicans blocked removal of medical debt from credit reports to help their donors.

  • Don’t forget that Johnson claimed in October they were “working around the clock,” then admitted in November any vote would be impossible.

  • Don’t forget that in 50 days, 24 million Americans will face 114% premium increases that Republicans refused to prevent.

  • And don’t forget that when finally pressed after 15 years of promises, the best Republicans could offer was “concepts of a plan.”

Because if that’s what “working around the clock” looks like, American families can’t afford their work ethic.

The Hypocrisy Chronicles continue. Because sometimes the biggest lie is claiming you’re working on something when you’ve spent 15 years proving you never intend to.
