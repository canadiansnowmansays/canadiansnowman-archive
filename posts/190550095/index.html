The Iran Crisis

“The Endgame”

Part XLIV: Phase Two of Four: The Administration That Cannot Define Victory, Cannot Sell the War, and Cannot Find the Door

March 13, 2026

Majid Saeedi / Getty

I. The Four Phases

The United States military is conducting its war against Iran in four phases. The first was the initial strikes. The third will be stabilization. The fourth will be withdrawal. The military is currently in the second phase: establishing control of Iranian airspace.

This is, according to officials briefed on the campaign, the longest phase. It requires less reliance on long-range missiles and more aircraft flying directly over Iran to strike targets. The campaign is, by the classified metrics shared with Congress, ahead of schedule. General Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs, has cited the statistics: Iranian ballistic missile strikes are down 90 percent from their opening pace; drone strikes are down 83 percent; more than 50 Iranian vessels have been damaged or destroyed; senior Iranian leaders have been killed; US strikes have moved farther east into the country. The military has what it was asked to do, and it is doing it.

The problem — the one that nobody in the administration can answer when congressional officials press for it in classified briefings — is what comes after phase four.

“The administration still hasn’t decided what they want to see happen,” a Defense Department official told The Atlantic’s Nancy Youssef. In a closed-door Capitol Hill briefing, Pentagon officials gave legislators no more clarity. “They can’t give answers other than acknowledge the immediate military objectives,” a congressional official told her. The military plan has four phases. The political plan, as best anyone can determine from those with access to it, does not exist.

Trump, at a rally in Kentucky on Wednesday night, defined the objective with characteristic precision. “We don’t want to leave early, do we? We’ve got to finish the job, right?” The job is to not need to come back “every two years.” The shape of the Iran that would make that unnecessary has not been described.

II. What Winning Looks Like When Nobody Knows What Winning Looks Like

The administration has offered ten distinct rationales for this war in its first six days. Iran was an imminent threat. Israel made them do it. They’re doing it for the grandkids. The explanations have not converged. The objectives have not stabilized. Only on Tuesday — Day Eleven — did Defense Secretary Hegseth and General Caine lay out three clear goals in a single statement: destroy Iran’s missiles and its capacity to build them; cripple its navy; permanently end its nuclear program. Trump immediately stepped on it, musing in separate remarks about regime change and his desire to participate in choosing Iran’s next leader.

The nuclear objective is worth pausing on. CENTCOM’s growing roster of strike targets does not cite new damage to Iran’s nuclear program. Trump claimed Iran’s nuclear capabilities were “obliterated” in last June’s strikes. Hegseth said Tuesday that permanently denying Iran nuclear weapons “forever” remains a US objective — which implies obliteration did not occur. Caine, notably, did not mention Iran’s nuclear capabilities at the same event. The gap between what the administration claims was already accomplished and what it says it is still trying to accomplish has not been explained.

What would a verifiable end to Iran’s nuclear program require? Potentially a Special Forces operation to seize the 441 kilograms of near-weapons-grade uranium that survived at Isfahan — uranium that the IAEA can no longer monitor because Iran has been under internet blackout since Day One. The Pentagon has been silent on this prospect. Rubio has said that “people are going to have to go and get it.” Nobody has said who, when, or how.

Senator Lindsey Graham, one of the war’s most vocal Senate supporters, was direct about the endgame problem from the other direction. “There’s no way you can say you won this war with an ayatollah in charge.” That sets a bar — regime change — that the administration has simultaneously sought, soft-pedaled, and abandoned depending on which official is speaking on which day. US intelligence assessments, according to Reuters, now find the regime is not at imminent risk. Netanyahu, in his Thursday press conference, scaled back his own regime-change rhetoric: “I can’t say for certain that the Iranian people would topple it. A regime is toppled from within.” The maximalist outcome has receded. What replaced it has not been named.

Christopher Preble of the Stimson Center offered the frame that clarifies what is missing. “Tactical success does not guarantee a successful post-regime Iran. A strategic victory means that you are in a stronger position than when you started the war.” The United States has a plan for the tactical. It does not have a definition of the strategic. Whether that ambiguity is deliberate — keeping the president’s options open, giving him multiple paths to declaring victory — or reflects a genuine absence of coordination is, as Youssef notes, not clear. Both possibilities are troubling, for different reasons.

III. The Failed State Problem

The scenario the administration cannot publicly discuss is the one its own officials are most worried about.

Not a surviving Iranian regime — that is the manageable failure, the one that produces continued deterrence challenges, continued nuclear ambitions, continued proxy networks, continued harassment of the Strait. Damaging but known. Not the best outcome. Tolerable.

The unmanageable scenario is a collapsed Iranian state. An Iran that does not survive the war as a functioning government. An Iran of competing factions, fractured authority, and a Revolutionary Guards Corps that has been operating with increasing independence since Ali Khamenei’s death and may not answer to whoever nominally leads the country. The US military is divided on this. Some commanders see an argument for it: only a functional state can sustain a nuclear program. Others see the catastrophe it would produce.

H.A. Hellyer of the Royal United Services Institute named the Israeli calculus directly. “The Israelis are looking to ensure that they don’t have a threat from the Iranians, but their way of going about it means if they are successful, Iran is a failed state, sooner or later.” A broken Iran consumed by internal fighting poses a minimal security threat to Israel. It poses an enormous one to everyone else. Danny Citrinowicz of the Atlantic Council noted that Israelis consider elevated gas prices from a closed Strait of Hormuz “a small price to pay” to eliminate their chief nemesis. The Strait is not their problem. It is everyone else’s.

A failed Iranian state would put 92 million people in jeopardy from internal instability or from whatever replaces the current government. It would become a haven for the terrorist groups whose regional reach the war is ostensibly designed to eliminate. It would potentially activate Kurdish separatist movements with implications for Turkey and Iraq — both of which are watching the US conduct airstrikes on Iraqi government-controlled militias and drawing their own conclusions. And it could trigger mass migration on the scale of Syria’s civil war — a calamity, as Youssef notes, that the Middle East and Europe are still reckoning with fifteen years later. Three point two million Iranians are already internally displaced. The question of where they go if the state that is displacing them ceases to function is not abstract.

The administration’s public answer to all of this is “unconditional surrender.” White House Press Secretary Leavitt has said the war ends at a time of the president’s choosing after Iran’s unconditional surrender. But as Peter Feaver of Duke observed, wars rarely end so neatly — and all wars “end with negotiations leading to compromises.” He cited as example the US concession to retain Japan’s emperor as a condition for ending World War II. There are no US or Israeli ground forces in Iran. There is no mechanism for surrender. There is no interlocutor. To whom would Iran surrender, and what would they be surrendering?

IV. The Pattern

Trump’s pattern with military force has been consistent across his second term, and it is worth naming because it illuminates what is happening in Iran.

In Somalia, he has ordered more strikes than any US president. He has not articulated what government he believes would effectively stop the extremist groups that the strikes are targeting. In Venezuela, he sent Special Forces into Caracas in January to remove Maduro. He has not said whether a transition to democracy is a necessary condition for enduring US support, or what comes next. He said the day before the Iran strikes that the US “could very well end up having a friendly takeover of Cuba.” He has not described what should replace the Castro government, or what US involvement in that transition would look like.

The common thread is not aggression. It is aggression without architecture. Trump told a 2025 US-Saudi investment forum that “nation builders wrecked far more nations than they built” and that interventionists were “intervening in complex societies that they did not even understand themselves.” This is not an indefensible position — it is, in fact, a reasonable reading of the last twenty years of American foreign policy. The difficulty is that the alternative to bad nation-building is not the absence of a post-conflict plan. The alternative is a better one.

US history is studded with wars won on the battlefield and lost in the aftermath. Vietnam: military victories accumulated while strategic position deteriorated; the Vietcong needed only to not lose in order to effectively win. Iraq 2003: “mission accomplished” six weeks after Saddam fell; eight years of insurgency followed. Afghanistan: Kabul in a month; the Taliban waited twenty years and returned to power hours after US withdrawal. In each case, the US had stated strategic objectives — even unachievable ones — that gave the military campaign a frame. Trump’s Iran war, by contrast, has offered shifting rationales and no defined endpoint.

“But perhaps the only thing worse than having an ineffective post-conflict plan,” Youssef writes, “may be having no plan at all.”

V. The War Nobody Is Selling

Three weeks before the bombs fell, Trump stood before Congress for the State of the Union and gave Iran a passing mention — a few lines near the end of a 108-minute speech. When the war began, it was announced via a Truth Social video filmed at Mar-a-Lago, in the middle of the night. The president wore a baseball cap. He did not offer a clear rationale. There was no prime-time Oval Office address. Congress was barely briefed. Aides later claimed the minimal briefing was to preserve the element of surprise — a curious explanation given the unmissable size of the US armada that had been parked in the waters off Iran for weeks.

Illustration by The Atlantic. Source: Getty

Jonathan Lemire, writing in The Atlantic, identified what this represents against the historical grain. Franklin Roosevelt made the case for World War II, and the nation endured years of rationing while sending a generation to war. George H.W. Bush built a coalition and sold the public on Kuwait. George W. Bush made the case for Iraq — the public eventually soured, but he made it. Trump has done none of this. He has the biggest audience of his year in the State of the Union and uses it to give Iran a few lines. He launches the most consequential military action of his presidency from behind a Mar-a-Lago baseball cap at midnight.

The political stakes could not be higher. The midterms were supposed to be fought on the economy — Trump’s strongest ground. Gas prices had been a Republican talking point since the State of the Union, a visible, daily indicator that things were improving. Ron Klain, Biden’s first chief of staff, told Lemire that the first thing he did every morning — before checking whether the president had called — was look at the gas price. Bill Clinton was equally attentive: gas station signs, he understood, were billboards for the state of the nation’s economy. Trump made low gas prices a centerpiece of his stump speech. They were the Republican Party’s most legible argument heading into November.

That argument is now gone. Gas is up more than 50 cents per gallon since the war began. Seven American soldiers are dead. Approximately 140 have been wounded. Tens of thousands of Americans were stranded in the Middle East when the administration failed to facilitate their departure before Iran retaliated. A Navigator poll taken just before the conflict found that voters already perceived Republicans as caring too much about foreign conflicts rather than the economy. That perception has now been validated by the largest oil supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.

Rand Paul said the war could lead to “disastrous” midterms for Republicans. Lindsey Graham and a few pro-war Senate allies have privately argued the GOP was going to lose the House anyway. Senate Majority Leader Thune told reporters that gas prices are “always kind of a benchmark” and “something obviously we’ve got to pay attention to.” Steve Bannon, Tucker Carlson, and Megyn Kelly have argued that the Iran war conflicts with the “America First” commitments Trump made in 2016. Representative Thomas Massie has voted against the war. Trump, en route to a rally in Cincinnati on Wednesday, addressed the political opposition to his war with a lengthy Truth Social post. It was not about Iran. It was about Massie. “Thomas Massie is disloyal to the United States of America! He is a MISFIT, who should be voted out of Office, ASAP.”

A war is going. The president attacked a congressman.

Senator Murphy put it plainly: “All the briefings are closed because Trump can’t defend this war in public.” Leavitt disputed this, insisting objectives had been “clearly outlined by the President” since the first strikes. Trump, to reporters at the White House on Wednesday: “Let me tell you. We’ve won. You know, you never liked to say too early you won. But we won.”

Phase two of four. The longest phase. No endgame defined. The administration’s senior official, speaking privately, offered the summary: “Short-term pain; long-term gain.”

The Strait of Hormuz is mined. Oil is above $100. The KC-135 crew is still being searched for in the western Iraqi desert. Mojtaba Khamenei has issued his instructions and they are being obeyed. Three point two million Iranians are displaced. Nearly 2,000 are dead. The fertilizer for spring planting in the American heartland is sitting in ships that cannot move.

On Thursday night, Treasury Secretary Bessent announced that the United States was temporarily lifting sanctions on Russian oil currently at sea, allowing it to flow to buyers around the world. The Witkoff-Kushner-Dmitriev meeting in Florida earlier this week — framed publicly as a discussion of “global energy markets” — had a concrete outcome after all. The accommodation has arrived. The price: lifting pressure on Vladimir Putin, whose economy runs on oil above $90, at the precise moment the administration is conducting the largest military operation of Trump’s presidency.

The market’s verdict was unambiguous. Oil barely moved. Brent hovered around $100. The sanctions lift “slowed but did not reverse” the increase, in the words of market reporters watching the price tick in real time. The largest concession the administration has made to Russia since the war began produced no meaningful relief. The Strait is still mined. The ships are still not moving. The fertilizer is still sitting.

Senator Kennedy of Louisiana remains the only Republican to have voted against the war.

Phase two of four. The longest phase.

And somewhere in a location his enemies cannot find, the man who lost his father, his mother, his wife, his son, and his sister is waiting.
