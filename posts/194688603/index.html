The Anti-Woke Preschool Inspection

Or: How a Room Full of Three-Year-Olds Exposed the Entire Project — and What It Means for Virginia on April 21

April 20, 2026

Yes, this is a photograph from a preschool in the Bronx. A local scene — former President Barack Obama and Mayor Zohran Mamdani visiting a child care centre to promote universal pre-K, the kind of routine photo opportunity that happens in every major city and disappears from the news cycle within hours.

But local scenes have a way of illuminating national questions. And as Virginia voters head to the polls tomorrow for a redistricting referendum that could reshape the U.S. House of Representatives, and as the federal government continues its systematic dismantling of diversity and inclusion programs across every institution it can reach, this particular local scene deserves a closer look. Because what is happening on that preschool carpet — quietly, without policy guidance or executive orders — is the most devastating rebuttal of the anti-woke project that anyone has yet produced.

The photograph deserves to be described.

Two men — a former president of the United States and a thirty-four-year-old democratic socialist mayor — are seated cross-legged on the floor of the classroom. They are surrounded by approximately twenty preschoolers in school uniforms. The children are Black and white and brown. Some are wearing plaid jumpers. Some have braids. One appears to be examining his own shoe with the deep concentration that only a four-year-old can bring to footwear. Nobody in the photograph is performing diversity. Nobody is signaling anything. The children are simply sitting next to whichever child happened to sit down next to them, which is what children do when adults have not yet taught them to do otherwise.

This is the photograph the anti-woke movement cannot survive. Not because it is politically powerful — it is not; it is a routine school visit — but because it is a field test. And the movement fails it completely.

Let us conduct a thought experiment.

Imagine that the United States government, in its current posture, were to dispatch an Anti-Woke Compliance Inspector to this classroom. The inspector arrives with a clipboard. He has been trained at whatever institution now trains people to detect the presence of woke ideology in federally adjacent environments. He is wearing a lanyard. He is very serious about the lanyard.

He enters the classroom and immediately identifies several violations.

Violation One: Integrated Seating. The children are seated in no discernible pattern. A white child is leaning against a Black child’s shoulder. Two girls of different ethnicities appear to be sharing a secret. A boy in glasses is sitting in the front row next to a boy without glasses, and neither of them seems to have drawn any conclusions about what this means for Western civilization. The inspector notes that no one has explained to these children that this arrangement is, according to the current executive guidance, a social experiment being conducted on them without parental consent.

Violation Two: Unmarked Differences. The classroom contains children of at least four racial backgrounds, and at no point during the inspector’s observation does any child ask another child to account for this. No child requests documentation. No child suggests that another child’s family may have arrived through an insufficiently rigorous immigration process. The inspector finds this suspicious. In his experience, differences that go unremarked are differences that have been normalized, and normalization is the first stage of the pipeline.

Violation Three: Shared Materials. The crayons are in a communal bin. The books are on a shared shelf. At one point, a child hands another child a marker without conducting any background inquiry into whether the second child’s presence in the classroom is the result of a DEI admissions policy. The inspector makes a note. Sharing, if left unexamined, leads to the assumption of equality, which leads to the expectation of equity, which leads to — and here his handwriting becomes slightly frantic — socialism.

Violation Four: The Adults Are on the Floor. Two powerful men are sitting cross-legged among the children, at child-height, making no effort to establish hierarchical distance. This, the inspector recognizes, is a form of structural levelling. Authority figures who sit on the floor are authority figures who have abdicated the visual grammar of authority. He has read a memorandum about this.

Violation Five: Joy. The children appear happy. Several are smiling. One is laughing. The inspector is trained to recognize joy in integrated settings as a particularly dangerous indicator, because it suggests that the participants do not experience diversity as a burden, a compromise, or a problem to be managed — which contradicts the foundational premise of the entire project he has been sent to enforce.

The inspector leaves. His report is seven pages long. His recommendation is that the classroom be restructured to ensure that differences are properly acknowledged, which in the current vocabulary means made into problems.

The children do not notice he has gone.

I am being satirical, but only barely. The distance between this thought experiment and American policy in 2026 is measurable in inches, not miles.

It is worth noting that the mayor on the carpet — the one sitting cross-legged among three-year-olds, singing “Wheels on the Bus” and plugging affordable housing — has met with President Trump twice at the White House. The same man has sat in both rooms. In one, he was surrounded by children who had not yet learned to sort each other. In the other, he sat across from the administration that is spending billions of dollars and thousands of executive orders to ensure that they will.

The current administration has issued executive orders eliminating diversity, equity, and inclusion programs across the federal government. It has instructed military branches to dismantle DEI offices. It has pressured universities to abandon race-conscious policies. It has targeted curricula that teach children about the history of racial exclusion in America — the very history that explains why the photograph is remarkable at all. It has, in effect, declared that the observable, lived, functioning diversity in that preschool classroom is a problem to be solved rather than an achievement to be protected.

And this is where the Classroom Test applies.

The Classroom Test is simple. If your political philosophy requires you to walk into a room full of three- and four-year-olds and explain to them why some of their friends are problems — why the boy sitting next to them should not, in fact, be sitting next to them; why the girl sharing crayons has been placed there by a policy that undermines merit; why the ease and comfort they feel in each other’s company is actually a symptom of ideological capture — then your political philosophy has failed the most basic field test available to a civilized society.

Children are not born with the instinct to sort each other by race. That instinct is taught. It is taught by policy and by culture and by the daily signals that adults send about who belongs and who does not. The anti-woke movement in America is, at its operational core, a project to reinstall those signals in institutions that had begun, slowly and imperfectly, to dismantle them.

The photograph from the preschool is what it looks like when the signals have not yet arrived.

Tomorrow — April 21, 2026 — the signals arrive at the map table.

Virginia voters will decide whether to approve a constitutional amendment allowing the state legislature to redraw the commonwealth’s eleven congressional districts. The amendment is a response to a national gerrymandering offensive: Texas, Missouri, and North Carolina have already redrawn their maps at the president’s encouragement, and Florida is in special session doing the same. The combined effect could swing as many as seven additional Republican seats in the House before a single voter casts a midterm ballot. Virginia’s Democratic legislature is proposing to redraw in response. The current delegation splits 6–5. The proposed map would split it 10–1.

Both parties will tell you the other side started it. Both parties are correct. That is what arms races look like.

But here is what the preschool photograph has to do with the redistricting referendum, and it is not what either party’s campaign literature will tell you:

Gerrymandering is the adult version of what the inspector was doing in that classroom.

The children sat where they sat. They did not sort themselves. Nobody drew a line down the middle of the carpet to ensure that the children on one side would reliably outnumber the children on the other. Nobody carved the reading corner away from the block corner to dilute the concentration of children who might, if left unsorted, develop the wrong kind of consensus about sharing.

That is what redistricting does. It takes communities that sit next to each other — naturally, organically, the way children sit on a carpet — and draws lines around them to predetermine the outcome before anyone votes. It is sorting. It is the same project. The only difference is scale.

Virginia voters who go to the polls tomorrow are being asked to participate in a sorting exercise. The question is not whether sorting is happening — it is happening in every state that has already redrawn its maps. The question is whether Virginia will respond by sorting back, or whether it will accept being sorted by others and call that principle.

I am not a Virginia voter. I am a Canadian watching from Toronto, and I do not presume to tell Virginians how to vote. But I will observe this: the children in that preschool photograph did not need a bipartisan commission to decide where to sit. They did not need a constitutional amendment. They did not need a referendum. They sat down, and the arrangement was fair, because nobody had yet drawn a line to make it unfair.

The adults could learn something from that carpet.

I write this from Canada, where we have our own contradictions and our own history of exclusion — residential schools, the Chinese head tax, the internment of Japanese Canadians, the ongoing failures toward Indigenous communities. We are not in a position to lecture. But we are in a position to observe. And what we observe, from this side of the border, is a country that appears to be voluntarily dismantling one of its greatest competitive advantages.

I spent ten essays last year documenting this pattern. Every group now targeted for exclusion in America — immigrants, LGBTQ+ individuals, religious minorities, racial minorities who have the audacity to ask for equity rather than merely tolerance — follows the identical historical arc as the Irish, the Chinese railroad workers, the Jewish refugees, and the Black soldiers who won wars for a country that would not serve them lunch. The pattern is exclusion, followed by grudging inclusion, followed by extraordinary contribution, followed by historical amnesia, followed by the exclusion of the next group using the same discredited arguments.

Here is the fact that the anti-woke movement cannot accommodate: the thing they are fighting is not an ideology imposed on children. It is the condition children arrive in. The ideology is what comes later — the sorting, the ranking, the naming of some children as default and others as diverse, the slow and patient work of teaching a four-year-old that the kid sitting next to him is, in some way that the four-year-old cannot yet perceive, different in a way that matters.

Every executive order aimed at dismantling DEI. Every curriculum restriction. Every map redrawn to predetermine an outcome. All of it is in service of the same project: teaching people what that preschool classroom has not yet taught its children.

That some of their neighbours are problems.

The photograph will be forgotten by next week. The children will grow up and enter the machine. Some of them will grow up in Virginia. Some of them will vote tomorrow, standing inside lines that someone else drew around them.

But for one afternoon, on the floor of a classroom, in their little uniforms, leaning against each other, ignoring the categories that adults have spent centuries building and billions enforcing — they showed us what it looks like before the work of division begins.

The anti-woke movement looked at that classroom and saw a problem.

The gerrymandering machine looked at it and saw raw material.

The rest of us looked at it and saw the answer.
