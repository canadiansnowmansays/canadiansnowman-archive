The Great Canadian Trade War

The Blunter Tools Arrive — And Canada Is in the Dock

Part XII: How a Forced Labor Pretext Became a Tariff Replacement Machine

March 14, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 14, 2026

U.S. President Donald Trump speaks during an event at the White House on Thursday in Washington. His administration has expanded its trade investigations to 60 countries, including Canada, in an effort to shore up its tariff policies. (Julia Demaree Nikhinson/AP)

Candace Laing called it.

Three weeks ago, when the Supreme Court struck down Trump’s IEEPA tariff empire, the president of the Canadian Chamber of Commerce chose not to celebrate. Instead, she issued a warning that Part XI of this series built its entire argument around: “Canada should prepare for new, blunter mechanisms to be used to reassert trade pressure, potentially with broader and more disruptive effects.”

The mechanisms arrived yesterday.

Yesterday was March 13 — the one-year anniversary of Canada’s first round of retaliatory tariffs against the United States. And on this date, the U.S. Trade Representative launched Section 301 investigations into 60 countries and territories for allegedly failing to prohibit imports of goods made with forced labor. The list includes China, the EU, Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, Australia, the United Kingdom, Mexico — and Canada.

Canada. A country that passed the Fighting Against Forced Labour and Child Labour in Supply Chains Act in 2023. A country that requires companies to report annually on steps taken to eliminate forced labor from their supply chains. A country ranked among the world’s most rigorous enforcers of labor standards by virtually every international index.

But before we get to the absurdity of Canada’s inclusion, we need to understand what Section 301 actually is, what fast-tracking it to 60 economies simultaneously actually means, and what happens to countries that genuinely don’t have the legislation to pass muster. Because that’s where this gets dangerous — not just for Canada, but for the global trading order.

The Mechanism: Section 301 and How It Works

Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 is, at its core, an unfair trade practices law. It authorizes the U.S. Trade Representative to investigate foreign governments whose policies “burden or restrict U.S. commerce” — and to impose tariffs, suspend trade concessions, or extract binding commitments if those policies are found to be “unreasonable or discriminatory.”

It is a legitimate and well-established instrument. The first Trump administration used it six times. The Biden administration used it. It produced the China tariffs of 2018 that are still in force today. When wielded surgically against a specific country for a specific documented practice — China’s forced technology transfer, for instance — it has real legal standing.

What is happening today is not surgical. It is a chainsaw.

In the space of 48 hours, the USTR launched two simultaneous Section 301 investigations — the first targeting 16 trading partners for “structural excess capacity in manufacturing,” and the second targeting 60 economies for failure to prohibit forced labor imports. Combined, that is a Section 301 net cast over virtually the entire trading world, and virtually every major U.S. trade partner, at once.

The distinction that makes the forced labor investigation particularly brazen: the USTR is not investigating whether these countries use forced labor. It is investigating whether they have adequate laws prohibiting the import of goods made with forced labor. That is a subtle but crucial difference. The allegation against Canada — one of the world’s most progressive labor rights nations — is not that Canadian factories use coerced workers. It is that Canada’s import prohibition regime may not be sufficiently robust to satisfy Washington.

This is the legal hook. Thin, but sufficient to initiate the process.

The July Clock: Why Everything Is Moving at Warp Speed

To understand why this is happening now, and why the word “fast-track” is the right framing, you need to understand the timeline pressure the administration is operating under.

On February 20, the Supreme Court struck down the IEEPA tariffs. Trump immediately replaced them with a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. That was the stop-gap. But Section 122 is explicitly temporary — it expires after 150 days. Mark July 24, 2026 in your calendar. That is the cliff.

In a news conference after the ruling, Trump said Section 301 investigations would be his vehicle for rebuilding the tariff architecture. USTR Greer has since confirmed that the goal is to conclude the new Section 301 investigations before the Section 122 tariffs expire — meaning before July 24. He added the honest caveat that he “can’t predetermine how long the investigations will take.”

Here is why that caveat matters enormously: the standard Section 301 investigation timeline is 12 months. Greer is publicly targeting 5 months. He has publicly acknowledged that the April 28 to May 1 hearing window covering all 60 countries has already been called “unrealistically short” by trade experts. Deborah Elms, head of trade policy at the Hinrich Foundation, put it plainly: the scope of these investigations is sweeping, and the timeline is compressed to the point where meaningful analysis of 60 different legal frameworks is practically impossible.

What does this mean in practice? It means the process is designed to reach a predetermined conclusion on a politically necessary timeline, not to conduct a genuine assessment of whether Cambodia has adequate import prohibition statutes.

That is not an accusation. It is what every serious trade analyst watching this unfold is saying out loud.

What Happens to Countries Without Adequate Legislation?

I raise this question, and it is the right one to ask — because for most of the 60 countries on this list, it is not a hypothetical.

The United States has, since 2016, prohibited the importation of goods made with forced labor under Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930. The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act of 2022 strengthened this further, establishing a rebuttable presumption that goods from China’s Xinjiang region are made with forced labor. The U.S. legal framework here is genuinely robust.

Many of the 60 countries under investigation cannot say the same. Cambodia, Bangladesh, Vietnam, and several other manufacturing powerhouses lack comprehensive import prohibition regimes matching U.S. standards. Some have domestic labor protections of varying quality. Many lack the customs and enforcement infrastructure to meaningfully track whether goods passing through their ports were produced under coercive conditions in third countries.

Under Section 301, if an investigation determines that a country’s acts, policies, or practices are “unreasonable or discriminatory” and burden U.S. commerce, the USTR has three available remedies: impose tariffs or import restrictions; withdraw or suspend trade agreement concessions; or extract a binding agreement requiring the country to either change its conduct or compensate the United States.

The law requires that tariffs be the priority remedy if import restrictions are chosen. And critically, the level of the remedy is supposed to “affect goods or services of the foreign country in an amount that is equivalent in value to the burden or restriction being imposed.”

For a country like Bangladesh — where garment manufacturing accounts for roughly 80% of export earnings, and where a determination of inadequate forced labor import prohibitions could trigger tariffs on textile imports — the consequences of an adverse finding, fast-tracked in five months, would be economically catastrophic. Bangladesh exported approximately $7.7 billion in goods to the United States in 2024. A 25% tariff — the level Trump applied to China in his first term — would represent a devastating blow to an economy still developing the institutional capacity to build the legal framework Washington is now demanding.

The humanitarian dimension here should not be lost. The countries most likely to fail the fast-track test are the poorest countries on the list. The countries that would suffer most from punitive tariffs as a result of inadequate legislation are the countries least able to implement new legal frameworks in five months. And the countries that would be most desperate to reach a bilateral deal with Washington — the “binding agreement” option under Section 301 — are precisely the countries that would accept the most one-sided terms.

This is leverage applied to the most vulnerable, dressed in the language of human rights.

The Irony of Canada’s Inclusion

Against that backdrop, Canada’s presence on this list belongs in a class by itself.

Canada’s Fighting Against Forced Labour and Child Labour in Supply Chains Act — Bill S-211 — received Royal Assent in May 2023 and came into force January 1, 2024. It requires entities within federal jurisdiction and private-sector entities meeting certain size thresholds to report annually to the Minister of Public Safety on steps taken to prevent and reduce the risk of forced labor and child labor in their supply chains. The reporting requirements cover due diligence processes, risk assessment, remediation steps, and training provided to staff.

Canada is a signatory to ILO Convention 29 on Forced Labour, ILO Convention 105 on the Abolition of Forced Labour, and the UN Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime’s trafficking protocols. Canada’s Criminal Code explicitly prohibits trafficking in persons and forced labor domestically.

The USTR’s stated basis for the investigation is not that Canada uses forced labor. It is that Canada may lack sufficient external-facing import prohibition laws — laws banning goods made with forced labor abroad from entering Canadian markets. This is an arguable gap. Bill S-211 focuses on supply chain transparency and reporting, not on a blanket import prohibition equivalent to Section 307.

But here is the political context: Canada’s forced labor supply chain legislation was stronger in 2026 than the United States’ was in 2015. The U.S. closed its own “consumptive demand” loophole — which had allowed forced labor goods to be imported if there was insufficient domestic supply — only in 2016. Canada is now being investigated by a country that only closed the same loophole ten years ago.

The investigation of Canada is not about forced labor. It is about tariffs, CUSMA leverage, and the July deadline.

The Real Strategy: Tariff Architecture Replacement

Every trade analyst watching this process has reached the same conclusion, and they are saying it clearly enough that it bears repeating here. U.S. Trade Representative Greer told CNBC this week that Section 301 “will be essentially a proxy for the trade tariffs that were imposed but subsequently blocked by the Supreme Court.” He said the U.S. would use the investigations as leverage for further trade negotiations.

That is not a leak. That is the stated policy.

The sequence is straightforward: IEEPA tariffs struck down → Section 122 stop-gap imposed, expiring July 24 → Section 301 investigations launched on compressed timeline targeting July conclusion → findings used to reimpose country-specific tariffs before the stop-gap expires → tariff architecture rebuilt on Section 301 legal footing that may survive court challenge.

There is an additional Section 301 track running parallel to the forced labor probe: 16 economies are also under investigation for “structural excess capacity in manufacturing.” Canada is not on that list — but China, Mexico, the EU, South Korea, Japan, Vietnam, Taiwan, and others are. Between the two investigations, virtually every major U.S. trading partner is now in some stage of a Section 301 process.

The administration is simultaneously pursuing Section 232 expansions — the national security tariff authority that survived the Supreme Court ruling intact — across lumber, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, and other sectors. And Greer has hinted that further Section 301 investigations on digital services taxes, pharmaceutical pricing, and ocean pollution are forthcoming.

This is not trade policy. This is the construction of a permanent leverage infrastructure.

A Canadian Sidebar Worth Watching: The Loyal Opposition Goes to Detroit

Pierre Poilievre is headed to the U.S. for his first visit as Conservative leader, pushing for a free trade deal in the auto sector / CTV News

While the Carney government navigates the Section 301 minefield through official channels, something unusual is happening on the opposition benches. Conservative leader Pierre Poilievre — the man who lost to Carney in last year’s federal election partly on the trade file — is in Detroit today, standing in front of a podium bearing the Canadian maple leaf, pushing for a tariff-free auto pact.

Poilievre is promising a new auto pact that would remove the GST on all Canadian-made automobiles and eliminate tariffs for the sale of Canadian vehicles to the United States. CP24 He’s meeting with GM and Ford executives in Michigan, heading to Texas on energy, and wrapping up with a Foreign Policy Association address in New York.

What he is deliberately not doing is visiting Washington, D.C.

CTV political analyst Eric Ham called that a mistake, saying it makes clear that Poilievre’s voice “not only is not welcome, but doesn’t even remotely resonate with the Trump White House and even with many rank and file Republicans.” CP24

The more charitable read — offered by former Harper cabinet minister James Moore — is that Poilievre is traveling to show solidarity with the federal government, singing from the same song sheet as Canada advocates for tariff-free trade to audiences the prime minister might not be able to reach. CP24

Both reads are probably correct. Poilievre is conducting image rehabilitation after an election campaign where his soft-pedaling of the Trump threat cost him votes in the very Windsor-area ridings he’s standing in today. But the fact that Canada’s opposition leader is in Motor City making the free-trade case — while the government’s trade minister is in Washington making the same case, and the USTR is simultaneously launching forced labor investigations against Canada — illustrates something important: on this file, Canada is playing every card it has, from every direction, simultaneously.

The Trump administration gets to decide whether that looks like a unified, credible partner — or a country desperate enough to leverage its own opposition politics.

What This Means for CUSMA

The formal CUSMA review is scheduled to begin July 1, 2026. The Section 122 temporary tariffs expire July 24. The Section 301 investigation conclusions are targeted for late June or early July. These three dates are not coincidental.

The administration is engineering a situation where Canada enters the formal CUSMA review simultaneously facing the expiration of the temporary tariff regime and the potential imposition of new Section 301 tariffs — unless it reaches bilateral agreements on terms Washington will dictate. This is what the Candace Laing warning actually meant. The IEEPA tariffs were blunt. Section 301, deployed across multiple simultaneous investigations with compressed timelines and a July cliff, is the mechanism that forces every country to the table on Washington’s terms or face the consequences.

Canada trade minister Dominic LeBlanc’s March 6 meeting with Greer — the first face-to-face meeting since October’s Reagan ad rupture — is now legible in this context. Canada was told what was coming. The meeting was described as “constructive and substantive.” Greer has made clear that tariffs will be part of whatever trade deal is negotiated.

LeBlanc got into his waiting car and said “Have a good weekend.”

That is a man who understands the game he is playing.

The Math, One Year In

March 13, 2025 was the day Canada imposed its first major retaliatory tariffs — CA$29.8 billion worth — in response to U.S. steel and aluminum duties. One year later, to the day, the United States has opened a second Section 301 front that includes Canada in a forced labor investigation alongside Cambodia and Bangladesh.

The relationship that the Canadian Chamber of Commerce once called “the most integrated bilateral economic relationship in the world” has been reduced to this: two countries in simultaneous WTO dispute consultations, Section 301 investigations, and CUSMA renegotiation talks — all while Canada’s trade minister gets into a car without comment and the U.S. trade representative acknowledges his president is “half inclined to leave.”

Candace Laing warned us. Canada is preparing. The question is whether the tools now arriving are blunt enough to break something that both sides will spend a generation trying to repair.

The math always wins. But sometimes it takes a year to count the damage.
