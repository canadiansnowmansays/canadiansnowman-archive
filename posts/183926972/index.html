The Counterfactual President

Defense and Intelligence

Part 4: Competence Over Loyalty

April 10, 2026

The Pentagon — five sides, one oath. The building endures; the question Year One answered is whether the institution inside it still does.

General Mark Milley, former Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, remained on Donald Trump’s enemies list throughout his first year back in power.

His crime? Upholding his constitutional oath during Trump’s final days in his first term. When Trump wanted to use active-duty military against American protesters, Milley said no. When Trump floated ideas about martial law, Milley maintained that the military serves the Constitution. When concerns arose about nuclear weapons security, Milley ensured proper protocols remained in place.

For doing his job, Milley spent Trump’s first year back facing threats of court-martial or worse. Trump called him treasonous repeatedly. Trump suggested he deserved execution.

What this meant for every officer serving during year one:

The message was crystal clear throughout those twelve months: Constitutional duty matters less than personal loyalty to Trump. Legal boundaries are obstacles to be ignored. Officers who maintain professional standards will be punished.

This is how we watched military institutions begin to collapse during the year. Not through foreign conquest—through internal corruption, when competence became secondary to compliance, when strategic wisdom became less valuable than willingness to execute whatever orders the President gave, legal or not.

But there’s a counterfactual. One where military leadership during that first year was selected for competence, experience, and strategic vision. Where intelligence professionals could tell leaders uncomfortable truths. Where civilian control meant adherence to law, not blind obedience to personality.

That’s not idealism. That’s how American defense and intelligence functioned for generations.

Let’s examine what that alternative first year would have looked like—and what we actually lost.

The Military: What Year One of Loyalty Purges Produced

Every officer in the United States military swears the same oath: “I do solemnly swear that I will support and defend the Constitution of the United States against all enemies, foreign and domestic.”

Not loyalty to any president. Not obedience to any individual. Loyalty to the Constitution.

That oath prevented military coups when presidents lost elections. It maintained civilian control during constitutional crises. It distinguished American military professionalism from the politicized militaries that plague fragile democracies.

What we watched during Trump’s first year back:

Trump began systematic purges of military leadership deemed insufficiently loyal. Officers who upheld constitutional boundaries during his previous term faced court-martial threats throughout the year. Generals who refused illegal orders were targeted for prosecution. The criterion for promotion shifted from competence to compliance over twelve months.

General Milley remained the most prominent target, but he wasn’t alone. Any officer who demonstrated commitment to law over loyalty during the previous administration spent the year under threat.

This produced predictable consequences throughout those twelve months:

Wars became more likely. Officers afraid to give honest assessments provided rosy scenarios all year. Leaders heard what they wanted to hear rather than what they needed to hear.

Tactical expertise mattered less. Political favor determined advancement throughout the year. Bad plans proceeded because no one dared object.

Constitutional boundaries eroded. When officers knew their careers depended on following any order throughout the year, what stopped the President from using the military for domestic political purposes?

International adversaries were emboldened. When they saw American military leadership selected for loyalty rather than competence during that year, they calculated that American military effectiveness was declining.

This isn’t hypothetical danger. This is what we watched begin to happen.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, military leadership would have been selected by traditional criteria: competence, experience, strategic vision, commitment to constitutional civilian control. Generals would have offered frank military advice without fear of being fired for honesty throughout those twelve months.

General Milley would have retired with honors, not faced prosecution threats. Officers who maintained constitutional boundaries would have been promoted, not purged. The military would have continued providing honest assessments civilian leaders need.

But Harris would have faced challenges throughout that first year: Republican accusations of weakness on defense. Pressure from some progressives to dramatically reduce military spending. Tension between maintaining readiness and addressing internal issues like extremism and sexual assault. Resistance from military traditionalists to reforms. The military-industrial complex opposing procurement changes. Finding the balance between civilian oversight and military expertise wouldn’t have been simple.

This isn’t about Harris having better military judgment than Trump. It’s about maintaining the institutional culture that keeps the military professional, apolitical, and effective.

And we spent a year watching that culture begin to corrode.

Nuclear Weapons: What a Year Under Trump’s Whims Looked Like

During Trump’s first term, he reportedly asked why we couldn’t use nuclear weapons on hurricanes, suggested nuking drug cartels in Mexico, made casual references to nuclear strikes, and required constant management by senior officials.

What his first year back produced:

With loyalists installed throughout the chain of command over twelve months, the people who previously provided sanity checks were gone. Officers knew their careers depended on compliance throughout the year. When Trump casually suggested nuclear options, who was left to say no?

An ICBM launch silo — the infrastructure of deterrence that depends not on presidential whim, but on the professional judgment of officers sworn to the Constitution. Source: Titan Missile Museum / public domain archive

The risk of nuclear catastrophe increased throughout the year as protocols were undermined and personnel were selected for loyalty rather than judgment.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, nuclear protocols would have remained rigorous. Senior military officials would have maintained their duty to ensure proper procedures throughout those twelve months. The careful balance between civilian authority and professional judgment that has prevented nuclear war for 80 years would have continued.

No president should have unchecked nuclear authority. But the difference between Trump and Harris is whether the President views security protocols as annoying obstacles or as essential safeguards.

We spent a year watching protocols undermined.

Ukraine: What a Year of Political Interference Produced

U.S. M1 Abrams tanks delivered to Ukraine Caption: American Abrams tanks arrive in Ukraine — military aid that military professionals assessed as strategically essential and that political interference began to condition on loyalty calculations. Source: U.S. Army / Department of Defense

American military support for Ukraine had been remarkable in its effectiveness before Trump returned. Ukrainian forces held off a much larger Russian military, reclaimed territory, demonstrated that Russian military capability was vastly overestimated.

This success depended on coordinated military assistance based on Ukrainian military needs as assessed by professional military planners.

What Trump’s first year produced:

Military assistance was conditioned on political calculations throughout the year. Ukrainian defense needs mattered less than Trump’s relationship with Putin. Professional military assessments were overridden by political considerations month after month.

The message to Ukraine and allies became clear over twelve months: Don’t count on American military commitments.

This affected more than Ukraine throughout the year. Every nation depending on American security guarantees spent those twelve months recalculating.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, military support would have continued based on strategic assessment: Allowing Russian territorial conquest undermines international order and makes future conflicts more likely.

Military professionals would have determined what weapons, training, and intelligence support Ukraine needed throughout the year. Ukrainian forces would have received predictable support allowing effective planning.

The result after one year: Stronger deterrence against future aggression, maintained credibility of American commitments, preserved international norms.

Intelligence: What a Year of Politicization Produced

CIA headquarters, Langley, Virginia — aerial view, 1984 Caption: The CIA's Langley campus — home to the analysts whose entire professional purpose is to tell leaders things they don't want to hear, and who spent the year being punished for doing exactly that. Source: U.S. Government / National Archives

Intelligence agencies exist to tell leaders things they don’t want to hear. That’s their entire purpose—providing assessments based on evidence rather than on what pleases the audience.

Trump has treated intelligence professionals as enemies since before his inauguration. During his first year back, he restructured agencies to ensure political loyalty took precedence over analytical integrity.

What intelligence analysts experienced during year one:

They faced a career-defining choice throughout those twelve months: Provide assessments based on evidence, or provide assessments the President wanted to hear. Those who chose evidence over politics were reassigned, fired, or investigated. Those who complied advanced.

This produced catastrophic consequences for national security throughout the year:

Threats went undetected. Evidence of adversary capabilities, hostile intentions, emerging dangers—if it contradicted presidential preferences during that year, it got buried.

Opportunities were missed. Intelligence that could have enabled strategic advantage didn’t reach decision-makers throughout the year if it didn’t fit preferred narratives.

Strategic blunders multiplied. Policy based on politicized intelligence rather than accurate assessment produced disasters throughout the year.

Iran’s nuclear program was assessed based on whether Trump wanted war or deals. Chinese capabilities were evaluated based on whether it supported Trump’s narrative. Russian operations were countered or ignored based on whether acknowledging them embarrassed Trump.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, intelligence directors would have been chosen for expertise, not political loyalty. Analysts would have provided assessments based on evidence throughout those twelve months. Briefings would have included uncomfortable truths. Intelligence suggesting threats—domestic terrorism from right-wing extremists, climate security risks, Russian interference—would have reached decision-makers.

Most critically: Whistleblowers could have reported wrongdoing without expecting career destruction. When intelligence professionals saw manipulation for political purposes, they could have reported through proper channels.

But Harris would have faced intelligence challenges throughout that first year: Balancing transparency with classification needs. Managing tensions between intelligence community and skeptical progressives. Rebuilding trust after Trump’s damage. Addressing legitimate concerns about surveillance overreach. Navigating conflicts between intelligence assessments and political priorities.

This enables the intelligence community to keep America safe. Not loyalty to individuals. Loyalty to truth.

And we spent a year watching loyalty to Trump replace it.

Veterans Affairs: What a Year of Political Theater Produced

A VA medical facility — built to serve those who served, and reduced during Year One to a battleground in culture wars its patients never enlisted to fight. Source: U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs

The Department of Veterans Affairs serves those who served—healthcare, benefits, support for transition to civilian life.

What Trump’s first year back produced:

VA resources were diverted to culture war battles throughout the year. Political appointees with no healthcare experience ran a massive medical system. Benefits were delayed or denied. Veterans organizations were pressured to support Trump politically.

Meanwhile, veterans faced actual challenges throughout that year: traumatic brain injuries, PTSD, exposure to burn pits, homelessness, suicide epidemic. These required competent administration, adequate funding, professional medical care—not political theater.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, VA leadership would have been chosen for healthcare and benefits administration expertise. Resources would have been directed toward veteran needs throughout those twelve months. Benefits processing would have been streamlined. Mental health services would have been expanded.

This wouldn’t have required massive innovation—just allowing the VA to focus on its mission for a year rather than being a political football.

Transgender Service Members: What a Year of Culture War Produced

Trump reinstated the ban on transgender individuals serving in the military during his first year back. Not based on military readiness assessments—military leaders opposed the ban. Not based on unit cohesion evidence—studies showed no negative impact. Based on culture war politics.

What this produced over twelve months:

Qualified service members were discharged based on identity rather than performance throughout the year. Military leaders who objected were overruled or fired. Culture war politics overrode military readiness assessments for twelve months.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, personnel policy would have been based on military readiness. If service members met standards and served with distinction, they served. If they didn’t meet standards, they were separated—regardless of identity.

This is the military’s traditional approach: Standards apply equally. Politics don’t determine who serves. Readiness is the only criterion.

We spent a year watching politics override readiness.

The Allies Question: What a Year of Uncertainty Produced

Every allied military spent Trump’s first year back conducting strategic reassessments based on one question: Is American military commitment reliable?

What allies experienced throughout year one:

Uncertainty dominated every planning discussion for twelve months. They couldn’t plan jointly if they didn’t know whether American support was reliable. Deterrence failed throughout the year when adversaries questioned whether alliance commitments would be honored.

European nations spent the year urgently increasing defense spending and exploring autonomous capabilities—not because Trump bullied them, but because they couldn’t count on American protection. Asian allies hedged their security relationships throughout those twelve months.

The alliance structure that prevented great power war for 75 years spent a year fracturing.

The counterfactual:

Under Harris’s first year, American military commitments would have been clear and consistent. NATO Article 5 would have been unequivocal. Asian security partnerships would have been reinforced. Allied militaries could have conducted joint planning knowing American support was reliable.

Deterrence would have functioned throughout that year because adversaries would have believed alliance commitments would be enforced.

We spent a year watching the opposite.

The Republican Security Question After Year One

I want to address Republican readers who claim to care about national security, military strength, and defense of American interests.

You’ve spent decades arguing that military competence matters, intelligence professionals should be respected, veterans deserve proper care, America should lead from strength, allies should trust American commitments.

What you watched during Trump’s first year back:

Competent military leaders purged for insufficient loyalty. Intelligence agencies restructured to serve political needs. VA weaponized for culture wars. Allied nations questioning American reliability. Military leadership selected based on willingness to follow unlawful orders.

If you actually believe in military professionalism and national security, this past year should have horrified you. Because we watched the military become politicized, intelligence corrupted, allied trust destroyed over twelve months.

The counterfactual asks: Would you rather have lived through a year where military leadership was selected for competence or loyalty? Where intelligence was based on evidence or political convenience? Where allies trusted America or explored alternatives?

If you can’t answer honestly after watching this past year, you never cared about national security. You just cared about power.

Why This Past Year Mattered Globally

From my Canadian perspective, I watched American defense policy over twelve months with particular attention. Our security depends on American reliability. Our military cooperates intimately with American forces.

What we experienced throughout year one:

When American military leadership was compromised by loyalty purges over twelve months, when American intelligence was corrupted by political pressure, when American commitments became unreliable—the ripples reached every allied nation.

We all spent the year recalculating what security means if America can’t be counted on. We all conducted thought experiments about defense without reliable American partnership. We all mourned the loss of the alliance structure that kept the democratic world secure.

The counterfactual matters internationally because American defense competence doesn’t just protect Americans. It’s the foundation of collective security that has prevented great power war, contained authoritarian expansion, and maintained international order.

We spent a year watching it corrode.

The Exhaustion of Watching a Year of Competence Die

I need to acknowledge: Writing about what we watched this past year feels particularly desperate because the stakes are existential.

Get economic policy wrong, recovery is possible. Get social policy wrong, corrections can be made. Get defense and intelligence wrong—wars start, people die, nations fall, damage is irreversible.

We spent a year watching the corruption of the institutions that prevent catastrophic mistakes. We watched competence become secondary to compliance. We watched professional military culture erode under political pressure.

And having to explain why this year mattered—why generals should be selected for competence, why intelligence should be based on truth, why nuclear protocols should be maintained, why allies should trust American commitments—feels like arguing for gravity.

Billions of people conducted the same thought experiment throughout that year: What would security feel like if American defense institutions still functioned professionally? What would alliance coherence look like? What would intelligence based on truth produce?

That’s recognition that when the world’s most powerful military and intelligence apparatus is corrupted by loyalty demands over twelve months, everyone’s security degrades.

The counterfactual reminds us this past year didn’t have to be this way.
