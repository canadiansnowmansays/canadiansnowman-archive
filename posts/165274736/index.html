America's Next Industrial Revolution

America's Next Industrial Revolution

An 8-Part Series on EVs, AI, and Robotics

June 5, 2025

Series Introduction

Over the next eight weeks, I'll be sharing a comprehensive analysis that explores the most significant economic opportunity America has faced since the dawn of the automobile age. Writing from my perspective as a Canadian observer, I've witnessed firsthand how the convergence of electric vehicles, artificial intelligence, and robotics is reshaping not just transportation, but the entire fabric of how we live, work, and compete globally.

Why This Series Matters Now

While Americans debate gas prices and energy policy, a fundamental transformation is already underway. Tesla's Robotaxi service launches in Austin this June. Amazon is testing robots with Rivian delivery vans. China controls 50% of global EV sales and is rapidly expanding. The question isn't whether this revolution will happen—it's whether America will lead it or follow China's direction.

A Balanced Transition Approach

Let me be clear from the start: this isn't about eliminating gasoline vehicles overnight or forcing anyone to buy an EV. This is about building America's capacity to lead in the technologies that will dominate the next economy while supporting our existing energy infrastructure during a thoughtful, market-driven transition.

The reality is that internal combustion engines and electric vehicles will coexist for the next 10-15 years. Rural Americans with long commutes, commercial fleet operators with specific needs, and families in areas with limited charging infrastructure should have choices that work for their situations. Plug-in hybrids offer an excellent bridge technology, providing electric driving for daily use while maintaining gasoline backup for longer trips.

The goal isn't to shut down oil production or eliminate consumer choice—it's to ensure America doesn't cede the manufacturing jobs of the future to China while we debate the past.

The Canadian Perspective

From Toronto, where our Ultra-Low Overnight electricity rates make EV charging cost just 2.8¢ per kWh (93% less than gasoline), the economic advantages of electric transportation aren't theoretical—they're mathematical reality. But more importantly, from our vantage point north of the border, we can see how this technology convergence represents the largest industrial opportunity since the assembly line revolutionized manufacturing.

What You'll Discover

This series will take you beyond the partisan debates to examine the real economics, technology, and political realities of America's energy future. We'll explore how Tesla's Optimus robots could revolutionize daily life (imagine your car driving itself to Costco while a robot does your shopping), why the proposed elimination of EV tax credits threatens American manufacturing competitiveness, and how this transformation could create nearly half a million high-paying jobs while achieving energy independence—all while supporting oil workers and communities during the transition.

A Non-Partisan, Pro-Choice Approach

While I focus on winning over Republican skeptics—because their support is essential for success—this isn't about left versus right, and it's definitely not about government mandates forcing vehicle choices. It's about American manufacturing dominance versus Chinese industrial control. It's about $6,500 in annual household savings for those who choose EVs versus continued dependence on volatile oil markets for everyone. It's about leading the next industrial revolution versus watching it happen elsewhere.

The Both/And Strategy

This series advocates for a "both/and" rather than "either/or" approach:

  • Both oil production AND EV manufacturing

  • Both supporting current energy workers AND creating new technology jobs

  • Both rural charging infrastructure AND urban EV adoption

  • Both consumer choice AND industrial competitiveness

  • Both immediate economic relief AND long-term strategic advantage

Setting Aside the Noise: A Call for Strategic Focus

I know this is controversial territory, but it must be addressed: Americans—and President Trump—need to set aside negative feelings about Elon Musk and focus on his vision and genuine genius. Yes, Musk's political evolution and vocal stances have polarized opinions. Yes, his support for Trump has alienated some former supporters. Yes, his criticism of Trump's "Big Beautiful Bill" has created new tensions.

But here's the hard truth: allowing personal feelings about Musk to derail American EV leadership would be a strategic disaster that hands China exactly what they want. Tesla didn't become the world's most valuable automaker by accident. Musk didn't revolutionize space travel, internet payments, and electric vehicles through luck. Love him or hate him, his companies represent America's best chance to lead the next industrial revolution.

The same principle applies to President Trump. His pattern of retaliating against perceived opposition—even from allies—could be catastrophic if directed at American EV leadership out of spite over fiscal policy disagreements. The stakes are too high for personality-driven decisions that benefit only China.

This series will address these political dynamics honestly because they represent one of the biggest threats to American manufacturing dominance. Sometimes the greatest enemy of success is letting perfect be the enemy of good, or allowing personal conflicts to overshadow strategic interests.

My Hope

By the end of this series, you'll understand why I believe America stands at its most critical industrial crossroads since World War II, and why the decisions made in the next two years will determine whether the United States leads or follows in the technologies that will define the next fifty years of global economic competition.

This isn't about choosing between old and new—it's about adding new capabilities while protecting existing communities and workers. The future isn't coming—it's here. The only question is whether America will write the rules or play by someone else's.
