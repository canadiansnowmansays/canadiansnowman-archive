The Pinocchio Presidency

The Pinocchio Presidency

When $20T Dreams Meet $8.8T Reality: A Pinocchio Parable for the Post-Truth Presidency

October 19, 2025

Prologue: The Workshop of Whimsy

Down in Mar-a-Lago, where the Atlantic breeze carries whispers of deals past and future, stood a workshop unlike any other. No sawdust or half-carved toys here—this was a factory of narratives, where stories grew bigger with each telling, and numbers multiplied like rabbits in springtime.

The master of this workshop was Donald, a man whose hair defied both gravity and explanation, whose ties hung perpetually askew, and whose voice could fill any room twice over. He wasn’t just a storyteller—he was an alchemist of economics, spinning thread into gold, tweets into trillions.

His latest creation? A towering tale of investments so vast they’d make Midas blush. But here’s the thing about tall tales: sometimes they grow in ways you don’t expect. And Donald was about to learn that every fib has its price—measured not in dollars, but in inches.

You see, some cosmic accountant (call her the Blue Fairy’s CPA) had decided to audit reality itself. The rules were simple: stretch the truth, and something else stretches too. In Donald’s case? His nose.

What follows is the curious chronicle of that appendage—a cautionary tale of compound interest, where the interest compounds not quarterly, but nasally.

Chapter One: The First Fib – A Centimeter of Confidence

January 21, 2025. Day one of the sequel nobody asked for but everyone got tickets to anyway.

The East Room hummed with anticipation. Cameras clicked like cicadas. Donald stepped to the podium, his voice cutting through the chatter: “Before the end of my first full business day, we’ve already secured nearly $3 trillion of new investments!”

Three. Trillion. Dollars.

The reporters scribbled furiously. X (Twitter) caught fire. Cable news hosts reached for their graphics departments.

Behind the scenes, White House staffers scrambled like contestants on a game show where nobody knew the rules. The official release, when it finally emerged, claimed a more modest $1 trillion over the “first 100 hours.” This included Apple’s $600 billion pledge (which sounded suspiciously like something they’d already promised the last guy), Nvidia’s $500 billion in “AI infrastructure” (heavy on the artificial, light on the structure), and various other commitments that read like IOUs written on cocktail napkins.

Donald’s nose twitched. Just a centimeter—barely noticeable, really. Like a typo in a tweet that autocorrect missed.

The economists, those spoilsports with their calculators and facts, pointed out an inconvenient truth: America’s total business investment for all of 2024 had been $3.5 trillion. The entire year. But somehow, in less than 24 hours, we’d nearly matched that?

The nose grew another half-inch. Donald, busy celebrating his first-day triumph, didn’t notice. “Fake news allergies,” he’d later tell his makeup artist. “It’s all the paper they print lies on.”

Chapter Two: The Springtime Sprout – Inches of Inflation

March 28, 2025. Cherry blossom season in D.C., when everything blooms—including investment figures, apparently.

“Here we are in two months,” Donald announced at a swearing-in ceremony, his arms spread wide like a ringmaster introducing the greatest show on earth. “We think we’re up to close to $5 trillion of investment!”

The White House list had grown like kudzu in August. It now featured:

  • Japan: $550 billion in “investments” (mostly loans repackaged with a bow)

  • UAE: $1.4 trillion over 10 years (their entire GDP was only $450 billion, but who’s counting?)

  • Qatar: $1.2 trillion (which would require them to spend five times their annual GDP—perhaps they’d discovered how to drill for dollars?)

  • Saudi Arabia: $600 billion (14% of their GDP every single year, assuming oil flowed like wine at Cana)

The fact-checkers—those persistent party poopers—worked overtime. Politifact’s servers crashed twice. Reuters hired extra staff. But their protests were drowned out by the trumpet of triumph.

Donald’s nose had reached three inches now, curving slightly upward like a ski jump. Makeup artists experimented with contouring. “It’s just the lighting,” they assured each other, blending furiously.

Economists emerged from their caves of calculation, blinking in the harsh light of hyperbole. Adam Posen from the Peterson Institute adjusted his glasses: “These aren’t investments, they’re intentions. And intentions, as my grandmother used to say, don’t build factories.”

But the tale grew on, fed by a diet of wishful thinking and willful misunderstanding. Pfizer pledged $70 billion in “support” (translation: a press release). Softbank and OpenAI’s Stargate project promised $500 billion, though the groundbreaking had happened under the previous administration. (”Retroactive credit,” Donald called it. “Very legal, very cool.”)

By April’s end, the nose had reached a foot in length. It entered rooms three seconds before Donald did. Secret Service agents had to adjust their formations.

Chapter Three: The Summer Swell – Feet of Fancy

Summer arrived like a fever dream. The numbers grew with the temperature.

May 4: “I think we probably have close to $9 trillion of investments.” May 6: “The real number could be $9 or $10 trillion.” May 8: “We have now close to $10 trillion—think of that, $10 trillion!”

The nose had become a living thing, a creature of its own creation. It wrapped around the Resolute Desk like a pet python. Foreign dignitaries pretended not to notice as it brushed their shoulders during handshakes. (”American innovation,” they’d murmur politely.)

The White House list struggled to keep up, topping out at $6 trillion by midsummer. But even this was a Frankenstein’s monster of mathematics:

  • Half were recycled announcements from the previous administration

  • A quarter were trade goals masquerading as investments

  • The rest were promises so vague they made fortune cookies look specific

Birds began to perch on the nose. Economists used it as a case study. Mireya Solis observed from her perch on a knot near the two-foot mark: “Japan’s supposed $1 trillion seems extremely high, given that their total U.S. investments over decades amount to $800 billion.”

Richard Rossow, nesting near the India branch at three feet, chirped: “That $500 billion from India? It’s a trade target for 2030, not an investment. You can’t deposit a trade target.”

The American people, meanwhile, had opinions of their own. Polls showed economic approval at 37%, down from 56% during the previous administration. The nose might have been growing, but confidence was shrinking.

“Where’s the money?” demanded Rep. Robert Garcia at a press conference, standing carefully outside the nose’s swing radius.

“The money,” Donald replied, the nose punctuating his words with gentle oscillations, “is tremendous. So tremendous, you wouldn’t believe how tremendous.”

Chapter Four: The Autumn Ascent – Acres of Absurdity

By September, reality had left the building, possibly to join Elvis.

“Seventeen trillion dollars,” Donald announced at the UN, the nose now requiring its own diplomatic immunity. “No country has ever seen anything like it.”

October brought new heights—literal and figurative. “Over $17 trillion being invested now,” he told reporters on Tuesday. By Friday: “I think we just cracked $18 trillion.”

The nose had transcended mere measurement. It had its own weather system, a microclimate of hot air and high pressure. The Guinness Book of World Records created a new category. Architects studied its load-bearing properties.

The White House website, that last bastion of official record-keeping, wheezed along at $8.8 trillion. Even this was inflated like a Thanksgiving parade balloon:

  • UAE’s $1.4 trillion (more than double their GDP)

  • Qatar’s $1.2 trillion (five times their GDP—had they struck unobtainium?)

  • India’s $500 billion (a trade goal wearing an investment costume)

  • Trillions in “bilateral trade,” “economic exchange,” and other terms that meant “not actually investment”

Peter Schiff, economist and professional skeptic, did the math: “If $17 trillion were real, GDP would grow 50% overnight. We’d need a new word for boom. Mega-boom? Ultra-boom? Boom-zilla?”

But math was for mortals. The nose had achieved escape velocity, a red giant in the constellation of Fabrication Major. It required its own staff:

  • A Nose Wrangler (to prevent tangling)

  • A Nose Navigator (to plot safe routes through doorways)

  • A Nose Narrator (to explain its presence to confused visitors)

Foreign leaders learned to duck. The White House installed wider doors. GPS satellites adjusted their calculations.

Chapter Five: The October Crescendo

October 19, 2025. The day of the Fox News interview that would enter history books (the honest ones, anyway).

“Maria,” Donald said, leaning forward as far as the nose would allow, “we’re over $17 trillion now. Just cracked $18 trillion, actually. Maybe $20 trillion by Thanksgiving.”

The nose, as if celebrating this new milestone, performed a small loop-de-loop. Maria Bartiromo, professional that she was, maintained eye contact with Donald’s actual eyes, not the appendage that now required its own camera operator.

“Twenty trillion would represent two-thirds of America’s entire GDP,” she noted carefully.

“Exactly!” Donald beamed. “Two-thirds! Maybe three-fourths! Who knows? Could be four-fifths by Christmas!”

In the control room, fact-checkers wept openly. Economists considered new careers in poetry, where numbers could mean anything. The nose, now visible from space, had become a tourist attraction. Gift shops sold miniature versions. Children sang songs about it.

CNN’s Daniel Dale, who had dedicated his career to tracking these claims, published his final report: “The actual figure, based on credible commitments that might actually materialize, appears to be in the hundreds of billions, not trillions.”

The Peterson Institute’s Adam Posen, now speaking from a safe distance via satellite, offered a post-mortem: “What we’ve witnessed is the monetization of mythology. It’s like claiming you’re a billionaire because you bought a lottery ticket.”

Even Trump’s own White House website seemed exhausted, its $8.8 trillion figure looking almost quaint compared to the $20 trillion being claimed. The economists who’d analyzed the list found:

  • About half were announcements from the Biden era in new hats

  • Many were normal business operations dressed up as “new investment”

  • Some weren’t investments at all, but trade targets, purchase agreements, or wishes upon stars

  • Qatar’s commitment would require them to invest 5x their GDP (perhaps they’d discovered time travel?)

Epilogue: The Moral of the Measuring Tape

And so the nose grew on, a monument to monetary mythology, a testament to the power of positive thinking taken to its illogical conclusion. Though this tale focused on the trillions, observers noted the nose seemed to twitch and stretch with each day’s new claims—about crowd sizes, poll numbers, crime statistics, and the “biggest tax cuts in history.” It was as if every exaggeration, from the grand to the mundane, added its own millimeter to the monument. The investment trillions were just the headline act in a continuous circus of creativity.

By Halloween, children dressed as the nose for trick-or-treating. By Thanksgiving, it had its own float in the parade. By Christmas, scientists studied it as a new form of matter: pure, crystallized exaggeration.

The workshop in Mar-a-Lago added new wings to accommodate the appendage. Tour guides included it in their routes: “And here we see the famous nose, which experts believe will achieve sentence by 2026—err, sorry, sentience! Freudian slip. I was thinking about another topic entirely.

What became of the investments? Well, some materialized—a few billion here, a factory there. The economy puttered along, neither booming nor busting, indifferent to the tales told about it. The American people, as they always do, worked and hoped and paid their bills, while overhead the great nose cast its shadow, a reminder that in America, you can make anything grow—except, perhaps, the truth.

The moral? In the land of fiscal fairy tales, the fact-checker is king. But the storyteller? He’s the one with the biggest nose.

And it’s still growing.
