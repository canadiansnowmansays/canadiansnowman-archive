The American Contradiction

How Anti-"Woke" Politics Betrays the Nation's Foundational Promise

Week 8: Geographic and Generational Ironies

July 23, 2025

The Places Built by Those Now Excluded

One of the most striking aspects of contemporary American anti-inclusion politics is how frequently it emerges from geographic areas that were literally built by the diverse populations now targeted for exclusion. Cities, states, and regions that owe their development to immigrant labor, minority entrepreneurship, and cultural diversity now vote for politicians promising to restrict the very processes that created their prosperity.

This irony has become particularly acute under the current Trump administration's immigration policies. Since taking office in January 2025, President Trump has implemented sweeping immigration restrictions, declaring a national emergency at the border, expanding deportation operations, and invoking the rarely-used Alien Enemies Act for removals without due process. Recent polling shows this approach is viewed more negatively than positively—42% approve while 47% disapprove—yet it continues to find strong support in areas that were historically built by the very immigrant communities now being targeted.

From a Canadian perspective, this geographic irony appears particularly self-defeating. Canadian cities like Toronto and Vancouver explicitly celebrate their multicultural character as economic assets, attracting global investment and talent. American cities with similar diversity increasingly hide or downplay this advantage, potentially undermining their competitive position.

The Irish-Built Infrastructure

Perhaps nowhere is the geographic irony clearer than in the American Northeast, where much of the 19th-century infrastructure was built by Irish immigrants who faced systematic discrimination and exclusion.

Boston: The Transformation

Boston, now a global center of education and technology, was built largely by Irish labor during the mid-19th century. Irish immigrants:

  • Constructed the city's subway system, one of America's first

  • Built much of the harbor infrastructure that made Boston a major port

  • Provided labor for the factories that drove New England's industrial development

  • Established neighborhoods that became integral to the city's character

Yet during the peak of Irish immigration, Boston's established population viewed Irish newcomers as a threat to American values and economic stability. "No Irish Need Apply" signs were common throughout the city.

Today, Boston celebrates its Irish heritage with parades and cultural festivals, but the same political constituencies often support restrictions on contemporary immigration that echo the arguments once used against the Irish. ☘️

New York: The Immigrant Metropolis

New York City represents perhaps the ultimate example of immigrant-built prosperity facing contemporary anti-immigrant politics. Every major ethnic group contributed to New York's development:

  • Irish immigrants built the Erie Canal, connecting the Great Lakes to the Atlantic

  • Italian immigrants constructed much of the city's early skyscraper infrastructure

  • Jewish immigrants established the garment industry that clothed America

  • Chinese immigrants built the transcontinental railroad's eastern terminus

  • Puerto Rican and Dominican immigrants revitalized declining neighborhoods

Yet New York state politics increasingly features anti-immigrant rhetoric that ignores this fundamental reality of the city's development.

Chicago: The Railroad Hub

Chicago's development as America's railroad hub depended entirely on immigrant labor:

  • Irish immigrants built much of the railroad infrastructure

  • Italian immigrants provided construction labor for urban development

  • Polish immigrants staffed the stockyards that made Chicago the "hog butcher to the world"

  • Mexican immigrants maintained and expanded the railroad system

Contemporary Chicago-area politics often features anti-immigrant sentiment despite the city's complete dependence on immigrant labor for its historical development.

The German-American Heartland

The American Midwest presents a particularly striking example of geographic irony, as many areas settled by German immigrants now support politicians advocating policies that would have excluded their own ancestors.

Wisconsin: The German Heritage State

Wisconsin was heavily settled by German immigrants in the 19th century, who:

  • Established the dairy farming that became the state's signature industry

  • Built Milwaukee into a major industrial center

  • Created the beer brewing industry that defined Wisconsin's culture

  • Established educational and cultural institutions throughout the state

During World War I, German-Americans faced severe persecution:

  • German language instruction was banned in schools

  • German cultural organizations were forced to disband

  • German-Americans were accused of disloyalty and treason

  • Many changed their names to avoid discrimination

Today, Wisconsin political constituencies with German heritage often support exclusionary policies similar to those that targeted their ancestors, apparently unaware of this historical parallel.

The Prairie States: Scandinavian Settlement

Minnesota, Iowa, and the Dakotas were heavily settled by Scandinavian immigrants who faced initial suspicion and discrimination:

  • Norwegian and Swedish immigrants were considered potentially unassimilable

  • Their Lutheran religious practices were viewed with suspicion by Protestant Americans

  • They were accused of maintaining foreign loyalties rather than American patriotism

  • They faced employment discrimination and social exclusion

These immigrants transformed the prairie into productive agricultural land, established cooperative farming practices that increased productivity, and created stable communities that became the foundation of Midwestern prosperity.

Contemporary politics in these states often features anti-immigrant rhetoric that ignores the immigrant foundation of regional development.

The Western Expansion: Asian and Mexican Labor

The American West presents perhaps the most dramatic examples of geographic irony, as the region's development depended entirely on the labor of groups now targeted for exclusion.

California: The Ultimate Irony

California's development into America's most populous and economically productive state required labor from groups that faced systematic exclusion:

Chinese Labor: Built the western portion of the transcontinental railroad through impossible terrain, developed agricultural techniques that made California farming profitable, and established businesses that served growing communities.

Mexican Labor: Provided agricultural labor that made California the nation's leading farming state, maintained existing Spanish mission infrastructure, and continued cultural traditions that became integral to California identity.

Japanese Labor: Developed intensive farming techniques that increased agricultural productivity, established businesses serving diverse communities, and maintained cultural traditions despite internment and discrimination.

Filipino Labor: Provided essential agricultural labor, particularly in difficult and dangerous conditions, while facing exclusion from citizenship and land ownership.

Despite this history, California politics now features significant anti-immigrant sentiment, particularly regarding contemporary Mexican and Central American immigration.

Nevada: Mining and Railroad Development

Nevada's development as a state depended entirely on:

  • Chinese labor for railroad construction and maintenance

  • Irish labor for mining operations in Virginia City and other locations

  • Mexican labor for agricultural development in the southern part of the state

  • Italian labor for urban construction in Reno and Las Vegas

Contemporary Nevada politics often features anti-immigrant rhetoric despite the state's complete dependence on immigrant labor for its development.

Texas: The Contradiction State

Texas presents the most complex geographic irony because its development involved both immigrant labor and territorial conquest from Mexico:

  • Mexican residents became Texans through territorial acquisition, not immigration

  • Irish and German immigrants provided essential labor for cattle ranching and agriculture

  • Chinese immigrants built railroad connections that linked Texas to national markets

  • Italian immigrants developed urban areas including parts of Houston and San Antonio

Texas politics now features some of the strongest anti-immigrant rhetoric in America, despite the state's fundamental dependence on Mexican and other immigrant labor for both historical development and contemporary economic function.

The Generational Amnesia: Families Forgetting Their Own Stories

Perhaps even more striking than geographic irony is generational amnesia—politicians and voters whose own families benefited from inclusion processes now advocating exclusion of contemporary immigrants.

Surname Analysis: The Personal Hypocrisy

Many prominent anti-immigration politicians bear surnames indicating immigrant ancestry:

  • Politicians with Irish surnames advocating policies that would have excluded their ancestors

  • Italian-American politicians supporting restrictions their grandparents faced

  • Politicians with Polish, German, or Scandinavian names ignoring their families' immigrant experience

  • Even politicians with recent immigrant heritage advocating exclusion of current immigrants

The most striking example may be the current president himself. Donald Trump's mother, Mary Anne MacLeod, immigrated from Scotland in 1930 at age 18 seeking economic opportunity. Born in 1912 on the Isle of Lewis in the Outer Hebrides to a fisherman father, she worked as a domestic servant in New York before marrying Fred Trump in 1936 and becoming a U.S. citizen in 1942. His grandfather Friedrich Drumpf immigrated from Germany in 1885 at age 16, eventually changing the family name to Trump. Both faced the same skepticism about foreign newcomers that contemporary anti-immigrant politics promotes.

Make Donald Drumpf Again" - satirizing the irony of anti-immigrant politics from a family that changed their name from the original German "Drumpf" to the more American-sounding "Trump.

Frederick Trump, circa 1895-1905, possibly around the time he was courting or had recently married Elizabeth Christ

For decades, the Trump family denied their German heritage, claiming Swedish ancestry to avoid anti-German sentiment that could hurt their business—exactly the kind of strategic adaptation to American prejudices that current immigration rhetoric condemns. Yet Trump's current policies, including expanded expedited removal authority and the invocation of the Alien Enemies Act for deportations without due process, would treat today's immigrants with far harsher restrictions than his own family ever faced. The irony is particularly acute given that the family name change from Drumpf to Trump represents exactly the kind of cultural adaptation that anti-immigrant politics now condemns as insufficiently American.

This represents more than political hypocrisy—it demonstrates profound historical amnesia about the American experience itself. Trump's own niece, Mary Trump, recently criticized his birthright citizenship proposals by pointing out how the 14th Amendment helped establish their family's American roots, highlighting the disconnect between his policies and his family's immigrant success story.

The Assimilation Myth

Generational amnesia often involves romanticizing ancestors' assimilation while ignoring the discrimination they faced:

  • "My grandparents came legally" (ignoring that legal immigration was much easier and less restricted)

  • "They learned English and assimilated" (ignoring that many maintained native languages and cultural practices)

  • "They didn't ask for government help" (ignoring mutual aid societies, ethnic organizations, and community support systems)

  • "They became Americans immediately" (ignoring decades of discrimination and gradual acceptance)

The Selection Effect

Descendants of successful immigrants may forget that many of their compatriots faced continued exclusion, returned to home countries, or never achieved full acceptance. Survivor bias creates false narratives about easy assimilation.

Family Story Sanitization

Generational amnesia often involves sanitizing family immigration stories:

  • Forgetting discrimination faced by ancestors

  • Minimizing economic desperation that drove immigration

  • Ignoring mutual aid and community support that enabled success

  • Romanticizing "rugged individualism" that never actually existed

The Economic Geography of Irony

The geographic and generational ironies have significant economic implications that contradict the interests of the areas supporting exclusionary policies.

Rural Labor Dependence

Many rural areas supporting anti-immigrant politicians depend entirely on immigrant labor:

  • Agricultural regions require seasonal and permanent immigrant workers

  • Meat processing plants depend on immigrant labor for dangerous, low-paid work

  • Construction and maintenance work in rural areas often relies on immigrant workers

  • Service industries in rural areas increasingly depend on immigrant entrepreneurs

Anti-immigrant policies threaten the economic foundation of these areas.

Urban Revitalization

Many formerly declining urban areas have been revitalized by immigrant communities:

  • Immigrant entrepreneurs establish businesses in abandoned commercial districts

  • Immigrant families restore abandoned housing and stabilize neighborhoods

  • Immigrant cultural institutions attract tourism and investment

  • Immigrant workers staff essential services and infrastructure maintenance

Policies that discourage immigration threaten continued urban revitalization.

Debunking Contemporary Myths

The economic revitalization that immigrants bring to declining areas is often obscured by fabricated cultural fears that echo historical patterns of xenophobia. Recent claims about Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio allegedly eating pets—thoroughly debunked by local officials and fact-checkers—represent the same pattern of dehumanizing myths that targeted previous immigrant groups. Irish immigrants were once accused of being inherently violent and unassimilable. Chinese immigrants faced the "Yellow Peril" narrative claiming they would destroy American civilization. Italian immigrants were stereotyped as naturally criminal. Jewish immigrants were portrayed as economic parasites.

Today's immigrants, like their predecessors, are opening restaurants that become community gathering places, starting small businesses that employ local residents, renovating abandoned properties that increase neighborhood property values, and sending their children to local schools where they often excel academically. The Springfield case is particularly instructive: while false rumors about pet consumption spread rapidly on social media, the actual story was of Haitian immigrants revitalizing a declining rust belt city through entrepreneurship and community investment—exactly the pattern that has strengthened American communities for generations.

The persistence of these dehumanizing myths reveals how anti-immigrant politics relies on manufactured cultural anxieties rather than economic realities. Communities that embrace their new residents prosper; those that succumb to fear-mongering miss opportunities for renewal and growth.

Demographic Challenges

Many areas supporting anti-immigrant policies face demographic challenges that immigration could address:

  • Aging populations require younger workers and taxpayers

  • Population decline threatens economic viability and political representation

  • Brain drain to coastal cities leaves skill gaps that immigration could fill

  • Low birth rates require immigration to maintain population stability

Anti-immigrant policies exacerbate rather than address these demographic challenges.

The Canadian Example: Embracing Geographic Advantage

Canadian cities provide useful examples of how embracing rather than hiding diversity creates economic advantages:

Toronto: The Multicultural Metropolis

Toronto explicitly markets its diversity as an economic asset:

  • Over 50% of residents were born outside Canada

  • Over 200 ethnic origins are represented in the metropolitan area

  • Diversity is promoted as attracting international business and investment

  • Multicultural competence is viewed as essential for global competition

This approach has made Toronto one of North America's most economically dynamic cities.

Vancouver: The Pacific Gateway

Vancouver leverages its Asian population to compete for Pacific Rim business:

  • Large Chinese and South Asian populations provide cultural and linguistic connections

  • Immigration is viewed as essential for economic growth and competitiveness

  • Diversity is promoted as a competitive advantage in global markets

  • Cultural festivals and institutions attract tourism and investment

Montreal: Bilingual and Multicultural

Montreal combines French-English bilingualism with multiculturalism:

  • Immigration is seen as essential for maintaining population and economic growth

  • Diverse communities contribute to cultural industries and tourism

  • Multilingual capabilities attract international business

  • Cultural diversity is viewed as strengthening rather than weakening Quebec identity

These Canadian examples demonstrate how embracing diversity creates rather than threatens economic prosperity.

The Political Geography of Contradiction

The geographic distribution of anti-immigrant politics often contradicts local economic interests:

Swing State Contradictions

Key swing states in American elections often depend on immigrant labor while supporting anti-immigrant politicians:

  • Florida agriculture depends on immigrant labor while the state supports restrictive immigration policies

  • North Carolina relies on immigrant workers in agriculture and manufacturing while voting for anti-immigrant candidates

  • Arizona's economy depends significantly on Mexican trade and labor while supporting border restrictions

Congressional District Analysis

Many congressional districts that elect anti-immigrant representatives have economies dependent on immigrant labor:

  • Agricultural districts rely on seasonal immigrant workers

  • Manufacturing districts depend on immigrant workers for difficult, low-paid jobs

  • Service industry districts require immigrant entrepreneurs and workers

This suggests that anti-immigrant politics serves symbolic rather than economic functions.

Local vs. National Politics

The contradiction often appears between local and national politics:

  • Local governments recognize economic benefits of immigration

  • Local businesses depend on immigrant workers and consumers

  • Local communities benefit from immigrant cultural contributions

  • Yet the same areas elect national politicians advocating exclusionary policies

International Implications of Geographic Irony

The geographic ironies in American anti-immigrant politics have significant international implications:

Economic Competition

Countries that embrace immigration gain competitive advantages over those that restrict it:

  • Canada attracts immigrants that might otherwise go to the United States

  • Australia benefits from American immigration restrictions by attracting global talent

  • European countries compete for skilled immigrants that American policies discourage

Diplomatic Relations

Anti-immigrant policies in areas built by immigrants create diplomatic complications:

  • Mexico must navigate relations with Texas politicians who deny Mexican contributions to Texas development

  • Ireland observes Irish-American politicians advocating policies that would have excluded their ancestors

  • Italy sees Italian-American politicians supporting exclusion similar to what their families faced

Cultural Soft Power

America's cultural influence historically came partly from its immigrant success stories:

  • The "American Dream" attracted global admiration because immigrants could succeed

  • American multiculturalism was viewed as a model for other diverse societies

  • American inclusion policies enhanced soft power and global influence

Geographic and generational amnesia undermines this soft power advantage.

The Future of Geographic Politics

Demographic trends suggest that geographic and generational ironies will intensify:

Continuing Immigration

Despite political restrictions, immigration will continue due to:

  • Economic necessity in labor-dependent industries

  • Family reunification that continues regardless of policy changes

  • Global economic and political pressures that drive migration

  • Geographic proximity that makes some immigration inevitable

Generational Change

Younger Americans in all geographic areas tend to be more accepting of diversity:

  • Higher exposure to diversity through education and media

  • Greater likelihood of having diverse friends and romantic partners

  • Less attachment to exclusionary traditions and practices

  • Economic recognition that diversity enhances rather than threatens prosperity

Economic Reality

Economic pressures will eventually force recognition of immigration benefits:

  • Labor shortages in key industries will require policy adjustments

  • Demographic challenges will make immigration essential for economic stability

  • Global competition will require diverse, internationally connected workforces

  • Local economic benefits will eventually overcome symbolic politics

The Irony of Success

Perhaps the greatest irony is that many areas now supporting exclusionary policies are successful precisely because of the inclusion processes they now reject. The prosperity, infrastructure, and cultural richness that make these places desirable resulted from the same diversity and immigration that current politics seeks to restrict.

From a Canadian perspective, where diversity is explicitly celebrated as a source of strength rather than hidden as a source of embarrassment, the American pattern of geographic and generational amnesia appears tragically self-defeating.

The choice facing these communities is whether to recognize and build upon their diverse foundations or to continue pursuing exclusionary policies that contradict both their histories and their economic interests. The historical pattern suggests that inclusion will eventually prevail, but the question is whether American communities will choose this path proactively or only after the costs of exclusion become undeniable.

The geographic and generational ironies reveal the profound historical disconnection underlying American anti-inclusion politics. The very places and people that benefited most from inclusion now advocate most strongly for exclusion, creating contradictions that undermine both their stated values and practical interests. This suggests that anti-inclusion politics serves psychological rather than economic functions, appealing to identity concerns rather than addressing actual problems.

The current immigration crackdown, with its expanded deportation operations and invocation of historical emergency powers, represents the most aggressive implementation of these exclusionary policies in modern American history. Yet polling shows declining public support, and the fundamental demographic and economic pressures that make immigration beneficial remain unchanged.

The choice facing American communities is whether to recognize and build upon their diverse heritage or continue pursuing exclusionary policies that contradict both their history and economic interests.
