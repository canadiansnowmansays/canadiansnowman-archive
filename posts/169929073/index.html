How the GOP's Desperate Redistricting Tactics Could Get Even More Ridiculous

August 2, 2025

Editor's Introduction

In what appears to be a desperate attempt to shore up their razor-thin House majority ahead of the 2026 midterms, Republicans are pursuing increasingly aggressive gerrymandering tactics that suggest a party grasping at straws. With their current three-seat advantage looking precarious and historical patterns favoring the opposition party in midterm elections, the GOP's push for mid-decade redistricting in Texas—potentially netting them up to five additional seats—reads like the political equivalent of a Hail Mary pass.

The urgency is palpable: President Trump is personally pressuring governors in multiple red states to redraw their maps, while Republican leaders openly admit they're trying to "win elections long before people vote." This brazen acknowledgment that they're attempting to predetermine electoral outcomes through cartographic manipulation rather than winning voters through policy suggests a party that lacks confidence in its ability to maintain power through traditional democratic means.

What follows is a satirical exploration of how such desperate gerrymandering tactics might evolve if taken to their logical extreme—because sometimes the best way to highlight the absurdity of real political maneuvering is to imagine it becoming even more absurd.

The Great Gayermandering of Texas: A Political Satire

AUSTIN, TX — In what political scientists are calling the most fabulous redistricting scandal in American history, Texas Republicans have unveiled their latest electoral strategy: "gayermandering" — the systematic redrawing of district lines to exclude areas with high concentrations of rainbow flags, well-maintained lawns, and brunch establishments.

The Master Plan Unveiled

Governor Greg Abbott announced the initiative at a press conference held conspicuously far from Austin's famous South by Southwest venue. "We've discovered that certain neighborhoods have an unusually high density of people who know the difference between taupe and beige," Abbott declared, adjusting his tie nervously. "This clearly represents an unfair advantage in aesthetic judgment that could influence elections."

The proposed maps, leaked by an anonymous source known only as "Deep Throat... er, Deep Pride," reveal districts that snake around entire city blocks to avoid local drag brunches, carefully excluding any area within a five-mile radius of a Trader Joe's, and creating bizarre geographical formations that political cartographers describe as "looking like a boa constrictor trying to avoid a Pride parade."

Expert Analysis

Dr. Ima Straightlace, Director of the Institute for Very Serious Political Science at Definitely Not Biased University, explained the strategy: "Traditional gerrymandering targets party affiliation or race. Gayermandering is more sophisticated — it targets zip codes with above-average spending on home décor and neighborhoods where people actually know their neighbors' names."

The most controversial aspect involves the proposed "Don't Say Gay District," a bizarrely shaped constituency that connects rural areas through a thin corridor that carefully avoids every coffee shop that serves oat milk. Local barista Chad Pumpkinspice called it "literally the worst thing to happen to specialty beverages since Starbucks burned their espresso."

Republican Justifications

Members of Austin’s LGBTQ community gather on the steps of the Texas Capitol to celebrate the anniversary of the 1969 Stonewall riots. Republican lawmakers are pushing bills that could upend the lives of LGBTQ people this year.

State Senator Buck Tradition defended the maps at a heated legislative session. "Look, we're not anti-anybody," he insisted while nervously eyeing a rainbow bumper sticker on a nearby car. "We just think districts should be drawn based on... uh... traditional geometric principles. You know, like avoiding areas where people coordinate their outfits too well."

Representative Mary Normalcy added, "These new districts will ensure that every voter has equal representation, as long as they shop exclusively at Bass Pro Shops and consider khaki a fashion statement."

Democratic Response

Democratic leaders have denounced the practice, with Representative Alexandria Openly-Fabulous calling it "the most transparent attempt to silence voices since someone tried to cancel RuPaul."

The Texas Democratic Party issued a statement: "This gayermandering scheme is clearly designed to create districts where the only Pride month is about being proud of your truck's horsepower. We demand fair representation for all Texans, regardless of whether they can parallel park or coordinate seasonal decorations."

Legal Challenges Brewing

Civil rights attorney Sue Everybody announced plans to challenge the maps in federal court. "You can't just exclude people because they throw better parties than you," she argued. "The Constitution guarantees equal protection, not equal protection unless you know how to fold fitted sheets properly."

The case, Fabulous v. Texas, is expected to reach the Supreme Court, where Justice Clarence Thomas will presumably recuse himself due to his wife's extensive collection of lawn flamingos.

Community Impact

The gayermandering has already had tangible effects on affected communities. West Austin resident Kevin Whatshisface reported, "Ever since the new district lines were drawn around our block, we've been politically orphaned. Our votes literally don't count because we live within walking distance of a farmers market."

Local business owner Tiffany Sparkles noted the economic impact: "My boutique cupcake shop is now technically in three different districts. The regulatory nightmare is affecting my gluten-free rainbow layer cake production."

The Opposition Organizes

In response to the gayermandering, a grassroots organization called "Queers for Fair Squares" has formed to fight the redistricting. Their slogan: "Our votes are straight even if we're not!"

Co-founder Derek Absolutely-Not-Compensating explained their strategy: "We're organizing voter registration drives at every Home Depot in Texas. If they want to play geographic politics, we'll show them that rainbow voters exist everywhere — even in places that sell cargo shorts."

Expert Predictions

Political analyst Dr. Noway Thishappens warns that gayermandering could backfire spectacularly. "When you try to concentrate all the people with good taste into a few districts, you might accidentally create the most effectively organized political force in state history. Plus, they'll probably have really good campaign event catering."

A Slippery Slope?

Some worry that gayermandering could inspire copycat efforts nationwide. Already, rumors suggest Florida is considering "straightomandering" — creating districts specifically designed to include areas with higher concentrations of cargo shorts and unseasoned chicken.

Meanwhile, California is reportedly exploring "organicomandering," which would group voters based on their grocery shopping habits and kombucha consumption patterns.

The Bottom Line

As Texas prepares for what promises to be the most colorful legal battle in recent memory, one thing is certain: the Lone Star State's attempt to rainbow-proof its electoral maps has only succeeded in drawing more attention to the very communities it sought to marginalize.

As one anonymous Capitol insider put it: "You can gerrymander all you want, but you can't gerrymander good taste. And honey, after this mess, we know who has it and who doesn't."

This is a work of satirical fiction. No actual gayermandering schemes were harmed in the making of this article.
