America's Next Industrial Revolution

The Race for Full Self-Driving

Episode 4: “Global Competition: Mapping the Autonomous Vehicle Landscape”

October 23, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
October 23, 2025

In the conference rooms of Beijing, executives at Baidu are studying Tesla’s Austin robotaxi deployment with intense interest. Not because they’re worried about falling behind—but because they’re confident they’re already ahead.

While Silicon Valley has been writing obituaries for failed autonomous vehicle projects, Chinese companies have quietly been deploying robotaxis across multiple cities. Baidu’s Apollo Go operates in over 10 Chinese cities. Pony.ai has expanded internationally. WeRide runs commercial operations across Asia.

After the industry’s massive consolidation, a new competitive landscape has emerged. The question is no longer whether autonomous vehicles will work, but which approach will scale globally and which regions will control the technology that reshapes transportation.

The September 2025 Breakthrough: Dubai, Singapore, and the Race Goes Global

September 24, 2025 marked a watershed moment for the autonomous vehicle industry—just not where most American observers were looking.

On that day, Baidu received Dubai’s first autonomous driving trial permit, with 50 test licenses issued and an ambitious plan to deploy 1,000+ fully driverless vehicles by 2028. This followed Baidu’s July-August partnerships with Uber (multi-year agreement for Asia and Middle East deployment) and Lyft (European deployment starting Germany and UK).

Four days earlier, on September 20, both Pony.ai and WeRide simultaneously announced Singapore launches in the same Punggol district—creating the world’s most competitive autonomous vehicle testing ground in a single neighborhood.

Pony.ai partnered with ComfortDelGro, Singapore’s largest taxi operator. WeRide partnered with Grab Holdings, Southeast Asia’s dominant ride-hailing platform. Singapore’s Ministry of Transport aims for AV integration in public housing by end-2025, making the city-state the critical battleground for international expansion.

The message was clear: while America consolidates, China expands.

Waymo’s Dominance—And Its Limits

Let’s start with the unambiguous U.S. leader. Waymo now provides 250,000+ paid trips per week (as of June 2025, continuing through October), operating a fleet of 700-1,500 vehicles across four cities (San Francisco, Phoenix, Los Angeles, Austin) with over 5 million cumulative lifetime rides.

The company achieved what separates it from all U.S. competitors: full driverless operation—with no safety driver in the vehicle—years before others. This isn’t supervised autonomy. This is true Level 4.

The company’s 2025 expansion demonstrates aggressive scaling:

  • Atlanta, Georgia launched June 24, 2025 through Uber partnership

  • Bay Area expansion to Burlingame, Brisbane, South San Francisco, San Bruno, Millbrae, Hillsborough, Mountain View, Palo Alto, Los Altos, Sunnyvale

  • Los Angeles extended to 120+ square miles covering Playa del Rey, Ladera Heights, Echo Park, Silver Lake, full Sunset Boulevard

  • Future launches scheduled: Miami, Washington D.C., Dallas (2026), Nashville, with Avis Budget Group partnership

Waymo’s safety performance continues improving, approaching U.S. human accident rates at approximately one accident per 700,000 miles—representing 3x improvement since mid-2024. The company operates a new manufacturing facility in Metro Phoenix producing 2,000+ Jaguar I-PACEs annually, with plans to integrate 6th generation Waymo Driver on Zeekr RT platforms.

But here’s what’s striking: Waymo operates exclusively in the United States. Zero international presence. No international partnerships announced. Complete focus on domestic expansion.

Meanwhile, Chinese competitors are going everywhere.

The Chinese Expansion Blitz

Baidu Apollo Go’s Q2 2025 numbers tell a story of explosive growth:

  • 2.2 million fully driverless rides (148% year-over-year growth)

  • 14+ million cumulative rides as of August 2025

  • 16 cities globally with operations

  • Unit-level profitability achieved in Wuhan—the first major AV operator to reach this milestone

That last point deserves emphasis. While Waymo burns $1.2+ billion quarterly in Alphabet’s Other Bets losses, Baidu has proven robotaxi unit economics can work by leveraging Wuhan’s taxi fares (30% below tier-one cities) and the RT6 robotaxi platform with production costs below $30,000 per unit.

William Blair analyst Jed Dorschimer’s August 2025 assessment of Tesla versus Waymo included a telling observation about Chinese competition: “The real question isn’t whether Tesla can compete with Waymo—it’s whether either can compete with Chinese companies on cost.”

Pony.ai and WeRide: The International Expansion Playbook

Pony.ai’s September 2025 surge captured global attention. The stock jumped 70%+ during September following Citigroup’s initiation of coverage with a $29 price target, as investors recognized the company’s international expansion momentum.

Beyond Singapore, Pony.ai operates in:

  • Dubai and Qatar (September 2025 expansion)

  • Multiple Chinese cities with commercial operations

  • U.S. testing (though commercial operations blocked)

WeRide launched its “Autonomously Intelligent Ride” (Ai.R) service in Singapore with an 11-vehicle fleet on two designated routes, while maintaining operations across:

  • Belgium (Leuven Robobus)

  • France (fully driverless)

  • 11 international markets as of September 2025

The playbook is clear: Chinese AV companies blocked from U.S. commercial operations are targeting every other major market. Europe, Middle East, and Asia are becoming the primary battlegrounds.

Zoox’s Purpose-Built Gambit

Zoox marked a significant September 2025 milestone with its Las Vegas Strip launch on September 10, offering free rides from Top Golf, Area15, Resorts World, and NY-NY Hotel & Casino locations while awaiting regulatory approval for paid service.

What makes Zoox different: a purpose-built vehicle with:

  • No steering wheel or pedals

  • Bidirectional operation (drives equally well forward or reverse)

  • Four-passenger face-to-face seating

  • Designed from scratch for autonomous operation

Zoox’s Hayward, California production facility opened June 2025 with capacity for 10,000 robotaxis annually. The company applied for NHTSA’s “555 exemption” on September 24, 2025 to enable commercial operation despite not meeting 8 Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards designed for human-driven vehicles.

Amazon’s backing gives Zoox the patient capital to pursue this fundamentally different approach. Whether purpose-built vehicles or retrofitted production cars win the robotaxi race remains an open question.

Geographic Segmentation: Three Distinct Competitive Spheres

The October 2025 competitive landscape reveals stark geographic segmentation:

United States:

  • Waymo maintains commercial dominance (250,000+ weekly rides)

  • Tesla expanding but constrained by safety driver requirements

  • Zoox launching limited operations in Las Vegas

  • Chinese companies blocked from commercial operations

  • Regulatory environment: State-by-state complexity, Trump administration support for easing restrictions

China:

  • 2,300+ robotaxis operating across 30 cities

  • Baidu, Pony.ai, WeRide, AutoX, SAIC all scaling

  • Government coordination enabling rapid deployment

  • 85% consumer comfort with unsupervised autonomy (vs. 39% in U.S.)

  • National regulatory framework versus America’s patchwork

International Battleground (Europe, Middle East, Asia):

  • Chinese companies expanding aggressively (Baidu Dubai, Pony.ai/WeRide Singapore)

  • European markets as primary competition zone where Chinese tech competes with local players

  • Middle East emerging as surprisingly fertile ground (GM Super Cruise launch September 2025, Baidu Dubai permits)

  • QCraft opening German headquarters, DeepRoute.ai at IAA Mobility Munich, Momenta partnering with Uber for Munich testing 2026

Consumer Acceptance: The Cultural Divide

The data reveals a massive advantage for Chinese deployment:

  • 85% of Chinese consumers report comfort with unsupervised autonomy

  • 39% of U.S. adults report similar comfort

  • 72% of Chinese adults trust AI generally

  • 32% of U.S. adults trust AI per 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer

This isn’t just a technology race—it’s a cultural acceptance race. China’s combination of higher AI trust, government coordination, and unified regulatory framework creates fundamentally different conditions for scaling autonomous vehicle services.

Phil Beisel’s analysis: “The technology debate matters less than the economics debate. Whoever achieves acceptable safety at the lowest cost wins. Right now, Chinese companies are demonstrating that path.”

The Policy Contradiction: Regulatory Support Without Economic Backing

The geographic and cultural advantages China demonstrates raise questions about U.S. policy coherence. While the Trump administration signals support for easing federal regulations to help U.S. companies “beat China,” the elimination of the $7,500 EV tax credit (expired September 30, 2025) removes a key competitive advantage for U.S. manufacturers.

The visual evidence in this episode tells the story: Chinese companies expanding to Dubai, Singapore, and Europe while U.S. players remain domestic. This isn’t just about technology—it’s about government support structures. China’s RT6 platform achieves $30,000 production costs through extensive supply chain subsidies and coordinated industrial policy. Meanwhile, Tesla competes against subsidized Chinese vehicles with diminishing federal incentives.

The September 2025 expansion announcements crystallize the asymmetry: Baidu, Pony.ai, and WeRide receive both regulatory coordination and economic support. U.S. companies get regulatory reform promises but reduced financial backing. Achieving the administration’s stated goal to “beat China” requires examining whether regulatory streamlining alone can compensate for the competitive advantage that coordinated government support—regulatory and financial—provides.

Conclusion: A Divided World

The October 2025 autonomous vehicle landscape reveals a world dividing into distinct competitive spheres rather than a single global market.

Waymo dominates U.S. commercial operations but shows no international expansion plans. Tesla pursues its unique vision-only approach with uncertain timelines. Chinese companies achieve unit-level profitability and expand aggressively into international markets where U.S. players aren’t competing.

The race isn’t just about technology—it’s about which strategic approach can achieve global scale while maintaining sustainable economics. The September 2025 announcements crystallized what industry insiders already knew: the autonomous vehicle future won’t be controlled by a single player or approach. Instead, different strategies will succeed in different markets, shaped by regulatory environments, cultural acceptance, and economic constraints.

As we’ll explore in the next episode, these strategic differences stem from fundamentally divergent technology approaches—and Tesla’s rapid expansion represents a bet that may reshape the entire competitive landscape if it succeeds.
