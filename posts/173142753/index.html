The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

Part 2: Prayer Without Policy - The Minneapolis Test Case

September 9, 2025

"It is shocking to me that so many left wing politicians attack the idea of prayer in response to a tragedy. Why do you feel the need to attack other people for praying when kids were just killed praying?" — Vice President JD Vance

Those words came from the Vice President of the United States just hours after two children—ages 8 and 10—were gunned down while attending Mass at Annunciation Catholic Church in Minneapolis. Seventeen others were wounded, including 14 children between ages 6 and 15. These kids were literally praying when they died.

Vance's response perfectly encapsulates the spiritual fraud at the heart of this administration: when confronted with the failure of their "thoughts and prayers" approach to America's gun violence epidemic, they don't offer solutions—they attack those calling for action as somehow anti-faith.

This is prayer without policy. Performance without protection. Sacred theater while children die in sacred spaces.

The Shooting: When Prayers Met Bullets

On August 27, 2025, Robin Westman opened fire through the windows of Annunciation Catholic Church during the school's weekly Mass. The 23-year-old shooter had legally purchased multiple weapons—a rifle, shotgun, and pistol—and planned the attack carefully, even placing wooden boards through door handles to trap victims inside.

The children killed were Fletcher Merkel, 8, and Harper Moyski, 10. Among the wounded was 12-year-old Lydia Kaiser, who suffered a traumatic brain injury while shielding a younger student. She underwent multiple brain surgeries and required a decompressive hemicraniectomy to relieve pressure on her brain.

These weren't statistics. These were children whose only "crime" was gathering for prayer in what should have been a sanctuary.

The Performative Response

The administration's response followed a predictable script: offer prayers, attack critics, change nothing.

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt called criticism of empty "thoughts and prayers" "incredibly insensitive and disrespectful to the tens of millions of Americans who find comfort in prayer." She missed the point entirely—no one was attacking prayer itself, but rather the cynical use of prayer as a substitute for action.

House Speaker Mike Johnson, following the same playbook, declared: "At the end of the day the problem is not guns. The problem is the human heart. It's mental health." This deflection has become so routine it's almost liturgical: blame everything except the weapons that make America uniquely deadly among developed nations.

But the most revealing response came from Vance himself, who framed any call for policy action as an attack on faith: "Why does it have to be one or the other? Why can't you pray for the speedy recovery of these kids who literally just got shot yesterday?"

The answer is simple: because prayer without policy isn't protection—it's performance.

The Minneapolis Mayor's Challenge

Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey cut through the performative fog with moral clarity that exposed the administration's spiritual fraud:

"Don't just say this is about thoughts and prayers right now. These kids were literally praying. These are sacred places. But these are not the only sacred places. Schools are sacred. Classrooms are sacred. You should be able to send your kid off to enjoy their day without the concern of them having this severe safety risk."

Frey continued: "Prayers are welcome. Thoughts certainly welcome. But that's not enough. These kids were literally praying in church. And it is on us right now, not just to sit back. It's on all of us to take the necessary actions so that this kind of thing doesn't happen again."

This is what authentic faith-based leadership looks like: acknowledging the power of prayer while recognizing that God calls believers to action, not just contemplation.

The Families' Plea for Action

Perhaps most damning was the response from the victims' families themselves. The Kaisers, whose daughter Lydia was critically wounded, met with Vice President Vance and issued a direct challenge:

"Will you please promise to pursue, despite powerful lobbies, some commonsense bipartisan legislation as a starting point, so we can come out of our corners and find the values that we share so that this time some progress is made? Thoughts and prayers haven't been enough. Many policies have been dismissed without even being studied or tried."

The family of former Minnesota State Rep. Melissa Hortman and her husband Mark—themselves victims of gun violence in June—echoed this call: "We hope this tragedy spurs elected officials to take action towards common-sense measures on access to high-powered weapons so that no one else must suffer."

These weren't political operatives making partisan attacks. These were grieving families, speaking from the deepest pain imaginable, begging leaders to do more than pray.

The Theological Problem with Performance Prayer

The administration's response to Minneapolis reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of Christian teaching about prayer and action. In Christian theology, prayer isn't meant to replace action—it's meant to inspire and guide it.

James 2:17 states explicitly: "Faith by itself, if it is not accompanied by action, is dead." The Epistle of James continues: "Suppose a brother or sister is without clothes and daily food. If one of you says to them, 'Go in peace; keep warm and well fed,' but does nothing about their physical needs, what good is it?"

This is precisely what the administration does with gun violence: they offer spiritual comfort while refusing to address physical needs. They say "go in peace" to grieving families while maintaining policies that ensure more families will grieve.

Real Christian teaching demands both prayer AND action. When Jesus fed the multitudes, he didn't just pray for their hunger to be satisfied—he fed them. When he encountered the sick, he didn't just offer thoughts and prayers—he healed them.

The International Embarrassment

The administration's "thoughts and prayers" response becomes even more inexcusable when compared to international examples. After the Dunblane school shooting in 1996, the United Kingdom implemented strict gun controls. Mass school shootings in the UK: zero since then.

After the Port Arthur massacre in 1996, Australia enacted comprehensive gun reforms. Mass shootings in Australia: virtually eliminated.

After the Christchurch shooting in 2019, New Zealand banned military-style weapons within weeks. Prime Minister Jacinda Ardern didn't just pray—she acted.

As Mayor Frey noted: "There are certainly other countries around the world where a horrific act takes place and then they make the change to ensure that out of love for their kids, out of love for their children, it doesn't happen again."

These nations understood what the Trump administration refuses to acknowledge: that prayer without policy isn't protection—it's negligent performance while children die.

The Weaponization of Faith Against Reform

Perhaps most insidious is how the administration weaponizes faith itself against common-sense reforms. By framing gun control advocacy as attacks on prayer, they create a false theological choice: you can either be faithful and support gun rights, or be secular and support gun control.

This is spiritual manipulation at its worst. It forces Christian voters to choose between their faith and their children's safety—a choice that authentic Christianity never demands.

Real Christian teaching supports both prayer and action to protect the innocent. The prophet Isaiah commanded: "Learn to do right; seek justice. Defend the oppressed. Take up the cause of the fatherless; plead the case of the widow" (Isaiah 1:17).

When children are dying in schools and churches, authentic Christian faith demands we both pray for comfort AND act for protection. The administration's false choice between faith and action is itself a betrayal of Christian teaching.

The Shooter's Profile: When Hate Meets Easy Access

The Minneapolis shooting also revealed the deadly combination of hate and easy access to weapons. Police Chief Brian O'Hara reported that Westman "harbored a whole lot of hate towards a wide variety of people and groups" and "had a deranged obsession with previous mass shooters."

Acting U.S. Attorney Joe Thompson confirmed: "The shooter expressed hate towards almost every group. The shooter expressed hate towards Black people, the shooter expressed hate towards Mexican people, the shooter expressed hate towards Christian people, the shooter expressed hate towards [Jews and Trump]."

Yet this person, filled with documented hatred toward multiple groups, was able to legally purchase multiple weapons. The system worked exactly as the administration wants it to—guns available to anyone, regardless of their documented hatred or instability.

This isn't about mental health, as Speaker Johnson claimed. This is about a system that makes it easier to buy weapons of mass killing than to get a driver's license.

The Failed Deflection Strategy

The administration's deflection from guns to mental health might be more credible if they actually invested in mental health services. Instead, they've consistently cut mental health funding while expanding gun access.

Their deflection to "the human heart" might be more persuasive if they demonstrated any concern for the human hearts of victims' families. Instead, they offer empty prayers while maintaining policies that guarantee more hearts will be broken.

The Minneapolis shooting exposed this deflection strategy as morally bankrupt. When pressed by grieving families for action, the administration had nothing to offer but more thoughts, more prayers, and more attacks on those demanding change.

The Cost of Performance Prayer

The real cost of the administration's "prayer without policy" approach isn't measured in political points—it's measured in children's lives. Since taking office, the administration has overseen:

  • Multiple school shootings, including Minneapolis

  • Continued easy access to weapons for hate-filled individuals

  • Cuts to violence prevention programs

  • Attacks on common-sense safety measures

Each "thoughts and prayers" response enables the next tragedy. Each deflection from policy to prayer virtually guarantees more families will need comfort because their children won't come home.

This isn't governance—it's gross negligence wrapped in religious language.

What Authentic Faith-Based Leadership Looks Like

Real Christian leadership in the face of gun violence would look fundamentally different. It would combine genuine prayer with decisive action. It would seek God's guidance AND implement God's call to protect the innocent.

Authentic faith-based responses to Minneapolis would include:

  • Universal background checks - ensuring dangerous individuals can't easily access weapons

  • Red flag laws - removing weapons from those who pose dangers to themselves or others

  • Assault weapon restrictions - limiting access to weapons designed for maximum casualties

  • Safe storage requirements - preventing children from accessing deadly weapons

  • Violence prevention funding - investing in programs that address root causes

These aren't anti-faith positions—they're pro-life positions that recognize God's call to protect the vulnerable.

The Families' Continued Advocacy

The victims' families haven't been deterred by the administration's performative responses. The Kaisers continue advocating for reform, with Lydia's father Harry stating: "Thoughts and prayers haven't been enough. Many policies have been dismissed without even being studied or tried."

Their advocacy represents authentic faith in action—people who've experienced the worst possible loss using their pain to prevent others from suffering similarly. This is what real Christian witness looks like in the face of tragedy.

The International Response

The international community watched Minneapolis with familiar horror—another American tragedy that could have been prevented with policies that work everywhere else. European leaders offered condolences while privately wondering how Americans can watch children die in churches and still maintain policies that guarantee more will die.

The administration's response reinforced global perceptions of American exceptionalism at its most destructive: the only developed nation where children regularly die in schools and churches, led by politicians who offer prayer instead of protection.

The Theological Verdict

From a Christian perspective, the administration's response to Minneapolis represents a fundamental betrayal of biblical teaching. When Jesus said "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them" (Matthew 19:14), he didn't mean "offer thoughts and prayers after they're gunned down in church."

Christian scripture consistently calls believers to both prayer and action. The parable of the Good Samaritan doesn't end with the Samaritan praying for the beaten man—it ends with him providing practical help. The story of feeding the 5,000 doesn't conclude with Jesus praying for their hunger—it concludes with him feeding them.

The administration's "prayer without policy" approach would be foreign to the Jesus they claim to follow.

The Minneapolis Test: Failed

The Minneapolis shooting was a test of the administration's claimed Christian values. When children were killed while praying, would leaders who wrap themselves in religious imagery respond with genuine Christian action?

They failed spectacularly. Instead of protection, they offered performance. Instead of policy, they offered prayer. Instead of action, they offered attacks on those demanding change.

The families of Fletcher Merkel and Harper Moyski deserved better. The wounded children deserved better. The American people deserve better.

Prayer without policy isn't protection—it's performance that enables the next tragedy. Until the administration understands this basic truth, more children will die while their leaders pray for the cameras and do nothing to save lives.

Minneapolis was their test. Their response was their verdict: guilty of gross negligence wrapped in sacred theater.

The children deserve leaders who understand that authentic faith demands both prayer and action. They deserve protection, not performance.

They deserve better than thoughts and prayers from leaders whose policies guarantee more prayers will be needed.

The Charlie Kirk Test: Performance Prayer's Ultimate Failure

Just days after this analysis was written, America received perhaps the most devastating example yet of how "prayer without policy" enables tragedy rather than preventing it. On September 10, 2025, Charlie Kirk—a 31-year-old conservative activist and founder of Turning Point USA—was assassinated while speaking at Utah Valley University, shot by a single bullet to the neck in front of hundreds of witnesses.

Here was a tragedy that should have united Americans around shared democratic values: a young father of two, killed for his political beliefs, gunned down while exercising the very free speech rights that define American democracy. Instead, the administration's response followed the exact same performative playbook documented throughout this series—and revealed the moral bankruptcy of "prayer without policy" in its starkest form.

Trump's Immediate Exploitation

Within six hours of Kirk's death, Trump delivered an Oval Office address that prioritized partisan blame over national unity, offering no evidence, no policy solutions, and no calls for healing. Political commentator Ed Krassenstein called it "one of the most divisive and disgraceful speeches by any president in the aftermath of someone's death."

Trump declared: "For years, those on the radical left have compared wonderful Americans like Charlie to Nazis and the world's worst mass murderers and criminals. This rhetoric is directly responsible for the terrorism that we're seeing in our country today, and it must stop right now."

The problems with this response are identical to those seen after Minneapolis:

  • No evidence: At the time of Trump's speech, no suspect had been identified or arrested

  • Immediate partisan blame: Without facts, Trump assigned causation to "radical left" rhetoric

  • No policy solutions: Despite Kirk's death raising obvious questions about campus security and protection for speakers, Trump proposed nothing

  • Divisive rather than unifying: The speech deepened divisions rather than bringing Americans together

The Theological Fraud Revealed

Most damning was how Trump's response revealed the complete hollowness of the administration's claimed Christian values. When faced with the assassination of a political ally, Trump didn't call for prayer—he called for political warfare. There were no staged prayer circles, no calls for divine guidance, no appeals to Christian forgiveness or healing.

The contrast with the administration's performative prayer responses to other tragedies couldn't be starker. When children die in schools, the response is elaborate prayer theater. When a conservative activist is murdered, the response is immediate calls for political retaliation.

This reveals the truth about the administration's relationship with prayer: it's not a spiritual practice but a political tool, deployed strategically when convenient and abandoned when revenge seems more useful.

The Pattern Confirmed

Kirk's assassination confirmed every element of this analysis about "prayer without policy":

Performance Over Substance: No genuine spiritual response, just political exploitation

Blame Over Solutions: Immediate partisan finger-pointing rather than policy proposals to prevent future violence

Division Over Unity: Using tragedy to deepen political divisions rather than heal them

Exploitation Over Leadership: Weaponizing a family's grief for political advantage

What Authentic Christian Leadership Would Have Done

A president guided by genuine Christian principles would have responded to Kirk's assassination completely differently:

  • Called for national unity around shared democratic values

  • Proposed concrete solutions to protect speakers and prevent political violence

  • Led the nation in prayer for Kirk's family and for healing of political divisions

  • Worked across party lines to address the roots of political violence

  • Honored Kirk's actual legacy of respectful political engagement

Instead, Americans got the same performative playbook: exploit tragedy for partisan gain while offering no real solutions.

The Victims of Performance Prayer

The real cost of "prayer without policy" is measured in continued tragedy:

  • Fletcher Merkel and Harper Moyski: Still dead from the Minneapolis shooting

  • Charlie Kirk: Dead from political violence the administration claims to oppose

  • Future victims: Guaranteed by policies that prioritize performance over protection

Each "thoughts and prayers" response without accompanying action enables the next tragedy. Each exploitation of grief for political gain makes healing less likely and division more certain.

The Ultimate Test Failed

Charlie Kirk's assassination was the ultimate test of the administration's claimed Christian values. Here was a moment that called for the deepest spiritual response—grief over a young father killed for his beliefs, concern for his children who will grow up without their father, compassion for a nation torn by political violence.

The administration failed completely. No prayer circles, no spiritual leadership, no calls for the Christian virtues of forgiveness and healing. Just immediate political warfare and exploitation of tragedy for partisan gain.

This failure reveals the truth about the administration's Christianity: it's not a faith tradition that guides their actions, but a political costume worn when convenient and discarded when revenge seems more profitable.

Prayer without policy isn't protection—it's performance. And when the performance is abandoned at the moment of greatest need, it reveals the spiritual emptiness at the heart of this administration's claimed faith.

The families of Minneapolis deserve better. Charlie Kirk's family deserves better. The American people deserve better than leaders who exploit tragedy for political gain while offering no real solutions to prevent future suffering.

Until leaders understand that authentic faith demands both prayer and action, both spiritual response and practical solutions, more children will die while their leaders pray for the cameras and do nothing to save lives.
