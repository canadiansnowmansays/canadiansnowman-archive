The Iran Crisis

Ten People

Part XVI: The Succession Problem Nobody Is Discussing

February 28, 2026

Hossein Fatemi / Panos Pictures / Redux

Editor’s Note — Published February 28, 2026

This essay was written and completed on Friday, February 27, 2026 — hours before the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury against Iran in the early hours of Saturday morning.

It is being published now, after the strikes began, without alteration.

That matters. Part XVI asked the question that Washington was not publicly asking while the decision to go to war was still being made: not whether to strike, but what happens after. Who governs Iran the morning the bombs stop falling? What is the plan if the regime survives but is radicalized? What happens if Mojtaba Khamenei — described here as more dangerous and more extreme than his father — consolidates power in the chaos of succession?

Part XVII, publishing shortly, documents what happened in the first hours of the war: the strikes, the targets, the Iranian response, the voices of ordinary people sheltering with their children.

Part XVI is the question. Part XVII is the beginning of what happens when nobody answered it.

As Parts XIV and XV of this series were being finalized — documenting the evacuation signals, the Geneva silence, and Trump’s Friday afternoon declaration that he is “not happy” with Iran’s negotiating position — The Atlantic published a piece by Graeme Wood that reframes the entire crisis.

Not because it changes what is happening. But because it reveals what comes after.

The question Washington is debating is whether to strike Iran’s nuclear and military sites. The question Washington is not debating — at least not publicly — is what happens to Iran the day after.

That gap may be the most consequential blind spot in American foreign policy since 2003.

The Man in the Maybach

Last month, Wood traveled to Dubai to meet Jaber Rajabi — a figure whose biography reads like a novel nobody would publish for being too implausible.

Rajabi is 40 years old, though he looks younger, partly because the poisoning that rotted out his teeth also gave his skin the waxy tautness of someone who recently survived something that should have killed him. He guided Wood’s hand across the dome of his skull — bumpy and compromised, he said, from a sniper’s bullet that ripped across his head in Samarra in 2017 while he was fighting the Islamic State.

Before that, he was fighting Americans in Iraq. Before that, he was a study partner of Mojtaba Khamenei — the supreme leader’s son and potential successor — in Qom, Iran’s center of clerical learning. Before that, his mother worked in the office of Ali Khamenei himself.

He is now a regime opponent, vocal in exile, living under protection in the UAE while Iranian networks periodically send him threats through his old militia contacts in Iraq.

Rajabi spent most of a decade building two Iranian proxy militias that continue operating today. He met Qassem Suleimani — the architect of Iran’s Axis of Resistance, killed by a Trump-ordered drone strike in 2020 — when Suleimani personally welcomed him to a villa in north Tehran after he was wounded in Najaf. He worked as a foreign policy adviser to President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad. He claims to have survived multiple assassination attempts by the regime he once served.

Whether every element of this biography is precisely accurate is difficult to confirm. Wood notes this explicitly. But the picture that emerges across multiple interviews — conducted with unusually thorough security protocols, Rajabi’s answers consistent across sessions — is of someone with genuine deep knowledge of the Islamic Republic’s internal structure.

And what Rajabi says about what comes next deserves serious attention, regardless of what Washington decides about the next few days.

The Problem With Both Options

Danny Citrinowicz — who led Iran analysis for Israel Defense Intelligence and has appeared throughout this series — told Wood something that directly challenges the urgency Trump projected on Friday afternoon.

“There is no time pressure: The regime is already a goner.”

Iran’s currency is sliding toward zero. Its Axis of Resistance — the military strategy that appeared invincible just two years ago — has been dismantled by Israeli-led assassinations, bombings, and the infamous exploding beeper operation. Its people want to remove the supreme leader, and biology (Khamenei is an 86-year-old cancer survivor) may do it first.

The problem, Citrinowicz told Wood, is not whether the regime will fall. It is the absence of a coalition ready to take over the government.

“Without a replacement government, the result of an attack could be civil war. There are bad options and worse options right now, and an attack, when you don’t have a viable opposition, is definitely worse.”

This is the gap that neither Washington’s hawks nor its diplomats are addressing publicly. The military debate is about targets — nuclear sites, military infrastructure, air defense systems. The political debate is about whether to strike at all.

What is not being debated: who governs Iran the morning after.

“Ten People — That’s It”

Rajabi’s proposal sits in the space between bombing and waiting.

“There are about 10 people who need to be eliminated,” he told Wood, “and another 2,700 who need to be sidelined and offered amnesty.”

The logic is surgical rather than comprehensive. Rajabi claims that networks of regime enemies have already infiltrated the Iranian government at multiple levels — 50 parliamentarians, he says, and people throughout the security apparatus. These networks have already categorized every significant figure on the regime payroll into three groups: who can work with a new government, who needs to be retired, who needs to die.

The 10 at the top — including the supreme leader and his son Mojtaba — would be the targets. The 2,700 in the middle would be moved against on the first night, before they could organize. Then bought off. “Even the ones who were killers — we will say: ‘Go home.’” No trials. Early retirement. For the millions of ordinary Iranians who depend on the regime for their livelihood, Rajabi argues, little would change day-to-day.

“If the United States does not kill the ones who need to be killed,” he told Wood, “we will do it ourselves.”

There is a long tradition of exile figures claiming embedded networks and promising seamless transitions. Iraq in 2003 was full of such claims, and every one of them was wrong. Rajabi himself acknowledges the skepticism is reasonable.

But the question his proposal raises is worth taking seriously even if his specific network isn’t: Is there a path between comprehensive bombing and doing nothing that Washington has failed to seriously consider?

The Successor Nobody Is Talking About

The most disturbing section of Wood’s Atlantic piece is not about what happens if the regime falls. It is about what happens if it doesn’t.

Rajabi spent years studying alongside Mojtaba Khamenei in Qom. He came to view the supreme leader’s son as a brilliant zealot — more extreme and uncompromising than his 86-year-old father, and a uniquely dangerous potential successor.

“Mojtaba is more dangerous than 50 nuclear bombs,” Rajabi told Wood.

The elder Khamenei, for all his decades of confrontation with the West, has demonstrated a willingness to bend when regime survival required it. He accepted a nuclear deal in 2015. He has shown, in Rajabi’s assessment, that he cares what Iranians think of him.

Mojtaba is different.

As a student, he was apocalypse-obsessed — keeping corkboards and diagrams at a house near the Jamkaran mosque, mapping out the milestones between the present and the end times. “He thinks there are milestones on the path to the end of the world,” Rajabi told Wood, “and he himself will have a special part in hastening humanity down that path.”

The killing of Iranian protesters — hundreds of them, in the January 2026 crackdown — was, in Rajabi’s assessment, nothing. “He is willing to kill 13,000 Iranians. Why would he hesitate to kill 100,000 people in Tel Aviv?”

This is the cost of waiting that nobody in Washington is pricing into the debate: a regime that survives the current crisis may emerge from it less risk-averse, more ideological, and governed by a successor who views mass destruction as a theological milestone rather than a strategic catastrophe.

The nuclear program, in that scenario, is not a bargaining chip. It is a sacred instrument.

Russia, China, and the Vassal State Problem

Rajabi offered a second reason why time is not as favorable to the regime’s enemies as Citrinowicz suggested.

Russia and China are watching Iran right now with a specific question: Is this regime an ally worth salvaging?

“The regime is using time to restructure itself,” Rajabi told Wood. “They know China in particular is watching them, because they saw a good chance the regime would fall.”

If the Islamic Republic endures the current pressure — military, economic, diplomatic — Rajabi warns that Russia and China will not merely support it. They will call in favors. Military bases along the Persian Gulf. Transformation of Iran into something closer to Assad’s Syria before Assad fell — a vassal state whose sovereignty is nominal and whose territory serves external interests.

This reframes what “winning” and “losing” actually mean in the current confrontation.

A strike that degrades Iran’s nuclear program but leaves the regime intact — and leaves Mojtaba Khamenei positioned to succeed his father — may not produce a weakened Iran. It may produce a radicalized Iran with Russian and Chinese military infrastructure embedded along the Gulf.

That is not a victory. That is a different problem, larger and harder to solve.

What the Debate Is Missing

This series has documented, across sixteen parts, the military buildup, the diplomatic signals, the evacuation warnings, and the internal disagreements within the Trump administration about what a strike can actually achieve.

What it has not documented — because Washington has not been discussing it publicly — is the day-after question.

The Rajabi interview makes that absence visible.

The debate in Washington is binary: strike or don’t strike. The military planning, as documented in Part XIV citing Nancy Youssef’s Atlantic reporting, involves four distinct scenarios ranging from a negotiating lever to regime decapitation to comprehensive nuclear site destruction. Each scenario is a military question.

None of them is a governance question.

Who runs Iran if the strikes succeed? What is the plan if the regime survives but is radicalized? Who is the recognized opposition? What does Iranian society look like after a comprehensive air campaign against a country of 90 million people?

These questions don’t appear in the public debate. They didn’t appear in Trump’s Friday afternoon statement. They didn’t appear in Witkoff’s Fox News interviews. They didn’t appear in the Geneva communiqués.

Rajabi’s proposal — whatever its practical merits — at least asks them.

As Michael Mulroy, former Pentagon official, told Youssef for Part XIV: “We can degrade something. We can destroy something. But that doesn’t mean they can’t rebuild it.”

Rebuilding, in Mojtaba Khamenei’s Iran, with Russian and Chinese backing, looks different from rebuilding under Ali Khamenei.

That difference may be the most important variable in this entire crisis. And it is the one variable nobody in Washington appears to be weighing publicly.

“A Year of Suicide or Victory”

Rajabi told Wood as he was leaving: “I will be back in Iran this year.”

Wood noted that if that happens, things will have either gone very well or very badly for him. Rajabi implied that one scenario involved him returning to the battlefield — fighting in the mountains of northern Iran.

“This is a year of suicide or victory,” he said.

That binary has a particular resonance on a Friday afternoon when the US Embassy in Jerusalem has told its staff to leave today if they intend to leave at all, when the President of the United States has said he is “not happy” with Iran’s negotiating position, and when a six-hour session in Geneva has produced two contradictory accounts of whether talks will continue.

The decision about whether to strike appears imminent. The decision about what comes after — the governance question, the succession question, the Russia-China question — appears not to have been made at all.

That gap, more than the military buildup or the diplomatic impasse, is what should concern us.

Rajabi promised his network is ready. Washington, as best this series can document, has not asked what it is ready for.
