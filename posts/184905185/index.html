The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 3: Bloody Aban

January 27, 2026

The 2019-2020 Fuel Price Uprising and the Massacre in the Dark

On the night of November 14, 2019, the Iranian government announced fuel price increases of 50-200% effective immediately. By morning, gas stations across the country faced long queues of angry drivers. By afternoon, protests had erupted in Mashhad. By evening, demonstrations were spreading to cities across Iran.

Within 24 hours, protests engulfed more than 140 cities and towns—the most geographically widespread unrest since the 1979 revolution.

What happened next would become known as “Bloody Aban” (Aban being the Iranian calendar month). It would be the most violent government crackdown in the Islamic Republic’s history—and also the most hidden, conducted behind a six-day internet blackout that isolated Iran from the world while security forces systematically killed protesters.

The death toll remains disputed: the government eventually admitted to over 2,000 killed, human rights groups documented 544-600 with evidence, opposition sources claim 3,000+. The truth likely lies somewhere in that grim range.

What isn’t disputed: Ayatollah Khamenei’s government killed more Iranians in November 2019 than in any previous crackdown. And it did so in calculated darkness, severing Iran’s connection to the internet to prevent the world from watching.

By the Guardian’s own standards—protecting the Iranian people, upholding Islamic justice, serving the oppressed masses—November 2019 stands as damning evidence of systematic failure.

I. THE MATCH

The fuel price increase wasn’t just economic policy—it was betrayal of the revolution’s core promise.

The Islamic Revolution had been fought partly over economic inequality. One rallying cry in 1978-79: “Economic justice for all Iranians!” Subsidized fuel, bread, and basic goods were presented as the revolution’s gift to ordinary people, compensation for decades of monarchical exploitation.

For forty years, despite all else that failed, fuel subsidies remained—a social contract written in gasoline. Even as inflation destroyed savings and poverty spread, Iranians could at least afford to drive, to heat homes, to transport goods.

On November 14, 2019, the government broke that contract overnight. No warning. No debate. No gradual phase-in. Prices jumped 50% for rationed fuel, 200% for anything beyond quota.

For working-class Iranians—taxi drivers, truckers, delivery workers, families in provinces where public transit barely exists—this wasn’t policy adjustment. This was economic catastrophe.

Within Hours: Mashhad to the Nation

Protests began in Mashhad, Iran’s second-largest city and a traditionally conservative stronghold. That mattered. These weren’t Tehran intellectuals or university students. These were the revolution’s supposed base—working-class believers from religious cities.

By November 15, demonstrations had spread to Kermanshah, Shiraz, Bushehr, and dozens of smaller cities. By November 16, over 140 locations across Iran witnessed protests—from major metropolitan areas to provincial towns that had never seen significant unrest.

Social media and messaging apps spread videos showing blocked highways, burning tires, confrontations with security forces. The speed stunned observers. The geographic spread was unprecedented.

And the anger wasn’t just about fuel prices. Slogans evolved within hours from economic grievances to political demands:

“Death to the Dictator!” (meaning Khamenei)

“Death to Rouhani!” (the president who announced the increases)

“Leave Syria, think about us!”

“Khamenei, shame on you, leave the country alone!”

For the first time en masse, working-class protesters from conservative regions were openly calling for the Supreme Leader’s removal.

II. THE SIX-DAY DARKNESS

Iranian protesters gather around a burning car during a demonstration against an increase in gasoline prices in the capital Tehran, on November 16, 2019. © AFP/Getty Images

On November 16, 2019, around midday, Iran’s internet began shutting down. Province by province, city by city, mobile networks and home connections went dark. Within hours, Iran experienced near-total internet blackout—the most comprehensive information shutdown in modern history for a nation of Iran’s size.

For six days, Iran was isolated from the world and Iranians were isolated from each other. Families couldn’t locate missing relatives. Protesters couldn’t coordinate. International media couldn’t verify reports. Human rights monitors couldn’t document abuses in real time.

This was calculated. The government had watched how social media amplified the 2009 Green Movement and the 2017-18 protests. Videos of violence going viral complicated the repression narrative. International pressure mounted when the world could watch.

In November 2019, the regime decided: conduct the massacre in the dark.

What the Blackout Hid

Without internet, the world received only fragments: occasional reports from people with satellite phones, delayed accounts from Iranians who fled to neighboring countries, second-hand information from families with members inside Iran.

The full picture emerged only weeks later when the internet restored and survivors could upload videos, share photographs, tell stories.

What they revealed was systematic slaughter.

Security forces—primarily Revolutionary Guards and Basij militia—used live ammunition from the first days. Not rubber bullets. Not warning shots. Not crowd-control tactics. Live fire aimed at protesters.

Documented cases included:

  • Snipers on rooftops targeting demonstrators

  • Security forces shooting protesters at close range

  • Ambulances prevented from reaching wounded

  • Hospitals ordered not to treat injured protesters

  • Families threatened not to hold public funerals

  • Bodies taken by authorities, locations undisclosed

One human rights monitor compiled testimonies describing security forces “hunting” protesters, pursuing fleeing demonstrators into alleys and side streets to shoot them execution-style.

The internet blackout wasn’t about preventing coordination. It was about preventing documentation.

The Revolutionary Standard Violated

In 2018, before the protests, Khamenei had proclaimed: “The Islamic Republic values every Iranian life. We protect our people as a shepherd protects his flock.”

Test that against November 2019: His government announced policies impoverishing millions, responded to protests with live ammunition, then shut down the internet to hide the body count while systematically killing citizens in darkness.

Islam emphasizes transparency and justice. The Prophet Muhammad said: “Actions done in secret that you’d be ashamed to do publicly are forbidden.” Khamenei’s government conducted massacre behind information blackout—literally in darkness, literally in secret, literally shameful.

By Islamic standards he claims to uphold, this was profound moral failure. By revolutionary principles promising accountability and transparency, this was betrayal. By basic human decency, this was murder.

III. THE BODY COUNT

Iran: November 2019 “We were all struggling to breathe”

When internet restored around November 22, the true scale began emerging.

Human rights organizations documented at least 544 confirmed deaths through eyewitness testimony, hospital records, burial documentation, and video evidence. The organization HRANA, which meticulously tracks protest-related violence, compiled names, ages, locations, and circumstances of death for hundreds of victims.

Opposition groups, particularly the National Council of Resistance of Iran (NCRI), claimed the death toll exceeded 3,000. These figures remain unverified but aren’t implausible given the systematic nature of the violence and government efforts to conceal casualties.

Remarkably, the Iranian government itself eventually admitted the death toll was massive. In January 2020, an Iranian official told Reuters that approximately 2,000 people had been killed—though he blamed “terrorists” and “foreign agents” for the deaths.

By any count, this was the deadliest government crackdown since 1979. More Iranians killed in six days than in all of 2009’s Green Movement. More than 1999 and 2017-18 protests combined.

But Numbers Don’t Capture Everything

Behind statistics were human beings:

  • Pouya Bakhtiari, 27, shot near his home in Tehran. His mother’s grief-stricken video, begging for justice, circulated widely despite government efforts to suppress it.

  • Nikta Esfandani, a young woman shot in the head while driving, her car riddled with bullets.

  • Countless unnamed victims whose families were threatened into silence, whose bodies were seized by authorities, whose funerals were banned.

The Arrests

Beyond killings, at least 7,000-8,000 protesters were arrested. Many faced torture. Reports emerged of rape, electric shocks, severe beatings in detention. Some died under interrogation—”suicides,” the government claimed.

Two judges later sanctioned by the United States—Abolghassem Salavati and Mohammad Moghisseh—earned particular infamy for issuing death sentences and long prison terms against protesters in rushed trials.

As of 2026, thousands remain imprisoned from November 2019. Some face execution. Their crime: protesting fuel price increases and demanding the revolution’s promises be kept.

IV. WHAT MADE THIS DIFFERENT

Previous protests—1999, 2009, 2017-18—had been primarily student-led or middle-class movements centered in major cities, especially Tehran. The 2019 uprising broke all previous patterns.

Geographic Spread

More than 140 cities and towns, including:

  • Major cities: Tehran, Mashhad, Isfahan, Shiraz, Tabriz

  • Provincial capitals previously quiet

  • Small towns never before seeing unrest

  • Rural areas considered regime strongholds

Every province of Iran’s 31 provinces experienced protests. This wasn’t Tehran intellectuals—this was nationwide rejection.

Class Composition

Protesters were overwhelmingly working-class: taxi drivers, laborers, small shop owners, truckers, mechanics. The revolution’s supposed constituency—the mostazafin (oppressed masses) whom the Islamic Republic claimed to represent.

These weren’t people demanding liberal democracy or Western-style reforms. These were people who couldn’t afford to live, who watched IRGC commanders enrich themselves while working families went hungry.

Violence Against Symbols

Protesters didn’t just target government buildings. They targeted revolutionary symbols themselves:

  • 731 banks destroyed or damaged—including branches of Bank Melli, the central bank

  • 50 military bases attacked

  • 9 Islamic religious centers damaged

  • Statues of Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini toppled

  • Posters of Ayatollah Khamenei torn down and burned

  • Anti-American billboards destroyed

This was symbolic annihilation—rejection not just of current leadership but of the entire revolutionary project.

The Slogans

Previous protests had been careful, qualified. In 2009: “Where is my vote?”—implying the system could work if elections were honest.

In 2019, protesters abandoned that pretense:

“Death to the Dictator!” (Khamenei)

“Death to Rouhani!” (President)

“Death to the Revolutionary Guards!”

“Let go of Syria, think about us!” (criticizing foreign spending)

“This isn’t the year ‘88, this year we’ll fight back!” (reference to 1988 mass executions)

“Reformist, hardliner, the game is over!” (rejecting both political factions)

No ambiguity. No hope for reform. The system itself—the Islamic Republic as conceived and governed—was the problem.

The Speed and Spontaneity

Unlike 2009, which built gradually, 2019 exploded within hours and spread nationwide within a day. No central coordination. No opposition leadership directing actions. Pure organic rage.

This terrified the regime. Organized movements can be infiltrated, leaders arrested, structures dismantled. Spontaneous nationwide eruption? Impossible to decapitate.

The only response: massacre enough people, fast enough, to terrorize the rest into submission.

V. HIS OWN WORDS INDICT HIM

Throughout his rule, Khamenei had positioned himself as champion of the oppressed. Speech after speech emphasized the Islamic Republic’s commitment to the mostazafin—the downtrodden masses exploited under the Shah.

In a 2016 speech: “The Islamic Revolution was for the oppressed people. We will always stand with workers, with the poor, with those struggling to make ends meet. They are the foundation of the revolution.”

In 2018: “Economic justice is our priority. We don’t serve the rich and powerful. We serve ordinary Iranians.”

Test these claims against November 2019:

The working poor—taxi drivers, truckers, laborers—protested policies making their lives unlivable. The regime responded with bullets. Over 2,000 of the “revolution’s foundation” were killed. Thousands more imprisoned.

Meanwhile, IRGC commanders—who control 20-40% of Iran’s economy—remained insulated from fuel price increases. The elite who enriched themselves through corruption faced no hardship.

The revolutionary betrayal becomes explicit: The revolution fought for economic justice for the oppressed. Forty years later, the revolution’s Guardian killed the oppressed to protect the system enriching the elite.

The Islamic Standard

Islam emphasizes justice (adalah) and protection of the weak. The Quran states: “O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves” (4:135).

Apply that standard: When working-class Iranians protested economic policies driving them into poverty, Islamic justice demanded their grievances be heard. Instead, they were shot.

When families couldn’t afford fuel to heat homes or drive to work, Islamic compassion demanded economic relief. Instead, they got bullets in the dark behind an internet blackout.

By Islamic standards Khamenei claims as his authority, November 2019 was profound moral failure.

VI. THE ENEMY EXCUSE WEARS THIN

Predictably, Khamenei blamed foreign powers. In remarks after the protests subsided: “These were not ordinary people protesting. These were trained agents of America and Israel creating chaos. The enemy planned this carefully to destabilize our country.”

Apply his logic rigorously:

If CIA and Mossad organized simultaneous protests across 140 cities involving tens of thousands of working-class Iranians from conservative provinces—if they coordinated taxi drivers in Mashhad, truckers in Kermanshah, laborers in Shiraz to act in unison—then:

  1. Iran’s intelligence apparatus has failed catastrophically

  2. Foreign powers penetrate Iranian society completely

  3. The Guardian has been guarding nothing

The logistics problem is insurmountable. How would foreign intelligence recruit and coordinate thousands of Iranians across every province simultaneously? How would they create organic rage over fuel prices? How would they make working-class believers in religious cities spontaneously topple Khomeini statues?

The simpler explanation: People were genuinely angry about genuine grievances. Forty years of accumulated frustration over economic mismanagement, corruption, inequality, and repression finally exploded when the last social contract—subsidized fuel—was broken without warning.

But accepting that explanation would require Khamenei to admit responsibility. Better to blame phantoms than face failure.

The Iranian People’s Response

Notably, by 2019, fewer Iranians believed the foreign conspiracy narrative. Social media comments and private conversations documented increasing skepticism.

One comment circulated widely: “If America can organize protests in 140 cities but our government can’t organize basic services like electricity and water, maybe we have the wrong government.”

The regime’s own logic was turning against it.

CLOSING: THE REVOLUTION REJECTED ITS GUARDIAN

By December 2019, the immediate protests had subsided—not through resolution but through terror. Killing 2,000+ people and imprisoning thousands more created temporary quiet.

But something fundamental had shifted. The Islamic Republic could no longer claim to represent the oppressed masses. The oppressed masses had taken to the streets in 140 cities explicitly rejecting the system.

When working-class Iranians from conservative strongholds topple statues of Khomeini and burn images of Khamenei, the revolution has lost its ideological foundation. When taxi drivers and truckers—the mostazafin—chant “Death to the Dictator,” the Guardian’s claim to guardianship collapses.

Forty years earlier, in 1979, these same social classes had filled streets demanding the Shah’s overthrow. They had believed the revolution’s promises: economic justice, Islamic governance, dignity for ordinary Iranians.

In November 2019, they filled streets again—this time rejecting the revolution that betrayed them.

Khamenei responded the only way his system could: with bullets, internet blackouts, and mass graves.

By revolutionary standards—serving the people, upholding justice, protecting the oppressed—November 2019 was the Guardian’s complete failure.

By Islamic standards—mercy, compassion, accountability—it was moral catastrophe.

By simple human decency—shooting thousands of citizens demanding they be able to afford fuel—it was murder.

The bunker beneath his compound was looking more necessary every year. Another massive uprising was inevitable. The only questions: When? And would the bullets run out before the protesters did?
