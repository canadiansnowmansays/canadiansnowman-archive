When Congressional Subpoenas Meet War Distractions (And Win)

Part 25: The Accountability Calendar Doesn’t Care About Your War

March 6, 2026

Highlights from Bill Clinton’s Epstein Files Deposition (Source: Instagram)

On February 28, 2026, at exactly 2:30 AM Eastern time, President Donald Trump released an eight-minute video on Truth Social announcing that the United States and Israel had begun military strikes against Iran.

The stated purpose: regime change.

Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in the initial strikes. By March 1, three American soldiers were dead. Iranian counter-strikes hit U.S. bases across the Middle East. The conflict was escalating into a regional war.

It was, by any measure, the ultimate news cycle domination. Everything else would be buried under wall-to-wall war coverage.

Except for one problem.

The Republican-led House Oversight Committee had subpoenaed Bill and Hillary Clinton to testify about Jeffrey Epstein back in August 2025. When the Clintons fought the subpoenas, the committee threatened them with contempt of Congress in January 2026. The depositions were scheduled for February 26 and 27.

The war started February 28.

So on March 2, 2026—Day Three of Operation Epic Fury, with American casualties mounting and the Middle East in flames—the House Oversight Committee released nine hours of video testimony from the Clintons’ Epstein depositions.

The accountability calendar, it turned out, didn’t care about Trump’s distraction timeline.

February 26-27: The Depositions Happen Anyway

Former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton speaks outside the Chappaqua Performing Arts Center, after testifying before House lawmakers as part of a congressional investigation into convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein Thursday in Chappaqua, N.Y.(Yuki Iwamura/AP)

While Trump was preparing to assassinate a foreign leader and launch a regime change war, Bill and Hillary Clinton were sitting for depositions in their hometown of Chappaqua, New York.

The contrast was stark. Trump was about to dominate every headline with military action. The Clintons were being forced to answer questions about Jeffrey Epstein—questions that some observers noted could establish a precedent for Trump himself having to testify.

Hillary Clinton went first, on Thursday, February 26. She did not want to be there.

“How can I answer questions about a person that I don’t believe I ever even met,” she said at one point, her voice dripping with exasperation.

Later: “I don’t know how many times you have to say the same thing over, and over, and over again. And I just am struggling with the relevance of all of this.”

She denied ever meeting Epstein. She said she had no knowledge of his crimes. She insisted she had no new information to provide.

Her attorney, Cheryl Mills, was barely an hour into the deposition when she interrupted with news: Representative Lauren Boebert had leaked a photo from inside the hearing room to a right-wing blogger.

Hillary Clinton gathered her papers and walked out. “I’m done with this,” she said, raising her voice. “If you guys are doing that, I am done. You can hold me in contempt from now until the cows come home.”

She returned thirty minutes later, after receiving assurances it wouldn’t happen again.

The questioning continued for more than four hours. Representative Nancy Mace led much of it, pressing Clinton about her “feelings” regarding photos of her husband with young women.

“When you saw your husband in these photos with young women, being massaged, what went through your mind?” Mace asked.

“I am not going to speculate,” Clinton responded coolly. She’d had plenty of practice over the years fielding questions about her husband’s relationships with other women.

But Mace got the confrontation she was seeking when she asked about Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, who had visited Epstein’s island with his family in 2012—four years after Epstein’s conviction.

When Mace reminded Clinton that she was a sexual abuse survivor “trying to look out for other survivors,” Clinton lost her cool.

“Well, and I was taking care of the people who lost 3,000 lives at the World Trade Center,” she said, banging her hand on the table. She’d known Lutnick in her capacity as New York senator after 9/11, when Cantor Fitzgerald lost hundreds of employees.

“And now you’re being defiant and indignant today,” Mace shot back.

At another point, Boebert raised “Pizzagate”—the debunked conspiracy theory that claimed Democrats ran a child sex trafficking ring out of a Washington pizzeria.

“Pizzagate was totally made up,” Clinton responded with visible frustration. “It was an outrageous allegation that ended up hurting a number of people, that caused a deranged young man to show up with his assault rifle and shoot up a local pizzeria.”

After three more rounds on the subject, Clinton appeared to give up. “I expected a lot of interesting questions today,” she said, “but Pizzagate was not on my list.”

The next day, Bill Clinton appeared. His demeanor was more relaxed.

Bill Clinton testifying about Jeffrey Epstein on February 27, 2026—while Trump's war with Iran dominated headlines. The accountability calendar had other plans (GOPoversight)

He described his relationship with Epstein as “cordial” but not a true friendship. He said he’d met Epstein in 2001 or 2002 through Larry Summers, his former Treasury Secretary. Summers had described Epstein as “an information-hungry person” who wanted to talk about politics and economics.

In exchange, Epstein would let Clinton use his plane for humanitarian trips.

“I thought we had an understanding about the airplane,” Clinton said. “That he would let me use the airplane to set up my AIDS programs around the world if I agreed to talk to him for an hour about economics and politics on every long leg. I kept my word, and he seemed to honor his.”

After several trips, Clinton said, he felt that Epstein didn’t share his humanitarian aims. So he began working with other donors instead.

Clinton denied ever witnessing Epstein abuse anyone. He denied engaging in any sexual activity with women or girls Epstein introduced him to. He said he never visited Epstein’s private island.

As for the now-infamous photo of him in a hot tub with a woman whose face is blacked out: “I don’t know who that is,” he said. But he insisted he never had sex with her.

The photo was taken in Brunei, he explained, during a trip for the Clinton Foundation’s AIDS initiative.

And then Clinton dropped something interesting.

He said he’d discussed Epstein with Donald Trump about twenty years ago at one of Trump’s golf courses. Trump told him they’d had a falling out over a property deal.

“He said, ‘You know, we had some great times together over the years, but we fell out all because of a real estate deal,’” Clinton recalled. “And he said, ‘I’m sorry it happened.’ That’s all.”

Clinton was careful not to accuse Trump of anything. “He never said anything to me to make me think he was involved in anything improper with regard to Epstein either.”

But when asked whether he thought Trump should be called to testify before the committee, Clinton didn’t exactly help Trump’s case.

“That’s for you to decide,” he said. “But he did know him well.”

Hillary Clinton, when asked the same question, was more direct: “He’d be on my list.”

The Clintons had spent weeks fighting the subpoenas. They’d wanted a public hearing where their testimony couldn’t be misrepresented. They’d been threatened with contempt.

But they showed up. They answered questions. They cooperated.

In his opening statement, Bill Clinton explained why: “America was built on the idea that no person is above the law, even presidents—especially presidents.”

That statement would prove significant.

March 2: The Videos Drop (During Day 3 of the War)

By Sunday, March 1, 2026, three American soldiers were dead. Iran had launched counter-strikes against U.S. bases across the Middle East. Israel was conducting its tenth wave of strikes on Tehran.

The death toll in Iran had reached over 1,200. Six American service members killed. The conflict was spreading across the region.

Every cable news network was running wall-to-wall war coverage.

And then, on Monday, March 2, the House Oversight Committee released the full videos of the Clinton depositions.

Nine hours of testimony. Roughly four and a half hours for each Clinton.

The media was forced to cover both stories. American casualties in a war Trump started. And testimony about Jeffrey Epstein that Trump’s own party had demanded.

Chairman James Comer built the accountability machine Trump couldn't stop. The Clinton depositions his committee demanded happened exactly as scheduled—war or no war.

Representative James Comer, the Kentucky Republican who chairs the committee, had been clear about why this mattered. The Clintons appeared in the files. They had relationships with Epstein. The public deserved answers.

What Comer didn’t say—but what Democrats immediately seized on—was that the Clintons had now set a precedent.

If a former president could be compelled to testify before Congress about Epstein, then the current president—who also appeared extensively in the files, who had a documented friendship with Epstein, and whose FBI interview files about accusations from a 13-year-old were missing from the public release—should face the same scrutiny.

The Clintons had cooperated. They’d submitted to depositions. They’d answered questions.

Would Trump?

March 4: Five Republicans Join the Revolt

By Wednesday, March 4, the war had been raging for five days. Six American soldiers confirmed dead. Over 1,200 killed in Iran. Iranian missiles had hit the U.S. embassy in Kuwait. Amazon data centers in Bahrain and the UAE were offline after drone strikes.

And the House Oversight Committee voted to subpoena Attorney General Pam Bondi.

Five Republicans joined Democrats in the vote.

They were demanding that Bondi testify under oath about how the Justice Department had handled the Epstein investigation and the release of the files.

This was Trump’s own party. His own attorney general. And his own congressional majority refusing to let the story die—even as he was trying to dominate the news cycle with a war.

Representative Thomas Massie, a Kentucky Republican who’d sponsored the legislation requiring the Justice Department to release the Epstein files, spoke on the House floor:

“Over two dozen people have resigned—CEOs, members of government—worldwide. But I haven’t seen any arrests or investigations here in the United States from this Department of Justice.”

Representative Nancy Mace was more blunt. On X, she wrote: “This isn’t going away until people go to jail.”

Representative Ro Khanna, a California Democrat, pointed out the absurdity of the Justice Department’s position. The DOJ had identified over 6 million potentially responsive pages. They’d released 3.5 million. They were claiming they’d fulfilled their legal obligations.

But where were the other 2.5 million pages?

The bipartisan frustration was mounting. The Trump administration had faced constant political headaches since the file releases began in December. Critics accused the department of hiding documents, over-redacting some files while not redacting enough in others.

Victims’ names and faces had been exposed through “technical errors” while powerful men’s identities remained protected.

Representative Massie had been right to be suspicious. And what happened two days later would prove it.

AG Pam Bondi, March 4, 2026. Trump's own party just voted to subpoena her about the missing Epstein files. The war didn't stop the accountability machine.

March 6: The Missing Files Surface

On Thursday, March 6—Day Seven of the war—the Justice Department made a quiet announcement.

They were releasing “additional Jeffrey Epstein files involving uncorroborated accusations made by a woman against President Donald Trump that the department said had been mistakenly withheld during an earlier review.”

The files in question: FBI interview summaries from 2019.

A woman had contacted the FBI shortly after Epstein’s arrest and claimed that a man named “Jeff” living in Hilton Head, South Carolina, had raped her there in the 1980s when she was around 13 years old.

She didn’t know the man’s identity at the time, but decades later concluded he was Jeffrey Epstein when a friend texted her his photo from a news story.

In a follow-up interview a month later, the woman added additional claims, including that Epstein had flown her to either New Jersey or New York, where she claimed Donald Trump had tried to sexually assault her.

FBI agents interviewed her four times in 2019. Only one interview summary had appeared in the publicly released files.

Now, after MSNBC’s Lisa Rubin and NPR had reported on the missing documents—after journalists had tracked down approximately 50 missing pages by comparing witness indices from Maxwell’s trial discovery—the Justice Department was finally releasing them.

They called it a mistake. The files had been “improperly tagged.”

This was the same language they’d used before. When co-conspirators’ names were redacted in the 2007 draft indictment: “redaction errors.” When victims’ faces were left unredacted while powerful men were protected: “technical errors.”

Now approximately 50 pages of FBI interviews containing accusations against the sitting president: “improperly tagged.”

The files surfaced on Day Seven of a war Trump started.

The Justice Department had spent weeks insisting they’d released everything. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche had said on CNN: “We then released over 3½ million pieces of paper, which the entire world can look at now and see if we got it wrong.”

The world looked. And found 50 missing pages. About Trump. Containing accusations from when the witness was 13 years old.

And the Justice Department’s response was: oops, mistakenly withheld, improperly tagged.

Meanwhile, CBS News was tracking something else. The massive tranche of files the Justice Department maintained was more than 65,000 pages shorter than what the agency had initially released.

Files weren’t just being withheld. They were being actively removed.

The Accountability Machine You Built

Here’s what makes all of this so devastatingly ironic:

Trump launched the ultimate distraction war. Assassinating a supreme leader. Pursuing regime change. American casualties. A regional conflagration that could escalate into something far worse.

But the Republican-led Congress had built an accountability machine months earlier.

They’d subpoenaed the Clintons in August 2025. They’d threatened contempt in January 2026. They’d scheduled depositions for February 26 and 27.

Trump started his war February 28.

The videos dropped March 2, right on schedule.

His own party subpoenaed his attorney general March 4.

The missing Trump files surfaced March 6.

Every step of the way, the accountability mechanisms Trump’s own party had demanded kept running—regardless of his war, regardless of his news cycle, regardless of his attempts to dominate the headlines with military action.

Representative Comer had insisted on the Clinton depositions. Representative Massie had authored the Epstein Files Transparency Act. Representative Mace was demanding arrests.

These were Republicans. Trump’s party. His majority in Congress.

And they wouldn’t let it go.

The Clintons—who appeared in the files but had never been accused of crimes related to Epstein—submitted to depositions and cooperated fully.

President Trump—whose FBI interview files about accusations from a 13-year-old were missing from the public release, whose Justice Department had “improperly tagged” approximately 50 pages of documents—had not agreed to testify.

Hillary Clinton had made it clear: “He’d be on my list.”

Bill Clinton had set the precedent and explained why it mattered: “No person is above the law, even presidents—especially presidents.”

The question now was whether Trump would follow the standard he’d demanded of others.

The accountability calendar had already proven it didn’t care about his distraction timeline.

The depositions happened anyway. The videos dropped during the war. The subpoenas kept coming. The missing files surfaced.

You can start all the wars you want.

But your own party scheduled these hearings months ago.

And the Epstein Files just won’t die—no matter how many supreme leaders you assassinate.
