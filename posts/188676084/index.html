Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 2: Sugar, Slavery, and Spanish Indifference (1600–1868)

February 25, 2026

The sugar mill never stopped. Mounted guards, machetes, and smoke: the industrial logic of Cuban slavery reduced human beings to the fuel that kept the engines running. Illustrative image

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

There is a phrase that runs through the history of colonial Cuba like a dark thread: la isla siempre paga — the island always pays. Whoever held power in Havana’s grand colonial palaces, whatever empire made its calculations in distant capitals, whatever commodity the world market happened to desire at a given moment, it was Cuba’s enslaved and exploited population that paid the bill. For three centuries under Spanish colonial rule, that bill was enormous.

The story of Cuba between 1600 and 1868 is, at its heart, the story of sugar. The island’s extraordinary agricultural potential — its fertile soil, its tropical climate, its abundant rainfall — made it ideally suited to grow sugarcane, and the world’s insatiable appetite for sweetness made that potential enormously profitable. The problem, from the perspective of the Spanish colonizers who owned the land, was that sugar cultivation was brutally labour-intensive. It required armies of workers willing to endure conditions that no free person would voluntarily accept.

The solution the Spanish chose was slavery. Over the course of three centuries, somewhere between 700,000 and 800,000 enslaved Africans were brought to Cuba in chains. They built the sugar economy that made Cuba one of the most valuable colonies in the world. They were worked to death in the cane fields and the sugar mills — the ingenios — that processed the harvest. They were bought and sold as property, their families separated, their languages suppressed, their cultures driven underground. They suffered at a scale and duration that makes the Cuban story not just an account of Spanish imperialism but one of the great crimes of the Atlantic world.

And yet their story is also one of resistance, cultural survival, and ultimately transformation — because the Africans brought to Cuba did not simply disappear into the machinery of slavery. They preserved what they could of their cultures, created new ones from the fragments, and shaped Cuban identity in ways that persist to this day. To understand Cuba, you must understand that it is as much an African country as a Spanish one — that its music, its religion, its food, its sensibility are the products of that forced encounter, and that the people who suffered most profoundly in the making of Cuba also made Cuba most distinctively itself.

How Sugar Came to Define Cuba

Cuba was not always a sugar colony. For the first century and a half after Spanish settlement, it served primarily as a staging post — Havana’s magnificent natural harbour made it the assembly point for the Spanish treasure fleets that carried silver and gold from Peru and Mexico back to Seville. The city grew wealthy on the trade that passed through it, not on what it produced itself. The interior of the island remained largely undeveloped, its forests intact, its agricultural potential unrealized.

That began to change in the late seventeenth century, as the Spanish Crown gradually opened Cuba to more direct agricultural development, and accelerated dramatically after 1762, when the British briefly captured Havana during the Seven Years’ War.

The British occupation lasted only eleven months before Spain reclaimed the city in the peace settlement, but those eleven months transformed Cuba’s economic trajectory. The British brought with them approximately 10,000 enslaved Africans and opened Havana’s port to free trade — liberating it from the restrictions of the Spanish mercantilist system that had throttled its commercial development for two centuries. Cuban planters got their first taste of an open market, and they liked it enormously. When the Spanish returned, they were faced with a colonial elite that had experienced something different and wanted more of it.

The Spanish response was gradual liberalization of the slave trade, allowing Cuban planters to import enslaved people in much larger numbers than before. The timing was propitious. The sugar economy of Haiti — then the world’s largest sugar producer, operating under French colonial rule — was destroyed by the Haitian Revolution of 1791, which eliminated the competition and sent sugar prices soaring. Cuban planters rushed to fill the gap. Forests were cleared. New ingenios were built. Enslaved people were imported in industrial quantities.

By 1800, Cuba had become one of the world’s leading sugar producers. By 1850, it was the largest. The island that Columbus had admired for its beauty was being systematically stripped of that beauty — its forests felled, its rivers fouled, its soil depleted by monoculture — in the service of a commodity that sweetened European and North American tea and coffee.

The Arithmetic of Slavery

Cutting the Sugar-Cane” (colored engraving, early 19th century, showing workers with machetes in dense cane fields, overseers on horseback, Caribbean/West Indies context applicable to Cuba)

The numbers of the Cuban slave trade are worth dwelling on, because they represent human lives reduced to cargo manifests and account ledgers.

Between 1790 and 1867, when the slave trade to Cuba was finally suppressed, approximately 550,000 enslaved Africans were transported to the island — more than had been brought in the previous two and a half centuries combined. They came primarily from West and Central Africa: from the Yoruba people of what is now Nigeria, from the Fon of Dahomey, from the Bantu-speaking peoples of the Congo basin, from the Mandé of West Africa. They were captured in wars, raided from villages, sold by rivals, marched to the coast in coffles, loaded into ships in conditions of almost indescribable horror, and deposited in Cuba to be sold at auction.

The mortality rate in the sugar ingenios was staggering. Working conditions during harvest season — the zafra — required eighteen or more hours of continuous labour in intense heat, processing cane that had to be crushed and boiled before it spoiled. The machinery of the sugar mill was extraordinarily dangerous; accidents were common and catastrophic. Disease was endemic in the crowded barracks where enslaved people were housed. The birth rate among enslaved Cubans was consistently lower than the death rate, which meant that the system could only sustain itself through continuous importation — a fact that drove the slave trade long after it had been formally prohibited.

Some Cuban planters worked their enslaved labourers with the calculated brutality of men who knew that it was cheaper to work a person to death and buy a replacement than to maintain a healthy, long-lived workforce. Others operated somewhat less brutally, but the institution itself allowed no genuine humanity. Enslaved people could be beaten, branded, and killed with minimal legal consequence. Families could be separated by sale. Children born to enslaved mothers were enslaved from birth.

Against all of this, enslaved Cubans resisted. Some ran away — the communities of escaped slaves, called palenques, established in Cuba’s mountains and forests were a persistent feature of Cuban life throughout the slavery era. Some committed acts of sabotage — burning cane fields, breaking machinery, working slowly. Some poisoned their enslavers. And occasionally, some rose in open revolt.

The most significant of these revolts was the Ladder Conspiracy of 1844 — La Escalera — named for the ladders to which suspected conspirators were tied while being flogged for confessions. The Spanish colonial authorities, panicked by the success of the Haitian Revolution and terrified that Cuba’s numerically dominant Black population might follow the Haitian example, launched a wave of repression in which thousands of free and enslaved Black Cubans were arrested, tortured, and killed, many of them almost certainly innocent of any conspiracy. Among those killed was the poet Gabriel de la Concepción Valdés, known as Plácido, one of the most significant Cuban literary voices of his generation.

La Escalera was not a revolution. It may not even have been a conspiracy in any organized sense — the evidence suggests the Spanish authorities invented much of it. But its repression revealed the terror that lay beneath the surface of Cuban colonial society: the constant awareness, on both sides, that the system could not survive if its victims chose to resist it at scale.

The Spanish Relationship with Cuba

Spain’s relationship with its Cuban colony was, from the perspective of ordinary Cubans, one of systematic extraction and deliberate neglect. The island existed to generate revenue for the Crown — through taxes, through the profits of the sugar trade, through the commercial advantages that flowed from controlling the Caribbean’s finest harbour. What it received in return was minimal.

Cuba had no university until 1728, when the University of Havana was founded — more than two centuries after Spanish settlement. It had virtually no public education system. Its infrastructure — roads, bridges, hospitals — was built primarily to serve the sugar economy rather than the population. Its laws were made in Madrid by people who had never seen the island, and administered locally by officials appointed by the Crown whose primary loyalty was to their own enrichment.

The Spanish also maintained a policy of deliberate political exclusion. Cuban-born whites — the criollos — were systematically barred from the highest administrative positions in their own country, which were reserved for peninsulares, people born in Spain. This exclusion bred a creole class that was increasingly wealthy, increasingly Cuban in identity, and increasingly resentful of a colonial system that taxed them heavily while denying them political power.

By the mid-nineteenth century, Cuban society was deeply fractured. There were Spanish-born colonials loyal to the Crown, Cuban-born whites of varying degrees of loyalty, free Black Cubans who occupied a precarious middle position, and the enormous enslaved population at the bottom. There were those who wanted reform within the colonial system, those who wanted independence from Spain, and those who — horrifyingly — wanted annexation to the United States, calculating that American power might protect their slave economy against the threat of British abolitionism.

It was not a society at peace with itself. It was a society waiting to explode.

The Shadow of Haiti

The spectre of Haiti haunted Cuba throughout the slavery era, and it is impossible to understand Cuban colonial history without understanding that haunting.

Haiti’s revolution, which began in 1791 and concluded with independence in 1804, was the only successful slave revolution in history — a twelve-year war in which enslaved people defeated in succession the armies of France, Spain, and Britain and established the first Black republic in the Western Hemisphere. The victory was extraordinary. Its consequences for the Haitian people were devastating — France imposed ruinous reparations as the price of diplomatic recognition, a debt that crippled the Haitian economy for generations — but its symbolic significance reverberated across the entire Atlantic world.

For Cuban planters, Haiti was a nightmare scenario and a permanent warning. The Cuban sugar economy depended on an enslaved Black majority, and the planters knew it. They looked at Haiti and saw what could happen if that majority found its moment. The result was a security state more elaborate and intrusive than almost anywhere else in the Americas — elaborate systems of passes and surveillance, restrictions on the movement and assembly of Black Cubans free and enslaved, a garrison mentality that shaped Cuban colonial life for decades.

It also produced a peculiar political calculation: many Cuban planters preferred continued Spanish rule, with all its frustrations and exclusions, to the risks of independence. An independent Cuba might look like Haiti. Better the known devil. This calculation delayed Cuba’s independence movement by decades compared to the rest of Latin America, most of which broke free from Spain in the 1810s and 1820s while Cuba remained a colony.

When independence finally came, it would come at an extraordinary cost.

The World Slavery Made

19th Century sugar cane plantation. 1867 illustration of workers harvesting sugar cane (Saccharum sp.) on a plantation in Cuba. Source: Collection Abecasis. Science photo Library

Before we reach that independence struggle, it is worth pausing again — as we paused after the Taíno extinction — to consider what this period meant in human terms.

The sugar economy created by slavery shaped Cuba at the deepest level. It shaped the racial composition of the country — Cuba became majority Black and mixed-race during the slavery era, a demographic reality that persisted long after emancipation. It shaped the culture — Afro-Cuban music, from son to rumba to the sacred music of Santería, grew from the cultural survivals and creations of enslaved Africans and their descendants. It shaped the geography — the treeless, cane-covered landscapes of western Cuba are the legacy of three centuries of agricultural exploitation. It shaped the economy — the dependency on a single commodity export, the concentration of land ownership, the vulnerability to world market prices, all of these structural features of the Cuban economy that would cause problems well into the twentieth century and beyond were established in the sugar era.

And it shaped Cuban political consciousness in ways that are still visible today. The Cuban Revolution’s emphasis on racial equality, on the dignity of the formerly dispossessed, on resistance to outside power — these were not invented by Fidel Castro. They grew from the soil of a society that had been built on the suffering of enslaved people and had spent centuries working out what justice might require.

The people who were brought to Cuba in chains, who were worked in the cane fields and the sugar mills, who resisted and survived and built lives from the fragments they were permitted — they are as much the authors of Cuba as any of the figures whose names appear in the history books. Their suffering built the island’s wealth. Their cultures built its identity. Their descendants are still there.

La isla siempre paga. The island always pays.

But who it pays to, and why, and whether it must always be this way — that is the question the rest of this series pursues.
