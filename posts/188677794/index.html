Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 11: Rubio’s War — Trump, the Blockade, and the Question of Endgame

March 30, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

On January 20, 2025, Marco Rubio was confirmed as the 72nd Secretary of State of the United States. He was the son of Cuban immigrants, raised in Miami’s exile community, and had spent his entire political career building a reputation as one of the most uncompromising hardliners on Cuba policy in American public life. He was now the most powerful figure in American foreign policy, serving a president who had already demonstrated in his first term that Cuba was a target and who had given Rubio, by all available accounts, substantial authority to design the hemisphere’s policy.

The question of who is driving the Cuba blockade of 2026 is thus not difficult to answer. The more important question — the one this series has been building toward — is what they expect to happen. What is the endgame? What does success look like from the perspective of the people making this policy? And is the answer compatible with anything that could be described as the welfare of the Cuban people?

Rubio: The Biography That Explains the Policy

Marco Rubio’s parents, Mario and Oriales Rubio, came to the United States from Cuba in 1956 — three years before the Revolution succeeded. They were not fleeing Castro; they were economic immigrants seeking better opportunities. Rubio told a different story for years during his early political career, describing his parents as exiles who had “fled Cuba’s brutal communist dictatorship,” a narrative that was demonstrably inaccurate and that was corrected by the Washington Post in 2011 after examining Rubio’s own family documents.

The correction matters not because it disqualifies Rubio’s perspective on Cuba — it does not — but because it illustrates the way in which the narrative of exile and loss has been central to the political identity that brought him to power, and the way that narrative can be shaped and shaded for political purposes. Rubio grew up in a Miami where the Cuba that his family had left — a Cuba before the Revolution, before the embargo, before the entire catastrophe of the past sixty-five years — was remembered with a specific combination of nostalgia and grievance. The Cuba of the imagination, the Cuba that should have been and wasn’t, became the political cause.

This is not unusual in diaspora politics. The homeland that emigrants carry in their minds tends to be frozen at the moment of departure, preserved against the changes that time and circumstance have imposed on the real place. The Cuban-American political class that shaped Miami’s political culture for decades was in many respects fighting to restore a Cuba that had not existed since before the Revolution — a Cuba that was itself deeply imperfect, as this series has documented, but that was their Cuba, the one they or their parents had left and could not return to.

For Rubio, Cuba policy has always been about more than strategic calculation. It is about a family story, a community’s grievance, and a political identity built on promises to that community. When he was asked during his confirmation hearing whether he would support a diplomatic resolution with Cuba, his answer was essentially that there was nothing to negotiate. The Cuban government needed to go.

The Venezuela Template

To understand what Rubio’s Cuba policy might produce, it is worth examining what has already happened with Venezuela — because Venezuela is not just an analogous situation. It is the template, and Cuba is explicitly next.

In late 2025, the United States executed what can only be described as a foreign policy coup against Venezuela. Through a combination of crushing economic sanctions, the seizure of Venezuelan oil assets and tankers, and ultimately the physical capture of President Nicolás Maduro — who was brought to the United States under circumstances that remain somewhat opaque but appear to have involved a combination of inducement and coercion — Washington effectively dismantled the Maduro government and began the process of installing a new political order more to its liking.

The capture of Maduro was unprecedented. No sitting head of state of a recognized country had been physically seized and transported to face American legal proceedings in modern diplomatic history. The Trump administration justified it through the legal mechanism of drug trafficking indictments that had been prepared years in advance. The international reaction ranged from alarm to condemnation, but the administration absorbed the criticism without apparent concern.

Aircraft carrier U.S.S. Gerald R. Ford in the Caribbean Sea prior to Middle East deployment. Credit...US Navy/Seaman Apprentice Nathan Sears

Venezuela’s oil — which had been Cuba’s primary energy lifeline for years — was now under effective American control. The tankers that had supplied Cuba were no longer available. The blockade of Cuba that followed was, in this context, not a separate initiative but the completion of a regional strategy that had been building for years: eliminate Venezuela as a source of support for Cuba, then eliminate Cuba’s access to alternative suppliers, then wait for the Cuban government to collapse.

The question of whether this constitutes a coherent strategy or an act of collective punishment against civilian populations is not, apparently, one that the current American administration has spent much time examining.

The Endgame Question

What does Trump-Rubio Cuba policy actually want to happen?

The public answer is some version of: democratic transition, free elections, release of political prisoners, compensation for expropriated properties, and the emergence of a government that respects civil liberties and maintains normal relations with the United States. These are, in principle, defensible objectives. The problem is that the means being deployed to achieve them — a naval blockade designed to exhaust Cuban fuel reserves and trigger a humanitarian crisis — bear no proportionate relationship to the stated goals and no credible theoretical mechanism by which they would achieve them.

History provides ample evidence that blockades and embargoes do not, in general, produce the democratic transformations their architects envision. Iraq under sanctions between 1990 and 2003 did not produce a democratic uprising against Saddam Hussein — it produced the deaths of hundreds of thousands of Iraqi civilians from preventable disease and malnutrition, and ultimately a military invasion. North Korea has been under comprehensive international sanctions for decades and remains one of the most repressive and isolated states on earth. Iran has been under American sanctions for forty-five years and remains under theocratic rule. Cuba has been under embargo for sixty-five years and is still governed by the Communist Party.

The mechanism by which starvation of civilian populations is supposed to produce democratic governance has never been convincingly explained, because no convincing explanation exists. Populations under extreme stress tend toward solidarity and resentment of external enemies rather than organized political opposition to their own governments. Castro survived the Special Period precisely because the American embargo gave him a credible external enemy to blame for Cuba’s suffering. The 2026 blockade is, if anything, an even more explicit gift to the Cuban government’s political narrative: look, it can say, here is the American blockade proving once again that our enemy wants to destroy us.

The more parsimonious explanation for current American Cuba policy is not that it is a carefully designed strategy for democratic transition. It is that it is the satisfaction of a political debt. Rubio, Cruz, and the Cuban exile community’s political wing have been promised maximalist Cuba policy as the price of their electoral support. The blockade is the delivery of that promise. Whether it achieves anything beyond satisfying that political obligation is secondary.

The Regime Change Question

There is, however, a more alarming interpretation of the current policy that must be examined honestly.

The capture of Maduro established that the Trump administration is willing to physically remove heads of state. Trump has discussed regime change in Iran openly and repeatedly. The combination of the Venezuela operation and the Cuba blockade suggests a regional strategy aimed at eliminating governments the administration defines as hostile — not through diplomatic pressure or even economic sanctions in the conventional sense, but through the application of force sufficient to collapse or remove them.

BreakThrough News

If the Cuba blockade is designed not merely to pressure the Cuban government into concessions but to create the conditions for its collapse — fuel reserves exhausted, hospitals failing, social unrest escalating to the point where the military or security forces either refuse orders or stage their own intervention — then what comes next matters enormously. Who would govern Cuba after the Cuban Communist Party? The exile community in Miami? A military council? An American-supervised transition?

The historical record of American-supported regime transitions in the hemisphere is not encouraging. The governments that replaced American-opposed regimes in Haiti, Guatemala, Chile, and Panama were not reliably more democratic or more respectful of their citizens’ rights than the ones they replaced. The chaos that follows sudden state collapse tends to serve the interests of well-organized armed groups rather than civilian democratic movements, and Cuba’s geography as an island makes its post-collapse stabilization a particularly complex military and logistical challenge.

Cubans who oppose the current government — and they are many, as the 2021 protests demonstrated — have not generally been calling for American military intervention or naval blockade as their preferred path to democratic transition. They have been calling for the Cuban government to reform itself, to release political prisoners, to allow genuine economic freedom, and to permit political competition. The blockade does not serve those Cubans. It serves the exile community’s political agenda, which has never been entirely congruent with the agenda of Cubans who actually live on the island.

What Mexico Said

Claudia Sheinbaum press conference 2026, discusses aid to Mexico / Reuters

Mexican President Claudia Sheinbaum’s statement — “You cannot suffocate a people like this” — is worth dwelling on, because it represents something increasingly rare in international politics: a regional leader willing to say plainly what is happening rather than wrapping it in diplomatic language.

Sheinbaum was in a genuinely difficult position. Mexico is economically dependent on the United States and locked in negotiations over an expiring trade deal. The cost of antagonizing Trump was real and immediate. And yet she said it. The statement was not a comprehensive foreign policy position or a declaration of hostility toward the United States. It was a moral observation about what is being done to human beings on an island ninety miles from American shores.

Canada has not found an equivalent clarity of expression, and that silence is itself a foreign policy position — one that represents a retreat from the tradition that Pierre Trudeau embodied in 1976 when he stood in Havana and refused to pretend that Washington’s Cuba policy was anything other than what it was.

The world — including its democracies, including America’s friends and allies — has watched the United States apply its power in the Western Hemisphere with a combination of alarm and resignation that reflects the current state of international order. When Mexico says you cannot suffocate a people like this, it is speaking for a much wider audience that has not yet found the language, or the courage, to say the same thing in those terms.

The Unanswerable Question

A blackout in Havana in February. Yamil Lage/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

There is a question at the center of this series that has no comfortable answer.

If the blockade achieves its maximum impact — if Cuba’s fuel runs out, if the government loses the capacity to function, if the social order breaks down sufficiently to trigger a political transition — what happens to the Cuban people in the meantime, and who bears responsibility for it?

The Trump administration would say the Cuban government bears responsibility, for its years of mismanagement and its refusal to reform. That is not entirely wrong. But it is not a morally adequate answer to the image of hospitals suspending surgeries, of children in blackout-darkened schools, of water pumps failing in the August heat, of the Ocean Mariner making its U-turn sixty-five miles from ten million people who needed what it was carrying.

In five hundred and thirty years of Cuban history, the people who bear the cost of the decisions made about Cuba have almost never been the people making those decisions. The Taíno bore the cost of Spanish colonialism. Enslaved Africans bore the cost of the plantation economy. The mambises bore the cost of the independence struggle. The rural poor bore the cost of Batista’s corruption. Cubans of every description have borne the cost of the Revolution’s political rigidity and economic mismanagement. And ordinary Cubans — not the party officials, not the security forces, not the political class — are bearing the cost of the blockade.

It has always been this way. Knowing that does not make it acceptable.
