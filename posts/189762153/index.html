The Iran Crisis

The War Without a Name

Part XXIX: Days Three and Four — The Objective Problem, the Ground That Moves, and the Democracy That Isn’t There

March 3, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 3, 2026

By Monday afternoon, the United States Secretary of State was telling Congress that “the hardest hits are yet to come from the U.S. military.” The president was telling a CNN anchor: “We haven’t even started hitting them hard. The big wave hasn’t even happened. The big one is coming soon.” And the Defense Secretary was standing at a Pentagon podium saying: “This is not Iraq. This is not endless.”

Three American officials. Three audiences. One war. No shared description of what it is for.

Smoke and minor structural damage visible on the roof of the US Embassy compound in Riyadh following Iranian drone strikes, highlighting the vulnerability and escalation. Source: Fox News

The Iran Crisis entered its fourth day on Tuesday with the US closing embassies in Saudi Arabia and Kuwait after drone strikes on both compounds. Israel has seized new territory in southern Lebanon and warned residents of over 80 villages not to return. Hezbollah has declared that “the phase of patience has ended” and that if Israel wants an open war, “then let it be an open war.” Iran’s Red Crescent has confirmed 787 dead inside Iran — 1,039 recorded attacks on 504 sites. European natural gas prices have roughly doubled in 48 hours. South Korea’s stock index fell 7.2% on Tuesday morning. Germany’s Chancellor Friedrich Merz — the first world leader to meet Trump face-to-face since the war began — arrived in Washington.

What began on Saturday as a defined military operation against a defined target has become, by Tuesday morning, a war without a clearly stated objective, an open-ended timeline, two active fronts, closed embassies, shuttered airports, a halted global LNG supply, and a democratic opposition inside Iran so underdeveloped that its own most committed advocates measure it at “not even five out of a hundred.”

The Objective Problem

On Monday, the Trump administration offered the following explanations of what Operation Epic Fury is actually for:

Trump (Medal of Honor ceremony, his first public appearance since the war began): Four objectives — destroy Iran’s missile capabilities, “annihilate” its navy, prevent Iran from obtaining nuclear weapons, stop Iran from funding regional proxies. No mention of regime change. Then, to CNN’s Jake Tapper minutes later: “We haven’t even started hitting them hard. The big wave is coming soon.”

Rubio (Capitol Hill, outside a classified congressional briefing): The war is a response to an “imminent threat.” Also, it is preemptive, launched before Iran could strike first. Also, the primary purpose is destroying Iran’s missile arsenal as a “shield” for its nuclear ambitions. Also: “We will do this as long as it takes to achieve those objectives.” Also: he does not know how long. Also: ground troops are not ruled out.

Hegseth (Pentagon briefing): “This is not a so-called regime change war, but the regime sure did change.” Also: “This is not Iraq. This is not endless.”

Johnson (after the classified briefing): “The objective is not regime change. The objective was to take out those missiles.”

Netanyahu (same day, from Israel): Objectives include “forging the conditions for the brave Iranian people to remove the yoke of tyranny.”

These are not variations on a theme. They are different wars with different purposes, simultaneously being prosecuted by parties who are nominally on the same side. The Rubio formulation — destroying Iran’s conventional missile capability as a “shield” for its nuclear program — is the most consequential, because it sets the lowest bar for military action. It rests on a speculative chain: Iran builds missiles → missiles shield nuclear program → therefore destroy missiles preemptively → therefore this is defensive. It also, as Senator Mark Warner observed on exiting the classified briefing, contains no evidence of an imminent threat to the United States. “If we equate a threat to Israel as the equivalent of an imminent threat to the United States,” Warner said, “then we are in uncharted territory.”

Rubio himself quietly abandoned the argument he had been making the previous week — that Iran was developing missiles capable of reaching the American mainland. His own intelligence agencies had publicly contradicted that claim. By Monday he was no longer making it. The previous rationale had been quietly retired and replaced with a new one, and no one in the briefing room appears to have asked him to account for the difference.

There is one detail from Monday that captures the administration’s relationship with its own stated objectives better than any policy statement. In the middle of the Medal of Honor ceremony — an event honoring men who died in Vietnam and Afghanistan — Trump briefly mentioned the six Americans killed in his war, then moved on to discuss the curtains he picked out for the White House in his first term. “I always liked gold,” he said. There was, the Times reported, “light chuckling in the room.”

“I Don’t Have the Yips”

The most significant military statement of Monday was not delivered at a Pentagon briefing or in a congressional hearing. It was a single line in the New York Post.

“I don’t have the yips with respect to boots on the ground — like every president says, ‘There will be no boots on the ground.’ I don’t say it.”

Every American military intervention in recent decades has been predicated, at least rhetorically, on the absence of ground troops. Trump has now explicitly declined to make that commitment. Rubio, separately, told reporters: “Right now we’re not postured for ground forces. But obviously the president has those options. He’s never going to rule out anything.”

The military rationale for this is not irrational in isolation. General Caine acknowledged in briefings that destroying Iran’s underground ballistic missile facilities from the air may require special forces on the ground to inspect known or suspected sites. History supports the concern: a CIA post-mortem on Operation Desert Storm concluded that Allied pilots were “notoriously optimistic” about their Scud-hunting success in 1991, and Iran’s missile program is substantially more dispersed and hardened than Iraq’s was.

But the political implications of the “yips” comment run deeper than military logistics. The MAGA base that has provided the most vocal skepticism of this war — Tucker Carlson, Marjorie Taylor Greene, Erik Prince — did so on the explicit premise that this was not a ground war, not another Iraq, not another Middle East commitment measured in years and body bags. The White House official’s reassurance — that MAGA anger will blow over “if the conflict is short” — was premised on “short” meaning weeks of airstrikes with no ground commitment. That premise has now been explicitly withdrawn by the president himself.

Breitbart’s Matthew Boyle, after his three-hour radio program Saturday, reported that listeners were more interested in the economy and immigration than in the Iran war. “It all comes down to the results,” he said. By Tuesday, the results so far are six dead Americans, three F-15s destroyed by friendly fire, two US embassies struck, and a Secretary of State who cannot state the war’s legal justification without contradicting himself within a single press conference.

The Energy War

A gas station in New York City in 1973. When oil producers cut supply, Americans waited in line to pay higher prices for gasoline. Credit Marty Lederhandler/AP

The most consequential single development of Days Three and Four has not been military. It has been economic.

On Monday, QatarEnergy — one of the world’s largest exporters of liquefied natural gas — announced it was halting LNG production after Iranian drones struck two of its gas facilities and a power plant. On Tuesday, QatarEnergy expanded the pause to “some downstream products” including polymers and aluminum. European natural gas prices surged nearly 50% on Monday. Then another 25% on Tuesday. In 48 hours, they have roughly doubled. The last LNG tanker transited the Strait of Hormuz on Saturday. It has now been four days.

Ras Laffan LNG terminal in Qatar Industrial complex at Ras Laffan with massive storage tanks, cranes, and flaring stacks — the key site where QatarEnergy halted LNG production after strikes. Source Wikipedia

A senior IRGC official stated on Iranian state television that Iran would “set on fire” any ships attempting to transit the Strait. “Not a single drop of oil” will leave the region, he said. This is no longer a warning. It is a stated policy of energy warfare.

The market picture as of Tuesday morning: Brent crude at $83, highest level since mid-2024. European gas futures surged 25% — on top of Monday’s 50%. Asian LNG cargo prices rose 45% on Tuesday. South Korea’s Kospi index fell 7.2% — its worst day in years. Japan’s Topix fell 3%. Taiwan fell 2%. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell more than 3%, every market on the continent in the red. S&P 500 futures down 1.8% before the New York open. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.1%, with yields on British, German, and Japanese government bonds rising even more steeply. Gold flat at approximately $5,300, suggesting less safe-haven panic and more a generalized confidence shock.

ING analysts, in a note to clients, summarized the macro situation with unusual bluntness: “In a prolonged conflict, the combination of higher energy costs, disrupted logistics, and a generalized confidence shock would constitute a meaningful drag on global trade volumes at precisely the moment the world economy was still digesting the inflationary and growth consequences of the tariff shock. The mother of all bad timings.”

The Harvard economist Kenneth Rogoff invoked a more distant historical comparison. “It’s a little bit like asking, when the Archduke Ferdinand got killed, what the macroeconomic consequences would be, and having no idea what was next,” he told the Times. “When World War I started, everyone thought it would end in a month.”

That analogy — from a former IMF chief economist, a chess grandmaster, and a student of historical cascades — deserves to be taken seriously. Not as prophecy, but as a reminder that wars which begin with defined objectives and projected four-to-five-week timelines have a documented tendency to escape both.

A secondary energy story is developing that has received less attention than the oil and LNG headlines. The Fujairah Oil Industry Zone — one of the Middle East’s largest oil storage sites, a key refueling hub for ships transiting the region — caught fire Tuesday morning after debris from an intercepted drone fell on it. The fire was brought under control with no casualties. But Fujairah is not a military target. It is the physical infrastructure of global maritime commerce. Its targeting, even inadvertently, is a statement about what the war now touches.

India’s situation deserves particular attention as the global economic picture develops. Indian workers — nine million in the Persian Gulf, contributing 38% of India’s total remittances — are caught between the US war and the region they depend on for their livelihoods. India also committed to Trump last month to reduce Russian oil imports, seeking tariff relief, and has been replacing them with Gulf supplies. Those Gulf supplies are now under threat. India is simultaneously caught in the trade war and the energy war.

Lebanon: The Ground Moves

Merkava tanks and armored vehicles moving through rocky terrain in southern Lebanon during ground operations to seize new territory and expand the buffer zone. Source: JNS.org

On Tuesday morning, the Israeli military did not merely strike Lebanon. It seized it.

Israeli forces advanced into southern Lebanon, establishing what the military described as a “barrier” — an expanded buffer zone beyond the five small points Israel had maintained inside Lebanon since refusing to fully withdraw after the November 2024 ceasefire. Defense Minister Katz ordered forces to “advance and take control of additional strategic locations.” The Israeli army published a list of over 80 villages and towns in southern Lebanon, warning residents not to return. Tens of thousands of people, already registered at collective shelters, found their displacement extended indefinitely.

The Israeli military chief of staff, General Zamir, had already vowed the IDF would not end its Lebanon campaign “before the threat from Lebanon is eliminated.” The ground advance makes that formulation concrete. Israel is not conducting a punitive air campaign against Hezbollah for firing rockets. It is retaking land.

Hezbollah’s response was unambiguous. Senior official Mahmoud Qamati issued a statement: “The phase of patience has ended, leaving us with no option but to return to resistance. If Israel wants an open war, then let it be an open war.”

This is the Lebanese front that the “Axis Alone” analysis in Part XXVII assessed as degraded and unlikely to fully re-enter. It has re-entered. The question now is not whether Hezbollah will fight but how much of its depleted capacity it can bring to bear, and whether Israeli ground forces in Lebanon trigger a different caliber of engagement than aerial exchange.

The Lebanese Prime Minister Nawaf Salam’s condemnation of Hezbollah’s initial decision to fire rockets — calling it “unlawful” and calling for Hezbollah’s military activities to be banned — represents an extraordinary political rupture within Lebanon. It is the Lebanese state, however weak, attempting to separate itself from the armed group it has never fully controlled. Whether that separation has any operational meaning while Israeli forces are physically inside the country is another question.

The displacement numbers are now in the tens of thousands. Thirty thousand registered at collective shelters, per the UN refugee agency, with many more in cars or informal arrangements. The 171 shelters opened by Lebanon’s Ministry of Social Affairs are already 79 overwhelmed. And these numbers reflect the beginning of a ground campaign, not its aftermath.

The Embassies Close

On Monday, a drone struck the US Embassy compound in Kuwait. On Tuesday, two drones struck the US Embassy in Riyadh, blackening a structure on the roof, causing minor damage and a small fire.

Both embassies closed. The State Department ordered nonessential staff and their families out of six countries: UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Iraq, Jordan, and Bahrain. Americans in 14 Middle Eastern countries were told to depart immediately. The US Embassy in Amman temporarily evacuated staff after an unspecified threat. The US Embassy in Pakistan — where protests over the Iran strikes killed more than 20 people and crowds stormed the Karachi consulate — canceled all visa appointments through Friday.

The fortifications of the Riyadh embassy’s Diplomatic Quarter, a Times reporter noted, were built to prevent suicide attacks. Not drones.

This sequence carries a specific resonance in American political memory. The Carter presidency ended, in significant part, on the image of American diplomatic facilities under siege in Iran. Trump, in the days leading up to Operation Epic Fury, mentioned Carter’s failed 1980 hostage rescue mission as one of the historical failures he was determined not to replicate. He is now presiding over American embassies being struck by drones across the Middle East, diplomatic staff being evacuated from six countries simultaneously, and American citizens sheltering in place across fourteen nations.

The Canadian Embassy in Riyadh also closed for the day and canceled appointments through Friday. As a Canadian journalist observing this from Toronto, I note without particular pleasure that Canada’s diplomatic footprint is now also being retracted because of a war our government was given no meaningful notice of and no opportunity to affect.

The Democracy That Isn’t There

The Atlantic’s Radio Atlantic devoted its Monday episode to a question the Trump administration has been unable to answer: After Khamenei, what now?

The conversation between host Hanna Rosin, Iranian-American writer Arash Azizi, and Atlantic staff writer Anne Applebaum produced the most analytically useful single hour of commentary this series has encountered since the war began. Their core finding: Trump’s stated hope — that the Iranian people will “seize control of their destiny” — collides with a structural reality the administration appears not to have fully processed before launching the operation.

The Iranian democratic opposition, Azizi said, is “not even five out of a hundred” in terms of organizational readiness. “We don’t even have a basic organization. This is the reality that we are facing.” Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi has supporters inside Iran but no on-the-ground game. The movements that have tried to organize — the 2019 protests, the 2022 Mahsa Amini uprising, the January 2026 demonstrations that the regime suppressed with mass casualties — have been systematically destroyed by decades of regime repression.

Trump suggested the IRGC should “surrender their weapons to the people.” Applebaum’s response cuts to the core of the problem: “To whom should they surrender? There’s no body to surrender.” More than a million people remain under arms defending the regime. Approximately 20% of the population supports it. The mechanisms for a popular takeover, under bombardment, facing that force structure, are not clear to anyone — including the people who launched the war.

This is not merely an analytical problem. It is an accountability problem. The administration told the American public, and the world, that this operation had objectives. Rubio has listed them variously as: destroying missile capability, preventing nuclear development, stopping proxy funding, responding to imminent threat, conducting pre-emptive defense, and weakening Iran’s “shield.” None of these stated objectives include the democratic transition Trump keeps invoking as the moral justification for the operation. And the US has, in the year before launching this war, actively dismantled the infrastructure that might have supported such a transition.

Applebaum documented this with precision: the Trump administration defunded human-rights monitoring groups working on Iran; Kari Lake’s tenure at US international broadcasting resulted in Radio Free Europe losing access to its transmission towers; an “eccentric figure” was placed in charge of Voice of America’s Persian service who is openly hostile to the Crown Prince. “We don’t really have channels to communicate with the democratic opposition or with other opposition groups inside the country,” Applebaum said. “We don’t really have people inside the State Department anymore with experience of political transition and negotiation.”

The administration that is calling for Iranians to rise up and take power has spent the past year defunding every organization that might have helped them do it.

Azizi’s prediction, offered with characteristic precision: it is “quite likely for Trump to deal with the current leaders of the Islamic Republic at some point in the next few days and try to get some sort of a ceasefire in which he can declare a victory.” The Larijani-Qalibaf faction — technocratic, West-facing, privately calculating that “our resources are done” — wants the Chinese model: economic opening, relaxed social repression, diplomatic normalization, while maintaining political control. No democracy. No rule of law. No accountability for four decades of theocratic governance. But stability. And survivable.

Applebaum’s note on the Chinese model is the necessary cold water: it “implies no democracy. It implies continued military control. It implies no rule of law, and therefore it also implies corruption — a kind of Russian-style, or in the best-case scenario China, but in the more worst-case scenario Russia- or Zimbabwe-style regime.” Technocratically more competent. Extracting wealth from society. Staying in power through force.

That is the most probable endgame of a war launched partly in the name of the Iranian people’s freedom.

Netanyahu’s Transformation

Yair Rosenberg’s Atlantic piece on Netanyahu provides the Israeli half of the analytical picture, and it is essential to understanding how this war became possible.

The Netanyahu who is commanding this assault on Tehran, Rosenberg argues, is not the Netanyahu who governed Israel for nearly two decades before October 7. A senior Obama official famously called him “chickenshit” in 2014, explaining he was “scared to launch wars.” Trump himself told a campaign rally in 2023 that Netanyahu had committed to join the Soleimani strike and backed out at the last minute. “I’ll never forget that Bibi Netanyahu let us down,” Trump said. Netanyahu’s biographer, writing in 2018, predicted correctly that Israel would not go to war with Iran even with a sympathetic Trump administration — because Netanyahu was constitutionally risk-averse.

October 7 changed that. An Israeli public that felt profoundly, catastrophically insecure demanded dramatic action. Netanyahu’s caution had been rebranded as catastrophic complacency. He initiated the very Gaza campaign he had long warned against in his own memoir, noting the costs: “many hundreds on the Israeli side and many thousands on the Palestinian side.” Then, confronted with Hezbollah’s rockets destroying northern Israel’s towns and forcing 70,000 citizens from their homes, he launched against Hezbollah too.

And something unexpected happened. Nasrallah was assassinated, along with nearly his entire chain of command. The ceasefire followed on Israel’s terms. Then came June 2025 — the twelve-day war in which Israel achieved air dominance over Iran, struck nuclear sites, killed military and intelligence leadership, and suffered only 28 casualties on the home front against pre-war projections of over 400.

Each successful escalation bred the confidence for the next. The Netanyahu governing the current assault on Tehran was shaped by October 7’s horror and tempered by a sequence of military successes that made his advisers’ pre-war casualty projections look catastrophically pessimistic. The lesson he drew — the lesson the Israeli military drew — was that bold action works. That the costs they had feared were lower than projected. That Iran’s defenses were penetrable.

Whether that lesson will survive contact with the current war’s expanding scope is, as Rosenberg writes, genuinely unclear. He is now managing “a mercurial Trump who remains liable to declare a premature victory and exit the stage at any moment.” The ceasefire with Hezbollah that Netanyahu achieved in November 2024 held for barely fifteen months before Hezbollah re-entered the war Monday morning. The June 2025 war degraded Iran’s missile program — and Iran rebuilt it within months. Each successful escalation has deferred rather than resolved the underlying problem, while raising the stakes for the next round.

The Netanyahu of October 6, 2023 is never coming back. Whether the Netanyahu of March 2026 will prove to have made his country safer or simply to have raised the ceiling on what both sides are willing to attempt — that question will be answered in the weeks ahead.

The Archduke Problem, and the German Question

Chancellor Friedrich Merz of Germany arrived in Washington on Monday for a meeting with Trump that had been planned around other topics entirely. He is the first world leader to sit with Trump face-to-face since the war began. The German government received what the Times described as “a courtesy call” before the strikes — not a consultation, a notification.

Merz’s position is among the more carefully calibrated in Europe. He acknowledged to reporters that European efforts to restrain Iran through diplomacy had failed. “Now is not the moment to lecture our partners and allies,” he said, “despite all doubts, we share many of their goals without being able to actually achieve them ourselves.” That is the candid admission of a leader who recognizes both the strategic problem the war was meant to address and the recklessness of how it was launched.

The E3 — Britain, France, Germany — issued a joint statement Sunday evening indicating they would “take steps to defend our interests and those of our allies in the region, potentially through enabling necessary and proportionate defensive action to destroy Iran’s capability to fire missiles and drones at their source.” That is European military engagement language, however hedged. How far Merz can move Trump on the question of an off-ramp — given that Trump has publicly contradicted every diplomatic signal he himself has sent within 24 hours of sending it — is unclear.

Applebaum noted, in the Radio Atlantic conversation, that Canada’s Prime Minister Mark Carney has spoken of the United States “extracting tribute essentially from allies who are integrated with the U.S.” — and that it is “actually dangerous for some countries to be too integrated.” That framing, from the leader of Canada, directed at the United States, is not the language of allied partnership. It is the language of a smaller power assessing the risks of dependency on an unpredictable larger power. The war in Iran has not created this dynamic; it has made it impossible to ignore.

Rogoff’s Archduke Ferdinand comparison deserves a final note here. He made it as an economist warning about cascading macroeconomic effects from an unexpected shock. But it is also a warning about the limits of confident projection at the start of wars that feel defined. The Archduke’s assassination was, at the moment, a manageable event. The first mobilizations looked contained. The first calculations looked rational. The war that followed killed seventeen million people and reshaped the map of the world for the rest of the century.

No one in the Trump administration appears to be doing this kind of historical stress-testing. The general told them it would be “something easily won.” The secretary of state told them it would not last. The president moved on to discuss his curtains.

Tuesday Morning: The Food Ban and the Fraying Gulf

Four developments from Tuesday morning deserve to be read together, because individually they are news items and collectively they describe the shape of what this war is becoming.

Iran banned all exports of food and agricultural products until further notice. The government stated the decision was made “to prioritize the supply of essential, sensitive, and necessary goods for the public.” Iran is a significant exporter of fruits, vegetables, and spices to Gulf countries, Asia, and the European Union. This is a domestic wartime rationing measure — a government telling its population that the food supply must be protected before commerce continues. It is also, simultaneously, an economic severing of ties with trading partners who are already absorbing the energy shock from the Hormuz closure. The Hormuz closure stops oil and LNG leaving Iran’s neighborhood. The food export ban stops agricultural goods leaving Iran itself. The country is now simultaneously conducting energy warfare against its neighbors and rationing its own domestic supply. Both of these things can be true, and both are happening.

The food ban connects to a warning buried in the NYT’s economic analysis that deserves to be brought forward: if refineries are taken out and petrochemical production slows, fertilizer production follows. Constrained fertilizer supply raises the cost of growing food. That chain — war, refinery damage, petrochemical disruption, fertilizer shortage, food price increase — threatens malnutrition in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. The food export ban is Iran’s immediate response to its own wartime supply needs. The fertilizer chain is the slower-moving humanitarian consequence of the broader economic disruption. Both are real.

The European Union convened emergency energy meetings for every remaining day of the week. The gas coordination group meets Wednesday. The oil coordination group meets Wednesday. The energy task force, previously scheduled for Thursday, has been expanded to include a Middle East briefing. On Friday, commissioners will discuss energy security and prices alongside an International Energy Agency official. Five institutional energy meetings in four days is not monitoring. It is crisis management. Europe, with German gas storage at 21% capacity and European natural gas prices having doubled in 48 hours, is treating this as a supply emergency, not a market disruption.

The UAE defense ministry held a press briefing to assure reporters it has sufficient interceptor stockpiles “over long periods.” You do not hold that press briefing unless people are asking whether the stockpiles are running low. The numbers driving the question: 800-plus drone attacks and nearly 200 missile attacks against the Emirates in four days. Three people killed, 68 injured. Fifty-seven drones and one ballistic missile hit inside Emirati territory despite the interception effort. The UAE’s air defenses have performed better than almost any pre-war analysis would have predicted. The question being asked — quietly, then publicly enough to require a ministerial response — is how long that performance is sustainable.

The UAE’s minister of state called diplomacy “the only viable path.” Reem Al Hashimy, at a press briefing Tuesday morning: “The military solution will only lead to more crises, while the rational path to not expanding the military conflict remains serious and responsible dialogue.” The UAE simultaneously reserved “our full right to defend ourselves.”

This is the clearest public statement yet from a Gulf ally about where it stands. The UAE is being hit — 57 drones and a ballistic missile through its defenses in four days. It is not neutral. It is also not, in any meaningful sense, a co-belligerent. It is a country caught between its American security guarantor, who launched this war without meaningful Gulf consultation, and its Iranian neighbor, who is punishing Gulf states for hosting American bases. Al Hashimy’s call for “serious and responsible dialogue” is Gulf-state language for: someone needs to stop this war before our position becomes untenable.

Oman made a similar plea on Saturday. Qatar shot down Iranian bombers while calling the attacks “unanswerable.” Saudi Arabia’s embassy was struck and its defense ministry intercepted eight drones over Riyadh. Kuwait’s embassy was struck and closed. Jordan has been attacked. These are not Iran’s enemies. Several of them have spent decades cultivating working relationships with Tehran precisely to avoid being caught in exactly this position.

The Gulf states are discovering, in real time, that there was no middle position available to them. Their American alliance made them targets. Their geographic proximity to Iran made them vulnerable. Their economic model — built on stability, predictability, and the assumption that great-power conflict would remain at least one step removed from their territory — is being stress-tested in ways it was not designed to survive. The UAE minister said the right things. What she cannot say is who, exactly, she expects to conduct the serious and responsible dialogue she is calling for, given that the US Secretary of State told reporters Monday there is “no diplomatic exchange happening between the United States and Iran.”

Tuesday Morning Markets: The Bill Comes Due

The financial markets on Tuesday morning are doing what markets do when a war expands faster than the consensus expected: they are pricing fear.

US equity futures are down sharply ahead of the New York open, with S&P 500 futures off 1.8%. Monday’s brief late-session recovery — lifted by software and energy stocks — has been fully reversed in premarket trading. The pattern now visible across two full trading days is one of initial sell-off, partial stabilization, then renewed decline as each overnight news cycle brings fresh escalation. The market is learning, session by session, that this war is not contracting toward resolution.

In Asia, Tuesday’s session was the worst yet. South Korea’s Kospi index — one of the year’s better performers before this week — fell 7.2%, its steepest single-day drop in years. Japan’s Topix fell 3%. Taiwan fell 2%. The common thread across East Asian markets is energy dependence: South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan import the vast majority of their oil and gas, much of it transiting the Strait of Hormuz, and they have no short-term alternatives to the supply that is now being disrupted. These economies were already navigating Trump’s tariff regime. They are now absorbing an energy shock on top of a trade shock. ING’s analysts called it “the mother of all bad timings.” The Asian market numbers are what that phrase looks like in practice.

In Europe, the Stoxx Europe 600 fell more than 3% — its steepest drop since last April — with every market on the continent in the red. London, Paris, and Frankfurt all fell between 2% and 3%. The EU’s emergency energy meetings, convened for every remaining day of the week, are the institutional response; the market declines are the economic one.

Oil continues to climb. Brent crude reached $83 a barrel on Tuesday morning — up more than 6% on the day and the highest level since mid-2024. For context: when Trump began his second term, oil was cheaper than it is now. The president who ran partly on lower energy costs is presiding over an oil price that is approaching levels his base will feel at the pump within weeks, not months.

The natural gas picture is more acute. European gas futures surged another 25% on Tuesday, extending Monday’s near-50% jump. In 48 hours, European natural gas prices have roughly doubled. Qatar has halted not just LNG production but downstream products — polymers, aluminum — meaning the supply disruption is radiating outward from fuel into industrial inputs. Goldman Sachs has projected that a one-month Hormuz closure could push European gas prices up 130% from pre-war levels. We are now past the 100% mark and the strait has been closed for four days.

Two safe-haven signals are worth noting for what they reveal about the market’s current psychology. Gold is trading flat at approximately $5,300 per ounce — not the dramatic surge one might expect in a full-scale war scenario. The dollar, by contrast, is up 0.8%, its second consecutive day of similar gains. The divergence suggests investors are not yet pricing a catastrophic breakdown — they are pricing a prolonged, damaging, inflation-generating conflict that benefits the dollar as a haven currency while not yet triggering the kind of systemic fear that sends gold sharply higher. Bond markets are moving more aggressively: the 10-year US Treasury yield rose to 4.1%, with British, German, and Japanese government bonds rising even more steeply. The bond market is pricing higher inflation and higher interest rates. That means higher mortgage costs, higher borrowing costs for businesses, and a headwind for the equity valuations that have already been marked down twice this week.

The political economy of this is the number Trump will be watching more closely than any battlefield report. He owes his presidency in significant part to public anger over the price of groceries. Higher oil means higher fuel costs means higher transport costs means higher prices for everything that moves on a truck. Kenneth Rogoff put it plainly: “We’re going to end up spending a lot more on the military, and it’s going to have implications for interest rates and inflation. That’s baked in the cake.” The cake, on Tuesday morning, is rising faster than anyone in the administration publicly acknowledged it would.

What Has Settled, What Has Not

As of Tuesday morning, Day Four of Operation Epic Fury / Roaring Lion:

Settled: The administration’s legal rationale for the war has collapsed in public, contradicted by the Secretary of State’s own words within a single press conference, abandoned quietly when intelligence agencies contradicted it.

Settled: The energy war is real and worsening. European gas prices have doubled in 48 hours. QatarEnergy has halted LNG and downstream production. The Hormuz closure is an explicit IRGC policy. The Fujairah refueling hub has been touched. This is no longer a risk scenario. It is underway.

Settled: Israel is conducting a ground operation in Lebanon. It is not a patrol. It is territorial seizure with evacuation orders covering over 80 villages. Hezbollah has declared open war.

Settled: Ground troops in Iran are not ruled out. The president said so himself.

Settled: The democratic transition being invoked as moral justification for this war has no organizational infrastructure, no alternative elite, no on-the-ground game. The administration dismantled what little existed before launching the operation.

Not settled: Whether the Larijani-Qalibaf back-channel survives the public repudiations from all three parties and produces a ceasefire framework. Whether the “big wave” Trump promised produces an Iranian decision point or simply more dead on both sides. Whether Israeli ground forces in Lebanon trigger a different scale of engagement. Whether European powers move from defensive engagement language to actual participation. Whether the munitions supplemental funding request passes a Congress that is simultaneously watching the war’s approval ratings (27% overall, 55% Republicans) and its own upcoming midterm arithmetic.

Not settled: What this war is for.

What is settled is the trajectory. A war launched with defined objectives and a four-to-five-week timeline has, in four days, opened a second ground front, closed American embassies, doubled European energy prices, and produced an endgame scenario — the “Chinese model” for Iran — that has nothing to do with what the administration told the public it was fighting for.

The Iranian people, Azizi said, feel “powerless.” The big decisions affecting their lives rest with the president of the United States, with Israel, and with the factions of their own underrepresented regime. “There’s not a lot they can do about it.”

That is also, it turns out, roughly the position of the American public. Sixty-three percent disapprove. Seventeen million people follow Breitbart. It all comes down to the results. The results, on Day Four, are six dead, two closed embassies, one open ground war in Lebanon, and an energy shock that Kenneth Rogoff is comparing to the assassination that started the First World War.
