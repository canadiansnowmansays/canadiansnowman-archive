When Democracy Fights Back

When Hoosiers Said No

Part 8: How Indiana Republicans Defeated Trump’s Redistricting Juggernaut

December 12, 2025

Republican Indiana State Sen. Mike Gaskill, left, sponsor of the redistricting legislation, is questioned by Sen. Greg Walker before a vote to redistrict the state’s congressional map on Thursday, at the Statehouse in Indianapolis. Michael Conroy/AP

They threatened federal funding, swatted senators, and harassed grandmothers. Indiana Republicans said: “We don’t take orders.”

After Part 7’s darkness—watching SCOTUS use its shadow docket to override careful fact-finding with a cursory holiday weekend order—I need to tell you about something remarkable that happened in Indiana.

Sometimes democracy actually wins.

Not because the courts protected it. Not because the legal system worked. But because 21 Republican state senators looked at the most powerful people in their party demanding obedience, looked at the threats and intimidation and harassment, and said: “No.”

On Thursday, December 5th, the Indiana Senate voted 31-19 against Trump’s redistricting push. That vote included a majority of Republicans who defied the President, the Vice President, Heritage Foundation, Club for Growth, Turning Point USA, and the entire MAGA apparatus.

This is the story of how they did it. And why it matters.

The Grandmother and the Basketball Team

Jean Leising is 76 years old. She’s a grandmother of eight. She was first elected to the Indiana Senate in 1988—before some of her colleagues were born. She voted for Donald Trump three times.

This fall, she spoke at a breakfast at her 8th grade grandson’s school about civic engagement and public service.

Hours later, when she was set to give him a ride home from basketball practice, he bashfully told her that his entire team had received text messages about her that day.

“And they were all bad,” he said.

Leising told CNN she laughed it off with her grandson at the moment. But that night, alone, she got angry.

“Boy, when I got home that night, that’s when I decided,” she said. “I was angry. So the next day, I said, ‘I’ve got to talk about this.’ Because this is over the top. This shouldn’t be the way it was.”

“But that was the beginning,” she added. “It only got worse from there.”

Think about that for a moment. A 76-year-old grandmother, elected official for 37 years, who voted for Trump three times—and Trump supporters harassed her grandson’s entire basketball team because she wasn’t falling in line fast enough on redistricting.

That’s not politics. That’s intimidation. That’s using children to terrorize their grandparents into political compliance.

And it backfired spectacularly.

The Father and the Slur

Senator Mike Bohacek has a daughter with Down syndrome.

When Trump used a disability slur in a Truth Social post mocking Minnesota Governor Tim Walz, Bohacek was offended. He said Trump’s “choices of words have consequences.”

For Bohacek, redistricting wasn’t an abstract question about partisan advantage. It was about what kind of political culture we normalize. What kind of behavior we reward.

If a president can casually use disability slurs, face no consequences, and then demand absolute loyalty on redistricting—what message does that send?

Bohacek voted no.

The Swatting Victim

Senator Greg Walker represents Columbus, Indiana—former Vice President Mike Pence’s hometown.

In the weeks leading up to the vote, Walker was targeted by a swatting attempt. For those unfamiliar with the term: someone calls in a fake emergency to law enforcement, usually claiming violence or hostages, designed to provoke a SWAT team response at the target’s home.

Heavily armed police responded to Walker’s home based on a false emergency call. He later said he was fortunate because, unlike other Republicans subjected to swatting attempts, he didn’t have small children who might have been traumatized.

Walker said that while law enforcement hasn’t publicly linked the swatting to a political motive, he felt that voting yes would reward wrongdoing and set a dangerous precedent.

In the hearing’s closing moments, through tears, Walker described having held a friend’s newborn the night before and worrying about the world the child would inherit.

“I fear for this institution,” Walker said. “I fear for the state of Indiana and I fear for all states if we allow threats and intimidation to become the norm.”

Then he voted no.

The Pressure Campaign

Trump didn’t just ask Indiana Republicans to redistrict. He launched an unprecedented pressure campaign.

Trump himself:

  • Posted repeatedly on Truth Social naming individual senators

  • Threatened primary challengers against anyone who voted no

  • Called Senate President Pro Tem Rodric Bray repeatedly, targeting him specifically

  • Said Bray “enjoys being the only person in the United States of America who’s against Republicans picking up extra seats”

Vice President JD Vance:

  • Traveled to Indiana twice to meet with lawmakers

  • Accused Bray of dishonesty: “Rod Bray has consistently told us he wouldn’t fight redistricting while simultaneously whipping his members against it. That level of dishonesty cannot be rewarded, and the Indiana GOP needs to choose a side.”

Heritage Foundation: Tweeted on the day of the vote: “President Trump has made it clear to Indiana leaders if the Indiana Senate fails to pass the map, all federal funding will be stripped from the state, roads will not be paved, guard bases will close, major projects will stop. These are the stakes, and every no vote will be to blame.”

Read that again. Heritage Foundation—the organization behind Project 2025—threatened to strip ALL federal funding from Indiana. Roads wouldn’t be paved. Military bases would close. Major projects would stop.

That’s not persuasion. That’s extortion.

Club for Growth & Trump Campaign Veterans: Aired ads threatening to oust incumbent senators who voted against redistricting.

Turning Point USA: The organization founded by Charlie Kirk (before his assassination in September) vowed to back primary challenges and hosted a rally at the Indiana Statehouse.

Don Jr.: “If Indiana Republicans side with these never Trumpers to do the dirty work of Democrats, I’ll be spending a lot of time in Indiana next year campaigning against every single one of them.”

This wasn’t a policy debate. This was a loyalty test enforced with threats, intimidation, harassment, and violence.

“You Wouldn’t Change Minds By Being Mean”

Senator Sue Glick, elected in 2010, summed up why the pressure campaign backfired:

“Hoosiers are a hardy lot, and they don’t like to be threatened. They don’t like to be intimidated. They don’t like to be bullied in any fashion. And I think a lot of them responded with, ‘That isn’t going to work.’ And it didn’t.”

Senator Greg Goode, whose town hall in Terre Haute revealed massive public opposition to mid-decade redistricting, criticized the “over-the-top pressure from inside the Statehouse and outside,” as well as “threats of violence, acts of violence.”

“Whether we realize it or not, whether we accept it or not, the forces that define this vitriolic political affairs in places outside of Indiana have been gradually and now very blatantly infiltrated the political affairs in Indiana,” he said before voting no.

Jean Leising put it simply: “You wouldn’t change minds by being mean. And the efforts were mean-spirited from the get-go. If you were wanting to change votes, you would probably try to explain why we should be doing this, in a positive way. That never happened, so, you know, I think they get what they get.”

Several senators noted that their constituents opposed mid-decade redistricting. That the maps would splinter communities with similar interests. That the precedent was dangerous.

But underlying all of it was a deeper question: Do we reward threats and intimidation? Do we legitimize swatting, harassment of children, and extortion as acceptable tools of political persuasion?

Twenty-one Republican senators said no.

The Vote and the Aftermath

The final vote was 31-19 against. A majority of Republicans voted no.

Senate President Pro Tem Rodric Bray announced after the vote that under Indiana Senate rules, the chamber cannot take up the maps again during its 2026 session. The redistricting effort is dead.

Trump won Indiana by nearly 20 points in 2024. It’s a deep red state where Republican control seemed absolute. And yet when Trump demanded obedience, Indiana Republicans gave him a massive political black eye.

“I Wasn’t Very Much Involved”

When asked about the failure, Trump gave the most revealing response possible:

“Well, we won every other state. That’s the only state. It’s funny cuz I won Indiana all three times by a landslide. And I wasn’t working on it very hard. Would have been nice. I think we would have picked up two seats if we did that. You had one gentleman, the head of the Senate, I guess, Bray, whatever his name is. I heard he was against it. He’ll probably lose his next primary, whenever that is. I hope he does, but because he’s done a tremendous disservice.”

Later: “I wasn’t very much involved. But there’s a man named... Bray? Is that the name Bray? And uh I mean he’ll I I’m sure that whatever his primary is, it’s I think in two years, but I’m sure he’ll go down. He’ll go down. I’ll certainly support anybody that wants to go against him.“

Let’s review what “I wasn’t very much involved” apparently means:

  • Posting repeatedly on Truth Social about Indiana

  • Naming individual senators and threatening primaries

  • Calling Bray repeatedly

  • Dispatching the Vice President to Indiana twice

  • Mobilizing Heritage Foundation, Club for Growth, Turning Point USA, and Don Jr.

  • Threatening to strip all federal funding from the state

If that’s not “very much involved,” what would actual involvement look like?

This is classic Trump: take credit for wins, deny responsibility for losses. When Texas passed redistricting, it was the “Big Beautiful Map.” When Indiana rejected it, suddenly he “wasn’t very much involved.”

Mike Johnson’s Pathetic Spin

House Speaker Mike Johnson tried even harder to rewrite reality:

“Not at all. He is not a lame duck. He’s the most powerful president of this generation and many others. We have a lot more work to do together. The outcome in Indiana is inconsequential to that.“

“No, he did not put a major pressure campaign for it. He let his opinion be known as he does on everything all the time, all day long. We win some, we lose some. It is no indication whatsoever about what’s going to happen next year in the midterm elections.”

Inconsequential. Win some, lose some. No big deal.

This from the Speaker of the House after Trump, Vance, Heritage Foundation, and the entire MAGA apparatus spent weeks trying to bully Indiana into compliance.

The only thing more pathetic than Johnson’s obsequious servility to Trump is watching him play make-believe about Trump’s waning influence.

The Updated Scoreboard

Here’s where the redistricting wars stand after Indiana:

Republican Gains:

  • Texas: +5 seats (upheld by SCOTUS shadow docket)

  • North Carolina: +1 seat

  • Missouri: +1 seat

  • Ohio: +2 seats

  • Total: +9 seats

Democratic Gains:

  • California: +5 seats

  • Virginia: +4 seats (in progress)

  • Utah: +1 seat (Republican gerrymander blocked in court)

  • Total: +10 seats (potentially)

Failed:

  • Indiana: Would have been +2 Republican seats - FAILED

Still Pending:

  • Florida (Republican)

  • Maryland (Democratic)

It may already be a wash. And that’s before considering the possibility of “dummymanders”—where Republican districts were drawn too thin and a Democratic wave flips more seats than Republicans intended.

But here’s what’s critical to understand: even if the scoreboard ends up balanced, the damage to democracy is real and lasting.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Power

Here’s what Trump and his allies learned in Indiana: there are still limits.

Not legal limits—SCOTUS proved those are dead. Not institutional limits—the DOJ is fully weaponized. But human limits. Limits imposed by people who simply refuse to comply.

Trump won Indiana by 20 points. He has unprecedented control over the Republican Party. He commands a media apparatus that can mobilize millions. He has billionaire allies and Supreme Court justices and the Heritage Foundation and every lever of power imaginable.

And 21 Republican state senators in Indianapolis said no.

That terrifies Trump more than any court ruling ever could. Because court rulings can be appealed, reversed, ignored. But when people stop being afraid of you—when the threats don’t work anymore—your power evaporates.

Trump’s response (”I wasn’t very much involved”) and Johnson’s spin (”inconsequential”) show they understand this. They’re trying to minimize the loss because they know what it signals: the emperor might not have any clothes.

Think about what Indiana prevented. If the vote had succeeded—if threats, intimidation, swatting, and harassment of grandchildren had worked—it would have established that these tactics are effective. Every future political fight would have escalated. Swatting would become routine. Harassing family members would be normalized. Threatening to strip federal funding would be the opening bid.

Indiana Republicans said: we refuse to legitimize that system.

Conclusion: When People Say No

Indiana doesn’t erase Texas. It doesn’t fix the shadow docket or stop future gerrymandering or save the Voting Rights Act. The doom loop continues—the 2026 House will still be determined more by maps than by voters.

But Indiana proves something critical: the loop requires compliance.

When people refuse to be intimidated, when they prioritize institutional integrity over partisan gain, when they say “we don’t take orders”—the machine can be stopped.

Not everywhere. Not permanently. But sometimes. In some places. By some people.

In Part 7, I wrote that the rule of law is already dead. That’s still true. The Supreme Court has become a partisan weapon that uses procedural shortcuts to protect Republican interests.

But the rule of law isn’t the only thing that matters. Sometimes democracy survives not because the law protects it, but because people protect it.

Twenty-one Republican senators in Indianapolis protected it. They stood up to the President, the Vice President, Heritage Foundation, and the entire MAGA apparatus. They refused to be intimidated. They refused to reward violence.

And for one week in December 2025, in Indianapolis, democracy fought back.

And democracy won.
