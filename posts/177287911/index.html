The Great Gayermandering Goes National

Part 2: Fifty Shades of Gerrymander or When One State’s Ridiculous Redistricting Scheme Becomes a Nationwide Trend

October 27, 2025

Editor’s Introduction

What began as Texas’s desperate attempt to gerrymander away inconvenient voters has metastasized into a full-blown national crisis—or opportunity, depending on which side of the aisle you sit on. Since our initial report on Texas’s pioneering “gayermandering” scheme, the practice has spread faster than a rumor at a book club meeting.

With Republicans now pursuing aggressive redistricting in Missouri, North Carolina, Indiana, Kansas, and Florida, while Democrats counter-program in California, Illinois, New York, and Maryland, the 2026 midterms have devolved into what political scientists are calling “the Great Redistricting Arms Race” or, more accurately, “Democracy’s Ugliest Moment Since That Time We All Pretended Hanging Chads Made Sense.”

The GOP’s three-seat House majority remains precarious, and rather than, say, developing popular policies, both parties have decided that redrawing maps to predetermine electoral outcomes is the way forward. As one anonymous political operative told us: “Why bother persuading voters when you can just... not count them?”

What follows is an update on how America’s states have each found their own creative way to make a mockery of representative democracy. Buckle up—it’s going to be fabulously dystopian.

Missouri: The “Show-Me-Your-Browser-History State”

Fresh off their successful September 2025 redistricting that targeted Kansas City Democrat Emanuel Cleaver, Missouri Republicans have doubled down with what they’re calling “Gayermandering 2.0: The Revenge.”

The new proposal would create districts based on “online shopping patterns.” State Senator Buck Rogers (R-Definitely-Not-Making-This-Up) explained: “We’ve noticed that certain zip codes have suspiciously high rates of home décor purchases from Anthropologie. This represents a clear and present danger to traditional values and geometrically sensible districts.”

The most controversial district, dubbed “The Bowtie Corridor,” connects five counties through a path that carefully avoids every Whole Foods, farmers market, and store that sells artisanal anything. Local cartographers describe it as “looking like someone tried to draw around their ex’s neighborhood but got really petty about it.”

Kansas City resident Tamara Brewster summed up the frustration: “My voting location is now 47 miles away in a town I’ve never heard of, but my neighbor across the street votes somewhere completely different. We have the same ZIP code but apparently different ‘cultural compatibility scores,’ whatever that means.”

North Carolina: Swing State, Swinging Wildly

Having just approved new maps in October 2025 to gain an additional Republican seat, North Carolina has become the first swing state to fully embrace gayermandering. But in a purple state, things get complicated.

The “Raleigh Rainbow Redistribution Act” attempts to identify voters based on what Republicans call “latte density”—the number of coffee shops per square mile that offer non-dairy milk alternatives. Districts now snake through neighborhoods like a caffeinated serpent trying to avoid bumping into anyone who’s ever used the term “farmhouse chic” unironically.

Democratic State Senator Reginald Common-Sense protested: “They’ve drawn our state capital into four different districts connected by roads so narrow you’d have trouble getting a Smart Car through. And they specifically excluded my neighborhood because—and I quote from the official legislative record—’suspiciously well-coordinated outdoor holiday decorations.’”

The most absurd result: One district is now 97% rural farmland connected to a single city block in Charlotte by a corridor exactly as wide as Interstate 77. Cartographers have named it “The Spite Snake.”

Local activist groups organized a “District Walking Tour” where participants attempted to walk the perimeter of their new district. The event was canceled after three days when organizers realized completing the journey would require passports to briefly enter Virginia and South Carolina.

California: The Democratic Counter-Offensive

Not to be outdone, California Democrats responded with their own ballot initiative (scheduled for November 4, 2025) that would implement “Progressive Gayermandering” or as Governor Gavin Newsom calls it, “Strategic Community-Based Electoral Justice Mapping.”

The proposal would create districts based on “cultural sophistication indices” including:

  • Kombucha consumption per capita

  • Percentage of residents who can pronounce “quinoa” correctly

  • Number of therapy appointments per 1,000 residents

  • Ratio of rescue dogs to purchased dogs

  • Frequency of phrases like “I’m just really into mindfulness right now”

State Republicans are furious. “This is discrimination!” shouted GOP leader Miles Behind. “Just because someone drives a lifted truck and has never heard of oat milk doesn’t mean their vote should be diluted!”

Democratic strategist Chandra Obvious-Point responded: “We’re literally doing exactly what you did in Texas, but with better farmers markets nearby.”

The most controversial proposed district connects San Francisco to Santa Barbara through a thin coastal corridor that’s been nicknamed “The Avocado Toast Trail.” It carefully excludes Bakersfield, Fresno, and any area where people have strong opinions about rodeos.

Former Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger, who helped create California’s independent redistricting commission, called the proposal “completely unacceptable” and urged voters to reject it. He was immediately labeled “out of touch with modern Democratic strategy” by party officials.

Kansas: Targeting Sharice Davids with Surgical Precision

Having allocated $460,000 for a special redistricting session, Kansas Republicans have unveiled the “Goodbye Sharice” district—a masterpiece of political cartography so specific it excludes any neighborhood where people know who Sharice Davids is.

The new 3rd District resembles what one political scientist described as “an octopus trying to hug everyone except that one annoying person at the party.” It connects suburban Kansas City to rural areas through a corridor that specifically avoids:

  • Any street with a Pride flag

  • Neighborhoods with community gardens

  • Areas where people compost

  • The entire city of Lawrence (too many college students who’ve taken gender studies classes)

Rep. Sharice Davids herself responded with characteristic grace: “My district now includes parts of three time zones, which is impressive considering Kansas only has two.”

The proposed map also features what locals have dubbed “The Anti-Topeka Tentacle”—a portion of the district that reaches around the state capital like it’s trying to avoid catching something contagious.

Indiana: The Hoosier Hysteria

Republican Governor Mike Braun, after private meetings with Trump and VP JD Vance, has proposed what insiders are calling “the most accidentally honest gerrymandering attempt in American history.”

The plan, officially titled “The Traditional Values Geographic Realignment Initiative” but internally referred to as “Operation: Make Indiana Even Redder,” would target Democratic Reps. Frank Mrvan and Andre Carson by creating districts based on “cultural compatibility zones.”

These zones are determined by:

  • Casserole-to-avocado-toast ratio at local restaurants

  • Percentage of residents who own more than three flannel shirts

  • Number of trucks with truck nuts per capita

  • Frequency of people saying “ope” when squeezing past someone

The Indianapolis-based district currently held by Andre Carson would be split into five pieces, each connected to rural areas through what the proposal literally describes as “Real America Corridors.”

One proposed district includes downtown Indianapolis connected to a rural county by a corridor that follows State Road 37 exactly—literally just the road itself. Residents living 50 feet from the highway would be in completely different districts.

“I can see my polling place from my backyard,” complained Indianapolis resident Marcus Johnson, “but according to these maps, I’m somehow in the same district as someone who lives next to a soybean field 80 miles away.”

Florida: Ron DeSantis Wants In

Governor DeSantis, not wanting to be left out of the redistricting bonanza, has proposed “Freedom Mapping”—a system so convoluted it makes Florida’s existing reputation for electoral chaos look quaint.

Florida Republicans insist their Freedom Mapping has nothing to do with discrimination, unlike that unpleasantness with Anita Bryant and the orange juice in the ‘70s. This is completely different, they assure us, while drawing districts that look suspiciously like they’re avoiding Pride parades.

The plan would create districts based on “Freedom Scores” calculated by:

  • Proximity to beaches (freedom™)

  • Distance from major universities (too much thinking reduces freedom)

  • Number of boats per household (peak freedom)

  • Frequency of saying “I moved here for the weather and no state income tax”

The most egregious example: A proposed district that includes all of the Florida Keys connected to Pensacola by a 600-mile-long corridor that’s exactly one house wide and carefully excludes Miami, Orlando, and Tampa entirely.

Marine biologists have pointed out that the corridor is “mostly underwater during high tide,” to which DeSantis responded, “That’s what makes it free.”

The proposal also includes what’s being called the “Villages Victory District”—a single district that contains The Villages retirement community plus rural areas in North Florida, carefully excluding any municipality with a public university or art museum.

Environmental activists have noted that one proposed district boundary runs directly through the Everglades, requiring voters to take an airboat to reach their polling place.

Ohio: Legally Required Chaos

Ohio finds itself in the unique position of being legally required to redistrict by state law, giving Republicans another opportunity to draw favorable maps targeting Democratic Reps. Marcy Kaptur and Emilia Sykes.

However, Ohio Republicans made a critical error: they asked an AI to help draw the maps based on “traditional Ohio values.” The AI, having been trained on social media data, created districts based on:

  • Strength of opinions about Ohio State vs. Michigan

  • Skyline Chili consumption patterns

  • Intensity of feelings about whether Ohio is technically Midwest or East Coast

  • Number of residents who’ve ever said “it’s not the heat, it’s the humidity”

The result is maps that make absolutely no sense geographically but perfectly capture the state’s identity crisis. One district is entirely based around I-71 and is literally just the highway plus a 50-foot buffer on each side. Residents are required to vote at rest stops.

Another district, dubbed “The Mistake by the Lake” (official title: “District 8”), includes Cleveland’s lakefront, Toledo, and—somehow—a portion of Portsmouth on the Ohio River, connected by the most convoluted path anyone has ever seen.

“The AI seems to have created a district based entirely on people who complain about Ohio weather,” explained political analyst Dr. Sarah Buckeye. “Which, frankly, is the most Ohio thing I’ve ever heard.”

New York, Illinois, and Maryland: Democratic Temptation

Democratic governors in these blue states have “floated the possibility” of counter-gerrymandering, which is political speak for “we’re totally doing this but want to seem reluctant.”

New York’s proposal, called “The Cuomo-Is-Gone-So-Anything-Goes Act,” would create districts based on bagel shop density and strong opinions about pizza. One proposed district connects Brooklyn to Buffalo through a corridor that follows the Erie Canal but is exactly 50 feet narrower than the canal itself, meaning the district technically exists but is mostly water.

The New York City-based districts would be drawn using what Democratic consultants call “True New York™ Algorithms” that measure:

  • Ratio of bodegas to Starbucks

  • Subway complaint frequency

  • Whether residents pronounce it “HOW-ston” or “HEW-ston” Street

Illinois is considering maps drawn around “cultural sophistication zones” determined by deep-dish pizza loyalty and ability to correctly pronounce “Des Plaines.” The proposed Chicago-area districts look like someone tried to create a jigsaw puzzle while blindfolded.

One district connects Chicago’s North Side to Carbondale through a single-lane corridor that political scientists have described as “geographically creative and constitutionally questionable.”

Maryland already has some of the most gerrymandered districts in America (the 3rd District famously looks like someone spilled spaghetti on a map), so their response is simply: “You think THIS is bad? Hold my Old Bay.”

The proposed new Maryland map includes a district that Democratic strategists describe as “optimized for electoral efficiency” and everyone else describes as “looking like a drunk octopus tried to draw a crab.”
