We’re Not in Kansas Anymore: A Warning About Normalization

If you’re reading this title and thinking “Oh, another Trump-Russia piece—we’ve heard all this before,” then congratulations: you’ve been successfully conditioned. That reflexive dismissal, that sense of “old news,” that fatigue with the whole topic—that’s not organic. It’s cultivated. It’s the goal.

Trump’s greatest accomplishment isn’t any policy achievement or political victory. It’s this: he’s made the unthinkable routine. He’s transformed “the President of the United States takes direction from Russia’s intelligence chief” from a civilization-ending scandal into background noise. Something we’re tired of discussing. Something that feels partisan rather than existential.

This is how democracies die—not in sudden coups, but in gradual normalization. Not when citizens are shocked by corruption, but when they’re bored by it.

So before you scroll past this thinking you’ve “heard it all before,” ask yourself: When was the last time you actually sat with the full weight of what’s happening? When did you last consider that an American president might be compromised by a hostile foreign power not as abstract political theory, but as documented reality unfolding in real-time?

We’re not in Kansas anymore. We haven’t been for a long time. The tornado hit in 2016, and we’re still pretending the house is on its foundation.

This is your refresher course. This is your reminder that none of this is normal. This is documentation that what you’ve been trained to dismiss as “conspiracy theory” or “old news” is actually the most important story in American political history—still unfolding, still metastasizing, still destroying democratic institutions while we scroll past to the next outrage.

If talking about Trump’s relationship with Putin feels redundant, if it triggers eye-rolls rather than alarm, if you find yourself thinking “can’t we move on?”—then Trump has accomplished exactly what he set out to do. He’s made you complicit in your own gaslighting.

So let’s start over. Let’s look at this as if we’re seeing it for the first time. Let’s apply what Elon Musk loves to call “first principles thinking”—stripping away all the accumulated assumptions, the political tribalism, the normalization, and asking the most basic question:

What would we conclude if we examined the relationship between Donald Trump and Vladimir Putin from first principles, based purely on documented evidence, without any prior political allegiances or fatigue with the topic?

The First Principles Framework

First principles thinking means breaking down a problem to its fundamental truths and reasoning up from there. Forget everything you think you know about Trump-Russia. Forget the Mueller Report controversies, the partisan battles, the media narratives. Start from scratch with the most basic logical foundations:

First Principle #1: A president’s primary obligation is to American interests, not personal interests.

This isn’t partisan. It’s constitutional. The presidential oath requires defending the Constitution and serving the American people. When personal financial interests, ego needs, or fear of exposure conflicts with national interest, the president faces a fundamental conflict of obligation.

First Principle #2: Financial dependency creates leverage and potential conflicts of interest.

This is basic human psychology and economic reality. When Person A depends on Person B for financial survival, Person A becomes vulnerable to Person B’s influence. This is why we have conflict-of-interest laws, why judges recuse themselves, why politicians are supposed to divest from businesses.

First Principle #3: Intelligence services use kompromat (compromising material) to control assets.

This isn’t speculation—it’s documented intelligence tradecraft used by every major intelligence service for centuries. The KGB and its successor services are particularly sophisticated at collecting compromising material and using it for long-term asset management.

First Principle #4: Patterns of behavior reveal underlying motivations better than individual incidents.

One suspicious action might be coincidence. Two might be correlation. But consistent patterns across years and multiple policy areas reveal systematic alignment. This is how we identify any form of organizational loyalty or hidden allegiance.

First Principle #5: When someone consistently acts against their stated interests while benefiting another party, we should examine whether they’re actually serving that other party.

If a corporate executive consistently made decisions that harmed their own company while benefiting a competitor, we’d investigate whether they were on the competitor’s payroll. If a general consistently made tactical decisions that helped the enemy, we’d investigate whether they were a traitor. The same logic applies to presidents.

Now, from these basic truths, let’s reason upward through the documented evidence. No assumptions. No tribal loyalty. No fatigue. Just facts and logic.

Because the alternative—accepting this as the new normal—means democracy ends not with a bang but with a shrug.

The Wrong Whisperer

Stephen Miller stands behind Trump, whispering cruelty into domestic policy. But Miller serves Trump's existing instincts—instincts Putin spent forty years creating. Miller is an advisor. Putin is the handler.

In September, I wrote about Stephen Miller as “The Political Whisperer”—examining how Trump’s Deputy Chief of Staff had mastered the psychology of influence, turning grief into rage at Charlie Kirk’s memorial service and systematically dismantling democratic norms through calculated manipulation. I detailed Miller’s cruel policies, his white nationalist connections, his fascist tactics, and his sophisticated understanding of how to whisper authoritarianism into a president’s ear.

But this morning, looking at that striking image—Putin leaning close to Trump, their profiles nearly touching against a blood-red background—I realized I’d been writing about the wrong whisperer.

Stephen Miller is undoubtedly dangerous. His fingerprints are all over family separation policies that deliberately traumatized thousands of children. The Southern Poverty Law Center documented over 900 emails showing Miller’s promotion of white nationalist literature, racist propaganda, and conspiracy theories. He’s the architect of Muslim bans, the pusher of ending DACA protections, the advocate for using military force against domestic protesters.

Miller understands how to manipulate Trump’s psychology, presenting radical policies as expressions of strength rather than capitulations to extremism. He whispers cruelty—that immigrants are invaders, that diversity is weakness, that authoritarian efficiency beats democratic deliberation.

But Miller is an advisor. A deputy. A functionary who serves at the president’s pleasure.

Vladimir Putin may be something far more consequential: Trump’s handler.

Advisors vs. Handlers: Understanding the Distinction

The distinction is crucial, and it cuts to the heart of what we’re actually witnessing.

An advisor suggests. A handler directs.

An advisor works within the subject’s existing framework. A handler shapes that framework itself.

An advisor serves the subject’s stated goals. A handler creates those goals to serve the handler’s interests.

Miller whispers how to implement authoritarian policies—the tactics, the messaging, the legal frameworks. Putin whispers why authoritarianism serves Russian strategic interests—the worldview, the philosophy, the strategic direction.

Miller’s influence is transactional and observable. We can track his policies, read his emails, watch his speeches, document his meetings. Putin’s influence operates in shadows—private meetings without note-takers, financial entanglements hidden through layers of intermediaries, kompromat that works through mere possibility of exposure.

Most tellingly: Miller serves Trump’s existing authoritarian instincts. But where did those instincts come from? What if Putin spent forty years creating the very psychological patterns that advisors like Miller now exploit?

The Horse Whisperer Metaphor

In the world of animal training, a “whisperer” possesses an almost mystical ability to communicate with creatures that others find unpredictable or uncontrollable. The horse whisperer calms wild stallions with subtle gestures. The dog whisperer transforms aggressive animals into obedient companions. They work not through force, but through intuitive understanding of psychology, instinct, and the deep currents that drive behavior.

The key to understanding whisperers is recognizing that their power comes from patience. They don’t break an animal in a single session. They study the creature over time, learning what motivates it, what frightens it, what it desires. They build trust slowly, making the animal believe that obeying the whisperer serves the animal’s own interests.

Stephen Miller operates with this kind of influence over crowds and movements. His Kirk memorial speech demonstrated his mastery: converting grief into rage, elevating the audience’s sense of mission while degrading their opponents. “You are nothing,” he told mourners about their enemies, then immediately pivoted to “We are the ones who build. We are the ones who create.”

But Miller’s whispering has limits. He can influence Trump’s tactics and messaging, but he can’t fundamentally redirect Trump’s strategic orientation. He’s working within a framework that already exists.

Putin, on the other hand, may have built the framework itself.

The Multiple Whisperers: A Chorus of Influence

Secretary of State Marco Rubio leans in to whisper, joining the chorus of domestic advisors competing for Trump's attention. But while Rubio whispers tactics, Putin whispered the strategic worldview that makes all other whispers possible.

Miller doesn’t whisper alone. A photograph from October 2025 captures Secretary of State Marco Rubio leaning close to Trump, passing a handwritten note urging him to “Approve a Truth Social post soon so you can announce [the] deal first” about the Gaza peace agreement.

The image is haunting in its resemblance to another famous photograph: Andy Card whispering into President George W. Bush’s ear on September 11, 2001, informing him that “a second plane hit the second tower. America is under attack.” But where Card delivered urgent breaking news in a national crisis, Rubio’s whisper was about message timing and political optics—a reminder of how the art of whispering has evolved from crisis management to narrative control.

Rubio represents a different kind of whisperer than Miller. Where Miller traffics in ideology and fear, Rubio specializes in diplomatic finesse and international relationship-building. Rubio understands that Trump responds to being positioned as a historic peacemaker, and he frames diplomatic initiatives accordingly.

Then there’s Jared Kushner, who operates through family bonds. He can survive failures that would doom others because his connection to Trump transcends professional relationships. When Kushner whispers, Trump hears family—perhaps the only voice that can sometimes override darker urgings.

Steve Witkoff, the real estate dealer turned “everything envoy,” speaks Trump’s native language of deals and leverage. Trump trusts Witkoff because he reminds Trump of himself.

These whisperers don’t always agree. Miller pushes for maximum confrontation; Rubio seeks diplomatic wins; Kushner focuses on legacy; Witkoff wants to close deals.

But they share crucial understanding: Trump doesn’t respond to policy briefings or detailed analysis. He responds to narrative, emotion, and ego. Each whisperer has mastered a different psychological key.

But There’s One Whisperer They All Defer To

Helsinki Summit, July 2018. After two hours alone with Putin (no American witnesses, notes confiscated afterward), Trump stood beside his handler and declared greater faith in Russia than in 17 American intelligence agencies.

The question is which whispers ultimately shape not just individual policies, but the fundamental strategic orientation of American foreign policy.

Because while Miller, Rubio, Kushner, and Witkoff compete for Trump’s attention in the West Wing, Vladimir Putin has been whispering to Trump for forty years—long before any of them entered the picture, long before Trump had political power, long before anyone imagined he could become president.

And unlike the domestic whisperers who shape tactics and messaging, Putin may have shaped something far more fundamental: Trump’s entire worldview, his understanding of power, his relationship to truth itself.

Miller can whisper cruelty into immigration policy. Rubio can whisper diplomatic strategy into Middle East peace efforts. Kushner can whisper family loyalty into personnel decisions. Witkoff can whisper deal-making into trade negotiations.

But Putin may have whispered the very framework within which all those other whispers operate. The attraction to authoritarianism? Putin’s whisper. The contempt for democratic allies? Putin’s whisper. The belief that strength means dominance and weakness means democracy? Putin’s whisper.

The domestic whisperers are playing a game whose rules Putin wrote.

The Evidence Demands a Reset

Here’s what we know, stripped of all partisan framing and normalization:

Financial Reality: Trump has documented financial dependencies on Russian sources spanning decades. His own sons have confirmed this in public statements. When American banks cut him off after casino bankruptcies, Russian money kept him afloat.

Behavioral Reality: Trump shows extraordinary deference to Putin that he shows to no other world leader. He believes Putin over his own intelligence agencies. He confiscates records of their private meetings. He consistently advances Russian strategic interests even when politically costly.

Pattern Reality: Across eight years and every major foreign policy area—Ukraine, NATO, Syria, intelligence sharing, election security—Trump’s decisions systematically benefit Russia while harming stated American interests.

Tradecraft Reality: Everything we know about how intelligence services cultivate long-term assets matches the Trump-Putin relationship. Financial leverage. Ego manipulation. Potential kompromat. Private cultivation over decades. Policy outcomes that serve the handler’s interests.

This isn’t connecting dots that don’t exist. This is looking at a completed picture and finally admitting what we’re seeing.

The Normalization Trap

But here’s the trap we’ve fallen into: We’ve heard versions of this story so many times that it no longer shocks us. The Mueller Report came and went. Impeachment happened and failed. Headlines about Russian connections scroll past daily. We’ve been conditioned to treat this as background noise, as partisan warfare, as “old news.”

This is exactly what normalization looks like. Not the sudden acceptance of dictatorship, but the gradual erosion of our capacity for alarm.

When Soviet dissidents tried to warn the West about Stalin’s purges in the 1930s, they encountered a similar problem. Western intellectuals had heard so many anti-Soviet stories that they’d become numb to them, dismissing even documented atrocities as propaganda. The normalization wasn’t that people accepted the purges—it’s that they stopped being able to process new information about them.

We’re in that moment now with Trump and Russia. The evidence keeps mounting, but our capacity to be shocked by it keeps diminishing.

That’s not an accident. That’s the strategy.

Trump’s approach to scandal has always been to create so many scandals that each individual one loses impact. Flood the zone with so much corruption that citizens experience outrage fatigue. Make the abnormal so constant that it becomes ambient.

But some truths are too important to let fatigue win.
