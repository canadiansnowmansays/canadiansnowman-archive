The Trump Accountability Project

Emoluments Violations

Part 24: Profiting from the Presidency (2017-2021)

February 26, 2026

The Trump International Hotel in Washington, D.C., at the Old Post Office in Washington, D.C., Oct. 30, 2016. (AaronP/Bauer-Griffin/GC Images, File / Getty Images)

The U.S. Constitution contains two “Emoluments Clauses” designed to prevent exactly what Donald Trump did during his presidency:

The Foreign Emoluments Clause (Article I, Section 9): No federal official shall “accept of any present, Emolument, Office, or Title, of any kind whatever, from any King, Prince, or foreign State” without congressional consent.

The Domestic Emoluments Clause (Article II, Section 1): The president “shall not receive... any other Emolument from the United States, or any of them” beyond the presidential salary.

The Framers wrote these clauses to prevent foreign governments and domestic entities from influencing the president through payments. As Alexander Hamilton wrote in Federalist No. 73: the emoluments ban would ensure that foreign governments “can neither weaken (the president’s) fortitude by operating on his necessities, nor corrupt his integrity by appealing to his avarice.”

Every previous modern president understood this. Jimmy Carter put his peanut farm in a blind trust. Barack Obama asked the Office of Legal Counsel if he could even accept his Nobel Prize money, since it technically comes from the government of Sweden.

Donald Trump did the opposite. He refused to divest from his businesses, installed his children to run them, and proceeded to use the presidency to funnel millions of dollars to his properties.

This is the story of the most brazen presidential self-dealing in American history—and how Trump faced zero consequences for systematic constitutional violations.

The Setup: Refusing to Divest

In January 2017, shortly before his inauguration, Trump held a press conference announcing how he would handle his business conflicts.

He would not divest. He would not use a blind trust. Instead, Trump would transfer management of his companies to his sons, Donald Jr. and Eric, while retaining full ownership.

Attorney Sheri Dillon claimed this arrangement avoided conflicts of interest. But every ethics expert disagreed. Trump would still profit from his businesses. He would still know what properties foreign leaders visited. He would still make decisions affecting his companies.

Trump promised to donate all profits from foreign governments to the U.S. Treasury. But the Trump Organization wouldn’t track which payments came from foreign governments, making it impossible to fulfill this pledge.

The stage was set for four years of systematic self-dealing.

Foreign Governments: $7.8 Million and Counting

In January 2024, House Oversight Democrats released a bombshell report: Trump’s businesses received at least $7.8 million from at least 20 foreign governments during his presidency.

The report covered just four Trump properties over two years (2017-2019). The real total is certainly much higher.

The Foreign Payors Included:

  • China (including the state-owned Industrial and Commercial Bank of China)

  • Saudi Arabia

  • Qatar

  • Kuwait

  • United Arab Emirates

  • Malaysia

  • Turkey

  • Afghanistan

  • India

  • Albania

NBC News found representatives of at least 22 foreign governments spent money at Trump properties, though the full extent remains unknown because the Trump Organization is private and refuses to disclose details.

The Trump International Hotel: A Foreign Government Magnet

A view of the lobby in the Trump International Hotel just after it opened on Pennsylvania Avenue in Washington, DC, on Sept. 12, 2016.Shawn Thew—EPA

Trump’s Washington, D.C. hotel—housed in a building leased from the federal government—became ground zero for influence-peddling.

Foreign Governments That Stayed or Held Events:

  • Bahrain

  • Kuwait

  • Azerbaijan

  • Saudi Arabia

  • Malaysia

  • Turkey

  • Afghanistan

  • Philippines

The hotel was perfectly positioned: blocks from the White House, owned by the president, visited by foreign officials seeking favor.

A House Oversight report from October 2024 detailed how Trump used the hotel to violate the Domestic Emoluments Clause, noting: “Despite the tall tale told by Eric Trump that U.S. Secret Service agents ‘stay at our properties for free,’ Donald Trump’s hotel repeatedly fleeced the U.S. Secret Service.”

The Hotel Overcharged the Secret Service:

  • Secret Service was charged $895 for a single room during a February 2018 Eric Trump visit—more than four times the government per diem rate of $201

  • That same night, rooms for Qatar’s royal family were charged less than $895

  • Trump charged taxpayers more than he charged foreign royals

Mar-a-Lago: The Winter White House Becomes a Profit Center

Mar-a-Lago cost the cereal heiress Marjorie Merriweather Post $7m to build in the 1920s – around $100m today (Image credit: Joe Raedle/Staff via Getty images)

Trump visited Mar-a-Lago 146 times during his presidency—nearly 10% of his days in office.

Each visit generated tens of thousands of dollars in revenue for Trump’s private club. The Secret Service alone spent over $300,000 at Mar-a-Lago protecting Trump and his family.

The Mar-a-Lago Money Machine:

After Trump became president, membership fees doubled from $100,000 to $200,000 annually. Members got access to the president—foreign diplomats, lobbyists, and anyone willing to pay could join and potentially interact with Trump.

Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe visited in February 2017. When Trump and Abe dined on the Mar-a-Lago patio, they discussed North Korea’s missile launch in front of club members and guests—a shocking national security breach.

The Government Accountability Office found Trump’s first four trips to Mar-a-Lago cost taxpayers an average of $3.4 million per trip—money that flowed to Trump’s property in the form of Secret Service charges, staff accommodations, and event costs.

The State Department: Promoting Trump Properties

In June 2018, the State Department promoted Mar-a-Lago in an official blog post, describing it as Trump’s “home away from home.”

American Oversight obtained documents showing the State Department approved Trump World Tower leases to representatives of foreign governments including Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE, Greece, Iraq, and Qatar from early 2017 through summer 2019—additional emoluments violations.

House Foreign Affairs Chairman Eliot Engel revealed in July 2019 that the State Department paid “exorbitant hotel and conference room fees and bar tabs” at Mar-a-Lago and over $90,000 to Trump Turnberry golf resort in Scotland.

The State Department reportedly proposed a $200,000 contract with Mar-a-Lago for all federal employee room costs through 2020. Mar-a-Lago refused, insisting on charging maximum federal rates—nearly double rates negotiated at nearby hotels—without competitive bidding.

The Secret Service: Nearly $2 Million at Trump Properties

Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) obtained Secret Service records through FOIA showing the agency spent nearly $2 million at Trump properties between 2017 and 2021.

The Breakdown:

  • Over $850,000 at Trump golf courses

  • Over $300,000 at Mar-a-Lago

  • Tens of thousands more at other Trump properties

Every time Trump visited his properties, taxpayers paid Trump to protect him. The more Trump traveled to his businesses, the more money flowed to Trump.

Eric Trump claimed agents “stay at our properties for free”—a claim documents prove was false.

Trump’s Ambassadors: Directing Business to the Boss

Several Trump ambassadors actively steered government business to Trump properties:

Kelly Craft, Trump’s ambassador to Canada, blatantly directed government business to the Trump International Hotel.

Eight ambassadors stayed at Trump’s D.C. hotel during their time in office, often on official business—direct violations of the domestic emoluments clause when using taxpayer funds.

Lana Marks, a Mar-a-Lago member with no diplomatic experience (she designed handbags), was nominated as Ambassador to South Africa in October 2018.

Cabinet Members and Federal Employees

Department of Defense officials spent nearly $1 million at Trump properties between July 2017 and November 2019.

Justice Department officials spent over $5,700 at Trump’s Doral and Soho properties in 2018.

DHS official Miles Taylor had a $1,600 dinner with a foreign government representative at the Trump International Hotel restaurant.

Even Ivanka Trump and Jared Kushner—senior White House advisors—were apparently charged for their Trump hotel stays, though records show Eric Trump, Lara Trump, and Donald Trump Jr. were not charged—raising questions about whether Ivanka and Jared used taxpayer funds.

China’s Gift to Ivanka: Strategic Trademark Approvals

Ivanka Trump with Xi Jinping at Mar-a-Lago

While Trump waged a trade war with China, the Chinese government granted Ivanka Trump’s business at least 34 trademarks in a single year.

The Suspicious Timing:

March 2017: Ivanka’s business received three Chinese trademarks the same day she dined with Chinese President Xi Jinping at Mar-a-Lago.

May 2018: Ivanka received approval for several Chinese trademarks a week before Trump announced he would lift the ban on Chinese company ZTE for violating U.S. sanctions.

May 2018: China also agreed to loan $500 million to a project in Indonesia where the Trump Organization was a partner—72 hours before Trump’s ZTE announcement.

October 2018: China granted 16 trademarks to Ivanka—including for voting machines and sausage casings—three months after Ivanka announced her brand was shutting down.

The trademarks covered fashion items, beauty services, hotels, and bizarrely, voting machines—raising eyebrows given Trump’s later false claims about voting machine fraud.

CREW noted: “Since she has retained her foreign trademarks, the public will continue to have to ask whether President Trump has made foreign policy decisions in the interest of his and his family’s businesses.”

The Emoluments Lawsuits: Killed by Standing Issues

Three major lawsuits challenged Trump’s emoluments violations:

1. CREW (Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington): Filed the day after Trump’s inauguration, arguing Trump’s businesses benefited unconstitutionally from foreign payments.

2. Maryland and D.C.: State attorneys general sued, arguing Trump’s D.C. hotel gave him unfair business advantages and harmed their tax revenues.

3. Congressional Democrats: Over 200 Democratic members of Congress sued, arguing Trump violated the foreign emoluments clause.

All three lawsuits were ultimately dismissed on standing grounds—courts ruled the plaintiffs lacked the legal right to sue. Critically, this means courts never ruled on whether Trump actually violated the Constitution; they simply decided the plaintiffs couldn’t bring the case. The constitutional questions remain unresolved.

The Maryland/D.C. case came closest. A federal judge ruled the case could proceed, asking: “When a President maintains a premier hotel property that generates millions of dollars a year in profits, how likely is it that he will not be swayed, whether consciously or subconsciously, in any and all of his dealings with foreign or domestic governments that might choose to spend large sums of money at that hotel property?”

But Trump appealed, and the cases dragged through courts. By the time Trump left office, the issues became moot. The violations stopped because Trump was no longer president—not because courts held him accountable.

What Made It Possible: The Honor System

NPR reporter David Fahrenthold explained how Trump exploited the system: “The safeguards that kept the president from using the presidency to benefit themselves were mostly followed as kind of an honor system.”

Previous presidents followed norms:

  • Divesting from businesses

  • Using blind trusts

  • Avoiding even the appearance of conflicts

  • Respecting constitutional restrictions

Trump ignored all of it. And the enforcement mechanisms—lawsuits that take years, congressional oversight he refused to cooperate with, ethics guidelines with no teeth—proved inadequate.

As Fahrenthold noted: “He exploits honor systems. He uses honor systems as a way... he’s violated the honor system. And it takes everybody else a few... you know, a while to figure out what’s going on because they’re so used to people following it.”

The Total Cost: Unknown and Unknowable

We know Trump’s businesses received:

  • $7.8 million minimum from foreign governments (documented in just 4 properties over 2 years)

  • Nearly $2 million from Secret Service

  • Nearly $1 million from the Defense Department

  • Hundreds of thousands from other federal agencies

  • Unknown millions from state and local governments, foreign visitors, lobbyists seeking access

But the full amount is unknowable because:

  • The Trump Organization is private and won’t disclose figures

  • Trump refused to track which payments came from foreign governments

  • Many transactions were obscured through shell companies

  • Post-presidency benefits (like China’s trademark approvals) have ongoing value

The Accountability That Never Came

Trump faced zero legal consequences for systematically violating the Constitution’s anti-corruption provisions.

The emoluments lawsuits were dismissed on standing without ever determining if Trump broke the law. No criminal charges were filed. No impeachment articles addressed the emoluments violations. Congress held some hearings but had no enforcement power.

Trump not only avoided punishment—he profited massively. His D.C. hotel alone generated millions in revenue from foreign governments, federal agencies, and influence-seekers.

The message was clear: a president can use the office to enrich himself, violate explicit constitutional prohibitions, and face no consequences—as long as courts can’t agree on who has standing to sue.

Conclusion: The Presidency as Business Opportunity

The Framers wrote the Emoluments Clauses to prevent exactly what Trump did: using the presidency to extract payments from foreign and domestic governments.

Alexander Hamilton warned that without these protections, foreign governments could appeal to a president’s “avarice.” That’s exactly what happened.

Trump made the presidency a business opportunity. He funneled millions to his properties through government travel, foreign visitors seeking favor, and memberships that granted access. He used his office to secure Chinese trademarks for his daughter. He turned the White House into a profit center.

And he faced no accountability.

The emoluments lawsuits died on procedural grounds. The House investigated but couldn’t enforce. Ethics watchdogs documented violations but had no power.

Trump’s presidency proved that constitutional anti-corruption provisions mean nothing if courts won’t enforce them and Congress won’t impeach over them.

The Framers tried to prevent presidential avarice. Trump proved their safeguards weren’t enough.

Trump violated the Constitution's emoluments clauses systematically for four years, profiting millions from the presidency while courts dismissed lawsuits on procedural grounds. But constitutional violations weren't the only laws Trump's administration treated as suggestions. The Hatch Act—designed to prevent federal officials from using their positions for partisan political activities—was shredded just as thoroughly.
