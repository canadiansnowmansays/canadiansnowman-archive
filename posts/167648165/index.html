Episode 6: The Putin TACO, Part 2 - From "Children Fighting" to Complete Capitulation

July 8, 2025

Stephen Lawton
July 08, 2025

Welcome to Episode 6 of Trump TACO Tuesdays, where we continue tracking the most psychologically revealing TACO pattern of Trump's presidency. In Episode 2, we documented how Trump's "24 hours" Ukraine promise evolved through desperate "please stop" pleas to the ultimate surrender of comparing Ukraine and Russia to "two young children fighting" he might "let fight for a while."

Six weeks later, the Putin TACO has reached an even more complete capitulation. Trump's July 3rd call with Putin—their sixth since January—represents the final collapse of any pretense that American pressure might influence Russian behavior. What we're witnessing isn't just policy accommodation but the complete psychological submission of American power to Putin's strategic manipulation.

Where We Left Off: The Pattern Established

Our June 10th analysis documented the classic TACO progression:

  • Bold Promise: "I will have that war settled in one day – 24 hours"

  • Reality Check: Public "please stop" plea followed by 400-drone Russian response

  • Accommodation: Request for Senate sanctions delay, focus on "trade opportunities"

  • Surrender: "Sometimes you're better off letting them fight for a while"

The psychological dynamics were clear: Putin's calculated humiliation (taking calls from provincial music schools), Trump's fear-driven decision making, and the exploitation of personal vulnerabilities that transcend normal diplomatic relations.

What we predicted was further escalation of Russian aggression met with deeper American accommodation. The July 3rd call has confirmed this prediction in the starkest possible terms.

The Latest Reality Check: Six Months of Complete Failure

The numbers tell the TACO story better than any rhetoric. Six calls with Putin since January. Zero progress toward peace. Trump admitted after their latest conversation: "I didn't make any progress with him at all".

This represents the complete failure of Trump's foundational foreign policy promise. The candidate who claimed unique ability to end the war through personal relationships and superior deal-making has been reduced to acknowledging total ineffectiveness after six months of direct engagement.

The pattern we identified in June has accelerated beyond our predictions. Rather than gradual accommodation, we're seeing outright capitulation disguised as frustrated honesty.

The Escalation-Accommodation Cycle Perfected

The timing of the July 3rd call validates our analysis of Putin's psychological dominance. It came just hours after Ukrainian forces launched a spectacular drone operation against Russia's nuclear-capable bombers, damaging many of them and drawing a vow of reprisal from Putin.

Putin's response followed the exact playbook we documented: escalate military pressure, then offer Trump the psychological comfort of direct dialogue. Putin told Trump that Moscow will not give up on its goal of eliminating the "root causes" of the war in Ukraine, using language that reinforces his maximalist position while appearing to engage diplomatically.

Trump's reaction revealed the complete success of this manipulation. Rather than leverage Ukraine's successful operation to pressure Putin, Trump told reporters on Thursday that he came away disappointed from a telephone call with Russian President Vladimir Putin because it does not appear the latter is looking to stop Russia's war against Ukraine.

This represents the ultimate TACO psychology: accepting responsibility for diplomatic failure while absolving the aggressor of accountability.

The New Low: "Very Unhappy" But Still No Action

The most revealing aspect of Trump's latest accommodation involves his escalating rhetoric about Putin matched by continued refusal to impose consequences. "It's a very tough situation. I told you I was very unhappy with my call with President Putin. He wants to go all the way, just keep killing people, it's no good," Trump told reporters aboard Air Force One.

This represents a new stage in the TACO pattern: explicit acknowledgment of Putin's intransigence combined with continued accommodation. Trump has moved beyond the "children fighting" analogy to direct criticism of Putin's bloodthirstiness—yet still refuses to apply meaningful pressure.

The sanctions threat remains pure theater: "We talk about sanctions a lot," Trump said. "He understands that it may be coming". This conditional language after six months of failed diplomacy reveals the complete emptiness of American deterrence under Trump's leadership.

The Accommodation Contradiction

The most damaging revelation came immediately after Trump's "very unhappy" comments: While Trump has expressed frustration with Russia's continued aggression, his administration has not imposed new sanctions nor taken steps to pressure the Kremlin directly.

This perfectly encapsulates the Putin TACO's final stage. Trump maintains the rhetoric of strength ("very unhappy," sanctions "may be coming") while providing the reality of weakness (no new sanctions, no pressure, continued dialogue despite admitting "no progress").

Putin reads these contradictions perfectly. The rhetoric provides Trump with domestic face-saving while the lack of action confirms American impotence. This dynamic enables continued Russian aggression while Trump maintains the illusion of tough diplomacy.

The Ultimate Validation: Weapons Pause During Russian Escalation

The most dramatic confirmation of our TACO analysis came with perhaps the most strategically damaging decision of Trump's presidency: The strikes come as the Trump administration pauses some weapons shipments to Ukraine, including air defense missiles, following a review of military spending and American support to foreign countries.

The timing could not be more revealing. As Russia launched a record Russian drone attack that came just hours after US President Donald Trump said he had made "no progress" towards a ceasefire in a call with Russian counterpart Vladimir Putin, Trump's response was to reduce Ukrainian defense capabilities.

This sequence validates every aspect of our Putin TACO analysis:

  • Putin's Escalation Strategy: Launch massive attacks immediately after diplomatic contact to test American resolve

  • Trump's Accommodation Response: Reduce support for the victim rather than pressure the aggressor

  • Psychological Manipulation: Putin's confidence that Trump will frame any escalation as justification for American disengagement

The weapons pause represents the ultimate strategic incoherence: punishing allies for being attacked while rewarding adversaries for attacking them.

The Congressional Reality Check: Bipartisan Recognition of Failure

The institutional constraints we predicted in Episode 2 are finally emerging, but too late to prevent strategic damage. Democratic and Republican lawmakers have begun lobbying Trump to significantly ratchet up US sanctions after the weekend attacks.

More significantly, More than 80 senators have signed onto the bill so far supporting "crippling" new measures on Moscow, including "secondary sanctions" like massive 500% tariffs on countries buying Russian energy.

This bipartisan pressure represents the institutional wisdom recognizing Trump's diplomatic failure. When 80 senators from both parties unite behind sanctions that the president refuses to implement, it signals complete congressional loss of confidence in executive foreign policy leadership.

Yet even this pressure faces the TACO accommodation pattern: After speaking with Putin last week, Trump told European leaders on a telephone call that he would not join them, for now, in applying new measures on Moscow.

The Allied Dimension: European Frustration Reaches Breaking Point

Our analysis predicted European frustration with Trump's bilateral approach to Russia. The latest developments confirm this prediction while revealing even deeper alliance strain.

After Trump's most recent comments, French President Emmanuel Macron voiced hope the US leader would change course. "President Trump realizes that when President Putin said on the phone he was ready for peace, or told his envoys he was ready for peace, he lied," Macron said Monday.

This represents unprecedented allied criticism of American presidential judgment. When the French president publicly declares that Trump has been deceived by Putin's lies, it signals complete European loss of confidence in American leadership.

The alliance implications extend beyond rhetoric. European leaders are now openly strategizing around American unreliability rather than with American coordination. This represents the exact strategic isolation we predicted would result from the Putin TACO's progression.

The Strategic Catastrophe: Institutional Corruption Spreads

The most troubling development involves how Putin TACO psychology is corrupting other policy areas. The same accommodation patterns we see with Russia are simultaneously playing out in Trump's trade policy, where he admitted this weekend that he has not made trade deals with any countries. Instead, he is sending them letters to tell them what their tariffs are going to be, and Trump says he will call this a deal.

This reveals the broader psychological pattern: when bold promises ("24 hours" for Ukraine, "90 deals in 90 days" for trade) meet reality constraints, Trump accommodates through linguistic redefinition rather than strategic adjustment. Sending form letters becomes "making deals" just as continued dialogue with Putin becomes "diplomatic progress" despite admitting zero results.

Beyond trade accommodation, Trump has said privately he is concerned new sanctions could push Russia away from peace talks.

This logic represents the complete inversion of strategic thinking. Rather than using pressure to create incentives for negotiation, Trump avoids pressure to preserve the illusion of diplomatic progress. This accommodation mindset validates every aspect of our psychological analysis.

The institutional spread became clear when Trump "believes, that right now if you start threatening sanctions, the Russians will stop talking, and there is value in us being able to talk to them and to drive them to get to the table," Rubio told lawmakers.

When the Secretary of State adopts language justifying accommodation to Russian aggression, the psychological corruption extends beyond the president to core foreign policy institutions.

The Kompromat Validation: Behavior Beyond Explanation

The pattern of Trump's behavior with Putin continues to suggest leverage beyond normal diplomatic relations. No strategic framework can explain why the world's most powerful military responds to aggression by reducing support for victims while maintaining dialogue with aggressors.

The weapons pause during maximum Russian escalation represents behavior so contrary to American interests that it requires psychological rather than strategic explanation. Our "frightened little boy" analysis from Episode 2 provides that framework, but the implications have grown even more serious.

When American policy consistently serves Russian interests despite overwhelming American advantages, it suggests vulnerabilities that transcend normal political accommodation. The Putin TACO has revealed these vulnerabilities in their starkest form.

The Final Stage: Complete Strategic Surrender

The July 3rd call and its aftermath represent the Putin TACO's completion. Trump has moved beyond accommodation to explicit acknowledgment of failure while maintaining the very policies that ensure continued failure.

The linguistic evolution tells the complete story:

  • January: "24 hours" to end the war

  • April: "Please stop" public plea

  • June: "Let them fight for a while"

  • July: "I didn't make any progress with him at all"

This represents the most complete TACO sequence we've documented: from absolute confidence to explicit admission of total failure, yet with continued pursuit of the same failed approach.

What This Means: American Credibility in Free Fall

The Putin TACO's completion has implications far beyond Ukraine policy. When the American president admits total ineffectiveness against strategic adversaries while refusing to use available leverage, it signals fundamental weakness that other adversaries will exploit.

For China: Beijing now has six months of evidence that American threats lack credibility when confronting major powers. The Taiwan implications are profound.

For Iran: Tehran observes American accommodation to nuclear-armed adversaries and adjusts its own calculations accordingly.

For Allies: European and Indo-Pacific partners must plan around American unreliability rather than American strength.

For Markets: The weapons pause during maximum Russian aggression signals policy incoherence that makes strategic planning impossible.

Conclusion: The Ultimate TACO Revealed

The Putin TACO has demonstrated the complete collapse of American deterrence when psychological manipulation neutralizes institutional advantages. Six months of failed diplomacy, six calls with Putin, zero progress toward peace—yet continued accommodation disguised as strategic patience.

Most damaging is Trump's own admission of failure combined with refusal to change approach. When leaders acknowledge ineffectiveness while maintaining failed policies, it signals psychological rather than strategic decision-making.

Putin's masterful exploitation of Trump's vulnerabilities has achieved what no adversary has accomplished since World War II: the voluntary self-constraint of American power despite overwhelming advantages. The frightened little boy hasn't just failed to confront the strategic strongman—he has handed him the psychological victory of American withdrawal from global leadership.

The Putin TACO validates our framework's most serious insight: when personal psychology overrides institutional constraints and strategic interests, accommodation becomes indistinguishable from surrender. The international community now faces the reality that American power can be neutralized through psychological manipulation rather than military confrontation.

This represents more than policy failure—it's the revelation that some TACO patterns threaten the foundations of international order itself. When the world's dominant power consistently accommodates aggression while admitting failure, it creates precedents that will define the global system for decades to come.

Trump Always Chickens Out—even when the stakes involve American credibility, allied confidence, and global stability.
