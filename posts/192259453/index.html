"Take the Exit, Donald"

A Travel Advisory for a Man Who Has Never Used GPS and Would Not Trust It If He Had

March 26, 2026

The men who put Trump’s signature on the dollar, sent the 82nd Airborne to the Persian Gulf, gave Iran $14 billion in sanctions relief, and are now being asked by 800 tanker captains to please, at some point, find an exit ramp. Trump cabinet meeting, Thursday, White House — Doug Mills/The New York Times

A note to my readers: “The Sunday Satire” publishes on Sundays. This is being published Thursday evening. I am aware of this. However, the situation in the Persian Gulf is deteriorating at a pace that does not respect publishing schedules, and I have reason to believe that by Sunday, the following advice will either have been taken — in which case it will be historical comedy — or ignored — in which case it will be tragic comedy. Either way, it seemed inadvisable to wait. Consider this an emergency edition. The Sunday Satire regrets the inconvenience and asks that you adjust your weekend reading schedule accordingly. The Iranian regime, the bond market, and approximately 800 stranded tanker captains thank you for your flexibility and attention to this matter. The tanker captains in particular have had nothing but time.

Donald, a word. Pull over. Not literally — we know you do not drive, have not driven since 1987, and would not recognize a steering wheel if Eric handed it to you as a drone component. Pull over metaphorically. Put the war in park. Roll down the window. Listen to the man with the map.

It is time to TACO.

Not the food, although given the trajectory of this war, a Taco Bell drive-through represents a more dignified exit than most of the options currently on the table. We mean Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate — your signature diplomatic framework, the one you have deployed against Canada, the European Union, the bond market, and now the Islamic Republic of Iran. You have completed the first three steps. You threatened to obliterate Iran’s power plants. You accommodated through Pakistani intermediaries carrying a 15-point plan that Iran is using as a coaster. You capitulated at 7:17 AM Eastern on March 23 when Brent crude hit $114 and the 10-year yield touched 4.40 percent and you posted on Truth Social that everything was going beautifully.

All that remains is Obfuscate. And Donald — you were born for this part. You have been training for this moment your entire life. The art of the deal is knowing when the deal is done and declaring victory before anyone checks the receipts.

A Note on the Off-Ramp

There is an off-ramp available. Several, in fact. Pakistan is holding one open with both hands and a flare gun. Oman built one years ago and keeps it freshly paved for exactly this purpose. The bond market has been pointing at one every time the 10-year yield approaches 4.40 percent, which is apparently your personal “abandon all principles” threshold — a number now so well documented that Iran has it written on a whiteboard in whatever basement Ghalibaf is operating from.

Take any of them. Take all of them simultaneously. Drive through Pakistan, wave at Oman, and let the 10-year yield retreat to 4.35 percent as you accelerate toward the exit. We will not inspect your passport. We will not ask where you have been. We will simply be grateful that you found the ramp before you reached the crevasse.

Because Donald — and we say this with the kind of affection one reserves for a man holding a lit match near a gas pipeline — things have gone south.

We mean this in both senses. In the colloquial sense: the war has not gone as planned, the strait is still closed, the gas is still rising, the Republicans are still emerging from classified briefings looking like men who have just been told the building they are standing in has no fire exits. And in the geographical sense: you have traveled so far in the wrong direction that you are no longer merely south — you are Antarctic south. You are so far south that the next thing you will see, if you keep going, is a crevasse.

Not a pothole. Not a ditch. Not the kind of significant geological feature that slows a golf cart. The Antarctic crevasse — the one that appears perfectly flat and white and safe until the snow bridge collapses and suddenly you are falling through a void of indeterminate depth with 800 tankers, a $200 billion supplemental appropriation request, the South Korean Kospi, and whatever is left of Pete Hegseth’s credibility.

It is, by all available assessments, not a short fall.

A Note on Kharg Island

Do not invade Kharg Island.

We understand the appeal. It is on a map. It has a name. Seizing it would make an excellent two-word Truth Social post: “KHARG ISLAND!!!” with the requisite number of exclamation points and a follow-up noting that it is the most beautiful island anyone has ever taken, perhaps the most beautiful military seizure in human history, the likes of which has not been seen since — well, since you annexed Greenland, which you have not technically done yet but which you continue to mention at state dinners as though it is a formality.

But here is the operational reality, delivered not by the mainstream media you distrust or the allies you have alienated or the congressional Republicans now emerging blinking from classified briefings demanding to know what is happening — but by a former Israeli prime minister. Ehud Barak, who knows a thing or two about military operations in this region, told Israeli television that reopening the Strait of Hormuz requires two American divisions prepared to be there “for the long haul.”

Two divisions. Twenty thousand troops. The long haul.

Donald, “the long haul” in Middle Eastern military terms is measured in years. You know this because you spent your entire 2016 campaign explaining it to crowds who agreed with you enthusiastically. The Iraq War started in 2003. American forces remained until 2011. This is, as the mathematically inclined among your supporters may note, eight years. Eight years is not a long haul. Eight years is a long mortgage. And you of all people understand what happens to things bought on leverage that cannot be exited on schedule.

Leave Kharg Island on the map. It will still be there after the war. It has been there for centuries. It is not going anywhere. Unlike your approval rating among voters paying $3.98 for gas and $5.38 for diesel, which is going somewhere — specifically, down.

A Note on Your Billionaire Friends

The market is speaking to you, Donald. You know this because you watch the ticker in real time on the screens at Mar-a-Lago, and also because your phone has not stopped ringing.

Those calls. You know the ones. The hedge fund managers whose leveraged oil positions are getting whipsawed by a war that oscillates between escalation and fictional diplomacy on a forty-eight-hour cycle. The private equity partners whose portfolio companies cannot get cargo through the Gulf. The tech titans whose supply chains run through Asia — the same Asia that imports 80 percent of everything that moves through the Strait of Hormuz, which is currently moving nothing. The real estate developers whose projects in Dubai and Riyadh and Doha are stalling because the entire Gulf is under drone attack every second night.

These are not abstract market forces. These are men with your private cell number. Men who attended your dinners and donated to your inaugural committee and bought into your cryptocurrency ventures and showed up at the White House — before you demolished the East Wing — with the kind of enthusiasm that suggests they expected a return on their investment that was not denominated in cruise missile strikes on their logistics chains.

They did not write those checks to watch their net worth evaporate.

They are calling, Donald. Pick up. Listen to the part where they say “off-ramp.” They know where it is. They have GPS.

A Note on Priorities

We pause this travel advisory to acknowledge that while the war in Iran enters its thirty-first day, while 800 tanker captains contemplate the existential nature of time, while the bond market hyperventilates and the 82nd Airborne packs its parachutes, the Trump administration has made a decision of surpassing historical importance.

Your signature is going on the money.

Not metaphorically. Not in the sense that your policies shape the economy or your tariffs redistribute wealth or your cryptocurrency ventures have extracted billions from retail investors who believed in the brand. Literally. Physically. The United States Treasury Department announced Thursday that US dollar bills will bear the signature of President Donald J. Trump later this year — replacing the name of the US Treasurer, whose signature has appeared on American currency for more than a century, with yours.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent explained the reasoning with the kind of clarity that has become his trademark: “There is no more powerful way to recognize the historic achievements of our great country and President Donald J. Trump than U.S. dollar bills bearing his name.”

No more powerful way. Not ending the war. Not reopening the Strait of Hormuz. Not bringing gas below $3.98. Not explaining to congressional Republicans what the military objectives are, or what the $200 billion is for, or why Iran is simultaneously being bombed and given $14 billion in sanctions relief. Not any of those things.

The signature on the money.

Donald, we want you to sit with this for a moment — not because we object, but because we want to make sure you have fully appreciated what you have achieved. Every dollar bill in America will say your name. Every transaction at every gas station where Americans are paying $3.98 per gallon will be conducted in Trumps. Every tanker captain idling in the Persian Gulf with nothing but time will be sitting on a vessel whose cargo, when it eventually moves, will be purchased in a currency bearing the signature of the man whose war stopped it from moving.

It is, we must admit, on-brand.

Scott Bessent — the same Scott Bessent who described giving Iran $14 billion in sanctions relief as “jiu-jitsuing the Iranians” — has now described putting your signature on the dollar as the most powerful possible recognition of historic achievement. This man has a gift. In a different administration, in a different century, he would have been employed writing the inscriptions on pharaonic monuments. “There is no more powerful way to honor the achievements of Ramesses II than to put his name on all the grain.”

The bills are expected later this year. We assume they will be beautiful — perhaps the most beautiful currency ever issued, certainly the most beautiful currency bearing the signature of a man currently conducting major combat operations in the Persian Gulf, which is a narrower category but still.

One small suggestion, offered in the spirit of the travel advisory: the off-ramp, when you take it, will be considerably more dignified if the currency bearing your name is worth something when you land.

Just a thought.

A Note on the TACO Checklist

For ease of reference, here is where you stand:

✓ Threaten — Saturday night ultimatum. Obliterate power plants. Biggest one first. Excellent work. Very threatening. Iran was not visibly threatened but the bond market definitely was.

✓ Accommodate — Sunday intermediaries. Pakistan. Oman. Fifteen points. Delivered via Field Marshal whose name you pronounce differently every time you say it but whom you have called your “favorite field marshal” often enough that he has business cards printed with the quote.

✓ Capitulate — Monday 7:17 AM Eastern. Truth Social. “VERY GOOD AND PRODUCTIVE CONVERSATIONS.” Brent fell from $114 to $96 in minutes. The S&P bounced. You watched the ticker. You acknowledged you watched the ticker. “The price of oil will drop like a rock as soon as a deal is done. I guess it already is today.” Magnificent. Textbook.

☐ Obfuscate — This is the part where you declare victory, describe the outcome as unprecedented in human history, take credit for ending a war that Iran says is still happening, and move the news cycle to something involving either a country you want to buy or a person you want to prosecute. You have the tools. You have the platform. You have the instincts. You simply need to deploy them in the direction of the exit rather than the direction of Kharg Island.

A Note on the Crevasse

The crevasse, for those who have not visited Antarctica and do not plan to, is deceptive. It does not announce itself. It does not have warning signs, or flashing lights, or a congressional briefing explaining its depth and the likely cost of falling into it. It looks, from the surface, exactly like the rest of the ice — flat, white, navigable.

And then the snow bridge goes.

The crevasse in this metaphor is the point at which the war becomes self-sustaining — not because anyone wants it to continue, but because the cost of stopping it exceeds the cost of continuing it, and the cost of continuing it keeps growing, and the bond market hits 4.40 percent again, and the billionaires are calling again, and the gas is $4.50 nationally, and the 82nd Airborne is somewhere in the Persian Gulf that is not Fort Bragg, and Israel is still bombing because Netanyahu’s 48-hour push became a 72-hour push became a standing order, and Iran’s counter-terms still include reparations and Hormuz sovereignty and Hezbollah protection, and Ghalibaf is still by the phone but has stopped answering on the first ring.

That is the crevasse.

You are, as of Day Thirty-One, close enough to see the edge.

The off-ramp is behind you. Pakistan is still holding it open. The Field Marshal is available. The obfuscation writes itself — you have never needed help with that part.

Take the exit, Donald.

Head home.

The Antarctic ice does not negotiate.

And the dollar bills with your name on them will be worth considerably more if you do.
