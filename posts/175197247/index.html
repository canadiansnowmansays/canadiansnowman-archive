The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 4 - The Architect Who Builds It

Russ Vought and the Systematic Construction of American Authoritarianism

October 3, 2025

Office of management and budget director Russ Vought in Washington DC on 29 September 2025. Photograph: Anadolu/Getty Images

Part of the series: The Uncomfortable Truth, Fascism Exists in America Today

In Parts 1-3, we established what fascism actually is—drawing from historians like Umberto Eco and Robert Paxton, its hallmarks include ultranationalism, violent suppression of opposition, scapegoating of “others,” fusion of state and corporate power, control of information, rewriting of education and history, cult of personality, rejection of democratic norms, glorification of violence and militarism, and nostalgia for a mythical golden age. We measured Trump and MAGA against these ten defining characteristics (finding alignment on nine), and showed that the left doesn’t fit the pattern (matching only one characteristic partially, and in the opposite direction). We documented the warning signs, compared the rhetoric, and traced the parallels to Mussolini’s Italy and Hitler’s Germany.

But understanding the threat is only half the battle. The other half is understanding how authoritarian transformation actually happens—not through speeches and rallies alone, but through the systematic dismantling of democratic infrastructure and the patient construction of authoritarian machinery.

If Stephen Miller is the whisperer who shapes Trump’s cruelest instincts, then Russ Vought is the architect who builds the entire fascist infrastructure. And right now, as this shutdown unfolds—now in its third day—he’s watching him do it in real time.

The Man Behind Project 2025

Russ Vought isn’t a household name like Trump or Miller, but he should be. As Director of the Office of Management and Budget (OMB)—what he calls “the nerve center” of government—Vought controls the machinery that turns authoritarian vision into bureaucratic reality. He’s not interested in the spotlight; he’s interested in power. And he’s using it with precision and purpose.

I’ve written about Vought before, in my series “Blind Leading the Blind.” The irony is profound: even if Trump is blind to what’s happening around him, and even if the intent was to blindside the American people, the reality is that Russ Vought is not blind at all. He’s far from it. He sees everything clearly, understands exactly what he’s building, and knows precisely how to get there. He just hopes everyone else remains blind until it’s too late.

Kamala Harris tried to warn us. Throughout her 2024 campaign, she called out Project 2025 repeatedly, explaining in detail what it would mean for American democracy. She warned about the systematic dismantling of institutions, the purging of civil servants, the concentration of executive power. The warnings were specific, documented, urgent.

Why didn’t we listen?

Maybe because the scale was too large to believe. Maybe because it sounded like partisan exaggeration. Maybe because we’ve become numb to warnings. Or maybe because calling something “fascist” has become so common that we’ve lost the ability to recognize it when it’s actually happening.

But Kamala wasn’t the first to warn us. Not by decades.

The 40-Year Trajectory: From Zappa’s Warning to Vought’s Implementation

In March 1986—nearly 40 years ago—musician Frank Zappa appeared on CNN’s “Crossfire” to debate censorship with conservative commentators Robert Novak and John Lofton. While Reagan-era America obsessed over the Soviet threat, Zappa identified a different danger:

“The biggest threat to America today is not communism. It’s moving America toward a fascist theocracy, and everything that’s happened during the Reagan administration is steering us right down that pipe.”

When challenged to define what he meant by “fascist theocracy,” Zappa explained:

“When you have a government that prefers a certain moral code derived from a certain religion, and that moral code turns into legislation to suit one certain religious point of view, and if that code happens to be very very right wing, almost toward Attila the Hun...”

Conservative columnist John Lofton interrupted: “Well then you are an anarchist. Every form of civil government is based on some kind of morality, Frank.”

Zappa’s response cuts to the heart of what we’re facing today: “Morality in terms of behavior, not in terms of theology!”

The year before that Crossfire appearance, Zappa had testified before the Senate Commerce Committee, opposing efforts by Tipper Gore’s Parents’ Music Resource Center to impose religious moral codes on artistic expression. He understood what many missed: censorship based on religious values wasn’t just about music—it was about establishing precedent for government enforcement of theological doctrine.

Zappa wasn’t talking about Trump in 1986. Trump was just a real estate developer. He wasn’t even warning about a specific person. He was identifying a trajectory—one that would run from Reagan’s religious right alliance, through decades of increasing Christian nationalist political influence, through the Tea Party’s fusion of evangelical Christianity and radical conservatism, and finally to its operational endpoint in 2025.

That endpoint has a name: Russ Vought.

What Zappa warned about for 40 years, Vought is implementing right now.

And he’s not improvising. He has a blueprint.

Vought was the key architect of Project 2025, the 900-page Heritage Foundation blueprint that Trump repeatedly claimed to know nothing about during his campaign. Remember Trump’s denials? “I know nothing about Project 2025. I have not seen it, have no idea who is in charge of it.” Yet this week, Trump posted on Truth Social that he’s meeting with Vought—”he of PROJECT 2025 fame”—to determine which “Democrat Agencies” to cut, perhaps permanently.

The mask is off. Project 2025 isn’t some theoretical document from a think tank. It’s the operational manual being implemented right now, and Vought is its primary implementer. According to tracking by multiple organizations, 101 of Project 2025’s 313 policy objectives have already been executed. Paul Dans, the former director of Project 2025, said the administration’s policies are “beyond my wildest dreams.”

Trump didn’t stop at words—he amplified Vought’s reaper role with dark humor. That same evening, he shared an AI-generated video on Truth Social portraying Vought as the Grim Reaper: hooded and bespectacled, scythe gleaming, looming over the Capitol like a budgetary angel of death. Set to a parody of Blue Öyster Cult’s “Don’t Fear the Reaper,” the clip mocks Democratic priorities—slashing agencies to the tune of “Come on, baby, don’t fear the cutter.” It’s not just a meme; it’s Trump reveling in the destruction, handing Vought the keys to the kingdom as Senate Majority Leader John Thune warned: “This is the risk of shutting down the government.”

Kamala was right. We should have listened.

The OMB as Weapon

To understand Vought’s power, you need to understand the Office of Management and Budget. Traditionally, OMB is the government’s accounting office—the place where budget wonks coordinate federal spending according to congressional appropriations. It’s supposed to be boring, technical, apolitical.

Vought has transformed it into Trump’s primary instrument to dismantle the federal government. As CNN reported, he has “transformed a role typically focused on the weeds of congressional appropriations into Trump’s primary instrument to dismantle, piece-by-piece, federal agencies and spending plans.”

Since his confirmation in February 2025, Vought has signed more than two dozen memos that track the full scale of authority he’s leveraging. These aren’t benign administrative updates—they’re directives to eliminate agency independence, withhold congressionally appropriated funds (which is illegal), and prepare for mass layoffs of federal workers.

During a speech to conservative activists, Vought laid out his philosophy: “If Congress has given us authority that is too broad, then we’re going to use that authority aggressively. Be aggressive—and not let this town define the art of politically possible.”

This is the language of someone who views legal constraints as obstacles to overcome, not principles to respect. It’s characteristic #8 from our fascism checklist: rejection of democratic norms and institutions.

The “Post-Constitutional” Vision

Vought has spoken openly about his vision for a “post-constitutional” era. Let that sink in. The man controlling the federal budget believes we should move beyond constitutional constraints on executive power.

In an interview with Tucker Carlson after Trump’s reelection, Vought explained his strategy: “We have to solve the woke and weaponized bureaucracy and have the president take control of the executive branch. The president has to move executively as fast and as aggressively as possible with a radical constitutional perspective to be able to dismantle that bureaucracy in their power centers.”

What does a “radical constitutional perspective” mean? It means Vought believes:

  • The president can slash spending programs without Congress

  • The president can use the military during civil unrest

  • The president should assert direct control over the Justice Department

  • There should be no independent agencies—not DOJ, not GAO, not any of them

  • Federal workers should be stripped of job protections and replaced with political appointees

  • The entire concept of agency independence should be “thrown out”

Vought’s shutdown playbook, honed during the Biden years, treats this crisis as a “very tough and very determined” chance for lasting change—permanent layoffs via 60-day notices, fund impoundments that skirt Congress, all under the grey-area cover of fiscal emergency. Yet economists warn these “reductions” are mere theater, offset by deficit-ballooning tax cuts that mock Republican fiscal piety.

This is fascist consolidation of power. It’s Mussolini’s fusion of state authority under a single strongman leader (characteristic #4), rejection of democratic constraints (characteristic #8), and systematic dismantling of institutional independence.

The Christian Nationalist Agenda

Vought isn’t just an authoritarian technocrat—he’s a self-described “Christian Nation-ist” who believes Christianity should be embedded in government and public life. This is characteristic #6: rewriting education and history to serve ideological control.

Project 2025, which Vought co-authored, calls for:

  • Draconian restrictions on abortion and reproductive freedom

  • Withholding government contracts from companies with “woke policies”

  • Eliminating entire federal agencies that don’t align with conservative Christian values

  • Mandatory Christian instruction as part of a broader nationalist education agenda

After Trump’s first term, Vought founded two organizations—the Center for Renewing America and another pro-Trump group—both focused on discrediting structural racism and curtailing diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs. His Project 2025 chapter detailed exactly how the budget agency could be weaponized to eliminate DEI, purge federal workers who aren’t loyal to Trump, and impose a Christian nationalist vision on government.

Senator Chuck Schumer called Vought “the most radical nominee” with “the most extreme agenda,” warning that Americans needed to understand the danger he poses to their daily lives. Senate Democrats held an all-night, 30-hour debate to protest his confirmation. It didn’t matter—all 53 Republicans voted to confirm him anyway.

The Pattern of Chaos and Incompetence

But Vought’s dangerous vision is matched by dangerous incompetence. In February 2025, his mishandling of budget reconciliation processes led to legislative failures and increased national debt projections. Congressional Budget Office assessments show declining budget management effectiveness and increased fiscal risks under Vought’s leadership.

His ideological approach to budgeting has strained relationships with both Republican and Democratic congressional leadership. Even allies like Sen. Kevin Cramer fret that Vought’s scythe-swinging could backfire, squandering GOP gains as public backlash mounts—echoing how Musk’s earlier DOGE cuts tanked Trump’s ratings. This isn’t the work of a skilled technocrat—it’s the work of an ideologue who prioritizes political goals over fiscal responsibility, constitutional constraints over institutional competence.

The chaos isn’t a bug—it’s a feature. Vought’s “radical constitutional perspective” means treating budgetary laws as suggestions, congressional appropriations as optional, and institutional norms as obstacles. The result is exactly what we’re seeing: fiscal chaos, legislative dysfunction, and the systematic breakdown of how government is supposed to work.

This combination—authoritarian ambition plus bureaucratic chaos—is particularly dangerous. It means Vought is dismantling democratic systems faster than he can build authoritarian replacements, leaving citizens caught in the wreckage.

This week, we’re seeing Vought’s strategy in its purest form. The government shutdown isn’t an accident or a negotiating tactic—it’s a pretext for implementing the permanent changes Vought has been planning.

Before the shutdown even began, Vought sent a memo to federal agencies requiring them to submit detailed plans for mass layoffs. Not temporary furloughs—permanent layoffs. Those plans would only be shelved if Democrats agreed to Republican demands they’d already rejected.

Once the shutdown started, Vought immediately began executing. As The Atlantic reported, the administration moved within 24 hours to weaponize the crisis:

These holds, announced for sham “reviews of illegal racial hiring practices” that can’t even legally proceed during the shutdown, target Democratic bastions with surgical precision—home to Schumer and Jeffries—while $8 billion in clean energy projects evaporate in 16 Harris-won states.

Mass layoffs threatened: Within days, Vought has promised that reductions-in-force will begin immediately, though agencies would have to follow certain procedures.

This is characteristic #3 from our fascism checklist: scapegoating and targeting of “others”—but instead of ethnic minorities, it’s political opponents and the cities where they live. It’s also characteristic #2: violent suppression through bureaucratic means rather than paramilitary forces. The weapon isn’t the Blackshirts—it’s the federal budget.

Senator Tim Kaine captured it perfectly: “For me the most important thing about this budget negotiation is getting the president to agree that a deal is a deal, because he’s just acting like a king unilaterally—go after enemies, go after cities I don’t like.”

This is systematic, targeted punishment of political enemies using the machinery of government. It’s not governance—it’s warfare.

The Pattern of Partisan Enforcement

Let’s be crystal clear about what’s happening: Vought is using federal funding as a weapon to punish Democratic cities and states while protecting Republican ones.

Protests and unrest have occurred across the country—in Texas cities like Austin, Houston, Dallas, and Fort Worth; in Florida; in Indiana; in Republican-controlled states nationwide. Where are the federal interventions there? Where are the funding freezes? Where are the threats of mass layoffs?

They’re absent. Because this isn’t about law and order, public safety, or fiscal responsibility. It’s about using the power of the federal government to reward allies and punish enemies. It’s about selective enforcement based on political loyalty.

This selective targeting based on political affiliation is a hallmark of authoritarian regimes. When Mussolini controlled Italy’s budget and state apparatus, he directed resources to regions that supported him and starved those that didn’t. When Hitler consolidated power, he rewarded loyal areas and punished resistance. This is how authoritarianism works—not through dramatic announcements, but through the patient application of bureaucratic power to crush opposition.

Representative Mark Veasey of Texas said it plainly about the Los Angeles National Guard deployment: “What was happening within this very small radius of one square mile did not require the intervention of the National Guard, according to the governor and the mayor.”

The intervention happened anyway. Because the point wasn’t addressing a crisis—the point was demonstrating federal dominance over Democratic local control.

The Ukraine Precedent

This isn’t Vought’s first time weaponizing OMB. During Trump’s first term, Vought orchestrated the illegal impoundment of aid to Ukraine in the quid-pro-quo scheme that led to Trump’s first impeachment.

That was the test run. Vought learned that he could use budget authority to circumvent Congress, serve Trump’s personal political interests, and face no real consequences. Now he’s applying those lessons at a vastly larger scale.

Congressman Gerry Connolly, then Ranking Member of the Oversight Committee, warned at the time of Vought’s confirmation: “This is a man who has referred to a ‘post-constitutional’ era, and has bragged about wanting to traumatize our dedicated public servants and help President Trump abuse the powers of the nation’s highest office.”

Traumatize. That’s the word Vought himself used. Not “reform” or “streamline” or “improve efficiency.” Traumatize. The cruelty is the point. The fear is the tool. This is characteristic #2 again: using state power to terrorize, not just to govern.

The Systematic Purge

Vought’s vision for the federal workforce is straight out of the authoritarian playbook: replace career civil servants with political loyalists.

Project 2025 explicitly calls for stripping federal workers of their civil service protections and replacing tens of thousands of them with Trump appointees. Vought has already begun this process, using every tool at his disposal:

  • Mandatory return-to-office policies designed to force resignations

  • Eliminating entire offices and programs

  • Threatening mass layoffs during the shutdown

  • Creating hostile work environments for anyone not personally loyal to Trump

  • Using OMB authority to review “all major active FBI investigations” and terminate any deemed contrary to Trump’s interests

This is how you build an authoritarian state: you remove anyone with institutional loyalty to the Constitution and replace them with people whose loyalty is to the leader. You eliminate the career experts who might say “that’s illegal” or “we can’t do that” and install yes-men who will do whatever they’re told.

Everett Kelley, president of the American Federation of Government Employees, representing over 800,000 federal workers, called it what it is: “It’s an illegal abuse of power designed to punish patriotic civil servants and put pressure on Congress.”

Labor unions filed suit against Vought on the eve of the shutdown, arguing his threats to dismantle federal services through mass firings are unlawful. The suit is still pending, but Vought is proceeding anyway—because in his post-constitutional vision, courts are just another obstacle to overcome.

The Scope of Ambition

The scale of Vought’s plans is staggering. Project 2025 proposes:

  • Eliminating the Department of Education entirely

  • Slashing NOAA (the weather service)

  • Gutting the social safety net

  • Ending student debt relief programs

  • Massive expansion of oil and gas drilling

  • Using military force for domestic deportations

  • Restricting abortion access nationwide

  • Purging anyone who worked on DEI initiatives

  • Eliminating the Government Accountability Office (Congress’s watchdog)

And all of this is being implemented not through legislation—which would require congressional approval and democratic debate—but through executive action and budget manipulation.

Who is the whisperer now?

Vought told conservative activists: “We would be suckers to sign onto a budget in which none of the funding ever gets spent in Democratic states and all the funding gets spent in Republican states.”

Wait—read that again. He’s openly admitting that the goal is to direct federal funding based on political loyalty, not need or law or equity. That’s not governance. That’s kleptocracy wrapped in authoritarianism.

How This Connects to Historical Fascism

Let’s measure Vought’s actions against the ten characteristics we established in Part 1:

1. Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology - Vought’s Christian nationalism and Project 2025’s emphasis on “traditional values” aligns with this, though expressed through religious rather than explicitly racial terms.

2. Violent suppression of opposition - Using budget cuts, mass layoffs, and federal force to terrorize and punish political opponents and their constituencies. Not paramilitary violence, but bureaucratic violence that destroys lives and communities.

3. Scapegoating of minorities and “others” - Democratic cities, federal workers, immigrants, LGBTQ people, anyone who worked on DEI programs—all presented as threats to be eliminated.

4. Fusion of state and corporate power - Project 2025’s provisions to withhold contracts from companies with “woke policies” while rewarding loyal corporations, using government power to enforce corporate compliance with conservative ideology.

5. Control of information and attack on press - While Vought focuses on bureaucratic control, his elimination of transparency measures and watchdog agencies serves the same end.

6. Rewriting of education and history - Project 2025 explicitly calls for eliminating DEI, restructuring education around conservative Christian values, and purging “woke” ideology from government.

7. Cult of personality and the strongman - Vought serves Trump with absolute loyalty, implementing policies designed to concentrate all power in the presidency.

8. Rejection of democratic norms and institutions - The “post-constitutional” vision, illegal withholding of funds, ignoring court orders, eliminating independent agencies—this is the most complete alignment.

9. Glorification of violence and militarism - Project 2025’s provisions for using military force domestically, combined with the explicit goal to “traumatize” federal workers.

10. Nostalgia for a mythical golden age - The Christian nationalist vision of returning to a time when Christianity dominated public life and traditional hierarchies were enforced.

Vought aligns with every single characteristic. While Miller whispers and Trump performs, Vought builds. He’s the engineer of American authoritarianism.

The Constitutional Crisis Deepens

Here’s where we are right now, this week, as you read this:

A federal official who openly advocates for a “post-constitutional” era controls the machinery that funds the entire federal government. He’s using a shutdown—which he helped engineer—as pretext to permanently dismantle agencies, fire career civil servants, and redirect federal resources based on political loyalty.

He’s ignoring legal requirements to spend appropriated funds. He’s defying the principle that Congress controls the purse. He’s targeting Democratic cities with surgical precision while protecting Republican areas. He’s threatening to traumatize federal workers into submission.

And when courts intervene? Earlier this week, a federal judge issued a temporary restraining order extending a pause on OMB’s directive to freeze federal assistance. Vought is looking for ways around it.

This is the pattern we warned about at the end of Part 3: When the executive branch simply refuses to comply with legal constraints, traditional enforcement mechanisms collapse. Courts can issue orders, but if the president’s operatives ignore them, who enforces against the enforcers?

Why Vought Is More Dangerous Than Miller

Stephen Miller is terrifying because he whispers cruelty and white nationalism into Trump’s ear, shaping the administration’s most inhumane policies. But Miller’s power is largely confined to immigration and domestic enforcement.

Vought’s power is total. He controls the budget—which means he controls everything. Every agency, every program, every federal worker, every grant, every contract. If you can control the money, you can control the government.

Miller makes policy. Vought makes infrastructure. Miller creates individual horrors like family separation. Vought creates the systematic machinery that can implement thousands of horrors simultaneously across every agency.

Miller is the id—pure instinct and rage. Vought is the superego—methodical, patient, strategic. Miller wants to hurt people. Vought wants to rebuild the entire structure of government to ensure that the “right” people have power forever.

This is why his confirmation was such a pivotal moment. This is why Senate Democrats held an all-night debate trying to stop it. They understood what was at stake. Republicans confirmed him anyway, 53-47, straight party-line vote.

The Methodical March to Autocracy

What makes Vought so dangerous is his patience. He’s not interested in dramatic gestures or inflammatory rhetoric. He’s interested in accumulating power methodically, using the bureaucracy’s own tools to dismantle it.

His memos don’t announce a coup—they reinterpret existing authorities to expand executive power. His strategies don’t require new laws—they exploit loopholes and broad grants of discretion. His purges don’t involve mass arrests—they use resignations, firings, and hostile work environments.

This is how democracies die in the modern era. Not through tanks in the streets, but through the patient bureaucratic accumulation of power by people who know how the system works and how to game it.

Vought spent years planning this. He studied the bureaucracy during Trump’s first term. He wrote Project 2025 during the Biden administration. He set up organizations to promote his vision. He built networks of loyal operatives. He identified every lever of power and every legal ambiguity.

Now he’s executing the plan, step by methodical step.

What This Means for Democracy

We are watching the systematic construction of authoritarian infrastructure in real time. Not theory. Not warnings. Not speculation. Actual implementation.

Every characteristic of fascism we identified in Part 1, every warning sign we documented in Part 2, every distinction we drew in Part 3—Vought is making them concrete. He’s the architect who takes the strongman’s vision and builds it into permanent institutional reality.

When Trump leaves office—whether in four years or whenever—the machinery Vought is building will remain. The precedents will be set. The institutions will be weakened. The norms will be broken. The barriers will be removed.

That’s the genius of Vought’s approach: he’s not just serving Trump’s immediate interests. He’s fundamentally transforming how the American government works, making it easier for the next Trump to consolidate power even faster.

This is how you make authoritarianism permanent. You don’t just install a dictator—you rebuild the institutions to serve authoritarian ends. You replace merit-based civil service with loyalty-based political appointments. You eliminate agency independence. You concentrate power in the executive. You use budget authority to reward allies and punish enemies. You make democratic norms legally unenforceable.

Then, when the strongman eventually leaves, the machine remains. Ready for the next one.

What We Do About It

The same principles from Part 3 apply, but with even more urgency:

Don’t wait for the catastrophe. By the time the machinery is complete, it’s too late to dismantle it. The warning signs aren’t subtle—they’re blazing in front of us. Vought is literally announcing his plans and implementing them in real time.

Protect what remains. Every independent agency, every civil service protection, every congressional power of the purse, every court order—these are the barriers. Once Vought removes them, they don’t come back easily.

Demand accountability. Call your representatives. Demand they challenge OMB overreach. Support lawsuits against illegal budget withholding. Refuse to normalize this. Every capitulation makes the next one easier.

Document everything. When this shutdown ends, Vought’s actions need to be investigated thoroughly. Congress has oversight power—it needs to use it. The illegal withholding of funds, the targeted punishment of Democratic cities, the threats to traumatize federal workers—all of it needs to be documented and prosecuted where applicable.

Support federal workers. These are the people standing between us and complete executive control. They’re being threatened, traumatized, and purged for refusing to abandon their oaths. They need public support and legal protection.

Build coalitions. The threat isn’t theoretical anymore—it’s concrete and immediate. Anyone who cares about democracy needs to work together, regardless of other policy disagreements.

The Final Verdict

In Part 3, we concluded that Trump and MAGA exhibit the defining characteristics of fascism while the left does not. That was based on rhetoric, behavior, and political strategy.

Vought gives us something more: proof of systematic implementation. He’s not just talking about authoritarian transformation—he’s building it, piece by piece, using the government’s own machinery against itself.

This isn’t a drill. This isn’t exaggeration. This isn’t partisan hysteria. This is fascist infrastructure being constructed by someone who has studied how to do it, written a 920-page manual for it, and is now implementing it with the full backing of the president and the Republican Party.

When historians look back at this moment, they won’t ask whether we had warning. We have Vought on record talking about a “post-constitutional” era. We have his Project 2025 blueprint. We have his actions targeting Democratic cities this very week. We have his threats to traumatize federal workers. We have his illegal withholding of congressionally appropriated funds.

They’ll ask whether we cared enough to stop it.

The machinery is being built right now. The question is whether American democracy is strong enough, and Americans are vigilant enough, to dismantle it before it’s too late.

Mussolini’s fascism took years to build. Hitler’s took only months once he had the opportunity. Vought has already been working for months, and he’s accelerating.

The warning signs are all there. The historical parallels are undeniable. The systematic implementation is documented. The only question is whether we’ve learned anything from history, or whether we’ll watch it repeat while telling ourselves it can’t happen here.

It’s happening here. Russ Vought is making sure of it.
