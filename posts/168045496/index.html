The Great Canadian Trade War

When the Numbers Don't Add Up

A deep dive into Trump's tariff justifications and what the data actually shows

July 11, 2025

The trade war between the United States and Canada has escalated dramatically. After Trump imposed 25% tariffs in March 2025 citing fentanyl, immigration, and dairy concerns, Canada retaliated with its own 25% tariffs on US goods. Trump then announced 35% tariffs starting August 1st, explicitly citing Canada's retaliation as justification, writing: "Instead of working with the United States, Canada retaliated with its own Tariffs."

Trump's letter to Canadian PM Mark Carney announcing 35% tariffs, citing fentanyl, retaliation, and dairy concerns as justification

This $762 billion trade relationship is now caught in a tit-for-tat escalation. But when you examine the data behind Trump's original justifications, a striking pattern emerges. The problems Trump initially cited are real, but Canada's role in them is remarkably small compared to the massive economic response.

Here's what the numbers actually show.

The Fentanyl Story That Doesn't Add Up

Trump's biggest claim? That Canada is flooding America with deadly fentanyl. He's called it "massive" and said the amount crossing the northern border "could kill 9.5 million Americans." It sounds terrifying—and fentanyl is terrifying. But here's what the actual seizure data shows:

In 2024, Canada accounted for 43 pounds of the 21,889 pounds of fentanyl seized at all US borders.

That's 0.2%. Not 20%. Not 2%. Two-tenths of one percent.

Meanwhile, Mexico accounted for 21,148 pounds—over 96% of all fentanyl intercepted. To put this in perspective: for every pound of fentanyl coming from Canada, 500 pounds came from Mexico. Yet Trump escalated Canada's tariffs to 35% while Mexico faces the standard 25% rate.

This is like declaring war on Canada because they sent you one mosquito while Mexico sent you a swarm of 500 mosquitos. The response doesn't match the problem.

But wait, it gets better.

Since Trump started complaining about Canadian fentanyl, what has Canada done? They've spent $1.3 billion on border security, bought Black Hawk helicopters, hired 10,000 more border agents, and created a "Fentanyl Czar" position. The result? Fentanyl seizures at the Canadian border dropped 97% from December 2024 to January 2025.

So Canada essentially solved a problem that barely existed in the first place. Yet the trade war continues.

The Immigration Numbers Game

Trump's second big complaint: Canada has "ridiculous Open Borders" that are letting criminals and immigrants flood into America. Again, let's check the math.

In 2024, US Border Patrol made 23,721 apprehensions at the entire 5,525-mile Canadian border.

For context, that's 1.5% of total US border apprehensions. The remaining 98.5% happened at the southern border with Mexico. During Trump's entire first presidency (2017-2020), only about 14,000 people total were apprehended crossing from Canada.

Here's another way to think about it: the Canadian border processes about 400,000 legal crossings every single day. The illegal crossings represent about 0.016% of total border activity. And by December 2024, even these tiny numbers had dropped 90% from their peak.

This is like complaining that your neighbor's garden hose is flooding your basement while ignoring the burst dam in your backyard.

The Dairy Drama: Tariffs Trump Agreed To

Now we get to Trump's favorite talking point: Canadian dairy tariffs of "up to 400%" that "rip off American farmers." This one's my personal favorite because it's technically true and completely misleading at the same time.

Here's the kicker: Trump himself negotiated and agreed to these very tariffs as part of the USMCA trade deal during his first presidency. The current Canadian dairy system, including those high tariff rates, was explicitly preserved in the trade agreement Trump called "the best trade deal ever made."

Yes, Canada does have tariffs of 241% on milk and 298% on butter. But here's the catch: these tariffs only apply to imports that exceed specific quotas—quotas that American exporters have never even come close to filling.

It's like complaining about expensive airplane seats when you've never bought a plane ticket. Canada allows certain amounts of US dairy products to enter duty-free or at very low tariffs. American farmers use only about 27% of this available quota space. The scary high tariffs? They've never actually been paid by American dairy farmers because we never export enough to trigger them.

But there's an even bigger irony here: Canada's dairy tariffs exist largely to counter massive US government subsidies to American dairy farmers. The USDA provides billions in direct payments, crop insurance subsidies, and price supports to US dairy producers. Canada's tariff system prevents subsidized American dairy from flooding their market and destroying their own farmers—which is exactly what tariffs are supposed to do.

It's essentially: "How dare you put tariffs on our subsidized products to protect your farmers from unfair competition!"

Even better: US dairy exports to Canada hit $1.14 billion in 2024—a 67% increase since 2021. Canada is literally the second-biggest buyer of American dairy products in the world. The US has a massive dairy trade surplus with Canada ($877 million in exports vs. $358 million in imports).

So Trump is threatening a trade war with America's second-biggest dairy customer over tariffs that he himself agreed to, that have never been charged to American farmers, for products Americans are selling in record quantities, while complaining that Canada won't let subsidized US dairy undercut the Canadian domestic market.

The Retaliation Cycle and Supply Chain Reality

When Trump imposed the initial 25% tariffs in March 2025, Canada didn't just accept them quietly. Canada immediately retaliated with its own 25% tariffs on $30 billion worth of US goods—everything from bourbon and coffee to machinery and lumber. This triggered Trump's escalation to 35% tariffs, citing Canada's "retaliation" as justification.

But the trade war extends beyond the headline 25%-then-35% tariffs. Trump also imposed separate tariffs on steel and aluminum imports, hitting Canada particularly hard since it's America's largest supplier of both metals. Canada responded with additional retaliatory tariffs on $20.6 billion in US steel and aluminum products.

Here's where it gets complicated: modern manufacturing doesn't respect borders.

Take automobiles—a car "made in America" might have Canadian steel in its frame, Mexican-assembled components, and parts that cross the border multiple times during production. A single vehicle can involve:

  • Canadian raw materials (steel, aluminum, lumber for transport)

  • US manufacturing of engines and major components

  • Mexican assembly of smaller parts

  • Back-and-forth shipping for different production stages

Trump's tariffs don't just hit "foreign" products—they hit the integrated North American supply chain that makes American manufacturing competitive. When Ford has to pay 35% more for Canadian steel, that cost gets passed to American car buyers. When a car part crosses the border three times during production, it potentially gets hit with tariffs three times.

The automotive sector alone represents about $100 billion in annual US-Canada trade, with deeply integrated supply chains that took decades to build. These tariffs are essentially taxing the efficiency that makes North American manufacturing globally competitive.

Canada's retaliation was strategic, targeting products from Republican-leaning states and industries with political influence—bourbon from Kentucky, lumber affecting US construction costs, agricultural products from farm states. This wasn't random; it was designed to create political pressure within the US to end the trade war.

So the "35% tariff on Canada" is actually multiple overlapping tariff regimes hitting different parts of an integrated economy, with retaliation creating additional costs for American businesses and consumers across numerous sectors.

When $762 Billion Goes to War Over 43 Pounds

Here's where this gets truly absurd.

  • 43 pounds of fentanyl (0.2% of the US total)

  • 23,721 border apprehensions (1.5% of the US total)

  • Dairy tariffs that don't actually affect American farmers and that he agreed to himself

The economic response is roughly 10 million times larger than the problems it's supposed to solve.

The impact? American households are paying an estimated $1,200+ more per year due to these tariffs, with costs rising as rates increased to 35%. Canada's economy could shrink by 2.6%—recession territory. This has created the first full-scale trade war between the closest allies and largest trading partners.

Meanwhile, the actual problems Trump cited remain largely unchanged, because they were never really about Canada in the first place.

The Plot Thickens

By June 2025, Trump had moved the goalposts entirely. He terminated trade talks over Canada's 3% digital services tax on tech companies—a completely different issue from fentanyl, immigration, or dairy. Canada rescinded the tax within 48 hours to restart negotiations, showing just how much economic leverage these tariffs created.

This reveals the real game: the specific justifications matter less than the broader goal of renegotiating the entire trade relationship.

The fentanyl, immigration, and dairy complaints appear to be pretexts—real issues that sound serious enough to justify dramatic action, even though Canada's actual involvement in these problems is minimal.

The Personal Factor: When Trade Policy Meets Grudges

There's another element that can't be ignored: Trump's well-documented personal animosity toward former Prime Minister Justin Trudeau, and now potentially toward current PM Mark Carney.

Trump's relationship with Trudeau was notoriously hostile. He repeatedly called Trudeau "dishonest," walked out of G7 meetings, and seemed to take personal offense at Trudeau's diplomatic approach. For someone who views relationships through a dominance lens, Trudeau's ability to maintain Canada's positions while appearing reasonable likely rankled Trump considerably.

Now there's Mark Carney—a former Bank of England Governor with impressive credentials who recently became Canada's Prime Minister. If Trump felt "bested" during their Oval Office meetings, or if Carney's economic expertise highlighted Trump's own knowledge gaps, that could easily translate into policy vindictiveness.

This matters because Trump's decision-making often appears driven by personal relationships rather than national interests. Trade wars become personal wars. Economic policy becomes a way to "show" someone who's boss. The billions of dollars in disrupted trade and the thousands of jobs affected become collateral damage in what's essentially a personal dispute.

The Brazil example perfectly illustrates this pattern. Trump imposed 50% tariffs on Brazil not for economic reasons, but because he disagrees with Brazil's legal proceedings against his personal friend, former President Jair Bolsonaro—calling it a "witch hunt." The two developed a close personal friendship during Trump's first term, with Bolsonaro earning the nickname "Trump of the Tropics" and Trump calling him "a great friend" during their 2020 Mar-a-Lago meeting. American consumers will now pay higher prices for coffee and orange juice because Trump doesn't like how Brazil's courts are treating his buddy.

One has to wonder: what financial interests, if any, do these two men share? The willingness to disrupt billions in trade over personal loyalty raises questions about whether there are business relationships we don't know about.

When you combine this personality-driven approach with the businessman's "more tariff revenue = winning" mentality and advisers feeding him justifications, the disproportionate response starts to make psychological sense. It's not really about fentanyl or dairy tariffs—it's about Trump feeling he needs to demonstrate dominance over leaders who he perceives as having slighted or outmaneuvered him.

This personal element explains why the problems and solutions don't match up logically. Logic isn't the primary driver—ego and perceived slights are.

Breaking: The 35% Escalation Confirmed

As this piece was being finalized, Trump's 35% tariff announcement was confirmed overnight. Canadian PM Carney responded by extending the trade negotiation deadline to August 1st and reaffirming Canada's fentanyl cooperation efforts, stating that "Canada has made vital progress to stop the scourge of fentanyl in North America."

Notably, even Canada's Conservative opposition leader Pierre Poilievre criticized the tariffs as "another unjustified attack on Canada's economy," saying "Canada has long been a reliable partner and trusted friend to the United States. These tariffs will damage both our countries." This rare cross-party unity demonstrates that even political opponents agree the response is disproportionate to the actual problems cited.

Trump also escalated his threats, warning that any Canadian retaliation would be "added onto the 35% that we charge"—turning trade policy into a game of escalating personal dominance rather than economic problem-solving.

What This Really Tells Us

I'm not here to defend or attack anyone's trade policy. But I am fascinated by what happens when you compare political rhetoric to actual data. In this case, we have.

This pattern suggests something simpler than grand strategic thinking. Trump, fundamentally a businessman, appears focused on one metric: how much money tariffs bring into government coffers. The fentanyl, immigration, and dairy justifications may be less about solving those specific problems and more about providing politically acceptable cover for what is essentially a revenue-generation strategy.

When you combine this dollar-focused mindset with advisers like Peter Navarro (who has long advocated aggressive tariff policies) and a demonstrated pattern of constitutional confusion, the disconnect between problem and solution makes more sense. It's not necessarily about strategic trade renegotiation—it's about seeing tariffs as a simple way to collect money, with the specific justifications serving as necessary political packaging.

The numbers don't lie, even when the underlying motivations may be more straightforward than they appear. And in this case, the numbers tell a story about using minimal problems to justify maximum revenue collection.
