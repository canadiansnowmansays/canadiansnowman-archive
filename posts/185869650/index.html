Yes, It’s Fascism

Mainstream Validation, New Evidence, and the Complete Authoritarian Philosophy

January 26, 2026

January 26, 2026
Stephen Gordon

On January 25, 2026, The Atlantic published something remarkable: a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution declaring that Donald Trump is, in fact, a fascist president.

Jonathan Rauch’s article “Yes, It’s Fascism” matters not because it breaks new analytical ground—this series documented the same conclusion four months ago—but because of who is saying it, how reluctantly he arrived there, and what new evidence convinced him.

Rauch writes: “Until recently, I resisted using the F-word to describe President Trump... When the facts change, I change my mind. Recent events have brought Trump’s governing style into sharper focus. Fascist best describes it, and reluctance to use the term has now become perverse.”

This is Part 12: What happened between this series’ conclusion in January and The Atlantic’s admission two weeks later. The new evidence Rauch documents. The philosophical framework he reveals. And why mainstream validation matters—not because truth depends on The Atlantic’s permission, but because it shows the pattern is undeniable even to cautious, credentialed skeptics.

The Reluctant Convert: Why Rauch’s Methodology Matters

Rauch’s resistance to the term “fascist” mirrors the approach this series took from the beginning. He initially accepted Biden’s “semi-fascist” characterization, preferring John Bolton’s psychological diagnosis: Trump sees strongmen governing “unburdened by uncooperative legislatures” and thinks “Why can’t I do that?”

A year ago, Rauch argued Trump’s regime was best understood as patrimonialism—treating the state as personal property. But he now concludes: “What originally looked like an effort to make the government his personal plaything has drifted distinctly toward doctrinal and operational fascism.”

Why does this matter? Because Rauch is a senior fellow at Brookings, not a partisan activist. He applied the same historical standard this series used: “Fascism is not a territory with clearly marked boundaries but a constellation of characteristics. When you view the stars together, the constellation plainly appears.”

When two analysts—one a Canadian blogger in October, one a Brookings fellow in January—independently apply historical standards and reach identical conclusions, that’s not coincidence. It’s evidence.

The New Evidence: January’s Escalation

When this series concluded on January 12, it documented Trump/MAGA alignment with nine out of ten fascism characteristics through October 2025. Rauch’s January 25 article reveals what happened in the following weeks.

Venezuela: Territorial Aggression for Lebensraum

Rauch documents Trump’s “appetite for lebensraum”—the Nazi term for territorial expansion. On January 20, 2026, Trump deployed military force to overthrow Venezuela’s government and seize its oil fields.

This isn’t regime change for humanitarian reasons. Trump explicitly announced the U.S. has “already put dibs on 30 to 50 million barrels of Venezuelan oil.” As Rauch notes, this is “explicitly predatory use of [force] to grab Venezuela’s oil”—1930s-style authoritarian aggression.

The philosophical framework connects directly to Part 11’s analysis of Stephen Miller’s worldview. Miller told CNN: “We live in a world, in the real world, that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power.”

Venezuela is Miller’s Hobbesian philosophy made foreign policy.

Greenland: Gangster-Style Coercion of Allies

Trump’s threat to take Greenland from Denmark “the easy way” or “the hard way” represents mafia-style intimidation of a NATO ally. This connects to my earlier Greenland analysis: Katie Miller posted about taking Greenland “by any means necessary” on January 3, and Stephen Miller articulated the philosophical justification on CNN days later.

ICE: A Paramilitary Larger Than Most Nations’ Militaries

Rauch provides specific numbers on ICE expansion unavailable in October:

“Trump... more than doubled the agency’s size in 2025, and its budget is now larger than those of all other federal law-enforcement agencies combined, and larger than the entire military budgets of all but 15 countries.”

ICE’s budget exceeds the entire military budgets of all but 15 countries. This isn’t immigration enforcement—it’s construction of a national paramilitary force.

ICE agents operate “behind masks, receives skimpy training, lies about its activities, and has been told that it enjoys ‘absolute immunity.’” They conduct “military-style raids” that the government films and publicizes—”performative brutality” designed to intimidate.

The Retribution Machine: 470+ Targets

Rauch reports: “At least 470 people, organizations and institutions have been targeted for retribution since Trump took office—an average of more than one a day.”

This includes Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell—unprecedented targeting of the Fed’s independence—plus “blatantly retaliatory prosecutions of James Comey and Letitia James.”

Part 6 examined how Miller redefined anti-fascism as terrorism. Rauch documents the systematic implementation: critics become targets for federal law enforcement persecution.

The 2026 Election Threat

Rauch documents something unknowable in early January: “Trump’s recent musing that there should be no 2026 election.”

The White House claims this was “jocular,” but Rauch notes Trump and MAGA “believe they never lose an election, period” and warns: “We must not expect that he and his MAGA loyalists will voluntarily turn over the White House to a Democrat in 2029... and the second insurrection will be far better organized than the first.”

This transforms Part 8’s electoral organizing from important to existential.

Schmitt Meets Hobbes: The Complete Authoritarian Philosophy

Rauch introduces a philosophical framework I didn’t examine in Part 11: Carl Schmitt, the early-20th-century German political theorist whose doctrines legitimized Nazism.

Schmitt’s theory combines with Miller’s Hobbesian worldview to create a comprehensive authoritarian philosophy.

Schmitt’s Politics-as-War

Rauch explains: “Schmitt rejected the Madisonian view of politics as a social negotiation in which different factions, interests, and ideology come to agreement, the core idea of our Constitution. Rather, he saw politics as a state of war between enemies, neither of which can understand the other and both of which feel existentially threatened—and only one of which can win.”

Political opponents aren’t fellow citizens with different views—they’re enemies who must be destroyed because coexistence is impossible. As Rauch notes: “The aim of Schmittian politics is not to share the country but to dominate or destroy the other side.”

The clearest expression came from Miller at Charlie Kirk’s memorial service:

“We are the storm. And our enemies cannot comprehend our strength, our determination, our resolve, our passion... You are nothing. You are wickedness.”

Parse this language:

  • “Our enemies” - not opponents, but enemies

  • “Cannot comprehend” - can’t be reasoned with

  • “You are nothing” - dehumanization

  • “You are wickedness” - moral condemnation justifying elimination

The Combined Framework

Part 11 examined Miller’s Hobbesian worldview: the world is governed by strength, force, and power alone. Rauch’s Schmittian analysis completes the picture:

From Hobbes: The world is a war of all against all, resolved only by sovereign power enforcing order through strength.

From Schmitt: Politics itself is war between irreconcilable enemies where only one side can win.

Combined result: Democratic deliberation is impossible because enemies can’t negotiate. Constitutional constraints are obstacles because war demands unchecked power. Opposition isn’t legitimate because enemies deserve destruction, not representation.

This explains everything documented in Parts 1-11:

  • Why Miller demands 3,000 arrests per day (enemies must be removed)

  • Why Trump ignores court orders (legal constraints don’t bind sovereign power in war)

  • Why critics face federal prosecution (enemies forfeit legal protection)

  • Why democratic norms are demolished (war doesn’t follow peacetime rules)

  • Why international law is dismissed (only power matters between enemies)

The philosophy isn’t incoherent. It’s Hobbes plus Schmitt: authoritarian power applied through politics-as-war.

Two analysts, different timeframes, different evidence, same conclusion. The constellation appears.

The Hybrid State: What Happens Next

Rauch’s most important conceptual contribution is his “hybrid state” framework:

“The United States, once the world’s exemplary liberal democracy, is now a hybrid state combining a fascist leader and a liberal Constitution; but no, it has not fallen to fascism. And it will not.”

This captures what Parts 7-8 examined: we have simultaneous realities.

The Fascist Reality: Leader rejecting democratic constraints, might-makes-right philosophy, systematic targeting of opponents, paramilitary force larger than most nations’ militaries, territorial aggression.

The Liberal Constitutional Reality: Courts still blocking illegal actions, Congress still capable of resistance, states defying federal overreach, media still independent, elections still occurring.

The question is which reality wins.

Rauch expresses optimism: “He has not succeeded in molding public opinion, except against himself. He has outrun the mandate of his voters, his coalition is fracturing.”

But note the tension: “They may defy the Constitution, but they cannot rewrite it” appears alongside “We must not expect that he and his MAGA loyalists will voluntarily turn over the White House to a Democrat in 2029... and the second insurrection will be far better organized than the first.”

Which is it? Are constitutional safeguards holding, or being systematically dismantled?

The answer determines whether Parts 7-10’s resistance blueprint—reinforce courts, reclaim Congress, build electoral armor, sustain citizen action—can work before the 2026 midterms become moot.

Why Mainstream Validation Matters (and Doesn’t)

Rauch’s Atlantic piece matters for credibility and permission. When a Brookings senior fellow says “fascist,” it’s harder to dismiss as partisan hyperbole. His reluctant journey from resistance to acceptance gives others permission to acknowledge what they’re seeing.

But the truth doesn’t depend on The Atlantic’s validation. This series established in October what Rauch concluded in January. The four-month lag doesn’t make the analysis more accurate—it just means mainstream institutions are slower to acknowledge uncomfortable truths.

The danger is waiting for permission before acting. By the time The Atlantic says “yes, it’s fascism,” we’re already months into the acceleration documented above.

Part 9’s international examples showed successful resistance requires early action—not waiting until authoritarianism is so obvious that even cautious analysts can’t deny it.

Conclusion: The Pattern Is Undeniable

This series began in September 2025 asking: What if we measured today’s politics against historical reality?

The verdict in October: 9/10 alignment, systematic construction, clear evidence.

The Atlantic’s verdict in January: Complete agreement, with additional evidence of acceleration.

Between Part 11 (January 12) and Rauch’s publication (January 25):

  • Territorial aggression (Venezuela, Greenland)

  • Larger paramilitary (ICE exceeding all federal law enforcement)

  • More explicit philosophy (Miller’s Schmittian “you are wickedness”)

  • Broader retribution (470+ targets including Fed Chair)

  • Electoral threat (2026 cancellation musing)

Two independent analysts, different timeframes, same conclusion.

Rauch concludes: “The resemblances are too many and too strong to deny. Americans who support liberal democracy need to recognize what we’re dealing with in order to cope with it, and to recognize something, one must name it.”

This series named it in October. The Atlantic named it in January. The evidence continues accumulating.

The question is no longer “Is it fascism?”

The question is whether enough Americans will act on what we now clearly see—before the hybrid state’s fascist component overwhelms its liberal constitutional safeguards.

South Korea needed 20 weeks. Brazil needed 4 years. Poland needed 8 years. All succeeded because citizens sustained resistance despite authoritarian escalation.

The machinery is running. The philosophy is explicit. The pattern is undeniable.

What happens next depends on whether Americans choose sustained resistance or wait for the next mainstream validation while the consolidation continues.
