When Citizens Say No

The Grand Jury That Refused to Become a Rubber Stamp

December 5, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays
December 5, 2025

November 24, 2025: Judge Cameron McGowan Currie dismisses the Comey and James prosecutions. The Justice Department immediately promises an “immediate appeal.”

December 1, 2025: No appeal filed. Instead, CNN reports the DOJ “could present new indictments as soon as this week.”

December 4, 2025: A grand jury in Norfolk, Virginia does something extraordinarily rare.

They say no.

In fiscal year 2016, federal prosecutors investigated more than 150,000 people. Grand juries declined to indict in exactly six cases.

On Thursday, Letitia James became the seventh.

The Promise That Never Came

Within hours of the dismissals, both Attorney General Pam Bondi and White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt announced their intentions:

Bondi: “We’ll be taking all available legal action, including an immediate appeal, to hold Letitia James and James Comey accountable for their unlawful conduct.”

Leavitt: “The Justice Department will be appealing the ruling very soon.”

That was November 24. By December 2, no appeal had been filed with the 4th Circuit Court of Appeals.

Under federal procedural rules, the DOJ has 30 days to notify the court of its intention to appeal—meaning they have until around Christmas. But appeals that are actually “immediate” get filed immediately, not weeks later.

The answer appeared to be strategic. They weren’t appealing because they’d chosen a different path: re-indictment.

What Happened on Thursday

December 4, 2025. The Walter E. Hoffman courthouse in Norfolk, Virginia. Shortly after 9 a.m., grand jurors convened on the fourth floor.

Roger Keller—a federal prosecutor brought in from Missouri after career prosecutors determined the evidence was too weak—presented a new indictment against Letitia James. The same charges. The same alleged mortgage fraud from 2020. The same case that Judge Currie had just thrown out for being brought by an illegally appointed prosecutor.

By lunchtime, it was over.

The grand jury refused to indict.

According to the New York Times, the deliberations “seemed to be brief and uncomplicated.” The citizens listened to the evidence and said no.

How Rare Is This?

The saying goes that a prosecutor could “indict a ham sandwich.” The numbers prove it.

According to Justice Department figures, in fiscal year 2016, federal prosecutors investigated more than 150,000 people. Grand juries declined to indict in only six cases.

Six. Out of 150,000.

That’s a 99.996% indictment rate. When a grand jury says no, it means the evidence is so weak that even the low bar of probable cause couldn’t be met.

On Thursday, after a properly appointed prosecutor presented the case, after the DOJ had weeks to shore up evidence, after they knew the defense arguments—the grand jury decided there wasn’t probable cause.

Letitia James just became case number seven.

What the Rejection Means

Grand jury rejection is a substantive finding that the evidence doesn’t support the charges. When Judge Currie dismissed the cases, she didn’t rule on the evidence—only on Halligan’s illegal appointment. The dismissal was “without prejudice” specifically to allow the government to try again with a proper prosecutor.

So they did. Roger Keller presented to a new grand jury. The citizens said: This isn’t a crime.

Think about this timeline:

  • Career prosecutors reviewed and recommended against charges

  • Lindsey Halligan overruled them and got October indictment

  • Judge Currie threw it out for illegal appointment

  • Roger Keller presented to fresh grand jury in December

  • That grand jury refused to indict

The second grand jury got it right. The career prosecutors were correct all along.

As Abbe Lowell stated: “This should be the end of this case. If they continue, undeterred by a court ruling and a grand jury’s rejection of the charges, it will be a shocking assault on the rule of law.”

The Comey Silence

As of December 5, no reported attempt to re-indict Comey. CNN said prosecutors “could present new indictments as soon as this week.” They tried with James on December 4 and failed.

For Comey? Silence.

The statute of limitations problem looms. His alleged false statements occurred September 30, 2020. The five-year limit expired September 30, 2025. Judge Currie ruled Halligan’s appointment invalid, meaning her grand jury presentation was invalid. Patrick Fitzgerald argued: “Because the indictment is void, the statute of limitations has run.”

The DOJ believes 18 U.S.C. § 3288 gives them a six-month extension. But that’s dubious when the indictment was void from the start.

More likely: if a grand jury wouldn’t indict James on straightforward mortgage fraud, they won’t indict Comey on complicated perjury about an ambiguous five-year-old question when the statute has arguably expired.

Why risk another embarrassing rejection?

Why They Chose Re-Indictment Over Appeal

The decision not to appeal made strategic sense:

Judge Currie’s constitutional analysis was clearly correct—no appellate court would overturn it. An appeal would highlight every problem: Trump’s demands, Siebert’s firing, Halligan’s inexperience, the four-day rush, the grand jury corruption from Part 10.

Better to start fresh with a properly appointed prosecutor presenting to a new grand jury.

What could go wrong?

The Pattern: Never Accept Defeat

Step back and look at the pattern that’s emerged over eleven weeks:

When career prosecutors say no, install loyalists who say yes:

  • Siebert refused to indict → Halligan appointed → Comey and James indicted

When civilian courts assert independence, use military courts:

  • Judge Currie dismisses Comey/James cases → Pentagon investigates Kelly

When cases get dismissed, re-indict with new prosecutors:

  • November 24: Cases dismissed → December 2: New indictments coming

The through-line is clear: They never accept defeat. They just find new venues, new prosecutors, new targets, new methods.

This isn’t a legal system. It’s a persecution system with unlimited resources and unlimited determination to punish Trump’s perceived enemies.

The Compounding Punishment

Even if the new indictments ultimately fail, the process itself serves Trump’s purposes.

Consider what James Comey has already endured:

  • Publicly accused of crimes by the President

  • Indicted by a federal grand jury

  • Months of legal proceedings

  • Arraignment in federal court

  • Motion hearings

  • Legal fees estimated at $450,000+ in just the first month

  • His name forever associated with criminal charges

And now? If they re-indict, he gets to do it all again.

New arraignment. New motion practice. New legal bills. New headlines about “Comey indicted.”

The same for Letitia James. She just spent months fighting bank fraud charges over a 20-year-old mortgage. She won. And now she might have to do it again.

The process is the punishment. Even if they ultimately lose these cases—even if judges dismiss them again—Trump’s enemies have been harassed, bankrupted, and smeared.

And every other potential critic watches and learns: cross Trump, and this is what happens. Not once, but over and over until you’re financially ruined or politically destroyed.

What About Bolton?

John Bolton is still facing 18 Espionage Act counts in Maryland. His case wasn’t brought by Halligan, so it wasn’t dismissed with the others.

But Bolton’s attorneys are surely watching the re-indictment strategy and preparing accordingly.

If the DOJ re-indicts Comey and James, Bolton’s lawyers will argue it proves these are political prosecutions. They’ll point to the pattern:

  1. Biden DOJ investigates, finds insufficient evidence, closes cases

  2. Trump reopens them and installs loyalist prosecutors

  3. Cases dismissed for procedural violations

  4. Trump re-indicts anyway

That’s not law enforcement. That’s harassment.

Will Maryland judges be more sympathetic to this argument after watching what happened in Virginia? We’ll find out.

The Broader Threat

The re-indictment strategy isn’t just about Comey and James. It’s a signal to everyone in the federal government about how this administration operates.

Message to federal judges: You can dismiss our cases, but we’ll just bring them again. We have unlimited resources and unlimited patience. You’ll have to defeat us over and over.

Message to defense attorneys: Every victory is temporary. Your clients can never relax. We can always find a new prosecutor to start over.

Message to potential targets: Fighting back is futile. Even if you win, you lose. The legal fees alone will destroy you, and we’ll keep coming until you’re broken.

Message to career prosecutors: Get with the program or get out. The prosecutors who said no are gone. The prosecutor who said yes is being protected despite documented misconduct.

This is how authoritarian regimes operate. Not with a single dramatic crackdown, but with relentless, grinding pressure that never stops until the target is destroyed or surrenders.

The Constitutional Crisis Continues

When I began this series, I argued we were witnessing the construction of an enemies list. Critics said our institutions would hold.

Ten weeks later:

  • Three Trump critics indicted

  • Two cases dismissed for illegal appointments

  • One senator threatened with court-martial

  • Grand jury process documented as corrupt

  • DOJ preparing to re-indict anyway

  • FBI Director publicly promising continued prosecution

Which institution, exactly, is holding?

Not the Justice Department—it’s been weaponized.

Not the FBI—its director is coordinating political prosecutions.

Not the Pentagon—it’s investigating senators for protected speech.

Federal judges are doing their jobs. But even they can only review what’s brought before them. They can dismiss individual cases. They can’t stop the Trump administration from simply bringing new ones.

What’s Coming Next

Based on the CNN reporting and Patel’s statements, here’s what we can expect:

This Week (December 2-6):

  • New prosecutor appointed in Eastern District of Virginia

  • Grand jury presentation on Comey and/or James

  • Possible new indictments announced

Next Week (December 9-13):

  • If indicted, new arraignments scheduled

  • Defense motions filed immediately

  • Arguments about statute of limitations (Comey)

  • Arguments about vindictive prosecution (both)

Over the Holidays:

  • Appeals court briefing if DOJ does appeal the original dismissals

  • Motion practice in any new cases

  • Possible developments in Kelly court-martial investigation

  • Continued Bolton prosecution in Maryland

January 2026:

  • Trial dates set in any new cases

  • Mark Kelly potentially recalled to active duty

  • New year, same enemies list

The Only Thing That Stops Them

At some point, we have to confront an uncomfortable truth: The only thing that stops this is political will.

Judges can dismiss individual cases. But they can’t stop the administration from bringing new ones.

Congress could hold hearings. But the Republican majority won’t.

The Senate could refuse to confirm loyalist prosecutors. But it’s confirming them.

The media can document the abuses. But half the country doesn’t care.

The only real check is electoral: voting out politicians who enable this behavior and voting in ones who will restore DOJ independence.

But that’s two years away for Congress and four years away for the presidency. What happens in the meantime?

How many re-indictments? How many more targets? How many courtiers-martial of senators? How many billions in legal fees spent defending against politically motivated prosecutions?

What About Bolton?

John Bolton still faces 18 Espionage Act counts in Maryland. His attorneys now have powerful ammunition: Judge Currie’s illegal appointment findings, Judge Fitzpatrick’s documentation of “profound investigative missteps,” and a grand jury’s refusal to re-indict.

When presented to neutral grand juries without political pressure, citizens say no.

The Pattern: Institutions Pushing Back

Federal judges have now found problems with interim U.S. Attorney appointments in Virginia (Halligan), New Jersey (Habba), Albany (Sarcone), Los Angeles, and Nevada. On December 4, Albany Judge Schofield expressed skepticism about Sarcone’s appointment in a separate civil rights investigation into James.

The Trump administration tried to bypass Senate confirmation by stacking interim appointments. Judges said no. They tried to resurrect a dismissed prosecution. A grand jury said no.

The system is holding—barely, and only because multiple institutions are pushing back simultaneously.

What Happens Next

Based on the December 4 grand jury rejection, here’s what we can expect:

For Letitia James: The Justice Department has three options:

  1. Accept defeat - The grand jury has spoken. Move on.

  2. Try again - Present to yet another grand jury, risking a third rejection

  3. Pivot to the other investigation - Focus on the separate civil rights investigation in Albany (though Judge Schofield has already expressed skepticism about that prosecutor’s appointment)

Abbe Lowell warned: “If they continue, undeterred by a court ruling and a grand jury’s rejection of the charges, it will be a shocking assault on the rule of law.”

For James Comey: Unless the DOJ finds a creative legal argument around the statute of limitations problem, the Comey prosecution is likely dead. The combination of:

  • Judge Currie finding the original indictment void

  • The five-year statute having expired

  • A grand jury’s rejection of the weaker James case

  • The documented grand jury corruption from Part 10

...makes re-indictment extremely difficult both legally and practically.

For John Bolton: His Maryland prosecution continues, but his defense team now has powerful ammunition: two judges found illegal appointments, documented grand jury corruption, and a grand jury that refused to re-indict. The pattern of vindictive prosecution has never been clearer.

For Mark Kelly: The Pentagon’s court-martial investigation continues. If the DOJ can’t make criminal cases stick in civilian courts, will they have better luck in military tribunals where the President is the ultimate authority? Part 9 of this series documented that threat.

The Third Attempt? Grand Jury Shopping

On December 5, Abbe Lowell appeared on CNN and issued a stark warning: he’d heard the government might try presenting the case to a different grand jury in a different city in Virginia.

“If they do that, people need to pay attention,” Lowell told Kaitlan Collins. “It’s not like this is normal, but what it does do is show how far they’ll go to break the rule of law, to do a revenge tour that President Trump has ordered.”

This would be grand jury shopping—presenting the same case to multiple grand juries in different locations until you find one willing to indict.

Think about what that means for the grand jury system. If prosecutors can keep trying in Norfolk, then Alexandria, then Richmond, then wherever else until they finally get a “yes,” the grand jury ceases to function as a check on prosecutorial power. It becomes a venue-shopping exercise where prosecutors just need to find the right 23 people.

James herself warned on December 5: “It’s not over. They’re not finished.” She’s right to be cautious. This is already the second illegally appointed prosecutor trying to indict her—Lindsey Halligan in the criminal case, John Sarcone in the separate Albany civil rights investigation. If Judge Schofield disqualifies Sarcone as expected, it will be the fifth Trump-appointed U.S. Attorney found to be illegally serving.

Someone at James’ public appearance asked the devastating question: “Why aren’t there any legitimate prosecutors, legitimate ones, who are willing to put their name on an indictment?”

That’s the answer right there. No legitimate prosecutor will touch these cases. So they keep installing illegitimate ones and shopping for grand juries until they get the result Trump demands.

The AP reports that “prosecutors are expected to try again for an indictment.” Whether that means a third attempt in Norfolk or shopping for a friendlier jury elsewhere remains to be seen.

If they try a third time—especially in a different city—it will confirm what Lowell said: this isn’t about justice. It’s about revenge, whatever it takes.

The Broader Threat Remains

The grand jury’s rejection matters, but it doesn’t erase what happened. Comey faced indictment, four months of proceedings, and legal fees over $450,000. James fought bank fraud charges over a 20-year-old transaction while serving as Attorney General. Bolton faces 18 Espionage Act counts for diary entries. Kelly faces possible court-martial for reminding troops to follow the law.

Even when prosecutions fail—even when judges dismiss them and grand juries reject them—the targets are harassed, bankrupted, and smeared. The process is the punishment. Every potential critic watches and learns.

Conclusion: When Citizens Do What Officials Won’t

For eleven weeks, I’ve documented the construction of an enemies list. Three Trump critics indicted on weak charges. Career prosecutors fired. Loyalists without experience installed. Grand jury procedures corrupted. Federal judges documented “profound investigative missteps.” Constitutional violations exposed.

But then something remarkable happened. Not a judge. Not a senator. Not a prosecutor.

Twenty-three ordinary citizens in Norfolk, Virginia.

They listened to the evidence. They applied the law. They fulfilled their constitutional duty as a check on prosecutorial power. And they said: No.

This is how the system is supposed to work. Grand juries exist precisely to prevent politically motivated prosecutions—the final safeguard when the people say “even if you have the legal authority, these charges are wrong.”

For eleven weeks, almost every institution failed: DOJ weaponized itself, FBI Director promised “multiple responses,” Congress enabled it. Federal judges tried, dismissing cases and documenting misconduct. But judges can only review what’s brought before them. They can dismiss individual cases. They can’t stop new ones.

On Thursday, twenty-three citizens did what federal judges couldn’t: they ended it. Not on a technicality. On the merits. They looked at the evidence and decided: This prosecution should never have been brought.

What You Can Do

The Norfolk grand jury showed us: Ordinary citizens still have power.

When called for jury duty—grand jury or trial jury—you have the power to say no to unjust prosecutions. That’s not just civic duty. In times like these, it’s a moral imperative.

  1. Support legal defense funds for those targeted

  2. Demand legislative action protecting against vindictive re-prosecution

  3. Document everything - articles, timelines, evidence

  4. Contact representatives demanding prosecutorial accountability

  5. Remember this in 2026 and 2028 when you vote

The path forward isn’t through corrupted institutions. It’s through the power citizens retain: jury boxes and ballot boxes.

The Hope in Norfolk

On December 5, an interviewer referred to Trump as “the most powerful person in the world.” Letitia James interrupted him:

“Can I push back? He’s not the most powerful person in the world. He’s just not. We got to push back on that, this notion that he’s all powerful. The fact is that we’re powerful. We’re powerful.“

The next day, twenty-three citizens in Norfolk proved her right.

I titled this series “The Enemies List” because that’s what Trump created: people to be punished through prosecution for opposing him.

For eleven weeks, it looked unstoppable. Then twenty-three people in a Norfolk courthouse proved the system can still work—if citizens use their power.

The grand jury didn’t end the threat. The enemies list still exists. Kelly faces court-martial. Bolton can’t leave the country. The administration still controls DOJ and will try again.

But on December 4, 2025, ordinary citizens reminded us: No president is above the law. No prosecutor can force a conviction. No political pressure can make citizens indict someone they believe is innocent.

The enemies list is wounded. Not because institutions saved us, but because citizens remembered their power and used it.

That’s the hope we needed.

This is the eleventh essay in “The Enemies List” series. The pattern that began with rushed indictments has now been checked—not by Congress, not by the Justice Department, but by twenty-three citizens who said no.
