America's 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Epilogue: Trump's "Great Healthcare Plan" Proves We Were Right

January 16, 2026

Four weeks ago, I published The American Health Security Act of 2026—complete legislation ready to introduce. Ten titles. Fifty-two sections. Swiss-German legal frameworks. Revenue sources. Implementation timeline. Ready for Congressional markup.

Yesterday, President Donald Trump announced his healthcare plan.

Let me show you what nine years of promises gets you.

The “Great Healthcare Plan”

President Donald Trump in the Oval Office on Wednesday.Demetrius Freeman / The Washington Post / Getty Images

On January 15, 2026—the last day of ACA open enrollment in most states—the Trump administration released what it calls “The Great Healthcare Plan.”

Here’s what it promises:

  • Lower prescription drug prices

  • Money sent directly to patients instead of insurers

  • Expanded price transparency

  • Better, cheaper healthcare for everyone

Here’s what it contains:

  • No details on payment amounts

  • No details on eligibility requirements

  • No details on distribution mechanism

  • No timeline for implementation

  • No draft legislation

An administration official on the announcement call: “There are a lot of different ways that could take place. We are open to working with Congress on how to effectuate that.”

Dr. Mehmet Oz, Administrator of CMS: “Directing it directly to the American people who are eligible, it will allow them to buy health insurance and other programs like health savings accounts of their choice.”

Translation: We have no plan. We have concepts. Again.

Let’s Compare

The American Health Security Act of 2026 (Published December 18, 2025):

Title I: Immediate Reforms (Year 1)

  • Enhanced ACA enrollment (automatic, year-round, pre-populated)

  • Medicaid expansion incentives (95% federal match, $500M rural hospital fund)

  • Expanded drug negotiation (50 drugs by 2028, international reference pricing)

  • Surprise billing elimination (including ground ambulances)

  • Hospital price transparency (mandatory posting, $10K/day penalties)

Title II: Public Option and Medicare Expansion (Years 2-3)

  • Medicare Part E (public option phased by age)

  • Medicare age reduction to 60

  • Permanent ACA subsidies (extend to 600% FPL)

  • Comprehensive drug negotiation (all Medicare Part D drugs)

  • Out-of-pocket cap ($2,000 individual/$4,000 family)

Title III: Universal Coverage (Years 4-6)

  • Individual mandate with exemptions

  • Standardized Basic Health Plan (Swiss KVG 1996 model)

  • Federal price controls (German SGB V model)

  • Premium subsidies (0-138% FPL free, sliding scale to 600%)

  • Administrative simplification (single billing code, eliminate prior auth)

Title IV: Medical Education (Years 7-10)

  • Medical school debt relief ($100K forgiveness over 10 years)

  • Medical school expansion ($5B annually, 25% capacity increase)

  • National Health Service Corps (free medical school for rural service)

  • Malpractice reform (German health court system)

Title V: Funding

  • Top rate 39.6% over $400K: $60B

  • Close carried interest: $18B

  • 3% Medicare surcharge on income >$500K: $75-85B

  • Employer payroll redirection: $200-250B

  • Total new revenue: $153-163B annually

  • Net deficit reduction: $200-400B annually

Committee jurisdictions: Senate Finance (primary), HELP (secondary); House Ways & Means (primary), Energy & Commerce (secondary)

Legal basis: Copy-paste provisions from Swiss Federal Health Insurance Act (KVG) 1996 and German Social Code Book V (SGB V)

CBO scoring: All provisions scorable with existing methodologies

Trump’s “Great Healthcare Plan” (Announced January 15, 2026):

  • Send money directly to patients (amount: TBD)

  • Let people buy their own healthcare (mechanism: TBD)

  • Use health savings accounts (details: TBD)

  • Work with Congress to figure it out (timeline: TBD)

  • TrumpRx prescription platform (launching “later this month”)

  • Most favored nation drug pricing (already announced last May)

Committee jurisdictions: Not specified

Legal basis: Not provided

Revenue sources: Not identified

Cost estimates: Not provided

Implementation timeline: “We want to start to see that money moved directly to people”

Why This Matters

This isn’t just vague. It’s dangerous.

Cynthia Cox, Director of the ACA Program at KFF: “When it comes to health reform, the devil is in the details, and this is very light on details.”

Art Caplan, Head of Medical Ethics at NYU Grossman School of Medicine: “It’s a broken plan and a broken idea. The average consumer doesn’t really have time to go shopping when they need to go to the hospital for a premature birth or a car accident.”

Cox warned the plan could send the ACA marketplace into a “death spiral”: “It has the potential to severely impact the stability of the ACA marketplaces and leave people with pre-existing conditions without an option for comprehensive coverage.”

Meanwhile, in the real world:

  • Enhanced ACA subsidies expired December 31, 2025

  • Premiums doubled for millions of Americans

  • ACA enrollment is down 800,000 compared to last year (as of January 13)

  • The House passed a three-year extension January 8 (230-196)

  • The Senate is negotiating its own version

  • Trump has suggested he might veto an extension

Why would he veto it? Because he wants money to go “directly to patients.”

How would that work? [No answer provided]

The Pattern

Let me remind you of the timeline:

February 2017: President Trump, one month into his first term: “Nobody knew healthcare could be so complicated.”

September 2024: Presidential candidate Trump at the debate: “I have concepts of a plan.”

January 2026: President Trump announces: “There are a lot of different ways that could take place. We are open to working with Congress.”

Nine years. Same result: No plan.

The Real Story

Trump released this “plan” on January 15—the last day of ACA open enrollment in most states.

That’s not coincidence. That’s theater.

Midterm elections are 10 months away. Republicans need to look like they’re doing something about healthcare costs. So they released a press release with no details, no legislation, and no timeline.

Meanwhile:

  • 800,000 fewer Americans have health insurance this year

  • Premiums have doubled for millions

  • Four House Republicans had to force a vote via discharge petition because Speaker Johnson blocked their compromise proposals

  • The Senate already rejected similar bills in December

  • Congress is negotiating another temporary extension while people lose coverage

And nobody—not Trump, not Johnson, not Hawley, not anyone—is introducing comprehensive reform.

The frameworks exist. I published one. So have policy experts. So have think tanks. Switzerland’s KVG 1996 and Germany’s SGB V are available online in English. The legal text is ready to copy-paste.

What doesn’t exist is political will.

Nine years ago, Trump said “nobody knew healthcare could be so complicated.”
