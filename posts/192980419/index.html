The Great American Easter Egg Hunt

Stephen’s Sunday Satire | Part XVI | Easter Special, Part Two of Two

April 5, 2026

This is the companion piece to He Is Risen (Again): A Theological Inquiry Into America’s Most Durable Miracle, also published today. You may read them in either order. The conclusion is the same.

For children of all ages, particularly those with a net worth exceeding $50 million.

It is Easter morning in America, and the eggs have been hidden.

They are not ordinary eggs. They are beautiful eggs — the best eggs, many people are saying — gold-plated and monogrammed, scattered across eighteen holes of the most beautiful golf course in the history of civilization, which happens to be owned by the man who hid them.

The children have gathered at the gate. There are many children. They are wearing their Easter best. Some of them are wearing suits that cost more than a schoolteacher’s monthly salary, which is not a coincidence.

“Welcome,” says the man at the podium. He is also wearing a suit. His tie reaches his belt buckle, which is a style choice and not a metaphor, though it is available as one. “Welcome to the greatest Easter egg hunt in the history of Easter. Possibly in the history of eggs.”

The children cheer.

The Rules of the Hunt

The rules are explained by a very serious man in a very serious voice. They are as follows:

Rule One: The hunt is open to all Americans. All Americans who have made a donation of no less than $250,000 to the relevant PAC. Children of donors at the Platinum Patriot level ($1,000,000 and above) receive a map.

Rule Two: The eggs contain prizes. The prizes vary by egg colour. Children are advised to be strategic.

Rule Three: There are no other rules. Rules are, frankly, overrated.

The man at the podium nods. This is correct.

The Eggs and What Is Inside Them

The Bronze Egg contains a certificate of appreciation, suitable for framing, personally signed by an autopen. It also contains a tariff exemption for one (1) imported product of the winner’s choosing, valid for ninety days or until the policy changes, whichever comes first, which is usually the policy.

The Silver Egg contains a fifteen-minute phone call with a cabinet secretary, during which the secretary will listen attentively and then do whatever was already planned. Also included: a personally autographed copy of the Art of the Deal (the new edition, with updated chapter on not paying contractors).

The Gold Egg contains a dinner reservation at a private club where the membership fee is not publicly disclosed but can be inferred from the parking lot. Also included: advance notice of next week’s tariff announcement, which is technically legal if you don’t ask too many questions, and the general posture is not to ask too many questions.

The Platinum Egg — there is only one — contains a pardoning. Not for anything specific. A general pardoning, pre-emptive and comprehensive, for use as needed. The recipient will know when to deploy it. Everyone in this crowd will know when to deploy it.

The Hunt Itself

The children spread across the golf course in the warm Florida morning. Some of them are actual children. Most of them are hedge fund managers who have brought their actual children as props, which is permitted under Rule One and also under the general laws of optics.

The man at the podium watches from the clubhouse, where he is eating a cheeseburger. “Beautiful,” he says. He means the cheeseburger. He also means the hunt. He means, in a general sense, all of this — the eggs, the golf course, the people, the morning. Everything is beautiful when you own it.

A child — an actual child, age seven, wearing a blazer — finds a Bronze Egg near the fourteenth hole and holds it up. Her father, a private equity manager from Greenwich, photographs this for Instagram. The photograph will later be used in a fundraising email with the subject line: He’s Fighting For Our Kids.

Near the water hazard, a senator finds a Silver Egg. He tucks it into his jacket and returns to the clubhouse. He will not mention it in his financial disclosures. This is not because he is dishonest. It is because the disclosure form has not been updated to include egg-based assets, which is an oversight the relevant committee has not yet addressed, because the committee chair also attended the hunt.

The Platinum Egg is found, eventually, by a man who arrived by helicopter and who, when asked afterward what he intends to do with the pardon, smiles in a way that answers the question without answering the question.

“It’s for my kids,” he says.

He is already on the phone to his attorney before he reaches the helicopter. The attorney says nothing. The attorney has been waiting for this call for eighteen months.

The Closing Prayer

The hunt concludes. The children — real and metaphorical — gather again at the podium.

The man’s spiritual adviser takes the microphone. She thanks God. She thanks the President. She explains that the President, like Christ, has suffered greatly so that others might prosper, and that this Easter egg hunt is therefore not merely a fundraiser but a sacred act of national renewal, and possibly also a 501(c)(4) if the paperwork comes through.

A bishop stands behind her and nods, which is the thing the bishop is there to do.

The man at the podium is moved. He has done so much for these people. The tariffs alone. The arrows he has taken. The bullets. You have no idea.

“This,” he says, gesturing broadly at the golf course, the monogrammed eggs, the helicopter on the far fairway, “is what Easter is all about.”

The crowd applauds.

From somewhere on the fourteenth hole, a child waves a Bronze Egg in the Florida sun. It catches the light. It gleams.

It is, everyone agrees, the greatest egg hunt in history.

Possibly in the history of eggs.

A Note for Canadian Readers

Canada does not hold Easter egg hunts at private golf clubs with tiered prize structures based on PAC donations. Canada holds Easter egg hunts in municipal parks where all the eggs contain the same thing (a small chocolate and a coupon for a local business) and the only hierarchy is speed.

This is either a profound moral advantage or evidence that we lack ambition. The jury, like several of the eggs, remains hidden.

Happy Easter. Check the fine print on your pardon.
