The Iran Crisis

The Coalition Iran Built Against Itself

Part XVIII: The First Hours of the Gulf War

February 28, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | February 28, 2026

In the opening hours of the war, Iran attempted to retaliate against Israel and the United States by firing ballistic missiles at military targets across the Middle East.

What Iran actually did was fire ballistic missiles at six sovereign nations simultaneously — not one of which had attacked it.

That distinction will define the next phase of this war.

By mid-morning, Iran’s Red Crescent confirmed the geographic scale of the US and Israeli strikes: 20 of Iran’s 31 provinces had been affected. Two-thirds of the country. “All relief bases, ambulance teams, debris removal teams, search and rescue teams, and medical units are on standby,” the Red Crescent spokesman said. This is not a strike on Tehran. This is a strike on Iran.

What Happened in Each Country

The facts, from primary sources, country by country.

Bahrain. The US Navy Fifth Fleet headquarters was struck. Bahrain’s own state news agency confirmed the hit. No casualty figures have been released. This is the command center for every American naval operation in the Persian Gulf. It was struck. That is the single confirmed direct hit of Iran’s opening salvo — on a US military facility, on the sovereign territory of a country that did not bomb Tehran.

The United Arab Emirates. Multiple missiles were intercepted by Emirati air defenses. One civilian was killed in Abu Dhabi from falling debris. The UAE defense ministry confirmed both the intercepts and the death. The Emirates just absorbed an act of war on its sovereign territory from a country it shares a maritime border with. The UAE has been one of the most careful states in the Gulf at maintaining working relationships across every regional divide. That calculation changed this morning.

Qatar. A missile was intercepted. Zero damage confirmed by the Qatari Interior Ministry. Qatar is the country that has hosted Al Udeid Air Base — the largest US military base in the Middle East — for two decades as a gesture of regional balance. Qatar also hosted the diplomatic back channels that Iran itself used to communicate with Washington. Iran just fired a ballistic missile at the country that was carrying its messages.

Kuwait. Kuwait’s state news agency KUNA confirmed that missiles were “dealt with” in Kuwaiti airspace. No reported damage. Kuwait maintained neutrality through every Gulf crisis since 1991. Iranian ballistic missiles flew over Kuwaiti cities this morning.

Jordan. Two Iranian ballistic missiles were shot down by the Jordanian military. Jordan’s armed forces confirmed this directly. Jordan intercepted Iranian missiles in June 2025 as well — but that interception was in defense of Israel. This time, Iran targeted Jordan itself. Jordan, which has been one of the quieter mediating voices in the region, just had its airspace violated and its military activated against an Iranian salvo.

Syria. Four civilians were killed in the southern city of Sweida after an Iranian missile struck a building in an industrial zone. Syria’s state news agency SANA confirmed the deaths. The missile was almost certainly aimed at Israel — Sweida is close to Israeli-controlled territory — and missed. Iran now has civilian blood on its hands in a country that had nothing to do with this war. The salvo that was supposed to demonstrate Iranian resolve instead killed Syrians.

Saudi Arabia. Iran’s state-aligned Fars News Agency claimed strikes on Saudi territory. No confirmation has come from any Saudi source. No Tier 1 or Tier 2 verification exists. Either the strikes did not happen, or Riyadh is not yet ready to say they did. Both possibilities carry enormous implications — one for the credibility of Iranian claims, the other for what Saudi Arabia is preparing to do next.

The Asymmetry

Compare what happened on each side in the opening hours.

US and Israeli strikes hit the IRGC Intelligence Directorate. The area surrounding the Supreme Leader’s compound. The presidential palace district. The National Security Council. Satellite imagery confirmed collapsed buildings at Khamenei’s secure compound. Iranian state media itself confirmed three detonations in central Tehran.

Iran’s retaliatory salvo — fired across five or six countries simultaneously, against 40,000 US troops in theater — killed one civilian. From debris.

One side hit what it aimed at. The other side demonstrated intent to strike everywhere and capability to hit almost nothing.

This asymmetry is not a minor operational footnote. It is the central military fact of the opening phase of this war. Iran’s deterrence posture for years rested on the threat of its missile arsenal — 2,000 to 6,000 weapons, the largest stockpile in the Middle East. The Gulf states and their US-supplied air defense systems just caught nearly every missile Iran threw at them in the first salvo.

Iran did not retaliate against Israel this morning. Iran gave every country in the Middle East a reason to retaliate against Iran.

The Coalition Iran Just Built Against Itself

This is the strategic consequence that will define the next phase of the war, and it deserves to be stated plainly.

In a single morning, Iran violated the airspace of Bahrain, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Jordan, and possibly Saudi Arabia. Not one of these countries attacked Iran. Not one of them launched strikes on Tehran. Several of them were actively neutral, or actively mediating, or actively hosting the diplomatic back channels through which Iran communicated with Washington.

Every nation whose airspace was violated, whose civilians were killed, whose sovereignty was breached now has legal and political justification to join whatever coalition forms next.

Watch for the joint statement. Watch for airspace coordination between Riyadh, Abu Dhabi, Manama, Kuwait City, and Amman. Watch for the moment Saudi Arabia decides whether it confirms or denies the Fars News claims — because that decision will tell you everything about what Riyadh has already decided to do.

The Gulf Cooperation Council states have spent thirty years threading a needle between Iran and the United States, maintaining working relationships with both, refusing to be fully conscripted into anyone’s war. That era may have ended this morning with a single salvo that Iran almost certainly launched expecting it to demonstrate strength.

It demonstrated the opposite.

The Economic Shock Begins

While the missiles flew, the global economy started absorbing the consequences.

Dubai suspended all flight operations at both Dubai International and Al Maktoum International airports. Dubai International handles between 2,000 and 2,500 flights daily — one of the highest volumes on earth. Both airports went dark simultaneously. Doha had already suspended service. The two largest aviation hubs in the Middle East, which together form the connective tissue of air travel between Europe, Asia, and Africa, are now closed.

Moody’s Ratings issued a warning that the strikes carry potential to disrupt the global economy, with the extent depending largely on what happens to energy infrastructure. The specific concern: the Strait of Hormuz. Approximately one-fifth of the world’s oil supply passes through that narrow waterway between Iran and Oman. Iran has threatened to close it before. Whether it attempts to do so now — and whether it has the remaining naval capability to make that threat real after US strikes on its navy — is the economic question that will define the next 72 hours for markets that open Monday morning.

The Speech

At 2:30 in the morning, standing behind a lectern at Mar-a-Lago wearing a white USA hat, Donald Trump posted an eight-minute video to Truth Social explaining why the United States had just gone to war.

The speech is now on the record. It deserves to be analyzed as such.

Trump opened with a 47-year grievance list — the 1979 hostage crisis, the 1983 Beirut barracks bombing that killed 241 American military personnel, the USS Cole, American soldiers killed by Iranian-backed IEDs in Iraq, October 7. These are real events. The history is accurate. What is absent from the list is any mention of the nuclear talks, their content, or what Iran actually offered before the strikes began. The speech has no diplomatic history in it. The negotiations that the Omani mediator says were producing meaningful concessions do not exist in Trump’s telling.

He named the June 2024 strikes “Operation Midnight Hammer” and said they “obliterated” Iran’s nuclear program. American intelligence later assessed the program had been badly damaged, not destroyed. He used the word “obliterated” again tonight, for a second campaign against the same program he previously claimed to have already obliterated.

He told the IRGC and Iranian security forces: “Lay down your arms and have complete immunity, or in the alternative, face certain death.” An unconditional surrender demand, delivered by video, with no mechanism, no intermediary, no negotiating channel. No one to call. No terms to discuss. Lay down your arms or face certain death.

Then came the sentence that belongs in the historical record exactly as spoken.

“Stay sheltered. Don’t leave your home. It’s very dangerous outside. Bombs will be dropping everywhere.”

He said this to Iranian civilians. In the same speech in which he told them their hour of freedom was at hand.

Stay home. Don’t go outside. Bombs will be dropping everywhere. Take over your government.

This is the plan.

“No president was willing to do what I am willing to do tonight.” He said this himself. He is correct. And he appears to mean it as a compliment to himself — the constitutional bypass, the unilateral decision, the middle-of-the-night video, the absence of congressional notification — these are not things he is hiding. They are things he is proud of.

He closed by telling the Iranian people: “So let’s see how you respond.”

A population of 90 million people, under bombardment, hiding in bathrooms and underground parking garages, watching highways clog as they try to flee a city of 15 million — and the President of the United States is waiting to see how they respond.

The speech tells you everything about the theory of this war. Destroy enough, frighten enough, and a population that has already been killing its own government in the streets will finish the job. Trump is not wrong that Iranians despise their government. He is assuming that despising your government and welcoming foreign bombs as liberation are the same thing.

History has not been kind to that assumption.

What Jeffrey Sachs Said

As the bombs were falling, economist Jeffrey Sachs — Columbia University professor, former UN adviser, and one of the most credentialed critics of US foreign policy interventionism — went on the record with an assessment that deserves to sit alongside the official statements.

“This has never been about nuclear weapons,” Sachs said. “This has been about Israel’s regional hegemony in the Middle East. Israel wants to topple the Iranian regime. Period. It has said so.”

That is not a fringe claim. It is documented Israeli policy, repeated across multiple administrations and governments. The question Sachs is raising is not whether Israel wants regime change in Iran — that is established — but why the United States has consistently subordinated its own diplomatic interests to that goal.

His answer to that question is structural, not conspiratorial: “The United States does not accept yes for an answer because it works for the Government of Israel.”

The JCPOA history is the evidence. Barack Obama negotiated a nuclear deal in 2015. Trump withdrew from it in his first term. Iran repeatedly offered to return to the framework. The offers were not accepted. Iran came back to the table in Geneva last week and offered to suspend enrichment for three to five years. The US struck while the mediator was still describing the talks as producing meaningful progress.

Sachs frames this as a 30-year pattern, not an isolated decision: “Why has the United States done what Israel has told the United States to do for 30 years? And I don’t just mean about Iran. I mean across the Middle East.”

That is the structural argument. The reader can assess its weight.

Sachs also raised, as one possible explanation among several, the Epstein files — the partially sealed documents that multiple courts and legislators have sought to release, and whose full contents remain unknown. “It may be what’s in the Epstein files that haven’t been released,” he said. “It may be blackmail. It may be corruption. Maybe many other things.”

This series has documented the Epstein files extensively in a separate series. The files exist. Their contents are partially known and partially sealed. Sachs is not the first credentialed voice to raise the question of what remains hidden and why. This series does not endorse his speculation as the explanation. It notes that he raised it, on the record, on the morning the bombs started falling.

What Sachs is certain about — and what the documented record supports — is the conclusion he lands on: “It is not about America’s interests. We have no interest whatsoever in going to war with Iran right now.”

The Omani mediator said the same thing in different words: “This is not your war.”

Two voices, from very different vantage points, saying the same thing on the same morning. That is worth recording.

The People Iran’s Government Left Behind

In Tehran on Saturday. In telephone and text interviews in the hours after the attacks, many Iranians said they had been left to fend for themselves.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

While the missiles flew, fifteen million people in Tehran were left to fend for themselves.

The New York Times documented what the Iranian government did in the hours after the strikes began: state television broadcast revolutionary songs from the 1980s Iran-Iraq war. The Supreme National Security Council issued a statement saying “there is no need to worry.” School principals, flooded with frantic calls from parents, had received no emergency instructions from anyone.

Highways out of Tehran backed up for miles as residents tried to flee north toward the Caspian Sea. Long lines formed outside bakeries and gas stations. A grocery store posted on social media that it would accept installment payments for legumes and diapers.

“Iranian people are completely on their own,” said Roya, a 62-year-old translator. “We are helping each other. The government is no-show.”

This is not a marginal detail. It is a portrait of a government that is either unwilling or incapable of functioning in a crisis of its own making. The regime that has spent forty years justifying its existence by warning its people about American aggression — when American aggression arrived, it had nothing to tell them except to watch revolutionary television and not worry.

This also lands on top of something the international coverage has moved past too quickly. In January, the Iranian government killed at least 7,000 of its own citizens in three days while suppressing protests against the regime and the collapsing economy. By February 21, protests had resumed — students at universities across a dozen cities chanting “Khamenei is a murderer.”

Trump told Iranians this would be “probably your only chance for generations” to take over their government.

Whether those Iranians — the ones hiding in underground parking garages beneath Pilates studios, the ones sending their children to shelter under school stairs, the ones standing in line for bread in sixty-percent inflation — hear that as liberation or as one more powerful man making decisions about their lives without asking them, is not a question Washington appears to have asked.

The Man Who Stepped Forward

Into that vacuum, one man stepped forward.

Within hours of the strikes beginning, Reza Pahlavi — son of the last Shah of Iran, monarchist pretender in exile for 45 years — posted a statement addressed to his “dear compatriots.”

“The assistance that the President of the United States had promised to the brave people of Iran has now arrived,” he wrote. “This is a humanitarian intervention, and its target is the Islamic Republic, its apparatus of repression, and its machinery of killing — not the country and great nation of Iran.”

He told the IRGC and security forces to stand down: “You have sworn an oath to protect Iran and the Iranian nation, not the Islamic Republic and its leaders. Join the nation and help ensure a stable and secure transition. Otherwise, you will sink with Khamenei’s ship and his crumbling regime.”

He told Iranians to stay home for now, but be ready: “The time to return to the streets is approaching. I will communicate to you.”

And he told Trump: exercise “the utmost possible caution to preserve the lives of civilians.” He called Iranians “your natural allies and the allies of the free world.”

This statement is significant. It is also, in its current form, a claim without an organization behind it.

Part XVI of this series documented the governance gap — the question Washington was not publicly asking before the bombs fell. Reza Pahlavi is now the answer Washington appears to have had in mind, or at minimum the answer that has presented itself. He is a symbol with genuine resonance among a segment of the Iranian diaspora and some Iranians inside the country who associate the monarchy with the era before the Islamic Republic.

He is not a government. He has no military force. He has no institutional base inside Iran. He has lived outside the country since he was 17 years old. His father’s reign ended in revolution in 1979 — a revolution that a significant portion of Iranians, including many who despise the Islamic Republic, remember as having also been necessary.

Trump told Iranians to take over their government. Reza Pahlavi told Iranians he would tell them when.

“Follow my messages through social media and satellite media,” he said. “If disruptions occur in the internet and satellite broadcasts, I will remain in contact with you via radio.”

Iran’s internet had already dropped to 4 percent of normal levels.

The governance question from Part XVI just got a face. Whether that face has anything behind it — any plan, any coalition, any mechanism for the morning after — is the question this war will answer, one way or another.

What the First Hours Tell Us

Part XVI of this series asked who governs Iran after. The question has not been answered. Part XVII documented how the war began. Part XVIII must document what the first hours revealed about how it will proceed.

Iran’s missile doctrine — fire at everything, demonstrate resolve, accept that interceptions will occur — has in one morning converted the Gulf’s carefully maintained neutrality into justified grievance. The military asymmetry between precision US-Israeli strikes and Iran’s scattered salvo suggests Iran’s deterrence posture was more threat than capability. And the Iranian government’s abandonment of its own population in the first hours of the war it has spent decades preparing for raises the most important question of all:

If the regime falls, who among the Iranian people will mourn it?

And if it doesn’t fall — if it survives this campaign radicalized, backed into Russia and China’s orbit, with Mojtaba Khamenei consolidating power in the chaos — who among the Gulf states, having just absorbed Iranian missiles on their sovereign territory, will argue for restraint?

The coalition Iran built against itself this morning may turn out to be the most consequential strategic development of the opening phase of this war.

Trump wanted regime change. Iran may have just made it easier.

Addendum — Published February 28, 2026, Mid-Morning

The following updates were confirmed after initial publication.

Saudi Arabia confirmed. The Fars News claim documented above has now been officially verified. Saudi Arabia’s foreign ministry confirmed “blatant and cowardly Iranian attacks” on Riyadh and the country’s oil-rich eastern province. The statement noted the attacks came even after Saudi Arabia had explicitly guaranteed it would not allow its airspace or territory to be used to target Iran. Iran attacked a country that had publicly declared neutrality. Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman has since told UAE President Sheikh Mohamed bin Zayed that Saudi Arabia would be placing “all its capabilities at its disposal” to support the Emirates. The coalition Iran built against itself this morning has its first formal military commitment.

No backchannel negotiations. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed on NBC News that there are currently no negotiations taking place to end the war. “If the Americans want to talk to us, they know how they can contact me,” he said. On whether Supreme Leader Khamenei and President Pezeshkian were still alive, Araghchi said “as far as I know” — a hedge that speaks for itself. On Trump’s call for Iranians to overthrow their government, Araghchi’s response was two words: “Mission Impossible.”

The US messaging was a shambles. The Times reports the overall US government messaging to the Iranian people was “muddled.” There were no instructions on how IRGC soldiers were actually supposed to surrender following Trump’s video demand. Voice of America’s Persian-language service — the primary vehicle for reaching Iranians directly — had been gutted by Kari Lake’s cuts to US-funded media abroad. Trump told 90 million Iranians to take over their government via an agency he had already partially dismantled. Kari Lake posted on X: “Iran will be FREE.”

The Gang of Eight briefing. House Speaker Mike Johnson stated that congressional leaders were briefed “earlier this week” that military action “may become necessary.” This deserves precise reading. Briefed that action may happen is notification. It is not consultation. It is not authorization. The Constitution does not grant the executive the power to inform Congress of wars it has already decided to fight. It grants Congress the power to declare them.

The E3 joint statement. Britain, France, and Germany issued a careful joint communiqué: they did not participate in the strikes, called for a resumption of negotiations, criticized Iran’s nuclear and regional policies, but reaffirmed “commitment to the protection of civilian life.” It is neither endorsement nor condemnation. It is three major democracies declining to say whether a war launched by their closest ally was justified.

Representative Jim Himes, Democrat, House Intelligence Committee: “Everything I have heard from the administration before and after these strikes on Iran confirms this is a war of choice with no strategic endgame.”

Tehran. By mid-morning the city had gone from gridlocked chaos — people abandoning their cars and walking away from highways — to what Mahsa, 39, described as “deserted, silent and motionless.” A city of fifteen million people, silenced.
