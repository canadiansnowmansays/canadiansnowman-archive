Two Impeachments, Two Acquittals

Parrt 10: When the Senate Failed America (2019-2021)

January 9, 2026

Donald Trump holds a unique distinction in American history: he is the only president ever impeached twice. The House of Representatives, exercising its constitutional responsibility, found sufficient evidence to charge Trump with abuse of power, obstruction of Congress, and incitement of insurrection.

But impeachment is only half of the constitutional accountability mechanism. Conviction requires a two-thirds vote in the Senate, and in both instances, Senate Republicans chose partisan protection over constitutional duty. Their refusals to hold Trump accountable established a dangerous precedent: a president can commit virtually any misconduct and escape consequences as long as his political party controls sufficient Senate votes.

This essay examines both impeachments, the evidence presented, and the Senate Republicans’ twin failures of constitutional responsibility.

First Impeachment: The Ukraine Extortion (2019-2020)

The Scheme

In July 2019, President Trump suspended nearly $400 million in military aid to Ukraine that Congress had already appropriated. Ukraine desperately needed this aid to defend against Russian aggression. Trump froze it without explanation and without notifying Congress.

On July 25, 2019, Trump called Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky. The call, later memorialized in a White House summary and confirmed by witnesses, revealed Trump’s corrupt purpose.

After Zelensky thanked Trump for U.S. military support, Trump’s response was immediate:

“I would like you to do us a favor though...”

The favor was investigating Joe Biden, Trump’s likely 2020 opponent, and Biden’s son Hunter. Trump wanted Ukraine to announce investigations that would damage Biden politically. He was using congressionally appropriated military aid—aid Ukraine needed to defend against Russian invasion—as leverage to pressure a foreign government to interfere in the 2020 American election.

This wasn’t subtle. Trump’s own acting White House Chief of Staff Mick Mulvaney admitted the quid pro quo during a press conference, telling reporters to “get over it” because this is how foreign policy works. (It isn’t.)

The Evidence

House Democrats uncovered extensive evidence of Trump’s scheme:

The July 25 Call: Trump explicitly asked Zelensky to investigate Biden and work with Trump’s personal attorney Rudy Giuliani and Attorney General Bill Barr on the investigations.

The Aid Freeze: Trump suspended the military aid shortly before the call, without explaining why. Ukrainian officials were frantically asking U.S. counterparts what happened.

The Conditioning: Multiple witnesses, including Ambassador Gordon Sondland (a Trump donor), testified that “everyone” understood military aid and a White House meeting were conditioned on Ukraine announcing Biden investigations.

The Cover-Up: After a whistleblower reported Trump’s conduct, the White House moved the call transcript to a classified server to hide it. Trump’s

team withheld the transcript from Congress and directed officials not to cooperate with the impeachment inquiry.

The Pressure Campaign: Witnesses described months of pressure on Ukraine to announce investigations, coordinated by Giuliani and involving multiple administration officials.

The Articles of Impeachment

The House impeached Trump on two articles on December 18, 2019:

Article I: Abuse of Power – Trump solicited foreign interference in the 2020 election by pressuring Ukraine to investigate his political rival using military aid as leverage.

Article II: Obstruction of Congress – Trump directed a blanket defiance of the impeachment inquiry, ordering officials not to testify and refusing to produce any documents.

The Senate Trial and Acquittal

Democrats Who Led Trump’s First Impeachment See Grim Validation in Ukraine Invasion

The Senate trial began in January 2020. Senate Republicans made clear from the start they would protect Trump regardless of evidence. Majority Leader Mitch McConnell coordinated trial strategy with the White House and announced he was “not an impartial juror.”

The trial lasted just two weeks. Republicans voted against calling witnesses who had direct knowledge of Trump’s scheme. Trump’s defense team didn’t dispute the facts—they argued that abuse of power wasn’t impeachable unless it violated criminal law (a position at odds with constitutional history and the Federalist Papers).

On February 5, 2020, the Senate voted:

Article I (Abuse of Power): 52-48 to acquit (Mitt Romney was the lone Republican voting to convict) Article II (Obstruction): 53-47 to acquit

Trump was acquitted on party-line votes despite overwhelming evidence of serious constitutional misconduct.

Second Impeachment: Inciting the January 6 Insurrection (2021)

The Buildup – Two Months of Lies

After losing the 2020 election to Joe Biden, Trump spent two months lying about election fraud. He filed more than 60 lawsuits, losing nearly all. His own Attorney General Bill Barr announced there was no evidence of fraud sufficient to change the outcome. Multiple recounts and audits confirmed Biden’s victory.

Trump didn’t care about evidence. He wanted power. So he lied to his supporters, claiming the election was stolen. He pressured state officials to “find” votes. He demanded Vice President Pence refuse to certify electoral votes. And he summoned his supporters to Washington on January 6, 2021—the day Congress would certify Biden’s victory.

The Rally and the Speech

On the morning of January 6, Trump held a rally near the White House. For more than an hour, he told thousands of supporters that the election was stolen and that Pence could overturn the results. Trump’s rhetoric was violent and direct:

“We will never give up. We will never concede.” “We will stop the steal.” “You’ll never take back our country with weakness.” “If you don’t fight like hell, you’re not going to have a country anymore.”

Then Trump directed the crowd to march to the Capitol: “We’re going to walk down to the Capitol... We’re going to try and give our Republicans, the weak ones... the kind of pride and boldness that they need to take back our country.”

The Attack

The mob marched to the Capitol and attacked. Thousands of Trump supporters breached security barriers, smashed windows, and invaded the building. Members of Congress fled for their lives. Capitol Police officers were beaten. Rioters hunted for Vice President Pence, who was presiding over the electoral count. They erected a gallows outside and chanted “Hang Mike Pence.”

The mob ransacked congressional offices, stole documents, and temporarily halted the constitutional process of certifying a presidential election—an unprecedented attack on American democracy.

Trump’s Three-Hour Delay

As the violence unfolded on live television, Trump watched from the White House. For more than three hours, he refused to call off the mob. His own staff begged him to tell rioters to leave. Congressional leaders pleaded for help. Trump’s children texted White House Chief of Staff Mark Meadows urging action.

Trump resisted. Multiple witnesses later testified that Trump was enjoying the violence, that he was pleased his supporters were “fighting for him.” When he finally released a video message at 4:17 PM, it was not a condemnation. Trump told the rioters:

“We love you. You’re very special... Go home. We love you.”

Five people died as a result of the attack. More than 140 police officers were injured. The Capitol was desecrated. And Trump expressed love for those who did it.

The Second Impeachment

One week later, on January 13, 2021, the House impeached Trump for “Incitement of Insurrection.” The article charged that Trump:

  • Made false claims about the election

  • Pressured state and federal officials to overturn results

  • Summoned supporters to Washington and directed them to the Capitol

  • Incited violence and refused to stop it for hours

  • Demonstrated he poses an ongoing threat

Ten Republicans joined all Democrats in voting to impeach—the most bipartisan presidential impeachment in American history. The vote was 232-197.

The Second Senate Trial and Acquittal

The Senate trial occurred after Trump left office, raising questions about jurisdiction. But the Senate voted 56-44 that trying a former president was constitutional, with six Republicans joining Democrats.

The House managers presented devastating evidence: video of Trump’s speech, timeline of the attack, Trump’s three-hour delay, and witness testimony. They showed rioters explicitly stating they attacked because Trump told them to.

Trump’s defense was incoherent, claiming Trump’s speech was protected by the First Amendment (it isn’t—incitement to violence has never been protected speech) and that he bore no responsibility for his supporters’ actions despite months of lies and a direct order to march to the Capitol.

On February 13, 2021, the Senate voted 57-43 to convict. Seven Republicans joined all Democrats. But 67 votes were required for conviction. Trump was acquitted again.

Mitch McConnell’s Cowardice

‘Practically and morally responsible’: McConnell votes to acquit, then condemns Trump

Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell’s conduct after the second impeachment deserves special contempt. McConnell voted to acquit Trump. But immediately after the vote, McConnell delivered a speech acknowledging Trump’s guilt:

“There is no question that President Trump is practically and morally responsible for provoking the events of that day. The people who stormed this building believed they were acting on the wishes and instructions of their President.”

McConnell explicitly stated Trump was responsible for the insurrection. But McConnell had just voted to acquit on the procedural ground that the Senate lacked jurisdiction to try a former president—a jurisdictional question McConnell himself had delayed the trial to create by refusing to begin the trial while Trump was still president.

This was cowardice of historic proportions. McConnell acknowledged Trump’s guilt but refused to hold him accountable, using a procedural argument McConnell himself engineered. He wanted to have it both ways: appear reasonable by condemning Trump’s conduct while protecting Trump from actual consequences.

The Precedent Set

Trump’s two acquittals establish a dangerous precedent: a president can abuse power to extort foreign governments for political benefit and incite violent insurrection to overturn election results, and face no consequences as long as his political party controls 34 Senate votes.

The Constitution’s impeachment mechanism has been revealed as insufficient. The Framers anticipated many forms of presidential misconduct, but they didn’t anticipate a political party so debased that senators would refuse to convict a president even when they privately acknowledged his guilt.

Trump’s acquittals don’t prove he was innocent—they prove that constitutional accountability requires political courage, and Republican senators lacked it. By choosing partisan loyalty over constitutional duty, they told future presidents: you can get away with anything if your party supports you.
