Georgia RICO

Part 16: The Dismissal (2024)

January 29, 2026

After Judge McAfee ruled in March 2024 that Fani Willis could remain on the case if Nathan Wade resigned, Trump’s attorneys immediately appealed. They argued the “appearance of impropriety” required Willis’s complete disqualification—not just Wade’s resignation.

The appeal created another massive delay. While the Georgia Court of Appeals considered the issue, all proceedings in the criminal case were stayed. No trial date. No plea negotiations. No progress.

Months passed. Trump campaigned for president while his Georgia case sat frozen.

On December 19, 2024, the Georgia Court of Appeals issued its bombshell ruling: Fani Willis was disqualified from the case entirely.

The court held that the “appearance of impropriety” was so significant it undermined public confidence in the prosecution. Even without proof that Willis actually financially benefited, the appearance alone was disqualifying.

Willis immediately appealed to the Georgia Supreme Court, arguing she should be allowed to continue. But on September 5, 2024, the Georgia Supreme Court declined to hear her appeal.

Willis was out. Permanently. The case that she had spent two and a half years building would have to be handed to someone else.

But by then, Trump had won the 2024 presidential election.

The Search Nobody Wanted: Finding a Replacement Prosecutor

After the Georgia Supreme Court’s decision, the Prosecuting Attorneys’ Council of Georgia was tasked with finding a new prosecutor.

Pete Skandalakis, the Council’s executive director, began reaching out to district attorneys across Georgia. He contacted “several” prosecutors, asking if they would take the Trump case.

Every single one declined.

The reasons were obvious:

  • Trump had won the presidency in November 2024

  • Prosecuting a sitting president seemed practically impossible

  • The case was extraordinarily complex and resource-intensive

  • Any prosecutor who took it would face intense scrutiny, threats, and political attacks

  • The case had become politically radioactive

Judge McAfee set a deadline: November 14, 2025. Either appoint a new prosecutor by that date, or the case would be dismissed for want of prosecution.

On November 14, with the deadline looming and no takers, Skandalakis made a surprising decision: he would appoint himself as prosecutor.

“The decision to assume responsibility for this matter was reached only after careful and deliberate consideration,” Skandalakis wrote. “While it would have been simple to allow Judge McAfee’s deadline to lapse... thereby allowing the case to be dismissed for want of prosecution—I did not believe that to be the right course of action.”

Who Is Pete Skandalakis?

Pete Skandalakis, 64, had an unusual pedigree for such a high-profile case.

He served as district attorney in the Coweta Judicial Circuit (southwest of Atlanta) for 25 years. Notably, he ran for office as both a Democrat and a Republican at different times, and now led the nonpartisan Prosecuting Attorneys’ Council.

This bipartisan background was important. Skandalakis wanted to be seen as above politics—neither a Trump ally nor a Trump enemy, just a prosecutor evaluating evidence.

Willis’s office delivered the case file to Skandalakis: 101 banker boxes and an 8-terabyte hard drive containing the complete investigative file.

Skandalakis began his review. He said he would “meticulously examine the indictment, the law, and the evidence” before deciding whether to proceed.

“It’s important that someone makes a decision on this case,” Skandalakis said in September 2025. “It’s important for the public, frankly for the nation, for the defendants, for all the interested parties.”

That decision came today—November 26, 2025.

The Dismissal: “Nothing Short of a Remarkable Feat”

This morning, Pete Skandalakis filed a 22-page motion requesting dismissal of all charges against Trump and his remaining co-defendants.

Within hours, Judge McAfee granted the motion. The Georgia case—the last criminal prosecution of Donald Trump—was dead.

In his detailed explanation, Skandalakis acknowledged the seriousness of Willis’s charges:

“The indictment alleges a compelling set of acts that, if proven beyond a reasonable doubt, would establish a conspiracy undertaken by multiple individuals... to overturn the results of the November 2020 Presidential Election.”

But Skandalakis concluded the case was not viable for several reasons:

1. The Timing Problem

“There is no realistic prospect that a sitting President will be compelled to appear in Georgia to stand trial on the allegations in this indictment,” Skandalakis wrote.

Trump’s current term doesn’t end until January 20, 2029. By that point, eight years will have elapsed since the January 2, 2021 phone call that was the centerpiece of the case.

“And even if, by some extraordinary circumstance, Trump were to appear in Georgia on January 21, 2029... an immediate jury trial would be impossible.”

2. The Complexity Problem

Given the “complexity of the legal issues at hand”—constitutional questions, Supremacy Clause arguments, immunity, jurisdiction, venue, speedy-trial concerns, and access to federal records—Skandalakis concluded:

“Bringing this case before a jury in 2029, 2030, or even 2031 would be nothing short of a remarkable feat.”

This was the key phrase. Even if every legal issue was resolved in the prosecution’s favor, even if Trump could somehow be compelled to appear, even if everything went perfectly—trial by 2031 would still be “remarkable.”

3. The Co-Defendant Problem

Skandalakis considered severing Trump’s case from his co-defendants—trying the others first while waiting for Trump’s presidency to end.

He concluded this “would be both illogical and unduly burdensome and costly for the State and for Fulton County.”

The co-defendants’ speedy trial rights would be compromised. The evidence was interconnected. Trying some defendants while waiting years for others made no sense.

4. The Venue Problem

Skandalakis argued: “The criminal conduct alleged in the Atlanta Judicial Circuit’s prosecution was conceived in Washington, D.C., not the State of Georgia. The federal government is the appropriate venue for this prosecution, not the State of Georgia.”

He noted that “the strongest and most prosecutable case” against those seeking to overturn the election was Jack Smith’s federal prosecution—which had already been dropped after Trump’s electoral victory.

5. Weaknesses in Specific Charges

Skandalakis identified problems with parts of Willis’s case:

  • The Republican fake electors may have lacked criminal intent

  • Charges against Mark Meadows and Jeffrey Clark might not survive because of their federal official status

  • The case theory might not meet the “rigorous standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt”

6. Not About Politics

Skandalakis emphasized: “I recognize that, given the deep political divisions in our country, this decision will not be universally popular.”

He noted his family had received threats after taking the case. But his decision was “not guided by a desire to advance an agenda but is based on my beliefs and understanding of the law.”

Bottom line: “In my professional judgment, the citizens of Georgia are not served by pursuing this case in full for another five to ten years.”

Skandalakis used the Latin term “Nolle prosequi”—”to be unwilling to prosecute.”

The case was over.

Trump’s Reaction: “LAW and JUSTICE”

President Donald Trump walks from Marine One to the White House soon after the Georgia case was dismissed

Trump celebrated immediately on social media:

“LAW and JUSTICE have prevailed in the Great State of Georgia, as the corrupt Fani Willis Witch Hunt against me, and other Great American Patriots, has been DISMISSED in its entirety... We are going to keep winning, and continue to, MAKE AMERICA GREAT AGAIN!”

His lead attorney Steve Sadow declared: “The political persecution of President Trump by disqualified DA Fani Willis is finally over. This case should never have been brought. A fair and impartial prosecutor has put an end to this lawfare.”

Defense attorney Ashleigh Merchant, who first exposed the Willis-Wade relationship, said: “All we ever wanted was for a neutral prosecutor to evaluate this case without any political or personal motivation.”

Trump’s framing was consistent with his reaction to every accountability failure: transform dismissal on procedural grounds into claims of innocence and persecution.

What About Ruby Freeman?

Lost in the celebration was Ruby Freeman, the election worker whose life was destroyed by Trump’s false accusations.

Freeman testified before the January 6th Committee about the harassment she endured:

  • Death threats

  • Racist attacks

  • FBI agents at her door

  • Having to leave her home

She won $148 million in damages from Rudy Giuliani in federal civil court. But Giuliani declared bankruptcy and will likely never pay.

The Georgia criminal case would have held Trump accountable for what happened to Freeman. Now that accountability will never come.

Skandalakis noted in his dismissal that charges related to Freeman “could continue—but should be brought in Cobb, not Fulton, County.” Whether that will happen remains unclear.

Freeman destroyed Ruby Freeman’s life with lies. Freeman won in civil court but faces an insolvent defendant. The criminal case that would have provided real accountability is dead.

Trump’s Pardons: Preemptive Victory

On November 10, 2025—two weeks before Skandalakis’s dismissal—Trump issued pardons for all of the alternate electors charged in the Georgia case.

The pardons were technically federal and couldn’t affect state charges. But they sent a clear message: Trump viewed the fake electors as patriots, not criminals.

The pardons also made state prosecution more difficult. Why would fake electors cooperate with prosecutors when the president has already declared them innocent?

The Systemic Failures: Why Georgia Couldn’t Hold Trump Accountable

The Georgia case’s collapse reveals multiple systemic problems:

1. Personal Conduct Can Kill Strong Cases

Willis made a catastrophic error hiring her romantic partner. Whether or not she financially benefited, the appearance of impropriety was devastating. Her “tremendous lapse in judgment” gave Trump’s attorneys the opening they needed.

2. States Can’t Really Prosecute Presidents

Even though Georgia isn’t bound by federal DOJ policy, practically speaking it’s nearly impossible to haul a sitting president into state court. Federal supremacy claims, security concerns, and political realities make it infeasible.

3. Nobody Wants the Radioactive Case

Multiple prosecutors declined to take over after Willis was disqualified. The case was too controversial, too complex, too dangerous. This speaks to a systemic problem: prosecutors rationally avoid cases that will destroy their careers, even if evidence is strong.

4. Delay Works

From Willis’s indictment in August 2023 to today’s dismissal is 28 months. During that time:

  • The Willis-Wade scandal consumed months

  • Disqualification appeals consumed months

  • Finding a replacement consumed months

  • Trump won the presidency

  • Prosecution became impossible

Trump didn’t beat the case on the merits. He just delayed long enough that circumstances changed.

5. Electoral Victory = Immunity

Trump’s win in November 2024 effectively ended all criminal accountability. Federal cases were dismissed under DOJ policy. The Georgia case became impractical. New York’s sentencing was postponed indefinitely.

Winning the presidency gave Trump immunity more complete than any court ruling could provide.

What Should Have Happened

In a functional justice system:

2023: Willis indicts Trump and co-defendants 2024: Trial begins in summer. Jury hears the Raffensperger tape, fake elector documents, witness testimony 2024: Jury deliberates and reaches verdict before November election 2025: If convicted, Trump faces sentencing and appeals

Instead:

2023: Indictment 2024: Willis-Wade scandal, months of hearings, disqualification, appeals, Trump wins presidency
2025: New prosecutor reviews case, concludes prosecution impossible, dismisses all charges

The evidence was never tested. The jury never heard the tape. Trump never faced accountability.

Conclusion: How Romance Killed the Rule of Law

The Georgia case was Trump’s last real jeopardy. After the federal cases collapsed, Georgia was all that remained.

The case was strong:

  • Recorded evidence (Raffensperger call)

  • Documentary evidence (fake elector certificates)

  • Cooperative witnesses (four co-defendants who pleaded guilty)

  • State sovereignty (not subject to federal immunity doctrines)

And it died because Fani Willis hired her boyfriend.

Willis’s relationship with Nathan Wade will be studied in legal ethics courses for decades. Her personal decision gave Trump the procedural opening he needed. The Georgia Court of Appeals disqualified her. No replacement prosecutor wanted the case. Skandalakis took it but concluded prosecution was impractical. The case was dismissed.

Trump demanded Brad Raffensperger “find 11,780 votes.” That recorded demand—clear evidence of criminal solicitation—should have sent Trump to prison.

Instead, Trump became president again, pardoned his co-conspirators, and watched the last case against him die.

Today—November 26, 2025—Trump posted on social media calling the dismissal a triumph of “LAW and JUSTICE.”

The truth is the opposite. Trump allegedly tried to steal Georgia’s election. The evidence was strong. The case was real.

But Trump will never face trial. Not because he’s innocent. Because Fani Willis made a terrible decision, the system couldn’t recover, and Trump won the presidency.

The rule of law requires that evidence matters, crimes have consequences, and no one is above accountability.

In Georgia, all three principles failed.

And Trump—once again—walked free.
