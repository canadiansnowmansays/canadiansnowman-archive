The Great Energy Challenge

“The Price of Not Building”

Essay #21: How Decades of EV Hesitation, One Killed Tax Credit, and a Deliberate Policy Unravelling Left America Paying an Iran Tax at Every Gas Station in the Country

March 14, 2026

Creator: Justin L. Stewart | Credit: News Enterprises Inc Commissioned by The Times

The price of a gallon of regular gasoline at a Chevron station in downtown Los Angeles this week was $8.38. People were stopping to photograph the sign.

The national average, as of Saturday, was $3.68 — up from $2.94 a month ago, a 25 percent increase in roughly two weeks. The Iran war is the immediate cause. But the immediate cause has a longer history than the war. The pain at the pump that tens of millions of Americans are absorbing right now was not inevitable. It was built. Over decades, through political choices, policy reversals, and a sustained national commitment to keeping the country dependent on exactly the commodity whose supply chain just got destroyed.

This essay is about what those choices cost.

I. The Structural Exposure

The United States is, by some measures, better positioned to absorb an oil shock than it was fifty years ago. Lydia DePillis documented this carefully in the New York Times this week. The US economy is predominantly services now — 114 million private services sector jobs against 21 million in goods-producing industries. Services require far less energy per dollar of output than manufacturing. The average new light-duty vehicle gets 28 miles per gallon, up from 13 in 1975. Economists at Wells Fargo estimate that a sustained 50 percent oil price rise would have roughly twice the impact today that it would have had in the 1980s — meaning the current shock, devastating as it is, would have been catastrophic in the era before efficiency gains.

The United States is also, now, the world’s largest oil and gas producer. The shale revolution added roughly one percent to GDP and fundamentally altered the global supply picture. In 1973, an Arab embargo could weaponize the United States’ dependency directly. Today, the country exports more petroleum than it imports.

And yet: gas is $3.68 nationally, trending toward $4. Mortgages are rising. Diesel is up nearly 30 percent. Fertilizer for spring planting is sitting on ships that cannot move through a strait the United States itself closed. The structural improvements are real. The exposure that remains is also real. The question this essay asks is why that exposure is so much larger than it needed to be — and who made it that way.

II. What the Drillers Are Not Doing

The standard assumption embedded in American energy security thinking has been that when prices rise, US producers will pump more oil, offsetting global supply shocks. That assumption is no longer operative.

DePillis identified why. The fracking boom was economically ruinous for investors. Companies borrowed heavily to build extraction infrastructure, oil prices collapsed in 2014 and again in 2020, many went bankrupt, and the survivors learned the lesson that Wall Street had been trying to teach them for a decade: don’t invest heavily in pumping more just because prices rise. Especially when you’re not sure the prices will hold.

“I don’t see much happening from the production side in order to mitigate the effects for the US economy,” said Christiane Baumeister, an economics professor at the University of Notre Dame who studies oil markets. “Companies just prioritize delivering returns to shareholders. I think they would rather take advantage of the current situation to increase profits rather than investing that back into expanding output.”

The number of US oil rigs actively pumping is down seven percent from this time last year. Part of the reason: steel and aluminum tariffs — imposed by this administration — have raised the cost of the pipes and valves needed for expansion. The industry that is supposed to be America’s buffer against oil shocks is not expanding because its investors have been burned before and because the policies designed to lower energy costs have raised the cost of the infrastructure needed to produce energy.

Clark Williams-Derry of the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis put the industry’s business model with painful clarity: “The oil and gas industry’s financial strategy has been ‘pray for war,’ because those are the conditions under which they make money. They have to have a big price spike every few years literally to make ends meet.” The war in Iran is, for the oil sector, a financial event they have been waiting for. For the 363,000 people the sector employs — 0.2 percent of total US employment — the profits will not be widely shared. For the Americans who cannot afford to own electric vehicles and have no choice but to fill their tanks, the spike arrives as a tax.

The lower your income, the heavier that tax. DePillis documented the distribution: lower-income households spend a disproportionate share of their budgets on gasoline and electricity. Electric vehicles — which could insulate their owners from exactly this kind of shock — cost more upfront and are disproportionately owned by affluent, well-educated buyers. The Trump administration, in the meantime, is rolling back fuel efficiency standards for appliances and fuel economy rules for vehicles. The people least able to absorb this shock are being made more dependent on the commodity causing it.

III. Only 8 Percent

In the United States, just under 8 percent of new cars sold last year were electric. In Europe, the figure is roughly 20 percent. In China, it is one in three.

Tim Levin in The Atlantic this week identified what this gap represents in the current moment: “America could have entered this fiasco with a better hand. The current spike in gas prices — and whatever comes next — could have been much more manageable if more people had electric vehicles in their driveway.”

Americans who do own EVs are, as of this week, smug on social media and right to be. A Chihuahua dancing in front of a Tesla while gas prices scroll upward captures the arithmetic precisely. Charging at home costs roughly $5 to drive 100 miles at current electricity rates. To match that in a gasoline-powered car at today’s prices would require a vehicle that achieves 70 miles per gallon. No such vehicle exists for sale in the United States.

The reasons why so few Americans own EVs are partly structural — upfront cost, charging infrastructure, range anxiety — and partly manufactured. The $7,500 federal EV tax credit, created to reduce the price gap between electric and gasoline vehicles, was killed by the Republican-controlled Congress as part of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. After the credit expired at the end of September, the all-electric share of new car sales dropped by roughly half and has not recovered. Regulations requiring automakers to sell cleaner vehicles — which pushed manufacturers toward EVs — have been dismantled. Freed from regulatory requirements and facing declining demand, automakers have seized the opportunity to retreat. This week Honda became the latest to join the trend, announcing the cancellation of three planned EV models before production had even begun.

This is the policy architecture of exposure. The administration that launched the war causing the gas price spike is the same administration that killed the mechanism that would have reduced American vulnerability to precisely this kind of shock. The connection is not subtle. It is direct, causal, and now visible in the price on every gas station sign in the country.

IV. What China Built Instead

In 2004, a Chinese state-owned newspaper published an editorial that has aged into prophecy. “It is no exaggeration to say,” it read, “that whoever controls the Strait of Malacca will also have a stranglehold on the energy route of China.”

The Strait of Malacca, the narrow waterway between Indonesia and Malaysia, carried 25 percent of global trade at the time and was China’s primary oil import corridor. Chinese leader Hu Jintao was preoccupied with it — the same way energy strategists have always been preoccupied with single points of catastrophic vulnerability. His response was to treat energy security as an existential national project and begin building a way out.

Twenty-two years later, the Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed, carrying less than ten percent of its prewar oil volume. The global economy is absorbing the largest oil supply disruption in history. China, which imports roughly half its seaborne crude from the Middle East, is watching this unfold from a position of relative insulation that it spent two decades and hundreds of billions of dollars deliberately constructing.

Alexandra Stevenson and Murphy Zhao documented the dimensions of that insulation in the New York Times this week. Chinese demand for refined oil, gasoline, and diesel fell last year — the second consecutive annual decline. China sold more electric vehicles in 2025 than the rest of the world combined. Half of all new cars sold in China are electric or hybrid; one-third of new heavy-duty trucks are purely electric. The government poured $300 billion into a campaign to develop domestic high-tech capacity and reduce foreign energy dependency. Direct subsidies from 2016 to 2022 alone totalled more than $5 billion for EV automakers. BYD, the beneficiary of the largest share, last year overtook Tesla as the world’s largest electric vehicle seller.

Chinese demand for refined oil, gasoline and diesel fell last year as many people in China have switched to electric vehicles.Credit...Gilles Sabrié for The New York Times

The result: China has a buffer. While other Asian economies have adopted four-day workweeks and closed universities to conserve energy, China is managing. Michal Meidan of the Oxford Institute for Energy Studies: “China has a bit of a buffer and cushion compared to other countries. The supply outages and price hikes do not significantly impact the running of its economy.”

China is still vulnerable at the margins — gas station queues formed in dozens of cities when oil hit $120, and the government posted its largest retail fuel price increase in four years. But the scale of insulation is structural. It was built over decades. It was built deliberately. And it was built precisely because Chinese leadership looked at a narrow waterway and decided to build a country that could not be strangled through it.

Two days after the United States and Israel attacked Iran, the People’s Daily ran the story on its front page. “My country has built the world’s largest renewable energy system.” It was not commentary on the war. It was a statement of position.

V. The Canadian Calculation

Chinese automakers are preparing to enter the Canadian electric vehicle (EV) market, with several major brands aiming to begin sales before the end of 2026. BYD, Chery, and Geely are all reportedly working through regulatory approvals, dealer partnerships, and certification requirements as they position themselves to launch consumer vehicles in Canada for the first time.

On January 16, Prime Minister Mark Carney stood in Beijing and announced that Canada was breaking with Washington on Chinese electric vehicles.

The deal he brought home: up to 49,000 Chinese-built EVs per year into Canada at a tariff of 6.1 percent — down from the 100 percent rate Canada had imposed in October 2024, following the United States’ lead. Permits began issuing on March 1. The quota rises to 70,000 vehicles annually by 2030, with a requirement that at least half be priced under $35,000 Canadian — roughly $25,600 US. In exchange, China cut its tariff on Canadian canola from 85 percent to 15 percent and lifted restrictions on Canadian lobster, crab, and peas.

Carney’s explanation for the reversal was direct. He called China “more predictable” than Washington. He was not being provocative. He was describing observable reality: Canadian EV registrations had fallen by roughly one-third in the first three quarters of 2025, partly because the 100 percent tariff had removed the affordable options from the market. The deal was designed to reverse that decline.

This is the sharpest available illustration of where the two countries now stand. The United States killed its $7,500 EV tax credit, dismantled fuel efficiency standards, and watched Honda cancel three planned EV models — all while gas prices climbed toward four dollars. Canada, in the same fortnight that Americans began photographing $8.38 gas signs in Los Angeles, opened its border to Chinese EVs at 6.1 percent because it looked at the same oil shock and decided affordability mattered more than alignment with American industrial policy.

The risks are real and debated. Questions about industrial sovereignty, cold-weather battery performance, and US officials’ warnings that Chinese-built vehicles could face restrictions at the border — potentially complicating cross-border travel for Canadian owners — all deserve serious consideration. The Policy Options Institute warned that the deal, without binding domestic investment requirements, could create dependence on an external industrial system over which Canada has limited leverage.

But the direction of the choice is unambiguous. One country, facing the largest oil supply disruption in history, is accelerating its exposure to oil price shocks. The other is trying to reduce it. From a Canadian outsider’s vantage point, the contrast does not require elaboration.

VI. The Lesson That Is Not Being Learned

This series has, from its earliest essays, returned to a central observation: that the energy transition is not primarily an environmental question. It is a security question. An economic question. A question about whether a country chooses to control its own energy future or to remain dependent on the geopolitical stability of a region it cannot control and, as of February 28, has chosen to destabilize.

The 1973 Arab oil embargo produced, eventually, the fuel efficiency revolution, the shale boom, and the conservation movement that made the United States less energy-intensive than it was. The lesson of 1973 was incorporated, imperfectly but genuinely, into American policy for decades. The current shock is producing the opposite policy response: the administration is killing EV incentives, rolling back efficiency standards, and celebrating the oil industry whose price spikes are now landing on the least economically resilient Americans.

Meg Jacobs, the historian whose work on the 1970s energy crisis has informed several essays in this series, observed in her recent writing on the current moment that oil shocks produce unintended consequences their architects never envision. The 1973 embargo ultimately broke the Arab oil monopoly it was designed to protect. Oil prices collapsed in 1986. The shock created the very conditions that ended the strategic leverage it was meant to exercise.

The unintended consequence of this shock may be different in character but similar in direction. GasBuddy daily active users have more than doubled in ten days. Car-buying site Edmunds reports a “slight uptick” in shoppers considering hybrids and EVs. The mathematics of home charging versus pump prices — $5 per 100 miles versus the equivalent of 70 miles per gallon — are now appearing in mainstream coverage. The pain that was supposed to remain abstract has arrived at every gas station sign in the country.

The administration’s EV policy has created demand for the product it is suppressing. Honda is canceling EVs at the moment the market has its most compelling case in years. If prices hold at current levels — and there is no mechanism in place to bring them down quickly — the Iran shock may become the most effective EV advertisement in American history, deployed entirely against the intentions of the people who caused it.

That would be some comfort. It is not enough.

VII. The Bill

There is a number worth keeping in front of us as gas prices rise and the full accounting of this war accumulates.

The United States has spent fifteen days, as of this writing, conducting the largest oil supply disruption in history — a disruption it caused, in a region its own forces have closed. The cost of that disruption is being paid, in real time, at gas stations, at grocery stores, on farm equipment, in mortgage applications, in airline ticket prices, and in the fertilizer sitting in ships that cannot move.

The technology that could have reduced American exposure to this disruption — electric vehicles — covers 8 percent of new car sales in the United States, against 33 percent in China and 20 percent in Europe. The policy mechanism most directly responsible for expanding that coverage was killed four months ago. The efficiency standards that would have accelerated the transition are being dismantled. The automakers who were building the vehicles are canceling them.

This is not bad luck. It is not the unforeseeable consequence of neutral decisions. It is the compounded result of a political choice, made consistently over the past year, to keep the United States dependent on the commodity whose supply chain is now on fire.

The people paying the bill are not the people who made that choice.

They are the ones stopping to photograph the sign.
