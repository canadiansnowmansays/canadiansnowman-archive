December 21-23 Addendum

A Pattern Accelerates

December 23, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 23, 2025

This addendum documents developments from December 21-23, 2025, following the publication of Parts 14 and 15 of The Epstein Files series.

I apologize for the length of this update, but the last few days have produced developments significant enough to warrant comprehensive documentation: a third major file release revealing what federal prosecutors knew in 2020 about Trump’s flights with Epstein, concrete legal action from both chambers of Congress, powerful testimony from survivors about the Justice Department’s failures, and a New York Times investigation documenting Trump’s parties with 14-year-old girls at Mar-a-Lago. Each element matters. Each deserves proper context. Together, they show a pattern accelerating toward enforcement.

The Third Release: What Prosecutors Knew in 2020

Late Monday night, December 22, the Department of Justice released its third batch of Epstein files—more than 11,000 documents totaling approximately 30,000 pages. The release was briefly posted Monday afternoon, then removed around 8 p.m. without explanation, before being restored Tuesday morning.

Among the documents: a January 8, 2020 email from an assistant U.S. attorney in the Southern District of New York that reveals what federal prosecutors knew about Donald Trump’s flights on Jeffrey Epstein’s private jet during the investigation of Ghislaine Maxwell.

The email’s subject line and recipient remain redacted. But its message is clear:

“For your situational awareness, wanted to let you know that the flight records we received yesterday reflect that Donald Trump traveled on Epstein’s private jet many more times than previously has been reported (or that we were aware), including during the period we would expect to charge in a Maxwell case.”

The prosecutor then details what the flight logs show:

“In particular, he is listed as a passenger on at least eight flights between 1993 and 1996, including at least four flights on which Maxwell was also present. He is listed as having traveled with, among others and at various times, Marla Maples, his daughter Tiffany, and his son Eric.”

Then come the most significant entries:

“On one flight in 1993, he and Epstein are the only two listed passengers; on another, the only three passengers are Epstein, Trump, and then-20-year-old [REDACTED]. On two other flights, two of the passengers, respectively, were women who would be possible witnesses in a Maxwell case.”

The email concludes: “We’ve just finished reviewing the full records (more than 100 pages of very small script) and didn’t want any of this to be a surprise down the road.”

Federal prosecutors in January 2020 knew this information was explosive. They documented it carefully. They flagged it for their colleagues. And they made clear they expected it might become relevant “down the road.”

That road has now arrived.

The Monday night release also included a November 29, 2021 federal subpoena sent to Mar-a-Lago demanding “Any and all employment records relating to [REDACTED]” in connection with the Maxwell prosecution. The Washington Post reports the documents include “several tips that were collected by the FBI about Trump’s involvement with Epstein and parties at their properties in the early 2000s,” though it remains unclear whether those tips were ever investigated.

Among those tips: Data Set 8 contains a heavily redacted report describing allegations from an unidentified individual. According to the redacted document, a limousine driver stated he picked up Trump in 1995 and drove him to DFW Airport. The driver reported Trump was on his cell phone during the drive, “continuously stated the name ‘Jeffrey,’” and became “immediately stone cold” when the person on the phone mentioned “abusing some girl.” The document states the driver was unsure who Trump was referencing.

The same data set includes an allegation that Trump and Epstein raped an individual whose name is redacted. According to the document, the individual expressed fear about reporting to police, saying “I can’t they will kill me.” On Christmas Day, an unidentified person contacted the individual and told her she had “done good.”

The document states that on January 20, 2000, someone reached out saying the individual was dead and had been found with her head “blown off” in Altafer, Oklahoma. Officers on the scene stated “there was no way it was suicide,” according to the document. The coroner ruled it a suicide. The document later states the individual “committed suicide because [she] had gotten cocaine from a Mexican drug cartel.”

The document’s author states they feel “the murder is a cover for Ghislaine.”

The extensive redactions make it impossible to verify the identities of those involved, the source of the allegations, or whether the FBI conducted any investigation into the claims. The document provides no context about when it was submitted to the FBI or what action, if any, was taken.

On Tuesday morning, the Department of Justice posted a statement on social media claiming some documents in Monday’s release contained “untrue and sensationalist claims made against President Trump that were submitted to the FBI right before the 2020 election.” The statement emphasized: “To be clear: the claims are unfounded and false, and if they had a shred of credibility, they certainly would have been weaponized against President Trump already.”

The timing of this damage control—issued the morning after both the prosecutor’s email about the flight logs and the Data Set 8 allegations became public—speaks for itself.

Legal Escalation: Two Paths to Enforcement

On Monday, December 22, Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer announced he will introduce a resolution directing the Senate to initiate legal action against the Department of Justice for violating the Epstein Files Transparency Act.

“The law Congress passed is crystal clear: release the Epstein files in full so Americans can see the truth,” Schumer said in a statement. “Instead, the Trump Department of Justice dumped redactions and withheld the evidence—that breaks the law.”

Schumer will force consideration of the resolution when the Senate reconvenes on Monday, January 5. The measure would direct the Senate to file or join lawsuits aimed at forcing DOJ compliance. It will likely require unanimous consent to pass—the same standard that allowed the original Epstein Files Transparency Act to pass the Senate unanimously last month.

But Schumer’s resolution represents only one enforcement path. Representatives Thomas Massie (R-KY) and Ro Khanna (D-CA)—the bipartisan authors of the transparency law—announced over the weekend they are drafting “inherent contempt” charges against Attorney General Pam Bondi.

Inherent contempt is an older, more direct enforcement mechanism. It allows Congress to rely on its own constitutional authority to detain and imprison a contemnor until they comply with congressional demands—or, more commonly in modern practice, to impose daily monetary fines.

“The quickest way, and I think most expeditious way, to get justice for these victims is to bring inherent contempt against Pam Bondi,” Massie said on CBS’s “Face the Nation” Sunday.

Khanna, appearing on the same program, explained the practical effect: “We’re building a bipartisan coalition, and it would fine Pam Bondi for every day that she’s not releasing these documents.”

The inherent contempt resolution will be introduced as “privileged” when the House returns in January, forcing a floor vote within two legislative days. Unlike traditional contempt referrals that require cooperation from the Justice Department, inherent contempt allows the House to act independently. And unlike Schumer’s Senate resolution, it requires only a House majority—not unanimous consent.

Both mechanisms share one characteristic: they convert the Department of Justice’s violation from a political scandal into a legal confrontation with concrete consequences.

“Protecting the Wrong People”: Survivors Speak Out

On Monday, 18 Epstein survivors issued an open letter to Congress. Their message was direct:

“The public received a fraction of the files, and what we received was riddled with abnormal and extreme redactions with no explanation. At the same time, numerous victim identities were left unredacted, causing real and immediate harm.”

The survivors called for “immediate congressional oversight, including hearings, formal demands for compliance, and legal action, to ensure the Department of Justice fulfills its legal obligations.”

The same night, two of those survivors—Jenna Lisa Jones and Marina Lacerda—appeared on MSNBC’s “The Last Word with Lawrence O’Donnell” to describe what they found when they examined the released files.

Both women met Jeffrey Epstein when they were 14 years old. Both have chosen to speak publicly about what happened to them. Both spent the weekend reading through the Department of Justice’s Friday release.

Jenna Lisa Jones described the experience:

“I found numerous girls that I was friends with that I didn’t even know were victims of Jeffrey Epstein... I cried last night reading their names because I know how hard this is. And to not have a choice whether your name is released or not is just—it’s a crime and people need to be punished for it now.”

She explained the context many outside Palm Beach might not understand: “It was such a hush hush thing that your group of friends, you thought it was only your group of friends. So seeing some of these girls’ names that I had went to high school with and later on even once we had kids—it’s just really horrific to see because they didn’t ask for this and they’re getting thrown into this circus act.”

Some of the girls whose names appeared were women she’d known for years without ever knowing they shared this history: “It’s just something we didn’t know about each other and now I know that about them and I feel so sad because they didn’t ask for this.”

Marina Lacerda was more direct in her assessment:

“I think all of us survivors are feeling very frustrated that the DOJ did not do their job correctly. I mean, they had one job here. It was to redact the victims’ names and it seems like they’re protecting the wrong people here.”

When asked if the improper redactions felt intentional, Lacerda didn’t hesitate: “It almost feels like they did this on purpose... This has become a circus act. It is something that the whole world is waiting for at this point. And here we are again protecting the wealthy elite men. It is ridiculous at this point. And we are tired of it. But we will not stop fighting for this.”

The survivors’ testimony makes clear that the Department of Justice managed to accomplish something remarkable in its botched release: it simultaneously over-redacted information the public has a legal right to see while under-redacting the identities of victims the law specifically requires be protected.

Jenna Lisa Jones put the failure in its proper context:

“This is not just retraumatizing of what happened to us. We’re being retraumatized by our government. And that speaks volumes because we’re supposed to be the most wonderful place. This is the United States of America. We should be taking and protecting our children, and this seems like this is the hardest thing to do.”

The DOJ’s response to these concerns? On Tuesday morning, the same social media statement that dismissed “untrue and sensationalist claims” about Trump also insisted the department was releasing documents “with the legally required protections for Epstein’s victims.”

The survivors who have actually read the files know otherwise.

What The New York Times Found: Trump’s 14-Year-Old Parties

The same day DOJ released its incomplete files, The New York Times published the results of a months-long investigation into Donald Trump’s relationship with Jeffrey Epstein. The investigation, based on interviews with more than 30 former Epstein employees and review of new documents including previously unpublished diaries, reveals what the Times describes as an “intense and complex relationship” spanning nearly two decades.

Among the investigation’s most disturbing findings: Donald Trump held parties at Mar-a-Lago in the 1990s where 14-year-old girls were present, served alcohol, and warned away from Trump himself.

Tina Davis was 14 years old in late 1994 when her Ford Models booker instructed her to “get dressed up and attend a Mar-a-Lago party,” according to her mother, Sandra Coleman, who accompanied her daughter to Florida. The booker told Davis to “dress sexy.”

Coleman described what happened when they arrived with eight or nine other models on a bus: “All the girls were really young. Some of them could have been in training bras.”

Davis was immediately handed a glass of champagne. Coleman took it away. Waiters kept offering more. Each time one of the middle-aged men at the party approached her daughter, Coleman would walk over and introduce herself as Davis’s mother.

Then came a trip to the bathroom, where they encountered Trump’s wife, Marla Maples, whom they had met earlier. Coleman described the moment:

“Marla Maples clasped her hands and looked her in the eye. ‘Whatever you do, do not let her around any of these men and especially my husband,’ she told Miss Coleman. ‘Protect her.’”

Maples, in a statement to the Times, denied making the specific comment: “I would always protect young women in any way I could, but I am sure I didn’t specifically say that about my daughter’s father.”

Maples received a substantial financial settlement in her divorce from Trump. The Times reports the couple separated in 1997 and divorced in 1999.

The Times investigation documents other Trump-Epstein parties with troubling patterns:

A woman who said Epstein trafficked her in the late 1990s and early 2000s recalled attending at least half a dozen Mar-a-Lago parties beginning when she was 17. The invitations she received, according to one she shared with the Times, were “to be Donald Trump’s guest at Mar-a-Lago.” The parties were “open bar and no one checked IDs.” She said Trump “was always all over them.”

A 20-year-old model who attended a Trump party at Mar-a-Lago told the Times: “I had a glass of champagne and I immediately felt not well. I thought my drink had been spiked.”

One woman told the Times that Epstein coerced her into attending four parties at Epstein’s home. Trump attended all four. At two of them, she said Epstein directed her to have sex with other male guests.

George Houraney, a Florida businessman, told the Times that Trump asked him to organize a “calendar girl competition” at Mar-a-Lago in 1992. “I arranged to have some contestants fly in,” Houraney said. “At the very first party, I said, ‘Who’s coming tonight? I have 28 girls coming.’ It was him and Epstein.”

Houraney recalled his surprise: “I said, ‘Donald, this is supposed to be a party with V.I.P.s. You’re telling me it’s you and Epstein?’”

The Times report notes that at least six women who have accused Epstein or Maxwell of sexual abuse were introduced to Trump over the years. None of them have accused Trump of inappropriate behavior toward themselves.

But the investigation reveals the depth of the Trump-Epstein friendship during the years when Epstein’s abuse was most active. Former Epstein employees recalled Trump being “a constant presence in Epstein’s life” in the mid-1990s. One employee said Epstein would put Trump on speakerphone when the office cleared out, and Trump “seemed to enjoy regaling Mr. Epstein with tales of his sexual exploits.”

According to the employee, Epstein appeared to take pleasure in how uncomfortable these conversations made her. On one call, the two men allegedly discussed “how much pubic hair a particular woman had, and whether there was enough for Mr. Epstein to floss his teeth with.” On another call, Trump reportedly described having sex with a woman on a pool table.

The Times investigation emphasizes a point that matters: “The Epstein controversy has shaken Mr. Trump’s iron hold on his base like no other.”

The evidence comes from Trump’s own behavior. In November, facing what the Times describes as “an almost unheard-of revolt among Republican lawmakers,” Trump reversed course and signed the legislation requiring release of Epstein-related documents. Trump had previously ordered his Justice Department to investigate Bill Clinton’s ties to Epstein while insisting his own relationship was minimal.

The Times found otherwise. The investigation documents Trump praising Epstein in 2002 (”I’ve known Jeff for 15 years. Terrific guy. He’s a lot of fun to be with. It is even said that he likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side”), then systematically distancing himself as Epstein’s crimes became public.

By 2015, Trump’s lawyer told BuzzFeed that Epstein was merely “one of thousands of people who has visited Mar-a-Lago.” By 2016, the same lawyer went further: “There was no relationship between Jeffrey Epstein and Donald Trump.”

The flight logs released Monday—showing Trump as a passenger on Epstein’s plane at least eight times between 1993 and 1996, including one flight with only Trump, Epstein, and a 20-year-old woman—demonstrate that this statement was false.

A Study in Contrasts: Howard Lutnick vs. Donald Trump

When Donald Trump said Monday that “everybody was friendly with this guy,” he was lying.

Howard Lutnick, Trump’s Commerce Secretary, lived next door to Jeffrey Epstein in Manhattan. On Lawrence O’Donnell’s show Monday night, a clip from an earlier interview captured Lutnick describing exactly one encounter with his neighbor:

“My wife is standing here. So she looks at me and I look at her and we say, ‘I’m sorry. We have to go.’ And we left. And in the six or eight steps it takes to get from his house to my house, my wife and I decided that I will never be in the room with that disgusting person ever again.”

Lutnick was clear about what followed: “I was never in the room with him socially, for business, or even philanthropy. If that guy was there, I wasn’t going, because he’s gross.”

Lutnick and his wife Allison had moved into their Upper East Side townhouse at 11 East 71st Street in 2005. Epstein’s notorious mansion was next door at 9 East 71st Street. After one visit—where Epstein showed them a massage table “in the middle of the room” and made a disturbing comment about getting “the right kind of massage” daily—the Lutnicks made their decision.

One encounter. One conversation. One immediate decision to have nothing more to do with Jeffrey Epstein.

Compare that to Donald Trump’s behavior during the same years:

At least eight flights on Epstein’s private jet between 1993 and 1996, including flights with Maxwell present and flights with women who would become witnesses in Maxwell’s prosecution.

Regular phone conversations—at least three times a week in the mid-to-late 1990s, according to Epstein’s assistant.

Parties at each other’s homes in New York and Florida, attended by both men over nearly two decades.

Visits to each other’s offices in Manhattan.

The 1992 “calendar girl competition” where Epstein was the only other male guest.

The 1993 party for 28 women where the event planner recalled: “At the very first party, I said, ‘Who’s coming tonight? I have 28 girls coming.’ It was him and Epstein.”

A 2002 interview where Trump praised Epstein as “a terrific guy” who “likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side.”

Email records and documents showing Epstein boasted about “giving” Trump a 20-year-old woman.

Everybody was not friendly with Jeffrey Epstein. Howard Lutnick refused to be in a room with him after a single encounter.

Donald Trump spent nearly 15 years as perhaps Epstein’s closest friend.

Bill Clinton’s Reversal: “We Need No Such Protection”

Bill Clinton stands with Jeffrey Epstein in an image released by the Department of Justice on December 19 © US Department of Justice/Reuters

The Friday release of Epstein files appeared to focus significantly on material connected to Bill Clinton—a strategic choice at a moment when Republicans have fought to shift public attention away from Epstein’s friendship with Trump. Many of the photographs included in the files were of the former president, including an image of Clinton reclining in a hot tub with a person whose face had been blacked out.

The White House immediately sought to weaponize the release. Abigail Jackson, a White House spokeswoman, posted the hot tub photo on social media with a message clearly aimed at Democrats: “We did see something. Just not what you wanted.”

On Friday, Clinton spokesman Angel Ureña pushed back: “The White House hasn’t been hiding these files for months only to dump them late on a Friday to protect Bill Clinton. This is about shielding themselves from what comes next, or from what they’ll try and hide forever.”

By Monday, Clinton’s position had evolved from defensive to offensive. Ureña issued a new statement calling for the full release of all Epstein files related to the former president.

Writing on social media, Ureña said: “The way the files are being released makes one thing clear: someone or something is being protected. We do not know whom, what or why. We need no such protection. Accordingly, we call on President Trump to direct Attorney General Pam Bondi to immediately release any remaining materials referring to, mentioning or containing a photograph of Bill Clinton.”

Clinton’s challenge to Trump is strategic. Clinton has long maintained he knew nothing of Epstein’s crimes despite flying on Epstein’s plane multiple times in 2002 and 2003 for Clinton Foundation trips. But by calling for immediate and complete release of all Clinton-related files, Clinton’s team removes any shield the administration might claim to be providing—and challenges Trump to prove he isn’t selectively withholding material.

The challenge went unanswered Monday. Trump, speaking at Mar-a-Lago earlier in the day, had tried deflection: “This whole thing with Epstein is a way of trying to deflect from the tremendous success that the Republican Party has.”

Trump also complained about the release harming innocent people: “I don’t like the pictures of Bill Clinton being shown. I think it’s a terrible thing. A lot of people are very angry that pictures are being released of other people that really had nothing to do with Epstein. But they’re in a picture with him because he was at a party, and you ruined a reputation of somebody.”

This from the president who ordered his Justice Department to investigate Clinton’s “involvement and relationship” with Epstein, and whose attorney general initially promised the files would reveal a shocking “client list.”

Todd Blanche, the deputy attorney general, acknowledged that a small number of documents related to the files on Jeffrey Epstein were removed from the online collection because of concerns raised by victims. Credit...Kenny Holston/The New York Times

Asked on NBC’s “Meet the Press” Sunday whether Clinton is now under investigation, Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche refused to answer: “I will never talk about ongoing criminal investigations, so I am not going to address that question.”

The contrast between Trump’s public complaints about photos damaging reputations and his private orders to investigate Clinton captures the accommodation psychology at its clearest: use investigations as weapons against others while claiming victimization when the same scrutiny might apply to yourself.

The Pattern Holds

President Donald Trump takes questions during a press conference at Mar-a-Lago in Palm Beach, Florida, on Dec. 22. Photographer: Tasos Katopodis/Getty Images

This addendum documents 72 hours in a pattern that now spans eleven months. The pattern documented in Parts 1-15 of this series continues: Information manipulation. Strategic investigations as shields. Weaponizing DOJ against political opponents while claiming persecution when scrutiny applies to Trump. Threatening Congress while exploiting the law’s lack of enforcement mechanisms. Making strategic retreats—partial compliance, delete-and-restore cycles—while running out the clock through rolling releases with no clear timeline.

And now: active post-release censorship, improper redactions both excessive and inadequate, survivor retraumatization by government, and mounting bipartisan legal action to force compliance.

The Times investigation reveals what Trump knew, when he knew it, and how long he maintained his friendship with Epstein during the years of Epstein’s most active abuse. The prosecutor’s email from January 2020 shows what federal investigators knew about Trump’s travel with Epstein. The survivors’ testimony documents the human cost of DOJ’s failures.

And Trump’s response Monday—”This whole thing with Epstein is a way of trying to deflect from the tremendous success that the Republican Party has”—reveals how he intends to weather what comes next.

Lawrence O’Donnell, closing Monday night’s program, reminded viewers of an important difference between now and Watergate:

“Everyone working for Donald Trump knows that they do not have the same immunity that the Supreme Court gave to Donald Trump. And everyone working for Donald Trump should know that Richard Nixon’s White House chief of staff, his White House counsel, his attorney general, and dozens of other people working for Richard Nixon were convicted of crimes and sent to prison.”

Trump can pardon his subordinates. He cannot erase the documentary evidence of what federal prosecutors knew in 2020, what The New York Times documented in its investigation, or what the flight logs show about his eight trips on Epstein’s plane between 1993 and 1996.

Congress reconvenes January 5. The enforcement mechanisms are now in motion. The survivors are not stopping. And the files—all of them—are supposed to be public under federal law.

The test continues.
