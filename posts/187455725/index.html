When Democracy Fights Back

Nationalize the Vote

Part 11: Trump’s Endgame for 2026 — and the States That Refuse to Let It Happen

February 11, 2026

In Part 9, we documented the quiet construction of a national voter database — the DOJ demanding personal data from 47 states and suing 24 that refused. In Part 10, we watched the dramatic escalation in Fulton County — 700 boxes of ballots seized, the Director of National Intelligence lurking in the parking lot, the president on speakerphone with the agents investigating his personal grievance.

Now comes the question that matters: what is all of this for?

The answer arrived the same week as the Fulton County raid, spoken aloud by the president himself, amplified by his loudest allies, and laid out in language so plain that it barely requires interpretation.

They’re coming for the 2026 midterms.

“Nationalize the Voting”

President Donald Trump speaking about nationalizing elections, as he did in early February 2026, expressing frustration over potential midterm losses.

On Monday, February 3, 2026 — six days after the Fulton County raid — President Trump appeared on a radio program and said what his actions had already been telegraphing for months.

“Republicans should say, we want to take over. We should take over the voting in at least many — 15 places. The Republicans ought to nationalize the voting.”

Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer responded within hours: “Does Donald Trump need a copy of the Constitution? What he’s saying is outlandishly illegal. Once again, the president is talking no differently than a dictator.”

On Tuesday, White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt tried to walk it back, claiming the president was referring to federal election legislation in Congress. By Wednesday, Trump walked it forward again, telling NBC News: “Look at some of the places — that horrible corruption on elections — and the federal government should not allow that. The federal government should get involved.”

By Thursday, in an interview with Reuters, Trump said something that should have led every newscast in the country: “We shouldn’t even have an election.” He was expressing frustration that his party could lose control of the House or Senate in this year’s midterms.

Let me repeat that. The President of the United States said “we shouldn’t even have an election” — and by Friday it was buried under the next news cycle.

This is what normalization looks like. A sitting president openly musing about cancelling elections, and the country barely flinching.

The Insurrection Act at the Ballot Box

If Trump’s comments were the policy statement, Steve Bannon provided the operational plan.

Bannon — pardoned felon, former White House chief strategist, and host of the War Room podcast — spent the first week of February laying out a vision for November 2026 that would have been unimaginable in any previous era of American politics.

“You’re damn right we’re gonna have ICE surround the polls come November,” Bannon told his listeners on Tuesday. “We’re not going to sit here and allow you to steal the country again.”

By Wednesday, he escalated: “President Trump has to nationalize the election. You’ve got to put — not just, I think, ICE — you’ve got to call up the 82nd and 101st Airborne on the Insurrection Act. You’ve got to get around every poll and make sure only people with IDs, people are actually registered to vote, and people that are United States citizens vote in this election. Full stop. We will not accept anything less.”

The 82nd Airborne. The Insurrection Act. At polling places.

Steve Bannon hosting his War Room podcast, where he called for deploying the 82nd Airborne and ICE at polling places under the Insurrection Act.

Let’s be precise about what Bannon is proposing. The Insurrection Act allows the president to deploy active-duty military forces domestically in limited circumstances, primarily to suppress insurrection or enforce federal law when state authorities cannot or will not act. It has been invoked sparingly — during the Civil Rights era, during the 1992 Los Angeles riots. It has never been used to place military forces at polling stations.

There is, in fact, a federal law — 18 U.S.C. § 593, enacted in 1948 — that makes it a crime for military personnel to interfere at polling places. Article I, Section 4 of the Constitution reserves election administration to the states. The Posse Comitatus Act restricts the use of federal military forces for domestic law enforcement.

Bannon is calling for something that is simultaneously unconstitutional, illegal under multiple statutes, and historically unprecedented. And the White House response was not to condemn it. Leavitt merely said the administration had “no plans” to send immigration enforcement to polling places — a carefully worded non-denial that said nothing about the military and left every door open for November.

Meanwhile, Cleta Mitchell — a Republican activist who advised Trump during his efforts to overturn the 2020 election and who helped draft the strategy for pressuring state officials — has argued that a federal election takeover would be legally possible after the president declares a national emergency based on a threat to the “sovereignty” of the country. In other words: manufacture a crisis, invoke emergency powers, deploy forces, control the vote.

This isn’t speculation about distant possibilities. This is the playbook being discussed, out loud, by people with the president’s ear, eight months before Americans go to the polls.

Soldiers from the 82nd Airborne Division, referenced by Bannon in his proposal for military presence at polls to enforce voter ID and citizenship checks.

Why November Matters

The urgency behind all of this has a simple explanation: the numbers.

Republicans hold a four-seat majority in the House of Representatives. The Cook Political Report projects roughly 20 swing seats in play. Democrats are favored to make significant gains. A Democratic House would mean subpoena power, oversight hearings, and the ability to block Trump’s legislative agenda for his final two years in office.

The Senate math is steeper for Democrats — they would need to flip four Republican-held seats — but it’s not impossible, particularly given the political headwinds the president’s party traditionally faces in midterms.

Trump knows this. He told Reuters he’s frustrated about the possibility of losing congressional control. His allies know it too. Some Republican campaign consultants have privately warned that the election integrity crusade could backfire by depressing their own base turnout — if your voters believe the system is rigged, why would they show up? — while energizing Democratic voters who feel their right to vote is under direct threat.

That political risk hasn’t stopped the campaign. If anything, it has accelerated it. The calculation appears to be that it’s better to build the infrastructure to contest unfavorable results than to risk accepting them.

Consider what’s already in place:

A national voter database with personal information from cooperating states, and lawsuits pending against 24 more. A Missouri prosecutor with nationwide jurisdiction investigating “election integrity” in multiple states. Seven hundred boxes of Georgia ballots in federal custody with a broken chain of custody. An intelligence agency investigating voting machines without evidence of foreign interference. A president who has told FBI agents personally that he wants results. And a White House working group that meets regularly to coordinate federal efforts on elections.

As Senator Mark Warner, Vice Chairman of the Senate Intelligence Committee, told reporters on Thursday: “It appears there may be a coordinated effort to try to interfere in the ‘26 midterms. They may even start to interfere in, prior to, in the primaries.”

Warner isn’t a bomb-thrower. He’s a former telecommunications executive and former Virginia governor who chooses his words with the precision of a man who knows what classified intelligence looks like. When he says “coordinated effort to interfere,” he’s not guessing.

A typical American polling place, amid discussions of potential military or federal interference in the 2026 elections.

The States Fight Back

Here is where this series earns its title.

Because the states are not rolling over. And critically, the resistance is bipartisan.

In Utah, Lieutenant Governor Deidre Henderson — a Republican who oversees the state’s elections — publicly confronted a White House official at the National Association of Secretaries of State conference in Washington last month. Her target was the DOJ’s pressure campaign on voter rolls, and she didn’t hold back.

“She’s pretty much slandered all of us,” Henderson said, referring to the administration’s insinuations that state officials aren’t doing their jobs. “And to me, that’s problematic to publicly claim that secretaries of state are not doing our jobs and the federal government has to do it for us. Not OK.”

In Kentucky, Republican Secretary of State Michael Adams quoted Ronald Reagan — “The federal government did not create the states; the states created the federal government” — and pointed to the Constitution’s clear delineation of election authority to the states.

In Michigan, Ottawa County Clerk Justin Roebuck, a Republican, said of the Fulton County search: “I think about what that would mean when it comes to defending our elections from any and all threats. And we would never want that threat to be our own federal government.”

That last sentence deserves a moment. A Republican county clerk in Michigan — not a Democratic senator, not a cable news commentator, not an activist — identifying the federal government as a potential threat to elections. That’s not partisan rhetoric. That’s a local official who runs elections for a living describing what he sees.

On the Democratic side, the resistance has been louder but no less substantive. Maine Secretary of State Shenna Bellows, who also serves on her state’s emergency response team for elections, delivered the most quotable verdict: “The trust has been absolutely destroyed. We can’t trust the federal government, and they are now adversaries of the states.”

Vermont Secretary of State Sarah Copeland Hanzas called Gabbard’s involvement “an absolute travesty and a waste of taxpayer dollars — chasing down some boogey monster, some phantom, some fiction from six years ago.”

Connecticut Secretary of State Stephanie Thomas captured the tonal shift: “Since last year, I would say that it feels like the approach is using a sledgehammer when a conversation might suffice.”

And the courts are holding. Federal judges in California and Oregon have already dismissed the DOJ’s voter data lawsuits, with the California judge noting that the government’s stated purpose was contradicted by its own officials’ statements. More rulings are pending. States are retaining outside legal counsel. At least one state is drafting legislation to make it harder for the federal government to access election materials. Fulton County has filed its own court challenge demanding the return of the seized ballots.

The Partnership That Was

To understand what’s been lost, you have to understand what existed before.

For decades, state election officials relied on the federal government for things that only a national government can provide: intelligence on foreign cyber threats, early warning systems for attacks on election infrastructure, coordination among states facing common adversaries. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency ran information-sharing programs and maintained an Election Day situation room where state officials could get real-time alerts about threats.

That infrastructure has been dismantled. CISA’s election security programs were shut down last year with no replacement. On Election Day 2024, there was no federal situation room for the first time in memory. Maine’s Bellows told the secretaries of state conference that her state learned about bomb threats in New Jersey “from public media, rather than the Sit Room, as would have happened in the past.”

The federal government eliminated the tools states need to defend against foreign threats to elections — then turned around and became the threat itself.

Attorney General Bondi and Director of National Intelligence Gabbard were both scheduled to speak at the secretaries of state conference. Neither showed up. The secretary of state officials had planned to press them for answers about the Fulton County raid. Instead, they got an email from a career FBI official inviting them to a routine coordination call — and multiple secretaries of state told The Atlantic they viewed even that email as a potential threat.

Some officials said they would attend the FBI’s scheduled February 25 meeting but would not speak, out of concern that their information could be turned against them. Others said they wouldn’t go at all.

This is the state of American election security in February 2026: the people who run elections are afraid to talk to the people who are supposed to help them run elections.

The View from Here

I’ve been writing about American politics from Toronto for 500+ essays now, and I’ve learned to be careful about the word “unprecedented.” Americans use it the way Canadians use “sorry” — reflexively, often without meaning it. But what’s happening to American election infrastructure right now is, in the most literal sense, without precedent.

No previous administration has demanded personal voter data from every state in the union. No previous DOJ has sued two dozen states simultaneously for refusing to share their citizens’ Social Security numbers. No Director of National Intelligence has ever appeared at a domestic FBI search, let alone patched the president through to the agents on speakerphone. No president has called for “nationalizing” elections while his allies demand paratroopers at polling stations.

And no previous president has said “we shouldn’t even have an election” and had anyone take it as anything other than a joke.

This series started with gerrymandering — the slow-motion rigging of district maps that both parties have practiced for generations. That was bad enough. What we’ve documented in Parts 9 through 11 is something qualitatively different. Gerrymandering manipulates which voters end up in which districts. The ballot grab threatens to determine whether those voters get to vote at all — and whether their votes, once cast, will be counted honestly by a government that has seized custody of the ballots.

The saving grace, if there is one, is the resistance. Republican election officials in Utah, Kentucky, and Michigan standing alongside Democrats in Maine, Vermont, and Connecticut. Federal judges dismissing bad-faith lawsuits. State legislatures drafting protective legislation. County officials going to court to demand their ballots back.

Democracy is fighting back. The question is whether it’s fighting back fast enough.

November is eight months away. The infrastructure for interference is already in place. The legal challenges are being filed. The rhetoric is escalating daily.

And somewhere, in a federal facility whose location no one will disclose, 700 boxes of Georgia ballots sit in the custody of people who have spent six years insisting those ballots were fraudulent. The most investigated election in American history isn’t being re-investigated because new evidence emerged. It’s being re-investigated because the president demands a different answer.

Whether the states, the courts, and ultimately the voters can hold the line will define not just the 2026 midterms, but whether American democracy survives this era intact.

As that anonymous Republican election official from Arizona told The Atlantic: “It’s a five-alarm fire.”

He’s right. And the fire trucks are being driven by the arsonists.
