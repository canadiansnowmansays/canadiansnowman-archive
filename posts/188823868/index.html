Retribution With Government Power

Volume 5: How Trump Turned the Federal Government Into a Weapon Against Its Own People (2025–Present)

March 18, 2026

A promotional graphic for a news series on Trump’s second term, emphasizing policy impacts from Illinois’ perspective. Source: Illinois Public Media (will.illinois.edu)

The previous volumes of this series traced a pattern across six decades — from Roy Cohn’s countersuit playbook in 1973, through the destruction of Atlantic City contractors, through the pardon loyalty tests of the first term, through the systematic purge of every Republican who voted for impeachment. Each stage represented an escalation. Each stage gave Trump access to more powerful weapons.

This volume documents the final escalation: the deployment of the entire federal government as a retribution machine.

When Donald Trump took office for his second term in January 2025, he had spent decades perfecting the art of destroying individuals. What he built in the year that followed was something fundamentally different — the capacity to destroy entire institutions, entire workforces, entire sectors of American life, not one person at a time but thousands at a stroke.

The numbers alone tell a story that should stop anyone who cares about democratic governance in their tracks: 317,000 federal employees gone in a single year. The largest reduction in the federal workforce since the end of World War II. Universities extorted into paying hundreds of millions of dollars. Disaster aid withheld from states whose governors had the wrong politics. Research grants cancelled across hundreds of institutions. And at the center of all of it, one consistent principle: submit or be destroyed.

The weapons changed. The instinct never did.

The Federal Workforce Purge

To understand the scale of what happened to the federal government in 2025, you need one comparison. In all four years of Trump’s first term, fewer than 600 federal employees were terminated through the formal process known as a reduction in force. In 2025 alone, 17,000 were.

But the RIF number dramatically understates the actual destruction. According to the Office of Personnel Management’s own accounting, released in December 2025, 317,000 federal employees left their jobs that year. The administration characterized the “overwhelming majority” as voluntary. The word voluntary, in this context, requires examination.

Federal workers protesting mass layoffs under the Trump administration, holding signs like “Fed Workers Stay Strong” and “Stop.” Source: Alaska Beacon (alaskabeacon.com)

In February 2025, two million federal employees received an email from OPM with a subject line borrowed from Elon Musk’s playbook at Twitter: “A Fork in the Road.” It offered a deferred resignation program — take a buyout, collect pay through September, and leave quietly. Approximately 154,000 employees accepted. A hundred thousand of them formally exited on September 30, 2025, the largest mass resignation in American history.

Were these departures voluntary? Employees described an environment of “fear” and “madness.” One Equal Employment Opportunity Commission staffer told reporters that the emails were “clearly designed to disturb and scare people.” A Department of Transportation worker said: “I am concerned we will all be mass fired. Teams would be hobbled and gutted. It would be like closing your eyes and randomly throwing acid on a flower garden.” Many employees said they didn’t want to leave but accepted the deal because they feared being laid off, forced to relocate, or stripped of civil service protections through Schedule F — a policy designed to reclassify up to 50,000 policy-related positions as at-will employees, fireable for any reason.

For those who stayed and didn’t take the deal, the retribution was more direct. In February, the administration fired more than 24,000 probationary employees in a single wave. The acting director of OPM, Charles Ezell, signed a directive instructing agencies to dismiss these workers and tell them their performance was inadequate — while explicitly instructing agencies to cite no evidence. Federal judges blocked many of these firings as likely illegal. The administration appealed. On July 8, 2025, the Supreme Court overrode the lower court orders and allowed the workforce reductions to continue.

The targets were not random. Agencies with the highest planned workforce reductions were also those with the highest percentages of women, minority, and Black employees. By June 2025, more than 300,000 Black women had left the labor force. Over the same period, there were job increases of 142,000 for white women and 365,000 for white men. Trump’s firings of senior military officers disproportionately targeted women, and sometimes came after conservative organizations flagged specific individuals for criticism.

Federal judge Susan Illston temporarily blocked one wave of firings in October 2025, citing public statements by OMB Director Russell Vought and Trump himself. Trump had said the cuts would only target “Democrat agencies.” Illston ruled the statements showed explicit political motives for the layoffs.

The Department of Education lost nearly half its workforce. The Institute of Education Sciences — which produces the statistics used to calculate Title I funding for schools — saw its staffing cut by 90 percent. The IRS lost 7,000 employees, with plans to cut up to 40 percent of its 90,000-person workforce. Veterans Affairs was targeted for 80,000 reductions. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau was effectively gutted. USAID was eliminated entirely, its employees fired, its mission dissolved into the State Department.

DOGE — the Department of Government Efficiency, led by Elon Musk — claimed $215 billion in savings. A New York Times analysis found many of its top savings claims were inaccurate. Federal spending actually increased in 2025, from $6.95 trillion to more than $7 trillion. The Cato Institute — a libertarian think tank sympathetic to shrinking government — called the results disappointing. Musk himself later said DOGE was “somewhat successful” but suggested he wouldn’t do it again.

The workforce was devastated. The savings never materialized. But the message was delivered: federal employment is conditional on political loyalty. New federal hiring guidelines now require applicants to explain how they would “help implement” Trump’s executive orders and policy priorities. The merit-based civil service that has been the backbone of American governance since the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883 has been replaced, in practice if not yet in law, with a loyalty-based patronage system.

Roy Cohn filed frivolous countersuits to intimidate plaintiffs. Donald Trump fired 317,000 people to intimidate an entire workforce. The scale changed. The principle didn’t.

The University Shakedown

If the federal workforce purge demonstrated retribution through termination, the war on universities demonstrated retribution through extortion. The pattern was identical to the one Trump perfected in Atlantic City — and the one Roy Cohn taught him in 1973: use the threat of overwhelming force to extract submission, regardless of whether the underlying claims have merit.

Harvard community members rally against the Trump administration’s funding freezes and demands in 2025. Source: Al Jazeera/Axios/France 24

Harvard University was the primary target. In April 2025, the Trump administration froze $2.2 billion in research grants and $60 million in contracts after the university refused a list of White House demands that included auditing its curriculum, surveying students’ political ideologies, and adopting specific policy changes on admissions and campus governance. One month later, the administration cut an additional $450 million. Harvard laid off employees in waves. Laboratories closed. Biospecimens died. A tuberculosis researcher received a direct federal order to halt her research. One lab that employed 300 people saw 240 contracts terminated. Eight grants — worth $7.1 million per year — were cancelled in a single stroke.

The administration claimed the funding freeze was about antisemitism. A federal judge ruled otherwise. In September 2025, U.S. District Judge Allison Burroughs ordered the cuts reversed, finding they amounted to illegal retaliation for Harvard’s rejection of White House demands. The judge noted that the federally funded research had little connection to antisemitism — it included cancer studies, pandemic preparedness, and tuberculosis vaccine research.

Harvard fought. Most universities couldn’t afford to.

Columbia University paid $221 million — a $200 million settlement plus $21 million to resolve EEOC investigations — to restore access to $1.3 billion in frozen federal funding. The university did not admit wrongdoing. But it agreed to review its Middle East curriculum, appoint new faculty to its Institute for Israel and Jewish Studies, end programs promoting “diversity targets,” share data on disciplinary actions involving student-visa holders, and ask prospective international students “questions designed to elicit their reasons for wishing to study in the United States.” Days before the settlement, Columbia suspended, expelled, or revoked degrees from nearly 80 students who had participated in pro-Palestinian protests.

On Truth Social, Trump celebrated the Columbia settlement and warned that “numerous other Higher Education Institutions that have hurt so many, and have wrongly spent federal money, are upcoming.”

Brown University had $510 million in grants frozen. It paid $50 million in grants to workforce development organizations and agreed to ban transgender women from women’s sports. The administration was seeking $1 billion from Harvard in settlement negotiations and $1 billion from UCLA, where $584 million in grants were frozen. In total, the administration was targeting ten universities it deemed “noteworthy” in its campaign — Columbia, Harvard, Brown, UCLA, George Washington University, Johns Hopkins, NYU, Northwestern, UC Berkeley, and the University of Michigan.

Across the broader academic landscape, more than 1,600 NSF grants totaling over $1.5 billion were terminated. The Trump administration’s budget proposal called for a 57 percent cut to the National Science Foundation, a 40 percent cut to the National Institutes of Health, and a 24 percent cut to NASA. Congress largely rebuffed these proposals — a bipartisan rejection that itself tells you how extreme they were — but the damage from grant cancellations and the uncertainty surrounding future funding was already cascading through the research ecosystem.

At the University of Washington School of Medicine, doctoral enrollment dropped by a third because lead scientists were unsure whether their grants would be funded. NIH grants are projected to drop from nearly 40,000 in 2025 to 27,000 in 2026 — a one-third reduction in funded research projects. The vice dean for research told reporters: “I wake up sometimes at night and just can’t sleep. How am I going to fund my lab?” The only constant, she said, was “whiplash.”

This is the Roy Cohn playbook applied to higher education. The underlying legal claims may be weak — a federal judge said so explicitly in the Harvard case. But the threat is overwhelming. Universities that resist face years of litigation and billions in frozen funding. Universities that submit pay hundreds of millions in what amounts to a protection payment — money that flows directly to the U.S. Treasury while Trump posts about it on social media.

In my “Enforcers” series, I used the language of organized crime to describe Trump’s governance. When a president freezes a university’s research funding and then says “I think we’re going to probably settle — they want to settle very badly — there’s no rush,” that is not governance. That is a shakedown. And the fact that the settlement money ends up in the same Treasury account as your federal tax dollars doesn’t make it less of one.

States as Targets

The pattern extends to entire states. The weapon is federal funding — disaster aid, infrastructure money, health care reimbursements — and the trigger is political opposition.

A nighttime view of raging flames and smoke from the 2025 California wildfires, highlighting the unprecedented scale of destruction. Source: World Weather Blog

The most documented example is California. Trump’s feud with Governor Gavin Newsom predates the second term, but the deployment of federal power against the state escalated dramatically in 2025. After the devastating Los Angeles wildfires in January — the Eaton and Palisades fires, the second and third most destructive wildfires in California history — Trump blamed Newsom’s water policies for the disaster (a claim that fact-checkers universally debunked), called the governor “Newscum,” and threatened to withhold federal aid.

Two former Trump administration officials later told Politico that Trump initially withheld approval for disaster aid for California’s deadly 2018 wildfires — until aides showed him that many residents of the affected areas had voted for him. When the fire victims were Trump voters, the aid flowed. When they weren’t, it didn’t. That single detail reveals the retribution architecture in its purest form.

In April, the administration cancelled the Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities program — a FEMA initiative for disaster-preparedness projects. Over $880 million in federal funding was rescinded. FEMA denied California’s appeal for aid following 2024 wildfires and stalled billions in requested recovery funds. FEMA staffing decreased 9.5 percent in the first six months, and the acting FEMA administrator resigned in November.

California was just the most visible target. In April 2025, a federal judge found the Trump administration in violation of a court order to halt funding freezes, ruling that FEMA was “covertly” withholding disaster aid from at least 19 states — all with Democratic attorneys general. Oregon was owed more than $120 million for winter storms, flooding, and wildfire mitigation. Hawaii was waiting on $6 million to rebuild after the 2023 Maui wildfires. New York had “hundreds of millions of dollars in FEMA grants that are inaccessible.”

The administration claimed it was implementing a “manual review process.” The judge ruled it was a covert implementation of Trump’s executive order on immigration enforcement — punishment directed at states with sanctuary policies. Meanwhile, Arkansas — governed by Trump ally Sarah Huckabee Sanders — received disaster funding for tornado damage after Sanders personally begged the president for help. The contrast between Sanders personally pleading for aid and blue-state governors being told their money was “under review” is the retribution system operating exactly as designed.

As of October 2025, at least 12 state requests for aid had been denied since January. FEMA was simultaneously considering raising the damage threshold for qualifying for public assistance by four times — a change that would make it harder for communities with lower property values to qualify, effectively redirecting aid away from poorer and more diverse communities.

Trump and Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem have repeatedly said they intend to dismantle or significantly restructure FEMA. Governor Newsom put it simply: no state in America, not even one with a $4.1 trillion economy, can replace FEMA.

This is retribution scaled to geography. States that resist Trump’s agenda find their disaster aid delayed, their grants frozen, and their residents left to wonder whether the federal government they fund through their taxes will be there when the next fire or flood hits.

The Media Shakedown

The retribution architecture extends to the press — and here the parallels to Roy Cohn’s litigation strategy are most explicit.

Trump and his legal team have filed a series of lawsuits against media organizations that follow Cohn’s template precisely: make an aggressive claim, demand an enormous sum, and count on the target’s fear of prolonged litigation and regulatory retaliation to produce a settlement. The results have been extraordinary.

CBS parent Paramount paid $16 million to settle a lawsuit as it sought regulatory approval for its merger with Skydance — giving Trump leverage that had nothing to do with the merits of the case. ABC paid $15 million. Meta paid $25 million. YouTube paid $24.5 million. Twitter/X paid $10 million. Each payment was directed toward Trump personally or to his presidential library foundation.

The media settlements alone have generated more than $63 million for Trump’s presidential library. As one columnist observed, the lawsuits follow a pattern: find an offense, claim enormous damages, and rely on the implicit threat of regulatory retaliation to extract payment. The claims rarely have to survive a courtroom test — they just have to create enough fear to produce a check.

Roy Cohn would recognize every element of this strategy. He invented it.

The $10 Billion Capstone

And then there is the IRS lawsuit — the capstone of the entire Cohn architecture.

On January 29, 2026, Trump filed a $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS and the Treasury Department over the leak of his tax returns during his first term — returns he had fought to keep secret, that every other modern president had released voluntarily, and that were leaked by a contractor already serving a five-year prison sentence.

The lawsuit demands roughly two-thirds of the IRS’s entire annual budget. The agencies he is suing are agencies he controls. The Justice Department lawyers who would normally defend the government report to an attorney general he appointed. Trump told reporters, “I’m supposed to work out a settlement with myself.”

As I documented in Volume 1, this is the $100 million countersuit of 1973 taken to its logical conclusion. Cohn taught Trump to countersue the DOJ for $100 million when they had overwhelming evidence of housing discrimination. Fifty-three years later, Trump is suing the IRS for $10 billion — for exposing tax returns the public had a right to see. The playbook is identical. The zeroes have multiplied. And now the plaintiff controls the defendant.

The Architecture Completed

This series began with a twenty-seven-year-old real estate developer in Brooklyn who hired Roy Cohn to countersue the United States government. It traced the arc from there through Atlantic City, through the birther campaign, through the pardon loyalty tests, through the Republican purge, to this moment — where the tools of retribution include not just lawsuits and nicknames and primary opponents, but the full machinery of the federal government.

The retribution ecosystem documented across these five volumes constitutes something that cannot be described as normal democratic governance. When 317,000 federal employees lose their jobs in a single year. When universities are extorted into paying hundreds of millions of dollars. When disaster aid is withheld from states based on their governors’ politics. When a sitting president sues his own government for $10 billion and says he’ll “work out a settlement with myself.” When new federal employees must explain how they will implement the president’s personal policy priorities as a condition of employment.

This is not a democracy disagreeing about policy. This is a system designed to ensure that opposition — to any policy, on any issue, at any level — carries consequences severe enough to deter everyone watching.

In Volume 1, I wrote that Roy Cohn gave Donald Trump a worldview: the world is hostile, everyone is an adversary or a tool, loyalty is the only currency that matters, and any challenge must be met with disproportionate aggression. That worldview now operates through the most powerful government on earth.

In my “Enemies List” series, I document the most dangerous manifestation: the weaponization of the justice system against political opponents. In my “Enforcers” series, I document how cabinet members have internalized the retribution instinct and run independent operations across every agency. In my “Clemency Chronicles,” I documented how even the mercy of presidential pardons was converted into a loyalty test. In my “World According to Trump,” I documented the binary psychology that drives the entire system.

The Retribution Report has attempted to connect all of these into one coherent picture. What emerges is not a collection of separate stories — a congressman here, a university there, a state somewhere else. It is a single architecture of control, built over six decades, refined through failure and success, and now operating at a scale that its architect’s mentor could never have imagined.

Roy Cohn died in 1986, disbarred and disgraced, of a disease he refused to acknowledge.

His student became president. Twice.

And the retribution machine Cohn built in a Manhattan law office now runs through the White House, the Department of Justice, the IRS, FEMA, the Department of Education, the National Institutes of Health, and every agency of the federal government.

The playbook hasn’t changed. The weapons have.
