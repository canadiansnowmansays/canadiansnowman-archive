When Toronto Ruled October

Remembering Our Championship Magic

Elbows up. Batter up. This is our moment.

October 21, 2025

Joe Carter celebrates with third base coach Nick Leyva after launching the walk-off homer that won the 1993 World Series - a moment of pure Canadian joy that echoes through the decades. Relive the magic: bit.ly/4hiWj4K

As a lifelong Torontonian and Blue Jays fan, there are moments burned into my memory that define what it means to love this team. But nothing—absolutely nothing—compares to those two magical Octobers in 1992 and 1993 when our city stood on top of the baseball world. And now, after 32 long years, we’re back in the World Series, ready to add another chapter to our October legacy.

I still get goosebumps thinking about it. After years of heartbreak, after watching American teams celebrate while we wondered if a Canadian team could ever break through, we didn’t just win once—we won twice. Back-to-back. And we did it our way. And let’s not forget that 26 years later, the Raptors would show the basketball world the same thing—that Canadian teams don’t just participate, we dominate, winning the NBA championship on American soil in Oakland. That’s the Toronto way.

1992 was our announcement to the world. Taking down the Atlanta Braves, with their seemingly unbeatable pitching rotation, felt like David conquering Goliath. When Otis Nixon’s bunt attempt died in Mike Timlin’s glove for the final out, the SkyDome erupted in a way I’d never experienced before. Dave Winfield finally getting his championship at 41, Cito Gaston becoming the first Black manager to win a World Series, and Toronto—our Toronto—becoming the first non-American city to claim baseball’s ultimate prize.

But if 1992 was sweet, 1993 was pure electricity.

That team was special in a different way. The WAMCO lineup—White, Alomar, Molitor, Carter, and Olerud—was poetry in motion. Watching Roberto Alomar turn double plays was like watching an artist at work. John Olerud, wearing his protective helmet in the field, quietly putting together one of the best hitting seasons in Blue Jays history. Paul Molitor, the veteran presence, coming through in clutch moment after clutch moment.

And then came October 23, 1993. Game 6. Bottom of the ninth.

I was watching with my family, crowded around our TV, nervous energy filling the room. Down 6-5 to the Phillies. When Joe Carter stepped to the plate with runners on, I remember my heart pounding so hard I thought everyone could hear it.

Mitch Williams delivered. Joe swung.

The split second of silence before the eruption. Tom Cheek’s immortal call: “Touch ‘em all, Joe! You’ll never hit a bigger home run in your life!”

I’ve never hugged my family harder. We ran outside, and our entire neighborhood was in the streets. Car horns blaring, strangers high-fiving, spontaneous renditions of O Canada breaking out on every corner. Toronto had never felt more alive, more united, more proud.

Those championships meant everything because they were ours. In a country where hockey dominates the sports landscape, where many said baseball could never truly take root, we proved them all wrong. The SkyDome became our cathedral, and for two glorious years, we were unstoppable.

Now, over 30 years later, those memories fuel my hope every spring. When I see the current Blue Jays take the field, I remember what’s possible. I remember Joe’s leap of joy as he rounded first base. I remember the sea of blue filling the streets of Toronto. I remember what it feels like when an entire country rallies behind one team.

And right now, in this moment, we need that feeling more than ever. As Trump bloviates about making Canada the “51st state,” treating our sovereignty like a real estate deal he can negotiate—while posting crude AI videos of bombing runs dropping excrement on his own citizens, the October 18 “No Kings” protesters—there’s something beautifully defiant about what just happened last night. A man who attacks Americans standing up for democracy wants to absorb Canada? While Trump resorts to digital feces aimed at his own people, we show him what real strength looks like. While our nation keeps its elbows up against authoritarian fantasies and juvenile provocations, the Blue Jays just showed the world what “batter up” really means to Canada. This isn’t just about baseball anymore—it’s about dignity versus degradation, class versus crass, showing the world that Canada doesn’t bow to bullies, whether they’re attacking their own citizens or eyeing our sovereignty.

And last night—my God, last night—we got exactly what we needed. Down 3-1 to Seattle in Game 7 of the ALCS, our season on the brink, the Rogers Centre holding its collective breath. Then George Springer, hobbled from a pitch to the knee in Game 5, stepped to the plate in the seventh inning with two on. One swing. One majestic three-run blast that sent 52,000 Canadians into absolute delirium. I haven’t heard Toronto that loud since Joe Carter touched ‘em all. Springer bouncing around the bases, the crowd losing its mind, grown men crying in the stands—this is what we’ve been waiting 32 years to feel again. Vladimir Guerrero Jr. taking ALCS MVP honors, the team pouring champagne until 2 AM, and the whole country remembering what October baseball means to us.

Toronto’s George Springer celebrates while rounding the bases after his go-ahead home run in Game 7 of the ALCS against the Mariners. Daniel Shirey / MLB Photos via Getty Images

Think about the poetry of it: Trump says “51st state,” we say “elbows up.” He talks annexation, we answer with elevation—elevating baseballs over outfield walls, elevating our game when it matters most, elevating an entire nation’s spirit. While American politicians talk about absorbing us like we’re some vacant lot waiting to be developed, our team—Canada’s only MLB team—just proved that when you come at us, we come back harder. Every swing is sovereignty. Every strikeout is self-determination. Every home run is a reminder that we’re not anybody’s subsidiary. We’re not a state-in-waiting. We’re a proud nation that plays ball on its own terms.

Sure, we’re the underdogs against the Dodgers with their Hollywood money and star-studded lineup—Shohei Ohtani, Mookie Betts, Freddie Freeman—while we’ve got our lunch-pail work ethic and Canadian grit. But that’s okay—that’s exactly where we want to be. We’ve always been underdogs. We were underdogs in ‘92 and ‘93 too. Being the underdog is the Canadian way, and we wear it like a badge of honor. Because when you’re the underdog, every victory tastes sweeter, every home run travels further, and when you finally win it all, the celebration means that much more.

The timing couldn’t be more perfect. “Elbows up” meets “batter up”—our national stance of defiance translated into the batter’s box. As our leaders keep their elbows up against authoritarian pressure, as Canadians unite in rolling our eyes at the “51st state” nonsense, the Blue Jays are giving us the ultimate response: We’re in the World Series, ready to show that Canadian teams don’t just participate in American games—we excel at them. Not with words or tweets or diplomatic statements, but with doubles down the line, strikeouts in the clutch, and celebrations that shake the CN Tower. That’s the Canadian way—we don’t just talk about our independence, we demonstrate it. We earn it. We win it, one game at a time, one swing at a time.

The 2015 and 2016 playoff runs gave us glimpses—Jose Bautista’s bat flip still gives me chills. But those glimpses turned into destiny this October. After being down 3-2 to Seattle, after being down 3-1 in Game 7, we did what champions do: we found a way.

To younger fans who never experienced those championships: this is your moment. This is what we’ve been waiting for. You’ll finally understand why those of us who lived through ‘92 and ‘93 can never quite let go of those memories. Because last night proved it can happen again. George Springer’s blast proved that magic is still real in October.

They weren’t just championships. They were proof that Toronto belongs. That Canada can compete with anyone. That sometimes, on crisp October nights, magic is real and dreams do come true.

And now, 32 years later, we’re back. The World Series returns to Toronto on Friday night. The Dodgers are coming to our house, where the crowd will be electric, where every pitch matters, where a nation will be watching and believing.

Elbows up. Batter up. This is our moment.

Touch ‘em all, Toronto. Let’s finish what we started.
