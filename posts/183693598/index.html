The Senator They’re Trying to Silence

Pete Hegseth Moves to Punish Mark Kelly for Reminding Troops to Follow the Law

January 6, 2026

Yesterday, January 5, 2026

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced he is taking administrative action against Senator Mark Kelly for “seditious statements” and a “pattern of reckless misconduct.”

Kelly’s crime? In November, he released a video with five other Democratic veteran lawmakers reminding service members of their legal obligation to refuse illegal orders.

Yesterday, Hegseth issued:

  • A formal Letter of Censure placed in Kelly’s permanent military file

  • Retirement grade determination proceedings that could demote him from Navy Captain to Commander

  • Potential pay reduction of ~$1,000/month from his military pension

  • Charges under Articles 133 and 134 of the Uniform Code of Military Justice

The message is clear: Remind troops to follow the Constitution, and we’ll punish you.

Part 9 of this series, published months ago, predicted exactly this outcome. Yesterday, the prediction became reality.

What Kelly Actually Said

On November 18, 2025, Senator Mark Kelly and five other Democratic lawmakers—all military veterans or former intelligence officers—released a short video message to service members.

The video’s message was simple and legally uncontroversial:

“No one has to carry out orders that violate the law or our Constitution. Our laws are clear. You can refuse illegal orders... you must refuse illegal orders.”

This is not radical. This is not seditious. This is established military law.

The Uniform Code of Military Justice requires service members to refuse illegal orders. The Nuremberg trials established that “following orders” is not a defense for war crimes. Every military lawyer, every JAG officer, every military ethics course teaches this principle.

Kelly, a retired Navy Captain, former astronaut, and combat veteran, was doing what any responsible former officer would do: reminding troops of their legal and moral obligations.

The other five lawmakers in the video:

  • Senator Elissa Slotkin (D-MI) - Former intelligence officer

  • Rep. Jason Crow (D-CO) - Army veteran, Bronze Star recipient

  • Rep. Chris Deluzio (D-PA) - Navy veteran

  • Rep. Chrissy Houlahan (D-PA) - Air Force veteran

  • Rep. Maggie Goodlander (D-NH) - Navy Reserve officer

All veterans. All reminding troops of established law.

Why They Made the Video

The video came amid growing concerns about Trump administration military actions and statements:

Venezuela operation: Trump ordered military action to capture Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro without congressional authorization. After the January 3 operation, Kelly said: “Over the past year, Trump’s foreign policy has been reckless, chaotic, self-serving, and unconstitutional.”

Legal authority questions: Secretary of State Marco Rubio, when asked on ABC’s This Week, struggled to identify clear legal authority for the Venezuela operation.

Earlier Trump statements: Throughout 2025, Trump made statements suggesting potential misuse of military force against domestic opponents or without proper legal authority.

Kelly and his colleagues saw this pattern and reminded troops: You have not just the right but the duty to refuse illegal orders.

This is what every officer learns. This is what every service member swears to uphold. The oath is to the Constitution, not to any president.

Trump’s Response: “Punishable by DEATH”

Trump’s reaction to the video was immediate and extreme.

On November 21, he reposted messages calling the lawmakers’ video “seditious” and added his own commentary calling it “punishable by DEATH.”

Let that sink in. The President of the United States suggested that reminding troops to follow the law could warrant execution.

He later tried to walk it back, telling Fox News Radio: “I’m not threatening death, but I think they’re in serious trouble.”

But the damage was done. Trump made clear what he wanted: punishment for any lawmaker who reminded troops that presidential orders aren’t automatically legal.

Within days, Democrats reported receiving death threats related to the video.

The Devastating Hypocrisy

Here’s what makes this particularly grotesque: Kelly cited Hegseth’s own words in his defense.

Pete Hegseth, in past statements, said: “If you’re doing something that is just completely unlawful and ruthless, then there is a consequence for that.”

Hegseth acknowledged that unlawful orders have consequences. He said service members shouldn’t blindly follow illegal commands.

Kelly repeated the exact same principle—that troops must refuse illegal orders—and Hegseth is now calling it “seditious.”

This isn’t about the substance of what Kelly said. Hegseth himself has said the same thing. This is about who Kelly is: a Democratic senator who criticized Trump.

The principle is fine when Hegseth says it. It’s “seditious” when Kelly says it.

That’s not law enforcement. That’s political retribution.

What Monday’s Action Means

Hegseth announced three specific actions against Kelly:

1. Formal Letter of Censure

A censure letter will be placed in Kelly’s permanent military personnel file. This is a formal written reprimand stating that Kelly engaged in “reckless misconduct” that was “seditious in nature.”

For a retired officer with Kelly’s distinguished record—Navy pilot, combat missions, NASA astronaut, four space missions—this is a stain on decades of honorable service.

2. Retirement Grade Determination Proceedings

Under 10 U.S.C. § 1370(f), the Pentagon has initiated proceedings to potentially reduce Kelly’s retirement rank from O-6 (Navy Captain) to O-5 (Commander).

This isn’t just symbolic. Kelly’s military pension is based on his retirement rank. A one-rank reduction would cost him approximately $1,000 per month from his current ~$6,000/month military retirement pay.

That’s $12,000 per year. Over the expected 20+ years of his retirement, that’s potentially $240,000+ in lost pension benefits.

3. Threat of Further Action

Hegseth’s statement explicitly warned: “Captain Kelly’s status as a sitting United States Senator does not exempt him from accountability, and further violations could result in further action.”

Translation: We can do more. Recall to active duty. Court-martial. Whatever it takes.

This is the threat model from Part 9 in action: Keep a sword hanging over every critic’s head. Make them know that any further criticism will trigger escalation.

The Timeline Kelly Has

According to Hegseth’s announcement:

  • 30 days: Kelly has 30 days to submit a written response to the charges

  • 45 days: Final determination on rank reduction will be made within 45 days

  • Appeals: Kelly can appeal any adverse decision, but given the political nature of this action, military appeals may be futile

So by mid-February 2026, we’ll know whether the Pentagon will actually reduce Kelly’s rank or if the proceedings collapse under legal or political pressure.

The Constitutional Problems

What Hegseth is doing raises massive constitutional issues:

Speech or Debate Clause (Article I, Section 6)

The Speech or Debate Clause protects members of Congress from being questioned for their legislative acts. While Kelly’s video wasn’t technically “legislative,” courts have interpreted this protection broadly to include communications about official duties and policy matters.

Punishing a sitting senator for making a policy statement about military law could violate this constitutional protection.

First Amendment

Kelly has a constitutional right to free speech, even as a retired military officer. Courts have consistently held that retired officers retain substantial First Amendment rights.

The only permissible restrictions are narrow: classified information, direct incitement to lawless action, or statements that materially interfere with military operations.

Reminding troops about established law does none of these things.

Separation of Powers

The Executive Branch (Pentagon) is attempting to punish a Legislative Branch member (Senator) for policy criticism and statements about law.

This crosses fundamental separation of powers boundaries. The Founders specifically prohibited executive retaliation against legislators for their official statements.

Vagueness of “Seditious Conduct”

Hegseth claims Kelly’s statements were “seditious in nature” and violated Articles 133 and 134 of UCMJ.

Article 133 prohibits “conduct unbecoming an officer.” But reminding troops to follow the law is the opposite of unbecoming—it’s exactly what officers should do.

Article 134 covers “disorders and neglects to the prejudice of good order and discipline.” But Kelly’s statement promoted good order by reinforcing legal obligations.

Neither article can constitutionally be applied to protected speech about legal obligations.

This is unprecedented: using military justice to punish a sitting senator for policy criticism.

The Chilling Effect on Congress

If Hegseth succeeds in punishing Kelly, the implications are staggering:

Any member of Congress who:

  • Served in the military (about 95 current members)

  • Receives military retirement pay (about 20 senators and representatives)

  • Criticizes military policy

  • Questions presidential authority

  • Reminds troops of their legal obligations

...can be:

  • Censured by the Pentagon

  • Have their rank reduced

  • Have their pension cut

  • Threatened with court-martial

  • Recalled to active duty

This creates a two-tiered Congress: those vulnerable to military punishment and those who aren’t.

Every veteran in Congress—Democrat or Republican—should be terrified. Yesterday it’s Mark Kelly for reminding troops about illegal orders. Tomorrow it could be anyone who questions defense policy, military spending, or use of force.

The Pentagon would have veto power over congressional speech.

Why Kelly, Not the Other Five?

Six lawmakers made the video. Hegseth is only going after Kelly. Why?

The Pentagon’s answer: Kelly is the only one who receives military retirement pay, so he’s the only one subject to UCMJ.

But the real reasons are more political:

High profile: Kelly is a nationally known figure—astronaut, combat veteran, senator from a swing state.

Vulnerable seat: Kelly is up for reelection in 2026. Attacking him now helps Republicans target his seat.

Made it personal: Kelly didn’t just make the video. He specifically criticized Trump’s Venezuela operation and called his foreign policy “unconstitutional.”

Provable deterrent: Punishing a sitting senator sends a message to everyone else: We can reach you wherever you are.

The other five lawmakers—Slotkin, Crow, Deluzio, Houlahan, Goodlander—don’t receive military pensions, so Hegseth publicly stated they’re not under his jurisdiction. But his statement made clear he would punish them if he could.

The Precedent This Sets

If this action stands, we’ve established:

  1. The Pentagon can punish sitting senators for policy speech

  2. Reminding troops about law is “seditious” if you’re Trump’s enemy

  3. Military retirees in Congress are second-class members subject to executive retaliation

  4. The Speech or Debate Clause doesn’t protect statements about military policy

  5. Separation of powers doesn’t prevent executive punishment of legislators

These are the building blocks of authoritarian government. When the executive can punish legislators for criticizing policy, you don’t have co-equal branches. You have a dictator with a rubber-stamp legislature.
