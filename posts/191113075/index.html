The Iran Crisis

“The Succession”

Part XLVII: Day Seventeen: How the Revolutionary Guards Won Iran's Wartime Throne, and What It Means for the War That Put Them There

March 16, 2026

A photo of the new supreme leader of Iran, Mojtaba Khamenei, at a ceremony for military officials killed by U.S.-Israeli airstrikes, in Tehran, Iran, last week.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

I. The Crown

On the night of February 28, as American and Israeli bombs fell on Tehran, Mojtaba Khamenei was in his father’s compound. He survived. His father did not. His wife did not. His son did not. His mother died from her wounds three days later. His sister was killed in the same strikes.

What followed — the weeklong struggle over who would lead Iran after Ali Khamenei — is only now becoming visible, through reporting by Farnaz Fassihi of the New York Times based on interviews with five senior Iranian officials, two clerics, two members of the supreme leader’s office, and three Revolutionary Guards officers with direct knowledge of the process. The picture that emerges is not predestination. It is barely controlled chaos: a succession fight conducted by wax-sealed ballots carried by couriers through an active war zone, between a generals’ faction and a moderate camp, with a dead man’s final wishes thrown into the middle.

Mojtaba Khamenei was not on his father’s list of preferred successors. The elder Khamenei had given close advisers three names. His son was none of them. By all accounts, according to Fassihi’s sources, Mojtaba most likely would not have risen if his father had died a natural death.

II. The Game of Thrones

The Assembly of Experts — 88 senior clerics constitutionally responsible for selecting a supreme leader — held their first virtual meeting on March 3. That same day, Israel bombed the assembly’s headquarters in Qom, killing administrative staff. The clerics voted by remote, each writing a name on paper, folding it into an envelope, and sealing it with wax. Couriers hand-carried the ballots to a counting committee.

Two factions were fighting for the throne.

The hard-liner camp backed Mojtaba, supported by the Revolutionary Guards and their newly appointed commander in chief, General Ahmad Vahidi; General Mohammad Ali Aziz Jaffari, a Guards strategist running the current war; General Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, parliament speaker and former Guards commander; and Hossein Taeb, the former head of Guards intelligence and — in Fassihi’s characterisation — “the mastermind of cross-border assassination plots.”

The moderate camp pushed three alternatives: Hassan Rouhani, the former president who had presided over the 2015 nuclear deal; Hassan Khomeini, the grandson of the revolution’s founding father and a reformist ally; and Alireza Aarafi, a scholar with solid religious credentials but no military or political power base — the compromise candidate, “easy to manage.”

The newly appointed commander in chief of the Revolutionary Guards, Gen. Ahmad Vahidi, was among the powerful figures pushing for Mojtaba Khamenei’s appointment as Iran’s supreme leader. Credit...Vahid Salemi/Associated Press

Opposition to Mojtaba came from unexpected corners. Ali Larijani, the head of Iran’s National Security Council and the de facto ruler of Iran during the succession period, told assembly members that the country needed a moderate and unifying figure. Mojtaba, he argued, would be polarizing. President Pezeshkian, a moderate, joined the naysayers.

The fuel for the hard-liner camp was rage. As the assembly debated, US and Israeli bombs continued to fall. “As the discussion evolved,” Fassihi reports, “the members of the assembly appeared less interested in a leader to rescue the country from its current state of acute crisis than in a reincarnation of their ‘martyred’ leader to avenge his death.”

In an initial round of voting on March 3, Mojtaba achieved the necessary two-thirds majority. The announcement was prepared. State media were alerted to prepare for the dawn call to prayer on March 4.

Then Larijani called it off.

III. The Dead Man’s Will

Larijani’s argument was tactical: announcing Mojtaba’s succession would make him an immediate assassination target. Trump and Israel’s defense minister Katz had both threatened to eliminate any successor. Better to wait until after the war ended.

On March 6, Israel dropped bunker-busting bombs on Ali Khamenei’s downtown Tehran compound, reducing it to rubble. Mojtaba was not there.

The delay gave the moderate camp an opening. They mounted a last-ditch effort. Their argument had two components. The first was procedural: the Constitution mandates that assembly members vote in person, and the virtual vote was therefore invalid. The second was personal and devastating: Mojtaba himself, when told he had been elected, had said he did not want the job.

“When they told Mojtaba he was elected, he said, ‘I don’t want to accept it, pick someone else,’” a politician close to the Khamenei family told Fassihi by telephone from Tehran. He added: “It’s a Shia custom of polite refusal among clerics to say ‘I’m not after power,’ but then they eventually accept.”

But the moderates had more. At a meeting with the assembly’s leadership board, two of Ali Khamenei’s closest aides — one a top military adviser, the other his chief of staff, Asghar Hejazi — testified that Khamenei had told them, before his death, that he did not want his son or any member of his family to succeed him. He had forbidden hereditary succession because, he said, it would violate the essence of the 1979 Islamic Revolution that had overthrown monarchical rule. They presented a written will with the same message. They urged the assembly to rescind its initial vote.

The testimony stunned the clerics present. The Guards generals running the war were alarmed. They set in motion a counteroffensive.

Hossein Taeb — the former spy chief — personally called all 88 members of the Assembly of Experts. Voting for Mojtaba Khamenei, he told each of them, was “a moral, religious and ideological duty.”

On March 8, the assembly met again, also virtually. The wax-sealed envelopes were carried by couriers again. Mojtaba Khamenei received 59 out of 88 votes — a clear two-thirds majority, but far from unanimous. Shortly before midnight, state media announced Iran’s third supreme leader.

The IRGC had won. The father’s wishes had been overridden. The moderates had lost. The man who did not want to be supreme leader became supreme leader, in a war that killed his family, in a country he has not been photographed in since.

IV. What the Succession Means

Iran’s supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, in 2024. Some officials said he did not want his son to succeed him, because hereditary succession would violate the essence of the 1979 Islamic revolution.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

The significance is not obscure. Mojtaba’s ascension was engineered by the people running the war — the Guards generals who benefit institutionally from its continuation, who have operational command of Iran’s missile and drone forces, who are simultaneously Iran’s military leadership and its political kingmakers. The man they installed is, by his father’s own assessment according to US intelligence shared with Trump, “not very bright, and viewed as unqualified to be leader.” He has “issues in his personal life.” He was not wanted by his father. He was not wanted by the moderates. He was not wanted by the man who actually ran Iran during the succession week.

He was wanted by the generals.

The war’s outcome, on the Iranian side, now runs through men who have every incentive to continue fighting and no institutional interest in a negotiated end. Larijani — who pushed for a moderate leader and an end to Gulf strikes — remains the de facto administrator of the country. But Mojtaba, under the Guards’ influence, is the supreme leader. The statement Mojtaba issued on Day 13 named Minab specifically. He has a list. The generals gave him the throne and the list has their signature on it.

Araghchi, Iran’s foreign minister, told CBS on Sunday: “Whoever is making decisions is not going to show any sign of compromise or appeasement.” This was Hamidreza Azizi’s assessment earlier in the war. It has been confirmed by every subsequent development, including the succession itself.

V. The Alliance That No Longer Exists

Trump went to war without preparing public opinion, seeking UN approval, or consulting any ally except Israel. This is not a secondary observation about diplomatic style. It is the description of a structural break.

Steven Erlanger of the New York Times compared the 2003 Iraq war with the current conflict. The comparison is instructive precisely because 2003 was itself considered a diplomatic disaster. In 2003, the Bush administration consulted allies, worked the alliance, brought the case to the United Nations Security Council, involved Congress, and prepared public opinion for months. France and Germany refused to join — but the Americans worked hard to keep NATO together, and when they failed, they acknowledged the failure and managed it. “That’s the difference with President Trump,” said Nicholas Burns, who was US ambassador to NATO during the Iraq war.

In 2026, François Heisbourg, a French defense analyst, was more direct: “This time there was not even the beginning of a pretense of a discussion. As a place for consultation and decision-making, the alliance is basically over, because the Americans are not interested.”

Trump’s response to the resulting isolation is to demand that allies join a war he launched without telling them, on terms he has not defined, toward an objective he cannot articulate. On Sunday, he told the Financial Times that failure to help open the Strait of Hormuz would be “very bad for the future of NATO.” He threatened to postpone his planned April summit with Xi Jinping if China did not send warships. Australia said it had not been asked and would not be contributing. Japan’s prime minister said any response would face “high hurdles” under Japan’s pacifist constitution. South Korea said it would “communicate closely.” China said both sides were “still discussing” the summit.

The responses ranged from cautious to no. None were yes.

John Sawers, former head of MI6, named what this reveals: “Trump has always treated the Europeans with a degree of contempt and doesn’t seem to think he needs allies, that allies hold America back. He hasn’t changed his attitude toward Europe, and Europe hasn’t changed its attitude toward him.”

Europe’s great fear, Sawers added, is a collapsed Iranian state — fragmentation, civil conflict, a refugee crisis that dwarfs Syria. “What is astonishing is that the U.S. doesn’t see the dangers of the aftermath. The countries of the region and Europe and the rest of us will be clearing up the mess of this for the next 10 to 15 years.”

VI. The World at Day Seventeen

Brent crude crossed $106 a barrel Monday morning — up more than 40 percent since February 28, trading near the day’s highs. Diesel in the United States is now $4.99 per gallon, up 33 percent since the war began. Gas is $3.72 nationally, up 25 percent.

Dubai International Airport — normally one of the world’s busiest — briefly suspended all flights Monday after a drone set a nearby fuel tank on fire. Flights resumed on a limited schedule hours later. No injuries were reported. It was the latest in a series of Iranian drone and missile attacks on the Emirates throughout the war.

In Baghdad, five missiles struck the airport complex Sunday evening, injuring four people including airport staff and security personnel. Kataib Hezbollah claimed the attack, saying its rockets struck Camp Victory, a former US base that still hosts a diplomatic logistics hub. The US Embassy, which has already evacuated all American civilians from Iraq, issued no further guidance. Americans are being told to leave by land. Commercial flights are not operating.

Israel has carried out more than 1,100 strikes on Lebanon since March 2. On Monday the Israeli military announced it was expanding its ground assault in southern Lebanon into “new areas” — pressing beyond the immediate border zone. Israel’s military spokesman said the scope of the operation had yet to be determined. Lebanese officials fear a much broader invasion is being prepared.

In Bangladesh, the government has closed universities and imposed power blackouts to conserve electricity. About half the country’s power comes from gas-fired stations; nearly a third of that gas comes from Qatar, now blocked by the Strait closure. University students are on a Ramadan break they did not want. The country’s garment factories — producing 85 percent of Bangladesh’s exports — are running on diesel generators. In Pakistan, fuel prices rose 20 percent overnight. In India, restaurants have removed slow-simmered dishes from their menus because they use too much gas. Crematories, laundries, and hospital kitchens are struggling to keep running.

Vladimir Putin, speaking Wednesday about European plans to phase out Russian gas: “Now other markets are opening up, and perhaps it’s more advantageous for us to stop supplying the European market.” The mood in Moscow, in Katrin Bennhold’s formulation for the New York Times, is “triumphant.” The carrier strike group that was in the South China Sea has been redirected to the Gulf. THAAD missile defense systems from South Korea have been moved to defend against Iranian drones. Japan and Taiwan are facing delays in US arms deliveries. China’s freedom of action in the Pacific has expanded while Washington’s attention is consumed by the Strait of Hormuz.

Araghchi, on Sunday, clarified Iran’s actual Strait policy: it is not closed universally. Nations that approach Iran diplomatically for safe passage are being considered on a case-by-case basis. China and India have already been granted passage. The yuan gambit is operational. The Strait is not simply closed — it is selectively open, on Iran’s terms, to countries willing to negotiate directly with Tehran rather than through Washington.

This is not a blockade. It is a reorganisation of the global energy order, conducted with drones and mines and missiles, by a country that has been at war for seventeen days, whose supreme leader has not been seen in public, and whose military is being run by the generals who made him supreme leader.
