When Democracy Fights Back

The Southern Cascade

Part XXI: What Callais Unleashed in Twenty-Four Hours

May 1, 2026

By Stephen Gordon | CanadianSnowmanSays

It took less than twenty-four hours.

On Wednesday morning, the Supreme Court handed down Louisiana v. Callais. By Wednesday afternoon, Florida’s legislature had passed a new congressional map adding four Republican-leaning seats. By Thursday morning, Louisiana had suspended its House primaries. By Thursday afternoon, Donald Trump was on social media praising Louisiana’s governor for his “tremendous Vision, Strength, and Leadership” and publicly pressuring Tennessee’s governor to redraw his state’s maps, writing that doing so “should give us one extra seat, and help Save our Country.”

One ruling. Twenty-four hours. A cascade that has no visible stopping point.

Rep. Cleo Fields (D-LA), whose majority-Black district sits at the heart of the Louisiana v. Callais case. ‘The Supreme Court has opened a Pandora’s box.

Representative Cleo Fields, the Louisiana Democrat whose majority-Black district sits at the center of the Supreme Court case, said it plainly: “I really think the Supreme Court has opened a Pandora’s box.”

He is right. And unlike most Pandora’s boxes in political rhetoric, this one comes with a specific contents list.

The Louisiana Chaos

The two majority-Black districts (2 and 6) at the center of the legal fight. Republicans are expected to eliminate or significantly weaken at least one in the coming redraw.

Louisiana’s situation is the most immediately chaotic, because it was the state at the center of the case, and because its legislature remains in session — which means the machinery for drawing a new map is already assembled and running.

The state’s secretary of state, Nancy Landry, issued an emergency declaration Thursday suspending House primary elections. The Senate primary proceeds as scheduled, with early voting beginning Saturday for the May 16th contest. House candidates will remain on the ballot, but their votes will not be counted — a notice posted at early voting sites informing voters of the change, as if a Supreme Court ruling that upends sixty years of voting rights law is the kind of thing adequately communicated by a placard at a folding table.

Governor Jeff Landry and Attorney General Liz Murrill issued a joint statement: the current map, declared an illegal racial gerrymander, cannot be used. Speaker Mike Johnson — who represents Louisiana and therefore has personal knowledge of what he is describing — told reporters that Landry “has no choice but to suspend” the primaries.

Johnson then added, with the practiced innocence of a man who knows exactly what he is doing: “My fingerprints aren’t on it. It’s the decision of the State Legislature.”

The fingerprints are everywhere. Johnson’s party spent years litigating to produce Wednesday’s ruling. His party will now redraw Louisiana’s map to eliminate at least one majority-Black district. His party will gain at least one House seat in a state he represents. But the fingerprints aren’t on it.

This is the fight-club rule, Louisiana edition. What Florida’s Republicans would not say out loud about partisan gerrymandering, Louisiana’s Republicans will not say out loud about racial motivation. The outcome speaks for itself. The words are carefully managed.

The Pipeline

There is a number buried in the coverage of Wednesday’s ruling that deserves to be pulled forward, because it contains the full weight of what Callais actually means beyond the seat count.

Evan Turnage: ‘I’m in the fight.’ The Mississippi candidate embodies the next generation of Black Southern leadership whose pipeline is now threatened.

Evan Turnage is thirty-four years old. He left a senior staff position on Capitol Hill — working for both Chuck Schumer and Elizabeth Warren — and returned home to Mississippi to run for Congress. He lost a Democratic primary in March against the popular incumbent Bennie Thompson. He was not discouraged. He had crisscrossed the district, built name recognition, laid groundwork. The next cycle, he thought, the groundwork would pay off.

On Wednesday, the district he is preparing to run in became a primary target for Republican redistricting. The majority-Black constituency that has made Thompson’s seat a Democratic hold for a generation could be redrawn into something practically impossible for a Black Democrat to win.

“It’s going to mean for a lot of people that they leave politics altogether,” Turnage told the Times, “because there aren’t districts that make sense. It’s definitely going to be devastating.”

This is the dimension of Callais that seat-count analyses miss. The majority-minority districts created and protected under the Voting Rights Act were not only about current representation. They were the pipeline through which the next generation of Black Southern political leadership was being built. They were the districts where a thirty-four-year-old from Mississippi could lose narrowly and come back. Where a congressional aide could return home and run. Where groundwork laid over one cycle could bear fruit in the next.

That pipeline is now at risk — not in the abstract, but specifically, district by district, candidate by candidate, career by career.

Representative Shomari Figures of Alabama is forty years old. A former congressional and White House aide, he was elected in 2024 to a newly drawn majority-minority district created after a legal fight that itself reached the Supreme Court. “None of us working on Capitol Hill would have gotten there without that foot in the door,” he said. Without the district, the congressional staff positions that feed the next generation of candidates don’t follow. Without those positions, the pipeline narrows to almost nothing.

“What do the next ten years look like if we disrupt the pipeline?” asked Glynda Carr of Higher Heights for America. The question is not rhetorical. It does not have a reassuring answer.

Professor Emmitt Riley of Sewanee was more direct: “This case has the potential to essentially stop Black political representation from advancing in the way that we know it.”

Justice Alito, in his majority opinion, cited “vast social change” in the South as evidence that the racial gap in voter registration and turnout had largely disappeared. He did not address the gap in representation. He did not address the pipeline. He did not address Evan Turnage.

Peak Gerrymandering

Nate Cohn, the Times’s chief political analyst, mapped the full territory of what Callais has now made possible in an analysis published Thursday afternoon.

The core finding: across much of the country, Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act was the only legal limitation on partisan gerrymandering. It prevented states from dismantling House districts where a specific minority group comprised a majority of the voting population. The Supreme Court has now held that Section 2 “does not intrude on states’ prerogative to draw districts based on nonracial factors, including to achieve partisan advantage.” In plain language: both parties are now permitted to eliminate majority-minority districts if doing so serves partisan interests. And so they will.

The plausible Republican redistricting scenario across eight Southern states — Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Tennessee — yields thirteen additional Republican House seats beyond what existed before the redistricting war began. Democrats currently hold twenty-four seats across those states. In the plausible scenario, they hold eleven.

For November 2026, the immediate gains are more limited. Filing deadlines have passed in many states. Primaries have been held in others. Louisiana, South Carolina, and Tennessee have the clearest paths to redistricting before the midterms — yielding potentially four additional Republican seats this cycle, on top of Florida’s four. Eight seats, in a House where the majority currently stands at a handful of votes, is not a rounding error. It is the majority.

Cohn’s saving caveat for 2026 is that the political environment may overwhelm the structural advantage. Trump’s approval rating sits below forty percent. Democrats have been outperforming their 2024 baselines in special elections across the country. In a wave environment, structural disadvantages get washed out. Democrats may not need to flip Republican-leaning districts if they are winning by enough in the districts they already hold.

But 2028 is not a wave environment in waiting. 2028 is a presidential year, likely without an incumbent, likely competitive. In a close election, eight structural seats could determine who controls the House. And by 2028, the remaining Southern states that missed the 2026 window will have had time to redistrict. The plausible scenario becomes the baseline.

The Retaliation Logic

Democrats will not sit still. They have said so explicitly. One Democratic House member, speaking Wednesday, raised the possibility of a 52-to-0 Democratic congressional map in California and a 17-to-0 map in Illinois.

These are not serious proposals. They are negotiating positions — signals to Republican-controlled Southern legislatures that escalation has a ceiling only if both sides agree on one. In theory, extreme blue-state gerrymanders would give Democrats a path to canceling out Republican gains and potentially winning the redistricting wars outright, since Democrats control more Republican-held districts in solid blue states than Republicans hold in solid blue ones.

In practice, the obstacles are real. Drawing a 52-to-0 California map would require pinwheel-shaped districts stretching from Democratic cities deep into Republican countryside — shapes that would offend moderate voters, destabilise incumbent bases, and require splitting up the very majority-minority districts that the post-Callais political moment makes radioactive to touch. Many blue states also have constitutional limitations on gerrymandering requiring voter approval to override. Colorado’s threshold is 55 percent. Not every state is as amendable as Virginia.

The more realistic Democratic retaliation — New York following Virginia’s example, Colorado following California’s, blue states pushing their maps closer to their theoretical partisan maximum without reaching the absurd extreme — roughly cancels out Republican gains. The result is a draw, but a draw at a far more aggressive baseline, with far fewer competitive districts, in a political landscape where the norm has shifted permanently toward maximal partisan extraction.

Cohn identifies a narrow window. If both parties’ gerrymanders roughly cancel out, there may be an opportunity before the 2032 census-driven redistricting cycle for the parties to agree on limits. Independent commissions. Mathematical compactness requirements. Some structural constraint that neither party will voluntarily accept but that both might accept if the alternative is permanent mutually assured gerrymandering.

That window is narrow and requires both parties to believe simultaneously that the cost of escalation exceeds the benefit. Given the incentives currently in front of every Republican-controlled Southern legislature, that calculation is not being made today.

Turnage’s Grandparents

Black Southern voters exercising their rights after decades of suppression. The pipeline of Black political leadership built on these gains is now threatened.

I want to return to Evan Turnage, because he said something that deserves to close this essay.

When asked how he was processing Wednesday’s ruling, he reached back a generation. His grandparents were born in the 1920s. They could not vote until they were in their forties. After the Voting Rights Act passed in 1965, they were able to support Black congressmen, see Bennie Thompson and his predecessor Mike Espy go to Washington, and live long enough to vote for Barack Obama.

That arc — from disenfranchisement to representation to the presidency, in a single lifetime — is what the Voting Rights Act made possible. It is what Justice Alito was describing, in his way, when he cited “vast social change” as evidence that the original remedies were no longer necessary. The change was real. It was also the direct result of the remedies. Removing the remedies because the change has been partially achieved is not the end of the journey. It is the removal of the vehicle that was making the journey possible.

Turnage has not decided what he will do next. He has not retreated.

“I’m in it,” he said. “I’m in the fight.”

From across the border, I find that sentence more clarifying than anything the Supreme Court majority wrote on Wednesday. The fight is not over because the seatbelt has been removed. It is harder. The car is still moving. The road ahead is more dangerous than it was forty-eight hours ago.

Democracy fights back. In Mississippi, at thirty-four years old, Evan Turnage is still planning to make it do exactly that.
