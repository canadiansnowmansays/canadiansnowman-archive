The Trump Accountability Project

Monetizing from Mar-a-Lago

Part 38: From Mugshots to NFTs (2021-2025)

April 16, 2026

“America’s most historically significant artifact.”

That’s what Donald Trump called his suit.

Not the Declaration of Independence. Not the Constitution. Not Abraham Lincoln’s Gettysburg Address or the flag that survived Fort McHenry.

Trump’s blue suit—the one he wore for his August 24, 2023 mugshot at the Fulton County Jail in Georgia—is, according to Trump, America’s most historically significant artifact.

And for $4,653, you could own a piece of it.

Welcome to Trump’s post-presidency business model: turning criminal indictments, constitutional violations, and legal jeopardy into profit opportunities. It’s the grift perfected—and it’s made him millions while he faces zero accountability for commodifying his own criminality.

The Mugshot: From Arrest to Merchandise in 24 Hours

On August 24, 2023, Trump surrendered at the Fulton County Jail after being indicted on racketeering charges for attempting to overturn Georgia’s 2020 election results.

For just over 20 minutes, he was processed, fingerprinted, and photographed. The resulting image—Trump glowering at the camera, prisoner number P01135809—became the first and only police booking photograph of a U.S. president.

Within 24 hours, Trump had turned his arrest into a fundraising bonanza.

His campaign reportedly raised over $4 million—his highest-grossing 24-hour period of the primary cycle. The mugshot appeared on:

  • T-shirts

  • Coffee mugs

  • Bumper stickers

  • Christmas stockings

  • Campaign merchandise

Trump even returned to Twitter/X for the first time since January 6, 2021, posting the mugshot with text that read: “ELECTION INTERFERENCE. NEVER SURRENDER!”

The arrest—ostensibly a moment of legal accountability—became a profit center and campaign asset.

The NFT Empire: Three Series of Digital Grift

The mug shot edition is just the latest in a series of NFT cards released on the site portraying former President Donald J. Trump.Credit...NFT INT

But Trump didn’t stop with merchandise. He launched an entire NFT (non-fungible token) empire, creating three separate series of digital trading cards between December 2022 and December 2023.

Series 1 (December 2022): The Original Grift

Trump launched 45,000 digital trading cards at $99 each, depicting himself as a superhero, astronaut, cowboy, and various other fantastical figures.

The cards sold out in less than 24 hours, raising nearly $4.5 million.

Trump later reported earning between $100,001 and $1 million from licensing his name and image to NFT INT LLC, the company producing the cards.

Series 2 (April 2023): 47,000 More Fantasy Cards

A second batch of 47,000 cards hit the market in April 2023, again at $99 each. These featured similar superhero-Trump imagery.

Reception was less enthusiastic than Series 1, but Trump still pocketed licensing fees.

Series 3 (December 2023): The “Mugshot Edition”

This is where things got truly bizarre.

In December 2023, Trump launched the “Mugshot Edition”—100,000 NFTs capitalizing on his Georgia arrest three months earlier.

The Pitch (in Trump’s own words from the promotional video):

“This is your favorite president Donald J. Trump with some very exciting news... Due to the great Excitement and Success of my previous TRUMP DIGITAL TRADING CARDS, we’re doing it again – The MugShot Edition!”

The Pricing Structure:

  • $99 per card (standard price)

  • Buy 47 cards ($4,653): Receive a physical trading card containing a piece of Trump’s actual mugshot suit, plus an invitation to dine with Trump at Mar-a-Lago

  • Buy 100 cards ($9,900): Receive TWO physical cards (one with suit fabric, one with suit AND tie fabric), two tickets to a cocktail reception with Trump, and a unique 1-of-1 NFT

The suit was authenticated by MEARS, a sports memorabilia authenticator. Company president Troy Kinunen called Trump’s suit “the most historically significant artifact in United States history”—though he later qualified that as “definitely one of them, especially in modern times.”

Trump literally cut his suit into 2,024 pieces to distribute to NFT buyers.

The Imagery: Buff Trump Defeats America

Trump as cyborg, samurai, and muscular superhero — the official NFT trading card promotional imagery. Creative Bloq called the series "the year's biggest design crime. CollectTrumpCards.com / NFT INT LLC

Photo by: CNN

The NFT artwork is worth examining. The cards depict Trump as:

  • A cyborg

  • A samurai warrior

  • Dethroning Abraham Lincoln on the Lincoln Memorial

  • Throwing lightning bolts

  • Breaking shackles

  • Dancing

  • Holding Bitcoin icons

Creative Bloq called them “the year’s biggest design crime,” noting: “When Donald Trump’s mugshot was taken in August it was for alleged election racketeering, but perhaps it could also have been for crimes against design.”

The images are cringy, bizarre fantasy versions of Trump as a muscular hero—despite Trump being a 77-year-old man who famously avoids exercise and lives on fast food.

The Market Response: From Boom to Bust

After the mugshot arrest, Trump’s original NFTs surged in value.

August 24, 2023 (arrest day):

  • 12:01 AM: Each Trump NFT valued at 0.137 Ethereum ($181)

  • 11:00 PM: Surged to 0.223 Ethereum ($294) after mugshot release

  • Next day: Settled at 0.189 Ethereum ($249)

The mugshot drove a 62% single-day price increase—Trump literally profited from his arrest.

But the market cooled considerably. By March 2024, Trump’s NFT collections were pulled from OpenSea, the major NFT trading platform, until end of year. Floor prices for Series 1 dropped to around 0.12 Ethereum ($258).

The Mugshot Edition had transfer restrictions until December 31, 2024—buyers couldn’t even sell them for over a year.

The Legal Disclaimer: “Not Political”

Despite being sold by a presidential candidate and depicting him as a superhero, the NFT site includes this disclaimer:

“Trump Digital Trading Cards (NFTs) are intended as collectible items for individual enjoyment only, not for investment purposes.”

The cards also claim to be “not political and have nothing to do with any political campaign”—despite offering access to a former president and leading presidential candidate through Mar-a-Lago dinners.

This raises obvious questions about campaign finance. By offering buyers in-person access to Trump without campaign finance guardrails, Trump essentially created a way to purchase face-time with a presidential candidate outside federal campaign finance rules.

Beyond NFTs: The Crypto Empire

Trump didn’t stop at NFTs. By 2025, the grift had metastasized into something far larger—a full-spectrum cryptocurrency empire operated from the White House itself.

Series 4 (August 2024): The Debate Suit

The “America First Collection” launched in August 2024, again at $99 per card. The new hook: pieces of the suit Trump wore for his June 27 debate against Joe Biden. Trump cut that suit into 2,024 pieces as well—the debate suit following the mugshot suit into the merchandise pipeline. Perks included limited-edition gold sneakers for bulk buyers.

The TRUMP and MELANIA Meme Coins

The TRUMP meme coin launched January 17, 2025 — three days before inauguration. It peaked near $75, then lost 96% of its value. Buyers absorbed the loss. The Trumps kept the proceeds. Source: CoinGecko / Official Trump Coin

Three days before his inauguration, on January 17, 2025, Trump launched the TRUMP meme coin. Within days it climbed to an all-time high of $73–75, briefly pushing its market capitalization to between $12 billion and $15 billion. Two days later, Melania launched her own coin, which peaked near $13.73.

The coins have since collapsed. TRUMP now trades near $2.80–2.90—a decline of approximately 96% from its peak. MELANIA trades between $0.10 and $0.17—down roughly 99%. The buyers who flooded in at the top lost nearly everything. The Trumps kept the cash.

World Liberty Financial: The DeFi Extraction Machine

Through World Liberty Financial, a decentralized finance platform launched in 2024, the Trump family extended the grift into institutional crypto infrastructure. The platform offers a stablecoin (USD1) and a governance token (WLFI). According to a Wall Street Journal report, the Trump family receives 75% of net proceeds from token sales.

The WLFI token is now down approximately 76% from its peak. The USD1 stablecoin has grown—it now ranks as the sixth-largest stablecoin by market capitalization—meaning the extraction machine is still running, even as early token buyers nurse their losses.

World Liberty Financial — the Trump family's decentralized finance platform. Per the Wall Street Journal, the family receives 75% of net token proceeds. The WLFI governance token is down 76% from its peak. Source: World Liberty Financial / CoinGecko

The Meme Coin Gala: Selling Presidential Access Through Crypto

The most brazen iteration arrived in early 2026: a gala at Mar-a-Lago for top TRUMP coin holders. Purchase enough of the president’s meme coin, and you get dinner with the president. It is the NFT Mar-a-Lago dinner model—selling face-time with a candidate outside campaign finance guardrails—now running in real time from the presidency itself, dressed up as crypto community building.

The campaign finance question that was obvious in 2023 is now urgent: no FEC action, no ethics enforcement, no fraud charges.

The Accountability That Never Came

Trump faced no consequences for commodifying his criminality:

No Campaign Finance Violations: Despite offering access to a presidential candidate, no FEC action was taken against the NFT sales structure—or the meme coin gala.

No Ethics Violations: There are no post-presidency ethics rules preventing former presidents from selling access or profiting from their office. And apparently no in-office rules either.

No Fraud Charges: Despite the NFTs losing value, the meme coins collapsing 96–99%, and transfer restrictions on multiple series, no securities or fraud charges were filed.

No Public Shame: Trump’s base embraced the grift, seeing it as entrepreneurship rather than exploitation.

The mugshot that should have been a low point—arrested for allegedly attempting to overturn an election—became Trump’s highest single-day fundraising success and spawned a multi-million-dollar crypto empire.

What It Means: Criminality as Brand

Trump has perfected something unique in American political history: turning legal accountability into profit.

Every indictment drove donations. Every arrest generated merchandise. Every court appearance became content for fundraising emails. And now, every instrument of executive power—crypto regulation, SEC enforcement priorities, Justice Department resources—has been bent to protect the empire that sells access to that same executive power.

The system had no answer. Campaign finance law didn’t cover NFTs. Ethics rules don’t apply to ex-presidents. Fraud statutes weren’t triggered by overpriced digital collectibles. And a sitting president who controls his own regulators is, for practical purposes, unregulatable.

Trump turned the American justice system into a business opportunity. And it worked.

Conclusion: The Grift Never Stops

From emoluments violations during his presidency to NFT scams after leaving office to meme coins launched from the Oval Office anteroom, Trump has spent decades perfecting the art of monetizing power, celebrity, and victimhood.

The mugshot suit—cut into 2,024 pieces and sold to NFT buyers for $4,653 per bundle—is the perfect metaphor. Trump took the physical evidence of his arrest for allegedly attempting to overturn an election and turned it into “the most historically significant artifact in United States history.”

He didn’t face accountability. He sold pieces of his suit.

He didn’t accept responsibility. He raised $4 million in 24 hours.

He didn’t learn a lesson. He launched a crypto empire that collapsed 96% and kept the money anyway.

This is Trump’s genius: every moment of potential accountability becomes a profit opportunity. Every lawsuit is “political persecution” that drives donations. Every indictment is content for fundraising. Every arrest is merchandise. And every regulatory agency you control is one less obstacle to the next grift.

And American voters, the justice system, campaign finance regulators, and ethics watchdogs have proven utterly incapable of stopping it.

The grift never stops. And Trump never faces consequences.

Because the grift is the consequence. Instead of jail or fines or accountability, Trump gets millions of dollars and a White House comeback.

He cut up his mugshot suit and sold it to supporters. Then he hung his mugshot in a golden frame outside the Oval Office. Then he launched a meme coin.

That’s not accountability. That’s victory.
