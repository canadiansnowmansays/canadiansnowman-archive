Introduction: Why This Trial Matters — And Why It Cannot Be Watched Alone

April 29, 2026

A companion series to The Asimov Gap, CanadianSnowmanSays

On April 28, 2026, in a federal courthouse in downtown Oakland, California, a nine-person jury was seated to hear what may be the most consequential corporate governance case in the history of artificial intelligence.

The case is Musk v. Altman. The plaintiff is the world’s richest man. The defendant is the chief executive of the world’s most influential AI company. The question before the court is whether a public promise — made in 2015, in writing, to build artificial intelligence for the benefit of all humanity rather than for the profit of any particular group or company — means anything in a courtroom.

The answer will not resolve the AI governance crisis. It will not produce the regulatory architecture that democratic societies need to govern transformative technology. It will not close the Asimov Gap — the widening distance between what AI can do and what any public institution is empowered to prevent, constrain, or direct.

But it will produce a record. And the record will matter.

What The Oakland Transcript Is

This is a real-time trial diary. It will be published as frequently as the proceedings warrant — daily when testimony is substantive, less frequently when the courtroom is occupied with procedural matters. It will draw on wire reporting, newspaper accounts, and courtroom dispatches from the journalists covering the trial in Oakland, synthesized through the analytical framework this Substack has been building since May 2025.

The Oakland Transcript is not a summary service. If you want a summary of who said what on a given day, the New York Times live updates will serve you better. What this diary offers is context — the accumulated weight of thirty-one essays on AI governance, applied to the specific testimony, exhibits, and rulings emerging from Courtroom 4 of the Ronald V. Dellums Federal Building.

Each dispatch will be organized around what the day’s proceedings revealed, what they confirmed, and what they changed — in terms of the trial’s likely outcome, and in terms of our understanding of how AI governance has failed and might yet be recovered.

Why This Trial Cannot Be Watched Alone

The Asimov Gap series began, in Part 1, with a simple observation: the gap between what AI can do and what democratic institutions are empowered to govern is widening faster than any regulatory response can close it. Thirty-one parts later, that observation has accumulated a substantial evidentiary record.

The Farrow-Marantz investigation — that I will examine in Part 28 — documented the governance vacuum at OpenAI through eighteen months of reporting, more than a hundred interviews, and hundreds of pages of internal records. It found a company that had built elaborate guardrails to keep its CEO in check, watched him navigate around them, and then replaced the board that tried to enforce them with people of his choosing. It found an outside law firm investigation that produced no written report. It found a culture in which the most common description of the chief executive, offered unprompted by people who had worked closely with him, was that he was a pathological liar.

The Musk v. Altman trial is, among other things, the legal proceeding that will put that record under oath.

The AGI clause deletion — that I shall discuss in Part 30 — removed the last contractual mechanism that would have required the parties to pause and reckon with what AGI’s arrival means. It was deleted on the morning the trial opened. The founding charter is in court. The last governance clause is in the bin. These are not unrelated events.

Project Glasswing and the Mythos announcement — examined in Part 25 — established that Anthropic has built a model capable of compromising every major piece of software infrastructure on earth, and has decided, unilaterally, who gets to use it. The Bank of England warned it could “crack the whole cyber-risk world open.” Canada’s finance minister compared it to closing the Strait of Hormuz. The trial proceeding simultaneously in Oakland involves the company that was founded specifically because people inside OpenAI did not trust its leadership to handle exactly this kind of capability responsibly.

The Atlantic’s examination of AI nationalization — to be discussed in Part 30 — mapped the Scylla and Charybdis between private oligarchy and government seizure, with no navigable passage between them. The Pentagon is simultaneously threatening to invoke the Defence Production Act against Anthropic and negotiating to bring it back into the fold. Google has signed a Pentagon deal for “any lawful governmental purpose.” OpenAI’s engineers are being deployed alongside the military. Soft nationalization is not coming — it is already here.

None of this is background to the trial. All of it is the trial’s context — the landscape of governance failure within which twelve people in Oakland are being asked to determine whether a specific public promise was broken, and what that means for the rule of law in the AI era.

The Analytical Framework

The Asimov Gap series has developed several analytical tools that will be applied throughout The Oakland Transcript:

The Asimov Frame — Isaac Asimov understood that governance of transformative technology requires laws that are explicit, public, and prior — established before the capability exists, not improvised after the damage is done. The trial in Oakland is, precisely, governance improvised after the damage is done. Ultra vires — the nineteenth century legal doctrine on which Musk’s case rests — is not an AI governance framework. It is what you use when no AI governance framework was built.

The TACO Framework — Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. The series developed this framework to describe how the Trump administration navigates trade disputes. It applies with equal precision to how AI companies navigate governance pressure. Watch for it in the testimony as witnesses describe how OpenAI responded to internal concerns, board pressure, and regulatory inquiry.

The Governance Vacuum — The trial will produce evidence. Evidence is not governance. A verdict is not regulation. The Transcript will track, throughout the proceedings, the gap between what the trial reveals and what any institution is empowered to do about it.

The Canadian Outsider Perspective — This series has always been written from the vantage point of someone watching American AI governance from across a border that has its own exposure to everything being decided in Oakland. Canada has no more democratic control over the outcome of this trial than any other nation that will live with the technology it governs. That distance is a feature, not a limitation.

The Cast

For readers coming to The Oakland Transcript without the full Asimov Gap context, here is the minimum you need to understand what is happening in Courtroom 4:

Sam Altman — Chief executive of OpenAI, co-founder of the company with Musk in 2015. The Farrow-Marantz investigation documented, across eighteen months of reporting, a consistent pattern of telling different things to different people, navigating around governance structures, and operating in ways that prompted board members who had worked closely with him to use the word “sociopathic” unprompted. He will testify under oath.

Elon Musk — Co-founder of OpenAI, left in 2018 after a power struggle with Altman. Now runs xAI, a direct competitor. Has been found partly liable by a separate jury for defrauding investors in his Twitter acquisition. Posted more than two dozen times about the trial on the platform he owns before opening statements began. Judge Gonzalez Rogers asked him to stop. He agreed. He is the world’s richest man and the plaintiff in this case, and a 2018 email in the trial record shows he told OpenAI leadership that merging with Tesla was “the only path that could even hope to hold a candle to Google” — the same conclusion he is now suing Altman for reaching.

Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers — Presided over Epic Games v. Apple. Has a demonstrated tolerance for complex technology cases with governance implications. Called Musk to the bench before testimony began to discuss social media. Her question: “How can we get things done without you making things worse outside the courtroom?” It is a reasonable description of the AI governance problem in general.

Satya Nadella — Microsoft’s chief executive. His company is named in Musk’s complaint as a co-defendant. He will testify about the partnership agreement, the AGI clause — now deleted — and what he was told during the five-day crisis in November 2023 when the board fired Altman and then reinstated him.

Ilya Sutskever — OpenAI’s former chief scientist, who sent the board a set of memos running to approximately seventy pages before the firing. The first item on the list: “Sam exhibits a consistent pattern of lying.” If those memos are read into the record as trial exhibits, they will constitute the most significant public accountability moment in OpenAI’s history.

Helen Toner and Tasha McCauley — The board members who voted to fire Altman in November 2023. Their testimony about what they knew, when they knew it, and what the law firm investigation was actually asked to determine will fill in the gaps that the 800-word press release left empty.

Shivon Zilis — Former OpenAI board member, mother of at least four of Musk’s children, his self-described romantic partner, and — according to OpenAI — the person Musk used to keep tabs on the company after he left. Her testimony will be watched closely by both sides.

A Note on Sources

The Oakland Transcript will draw on reporting from the New York Times, The Atlantic, Axios, Platformer, and other publications covering the trial. All sources will be credited. Where I am synthesizing multiple accounts, I will say so. Where testimony is quoted, it will be attributed to the court record.

This diary is written from Toronto, not Oakland. I am not in the courtroom. What I bring to the proceedings is not proximity but context — thirty-one essays of accumulated analysis that provide the framework within which the daily testimony can be understood.

The trial is expected to last approximately four weeks. The Oakland Transcript will run for as long as it does.

A Final Note on Why This Matters

The Oakland Transcript does not exist because the trial will resolve the AI governance crisis. It exists because the trial is the closest thing to a public accountability moment that the AI era has yet produced — and because watching it carefully, in the context of everything that has led to it, is the least a Canadian outsider with thirty-one essays of accumulated analysis can do.

The microphones are working now. Let’s see what gets said.
