The Masculinity Con

Part 3: The Influence Laundering Machine: How Conservative Media Legitimized Alleged Traffickers

December 16, 2025

In 2024, HBO and the BBC released “Adolescence” - a critically acclaimed international miniseries about teenagers navigating modern life. The show depicted young male characters discussing and being influenced by Andrew Tate’s content, treating him as a significant cultural force shaping adolescent masculinity.

This wasn’t a niche reference in conservative media. This was mainstream entertainment on HBO and BBC, acknowledging that Tate had become influential enough with young men that a series about adolescence had to address his impact. The miniseries became a global phenomenon, watched by millions across continents.

Think about the timing. This happened while Tate was under criminal indictment in Romania, facing charges involving 30+ women across multiple countries. A prestige international production had to acknowledge him as culturally significant enough to shape how millions of teenagers understood gender and relationships.

When mainstream entertainment treats someone as a defining cultural force for modern adolescence - even while they face criminal indictments in three countries - the normalization is complete.

But how did we get here? How does someone go from facing trafficking charges to being referenced in HBO/BBC productions as an unavoidable part of youth culture?

The answer: a sophisticated influence laundering operation by conservative media that sanitized the Tates’ reputation while gaining access to their massive young male audience. Both sides got what they needed - the Tates got legitimacy and narrative control, while conservative influencers got audience expansion and engagement metrics.

This is the story of that laundering machine.

The First Wash: Tucker Carlson Sets The Template

When Joseph McBride, the Tate brothers’ lawyer, pitched Tucker Carlson on interviewing his clients in Romania, he knew exactly what he was selling:

“I know we’ll break the internet.”

He was right. The 2023 interview has drawn more than 112 million views. It helped transform Andrew and Tristan Tate from fringe internet personalities facing serious criminal charges into conservative cause célèbres.

Tucker Carlson wasn’t an obvious fit for the Tates. He was a proud husband who promoted Christian family values and traditional morality. The Tates ran a pornography operation that earned £21 million by allegedly coercing women through romance fraud. But Carlson had been ousted from Fox News and was building his own show on Twitter/X. He needed content that would drive massive engagement. The Tates had millions of followers there.

The 2.5-hour conversation focused almost entirely on shared beliefs about what they characterized as a left-wing war on masculinity. They discussed censorship, cancel culture, the persecution of strong men, the feminization of society. It was red meat for Carlson’s audience and perfect positioning for the Tates.

What the interview didn’t include: any serious examination of the criminal allegations. Carlson didn’t press Tate on the specifics of his pornography operation. He didn’t ask about the text messages in the indictment. He didn’t inquire about the 30+ women prosecutors said had been coerced since 2014.

Instead, Carlson actively misrepresented the charges:

“Well, the fact that you’re not accused of a sex crime or violence, which I think most people don’t really understand, and they can look it up. I mean, but you’re not actually accused of rape, selling anyone, pimping, O.K.”

This was comprehensively false. The 360-page indictment explicitly charged Andrew Tate with rape. It detailed seven women trafficked through “Lover Boy” methods involving romance fraud, psychological coercion, surveillance, and violence. But 112 million people heard Carlson’s version instead.

When The Times asked Carlson about this recently, he apologized for any misstatement but added: “It doesn’t change my view that the Tates, whatever their personal behavior, had a message worth hearing.”

That phrase - “whatever their personal behavior” - does extraordinary work. It suggests the allegations are somehow separate from the message about masculinity. But you can’t separate Andrew Tate’s philosophy about women (”they’re just not built to be completely independent creatures”) from allegations he coerced women through psychological manipulation. The worldview and the alleged conduct aren’t contradictory - they’re consistent.

Carlson’s interview established the template:

  1. Focus on shared ideological ground

  2. Frame the charges as political persecution

  3. Minimize or misrepresent the actual allegations

  4. Position the interview as brave truth-telling

Most importantly, it gave the Tates something they desperately needed: legitimacy through association with an established conservative voice. If Tucker Carlson was willing to fly to Romania and spend 2.5 hours with them, maybe they weren’t the criminals prosecutors claimed. Maybe they were victims.

The Cascade: How Each Validator Made The Next Easier

Charlie Kirk, the conservative youth organizer and Trump adviser, watched the Carlson interview and had what he described as an epiphany.

“Now I see why he’s popular,” Kirk said on his podcast. He acknowledged the pornography business was wrong, expressing disgust at that aspect. But Kirk agreed that masculinity was under siege. “He is hitting on something that you’re not allowed to say, where there’s a lot of truth to it.”

Notice the progression: Carlson misrepresented the charges entirely. Kirk acknowledged problematic behavior but embraced the message anyway. The Overton window was moving.

Candace Owens took it further. She flew to Romania and conducted her own interview with Tate. She told her audience it was a mistake to judge him for past comments or ostentatious content. “This is how guys have fun,” she said, “the same way that girls sit around and talk about ‘Real Housewives.’”

The normalization was complete. Carlson had made it acceptable to platform the Tates. Kirk had made it acceptable to separate their message from their business. Owens made it acceptable to dismiss the entire package as just “how guys have fun.”

Each validator made it easier for the next. And all of them helped the Tates execute the crucial reframe: this wasn’t about criminal allegations involving 30+ women - it was about censorship, political persecution, and defending masculinity from woke attacks.

This is accommodation psychology in action - the same pattern documented in your Trump Accountability Project. Each step seems defensible in isolation:

  • Carlson: “I’m just interviewing him, people can decide for themselves”

  • Kirk: “I disagree with his business but his message resonates”

  • Owens: “We shouldn’t rush to judgment based on allegations”

But the cumulative effect creates a permission structure. By the time casual conservatives encountered the Tates, they’d been pre-validated by trusted voices. The criminal allegations had become background noise. The story was about masculinity under attack, not women alleging exploitation.

And it worked. By the time “Adolescence” aired, the Tates weren’t just controversial figures conservatives debated - they were cultural forces that mainstream storytellers felt compelled to address.

The Mutual Exploitation Model

This wasn’t charity. Both sides gained from the arrangement.

What the Tates got:

  • Legitimacy through association with established conservative voices

  • Narrative reframing from “accused traffickers” to “censored truth-tellers”

  • Access to Republican political networks

  • Path to Trump world and eventual diplomatic intervention

  • Massive platform expansion beyond their core audience

What influencers got:

  • Access to the Tates’ millions of young male followers

  • Content that generated extraordinary engagement (112 million views for Carlson)

  • Positioning as “anti-censorship” champions

  • Street cred for platforming controversial figures

  • Audience expansion into male-dominated spaces that were previously hard to reach

The transaction was explicit. Justin Waller, who has helped run the Tates’ courses and is widely seen as the “third brother,” developed a relationship with Barron Trump. About Barron, Waller said: “He’s not a bad ally to have — let’s be frank.”

That quote captures the calculation perfectly. This wasn’t about truth or justice or even about what happened to 30+ women. It was about political utility. The Tates could deliver young male voters. That made them valuable. Everything else became negotiable.

Conservative media figures made a choice: the Tates’ massive young male audience was worth more than the allegations from three dozen women across multiple countries.

The Reach Beyond Politics: Why Adolescence Matters

The “Adolescence” reference revealed something crucial: the Tates’ audience wasn’t just political. It wasn’t just young conservatives or MAGA supporters. It was teenagers globally, across political spectrums, who were encountering his content and messaging.

This is what successful influence laundering looks like. When mainstream entertainment has to acknowledge your cultural impact, when you become a reference point that global audiences understand, you’ve transcended “alleged criminal” and become “cultural phenomenon.”

The show’s creators weren’t endorsing Tate - they were documenting his influence on young men. But documentation itself confers a form of legitimacy: he matters enough to be part of the conversation about how modern teenagers develop their identities.

This is why the Tates were valuable to Trump world - they could deliver young male voters not because those men were already conservative, but because Tate was reaching them first with masculinity content, which could then be channeled toward political engagement.

Conservative media figures like Tucker Carlson and Charlie Kirk understood this. They weren’t just validating Tate to their existing audiences - they were gaining access to his massive youth following, many of whom weren’t politically engaged yet. The Tates were a pipeline: capture young men through masculinity content, validate them through conservative media, channel them toward political action.

By the time a globally-watched miniseries depicts teenage boys discussing Andrew Tate as casually as they’d discuss any other internet personality, the criminal allegations have faded into background noise. The pattern across multiple countries becomes trivia. What remains is cultural relevance.

And it happened while prosecutors in three countries were building cases involving dozens of alleged victims.

The Accommodation Framework: TACO in Action

Your TACO methodology (Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate) perfectly describes what happened:

Threaten: The Tates positioned themselves as victims of persecution, creating urgency. If “they” can silence the Tates for telling uncomfortable truths about masculinity, they can silence anyone. The threat wasn’t just to the Tates - it was to free speech itself.

Accommodate: Conservative influencers rationalized platforming them:

  • “We’re defending free speech”

  • “War on masculinity is real, even if his methods are wrong”

  • “Charges may be political”

  • “People deserve to hear both sides”

Each accommodation made the next easier. Carlson’s misrepresentation meant Kirk didn’t have to address the full allegations. Kirk’s embrace of the message meant Owens could normalize the entire package.

Capitulate: Full embrace followed. The Tates became regular features in conservative media. Donald Trump Jr. went on livestream with Andrew: “They just want to silence you.” Trump himself shared videos of Tristan defending him: “Thank you!”

Obfuscate: When challenged, defenders muddied the waters:

  • “These are just allegations”

  • “Romanian justice system may be corrupt”

  • “We don’t know what really happened”

  • “Innocent until proven guilty”

The obfuscation ignores the pattern: 30+ women, three countries, over a decade, including minors, with the accused’s own text messages providing evidence. At some point, “we don’t know what really happened” becomes willful blindness.

The Trump Parallel: Validated By Association

The Tates explicitly modeled themselves after Trump’s persecution narrative. Both kicked off social media. Both criminally prosecuted. Both victims of political attacks.

But they went beyond modeling - they secured actual Trump world endorsement:

September 2023: Trump shares video of Tristan defending him on Truth Social. “Thank you!” Trump wrote. This wasn’t passive - Trump was actively supporting them publicly.

June 2024: Donald Trump Jr. tells Andrew on livestream: “You’ve got people attacking you as far as I’m concerned. They just want to silence you.”

Spring 2024: Barron Trump hosts influencer dinner at Mar-a-Lago. Justin Waller attends, developing relationship with Barron. Later, Waller and Barron speak with Andrew via Zoom while Barron gets suit fitted. They discuss the Romanian case and supporting Trump’s campaign.

The validation ran both ways. The Tates got Trump family endorsement, which provided massive legitimacy. The Trumps got access to the Tates’ millions of young male followers - exactly the demographic that helped secure the 2024 election.

This is how influence laundering works in the Trump era: If you deliver a useful voting bloc and wrap yourself in culture war rhetoric, the movement will defend you reflexively. The criminal allegations become evidence of persecution rather than misconduct. Each attack makes you more valuable to defenders because it proves the enemy is targeting you.

Conservative media didn’t just fail to investigate the allegations - they actively misrepresented them while incorporating the Tates into the movement’s infrastructure.

When The Rift Finally Came

The accommodation worked until the Tates were actually released from Romania in February 2025 and flew to the United States. Suddenly, the abstraction became concrete. These weren’t just controversial figures in Romania - they were in America, celebrating their freedom, and some conservatives finally drew a line.

Megyn Kelly: “This actually is toxic masculinity, people. This is the real version of it.”

Ben Shapiro: “America does not need more self-proclaimed pimps.”

Ron DeSantis: “Florida is not a place where you’re welcome with that type of conduct in the air.” Florida’s attorney general announced a criminal investigation.

Josh Hawley: “I certainly don’t think that we should be using any influence in our government to try to get him out of what seemed to be extremely serious charges in Romania.”

Some likened the Tates to Jeffrey Epstein - another wealthy man with international connections, allegations of systematic exploitation, and political allies who looked the other way.

The backlash revealed something crucial: many conservatives had been willing to give the Tates benefit of the doubt while they were confined in Romania, facing charges that seemed abstract. But their actual presence in America, their celebration of freedom despite the allegations, their continued promotion of their business - that made accommodation harder.

Roger Stone deleted a tweet saying Richard Grenell had secured their release. Alina Habba, who’d told Andrew on a podcast “I got your back over here,” claimed she had “no part” in lifting the travel ban. Even some who’d platformed them started distancing.

But the damage was done. For two years, the Tates had been legitimized, normalized, and incorporated into conservative movement infrastructure. Their courses continued selling. Their following continued growing. Their brand was stronger than ever, precisely because they’d been validated by major conservative voices.

The HBO/BBC “Adolescence” series had already cemented their cultural relevance. Millions of teenagers globally had been exposed to their content. The pipeline from masculinity content to political engagement was established and operating.

The Cost of Influence Laundering

What gets legitimized when Tucker Carlson misrepresents charges to 112 million people?

What gets normalized when Charlie Kirk says a pornography trafficker “is hitting on something that you’re not allowed to say”?

What gets excused when Candace Owens calls it “how guys have fun, the same way that girls sit around and talk about ‘Real Housewives’”?

What gets validated when HBO/BBC acknowledges someone as culturally significant enough to shape adolescent identity, even while facing charges involving 30+ women?

The cost isn’t abstract:

For victims: Their allegations become background noise. Their testimony gets dismissed as part of a persecution narrative. Their trauma becomes politically inconvenient.

For the justice system: Romanian prosecutors’ work is undermined. British charges are questioned. American investigation faces political pressure. Diplomatic intervention overrides prosecutorial independence.

For the conservative movement: It establishes that political utility matters more than accountability. If you deliver a useful demographic and position yourself correctly, the movement will defend you regardless of allegations.

For young men: They learn that exploitation can be rebranded as entrepreneurship, that coercion can be called leadership, that “pimping hoes degree” is acceptable business education if you wrap it in masculinity rhetoric.

For the culture: It normalizes the idea that anyone facing serious allegations is probably being persecuted, that patterns don’t matter, that evidence is optional if the politics are right.

Tucker Carlson didn’t have to fly to Romania. He chose to. He chose to misrepresent the charges. He chose to give the Tates 2.5 hours of soft-ball questions and a platform to 112 million people.

That’s not journalism. That’s influence laundering. And when Carlson says “whatever their personal behavior, had a message worth hearing,” he’s telling you political messaging matters more than allegations involving 30+ women, including minors.

Conclusion: Complicity By Choice

The conservative media figures who platformed the Tates made a series of choices:

They chose not to seriously investigate the allegations before providing platforms.

They chose to minimize, misrepresent, or ignore evidence while focusing on culture war narratives.

They chose to prioritize access to young male audiences over basic journalistic standards.

They chose to normalize alleged criminal conduct by treating it as political persecution.

They chose to validate men accused by 30+ women because those men delivered a useful voting demographic.

None of this was inevitable. They could have said: “These are serious allegations involving multiple countries and dozens of women. Until this is resolved, we’re not providing a platform.” They could have investigated before platforming. They could have pressed on specifics instead of accepting culture war framing.

But that would have cost them access to millions of young male followers. That would have positioned them as part of the “cancel culture” they rail against. That would have complicated their masculinity-under-siege narrative.

So they made the other choice. They laundered the Tates’ reputation while expanding their own reach. Both sides got what they needed.

The only people who didn’t get what they needed were the 30+ women whose allegations got buried under 112 million views of Tucker Carlson misrepresenting criminal charges, and the millions of teenagers globally who encountered Tate’s content through social media and had it validated by mainstream cultural recognition.

When someone tells you they’re just “letting people decide for themselves,” ask what information they’re providing for that decision. When they misrepresent charges, minimize allegations, and focus entirely on culture war themes, they’re not enabling informed choice - they’re engineering a preferred conclusion.

That’s influence laundering. And it worked so well that a prestige HBO/BBC production had to acknowledge Tate as a defining cultural force for modern adolescence, even while he faced criminal indictments in three countries.
