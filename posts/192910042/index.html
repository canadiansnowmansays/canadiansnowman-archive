The Radio and the Static

Part 30: Pam Bondi discovers that being tuned to one channel is fine — until the signal starts to break up

April 2, 2026

I. The Binder as Destiny

Ms. Bondi did not make eye contact with Epstein victims seated behind her during a House Judiciary Committee hearing last month.Credit...Eric Lee for The New York Times

There is something almost Shakespearean about Pam Bondi’s relationship with binders. Not the tragedy of Hamlet, exactly. More the farce of Malvolio — the character convinced of his own importance, caught by his own props, undone by his own theatrical instincts in the worst possible lighting.

As Florida attorney general, binders worked. They conveyed authority. They suggested preparation. In Tallahassee, a well-deployed binder signals gravitas. In a House Judiciary Committee hearing room with sixty iPhone cameras and a Democratic minority that has been waiting sixteen months for exactly this moment, a binder is a confession waiting to happen.

On the table before her in February: three binders. Splayed open. Visible to any photographer with a telephoto lens. Containing, among other things, pre-written put-downs of Democratic lawmakers.

Her staff had warned her to go paperless. She was resistant to change.

This, in miniature, is the Bondi story: a politician who learned her craft in a media environment that no longer exists, deployed into one that has never been less forgiving, performing a loyalty that earns her continued employment while costing her everything else.

II. One Channel

The New York Times published a detailed reconstruction of Bondi’s increasingly precarious position on April 1, 2026 — and whatever one thinks of the sourcing conventions of anonymous officials speaking on condition of anonymity, the picture that emerges is coherent and consistent: an attorney general who functions, as the Times puts it, like “a radio built to pick only one channel.”

The channel is Donald Trump’s approval.

The problem with single-channel radios is that they have no redundancy. When the signal degrades — and Trump’s signals always, eventually, degrade — there is no fallback frequency. Bondi has no institutional base at the Justice Department, having spent her tenure dismantling its independent culture. She has no congressional protection, having burned those bridges with what even Republican allies describe as messaging misfires. She has no public credibility on the Epstein question, having squandered it with the white binders of nothingness in February 2025.

What she has is Trump’s current mood. Which, per four separate sources speaking to the Times, has recently included discussions of firing her and replacing her with Lee Zeldin, currently administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency.

The Dow is over 50,000 now! she told the Judiciary Committee in February, apparently as a defense of her performance as attorney general.

The static is audible.

III. The Confrontation That Wasn’t Supposed to Happen

Kash Patel, the F.B.I. director, right, and his former deputy, Dan Bongino, left, both pushed for a much broader release of Epstein material after finding little in the files that added to what was already widely known about the president’s interactions with Jeffrey Epstein. Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

The most revealing new detail in the Times reconstruction is the July 2025 confrontation between Bondi and Dan Bongino, then FBI deputy director.

Bongino — not a figure historically associated with governmental transparency or media restraint — had been pushing for a far broader release of Epstein documents. His reasoning was straightforward: the files contained little that added to what was already publicly known about Trump’s relationship with Epstein, and releasing them would pre-empt the political disaster that was otherwise building.

Bondi was more cautious. She cited her background as a trafficking prosecutor in Tampa — a legitimate credential, it must be said — and argued that releasing unredacted documents risked exposing victims, including children.

The confrontation became angry. Bondi accused Bongino of leaking unflattering information about her to the press.

Let us pause on the geometry of this moment. The FBI deputy director, a man whose media career was built on aggressive counter-narrative and loyalty performance, was urging the attorney general toward transparency. The attorney general, a former prosecutor who made her name on victims’ rights, was urging restraint. And the political valence of each position was the exact inverse of what their public personas would suggest.

This is what the Epstein files have done to everyone who touches them: inverted their stated positions, exposed their actual ones.

IV. The Subpoena That Cannot Be Unrung

On March 18, 2026, five House Oversight Committee Republicans — Representatives Mace, Boebert, Burchett, Cloud, and one other — joined Democrats to vote for a subpoena requiring Bondi to testify under oath, behind closed doors, about the Epstein case.

This blindsided Republican leadership. It blindsided Bondi.

The deposition is scheduled for April 14.

Since then, Bondi has been working the phones. Several Republicans who voted for the subpoena have softened. Boebert and Burchett have retreated. Bondi and committee chairman James Comer of Kentucky have been “quietly working together to avoid the deposition” — though, the Times notes pointedly, it is unclear whether a subpoena can legally be withdrawn once issued.

This is the procedural equivalent of trying to un-ring a bell while wearing oven mitts.

Representative Nancy Mace of South Carolina, Bondi’s most vocal Republican critic, has not softened. “We want to know why the DOJ is more focused on shielding the powerful than delivering justice,” she said. The Epstein case is, in her formulation, “one of the greatest cover-ups in American history.”

Mace represents a specific gravitational field within the Republican Party — the far-right populist flank that was supposed to be satisfied by the Trump administration’s handling of this case. Instead, they are the loudest critics. The cover-up narrative has migrated from the left to the right, and it has taken root in precisely the constituency whose trust Bondi most needed to maintain.

V. What Was in the Maryland Moment

The Times piece contains a detail that deserves more attention than it has received.

At a reception for US attorneys last December, Donald Trump berated the top federal prosecutor in Maryland, Kelly O. Hayes, for failing to indict Senator Adam Schiff on mortgage fraud charges. Bondi was present. Senior officials were present. Everyone stood and watched the president of the United States demand a specific criminal indictment of a specific political opponent at what was ostensibly a social gathering.

Hayes has not charged Schiff. The case, to the extent it exists, has not produced an indictment.

The Times reports that Bondi and her deputy Todd Blanche “have gotten the president’s message.” They have stepped up investigations of ActBlue, former CIA director John Brennan, and — most troublingly — Cassidy Hutchinson, the former White House aide whose Jan. 6 testimony became one of the most consequential of the entire post-insurrection period. The stated rationale is that she lied. The unstated rationale writes itself.

This is not tangential to the Epstein story. It is the same story. The same attorney general. The same single-channel radio. The same dynamic in which loyalty to Trump requires the active weaponization of the Justice Department against his perceived enemies, and in which Bondi serves as the instrument.

The Epstein files are the thing that makes that instrument visible.

VI. The Catastrophic Miscalculation and Its Aftermath

For the record, because records matter: it was February 2025 when Bondi appeared on Fox News to hype “breaking news” on the Epstein case while claiming she had the client list on her desk. Days later, she distributed half-filled white binders labeled Epstein Files: Phase 1 to an assembly of far-right influencers at the White House.

The binders contained virtually no new information.

Her team suggested there might not be a Phase 2.

Last July, after the FBI review, she and the bureau issued a joint statement: no basis for new charges, no client list, no evidence of blackmail of prominent individuals, releasing sealed files would endanger victims.

The backlash was immediate and severe. She reversed course and reopened the inquiry.

She told Jim Jordan and other Judiciary Committee members: “All that’s left in there is child pornography, and nobody wants to see that.”

Susie Wiles, Trump’s chief of staff and Bondi’s longtime friend, told an interviewer that Bondi had “completely whiffed” in her initial handling and handed out “binders full of nothingness.”

She also recently referred to Bondi as “my sister.”

Washington friendship contains multitudes.

VII. The Expendability Calculation

The Times frames Bondi’s danger in terms of a precedent: Trump was able to quell Republican criticism of his immigration policy by removing Kristi Noem as homeland security secretary. The Noem maneuver worked. The pressure dissipated. The political cost of the firing was lower than the political cost of keeping her.

The question now being quietly asked in Republican circles is whether the same calculation applies to Bondi.

Among President Trump’s top complaints about Attorney General Pam Bondi is her handling of the Epstein files, which has become a political liability for the president among his supporters.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

The midterms are coming. Congressional Republicans who gave the administration extraordinary deference in the wake of 2024 are beginning to recalibrate. The Epstein case is the one issue on which the far-right populist base and the institutional oversight impulse briefly converge.

Lee Zeldin, the rumoured replacement, is a known quantity — a loyalist, but a more polished communicator, and a figure without Bondi’s specific credibility problem on this specific file.

Trump, per the Times, has praised Bondi’s loyalty in public and speaks with her several times a week. He said in a statement she is “a wonderful person and she is doing a good job.”

He said roughly equivalent things about Noem.

Scorecard

Bondi’s position: Formally secure. Substantively precarious. The Noem precedent is not reassuring.

The April 14 deposition: Still on the calendar. The subpoena cannot clearly be withdrawn. Watch this date.

The Bongino angle: He is no longer FBI deputy director. His push for transparency, however self-interested, proved more strategically coherent than Bondi’s caution. History will note this without irony.

The Hutchinson investigation: A significant escalation. A Jan. 6 witness facing DOJ pressure from the administration she testified against. Worth its own essay.

The binders: Still present. Three of them. In a federal hearing room. In 2026.

Senator Kennedy of Louisiana: Has not yet weighed in on whether the Dow being over 50,000 constitutes a defense to a congressional subpoena. We await his assessment.

The Epstein Files series documents the longest-running exercise in strategic transparency in American legal history — in which everything is released, nothing is revealed, and the cover-up is the press release.
