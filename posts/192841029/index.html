To the Moon, Alice

Part I: Ralph Kramden Was a Prophet

April 1, 2026

Jackie Gleason as Ralph Kramden and Audrey Meadows as Alice Kramden in The Honeymooners (1955–56). ‘One of these days… pow! Right to the moon, Alice!’ / Public domain via Wikimedia Commons. Published without copyright notice.

April 1, 2026

If you’re under 70, you may not know why this series is called “To the Moon, Alice.”

If you’re over 70, you’re already smiling.

Ralph Kramden — the blustering, big-dreaming, perpetually broke bus driver played by Jackie Gleason in The Honeymooners — had a catchphrase he deployed whenever his wife Alice punctured one of his grand schemes. He’d puff up, point at the ceiling, and thunder: “To the moon, Alice! One of these days — pow — right to the moon!”

It was a punchline. The moon was the furthest, most ridiculous, most impossible place Ralph could imagine sending someone. It was 1955. Nobody was going to the moon. The moon was a joke.

A word to those who find the title cheesy. Good. Cheese is the point.

For centuries — from medieval folklore through John Heywood’s 1546 proverb collection right up to the edge of the scientific age — the popular imagination held that the moon was made of green cheese. Not literally, perhaps, but the phrase entered the language as the gold standard of gullible belief. “He would have you believe the moon is made of green cheese” meant: this person will swallow any absurdity.

And then we went there. We brought back rocks. The rocks were not cheese. But the larger point — that the moon could be reached, walked upon, sampled and returned from — turned out to be the greater absurdity, the one that came true.

So yes. This series has a cheesy title. It references a 1955 sitcom. It opens with a Brooklyn bus driver’s punchline.

I own all of it. Because if there is one consistent lesson in the history of space exploration, it is that the things that sound most preposterous have a habit of becoming Tuesday afternoon. A factory on the moon. A catapult launching AI satellites into orbit. Humans living in the permanent shadow of a lunar crater, mining ice to make rocket fuel.

Cheesy? Absolutely. Also: happening.

Fourteen years later, on a July evening in 1969, I watched Neil Armstrong step off a ladder onto the lunar surface. Like hundreds of millions of others around the world, I sat in front of a grainy black-and-white television set and tried to process the fact that a human being — a human being — was standing on the moon. The silence in the room felt different from ordinary silence. It felt like the world holding its breath.

Ralph Kramden was a prophet. He just didn’t know it.

Today

NASA’s Artemis II crew: (left to right) Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch, and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen. This mission will send humans around the Moon for the first time since 1972. / Public domain image courtesy of NASA. Artemis II official crew poster.

This morning, at 6:24 p.m. Eastern time, NASA launches Artemis II — the first crewed mission beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in December 1972. Four astronauts will climb into an Orion capsule atop a 322-foot rocket at Kennedy Space Center in Florida and ride fire into the sky. They will fly around the moon and come home ten days later, splashing down in the Pacific on April 10th.

They will not land. This is deliberately not a landing. It is, in NASA’s careful language, a test flight — verifying life support, checking systems, practicing the maneuvers that future missions will need when attempting to actually touch down. Think of it as the dress rehearsal for the performance humanity has been slowly, expensively, and frustratingly building toward for more than fifty years.

The crew is worth knowing. Commander Reid Wiseman leads the mission. Pilot Victor Glover, who will become the first Black man to travel beyond Earth orbit, sits at his right hand. Mission Specialist Christina Koch will be the first woman to make the journey. And Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen of the Canadian Space Agency will be the first non-American ever to travel to the vicinity of the moon — something this Canadian writer notes with quiet satisfaction.

The weather forecast as of this morning is 80% favorable. The countdown clock is running.

Why Now?

The short answer is: because it took this long.

NASA has spent more than $50 billion developing the Space Launch System rocket and the Orion capsule since the program began under the first Trump administration, continued under Biden, and has now come to launch day under Trump II — which tells you something about the rarity of genuine bipartisan consensus in modern America. When both parties agree on something, it is either obviously true or enormously expensive. Going to the moon managed to be both.

The longer answer involves everything this series intends to explore.

We are not simply going back to the moon to plant another flag and collect more rocks, although we will do both. We are going back because the moon has become something it was not in 1969: strategically valuable. The frozen water in the permanently shadowed craters near the lunar south pole can be converted to drinking water, oxygen, and rocket fuel. The regolith contains helium-3, a potential fuel for future fusion power plants. And the moon itself — its surface, its orbit, the cislunar space around it — is becoming contested territory in ways that would have seemed science fiction to the people who watched Armstrong take his first small step.

China knows this. Beijing’s Chang’e program has been methodical, patient, and increasingly impressive. Chang’e 7, targeted for launch later this year, will search the lunar south pole for water ice deposits. Chang’e 8 follows in 2028, testing in-situ resource utilization — the technical phrase for learning to build things using what you find there. China has publicly declared its intention to land astronauts on the moon by 2030, and unlike some declared intentions in the space business, this one is backed by hardware, funding, and a coherent long-term roadmap. NASA’s new administrator, Jared Isaacman, stated bluntly in December that the US is in “a great competition with a rival that has the will and means to challenge American exceptionalism.” He is not wrong.

India is not standing still. Chandrayaan-3 landed near the lunar south pole in 2023 — the first nation ever to do so — and India and Japan are now collaborating on a follow-on polar mission. India’s Gaganyaan program is conducting uncrewed test flights this year as it builds toward sending Indian astronauts to space. Russia keeps trying after its Luna-25 embarrassment in 2023. Even the UAE, Japan, Israel, and an expanding cohort of nations with serious space programs are looking upward with intent.

The moon is no longer a destination. It is becoming an address.

The Billionaire Dimension

A cylindrical 220 pound (100 kilogram) launch package is shown after emerging from the end of a lunar electromagnetic launcher. Image credit: General Atomics Electromagnetic Systems

And then there is Elon Musk, who tends to complicate most sentences in which his name appears.

SpaceX is already critical to the Artemis program — the company’s Starship is one of two contracted lunar landers for future missions, alongside Blue Origin’s vehicle. But Musk’s ambitions extend considerably beyond taxi service. He has spoken openly about a permanent moon base, and has described the moon as a staging point for his ultimate obsession: Mars. His stated logic is that humanity must become multi-planetary, and that every step toward Mars runs through the moon.

There is more. In January, Musk told the assembled at Davos that “the lowest-cost place to put AI will be in space, and that will be true within two years, maybe three at the latest.” He has since merged his AI startup xAI with SpaceX, filed plans with the FCC for a constellation of up to one million orbital satellites, and told employees at an all-hands meeting that xAI would ultimately need a factory on the moon to build AI satellites — along with, and this detail deserves to be read slowly, a massive catapult to launch them into space.

Jeff Bezos, not to be outdone, has had Blue Origin file plans with the FCC for a constellation of 51,600 satellites under the name Project Sunrise — an orbital AI data center network. Google has Project Suncatcher. NVIDIA has launched its Space-1 computing platform for orbital data centers. Starcloud, a startup that became Y Combinator’s fastest-ever unicorn, has already trained a large language model in space and is planning satellites that could deliver computing power at costs competitive with terrestrial data centers — if, and this is a meaningful if, Starship achieves commercial launch economics near $500 per kilogram.

The energy crisis driving AI’s insatiable appetite for electricity is, it turns out, one of the most compelling arguments for taking computing off-planet. Solar panels in orbit receive 36% more irradiance than on Earth’s surface. There are no permitting battles, no community opposition, no grid constraints, no cooling water wars, no clouds. Space, for all its genuinely terrifying engineering challenges, has a thermodynamics problem that is at least different from Earth’s, which counts for something when you are trying to power the next generation of artificial intelligence.

I wrote about this convergence — AI, energy, and the economics of space computing — in my series The Great Energy Challenge. The thread is worth pulling further, because it leads somewhere that would have seemed not merely improbable but delusional to anyone watching Apollo 11 in 1969. We are not just going to the moon to explore it. We are, at least in the dreams of some very wealthy and energetic people, going to the moon to industrialize it.

What This Series Will Cover

To the Moon, Alice is not a series about nostalgia, though it will begin with memory. It is a series about what is actually happening and what is actually at stake.

We will follow Artemis II through its ten days, beginning today and ending with the Pacific splashdown on April 10th. We will examine what success looks like, what failure would mean, and what the subsequent Artemis missions — III through V, aimed at actual landings by 2028 — are designed to accomplish.

We will take seriously the geopolitics of cislunar space — the uncomfortable reality that the race to the lunar south pole is not primarily about science, and that the first nation to establish durable infrastructure near those water ice deposits will hold an advantage that is not merely symbolic.

We will look at the commercial space economy that is being built in parallel with the government programs — the data centers, the resource extraction ambitions, the supply chains, the question of who owns what in a domain that is governed by the Outer Space Treaty of 1967, a document written in a world without SpaceX, Blue Origin, or orbital AI factories.

And we will ask, periodically, what Ralph Kramden would make of all this. A bus driver from Brooklyn who used the moon as a punchline. A man who could not, in his wildest dreams, have imagined that the punchline would come true — and then come true again, and again, and again, in ways that grow less funny and more consequential with each passing year.

The launch window opens in a few hours.

To the moon, Alice.
