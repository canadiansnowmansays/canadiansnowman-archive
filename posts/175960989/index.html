The Enemies List

A History Part 2

McCarthy's American Inquisition: When Congressional Power Became a Weapon

October 12, 2025

In Part 1, we examined how Stalin and Hitler weaponized their justice systems through show trials and political courts. But Americans often resist comparisons to totalitarian regimes, believing “it can’t happen here.” So let’s examine what has happened here—in 1950s America, when Senator Joseph McCarthy turned congressional investigations into instruments of political persecution.

Between 1950 and 1954, Senator Joseph McCarthy of Wisconsin conducted one of the most notorious campaigns of political persecution in American history. His crusade didn’t use Stalin’s firing squads or Hitler’s show trials, but it weaponized congressional investigations and destroyed thousands of lives through the power of accusation alone.

McCarthy’s reign offers the most direct American precedent for understanding what’s happening today.

The Wheeling Speech: A Lie That Launched a Crusade

On February 9, 1950, McCarthy addressed the Ohio County Women’s Republican Club in Wheeling, West Virginia. It was a Lincoln Day celebration—an annual Republican political holiday. McCarthy had prepared a speech on housing policy, but former Senator Francis Love, meeting him at the Wheeling airport, suggested he speak about the perils of communism instead.

What McCarthy said next would change American history.

Waving a piece of paper before his audience, McCarthy declared: “I have here in my hand a list of 205 [State Department employees] that were known to the Secretary of State as being members of the Communist Party and who nevertheless are still working and shaping the policy of the State Department.”

The claim was sensational. The timing was perfect.

Just weeks earlier, former State Department official Alger Hiss had been convicted of perjury in connection with espionage charges. China had fallen to communism. The Soviets had detonated an atomic bomb. The Korean War loomed on the horizon. Americans were frightened, and McCarthy offered them a simple explanation: traitors within the government were causing America to lose the Cold War.

McCarthy framed the conflict in stark moral terms. This wasn’t about capitalism versus communism—it was “a final, all-out battle between communistic atheism and Christianity.” He spoke of “enemies from within” who were more dangerous than any external threat because they occupied positions of trust within the American government.

There was just one problem: McCarthy had no such list.

Whether he actually said “205” in the speech isn’t even clear—the next day’s Wheeling Intelligencer article was written from McCarthy’s prepared remarks, not the actual speech he delivered. In subsequent speeches, McCarthy’s number changed repeatedly—sometimes 205, sometimes 81, sometimes 57, sometimes 10. He displayed various “lists” both in Wheeling and later on the Senate floor, but he never made any list public.

He never produced any solid evidence that there was even one communist in the State Department.

It didn’t matter.

McCarthy’s accusations struck a chord with the American people. Republicans rode to a huge victory in the 1950 elections. McCarthy was reelected to the Senate in 1952. And for the next four years, he wielded congressional power like a weapon, destroying careers and lives with impunity.

The Machinery of Destruction

As chairman of the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations, McCarthy conducted hearings on alleged communist infiltration throughout the federal government. He investigated the State Department, the Armed Forces, the Government Printing Office, the Voice of America, overseas libraries, and numerous other institutions.

Between February 1953 and March 1954 alone, McCarthy conducted public hearings and 160 closed executive sessions. There were 395 witnesses questioned in the closed sessions, not all of whom were questioned in public hearings. The sheer volume of investigations created an atmosphere of constant fear.

How the System Worked

McCarthy’s hearings followed a predictable and devastating pattern:

Public accusation without evidence. Simply being accused of Communist sympathies was sufficient to damage or end many careers. As scholars note, “The men and women accused in both the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations and the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) hearings had little chance to exonerate themselves once their identities were revealed to the public. Simply being accused of Communist sympathies was sufficient to damage or end many careers because those accused could not otherwise argue their innocence.”

The question itself as punishment. The phrase “Are you now or have you ever been a Communist?” became synonymous with political persecution. It was an impossible trap:

  • Answering “yes” meant immediate career destruction and potential prosecution

  • Answering “no” could lead to perjury charges if the accusation was later “proven”

  • Refusing to answer by invoking Fifth Amendment rights was treated as an admission of guilt

Blacklists that destroyed lives. In many cases, simply being subpoenaed by McCarthy’s committee or HUAC was sufficient cause to be fired. Thousands of Americans lost their jobs based on nothing more than being called to testify.

Expansion beyond communists. McCarthy expanded his targets to include “loyalty risks”—people who supposedly could be compromised. This included LGBTQ individuals, who were labeled “sexual perverts” and presumed to be security threats. Over 5,000 federal workers were fired on these grounds, and thousands more were harassed and denied employment in what became known as the “lavender scare.”

HUAC and the Hollywood Blacklist

McCarthy’s Senate investigations ran parallel to the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC), which had been conducting its own witch hunts since 1938. Together, they created a comprehensive system of political persecution.

HUAC aggressively investigated Hollywood, summoning 43 people to testify about alleged communist influence in the film industry. Ten refused to testify and were cited for contempt of Congress. These “Hollywood Ten” were convicted and sentenced to prison.

The publication “Red Channels” listed 151 entertainment industry figures as suspected communists or communist sympathizers. Being listed meant career destruction. No trial. No evidence. Just accusation and unemployability.

During this period, radio and television networks succumbed to intimidation from advertisers and civic organizations. The entertainment industry was paralyzed by fear. Actors, writers, directors, and producers found themselves blacklisted—unable to work in their professions—based on nothing more than accusations or associations.

Actor Sam Jaffe was subpoenaed to appear before HUAC after being listed in Red Channels. Although the subpoena was later discharged, Jaffe was not allowed to work in radio, television, or motion pictures for seven years.

Studios produced anti-communist propaganda films to prove their loyalty. The industry gave names of people allegedly connected to communism to HUAC—colleagues turning on colleagues to save their own careers.

The chilling effect extended far beyond those directly accused. Everyone in the industry learned the lesson: stay silent, don’t question, don’t associate with anyone suspect, and above all, don’t attract attention.

The Numbers: A Nation Terrorized

The statistical toll of McCarthyism reveals the scope of destruction:

  • Hundreds imprisoned for contempt of Congress or perjury

  • 10,000 to 12,000 people lost their jobs across government and private industry

  • 5,000+ federal workers fired in the “lavender scare”

  • Thousands more harassed and denied employment

  • Countless careers destroyed in entertainment, academia, government, and other fields

For the vast majority, both the potential for them to do harm to the nation and the nature of their communist affiliation were tenuous at best. Most had done nothing wrong. Many had never been communists at all. Some had attended a single meeting decades earlier. Others were guilty only by association.

The persecution extended to all levels of society. College professors, schoolteachers, union organizers, journalists, government clerks, scientists—anyone could be targeted. Being late to work could be interpreted as sabotage. Owning books by certain authors could be evidence of subversion. Having friends who knew people who might have communist sympathies could end your career.

Most of these reprisals were initiated by trial verdicts that were later overturned, laws that were later struck down as unconstitutional, dismissals for reasons later declared illegal, and extra-judiciary procedures like informal blacklists that would come into general disrepute. But by then, many lives had been ruined permanently.

The Turning Point: Army-McCarthy Hearings

McCarthy’s crusade reached its peak—and began its decline—in 1954 when he made a fatal mistake: he attacked the United States Army.

McCarthy alleged communist infiltration of the Armed Forces, initially investigating the Army Signal Corps Engineering Laboratories at Fort Monmouth. But the investigation expanded to question whether the Army had improperly promoted a dentist who had refused to answer questions for the Loyalty Security Screening Board.

The Army fought back. It compiled evidence showing that McCarthy and his chief counsel Roy Cohn had sought special treatment for Cohn’s assistant David Schine, who had been drafted into the Army. The accusation: McCarthy and Cohn had used threats and intimidation to demand that Schine be given plum assignments and easy duty—abuse of power for personal benefit.

The resulting investigation became known as the Army-McCarthy hearings. They were broadcast on national television—on the new American Broadcasting Company network—for 36 days between March and June 1954. For the first time, millions of Americans could watch McCarthy’s tactics firsthand.

“Have You No Sense of Decency?”

The defining moment came during an exchange between McCarthy and Joseph Welch, the Army’s attorney.

McCarthy, attempting to discredit Welch, revealed that Welch’s law firm employed a young attorney named Fred Fisher who had once belonged to the National Lawyers Guild, an organization that had been accused of communist sympathies. Welch had already removed Fisher from the Army case specifically to protect the young man from this kind of attack.

McCarthy’s gratuitous revelation—exposing Fisher to career destruction for no tactical reason—was too much even for the hardened atmosphere of 1954.

The gallery burst into applause. The moment was replayed on television broadcasts across the country. Welch’s rebuke discredited McCarthy and helped turn the tide of public opinion against him.

The Censure and the End

By early 1954, McCarthy’s credibility was in tatters. The televised hearings had exposed his bullying tactics and lack of substantive evidence to the entire nation. A Gallup poll in January 1954 found that only 50 percent of Americans still supported McCarthy—down from much higher approval ratings just months earlier.

On December 2, 1954, the U.S. Senate voted 67 to 22 to censure McCarthy for “conduct... unbecoming a Member of the United States Senate.”

The censure carefully avoided criticizing McCarthy’s anti-communist lies and exaggerations. Instead, it focused on procedural matters—his refusal to appear before a Senate Subcommittee on Privileges and Elections, and his abuse of General Ralph Zwicker at his hearings. The Senate condemned McCarthy’s methods, not his mission.

But the political effect was devastating. McCarthy’s credibility was destroyed. Starved of witnesses and abandoned by fellow senators, his power collapsed.

Within three years, Joseph McCarthy was dead from alcohol abuse at age 48. The New York Times obituary noted: “After the Senate voted in December 1954 to condemn his tactics, his political power waned.”

The Lasting Legacy

“McCarthyism,” as the hunt for communists came to be known, did untold damage to American society:

  • Lives and careers destroyed based on accusations without evidence

  • A muzzling effect on domestic debate about Cold War issues

  • Millions of Americans living in fear of accusation

  • Intellectual and cultural life paralyzed by intimidation

  • Democratic norms of due process and evidence-based judgment severely damaged

The term “McCarthyism” entered the dictionary as a permanent warning. The American Heritage Dictionary defines it as:

  1. The political practice of publicizing accusations of disloyalty or subversion with insufficient regard to evidence

  2. The use of methods of investigation and accusation regarded as unfair, in order to suppress opposition

A political cartoonist named Herbert Block coined the term in 1950, writing: “Nothing [was] particularly ingenious about the term, which is simply used to represent a national affliction that can hardly be described in any other way.”

McCarthy’s efforts never uncovered a single actual communist in the U.S. government. His name became synonymous with witch hunts and baseless allegations. Yet his crusade succeeded in terrorizing the nation for four years.

Why McCarthy Matters Now

McCarthy’s crusade offers crucial lessons for understanding political persecution in democracies:

Accusation became punishment. You didn’t need to be convicted—or even formally charged. Being named, being subpoenaed, being questioned was enough to destroy your career. The process itself was the weapon.

Evidence didn’t matter. McCarthy never produced coherent or reasonable evidence. He never located a single communist in government. His charges were undocumented, and he was unable to make plausible accusations against any person or institution. Yet thousands suffered anyway.

Congressional power was weaponized. McCarthy used his position as committee chairman to conduct investigations that were actually political persecutions. The veneer of legitimate oversight masked a campaign of intimidation.

It took years to stop. From 1950 to 1954, McCarthy operated with near-impunity. Institutional resistance was weak. Most officials complied rather than resist. Only when he attacked an institution too powerful to intimidate—the U.S. Army—did the system finally push back.

The damage outlasted the perpetrator. Even after McCarthy was censured and died, the precedent remained. The blacklists continued for years. Lives remained ruined. Careers never recovered. The culture of fear persisted long after the man who created it was gone.

Most tellingly: McCarthy succeeded despite operating within a functioning democracy, with a free press, constitutional protections, and institutional checks and balances. He succeeded not through formal convictions but through the chilling effect of accusation and investigation.

The Pattern Repeats

McCarthy demonstrated that you don’t need a dictator or a police state to weaponize government power against political enemies. You just need:

  • A climate of fear (Cold War anxiety, terrorist threats, partisan polarization)

  • A demagogue willing to make baseless accusations

  • Media willing to amplify those accusations

  • Institutions unwilling or unable to resist

  • A public desperate for simple explanations of complex problems

Once these conditions exist, the machinery of persecution can operate within democratic forms. Courts continue to function. Congress continues to meet. Elections continue to occur. But the substance of democracy—the ability to dissent, to oppose, to criticize without fear of persecution—erodes.

McCarthy’s crusade ended not because institutions automatically protected democracy, but because he finally attacked a target powerful enough to fight back effectively. The Army had resources, credibility, and broad public support that individual victims lacked.

What happens when there is no institution powerful enough to resist? What happens when accusations come directly from the presidency itself, backed by control of the Justice Department?

History provides the answer—and it’s unfolding before us now.
