The Iran Crisis

"The Lights Go Out in Tehran"

Part LV: Tehran Loses Power as the 48-Hour Clock Counts Down, the Uprising That Was Supposed to End the War Never Came, and the IEA Calls This Worse Than 1973 and 1979 Combined

March 23, 2026

I. The Darkness

As this essay is published Monday morning, large parts of Tehran are without power. Israel announced overnight that it had begun a new wave of strikes targeting Iranian infrastructure in the capital. Residents across eastern, western, and northern Tehran are reporting blackouts. Iranian state media is reporting explosions across the city. Israel has provided no further details about what exactly it targeted.

The 48-hour ultimatum Trump issued Saturday night — open the Strait of Hormuz fully or the United States would strike Iran’s power plants, starting “WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST” — expires Monday evening Eastern time. The strikes on Tehran’s power infrastructure began before it expired.

Ten million people live in Tehran. Every Iranian city, town, and village is connected to the power grid, clean water, and gas for cooking and heat. The country has a robust industrial sector. “Cutting off electricity means cutting off the lifeline,” activist Golshan Fathi wrote on social media Sunday, anticipating exactly this. “Gasoline, banks, water, health care, mobile phones, disruption to vital devices like ventilators and dialysis machines, home patients with oxygen generators, medical devices, cold storage and everything.”

Iran’s response has been defiant and expansive. First Vice President Mohammad Reza Aref: “The Islamic Republic of Iran did not start this war, but it will not hesitate in defending its people and its land.” Military spokesman Ebrahim Zolfaghari vowed that if energy sites were attacked, Iran would target fuel, energy, information technology, and desalination infrastructure throughout the region. Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf went further still — warning that the Strait of Hormuz would be “completely closed” until any damaged Iranian power plants were rebuilt, and adding a threat this war had not previously seen: that US-linked financial institutions holding American government bonds would be “legitimate targets.” “U.S. treasury bonds are soaked in Iranians’ blood,” he wrote on social media. “Purchase them, and you purchase a strike on your HQ and assets.”

Iranians have been watching and waiting since Saturday night. “We are sitting and waiting to see what will happen to us in 48 hours,” Ruhollah, a 35-year-old Tehran resident, wrote in a text message Sunday. “Everyone will suffer: We will lose power, the Arabs will lose power and water.” As of Monday morning EST, the power in parts of Tehran is already out.

The war that began as a campaign against Iran’s nuclear program has now produced threats against the global financial system, strikes on a capital city’s power grid, and a 48-hour ultimatum whose clock is still running. The escalation ladder has no visible top.

II. The Uprising That Never Came

Mark Mazzetti, Julian Barnes, Edward Wong, and Ronen Bergman, writing in the New York Times, have published the most significant accountability piece of the war: a detailed reconstruction of how the belief that an Iranian uprising would follow the opening strikes was not an optimistic assumption but a specific, named plan — and how that plan failed.

David Barnea, the Mossad chief, went to Netanyahu before the war with a proposal: within days of the opening strikes, his service would likely be able to galvanize the Iranian opposition and ignite riots that could lead to the collapse of Iran’s government. He presented the same proposal to senior Trump administration officials in Washington in mid-January. Netanyahu adopted the plan. Trump embraced the optimistic outlook. Killing Iran’s leaders at the outset, followed by intelligence operations to encourage regime change, they believed, could produce a mass uprising that would bring a swift end to the war.

“Take over your government,” Trump told Iranians on the first night. “It will be yours to take.”

Three weeks later, the uprising has not materialized. American and Israeli intelligence assessments have concluded that the theocratic Iranian government is weakened but intact. Widespread fear of Iran’s military and police forces — which killed over 6,700 people during the January protests, including more than 200 children — has dampened prospects for rebellion. The Kurdish militia option, which Mossad had also planned to activate, was explicitly shut down by Trump himself on March 7: “I don’t want the Kurds going in. I don’t want to see the Kurds get hurt, get killed.”

Netanyahu vented in one security meeting days after the war began that Mossad’s operations had yet to bear fruit. He has since acknowledged the gap: “You can’t do revolutions from the air. There has to be a ground component as well. There are many possibilities for this ground component.” He declined to name them. He added: “It is too early to tell if the Iranian people will exploit the conditions we are creating for them to take to the streets.”

The CIA had assessed before the war that a full government collapse was a relatively unlikely outcome. The most likely outcome, the reports concluded, was that hard-line elements would maintain control. Mossad’s previous chief, Yossi Cohen, had explicitly calculated the gap between the size of protests needed to threaten the government and the size of actual protests since 1979, and concluded: “We came to the conclusion that we couldn’t” bridge it. His successor reversed that conclusion. The war was launched in part on that reversal.

Nate Swanson, a former Trump administration Iran negotiator: “A lot of protesters are not coming into the street because they’ll get shot. They’re going to get slaughtered. That’s one thing. But the second thing is that there’s a good chunk of people who just want a better life, and they’re sidelined right now. They don’t like the regime, but they don’t want to die opposing it. That 60 percent is going to stay home.”

The foundational premise of the war has been falsified. The war continues.

III. The Interceptor Problem

Peering Sunday at damage where a missile struck the night before in Arad, Israel.Credit...Amit Elkayam for The New York Times

Isabel Kershner, writing in the New York Times, has documented what the Dimona and Arad strikes on Saturday revealed about the most sensitive military vulnerability Israel has: its interceptor stockpile.

Out of approximately 400 ballistic missiles fired by Iran into Israeli airspace over three weeks of war, only four have penetrated defenses intact, resulting in direct hits. A 99 percent interception rate sounds reassuring. It is not reassuring enough when the remaining 1 percent hits residential neighborhoods eight miles from a nuclear weapons facility.

The Israeli news media, operating under military censorship, reported Sunday that the Arrow 3 — Israel’s most advanced interceptor, which destroys ballistic missiles outside the earth’s atmosphere — was not deployed against the missiles that struck Arad and Dimona. The reason, pieced together from expert commentary: Arrow 3 interceptors are costly and time-consuming to produce and must be used judiciously. Israel’s director general of defense had traveled to Washington this month to ask for more interceptors and munitions. It was unclear whether the Americans had agreed to provide them.

“It is not a bottomless barrel,” said Ran Kochav, a former commander of Israel’s air and missile defense forces. “When we intercept, we also have to think of the next day’s battle.”

The military’s chief of staff said on Saturday that the campaign was only “midway.” If Israel is already conserving its most advanced interceptors in week three, the arithmetic of a prolonged war becomes alarming. Iran entered the war with approximately 3,000 missiles. It has been firing at a managed pace — enough to maintain pressure without depleting its arsenal too quickly. Iran has approximately 5,000 naval mines. It produces an estimated 10,000 drones per month.

“Survival is winning for Iran,” said Nate Swanson of the Atlantic Council. “They just need to hit one tanker every so often going through the Strait — they effectively have the bottleneck, even if it’s not fully mined.”

IV. Worse Than 1973 and 1979 Combined

Fatih Birol, the head of the International Energy Agency, speaking at Australia’s National Press Club Monday, said what the data has been pointing toward for days: this crisis is worse than the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 combined.

The 1970s shocks led to a loss of 10 million barrels per day. The current crisis has already hit 11 million barrels per day. The IEA, Birol said, is in talks with governments in Asia and Europe to release more stockpiled oil. “If it is necessary, of course, we will do it.”

Patricia Cohen, writing in the New York Times, has mapped the shift that occurred when Iran struck Ras Laffan: the war moved from “stopping transit” — a temporary measure — to “attacking infrastructure, which has long-term effects.” David Goldwyn, a former US diplomat and Energy Department official: “We have moved from stopping transit, which is a temporary measure, to attacking infrastructure, which has long-term effects.”

Jason Miller, a professor of supply chain management at Michigan State: “This is by far the largest disruption of crude oil and refined products that we’ve ever seen in history. Petroleum goes into everything.” Wood Mackenzie analysts have warned that $200 a barrel is not outside the realm of possibility in 2026. “I couldn’t fathom we would not start seeing economies fall into a recession with energy prices at that point,” Miller said.

Jan-Eric Fahnrich of Rystad Energy identified the longer-term damage that extends beyond the immediate outage: “Critical Gulf energy infrastructure that was presumed to be safe is now seen as vulnerable. A precedent has been set. Buyers will price that risk for longer than the initial outage itself.”

Brent crude hit $114 Monday morning. Gas nationally is at $3.96, approaching $4. Diesel is at $5.29. The South Korean Kospi plummeted 6.5 percent Monday. Japan’s Nikkei dropped 3.5 percent. Hong Kong’s Hang Seng fell 4 percent. European stocks opened lower across the board. S&P 500 futures pointed to further losses.

Goldman Sachs warned that prices could remain elevated into 2027. The IEA says this is already worse than the shocks that reshaped the global economy fifty years ago. The administration that launched this war expecting it to last weeks has produced the largest energy supply disruption in recorded history.

V. India, Britain, and the Geometry of Exposure

Waiting for liquefied petroleum gas refills last week outside a distribution agency in Lucknow, India. Credit...Ritesh Shukla/Getty Images

Prime Minister Narendra Modi told Indian lawmakers Monday that the war in Iran was having an “unprecedented” effect on his country and has disrupted its access to foreign markets. India imports approximately 40 percent of its oil and 80 percent of its gas from the Middle East. Nearly 10 million Indian citizens live in the Gulf, and many Indian sailors are stranded on ships in the region since the Strait has been effectively closed. India’s exposure is not abstract — it is personal, industrial, and existential in energy terms.

Britain’s Prime Minister Starmer, asked Monday about Iran’s attempted missile strike on Diego Garcia, said there was “no assessment” that the UK was being targeted by Iranian missiles. He added that “de-escalation” was needed and that it was important to defend British interests “without getting dragged into the war.” The phrasing — “without getting dragged” — captures the precise condition of every country in the orbital chart: pulled toward a war they did not choose, trying to maintain a distance that is closing by the day.

The geography of energy dependency, documented in a Times analysis, makes the exposure concrete. Four-fifths of Hormuz oil goes to Asia. China gets more than a third of its total supply from the region. Pakistan considered a four-day workweek to preserve energy stockpiles. India faces cooking gas shortages. Seychelles and Mauritius imported almost all their energy from Gulf states. Sub-Saharan Africa faces fertilizer disruptions that threaten food prices. The Americas and Europe, less directly exposed to Gulf imports, are still absorbing the price shock — avocados, automobiles, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors, everything that moves by truck, ship, or plane.

“Petroleum goes into everything,” Miller said. He is not being metaphorical.

VI. Gaza, Forgotten

Palestinians celebrating Eid al-Fitr on Friday amid the ruins of Gaza City.Credit...Saher Alghorra for The New York Times

In one corner of this war, the people who have suffered longest feel most invisible.

Gaza residents are in a kind of limbo, the Times reports. Israel briefly closed all crossings into Gaza when the Iran war broke out. Now only one cargo crossing operates. The sole crossing for people — including patients seeking medical treatment abroad — was closed for nearly three weeks and reopened last week for limited numbers. Panic buying has returned. Prices have surged. Unscrupulous merchants are hoarding stock.

“The bitter truth is that Gaza has been forgotten,” said Fuad Shahin, 40, a cafe owner in Deir al Balah. “The eyes of the world are on Iran and the Gulf,” said Adham al-Mabhouh, 46, a soccer coach who trains amputees. “Whatever happens, Gaza seems to lose.”

More than 670 people have been killed in Gaza since the ceasefire in October — the ceasefire that was supposed to be the beginning of reconstruction. Trump’s 20-point peace plan has stalled. The committee of Palestinian technocrats meant to replace Hamas administration has not entered Gaza. The international stabilization force has not materialized. Hamas has not responded to the proposal to relinquish its weapons. And some Gaza residents now fear that after the Iran war ends, Israel may resume its campaign against Hamas with renewed intensity.

“We still haven’t escaped either our past or our present crisis,” said Hanin al-Qishawi, 29, an unemployed university graduate who returned to her damaged home in Gaza City. “Gaza is always affected by whatever happens in the region. It moves with the wind.”

VII. Survival Is Winning

The strategic situation on Day Twenty-Six can be stated simply, because the parties have now stated it themselves.

Trump’s theory of the war: overwhelming military force would collapse Iran’s government quickly, produce an uprising, and allow the United States to install acceptable leadership. That theory has been falsified.

Iran’s theory of the war, stated by Nate Swanson of the Atlantic Council: “They don’t need to fight a symmetrical war. They just need to survive. Survival is winning for Iran.”

The gap between those two theories is the war itself. The United States has struck 8,000 targets. Iran’s navy is destroyed. Its air force is grounded. Its missile launches are down 90 percent. Its supreme leader is wounded and invisible. Its intelligence minister, Basij commander, and top security official are dead. And yet Tehran’s power is out Monday morning not because Iran is collapsing — but because Israel struck it. Iran is still firing. Iran is still threatening. Iran is still closing the strait.

The IEA has called this the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. The Mossad chief’s uprising plan has failed. The interceptor stockpile is being conserved. The Marines are still a week from the Gulf. The 48-hour clock is expiring. And Iran’s Parliament speaker is threatening the buyers of US Treasury bonds.

There is no off-ramp. There is no Plan B. There is only the war, and the darkness it has put over Tehran, and the question nobody has yet answered: what does winning actually look like from here?
