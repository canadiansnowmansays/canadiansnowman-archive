The Iran Crisis

Five Conditions for Revolution

Is Iran’s Regime on the Brink?

January 14, 2026

Part 5 of The Iran Crisis Series

MAHSA / Middle East Images / AFP / Getty

On January 10, 2026, The Atlantic published an analysis by Karim Sadjadpour and Jack Goldstone that asked the most important question about Iran’s crisis: “Is the Iranian Regime About to Collapse?”

Their answer was carefully hedged but ultimately ominous for Tehran: For the first time since 1979, Iran meets nearly all five conditions that determine whether revolutions succeed. But one critical element remains missing—and without it, even massive protests can fail.

This framework—developed by Goldstone, a leading scholar of revolutions—provides the analytical lens to understand what’s actually happening in Iran’s streets and whether this uprising differs from all the failed protests that came before.

The five conditions are:

  1. State fiscal crisis

  2. Elite divisions

  3. Diverse opposition coalition

  4. Convincing narrative

  5. Favorable international environment

Let’s examine each condition to see whether Iran in January 2026 meets the threshold for revolutionary change—and what happens if the missing piece never falls into place.

Khoshiran / Middle East Images / AFP / Getty

Condition 1: State Fiscal Crisis ✓

The Requirement: Revolutions require states to be financially bankrupt or near-bankrupt. When governments can pay security forces, fund social programs, and maintain basic services, they can usually suppress dissent. When money runs out, everything else unravels.

Iran’s Status: Catastrophic fiscal collapse.

The numbers tell the story:

Currency depreciation: The rial has lost 99% of its value since 1979. From approximately 70 rials per dollar in 1979 to 1.4 million in December 2025. The December crash alone—from 600,000 to 1.4 million in weeks—represented a loss of more than half the currency’s remaining value.

Inflation: Point-to-point inflation hit 52.6% in late December 2025. Average annual inflation has been 40-45% since 2018—seven years of grinding economic erosion. Food inflation exceeds 50%, hitting 70% by some estimates.

Empty state coffers: Years of sanctions, corruption, and economic mismanagement have drained foreign exchange reserves. The June 2025 reimposition of comprehensive UN sanctions after the Israel-U.S. war further limited Iran’s ability to access international banking and sell oil.

Economic contraction: The formal economy is shrinking. Unemployment is high, especially among young people. The informal/black market economy represents an increasing share of activity—a sign of state economic failure.

As Sadjadpour and Goldstone note: “The Iranian state’s coffers are essentially empty.”

This isn’t temporary belt-tightening. This is structural economic collapse. The government cannot fund normal operations, cannot stabilize the currency, cannot control inflation, cannot provide basic economic security.

Verdict: Iran decisively meets Condition 1. The fiscal crisis is severe and worsening.

Condition 2: Elite Divisions ✓ (But With Crucial Caveat)

The Requirement: Revolutions succeed when ruling elites fracture. When generals, ministers, business leaders, and bureaucrats stop believing the regime can survive, they begin hedging, defecting, or preparing for “the day after.” Unity among elites can suppress even massive popular uprisings. Elite splits make revolutions possible.

Iran’s Status: Significant elite alienation, but not yet elite defection.

Sadjadpour and Goldstone describe Iran’s elite evolution since 1979:

Then (1979): “80% ideologues”—true believers in the Islamic Revolution who were willing to die for the cause.

Now (2026): “80% charlatans”—opportunists using the system for personal enrichment who have no real ideological commitment.

This shift matters enormously. Ideologues will fight to the death for their vision. Charlatans will abandon ship when it starts sinking. The question is: Has the ship started sinking?

Evidence of elite alienation:

All living former presidents silenced or sidelined. This represents a remarkable concentration of power around Khamenei personally. When you can’t tolerate even former allies having platforms, you’re admitting internal opposition.

“One-man party: party of Ali Khamenei.” The Islamic Republic has become a personal dictatorship masquerading as theocracy. This creates resentment among elite factions who feel excluded.

Bazaar merchants displaced by IRGC economic empire. Traditional conservative business class—which supported the 1979 revolution—has been systematically pushed aside by Revolutionary Guards’ economic networks. These merchants closing their shops in December wasn’t just economics; it was political abandonment.

A senior Western official briefing journalists: “Some Iranian officials with whom he is in contact appear to be preparing for ‘the day after.’” This suggests hedging behavior—elites making contingency plans in case the regime falls.

But—and this is crucial—the official also noted: “He hasn’t seen credible reports of increased regime defections.”

This is the critical caveat. Elite alienation doesn’t equal elite defection. People can hate the regime, can privately prepare for its fall, but still support it publicly because:

  • Fear of purges if they show disloyalty

  • Lack of confidence protesters will succeed

  • No clear alternative to rally around

  • Personal enrichment depends on regime survival

  • Risk of prosecution if regime survives

The IRGC remains unified. This is the most important point. Iran’s Revolutionary Guards Corps—the ideological military force that protects the regime—shows no signs of fracturing. Individual commanders might doubt the system, but the institution remains intact.

But morale questions persist beneath the surface. According to multiple sources—including a former IRGC officer who fled Iran in 2024 and spoke to Western researchers—internal dynamics are more complex than public unity suggests:

“The older commanders—those who fought in the Iran-Iraq War, who remember Khomeini personally—they’re true believers. They’ll shoot protesters forever if ordered. But the younger officers? The ones who joined for career advancement, for economic opportunity? They’re asking questions quietly. Not openly defecting, but wondering: How long can this continue? How many thousands must we kill? Is this sustainable?”

Iranian journalist and analyst Maziar Bahari, citing contacts inside Iran’s security apparatus, noted in his January analysis: “The IRGC is holding together institutionally, but individual loyalty is being tested. When you order soldiers to massacre their countrymen by the hundreds, you create moral crises. Some compartmentalize. Some rationalize. But some start thinking: Maybe I’m on the wrong side.”

The challenge for analysts: Distinguishing between wishful thinking (hoping IRGC will defect) and emerging reality (actual fractures forming). So far, no senior IRGC commanders have defected publicly. But absence of public defections doesn’t mean absence of private doubt.

As Sadjadpour and Goldstone emphasize: “The most important element still missing: repressive forces deciding they too are no longer benefiting from the current system.”

Until IRGC commanders conclude they’d be better off in a post-Islamic Republic Iran, the security apparatus will keep fighting. And as long as they’re fighting, the regime can survive through violence.

Verdict: Iran partially meets Condition 2. Elites are alienated and some are hedging, but critical security forces remain unified. This is the condition that could determine everything.

Condition 3: Diverse Opposition Coalition ✓

The Requirement: Successful revolutions require cross-class, cross-demographic mobilization. If only students protest, regimes can isolate and crush them. If only the urban poor revolt, elites stay loyal. But when merchants, workers, professionals, students, women, men, ethnic minorities, and even some elites all unite against a regime—that’s revolutionary.

Iran’s Status: Unprecedented breadth of opposition.

The January 2026 protests represent the most diverse mobilization in Iranian history:

Ethnic minorities: Kurdish groups coordinating with Persian protesters. Azeri populations participating. Baloch regions joining. The Islamic Republic’s Persian-Shia dominance has always created ethnic resentment—now it’s mobilizing.

Labor: Workers in key sectors (oil, gas, transportation, bazaars) participating in strikes or protests. This crosses traditional union/religious divides.

Women: Building on the 2022 “Women, Life, Freedom” movement, women remain at the forefront. The hijab mandate and women’s oppression under Islamic law create gender-based opposition.

Bazaaris: Traditional merchants closing shops and walking away—symbolically crucial given their role in 1979 revolution.

Students: Universities mobilizing, especially in Tehran, Mashhad, Tabriz. Student movements have historically driven Iranian protests.

Affluent neighborhoods: Tehran’s Shahrak Gharb and similar wealthy areas joining protests for the first time. When the middle and upper-middle classes lose hope, regimes lose their stabilizing base.

Urban poor: Those hit hardest by inflation and unemployment—the Islamic Republic’s theoretical constituency—now protesting against it.

Rural poor: Protests spreading to impoverished towns in provinces usually ignored by both government and opposition. This geographical spread makes the uprising harder to suppress.

The protests spanned all 31 of Iran’s provinces. From wealthy Tehran neighborhoods to marginalized rural towns. From ethnic Persian heartlands to minority regions. From young students to elderly shopkeepers.

As Sadjadpour and Goldstone note, a particularly powerful chant emerged: “Their children are in Canada! Our children are in prison!”

This captures class rage perfectly. Elite children study abroad and live comfortable lives. Ordinary Iranians’ children face unemployment, inflation, and now prison or death for protesting. The contrast fuels revolutionary anger.

Verdict: Iran decisively meets Condition 3. The opposition coalition is broader and more diverse than any previous Iranian protest movement.

Condition 4: Convincing Narrative ✓

The Requirement: Revolutions need a compelling story about why the current system must end and what should replace it. This narrative must resonate across diverse groups, explain current suffering, and offer hope for the future.

Iran’s Status: A powerful nationalist narrative has replaced Islamic ideology.

The narrative shift is striking:

The Islamic Republic’s founding narrative (1979): Pan-Islamic revolution. Resistance against Western imperialism. Islamic governance as superior to secular systems. Support for oppressed Muslims globally (Palestine, Lebanon).

The 2026 opposition narrative: Fierce Persian nationalism. Rejection of foreign entanglements. Demand for “normal life” free from ideological impositions.

The protest slogans tell the story:

“No to Gaza; no to Lebanon; my life only for Iran!”

This rejects the entire basis of Islamic Republic foreign policy. Iranians are tired of their government spending resources on Hamas, Hezbollah, and Syrian allies while Iranians starve. They want Persian nationalism, not pan-Islamic ideology.

“Death to Khamenei!”

Direct personal attacks on the Supreme Leader. This is deeply taboo in the Islamic Republic—Khamenei is supposed to be God’s representative on Earth. Calling for his death represents total rejection of the system’s legitimacy.

“Long live the Shah!”

Monarchist slogans at mass protests would have been unthinkable even five years ago. Now they’re common. Whether this represents genuine monarchist support or just rejection of the current system, it shows a complete narrative flip.

“The street will prevail!”

Assertion of popular sovereignty against clerical rule. The street—ordinary people—will determine Iran’s future, not religious authorities.

“Their children are in Canada! Our children are in prison!”

Class-based narrative attacking elite hypocrisy. You tell us to sacrifice for the revolution while you send your kids abroad? You live in compounds while we can’t afford bread? The narrative resonates because it’s demonstrably true.

The demand is clear: “Normal life” free from moral policing, ideological requirements, and economic dysfunction. Iranians want to be like other countries—to have functioning economies, personal freedoms, and governments that serve citizens rather than ideology.

This narrative crosses demographic lines. Students, workers, merchants, women, minorities—all can unite around “we want normal life” even if they disagree about specifics of what comes next.

As Sadjadpour and Goldstone note, Reza Pahlavi has emerged as a “symbolic leader” for this narrative, with crowds chanting his name. Whether he could actually lead a post-revolutionary government is debatable, but symbolically he represents the rejection of Islamic Republic ideology in favor of pre-1979 Persian nationalism.

Verdict: Iran decisively meets Condition 4. The opposition has a powerful, unifying narrative that has replaced the Islamic Republic’s ideological foundation.

Condition 5: Favorable International Environment ✓

The Requirement: Revolutions succeed more easily when international conditions favor challengers. If a regime’s allies come to its defense, revolutions often fail. If allies stay neutral or abandon the regime, protesters have better odds.

Iran’s Status: More internationally isolated and vulnerable than at any point since 1979.

Sadjadpour and Goldstone catalog Iran’s collapsing regional position:

Hamas decimated: Israel’s Gaza operation destroyed much of Hamas’s military capacity and leadership. Iran’s key Palestinian ally is weakened beyond near-term recovery.

Hezbollah decimated: Israeli strikes devastated Hezbollah in Lebanon, killing senior commanders and destroying weapons stockpiles. Iran’s most powerful proxy is crippled.

Assad deposed: Syria’s regime collapsed in December 2024, ending a crucial Iranian ally and strategic partner since 1980. This represents Iran’s biggest regional loss in decades.

Maduro captured: Venezuela—Iran’s top Latin American ally—saw its president seized by U.S. forces in January 2026. This demonstrated Iran’s allies are vulnerable and that neither Russia nor China will necessarily protect them.

Nuclear facilities destroyed: The June 2025 12-day war with Israel and the U.S. saw Iran’s nuclear facilities bombed. While Iran retains nuclear knowledge, its physical infrastructure suffered severe damage.

Putin consumed by Ukraine: Russia, theoretically Iran’s major power ally, is focused entirely on Ukraine. Putin hasn’t even issued a statement about Iran’s protests—extraordinary for a country that “periodically threatened nuclear war” over smaller provocations.

China a predatory partner: China buys Iranian oil at steep discounts and hasn’t invested in protecting Iranian interests. Beijing prepares legal warfare over Venezuelan contracts but deploys zero military assets to defend allies.

UN sanctions reimposed: After the June 2025 war, comprehensive UN economic sanctions returned, limiting Iran’s international banking and trade access.

This represents Iran at its weakest internationally since the 1980s Iran-Iraq War. Every regional ally is either destroyed, weakened, or has abandoned Tehran. Major power patrons are absent or distracted. The country is militarily vulnerable, economically sanctioned, and diplomatically isolated.

Starlink as technological battleground (January 13 update): Iran is using “military-grade jamming tools” to disrupt Starlink satellite internet—the protesters’ primary communication lifeline. The regime specifically targets users’ ability to upload videos and images documenting the massacre. President Trump has asked Elon Musk to help restore connectivity, with Musk green-lighting his engineers to work on counter-jamming solutions. Even the internet has become a battlefield where the U.S. directly supports protesters against regime censorship—unprecedented coordination between American tech companies and anti-regime forces inside Iran.

Compare this to 2015, when Iran was negotiating the nuclear deal from a position of relative strength—when Syria was stable, Hezbollah was powerful, and Russia was actively engaged. The contrast is dramatic.

The Trump factor: Trump’s Venezuela precedent adds unique pressure. Iranian protesters believe American intervention might actually come. Iranian leaders fear it might too. This ambiguity creates instability—neither side knows if crossing certain lines will trigger U.S. military action.

Verdict: Iran decisively meets Condition 5. The international environment is more favorable for regime change than at any point in decades.

The Missing Piece: Security Force Defections

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps marching during a parade

So Iran meets four of five conditions decisively. Elite divisions exist but haven’t yet translated to defections. That brings us back to the most critical question:

“The most important element still missing: repressive forces deciding they too are no longer benefiting from the current system.”

This is where revolutions succeed or fail. The state can survive almost anything if security forces remain loyal. But once security forces start defecting—refusing orders, switching sides, protecting protesters instead of shooting them—regime collapse accelerates rapidly.

Examples from history:

Egypt (2011): Military refused to massacre protesters in Tahrir Square. Mubarak fell within 18 days.

Tunisia (2011): Police and military declined to violently suppress protests. Ben Ali fled within weeks.

East Germany (1989): When security forces didn’t shoot protesters in Leipzig and Berlin, the regime collapsed in months.

Contrast with:

China (1989): Military violently suppressed Tiananmen Square protests. Regime survived.

Syria (2011-2024): Military remained loyal to Assad through years of civil war. Regime only fell when Russia withdrew support and Turkey invaded.

Iran (2009, 2022): Security forces massacred protesters. Regimes survived.

The pattern is clear: When militaries shoot protesters, most revolutions fail. When militaries refuse to shoot, most revolutions succeed.

So what about Iran’s IRGC in January 2026?

No signs of fracture yet. The Revolutionary Guards Corps remains ideologically committed and institutionally unified. Individual commanders might doubt, but the institution isn’t cracking.

The senior Western official’s assessment is sobering: The regime is “holding it together for now” but “only with massive violence.”

That last phrase is key: “Only with massive violence.” This suggests the regime knows it cannot survive through legitimacy or consent. It survives through fear. Which means if fear fails—if security forces conclude they’d be better off in a post-revolutionary Iran—the regime has nothing else.

Why Security Forces Defect

Goldstone’s research identifies key factors that trigger security force defections:

1. Belief the regime will fall anyway. Once security forces conclude the regime is doomed, continuing to support it becomes career suicide. Better to switch sides early and hope for post-revolutionary rewards.

2. Economic incentives shifting. If the current system no longer provides economic benefits—if commanders aren’t getting paid, if corruption opportunities dry up, if the economy collapses so thoroughly that even elites suffer—financial self-interest argues for change.

3. Unwillingness to commit massacres. Some security forces will shoot protesters once or twice. Fewer will massacre hundreds or thousands repeatedly. At some point, even loyal soldiers ask: “Am I going to spend years killing my countrymen?”

4. Loss of external support. When patron states abandon a regime (like Russia eventually abandoning Syria), security forces realize they’re on their own. This changes calculations.

5. Credible alternative emerging. If security forces see a plausible post-revolutionary government that might protect their interests, defection becomes safer. If they see only chaos, they stick with the devil they know.

Does Iran in January 2026 meet these triggers?

#1 - Belief regime will fall? Not yet. Despite massive protests, the regime still looks like it can survive through violence.

#2 - Economic incentives shifting? Partially. The fiscal crisis hurts everyone, including IRGC commanders. But the Guards control significant economic networks independent of the state budget.

#3 - Unwillingness to massacre? Unknown. Iran has massacred protesters before (2009, 2019, 2022) and survived. Will commanders keep shooting indefinitely?

#4 - Loss of external support? Yes. Russia and China aren’t coming to save Iran. But Iran’s military doesn’t depend on external support the way Venezuela’s did.

#5 - Credible alternative? Unclear. Reza Pahlavi presents as an alternative, but does he have enough legitimacy that IRGC commanders would feel safe backing him? Uncertain.

The conditions for security force defection exist but haven’t matured. The massacre continues because commanders still believe they can win.

The “Zombie Regime” Problem

Sadjadpour and Goldstone use a powerful phrase to describe Iran’s current state: “Zombie regime—kept alive by lethal force.”

This captures the essential problem. Iran’s government has lost:

  • Economic legitimacy (fiscal collapse)

  • Political legitimacy (elite alienation)

  • Popular legitimacy (mass protests)

  • Ideological legitimacy (narrative rejected)

  • International legitimacy (isolated globally)

What does it still have? Guns.

Security forces willing to shoot protesters. That’s the regime’s only remaining foundation. Remove that, and the whole structure collapses instantly.

But here’s the zombie regime’s dilemma: The more you rely on violence, the faster you lose legitimacy. The more protesters you kill, the more people conclude you must be overthrown. The more elites see massacres, the more they quietly prepare to defect.

Violence buys time but accelerates delegitimization. You’re killing the patient to keep them alive.

This creates a race: Can the regime crush protests before security forces lose the will to keep killing? Or will protests persist long enough that commanders conclude massacring indefinitely is unsustainable?

January 2026 represents the opening phase of this race. Hundreds killed in days. Internet blacked out. More than 10,000 arrested. The regime is betting overwhelming force early will prevent prolonged conflict.

But what if protesters keep coming back? What if merchants keep shops closed? What if strikes spread to oil sectors? What if international pressure intensifies? What if Trump actually intervenes?

At some point—maybe not this month, maybe not this year, but at some point—IRGC commanders will ask: “How long can we keep doing this? How many thousands must we kill? Is this sustainable? Are we on the winning side?”

When enough commanders ask those questions, the answers become fatal for the regime.

Victoria Taylor’s Warning: Even Decapitation Might Not Work

Victoria Taylor, former deputy assistant secretary of state for Iraq and Iran (now at the Atlantic Council), offers a sobering counter-argument to the “just remove Khamenei” theory:

“Even if you take out the supreme leader, you could still wind up with another regime figure assuming leadership.”

This is the lesson from Iran’s own history. The 1979 revolution didn’t just remove the Shah—it destroyed the entire monarchical system. But Iran’s current protests might remove Khamenei while leaving the Revolutionary Guards, the clerical establishment, and the security apparatus intact.

If Trump strikes Khamenei personally, or if protesters somehow force him from power, the IRGC doesn’t automatically dissolve. The next day, some other Guards commander or cleric claims the Supreme Leader position. The system continues.

This is why security force defections matter more than leadership changes. You can remove individuals, but if institutions remain loyal to the old system, revolution fails.

The counter-example is East Germany 1989. When East German protesters chanted “We are the people,” they weren’t just demanding Honecker resign. They were demanding the entire Communist system end. When security forces agreed, the regime couldn’t replace Honecker with another Communist and continue. The whole structure collapsed.

Iran’s protesters are chanting “Death to Khamenei.” But are they demanding the entire Islamic Republic system end? Or just a change in leadership? The answer determines whether removing Khamenei actually changes anything.

Conclusion: Revolution Is Possible But Not Inevitable

Sadjadpour and Goldstone’s framework reveals both why Iran’s regime looks vulnerable and why it might still survive.

Why revolution is possible:

  • State fiscal crisis: ✓

  • Elite divisions: ✓ (partially)

  • Diverse opposition coalition: ✓

  • Convincing narrative: ✓

  • Favorable international environment: ✓

Four conditions definitively met. One partially met. That’s closer to revolution than Iran has been at any point since 1979.

Why revolution might still fail:

The fifth condition—security force defections—remains unfulfilled. And it’s the most important condition.

As long as the IRGC stays unified and willing to shoot protesters, the Islamic Republic can survive through violence. It will be a “zombie regime,” surviving on guns rather than legitimacy. But it will survive.

This means everything depends on one question: How long will Iran’s security forces keep killing their countrymen?

If commanders conclude massacring indefinitely is unsustainable—if they see the regime as doomed, if economic collapse affects them too severely, if international pressure becomes unbearable, if a credible alternative emerges—then defections start. Once they start, they accelerate. And once they accelerate, regimes fall quickly.

But if commanders conclude they can win through overwhelming force—if protests can be crushed, if elites can be intimidated into compliance, if international intervention doesn’t materialize, if no alternative emerges—then the regime survives. Weakened, delegitimized, isolated, but alive.

January 13 Update: Trump’s calculation may have just shifted. On Tuesday morning, he posted on Truth Social: “Iranian Patriots, KEEP PROTESTING — TAKE OVER YOUR INSTITUTIONS!!! Save the names of the killers and abusers. They will pay a big price. I have cancelled all meetings with Iranian Officials until the senseless killing of protesters STOPS. HELP IS ON ITS WAY.”

This explicit promise of help—combined with cancellation of all diplomatic meetings—represents the international intervention factor that could tip the balance. If IRGC commanders believe American military action is imminent, their calculation about regime survival changes dramatically.

The revolution has met four conditions decisively and one partially. Trump’s escalation might provide the final push that triggers security force defections.

But possibility is not probability. And probability is not inevitability.

The zombie regime survives on lethal force alone. The question is: For how long?

And now: Does Trump’s promised “help” arrive before the massacre becomes irreversible?
