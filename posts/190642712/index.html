Now This is (Not) Fake News

Part 9: The Vanity of War — When Bad Photos Matter More Than Dead Soldiers

March 11, 2026

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth and Middle East Envoy Steve Witkoff at the Pentagon, March 2, 2026. Photos from this briefing were deemed "unflattering" by Hegseth's staff, leading to a ban on independent press photographers at all subsequent briefings. (Brendan Smialowski/AFP via Getty Images)

A Canadian’s Analysis of Narcissism in the Department of War

By CanadianSnowmanSays March 11, 2026

Let me get this straight.

Eight American service members are dead. More than 140 are wounded. Thirty were hospitalized with brain trauma, shrapnel wounds, and burns so severe they required emergency airlift to Landstuhl Regional Medical Center in Germany. Six Army reservists — including a 20-year-old Drake University student and a mother days from coming home to her children — were killed when an Iranian drone hit their makeshift operations center in Kuwait. A center with no overhead protection. No counter-drone capability. No warning siren.

And Pete Hegseth’s priority is that the Associated Press made him look bad.

The Photo Op That Wasn’t

On March 2 — just four days after the joint U.S.-Israeli strikes that killed Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and started a war — Hegseth finally stepped behind the Pentagon briefing room podium. It was his first on-camera appearance since June 26. Nine months. Nine months of boats being blown up in the Caribbean, of press credentials being shredded, of a right-wing replacement press corps being installed, of a New York Times lawsuit working its way through federal court.

He appeared alongside General Dan Caine, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, to brief the nation on Operation Epic Fury. The AP, Reuters, and Getty Images sent photographers, as they have for every Defense Secretary in modern American history. Wire photographers are the backbone of global visual journalism. Their images are licensed by publications worldwide. They are how the world sees American power being exercised.

They took pictures. The pictures were published.

And then, according to two people familiar with the decision — who spoke to the Washington Post anonymously out of fear of retaliation — members of Hegseth’s staff told colleagues they didn’t like how the Secretary looked. The photos were, in the bureaucratic language of Pentagon vanity, “unflattering.”

So they banned the photographers.

On March 4, when photographers showed up for the next briefing, they were turned away. On March 10, same thing. Since then, only Department of War staff photographers — Hegseth’s own people, shooting Hegseth’s preferred angles — have been permitted inside,

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth during a March 2 Pentagon press briefing. (Mark Schiefelbein/AP)

The Excuse

Pentagon press secretary Kingsley Wilson — the same official previously accused of antisemitic social media posts — offered the standard-issue bureaucratic deflection: it was about “space” and “credentialing requirements.” Photographers from uncredentialed outlets were limited to one representative each, she said, and the Pentagon’s own photographs were being released online for anyone to use.

Think about that for a moment. The Pentagon purged its press corps in October. It replaced professional defense journalists with The Federalist, The Epoch Times, and Gateway Pundit. It demanded that reporters sign a 21-page agreement promising not to seek any information the government hadn’t authorized. When virtually every major outlet — including Fox News and Newsmax — refused, they were expelled. Their offices were emptied. Their equipment was carted away.

And now, the handful of wire service photographers who negotiated their way back into the building for a wartime briefing are being punished because the Defense Secretary doesn’t photograph well?

The credentialing “problem” was manufactured. The Pentagon created the credential crisis, then used the crisis it created to justify excluding the photographers it doesn’t want.

This is TACO in its purest form. Threaten the press with prosecution under the new policy. Accommodate a select few right-wing outlets willing to serve as stenographers. Capitulate to Hegseth’s personal vanity by replacing independent photographers with government-controlled image production. Obfuscate by claiming it’s all about “space.”

What the Photos Show

Defense secretary appeared animated in the early March press briefing (AFP via Getty Images)

Look at the AP image from March 2. Look at it carefully.

You see a man in a blue suit, standing at the Pentagon podium, mouth open mid-sentence, gesturing with apparent intensity. The Pentagon seal looms behind him. It’s a perfectly standard wire photo of a government official at a press conference. The kind of photo that has been taken of every Defense Secretary since cameras were allowed in the building.

But Hegseth isn’t every Defense Secretary. He’s a former television host. He knows what a good camera angle looks like. He knows about lighting and makeup and the difference between how you look on Fox News — where the hosts control the shot — and how you look when an AP photographer is positioned below the podium catching you mid-syllable.

This is a man who, according to the Wall Street Journal, now wears the same style of Florsheim Oxfords that Donald Trump has personally ordered for his inner circle. A man whose vanity is so fundamental to his identity that, while Americans are dying in a war he helped launch, his staff’s priority is image management. Not the military kind. The Instagram kind.

What the Photos Don’t Show

You know what those wire photographers would also document if they were still allowed inside?

The faces of Pentagon officials when asked hard questions about why six reservists were working from a triple-wide trailer in Kuwait with no drone defense. The body language when a reporter asks about the 30 troops airlifted to Germany with brain trauma. The reaction when someone brings up the CBS News report that military officials on the ground had questioned whether that operations center should have been used at all, since it concentrated too many troops in an indefensible location.

The AP photographer captures what the subject doesn’t want you to see. That’s the entire point. That’s why these photographers exist. That’s why, for decades, wire services have been granted access to every major government briefing — so that the public’s visual record of power is not curated by power itself.

Now the visual record of Operation Epic Fury will be curated by Hegseth’s staff photographers. The man who decides which bombs to drop will also decide which photos you see of him dropping them.

The Court Watches

The timing is exquisite.

Last Friday, March 6, U.S. District Judge Paul Friedman heard oral arguments in the New York Times’ lawsuit challenging the entire Pentagon press credential policy. The judge’s skepticism was barely concealed. When a Justice Department attorney tried to defend the policy, Friedman pointed out the obvious: asking a question isn’t a crime.

The judge noted what this series has documented since Part 1: that it is “more important than ever” for Americans to hear diverse perspectives about their government’s actions. Especially during a war.

The Times’ attorney, Theodore Boutrous, made the argument that the policy was never really about national security. It was about controlling what gets reported. The evidence? The Pentagon’s own inconsistency. Right-wing personality Laura Loomer — who signed the new policy — promoted a “tip line” soliciting information from Pentagon sources. The Pentagon had no problem with that. But the Washington Post’s tip line? That supposedly violated the policy because it “targets” military personnel.

And now, five days after that hearing — with the judge weighing whether the entire press purge is constitutional — Hegseth has handed the court a gift-wrapped exhibit. He’s banned photographers. During a war. Because they made him look bad. While his own staff photographers produce approved images.

If the judge needed evidence that the Pentagon’s press restrictions are about vanity rather than security, Hegseth just provided it.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth holds a briefing on the U.S.-Israeli conflict with Iran at the Pentagon, March 2, 2026. (Elizabeth Frantz/Reuters)

The Arc of the Ministry

In Part 1, I told you the press purge was coming. In Parts 2 and 3, I traced the constitutional implications and the first month of the Ministry. In Part 4, the body count started — not from war, but from drug interdiction boats in the Caribbean, monitored by a press corps that no longer existed inside the building. In Part 5, I documented the nuclear announcements and National Guard deployments that happened while nobody was watching. In Parts 6 and 7, we got the “kill everybody” order — Hegseth’s directive that sent a second missile into survivors clinging to burning wreckage. In Part 8, I argued the pattern was complete.

I was wrong. The pattern wasn’t complete. It was evolving.

The press purge was never just about controlling information during peacetime controversies. It was infrastructure. It was preparation. It was building the architecture that would allow a former television host to wage war while controlling his own image — literally, in the photographic sense.

Think about what Hegseth has achieved in twelve months:

He expelled every major news organization from the Pentagon. He replaced them with outlets willing to publish only what he authorizes. He forced the New York Times into federal court to fight for the basic right to ask questions. He oversaw Caribbean strikes that killed dozens while the replacement press corps asked softball questions. He ordered a second missile into survivors. And now, in the twelfth day of a war with Iran — with American troops dying in unprotected facilities, with Iranian drones striking across nine countries, with 5,000+ targets hit inside Iran — he has banned the wire service photographers who briefly returned to the building because they caught him at an unflattering angle.

From press purge to war crime to vanity ban. This is the trajectory of the Ministry of Truth.

A Canadian Observation

Up here in Toronto, we have a phrase for this. Actually, we don’t. We’re Canadian. We’re too polite to have a phrase for a defense secretary who bans cameras because he doesn’t like his own face during a war.

But I’ll borrow an American one: the mask slipped.

The Pentagon’s official position has always been that the press restrictions are about “national security” and “operational safety.” The 21-page policy was framed as “common sense” rules. The credential requirements were presented as necessary protections. Everything was justified by the solemn responsibility of protecting American troops.

And then it turned out to be about a bad photo.

Not a leaked document. Not a classified operational detail. Not the identity of a covert operative. A photo. An unflattering photo. Of a man in a blue suit at a podium.

Eight Americans dead. More than 140 wounded. Thirty with brain injuries. And the Secretary of War — the man responsible for their safety, their strategy, their survival — is worried about his jawline.

This is not a defense secretary. This is not a wartime leader. This is a man who spent years on television and cannot stop performing for the camera, even when the camera is documenting a war.

Especially when the camera is documenting a war.
