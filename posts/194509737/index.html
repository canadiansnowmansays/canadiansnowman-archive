Two Americas

If Hungary Can Do It

Part 11: Two Americas — An Ongoing Series

April 17, 2026

A drone view shows people gathering to celebrate across the River Danube from the Parliament building, following the partial results of the parliamentary election, in Budapest, Hungary, on April 12, 2026. Reuters

Stephen Gordon · April 2026

When I wrote The 3.5% Question before the March 28th No Kings rallies, I framed the math around Erica Chenoweth’s observation that no modern government has survived a sustained nonviolent movement in which 3.5% of the population actively participated. For the United States, that number is roughly 11.6 million. Eight million showed up. The trajectory was encouraging but the threshold remained unmet.

Then on Sunday night, in Budapest, the threshold became irrelevant — because Hungary had something the American resistance does not yet have. A ballot box.

The pictures were extraordinary. Tens of thousands along the banks of the Danube, fireworks over the Parliament building, the Hungarian tricolor mingling with the blue and gold of the European Union. Friends dancing on each other’s shoulders. Fathers embracing their children. A teenage girl weeping. An 18-year-old named Bendegúz Neszádeli, who had just voted for the first time, holding up a tricolor bracelet given to maiden voters and telling The Atlantic: “It feels like there’s a future again.”

Veronika Munk, a journalist at Denník N who was there with a microphone in her hand, described it as a once-in-a-lifetime experience. “The Hungarian society is quite closed,” she told Radio Atlantic’s Hanna Rosin. “It’s really hard to see people dance and hug each other.” She was 11 when the system changed in 1990. She remembers the adults being happy. She said Sunday night was incomparable.

Even the future health minister was dancing on stage. Apparently the man has moves.

I mention this not because the celebration itself is analytically significant — people dance when tyrants fall; this is not new — but because of what it took to get there. Sixteen years of Viktor Orbán. Sixteen years of institutional capture: media bought by allies, universities hobbled, courts packed, intelligence services bent to serve the party. A propaganda apparatus so complete that the journalist whose newsroom was dismantled underneath her — Munk watched 80 colleagues resign with her in a single day in 2020 — found it “super hard to believe” that the regime could actually end.

It felt, she said, like Orbán would always be there.

She was wrong. Nearly eighty percent of Hungary’s electorate showed up to tell her so.

The significance of Orbán’s fall extends well beyond Central Europe, because Orbán was not merely governing Hungary. He was running a franchise.

He was the first leader of a European democracy to openly call what he was building an “illiberal state” — his term, delivered proudly in a 2014 speech. He then set about exporting the model. Kevin Roberts, president of the Heritage Foundation, described Orbán’s Hungary not as a model for conservative governance but as “the model.” Americans flew to Budapest to learn how it was done. Hungarians came to Washington to teach them. Under Fidesz, the Hungarian capital became — as Michelle Goldberg wrote in The New York Times — a kind of Disneyland for reactionaries: government-funded think tanks, lavishly paid fellowships for conservative intellectuals, an annual CPAC conference that served as a gathering point for the global far right, and even a chain of cafés named after the British conservative philosopher Roger Scruton, with his aphorisms on the walls.

The parallels with the Trump administration were not vague. They were specific and operational. The conversion of neutral civil servants into party loyalists — what Trump’s people attempted through Schedule F and the gutting of the Justice Department and FBI — was a direct copy of the Orbán playbook. The use of friendly business allies to acquire major media companies followed the same template Orbán had deployed to dismantle Hungary’s independent press. As Anne Applebaum wrote in The Atlantic: “It’s not a kind of secret or underground movement. He was an open source of ideas for the illiberal and even autocratic part of the American MAGA movement.”

Trump made the relationship operationally explicit. He sent Vice President Vance to Budapest the week before the election to stump for Orbán. Vance called the Hungarian leader “wise and smart,” predicted his victory on camera, then turned to Orbán and asked, “Viktor, is that right?” Secretary of State Rubio had already visited in February, telling Orbán, “Your success is our success.” Trump phoned in from home, put on speakerphone before the crowd: “I’m a big fan of Viktor. I’m with him all the way.” Two days before the vote, he took to Truth Social to promise “the full Economic Might of the United States” to strengthen Hungary’s economy if Orbán won.

The promise landed with a thud. As Isaac Stanley-Becker reported in The Atlantic, a social media analysis by Redpoint Advisors found that negative Hungarian-language mentions of Trump spiked 47 percent in the hour after his pledge. Deep distrust of American motives, it turns out, is not exclusive to Canadians.

At one of Vance’s events at the Mathias Corvinus Collegium — a government-linked educational institution financed in part by shares in a company that processes Russian oil — Vance mocked European countries for their dependence on foreign fossil fuels. A student seated in the audience laughed intermittently throughout his remarks. When a reporter asked her afterward what she had found funny, she answered: “He doesn’t know much about Hungary.”

Here is where the story becomes instructive.

As Andrew Higgins observed in The New York Times, there is a Russian maxim about politics that says power always exists in tension between the television and the refrigerator — between the propaganda and the lived reality. Orbán put all his chips on the television. By 2026, the refrigerator was empty.

Hungary’s economy had stagnated for three consecutive years. Unemployment hit a decade-long high. Corruption, according to Transparency International, made Hungary the most corrupt country in the European Union — tied with Bulgaria. Hospitals lacked toilet paper. Schools were falling apart. The prime minister’s childhood friend, once a gas retrofitter, had become a billionaire through state contracts, while average Hungarian salaries languished at less than half the EU average. Opposition accusations surfaced that Orbán’s family had stocked a vast country estate with zebras.

Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán in 2024 / Piero Cruciatti / AFP / Getty

Into this gap between propaganda and reality, Orbán poured what Applebaum called the first “post-reality” campaign. Having exhausted immigrants and gender ideology as existential threats in previous cycles, he turned to Ukraine. The claim was that Ukraine — a country fighting a war of survival against Russia — was somehow planning to invade Hungary. To sell this, his apparatus built an entire ecosystem of AI-generated videos showing Zelensky snorting cocaine on a golden toilet. Sinister posters of Zelensky appeared across the country with the headline Don’t let him get the last laugh. Hungarian soldiers were deployed to guard energy installations against supposed Ukrainian sabotage.

Then the independent journalist Szabolcs Panyi and his colleagues at Direkt36 published leaked transcripts and audio of Orbán and his foreign minister strategizing with their Russian counterparts — with Putin and Lavrov — about advancing Kremlin interests inside the European Union. The man who had built his brand on Hungarian sovereignty had been recorded describing himself as a mouse and Putin as a lion. The sovereignty was a performance. The Russian dependence was the reality.

At a rock concert in Heroes’ Square the Friday before the election, tens of thousands of young Hungarians — people who had lived under Orbán for their entire adult lives — began chanting “Russians, go home.” The same chant their grandparents had used when Soviet tanks rolled into Budapest in 1956.

Péter Magyar, leader of the Tisza Party, holds a Hungarian flag during a rally ahead of a general election in Budapest, on March 15. Bloomberg / Getty

Péter Magyar won by doing something that sounds simple and is, in practice, extraordinarily difficult: he refused to fight on Orbán’s terms.

Magyar was a former Fidesz insider — an apparatchik who had been part of the machine. That made his credibility on corruption almost impossible for Orbán to dismiss. As one EU ambassador told The Atlantic: “People believe him when he talks about Fidesz corruption because he participated in it.” Two years ago, he released secretly recorded audio of his ex-wife, then the justice minister, discussing government meddling in politically sensitive prosecutions. The revelations brought mass protests. He used the occasion to announce his leadership of Tisza — a party that barely existed at the time.

His campaign slogan was: Not left, not right, only Hungarians.

Jonathan Ernst / AFP / Getty

That phrasing was not an accident. It was a strategic choice with precedent. As Idrees Kahloon documented in The Atlantic, Emmanuel Macron deployed a similar formulation in 2017. Barack Obama did it in 2008 — convincingly pitching himself as president for neither red America nor blue America. In each case, the winning candidate stepped outside the polarization framework the incumbent had constructed and built a different argument entirely.

Magyar did not repudiate Orbán’s hostility to immigration. Quite the opposite — he called Orbán a hypocrite for being outwardly hostile to immigration while maintaining a large guest-worker program. He pledged to continue zero tolerance for illegal immigration. He avoided being drawn into debates about LGBTQ rights. He shunned attempts by foreign reporters to profile him. He did not take the Ukraine bait. He traveled to hundreds of towns and villages — including rural constituencies considered Fidesz’s heartland — and talked about things people could verify with their own eyes: inflation, corruption, hospitals without resources, schools falling apart, an economy that had delivered three consecutive years of nothing.

He held five or six events a day in the final stretch. He went to places the opposition had never seriously contested. His grassroots campaign existed because it had to — Orbán’s allies controlled an estimated 80 percent of the country’s media, and even billboard space was largely in government hands. Magyar built around the blockade.

The opposition parties, for their part, did something almost unprecedented: they stood down. One by one, across the political spectrum, they withdrew their candidates to avoid splitting the anti-Fidesz vote. Factionalism was suspended. The rallying behind Magyar was, in The Atlantic’s Idrees Kahloon’s memorable framing, the Hungarian equivalent of an ex-MAGA Republican named Peter American winning the Democratic nomination with the endorsement of Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and Marjorie Taylor Greene simultaneously.

The result was a two-thirds supermajority. Tisza won 138 of 199 parliamentary seats on 53.6 percent of the vote. The turnout — nearly 80 percent — was the highest since the fall of communism. Orbán conceded on election night. No January 6th. No Stop the Steal. Just a swift, gracious concession — painful, as he told supporters — and a vow, reported by The New York Times: “We are not giving up. Never, never, never.”

Then the dancing started.

Péter Magyar, the Tisza-party candidate for Hungarian prime minister, greets supporters in Budapest after he won the parliamentary election on April 12, 2026.

Now for the number that matters.

Hungary has a population of approximately 9.7 million. Nearly 5.9 million people voted. Of those, 53.6 percent voted for Tisza — roughly 3.14 million Hungarians casting ballots specifically to end Orbán’s rule. That is approximately 32 percent of the entire population. Not 3.5 percent. Thirty-two percent.

The Chenoweth threshold was never the relevant measure for Hungary, because Hungary had the one thing the American resistance currently lacks: an election. When you can convert civic energy directly into parliamentary seats, you do not need to sustain 3.5 percent in the streets. You need to sustain it until the ballot box opens, and then you need turnout. Hungary got the highest turnout since the fall of communism.

This is the mirror that should keep both sides of the American divide awake at night.

The No Kings movement has produced three data points: five million in June 2025, seven million in October 2025, eight million on March 28th, 2026. That trajectory is real. It is growing. It is reaching into red-state geography that was previously considered safe for the administration. But it has no conversion mechanism until November 3rd. The midterms are seven months away. Seven months of sustaining and building pressure with no electoral release valve.

Hungary did not need to solve that problem. The protests built the momentum — the defiant Pride march of June 2025, which drew as many as 200,000 people in a country of under ten million; the competing October rallies that filled Budapest with hundreds of thousands more; the student demonstrations over education and the child abuse pardon scandal that had been building since 2024. Magyar’s 80-day national tour converted that energy into votes. The whole arc — protest to candidacy to supermajority — took roughly two years.

The American version of this story is harder. It requires sustaining the energy longer, across a vastly larger country, against an administration that — as Harvard’s Steven Levitsky has noted — has in some ways been more oppressive than Orbán’s. Trump has used the Justice Department to investigate political opponents. Federal agents have shot and killed American citizens during immigration enforcement operations. These are things Orbán never did.

There is, however, a structural reason for measured optimism that arrives from an unexpected direction: Trump’s own overreach on election mechanics. As law professor Justin Levitt told David Graham in The Atlantic, although Trump has abused his powers in many spheres, he simply does not have power to abuse in the election space. “There’s an awful lot of power that Congress has given the president where he’s got the switch,” Levitt said. “But in elections, he doesn’t have the switch in the first place.” Trump’s March executive order on mail voting has already been buried under four separate legal challenges from 23 state attorneys general. Three federal courts had already blocked key provisions of his earlier 2025 order. The judicial immune system, as I have observed in the When Democracy Fights Back series, is doing its work.

The ballot box, in other words, is likely to be there in November. The question is what the American electorate does with it.

There is a final detail worth noting, and then a warning.

The detail: When reporters asked Trump about the results in Hungary on Sunday night, he turned and walked away. The man who had deployed his vice president, phoned in from home, promised the full economic might of the United States, put his eldest son on social media to appeal directly to Hungarian voters — that man had nothing to say when the model collapsed.

Matt Schlapp, chairman of the American Conservative Union and a reliable MAGA weathervane — the same organization that co-sponsored Hungary’s CPAC, the annual gathering that served as base camp for the global far right — told ABC News: “Eventually, democracies just want change. In democracies, you don’t have kings, and the people in the end speak.”

Read that again. In democracies, you don’t have kings.

The No Kings organizers could not have written a better tagline.

And the warning: Rod Dreher, the conservative American writer who had been living in Budapest on the Hungarian government’s dime, announced he would be moving to Vienna. He rationalized Orbán’s defeat not as a loss for the national conservative movement but as a loss for the idea that you can govern as a populist with three years of no economic growth and indifference to insider enrichment. In Dreher’s reading, what Magyar offered was “Orbán, but without the corruption.”

That framing is too convenient by half — it lets the ideology off the hook and blames only the execution — but it contains a warning that democrats and small-l liberals should not ignore. Winning the election is only the first step. Poland’s Donald Tusk, who successfully dislodged the nationalist Law and Justice Party in 2023, has struggled to implement his reforms and lost the 2025 presidential election, meaning his constitutional changes may be blocked until 2030. In Brazil, Jair Bolsonaro sits in prison for inciting a coup, but his son is now leading the polls for the next presidential election. As Princeton’s Kim Lane Scheppele told The Atlantic: “You can’t overcome dictatorship without winning an election. But prevailing at the ballot box is only the first step.”

Magyar himself seems to understand this. In his victory speech, he called for the resignation of the president, the prosecutor general, and the president of the constitutional court. He announced that the think tank funding pipeline — the entire Washington-Budapest intellectual infrastructure that had served as the MAGA movement’s ideological incubator — would be shut down immediately, with an investigation into whether the funding structure constituted a criminal offense. Many think-tank leaders, a Tisza aide said, “will be out of a job very soon.”

That is not just a Hungarian story. That is the dismantling of MAGA’s overseas R&D lab.

Veronika Munk, the journalist who lost her newsroom and then watched the regime that took it fall in a single night, put it simply: “Even 16 years of ruling can be demolished in a day.”

Five million. Seven million. Eight million. The ballot box opens in November.

The sequence does not lie.
