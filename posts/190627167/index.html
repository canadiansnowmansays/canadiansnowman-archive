Thank God for the First Amendment

Let Me Tell You How I Really Feel

March 15, 2026

Sunday Satire Series | CanadianSnowmanSays

Its time to celebrate the Ides of March.

There are moments in political satire when the target has become so cartoonish that parody can barely keep up with reality. And then someone uses AI to make a four-minute country music video about Stephen Miller — and suddenly the gap closes.

The video in question is “The Maddest Little Hobbit (The Ballad of Stephen Miller)” by an artist called Sqwidx, from the album MAGA Country (2025). It is, from a Canadian’s respectful distance, one of the most gloriously unhinged pieces of AI-generated political satire I have ever encountered. And I have encountered quite a few.

I want to be clear about something: I cannot reproduce the lyrics here. Copyright is copyright, even when the song is generated with the assistance of artificial intelligence. What I can do is walk you through the visual fever dream that accompanies those lyrics — because the screenshots alone tell a story that words can barely contain.

You can watch the full video here:

I recommend you do so before reading further. Crank up the volume. Maybe pour yourself something strong.

The Opening Act: Escalator Redux

The video opens with a scene that should be familiar to anyone who has watched American politics descend — quite literally — over the past decade. A figure in a crisp dark suit rides an escalator downward, flanked by roaring flames on either side. It is, of course, a callback to the most famous escalator ride in political history. Except this time it isn’t the boss making the descent. It’s the enforcer. The deputy. The man behind the curtain who would very much prefer to be in front of the curtain.

And he’s eating an apple. Because of course he is. The Serpent in the Garden of Democracy, taking a bite out of the American experiment one policy memo at a time.

The Taco Cart Incident

Early in the video, we get what might be its most economical image: Miller, in his regulation grey suit, standing before a toppled taco cart. The vendor — colorful shirt, straw hat, an expression of wounded dignity — stands with hands on heart amid the wreckage of scattered tortillas and peppers.

It’s a single frame that contains an entire policy platform. No explanation required. The taco cart says everything that needs to be said about what Stephen Miller’s vision of America looks like when it meets actual Americans.

Superhero Stephen

Then the video shifts registers entirely. Suddenly we’re in an animated sequence straight out of a Pixar film your nightmares ordered off wish.com. Miller appears as a CGI superhero — bald dome gleaming, jaw set in heroic determination, the name “Stephen” emblazoned across his chest in a shield that would make a certain Kryptonian file a trademark complaint.

He soars over a desert landscape. He hovers menacingly above a crowd of terrified animated figures — men, women, children — their faces frozen in expressions of cartoon fear as the suited figure descends from above like some bureaucratic angel of deportation.

The juxtaposition is devastating: a man who has never served in the military, never run for elected office, never submitted himself to the judgment of voters, reimagined as a musclebound savior swooping down to save a nation from... the people who built it.

Washington Doesn’t Want You Here

Washington Crossing the Delaware, Except Washington Wishes He Hadn’t.

The historical sequences are where the video reaches its satirical peak. Miller appears in a boat on an icy river — a riff on Washington Crossing the Delaware — except he’s rowing alongside an AI-generated George Washington who looks less than pleased about the company. In one frame, Miller holds a giant snowball. In the next, Washington takes it full in the face.

There’s also a Liberty Bell sequence — Miller appearing among colonial revolutionaries who look variously confused and alarmed at the man in the modern suit who has materialized to ring their bell. The Founding Fathers, who created a nation of immigrants, confronted by the man who would like to un-create it.

And then there are the Founders themselves — gathered around the Constitution, the words “We the People” clearly visible, every AI-generated face frozen in an expression of mocking, open-mouthed laughter. They’re laughing at Miller. They’re laughing at the idea that a grandson of immigrants would build a career trying to slam the door on everyone who came after.

Lincoln’s Lament

And then there is Abraham Lincoln, playing the violin.

It is, for my money, the single most beautiful image in the entire video. Lincoln — the Great Emancipator, the man who held the Union together and paid for it with his life — rendered in soft candlelight, eyes closed, drawing a bow across the strings with the quiet concentration of a man who has seen everything and is trying to turn grief into music. It’s an AI-generated image, and yet there’s something almost unbearably poignant about it. Lincoln looks tired. He looks sad. He looks like a man who freed four million people and is now watching from whatever afterlife the algorithm has placed him in as a White House aide works methodically to make sure the door stays shut for the next four million.

If the Founding Fathers are laughing at Stephen Miller, Lincoln is doing something worse. He’s mourning what Miller has done to the country Lincoln died to save.

The Clown Portrait

At one point the video gives us perhaps its most on-the-nose image: Miller in a suit, staring directly at the camera, wearing a rainbow clown wig and a bright red nose. Behind him, two Founding Fathers — one who appears to be Washington, the other possibly Franklin — look on with expressions of weary, eighteenth-century disdain.

When even the Founding Fathers think you’ve gone too far, you’ve gone too far.

The Ice Cream Shop Scene

As a former ice cream shop owner of fifteen years, I have a particular fondness for one sequence: Miller stalking through an ice cream parlor full of little girls holding cones, looking furious. The children stare at him with wide eyes. He appears to be confiscating their ice cream.

Let me tell you something about ice cream shops: they are places of joy. They are where families come together. They are, in my experience, profoundly democratic spaces — the most diverse mix of humanity you’ll ever see united by a common purpose, which is the pursuit of a double scoop.

Stephen Miller would not do well in an ice cream shop. I can say this with professional authority.

The Wolves, the Birds, and the Pigs

The animal imagery is inspired. Miller strides across a green lawn flanked by wolves with glowing red eyes — the pack leader among predators, or perhaps just the only one wearing a suit. A piglet is engulfed in a cloud of dust, snout skyward. A bird of prey dives toward Miller’s head, which feels less like symbolism and more like wish fulfillment.

The Children

Then the video gets serious for a moment, and the laughter catches in your throat. Children behind bars, hands pressed against the cage, looking out at the man in the suit who walks away without glancing back.

This is the frame you can’t laugh at. This is the frame that turns the satire into something harder and sharper. Because this one isn’t exaggerated. This one is barely even parody.

The CNN Sequence

The video intersperses its AI fantasy sequences with what appear to be clips of Miller’s actual CNN appearances — those interviews where the veins bulge, the eyes narrow, and the talking points come rapid-fire like rounds from a weapon designed to wound the concept of good-faith dialogue. One frame captures a CNN anchor’s expression — a close-up of a face trying to process what it’s hearing while maintaining broadcast composure. It is the face of a man who has just been told that water is dry, by someone who believes it absolutely.

The Presidents’ Club

And then comes the image I want framed on my wall.

Five presidents — or rather, AI’s best approximation of five presidents — standing shoulder to shoulder in front of the Capitol dome, laughing so hard they can barely stand. Lincoln is there, all cheekbones and bow tie, head thrown back. Beside him, FDR — the president who tried to open America’s doors to Jewish refugees fleeing Hitler, and whose failure to do more haunts the historical conscience to this day. That Franklin Roosevelt is standing here laughing at a man whose own Jewish ancestors needed exactly that kind of welcome is an irony so dense it could bend light. Next to him, a figure who could be Teddy Roosevelt, the trust-buster who believed America’s greatness came from its willingness to welcome the world’s strivers. Then Eisenhower — the man who defeated actual fascists, not the metaphorical kind — grinning like he’s just heard the best joke of the twentieth century. And on the far right, Obama, laughing with the easy confidence of a man whose very existence in that lineup is the thing Stephen Miller lies awake at night trying to reverse.

They are laughing at Stephen Miller. They are laughing at the idea that a mid-level policy adviser with no electoral mandate and no military service could redefine what America means. They are laughing because they built the house he’s trying to burn down, and they know — as all builders know — that the house is stronger than the arsonist.

It may be the most devastating four seconds of AI-generated video ever produced.

The Grand Finale

The video builds toward a celebration — AI-generated figures of past presidents laughing together in front of the Capitol, and what appears to be AI-generated versions of the Obamas leading a full dance number, because apparently the algorithm decided this story needed a Broadway ending. A gospel choir in purple robes sings out in the desert sun. Latin American farmers sing together in open fields.

It is, in its AI-generated, occasionally uncanny-valley way, a vision of the America that Stephen Miller is trying to prevent: multiracial, multilingual, joyful, and absolutely unwilling to be quiet about it.

A Queen Victoria Cameo

And because no proper satirical video would be complete without a non sequitur, we get a black-and-white image of a figure who appears to be Queen Victoria — snarling at the camera, delivering the immortal line: “Oh Do Shut Up.”

Four words. The entire British Empire distilled into a single withering command. And honestly? It’s the most appropriate response to Stephen Miller that anyone — real, AI-generated, or deceased since 1901 — has yet produced. If only someone had said it to him in 2017, we might all have been spared a great deal of trouble.

A Note on the Medium

Let me pause here to say something about AI-generated satire. There is a legitimate debate about the ethics, the aesthetics, and the implications of using artificial intelligence to create art — including political satire. I’ve written extensively about AI governance in my Asimov Gap series, and I don’t take these questions lightly.

But I will say this: when AI satire punches up — when it takes aim at the powerful, the sanctimonious, and the cruel — it is doing what the best satire has always done. Jonathan Swift didn’t need a computer to suggest eating Irish babies. Mark Twain didn’t need an algorithm to expose American hypocrisy. Sqwidx is working in that tradition, using the tools of the moment to do what satirists have always done: show the powerful as they actually are, not as they wish to be seen.

Stephen Miller wants to be seen as a patriot. This video sees him as a clown in a suit, stealing ice cream from children and throwing snowballs at George Washington.

I know which version is closer to the truth.

Coda: A Personal Encounter

I’m told — and I will leave the source diplomatically unnamed — that someone recently met Stephen Miller for the first time and, admiring that famously smooth cranium, was heard to greet him with: “Glans to see you!”

If you don’t get the joke, I envy your innocence. If you do get the joke, you’re welcome.
