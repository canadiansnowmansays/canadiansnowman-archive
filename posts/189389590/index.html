The Iran Crisis

The Evacuation Signal

Part XV: When Embassies Leave, War Is No Longer Hypothetical

February 27, 2026

U.S. Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee at his office in the embassy in Jerusalem, in August. Credit...Ronen Zvulun/Reuters

There is a language that governments speak in the hours before military action. It is not the language of press conferences or Truth Social posts. It is the language of formal designations, coordinated movements, and carefully worded emails sent at dawn. That language was spoken loudly on Friday morning.

At 10:00 a.m. Jerusalem time, the United States Embassy shifted to what the State Department calls “authorized departure” status. Ambassador Mike Huckabee sent an email to all embassy personnel — reviewed and verified by the New York Times — with a message stripped of diplomatic ambiguity: those wishing to leave “should do so TODAY.” He warned that flights out of Ben-Gurion Airport would be in high demand by day’s end. He suggested staff book passage “to anyplace” from which they could continue travel to Washington. He added, almost as an afterthought: “There is no need to panic.”

When an American ambassador tells staff there is no need to panic, there is reason to pay very close attention.

What “Authorized Departure” Actually Means

Most news coverage treats embassy evacuations as dramatic but imprecise signals. They are neither imprecise nor discretionary. The State Department maintains a formal, legally defined hierarchy of evacuation statuses, each with specific trigger conditions.

“Authorized departure” — the designation activated Friday — allows nonessential personnel and their dependents to leave at government expense when “U.S. national interests or imminent threat to life requires it.” It is voluntary. Personnel may stay if they choose.

The next level, “ordered departure,” removes that choice. It is reserved for situations where the threat is assessed as immediate and certain.

The fact that Friday’s designation was authorized rather than ordered tells us something precise: the US government assessed the threat as serious enough to fund evacuations, but the timing as uncertain enough that individual judgment still applies. In plain terms: it is coming, but the exact moment is not yet fixed.

Huckabee’s email was not a standalone event. It followed what he described as “meetings and phone calls through the night” with the State Department. It was coordinated with a formal State Department travel advisory issued the same morning, urging all Americans to “reconsider travel” to both Israel and the West Bank, citing “terrorism and civil unrest.”

That coordination — embassy action and State Department advisory issued simultaneously — does not happen organically. It is the product of a decision made at the senior level, communicated downward, and executed in parallel across multiple departments. Someone gave an order.

The Allied Evacuation Pattern

What transforms the Huckabee email from a significant development into a near-certain signal is what surrounds it. Governments do not coordinate evacuations across multiple countries, across multiple allied nations, simultaneously, unless they are operating from shared intelligence about a shared timeline.

The sequence this week tells the story:

Monday: The US Embassy in Lebanon ordered the departure of all non-emergency personnel and the family members of all government staff. Not authorized — ordered. Lebanon, which hosts Hezbollah, is the most likely theatre for Iranian retaliation against Israel.

Wednesday: Australia announced that dependents of its diplomats in Israel and Lebanon should leave, and offered voluntary departures to dependents of staff in the UAE, Qatar, and Jordan — the precise geography of US military basing in the region.

Thursday: The UK pulled all its diplomats from Iran entirely and issued a warning against all travel to the country. France advised against travel to Israel.

Friday: The US moved its Israel mission to authorized departure. KLM, the Dutch airline, suspended all service to Israel, citing “commercial and operational considerations” — the airline industry’s standard formulation for “we have been told something we cannot say publicly.”

This is not a series of independent precautionary decisions made by sovereign governments acting on their own assessments. This is coordinated allied behaviour of the kind that precedes military action. Someone is sharing intelligence. The recipients are acting on it.

The Thursday Geneva Talks: What Silence Reveals

Against this backdrop of coordinated evacuations, Thursday’s third round of Geneva talks concluded after six hours with neither breakthrough nor collapse. Iranian officials and Omani mediators said both sides had agreed to continue talking next week. That is the optimistic framing.

The pessimistic framing is in what didn’t happen. Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner — Trump’s two lead negotiators — said nothing publicly after the talks ended. The White House declined to comment. In a negotiation where genuine progress exists, negotiators talk. Their silence after six hours in Geneva, on the same day their embassy was evacuating staff from Israel, is its own kind of statement.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio announced Friday that he would travel to Israel early next week to discuss Iran, Lebanon, and Gaza. A secretary of state visiting the frontline ally immediately after inconclusive nuclear talks, while embassy staff are being evacuated, is not a routine diplomatic visit.

Araghchi told Iranian state media the talks had “positive elements” and that both sides would meet again. He may be right. He may also be describing the last round of talks before the first strike.

The Timing Question

We are currently inside Ramadan, which began approximately February 19 and runs until around March 19-20, with Eid al-Fitr celebrations immediately following. The question of whether military action would come during or after the holy month is not advocacy — it is the analytical question that military historians and regional strategists are actively debating.

The historical record offers no clear rule. The United States launched Operation Desert Storm on January 17, 1991, deliberately before Ramadan began. Israel launched the Six-Day War in June 1967 with no consideration of Islamic observances. Egypt and Syria launched the Yom Kippur War during the Jewish day of fasting in 1973. The US invaded Iraq in March 2003, during Ramadan’s aftermath.

What the timing question does reveal is the tension between two strategic calculations. Striking during Ramadan carries diplomatic costs in the Arab world — the states Trump needs to at minimum stay quiet during any operation. The Huckabee interview on Tucker Carlson last week, in which he said Israel has a right to much of the Middle East, has already enraged Arab diplomats. Compounding that with strikes during Islam’s holiest month adds to a regional relations problem that is already serious.

But Trump’s own self-imposed timeline works against waiting. He told reporters on February 20 that 10 to 15 days was “pretty much maximum” for the negotiating window. That window closes around March 6-7 — well before Ramadan ends, well before Eid. If Trump means what he said, Ramadan is not a variable he has given himself room to consider.

The military, for its part, was briefed ready to strike as of last Saturday, February 21. The USS Gerald R. Ford is now within striking range. The USS Abraham Lincoln is in position. More than 100 aircraft, including F-35s, F-22s, and F-16s, have moved to the region. Forty to fifty percent of all deployable US airpower in the world is now concentrated around one country.

Forces at that level of readiness and cost cannot remain indefinitely on station. Aircraft carriers completing some of the longest deployments in recent memory cannot circle the Arabian Sea waiting for a diplomatic calendar to clear. Every day of inaction carries its own cost — in readiness, in credibility, and in the pressure Trump has publicly placed on himself.

What the Evidence Suggests

This essay is an act of journalism, not advocacy. It documents what governments do in the hours before military action, and matches that template against what is happening right now.

✓ Embassies activate authorized departure.
✓ Allied nations coordinate simultaneous evacuations.
✓ Commercial airlines suspend service.
✓ Negotiators go silent after inconclusive talks.
✓ The Secretary of State flies to the frontline ally.
✓ The military is briefed ready and in position.

Every box is checked.

The President Speaks

President Trump addressing reporters before leaving the White House for a trip to Texas on Friday.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

While Ambassador Huckabee’s evacuation email was circulating through the embassy in Jerusalem on Friday, President Trump was walking to his motorcade at the White House, headed for a trip to Texas.

Reporters asked about the Geneva talks.

“I’m not happy with the fact that they’re not willing to give us what we have to have,” he said. “I’m not thrilled with that. We’ll see what happens. We’re talking later.”

That last phrase — we’re talking later — is significant. On a Friday afternoon when US Embassy staff are being told to leave Israel today if they intend to leave at all, the President is indicating a conversation is imminent. With whom, about what, he did not say.

The gap between the two sides’ public statements is now a chasm. Oman’s foreign minister Badr Albusaidi, who has been mediating the talks, posted on social media Friday that he met with Vice President Vance and looked “forward to further and decisive progress in the coming days.” Iranian officials and Omani mediators said after Thursday’s six-hour Geneva session that both sides had agreed to continue talking next week.

Trump’s version: “They cannot have nuclear weapons and we’re not thrilled with the way they’re negotiating.”

This is no longer ambiguity. This is two parties describing two different realities after the same negotiation. One side is preparing its diplomats to depart. The other is posting about decisive progress.

The New York Times published both stories — Trump’s dissatisfaction and the Huckabee email — in the same article, on the same Friday afternoon. The paper of record is reading these two signals as connected.

So should we.
