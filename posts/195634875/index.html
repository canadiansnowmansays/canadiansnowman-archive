The Iran Crisis

No War, No Peace

Part LXIX: Day 58. The ceasefire holds — in name. The Lebanon truce holds — in name. Araghchi is in St. Petersburg. Trump cancelled the Islamabad flight. The stalemate has become the strategy. The spa

April 27, 2026

A mural in Tehran depicting Iranian missiles attacking a U.S. Navy ship.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

I. The Limbo Declared

On Saturday, Witkoff and Kushner were packed. The Serena Hotel was cleared. Pakistan security officials had distributed a statement to journalists confirming the Iranian delegation was expected. Araghchi had arrived in Islamabad with a written Iranian response to the US proposal. Then Trump cancelled the flight. “I’ve told my people a little while ago they were getting ready to leave, and I said, ‘Nope, you’re not making an 18 hour flight to go there. We have all the cards. They can call us anytime they want, but you’re not going to be making any more 18 hour flights to sit around talking about nothing.’”

Araghchi left for Oman. Returned to Pakistan Sunday for a second meeting with Pakistani mediators. Then flew to St. Petersburg to meet Putin. The diplomacy is not dead — it has become a circuit: Islamabad, Muscat, Moscow, back to Islamabad. No American in any of those rooms.

The Solomon analysis — “Iran and U.S. Sink Into Awkward Limbo of ‘No War, No Peace’” — is the frame for this week. Iran’s own prominent conservative newspaper Khorasan described it as “a strategic limbo” with considerable risks: “Both sides have stepped back from the costs of full-scale war but have not moved beyond the logic of force and pressure. This may be more dangerous than short-term war itself.”

Iran’s most prominent economic newspaper Donya-e-Eghtesad has modelled the scenarios with brutal clarity: best-case deal produces 49% inflation. No-war-no-peace produces 70%. Return to war produces hyperinflation above 120%. These are not American projections. They are Iranian ones. The Iranian press is telling the Iranian leadership exactly what is at stake. The generals are hearing it. They are choosing the status quo anyway, because any concession creates a domestic political liability that could be weaponized against them later.

Former Iranian official Karimi: in Iran, “the status quo is the most conservative way of behaving politically now, because any change raises the possibility of being blamed in the future if the plan fails.”

Both sides believe they can outlast the other. Both sides may be right about each other’s pain tolerance, which is precisely why the stalemate is stable and precisely why it is dangerous.

II. The Nuclear Problem Trump Helped Create

The Broad/Sanger analysis — “Trump Seeks to Abolish Iran’s Atomic Stockpile, a Problem He Helped Create” — is the essential historical accounting that Monday’s diplomatic stalemate demands. Trump withdrew from the JCPOA in 2018 while Iran was complying. Under that deal, Iran had shipped 97% of its uranium stockpile — 12.5 tons — to Russia, leaving too little for a single bomb. Then Trump pulled out. Then Iran enriched with a vengeance.

Today Iran has 11 tons of uranium at various enrichment levels. Enough, if further enriched, for 50 to 100 nuclear weapons. Harvard’s Cochran: 50-100 bombs. Union of Concerned Scientists’ Lyman: 35-55 weapons. Virtually all of this cache accumulated after 2018. The problem Trump is now trying to solve with bombs, blockades and 18-hour flights is one he created with a pen in the Rose Garden.

The enrichment took place in Iran’s newest nuclear facility, Fordo, shown here in a satellite image from 2013.Credit...DigitalGlobe, via Getty Images

The 970 pounds of 60%-enriched uranium at Isfahan — “nuclear dust” in Trump’s formulation — is the fraction that has received public attention. It is the smallest part of the problem. The full 11-ton stockpile is what Harvard’s Bunn calls the actual challenge. “We can’t bomb away their knowledge,” he said. And since an enrichment plant “can be comparable in size to a grocery store,” Iran’s mountainous terrain offers many hiding places.

Former CIA Director Burns, writing in the Times Friday, described what a good deal would require: “tight nuclear inspections, an extended moratorium on the enrichment of uranium and the export or dilution of Tehran’s existing stockpile of enriched uranium in exchange for tangible sanctions relief.” Burns: “Unless the lines are clearly drawn and strictly monitored, the Iranians will paint outside them.” That is exactly what happened in 2018. Vance demanded 20 years of enrichment suspension in Islamabad I. Trump then declared the right period was “unlimited.” The goalposts are not fixed. The technical complexity is not diminishing.

III. The Generals and the Circuit

Araghchi’s weekend itinerary — Islamabad, Oman, back to Islamabad, then St. Petersburg — is not random. It is a map of Iran’s diplomatic architecture under IRGC collective leadership.

Pakistan: the channel to Washington, the only venue where direct US-Iran contact has occurred. Munir is Tehran’s most trusted interlocutor. Interior Minister Naqvi — a Shiite Muslim — provides the religious credibility. Pakistan is also managing its own 35 million Shiites, who stormed the US Consulate in Karachi on March 1 and are watching every concession.

Oman: shares the Hormuz coastline. Iran sees coordination with Muscat as essential to any settlement framework on the strait. Oman has maintained neutrality and diplomatic relations with all parties throughout — the same role it played in the secret pre-JCPOA talks in 2013.

Moscow: Nechepurenko’s reporting — “Top Iranian Official Goes to Russia for Talks With Putin on Middle East War” — confirms what the alliance architecture suggests. Russia has provided satellite imagery of US warship locations to Iran. Diplomatic cover at the UN — vetoed the resolution calling for Hormuz reopening. Caspian trade links bypassing the maritime blockade. But Russia will not deliver advanced weapons, is not condemning Iranian attacks on Gulf states it needs, and is primarily using the Iran war as leverage on Ukraine negotiations with Trump. Moscow-linked facilities in Iran including the Bushehr nuclear plant have been attacked at least four times. Russia condemned the attacks and did nothing.

Putin’s interest in Araghchi’s visit is almost entirely instrumental: validate Russia’s mediator status, keep Iran in its orbit, avoid becoming entangled in a war that is benefiting Russia financially through the oil price surge without requiring any Russian sacrifice. Russian oil revenues get an estimated $200 million per day windfall from the Hormuz closure. Russia is the war’s clearest beneficiary. It has done nothing to end it.

The Fassihi IRGC power structure piece remains the essential context: the generals authorized Araghchi’s Islamabad trip — but Araghchi has been marginalized within the Iranian system. His attendance instead of Ghalibaf at Islamabad III was either a tactical softening by the pragmatist faction or a deliberate downgrade in commitment level. When Trump cancelled Witkoff and Kushner’s flight, the question became moot. But the circuit continues. Araghchi is not stopping. He is building the diplomatic infrastructure for a deal the generals have not yet authorized.

IV. The Rubio Vacancy

The Crowley analysis — “Marco Rubio’s Absence From Iran Talks Highlights His Stay-at-Home Role” — frames the American diplomatic architecture with equal precision. Kerry met his Iranian counterpart at least 18 times over 20 months for the JCPOA. Rubio has not attended a single Iran negotiating session. Has not visited the Middle East since a brief Israel stop last October. Six foreign cities visited all year, including Milan for the Winter Olympics.

While Vance, Kushner and Witkoff were in Islamabad I, Rubio was at Trump’s side at a UFC event. Stimson Center’s Emma Ashford: “Rubio clearly prefers to stay close to Trump.” His dual role as both Secretary of State and National Security Adviser — first since Kissinger — means he functions less as a diplomat than as an anchor at Trump’s side. Ashford: “I do think it’s to the detriment of the whole department of State and to America’s ability to conduct diplomacy in general that we effectively have the secretary of state position sitting vacant.”

The result is a negotiating team led by a real estate developer and the president’s son-in-law, advised by a vice president who privately opposed the war, operating without the institutional knowledge, expert backup, or diplomatic continuity that every previous major US-Iran negotiation required. The 2015 deal ran 160 pages and five technical annexes. The current written US proposal is three pages establishing a framework. The gap between the complexity of the problem and the weight of the diplomatic instrument being applied to it is the defining structural feature of this negotiation.

V. The Sanctions Zigzag

The Rappeport/Livni analysis — “U.S. Sanctions Zigzag in New World of Economic Warfare” — documents the incoherence with receipts. Bessent declared mid-April: no Russia oil waiver extension. Two days later, Friday evening, Treasury quietly issued another 30-day reprieve. Then Friday April 25, Bessent told AP the US does not plan to renew it again. Current waiver expires May 16. Russia earning an estimated $200 million per day from oil sales enabled by the waiver — while simultaneously providing Iran with satellite imagery of US warship locations.

Senator Coons: “No country has profited more from this war than Russia. You don’t have to read The Art of War to know that helping your adversaries gain money while you’re at war is a terrible idea.”

On Iran: US granted 30-day Iranian oil exemption in March, then let it expire and launched Operation Economic Fury with 40 shadow fleet sanctions plus the Hengli Petrochemical Refinery sanction — China’s second largest independent refinery. Defense Priorities’ Kavanagh: “It is a kind of whiplash in terms of policy. This whole back and forth is evidence the Trump administration did not expect this to last this long.” Council on Foreign Relations’ Fishman: “We don’t have a playbook for this kind of economic warfare, which may help explain some of the fumbling by the United States.”

VI. The Meme War

The Myers/Thompson analysis — “Iran’s Meme War Against Trump Ushers In a Future of ‘Slopaganda’” — is the information warfare dimension that every other account of this conflict has underweighted. 150 official Iranian accounts gained approximately 900 million views in the first 50 days of the war — a thirtyfold increase from the same period before. The Lego videos featuring a sweating, cowering Trump figure and resolute Iranian soldiers have been watched tens of millions of times.

The Institute for Strategic Dialogue’s Bret Schafer: “They have taken a regime that is brutal and didn’t have exactly a great global reputation and turned them into kind of a plucky, fun underdog.” The tactic has been named “slopaganda” — AI-generated propaganda designed to travel through algorithmic social media engines. Iran’s meme warfare “offers a blueprint that authoritarian actors can replicate in the future.”

The Chait formulation from last week sits beside this: “The posting will continue until morale improves.” Both sides are fighting a war in which social media is an operational domain. Trump posts “IRAN HAS JUST ANNOUNCED THAT THE STRAIT OF IRAN IS FULLY OPEN” and the IRGC seizes two ships the next morning. Iran posts Lego Trump cowering and the videos get 9.8 million views. The information battlefield is not separate from the kinetic one. It is the same battlefield.

VII. Lebanon: A Cease-Fire in Name Only

Sunday was one of the deadliest days in Lebanon since the truce was declared. Fourteen killed including two women and two children. One Israeli soldier killed — Lebanon toll now 16 Israeli soldiers, 2 civilians. Hezbollah leader Qassem Monday: will not “relinquish its weapons or its defenses.” Also condemned the Lebanese government for negotiating with Israel at all — called it “a gratuitous and humiliating concession.” The Lebanese government does not control Hezbollah. This has been true for decades. It is still true now.

The Dahir analysis — “Lebanese Ask, ‘What Cease-Fire?’ as Violence Simmers in the South” — provides the structural judgment: International Crisis Group’s David Wood: “This is not so much a cease-fire as a limited de-escalation.” Also: “One factor that makes the truce incredibly shaky is that it’s largely contingent on President Trump’s attention. President Trump forced through this cease-fire largely because he didn’t want continued fighting in Lebanon to scupper negotiations with Iran.”

A mass funeral in Ramadiyeh, Lebanon, on Friday, mourning those killed in recent weeks in the war with Israel.Credit...David Guttenfelder/The New York Times

The negotiations with Iran have been scuppered anyway. The Lebanon truce, now extended three weeks, is therefore depending on a diplomatic rationale that no longer exists. Netanyahu: “Hezbollah’s violations are essentially disintegrating the cease-fire.” Israel is continuing to demolish Lebanese border towns, operating outside the six-mile forward defense line, and killing people during the truce period. The Boxerman Sunday piece title captures the situation precisely: both truces are on shaky ground, neither yet at breaking point.

Kamil Mohamed Mansour, 78, displaced from Tallouseh, living in a tent at a Beirut stadium: “What cease-fire are you talking about? I have lost everything and am sitting here alone.”

VIII. Cooking Oil

The Torbati reporting from Kapikoy — “On Iran’s Border, Cooking Oil Trade Is a Snapshot of the Country’s Struggles” — is the most intimate document of the week. At Turkey’s border crossing with Iran, Iranians are crossing to buy cooking oil. Five-liter bottles for $10, reselling inside Iran for a $2 profit. A shopkeeper in a shed of stacked oil boxes reads his colleagues the latest news from his phone: peace talks postponed. Demand for cooking oil has spiked.

Iran’s minimum wage: $108 per month. IMF projects 70% inflation this year — highest since at least 1980. Tehran resident Bibijan, 71: three small chickens now cost what five or six used to. The government removed cooking oil subsidies in January and gave Iranians $7 per month in compensation. Mass layoffs spreading. Internet blackout continuing. Airspace reopening, slowly — international flights resumed Saturday from Imam Khomeini airport. Istanbul, Muscat, Medina.

The Lego team in Tehran is generating 9.8 million views per video. The people at the Kapikoy border are carrying bottles of cooking oil home on foot.

IX. The War Powers Clock

May 1. Four days away. Trump formally notified Congress of the operation on March 2 — the 60-day clock under the 1973 War Powers Resolution expires Thursday. Senate has blocked five Democratic war powers resolutions. Senator Curtis of Utah still on record: will not support military action past 60 days without congressional authorization. House Foreign Affairs chairman Mast: different vote count after 60 days. Murkowski working on an AUMF, not yet introduced.

Trump’s likely escape route remains the same: presidents of both parties have argued the War Powers Resolution is unconstitutional. He will probably ignore it. The question is whether enough Republicans will object to matter politically. Murphy of Connecticut: “I do think it will be harder for Republicans to continue to look the other way once we are out of the 60 days.”

The war has cost $28-35 billion. The US has fired more Tomahawk missiles than it buys in a decade. The JASSM-ER stockpile built for a China war is nearly depleted. The Patriot interceptors produced in a full year have been fired in eight weeks. Senator Reed: “At current production rates, reconstituting what we have expended could take years.” The Pentagon has not found the funding to begin the production expansion it announced in January.

This is the war at Day 58: a stalemate between two blockades, a ceasefire in name only in Lebanon, a nuclear problem that predates the war and will survive it, a diplomatic circuit that keeps moving without connecting, and a War Powers deadline four days away that everyone expects to be ignored.

In a shed in eastern Turkey, a shopkeeper reads his colleagues the news. Peace talks postponed. The cooking oil is selling well.

THE NUMBERS

Dead: Iran — more than 3,000, including 1,700 civilians. Lebanon — roughly 2,500, still rising. Gulf states — 32. Israel — 23 civilians in Israel, 16 soldiers in Lebanon. American service members — 13. UNIFIL — 6 peacekeepers killed. Lebanon journalist Amal Khalil — killed in Israeli airstrike.

Lebanon — Sunday: 14 killed in one of the deadliest days since truce declared. 1 Israeli soldier killed. Hezbollah: will not disarm. Qassem: government made “gratuitous and humiliating concession” by negotiating with Israel.

Diplomacy: Islamabad III: Araghchi arrived Friday with written Iranian response. Trump cancelled Witkoff/Kushner flight Saturday. Araghchi went to Oman, returned to Pakistan Sunday, now in St. Petersburg with Putin. Islamabad II never happened. Islamabad III half-happened. Next round: unscheduled.

Oil: Brent ~$105-107/barrel. Pre-war: ~$63. Gas national average $4.06. Diesel $5.49. Energy Secretary Wright: prices may not return to $3 until 2027.

Strait: 3 ships crossed Thursday (Kpler). 17 vessels Wednesday-Thursday combined. 34 merchant vessels turned back by US blockade. Touska in US custody. Majestic X and Tifani boarded in Indian Ocean. Iran parliament introducing legislation to charge tolls. Mines in the strait: US military and intelligence divided on how many.

Sanctions: 40 shadow fleet firms sanctioned Friday. Hengli Petrochemical Refinery sanctioned. Russia oil waiver extended to May 16 — then Bessent said won’t be renewed again. Russia earning ~$200 million/day from war-elevated oil prices.

Meme war: 900 million views from 150 Iranian government accounts in first 50 days — 30x increase. Lego Trump videos: up to 9.8 million views per clip.

Iran economy: IMF projects 70% inflation this year. Minimum wage $108/month. Cooking oil being smuggled across Turkish border. Internet blackout continuing. International flights partially resumed.

Nuclear: 11 tons total uranium stockpile. 970 pounds at 60% enrichment buried at Isfahan. Enough for 50-100 weapons if further enriched. Unknown location of undeclared enrichment facility. US position: 20-year suspension (Vance) / unlimited (Trump). Iran’s latest: 10+10 years.

War Powers: May 1 — 4 days. Fifth war powers resolution blocked Wednesday. Trump expected to ignore deadline.

Lebanon truce: Extended 3 weeks from Thursday April 24. Fraying. Hezbollah: won’t disarm. Israel: continuing strikes, demolitions. 14 killed Sunday.

Days of war: 58.
