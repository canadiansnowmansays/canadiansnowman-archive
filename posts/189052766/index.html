The Superfund Lie

Part 32: When Trump Cuts Cleanup While Polluters Win (2025-2026)

March 26, 2026

The Greatest Cleanup in History (According to Trump)

A sign and fence around a Superfund cleanup site. markzvo/Wikipedia

On April 29, 2025, the Environmental Protection Agency issued a press release with a triumphant headline: “EPA is Racking Up Massive Environmental Accomplishments Under the Trump Administration.”

The release detailed Superfund cleanup activities across the country: construction beginning at the Mohawk Tannery Site in New Hampshire for a future 546-unit residential development, dredging of contaminated sediment at the Callahan Mine in Maine, asbestos safety training in Boston, consent decrees for cleanup in New Jersey and Puerto Rico, vapor intrusion mitigation in New Mexico.

EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin touted his “historic” commitment to addressing PFAS contamination and cleaning up America’s most polluted sites. “EPA aims to implement comprehensive actions,” the announcement proclaimed, guided by “strengthening scientific research” and “holding polluters accountable.”

The message was clear: Trump’s EPA was getting things done. Superfund sites were being cleaned up. Communities were being protected. Polluters were being held responsible.

It was a lie.

One week later, on May 8, 2025, Trump released his fiscal year 2026 budget proposal for the EPA. It cut Superfund cleanup funding by $254 million. It slashed the EPA’s total budget by 54%—from $9.14 billion to $4.16 billion—the lowest level in more than two decades. It eliminated funding for civil and criminal enforcement of environmental laws by 69% and 50%, respectively. And it proposed cutting the EPA’s workforce to fewer than 13,000 employees—2,000 fewer than when Trump took office.

The “historic” Superfund accomplishments? They were being funded by residual Biden-era appropriations and Superfund tax revenues that Trump’s budget would redirect away from cleanup. The “comprehensive PFAS actions”? Trump’s EPA had just gutted drinking water protections for four PFAS chemicals and eliminated 97% of reporting requirements.

The press releases celebrated cleanup. The budget gutted the agency responsible for it. The rhetoric promised accountability. The reality protected polluters.

This is the story of how Trump claimed to clean up America’s most toxic sites while systematically dismantling the agency responsible for doing the work—and how polluters faced no consequences while communities continued to suffer.

The Superfund Program: What It’s Supposed to Do

The Superfund cleanup process Trump destroyed. Each step requires staff Trump eliminated, expertise Trump fired, and funding Trump cut by 54%. Result: 300+ toxic sites delayed indefinitely while Americans drink contaminated water. Source: EPA Superfund Site Assessment Process

The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act—known as CERCLA or Superfund—was enacted in 1980 following the Love Canal disaster, where a community in New York discovered their homes had been built on top of a toxic waste dump.

Superfund gives the EPA authority to:

  • Identify the most contaminated sites in America

  • Force responsible parties to clean them up or reimburse the government for cleanup costs

  • When no viable responsible party exists, use federal funds to clean up sites anyway

It’s how America deals with its most dangerous environmental contamination: abandoned chemical plants, illegal dumpsites, contaminated groundwater, toxic soil, industrial waste that threatens communities.

Since 1980, Superfund has addressed more than 1,300 sites on the National Priorities List—the most severely contaminated locations in the country. These aren’t minor pollution issues. These are sites where exposure to contamination causes cancer, birth defects, neurological damage, and chronic illness.

The program works through a combination of enforcement—making polluters pay—and federal funding for sites where responsible parties can’t be found or have gone bankrupt. Prior to 2021, Superfund was funded through annual congressional appropriations. In 2021-2022, Congress restored excise taxes on selected chemicals and petroleum products, creating a dedicated revenue stream.

Under Biden, the EPA reduced Superfund’s line in the general budget, expecting tax revenues would more than make up the difference. The approach worked: Superfund cleanup accelerated.

Then Trump took office.

And he moved immediately to redirect Superfund money away from cleanup while claiming his EPA was achieving “massive environmental accomplishments.”

The Budget Betrayal: 54% Cuts While Touting Success

Trump’s fiscal year 2026 budget proposal for the EPA, released in March 2025, reveals the gap between rhetoric and reality.

Total EPA Budget:

  • FY 2025 (enacted): $9.14 billion

  • FY 2026 (Trump proposal): $4.16 billion

  • Cut: 54%

This represents less than half of EPA’s budget in any year of Trump’s first term—and the lowest EPA funding level in more than two decades.

Superfund Specific:

  • Cut: $254 million from cleanup funding

  • Despite celebrating “historic” Superfund accomplishments weeks earlier

Enforcement:

  • Civil enforcement: Cut 69%

  • Criminal enforcement: Cut 50%

  • Brownfields program (cleaning up abandoned industrial sites for redevelopment): Cut 50%

Science:

  • All climate-related programs: Eliminated entirely

  • Greenhouse gas reporting and tracking: Eliminated

  • Energy Star program: Eliminated

  • Research and science programs: Drastically reduced

State and Tribal Grants:

  • Operating grants supporting local implementation of environmental laws: Eliminated entirely

  • State capitalization grants (revolving loans for water infrastructure): Drastically reduced

The administration argues in its budget document that “the federal government has done enough” and that protecting environmental gains “should not be paid for with federal money.” It claims the EPA will focus on “core statutory responsibilities” while eliminating programs that “hinder economic growth.”

In practice, this means:

  • Fewer EPA employees to conduct inspections

  • Fewer enforcement actions against polluters

  • Slower Superfund cleanups

  • Less support for states and tribes

  • Elimination of climate and science programs

  • Redirection of funding from public health to “economic growth”

Antonieta Cádiz, Deputy Executive Director for Climate Power, summarized the hypocrisy: “The Trump administration’s hypocrisy has no limits.”

The EPA releases press releases celebrating Superfund cleanups. Then Trump’s budget cuts the funding needed to continue them.

The Staffing Collapse: 2,000 EPA Employees Gone

EPA employees in Chicago protest attacks on the agency by the Trump administration on March 25 E. Jason Wambsgans/Chicago Tribune/Tribune News Service/Getty

Budget cuts were bad enough. But Trump didn’t stop there. He systematically destroyed the EPA’s workforce—the scientists, inspectors, and enforcement officers who actually do the work of protecting public health.

By the numbers:

  • FY 2025 (enacted): 14,130 EPA employees

  • FY 2026 (Trump proposal): 12,856 employees

  • Reduction: 1,274 employees (9%)

But the actual staffing losses were even worse. According to the Center for Biological Diversity’s January 2026 report, 2,000 EPA staff were lost in Trump’s first year—through firings, forced retirements, resignations, and eliminated positions.

This represents approximately 14% of the agency’s workforce—gone in twelve months.

The consequences are immediate and severe:

Inspections: Fewer inspectors means fewer site visits, delayed detection of violations, and reduced oversight of polluters.

Enforcement: Without staff to investigate, document violations, and pursue cases, polluters face diminished consequences.

Science: Research programs have been gutted, making it harder to identify emerging contaminants, assess health risks, and develop new protections.

Cleanup: Superfund sites require ongoing monitoring, remediation planning, and oversight. Fewer staff means slower cleanup.

Grant Administration: States and tribes need EPA support to implement environmental programs. Staff cuts leave them without technical assistance.

Former EPA leaders described the staffing collapse in stark terms. One noted that the reduction “cripples the agency’s ability to protect the public from poisonous pollution that sickens thousands of people every year.”

Stephanie Kurose, deputy government affairs director at the Center for Biological Diversity, said: “The United States was once the global leader in conservation and now it’s known for environmental destruction.”

President Trump announced in February 2025 that EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin planned to cut up to 65% of the agency’s staff—an even more catastrophic reduction that would leave the EPA unable to function.

As of February 2026, the administration continues working toward this goal.

The Enforcement Collapse: Polluters Face No Consequences

With budget cuts and staffing losses came the predictable result: enforcement of environmental laws collapsed.

Civil Enforcement:

  • Budget cut: 69%

  • Fewer lawsuits against polluters

  • Fewer consent decrees requiring cleanup

  • Fewer penalties imposed

Criminal Enforcement:

  • Budget cut: 50%

  • Fewer criminal prosecutions for environmental crimes

  • Reduced deterrent effect

  • Emboldened corporate malfeasance

This continues a pattern from Trump’s first term. According to the Environmental Integrity Project, a nonpartisan watchdog group, EPA pollution cases and charges against polluters dropped significantly under Trump’s first administration.

Now, with even deeper cuts, the collapse has accelerated.

Companies that violate environmental laws face diminished risk of consequences. Polluters know that understaffed EPA offices can’t conduct comprehensive inspections. Industries understand that enforcement budgets can’t sustain lengthy legal battles. Corporations recognize that an administration ideologically opposed to regulation won’t prioritize prosecution.

The result: systematic regulatory capture. The industries the EPA is supposed to regulate know they face minimal oversight and limited accountability. They can violate pollution limits, skip required reporting, delay mandated cleanups, and operate with impunity—confident that an understaffed, underfunded agency won’t have the capacity to stop them.

Superfund Tax Revenues: Redirected Away From Cleanup

Trump’s defenders claim Superfund doesn’t need increased appropriations because the restored excise tax on chemicals and petroleum provides dedicated revenue.

This is technically true. The Superfund tax, restored by Congress in 2021-2022, does generate revenue specifically for site cleanup.

But Trump’s budget proposal redirects how those funds are used.

Under Biden, Superfund tax revenues supplemented reduced appropriations, resulting in net increases for cleanup. The approach prioritized using polluter fees to fund remediation of the contamination polluters created.

Under Trump, the budget proposal treats Superfund tax revenues as justification for cutting federal appropriations—effectively zeroing out the net benefit.

The math:

  • Biden approach: Reduced appropriations + Superfund tax = Net increase for cleanup

  • Trump approach: Deeper appropriation cuts + Superfund tax = Net decrease for cleanup

The Superfund tax was supposed to ensure polluters contributed to cleaning up the mess they created. Trump’s budget uses it as an excuse to slash federal support.

The same polluters who created the contamination see their tax contributions offset by reduced federal enforcement, smaller EPA staff, and slower cleanup requirements. They pay the tax but face diminished accountability.

And Superfund sites—places where contaminated groundwater threatens drinking water, toxic soil endangers children, and industrial waste poisons communities—receive less attention, slower remediation, and diminished oversight.

The Rhetoric-Reality Gap: What Trump Says vs. What He Does

The disconnect between Trump’s environmental rhetoric and his environmental policy is comprehensive and systematic:

What Trump Says:

  • “EPA is racking up massive environmental accomplishments”

  • “Historic commitment to PFAS”

  • “Holding polluters accountable”

  • “Clean air, land, and water for all Americans”

  • “Protecting Long Islanders and all Americans from contaminated drinking water”

What Trump Does:

  • Cuts EPA budget 54%

  • Eliminates 2,000 staff

  • Slashes enforcement 50-69%

  • Guts PFAS protections

  • Delays coal ash cleanup

  • Redirects Superfund tax revenues

  • Eliminates state and tribal grants

The rhetoric celebrates cleanup. The budget prevents it.

The rhetoric promises accountability. The enforcement cuts protect polluters.

The rhetoric claims environmental protection. The policy destroys the agency responsible for it.

This pattern extends beyond Superfund. Across every environmental domain—air quality, water protection, toxic chemicals, climate change—Trump’s administration issues press releases celebrating accomplishments while implementing policies that undermine them.

In August 2020, Erin Brockovich wrote in a CNN op-ed about Trump’s environmental approach: “One of the most contentious talking points from the Trump administration is the idea that the environmental movement, and regulations in particular, hurts working-class people. I speak with some of these same people every day and I can tell you that they are not suffering because of regulations. Just ask residents of Flint, or those impacted by the Elk River spill in West Virginia what clean water means to them.”

She was describing Trump’s first term. But the pattern has only intensified in his second.

Trump claims to care about communities. Then he eliminates the grants that help them implement environmental protections.

Trump promises to hold polluters accountable. Then he cuts enforcement by 69%.

Trump celebrates Superfund cleanup. Then he slashes the funding and staff needed to do it.

The Project 2025 Blueprint: Gutting Environmental Protection by Design

Trump’s environmental destruction isn’t accidental. It’s the systematic implementation of Project 2025, the Heritage Foundation blueprint that outlined exactly how a second Trump administration would dismantle federal environmental protections.

Project 2025 called for:

  • Reducing EPA staffing

  • Eliminating climate programs

  • Weakening Superfund enforcement

  • Transferring authority to states (often with weaker standards)

  • Prioritizing economic development over environmental protection

According to the Center for Biological Diversity, “The Trump administration moved aggressively to implement Project 2025, completing more than half of the environmental rollbacks outlined in that far-right strategy blueprint” in the first year.

The Superfund budget cuts, EPA staffing reductions, enforcement collapse, and regulatory rollbacks aren’t policy failures—they’re policy successes, judged by Project 2025’s goals.

Trump isn’t failing to protect the environment. He’s succeeding at destroying environmental protection.

Who Wins, Who Loses

Winners:

  • Chemical companies that contaminated Superfund sites

  • Polluters who face reduced enforcement

  • Industries that benefit from regulatory rollbacks

  • Corporations that can violate environmental laws with impunity

Losers:

  • Communities living near Superfund sites

  • Families drinking contaminated water

  • Workers exposed to toxic chemicals

  • Children playing on contaminated soil

  • Taxpayers who fund cleanup when polluters aren’t held accountable

  • Future generations who inherit unremediated contamination

The pattern is consistent with everything Trump does: privatize the profits, socialize the costs. Companies profit from polluting. Communities pay with their health. Taxpayers pay for cleanup. Future generations pay forever.

And Trump faces no accountability for any of it.

The Accountability Void

No one has been held accountable for gutting the EPA while claiming to strengthen it.

Trump faces no legal consequences for cutting Superfund funding while celebrating cleanup accomplishments. EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin faces no discipline for eliminating 2,000 staff while promising to protect public health. The political appointees who slashed enforcement budgets face no penalties for letting polluters operate with impunity.

Congress has taken no action. Republicans celebrate the “deregulation” as economic progress. Democrats issue critical statements but pass no corrective legislation, file no articles of impeachment, hold no administrators in contempt.

The courts cannot restore budgets that Congress has the authority to set. Judicial review doesn’t help when the damage is political rather than legal.

The public has accepted it. EPA budget cuts barely make headlines. The elimination of 2,000 environmental protection jobs is treated as routine government downsizing rather than systematic destruction of public health infrastructure.

Environmental groups sound alarms. Activists organize resistance. Scientists warn of consequences. But without political accountability, institutional pressure, or public outrage sufficient to force change, the destruction continues.

Trump issues press releases celebrating cleanup while implementing budgets that prevent it. He promises accountability while slashing enforcement. He claims to protect communities while eliminating the agency responsible for protecting them.

And he faces no consequences for the lie.

What Happens to Communities

This image provided by FlintWaterStudy.org shows water samples from a Flint, Mich. home. The bottles were collected, from left, on Jan. 15 (2), Jan. 16 and Jan. 21, 2015.FlintWaterStudy.org

While Trump celebrates and polluters profit, real communities continue to suffer.

Families near Superfund sites watch cleanup schedules slip as EPA staffing falls and funding dries up. Children drink water from systems that can’t afford remediation. Workers are exposed to toxic chemicals with reduced inspections.

The communities that depended on EPA grants for local environmental programs lose that support. Tribes that needed federal assistance to protect their lands face eliminated funding. States that relied on capitalization grants for water infrastructure must find other revenue sources or abandon projects.

Contaminated sites that should be cleaned up remain contaminated. Groundwater that should be monitored goes unmonitored. Polluters that should face enforcement face nothing.

And when communities try to fight—when they demand cleanup, when they call out contamination, when they ask for accountability—they face an EPA that has been systematically destroyed from within, stripped of staff, starved of budget, and directed by political appointees ideologically opposed to the agency’s mission.

Erin Brockovich built her career on the principle that communities matter, that health matters, that accountability matters. She defeated Pacific Gas & Electric because she refused to accept that corporate profits should come before public health.

Trump has systematically destroyed that principle. He has redirected government to serve polluters instead of communities, to protect corporate interests instead of public health, to celebrate rhetorical accomplishments while implementing policies that cause real harm.

And when no one is held accountable for destroying the agency that protects Americans from contamination, what protection do communities have left?
