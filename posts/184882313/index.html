Your Phone Bill and Mine

Why You’re Forced to Pay for Phone Service You’ll Never Receive (And How to Fix It)

The CRTC is finally reviewing Canada’s telecom rules. Here’s the one change that could save every Canadian from a billing scam hiding in plain sight

January 17, 2026

It started with a price increase.

My friend got the dreaded notification from Fido: their monthly wireless bill was going up. Again.

No problem, I thought. I’d dealt with this before. Every carrier has retention plans - special offers they reserve for customers threatening to leave. You just have to ask.

So I called Fido on their behalf while they were still a customer, navigated the automated system, and requested a callback to discuss retention options.

The callback never came.

I waited. Nothing.

Fine. If Fido wasn’t interested in keeping a customer, I’d see what their competitors were offering.

I called Koodo (my own provider) to see what they’d offer a new customer churning from Fido. I’d recently negotiated my own plan down to $20/month for 55GB at 4G speeds. I didn’t expect a new customer to get the same deal, but I figured Koodo could beat whatever Fido’s new increased price was.

I was right.

Koodo offered $30/month for 20GB at 5G speeds, plus an ongoing monthly bonus of 25GB. That’s 45GB total at 5G speeds for $30/month - better than what Fido was charging after their price increase.

We completed the port that same day. December 4th. Four days into Fido’s monthly billing cycle.

Simple math: 4 days of service at roughly $1 per day = about $4.10 owed to Fido.

Then the Fido bill arrived online.

The Problem Reveals Itself

The account balance showed: $42.38

That’s the full monthly charge. For a month of service that would never be provided.

I was determined not to overpay and then chase a refund. Why? Because I know how accounts payable departments work at these companies. Refunds for churned customers are not a priority. Best case scenario: a cheque in 6 weeks. Worst case: endless follow-up calls and disputes.

I just wanted to pay what was actually owed: roughly $4.10 for 4 days of service.

“No problem,” I thought. “I’ll just call Fido and clarify.”

Except now that the account was cancelled, everything had changed.

Fido has eliminated inbound calls. You can’t call them anymore. Period.

“Okay, I’ll use the online chat.”

The AI chatbot blocked access to a human specialist. Wouldn’t connect me.

“Fine, I’ll request a callback.”

Denied. The system informed me that cancelled accounts don’t qualify for callbacks.

Let me be clear about what just happened:

While my friend was a Fido customer, I could request a callback. That callback never came - which is why we switched providers.

After cancelling the account, I couldn’t request a callback anymore. I was locked out completely.

So here I was: willing and able to pay what we owed, unable to reach anyone to determine what that amount actually was, and facing a billing system that showed the wrong balance.

And then it got worse.

Because I didn’t pay the $42.38 we didn’t owe, Fido charged a $1.22 late payment fee.

A penalty. For not paying for service that was never provided.

The “Wait, What?” Moment

Here’s what I discovered when I dug into this:

Rogers, Bell, Telus, and virtually every telecom in Canada bills you 30 days IN ADVANCE.

Read that again.

You’re not paying for service you received. You’re paying for service you MIGHT receive next month.

Your January bill? It covers February service. Your March bill? It covers April service.

When you cancel mid-month, you’ve already paid for service you’ll never get. The carrier is supposed to refund you. Eventually. Maybe on next month’s “final bill.” If their system gets around to it. If you remember to follow up. If accounts payable processes it. If the cheque doesn’t get lost in the mail.

But here’s the thing: their account systems don’t update in real-time when you cancel.

The online balance? Still shows the full amount. The late payment calculations? Still based on the full amount. Your ability to see what you actually owe? Zero.

You’re left with an impossible choice:

  1. Overpay immediately (the full amount you don’t owe) and fight for a refund later - knowing that refund could take 6+ weeks and require multiple follow-up attempts

  2. Wait 30+ days for the “final bill” and risk late fees, credit bureau impacts, and collections harassment

  3. Try to calculate the proration yourself and hope you get it right

All because the carrier’s systems can’t—or won’t—show you a real-time account balance.

And if you try to call and ask? You can’t. Because you’re no longer a customer.

“But That’s Just How Billing Works, Right?”

Wrong.

Your electricity company doesn’t bill you in advance. Your water company doesn’t bill you in advance. Your natural gas company doesn’t bill you in advance.

They measure what you USE, then they bill you. You consume service, then you pay for it. It’s called billing in arrears, and it’s the standard practice for virtually every essential utility in Canada.

Except telecommunications.

Why? I wanted to know. So I started researching.

The Regulatory Gap Nobody Talks About

I read every relevant CRTC decision on wireless billing, consumer protection codes, and cancellation policies going back a decade.

Here’s what I found:

The CRTC has never—not once—examined whether advance billing should be allowed for telecommunications services.

When they created the Wireless Code in 2013, they just accepted the industry’s existing practice.

When they created the Internet Code in 2019, same thing.

When they issued Telecom Decision 2016-171 requiring prorated refunds, they framed it narrowly to prevent “double billing” (paying two carriers for the same period). They never questioned whether consumers should pay carriers in advance at all.

The Commission focused on managing the CONSEQUENCES of advance billing. They never examined the PRACTICE itself.

This is the regulatory gap.

Why Does Advance Billing Even Exist?

Good question.

I found historical precedent in the United States. The Virginia State Corporation Commission allowed advance billing for telecommunications decades ago “to ensure that utility companies had the required amount of cash for working capital. Without it, local rates would have been higher.”

This made sense for Ma Bell in 1975:

  • Credit markets were less accessible

  • Real-time payment systems didn’t exist

  • Smaller carriers had limited capital reserves

But it’s 2026.

Rogers is a multi-billion dollar corporation with access to capital markets at minimal rates. Bell has substantial cash reserves. Telus has sophisticated treasury management.

Meanwhile, your local electric utility—which has MUCH larger infrastructure costs than a wireless carrier—manages just fine billing you in arrears.

The “working capital” excuse is obsolete. Advance billing exists today for one reason: carrier cash flow convenience.

Not consumer protection. Convenience.

What Happened When I Complained

I escalated my complaint to the Commission for Complaints for Telecommunication Services (CCTS).

They told me something important: they can handle individual complaints, but systemic policy issues about billing practices fall outside their mandate. Those need to go to the CRTC.

So I researched the CRTC’s regulatory framework and submitted a formal policy letter.

The timing is perfect: the CRTC announced they’re combining the Wireless Code, Internet Code, TV Service Provider Code, and Deposit & Disconnection Code into one unified Consumer Protection Code in Spring 2026.

This is the first time the CRTC will have the opportunity to examine advance billing from first principles.

Not “how do we manage the problems it creates?”

But “should this practice exist at all?”

My Policy Recommendation

I submitted two options:

PRIMARY SOLUTION: Prohibit advance billing entirely.

Require all telecommunications carriers to bill in arrears—charging customers only for service actually provided, just like electricity, water, and gas.

This solves the problem at its root:

  • Customers pay only for service they receive

  • No overpayments requiring refunds

  • No late payment fees for amounts not actually owed

  • No credit bureau risk during processing delays

  • Account balances automatically reflect reality

ALTERNATIVE SOLUTION: If CRTC won’t go that far, require two things:

1. Real-time account balance updates when cancellations occur

The technology already exists. When you make a payment, your online account balance updates immediately. The same capability should apply when you cancel service—an unbilled credit can be applied in real-time, exactly like a payment.

Carriers have the infrastructure. They choose not to apply it to cancellations.

2. Late payment fees calculated on real-time balances, not original invoices

If I owe $4.10 after cancelling 4 days into a bill cycle, late payment fees should be calculated on $4.10—not on the $42.38 that was invoiced but never actually owed.

Real-time balance updates are meaningless if penalty fees still use the wrong number.

“Won’t This Hurt the Carriers?”

Let’s be clear: switching from advance billing to arrears billing would create a one-time cash flow adjustment.

For one billing cycle, carriers would be collecting payment for last month’s service while providing this month’s service. They’d be carrying that one month of accounts receivable.

Is this manageable?

Absolutely.

Rogers, Bell, and Telus are multi-billion dollar corporations that manage far larger financial transitions routinely:

  • Multi-billion dollar spectrum acquisitions

  • Network infrastructure upgrades

  • Corporate mergers and acquisitions

A one-month accounts receivable shift is trivial by comparison.

And remember: this is a one-time impact, not a permanent revenue reduction. They’d still receive the same total revenue—just on a different timeline.

The question isn’t “CAN carriers handle this?”

The question is: “Should consumer protection principles require them to?”

I say yes.

What You Can Do

The CRTC is opening public consultation on the unified Consumer Protection Code

You can submit a comment.

You don’t need to be a policy expert. You don’t need to write a formal legal brief.

You just need to tell the CRTC your experience and what you think should change.

If you’ve ever:

  • Paid for service you never received

  • Fought for a refund that should have been automatic

  • Faced late payment fees for amounts you never owed

  • Been unable to reach customer service to clarify what you actually owe

  • Wondered why telecoms get to bill in advance when electricity doesn’t

...then you have standing to comment.

Here’s what to include in your comment:

1. Your experience: Briefly describe a time when advance billing created a problem for you (cancelled service, couldn’t see real balance, late fees, etc.)

2. The policy ask: State whether you support:

  • Prohibiting advance billing (preferred), or

  • Requiring real-time balance updates and proper late fee calculations (alternative)

3. Why it matters: Explain why you think this change would protect consumers

Why This Matters

Every single Canadian with telecommunications service is affected by this.

We’re all paying in advance for service we might not receive. We’re all potentially facing late fees calculated on amounts we don’t owe. We’re all carrying the burden when carrier systems fail to reflect reality.

The only reason this seems “normal” is because the entire industry does it.

But universal adoption of a practice doesn’t make it fair.

Most essential utilities figured out decades ago that billing in arrears protects consumers. You use it, then you pay for it. Simple. Fair. Transparent.

Telecommunications should be the same.

The CRTC has never examined this question comprehensively. The Spring 2026 unified code consultation is the first opportunity.

Your comment matters. Regulators pay attention when they hear the same issue from multiple consumers.

This isn’t about Rogers vs. Bell vs. Telus. It’s about whether Canadian telecommunications billing will prioritize carrier cash flow convenience or consumer protection.

The question is simple:

Should you pay for what you get, when you get it?

Or should you pay for what carriers promise, and hope you actually get it?

I know which one I’m advocating for.

If you found this useful, please share it. The more Canadians understand this issue before the consultation opens, the more comments the CRTC will receive, and the more likely we are to see real change.

And if you submit a comment to CRTC, drop a note in the comments below—I’d love to hear your story.
