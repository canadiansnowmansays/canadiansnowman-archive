When They Come For the Fed

Jerome Powell Under Criminal Investigation for Setting Interest Rates Trump Doesn’t Like

January 11, 2026

U.S. Attorney for D.C. Jeanine Pirro and Federal Reserve Chair Jerome H. Powell. (Eric Lee and Valerie Plesch for The Post)


January 11, 2026

BREAKING: Today

The U.S. Attorney’s office in Washington, D.C. has opened a criminal investigation into Jerome Powell, Chairman of the Federal Reserve, over the central bank’s headquarters renovation and whether Powell lied to Congress about the project’s scope.

The investigation was approved in November by Jeanine Pirro, a longtime Trump ally who Trump appointed to run the D.C. U.S. Attorney’s office.

Grand jury subpoenas have already been served to the Federal Reserve.

In a rare video statement released today, Powell called the investigation “unprecedented” and said plainly: “Those are pretexts.”

“This is about whether the Fed will be able to continue to set interest rates based on evidence and economic conditions — or whether instead monetary policy will be directed by political pressure or intimidation.”

Welcome to the enemies list’s most dangerous escalation yet: the criminal investigation of the Federal Reserve Chairman for refusing to bow to presidential demands.

The Official Story: Marble and Elevators

Here’s what the investigation is nominally about:

The Federal Reserve is renovating its Washington headquarters—the Marriner S. Eccles Building and another building on Constitution Avenue. Both date to the 1930s and have never been comprehensively renovated.

The project:

  • Broke ground in 2022

  • Set to complete in 2027

  • Estimated $700 million over budget (total cost ~$2.5 billion)

  • Includes asbestos removal, lead contamination cleanup, ADA compliance

  • Modernizing 100-year-old buildings

The controversy:

In June 2025, Powell testified before Congress. Lawmakers pressed him about a 2021 proposal that mentioned:

  • Private elevators for top policymakers

  • VIP dining rooms

  • New marble features

  • A rooftop terrace for staff

Powell responded: “There’s no V.I.P. dining room; there’s no new marble. We took down the old marble, we’re putting it back up. We’ll have to use new marble where some of the old marble broke. But there’s no special elevators. There’s just old elevators that have been there.”

Powell explained the plans had “continued to evolve” and that some features from the 2021 proposal were scrapped.

After his testimony, the Fed published an FAQ reaffirming Powell’s answers, provided a virtual tour, shared photographs, and annotated the 2021 proposal to show what changed.

Now prosecutors are investigating whether Powell lied to Congress about marble and elevators.

Sound familiar? It should.

The Real Story: Interest Rates Trump Doesn’t Want

Here’s what this is actually about:

Trump wants Powell to slash interest rates. Powell won’t do it fast enough.

Trump has:

  • Continually attacked Powell for resisting demands to cut rates significantly

  • Threatened to fire Powell (even though Trump himself nominated Powell in 2017)

  • Raised the prospect of a lawsuit against Powell over the renovation, citing “incompetence”

  • Told The New York Times last week that he’s decided who should replace Powell

  • Made Kevin Hassett (Trump’s top economic adviser) the frontrunner for Fed chair

Powell’s term as Fed chair ends in May 2026. Trump wants him gone before then. And when Powell won’t resign voluntarily, Trump needs a reason to force him out or prosecute him.

Enter: the marble investigation.

Powell said it explicitly in his video statement today:

“The threat of criminal charges is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the president.”

This isn’t speculation. This isn’t reading between the lines. Powell is directly stating that he’s being criminally investigated for monetary policy decisions that Trump doesn’t like.

Why Federal Reserve Independence Matters

Most people don’t think much about the Federal Reserve. It’s wonky, it’s boring, it sets interest rates that affect mortgage payments and credit card bills.

But Federal Reserve independence is one of the most important institutional protections in the American economic system.

Here’s why:

Presidents want low interest rates. Low rates make the economy feel good in the short term: cheaper borrowing, rising stock markets, more jobs. This helps presidents win elections.

But sometimes the economy needs higher rates to prevent runaway inflation or asset bubbles. Raising rates is politically unpopular—it makes borrowing more expensive, slows economic growth, can cost jobs in the short term.

If presidents controlled interest rates, they’d always keep them too low for too long. They’d juice the economy before elections, damn the long-term consequences. We’d get boom-bust cycles, inflation crises, and economic instability.

That’s why Congress specifically designed the Federal Reserve to be independent. The Fed chair serves a four-year term that doesn’t align with presidential terms. Presidents can only remove Fed officials “for cause”—meaning malfeasance or dereliction of duty, not policy disagreements.

The Fed is supposed to set interest rates based on economic data and its dual mandate: stable prices (low inflation) and maximum employment. Not based on what the president wants for his reelection campaign.

This independence is why the U.S. economy is stable and the dollar is the world’s reserve currency.

Countries where presidents control central banks—Turkey, Argentina, Venezuela—suffer from chronic inflation, currency crises, and economic chaos. When central banks lose independence, economies collapse.

Trump is trying to destroy that independence by criminally investigating Powell for monetary policy decisions.

Powell’s Unprecedented Video Response

Fed chairs don’t normally make public video statements defending themselves from criminal investigations.

Because Fed chairs don’t normally get criminally investigated.

Today, Powell broke that norm because the situation demands it.

Key excerpts from his statement:

“This new threat is not about my testimony last June or about the renovation of the Federal Reserve buildings. It is not about Congress’s oversight role; the Fed through testimony and other public disclosures made every effort to keep Congress informed about the renovation project. Those are pretexts.“

“The threat of criminal charges is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the president.”

“This is about whether the Fed will be able to continue to set interest rates based on evidence and economic conditions — or whether instead monetary policy will be directed by political pressure or intimidation.“

Powell is calling Trump’s bluff. He’s saying directly: This prosecution is political retaliation for monetary policy.

This is extraordinary. Fed chairs are supposed to be above politics, neutral technocrats. But Powell recognizes that staying silent while being criminally investigated for interest rate decisions would be interpreted as weakness—and would encourage further attacks on Fed independence.

So he’s fighting back publicly, stating clearly what this investigation is really about.

Who approved this criminal investigation into the Federal Reserve Chairman?

Jeanine Pirro.

Yes, that Jeanine Pirro. The former Fox News host and longtime Trump ally.

Trump appointed Pirro to run the U.S. Attorney’s office for the District of Columbia. She approved the Powell investigation in November 2025—the same month that Comey and James’ prosecutions were dismissed for illegal prosecutor appointment, and the same month Kelly’s Pentagon investigation heated up.

Pirro has no prosecutorial experience at the federal level. Her qualifications for running one of the most important U.S. Attorney’s offices in the country: unwavering loyalty to Trump.

This is the pattern: When career prosecutors won’t bring political cases, Trump installs loyalists who will.

  • Lindsey Halligan (illegally appointed) brought Comey and James cases that career prosecutors rejected

  • Jeanine Pirro now investigates Powell after Trump demands his removal

The common thread: None of these prosecutors had career experience with these cases. They were brought in specifically to prosecute Trump’s enemies.

The Lisa Cook Connection

Powell isn’t the only Fed official under attack.

Lisa D. Cook is a Fed governor. Trump tried to fire her, claiming allegations of “mortgage fraud.”

Here’s the problem: Presidents can only remove Fed officials “for cause”—meaning malfeasance or dereliction of duty. Policy disagreements don’t count. Allegations that haven’t been proven don’t count.

Cook sued. The case is now heading to the Supreme Court, with arguments scheduled for January 21, 2026—just 10 days from now.

This case will determine whether presidents can fire Fed officials they don’t like.

If the Supreme Court sides with Trump and says he can fire Cook without proving “cause,” then:

  • Presidential control of the Fed becomes reality

  • Fed independence is dead

  • Every Fed official serves at the president’s pleasure

  • Monetary policy becomes political

If the Court sides with Cook and says Trump can’t fire her without proven cause, then:

  • Fed independence survives

  • But Trump will just ramp up criminal investigations instead (like Powell)

  • Because he can’t fire them, he’ll prosecute them

Either way, Fed independence is under existential threat.

The Cook case and the Powell investigation are two prongs of the same attack: destroy the Federal Reserve’s ability to set interest rates independent of presidential demands.

  3. Next president can’t fix it

    • Once Fed independence is broken, it’s hard to restore

Long-term effects:

    • Euro, yuan, or basket of currencies could replace dollar in international trade

  2. Higher borrowing costs for U.S. government

    • Treasury bonds are considered risk-free because U.S. institutions are stable

    • Lose that stability, Treasury yields rise

    • Federal government pays higher interest on debt

    • Fiscal crisis accelerates

  3. American economic decline
