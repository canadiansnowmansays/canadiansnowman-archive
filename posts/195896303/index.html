When Democracy Fights Back

The Callais Reckoning

Part XX: The Supreme Court Finishes What It Started in 2013

April 29, 2026

A series of rulings by the justices has weakened the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Haiyun Jiang/The New York Times

Stephen Gordon | CanadianSnowmanSays

Eight days ago, Virginia voted.

The redistricting war arrived at what I called, in Part XIX, a rough draw. Democrats had answered Republican gerrymandering with gerrymandering of their own. The scoreboard was close. The architecture was damaged but standing. I promised that Part XX would examine the Callais ruling when it came.

It came this morning. It was the second Supreme Court blow in the same week.

On Monday, the Court issued a summary reversal in Abbott v. LULAC — a brief, unsigned order clearing Texas’s redrawn congressional map for use in the 2026 midterms. The ruling overturned a 160-page judgment from a three-judge federal panel that had found, after a nine-day trial and 3,000 pages of evidence, substantial proof that Texas had engaged in racial gerrymandering. The Supreme Court discarded that judgment without written explanation. The redistricting war that Donald Trump started in Texas last summer — the war that set this entire cascade in motion — has now been judicially ratified. The map stands. The five Republican seats stand with it.

That was Monday. Today is Wednesday.

The Supreme Court of the United States, in a 6-to-3 decision split along ideological lines, struck down Louisiana’s congressional map on Wednesday. Justice Samuel Alito wrote the majority opinion. Justice Elena Kagan dissented — and then, in a gesture reserved for moments of genuine institutional alarm, read her dissent aloud from the bench.

Everything I told you to watch for in this series has now arrived at once.

What the Court Said

Let’s be precise, because precision matters when the architecture of democratic representation is being redrawn in real time.

The majority opinion held that Louisiana’s second majority-Black congressional district — drawn after a federal court found the previous map violated the Voting Rights Act — was itself an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. Justice Alito wrote that the court was merely “updating” a 40-year-old framework for evaluating race in redistricting. The update, in plain language, is this: to successfully challenge a voting map under the Voting Rights Act going forward, you will need to prove that a state intentionally drew its districts to diminish minority voting power because of race. A challenge that cannot cleanly separate racial motivation from partisan motivation will fail.

The practical consequence of that standard is not subtle. Race and partisanship in the American South are not cleanly separable. They never have been. The history of how Black voters came to vote overwhelmingly Democratic is inseparable from the history of how Southern white voters came to vote overwhelmingly Republican. A legal standard that requires courts to disentangle what history has entangled is not a neutral legal standard. It is a standard designed to fail in application.

Justice Alito cited, in support of his update, the observation that the racial gap in voter registration and turnout had “largely disappeared.” This is true, as far as it goes. It does not go very far. The gap in political representation has not disappeared. The gap in the willingness of some state legislatures to dilute minority voting power has not disappeared. The evidence for that second gap is sitting in the same courtroom where Justice Alito wrote his opinion.

What Kagan Said

Justice Kagan’s dissent is worth reading in full, but its architecture can be summarized in three steps.

First: Today’s decision completes a three-step plan to eliminate the functional protections of the Voting Rights Act that began with Shelby County v. Holder in 2013, which gutted the preclearance requirement that had forced Southern states to get federal approval before changing their voting laws. Step two was the steady erosion of Section 2 protections in subsequent rulings. Step three is today.

Second: The majority’s claim that it is “updating” rather than dismantling is, in Kagan’s word, eviscerating. The update requires proof of intentional racial discrimination in a legal environment where states have learned, over sixty years of litigation, to document their map-drawing decisions in terms of partisanship rather than race — even when the underlying motivation is racial. The new standard does not close a loophole. It creates one large enough to drive a congressional district through.

Third, and most directly: A state can now, “without legal consequence, systematically dilute minority citizens’ voting power” — provided it describes what it is doing in the language of partisan advantage rather than racial intent.

Kagan read that from the bench. That is not a routine act. Justices read their dissents aloud when they believe the majority has done something that requires the country to hear the dissent, not merely read it later. It is a signal. The signal today was not subtle.

The Timing Is Not Coincidental

The Supreme Court announced its decision this morning as the Florida Legislature was actively debating its new congressional map. Republican leaders in Florida had cited the looming Callais decision in their justification for the map. The Florida House passed it 83-to-28 while a Democratic state representative protested on the floor with a bullhorn, shouting that the vote was unconstitutional. Legislators paused their debate to read the Callais ruling, then resumed and voted. By late afternoon, the full Florida Legislature had approved the map. It now goes to Governor DeSantis for signature — a formality, not a question.

This is what the redistricting war looks like at full velocity. But the timing was not merely opportunistic — it was engineered. DeSantis’s office drew the new lines specifically anticipating that the Supreme Court would weaken the Voting Rights Act, insulating the map from federal challenge before the ruling even existed. The justices proved his presumption not only correct but exceptionally well timed.

There is one additional detail worth recording. Florida’s state constitution expressly prohibits partisan redistricting — the Fair Districts Amendment, passed by voters in 2010, bans both partisan and racial gerrymandering. This creates a requirement of silence that has governed the entire Florida campaign. Republicans cannot acknowledge what they are doing, because the state constitution forbids it. As one Republican consultant told the Atlantic, anonymously, because he too does not want to be hauled before a judge: “Anything you say will get you subpoenaed. You can’t say, ‘We need to make more Republican seats.’ You’re done. You’re toast, and then your map’s invalidated.”

DeSantis has observed this fight-club rule scrupulously. His official rationale for redistricting is Florida’s population growth. He gave his proposed map first to Fox News before sending it to the legislature. The map was drawn in shades of red and blue to show which seats Republicans could control. None of this, apparently, constitutes acknowledging partisan intent.

Senator Marsha Blackburn of Tennessee, who is running for governor, called on Tennessee’s legislature to reconvene immediately and pass a new map eliminating a majority-Black district in Memphis. She said she had vowed to keep Tennessee a red state. She said this publicly, without apparent awareness that it might be relevant to the question of whether racial and partisan motivations can be cleanly separated.

Mississippi’s governor had already announced he would call a special session 21 calendar days after the Callais ruling, whatever it said. That clock is now running.

South Carolina could move. Tennessee could move. Missouri could move. Not all of them will — some have primaries too advanced, filing deadlines already passed, logistics too tangled to resolve before November. But some of them will. And the maps they draw in the next weeks will govern elections for years beyond 2026.

The Scoreboard, Revised

Eight days ago I wrote that the redistricting war had arrived at a rough draw. I need to update that assessment — twice, as it turns out, because the Court moved twice this week.

The Virginia result — 50.7 to 49.3, forty thousand votes, Democrats gaining four seats — brought the national seat count to approximate parity. It was a draw on the map.

Monday’s Texas ruling broke that draw before today’s ruling compounded it.

Abbott v. LULAC cleared the Texas map — all five Republican-leaning seats — for 2026. A lower court had spent nine days in trial, heard dozens of expert witnesses, reviewed 3,000 pages of evidence, and concluded the map constituted racial gerrymandering. The Supreme Court reversed that judgment in a brief unsigned order, via the shadow docket, without explanation. Virginia’s four Democratic seats, gained eight days ago at the cost of the state’s bipartisan redistricting commission, were effectively neutralised by Monday morning. Then came today.

Florida’s map, approved today and awaiting DeSantis’s signature, targets four seats currently held by Democrats — Representatives Castor in Tampa, Soto near Orlando, and Wasserman Schultz and Moskowitz in southeast Florida. Whether those four seats actually flip is a separate question from whether the map was drawn to flip them. House Minority Leader Jeffries has called it “the DeSantis dummymander,” arguing that if Democrats turn out as they did in 2018 and 2020, the party could gain three to five seats that were not previously competitive. Democratic strategist Steve Schale offered a more measured version of the same point: “You can’t make them just disappear into the ocean.” Republicans, however, are not drawing for 2026 alone. They are drawing for the decade. Louisiana loses at least one Democratic district. Tennessee, South Carolina, and Mississippi are in motion. An analysis published by the New York Times last year estimated that a ruling of this kind could, over time, cost Democrats up to twelve seats across the South — including nine that would have been protected under the old Section 2 standard.

Twelve seats, in a House where the majority is currently measured in single digits, is not a footnote. It is a structural realignment. The redistricting war that Republicans started in Texas last summer was always going to end somewhere. It is ending here: at a Supreme Court majority that has now given Republican-controlled Southern legislatures a legal framework in which racial gerrymandering is permissible provided it is described as partisan gerrymandering — and a political environment in which every incentive points toward describing it exactly that way.

Don Scott, Virginia’s House Speaker, said eight days ago that “Virginia just changed the trajectory of the 2026 midterms.” He was right. Today’s ruling changes it back..

The Three-Step Plan, Complete

Justice Kagan named it explicitly, so we should name it too.

Step One — Shelby County v. Holder (2013): The preclearance requirement of the Voting Rights Act — which required states with histories of racial discrimination in elections to obtain federal approval before changing their voting laws — was struck down. Chief Justice Roberts wrote that the coverage formula was based on outdated data. The result was immediate: within 24 hours of the ruling, Texas announced new voter ID laws and redistricting plans it had previously been unable to implement. Within a year, multiple Southern states had enacted voting restrictions that would have been blocked under preclearance.

Step Two — Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee (2021): The court significantly narrowed the standard for proving a voting law violated Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. The ruling made it substantially harder to challenge facially neutral voting restrictions — early voting cutbacks, ballot collection restrictions, registration obstacles — even when their practical effect fell disproportionately on minority voters.

Step Three — Louisiana v. Callais (2026): Today. The final pillar. The standard for challenging racial gerrymandering under Section 2 has been rewritten to require proof of intentional racial motivation that cannot be “disentangled” from partisan motivation. In a country where race and partisanship in Southern elections are structurally entangled, this is the legal equivalent of requiring proof that a shadow is caused by the sun rather than the object casting it.

Thirteen years. Three cases. The crown jewel of the civil rights era has been, as the NAACP’s Derrick Johnson put it this morning, betrayed.

The Tariff Question Nobody Is Asking

Before we get to the White House statement, there is a question worth raising — because it will occur to careful observers, and it deserves a serious answer rather than a knowing wink.

On February 20th of this year, the Supreme Court ruled 6-to-3 in Learning Resources v. Trump that the president’s sweeping IEEPA tariffs were unconstitutional. The majority was Roberts, Gorsuch, and Barrett — joined by all three liberal justices. Alito, Thomas, and Kavanaugh dissented. Within hours, Trump called Gorsuch and Barrett a “disgrace,” said they “sicken me,” and announced replacement tariffs under a different statute.

Today’s Callais ruling was also 6-to-3 — but the lineup was different. Alito wrote the majority. Thomas and Kavanaugh joined him. So did Roberts, Gorsuch, and Barrett — the three justices Trump publicly humiliated two months ago.

The question writes itself: did Gorsuch and Barrett, stung by Trump’s attacks, tilt toward the conservative majority today to prove they were not the enemy?

The honest answer is probably no — but the reasoning matters.

Gorsuch and Barrett voted against Trump on tariffs because their judicial philosophy genuinely required it. During oral arguments, Gorsuch explicitly raised the non-delegation principle, warning that upholding IEEPA tariffs would create a “one-way ratchet” of congressional power to the executive. That is a principled textualist position, not a political one. Their VRA vote today is equally consistent with their judicial philosophy. These are not compensatory votes. They are the same framework applied to different constitutional questions. Conservative legal doctrine, applied consistently, produces a ruling that limits presidential tariff power and a ruling that limits minority voting rights protections. Both outcomes may be unwelcome to different observers, but they follow from the same intellectual architecture.

The more interesting case is Roberts.

Roberts has historically been the Court’s institutionalist. He cares — genuinely, by all accounts — about the Court’s legitimacy as an institution that operates above partisan politics. He has played the moderating role, written the narrow opinion, avoided the maximalist ruling when a minimalist one would do. It was Roberts who, in Allen v. Milligan just three years ago, joined the liberals to uphold Alabama’s Section 2 obligations under the Voting Rights Act — a ruling that directly constrained what Callais has now permitted.

Today Roberts joined the majority opinion gutting the standard he helped preserve in 2023.

What changed? One answer is that the legal arguments in Callais were simply more persuasive to him than those in Allen. Another is that the composition of cases evolved in ways that made the outcome more legally defensible. Both may be true.

But there is a third possibility worth considering. When Trump called the Court a “disgrace” in February — when he publicly humiliated his own appointees for ruling on the law rather than on his preferences — he may have inadvertently eliminated the political calculus that had made Roberts an institutionalist moderator. Roberts has always managed the Court’s reputation partly by managing its relationship with public expectations. If a president will attack the Court as corrupt and disgraceful regardless of how it rules, the reputational cost of a maximalist VRA decision drops considerably. Why pay the political price of restraint if restraint earns you the same contempt as overreach?

Trump spanked the Court in February. Today the Court’s conservative majority completed a thirteen-year project. Whether those two facts are connected is unknowable. That they occurred in the same calendar year, to the same institution, is a matter of record.

What the White House Said

Abigail Jackson, a White House spokeswoman, called the ruling “a complete and total victory for American voters.” She said: “The color of one’s skin should not dictate which congressional district you belong in.”

This statement was issued by an administration whose president triggered a mid-decade redistricting war explicitly designed to dictate, based on the political preferences of the communities where people live — preferences that correlate closely with the color of their skin — which congressional district they belong in.

I note this not to score a point but to record it. This is what the language of democratic principle looks like when it is used as a weapon rather than a standard. “The color of one’s skin should not dictate which congressional district you belong in” is a sentence that means exactly the opposite of what it says in the context of a ruling that makes it easier, not harder, to draw districts that pack minority voters into configurations chosen for partisan advantage.

The language is the tell. It always is.

Where Democracy Stands This Morning

In the months since this series began, I have watched the American immune system work. Courts blocked executive overreach. State attorneys general filed lawsuits. Election administrators held their ground. Voters turned out in April, in numbers that would have seemed implausible two years ago, for a ballot question about congressional lines.

Democracy was fighting back. I believed that, and I wrote it, across nineteen instalments.

Today requires a harder sentence.

The immune system cannot fully protect against a pathogen that has reached the Supreme Court. Courts can block an executive order. They can overturn an election law. They cannot overturn the Supreme Court. When the institution that is supposed to be the final guarantor of constitutional rights is itself the instrument through which those rights are being narrowed — not by a rogue actor, not by emergency decree, but by a 6-to-3 majority operating through the established procedures of judicial review — the normal antibodies do not apply.

What applies now is the longer arc. The Voting Rights Act was passed in 1965 because the ordinary mechanisms of democratic participation had failed Black Americans for a century after emancipation. It was designed as a structural correction to a structural failure. Today’s ruling does not restore that structural failure overnight. It removes the correction, and leaves the structure to whatever forces currently govern it.

Those forces, in the American South in 2026, are not neutral.

Alanah Odoms of the ACLU of Louisiana said this morning: “Today, we lost one of the last seatbelts of our democracy.”

She is right. And the car is still moving.

What Comes Next

The litigation will begin immediately. Florida’s map is already headed to DeSantis’s desk, and multiple voting rights groups have announced they will challenge it in court the moment he signs — on the grounds that it violates the Fair Districts Amendment, Florida’s own 2010 constitutional ban on partisan gerrymandering. The case for that challenge was inadvertently strengthened yesterday when a lawyer for the governor’s office acknowledged in testimony that partisan voter data was used in drafting the map. DeSantis’s general counsel has responded by arguing that the Fair Districts Amendment is itself unconstitutional — a position that will now be tested before a Florida Supreme Court composed entirely of Republican appointees, which upheld the previous GOP-tilted map in 2022. Any map drawn in the wake of today’s federal ruling that eliminates majority-minority districts will face additional challenges under the narrowed Callais standard. Courts will apply it. Some challenges will succeed. Many will not.

Louisiana will draw a new map. Mississippi’s special session clock is running. Tennessee and South Carolina are watching the calendar. And Texas — whose map was cleared Monday by a Court that offered no written reasoning for overturning a nine-day trial — now faces continued litigation on grounds that were not before the Court in Abbott v. LULAC. Those claims remain alive. Whether they survive the post-Callais legal environment is the question that will occupy redistricting lawyers for the rest of the decade.

The midterms are November 3rd. The primary calendar is still in motion. The window for new maps is narrow in most states and closed in others. Not every state that could move will move before November. But the longer game — the post-2030 redistricting cycle, when today’s ruling will govern the first full census-driven remapping since Callais — is the game that will define congressional representation for the next decade.

That game began this morning.

Virginia voted eight days ago. Democracy was fighting back.

This week, it absorbed two blows — Monday’s Texas ratification and today’s Callais ruling — that will take years to fully measure.
