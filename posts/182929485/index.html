The Asimov Gap

The November Warning

Part 1: Sanders’ Wake-Up Call

January 1, 2026

The Conversation Nobody Expected

Geoffrey Hinton and Senator Bernie Sanders at Georgetown University, November 18, 2025. Photo: Georgetown University Institute of Politics and Public Service

On November 18, 2025, Bernie Sanders convened a conversation at Georgetown University that should have been front-page news everywhere.

It wasn’t a typical Senate hearing with senators grandstanding and witnesses dodging questions. It was something different: a serious, extended conversation about artificial intelligence between people who actually understand the technology and people who actually represent the public.

The featured guest was Geoffrey Hinton, who had just won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics for his work on neural networks—the technology that powers every major AI system from ChatGPT to autonomous vehicles. Hinton is often called “the Godfather of AI” because his research in the 1980s and 2000s laid the groundwork for the current AI revolution.

In 2023, Hinton left his position at Google specifically so he could speak freely about AI risks without corporate constraints. When the person who literally invented the technology driving the AI boom quits his job to warn people about what’s coming, that’s worth paying attention to.

Sanders brought Hinton to Georgetown to ask a simple question: Are we building AI in a way that serves humanity, or in a way that concentrates power and wealth in the hands of a few?

The answer was clear and alarming.

What Hinton Said

Geoffrey Hinton standing at a whiteboard at the Google office in Toronto, 2017.

Hinton’s core message: AI is advancing faster than most people realize, and we’re approaching artificial general intelligence (AGI)—systems that can match or exceed human cognitive abilities across all domains—potentially within 5-20 years, though estimates vary widely and uncertainty is high.

His key point: AI will likely become “smarter than human beings” within a timeframe that should concern us now.

This matters because humanity has never created something more intelligent than ourselves. We’ve always been at the top of the cognitive hierarchy. That’s about to change.

Hinton emphasized two points that often get lost in AI hype:

First, the timeline is compressing. Progress that experts predicted would take decades is happening in years. GPT-2 (2019) was impressive but limited. GPT-4 (2023) can pass the bar exam and solve complex reasoning problems. Current frontier models (2025) perform at near-human levels on many cognitive tasks. The gap between “interesting tool” and “transformative technology” closed in less than five years.

Second, we don’t fully understand how these systems work. The neural networks powering modern AI are so complex that even their creators can’t always explain why they make particular decisions. We can see what they do, but not always why. This “black box” problem becomes critical when AI systems start making consequential decisions about jobs, credit, medical diagnosis, or warfare.

Hinton’s warning wasn’t “AI will definitely destroy humanity.” It was more nuanced and therefore more credible: We’re building something enormously powerful without adequate safeguards, and the people building it are racing each other rather than proceeding carefully.

What Sanders Heard

Sanders listened to Hinton and connected it to a pattern he’s been fighting his entire political career: concentrated power.

His response synthesized decades of experience combating oligarchy in various forms:

“The issue that we are talking about today is not just the future of AI. It is the future of humanity, the future of our country, and whether or not we will have a government that represents all of us, or whether we will have an oligarchic form of society in which a handful of billionaires control not only our economy but also our political life and the very future of our species.”

This wasn’t hyperbole. Sanders laid out the facts:

On concentration of power: “The question that we are asking today is a pretty simple one: Are we comfortable with a handful of billionaires shaping the future of humanity without any democratic input whatsoever?”

On job displacement: In his October 2025 report, Sanders’ committee estimated that AI could affect “nearly 100 million jobs in this country in the coming years.” The report, which used ChatGPT to analyze Bureau of Labor Statistics job descriptions, projected impacts including 40% of registered nurses, 47% of truck drivers, 64% of accountants, 65% of teaching assistants, and 89% of fast food workers. While these are estimates with significant uncertainty—and some economists dispute both the methodology and magnitude—they reflect serious concerns about workforce disruption that business leaders like Ford’s Jim Farley and Anthropic’s Dario Amodei have also raised.

On wealth inequality: “Are we going to allow artificial intelligence to benefit the people on top...or are we going to make certain that the transformative impact of this new technology benefits all of society?”

On surveillance: “Are we reaching the stage where every phone call that we make, every email and text that we send, every bit of research we do on the internet will be available to the owners of AI? How do we sustain a democracy under those conditions?”

On the speed of change: Sanders noted that tech oligarchs are “moving as fast as they can to replace human workers” while Congress has barely begun to understand what’s happening, let alone regulate it.

The Oligarchy Warning

Sanders framed AI as the latest battleground in a much older war: democracy versus oligarchy.

Throughout his career, Sanders has fought concentrated power in different forms:

  • 1980s: Railroad monopolies

  • 1990s: Corporate consolidation

  • 2000s: Wall Street banks

  • 2010s: Healthcare conglomerates and tech platforms

  • 2020s: AI companies

The pattern is always the same:

  1. New technology or industry emerges

  2. A few players consolidate control

  3. They capture enormous wealth

  4. They use that wealth to influence policy

  5. Democratic governance becomes difficult or impossible

  6. Society pays the price

AI fits this pattern perfectly, but with higher stakes.

Currently, AI development is dominated by a handful of companies:

  • OpenAI (led by Sam Altman)

  • Anthropic (led by Dario Amodei)

  • Google/DeepMind (led by Demis Hassabis)

  • Meta (led by Mark Zuckerberg)

  • xAI (led by Elon Musk)

  • Amazon (Jeff Bezos investing heavily)

These companies are racing each other to build AGI. The competitive dynamics incentivize speed over safety. Each company fears falling behind. Nobody wants to be second.

Many of these companies have established safety teams and some leaders have publicly called for regulation—Altman testified before Congress advocating for oversight, and Anthropic has published research on AI safety. But the fundamental dynamic remains: private companies making civilizational decisions without democratic accountability.

You didn’t vote for Altman or Zuckerberg. You can’t vote them out if you disagree with their decisions. They answer to shareholders and their own judgment—not to the public whose lives their technology will transform.

Sanders asked the core question: “Should a small number of incredibly powerful people be shaping the future of humanity?”

His implied answer: No. Civilizational decisions require democratic legitimacy.

The Jobs Crisis

Sanders focused heavily on employment because that’s where AI’s impact will be most immediate and visible.

The numbers from his October 2025 report are staggering, though contested:

The report, compiled with ChatGPT’s assistance in analyzing job tasks, found that 57% of work hours in the U.S. could be technically automated with technologies that exist today. This translates to potential impacts on “nearly 100 million” jobs over the next decade.

Important caveats: These are estimates of technical potential, not predictions of actual displacement. Many economists note that previous automation predictions have overestimated job losses while underestimating new job creation. The World Economic Forum’s 2025 report, for instance, projects 170 million new jobs created globally alongside 92 million displaced. Additionally, the methodology—using ChatGPT to assess automation potential—has limitations.

That said, the projected impacts by sector deserve attention:

  • 40% of registered nurses

  • 47% of truck drivers

  • 64% of accountants

  • 65% of teaching assistants

  • 89% of fast food workers

These aren’t fringe professions. They’re the backbone of the American middle class. Registered nurses earn $75,000+. Truck drivers earn $45,000-60,000. Accountants earn $60,000-80,000.

This is middle-class employment, not just low-wage work.

And it’s already happening. Just this week, a copywriter named Brian Groh wrote in The New York Times about losing his career to AI. He bought a chainsaw, started cutting trees for local homeowners—work the AI chatbot suggested. Then he badly injured his back doing physical labor. A neighbor offered him prescription painkillers to help him work through the pain.

Groh wrote: “In towns like mine, outsourcing and automation consumed jobs. Then purpose. Then people. Now the same forces are climbing the economic ladder.”

This is exactly what Sanders warned about: displacement without support, leading to desperation, injury, and the opioid pipeline that destroyed working-class communities.

Sanders contrasted this potential displacement with current policy: “At this moment, as you all know, artificial intelligence could erase nearly 100 million jobs...Yet with very few exceptions, Congress is not even talking about this issue.”

More than 90 AI-related bills have been introduced in Congress. Almost none have passed. There’s no comprehensive federal AI policy. No worker transition assistance. No requirements for companies automating jobs to help displaced workers.

The automation is happening. The response isn’t.

The Wealth Concentration Question

Sanders connected job displacement to his broader concern about inequality:

“Will it just make the rich richer?” he asked. “Is it moral that we have so much income and wealth inequality with a handful of billionaires...owning more wealth than the bottom half of American society, when AI is going to make those people even wealthier and eliminate the jobs of tens of millions of workers?”

This isn’t just redistribution rhetoric. It’s a practical question about economic stability:

If AI dramatically increases productivity but concentrates the gains among capital owners while eliminating jobs for workers, you get:

  • Enormous wealth at the top

  • Mass unemployment below

  • No consumer base to buy the products the AI-powered economy produces

This is classic market failure. Henry Ford understood it in 1914 when he raised wages to $5/day (double the going rate) so his workers could afford to buy the cars they built.

Sanders is asking whether we’ll repeat that insight or ignore it: Will AI productivity gains be shared broadly, or captured narrowly?

Currently, policy defaults to capture. Companies automate. Workers lose jobs. Shareholders profit. No mechanism ensures broad benefit.

Sal Khan, CEO of Khan Academy, proposed in The New York Times this week that companies dedicate 1% of profits to worker retraining. “This isn’t charity,” Khan wrote. “It is in the best interest of these companies. If the public sees corporate profits skyrocketing while livelihoods evaporate, backlash will follow.” He’s right—but 1% for retraining addresses only part of the problem. Sanders’ framework is more comprehensive: not just retraining, but shorter workweeks, transition support, and ensuring productivity gains maintain broad purchasing power.

The Surveillance Threat

Sanders raised another dimension often overlooked in AI discussions: surveillance.

“Are we reaching the stage where every phone call that we make, every email and text that we send, every bit of research we do on the internet will be available to the owners of AI?”

This isn’t paranoia. Consider:

Larry Ellison (Oracle founder, second richest person on Earth) said in September 2024: “Citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.”

Not hypothetical. Not warning about what could happen. Describing his vision.

AI makes ubiquitous surveillance practical:

  • Facial recognition tracking everyone in public

  • Natural language processing analyzing all communications

  • Pattern recognition predicting “concerning” behavior

  • Automated decision-making about who gets flagged

China’s social credit system already does this—AI-powered surveillance determining who can buy plane tickets, get good jobs, or access services based on “citizenship score.”

Sanders asked: “How do we sustain a democracy under those conditions?”

The answer isn’t obvious. Democracy requires:

  • Free speech (surveillance chills it)

  • Freedom of association (surveillance monitors it)

  • Privacy (surveillance eliminates it)

  • Dissent (surveillance punishes it)

Ubiquitous AI surveillance is fundamentally incompatible with democratic society.

The Regulatory Vacuum

Sanders emphasized that Congress is failing to respond:

“Congress is not even talking about this issue” despite AI potentially affecting “nearly 100 million jobs.”

More than 90 AI-related bills introduced. Almost none passed. No comprehensive framework. No worker protections. No privacy safeguards. No democratic oversight of AI development.

The technology is advancing exponentially. The governance is advancing not at all.

Why?

Speed mismatch: AI companies move faster than legislative processes. By the time Congress understands GPT-3, GPT-4 is already deployed.

Complexity: Most legislators don’t understand the technology. Hard to regulate what you don’t comprehend.

Lobbying: Tech companies spend heavily to prevent regulation. They’ve learned from past industries how to capture the regulatory process.

Ideology: “Innovation” is treated as sacred. Any regulation is framed as “killing innovation” and “helping China win.”

Fragmentation: Multiple committees claim jurisdiction. Nobody owns the issue.

The result: Oligarchs develop AI without democratic oversight because democratic institutions are too slow, too confused, or too captured to respond.

Michelle Goldberg, writing in The New York Times this week, captured the moment: “One major question, going into 2026, is which party will speak for the Americans who abhor the incursions of A.I. into their lives and want to see its reach restricted. Another is whether widespread public hostility to this technology even matters given all the money behind it.”

Sanders is trying to answer that first question. This series explores the second.

The Alternative Vision

Sanders didn’t just warn about problems. He sketched an alternative:

On jobs: “We must ensure that working people benefit from this new technology, not be thrown out on the street while billionaires get even richer.” Specific proposals include expanding employee ownership and implementing a “robot tax” on corporations that replace workers with machines, ensuring “the wealth created by these technologies are redistributed back to the workers impacted.”

On labor: Sanders called for “more than doubling union membership by passing the PRO Act and ending union busting” to ensure workers have power to negotiate how AI affects their industries.

On benefits: Ensure “the transformative impact of this new technology benefits all of society.”

On democracy: Democratic oversight of AI development. Public input. Accountability. Not just private companies making civilizational decisions.

This isn’t anti-technology. It’s pro-democratic-technology.

Sanders isn’t saying “stop AI development.” He’s saying “govern it democratically before oligarchs consolidate irreversible control.”

Why This Conversation Mattered

The Georgetown conversation wasn’t typical Washington theater. It was Sanders using his platform to:

  1. Elevate the issue: Force AI governance into public conversation

  2. Frame the question: Not “AI good or bad” but “AI for whom”

  3. Build coalition: Bring together labor, tech workers, researchers, policy experts

  4. Create record: Official documentation of concerns before crisis hits

  5. Signal intent: This will be a legislative priority

Most importantly: Sanders connected AI to his life’s work fighting concentrated power.

He’s not learning about this issue fresh. He’s applying 50 years of experience recognizing oligarchic patterns to the newest and most consequential technology.

That experience tells him:

  • Oligarchs always promise to self-regulate (they don’t)

  • They always claim democratic oversight will destroy innovation (it doesn’t)

  • They always say “trust us” (history says don’t)

  • They always capture wealth while socializing costs (absent policy preventing it)

Sanders sees the pattern. He’s sounding the alarm.

The Hinton-Sanders Synthesis

Bringing Hinton and Sanders together was strategic:

Hinton provides credibility: He literally invented the technology. Can’t be dismissed as ignorant or fearful.

Sanders provides urgency: He connects technological change to political economy and democratic governance.

Together they make the case: We’re building something transformative (Hinton), and we’re building it in a way that concentrates power and wealth (Sanders), and we need democratic governance before it’s too late (both).

Their synthesis:

  • Technology: AI approaching AGI, advancing faster than predicted, not fully understood

  • Economics: Potentially massive job displacement, wealth concentration, market instability

  • Politics: Oligarchic control, democratic deficit, surveillance threats

  • Timing: Window closing—need governance before AGI arrives

This isn’t alarmism. It’s pattern recognition by people who understand both the technology and the politics.

What Comes Next

Sanders’ conversation was opening salvo, not final word. He’s signaling:

Legislative action coming: As ranking member of the Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions, Sanders has platform to push AI worker protections and democratic oversight.

Coalition building: The conversation brought together diverse stakeholders—foundation for political movement.

Public education: Creating record that can’t be ignored when predicted harms materialize.

Reframing debate: From “AI inevitable, adapt or die” to “AI governable, democracy decides how.”

The conversation asked the question that will define this decade: Will AI be governed democratically, or will oligarchs control humanity’s future?

Sanders’ answer is clear: Democratic governance is both necessary and achievable.

The question is whether enough people will demand it before the window closes.

Why You Should Care

This isn’t abstract political theory. It affects your life directly:

Your job might be automated within a decade. Will there be transition support or are you on your own? Brian Groh had a college degree and a career—until AI replaced him. Now he’s injured, in pain, facing the same opioid temptation that destroyed his working-class neighbors.

Your data is being harvested to train AI. Will you have privacy or will surveillance become ubiquitous?

Your children are growing up with AI. Will it serve their development or exploit their attention? Philosophy professor Anastasia Berg warns students are becoming “subcognitive” from AI dependence—losing basic thinking capacities needed to participate in democracy.

Your elections will be influenced by AI. Will they remain fair or become manipulation exercises?

Your future will be shaped by AI. Will you have any say in how?

Currently, a handful of billionaires are making those decisions. Sanders is saying that’s unacceptable in a democracy.

Closing: The Choice We’re Making

The Georgetown conversation crystallized a choice most people don’t realize we’re making:

Path One: Continue current trajectory. Tech oligarchs race to AGI. No democratic oversight. Wealth concentrates. Jobs disappear. Surveillance expands. Democracy weakens.

Path Two: Assert democratic governance. Regulate AI development. Ensure broad benefits. Protect workers. Preserve privacy. Strengthen democracy.

Same technology. Completely different outcomes.

Sanders is forcing that choice into the open. The oligarchs building AI would prefer we don’t think about it—just let them build and hope it works out.

But hope isn’t a strategy. And oligarchy has never worked out well for anyone except the oligarchs.

Hinton gave us the technical warning: AGI is coming within a timeframe that matters, we don’t fully understand it, we need to be careful.

Sanders gave us the political warning: It’s being built by oligarchs, they’ll capture the benefits and socialize the risks, we need democratic governance.

Together, they’re telling us: The window is closing. Choose now, while choice is still possible.

The rest of this series will explore what that choice actually means—what we’re choosing between, how the choice is being made, what democratic AI governance looks like, and why acting now matters.

Because Sanders is right about one thing above all: This isn’t just about AI. It’s about whether democracy survives the 21st century.

And the answer to that question is being determined right now, mostly by a handful of people racing to build AGI without asking whether they should.

Sanders is asking. We should too.
