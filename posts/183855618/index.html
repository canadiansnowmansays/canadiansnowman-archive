TACO Supreme

From Wall Street Mockery to Framework for Understanding Authoritarianism

From Market Pattern to Constitutional Crisis

January 20, 2026

I. The Original Term: Robert Armstrong and “Trump Always Chickens Out”

TACO started on Wall Street.

In early 2025, Financial Times columnist Robert Armstrong coined the term “TACO trade”—short for “Trump Always Chickens Out”—to describe a profitable trading strategy. When Trump threatens dramatic tariffs, bet against follow-through. He’ll announce 145% tariffs on China, rattle markets, then quietly settle for something far less. Traders who recognized this pattern made money.

The term spread quickly across Wall Street and financial Twitter. It was mockery, but also pattern recognition. Trump would threaten, markets would panic, Trump would back down. The “TACO trade” became shorthand for betting on Trump’s bluster exceeding his bite.

When I launched my Substack in May 2025, I borrowed Armstrong’s term for “Trump TACO Tuesdays”—weekly documentation of bold promises collapsing when they hit reality.

But as I tracked case after case through summer 2025, I realized Armstrong’s insight, while correct, missed the deeper pattern. Trump wasn’t just chickening out. He was creating conditions where others did the backing down for him.

II. The Accommodation Revelation: Summer 2025

The turning point came when I started tracking not just what Trump did, but what others did in response to his threats.

Take Armstrong’s original China tariff example. Trump threatened 145% tariffs, settled for 55% (with China at just 10%). Classic chickening out. But look closer:

  • Federal Reserve adjusted monetary policy anticipating tariff damage

  • Corporate supply chains restructured preemptively

  • Republican senators defended “flexible” negotiations

  • Media coverage shifted from “Trump threatens trade war” to “how can compromise be reached?”

Trump didn’t just back down. Others accommodated his threats by adjusting their behavior first, making his retreat look like strategic flexibility rather than failure.

The pattern repeated everywhere. Mueller investigation threats—Trump didn’t fire him, but senators questioned investigation legitimacy and prosecutors left DOJ. “Largest deportation ever” promises—never materialized at scale, but immigration judges expedited cases and sanctuary cities quietly relaxed policies.

This was the revelation: TACO wasn’t about Trump backing down. It was about creating conditions where institutions accommodated his threats, making backing down unnecessary or invisible.

III. The Four-Phase Framework: Fall 2025

Once I understood accommodation as the key mechanism, I mapped a consistent four-phase pattern:

Phase 1 - THREATEN: Issue threats against anyone who might hold him accountable. Often vague (”We’ll see what happens”), sometimes absurd (”145% tariffs!”), but always costly enough that targets must take them seriously.

Phase 2 - ACCOMMODATE: Institutions adjust behavior to avoid threats. Small, calculated adjustments that seem reasonable in isolation: a prosecutor delays charges, a journalist softens coverage, a judge rules narrowly, an ally offers preemptive concessions.

Phase 3 - CAPITULATE: Accumulated accommodations lead to formal surrender. Charges dropped. Investigations ended. Trade deals signed far from original threats. What started as small adjustments becomes structural change.

Phase 4 - OBFUSCATE: With goals achieved through others’ accommodation, Trump rewrites history. Threats become “strategic positions.” Backing down becomes “getting exactly what I wanted.” Accommodations are forgotten. “Total victory.”

The acronym stayed TACO—Armstrong’s insight remained foundational. But the framework evolved from describing Trump’s behavior to describing how authoritarian patterns exploit democratic institutions designed to resist them.

IV. Testing the Framework

Through fall 2025, I applied TACO across domains:

Legal Accountability:

  • Threaten: “Prosecuting a president is election interference”

  • Accommodate: DOJ delays, judges slow-walk, prosecutors narrow charges

  • Capitulate: Charges dropped after election victory

  • Obfuscate: “Witch hunt proven false”

Media Coverage:

  • Threaten: “Fake news is enemy of the people”

  • Accommodate: Both-sides journalism, euphemisms, softened headlines

  • Capitulate: Access journalism replaces investigation

  • Obfuscate: “Media finally treating me fairly”

Military Journalism:

  • Threaten: Create hostile environment for reporters covering casualties and war crimes

  • Accommodate: Media outlets accept “pool” system and limited access rather than lose access entirely

  • Capitulate: Pentagon formally expels critical journalists, replaces with compliant pool reporters (October 2025)

  • Obfuscate: Frame restrictions as “fighting misinformation” and “operational security” while creating actual state propaganda

I documented this pattern extensively in my 8-part “Ministry of Truth” series (October-December 2025). The Pentagon’s press restrictions demonstrated TACO months before the White House webpage made it explicit. Same pattern, different domain—threaten those who might expose wrongdoing, watch institutions adjust to avoid threats, formalize the restrictions, then claim it’s all about “transparency” and “security.”

International Coordination Breaks the Cycle:

Greenland (January 2026) demonstrated what happens when targets refuse to accommodate:

  • Threaten: Trump spent 71 minutes at Davos demanding Greenland ownership, questioning NATO commitments, threatening tariffs

  • Expected Accommodate: Quiet bilateral talks, minor concessions to ease pressure

  • What Actually Happened: Denmark stood firm on sovereignty. Germany cancelled bilateral meetings in solidarity. European Parliament suspended $1 trillion trade deal ratification. Nordic countries coordinated crisis responses. Greenland rejected negotiations: “Nothing about us, without us.”

  • Attempted Obfuscate: Trump announced vague “framework,” dropped tariffs, claimed victory—but his own allies’ spokespersons contradicted him: no sovereignty compromise proposed

Result: Within 24 hours, Trump retreated from ownership demands to undefined framework. Markets rallied at threat removal, not framework announcement.

The lesson: When targets refuse to accommodate and coordinate resistance, TACO collapses. Coercion requires isolation. Multilateral solidarity defeats it. The pattern isn’t “Trump always chickens out”—it’s “Trump retreats when accommodation fails.”

I documented this extensively in my “Rupture in Davos” series (January 2026). The contrast is stark: Pentagon accommodated, Smithsonian accommodated, National Parks accommodated. Denmark coordinated resistance. Only one broke the cycle.

The framework worked everywhere institutions chose to accommodate. When they coordinated resistance instead—as Denmark and European allies did with Greenland—the pattern collapsed.

Chinese government strategists now explicitly reference “TACO” in U.S. policy analysis. They recognize the pattern and exploit it—waiting for American accommodation rather than responding to Trump’s threats.

V. The White House Webpage: TACO Supreme

Which brings us to January 6, 2026—the most egregious TACO yet.

Five years after the Capitol attack, Trump’s administration created a White House webpage systematically rewriting January 6th with documented falsehoods: blaming Capitol Police for starting violence, accusing Democrats of failing to prevent an attack aimed at them, declaring “Zero law enforcement officers lost their lives” despite 140 documented injuries, calling 1,600+ prosecuted participants “innocent.”

This isn’t Trump lying on social media. This is official U.S. government infrastructure weaponized to create alternate reality.

Map the webpage to TACO:

THREATEN (2021-2024): Anyone accurately describing January 6th faces consequences—witnesses intimidated, officers threatened, politicians primaried, prosecutors attacked.

ACCOMMODATE (2021-2025): Media adopts euphemisms. Republicans avoid “insurrection.” Courts grant delays. Video-documented events treated as politically contested.

CAPITULATE (2025): Trump wins election, pardons all 1,600+ participants, appoints loyalists. Accountability mechanisms formally surrender.

OBFUSCATE (2026): Official White House website institutionalizes false narrative. Not spin—government policy. Not opinion—official history.

This is Armstrong’s “Trump Always Chickens Out” reaching its logical endpoint: using government power to erase the record of chickening out entirely.

And the Accommodation Accelerates in Real Time

Four days after the White House webpage launched, the Smithsonian removed text from Trump’s National Portrait Gallery caption that mentioned his two impeachments and the January 6th Capitol attack. The institution replaced it with a minimal “tombstone label” listing only his years in office—despite other presidents’ portraits retaining detailed historical text, including Clinton’s impeachment.

The rationalization was textbook accommodation: “tombstone labels” are common at art museums. True in isolation. But the context reveals the pattern: a Trump official had specifically complained about the caption months earlier. The White House threatened to withhold Smithsonian funding unless the institution cooperated with reviews of “improper ideology.” The Portrait Gallery director was forced out. Then the text quietly disappeared.

Visitors view an updated portrait of President Donald Trump, with abridged text adjoining it, on Saturday at the National Portrait Gallery in D.C. (Maxine Wallace/The Washington Post)

The Smithsonian—the institution charged with preserving American history—just demonstrated how accommodation works in practice: make “reasonable” adjustments that seem defensible individually while collectively erasing documented reality. Each choice can be rationalized. The aggregate effect is the destruction of historical truth.

This is TACO’s Accommodate phase documented in real time, at an institution specifically designed to resist it.

The White House webpage was January 6th. The Smithsonian accommodation was January 10th. Between these dates, the framework I’d been documenting for seven months was validated faster than I could have imagined.

VI. Why TACO Matters: From Trading Strategy to Democratic Crisis

Armstrong coined TACO as a trading strategy. The White House webpage forces us to confront what it really represents: a systematic method for evading accountability that threatens democratic foundations.

Predictive Power: Understanding the four phases lets you anticipate what’s next. The webpage puts us deep into Obfuscate phase, meaning Threaten and Accommodate already succeeded. Obfuscate will feed back into new Threaten phase—the false narrative becomes basis for new threats.

Prescriptive Power: Each phase has specific vulnerabilities:

  • Threaten phase: Make threats costly through public pressure and collective resistance

  • Accommodate phase: Refuse small compromises—they prevent large collapses. Each refusal makes the next one easier

  • Capitulate phase: Nearly irreversible, but preserve evidence for future accountability

  • Obfuscate phase: Challenge false history with evidence every time—for exhausted middle, historians, next generation

The Accommodation Cascade: Each accommodation doesn’t just enable Trump—it makes the next accommodation easier for everyone else. This works multiplicatively, not additively. First accommodation creates template, second normalizes it, third makes it seem inevitable. By the tenth, resistance seems futile.

The Smithsonian example shows this perfectly: White House threatens funding → director forced out → caption quietly changed → framed as "unremarkable" adjustment. Four steps from threat to accommodation in less than a year. And now other institutions know: accommodate or face consequences.

Why Democratic Institutions Fail: They were designed to resist confrontational authoritarianism—coups, arrests, censorship. TACO works through threats that create accommodation without requiring follow-through, institutional adjustments that seem reasonable in isolation, gradual normalization, and post-hoc historical revision. Democratic institutions weren’t built for this.

VII. The Inflection Point

The White House webpage forces a choice previous TACOs allowed people to avoid:

Either you accept that official government channels can institutionalize documented falsehoods, or you recognize this as line-crossing requiring active resistance.

There’s no middle ground. You can’t accommodate this quietly while maintaining credibility. You can’t treat video-recorded events as “contested perspectives” and claim to value truth.

The webpage forces everyone—journalists, historians, judges, politicians, educators, citizens—to choose: defend documented reality or accommodate its erasure.

VIII. The Three Scenarios Ahead

TACO framework lets us model three pathways forward:

Scenario 1 - Institutional Collapse: Accommodation continues. Trump’s rewriting succeeds. By 2029, no political will for accountability. Democracy survives in form, not function.

Scenario 2 - Pendulum Swing: Webpage’s brazenness shocks institutions out of accommodation. Democrats use House control (2027-2029) to preserve evidence. Post-Trump accountability succeeds, though democracy is wounded.

Scenario 3 - The Fracture: Two Americas with incompatible historical narratives emerge. Not disagreement—competing realities. No shared accountability framework. Constitutional crisis.

The next three essays explore each scenario in depth, analyzing how current TACO patterns might develop, what choices lead toward each outcome, and what long-term consequences follow.

IX. A Canadian Perspective

I’m a 74-year-old retired journalist in Toronto who’s documented Trump’s accountability evasion for seven months. My Canadian perspective offers distance without disengagement—what happens to American democracy profoundly affects Canada, but I’m not embedded in partisan warfare.

I started documenting accommodation patterns in October 2025 when the Pentagon expelled critical journalists—my “Ministry of Truth” series tracked military journalism’s capture in real time. At the time, it seemed like isolated domain-specific failure. By January 2026, when the White House webpage launched and the Smithsonian accommodated four days later, the pattern became undeniable: this isn’t about military secrecy or museum curation. It’s systematic method for evading accountability across all domains through distributed institutional compliance.

This distance reveals patterns Americans inside the system might miss. The trajectory is stark, the stakes obvious. I’m documenting how democratic accountability can be systematically evaded through distributed institutional capture—not as partisan advocacy, but as pattern recognition that matters for democracy globally.
