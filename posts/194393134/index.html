When the President Attacked God’s Representative Until 4 AM

Part 13: The Night the Disinhibition Reached the Vatican

April 16, 2026

Alex Wroblewski / AFP / Getty

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays
April 15, 2026

In Part 12, I documented the Easter genocide threat—when the President of the United States posted at 8:03 AM on Easter Sunday that “a whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again.” Seventy million people. Ancient Persia. Posted, then reversed within 72 hours, then pivoted to a business partnership with the same country.

I wrote then that we had reached the point where cruelty stops being a character flaw and becomes a symptom. That the prefrontal cortex—the region most vulnerable to frontotemporal dementia—does not separate morality from cognition.

Three days later, the disinhibition reached the Vatican.

And it kept going until dawn.

Sunday Night, April 12: The Record

The president landed at Joint Base Andrews at approximately 9:00 PM, returning from Miami. According to the White House’s official schedule, he was likely posting from the plane.

What followed was not a late-night tweet. It was a seven-hour descent into what two Atlantic writers would call evidence of a president whose “emotional state seems to be fraying” and whose behavior raises “renewed questions about the president’s cognitive fitness.”

The timeline, as documented by Tom Nichols and Elizabeth Bruenig in The Atlantic:

9:00 PM - 300+ word screed against Pope Leo XIV
9:45 PM - AI image of himself as Jesus Christ healing the sick
9:50 PM - Trump Tower on the moon
10:10 PM - Meme about how old other politicians look compared to him
10:22 PM - Newsmax clip
10:42 PM - Another Newsmax clip
12:43 AM - U.S. Navy will blockade Iranian ports “as if it were just another stray factoid”
2:35-4:10 AM - Continued posting: Iran articles, Biden/Ukraine, Swalwell, same Biden story again, ballroom ruling, New York Post piece

Seven hours. Fifteen posts. From attacking the leader of 1.4 billion Catholics to announcing a naval blockade to reposting the same article twice.

Tom Nichols: “This is not the behavior of a stable, healthy leader.”

The Pope Who Dared to Criticize Him

President Donald Trump and Pope Leo XIV [AP Photo]

Pope Leo XIV is the first American-born pope in the 2,000-year history of the Catholic Church. Before his elevation, he was a science teacher in Chicago. Last week, he hosted a peace vigil at St. Peter’s Basilica, warning against “that delusion of omnipotence that surrounds us and is becoming increasingly unpredictable and aggressive.”

On Easter Sunday—the same day Trump posted his genocide threat against Iran—Leo said: “Enough of the idolatry of self and money! Enough of the display of power! Enough of war!”

At 9:00 PM on Sunday, April 12, Trump responded.

The 300+ Word Attack

The screed accused the pope of being “Weak on Crime” and “Weak on Nuclear Weapons.” Trump said Leo “wasn’t on any list to be Pope” and that he likes Leo’s brother Louis much better because “Louis is all MAGA.”

Trump claimed Leo was only chosen to deal with Trump: If not for the 47th president, “Leo wouldn’t be in the Vatican.”

This is a man claiming that his own existence motivated the Holy Spirit to guide the College of Cardinals toward selecting a particular successor to Saint Peter.

Trump had recommendations for how Leo could be a better Vicar of Christ: he “should get his act together as Pope, use Common Sense, stop catering to the Radical Left, and focus on being a Great Pope, not a Politician.”

Trump is not a Catholic. He has referred to Communion as “a little wine” with some “little crackers.” He is also now officially the most unpopular modern president ever, so the pope might understandably pass on accepting his spiritual advice.

And it was just the beginning of the night.

The Image He Posted, Then Claimed He Didn’t Understand

Forty-five minutes after attacking the pope, Trump posted an AI-generated image depicting himself as Jesus Christ. Flowing robes. Heavenly glow. Healing a sick man. Soldiers in reverence. Nurses in awe. American flag. Military jets overhead.

Elizabeth Bruenig, writing in The Atlantic, called it “a fairly clear-cut instance of profaning the sacred.” Douglas Wilson, the Calvinist pastor who counts Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth among his followers, promptly called the image “blasphemy.”

Trump deleted the post. Then claimed he thought the image depicted him as a doctor—a denial that Nichols notes “is not only laughable, but is also hardly reassuring about his cognitive health.”

“I do make people better,” Trump added.

Five minutes later, he posted Trump Tower on the moon.

The posting continued through the night. At 12:43 AM—three hours and forty-three minutes into this marathon—Trump announced that the U.S. Navy would be blockading Iranian ports in the morning. Not in a press conference. Not in a formal statement. Sandwiched between Newsmax clips and political memes.

This is how the President of the United States announces military action.

The posting continued until 4:10 AM.

The Coalition Breaks

Archbishop Paul S. Coakley, president of the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops, issued a statement saying he was “disheartened that the President chose to write such disparaging words about the Holy Father” and defending the pope as “the Vicar of Christ who speaks from the truth of the Gospel.”

Bishop Robert Barron—a longtime Trump supporter who smiled indulgently at a White House Easter gathering when Trump’s spiritual adviser favorably compared the president to Jesus—condemned Trump’s outburst as “entirely inappropriate and disrespectful.”

This matters because Barron is not a critic. He is a loyalist who has defended Trump through scandal after scandal. Even he could not defend attacking the Pope.

Vice President JD Vance—who will soon release a book about his own Catholic faith—has said nothing publicly. Twenty percent of Americans are Catholics. Most of them voted for Trump.

The president spent Sunday night attacking their spiritual leader, posting an image of himself as Christ, and then posting until dawn.

The Pope’s Response

Pope Leo XIV addresses journalists during the flight heading to Algiers on April 13, 2026.

Pope Leo told reporters aboard the papal plane that he does not fear the Trump administration and will not “shy away from announcing the message of the Gospel.” He invited all people to look “for ways to avoid war any time that’s possible.”

Bruenig notes the strategic reality: “Trump, accustomed to playing the bully to forge deals, is perhaps discovering that his tactics make little sense against a power that has little need for currying favor. The Vatican is a 2,000-year-old global institution with a divine remit. The 250-year-old United States is still only a footnote, and this president’s term is barely a thought.”

The Pope has no need to curry favor with the President of the United States. The Vatican’s authority, in Catholic theology, comes from God—not from Mar-a-Lago.

But Trump’s behavior on April 12 was not strategic. It was seven hours of compulsive posting with no apparent stopping point other than exhaustion or dawn.

The Frontotemporal Dementia Framework

In Part 9, I documented Dr. Frank George’s diagnosis: frontotemporal dementia. Not Alzheimer’s. The disease that doesn’t diminish the person but augments them by removing inhibitions.

“What’s being disinhibited is his underlying malignant narcissism,” Dr. George explained. “As the frontotemporal dementia progresses, that disinhibition just gets worse and worse. His decisions will become more erratic, more vengeful. It’s just getting expressed with no guard rails.”

The pattern Dr. George described is unfolding in real time: the disease removes inhibitions progressively. What Trump might have thought but not said a year ago, he says now. What he might have said but not done six months ago, he does now.

The disinhibition doesn’t stop at policy. It doesn’t stop at decorum. It doesn’t stop at attacking political opponents.

On April 12, it reached the Vatican and kept going for seven hours.

The Context

Tom Nichols frames the April 12 behavior in context:

“Perhaps Trump’s narcissistic insistence that he is always successful in everything he undertakes is feeling the sting and strain of multiple public failures, including the collapse of his campaign to dislodge the Iranian regime, plummeting approval ratings, the decline of the U.S. economy, and, on Sunday, the crushing defeat of one of his favorite fellow authoritarians, Hungary’s Viktor Orbán.”

The Iran war, as of April 12, had been underway for almost six weeks. China was reportedly helping Iran rearm. Trump had threatened genocide on Easter, reversed within 72 hours, and was now announcing a naval blockade at 12:43 AM between Newsmax clips.

Trump is now the most unpopular modern president in American history.

His response: attack the Pope for seven hours, post an image of himself as Jesus Christ, announce military action at nearly 1 AM, and keep posting until dawn.

Nichols concludes: “Americans must not shrug off the president’s latest implosion. They must demand that their elected representatives ask questions about the course of the war and whether Trump still has the capacity to fulfill his constitutional duty as commander in chief.”

“If an elderly parent did such things,” Nichols writes, “most people would be concerned. The president doing such things is far more alarming.”

The Pattern of Acceleration

January 1: “Nice, thin blood” - WSJ interview
January 19: “Dear Jonas” letter - demanding Greenland
March 16: Forgets he’s president - calls Newsom “the president of the United States”
April 6: 93-minute press conference - contradictions, “I don’t know” responses
April 7 (Easter): Genocide threat at 8:03 AM
April 10: 72-hour reversal - business partnership with Iran
April 11: MAGA coalition breaks - Carlson, Kelly, Owens, Jones, Greene condemn him
April 12: Attacks Pope, posts himself as Jesus, announces blockade at 12:43 AM, posts until 4:10 AM

From January 1 to April 12: 104 days.
From Easter genocide threat to attacking the Pope: 7 days.
From MAGA coalition breaking to attacking the Pope: 1 day.

The intervals are disappearing.

The Baseline Question

This series does not diagnose. It observes patterns and asks whether they represent a departure from baseline.

The April 12 record documents:

  • Seven hours of compulsive posting

  • Attacking the spiritual leader of 1.4 billion Catholics

  • Claiming his existence motivated the Holy Spirit to select this pope

  • Posting an image of himself as Jesus Christ, then claiming not to understand what it depicted

  • Announcing military action at 12:43 AM between memes

  • Breaking his own religious coalition—Catholic bishops including loyalists condemning the behavior

  • No awareness that any of this is unusual

The baseline question: does this represent Trump being Trump—provocative, willing to attack critics—or has something shifted beyond that established baseline?

Part 12 documented moral collapse accompanying cognitive collapse. The Easter genocide threat. The 72-hour reversal. The MAGA coalition breaking.

Three days later, the president attacked the Pope and posted until dawn.

Conclusion

On April 12, the question became: at what point does a president posting until 4 AM while attacking the Pope and depicting himself as Jesus Christ become a medical emergency rather than a political scandal?

The record shows seven hours of compulsive posting with no stopping point other than dawn and no awareness that anything is concerning.

From “nice, thin blood” to attacking the Pope: 104 days.
From Easter genocide threat to seven hours of posting: 7 days.

The acceleration is undeniable. The disinhibition is complete.

The question is whether anyone with constitutional authority will act on it before the next incident.
