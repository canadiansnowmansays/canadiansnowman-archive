A Canadian's Take on the Latest U.S. Drug War Escalation

Why "Just One More Strike" Risks War Crimes—and Our Shared Fight Against Fentanyl

October 28, 2025

As a Canadian peering south across the border, the overdose crisis south of us hits close to home. We’ve lost over 50,000 lives to apparent opioid toxicity since 2016, with approximately 2,700 reported in the first half of 2025 alone. It’s a shared tragedy—families shattered from Vancouver to Virginia. But the numbers tell a stark story: Provisional data for 2025 predicts around 76,500 overdose deaths in the U.S., or roughly 230 per million people, compared to Canada’s rate of about 200 per million. Both nations are grappling with fentanyl’s devastation, much of it tied to smuggling routes that don’t respect borders. So when the Trump administration turned to military strikes on suspected drug boats in the Caribbean and Pacific—resulting in 57 deaths since September—I felt that familiar mix of empathy and concern. The frustration is real; decades of unchecked smuggling have worn everyone down. Yet, as someone invested in solutions that save lives without costing more, I worry these actions cross a dangerous line.

I’m all for aggressive action against the drug trade—whether it’s precursors from China fueling Mexican labs or cocaine speedboats from Colombia, or even the fentanyl-laced pills crossing from Canada into the U.S. But lethal force without due process? That’s not the path forward. It’s a step toward isolation and potential legal reckoning. Let’s explore why, drawing on history’s lessons, and why approaches like Canada’s harm reduction model might offer a blueprint worth considering—without any finger-pointing from up north.

The Shared Frustration: A Crisis That Knows No Borders

Imagine the exhaustion: For over 40 years, both our countries have poured resources into interdiction, from border scanners to international pacts. Seizures have climbed, yet overdoses surged—up 38% in the U.S. during the pandemic, and similarly steep here in Canada. Fentanyl dominates now, not the cocaine these boats primarily target, and much of it travels over land borders rather than open seas. President Trump’s assertion that each destroyed vessel saves 25,000 lives feels like wishful thinking amid the reality of ongoing fentanyl fatalities.

The administration’s response—labeling cartels “narco-terrorists” and launching strikes starting with a September raid off Venezuela (11 killed), escalating to 14 more on October 27—stems from that same desperation we feel north of the 49th. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth’s social media updates frame it as an extension of counterterrorism efforts, but without public evidence of drugs recovered or precise locations, it raises more questions than it answers.

Online, the debate rages: Supporters see overdue resolve, while others decry potential war crimes. From my vantage point in Canada, I see a neighbor under immense pressure, but one that risks alienating allies in the process. We’ve both tried “tough on crime” before—let’s learn from that shared history instead of repeating it. And with President Trump’s upcoming meeting with China’s Xi Jinping on October 30 in South Korea—where fentanyl precursors are explicitly on the agenda alongside trade and other issues—this is a timely moment for diplomacy to take center stage over unilateral strikes.

Lessons from History: Militarized Approaches Have Backfired Before

This isn’t uncharted territory. Nixon’s 1971 declaration of a “War on Drugs” militarized policing on both sides of the border, leading to SWAT-style raids and skyrocketing incarceration—disproportionately affecting marginalized communities. Reagan’s 1980s escalation blurred military-civilian lines, equipping local forces with surplus weaponry through programs like 1033, which increased lethal encounters without curbing supply.

Closer to the maritime front, our joint operations like the 1980s-era efforts to interdict “go-fast” boats emphasized disabling vessels and boarding, not destruction—avoiding the evidentiary black holes that come with sinkings. And look at Plan Colombia in 2000: The U.S. invested $10 billion in aerial eradication and military pursuits, temporarily reducing coca cultivation but displacing violence, killing thousands in extrajudicial actions, and uprooting 4 million people. Cartels adapted, shifting to synthetics like fentanyl—mirroring today’s challenges.

Even our tacit support for the Philippines’ 2010s “drug war” under Duterte, which claimed 30,000 lives through summary executions, drew international condemnation as crimes against humanity. These precedents show that when frustration turns to firepower without accountability, resentment builds, networks evolve, and moral authority erodes. Hegseth’s parallels to post-9/11 drone campaigns echo this pattern: Civilian risks, legal entanglements, and no end in sight.

The Legal and Ethical Concerns: A Risky Path Forward

At its core, targeting unarmed suspects on boats violates international norms the U.S. helped establish. The Geneva Conventions prohibit lethal force against civilians not engaged in active hostilities—smuggling, serious as it is, doesn’t qualify. U.N. experts and groups like Human Rights Watch have flagged these as “extrajudicial executions,” breaching the right to life. Domestically, it sidesteps the War Powers Resolution, relying on a stretched interpretation of the 2001 AUMF.

From Canada, where we’ve navigated sovereignty sensitivities with our own U.S. partners, I see the backlash brewing: Leaders like Venezuela’s Maduro and Colombia’s Petro protesting incursions, straining hemispheric ties. The ACLU’s push for transparency and senators like Rand Paul gearing up for oversight votes highlight domestic fractures. Globally, this could invite ICC scrutiny or universal jurisdiction probes, much like past U.S. actions faced in Europe.

Ethically, those 57 lives—likely low-level operators or innocents misidentified—demand better. As Canadians, we’ve seen how de-escalation preserves trust; skipping due process does the opposite.

A Better Way: Learning from Harm Reduction Across the Border

Killing is not inevitable; capture is feasible and effective. The U.S. Coast Guard’s track record—seizing 1.5 tons of cocaine in 2023 through boardings and prosecutions—proves it: These yield intelligence, deter recruits, and build cases. Scaling that with naval support, drones for surveillance, and joint operations could deliver results without the fallout.

Yet boats are just one thread. Addressing roots means multilateral pressure: Tariffs on Chinese precursors (which have helped curb fentanyl flows into Mexico via the Mérida Initiative, contributing to reductions in deaths), enhanced U.S.-Canada patrols for cross-border fentanyl, and OAS-led intel sharing in South America. The Trump-Xi summit this week presents a prime opportunity to press China on cracking down on precursor exports—one of Trump’s stated demands—potentially amplifying these efforts without resorting to military overreach.

From up here, Canada’s harm reduction strategies offer a complementary model—one that’s contributing to our national decrease in opioid deaths observed in early 2025 data. Supervised consumption sites like Vancouver’s Insite have prevented more than 64,000 fatal overdoses since 2017, with studies showing up to 85% access to opioid agonist therapy (OAT) locally—versus about 20% in the U.S. Naloxone distribution to police and communities, syringe programs, and British Columbia’s decriminalization pilot are slowing the tide without judgment. These aren’t silver bullets, but they’ve bent the curve where prohibition alone hasn’t. Imagine blending that public health lens with strong interdiction—shared wins for both sides.

Toward a United Front: Solutions Over Division

We both want the same: Communities healed, borders secure, lives preserved. The U.S. strikes, born of valid anger, risk deepening divides when collaboration could amplify impact. Congress should invoke War Powers for oversight; the administration, prioritize pursuits and partnerships.As a Canadian, I’m rooting for my American friends—not critiquing from afar, but offering a hand. What resonates with you? Could harm reduction bridge our approaches? Share in the comments. If this sparks ideas, subscribe or share.

In solidarity,
Stephen
