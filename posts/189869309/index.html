The Iran Crisis

"The Clock Starts Now"

Part XXXI: Day 5

March 4, 2026

The war that began over Iran is now burning in Lebanon too / Ibrahim Amro/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

On Tuesday night, as Day Four turned to Day Five, a ship sank.

The IRIS Dena — a destroyer, the pride of the Iranian navy, 180 crew members aboard — sent a distress signal off the southern coast of Sri Lanka at 5:08 in the morning local time. Sri Lankan naval vessels responded. They rescued 32 critically injured sailors. They found bodies floating in the water. The rest of the crew, more than 140 people, were unaccounted for.

Admiral Brad Cooper of US Central Command had said Tuesday evening, with what can only be described as operational satisfaction: “We are sinking the Iranian Navy. The entire navy.” He confirmed 17 Iranian ships destroyed. “24/7 strikes into Iran from seabed to space and cyberspace.”

The IRIS Dena was not a warship threatening American forces. It was, as recently as last month, participating in an international naval exercise in India. It sank in the Indian Ocean, thousands of miles from the Strait of Hormuz, far from any declared theater of operations. Whether it was struck by US or Israeli forces, whether it suffered mechanical failure under the strain of a navy being systematically dismantled, or whether something else happened entirely — no one has said. The Sri Lankan foreign minister told parliament only that his country had responded “on a humane basis as is our responsibility.”

More than 140 sailors. The Indian Ocean. Wednesday morning.

This is Day Five.

I. Noah, Cody, Nicole, and Declan

The Pentagon released their names Tuesday afternoon. The New York Times found their families.

Noah L. Tietjens was 42 years old. He grew up in a military family, joined the Army in the early 2000s, and had completed at least four tours, including previous deployments to Kuwait and Iraq. He was a Sergeant First Class assigned to the 103rd Sustainment Command in Des Moines, Iowa. He was three months from the end of his current deployment.

His twin brother Nicholas told the Times: “He just wanted to get there, and get it over with, and get back.”

Noah Tietjens had found, in the years between deployments, something that grounded him: martial arts. He and his wife Shelly and their teenage son Dylan had taken it up together. He became a certified instructor at Martial Arts International in Bellevue, Nebraska. He dreamed of opening his own studio one day. Julius Melegrito, the studio’s owner, described him as calm, confident, and soft-spoken. “Since then, whenever we would have events or he would be at the school,” said Melegrito’s wife Faith, “I would always feel more calm when he’s around, because I knew he would look at what’s needed and he would take care of it.”

Dylan accepted a special award at his black belt ceremony. He asked his father to come onstage so he could dedicate it to him.

The governor of Nebraska ordered flags to half-staff.

Cody A. Khork was 35 years old. He was from Lakeland, Florida, though born in Winter Haven. He enlisted in the National Guard in 2009 as a multiple launch rocket system specialist, commissioned as a military police officer in the Army Reserve in 2014, and deployed to Saudi Arabia in 2018, Guantánamo Bay in 2021, and Poland in 2024. He earned a degree in political science from Florida Southern College, where he led the ROTC program. He had earned the Meritorious Service Medal and the Army Commendation Medal.

His family said in a statement: “He felt a calling to serve his country from an early age. He was deeply patriotic and took great pride in serving something greater than himself. He lived with purpose, loved deeply, and served honorably.”

Nicole M. Amor was 39 years old. She had been in the military for nearly twenty years. She was a Sergeant First Class from White Bear Lake, Minnesota, with a specialty in automated logistics. She had deployed to Iraq in 2019 and to Kuwait — the deployment that killed her — after that. She had an 18-year-old son who had just graduated high school. She had a 9-year-old daughter.

Her brother Derek Hoff, 42, of Eau Claire, Wisconsin, told the Times that this deployment was likely going to be her last. She was at a crossroads before she left in August — her children needed her, and she knew it.

“She just missed them,” Derek said. “It was a yearning for her kids.”

She was, her brother said, “a spitfire.” Her biggest accomplishment, he said, was becoming a mother and later a surrogate.

Declan J. Coady was 20 years old.

He was from Des Moines. He enlisted in the Army Reserve in 2023 as an information technology specialist. He had been posthumously promoted from specialist to sergeant. He was a sophomore at Drake University, studying information systems, cybersecurity, and computer science.

Drake University said he was “a well-loved and highly dedicated student” who “had an incredibly bright future ahead of him.”

He was 20 years old.

They were all assigned to the 103rd Sustainment Command in Des Moines, Iowa. They were Army Reserve soldiers — not career combatants, but civilians who had chosen to serve part-time. They were killed by an Iranian drone at the port of Shuaiba, Kuwait, in a temporary office space, not a fortified base. Two other service members killed in the same attack have not yet been publicly identified.

Trump said, before their names were released: “Sadly, there will likely be more before it ends. That’s the way it is.”

II. The War on American Eyes — Targeting the Communications Network

While the deaths at Shuaiba were the most visible American casualties of the war’s opening days, a New York Times analysis of satellite imagery published Wednesday revealed a parallel Iranian campaign that received almost no public attention: a systematic, targeted assault on US military communications and missile-defense coordination infrastructure across the entire Middle East.

Iranian strikes damaged structures at or near communications and radar systems at seven US military sites spanning Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates. This was not collateral damage. It was a targeting list.

At the US Navy’s Fifth Fleet headquarters in Manama, Bahrain — the primary hub for coordinating American naval operations across the Middle East — an Iranian one-way attack drone struck a radome on Saturday. Satellite imagery confirmed that at least two AN/GSC-52B SATCOM terminals, which facilitate high-capacity near-real-time military communications, were destroyed.

At Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar — the US military’s largest base in the Middle East, regional headquarters of US Central Command — satellite imagery showed a tent surrounded by satellite dishes destroyed, with dishes likely damaged. Iran struck the same base’s communications infrastructure during the 12-day war last June.

At Camp Arifjan in Kuwait, three radomes were damaged or destroyed by Sunday morning. At Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait, six buildings adjacent to satellite communications equipment were destroyed — and the same area was struck again on Tuesday, with two additional buildings near the satellite equipment heavily damaged.

Satellite imagery shows smoke rising from a building at Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia.

At Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia, a building connected to a radome was largely destroyed, based on satellite imagery showing a mile-long smoke plume the morning after the strike.

In the UAE, at a military installation near Al Ruwais, an AN/TPY-2 radar system — used to detect and track incoming ballistic missiles and crucial for coordinating intercepts — appears to have been stationed adjacent to buildings that were struck. Whether the radar itself was damaged remains unclear from the imagery. A separate facility at Al Dhafra Air Base also sustained damage in an area that previously housed satellite dishes and antennas.

Iran was attempting to blind the network — to degrade the American military’s ability to see incoming missiles, communicate between bases, and coordinate the layered air defense system that has been the primary barrier between Iranian drones and American personnel.

US Central Command declined to comment on the strikes.

The implications are direct: every degraded radome, every destroyed SATCOM terminal, every damaged radar system is a gap in the coverage that keeps Patriot batteries and THAAD systems firing at the right moment, at the right projectile. The munitions clock ticking toward exhaustion is only part of the air defense problem. The eyes that tell the interceptors where to aim are now partially damaged across seven installations. Iran has been methodical about this since June. They have had nine months to study the network’s vulnerabilities.

III. The War Powers Vote — and the 60-Day Clock

The Senate voted Wednesday on a War Powers Resolution to curtail Trump’s authority to continue military operations against Iran without congressional authorization. It failed, as expected, along near-party lines.

But the vote was not irrelevant. It started a clock.

The 1973 War Powers Resolution gives the president 60 days from the initiation of hostilities to wind down operations without congressional authorization. Day One was Saturday, February 28. Day 60 is April 28. If Trump has not secured congressional authorization by then, he is legally required to withdraw US forces from combat operations.

No one in Washington believes he will comply if Congress declines to authorize. No one believes the current Republican congressional majority will refuse him authorization, particularly if the operation appears to be succeeding by any measurable standard. But the vote, and the 60-day clock it acknowledged, placed the constitutional question formally on the record.

The debate exposed Republican fractures that will matter if the war drags on. Representative Nancy Mace of South Carolina voted no — “for now” — but said explicitly: “If this thing goes beyond a few weeks, I’m going to have a lot more concerns.” Senator Josh Hawley cited the 60-day statutory window as his entire justification for a no vote. Multiple Republicans made the deployment of ground troops their stated red line.

Speaker Mike Johnson, meanwhile, offered a remarkable illustration of the administration’s incoherence in a single twenty-four-hour period. On Monday, he told reporters the war was justified because without acting first, the US would have suffered “staggering losses” from Iranian retaliation to an Israeli strike. On Tuesday, he offered a completely different explanation: Iran’s ballistic missile buildup had “exceeded the ability of our regional allies to respond appropriately,” creating “an imminent and serious threat.” He made no mention of Israel.

Democrats were unified and unsparing. Senator Pramila Jayapal: “We got no additional information on what the imminent threat was. There were a lot of references to the 47 years of Iran being a problem. That is not imminent.” Representative Sara Jacobs: administration officials “pushed back on the idea that Israel was calling the timing. But then they kind of said that Israel was calling the timing. So it was all very incoherent.”

Senator Schumer, emerging from the classified briefing: “very unsatisfying,” “different answers every day.”

The administration’s legal position was further complicated by Trump’s own War Powers letter to Congress, which justified the strikes as advancing “vital United States national interests” and “neutralizing Iran’s malign activities” — language that describes a strategic choice, not an imminent threat. Rubio’s Monday rationale (Israel forced our hand), Tuesday rationale (Iran’s arsenal was the threat), and the letter’s rationale (national interests) are three different legal and political arguments for the same action. They cannot all be simultaneously true.

The House votes Thursday. It will also fail. But the record is being built, and the clock is running.

IV. The Shattered Compass — What Khamenei’s Death Actually Means

Gal Beckerman, writing in The Atlantic, offered the most psychologically precise account of what the death of a long-ruling tyrant actually does to the people who lived under him — and it is not what the policy briefings describe.

When Iranian state television announced Khamenei’s death, the announcer was not composing himself between sentences. He was heaving, gasping for air, reaching for tissues. In New York City, Masih Alinejad — a dissident whom the Islamic Republic had targeted for assassination — burst into the streets screaming “The dictator of my country is dead!” and could not stop crying. Loyalist and dissident, weeping simultaneously, for entirely different reasons.

This is, Beckerman argues, what always happens when a long-ruling tyrant dies. The Soviet poet Joseph Brodsky understood why Russian gutters ran with tears in March 1953 even as he had called Stalin “a spawn of hell.” Stalin had been “as reliable a presence as the sky” for three decades, Brodsky wrote — “no matter how nightmarish it had been.” What collapses with the dictator is not just a government but something Brodsky called “a category of consciousness” — the fixed reality that people built their entire sense of the possible around, even those who despised it.

Khamenei set the boundaries for morality, for thought, for what could be spoken and what could be worn, for thirty-five years. He had just ordered the killing of at least seven thousand protesters. He was represented by the state as an eternal being. Shiite Muslims in the streets of Tehran were describing themselves as “orphans” — and meaning it, even those who had reason to hate him, because orphan is precisely what you are when the only father you have ever known, however cruel, is suddenly gone.

Nadezhda Mandelstam, widow of the poet Osip Mandelstam who died in one of Stalin’s labor camps, wrote in 1970 — thirty-three years after her husband’s death: “Every section of the population has been through the terrible sickness caused by terror, and none has so far recovered, or become fit again for civic life. It is an illness that is passed on to the next generation, so that the sons pay for the sins of the fathers and perhaps only the grandchildren begin to get over it.”

This is the baseline condition of the society Trump is now attempting, without apparent preparation, to redirect toward democratic governance. Not a population waiting for liberation, but a population that has had its capacity for civic trust systematically destroyed across multiple generations. The tears that followed Khamenei’s death — both the loyalist’s grief and the dissident’s wail — come from the same source: the sudden disappearance of what felt, however monstrously, like a fixed reality.

Building something in that space requires exactly what the Trump administration has not built: patient, sustained investment in the organizations and leaders who could provide a new fixed reality. Instead, there are bombs, a $3.20 gas price, and a president who told reporters that most of the people he had in mind to run the country are dead.

V. The Succession Threat — and What Comes Next

A handout image from Iran state media of Mojtaba Khamenei, a son of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, in Tehran in 2024.Credit...Office of The Iranian Supreme Leader/Wana News Agency, via Reuters

Iranian officials were expected Wednesday to announce Mojtaba Khamenei — the slain supreme leader’s 56-year-old son, IRGC-backed, reclusive, hardline — as the new supreme leader. The announcement was being considered for Wednesday morning Tehran time, though some clerics expressed hesitation: naming a successor might make him an immediate target.

Israel’s Defense Minister Israel Katz removed any ambiguity on that point. He stated publicly Wednesday that if Iran’s next supreme leader follows Ayatollah Khamenei’s ideology — if he pursues the destruction of Israel and threatens the United States — he would be “an unequivocal target for elimination.”

Israel is threatening to assassinate the replacement before he is announced.

The Assembly of Experts, constitutionally responsible for the selection, held two virtual meetings Tuesday — its physical meeting site in Qom had been struck by Israeli forces. It was deliberating under bombardment, in secret, over a succession that the attacking power was simultaneously threatening to violate the moment it was complete.

This is the logical endpoint of a strategy that has no articulated endpoint. If the goal is a non-threatening Iran, and every candidate the US and Israel considered suitable is dead, and the IRGC is now installing a hardline successor, and Israel is threatening to kill whoever emerges — the war has not disrupted Iran’s succession. It has radicalized it. The IRGC, which has been the operational backbone of the Islamic Republic for forty-seven years, is emerging from this war with tighter control over the succession than it had before the first bomb fell.

Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi — the diplomat who sat across from Witkoff and Kushner in Geneva, who negotiated in good faith over nuclear enrichment levels, who believed until the end that a deal was possible — posted on social media Wednesday that Trump had treated the talks as “a real estate transaction” and had bombed “the negotiation table out of spite.”

That is the verdict of the man who was at the table.

VI. Canada, Carney, and the View from the 49th Parallel

Prime Minister Mark Carney addressed reporters Wednesday from Sydney, Australia, on an official visit — and offered the most carefully calibrated statement yet from any close American ally.

Canada’s support for the strikes, Carney said, came “with regret, because the current conflict is another example of the failure of the international order.” He criticized Iran for persisting in its nuclear ambitions in defiance of international law and years of diplomatic effort. He also criticized the United States and Israel, which “have acted without engaging the United Nations or consulting with allies, including Canada.”

He said Canada would not participate militarily. He called for “diplomatic engagement essential to avoid a wider and deeper conflict,” and said that engagement needed to “involve broader parties than just the United States, Israel and Iran.” He declined to say whether he had received intelligence confirming the imminent threat Trump cited. He declined to predict how long the war would last. He said he had not spoken with Trump since the strikes began.

A Canadian poll released Tuesday found that nearly half of Canadians oppose the attacks on Iran.

I want to be direct about Carney’s position, because it will be misconstrued in both directions. This is not an endorsement of the war. It is a statement that Canada supports the goal — ending Iran’s nuclear program — while rejecting the method, the secrecy, the absence of consultation, and the absence of a plan for what comes after. It is the position of a country that has spent decades building an international legal order it now watches being dismantled by its closest ally, unable to stop it, unwilling to simply cheer.

“Canada is actively engaging in the world as it is,” Carney said, “without passively waiting for the advent of a world as we want it to be.”

That sentence contains a great deal of pain, if you read it carefully.

A poll released Tuesday found nearly half of Canadians oppose the strikes. From the 49th parallel, watching the country that has been our security guarantor, our largest trading partner, and our nearest neighbor for two centuries, launch the largest American military operation since Iraq — without consultation, without a plan, without honest justification — the feeling is not opposition so much as grief. The kind of grief that comes when something you thought was fixed and reliable turns out not to be.

Which is, as Gal Beckerman would note, a very old feeling in this particular week.

VII. The Price of Everything

Gas was $3.20 Wednesday morning. Up nine cents overnight. Up twenty cents since Monday. Up from under $3.00 five days ago when the bombs had not yet started falling.

The IMF warned Tuesday of potentially “very impactful” consequences for the global economy. South Korea’s Kospi index fell so rapidly Wednesday that trading was temporarily suspended. Japan, Taiwan — all highly dependent on imported oil and natural gas — were among the hardest hit for a third consecutive day. Iran struck the Ras Tanura refinery in Saudi Arabia again Wednesday — the second hit this week on one of the world’s most critical oil processing facilities.

Trump told reporters Tuesday that oil prices would drop “as soon as this ends.” He seemed to believe that was a reassuring thing to say. It implied that “this” had a foreseeable end, and that the end was near. Neither of those things has been established.

Grand Ayatollah Ali al-Sistani — the supreme religious authority for Shia Muslims worldwide, based in Iraq, a figure who almost never speaks on political matters — issued a statement Wednesday condemning the US-Israeli attacks on Iran. He said the assault would have “grave consequences” internationally and lead to “widespread chaos and prolonged instability.” He called on all Muslims and “free people of the world” to work for the war’s “immediate cessation.”

When the most cautious and respected Shia religious authority on earth breaks his political silence to condemn your war, you have not limited the conflict. You have given it a religious dimension that will outlast the military campaign.

In Qatar, security forces arrested two IRGC-linked spy cells Tuesday — ten suspects, seven tasked with intelligence gathering on military and vital infrastructure, three trained in drone sabotage. They had coordinates of sensitive facilities, communication devices, and confessed links to Iranian intelligence. Qatar is home to Al Udeid Air Base, US Central Command’s regional headquarters, and one of the most consequential pieces of American military infrastructure in the world.

The war is inside the perimeter.

VIII. What Day Five Has Established

The soldier biographies tell us something the casualty numbers cannot. Noah Tietjens was three months from coming home to his martial arts studio and his teenage son. Nicole Amor was planning her last deployment, yearning for her 9-year-old daughter. Declan Coady was 20 and studying cybersecurity at a university in Des Moines. Cody Khork felt a calling to serve something greater than himself.

They were Army Reserve soldiers at a commercial port in Kuwait. The mission that killed them was described, in the War Powers letter, as designed to “ensure the free flow of maritime commerce through the Strait of Hormuz” and “advance vital United States national interests.” History will have to judge whether that framing was adequate to the cost.

Iran has spent five days systematically targeting the communications and radar infrastructure that underpins American missile defense across the entire Middle East. The interceptor stockpiles are finite. The missile launcher count Iran started with runs into the thousands. The hidden clock is running.

The War Powers 60-day clock is also running, formally, from Saturday. April 28 is the statutory deadline.

The succession announcement — expected Wednesday — will tell us whether the operation’s central strategic bet, the removal of Khamenei to create space for a pragmatic replacement, has paid off or produced the CIA’s worst-case scenario. Israel has already announced it will kill whoever is chosen if they continue Khamenei’s ideology. The regime the war was designed to end may be emerging from it more hardline, more IRGC-dominated, and more martyr-fortified than the one it replaced.

And the gas is $3.20.

Day Six begins tonight. The succession will be announced. The Senate debate will continue. The bombs will fall on Isfahan and Shiraz and Beirut. Whatever Declan Coady’s family is going through in Des Moines tonight, it will continue too, in the way that grief continues — without reference to news cycles or strategic objectives or War Powers clocks.

“It is an illness that is passed on to the next generation,” Nadezhda Mandelstam wrote, of what tyranny does to the people who survive it.

She was writing about Russia. She might have been writing about Iowa.
