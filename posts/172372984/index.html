How Trump's "National Security" Excuse Brings Back Colonial Tyranny

From Tea Tax to Tariff Tyranny: Same Violation, Higher Stakes

September 1, 2025

"Tariffs, you say? By George, why didn't I think of that! All I had was a measly 3-penny tea tax!"

TAXATION WITHOUT REPRESENTATION launched the American Revolution. Colonists refused to accept taxes imposed by a distant Parliament where they had no voice, no vote, and no say in how their money was spent.

Fast forward 250 years, and we've got the same constitutional crisis—but this time, it's an American president doing the taxing.

Trump has imposed $172 billion in new taxes on American families by calling trade deficits a "national security emergency." He's bypassed Congress entirely, claiming presidential war powers to tax everything from your morning coffee to your kid's car seat.

This isn't just bad policy. It's taxation without representation, plain and simple.

And last Fridays Federal Appeals Court ruling shows that at least some judges still remember what that phrase actually means.

Writing from Canada—a nation partly founded by 40,000 Loyalists who fled the American Revolution—lends particular weight to this analysis. The United Empire Loyalists chose King George III over colonial independence, yet their descendants now witness an American president wielding the very royal prerogatives those Loyalists once supported. History's wheel has come full circle: the country built by people escaping American republicanism now watches American democracy collapse into the monarchical system Canada's founders once embraced.

The Magic Words: "National Security Emergency"

Here's Trump's playbook for bypassing the Constitution: Just call anything a "national security emergency" and suddenly you can tax whatever you want without asking Congress.

Trade deficit with China? National security emergency. Need more steel production? National security emergency. Want to protect auto workers? National security emergency.

It's like a constitutional cheat code. Wave the national security wand, and suddenly the president can impose comprehensive tax systems that would normally require months of congressional debate, committee hearings, and public input.

The problem? Almost everything can be framed as "national security" if you're creative enough.

Economic competition with other countries? That's national security. Supply chain dependencies? National security. Trade imbalances? You guessed it—national security.

Under this logic, there's literally no limit to what a president can tax unilaterally. Congress becomes irrelevant. Democracy becomes optional.

Taxation Without Representation: The 2025 Version

Let's be crystal clear about what's happening here. When Trump imposes tariffs without congressional approval, he's taxing American families without their elected representatives having any say in the matter.

James Madison saw this threat coming at the Constitutional Convention. When Thomas Paine asked, "How can we tell that this president will not become a king?" Madison had the answer: because the president would never control the purse. Only Congress could "reach into the people's pockets." That division of power wasn't an afterthought—it was the firewall against monarchy.

Those tariffs don't get paid by foreign countries, despite what Trump claims on social media. They get paid by American importers, who pass the costs on to American consumers. Every time you buy a car, a computer, or a washing machine, you're paying Trump's unilateral tax increases.

The numbers are brutal:

  • $3,800 per household annually in additional costs

  • $6,400 extra for a new car

  • 17% price increases on computers and electronics

  • Lower-income families hit hardest—paying $1,700 more per year

And here's the kicker: Congress never voted for any of this. Your elected representatives—the people you actually voted for to make tax policy—were completely cut out of the process.

Trump just declared trade deficits a national emergency and started taxing. No hearings. No debate. No input from the people who are supposed to represent you in fiscal policy.

That's taxation without representation.

The Royal Court vs. The Golden Palace: History Repeating

The colonists understood something fundamental: when taxation decisions get made by distant authorities without local input, you get tyranny.

Think about the cast of characters in 1773:

King George III sat in his palace, surrounded by advisors who told him what he wanted to hear about colonial loyalty. He never set foot in America, never talked to a Boston merchant or Philadelphia farmer. To him, colonists were just revenue sources to fund his empire.

His ministers - Lord North, Charles Townshend, George Grenville - crafted taxation policies from London drawing rooms, convinced they knew what was best for people they'd never met living in places they'd never seen.

Parliament passed tax laws affecting colonial families while representing only British landowners who had never paid American prices or faced American economic realities.

Now look at Trump's operation:

Trump himself sits in his golden-appointed Oval Office, surrounded by Mar-a-Lago advisors and cable news hosts who tell him tariffs are brilliant. He's never worked a factory job, never stressed about paying for car repairs, never had to choose between buying groceries and fixing the washing machine.

His inner circle - trade advisors, economic nationalists, and political operatives - design taxation policies from luxury hotels and private clubs, convinced they know what's best for working families they've never lived among.

The "national security" cabinet imposes tax policies affecting every American household while representing only Trump's political base and donor class.

Both situations create the same toxic dynamic: wealthy, insulated elites making taxation decisions that devastate ordinary families they never have to face.

In 1773, British Parliament was 3,000 miles away from Boston. Today, Trump's Mar-a-Lago world feels just as distant from working families struggling to pay higher prices at the grocery store and car lot.

The colonists had no members in Parliament. Modern families have members in Congress—but Trump's "national security" claims bypass Congress entirely, cutting elected representatives out of taxation decisions just as completely as King George cut out colonial voices.

The Emergency Powers Loophole

The 1976 Senate report that warned against unchecked presidential emergency powers - one year before Congress created IEEPA to prevent exactly the kind of constitutional overreach Trump is now attempting.

Trump's favorite law is the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)—a post-Watergate statute designed to let presidents freeze foreign assets and impose targeted sanctions during genuine crises.

It was never intended to authorize comprehensive taxation systems.

But Trump has stretched IEEPA beyond all recognition, using "emergency" powers to impose permanent tariff regimes that reshape the entire American economy. He's turned emergency authority into a substitute for normal democratic governance.

The Federal Appeals Court saw right through this constitutional shell game. In their August 29th ruling, they basically said: "Nice try, but routine trade deficits aren't surprise attacks that justify bypassing Congress on taxation."

The court applied the "major questions doctrine"—the principle that government agencies (and presidents) need clear congressional authorization before making decisions with "vast economic and political significance."

$172 billion in new taxes definitely qualifies as "vast."

Why This Is Worse Than Colonial Taxation

Here's the cruel irony: Trump's version of "taxation without representation" is actually more oppressive than what sparked the American Revolution.

When the British taxed tea, colonists could dump it in Boston Harbor and switch to coffee. The boycott became a powerful symbol of resistance that ultimately helped launch a revolution.

When Trump taxes cars, computers, and appliances, families have no such option.

You can't dump your washing machine in the harbor and hand-wash your clothes. You can't boycott computers when your kids need them for school. You can't refuse to buy a car when you need to get to work.

Modern tariffs target necessities, not luxuries. They create economic coercion that makes resistance nearly impossible for ordinary families.

Colonists could make a dramatic political statement by rejecting British goods. Modern families just quietly absorb higher prices while struggling to make ends meet.

That's why constitutional limits on taxation authority are more important now, not less. When families can't boycott the taxed goods, democratic oversight becomes their only protection from fiscal abuse.

The "National Security" Shell Game: From Royal Prerogative to Presidential Power

Trump's genius (if you can call it that) was figuring out that "national security" is the modern equivalent of "royal prerogative"—the ultimate conversation-stopper in American politics.

King George III claimed "royal prerogative" gave him the right to tax colonists without their consent. Parliament could impose any burden on the colonies because the Crown's authority superseded colonial self-governance. When colonists complained, the response was simple: "The King's security takes precedence over your economic concerns."

Trump claims "national security emergency powers" give him the right to tax American families without congressional consent. The executive can impose any tariff because presidential authority supersedes legislative oversight. When families complain about higher prices, the response is identical: "National security takes precedence over your economic concerns."

Both used the same circular logic to escape accountability:

Trump's response to the Federal Appeals Court ruling his tariffs illegal: Rather than accept constitutional limits, he claims removing his unauthorized taxes would 'completely destroy' America - the same emergency rhetoric King George III used to justify taxation without representation.

King George's court: Question royal taxation? You must be disloyal to the Crown. Demand representation? We can't let colonists override imperial security. Point out economic damage? The empire's survival matters more than colonial prosperity.

Trump's operation: Question tariff authority? You must not care about national security. Demand congressional oversight? We can't wait for Congress during a national emergency. Point out the economic damage? America's security matters more than consumer prices.

It's the exact same power play, just with different words. "Royal prerogative" becomes "national security," but the constitutional violation remains identical: claiming emergency authority to bypass representative government on taxation.

What the Court Got Right

The Federal Appeals Court's ruling wasn't just about tariff law—it was about preserving the basic constitutional principle that sparked American independence.

"No taxation without representation" means that comprehensive tax policies require congressional approval, not just presidential decree.

The court found that Trump's emergency tariffs were:

  • "Unbounded in scope, amount, and duration" - affecting virtually all imports without clear limits

  • Beyond IEEPA's intended authority - designed for targeted sanctions, not comprehensive taxation

  • Lacking explicit congressional approval - emergency powers can't rewrite America's fiscal system

Most importantly, the court recognized that major taxation decisions require democratic deliberation, not unilateral executive action.

Even during genuine emergencies, presidents can't simply declare themselves exempt from constitutional constraints on taxation authority.

The Stakes: Democracy vs. Imperial Presidency—Lessons from 1776

Trump's response to the court ruling was predictable: He immediately announced plans to appeal to the Supreme Court.

This case will determine whether "no taxation without representation" still means anything in American democracy, or whether we're headed back to the same imperial system the Founders fought to escape.

The colonial precedent is chilling. When King George III refused to acknowledge colonial grievances about taxation, when Parliament dismissed American petitions for representation, when the Crown insisted on its absolute right to tax distant subjects—the result was revolution.

The modern parallel should terrify anyone who values democracy. When Trump refuses to acknowledge congressional authority over taxation, when his administration dismisses constitutional constraints on emergency powers, when the executive insists on its absolute right to tax American families—we're recreating the exact conditions that made the American Revolution inevitable

Think about the precedent if Trump wins at the Supreme Court:

  • Any president could impose comprehensive taxation systems by declaring economic competition a national security threat (just like King George declared colonial resistance a threat to royal authority)

  • Congress would become irrelevant to fiscal policy during any claimed "emergency" (just like colonial assemblies became irrelevant to British taxation policy)

  • Families would have no democratic recourse against unilateral tax increases (just like colonists had no recourse against parliamentary taxation)

  • The principle that launched American independence would become meaningless (just like it was meaningless under British rule)

The difference? King George's ministers at least had to sail across an ocean to ignore colonial voices. Trump's people can ignore American families from the comfort of Mar-a-Lago.

The Bottom Line: Constitutional Principles Matter

The colonists didn't rebel over the amount of the tea tax. They rebelled over the principle: No government has the right to tax people who have no voice in taxation decisions.

Today's stakes are even higher because the economic coercion is more complete. Families can't boycott the necessities Trump has chosen to tax.

They're trapped by economic necessity into paying whatever additional costs presidential "emergencies" impose. That makes democratic oversight of taxation more critical, not less.

Trump's "national security" rhetoric can't disguise what's really happening: An American president is imposing taxation without representation on American families.

The Federal Appeals Court recognized this constitutional violation and struck it down. The Supreme Court needs to do the same.

Because if presidents can bypass Congress on taxation by simply claiming "national security," then the phrase that launched American independence becomes just a quaint historical curiosity.

And American families become as powerless against unilateral taxation as colonists were under King George III.

The only difference? Colonists could dump the tea and walk away. Modern families are stuck paying whatever the president decides to charge.

The ultimate historical irony? This constitutional crisis is being analyzed from a country largely built by Americans who supported King George III's right to tax without representation. Canada's United Empire Loyalist founders chose monarchy over republicanism—yet 250 years later, their descendants understand American constitutional principles better than the American president violating them.

When Canadians—descendants of the Crown's most loyal subjects—are defending "no taxation without representation" against an American leader, American democracy has truly lost its way.

Is Trump's use of "national security" claims to bypass Congress on taxation a return to the colonial tyranny that sparked the American Revolution? And should the Supreme Court protect the principle of "no taxation without representation" that founded our democracy?
