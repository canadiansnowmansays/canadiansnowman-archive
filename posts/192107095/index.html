The Jevons Illusion

Part 20: When a Real Paradox Becomes a Policy Excuse

March 31, 2026

A Canadian’s notes on AI governance

There is a phrase circulating in Silicon Valley boardrooms and technology keynotes that sounds like economics but functions as theology: Jevons paradox. If you haven’t heard it yet, you will. Searches for the term spiked from near-zero to peak interest overnight in January 2025 when Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella tweeted it in response to the DeepSeek panic — essentially telling investors: cheaper AI means more AI, not less, so relax. Sam Altman has leaned on the same logic. He has publicly stated that the cost to use a given level of AI falls about 10x every twelve months, and that lower prices lead to much more use. The paradox is now doing double duty: it’s simultaneously a market reassurance and a governance deflection. Trust the paradox. Don’t regulate. Let us handle it.

Last week, The Atlantic published a fine piece by Annie Lowrey asking whether your job will exist in five years, framed around an elegant horse-and-coal metaphor. Horses got displaced permanently; coal got more valuable the more efficient its combustion became. Will my job even exist in five years? Pretty much every white-collar worker is asking themselves that question. Lowrey’s answer is careful and fair: it depends on whether AI is a substitute for your work or a complement to it. She is right about that. But she stops just short of the argument that matters most — the one this series has been making for nineteen parts.

The Jevons Paradox is real. The problem is what Silicon Valley is doing with it.

The Paradox, Briefly

William Stanley Jevons published The Coal Question in 1865. His insight was counterintuitive: as steam engines became more efficient at burning coal, he expected demand to fall. Instead, it soared. Efficiency didn’t conserve the resource. It made the resource economical for applications that had never previously been viable, which generated entirely new categories of demand. The more efficient the engine, the more coal England burned.

One hundred and sixty years later, the same paradox is playing out in artificial intelligence. The cost of AI inference has dropped roughly 92% since early 2023. Rather than shrinking the market for intelligence, that collapse has detonated an explosion of demand. This is not a controversial claim. The macro data support it: U.S. unemployment is sitting at 4.28%, AI capital expenditure is running at roughly $650 billion — about 2% of GDP — and software engineer job postings ended 2025 growing significantly faster than overall postings. By the paradox’s logic, the cheaper AI gets, the more of it — and of the humans working alongside it — we will need.

GitHub reported 43 million pull requests merged in 2025, up 23% year over year, with nearly one billion commits, up 25%. The Apple App Store saw 557,000 new apps, a 24% jump. If AI is replacing developers, it’s doing a terrible job.

So far, so reassuring. Now here is what no one in the keynote circuit is telling you.

What the Paradox Does Not Say

The Jevons Paradox is a statement about volume. It says nothing whatsoever about distribution.

England burned more coal after Watt’s steam engine improvements. Jevons documented that clearly. But Jevons did not tell you who owned the mines. He did not tell you what a miner was paid, whether he had a union, whether the black lung his family acquired was anybody’s legal problem, or whether the prosperity generated by all that efficient combustion reached the people who dug it out of the earth. The paradox is distribution-neutral. It always has been.

This is the sleight of hand that a recent academic paper on arxiv identified with admirable precision. The use of Jevons’ Paradox by industry leaders — a statement about price elasticity — could be viewed as a sleight of hand: a more indirect way to signal an AI capable of substantial displacement of human labor, a potentially less popular statement. In other words: when Satya Nadella tweets “Jevons paradox strikes again!” he is telling you that demand for AI will keep growing. He is not telling you anything about who captures the gains from that growth. Those are two entirely different conversations, and the first one is being used to avoid the second.

Companies including Microsoft, Amazon, and Salesforce have cited AI adoption as the reason behind over 50,000 job cuts in the United States in 2025. The same companies invoking the paradox as job-creation gospel are simultaneously using it as cover for mass displacement. More software will be written. Who writes it, at whose wages, under whose terms, with whose equity — those questions don’t appear in Jevons’ equation.

The Timing Problem the Paradox Ignores

There is a second silence in the standard Jevons argument, and it is where real human lives fall into the gap. Job losses tend to be sharp and localized, while Jevons-style job creation unfolds slowly and unevenly, often in different regions and occupations. That gap is where real pain lives.

This is not an academic quibble. It is the story of Youngstown, Ohio. It is the story of Dayton and Muncie and the communities The Atlantic itself has documented over decades of de-industrialization coverage. The aggregate employment number eventually recovers. The specific worker in the specific town who was replaced at fifty-two years old does not recover on the same timeline as the macroeconomic chart. Jevons tells you about the destination. It tells you nothing about who survives the journey.

Employment in “computer programmer” roles has fallen by roughly 27% since 2023, taking it to the lowest level since around 1980. At the same time, “software developer” employment is only marginally down and projected to grow about 18% across this decade. This split is exactly what the paradox predicts — the narrow, automatable task disappears while the higher-leverage role expands. But the person who held the automatable job is not automatically the person who fills the higher-leverage one. That transition requires retraining, relocation, time, and institutional support. None of those appear in the paradox. All of them require governance.

Inference costs have fallen roughly 92% since 2023. Instead of shrinking the market for intelligence, this has detonated an explosion of new applications and demand—exactly as Jevons described. Source: AI data center buildout visualization.

The Radiologist Problem, Revisited

The Atlantic piece cites the radiologist case — in 2016, the Nobel laureate Geoffrey Hinton argued that we “should stop training radiologists” because software would soon render them obsolete. But improvements in medical imaging unlocked new use cases for CTs and MRIs; patients demanded, and doctors ordered, more tests, and radiologists were the doctors administering and interpreting them. This is the Jevons story as Silicon Valley tells it: threat predicted, threat failed to materialize, paradox vindicated, everyone relax.

AI was once predicted to make radiologists obsolete. Instead, better imaging created more tests and more work for doctors—because FDA regulation gave humans time to adapt rather than face pure substitution. Source: Radiologist reviewing medical scans in clinical practice.

What Lowrey’s article doesn’t quite say — and what the standard Jevons invocation never quite says — is why the radiologist prediction failed. It wasn’t purely market dynamics. Medical-imaging software needs to go through the notoriously challenging FDA review process, slowing down its deployment and raising costs. Radiologists were protected, in part, by regulation. The paradox worked in their favor, yes — but it was governed deployment, with institutional friction built in, that gave the paradox time to operate as complement rather than substitute.

Remove the FDA process. Deploy freely. The outcome is not obviously the same.

This is the Asimov Gap in labor-market form. The rules that governed the transition — that gave humans time to adapt, that prevented pure substitution — were external constraints on the technology, not features of the paradox itself. And right now, the same voices invoking Jevons to reassure workers are simultaneously lobbying against the regulatory frameworks that made the radiologist story turn out the way it did.

Jevons as Governance Avoidance

For nineteen essays, this series has tracked the same recurring pattern: a claim about inevitability deployed to prevent accountability. The Zeroth Law — I know what’s good for humanity better than humanity does — shows up in governance arguments, labor arguments, concentration-of-power arguments, and now in labor-market macroeconomics. Jevons is its latest costume.

The argument runs as follows: AI efficiency will generate more demand for human labor (true, probably, at the aggregate level). Therefore, regulation is unnecessary interference with a self-correcting system. Therefore, democratic oversight of AI deployment is solved by economics. Trust the paradox. Trust us.

Each link in that chain is either false or incomplete. The paradox says nothing about distribution. It says nothing about timing. It says nothing about who owns the infrastructure generating the efficiency gains. And it says nothing about what happens to the workers displaced in the gap between the old demand and the new — the gap that is, in practice, measured not in macroeconomic quarters but in human lifetimes.

The real question isn’t whether AI will kill jobs. It’s where AI acts as a substitute and where it acts as a complement that expands demand. In the complementary zones, Jevons-style dynamics dominate. But in the substitution zones, permanent losses are entirely possible alongside aggregate employment growth. That distinction — between zones of complement and zones of substitute — is precisely what democratic governance is supposed to adjudicate. Who decides which jobs fall into which category? Who manages the timing gap? Who ensures the gains from the paradox are distributed rather than captured?

Jevons doesn’t answer those questions. A Victorian economist writing about coal mine output in 1865 was not, in fact, designing twenty-first century AI labor policy. He was describing a phenomenon. We are responsible for what we do with it.

The Coal Mines in the Room

All of England’s industrial-scale coal mines are closed; its final coal-fired power station shut down in 2024. More coal was burned for 160 years—yet the mining towns did not smoothly transition. Source: Ratcliffe-on-Soar power station at closure, 2024.

There is one final irony worth naming, because this series has always valued naming things precisely.

The Atlantic article ends with England’s coal industry: all of England’s industrial-scale coal mines have closed. Its last coal-fired power plant closed in 2024. Today, the country uses as much coal as it used in 1666, when the most common profession was peasant. Jevons was right that demand for coal would keep rising as long as the engines kept improving. He was wrong that England would run out of coal, because he underestimated global supply. And he could not have predicted oil.

The coal miners of Yorkshire and Wales did not benefit from Jevons being right. More coal was burned. The mines eventually closed. The communities built around them did not smoothly transition into the next growth sector. They are still, in some cases, waiting.

That is the story Silicon Valley’s version of Jevons doesn’t tell. More software will be written. More AI will be deployed. More cognition will be commoditized and more new applications will emerge. The paradox will likely hold at the aggregate level. And if we leave governance to the paradox — if we accept “Jevons says it’ll be fine” as a substitute for democratic oversight of who owns the infrastructure, who captures the gains, and who manages the displacement gap — we will produce, at scale, the twenty-first century equivalent of the mining towns that Jevons himself never thought to mention.

The Asimov Gap is not just about autonomous weapons and surveillance architectures. It is about whether the rules governing this transition are written by the people experiencing it, or imposed by the people profiting from it.

The paradox is real. The governance still isn’t.
