The Iran Crisis

The Nuclear Incentive

Why Iran’s Next Move Might Be a Bomb

January 15, 2026

Part 7 of The Iran Crisis Series

Three countries are central to understanding Iran’s nuclear calculations in January 2026:

Venezuela: Lacked nuclear weapons. President captured by U.S. forces. Country now under effective American control.

North Korea: Possesses nuclear weapons. Kim Jong Un still in power. Trump meets with him, threatens him, but never invades him.

Trump-Kim summit handshake

Iran: Currently at nuclear threshold—could build weapons quickly if it chose, but hasn’t yet.

The lesson is brutally simple: Nuclear weapons provide genuine deterrence against American military intervention. Without them, you’re vulnerable. With them, you’re untouchable.

This is the perverse incentive Trump’s Venezuela operation has created for Iran—and potentially for every country that fears American intervention. As Ellie Geranmayeh of the European Council on Foreign Relations warned: “Iran’s regime, if it survives these protests, may be more incentivized to obtain [nuclear weapons] than ever before.”

This essay examines why Venezuela might ultimately push Iran toward the very outcome Trump claims he wants to prevent: an Iranian nuclear bomb.

The Lesson Trump Taught

On January 3, 2026, the United States demonstrated that overwhelming military superiority allows regime change even in heavily-defended countries. Delta Force breached Maduro’s compound, killed 80+ defenders, extracted the president within hours, and established effective American control over Venezuela’s oil.

International law didn’t matter. UN condemnation didn’t matter. Russian and Chinese alliances didn’t matter. Sanctions and diplomatic isolation didn’t deter Trump. Military force succeeded completely.

But notice what Trump hasn’t done: invade North Korea.

North Korea is everything Venezuela is—authoritarian, internationally isolated, sanctioned, economically dysfunctional, led by a leader Trump personally despises—plus one thing: nuclear weapons.

Trump threatened Kim Jong Un with “fire and fury.” He called him “Little Rocket Man.” He said North Korea would face “total destruction” if it threatened the United States. These threats were as dramatic as any Trump has made against Venezuela or Iran.

But when it came to actual military action? Nothing. Trump eventually met with Kim, posed for photos, declared their relationship “very special,” then achieved zero substantive results. North Korea still has nuclear weapons—more now than when Trump took office—and Kim remains in power.

The contrast is stark:

Venezuela (no nukes): Invaded, president captured, effective U.S. control established

North Korea (has nukes): Threatened but not attacked, leader still in power, nuclear arsenal expanded

This is not lost on Iran’s leadership. Nor on any other country worried about American intervention.

Iran’s Nuclear Timeline: Closer Than You Think

Map of Iran’s nuclear sites

Iran doesn’t have nuclear weapons today. But it’s closer than most people realize.

According to U.N. nuclear watchdog reports and Western intelligence assessments:

Enriched uranium stockpile: Iran has enriched uranium to 60% purity—significantly higher than the 3.67% limit in the 2015 nuclear deal, and close to the 90% needed for weapons-grade material.

Technical knowledge: Iran has mastered the complete nuclear fuel cycle. It knows how to enrich uranium, build centrifuges, design warheads, and create delivery systems.

Breakout time: The time Iran would need to produce enough weapons-grade uranium for one nuclear bomb—sometimes called “breakout time”—has shrunk dramatically. Estimates range from weeks to a few months, depending on how many centrifuges Iran dedicates to the task.

Delivery systems: Iran has ballistic missiles capable of reaching Israel, U.S. bases in the region, and potentially parts of Europe. The missiles exist. They just don’t carry nuclear warheads yet.

What Iran lacks: The final weaponization—actually building a nuclear warhead small enough to fit on a missile and reliable enough to detonate properly. This requires testing and engineering that Iran hasn’t completed (or at least hasn’t been detected completing).

So Iran is what nonproliferation experts call “nuclear threshold”—technically capable of producing nuclear weapons relatively quickly, but choosing not to cross that final line.

The question is: How long will they choose not to?

June 2025: Why the Israel-U.S. Strikes Might Backfire

Damaged key Iranian nuclear site

In June 2025, Israel and the United States conducted a 12-day military campaign against Iran that destroyed much of its nuclear infrastructure. Enrichment facilities were bombed. Centrifuge production was disrupted. Research centers were damaged. Scientif programs were set back years.

At the time, this looked like success—Iran’s nuclear program had been significantly degraded.

But here’s the problem: You can destroy facilities. You cannot destroy knowledge.

Iran knows how to build centrifuges—the blueprints remain. Iranian scientists know how to enrich uranium—the expertise remains. Iranian engineers know how to design warheads—the technical understanding remains.

Physical infrastructure can be rebuilt. Knowledge can’t be bombed away.

So the June strikes achieved temporary setback but permanent incentive. Iran now knows:

  1. Its conventional deterrence failed (it couldn’t prevent the strikes)

  2. Its facilities are vulnerable to attack

  3. Only nuclear weapons provide true security

  4. International community will tolerate strikes on Iran’s nuclear program

The rational response to these lessons: Build nuclear weapons faster and better concealed next time.

This is the paradox of preventive strikes against nuclear programs. They’re sold as preventing nuclear weapons acquisition. But they often accelerate it by demonstrating to the target country that conventional deterrence doesn’t work.

The North Korea Precedent: Why Deterrence Works

North Korean ballistic missile launch

North Korea proves nuclear weapons provide genuine security against American intervention.

Pre-nuclear North Korea (1950-2006): Fought war with U.S. (1950-53), faced constant military threats, survived through Chinese support and conventional deterrence.

Nuclear-armed North Korea (2006-present): Possesses estimated 30-40 nuclear warheads, faces threats but no actual invasion, leaders remain secure.

Trump’s entire North Korea approach validated this:

2017: “Fire and fury” threats, military buildups, talk of preventive war 2018: Sudden pivot to diplomacy, Singapore summit, no military action 2019: Hanoi summit fails, negotiations end, no military action 2020-2026: Stalemate continues, no military action

Why no military action despite Trump’s clear frustration with North Korea? Because the costs would be catastrophic:

  • Seoul, a city of 10 million, is within artillery range of North Korea

  • North Korea could launch nuclear strikes on South Korea, Japan, possibly U.S. bases

  • China would likely intervene to prevent U.S. occupation of North Korea

  • Casualties would be in the hundreds of thousands or millions

  • No guarantee of successful regime change even if U.S. “wins”

So Trump rants, threatens, tweets, but doesn’t invade. Nuclear weapons work.

Iran’s leadership has studied this closely. Ali Larijani, Iran’s top security official, has referenced North Korea’s nuclear deterrent in multiple speeches. The message: This is how you make yourself invasion-proof.

The Venezuela Lesson: Without Nukes, You’re Vulnerable

Then came January 3, 2026, and Venezuela.

Everything Iranian leaders believed about conventional deterrence was proven wrong:

Myth: A country with strong military can resist U.S. invasion Reality: Delta Force breached heavily-fortified compound in hours

Myth: International law prevents such actions Reality: U.S. ignored international law completely

Myth: Russia and China will defend allies Reality: Moscow and Beijing did nothing

Myth: Economic leverage (oil) provides protection Reality: U.S. seized oil revenue streams through naval blockade

Venezuela had everything Iran has except one thing: nuclear weapons. And Venezuela’s president sits in a Miami jail.

This is the calculus shift. Before Venezuela, Iranian leaders could tell themselves: “Our missile arsenal, our regional proxies, our strategic depth—these deter U.S. invasion.”

After Venezuela: “Only nuclear weapons guarantee security.”

The Survival Calculation: What If the Regime Survives?

Everything discussed above assumes the Islamic Republic survives the current protests. That’s not guaranteed—as analyzed in Part 5, Iran meets most conditions for revolution.

But what if the regime does survive? What if the IRGC crushes protests through overwhelming violence? What if Trump doesn’t intervene? What if the opposition fractures?

Then Iran’s government faces a choice—though the debate is more complex than often presented:

The Hardliners’ Case for Weaponization:

Option A (Hardline Position): Race to build nuclear weapons as quickly as possible, secretly if necessary Logic: Only nuclear weapons guarantee security against U.S. intervention Risk: Triggers immediate military strikes before weapons are operational Support: IRGC commanders, conservative clerics, those who see Venezuela as proof conventional deterrence fails

The Moderates’ Counter-Argument:

Option B (Moderate/Pragmatist Position): Return to nuclear negotiations, accept intrusive inspections, maintain nuclear threshold status but not weaponize Logic: Weaponization confirms Western accusations, triggers strikes, accelerates isolation, doesn’t solve underlying problems Risk: Remain vulnerable to future U.S. intervention Support: Technocrats, business interests, reformist politicians, those who see weaponization as economic suicide

Option C (Ambiguity Position): Covertly build weapons without testing, maintain ambiguity about whether you have them Logic: Provides some deterrence without triggering immediate response Risk: Doesn’t provide reliable deterrence if adversaries don’t believe you have working weapons Support: Those seeking middle ground between hardline and moderate positions

The internal debate is genuine. According to Iran analysts with contacts in Tehran’s policy circles, the regime is not monolithically pro-weaponization despite Venezuela’s lesson. Key fault lines:

Economic pragmatists argue: “We’re already sanctioned to maximum. But nuclear weapons bring additional consequences—total economic isolation, Arab nuclear arms race, loss of remaining Chinese/Russian support, domestic resource drain. Our economic crisis gets worse, not better.”

IRGC hardliners respond: “And what’s your alternative? Hope Trump doesn’t invade? Hope the next U.S. president is nicer? Venezuela proved: without nukes, you’re vulnerable. Period. Economic pain is temporary. Losing sovereignty is permanent.”

Clerical establishment is divided: Some see nuclear weapons as prohibited by Islamic law (Khamenei issued fatwa against nuclear weapons in 2003). Others argue defensive weapons are permissible when faced with existential threat.

Which faction wins depends partly on how this crisis resolves. If the regime survives through brutal repression and faces continued U.S. threats, hardliners gain leverage: “See? They want regime change. Only nukes protect us.” If protests succeed and a new government emerges, the question becomes moot.

The Technical Challenge: Can Iran Build Weapons Quickly?

The question isn’t whether Iran could eventually build nuclear weapons—most experts agree it could. The question is: How quickly?

Optimistic scenario (for Iran):

  • Rebuild enrichment capacity within 12-18 months

  • Produce weapons-grade uranium within months after that

  • Build deliverable warhead within additional 6-12 months

  • Total time: 2-3 years from decision to operational weapon

Pessimistic scenario:

  • June 2025 strikes set program back 3-5 years

  • International monitoring makes covert progress difficult

  • Technical challenges in weaponization take longer than expected

  • Total time: 5+ years from decision to operational weapon

But either way, Iran could have nuclear weapons before the end of this decade if it commits fully to that path.

The question is: Will it?

The Hardliners’ Case for Going Nuclear

Within Iran’s government, hardliners—especially in the IRGC—have always argued for nuclear weapons. Their case is stronger now than ever:

1. Venezuela proved conventional deterrence fails. No matter how many missiles, how much military capability, how many regional proxies—without nukes, you’re vulnerable to U.S. invasion.

2. The June 2025 strikes proved Iran’s nuclear facilities are vulnerable. Can’t protect civilian nuclear program from attack. So why maintain civilian program? Better to weaponize and hide weapons.

3. Negotiations are pointless. The U.S. demands Iran give up everything—enrichment, missiles, proxies. Even if Iran agrees, it gets nothing but temporary sanctions relief. Better to have weapons than paper promises.

4. North Korea proves it works. Kim has nukes, Kim stays in power, Trump doesn’t invade. It’s that simple.

5. If regime survives protests, it proves violence works. If IRGC can crush protests through massacre, hardliners gain political power. And hardliners want nukes.

6. Regional environment is permissive. With Syria, Lebanon, Gaza all in chaos, Iran has room to maneuver. International attention is divided. Covert nuclear progress is easier.

7. Trump will not be president forever. If they can survive his term and build weapons before a new administration, they’re secure permanently.

This is a powerful case. After Venezuela, it’s likely winning the internal Iranian debate.

The Moderates’ Case Against Going Nuclear

Iran does have moderates and pragmatists who oppose weaponization. Their case:

1. Confirms Western accusations. For years, Iran has insisted its nuclear program is purely civilian. Weaponizing proves the West was right to distrust Iran, undermines any international support.

2. Triggers immediate strikes. The moment intelligence suggests Iran is building weapons, U.S./Israel will strike. Race to get weapons before being struck is unwinnable.

3. No regional support. Arab countries fear Iranian nuclear weapons. Creates incentive for regional nuclear arms race (Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Turkey all might seek weapons).

4. China won’t help. Beijing doesn’t want nuclear proliferation. Won’t provide technological assistance for weaponization.

5. Economic costs catastrophic. Already sanctioned to maximum. But nuclear weapons bring additional sanctions, isolation, economic warfare.

6. Doesn’t solve underlying problems. Iran’s economic crisis, protests, legitimacy deficit—nuclear weapons don’t fix any of this. Might even worsen it by diverting resources to weapons program.

This case is weaker after Venezuela. Moderates sound like they’re saying: “Stay vulnerable, hope for best, trust international law.” After January 3, that sounds naive.

The International Dilemma: How to Prevent Weaponization

The international community faces a dilemma:

Military force to prevent weapons:

  • Destroys facilities temporarily

  • Creates incentive to rebuild faster

  • Doesn’t eliminate knowledge

  • Might trigger decision to weaponize covertly

Negotiations to prevent weapons:

  • Require Iran to accept intrusive inspections

  • Iran likely won’t agree to terms after Venezuela

  • Even if deal reached, verification is difficult

  • Iran could cheat (like North Korea did)

Sanctions to pressure Iran:

  • Iran already maximally sanctioned

  • Sanctions haven’t stopped nuclear progress so far

  • Might accelerate program by demonstrating need for deterrence

Deterrence threats:

  • “If you build nukes, we’ll attack”

  • But what if Iran builds weapons before detection?

  • And what if attacks fail to destroy hidden facilities?

There’s no good option. Every approach has failed elsewhere or has obvious vulnerabilities.

The brutal truth: If Iran decides to build nuclear weapons, international community probably cannot stop it without full-scale war. And full-scale war carries costs (casualties, regional instability, oil disruption) that no one wants to pay.

The Israel Factor: Will Netanyahu Strike Again?

Israel has the most to lose from Iranian nuclear weapons. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has spent decades warning about Iranian nuclear program.

In June 2025, Israel conducted major strikes with U.S. support. Netanyahu might argue for doing it again if Iran rebuilds.

But Israel faces constraints:

Military: June strikes required U.S. support (refueling, targeting intelligence, defensive systems). Will Trump provide that support indefinitely?

Political: Israeli public war-weary after Gaza, Lebanon, Syria conflicts. Appetite for another major war limited.

Effectiveness: June strikes didn’t stop program permanently. Another strike wouldn’t either. Facilities can be rebuilt, knowledge remains.

Risk: Iran might retaliate directly against Israel with remaining missiles. Risk/benefit calculation gets worse each time.

Netanyahu’s speech Sunday said Israel “closely monitoring” Iran’s protests and “supports their struggle for freedom.” This suggests hoping protests solve the problem rather than military strikes.

But if protests fail and Iran rebuilds nuclear program? Netanyahu will face pressure to strike again. Which might accelerate Iran’s drive to weaponize.

The Political Opposition: “America First” vs. Middle East Intervention (January 13 Update)

Trump faces growing political pressure from his own base against Iran intervention.

Stephen K. Bannon, longtime Trump adviser and influential voice in MAGA movement, publicly argued against strikes on his popular online talk show Monday:

“We don’t care about making Iran great again. Israel and the United States in there, you’re just going to prolong the problem.”

This echoes the internal Republican divide that erupted after Trump’s June 2025 strikes on Iran’s nuclear facilities. Former Fox News host Tucker Carlson loudly criticized U.S. involvement then, prompting pushback from Trump. Carlson later apologized, and met with Trump for lunch at the White House on Friday.

Vice President JD Vance presents a more nuanced position. According to The Washington Post, Vance “supported Trump’s decision in June to bomb Iran’s main nuclear facilities but is wary of being dragged into a deeper Middle East entanglement,” said two people close to him who spoke anonymously.

The concern: Trump campaigned on “America First” foreign policy—bringing troops home, avoiding Middle East quagmires, focusing on domestic prosperity. Venezuela could be justified as Western Hemisphere security. But Iran?

As one person close to the White House told The Post: Trump’s current advisers, unlike those in his first term, are “less inclined to intervene in the Middle East.”

This political opposition creates constraint. If Trump strikes Iran and it goes badly—high casualties, protracted conflict, no clear victory—his base might revolt. The same supporters who cheered Venezuela as decisive action might condemn Iran as neoconservative overreach.

The “America First” dilemma: How do you square “Make Iran Great Again” (Trump’s mocking acronym: MIGA) with “America First”? How do you justify regime change in Tehran while criticizing regime change attempts in Iraq, Libya, Syria?

Bannon’s criticism cuts to this tension. Trump supporters don’t want endless Middle East wars. They want American strength used for American interests. Can Iran intervention be framed that way? Or does it look like exactly the foreign policy establishment adventurism Trump’s base rejected?

This political context helps explain why Trump sounds uncertain privately despite confident public rhetoric. He’s committed publicly (”help is on its way”), but faces internal opposition from allies who define “America First” as staying out of Middle Eastern conflicts.

Conclusion: The Unintended Consequence (Updated January 13, 2026)

President Trump invaded Venezuela to demonstrate American power. He wanted to send a message: Accommodate or face destruction.

But demonstrations of power send messages to multiple audiences. And sometimes the message received is not the message intended.

One possible message Iran might draw: Only nuclear weapons provide security.

This is a potential irony. Trump’s Venezuela operation—sold partly as demonstrating resolve against rogue regimes, with Iran specifically mentioned as another possible target—could ultimately accelerate Iran’s drive to become a nuclear power.

But weaponization is not inevitable. Several factors could prevent it:

  • Regime collapse: If protests succeed, new government might abandon nuclear ambitions

  • Economic constraints: Iran’s fiscal crisis makes weapons program expensive

  • Technical challenges: Rebuilding after June 2025 strikes takes time, covert progress is difficult

  • Moderate faction victory: Pragmatists might prevail in internal debate

  • Deterrence working: Trump’s threats might keep Iran below nuclear threshold without invasion

  • Diplomatic breakthrough: Negotiations might produce acceptable compromise

What Venezuela definitely changed: The risk calculation. Before January 3, Iran could tell itself conventional deterrence worked. After Venezuela, that confidence is shaken. Whether shaken confidence leads to weaponization or other responses remains uncertain.

January 13 Update: Trump just raised the stakes dramatically. On Tuesday morning, he posted on Truth Social: “I have cancelled all meetings with Iranian Officials until the senseless killing of protesters STOPS. HELP IS ON ITS WAY.”

This escalation fundamentally changes Iran’s nuclear calculus:

If the regime survives Trump’s promised “help” - whether that’s strikes, cyber attacks, or other intervention - the hardliners’ argument becomes overwhelming: “We survived only because Trump hesitated. Next time we need nuclear weapons to make him hesitate permanently.”

If the regime falls, the nuclear question becomes moot - at least temporarily. A new government might abandon weaponization. But it might also conclude the opposite lesson: “The Islamic Republic fell because it lacked credible deterrence. We won’t make that mistake.”

If Trump’s “help” never arrives - if it’s bluster rather than action - Iran learns a different lesson: “American threats are empty. We don’t need nuclear weapons because intervention won’t happen anyway.”

Each scenario produces different nuclear outcomes. But Trump’s cancellation of all diplomatic meetings narrows the paths available. There’s no longer a negotiation track that might prevent weaponization through compromise.

Before Venezuela: Iran at nuclear threshold, choosing not to weaponize, maintaining option value After Venezuela: Stronger incentive to weaponize, but internal debate continues, outcome undetermined After January 13: Diplomacy dead, intervention threatened, regime faces stark choice: weaponize or remain vulnerable

The paradox exists: Trump’s action intended to demonstrate American strength might accelerate nuclear proliferation by proving that only nuclear weapons reliably deter American intervention. But “might” is not “will.” The outcome depends on choices Iran’s leadership makes—choices shaped by Venezuela but not determined by it.

If Iran’s regime survives the current protests—and that’s still uncertain—the nuclear question will return with renewed urgency. Iranian hardliners will argue: “Venezuela proves we need weapons. North Korea proves weapons work. June 2025 proves our facilities are vulnerable. January 13 proves Trump won’t negotiate. We must build and hide them now.”

But moderates will counter-argue: “Weaponization confirms Western accusations, triggers immediate strikes, isolates us completely, and doesn’t solve our real problems—economic collapse, legitimacy deficit, popular opposition.”

Which argument prevails will determine whether Trump’s Venezuela demonstration—and his January 13 escalation—ultimately made Iran safer to confront, or pushed it toward the very outcome he claims he wants to prevent.

The next days or weeks will tell us whether “help is on its way” means military strikes on Iran. And that answer will determine whether Iran’s next move is surrender, revolution, or racing to build nuclear weapons before the bombs fall.
