The Stars They Couldn’t Earn On Merit, Race, and the Purging of the American Officer Corps

A Postscript to The American Contradiction

March 27, 2026

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has said repeatedly that he is determined to change a Pentagon culture corrupted by “foolish,” “reckless” and “woke” leaders from previous administrations.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Last August, I concluded The American Contradiction — my ten-part examination of how anti-”woke” politics betrays America’s own foundational promise — with a hopeful, if cautious, assessment. The constitutional system was under pressure, I wrote, but the principles remained. History showed, consistently, that exclusion weakens rather than strengthens. America would eventually recognize the cost.

Seven months later, I owe you a postscript.

Because Pete Hegseth has been systematically purging decorated officers from the United States military — and the evidence now suggests the primary criteria are race and gender, not readiness.

Case One: The Army List

Four officers have been struck from an Army promotion list by Hegseth — a list selected by a board of generals after a rigorous, competitive process in which only five percent of eligible colonels make general. Two of the four are Black. Two are women. The list itself consists of approximately three dozen candidates, most of them white men.

Army Secretary Daniel Driscoll — a Trump appointee — refused to remove their names for months, citing their decades of exemplary service. Hegseth overrode him.

The charges against the individual officers illuminate the logic at work. One Black armor officer and combat veteran was flagged not for poor performance, but for a paper he wrote nearly fifteen years earlier analyzing why African American officers historically gravitated toward support roles over frontline combat positions. He wrote about race in the military. That is now disqualifying.

A female logistics officer was targeted because she served in Afghanistan during the 2021 withdrawal. She performed her job well under catastrophic conditions not of her making. Her reward is removal from a list the Defense Secretary may not even have the legal authority to alter.

The other two have no publicly stated rationale at all.

And then there is Colonel Dave Butler — the spokesman for General Mark Milley, whom Trump loathes. Butler, a white man, resigned voluntarily in February hoping his sacrifice would convince Hegseth to send the list forward. He gave up his career to protect his colleagues.

It didn’t work.

The episode that stops me cold is not the promotion dispute itself. It is what Hegseth’s chief of staff, Ricky Buria, reportedly said to Driscoll about a separate appointment — that of Maj. Gen. Antoinette R. Gant, a combat engineer who served in Iraq and Afghanistan, to command the Military District of Washington.

Buria told Driscoll that President Trump would not want to stand next to a Black female officer at military events.

Maj. Gen. Antoinette R. Gant (far right) stands with President Donald Trump, Vice President JD Vance, and Veterans Affairs Secretary Doug Collins during the Veterans Day wreath-laying ceremony at the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, Arlington National Cemetery, November 11, 2025. Source: USA Today photo gallery (Daniel Torok / White House via official coverage)

Driscoll pushed back. He raised it with a senior White House official, who agreed with his assessment. Hegseth’s office retreated. General Gant was promoted to two-star rank and accompanied the President at Arlington Cemetery on Veterans Day.

Buria has called the account “completely false.” The Pentagon has not addressed the underlying promotion dispute at all.

I will let you draw your own conclusions about who is telling the truth.

Case Two: The SEAL Who Was the Best Man for the Job

The Army story does not stand alone.

In July 2025, a Navy captain was set to become the first woman ever to lead a Naval Special Warfare command overseeing Navy SEALs. She had been ranked the top officer for promotion in her cohort. She had earned a Purple Heart after being injured by an IED in Iraq. She had served as the first female troop commander with SEAL Team Six. She competed in Ironman races in her spare time and could perform twenty-five pound weighted pull-ups. A broad panel of the most elite SEAL leaders had selected her. Invitations for her change-of-command ceremony in San Diego had already gone out.

Two weeks before the ceremony, the Pentagon canceled it — not through formal channels, but through a series of phone calls specifically designed to avoid leaving a paper trail.

Under the Navy’s up-or-out promotion policy, losing a key billet at the wrong moment can effectively end a career. Hers did.

The reaction from the SEAL community — combat veterans, many of them Trump voters — was unambiguous. “She was the best man for the job,” one retired SEAL told CNN. “There is absolutely no DEI.” Another special operations source was blunter: “It pisses me off because it is clearly someone who is capable and has done extraordinary things and is being punished because of — weak-ass men.”

The Pentagon’s official explanation was that she wasn’t herself a SEAL. The SEALs who served with her rejected that explanation. A broad panel of SEAL leadership had chosen her. If the role were simply being restructured, the military wouldn’t have revoked an incoming officer’s orders days before she was due to take command.

The deliberate avoidance of a paper trail is worth pausing on. This was not an administrative error or a policy disagreement processed through normal channels. Someone made phone calls instead of filing paperwork. That is not incompetence. That is architecture.

The Culture Cascades Down

What happens at the top does not stay at the top.

After Hegseth’s speech at Marine Corps Base Quantico — in which he declared that the work of remaking the officer corps was “just beginning” — a male noncommissioned officer in one Army unit turned to a female soldier and said: “All you women are getting out now.”

Her response: “I want nothing to do with the military after this.”

A retired Coast Guard commander who had served on an advisory panel for women in the military — before that panel was shut down — put it plainly: “I am fearful for women in uniform right now. I definitely think there will be a retention issue for women. I also think that you’re going to see an increase in assaults, increase in harassment, increase in bullying, hazing — and a lack of accountability for those things.”

This is the downstream consequence of signals sent from the top. Hegseth’s own written words confirmed the design. In his book, he argued that the way to attract more recruits was to portray fewer Black people and women in recruitment advertising and focus efforts instead on predominantly white small towns. “Across America, from small town to small town,” he wrote, “there are still hundreds of thousands of patriotic, strong, manly men ripe for recruitment.”

That is not a policy position. It is a demographic preference for who the American military should be.

What History Says

In every part of The American Contradiction, I traced the same arc: a targeted group is deemed unfit, unreliable, or simply unwanted. The justifications change across generations. The mechanism doesn’t.

The American military has historically been one of the nation’s most powerful engines of integration — not because the institution was virtuous, but because competence, eventually, forced the issue. The Tuskegee Airmen flew. The Buffalo Soldiers served. Truman’s 1948 desegregation order was, in part, a recognition that segregation was a strategic liability. The modern officer corps — diverse, professional, combat-tested — is the product of that long, imperfect arc.

Hegseth has now dismantled the Command Assessment Program, which used double-blind interviews to ensure qualified officers of any background could compete for top positions. He replaced the oversight process with Anthony Tata — a retired general who once called Barack Obama a “terrorist leader” — and Stuart Scheller, who pleaded guilty to dereliction of duty for publicly excoriating the military leadership during the Afghanistan withdrawal.

The people now deciding who leads the United States Army include a man convicted of dereliction of duty and a man who called a former president a terrorist.

This is what “restoring meritocracy” looks like in practice.

The four Army officers struck from the promotion list are decorated combat veterans, in most cases. They survived the most competitive promotion process in the American military. The Navy captain topped her entire cohort, bled for her country, and was chosen by the best warriors in the world to lead them.

They are being removed because of what they look like, or what they wrote about, or who they worked for.

The argument I made across ten essays was simple: America has always been strongest when it drew from its full talent pool, and weakest when it chose fear over capacity.

Nothing that has happened in the seven months since I published that final essay has complicated that argument.

It has confirmed it.

The American Contradiction ran for ten parts from June through August 2025. This postscript was written in March 2026.
