The Iran Crisis

“The Fragments”

Part XL: Day Ten: March 9, 2026

March 10, 2026

I. Mahyar Zanganeh

A still from security camera footage captured moments after an apparent strike on a communications tower seen at the center top of the image, near an elementary school in Abyek, a small city west of Tehran.Credit...Nima Orazani, via Instagram

The morning of February 28 at the Imam Reza Elementary School for Boys in Abyek, a small city in Qazvin Province west of Tehran, began like any other school morning. Security camera footage shows approximately 40 boys on the playground. Some wander. Others linger by the soccer goal. A large group gathers in a circle. It is ordinary and unremarkable — the footage of children being children, recorded and forgotten until it wasn’t.

This was hours after the first joint US-Israeli strikes on Iran had begun. Schools were still open.

Then the footage shows a large explosion at the top of the frame, where a communications tower stands on a hill above the school. The blast rips through the area. Windows shatter. Children run, hands over their ears. A child falls to the ground by the soccer goalpost, struck by debris. Iran’s Tasnim news agency identified the child as Mahyar Zanganeh. He did not survive.

The video was posted online Friday and verified by the New York Times. Using satellite imagery, the Times and a geolocation expert determined that the communications tower — less than 400 feet from the playground — was reduced to rubble. It appears to have been the intended target. No side has claimed responsibility for the strike.

This is the second school now documented on video from Day One. The first was the Shajarah Tayyebeh Elementary School for girls in Minab — 175 people reported killed, many of them children. A Tomahawk cruise missile was confirmed by verified video striking the naval base beside it. The Tomahawk is a US-only weapon. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, standing beside Trump at Dover on Saturday, said: “The only side that targets civilians is Iran.” The Tomahawk video was released the following day.

Two schools. Day One. Both now on the record.

Shiva Amelirad, the international representative of the Coordinating Council of Iranian Teachers’ Unions, spoke from Toronto. Her organization has active members in Qazvin Province. “Unfortunately contact has not been possible yet,” she said, “due to widespread internet disruptions across the country.” She cannot reach her members. The internet blackout that prevents Iranians from telling the world what is happening to them also prevents the teachers’ union representative in Toronto from finding out whether the people she works with are alive.

The union issued a public statement: targeting schools and hospitals “was not only a violation of fundamental humanitarian principles, but also a clear breach of international law and human rights conventions.” The US and Israeli militaries did not respond to requests for comment.

There is a question that now hangs over the entire first day of this war, and it will not go away: how many schools were open in Iran on the morning of February 28? How many were in proximity to targets? How many security cameras were running? How many videos exist that have not yet been posted, verified, or seen?

II. What Was Found on the Table

A government handout photograph showed weapon remnants displayed on a table near the ruins of the Shajarah Tayyebeh elementary school, where a precision strike reportedly killed 175 people, mostly children, on Feb. 28. The remnants have been identified by The Times as components of a modern, U.S.-made Tomahawk missile.Credit...IRIB, via Telegram

Iranian state broadcaster IRIB posted photographs Monday of missile fragments laid out on a table near the ruins of the Shajarah Tayyebeh Elementary School in Minab. The photographs were characterized as showing “the remains of the American missile that landed on the children of Minab school.” The New York Times visual investigations team analyzed the fragments.

What they found is now in the public record and cannot be walked back.

One fragment is marked SDL ANTENNA — a satellite data link antenna installed in modern versions of the Tomahawk missile. A Department of Defense contract number on the component indicates it was supplied to the US military as part of a 2014 order. The name of Ball Aerospace Technologies Corp., a weapons manufacturer based in Boulder, Colorado, acquired by BAE Systems in 2024, is imprinted on the part.

A second fragment is stamped “Made in USA” and bears the name of Globe Motors, an Ohio-based manufacturer that has received millions of dollars in Department of Defense contracts for actuator motors — the components that move the guidance fins that steer Tomahawk missiles. The Globe Motors component matches remnants photographed in Tomahawk missile attacks in Yemen and Syria, archived by the Open Source Munitions Portal, a database of weapon fragments from conflict zones. Trevor Ball of Bellingcat, a former US Army explosive ordnance disposal technician, identified the components as Tomahawk parts. He has identified similar remnants at other Iranian strike sites since Day One.

The serial numbers are American. The manufacturer is American. The contract year is American. The missile is American.

At his Monday news conference at Trump National Doral in Miami, the president said: “Iran also has some Tomahawks. As you know, numerous other nations have Tomahawks. They buy them from us.”

Iran has no Tomahawks. Three countries are known to have them: the United States, Australia, and Britain. Two more — Japan and the Netherlands — have agreed to purchase them but have not yet received them. Any country the US has sold Tomahawks to would require State Department authorization before transferring them to a third party. Iran lacks the technical equipment to program a Tomahawk’s flight path or upload targeting data into its onboard computer. Iran’s own cruise missiles are visually distinct from a Tomahawk — their gas turbine engines bulge from the tail in a way that makes identification straightforward even at a distance.

When pressed by a New York Times reporter about why no one in his administration has echoed his claim that Iran struck the school, the president said: “Because I just don’t know enough about it.”

And then: “Whatever the report shows, I’m willing to live with that.”

One hundred and seventy-five people were reported killed at that school. Most of them children.

III. What Was Dismantled Before the War

The fragments on the table in Minab did not appear in a vacuum. The Atlantic’s Missy Ryan has documented what happened to the Pentagon’s civilian protection apparatus in the year before this war began, and the documentation is its own kind of accountability record.

In early 2025, Trump’s transition team asked military officials to review and potentially close the Civilian Protection Center of Excellence — a unit created by law in 2023 to study why thousands of civilians died during the campaign against ISIS and to develop better methods for avoiding noncombatant deaths in future operations. The center could not be closed outright because it was created by statute. But the administration dramatically reduced its staff and fired or reassigned personnel focused on civilian harm prevention across the military.

The numbers: nearly 200 people working on civilian harm prevention across the military at its peak. Reduced by approximately 90 percent over the past year. At CENTCOM specifically — the command now running the Iran campaign — the number working full time on the issue was cut by two-thirds, leaving only a handful, with some reassigned to the Iran campaign after it began.

The administration also canceled a planned database of errant strikes developed at a cost of millions of dollars. It closed a State Department initiative tracking civilian deaths caused by weapons provided to allies. It repealed a White House policy that emphasized human rights in weapons transfers.

Hegseth’s public framing is on the record: “No stupid rules of engagement, no nation-building quagmire, no democracy-building exercise, no politically correct wars.” His 2024 book refers to military lawyers whose job includes reviewing strikes for legal compliance as “jagoffs.” He recounted telling his platoon in Iraq to disregard the rules of engagement.

One person familiar with the matter described the overriding priority in the Iran campaign as lethality. Protecting civilians “has been de-emphasized across the targeting and strike community.” The White House has released social media posts mixing video game imagery with footage of exploding Iranian targets. “No pause. No hesitation,” one post says, punctuated by an explosion emoji.

The Civilian Protection Center of Excellence existed to ask, before a strike, whether there was a school within 400 feet of the target. The staff that would have asked that question had been reduced by 90 percent before the first Tomahawk was launched. A naval base in Minab was struck. The school beside it was struck. The fragments on the table have serial numbers.

Annie Shiel of the Center for Civilians in Conflict: “Political decisions and rhetoric at the top are undermining this work. Civilians are ultimately paying the price.”

IV. The Day the Markets Were Managed

Monday was the day the world learned that oil prices can be moved by a phone interview.

In the morning, Trump called CBS reporter Weijia Jiang. The war, he said, was “very complete, pretty much.” He said the US was “very far ahead of schedule.” Oil prices dropped. Stock markets rose. The G7’s signal that they were monitoring but not yet releasing strategic reserves amplified the retreat. By afternoon, Brent crude had pulled back from Sunday night’s $119.50 peak to under $90. Markets breathed.

Then markets closed.

That evening, speaking to a gathering of Republican lawmakers in Florida, Trump said: “We have won in many ways, but not enough. We go forward more determined than ever to achieve ultimate victory that will end this long-running danger once and for all.”

At his news conference, asked if the war would be over this week: “No. Soon, very soon.”

The CBS interview moved markets. The Republican gathering and the press conference moved them back. The war itself had not changed in the interval. RBC Capital Markets had written Sunday that without a clear definition of winning, the conflict was impossible to forecast. Monday provided a definition of sorts: winning is whatever Trump says it is at the moment he is saying it, subject to revision within the same afternoon.

Susie Wiles, the White House chief of staff, is reportedly pressing advisers to find ways to keep gas prices down. The president said he would waive “certain oil-related sanctions to reduce prices.” Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent floated the possibility of “unsanctioning” Russian oil — rolling back sanctions on a country whose forces are actively sharing intelligence with Iran to help target American troops — as a mechanism for increasing global supply. The IEA’s 32 member countries hold 1.2 billion barrels in reserve, with another 600 million accessible from company holdings. The G7 decided not to release them yet. France’s finance minister: “We’re not there yet.” The Strait remains closed. US gas reached $3.48 per gallon — up 17 percent since Day One.

Iran’s Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi named the war from the other side. The US and Israel called their campaign “Operation Epic Fury.” Araghchi called it “Operation Epic Mistake.” He boasted about oil surging past $100 and added: “We, too, have many surprises in store.” The renaming is deliberate counter-messaging and it is effective. It does not need elaboration. The scoreboard is visible.

V. Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington

Army Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington, 26, of Glendale, Ky. Source: U.S. Army

JD Vance was at Dover Air Force Base Monday night when the seventh American killed in the war came home.

Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington was seriously injured March 1 in the Iranian strike on Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia. He died from his injuries days later while being prepared for transfer to a military hospital in Germany. He was named Monday.

He joins Maj. Jeffrey R. O’Brien, Capt. Cody A. Khork, CWO3 Robert M. Marzan, Sgt. 1st Class Nicole M. Amor, Sgt. 1st Class Noah L. Tietjens, and Sgt. Declan J. Coady — the six Army Reservists killed in Kuwait on March 1, whose remains were received at Dover by Trump on Saturday, the day he stood at the ceremony and blamed Iran for the school strike.

Seven Americans. Ten days.

VI. The NATO Problem Nobody Is Discussing

A Turkish soldier stands guard in a field as officials search for debris from the interception of a missile launched toward Turkey. Credit...Sertac Kayar/Reuters

On Monday, a second Iranian ballistic missile entered Turkish airspace and was shot down by NATO defenses. Debris fell near Gaziantep, in southern Turkey near the Syrian border. No casualties were reported. Iran denied it. Turkey’s President Erdogan called Iranian President Pezeshkian, who denied his country had fired the missiles. Two missiles have now entered NATO airspace. Both were denied.

The first Iranian missile entered Turkish airspace on March 4 — Day Five — aimed, according to US and Western officials, at Incirlik Air Base, where US Air Force and other NATO forces operate. Turkey shot it down. Iran denied it.

That is two Iranian ballistic missiles into the airspace of a NATO member state in six days.

Turkey is bound by Article 5 of the NATO treaty — the mutual defense clause that treats an attack on one member as an attack on all. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte said after the first missile that Iran was “close to becoming a threat to Europe” but did not activate the clause. He has not activated it after the second. NATO has now deployed a Patriot air defense battery — the United States’ most advanced ground-based missile defense system — to Malatya in eastern Turkey.

The threshold question has not been answered publicly. Two missiles into a NATO member. Both intercepted. The third, if it comes and hits, will force an answer nobody in Brussels, Ankara, or Washington appears to have prepared for publicly.

Meanwhile the State Department ordered mandatory departure of US diplomats and family members from the consulate in Adana — the city five miles from Incirlik. All consular services in Adana are suspended. The full mandatory departure map now covers Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Jordan, Bahrain, Qatar, the UAE, Lebanon, and southern Turkey. Eight mission areas. The US diplomatic footprint in the Middle East is being systematically evacuated.

Emmanuel Macron — speaking Monday from the deck of the Charles de Gaulle aircraft carrier in the Mediterranean — announced France would mobilize eight frigates and two helicopter carriers to the Eastern Mediterranean, Red Sea, and Strait of Hormuz. “This mobilization of our navy is unprecedented,” he said. France has already sent a warship and air defense to Cyprus after an Iranian drone crashed into a British base there. Standing beside the leaders of Cyprus and Greece, Macron said: “When Cyprus is attacked, it is Europe that is attacked.”

And then, the sentence that named the central strategic problem more directly than any American official has managed in ten days: “The duration depends on what the ultimate objectives are.”

Europe is now in this. Whether it intended to be or not.

VII. The Ukraine Loop

This is the sentence that deserves a moment of stillness: Ukraine has sent interceptor drones and a team of drone experts to protect US military bases in Jordan from Iranian Shahed drones.

Zelensky confirmed it Monday. The United States made the request Thursday. The Ukrainian team was en route. Eleven countries have now asked Ukraine for security support against the Shahed threat — countries neighboring Iran, European nations, and the United States itself.

The loop is worth tracing in full. Iran developed the Shahed drone and supplied it to Russia beginning in 2022 for use against Ukrainian cities, power plants, and civilian infrastructure. Ukraine spent two years developing interception techniques, electronic warfare systems, and expertise in defeating the exact weapon now being used against American positions across the Middle East. The United States, whose intelligence services helped arm the conflict that has deployed Shaheds against its own troops, is now asking the country that those Shaheds were used to destroy to help defend American soldiers against them.

Zelensky is not unaware of the leverage this creates. “Ukraine is ready to respond positively to requests from those who help us protect the lives of Ukrainians and the independence of Ukraine.” The offer is genuine and it is also a negotiation. Ukraine’s most valuable export right now is not grain or steel. It is the institutional knowledge of how to survive Iranian drones at scale.

Iran armed Russia. Russia used those arms against Ukraine. Ukraine learned to defeat those arms. America now needs Ukraine to defeat those same arms in Jordan. The war in the Middle East and the war in Ukraine are no longer parallel events. They are the same supply chain running in reverse.

VIII. The Black Rain

Smoke blacks out most of the sky at an oil storage in Tehran on Sunday. Some flames are still burning in the background.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

Health experts are now describing what is falling on Tehran.

After the oil depot strikes, residents reported black rain. An oily film covering cars and rooftops. Then migraines, dizziness, coughing. One resident: “It almost feels like tear gas in the air. The war has entered our throats.”

The black debris raining from the sky is composed of fine particles known as PM2.5, including black carbon. Burning oil also releases carcinogenic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and heavy metals including lead, arsenic, and mercury — all on the WHO’s top ten list of chemicals of public health concern. After the particles fall, many dangerous compounds remain suspended in the air, linked to heart and lung disease, cancers, and neurological conditions including Alzheimer’s.

Jonathan Levy, chairman of environmental health at Boston University, called it a “flashback” to the burning Kuwaiti oil fields of the first Gulf War, which produced health consequences that lasted years. Marsha Wills-Karp of Johns Hopkins: burning oil releases carbon dioxide that displaces oxygen, causing the heart to race and people near the site to experience headaches, dizziness, and the sensation that they cannot get enough air. Compounds can also enter the body through the olfactory bulb directly to the brain.

Children, elderly people, those with pre-existing conditions, pregnant women, and fetuses are most vulnerable. Pakistan’s meteorological officials have issued an alert that winds could carry pollutants across the border. The WHO director-general has warned of potential food and water contamination.

Levy: “We recognize it’s a far more challenging set of recommendations to give to people in the midst of war.”

The people breathing black rain in Tehran were not in the oil depots.

IX. The Bayt and the Nepo-tollah

Neil MacFarquhar’s analysis of what Mojtaba Khamenei is actually inheriting deserves its own accounting.

The supreme leader’s office is called the Bayt-e Rahbari — the House of the Supreme Leader. Four thousand people work there. Forty thousand more are affiliated throughout the government. Under Ali Khamenei, the office created mirror operations for every government ministry — powerful deputies mandated to ensure all government policy conformed to the supreme leader’s wishes. Other teams handle military and intelligence. Saeid Golkar of the University of Tennessee: “A complete security, political and economic state within a state.”

Mojtaba was already the “mini supreme leader” — advising his father on this apparatus for decades, vetting the appointments of the latest generation of IRGC commanders. He is not inheriting an office. He is inheriting a parallel government.

The Atlantic’s Arash Azizi named him simply: the Nepo-tollah. A nation of 90 million led by a man who has never given a single public interview or speech. His only known audio message is a 2024 announcement that he was ending his seminary classes, without explanation. Even the biographies hastily published by the regime after his selection do not list any jobs beyond being his father’s son. They note that he speaks English and Arabic.

Azizi offered the most useful historical comparison: Mojtaba might be his country’s Hua Guofeng — the uninspiring placeholder who briefly held Mao’s seat before Deng Xiaoping changed everything. Or, as one analyst close to Mojtaba argued, he might emerge as Iran’s MbS — using his conservative credentials as cover for structural change that a reformist could never survive politically. Both possibilities remain open. What is certain is that Golkar’s framing holds: “Iran has already moved from a theocratic regime to a theocratic security system under Khamenei, and now it is moving toward a more complete security state under Mojtaba.”

The religious legitimacy that justified the office in 1979 is essentially gone. What remains is power, a parallel government of 44,000 people, the IRGC’s ballistic missile arsenal, and a new supreme leader who has never spoken in public.

X. The Expanding Coalitions

Two coalitions are forming simultaneously, neither formally named, both consequential.

The first is the coalition defending against Iran. Australia announced it would deploy an E-7A Wedgetail reconnaissance and command aircraft with 85 personnel to the UAE, along with medium-range air-to-air missiles for Gulf defense. Pakistan’s navy has begun escorting the country’s merchant vessels in the Middle East. Ukraine’s drone experts are en route to Jordan. France has eight frigates and two helicopter carriers deploying to three bodies of water. NATO has a Patriot battery in eastern Turkey. The UAE requested the Australian aircraft. Eleven countries have requested Ukrainian drone expertise. None of this was coordinated by American diplomacy before the war began. It is assembling itself in response to the damage.

The second coalition is the one forming against the first. Russia congratulated Mojtaba and pledged unwavering support. Russian satellites continue tracking US warship positions for Iranian targeting. Iran’s IRGC spokesman said Monday that Iranian missiles are growing more powerful and larger than in the early days of the war, directly contradicting Trump’s claim that launches were waning. The NYT tally through Monday: more than 2,000 Iranian drones and 500 ballistic missiles launched at American allies. This does not include launches at Israel, where authorities have declined to share the figures.

Lebanon’s President Joseph Aoun broke publicly with Hezbollah on Monday — his country was trapped, he said, between Israeli assault that “shows no respect for the laws of war” and “an armed group operating outside the law in Lebanon which has no regard for the interests of Lebanon and the lives of its people.” 486 Lebanese are dead. 600,000 displaced. Lebanon’s parliament postponed its May elections by two years.

The Emirati billionaire Khalaf al-Habtoor posted then deleted a statement rejecting Lindsey Graham’s call for Gulf states to join the war. “We know who took the whole region into this dangerous escalation without consulting their ‘allies’ in the region.” He posted it. He deleted it. It is in the record.

Bahrain’s defense forces have shot down 102 missiles and 171 drones since February 28. A 29-year-old Bahraini woman was killed in an Iranian strike on a residential building in Manama on Monday night. A 2-month-old baby is in hospital. Bapco Energies, Bahrain’s state-owned energy company, has declared force majeure. It cannot meet its contractual obligations. The state energy company of a US ally has officially declared it cannot function.

XI. Day Eleven Begins

Saturday Night Live broadcast. Pete Hegseth, in the cold open, described Iran as “not a war, it’s a situationship.”

The joke requires no elaboration. What requires elaboration is everything underneath it: the Tomahawk fragments on the table with Ball Aerospace serial numbers and Globe Motors actuators and “Made in USA” stamped into the metal; the black rain over Tehran and the migraines and the film on the cars; Mahyar Zanganeh falling by the soccer goalpost in Abyek; Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington at Dover on Monday night; the 90 percent reduction in civilian harm prevention staff before the first bomb fell; the oil at $90 after being at $119 thirty-six hours earlier; the Bayt with its 40,000 affiliates and its new supreme leader who has never given a public interview; the two Iranian missiles in Turkish airspace and the Patriot now deployed at Malatya; the teachers’ union representative in Toronto who cannot reach her members because the internet is down.

“We go forward, more determined than ever,” Trump said.

“We, too, have many surprises in store,” Araghchi said.

Day Eleven begins.
