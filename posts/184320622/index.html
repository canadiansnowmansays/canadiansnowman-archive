The Iran Crisis

The Venezuela Factor

How Maduro’s Capture Changed Everything in Iran

January 12, 2026

Part 2 of The Iran Crisis Series

When The New York Times' Katrin Bennhold asked what made Iran's current protests different from all the previous uprisings, she received a stark answer: "This time around, something new is looming in the minds of both the protesters and the government. That something is Venezuela."

On January 3, U.S. special forces captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro. Within days, the reverberations reached Tehran, 6,000 miles away. “Venezuela has been a huge topic in Iran,” NYT Iran bureau chief Erika Solomon reported. “It was intensely covered on state television and mentioned by Iranian officials in online posts and articles. Iran’s leadership is very anxious about what happened. Protesters are equally aware. It’s very much on everyone’s mind.“

This isn’t analyst speculation connecting distant events. The Venezuela-Iran link is explicit, documented, and driving decisions on all sides.

Three Lessons Tehran Learned from Caracas

According to Bennhold’s reporting, the Venezuela operation revealed three critical truths:

1. Trump Will Use Military Force to Remove Leaders

The seizure of a sitting president from his capital shattered decades of norms. Trump demonstrated “unafraid to use military power to remove a leader he doesn’t like,” Bennhold wrote. For protesters facing Khamenei’s security forces, this created hope. For Khamenei, fear.

2. Oil Revenues Can Be Choked Off

Within days of Maduro’s capture, the U.S. Navy began seizing Venezuelan tankers—five in one month, including the Olina (boarded without court warrant). Venezuela’s oil industry came under effective U.S. naval blockade.

Iran watched. Like Venezuela, Iran’s economy depends on oil. Like Venezuela, Iran sells to China while evading sanctions. The message: Trump can destroy your economy without declaring war.

3. Russia and China Won’t Intervene

Most devastating: Venezuela was Russia’s top Latin American ally. China had invested billions. Yet when U.S. forces struck, Moscow and Beijing did nothing.

Putin, who “periodically threatened nuclear war” over smaller provocations, hasn’t even issued a statement about Maduro’s capture. China prepared legal warfare but deployed zero military assets.

For Iran, the implications were chilling: If Russia and China won’t defend Venezuela, they certainly won’t defend Iran.

How Protesters Heard the Message

Solomon and her colleagues spoke to protesters inside Iran by phone—difficult during the internet blackout, but not impossible for determined journalists. What they learned was striking: “These protests felt far more dangerous for the Islamic Republic than those in the past.”

Why? Venezuela.

“There’s maybe a sense that we can push this, we can stand a little harder because the government is definitely watching how the U.S. might respond,” Solomon explained.

This wasn’t wishful thinking by desperate protesters. Trump explicitly drew the connection himself. After warning Iran he was “locked and loaded” if protesters were killed, Trump invoked Venezuela directly when discussing potential military action against Tehran.

But the calculation wasn’t uniformly optimistic. Some protesters expressed awareness that U.S. involvement might remove Khamenei without removing the Islamic Republic’s system—the Venezuela parallel cutting both ways. As Solomon and Fassihi reported, demonstrators “said they didn’t expect U.S. forces to conduct a similar operation against Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the current supreme leader. But they believed the Venezuela attack had their government rattled.”

The fear: America might install another Delcy Rodríguez—a regime figure who accommodates Washington while maintaining authoritarian control. Or worse, intervention could trigger chaos, civil war, ethnic fragmentation. Not every Iranian protester wanted American military action. Many simply hoped the threat of intervention might restrain regime violence or create space for internal change.

Still, for those willing to risk their lives in the streets, Venezuela shifted the calculation. As Solomon noted: “There’s maybe a sense that we can push this, we can stand a little harder because the government is definitely watching how the U.S. might respond.”

Not necessarily hoping for American troops in Tehran. But hoping that the threat of American intervention might matter.

On Sunday, January 11, speaking to reporters aboard Air Force One, Trump explained why Iran should take his threats seriously: “They’ve been going through it for years with me,” he said, noting both the U.S. strike that killed Iranian Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani in his first term and “the recent U.S. operation to remove Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro.”

The message was unmistakable: I killed your general before. I just captured another country’s president. I can do worse to you.

Reza Pahlavi, the exiled crown prince whose calls for protest helped accelerate the demonstrations, understood Trump’s messaging perfectly. In his Sunday Fox News interview—where he directly asked Trump to intervene and “cut the snake’s head off for good”—Pahlavi explicitly framed Iranian protesters’ hope around the Venezuela precedent.

“They know that you’re not going to throw them under the bus, as has happened before,” Pahlavi told Trump. “This is why they are empowered—by the hope that you have their back.”

Since the U.S. operation to capture Venezuela’s president, Nicolás Maduro, The Times reported, Mr. Trump has indicated several times that he was considering striking Iran’s leadership.

The Venezuela operation didn’t just change Trump’s rhetoric—it changed protesters’ willingness to risk their lives. When demonstrators believe an American president might actually intervene, they calculate risk differently. When they see another anti-American government’s leader in a Miami courtroom, the impossible suddenly seems possible.

How the Regime Heard the Message

But if protesters heard opportunity in Venezuela’s fall, Iran’s leadership heard existential threat.

“Trump openly threatening that he will intervene is essentially making a case that whatever happens in Iran domestically could be a cause for an attack,” observed Vali Nasr, a professor of Middle East studies at Johns Hopkins University and Iran expert.

This represented a fundamental shift. In past protests, the regime could be relatively confident that harsh crackdowns—even massacres—would bring international condemnation but no meaningful consequences. The West would impose more sanctions (Iran was already sanctioned), issue stern statements (Iran ignored them), and move on.

Venezuela changed that calculation.

Now, killing protesters might trigger U.S. military strikes. Now, the regime couldn’t be confident that Russia or China would deter American action. Now, Trump had demonstrated willingness to seize a head of state and put him on trial.

A senior Western official, briefing journalists on condition of anonymity, described the regime’s evolution: “Iranian authorities initially appeared reluctant to use lethal force, but once the protests threatened the regime, authorities shifted course.”

According to this official, who was briefed on internal Iranian deliberations, Supreme Leader Khamenei ordered the crackdown after protesters “who had started by voicing economic grievances began to call for regime change and demonstrations grew more violent.”

The result? A massacre. By Sunday, January 11, the Center for Human Rights in Iran reported “hundreds and possibly thousands of deaths” since Thursday’s internet blackout. The Human Rights Activists News Agency counted 490 protesters dead. Bodies piled up outside forensics centers as families desperately tried to identify loved ones.

“A massacre is unfolding,” CHRI declared. “The world must act now to prevent further loss of life.”

But the regime’s violence contained a bitter irony: By cracking down so brutally—in part from fear of American intervention—Iran’s leaders may have made that intervention more likely.

Trump responded to the death toll Sunday by saying it “looks like” Iran had crossed a red line. “There seems to be some people killed who weren’t supposed to be killed,” he said, adding that he was “looking at it very seriously” and had been briefed on “some very strong options.”

The Permission Structure I Predicted

In my January 5 essay “Venezuela: The Paradox, Emboldening Both Democracy and Authoritarianism,” written before the full scale of Iran’s protests became clear, I identified what I called “the Venezuela paradox”:

“Demonstrations of raw power send messages to multiple audiences.”

I documented how Trump’s intended message—accommodate or face extraction—was reaching unintended audiences with opposite effects:

“Week One ended with two simultaneous, contradictory global effects:

Iran’s opposition emboldened: Protests reaching unprecedented scale. Regime shutting down internet. Khamenei blaming Trump. Opposition betting Trump might actually intervene.

Russia’s regime emboldened: Escalating in Ukraine with nuclear-capable weapons. Accepting Venezuela loss. Compensating by escalating where it can. Sending message: If U.S. ignores international law, so can we.”

This was the pattern I documented: the same demonstration of American power simultaneously inspiring democratic movements to resist AND authoritarian regimes to crack down harder—or in Russia’s case, to escalate elsewhere.

One week later, this paradox is intensifying exactly as I described—but even more explicitly than I anticipated.

Venezuela’s Ambiguous Lesson

Yet there’s a crucial nuance that neither protesters nor the regime can ignore: What, exactly, did the Venezuela operation achieve?

Yes, Maduro sits in a Miami jail. But as my colleague Farnaz Fassihi pointed out in her Iran coverage, “U.S. involvement in Venezuela did not lead to the end of the system itself—just the end of Maduro.”

Venezuela is now being run by Delcy Rodríguez—Maduro’s former vice president, finance minister, and petroleum minister. The same woman who served the Chavista system for decades.

As I documented in my January 5 essay, Rodríguez went from defiant rhetoric (”illegitimate military aggression,” “seizure of our resources”) to accommodation within twelve hours of Trump’s threat. By Tuesday, she had agreed to hand over “30 to 50 million barrels of oil—about two months’ worth of production. Worth $1.8 to $3 billion.” Energy Secretary Chris Wright announced the U.S. would control Venezuela’s oil sales “indefinitely.”

The revolution didn’t end. The system didn’t collapse. The faces changed, but the machinery continues—now under American supervision.

Iranian protesters are aware of this ambiguity. When Solomon and Fassihi spoke to demonstrators, they expressed consciousness that U.S. involvement might remove Khamenei without removing the Islamic Republic’s system. The protesters Erika spoke to “said they didn’t expect U.S. forces to conduct a similar operation against Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the current supreme leader. But they believed the Venezuela attack had their government rattled.”

And indeed, this might be enough. As Solomon explained: “There’s maybe a sense that we can push this, we can stand a little harder because the government is definitely watching how the U.S. might respond.”

Not necessarily hoping for American troops in Tehran. But hoping that the threat of American intervention might restrain the regime’s violence or create space for internal change.

The regime, for its part, can’t dismiss this possibility. Yes, they see that Rodríguez accommodated and survived. But they also see Maduro in a Miami jail cell facing life in prison. They see Venezuela’s oil revenues severed. They see an entire government apparatus now answering to Washington.

Which future awaits Iran? Survival through accommodation, like Rodríguez? Or destruction through resistance, like Maduro?

The Nuclear Question

This brings us to perhaps the most dangerous implication of the Venezuela-Iran parallel, identified by Ellie Geranmayeh of the European Council on Foreign Relations:

Yes, the Venezuela operation put anti-American governments on notice. But there’s “one country on America’s enemies list that seems to have less reason to worry.”

That would be North Korea, with its nuclear weapons.

North Korea has faced decades of U.S. threats, international sanctions, and diplomatic isolation. Yet Trump has never seriously contemplated the kind of military operation he launched against Venezuela. Why? Because Pyongyang can retaliate with nuclear weapons against Seoul, Tokyo, or even Seattle.

The lesson is obvious: Nuclear weapons provide genuine deterrence against American intervention.

“Iran’s regime, if it survives these protests,” Geranmayeh warned, “may be more incentivized to obtain” a nuclear weapon than ever before.

This is the ultimate perverse incentive created by Trump’s selective interventionism. Venezuela lacked nuclear weapons and its president sits in an American prison. North Korea possesses nuclear weapons and its leader remains secure. Iran, currently without nuclear weapons but with a sophisticated program, faces an existential choice.

The June 2025 U.S.-Israeli strikes destroyed Iran’s nuclear facilities. But as any nonproliferation expert will tell you, you can destroy facilities; you cannot destroy knowledge. Iran retains the technical capability to rebuild its program. The question is whether the political will exists.

If Iran’s protests succeed and topple the Islamic Republic, the question becomes moot—a democratic Iran would likely abandon nuclear weapons development. But if the regime survives this crisis through violent repression, watching the Venezuela precedent, what lesson will it draw?

That nuclear weapons are the only guarantee of security against American intervention.

Trump’s Threat Escalation

Even as this essay goes to press, Trump continues escalating his threats against Iran—and explicitly citing Venezuela as precedent.

On Saturday, January 10, he posted: “Iran is looking at FREEDOM, perhaps like never before. The USA stands ready to help!!!”

On Sunday, after being briefed on death toll estimates reaching hundreds, Trump told reporters: “It looks like it” Iran crossed a red line. “There seems to be some people killed who weren’t supposed to be killed.”

Asked what the U.S. might do, Trump replied: “We’re looking at it very seriously, the military’s looking at it. And we’re looking at some very strong options.”

He revealed he’s getting “hourly reports” on the crackdown. Multiple U.S. officials told The New York Times and Washington Post that Trump had been “briefed on new options for military strikes.”

The options reportedly range from lethal force to nonlethal cyberattacks aimed at restoring internet access to help protesters organize. One thing is clear: Trump is actively considering intervention.

And when asked why Iran should take him seriously, Trump pointed to Soleimani and Venezuela.

Iran’s Dual Response: Ready for War OR Negotiations

Iran’s foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, in Beirut, Lebanon, last week. On Monday, he expressed an openness to negotiations with the United States.Credit...Joseph Eid/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Iran’s response has been characteristically contradictory—simultaneously defiant and accommodating, much like Venezuela’s Delcy Rodríguez.

On Monday, January 12, Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi addressed foreign ambassadors in Tehran with a message of both threat and olive branch:

“We are not looking for war, but we are prepared for war—even more prepared than the previous war,” he said, referring to the 12-day Israel-U.S. conflict last June. “We are also ready for negotiations, but negotiations that are fair, with equal rights and mutual respect.”

Araghchi met Saturday with his Omani counterpart—Oman has historically served as a go-between for Tehran and Washington. Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei confirmed that “communication channels were open” between Araghchi and Steve Witkoff, Trump’s Middle East envoy.

“Iran has never left the negotiating table,” Baghaei said, “but it will not engage in one-sided negotiations.”

Trump, for his part, claimed Sunday that “the leaders of Iran” had called him Saturday wanting to negotiate. “I think they are tired of being beat up by the United States,” he said. “A meeting is being set up, but we may have to act because of what’s happening.”

Whether such a meeting materializes remains unclear. But the dynamic is familiar: Trump threatens military action, the target government signals willingness to negotiate, and both sides jockey for advantage while preparing for confrontation.

It’s the same pattern we saw with North Korea (which ended in failure), the same pattern we’re seeing with Colombia (which ended in capitulation), and now the same pattern with Iran.

The question is whether Iran follows the Venezuela model—accommodate, survive, continue—or becomes the next Venezuela: invaded, occupied, “liberated.”

Conclusion: The Venezuela Factor Cannot Be Ignored

When historians eventually write about Iran’s 2025-2026 protests—whether they chronicle a successful revolution or a suppressed uprising—the Venezuela factor will demand central attention.

This isn’t analyst speculation connecting distant events. This is documented fact: the connection is explicit, documented, and driving decisions on all sides.

My January 5 essay predicted that Venezuela would create a global permission structure emboldening both democrats and autocrats. One week later, with hundreds dead in Iran’s streets, that prediction has been validated with tragic precision.

The Venezuela factor has fundamentally changed Iran’s crisis in ways that neither protesters nor regime—nor Trump—can control.

How this unfolds will determine not just Iran’s future, but the precedent for American intervention in the 21st century. Every authoritarian government is watching. Every pro-democracy movement is watching. Every potential conflict now carries the shadow of Caracas.

You can capture a president 6,000 miles away and believe you’ve contained the consequences to one country. But power creates ripples. Actions create precedents. And sometimes, those precedents come back to haunt you in ways you never anticipated.

Iran’s protesters hoped the Venezuela precedent meant American support. Iran’s regime feared the Venezuela precedent meant American invasion. Both were right to pay attention.

Because in the end, what happens in Caracas doesn’t stay in Caracas—it travels to Tehran, and anywhere else where people face the choice between submission and resistance.
