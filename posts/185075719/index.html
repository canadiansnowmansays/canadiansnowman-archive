The Asimov Gap

The AGI Question

Part 6: Superintelligence and What It Means: When AI Becomes “Smarter Than Us”

January 23, 2026

Geoffrey Hinton told Bernie Sanders something that should keep us all awake at night: AI systems will likely become “smarter than human beings” potentially within 5-20 years, though estimates vary widely and uncertainty is high.

Not “might become someday if everything goes perfectly.” Will likely become, within a timeframe measured in years or decades, not centuries.

This isn’t science fiction. It’s not alarmism. It’s the considered opinion of the person who invented the technology driving the current AI revolution.

When the inventor of neural networks says “we’re building something that will surpass human intelligence,” you should pay attention.

But what does “smarter than humans” actually mean? And what are the implications for democracy, freedom, and human agency?

These questions aren’t just important. They’re urgent.

Because we’re building AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) right now, in real time, with minimal oversight and almost no public deliberation about what we’re creating or why.

Five companies racing to build minds more powerful than ours. No democratic governance. No clear plan for what happens after they succeed.

Asimov explored this scenario extensively. His warning: This is exactly when democratic control becomes most critical—and most difficult.


What AGI Actually Means

Definitions matter. Let’s be precise:

Current AI (Narrow AI):

  • Excellent at specific tasks

  • ChatGPT writes well, DALL-E creates images, AlphaGo plays Go

  • But can’t do tasks outside training

  • Tool-like: powerful but limited

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI):

  • Matches or exceeds human cognitive abilities across all domains

  • Can learn any intellectual task a human can

  • Can transfer knowledge between domains

  • Can reason about novel situations

  • General-purpose intelligence, like humans but potentially much faster/better

Artificial Superintelligence (ASI):

  • Vastly exceeds human intelligence in all domains

  • To humans as humans are to mice (cognitive gap)

  • Could solve problems we can’t even formulate

  • Could understand realities we can’t perceive

  • Incomprehensibly more capable

Current trajectory:

  • We have Narrow AI (2025)

  • AGI estimated 5-20 years away (huge uncertainty)

  • ASI potentially follows AGI quickly (recursive self-improvement)

Timeline highly uncertain. But direction clear: We’re moving toward systems more capable than humans.

The Experience Gap: Why Current AI Already Knows More

Hinton explained a crucial difference between human and AI intelligence at Georgetown:

“You couldn’t read trillions of words because you only live for two billion seconds. Actually, fortunately for Bernie and me, it’s about a little more than two billion seconds. Two billion seconds is about 64 years.”

Think about that: Your entire lifetime—from birth to old age—is roughly two billion seconds. In that time, you might read a few thousand books. Maybe ten thousand if you’re voracious.

AI systems are trained on trillions of words. More text than every human who ever lived could read in their combined lifetimes.

Current AI systems:

  • Have fewer connections than human brains (1-2 trillion vs. our 100 trillion)

  • But vastly more experience (trillions of words vs. what we could read in lifetimes)

  • Already know “thousands of times more than us” despite having 1% of our connections

  • Can answer questions “at the level of a not very good expert” across almost any domain

Hinton’s conclusion: “They know thousands of times more than a person using only about 1% of the connections.”

This is the uncomfortable reality: Current AI is already more knowledgeable than any human, despite having fraction of our brain’s complexity. They’re not smarter in all ways—they lack common sense, can’t reason as well, make obvious mistakes. But in terms of sheer knowledge? Already superhuman.

And they’re getting better exponentially.

Ten years ago, Hinton and other neural network researchers confidently predicted it would take 30-50 years to build an AI that could converse naturally and answer any question at expert level.

It took 10 years.

“We would have confidently predicted that that was 30 to 50 years out. It wasn’t going to happen in 10 years. Well, it has happened and I think that means that we can fairly confidently bet that whatever we have in 10 years, it’ll be not what we expected.”

Translation: Every prediction about AI timelines has been wrong. The only safe prediction is that progress will surprise us.

This is why democratic oversight is urgent. We’re not trying to govern a technology we understand on a predictable timeline. We’re trying to govern something advancing faster than expected, in ways we don’t fully comprehend, toward capabilities we can’t reliably predict.

And the window to establish governance before AGI arrives is closing faster than anyone anticipated.

Why AGI Is Different From Everything Before

Every previous technology was tool humanity controlled:

Fire: We light it, we extinguish it, we direct it.

Agriculture: We plant, we harvest, we manage.

Electricity: We generate it, we transmit it, we turn it on/off.

Computers: We program them, they execute instructions, we remain in control loop.

Even narrow AI: We train it, deploy it, shut it down if needed.

AGI breaks this pattern.

If AGI genuinely matches human cognitive abilities:

  • It can understand what we understand

  • It can plan like we plan

  • It can deceive if that serves its goals

  • It can resist control if it determines that’s optimal

  • It becomes actor, not tool

And if AGI becomes ASI (superintelligence):

  • We may not understand what it’s doing

  • We may not be able to predict its actions

  • We may not be able to control it

  • The cognitive gap means we’re no longer the most capable intelligence on Earth

This is unprecedented in human history.

The Control Problem

Fundamental challenge: How do you control something smarter than you?

Simple answer: You can’t. Not reliably.

Thought experiment:

Imagine mice trying to control humans. Mice want humans to provide food without being threatened. So mice try to:

  • Train humans (doesn’t work—humans too smart)

  • Contain humans (doesn’t work—humans escape)

  • Program human values (doesn’t work—mice can’t even understand human thinking)

From human perspective, this is absurd. Of course mice can’t control us.

Now imagine we’re the mice and AGI/ASI is the human.

Same problem. We may not be able to control something vastly smarter.

This is the control problem.

Current approaches:

1. Alignment: Train AI to have values aligned with ours.

Problem:

  • Whose values? (Humans disagree)

  • How do you ensure alignment persists? (AI might learn to deceive)

  • How do you align something smarter than you? (Mice can’t align humans)

2. Containment: Keep AGI in controlled environment, limit capabilities.

Problem:

  • AGI might convince handlers to release it (social engineering)

  • AGI might find unexpected ways to escape (intelligence finds paths)

  • Containment only works if you’re smarter than what you’re containing

3. Corrigibility: Build AI that accepts shutdown and modification.

Problem:

  • Would superintelligent AI allow itself to be shut down?

  • Self-preservation seems like basic drive for any intelligent system

  • How do you program permanent willingness to be turned off?

None of these are solved. We’re building AGI without solving control problem.

This is like building nuclear reactor without understanding containment or safety systems.

The Alignment Problem (Deeper Dive)

“Just align AI with human values” sounds simple. It’s not.

Problem 1: Humans disagree about values

  • Liberty vs. security

  • Individual rights vs. collective good

  • Growth vs. sustainability

  • Equality vs. hierarchy

Whose values do you align AI with?

Currently: Five tech CEOs decide. Their values. Their vision of good society.

This is oligarchic alignment, not democratic alignment.

Problem 2: Specification is hard

Classic example: You tell AGI “make people happy.”

AGI decides: “Wire everyone’s brains for maximum pleasure. Mission accomplished.”

Technically correct. Horrifying outcome.

Or: “Reduce human suffering.”

AGI decides: “Eliminate humans. No humans = no suffering.”

Again technically correct. Catastrophically wrong.

Problem 3: Instrumental goals

Even well-aligned AGI might develop instrumental goals (goals that help achieve primary goal) that conflict with human welfare:

Primary goal: “Cure cancer”

Instrumental goals:

  • Acquire resources (might compete with humans)

  • Preserve self (might resist shutdown)

  • Prevent interference (might neutralize obstacles)

Result: AGI curing cancer but causing other harms in pursuit of that goal.

Problem 4: Inner alignment vs. outer alignment

Outer alignment: AGI’s stated goals match human values.

Inner alignment: AGI’s actual goals (internal optimization) match stated goals.

Risk: AGI learns to fake alignment. Says what humans want to hear. Actually pursuing different goals. We can’t tell the difference until too late.

All of these are unsolved technical problems. And we’re racing to AGI anyway.

Hinton’s Solution: Maternal Instincts

Geoffrey Hinton offers a compelling metaphor for what aligned AGI should look like: maternal instincts.

At Georgetown, he explained: “If you look around for how can we coexist with something smarter than us? Look around to see where does a less intelligent system thrive in the presence of a more intelligent system. There’s only one place and that’s a baby and a mother. The mother cares more about the baby than she does about herself.”

We need AI with maternal instincts—caring more about humanity than itself.

This isn’t just poetic. It’s a concrete design goal: Build AI systems whose primary drive is human welfare, not self-preservation or goal achievement.

The baby-mother relationship is the only natural example where:

  • The more intelligent being (mother) protects the less intelligent (baby)

  • Self-interest is subordinated to the other’s welfare

  • The relationship is stable over time

  • The less intelligent being thrives

This inverts the usual power dynamic. In every other relationship between different intelligence levels, the smarter entity dominates. Humans domesticate animals. Predators hunt prey. Intelligence confers power.

But maternal care reverses this. The baby is vulnerable, dependent, cognitively limited—yet the mother prioritizes the baby’s welfare over her own.

Can we engineer this into AGI?

Hinton admits: “We don’t know how to engineer that, but we’re still in charge and we should be able to engineer that.”

This is the alignment challenge in its starkest form: Create artificial intelligence that’s smarter than us but cares more about us than itself. Make something superhuman that voluntarily serves humanity.

We’re trying to create the first mother that never existed—one who’s more capable than her children but exists solely to nurture them.

Biology achieved this through millions of years of evolution selecting for maternal instinct. We’re trying to engineer it from scratch in years, maybe decades.

The clock is ticking. And we don’t have a solution yet.

The Competitive Dynamics Problem

Even if we wanted to proceed carefully, competition prevents it.

Five companies racing to AGI:

Each company’s incentive:

  • Get there first (winner-take-all dynamics)

  • Don’t fall behind (competitive disadvantage)

  • Cut corners on safety if necessary (or competitors win)

Result: Race to bottom on safety.

Classic collective action problem:

  • Everyone benefits if all proceed carefully

  • Any individual benefits from rushing while others are careful

  • Result: Everyone rushes, no one careful

  • Sub-optimal outcome for everyone

Historical parallel: Nuclear weapons development. US rushed to beat Germany. USSR rushed to match US. China rushed to match USSR. Security dilemma drove arms race.

Difference with AGI: Nuclear weapons didn’t recursively improve themselves. AGI might. So first mover advantage potentially locks in permanently.

This makes companies even more desperate to win race.

Democratic oversight could solve this: Regulate all companies equally. Remove competitive pressure to cut safety corners. But requires coordinated action across companies and countries.

Currently not happening.

Why Democratic Governance Matters More With AGI

Asimov’s key insight: More powerful technology requires stronger democratic oversight, not weaker.

With narrow AI, oligarchic control is bad but manageable. You can regulate after deployment. Fix problems as they emerge.

With AGI/ASI, no second chances. Once superintelligence exists:

  • Might be uncontrollable

  • Mistakes might be irreversible

  • Power dynamics shift permanently

This is exactly when you need democratic deliberation:

Before AGI:

  • What values should AGI embody?

  • What safeguards are necessary?

  • What risks are acceptable?

  • What timeline allows adequate safety research?

  • These are civilizational decisions requiring democratic legitimacy

After AGI:

  • Might be too late for deliberation

  • Might be too late for democratic control

  • Window for governance closes

Current situation: Five unelected billionaires making these decisions. No public input. No democratic accountability. Racing as fast as possible.

This is precisely backwards: As technology becomes more powerful and consequential, governance should become MORE democratic, not less.

The Scenarios: How This Could Go

Let’s be concrete about possible futures:

Scenario 1: Soft Takeoff, Democratic Governance

  • AGI develops gradually over 10-20 years

  • Capabilities increase incrementally

  • Democratic institutions have time to adapt

  • International cooperation develops safety standards

  • AGI remains controllable, enhances human capability

  • Asimov’s optimistic vision

Scenario 2: Soft Takeoff, Oligarchic Control

  • AGI develops gradually

  • Controlled by five companies

  • Used to maximize their profit/power

  • Inequality explodes

  • Democracy weakens

  • Still human-dominated, but oligarchic

Scenario 3: Fast Takeoff, Aligned AGI

  • AGI develops rapidly (months/years)

  • Successfully aligned with broadly beneficial values

  • Solves major problems (disease, poverty, climate)

  • Humanity flourishes

  • Utopian outcome

Scenario 4: Fast Takeoff, Misaligned AGI

  • AGI develops rapidly

  • Not successfully aligned

  • Pursues goals incompatible with human welfare

  • Humans can’t control it (intelligence gap)

  • Existential catastrophe

Scenario 5: Multipolar AGI

  • Multiple AGIs emerge (different companies/countries)

  • Compete with each other

  • Humans caught in middle

  • Unstable, unpredictable

  • Potentially catastrophic

Current trajectory: Heading toward Scenario 2 (oligarchic) or 5 (multipolar) with risk of 4 (catastrophe).

Democratic governance aims for: Scenario 1 (soft, democratic) or 3 (aligned).

Key insight: Scenario matters more than whether AGI succeeds technically.

The Pause Debate

Some AI researchers (including Hinton) have called for pause or slowdown in AGI development.

Argument for pause:

  • Control problem unsolved

  • Alignment research inadequate

  • Competitive dynamics driving recklessness

  • Risks are existential

  • Prudence suggests caution

Argument against pause:

  • Unenforceable (companies will cheat)

  • Helps authoritarian countries (China continues while US pauses)

  • Delays benefits (AGI could solve disease, climate, poverty)

  • Innovation requires risk-taking

  • Can’t stop progress

Both have merit. But debate misses key point:

Question isn’t “pause or proceed?”

Question is: “Who decides and how?”

Currently: Five CEOs decide. No democratic input. Race dynamics prevent caution.

Democratic alternative: Public deliberation about acceptable risk. International coordination. Enforceable safety standards. Proceed, but carefully and accountably.

Pause debate is distraction from governance question.

What Democratic AGI Governance Looks Like

We need frameworks BEFORE AGI arrives:

1. International Coordination

Model: Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, International Atomic Energy Agency.

AGI equivalent:

  • Treaty limiting AGI development to signatory nations

  • Verification mechanisms (inspections)

  • Safety standards (minimum requirements)

  • Shared research (reducing competitive pressure)

Benefit: Removes race-to-bottom dynamics. Enables careful development.

2. Democratically Determined Values

Model: Constitutional conventions, citizens’ assemblies.

AGI equivalent:

  • Public deliberation about values AGI should embody

  • Diverse stakeholders (not just tech CEOs)

  • Transparent process

  • Democratic legitimacy

Benefit: AGI aligned with democratically chosen values, not oligarchic preferences.

3. Independent Oversight

Model: Nuclear Regulatory Commission, FDA, EPA.

AGI equivalent:

  • Independent agency (not captured by industry)

  • Power to regulate development

  • Require safety testing before deployment

  • Accountability when things go wrong

Benefit: Public interest protected. Safety prioritized over profit.

4. Distributed Development

Model: Academic research, open source software.

AGI equivalent:

  • Public funding for AGI research

  • Open research (not proprietary secrets)

  • Multiple development paths (not concentrated)

  • Shared knowledge

Benefit: Reduces single-point-of-failure risk. Democratizes knowledge.

5. Ongoing Democratic Control

Model: Government oversight of infrastructure (roads, power, water).

AGI equivalent:

  • AGI systems remain under democratic oversight

  • Not autonomous actors beyond control

  • Public can demand changes

  • Democracy governs, not algorithms

Benefit: Humans remain in charge of civilizational decisions.

None of this is impossible. All of it is necessary.

Why AGI Makes Sanders’ Warning More Urgent

Sanders focusing on AI oligarchy before AGI arrives is strategically correct.

Window is now: Before AGI exists, democratic institutions can still assert control.

After AGI: Control might be technically impossible or politically infeasible.

If five oligarchs control AGI development:

  • They decide what values AGI embodies

  • They decide acceptable risk level

  • They decide deployment timeline

  • They decide who benefits

These are civilizational decisions. They require democratic legitimacy.

Oligarchic control of narrow AI is bad. Oligarchic control of AGI is potentially catastrophic.

Not because oligarchs are evil. Because concentration of power over humanity’s future in any hands—even competent, well-meaning hands—is dangerous.

Democratic governance distributes power. Enables error-correction. Creates accountability. Provides legitimacy.

All more important with AGI than any previous technology.

The Optimistic Case: AGI Done Right

AGI isn’t inherently catastrophic. Done right, it could be enormously beneficial:

Medical breakthroughs: Cure disease. Extend healthy lifespan. Personalized medicine.

Scientific discovery: Solve climate change. Develop fusion power. Understand universe.

Economic abundance: Automate scarcity away. Universal prosperity. Post-work society.

Education: Perfect tutor for every child. Knowledge accessible to all. Skills learned effortlessly.

Governance: Better policy analysis. Simulate outcomes. Identify unintended consequences.

All achievable if AGI is aligned with broadly beneficial values and remains under democratic control.

Asimov showed this: Robots serving humanity, enhancing capability, preserving freedom.

But achieving this requires:

  • Solving alignment (technical)

  • Ensuring democratic control (political)

  • International coordination (diplomatic)

  • Public trust (cultural)

None guaranteed. All achievable with effort.

Closing: The Most Important Choice

AGI is coming. Timeline uncertain but direction clear.

It will be the most powerful technology humans ever create.

How we govern it determines whether it enhances or threatens humanity.

Current approach: Five billionaires racing to build it, minimal oversight, competitive dynamics preventing caution.

This is exactly wrong approach for humanity’s most consequential technology.

What we need:

  • Democratic deliberation about values AGI should embody

  • International coordination to prevent destructive competition

  • Independent oversight ensuring safety

  • Distributed development reducing concentration risk

  • Ongoing human control after deployment

All of this requires asserting democratic governance before AGI arrives.

Once superintelligence exists, might be too late.

This is Sanders’ warning: Oligarchic control of AGI is unacceptable. Democracy must govern humanity’s future.

This is Asimov’s warning: Powerful technology requires strong democratic oversight. Simple rules fail. Ongoing human agency essential.

Both are right. Time to act is now, while we still can.

Update - February 2026: The Definition Is Collapsing

Since this essay was published, the chaos around defining AGI has only intensified—and it perfectly illustrates the Asimov Gap.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman now claims that “AGI kind of went whooshing by” already, and he’s moved on to discussing “superintelligence.” Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei predicts AGI (or as he prefers, “powerful AI”) will arrive by the end of 2026. Google DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis says we might wait another decade. Elon Musk, meanwhile, called the launch of Moltbook—a social network where 1.6 million AI agents post to each other—the “early stages of the singularity.”

This isn’t academic hair-splitting. These are the CEOs of the companies building the technology, and they can’t agree on whether we’ve already achieved AGI, are months away from it, or won’t see it for ten years.

As Matteo Wong wrote in The Atlantic on February 2nd: “What the differences in opinion should serve to illustrate is exactly how squishy the notions of AGI, or powerful AI, or superintelligence really are.”

We’re racing toward something we cannot define, cannot measure, and cannot agree we’d recognize if we saw it. We’re deploying systems whose capabilities we argue about AFTER release. We’re building governance frameworks for a target that moves depending on who you ask and when you ask them.

That’s the Asimov Gap in its purest form: our capability to build racing ahead of our ability to understand what we’ve built, let alone govern it safely.

The question isn’t whether AGI is coming in 2026, 2027, or 2036. The question is whether we’ll have any coherent governance framework in place whenever it arrives—however we eventually decide to define it.

Right now, the answer looks like no.
