The Baseline Problem

Part 7: When the Patient Starts Diagnosing Himself

January 3, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays
January 3, 2026

“I don’t want thick blood pouring through my heart. I want nice, thin blood pouring through my heart. Does that make sense?”

The Confession Interview

In November, I documented Trump threatening to execute members of Congress for reciting military law. Six weeks later, he sat down with The Wall Street Journal and calmly explained that he ignores his doctors’ advice because he prefers “nice, thin blood pouring through my heart.”

The cognitive decline isn’t hiding in speech patterns anymore. Trump is narrating it himself.

On January 1, 2026, the Wall Street Journal published what may be the most medically revealing presidential interview in American history. Trump didn’t just admit to health concerns—he volunteered explanations that demonstrate the very cognitive impairment experts have been warning about since 2017.

This wasn’t an ambush interview or gotcha journalism. This was Trump, unprompted, explaining his medical decision-making process to one of America’s most respected business publications. And what he revealed validates every pattern I’ve documented in this series since June.

The baseline problem predicted this moment: eventually, cognitive impairment becomes so pronounced that the person lacks insight into what they’re revealing about their own condition.

“Nice, Thin Blood” - When Biology Becomes Cartoon Logic

Let’s start with Trump’s exact words about aspirin, because the direct quote is more revealing than any summary:

Trump to WSJ: “They say aspirin is good for thinning out the blood, and I don’t want thick blood pouring through my heart. I want nice, thin blood pouring through my heart. Does that make sense?”

His physician’s confirmation: Dr. Sean Barbabella told the Journal that Trump takes 325 milligrams of aspirin daily for “cardiac prevention”—the maximum recommended dose, and significantly more than the 81mg “baby aspirin” most doctors prescribe for elderly patients.

Trump’s explanation for ignoring medical advice: “They’d rather have me take the smaller one. I take the larger one, but I’ve done it for years, and what it does do is it causes bruising.”

This represents multiple layers of cognitive dysfunction simultaneously:

1. Magical Thinking About Biology

Dr. Jonathan Reiner, cardiologist to former Vice President Dick Cheney, was unequivocal in his assessment on CNN: “That actually makes nonsense. First of all, when we use any kind of anticoagulant, medications to prevent clotting, those don’t thin the blood. It’s not like changing something from gumbo to chicken soup. It doesn’t make it thinner. It makes you less likely to clot.”

Trump’s mental model of aspirin as some kind of blood thinner that literally makes blood “thinner” reveals fundamental confusion about basic biology. This isn’t a metaphor or simplification for public consumption—Trump genuinely appears to believe blood viscosity works like motor oil.

2. Cause-and-Effect Confusion

Trump connects three facts but misunderstands the relationship:

  • Aspirin prevents clotting (TRUE)

  • He takes high-dose aspirin (TRUE)

  • He bruises easily (TRUE)

His conclusion: This is working as intended—he’s achieving “nice, thin blood.”

The actual medical reality: Excessive aspirin creates bleeding risk without additional cardiovascular benefit in elderly patients. The American Heart Association explicitly warns that for people over 70, aspirin therapy can “do more harm than good due to the risk of bleeding.”

3. Self-Medication Over Medical Expertise

Trump explicitly acknowledges his doctors want him to take less aspirin, then explains why he knows better than they do. His reasoning? He wants “nice, thin blood.”

This represents what geriatric specialists call “poor insight into medical condition”—inability to recognize when personal preferences override professional medical judgment. It’s the same pattern visible in his approach to Federal Reserve policy (”Maybe I should appoint myself Fed Chair”), intelligence assessments, and statistical agencies. When expertise conflicts with Trump’s preferences, expertise loses consistently.

The Delicate Skin Confession

The WSJ interview revealed another concerning admission that Trump appears to view as explanation rather than symptom:

Trump: “I have makeup that’s, you know, easy to put on, takes about 10 seconds.”

Context: Trump explained he applies makeup to his hands after getting “whacked again by someone.”

The Pam Bondi Incident: Trump confirmed that Attorney General Pam Bondi’s ring broke his skin during a high-five at the 2024 Republican National Convention. “The ring hit the back of my hand, and, yes, there was a slight little cut.”

WSJ reporting: “Inside sources told the WSJ that the incident had alarmed some witnesses, and was only one of several occasions in which his hand has suffered wounds in recent years.”

Let’s be clear about what this means clinically:

Normal aging: Skin becomes somewhat more fragile with age
What Trump is describing: Skin so fragile that routine social contact (high-fives, handshakes) regularly breaks it, requiring makeup concealment

Dr. Jeremy Faust, editor-in-chief of MedPage Today and assistant professor of emergency medicine at Harvard Medical School, noted on CNN that Trump’s aspirin explanation “shocked” him because 325mg daily is the dose given “to prevent a heart attack”—not for routine use, and particularly risky for someone whose skin breaks from handshakes.

The McDonald’s Witness

Republican National Committee Chairman Joe Gruters provided named, on-the-record testimony about Trump’s eating habits that would be concerning for any 79-year-old, let alone one with documented cardiovascular issues:

Gruters (October 2025 podcast): “He had hot fries waiting for him from McDonald’s. Then he had a Filet-O-Fish, a Quarter Pounder, and a Big Mac, and I think he combined two of them.”

The WSJ noted: “Republican National Committee Chairman Joe Gruters described being shocked by Trump’s eating habits when they traveled together during the campaign.”

Gruters’ direct quote: “I’m thinking to myself, ‘How does a guy that’s as senior as him get away with eating all this McDonald’s, probably on a consistent basis?’”

This isn’t anonymous gossip. This is the RNC Chairman—Trump’s own ally—expressing medical concern on the public record.

MSNBC analyst Eugene Robinson did the math: “That is like three dinners... a McDonald’s Quarter-Pounder with cheese and a Big Mac and a Filet-O-Fish.”

Combined with Trump’s admission that he finds exercise “boring” and refuses to use compression socks for his chronic venous insufficiency because he “didn’t like them,” we have a complete picture of someone making medical decisions based on immediate preference rather than long-term consequences.

The MRI/CT Scan Confusion

Perhaps most telling is Trump’s confusion about his own recent medical procedures. The WSJ interview revealed he can’t accurately describe what tests he’s had:

October 2025: Trump tells reporters he had an MRI, though he “had no idea” what part of his body was scanned

January 2026 WSJ interview: Trump clarifies it wasn’t an MRI but a CT scan: “It wasn’t an MRI. It was less than that. It was a scan.”

Trump’s explanation: “In retrospect, it’s too bad I took it because it gave them a little ammunition. I would have been a lot better off if they didn’t, because the fact that I took it said, ‘Oh gee, is something wrong?’ Well, nothing’s wrong.”

Think about what this reveals:

  1. Trump underwent medical imaging but couldn’t accurately describe what test he had

  2. He views the medical procedure itself as a mistake—not because of medical necessity, but because of public perception

  3. His reasoning: The test created the appearance of a problem, therefore he shouldn’t have taken it

  4. He regrets transparency, not any actual health concern

This is precisely the kind of “poor insight” that cognitive specialists document when assessing elderly patients. The inability to distinguish between addressing a medical issue and managing perception of that issue represents confused priority-setting.

“Sometimes They’ll Take a Picture of Me Blinking”

The WSJ asked Trump directly about appearing to fall asleep during public events—something documented repeatedly on video throughout 2025, including:

  • December 2 Cabinet meeting (eyes closed, head drooping)

  • November 6 Oval Office event (struggling to keep eyes open)

  • Multiple rally and diplomatic events throughout the year

Trump’s explanation: “I’ll just close. It’s very relaxing to me. Sometimes they’ll take a picture of me blinking, blinking, and they’ll catch me with the blink.”

This explanation doesn’t account for sustained eye closure with head forward—the posture documented in multiple videos. “Blinking” doesn’t last 30 seconds with accompanying head droop.

More concerning is the alternative explanation Trump offered: He doesn’t sleep much and finds closing his eyes “relaxing” during meetings.

If true, this means the President is so sleep-deprived that he reflexively closes his eyes for extended periods during official meetings. If false, it means he’s offering implausible explanations for observable behavior rather than acknowledging fatigue.

Neither explanation is reassuring.

The Medical Expert Response

The WSJ interview generated immediate professional assessment from multiple credentialed experts beyond those already quoted:

Multiple medical professionals noted:

  • Aspirin doesn’t “thin” blood in the way Trump describes

  • 325mg daily significantly increases bleeding risk in elderly patients

  • The combination of high-dose aspirin, fragile skin, and visible bruising suggests vascular issues beyond what’s been disclosed

  • Refusing compression socks for chronic venous insufficiency shows preference overriding medical necessity

What’s remarkable is the consensus: Trump’s own explanations reveal medical decision-making that would concern any geriatrician treating a 79-year-old patient.

When the Media Double Standard Finally Breaks

In Part 3, I documented how media had normalized Trump’s dysfunction through what observers call “sanewashing”—presenting his most coherent moments while ignoring obvious symptoms.

The WSJ interview represents a turning point in that normalization.

What’s Different Now:

The Wall Street Journal—a conservative-leaning business publication—ran Trump’s medical admissions relatively straight. They included:

  • His “nice, thin blood” explanation

  • His confusion about MRI vs CT scan

  • His “blinking” defense for sleeping

  • Expert medical response questioning his judgment

The Key Insight:

Media can sanewash Trump’s rambling speeches by selecting coherent clips. They can frame his confusion as “unconventional communication style.” They can normalize the abnormal through selective coverage.

But they can’t sanewash a direct quote where Trump explains he wants “nice, thin blood pouring through my heart.”

The baseline problem predicted this moment. Eventually, cognitive impairment becomes pronounced enough that the person’s own explanations reveal the dysfunction. No amount of diplomatic framing can obscure Trump explaining biology like a cartoon character.

The Contrast Biden Never Got

Imagine for a moment that Joe Biden had sat down with the Wall Street Journal and said:

“I take more blood pressure medication than my doctors recommend because I want nice, calm blood flowing through my arteries. Does that make sense? They’d rather I take the smaller dose, but I know what I’m doing. My skin is delicate and I bleed constantly, so I use makeup to cover it. Sometimes at meetings I close my eyes because it’s relaxing—people think I’m sleeping, but I’m just blinking. I had some kind of scan—MRI or CT, I don’t really know—and in retrospect I shouldn’t have done it because it made people ask questions.”

The media response would have been:

  • Immediate calls for comprehensive cognitive evaluation

  • 24/7 medical expert panels

  • Front-page NYT/WaPo analysis

  • Democratic leadership quietly discussing 25th Amendment

  • Biden would likely resign within weeks under the pressure

Trump said all of this. The coverage has been... a fraction of what Biden’s minor verbal stumbles received.

The double standard I documented in Part 3 persists, but it’s finally beginning to crack. Even sympathetic outlets can’t frame “I want nice, thin blood” as normal presidential discourse.

What We’re Watching

The WSJ interview represents a milestone in the cognitive decline timeline I’ve been documenting since June. For the first time, Trump’s own medical explanations provide clinical evidence of the impairment experts have been warning about.

This isn’t speculation based on anonymous sources. This is primary source evidence—Trump’s own words—assessed by named medical experts, with corroborating witnesses and physical documentation.

The baseline problem isn’t theoretical anymore. Trump is explaining it himself—while lacking the cognitive insight to recognize what he’s revealing.

When a patient self-diagnoses with cartoon biology and medication levels his doctors oppose, when he can’t accurately describe recent medical tests, when he explains visible bleeding as evidence his approach is working—these are exactly the warning signs that would trigger intervention for any other 79-year-old.

Except this 79-year-old controls nuclear weapons and federal agencies.

The Question Remains

Lawrence O’Donnell asked in June 2025: “If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?”

The WSJ interview suggests we already know. The question is whether any institution will act on that knowledge.

Governor Pritzker called for the 25th Amendment in October. Half of Americans perceive cognitive decline in polling. Medical experts have reached professional consensus. Now Trump himself is providing on-the-record evidence through explanations that reveal impaired judgment.

The cognitive decline has moved from expert assessment to self-reported symptom. Trump thinks he’s explaining away concerns about bleeding and bruising. He’s actually confirming that his medical decision-making operates on magical thinking rather than biological reality.

In November, I documented Trump threatening to execute members of Congress. In January, he’s calmly explaining why he knows better than his doctors about blood physiology.

Both represent the same underlying problem: cognitive impairment has progressed to the point where Trump lacks insight into his own dysfunction.

The dam hasn’t just broken. The patient is narrating its collapse—while insisting the water is supposed to be there.

“And Then He Invaded Venezuela”

As this essay was being finalized on January 3, 2026, America reels from the news that President Trump ordered a ‘large scale strike’ against Venezuela, captured President Nicolás Maduro, and announced he would hold a press conference at Mar-a-Lago at 11 AM.

He did not consult Congress. He did not seek authorization for use of military force. When asked about constitutional authority, Trump told The New York Times: ‘We’ll discuss that. We’re going to have a news conference.’

The same cognitive pattern visible in Trump’s medical decision-making—’I know better than the experts’—now applies to war powers. His doctors say take less aspirin. He takes more. The Constitution says get Congressional approval for war. He invades anyway.

Trump’s justification? Drugs and oil. The same playbook I documented in ‘The Threat Matrix’ essay: fabricate threat, deploy military, seize resources.

This morning, a 79-year-old man who self-medicates based on cartoon biology (’nice, thin blood’), can’t remember what medical tests he’s had, and prioritizes appearance over health ordered military strikes on a sovereign nation—then announced it from his private club.

The cognitive impairment isn’t just about remembering which scan he had. It’s about judgment. And today, that impaired judgment started a war.
