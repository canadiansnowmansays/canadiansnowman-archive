The Democratic Promise

The Dealmaker

Part 6: Sam Altman and OpenAI

April 13, 2026

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon

Sam Altman addresses TED 2025 in Vancouver, April 11, 2025 — weeks after OpenAI's conversion to a fully for-profit corporation. Photo: Steve Jurvetson / CC BY

The Charter

OpenAI was founded in December 2015 with a charter that read like a manifesto for the democratic promise applied to artificial intelligence.

The organization would be a nonprofit. Its mission would be to ensure that artificial general intelligence — AI systems that match or exceed human cognitive abilities across all domains — would benefit “all of humanity.” Its research would be open. Its governance would be transparent. Its structure would prevent any single company or individual from capturing the most powerful technology ever built.

The founding donors included Elon Musk, Peter Thiel, Reid Hoffman, and Sam Altman, who would become its president. The founding statement warned explicitly against the danger of AI being controlled by a small number of corporations or governments. The entire point was distribution — spreading the benefits of AI as widely as possible, ensuring that the most transformative technology since electricity would not become the private property of a handful of billionaires.

It was Jobs’s democratic promise, updated for the age of artificial intelligence. Distribute the intelligence. Literally.

Ten years later, OpenAI is a for-profit corporation valued at over five hundred billion dollars, controlled by Sam Altman, funded by Microsoft, and competing aggressively for government contracts — including contracts that the original charter would have found antithetical to the mission.

The journey from the charter to the current reality is the story of one man’s dealmaking, and it is the cleanest case study in this series of how the democratic promise gets hollowed out — not through malice, not through stupidity, but through a series of individually reasonable decisions that collectively produce an outcome the original vision was specifically designed to prevent.

The Reasonable Decisions

Each step in OpenAI’s transformation had a reasonable justification.

The shift from open to closed research — stopping the publication of AI model weights and training details — was justified on safety grounds. If you publish the blueprints for increasingly powerful AI systems, bad actors will use them for harmful purposes. This is a real concern, and reasonable people can disagree about where to draw the line. But the effect was to transform OpenAI from an organization that distributed AI knowledge to one that concentrated it.

The creation of a “capped profit” subsidiary in 2019 was justified on financial grounds. Training large AI models requires billions of dollars in computing infrastructure. Nonprofit funding could not scale to meet the need. A for-profit entity could attract the investment necessary to compete with Google, which was spending tens of billions on AI research. This too was a real constraint. But the effect was to transform OpenAI from an organization that served the public interest to one that served its investors — with the public interest as a secondary consideration, subject to the cap.

The partnership with Microsoft — which invested thirteen billion dollars in OpenAI and received exclusive licensing rights to its technology — was justified on competitive grounds. Microsoft provided the computing infrastructure and distribution network that OpenAI needed to reach billions of users. Without Microsoft, ChatGPT would have remained a research demonstration. With Microsoft, it became a product used by hundreds of millions of people. But the effect was to make OpenAI dependent on a single corporate partner whose interests would inevitably shape the organization’s decisions.

And the ongoing conversion to a fully for-profit entity — announced in 2024, still unfolding in 2026 — is justified on structural grounds. The hybrid nonprofit-profit structure creates governance complexity, legal uncertainty, and investor concern. A clean for-profit structure simplifies everything. But the effect is to complete the transformation: the organization founded to prevent the concentration of AI power in corporate hands becomes... a corporation concentrating AI power.

Each decision, taken individually, was defensible. Taken together, they constitute the most methodical dismantling of a democratic technology charter in history.

The Dealmaker's Instinct

Sam Altman’s genius — and it is a genuine genius — is for deals. He understands power dynamics, incentive structures, and the art of positioning. He navigated the OpenAI board crisis of November 2023, when the board attempted to fire him over concerns that he was prioritizing commercial interests over the safety mission, with a masterclass in corporate maneuvering. Within days, he had the board reconstituted, the dissenters removed, and his position strengthened.

The episode revealed something important about Altman and about the democratic promise more broadly. The OpenAI board — the governance mechanism designed to ensure the organization served the public interest — tried to exercise its authority. It failed. The commercial momentum, the investor pressure, the employee loyalty to Altman personally, and the sheer weight of the money involved overwhelmed the governance structure that was supposed to prevent exactly this outcome.

The democratic safeguard was tested. It broke.

What we now know makes the failure more troubling, not less. In April 2026, an 18-month investigation by Ronan Farrow and Andrew Marantz in The New Yorker — drawing on more than 200 pages of internal documents and over 100 sources — revealed that the board had acted on a roughly 70-page dossier compiled by then-chief scientist Ilya Sutskever, alleging that Altman “exhibits a consistent pattern of lying” and had repeatedly misrepresented facts to executives and board members on safety-related matters. The board, in other words, was not acting capriciously. It was acting on documented evidence of precisely the character traits that make unchecked dealmaking dangerous. It acted anyway. And it lost anyway. The dossier exists. The board does not.

This is the pattern that recurs throughout this series. Democratic governance of technology requires institutional structures that can resist the gravitational pull of concentrated power and money. Those structures are consistently too weak to do the job. Berners-Lee’s web standards bodies could not prevent the platform enclosure. The antitrust system could not prevent Microsoft’s monopoly in time to matter. The OpenAI board could not prevent the conversion of a nonprofit mission into a for-profit enterprise.

The dealmaker always wins. Not because the dealmaker is evil — Altman may sincerely believe that the for-profit OpenAI will benefit humanity more than the nonprofit version could have. But because the dealmaker operates within a system that rewards concentration and punishes distribution, that funds ambition and starves caution, that treats democratic governance as an obstacle to be navigated rather than a principle to be honored.

The Pentagon Hours

And then came the moment that crystallized everything.

In February 2026, when Anthropic refused to accept Pentagon contract terms that would have required its AI systems to be available for “any lawful use” — including domestic mass surveillance and fully autonomous weapons — a decision documented in detail in Part 7 of this series — Sam Altman moved within hours to fill the gap. The deal was signed before the news cycle was complete.

What Altman accepted was not the same contract Anthropic had rejected, but it achieved the same outcome through different means. Where Anthropic sought outright contractual prohibitions on the uses it found unconscionable, OpenAI agreed to the “any lawful use” framing while embedding its safety commitments as promises to comply with existing law — a structurally weaker form of the same protections, dependent on the good faith of a government that had just designated a competitor a national security risk for asking similar questions. Altman later acknowledged the deal was “definitely rushed.” One of his own executives, robotics lead Caitlin Kalinowski, resigned within days, citing decisions that she said deserved “more deliberation than they got.”

Altman did not pause to ask whether the contract was consistent with OpenAI’s founding charter. He did not convene a board discussion about whether the terms he was accepting — terms a peer company had just refused on principle — were consistent with ensuring AGI benefits “all of humanity.” He did not consider whether racing to fill a contract that another company had rejected on ethical grounds might signal something about the direction of his organization.

He saw a deal. He made a deal. That is what dealmakers do.

The founding charter of OpenAI warned against AI being captured by corporate or government interests. Within a decade, the organization’s CEO was racing to hand its technology to both — and revising the terms afterward.

The Judgment

Altman is not a villain in the way Zuckerberg is a villain. Zuckerberg weaponized the democratic promise for profit while knowing the damage it caused. Altman dismantled the democratic promise through a series of compromises, each of which had a reasonable justification and none of which, individually, looked like a betrayal.

But the cumulative effect is the same. OpenAI was founded to distribute AI power. It now concentrates AI power. It was founded to operate transparently. It now operates as a competitive corporation guarding its proprietary technology. It was founded to serve all of humanity. It now serves its investors, its corporate partner, and the government clients willing to pay.

The irony that completes the picture: in April 2026, OpenAI published a thirteen-page policy document calling for a public wealth fund, higher capital gains taxes, a robot tax, and a four-day workweek — the nonprofit-turned-corporation repositioning itself as the champion of democratic distribution. The democratic promise is not just dead in Altman’s hands. It has been composted and replanted as a marketing strategy.

The democratic promise, in Altman’s hands, died the death of a thousand reasonable decisions. No single cut was fatal. But the patient is dead.
