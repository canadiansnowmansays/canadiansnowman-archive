The Trump Accountability Project

What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 1: The Early Years — Building a Foundation of Impunity (1968-1975)

December 9, 2025

By Stephen Gordon
CanadianSnowmanSays

Introduction: A Pattern Established

In November 2025, a Georgia judge dismissed the last pending criminal prosecution against President Donald Trump, effectively ending efforts to hold him criminally responsible for attempts to overturn the 2020 election. With three criminal cases dissolved since his re-election, the pattern is unmistakable: Donald Trump never falls; he evades justice and accountability.

This is not a new phenomenon. It’s a pattern established over six decades, beginning in his youth and refined throughout his business career. Unlike Marjorie Taylor Greene’s rapid rise and fall, or Matt Gaetz’s brief tenure as a failed attorney general nominee, Trump represents something more enduring and insidious — a systematic architecture of accountability evasion that has become his defining characteristic.

This series will document what Trump should have been held accountable for but never was, chronicling the mechanisms by which wealth, privilege, and calculated manipulation of legal systems have shielded him from consequences that would have destroyed ordinary Americans. We begin historically, because to understand Trump’s current impunity, we must understand how it was forged.

The Bone Spurs Gambit: Uncle Sam Gets Stiffed (1968)

The Vietnam War was raging. American casualties peaked in 1968, with 16,899 deaths that year alone. By the time the war ended in 1975, 58,220 Americans had died in Southeast Asia. Many were working-class young men who couldn’t afford college deferments or family connections to dodge the draft.

Donald Trump was not among them.

Between 1964 and 1968, Trump received four student deferments while attending Fordham University and the University of Pennsylvania’s Wharton School. These were legal and, for wealthy young men, routine. But in 1968, at age 22, Trump graduated and became eligible for military service. On July 9, 1968, his local draft board classified him “1-A” — available for unrestricted military service.

Two months later, everything changed.

In September 1968, Trump received a medical deferment for bone spurs in his heels, reclassifying him to “1-Y” (qualified for service only in time of war or national emergency). This was later upgraded to “4-F” (not qualified for military service), ensuring Trump would never serve.

The bone spurs diagnosis was a fraud.

In December 2018, the daughters of Dr. Larry Braunstein, a Queens podiatrist who died in 2007, revealed to The New York Times what had long been “family lore”: their father had provided Trump with the bone spurs diagnosis as a “favor” to Fred Trump, Donald’s father and Dr. Braunstein’s landlord. The doctor rented office space from Fred Trump and, according to a podiatry colleague, received rent concessions in exchange for the fraudulent diagnosis.

“It was something we would always discuss” among family and friends, Dr. Elysa Braunstein told the Times. The quid pro quo was clear: a fake diagnosis in exchange for favorable treatment from a powerful landlord.

In February 2019, Trump’s former personal attorney Michael Cohen testified under oath before the House Oversight Committee, confirming what many had suspected. “Mr. Trump claimed it was because of a bone spur,” Cohen testified, “but when I asked for medical records, he gave me none and said there was no surgery.” Cohen revealed that Trump told him: “You think I’m stupid, I wasn’t going to Vietnam.”

Trump’s changing story about the bone spurs reveals the fraud. In a 2016 interview with The New York Times, he claimed the condition was “temporary” and “not a big problem, but it was enough of a problem.” He couldn’t remember which foot was affected, later claiming both feet. He couldn’t recall the doctor’s name and provided no medical records. Trump biographer Michael D’Antonio told CNN that Trump once removed his shoes to show him the bone spurs — D’Antonio saw nothing.

The contrast with genuine service members is stark. Senator John McCain, who spent five and a half years as a prisoner of war in Vietnam and was tortured repeatedly, called out the inequity in a 2017 C-SPAN interview: “One aspect of the conflict, by the way, that I will never ever countenance is that we drafted the lowest income level of America and the highest income level found a doctor that would say that they had a bone spur. That is wrong.”

Trump’s response to McCain was characteristically contemptuous. In July 2015, Trump said of McCain: “He was a war hero because he was captured. I like people who weren’t captured.”

The bone spurs fraud represents Trump’s first documented evasion of accountability to the nation. While working-class Americans died in Vietnam, Trump used his father’s wealth and connections to escape service through a fabricated medical condition. Years later, Trump would call avoiding sexually transmitted diseases in the 1980s his “personal Vietnam” — a grotesque comparison that reveals his contempt for those who actually served.

No consequences followed. No investigation. No accountability. The pattern was established.

Chip Off the Old Block: Learning Discrimination from Fred (1954-1973)

To understand Donald Trump’s approach to accountability, we must understand his father. Fred Trump built a real estate empire on government contracts and discriminatory practices, teaching his son that rules were for other people and that wealth could shield you from consequences.

In 1954, when Donald was eight years old, Fred Trump was subpoenaed to testify before the Senate Banking Committee. The allegations: profiteering off public contracts by wildly inflating construction costs to obtain larger FHA mortgages. Under oath, Fred reluctantly admitted he had overstated development costs for his Beach Haven apartment complex in Brooklyn by at least $3.7 million — a scheme that allowed him to extract windfall profits from taxpayer-backed loans.

Fred Trump’s testimony revealed his philosophy: exploit every loophole, maximize profit from government programs, and express no remorse. He believed there was “nothing wrong with what he had done, as everything was done within legal boundaries.” He complained that the investigating committee had “caused damage to his reputation” — not that he had defrauded the government, but that he’d been caught.

This was the business model Donald inherited.

But Fred Trump’s most pernicious legacy was systematic racial discrimination. Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, the Trump Organization maintained policies explicitly designed to exclude Black and Puerto Rican tenants from their properties. Former rental agent Stanley Leibowitz recalled Fred Trump’s instructions regarding a Black applicant in 1963: “Take the application and put it in a drawer and leave it there.”

Folk singer Woody Guthrie, who rented from Fred Trump in 1950, wrote a song capturing the discrimination: his lyrics made clear that African Americans were not welcome as tenants in Trump properties. By the time the Fair Housing Act became law in 1968, the Trump Organization had refined its discriminatory practices into a sophisticated system.

Donald Trump was present and learning. According to The New York Times, while there is no evidence Trump personally set the rental policies, “he was on hand while they were in place, working out of a cubicle in Trump Management’s Brooklyn offices as early as the summer of 1968.” Trump was being groomed for real estate success at his father’s knee, and racial discrimination was part of the curriculum.

The Justice Department Comes Knocking: Trump’s First Federal Case (1973)

In October 1973, the U.S. Department of Justice filed one of the largest housing discrimination cases of the era against Fred Trump, Donald Trump (then 27 and president of Trump Management), and their company. The complaint alleged systemic violations of the Fair Housing Act of 1968.

The evidence was overwhelming. Federal investigators documented a coordinated scheme to discriminate against Black and Puerto Rican applicants:

Coded Applications: Trump employees marked Black applicants’ rental applications with a “C” for “colored.” Some applications were marked with “No. 9” — a code referring to “low lifes” such as “blacks, Puerto Ricans, apparent drug users, or any other type of undesirable applicant.”

Systematic Rejection: Black applicants were routinely told apartments were unavailable, only to have white “testers” immediately shown the same apartments.

Explicit Instructions: Former superintendent Thomas Miranda testified that Trump Management employees instructed him to attach a separate piece of paper with a big letter “C” on it to any application filed by a Black apartment-seeker.

False Representations: Black applicants were falsely told there were no vacancies or that they needed to pay higher deposits.

The discrimination was brazen and well-documented. One rental agent who worked for Trump for two weeks said that when he asked Fred Trump if he should rent to Blacks, he was told it was “absolutely against the law to discriminate.” But when pressed, Fred instructed him “not to rent to blacks” and advised him to “get rid of the blacks that were in the building by telling them cheap housing was available for them at only $500 down payment, which Trump would offer to pay himself.”

Donald Trump’s response to the lawsuit revealed the playbook he would use for the next five decades. He:

  1. Denied everything: Called the allegations “absolutely ridiculous”

  2. Attacked the investigators: Claimed the government was trying to force him to rent to “welfare recipients”

  3. Hired the most aggressive lawyer possible: Retained Roy Cohn, Senator Joseph McCarthy’s former red-baiting counsel

  4. Went on offense: Filed a $100 million countersuit against the Justice Department for defamation

The judge dismissed Trump’s countersuit. After fighting for two years, Trump signed a consent decree in June 1975. The agreement was described as “one of the most far-reaching ever negotiated.” It prohibited the Trumps from discriminating, required them to advertise vacancies in minority newspapers, promote minorities to professional jobs, and list vacancies on a preferential basis. The decree also ordered the Trumps to “thoroughly acquaint themselves personally on a detailed basis” with the Fair Housing Act.

Crucially, the settlement included language that it was “in no way an admission” of wrongdoing — another Trump tactic that would become standard: settle without admitting guilt, declare victory, and claim vindication.

But Trump didn’t comply with the consent decree. In 1978, the Justice Department accused the Trumps of violating the agreement. A DOJ lawyer wrote to Roy Cohn: “We believe that an underlying pattern of discrimination continues to exist in the Trump Management organization.”

Again, no real accountability followed. The case was eventually closed in 1982, with Trump having faced no criminal charges, no significant financial penalty beyond legal fees, and no admission of wrongdoing. He had learned a crucial lesson: with good lawyers and aggressive counterattacks, you can fight the Justice Department to a standstill.

The Psychological Architecture: Understanding Trump’s Immunity Shield

These early experiences — the bone spurs fraud and the housing discrimination case — established patterns that would define Trump’s entire career:

1. Leverage Wealth and Connections: Fred Trump’s money bought the bone spurs diagnosis and the best lawyers to fight the Justice Department. Wealth creates options unavailable to ordinary citizens.

2. Never Admit Wrongdoing: From the consent decree onward, Trump has maintained that settlements without admissions of guilt constitute vindication. This allows him to claim he was “proven” innocent while avoiding the risk of trial.

3. Attack and Counterattack: Filing a $100 million countersuit against the Justice Department was audacious, but it worked. It put the government on defense and signaled Trump would make any prosecution expensive and painful.

4. Exploit Legal Technicalities: The “partnership-interest-for-debt” exception and other technical arguments would later allow Trump to avoid reporting hundreds of millions in forgiven debt as income. He learned early that sophisticated lawyers could find loopholes.

5. Delay, Delay, Delay: Fighting the discrimination case for two years, Trump learned that time favors defendants. Witnesses’ memories fade, public attention wanes, and prosecutors move on.

6. Control the Narrative: Trump consistently reframed the cases — the bone spurs were “temporary,” the Justice Department was “forcing” him to rent to welfare recipients. Truth became malleable through repetition and aggressive messaging.

The Privilege of Impunity

Most fundamentally, these early episodes reveal the role of class privilege in American accountability systems. Trump’s classmates at Penn who served in Vietnam didn’t have fathers who owned buildings where compliant doctors practiced. Black families facing discrimination from Trump properties couldn’t afford Roy Cohn’s fees or drag out litigation for years.

Trump learned that the legal system has two tiers: one for people with money, and one for everyone else. In the first tier, consequences are optional, accountability is negotiable, and settlements without admissions of guilt allow you to claim victory.

The bone spurs fraud should have resulted in criminal prosecution for draft evasion through false statements. It didn’t. The housing discrimination should have resulted in massive financial penalties and perhaps criminal charges under civil rights laws. It didn’t. Instead, Trump emerged largely unscathed, his reputation among fellow real estate developers enhanced by his willingness to fight the government.

Looking Ahead

These formative experiences from 1968-1975 established the template Trump would apply throughout his career. In the next installment, we’ll examine how Trump expanded this playbook in the 1980s and 1990s, as casino bankruptcies allowed him to dump billions in debt onto creditors, contractors, and bondholders while walking away personally enriched.

The story of Trump’s accountability evasion is ultimately a story about American institutions and their failure to hold powerful men responsible for their actions. From the Queens draft board to the Justice Department, from business creditors to voters, Trump has consistently found that consequences are for other people.

Understanding this history is essential because it reveals a profound truth: Trump’s current immunity from prosecution is not an aberration. It is the logical conclusion of a lifetime spent perfecting the art of evading accountability.

And until we confront the structural privileges that make such evasion possible, he won’t be the last.

Part 2 will examine Trump’s casino bankruptcies of the 1990s, where he left billions in debt and thousands of unpaid workers in his wake while extracting personal wealth and avoiding personal bankruptcy. The pattern established in his youth would scale up dramatically.
