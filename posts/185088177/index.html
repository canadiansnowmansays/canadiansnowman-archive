Two Americas on the Same Road

What This Reveals About America

January 19, 2026

Boots and Barefoot: Two Americas on the Same Road

On a January morning in South Carolina, Buddhist monks emerged from the fog like apparitions. Saffron and maroon robes cut through the gray winter landscape as they walked single file along a country road, some barefoot despite the cold pavement. Ahead of them trotted Aloka, a rescue dog from India who has become an unlikely symbol of their 2,300-mile pilgrimage. Small crowds lined the roadside in reverent silence, palms pressed together in respect, waiting to receive peace bracelets and blessings from strangers who had walked hundreds of miles to offer nothing but compassion.

That same morning, in a different part of America, ICE agents in tactical gear approached a home in pre-dawn darkness. The door came down. Families separated. Children crying. Neighbors watching from behind curtains, afraid to intervene, afraid to look away. The agents wore body armor and carried weapons. They demanded compliance and got it. When they left, they took fathers, mothers, people who had lived in America for decades. The administration would later celebrate this as a victory.

Two groups moving through America in January 2026. Both claiming purpose. Both operating under the vast umbrella of what we call American values. One barefoot, one in boots. One spreading peace, one spreading fear. The question isn’t which America is real. Both are. The question is which one we choose to be.

The Walk

Nineteen Buddhist monks from the Huong Dao Vipassana Bhavana Center in Fort Worth, Texas began walking on October 26, 2025. Their destination: Washington, D.C., 2,300 miles away. Their mission: to raise awareness of peace, loving kindness, and compassion across America. Their method: put one foot in front of the other, day after day, through ten states, maintaining a meditative pace that prioritizes presence over speed.

They follow a tradition established in the 1990s by the Venerable Maha Ghosananda, a Cambodian monk who led peace walks through landmine-riddled areas after genocide and civil war devastated his country. Ghosananda’s walks were acts of national healing, deliberate steps toward reconciliation in places where trust had been shattered and trauma ran deep. The monks walking through America today carry that same intention into a different landscape of division.

Three of the monks, including their leader Bhikkhu Paññākāra, walk the entire journey barefoot. Not as performance or publicity stunt, but as practice—vulnerability as spiritual discipline, discomfort as meditation, the physical reality of the path they’re walking. Their feet blister and bleed on the asphalt. They walk anyway.

On November 19, 2025, while walking along U.S. Highway 90 near Dayton, Texas, a distracted truck driver struck the group’s escort vehicle, which then hit several monks. Bhante Dam Phommasan suffered injuries so severe his leg had to be amputated. He underwent surgery in early December. In early January 2026, after weeks of recovery, Bhante Dam rejoined the group in Georgia. He continues the walk.

The monks didn’t stop. They didn’t demand justice or compensation. They didn’t sue or protest or hold press conferences. They absorbed the tragedy, continued their mission, and when their brother could walk again, they welcomed him back. This is what their version of strength looks like.

Aloka, the four-year-old Indian Pariah dog who joined a previous peace walk in India and became part of this American pilgrimage, walks alongside them. He recently had surgery for a chronic cruciate ligament injury but rejoined the group for brief stretches as he recovers. Nearly 600,000 people follow his dedicated Facebook page. Across all platforms, the Walk for Peace has gathered over three million followers who track their progress daily, watch their updates, and line the roads to witness their passage.

The crowds that gather are remarkable for their silence. No cheering, no shouting, no noise. People drive hundreds of miles to stand quietly by the roadside. They bring flowers. They receive bracelets. They bow. On Christmas night in Opelika, Alabama, a United Methodist church expected a small gathering and got 1,000 people. The reverend described it as feeling like the Magi appearing on Christ’s birthday—pilgrims from afar bringing gifts of peace.

Sherry Cobb drove 100 miles from Salem, South Carolina, to see the monks pass through Saluda. She had lost her father four months earlier. “There’s a lot of turmoil in my life right now,” she told reporters. A monk handed her a peace bracelet. “Very emotional,” she said. “That’s really all I can say about it.”

Former Duke basketball player Robert Brickey watched them in Charlotte and observed something crucial: “Rather than talking about peace, the monks are demonstrating what peace looks like. I think people are excited about that, because at this time in our world, in our nation, there’s a lot of chaos, and people are starved for love and unity.”

The monks continue through North Carolina, heading toward Raleigh and then north through Virginia and Maryland to Washington, D.C. in mid-February. There, they will ask Congress to recognize Buddha’s day of birth and enlightenment as a federal holiday. Not money, not power, not special treatment. Just recognition that compassion matters enough to mark on a calendar.

The Raids

While the monks walk through the Carolinas, ICE agents are conducting operations across America with increasing frequency and force. The Trump administration has made these raids central to its homeland security strategy, deploying tactical teams in pre-dawn hours to arrest undocumented immigrants, many of whom have lived in the United States for decades.

The operations follow a pattern: multiple agents in tactical gear arrive before sunrise. They surround homes. They knock, or they don’t. Doors open through compliance or force. Families are separated on the spot—parents taken, children left behind or handed to relatives who may or may not be present. The process is efficient, practiced, designed to minimize resistance and maximize arrests.

Arrests of people with no criminal record surged by 2,450 percent in Trump’s first year back in office. The targets are often people who have built lives here—landscapers, restaurant workers, construction laborers, housekeepers. They work jobs, pay taxes, send kids to school, participate in communities. Many fled violence or poverty or desperation. Many have been here so long this is the only home their children know.

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem attends ICE raid at Los Angeles County home

The administration calls these operations enforcement actions, security measures, promises kept. The message is clear: compliance or consequences. Federal immigration officers have shot eleven people since September, most fired into vehicles—a tactic law enforcement experts have tried for decades to curtail. Three people have died, including Renee Nicole Good, a U.S. citizen shot four times by an ICE agent in Minneapolis. The administration immediately defended the shooting, with Trump claiming Good “violently, willfully, and viciously ran over” an officer. Video evidence tells a different story, but nuance has no place in this narrative.

Communities respond with fear. Neighbors watch raids happen and wonder if they’re next. Schools see attendance drop. Churches become sanctuaries. Advocacy groups scramble to document who was taken and where they were sent. The agents wear body armor, carry weapons, have legal authority and federal backing. When ICE comes, you comply. You cooperate. You don’t fight back because fighting back makes everything worse.

And it’s celebrated. The administration points to arrest numbers as victories. There’s satisfaction in the show of force, pride in the display of authority. This is what happens when America gets serious. This is what strength looks like.

The Contrast

Put them side by side: monks in robes and agents in tactical gear, both moving through America in January 2026.

Authority and Vulnerability: The monks carry nothing but prayer beads and peace bracelets. They’re vulnerable by design—barefoot, unarmed, dependent on communities to shelter and feed them. They have no power to compel anyone to do anything. Their authority comes from what they’re willing to endure, not what they can force others to accept.

ICE agents carry weapons, wear armor, arrive in numbers with legal authority backed by federal power. They can compel compliance, break into homes, arrest people, separate families. Their authority comes from force—the capacity to impose consequences on those who resist. The monks ask for nothing except to be allowed to pass through. ICE demands everything: papers, compliance, submission.

Response to Tragedy: When Bhante Dam lost his leg, the monks absorbed the tragedy and continued their mission. No demands for compensation. No lawsuits. No stopping the walk in protest. They visited their brother in the hospital, supported his recovery, welcomed him back when he could walk again, and kept going. The injury became part of the journey, not an excuse to abandon it.

When ICE operations traumatize families—children separated from parents, decades-long residents deported, communities living in fear, people shot—the operations expand. More resources, more agents, more arrests. The trauma isn’t a problem to be solved but evidence that enforcement is working. One group encounters suffering and continues spreading peace. The other inflicts suffering and calls it victory.

What They Ask: The monks request quiet respect. Stand by the roadside if you want to witness their passage. Bow if you feel moved. Receive a bracelet, a blessing, a moment of stillness. Reflect on peace. They trust communities to receive them with kindness.

ICE demands compliance. Open your door. Show your papers. Answer questions. Submit to interrogation. Don’t resist. Don’t interfere. Don’t harbor those we’re seeking. Communities aren’t invited to participate—they’re required to cooperate or face obstruction charges. ICE treats communities as potential threats requiring management and control.

The Mission: The monks are walking 2,300 miles to ask Congress to recognize Buddha’s birthday as a federal holiday. It’s entirely symbolic. It won’t change anyone’s life materially. It’s simply a request that the country acknowledge, officially, that compassion and enlightenment matter.

ICE raids homes to arrest people for the crime of being present without permission. The stated goal is immigration enforcement, border security, protecting American jobs. The practical effect is families destroyed, communities terrorized, people who’ve lived here for decades suddenly vanished. The mission requires breaking into homes, separating parents from children, demonstrating that America’s authority extends into bedrooms and living rooms.

Leadership: Bhikkhu Paññākāra leads from the front, barefoot, the most vulnerable person in the group. He walks every mile his followers walk. He endures what they endure. He asks nothing of them he doesn’t ask of himself. His authority comes from example.

Trump directs ICE operations from golf courses and rally stages, protected by Secret Service, never at risk, never vulnerable. He demands that agents enforce policies he’ll never personally execute. He celebrates their arrests while remaining comfortably distant from the trauma inflicted. His authority comes from command.

Public Reception: Over three million people follow the monks’ journey online. Thousands line the roads. Communities host them, feed them, shelter them. Churches open their doors. Families bring children to witness people walking for peace simply because they believe peace matters.

The response to ICE operations is fear, resistance, and shame. Communities hide neighbors. Churches offer sanctuary. Schools coordinate safety networks. Americans watch raids happen and feel embarrassed that this is what we’re doing, that this is what our government represents.

What This Reveals About America

Both groups operate in the same country, under the same Constitution, claiming the same American values. Both say they’re serving a higher purpose. But only one has government backing, federal resources, legal authority, and presidential celebration.

Stephen Miller, Trump’s deputy chief of policy and the architect of these immigration operations, has built his career on a simple equation: enforcement is strength, compassion is weakness. In his worldview, borders protected by force demonstrate national power. Tactical teams breaking into homes at dawn show America is serious. The display of overwhelming force—body armor, weapons, pre-dawn raids—is the performance of strength.

The monks offer a different understanding entirely. They demonstrate that strength looks like walking 2,300 miles barefoot toward a principle you believe in. That real power comes from enduring suffering without inflicting it on others. That leadership means making yourself the most vulnerable person in the room, not the most protected. When Bhante Dam lost his leg and returned to continue the walk, he showed more strength than any armed agent breaking down a door.

Miller’s strength requires armor and weapons and the capacity to make others afraid. The monks’ strength requires nothing but the willingness to keep walking when your feet bleed, to bless strangers when your body aches, to spread peace when you’ve lost your brother’s leg to a distracted driver. Miller’s strength needs tools of violence. The monks’ strength needs only commitment to a path.

This isn’t abstract philosophy—it’s playing out right now on American roads and in American homes. The administration celebrates tactical operations while millions of Americans drive hundreds of miles to stand silently by roadsides and receive blessings from barefoot strangers. That gap—between what our government celebrates and what our people are starved for—tells you everything about where we are.

From my perspective as a Canadian watching this unfold, the contrast is stark. The America that inspired the world looks more like barefoot monks blessing strangers than armed agents breaking into homes. Religious pilgrims walking peacefully through the country seeking nothing but the opportunity to spread compassion—that’s supposed to be what America protects and celebrates. That’s First Amendment freedom.

Paramilitary operations targeting long-term residents, separating families, deploying overwhelming force against people who can’t fight back—that’s what America used to condemn when other countries did it. That’s the behavior we associated with authoritarian regimes, not democratic republics.

Yet here we are. Both happening simultaneously. Both claiming American legitimacy. The choice of which to support reveals what we actually value—not what we say we value, but what we value in practice when it costs us something.

The Nobel Peace Prize Question

When the monks reach Washington in mid-February, they should be nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize. Not as symbolic gesture or political statement, but because they meet the criteria in ways that expose how thoroughly we’ve corrupted our understanding of what deserves recognition.

The Nobel Peace Prize honors those who have “done the most or the best work for fraternity between nations, for the abolition or reduction of standing armies and for the holding and promotion of peace congresses.” The monks are walking 2,300 miles through a divided America to promote exactly that—fraternity, peace, the reduction of the impulse toward force and violence that dominates our politics.

Consider what they’ve sacrificed. Three months on the road, barefoot, vulnerable, dependent on the kindness of strangers. One of their brothers lost his leg and came back to continue the walk. They’ve endured weather, traffic, injury, and the constant physical toll of putting one foot in front of another, day after day, to demonstrate that peace requires sacrifice from those who claim to seek it.

Compare that to Trump, who has been nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize multiple times by his political allies. What has he sacrificed? What has he endured? He directs operations from safety, celebrates force from behind protection, demands others implement policies he’ll never personally execute. He’s never vulnerable. He’s never at risk. He asks nothing of himself that he asks of others.

The historical precedent supports the monks. The Venerable Maha Ghosananda, who established this peace walk tradition in Cambodia after genocide and civil war, walked through landmine-riddled areas to foster national healing. His work demonstrated that peace requires physical courage, personal sacrifice, and the willingness to walk through danger toward reconciliation. The monks walking through America today carry that same legacy into a nation torn by division, fear, and the worship of force as strength.

Trump’s claim to peace credentials rests on... what exactly? The Abraham Accords? Agreements made by people other than him while he signed papers? His threat to use military force against protesters, his celebration of ICE raids, his constant rhetoric of dominance and revenge—these aren’t the actions of someone working toward peace. These are the actions of someone who understands power only as the capacity to make others submit.

The monks understand power differently. They understand that real authority comes from example, not force. That real leadership means making yourself the most vulnerable person in the group, not the most protected. That real strength looks like continuing to walk when your feet bleed, not ordering others to break down doors while you remain safe.

If we lived in a world that actually valued peace over the performance of strength, the monks would be nominated and Trump would be seen as the authoritarian he is. But we don’t live in that world. We live in a world where Miller’s equation—force equals strength, compassion equals weakness—dominates our politics. Where tactical gear and pre-dawn raids are celebrated as victories. Where walking 2,300 miles barefoot to ask for recognition of compassion is seen as naive.

The Nobel Peace Prize won’t go to the monks. It might go to Trump, if his allies have their way. That fact alone tells you everything about what we’ve become, and what we’ve chosen to value.

Choosing Who We Are

The monks will reach Washington in mid-February. They’ll have walked 2,300 miles. Their feet will be destroyed. They’ll be exhausted. They’ll have endured weather, traffic, injury, and the loss of their brother’s leg to a distracted driver. They’ll have demonstrated, mile after mile, what they believe peace requires of those who claim to seek it.

And they’ll ask Congress for something simple: please recognize that Buddha’s birthday matters. Please acknowledge that enlightenment and compassion deserve space on the national calendar.

ICE will be operating blocks away. Agents in tactical gear will be conducting raids, making arrests, separating families. The administration will be celebrating enforcement victories. The machine of removal will continue its work, powerful and efficient and thoroughly backed by federal authority.

Both will claim to represent America.

We choose which one we are. We choose through what we celebrate, what we fund, what we authorize, what we tolerate, and what we refuse to accept in our name. We choose through whether we stand quietly by the roadside to receive a blessing from strangers who walked 2,300 miles to offer us peace, or whether we look away when tactical teams break into our neighbors’ homes.

The monks are barefoot. The agents wear boots. Both walk America’s roads. Only one asks for our blessing. Only one deserves it.
