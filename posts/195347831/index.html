The Iran Crisis

The Test of Wills

Part LXVIII Day 55: The ceasefire holds — indefinitely, on Trump’s word. The Lebanon truce has been extended three weeks. The Strait of Hormuz is closed by two simultaneous blockades

April 24, 2026

A mural in Tehran illustrates Iran’s grip on the Strait of Hormuz. Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

I. The Quagmire Declared

On Thursday morning, a senior Iranian official posted on X that his country’s fighters were hiding in sea caves near the Strait of Hormuz, preparing to “devastate the aggressors.” Eighteen minutes later, Trump posted on Truth Social that he had ordered the Navy to “shoot and kill any boat” putting mines in the waters of the strait.

This is how the war is being fought now. Not with B-2 bombers. With posts.

Troianovski’s analysis in The New York Times is the frame for Week Nine. The Brookings Institution’s Suzanne Maloney had assumed a diplomatic deal would resolve the standoff quickly. She is no longer assuming that. “He’s stuck with this, for as long as the strait remains closed,” she said. “The speed with which this became a quagmire for the United States has been, also, quite stunning.”

Trump told reporters Thursday that he had “all the time in the World, but Iran doesn’t.” He told Fox News Wednesday there was “no time pressure” and “no timeline” for ending the war. He told reporters Thursday that “if they don’t want to make a deal, I’ll finish it up militarily.” He said Americans would need to wait “a little while” before gas prices come down. Earlier that morning he had posted that the strait was “Sealed up Tight” until Iran could make a deal. The following morning, Brent crude was at $107.

The Eurasia Group’s Cliff Kupchan placed the strategic situation precisely: a monthslong shutdown of the strait is only beginning to “enter the collective mind of the markets. We’re in uncharted territory.”

II. The Generals Who Are Running Iran

The most important piece of reporting published since the war began appeared Thursday. Fassihi’s — “A New Era and New Leadership: The Generals Who Are Running Iran” — based on 23 interviews including senior officials, Revolutionary Guards members, and individuals with ties to both Khameneis, is the definitive account of who is actually running Iran.

Mojtaba Khamenei has not been seen or heard since his appointment in March. He is in hiding, surrounded by doctors. One leg operated on three times, awaiting a prosthetic. Surgery on one hand. Face and lips severely burned — cannot speak clearly. Messages reach him via handwritten notes passed through a human chain of couriers on back roads. His guidance returns the same way. He has not recorded a video or audio message because he does not want to appear vulnerable or sound weak in his first public address.

The real power is a collective of Revolutionary Guards commanders. Chatham House’s Sanam Vakil: “Mojtaba is not yet in full command or control. He is presented with fait accompli presentations right now.” Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group: “Mojtaba is subservient to the Revolutionary Guards because he owes his position and the survival of the system to them.”

Gen. Mohammad Bagher Zolghadr, Gen. Yahya Rahim Safavi and Brig. Gen. Ahmad Vahidi Credit...Associated Press; EPA, via Shutterstock; Associated Press

The key figures: IRGC commander in chief Brig. Gen. Ahmad Vahidi. Gen. Zolghadr, newly appointed head of the Supreme National Security Council, former hard-line IRGC commander. Ghalibaf — longtime friend of Mojtaba from the Iran-Iraq war, when both were teenagers in the Habib Battalion. The “triangle of power” — Mojtaba, IRGC intelligence chief Hossein Taeb, and Ghalibaf — met weekly for working lunches at the ayatollah’s compound for years. They are now governing Iran.

What happened when the second Islamabad round collapsed: IRGC commander Vahidi and several generals argued talks were futile because the blockade showed Trump wanted surrender, not negotiation. President Pezeshkian and Foreign Minister Araghchi disagreed — Pezeshkian warned of $300 billion in economic losses and the need for sanctions relief. The generals won. Talks fell apart. Araghchi has been marginalized. The elected government sidelined, told to focus only on domestic affairs.

A hard-line fringe — Saeed Jalili’s faction, whose brother runs state television — is not dominant but influential. When Araghchi announced the strait was opening on Friday, hard-liners attacked him for betrayal. Less than an hour later, Trump said the blockade continues. The next day Iran reimposed strict control.

Ghalibaf on state television Saturday: “Sometimes, I see our people say we destroyed them. No, we did not destroy them; you need to understand this. Our military gains do not mean that we are more powerful than the United States.”

Trump’s “new regime, more reasonable” claim is empirically false. Power is now in the hands of an entrenched, hard-line military. Clerical influence is waning. The IRGC is consolidating.

III. The Munitions Reckoning

The Schmitt/Swan reporting — “Iran War Has Drained U.S. Supplies of Critical, Costly Weapons” — is the military accounting that the White House called false. It is not false. It is sourced to internal Pentagon estimates and congressional officials, and it will define the strategic debate for months.

Since the war began: approximately 1,100 JASSM-ER stealth cruise missiles used — close to the total US stockpile, leaving roughly 1,500. These are the missiles designed for a war with China. More than 1,000 Tomahawk cruise missiles fired — roughly ten times annual production. More than 1,200 Patriot interceptors used at nearly $4 million each — the US produced only 600 in all of 2025. More than 1,000 Precision Strike and ATACMS missiles depleted.

First two days alone: $5.6 billion in munitions. Total war cost: $28-35 billion, approximately $1 billion per day. The Abraham Lincoln carrier strike group diverted from the South China Sea. Two Marine Expeditionary Units — 4,400 Marines — sent to the Middle East from the Pacific. THAAD interceptors moved from South Korea for the first time in history.

CSIS: “While sufficient munitions exist to wage this war, high expenditure of Tomahawks and other missiles in Operation Epic Fury creates risks for the United States in other theaters — particularly the Western Pacific.”

Senator Reed of Rhode Island: “At current production rates, reconstituting what we have expended could take years.”

The launch vehicle of a THAAD system in Seongju, South Korea. Patriot missiles and interceptors from THAAD have been redirected to the Middle East.Credit...Yonhap/EPA, via Shutterstock

Production expansion agreements to quadruple precision munitions exist on paper — a seven-year deal with Lockheed Martin announced in January. No movement to actually begin the expanded production, because the Pentagon cannot find the funding.

The Navy Secretary who was supposed to oversee naval shipbuilding — John Phelan, who championed the “Trump-class battleship” — was fired Wednesday amid feuding with Hegseth and Deputy Defense Secretary Feinberg. First service secretary to leave the administration. Pentagon leadership in turmoil during an active naval blockade.

IV. Negotiating Styles: A Collision

The Sanger analysis — “’Immediate Results’ vs. ‘The Long Game’: The U.S. and Iran Face Off” — frames the Islamabad impasse with the authority of someone who covered every round of JCPOA negotiations at Lausanne. Robert Malley, who negotiated the 2015 deal: “Trump is impulsive and temperamental; Iran’s leadership is stubborn and tenacious. Trump demands immediate results; Iran’s leadership plays the long game. Trump insists on a flashy, headline-grabbing outcome; Iran’s leadership sweats every detail. Trump believes brute force can compel obedience; Iran’s leadership is prepared to endure enormous pain rather than concede on core interests.”

The 2015 deal took the better part of two years, ran more than 160 pages plus five technical annexes. Kushner and Witkoff travel light, with no entourage of experts and no national laboratory backup. Chief 2015 negotiator Wendy Sherman: “We finally got the parameters agreed upon at the hotel. And then a few days later the supreme leader came out and said, ‘Actually, some very different terms were required.’”

Iran’s core grievance, documented across every round of talks: Trump pulled out of the 2015 deal in 2018 while Iran was complying. Then attacked Iran twice during active diplomatic negotiations. Iranian negotiator Karimi: “Negotiators do not even know whether they could be attacked or not when they are in the air.”

The Chait Atlantic piece — “The Posting Will Continue Until Morale Improves” — embedded the essential reporting: CNN reported Monday the US and Iran were on the verge of a deal when Trump’s posts undermined talks — “The Iranians didn’t appreciate POTUS negotiating through social media and making it appear as if they had signed off on issues they hadn’t yet agreed to.” Axios reported Trump is willing to give “another three to five days of ceasefire to allow the Iranians to get their shit together.” The Washington Post reported Trump “has authorized U.S. negotiators to consider a bargain that involves many of the same trade-offs one of his predecessors confronted.” The great dealmaker, operating under no pressure whatsoever, may end up with a pact similar to one of history’s worst deals ever.

V. Nuclear Dust

The Broadwater/Sanger piece — “Trump Keeps Talking About Iran’s ‘Nuclear Dust.’ What Is It?” — provides the technical demystification the war has needed. Trump’s “nuclear dust” is 970 pounds of uranium enriched to 60%, stored in canisters roughly the size of large scuba tanks at Isfahan, believed buried under US bombing rubble from the June 2025 strikes. It is not dust. It is a gas when stored, a solid at room temperature, volatile and highly toxic if exposed to moisture.

A satellite image from February showing construction on buildings at the Isfahan nuclear site in Iran that were destroyed during U.S. airstrikes last June Planet Labs, via Reuters

American intelligence believes the Iranians have dug down to access the material. No evidence any of it has been moved. Both sides face the same challenge: conditions of the underground storage are unknown, the tanks may be damaged, and the material is too volatile to move without expert handling. Cato Institute’s Justin Logan: “This would be a mission that would take a lot of time, and there would be a lot of nerds that aren’t good at killing people that would need to be involved here. So the idea of doing this while we have our swords drawn strikes me as crazy.”

Trump: “We’ll get all the nuclear dust.” Iranian foreign ministry: uranium transfer “not discussed.” The gap between Trump’s public claims and the documented negotiating reality is now so wide it has become a structural feature of the conflict.

VI. The Strait’s Numbers

Eavis’ reporting — “Iran Again Tightens Its Grip on Shipping in the Strait of Hormuz” — provides the definitive traffic accounting. Tuesday: one ship passed through the strait all day (S&P Global Market Intelligence). Down from 130 per day before the war. Wednesday: MSC Francesca and Epaminondas seized without radio warning. The Epaminondas — Greek-owned, Liberian-flagged, managed by Technomar Shipping — sustained what Greece’s foreign minister called “extremely wide damage.” IRGC video verified by the Times: balaclava-clad Guards scaling the vessel on a ladder from a speedboat flying the Iranian flag.

Lloyd’s List Intelligence data — disputed by CENTCOM: from March 2 through Sunday, 308 Iranian-linked ships transited versus 90 with no Iranian links. Seven Iranian-linked ships may have passed through both Hormuz and the US blockade since April 13.

Windward analyst Wiese Bockmann documented the Friday whipsaw in real time: “What I saw on Saturday morning was nascent confidence. Then, literally, before my eyes, I saw everything start to turn around.”

Kelanic of Defense Priorities: “It’s not going to reassure anybody to start up shipping again through Hormuz if the U.S. Navy itself refuses to operate in Hormuz.” Hapag-Lloyd’s Boenaes: “It makes the situation more unpredictable when warnings are not given before attacks are taking place.”

A second tanker — M/T Majestic X — boarded by Navy forces in the Indian Ocean. The M/T Tifani earlier this week. Four Iranian vessels now in US custody or under US escort. The blockade is global.

IEA head Birol on Thursday: the world faces “the biggest energy security threat in history.” Loss of approximately 13 million barrels per day. Potential jet fuel crunch in Europe within weeks.

VII. The War Powers Clock

May 1. Nine days away as of Monday, seven as of Friday. Trump formally notified Congress of the operation on March 2, starting the 60-day clock under the 1973 War Powers Resolution.

Senate blocked a Democratic war powers resolution for the fifth time Wednesday. But the Jimison reporting — “A 60-Day Deadline Could Pressure Trump on Ending the Iran War” — documents the cracks: Senator Curtis of Utah will “not support ongoing military action beyond a 60-day window without congressional approval.” House Foreign Affairs chairman Mast: there could be “a different vote count after 60 days.” Senator Murkowski working on an AUMF, not yet introduced. Congress has not voted to authorize military force since 2002.

Trump’s escape route: administrations of both parties have argued the War Powers Resolution is unconstitutional. Obama continued Libya operations past 60 days in 2011. Murphy of Connecticut: “Many Republicans are on record having set the 60-day mark as somehow legally important. I do think it will be harder for Republicans to continue to look the other way once we are out of the 60 days.”

Loading bombs onto a U.S. Air Force B-1 bomber at an air base in Britain last month.Credit...Henry Nicholls/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Hegseth at Thursday’s Pentagon press conference: blockade will continue “as long as it takes.” “The real endless war is the war Iran has waged on the United States for 47 years.” He did not mention that the US war prompted Iran to close the strait.

VIII. The World That Is Forming

The Goodman analysis — “Hoarding Is Driving Energy Prices Higher Everywhere” — is the economic equity indictment of the war. China and Thailand banned jet fuel exports. Europe outbidding distressed Asian rivals. Philippines imports 90% of its oil from the Persian Gulf — declared national emergency, now rationing fuel. India raiding businesses for hoarding cooking gas. Stiglitz: “The post-World War II framework was based on the idea that boundaries don’t matter. But once you have national hoarding, that’s no longer true. Borders matter.”

The Krolik analysis — “War and Sanctions Accelerate China’s Currency Push” — is the structural consequence that will outlast this war regardless of how it ends. At least two ships paid Iran in renminbi to secure safe passage through Hormuz. CIPS payments up nearly 50% last month. Average daily CIPS volume rose from $86 billion to $131 billion from February to March. CIPS participating institutions nearly tripled since Russia was removed from SWIFT in 2022. Atlantic Council’s Lipsky: Asian countries “switching to usage in this moment to buy restricted oil.” Cornell’s Prasad: “There is a desperate desire in the world to escape the clutches of the dollar-denominated system.”

The Hubbard Antalya Forum reporting — “In Turkey, Middle Powers Ponder Diplomacy With a Rogue U.S.” — captured the political consequence. Chatham House’s Dalay: how do you deal with a power that is “indispensable, coercive and unpredictable at the same time?” Turkey, Saudi Arabia, Egypt and Pakistan met on the sidelines. A new coalition is forming — not against America, but around the fact of America’s unpredictability.

The Schuetze piece — “Germany, a Steadfast Ally of Israel, Now Voices Some Frustration” — adds another pillar of the shifting alliance architecture. Merz publicly criticized Israel’s attacks in Lebanon. Said there must be “no de facto annexation of the West Bank.” Israel’s far-right Finance Minister Smotrich attacked Merz for “preaching morality.” Italy has already suspended its defense pact with Israel.

IX. Lebanon: A Pause Extended

Trump announced Thursday that the Lebanon ceasefire would be extended three weeks, after hosting Lebanese and Israeli ambassadors in the Oval Office. Lebanese ambassador: “I think with your help, your support, we can make Lebanon great again.” Hezbollah lawmaker Fayyad Friday: the extension “does not hold any meaning” given continued Israeli attacks.

Israel carried out strikes Friday morning — two rocket launchers destroyed, demolitions continuing. The Dahir reporting — “In Lebanon, Talks With Israel Stir a Mix of Anger, Unease and Faint Hope” — captured Lebanon’s social fracture: WhatsApp groups fracturing, Christians and Shiites splitting over whether direct talks with Israel constitute betrayal or necessity. Sunni engineer from Tripoli: “Lebanon has been dragged into a confrontation that does not serve its direct national interests.”

Lebanese journalist Amal Khalil of Al-Akhbar was killed Wednesday in an Israeli airstrike in Tayri. Lebanese Red Cross came under machine-gun fire and a warning strike while attempting rescue — forced to withdraw. Photojournalist Zeinab Faraj rescued. A second French UNIFIL soldier died from wounds sustained in the attack last week. An Indonesian UNIFIL peacekeeper also died from wounds sustained in March. Six UNIFIL peacekeepers killed since the Lebanon war began. Lebanese PM Salam pursuing ICC action against Israel.

Kuwait: two sites near the border with Iraq targeted by drones launched from Iraq by Iran-linked militias. US has suspended air shipments of dollars to Iraq to pressure Baghdad to distance itself from Iran.

X. Voices From the War

A family saying goodbye to their relatives who are leaving Iran, at the Kapikoy border crossing in Turkey on Wednesday.Credit...Kiana Hayeri for The New York Times

The Torbati reporting from the Kapikoy border crossing — “Whether for or Against the War, Iranians Say They’re Feeling Economic Pain” — is the human document of the week from inside Iran. Moji, 38, returning home: airstrike struck near her parents’ home while she was abroad, friends in Urmia struggling to afford food, factories closed. Milad, 37, from Khoy, holds up his Iranian passport: “Not anymore. Now they know what we are.” Woman from Tabriz: hopes negotiations fail so the government falls.

The Abdulrahim piece — “Iranian Expats in U.A.E. Caught Between Home and Homeland” — documented the 500,000-strong Iranian community in Dubai caught between their two countries. UAE revoking visas and residency permits. Iranian Hospital, cultural club, Islamic Azad University Dubai all closed. Ali, former kickboxer stuck in Paris after his wife’s residency was cancelled during a Nowruz holiday: “I don’t have any future. I can’t go back to Iran. I can’t go back to Europe. I couldn’t go back to the United Arab Emirates.”

The Peltier piece from Rawalpindi — filed as part of the Pakistani Officials reporting — brings the war to street level in the country mediating it. Rickshaw driver Mohsen Ali: “Outside of Pakistan, Sharif and the army chief are making big names for themselves, but inside, nothing. We should get some relief.” Chamber of Commerce president Shaukat: “Everybody says again and again, ‘It’s going to end in a couple of days,’ but in the meantime, real business is on hold at home.”

XI. The Gas Pump, Still

Energy Secretary Wright, CNN Sunday: prices may not return to $3 until 2027. Trump told The Hill that Wright was “totally wrong.” Brent closed the week above $100. The DePillis reporting — “Why Gas Prices Go Up Fast and Take So Long to Fall” — explains why Bessent’s June-September $3 gas prediction was always a political statement, not an energy market forecast. The Massachusetts gas station owner who explained it in the clearest terms available: “Because of the pressure, it feels to us like the price can never go up high enough to protect your margins. That’s why we’re temperamentally slow to take the price down.”

The Crowley JCPOA background piece — “What Was the 2015 Iran Nuclear Deal?” — sits as the implicit indictment of the week: Iran shipped out 98% of its uranium under that deal, dismantled two-thirds of its centrifuges, accepted intrusive IAEA monitoring. Trump pulled out in 2018 while Iran was complying. His critics now warn he may end up accepting terms little better than what Obama secured — after a war that has cost $28-35 billion, killed thousands, and closed the world’s most important energy chokepoint for two months.

XII. What Next Week Holds

May 1 is the War Powers deadline. The second Islamabad round has not happened. The ceasefire is extended indefinitely but Iran is seizing ships. The Lebanon truce has three weeks. The Touska and two Indian Ocean tankers are in US custody. Iran’s IRGC generals are running the country from a collective leadership structure with a wounded, hidden supreme leader communicating by courier. The nuclear gap remains unbridged. The uranium at Isfahan is buried under rubble.

Maloney’s formulation is the one to carry forward: the speed with which this became a quagmire has been “quite stunning.” CSIS’s Jones: “It’s kind of a game of chicken right now.” Both militaries are telling political leaders the military instrument “is not likely to be successful in getting to any kind of a permanent solution.”

The Sifton Numbers summary — “The Iran War, in Numbers” — provides the cleanest account of where things stand on Day 54: 130 ships per day through Hormuz before the war, 1 on Tuesday. More than 13,000 targets struck. More than 3,000 killed in Iran. Nearly 2,300 in Lebanon. 23 in Israel. 13 American service members. The Times has published 2,023 items on this conflict.

In Tehran’s national security museum, neat rows of Chinese currency bills sit behind glass alongside model fighter jets, attack helicopters and vials of rare-earth metals. The display makes the point the war has made: the renminbi is a pillar of Chinese national security. Two ships have now paid Iran in renminbi to pass through the Strait of Hormuz. The dollar system is not collapsing. But the alternative to it is growing, transaction by transaction, crisis by crisis, in the waters between Iran and Oman.

The posting will continue until morale improves. The mines may or may not be in the water. The ceasefire is extended until further notice. Seven days to May 1.

This is Day 55.
