The Watchdog That Bites Itself

The Commission for Complaints about Telecom asks if I am satisfied, and I consider the question carefully

April 16, 2026

The CCTS was created because institutions wouldn't stop bothering people. It would like to know if you found that helpful. Please respond within 48 hours.

This morning, at 9:29 a.m., I received an email from the Commission for Complaints for Telecom-Television Services — the CCTS — informing me, with evident concern, that they had recently sent me a survey and hadn’t received my response.

They would really appreciate my participation.

They thanked me for my time.

I want to pause here for a moment, because something remarkable has just happened and I don’t want you to miss it.

This was, by my count, their second reminder. The original survey went out in February. It is now April. In the intervening months, I have written formal letters to the CRTC, received two responses that were models of bureaucratic non-engagement, drafted a regulatory intervention, mailed physical correspondence to Professor Michael Geist at the University of Ottawa Faculty of Law, and prepared letters for the Executive Directors of OpenMedia and the Public Interest Advocacy Centre.

In other words: I have not been idle.

The CCTS, for their part, have been equally busy — wondering why I haven’t filled out their survey.

Here I should pause for an entirely different kind of reflection, because I am aware of how this looks.

I am 74 years old. I am retired. I have a husky named Dior who regards my regulatory crusades with the serene indifference of a creature that has correctly identified the hierarchy of important things, and placed herself at the top of it. I have, by any reasonable measure, the one resource that transforms a billing dispute into a full-scale policy intervention: time.

In my working years, when a senior colleague launched into an extended disquisition about some institutional injustice — a billing error, a procedural slight, a form that didn’t make sense — I had a thought I am not proud of. The thought was: this person has too much time on their hands.

Reader, I am now that person.

In Ottawa the machinery of concern runs on its own schedule, entirely indifferent to the feelings of the concerned.

And I want to tell my younger self something important: we were right, and it is magnificent.

What the retirees of previous generations had was time and grievance but limited ammunition. What I have, in addition to both of those things, is a Substack account, a drafting partnership with Claude, image generation via Grok, and access to forty years of CRTC regulatory decisions in searchable PDF format. I am a one-man policy shop with a husky for morale support and artificial intelligence for research assistance.

I should perhaps disclose at this point that I spent eighteen years working in the Office of the President complaints department at Rogers. I am therefore approaching this particular circle of institutional irony from a position of some expertise. In fact, if memory serves, the very first complaint the CCTS ever forwarded to Rogers landed on my desk to resolve — likely on the day they opened for business.

I have now, in the fullness of time, become one of their complainants.

They would like to know if I’m satisfied.

For those of you who just caught on and who are appalled I use AI to assist me, you can bite me.

The telecom industry was not prepared for this.

Neither, apparently, was the CCTS.

Let me explain why this particular survey carries a special flavour of irony. Regular readers know the backstory: $4.10 owed to Fido, $42.38 billed, customer service line eliminated, chatbot impassable, callback system unavailable to cancelled accounts, and — in what I still consider a masterwork of institutional audacity — a late payment fee for failing to pay the amount I didn't owe.

I filed a complaint with the CCTS.

The CCTS reviewed it and told me, helpfully, that while they could address individual complaints, the systemic issue I was raising — whether Canadian telecom carriers should be permitted to bill consumers in advance at all — fell outside their mandate. For that, I needed the CRTC.

The CRTC, upon receiving my detailed policy submission, informed me that billing practices are “business decisions” and suggested I take individual complaints to the CCTS.

This is the complete circuit. You may now appreciate why I didn’t fill out the survey.

What, precisely, was I being asked to rate? My satisfaction with being redirected? My experience of the loop? The quality of the referral that sent me back to the place that sent me forward?

I did not complete the survey. This was not an oversight. It was, I maintain, a position.

The CCTS was created because telcos were behaving badly toward their customers — billing unexpectedly, locking people into contracts written in the legal equivalent of invisible ink, and treating consumers with the warm regard one reserves for a speed bump. Parliament decided Canadians needed a champion. So they built the CCTS.

The CCTS, having been built, needed to know if it was doing a good job.

And how does any Canadian institution determine if it is doing a good job?

A survey, obviously.

And if you don’t answer the survey?

A reminder, obviously.

We have arrived, through a perfectly logical institutional sequence, at the regulatory equivalent of a bouncer hired to stop people from being bothered, who will not stop bothering you until you confirm that you haven’t been bothered.

Stephen Leacock, were he alive today, would nod slowly. He wrote about the peculiar Canadian genius for creating processes whose primary output is more process. In Mariposa, every problem was met with a committee, every committee required a subcommittee, and the minutes of the subcommittee were reviewed by a panel whose membership overlapped sufficiently with the original committee that nothing was ever actually resolved, but everyone felt that due diligence had been observed.

Mariposa always found a way to make the solution indistinguishable from the problem. We called it progress then. We call it a customer satisfaction framework now

We have simply digitized this. The advance billing creates a dispute. The dispute generates a CCTS complaint. The CCTS complaint produces a referral. The referral generates a CRTC submission. The submission receives a letter acknowledging the submission without engaging it. The letter generates a follow-up submission. The CRTC informs me the first submission doesn’t count and I should wait for the formal process. The formal process will eventually open, at which point I shall file an intervention, which will be reviewed alongside hundreds of other interventions, which will be summarized in a staff report, which will inform a decision that will be issued perhaps eighteen months from now, at which point the carriers will have two years to comply, and somewhere in that timeline, Dior will have departed for a better world and I will be 77 years old, still explaining why billing in arrears is how electricity works.

In the meantime: has anyone filled out the survey?

I do not want to be entirely uncharitable. The CCTS does genuinely important work. Individual Canadians with billing disputes and service failures have real recourse through the Commission, and that matters. The record high of 23,647 complaints in their most recent annual report — 46% of them billing-related — is evidence not of CCTS failure but of the scale of the underlying problem they’re absorbing.

The survey itself is reasonable in theory. Organizations should know whether complainants felt heard. Feedback loops are how institutions improve.

The problem is that “did you feel heard?” is a deeply interesting question to ask someone whose systemic concern was ruled out of scope, whose individual complaint was technically resolved, and who then spent three months in correspondence with regulators who responded to every substantive argument with procedural guidance.

Did I feel heard?

I felt processed. There is a difference, and it is not captured in a five-point scale.

I should mention, for completeness, that I have largely resolved my personal telemarketing problem through technology. My mobile provider offers a call-blocking feature that intercepts computer-generated calls and requires any caller to enter a numeric code before the call connects. Automated diallers cannot do this. It is elegant, effective, and — remarkably — not a universal carrier offering, which is one of the underappreciated reasons I chose them.

The telemarketers, having found the phone line defended, pivoted to email.

The siege continues. Different weapon, same army.

I defended the telephone with technology. They regrouped, and came for the inbox. The army is tireless. It has no feelings to hurt and no sense of irony to appeal to.

I have not, as of this writing, found an equivalent filter for survey reminders from consumer protection agencies. I consider this a gap in the market.

In the meantime, I have letters waiting to go to Matt Hatfield at OpenMedia and John Lawford at the Public Interest Advocacy Centre. I have a formal intervention draft ready for the moment CRTC announces the unified Consumer Protection Code consultation. I have, at my side, an AI research assistant that can locate and synthesize twenty years of CRTC decisions in the time it would have taken my younger self to find a parking spot.

Professor Geist has not yet responded. He is, I suspect, not yet retired. He has less time. I understand completely.

But I have Claude, I have Grok, I have Dior, and I have what the senior complainers of my working years only dimly grasped as the thing they actually possessed:

The institutional stamina of someone with nothing more pressing to do.

The CCTS would like to know if I’m satisfied.

I’ll tell them when we’re done.

Dior has reviewed this piece and declined to comment, which I take as editorial approval.
