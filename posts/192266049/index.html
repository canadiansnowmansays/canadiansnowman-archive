The Court Closes the Gap

Part 21: A Canadian notes on AI governance

April 2, 2026

CEO and co-founder of Anthropic Dario Amodei speak onstage during the 2025 New York Times Dealbook Summit at Jazz at Lincoln Center on December 03, 2025 in New York City. Michael M. Santiago | Getty Images

On March 26, 2026, a federal judge in San Francisco did something that Congress would not, the Pentagon could not, and the White House refused to: she drew a constitutional line.

U.S. District Judge Rita Lin granted Anthropic a preliminary injunction blocking the Pentagon’s supply chain risk designation — the blacklisting that Pete Hegseth issued in February after Anthropic refused to remove safety guardrails from Claude. The ruling is 43 pages. The language is extraordinary. And for this series, which began twenty parts ago asking whether democratic institutions could govern AI before the people building it governed everything else, today is the day we have been tracking toward.

The Asimov Gap has not closed. But a court just named it precisely, on the record, in constitutional terms.

What the Judge Actually Said

Preliminary injunctions are procedural instruments. They preserve the status quo while a case is litigated. They are not final verdicts.

But they require something significant: the judge must find that the plaintiff is likely to succeed on the merits. Judge Lin found that. Which means that on the central question — whether the Trump administration violated Anthropic’s First Amendment rights by blacklisting a company for publicly disagreeing with government policy — a federal judge has looked at the evidence and said: Anthropic will probably win.

The language in the ruling goes well beyond the minimum required. “Nothing in the governing statute,” Lin wrote, “supports the Orwellian notion that an American company may be branded a potential adversary and saboteur of the U.S. for expressing disagreement with the government.”

Orwellian. A sitting federal judge used that word about an act of the United States government. In a 43-page order. On the record.

She did not stop there. The Pentagon’s own internal records — submitted as evidence — showed that Anthropic was designated a supply chain risk not because of national security concerns, but because of its “hostile manner through the press.” The government’s documents convicted the government. The designation, Lin found, was retaliation for speech. “Punishing Anthropic for bringing public scrutiny to the government’s contracting position,” she wrote, “is classic illegal First Amendment retaliation.”

Classic. Illegal. Retaliation.

What the Government Argued

It is worth pausing on the government’s position, because it reveals exactly the logic this series has been documenting.

The Pentagon argued it needed “unfettered access” to Claude for “all lawful purposes.” It argued that Anthropic, by refusing to remove guardrails against autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, was not merely a difficult contractor — it was a potential saboteur. In a March 17 court filing, the Department of War said it was concerned Anthropic might “attempt to disable its technology or preemptively alter the behaviour of its model” during “warfighting operations” if the company felt its “corporate red lines were being crossed.”

Anthropic noted, pointedly, that this concern never appeared during any contract negotiation. It appeared for the first time in a court filing, after the blacklisting, constructed to justify a decision already made.

The government also argued that Trump’s Truth Social post — “WE will decide the fate of our Country — NOT some out-of-control, Radical Left AI company” — had no legal standing and therefore caused no irreparable harm. The judge was not persuaded. She fast-tracked the hearing after the government’s own lawyer refused to guarantee that no further retaliatory action would be taken before the hearing date.

Read that again. The government’s lawyer, in open court, declined to promise a federal judge that the government would not take additional punitive action against a company in active litigation. Judge Lin moved the hearing up immediately.

Anthropic has argued: ‘The constitution does not allow the government to wield its enormous power to punish a company for its protected speech.’ Photograph: Patrick Sison/AP

The Zeroth Law, In Court

For twenty parts, this series has used Isaac Asimov’s Zeroth Law as a frame for the AI governance crisis: the belief, held by the people building these systems, that they know what is good for humanity better than humanity’s own democratic institutions do.

The Pentagon’s position in this case is the Zeroth Law in reverse — applied not by AI builders, but by the government itself. The argument was: we know how AI must be used. We know what the mission requires. The company’s principles, its guardrails, its public disagreement — these are obstacles to our judgment about what is necessary. Submit, or be destroyed.

Anthropic’s position was simpler and more constitutionally durable: we are an American company. We have the right to say what we think. You can choose a different vendor. You cannot brand us a national security threat for having principles.

Judge Lin agreed. The First Amendment, she found, does not have a carve-out for government contractors who embarrass a cabinet secretary on the front page of the New York Times.

What This Does and Does Not Resolve

A preliminary injunction is not a final verdict. The case continues — in two courts simultaneously, since Anthropic filed both in the Northern District of California and in the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals, challenging the designation under two separate statutory frameworks. Lin delayed implementation of her ruling by one week to allow the government to appeal. The administration may yet seek a stay. The D.C. case proceeds independently.

A final verdict, Lin noted, could still be months away.

And even a full victory for Anthropic in both cases resolves only this dispute, with this administration, over this contract. It does not create the broader governance framework this series has been calling for. It does not establish who governs AI deployment across federal agencies. It does not set international standards. It does not address the autonomous weapons question at the heart of the underlying dispute — which remains, after all this, unresolved. The Pentagon still wants unfettered AI for warfighting. Anthropic still believes its systems are not ready for that. The court said the government cannot punish Anthropic for that position. It did not say who is right about the underlying question.

The Asimov Gap is about the absence of democratic frameworks for governing AI. A court ruling that the government broke the law in trying to circumvent one company’s safety standards is not the same as building those frameworks. It is, however, something this series never expected to be able to report: a constitutional boundary, clearly drawn, in a published federal ruling.

What Asimov Would Have Made of This

The Three Laws of Robotics were a governance framework. Asimov spent fifty years exploring why simple rules fail when technology becomes sufficiently powerful and the humans deploying it sufficiently certain of their own judgment.

The Anthropic case is not about robot laws. But it is about exactly the question Asimov kept circling: who decides what the machines may do, and by what authority?

The Pentagon’s answer was: we do. By executive order, by Truth Social post, by blacklist.

Judge Lin’s answer was: no. Not like that. Not without constitutional process. Not by branding a company Orwellian names because its CEO gave an interview.

This is the first time, in this series, that an institution of democratic governance has answered that question with the force of law. Not a regulatory framework, not a congressional committee, not an international treaty — those remain absent. But a federal judge, in a 43-page order, using the word Orwellian, has said: there are limits. The Constitution applies here. The gap is not ungovernable.

A Note on the Series

When I wrote Part 18 — “The Day the Gap Became Real” — I called Anthropic’s willingness to stake its Pentagon contract on two guardrails the most significant act of AI governance resistance since the field began. The 900 engineers from OpenAI and Google who signed letters of support. The amicus briefs. The First Amendment argument that most observers thought was a long shot.

Today, a federal judge called it “likely to succeed on the merits.”

I will continue tracking this case as it develops. The D.C. Circuit proceedings. The government’s appeal, if it comes. The final verdict, whenever it arrives. Each development will be documented here, in this series, because the Anthropic/Pentagon case is now the most consequential AI governance dispute in democratic history. Not because Anthropic is a hero — companies are not heroes. But because the questions at the center of this case are the questions at the center of everything this series has been arguing:

Who governs the machines? By what authority? Under what constraints? Accountable to whom?

Today, for the first time, a court gave an answer.

The gap is still there. But someone just measured it.
