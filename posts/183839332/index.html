Why DHS Secretary Kristi Noem Must Resign

An Analysis of Demonstrable Falsehoods Following the Minneapolis Fatal Shooting

January 7, 2026

January 7, 2026

Noem categorizes attack on ICE in Minneapolis as ‘act of domestic terrorism

On the morning of January 7, 2026, an ICE agent fatally shot Renee Nicole Good, a 37-year-old American citizen and mother of a 6-year-old child, on a residential street in south Minneapolis. Within hours, Department of Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem characterized this killing as an “act of domestic terrorism.” By evening, video evidence and eyewitness testimony had comprehensively demolished her narrative. By the next morning, President Trump himself—after watching video of the shooting in the Oval Office with New York Times reporters—insisted that Good “didn’t try to run him over. She ran him over,” despite the video showing no such thing.

The discrepancy between federal officials’ statements and documented reality is not a matter of perspective or interpretation—it is a matter of demonstrable falsehood that strikes at the heart of democratic accountability and the rule of law. This is not just Secretary Noem lying. This is the President of the United States, after watching video evidence, telling reporters the opposite of what that video shows.

Secretary Noem must resign immediately. Not because mistakes were made in the heat of a crisis, but because she deliberately constructed and disseminated a false narrative about a fatal shooting while video evidence existed that contradicted her claims. When the head of a law enforcement agency lies about the circumstances of a killing by one of her officers, the entire foundation of public trust collapses. There can be no accountability without truth, and there can be no justice when those charged with enforcing the law consider themselves above it.

Refusing to Accept the Unacceptable

Before examining the specific lies Secretary Noem told about this killing, we must confront a deeper problem: the normalization of dishonesty in American governance. Over the past decade, Americans have been systematically conditioned to accept lies from political leaders as routine, unremarkable, just part of the game. Donald Trump told over 30,000 documented falsehoods during his presidency, and the response from much of the country was a shrug. Lies became background noise, something to ignore rather than something to act upon.

This normalization is not an accident. It is a deliberate strategy. When everything becomes a lie, when dishonesty is constant and shameless, people eventually stop caring. They become exhausted by the effort of distinguishing truth from falsehood. They begin to accept that “everybody lies” and “all politicians are the same.” This learned helplessness is precisely the goal—if citizens believe they cannot trust anyone, they stop demanding accountability from everyone.

But we cannot afford that luxury when it comes to lies about killings by government officers. This is not a lie about crowd sizes or economic statistics or political opponents. This is a lie about why a federal agent shot and killed an American citizen. The stakes could not be higher, and the moment could not be more critical.

We must refuse to accept this as normal. We must refuse to shrug this off as just another Trump administration lie. We must refuse to let our expectations sink so low that Cabinet officials can lie about fatal shootings without consequence. Because once we accept lies about state violence, once we let government officials murder citizens and then lie about it with impunity, we have crossed a line from which there may be no return.

This essay is not just about Secretary Noem’s dishonesty. It is about drawing a line and saying: not this. Not lies about why government agents kill people. Not characterizing American citizens as terrorists to justify shooting them. Not anymore. The normalization stops here.

The Official Narrative: An Exercise in Fiction

Secretary Noem’s version of events, delivered with the confidence of someone who apparently believed Americans would accept it uncritically, painted a picture of heroic federal agents under attack. Speaking from Texas—notably not from the scene of the shooting—she claimed:

“What had happened was our ICE officers were out in enforcement action, they got stuck in the snow because of the adverse weather that is in Minneapolis. They were attempting to push out their vehicle and a woman attacked them and those surrounding them and attempted to run them over and ram them with her vehicle.”

DHS spokesperson Tricia McLaughlin amplified this narrative, describing the victim as a “violent rioter” who “weaponized her vehicle, attempting to run over our law enforcement officers in an attempt to kill them.” Both officials characterized the shooting as defensive action against “an act of domestic terrorism.”

This is the story Secretary Noem wanted Americans to believe. It is also, according to video evidence and eyewitness accounts, substantially false.

The Video Evidence: What Actually Happened

Multiple videos of the incident, obtained by news organizations including Minnesota Reformer, tell a markedly different story. The New York Times conducted a forensic analysis of footage from three different camera angles, synchronizing the videos to establish a definitive timeline. Their analysis is unambiguous: “the agent was not in the path of the victim’s SUV when he opened fire.”

The videos show masked ICE officers approaching a Honda Pilot SUV stopped in the middle of Portland Avenue near 34th Street. One officer is heard telling the driver to “get out of the f*cking car” while attempting to open the door. The driver backs up slightly, then begins to pull forward in what appears to be an attempt to leave the scene. An officer positioned at the front of the vehicle fires three shots. The SUV then travels a short distance before crashing into a parked car.

The Times’ analysis reveals critical details: “The moment the agent fires, he is standing here to the left of the SUV and the wheels are pointing to the right away from the agent. This appears to conflict with allegations that the SUV was ramming or about to ram the officer.” When synchronized with footage from multiple angles, the analysis definitively shows the agent was not being run over—”his feet are positioned away from the SUV.”

This sequence—captured on video by multiple witnesses and forensically analyzed by the Times—contradicts the federal narrative in every critical way. There is no evidence of agents “stuck in the snow” attempting to push their vehicle. There is no evidence of the driver “attacking” agents or those “surrounding them.” The vehicle’s movement is clearly an attempt to flee, not to ram officers. The shooting occurs within seconds of the driver attempting to leave. And most damningly: the agent was never in the path of the vehicle.

Witness Caitlin Callenson, who filmed the incident, reported seeing an ICE vehicle stuck in snow earlier, but described the shooting differently than federal officials. She noted that as more ICE vehicles arrived, bystanders blew whistles in protest—a common community alert system—and the driver appeared to be blocking ICE vehicles. Critically, Callenson stated she did not see ICE agents attempting to detain anyone either before or after the shooting.

Another witness, Emily Heller, described the aftermath: the driver was “completely slumped over in the vehicle.” Federal agents refused to allow a physician who was present to examine the driver. Emergency medical technicians could not reach the scene for 15 minutes because ICE agents did not move their vehicles to let them through. When EMTs finally arrived, they had to navigate through a gauntlet of federal vehicles that were “causing chaos,” according to witnesses.

The agent who shot Renee Nicole Good then got in his vehicle and drove away from the scene—apparently altering an active crime scene. Federal agents on scene did not appear to rush to provide emergency medical care to the woman who had just been shot in the head.

Who Was Renee Nicole Good

Renee Nicole Good was a 37-year-old U.S. citizen who lived in Minneapolis with her partner and 6-year-old child. Her mother, Donna Ganger, told the Minnesota Star Tribune that her daughter “was one of the kindest people I’ve ever known,” adding that she was “loving, forgiving and affectionate.”

State Representative Leigh Finke described Good as “a cherished Minnesotan” and “a loved and celebrated community member, who has now been stripped away from her family.” Minneapolis City Council President Elliott Payne said of her: “This was just a bystander looking to protect their community.”

She was not someone ICE was attempting to apprehend. She was not the target of any law enforcement investigation. She was an American citizen exercising her right to monitor law enforcement activity in her community—a legal observer watching out for her immigrant neighbors. For this, she was shot in the head by a federal agent who then drove away from the scene.

Local Officials: “Bullshit” and “Propaganda”

The gap between Secretary Noem’s version and reality was so stark that local officials—who have actually seen the video—issued unusually blunt responses.

Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey, after viewing the video, was unequivocal:

“So they are already trying to spin this as an action of self-defense. Having seen the video of myself, I want to tell everybody directly that is bullshit. This was an agent recklessly using power that resulted in somebody dying, getting killed.”

Frey continued: “I have a message for ICE, to ICE: Get the f--- out of Minneapolis. What they are doing is causing chaos and distrust. They’re ripping families apart. They’re sowing chaos on our streets, and in this case, quite literally killing people.”

Minnesota Governor Tim Walz echoed this assessment after viewing the same video evidence:

“Don’t believe this propaganda machine. The state will ensure there is a full, fair, and expeditious investigation to ensure accountability and justice.”

Minneapolis Police Chief Brian O’Hara provided additional crucial context: “There is nothing to indicate that this woman was the target of any law enforcement investigation or activity.” In other words, she was not someone ICE was attempting to apprehend. She was, according to Minneapolis City Council member Jason Chavez, “an observer” who was “watching out for our immigrant neighbors.” She was a 37-year-old American citizen exercising her right to monitor law enforcement activity in her community.

Chief O’Hara also noted that his department “has yet to see information indicating that the shooting was justified.” This is not ambiguous language from a police chief trying to reserve judgment. This is a professional law enforcement official saying, based on the evidence he has reviewed, there is no apparent justification for this killing.

Trump’s Personal Lies: When Video Evidence Doesn’t Matter

If Secretary Noem’s lies were the only problem, this would be serious enough. But the normalization of dishonesty goes straight to the top.

On Wednesday afternoon, just hours after the shooting, President Trump sat in the Oval Office with New York Times reporters and made his conclusion clear: Renee Nicole Good had tried to “run over” the ICE agent. When reporters pressed him on this claim given the unclear and contradictory video evidence, Trump didn’t hedge. He escalated.

“I want to see nobody get shot,” Trump said. “I want to see nobody screaming and trying to run over policemen either.”

When reporters pointed out that the videos circulating on social media were unclear and didn’t appear to show what he was claiming, Trump’s response was to double down. He asked his aide, Natalie Harp, to pull up the video on a laptop to prove his point.

“That was a vicious situation that took place,” Trump said, apparently referring to what federal officials claimed was an attempt by Good to run down an ICE agent.

The reporters pressed him: was firing into a vehicle like that acceptable? Trump’s answer revealed everything about how he views this killing: “She behaved horribly. And then she ran him over. She didn’t try to run him over. She ran him over.”

The reporters told him the videos were unclear. Trump insisted: “I’ll play the tape for you right now.”

As a slow-motion surveillance video played on the laptop, the Times reporters told Trump that this angle did not appear to show an ICE officer had been run over.

“Well,” Trump said. “I — the way I look at it … “

He trailed off. The video didn’t show what he claimed. But rather than acknowledge this, he pivoted: “It’s a terrible scene. I think it’s horrible to watch. No, I hate to see it.”

The next day, the Times published their forensic analysis of footage from three camera angles, definitively proving that the agent was not in the path of the vehicle when he opened fire. Trump had watched video evidence in the Oval Office with reporters and lied about what it showed. When confronted with the fact that the video didn’t support his claims, he simply moved on to blaming his predecessor’s immigration policies.

This is not a matter of interpretation. This is not a case of seeing the same events from different perspectives. This is the President of the United States watching video evidence and telling reporters the opposite of what that evidence shows. And when caught in the lie, simply changing the subject.

Even Trump’s own border czar, Tom Homan, seemed to understand the problem with making immediate conclusions. When asked about the shooting, Homan told CBS News he would not make a judgment call based on video circulating on social media: “Let the investigation play out, and hold people accountable based on the investigation.”

But Trump couldn’t help himself. He needed to immediately characterize an American citizen who was shot by his agents as someone who “behaved horribly” and “ran over” a federal officer—despite video evidence showing no such thing. The lie was more important than the truth. The narrative was more important than the evidence. Protecting his agents was more important than acknowledging that they killed an American citizen who was exercising her constitutional rights.

The TACO Framework: Anatomy of Institutional Deception

Secretary Noem’s response to this incident follows a predictable pattern that has become familiar in the current administration. I call it the TACO framework: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. In this case, we are witnessing the “O”—Obfuscate—in its most dangerous form.

The Obfuscation Strategy:

First, reframe the victim as the aggressor. A 37-year-old American citizen becomes a “violent rioter” engaged in “domestic terrorism.” A woman trying to leave becomes someone “attempting to run over” and “ram” federal agents. The rhetorical escalation is deliberate—if you can convince people that the victim was a terrorist, you can justify almost any use of force.

Second, create a false context. Agents weren’t conducting an enforcement operation that drew community resistance; they were “stuck in the snow” trying to help each other out when they were “attacked.” This makes the agents sympathetic figures rather than an occupying force that has arrested 1,400 people in Minneapolis since early December.

Third, characterize any use of force as heroic and defensive. The ICE officer “used his training and saved his own life and that of his fellow officers.” Never mind that video shows a woman trying to drive away and an officer who positions himself in front of her vehicle before firing. Never mind that witnesses say the vehicle wasn’t moving toward agents when shots were fired.

Fourth, pre-empt criticism by attacking critics. Secretary Noem’s statement blamed “sanctuary politicians who fuel and encourage rampant assaults on our law enforcement.” This is classic deflection—turn attention away from what your officers did and toward the supposed malice of those who dare to question it.

What makes this particularly egregious is that Secretary Noem was in Minneapolis filming herself observing ICE operations just the day before this shooting. She was on the ground, part of what Governor Walz accurately called “governing by reality TV.” She helped create the environment that led to this death, and when it happened, her first instinct was not to seek truth but to construct a narrative that would protect her agency and her reputation.

Why This Matters: The Collapse of Institutional Credibility

If Secretary Noem’s deception were merely about covering up an individual officer’s poor judgment, it would be serious but perhaps not unprecedented. What makes this a resignation-level offense is that it represents a fundamental betrayal of the public trust that makes democratic governance possible. And what makes it a constitutional crisis is that the President of the United States joined in the lie.

Consider what Secretary Noem and President Trump are asking Americans to believe: that despite video evidence forensically analyzed by the New York Times, despite eyewitness accounts, despite the assessment of local law enforcement officials who have reviewed that evidence, we should accept the federal government’s characterization of a fatal shooting as justified self-defense against domestic terrorism. They are asking us to disbelieve our own eyes and trust the government instead. They are asking us to accept that when the President watches video evidence and tells reporters it shows something it provably does not show, we should believe him anyway.

This is not a minor disagreement about facts. This is not a case of different people viewing the same events from different perspectives. This is the Secretary of Homeland Security and the President of the United States telling Americans something that video evidence demonstrates to be false, and doing so in service of protecting an agent who killed an American citizen.

The implications extend far beyond this single incident. If the President and the head of DHS can lie about a fatal shooting and face no consequences—if Trump can watch video in the Oval Office with reporters and claim it shows the opposite of what it actually shows—what message does that send to every ICE agent, every Border Patrol officer, every federal law enforcement official operating under their authority? It tells them that the truth is optional, that public accountability is a suggestion rather than a requirement, and that their leaders, up to and including the President, will cover for them no matter what they do.

The Historical Echo: Ten Blocks from George Floyd Square

There is bitter irony in the location of this shooting. It occurred just ten blocks—a three-minute drive—from George Perry Floyd Square, where on May 25, 2020, Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin murdered George Floyd by kneeling on his neck for over nine minutes while Floyd repeatedly said he couldn’t breathe.

Floyd’s death sparked a national reckoning about police brutality, accountability, and the value of Black lives. Minneapolis became a symbol of both the problem and the possibility of change. The community that arose from that tragedy emphasized transparency, civilian oversight, and the fundamental principle that law enforcement officers are not above the law.

Now, less than six years later and ten blocks away, federal agents have killed another person—this time a woman engaged in the constitutionally protected activity of observing law enforcement. And the response from federal officials has been to immediately characterize her as a terrorist and the killing as justified self-defense, before any independent investigation, before any review of the video evidence, before even allowing a doctor to examine her as she lay dying in her vehicle.

The lesson of George Floyd’s murder was supposed to be that we can no longer accept law enforcement narratives uncritically, that we must demand accountability and transparency, that the lives of civilians matter more than the convenience of officers. Secretary Noem’s response to this shooting suggests she learned nothing from that tragedy—or perhaps learned all too well that if you control the narrative quickly and forcefully enough, you might never have to face accountability at all.

The Broader Context: 2,000 Agents and Governing by Reality TV

This shooting did not occur in a vacuum. It is the predictable result of what Governor Walz accurately called “dangerous, sensationalized operations” designed to “generate fear, headlines and conflict.”

Since early December 2025, Minneapolis has been the site of what DHS calls the “largest ever” ICE operation. As many as 2,000 ICE and Homeland Security Investigations agents have been deployed to the Twin Cities area. By mid-December, they had arrested roughly 300 people. By early January, that number had surged to approximately 1,400. This is not routine law enforcement; this is a military-style occupation of an American city.

Secretary Noem traveled to Minneapolis to film herself observing operations—literal reality TV governance. The day before this shooting, she was on camera in tactical gear, projecting strength and determination. When her presence and her officers’ actions led to a death, her response was to lie about it.

This is governing as performance art, where the appearance of action matters more than the consequences of that action. It is governance designed to generate content for social media, not to actually address problems. And when that performance goes wrong—when someone dies—the response is to double down on the narrative rather than confront the reality.

The Moral Imperative: Rejecting the Normalization of Lies About State Violence

At its core, this is not a partisan issue. It is not about immigration policy or law enforcement tactics or even about the specific circumstances of this shooting. It is about whether we still believe that truth matters, that facts matter, that government officials—from Cabinet secretaries to the President himself—have an obligation to be honest with the American people, particularly when they have killed someone.

The Trump administration has systematically trained Americans to accept lies as normal political discourse. We have become desensitized to dishonesty. We scroll past thousands of false statements. We fact-check claims and then move on to the next lie. This constant barrage of falsehoods is not accidental; it is strategic. When lies are constant and shameless, people stop expecting truth. They stop demanding accountability. They accept that “this is just how things are now.”

But we cannot accept it. Not about this.

Secretary Noem and President Trump had multiple opportunities to tell the truth. They could have said, “We’re investigating what happened. We’ll release information as we confirm it.” They could have expressed regret that someone died. They could have acknowledged the community’s concern and promised transparency. Instead, they immediately constructed a false narrative designed to justify the killing and demonize the victim. And when confronted with video evidence—in Trump’s case, while watching it in the Oval Office—they doubled down on lies that the evidence definitively disproves.

This was not an accident. This was not a misunderstanding. These were deliberate choices to prioritize narrative control over factual accuracy. Noem oversees approximately 260,000 employees and a budget of over $60 billion. Trump is the President of the United States. If they will lie about a fatal shooting by one of their agents—lie when video evidence exists, lie when forensic analysis proves them wrong, lie while watching that evidence—what else will they lie about?

More importantly: if we accept their lies about this, what message are we sending? That Cabinet officials and the President can characterize American citizens as terrorists after killing them? That federal agents can shoot people and their superiors, up to and including the President, will immediately construct false narratives to justify it? That video evidence doesn’t matter—even forensic analysis by the New York Times doesn’t matter—if the government’s story is told quickly and forcefully enough?

This is where the normalization of lies becomes genuinely dangerous. When we accept dishonesty about crowd sizes or economic statistics, we erode civic discourse. When we accept lies about why government agents killed someone—when we accept the President watching video evidence and lying about what it shows—we abandon the rule of law entirely. There is no democratic governance without honest accounting of state violence. There is no justice when those who wield lethal force can lie about when and why they used it.

The question facing the American people is not simply whether Secretary Noem and President Trump lied—the video evidence makes that clear. The question is whether we have become so accustomed to lies from this administration that we will accept them even about killings by federal agents, even when the President himself lies about video evidence while watching it.

The answer must be no. Not because Noem and Trump are uniquely dishonest—though the brazenness is remarkable—but because if we accept these lies, we will have normalized something that should never be normal: government officials, up to and including the President, lying about why they killed citizens.

This is the line. This is where we say: no further. The normalization of lies stops when those lies are told to cover up and justify state violence. If we cannot hold that line, we have already lost the republic.

Conclusion: The Line We Cannot Cross

Governor Walz said of this incident: “What we’re seeing is the consequences of governance designed to generate fear, headlines and conflict. It’s governing by reality TV, and today, that recklessness cost someone their life.”

He is correct, but the cost extends beyond Renee Nicole Good’s life. The cost includes her 6-year-old child growing up without a mother. It includes every American’s ability to trust their government. It includes the credibility of every federal law enforcement officer who tries to do their job honorably. It includes the very possibility of accountability when government officials abuse their power.

Secretary Noem and President Trump think Americans are either blind, deaf, dumb, or stupid. They think we will accept their characterization of events despite video evidence—forensically analyzed by the New York Times—that contradicts it. They think we will believe that a woman trying to flee was actually engaged in domestic terrorism. More fundamentally, they think we have been so thoroughly conditioned to accept lies that we will accept them even about this. They are betting that years of constant dishonesty from Trump have broken our collective ability to be outraged by falsehoods—that we will treat their demonstrably false narrative about a fatal shooting as just another day in Trump’s America.

They are betting that we have normalized the unacceptable.

This is the test. This is the moment when Americans must decide whether we have surrendered our standards so completely that the President and his Cabinet can lie about killings by federal agents without consequence. This is when we determine whether truth still matters, whether accountability is still possible, whether we retain any capacity to be shocked when the President of the United States watches video evidence in the Oval Office and lies about what it shows.

If we accept these lies—from Noem, from Trump, from the entire federal apparatus—we accept a world where government agents can kill people and their superiors, up to and including the President, will immediately construct false narratives to justify it. We accept a world where video evidence doesn’t matter if the official story is told quickly enough. We accept a world where American citizens can be characterized as terrorists after being shot by federal agents, where mothers can be killed for watching out for their neighbors, and no one will be held accountable. That is not a world anyone should accept. That is not normal. That must never become normal.

Secretary Noem must resign. Not tomorrow. Not after the investigation. TODAY. Not as punishment, though her conduct deserves it. Not as a political statement, though her dishonesty demands a response. But as a declaration that Americans have not been so thoroughly broken by years of constant lies that we will accept them about everything, including killings by federal agents.

We draw the line here. We say: not this. Not lies about state violence. Not anymore.

The republic cannot survive if we accept this. Democracy cannot function if government officials can murder citizens and lie about it with impunity—with the President himself watching video evidence and lying about what it shows to reporters in the Oval Office. The rule of law means nothing if those who enforce it are above it. Lies about state violence are not just another Trump administration falsehood to be catalogued and fact-checked and then forgotten. They strike at the very foundation of legitimate governance.

Trump and Noem think Americans have been so thoroughly conditioned to accept lies that we will accept them even about this. They are wrong. They must be proven wrong. Because if they are right—if we have been broken to the point where we will accept government lies about killings with the same weary resignation we accept lies about everything else—then Renee Nicole Good died for nothing, and we have already lost everything worth fighting for.
