Episode 4: The Smoot-Hawley Disaster - When Protectionism Backfired

June 19, 2025

Welcome back to "Trump Tariff and Trade Thursdays." We've spent three weeks deconstructing Trump's tariff mythology. This week, we examine the most catastrophic failure of protectionist policy in American history—a disaster that offers chilling parallels to current proposals.

If you want to understand why economists react with horror to sweeping tariff proposals, you need to know about the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930. This legislation, passed with rhetoric remarkably similar to Trump's current promises, transformed an economic downturn into a global catastrophe.

The Promise vs. The Reality

Sound familiar? In 1930, politicians promised that higher tariffs would protect American jobs, help farmers, and restore economic prosperity. President Herbert Hoover initially opposed the legislation but eventually signed it on June 17, 1930, hoping it would provide temporary economic relief.

The Smoot-Hawley Act raised tariffs on over 20,000 imported goods, with increases averaging 60% above existing rates. Agricultural products saw tariff increases of 34% on average, while manufactured goods faced 41% increases. The overall average tariff rate reached 48%—the highest since the Civil War.

Politicians claimed these increases would be paid by foreigners, not Americans. They promised that protection would stimulate domestic production and create jobs. They dismissed warnings from economists and business leaders as partisan opposition to helping American workers.

The Opposition That Was Ignored

The parallels to today's debates are striking. Over 1,000 economists signed a petition urging President Hoover to veto the legislation, warning that it would trigger retaliation and worsen economic conditions. Their concerns were dismissed as academic theory divorced from practical politics.

Business leaders who understood global markets opposed the bill. Henry Ford called the tariff "an economic stupidity." Thomas Lamont of J.P. Morgan & Company warned that protection would harm American exporters more than it would help protected industries. Their voices were drowned out by protectionist rhetoric and political opportunism.

The Swift and Devastating Response

Foreign governments didn't wait to retaliate. Within months of Smoot-Hawley's passage, America's trading partners began imposing countermeasures designed to inflict maximum damage on American exporters.

Canada struck first and hardest: As America's largest trading partner, Canada imposed retaliatory tariffs that devastated American agricultural exports. Canadian duties on American wheat increased from 9 cents to 42 cents per bushel. Other agricultural products faced proportional increases.

Europe followed quickly: Great Britain abandoned its traditional free trade stance and implemented the Imperial Preference system in 1932, discriminating against American exports in favor of Commonwealth nations. France increased duties on American automobiles by 100%. Germany imposed prohibitive tariffs on American agricultural products.

Global retaliation spread: By 1932, over 60 countries had implemented discriminatory measures against American exports. Even smaller nations like Switzerland and the Netherlands, traditionally committed to liberal trade, raised barriers against American goods.

The Economic Catastrophe

The results were devastating for both international commerce and the American economy:

Trade Collapse: Global trade volumes fell by 25% between 1929 and 1932. American exports plummeted from $5.4 billion in 1929 to $1.7 billion in 1933—a 68% decline that far exceeded the overall economic contraction.

Agricultural Devastation: Agricultural exports fell from $1.8 billion to $590 million. Wheat farmers, who had supported the tariff hoping for price protection, saw export markets disappear as foreign buyers turned to Canadian, Argentine, and Australian suppliers. Wheat exports fell from 175 million bushels in 1929 to just 25 million bushels in 1933.

Industrial Job Losses: Rather than protecting employment, Smoot-Hawley contributed to massive job losses as export industries contracted. Manufacturing employment fell by 35% between 1929 and 1933. The automotive industry, despite tariff protection, saw overseas sales collapse by 75% for Ford and similar losses for General Motors.

By 1933, approximately 15 million Americans were jobless and almost half of the United States' banks had failed, demonstrating the human cost of protectionist overreach when economic theory meets political reality.

The Vicious Cycle

Smoot-Hawley created a self-reinforcing economic disaster. Export industries laid off workers, reducing domestic purchasing power and demand for goods from protected industries. International retaliation escalated as countries imposed increasingly severe measures, further reducing American export opportunities.

The tariff also disrupted established supply chains and trade relationships that had developed over decades. American companies lost market share to competitors in countries that hadn't imposed retaliatory measures, creating permanent competitive disadvantages that persisted long after the tariffs were reduced.

Industry Case Studies: Promises vs. Results

Automotive Industry: Despite tariff protection for domestic auto manufacturers, the industry suffered catastrophic losses due to retaliation. Ford's international sales fell from a profitable global operation to minimal overseas presence. Rather than creating jobs, the combination of protection and retaliation destroyed employment in both manufacturing and export sectors.

Agriculture: Farmers who supported tariffs believing they would raise commodity prices instead saw their export markets evaporate. The Midwest, which had supported protective measures, experienced widespread farm foreclosures and rural bank failures as agricultural income collapsed.

Steel and Manufacturing: Protected industries initially celebrated higher domestic prices, but soon faced reduced demand as other sectors contracted and international competitiveness declined. The temporary gains from protection were overwhelmed by the broader economic collapse.

The Political Reckoning

Smoot-Hawley's failure discredited protectionist arguments for a generation. The Roosevelt administration's Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act of 1934 explicitly repudiated unilateral protectionism, establishing a framework for bilateral trade negotiations that gradually reduced tariffs while maintaining limited protection for sensitive industries.

The legislation also demonstrated the futility of addressing macroeconomic problems through trade policy. The Great Depression's underlying causes—financial instability, monetary contraction, and deflationary pressures—required macroeconomic solutions, not microeconomic interventions like tariffs.

Lessons for Today

Smoot-Hawley provides clear lessons for contemporary policy debates:

  • Retaliation is inevitable: Modern economies have even greater retaliatory capacity than 1930s trading partners

  • Export industries suffer most: Protection of some sectors comes at the expense of others

  • Consumer costs are real: Tariffs function as regressive consumption taxes regardless of political rhetoric

  • Economic integration makes unilateral action self-defeating: Global supply chains create vulnerabilities that didn't exist in 1930
