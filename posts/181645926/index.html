Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 11: The Impossible Cooperation - How Rival Nations Will Govern the Moon

December 23, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 2025

In Part 10, I made the technical case for lunar data centers: the latency doesn’t matter for 70-80% of AI workloads, Lonestar already has operational infrastructure on the surface, permanently shadowed craters provide free cooling, and the Moon solves the orbital debris problem that threatens to make low Earth orbit unusable.

The technical advantages are overwhelming. The business case is compelling. The environmental sustainability is orders of magnitude better than orbital alternatives.

But there’s one problem that makes all of it seem irrelevant: Who governs the Moon?

Right now, we have competing geopolitical blocs racing for the same territory, no international agreement on resource extraction, and laws explicitly prohibiting cooperation between the major space powers. The governance situation isn’t just complicated—it’s deliberately structured to prevent the cooperation that lunar infrastructure would require.

The Moon solution seems technically perfect but politically impossible.

Except this exact problem has been solved before. Under worse conditions. For higher stakes. And the precedent proves that what seems impossible today becomes inevitable once crisis creates necessity.

The Governance Crisis Nobody Wants to Acknowledge

The optimistic answer to “who governs the Moon” would be the 1979 Moon Agreement, which declares lunar resources the “common heritage of mankind” and requires an international regime before commercial extraction begins. It’s exactly the kind of cooperative framework that would make lunar data centers work for everyone.

The problem? Only 18 countries have signed it. The United States, Russia, and China—the three nations with actual lunar capability—all refused to ratify it.

In 2020, President Trump issued an executive order explicitly calling the Moon Agreement “a failed attempt at constraining free enterprise.” In 2023, Saudi Arabia became the first nation to withdraw from the treaty—the first-ever withdrawal from any UN space treaty.

The Moon Agreement is legally dead.

Instead, we have competing geopolitical blocs racing for the same territory:

The Artemis Accords (US-led):

  • 60 countries signed (as of November 2025)

  • Explicitly allows commercial resource extraction

  • Includes “safety zones” around landing sites (de facto territorial claims)

  • Requirement for participating in NASA’s Artemis program

  • China and Russia reject it as “colonial land-taking methods”

  • Partners include: UK, France, Germany, Japan, India, Canada, Australia, UAE, most of Europe

The International Lunar Research Station (China/Russia-led):

  • 12-14 countries signed

  • Pakistan, South Africa, Belarus, Venezuela, Egypt, Turkey, Azerbaijan

  • Direct competitor to Artemis

  • Targeting the same location: lunar south pole permanently shadowed craters

  • Russia publicly downplaying partnership after Ukraine invasion

  • China trying to attract “Global South” partners

The critical detail: Not a single country has signed both. It’s a binary geopolitical choice. You pick Team USA or Team China.

Both programs plan to build permanent bases. Both plan resource extraction. Both plan manufacturing facilities. Both are targeting the same high-value real estate—the permanently shadowed craters where water ice exists and where data centers would get free cooling.

There are only so many of these craters. They’re limited, valuable, and about to become contested territory.

The Wolf Amendment—a 2011 US law—prohibits NASA from cooperating with China without explicit Congressional approval. China is excluded from the International Space Station for the same reason. The geopolitical division isn’t just diplomatic posturing; it’s encoded in law.

So the Moon solution faces an impossible problem: the technical advantages are overwhelming, but international cooperation seems unachievable. We’re heading toward a lunar land rush with competing claims, no enforcement mechanism, and no agreed rules for conflict resolution.

This is the governance crisis. And it makes lunar data centers seem like a pipe dream.

Except this “impossible” problem was solved once before—under conditions that were arguably worse.

December 1, 1959: When Sworn Enemies Cooperated

On December 1, 1959, at the absolute height of the Cold War, twelve nations signed the Antarctic Treaty in Washington, D.C.

The signatories included the United States and the Soviet Union—adversaries who’d brought the world to the brink of nuclear war just three years earlier during the Hungarian Revolution. They included Argentina and the United Kingdom, who’d exchanged military threats over Antarctic territory. They included seven nations with competing territorial claims that overlapped and contradicted each other.

The situation in Antarctica was worse than the current Moon crisis:

The Antarctica situation (1950s):

  • Argentina fired warning shots at British researchers in 1952

  • UK sent a warship and landed marines in response

  • USSR establishing bases at strategic locations, alarming the West

  • Nuclear weapons deployment was a real threat

  • Everyone wanted the resources: coal, fishing, whaling

  • No international legal framework existed

  • Seven nations had irreconcilable territorial claims

  • Argentina sent a fleet of eight warships in 1948

  • Cold War at its peak—ideological enemies, nuclear standoff, zero trust

Yet they reached agreement.

The Antarctic Treaty:

  • Demilitarized the entire continent—no military bases, no weapons testing, no nuclear deployment

  • Froze all territorial claims—didn’t resolve them, just shelved them indefinitely

  • Required transparency—all activities must be declared, all bases open to inspection

  • Mandated scientific cooperation—data must be shared freely

  • Established regular meetings—annual consultative meetings to manage conflicts

The treaty didn’t resolve the fundamental disputes. It created a framework to manage them peacefully while pursuing shared interests.

And it worked.

Sixty-five years later, through the entire Cold War, through dozens of leadership changes, through the collapse of the Soviet Union, through multiple international crises, the Antarctic Treaty still functions. It now has 58 parties. It’s considered one of the most successful international agreements in history.

The 1991 Madrid Protocol added a comprehensive ban on mining and strict environmental protections—demonstrating that the system can evolve to address new challenges.

What made it possible wasn’t trust or friendship or shared values. It was the recognition of mutual interest and mutual risk.

The Breakthrough: How Scientists Made Politics Possible

The key breakthrough came from the International Geophysical Year (1957-58), when scientists from opposing blocs worked together in Antarctica.

American meteorologists “wintered over” at the Soviet base Mirnyy. Soviet scientists worked at the American station Little America. They shared data, coordinated research, and proved that cooperation was physically possible.

The scientific cooperation created political space for the treaty. Once governments saw that their scientists could work together productively, the diplomatic framework became achievable.

President Eisenhower saw the model clearly. On September 22, 1960, he proposed that “the principles of the Antarctic Treaty be applied to outer space and celestial bodies.”

That proposal became the Outer Space Treaty of 1967—the foundational document that still governs space activities today. The Antarctic precedent directly inspired space law.

The pattern is clear: Scientific cooperation demonstrates feasibility → Business interests demand legal certainty → Crisis creates urgency → Diplomatic framework emerges

This isn’t idealistic thinking. It’s historical pattern recognition.

How Crisis Forces Cooperation: The Realistic Timeline

Here’s the likely path forward for lunar governance, based not on optimism but on historical patterns, current incentives, and the Antarctic precedent:

Phase 1: Competitive Sprint (2025-2030)

What’s happening:

  • SpaceX, Google, Blue Origin deploy orbital data centers

  • China and US race for lunar south pole landing sites

  • Both Artemis and ILRS establish initial bases

  • Competition is real, rhetoric is hostile, cooperation seems impossible

  • Media frames it as “New Space Race”

This is where we are now.

The momentum is all toward competition. Both sides want to be first. Both sides view this as strategic advantage. Cooperation is politically toxic in both Washington and Beijing.

Phase 2: The Forcing Event (2028-2032)

The orbital crisis: A major collision destroys high-value satellites. Not a near-miss—an actual debris-generating crash between $500 million+ assets. Insurance rates spike. Launch providers face liability concerns. Kessler Syndrome transitions from theoretical risk to observed reality.

Additional near-miss incidents become routine. The Chinese rocket stage near-miss of 2025 becomes monthly occurrences. Eventually one results in catastrophic collision.

The lunar proof: Simultaneously, both US and Chinese lunar missions achieve preliminary success. Water ice is confirmed in quantities that make extraction viable. Permanently shadowed craters prove suitable for infrastructure. Resource extraction becomes real, not theoretical.

Lonestar’s petabyte-scale facilities demonstrate commercial viability. Private operators want to scale up but can’t get financing without legal clarity.

The evidence becomes undeniable: Orbital is untenable at scale, and the Moon works technically.

Phase 3: Business Pressure for Legal Certainty (2030-2035)

Commercial operators demand frameworks:

SpaceX, Blue Origin, Chinese state enterprises, private data center companies—all face the same problem. They can’t invest billions without knowing:

  • Who owns the resources they extract?

  • What happens if bases overlap or conflict?

  • How are disputes resolved?

  • What are the liability rules for accidents?

  • Who has rights to specific crater sites?

Insurance companies won’t underwrite missions. Lunar operations are high-risk; without legal frameworks, they’re uninsurable at any price.

Banks won’t finance projects. Multi-billion dollar investments require predictable legal frameworks. Uncertainty means no loans.

Shareholders demand risk mitigation. Public companies can’t deploy capital into legal vacuums. Boards require governance frameworks before approving major lunar investments.

This is the pattern from:

  • Maritime law (Law of the Sea)

  • Aviation law (Chicago Convention)

  • Telecommunications (ITU spectrum allocation)

  • Deep seabed mining (although imperfect)

Commercial interests eventually force governments to cooperate because uncertainty costs more than compromise.

The business case for lunar infrastructure is compelling—but it requires legal certainty. Companies will pressure governments: “Give us a framework or we can’t proceed.”

Phase 4: The Negotiation Window (2033-2037)

Leadership changes create diplomatic opportunities:

  • Trump won’t be president forever (constitutionally limited even if re-elected)

  • Putin won’t lead Russia indefinitely (age 72 in 2025, historical patterns suggest succession within 10-15 years)

  • Xi Jinping will eventually be succeeded (currently 72, Chinese leadership transitions typically occur)

New leaders mean new diplomatic possibilities. Fresh faces aren’t wedded to old positions. Electoral politics reward solving crises, not perpetuating them.

Legal obstacles can be removed:

The Wolf Amendment is US law, not constitutional mandate. Congress can repeal it with simple majority vote. If business constituencies (SpaceX, Blue Origin, data center operators) demand it, Congressional dynamics shift.

China’s aggressive territorial stance can soften as practical needs emerge. When commercial operators demand cooperation for investment certainty, even authoritarian governments respond to economic imperatives.

Russia’s position as junior partner to China may shift as economic realities change post-Ukraine war settlement.

The convergence:

  • Crisis event (orbital debris) creates urgency

  • Proven lunar viability demonstrates feasibility

  • Business pressure demands legal frameworks

  • Leadership transitions open diplomatic space

  • Public pressure for conflict resolution

This creates the negotiating window.

Phase 5: The Lunar Governance Framework (2035-2040)

An agreement emerges, modeled on Antarctic Treaty:

The framework won’t be the failed 1979 Moon Agreement. It will be something new, pragmatic, focused on managing conflicts rather than preventing resource use.

Key provisions likely include:

  1. Resource extraction allowed but regulated

    • International oversight without prohibiting commercial activity

    • Notification requirements for extraction operations

    • Revenue sharing or fee structure for global benefit (similar to deep seabed mining proposals)

  2. Safety zones acknowledged but limited

    • Prevents unlimited territorial expansion

    • Defined radius around active bases

    • Time-limited (must be using the resource actively)

    • Registry system for claims

  3. Data and scientific cooperation required

    • Benefits everyone, costs little

    • Lunar climate data, seismic data, resource surveys shared openly

    • Builds trust through practical cooperation

  4. Regular consultative meetings

    • Annual or biannual meetings of all lunar operators

    • Conflict resolution before escalation

    • Inspection rights (like Antarctic Treaty)

  5. Transparency in all activities

    • Declaration of missions, locations, purposes

    • Open registry of who’s operating where

    • Warning system for potential conflicts

  6. Environmental protection standards

    • Prevents worst excesses

    • Protects scientifically valuable sites

    • Limits debris and waste

  7. Dispute resolution mechanism

    • Arbitration system for conflicts

    • Graduated response (negotiation → mediation → arbitration)

    • Enforcement through economic pressure (insurance, financing access)

What this framework does:

It doesn’t resolve who “owns” the Moon. It creates a functional governance system that allows everyone to operate while managing conflicts.

Like the Antarctic Treaty, it freezes the hardest questions while establishing rules for behavior.

Why This Is Realistic, Not Idealistic

1. Precedent exists and has succeeded for 65+ years

The Antarctic Treaty proved sworn enemies can cooperate on territorial governance. The Outer Space Treaty extended this to space. The ISS partnership continued through Ukraine war. Montreal Protocol achieved universal agreement on ozone during Cold War.

Historical evidence shows “impossible” cooperation happens when necessity demands it.

2. Mutual destruction from inaction is obvious

Orbital debris affects everyone equally. Legal uncertainty blocks everyone’s investments. Resource conflicts harm all parties. The costs of non-cooperation are clear and immediate.

3. Commercial pressure is enormous

Unlike government space programs, commercial operators demand legal certainty for investment. They have shareholders, boards, fiduciary duties. They will lobby governments aggressively for frameworks that enable business.

SpaceX wants to scale Starlink. Google wants orbital data centers. Chinese commercial space industry is booming. Everyone needs governance frameworks.

4. Technical proof of concept already exists

Lonestar demonstrates lunar viability now. This isn’t theoretical—it’s operational. Once both US and Chinese missions confirm water ice and resource viability, the business case becomes undeniable.

5. Leadership is temporary, not permanent

Current obstacles are people and policies, not structural impossibilities. Laws can be changed. Executives can be replaced. New leaders bring new possibilities.

The Wolf Amendment was passed in 2011. It can be repealed in 2031.

6. The alternative is unacceptable to everyone

Without governance:

  • Insurance industry won’t underwrite missions

  • Banks won’t finance infrastructure

  • Shareholders won’t approve investments

  • Orbital debris makes low Earth orbit unusable

  • Resource conflicts create international incidents

  • Everyone loses

With governance:

  • Legal certainty enables investment

  • Conflict resolution prevents escalation

  • Everyone can operate profitably

  • Sustainable infrastructure at scale

The incentive structure overwhelmingly favors cooperation once the crisis forces the choice.

The Timeline Matters

Artwork depicting lunar mining operations for Helium-3 involving harvesters, a solar power plant, rovers and return launchers. Credit: Interlune

This won’t happen in 2026 or 2028. The competitive phase is real and necessary. Both sides need to demonstrate capability, establish presence, and prove technical viability.

But by 2030-2035, the forcing events create urgency. Orbital debris incidents, lunar resource confirmation, business demands, and leadership transitions converge to open the negotiating window.

By 2035-2040, a pragmatic framework emerges—not because humans are noble, but because the alternative becomes intolerable to commercial interests, insurance industries, and political leaders who want investment and economic growth.

The cooperation doesn’t require trust. It requires recognition of shared necessity.

What This Means for Lunar Data Centers

The technical case for lunar data centers is overwhelming (as Part 10 demonstrated). The governance challenge is real but solvable.

The path forward:

Near-term (2025-2030): Competitive deployment. Artemis and ILRS both establish bases. Orbital data centers prove concept. No cooperation.

Mid-term (2030-2035): Crisis forces negotiation. Orbital debris, business pressure, leadership changes create opening. Pragmatic framework emerges.

Long-term (2035+): Lunar infrastructure scales under international framework. 70-80% of AI computing moves to Moon. Orbital handles minimal real-time workloads. System operates profitably for all participants.

The Antarctic Treaty took decades to develop environmental protections beyond its original scope. The lunar framework will evolve similarly—starting pragmatic, becoming more comprehensive as experience grows.

The Bottom Line

When I started researching lunar governance, I expected to conclude it was politically impossible. The competing blocs, the legal prohibitions, the territorial disputes—it all seemed insurmountable.

But history proves otherwise.

The Antarctic Treaty was signed when US-Soviet tensions were arguably worse than US-China tensions today. Nuclear war seemed imminent. Territorial disputes had already turned violent. Yet cooperation emerged because mutual interest outweighed mutual distrust.

The same pattern will repeat for the Moon:

  1. Scientific cooperation proves feasibility (Lonestar operational now)

  2. Crisis creates urgency (orbital debris, resource conflicts)

  3. Business demands legal certainty (insurance, financing, investment)

  4. Leadership transitions open diplomatic space (new faces, new possibilities)

  5. Framework emerges (pragmatic, focused on managing conflicts, modeled on Antarctic success)

This isn’t idealism. It’s pattern recognition based on historical precedent.

The technical case for the Moon is clear. The governance challenge is real but solvable. And the timeline—while measured in decades, not years—points toward inevitable cooperation.

Not because nations trust each other. But because the alternative is mutual failure, and eventually that becomes unacceptable to everyone with money at stake.

The Moon offers the solution to AI’s energy crisis. The Antarctic Treaty offers the blueprint for governance. The only question is whether we’re smart enough to cooperate before crisis forces our hand.

History suggests we’ll probably need the crisis first. But history also proves that once crisis creates necessity, cooperation becomes achievable—even between sworn enemies.
