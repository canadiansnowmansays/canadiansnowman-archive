The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 7: The Ring of Fire Burns Out

March 19, 2026

Editor’s Note

This essay was written in January 2026, documenting Iran’s regional proxy network collapse throughout 2024–2025. Syria fallen. Hezbollah crippled. The Houthis degraded. The “Ring of Fire” that Khamenei spent decades and billions constructing had already burned itself out.

When I wrote it, that collapse was the story. By February 28, 2026, it had become the prologue — the strategic weakening that stripped Iran’s defences before the US-Israeli strikes made Khamenei’s killing possible. Read this as what it was: the disintegration of the outer walls before the fortress itself fell.

The analysis requires no revision. History provided the footnote.

— Stephen, March 2026

Author’s Note: This essay documents the collapse of Iran’s regional proxy network, including the fall of the Assad regime in Syria (December 8, 2024), and the weakening of Hezbollah, Hamas, and other Iranian-backed groups. Primary sources include Syrian Observatory for Human Rights, Washington Post, Reuters, BBC, Al Jazeera, Brookings Institution, and regional analysts. Assad’s flight to Moscow is confirmed by Russian state media and multiple Western intelligence agencies.

How Iran’s Regional Empire Collapsed Throughout 2024-2025

Iran’s ‘Ring of Fire’ / Axis of Resistance: Proxies in Lebanon (Hezbollah), Gaza (Hamas), Syria (linchpin corridor), Iraq (Shia militias), and Yemen (Houthis) encircling Israel — the network Khamenei spent decades and hundreds of billions building. The Iran Primer / USIP

For four decades, Ayatollah Khamenei invested Iran’s resources—diplomatic, military, economic—in building what he called the “Axis of Resistance”: a network of proxy militias and allied groups encircling Israel and challenging American influence across the Middle East.

The strategy consumed tens of billions of dollars. Iran spent an estimated $30-50 billion propping up Bashar al-Assad’s regime in Syria alone. Billions more flowed annually to Hezbollah in Lebanon, Hamas in Gaza, the Houthis in Yemen, and Shia militias in Iraq.

The goal: regional hegemony, Israel’s eventual destruction, and Iran positioned as Umm al-Qura (Mother of Cities) of the Islamic world.

By December 2024, the entire architecture had collapsed.

Hamas was militarily devastated. Hezbollah was crippled and humiliated. Assad had fled to Moscow after his regime crumbled in less than two weeks. Iranian influence in Syria—the linchpin of the entire regional strategy—was gone.

Four decades of investment. Hundreds of billions spent. Thousands of Iranians killed in foreign wars. All while ordinary Iranians at home went hungry, faced unemployment, suffered economic crisis.

And in fourteen months—October 2023 to December 2024—the “Ring of Fire” became ash.

By Khamenei’s own standards—protecting Iranian interests, exporting the revolution, establishing regional power—the collapse stands as strategic catastrophe and evidence of profound failure.

I. THE ARCHITECTURE KHAMENEI BUILT

The “Axis of Resistance” wasn’t spontaneous—it was methodically constructed over decades.

Lebanon: Hezbollah

Iran’s relationship with Hezbollah dates to the 1980s Lebanese civil war. According to Iranian officials, including former Defense Minister Hossein Dehghan, Syria facilitated the IRGC’s entry into Lebanon in 1983, where they helped establish Hezbollah.

For four decades, Iran provided:

  • Training by IRGC Quds Force

  • Advanced weaponry (rockets, missiles, drones)

  • Annual funding estimated at hundreds of millions to over $1 billion

  • Strategic guidance and operational support

Hezbollah became Iran’s crown jewel—a sophisticated military force that fought Israel to standstill in 2006, maintained arsenal of 150,000+ rockets, controlled Lebanese politics, and projected Iranian power to Israel’s northern border.

Gaza: Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad

Though Hamas is Sunni (Iran is Shia), shared opposition to Israel created alliance. Iran provided:

  • Weapons smuggling through tunnel networks

  • Missile and drone technology

  • Training in Iran and Syria

  • Financial support

Palestinian Islamic Jihad, more ideologically aligned with Iran, received similar support.

Syria: The Linchpin

Syria was critical—the geographic and strategic connector. Assad’s regime provided:

  • Land corridor from Iran through Iraq to Lebanon

  • Bases for IRGC and proxy forces

  • Weapons production facilities

  • Transit route for arms to Hezbollah

  • Airspace and logistics support

Iran called Syria its “gateway to the Arab world” and “35th province.” Assad himself acknowledged Syria’s role as Iran’s regional anchor.

Iraq: Shia Militias

After 2003 U.S. invasion, Iran cultivated Iraqi Shia militias:

  • Asa’ib Ahl al-Haq

  • Kataib Hezbollah

  • Badr Organization

  • Harakat Hezbollah al-Nujaba

These groups fought both U.S. forces and ISIS, gained political influence, and operated under IRGC-Quds Force guidance.

Yemen: The Houthis

Iran supported Houthi rebels fighting Saudi-backed Yemeni government, providing:

  • Weapons (including missiles and drones)

  • Training

  • Strategic advice

  • Financial support

The Strategic Vision

The network encircled Israel: Hezbollah north, Hamas and PIJ southwest, Syrian-based forces east, and more distant pressure from Iraq and Yemen. Khamenei called it Iran’s “forward defense”—fighting enemies away from Iranian borders.

The investment was staggering. But the payoff, he promised, would be strategic: Israel under permanent threat, American influence challenged, Iranian hegemony established, and the revolution exported successfully.

II. OCTOBER 7, 2023: THE HAMAS GAMBIT

On October 7, 2023, Hamas launched surprise attacks on southern Israel, killing approximately 1,200 people and taking hundreds hostage.

Iran denied direct involvement in planning. But IRGC-Quds Force training, Iranian weapons, and years of strategic support made the attack possible—whatever Iran’s advance knowledge.

Israel’s response was overwhelming: a military campaign in Gaza designed to eliminate Hamas as military and governing force.

Hamas Degraded

Over 14 months, Israeli operations:

  • Killed Yahya Sinwar (October 7 architect)

  • Eliminated countless commanders and fighters

  • Destroyed tunnel networks

  • Degraded military infrastructure

  • Reduced Hamas from formidable force to guerrilla remnants

By late 2024, Hamas remained as organization but its military capability was shattered. Iran’s primary Palestinian proxy was devastated.

Iran’s First Loss

Decades of investment in Hamas—money, weapons, training—produced one spectacular attack followed by organizational destruction.

Iran couldn’t or wouldn’t intervene directly to save Hamas. The proxy was expendable. But the loss demonstrated limits of Iran’s “forward defense”: proxies can be crushed while Iran watches.

III. HEZBOLLAH: THE JEWEL SHATTERED

Throughout 2024, tensions between Israel and Hezbollah escalated. Low-intensity conflict along Lebanon-Israel border grew more intense.

Then came September 2024.

September 17-18: The Pager Attacks

Thousands of pagers used by Hezbollah operatives exploded simultaneously across Lebanon. The next day, walkie-talkies exploded. The attacks killed dozens, wounded thousands, and exposed catastrophic Israeli intelligence penetration of Hezbollah’s communications and supply chains.

The psychological impact was devastating: Hezbollah members couldn’t trust their equipment. Israeli intelligence had turned their communications devices into weapons.

September 27: Nasrallah Assassinated

Hassan Nasrallah, Hezbollah leader since 1992, assassinated in an Israeli airstrike on Sept 27, 2024 — a symbolic and strategic catastrophe for Iran’s crown jewel proxy. Los Angeles Times

Hassan Nasrallah, Hezbollah’s charismatic leader since 1992, was killed in an Israeli airstrike on Hezbollah’s underground headquarters in Beirut.

His death was strategic and symbolic catastrophe. Nasrallah had been Hezbollah’s face, voice, and strategic mind for over three decades. His assassination demonstrated Israel could reach anyone, anywhere in Lebanon.

October-November: Systematic Degradation

Israeli strikes continued systematically:

  • Senior commanders killed

  • Weapons depots destroyed

  • Command centers hit

  • Communication networks disrupted

  • Rocket launchers eliminated

November: The Ceasefire

By November 2024, Hezbollah accepted ceasefire on what analysts called “extremely unfavorable terms.” The organization that had claimed victory against Israel in 2006 was accepting defeat in 2024.

Post-ceasefire assessments:

  • Arsenal significantly reduced

  • Leadership decimated

  • Infrastructure damaged

  • Deterrent capability degraded

  • Political standing in Lebanon weakened

Experts estimated Hezbollah would need years and massive investment to rebuild—if rebuilding were even possible given Israeli intelligence advantages.

Iran’s Crown Jewel Destroyed

Four decades building Hezbollah into regional military power. Billions spent. IRGC advisors embedded. Strategic centerpiece of “Ring of Fire.”

Eighteen months later: devastated, deterrent broken, leader dead, forced into humiliating ceasefire.

IV. DECEMBER 8, 2024: ASSAD FALLS

The Syrian civil war had ground on since 2011. By 2024, front lines were mostly frozen. Assad controlled key territories with Russian and Iranian support. Rebels held pockets, primarily in Idlib province.

Then everything changed.

Late November: The Offensive Begins

Rebel forces led by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS) launched offensive from Idlib. Within days, they captured Aleppo—Syria’s second-largest city, which had been under Assad control since 2016.

The speed stunned observers. Syrian army units collapsed or retreated without serious resistance.

The Rapid Collapse

After Aleppo:

  • Hama fell within days

  • Homs fell shortly after

  • December 8: Rebels entered Damascus

Assad fled. Within hours, reports confirmed he’d reached Moscow. Russia granted him asylum.

Fifty-three years of Assad family rule ended in less than two weeks.

Why Iran Couldn’t Save Assad

Syrian rebels and civilians celebrate in Damascus as Assad’s regime collapses and he flees to Moscow, December 8, 2024 — Iran loses its strategic linchpin after $30-50 billion invested. CNN

Multiple factors:

  • Hezbollah too weakened to intervene (normally would send fighters)

  • Iraqi militias refused to deploy (wary of U.S./Israeli strikes)

  • IRGC presence in Syria was minimal (drawn down after Israeli strikes throughout 2024)

  • Syrian army demoralized, unwilling to fight

  • Iranian resources strained after June 2025 war

  • Russia focused on Ukraine, provided minimal support

Iran evacuated IRGC personnel and advisors as rebels advanced. No desperate defense. No last stand. Just evacuation.

What Iran Lost

Everything Syria provided:

  • Land corridor to Lebanon: Cut. No direct route to supply Hezbollah.

  • Weapons production facilities: Gone. Manufacturing sites for precision missiles and drones lost.

  • Strategic depth: Lost. Iran’s forward position in the Arab world eliminated.

  • Only Arab state ally: Gone. Every other regional Arab state opposed Iran.

  • $30-50 billion investment: Wasted. Decades of financial and military support for nothing.

  • Gateway to Arab world: Closed. Syria was Iran’s primary Arab partner.

Khamenei’s Initial Response

Days after Assad’s fall, Khamenei made no public statement. Finally, he dismissed it as “temporary setback.”

A week later, IRGC Commander called it a “bitter event”—acknowledging what Khamenei wouldn’t: this was catastrophic strategic loss.

V. THE “LOOK EAST” FAILURE

After the U.S. withdrew from the JCPOA nuclear deal in 2018, Khamenei pursued “Look East” strategy—pivoting from attempts at Western engagement toward Russia and China.

He promised these partnerships would protect Iran, provide economic alternatives, offer diplomatic cover.

June 2025: The Test

When Israel and the U.S. attacked Iran in June 2025, Russia and China’s responses:

Russia: Issued diplomatic statements condemning attacks. Provided zero military assistance. Putin was focused on Ukraine and unwilling to confront U.S./Israel militarily for Iran’s sake.

China: Issued diplomatic statements. Continued buying Iranian oil (their economic interest). Provided zero military assistance or diplomatic protection beyond words.

November-December 2024: Syria’s Collapse

When rebels threatened Assad:

Russia: Conducted some airstrikes but minimal compared to 2015-2019 intervention. Didn’t deploy ground forces. Didn’t prevent regime collapse. Accepted Assad in Moscow—and likely negotiated keeping military bases in Syria under new government.

China: Did nothing.

The Transactional Truth

Iran’s alliances with Russia and China were transactional, not strategic:

  • Russia wanted regional influence and military bases—not Iranian security

  • China wanted oil at discounts—not confrontation with U.S./Israel

  • Neither would sacrifice their interests for Iran’s

Khamenei’s “Look East” produced economic dependency (80% oil exports to China) without strategic protection.

Iran was more isolated, not less.

VI. THE COST ACCOUNTING

What did four decades of “Axis of Resistance” cost Iran?

Financial:

  • $30-50 billion to Syria (lost)

  • Billions annually to Hezbollah (devastated)

  • Billions to Hamas, PIJ (weakened)

  • Billions to Iraqi militias (limited effectiveness)

  • Billions to Houthis (only “success,” but marginal)

  • Nuclear program: $100-200 billion (damaged in June 2025)

Conservative estimate: $200-300 billion spent over four decades on regional strategy.

Human:

  • Thousands of IRGC members killed in Syria, Iraq, elsewhere

  • Qasem Soleimani (Quds Force commander) killed by U.S. in 2020

  • Hundreds of advisors and commanders lost

  • Countless proxy fighters dead (not Iranians but Iran-supported)

Political:

  • Regional isolation increased (Arab states normalized with Israel partly to counter Iran)

  • International sanctions intensified

  • Domestic resentment of foreign spending while Iranians suffered

What Iran Gained:

  • Temporary Syrian loyalty (lost December 2024)

  • Hezbollah deterrent (shattered 2024)

  • Hamas capability (destroyed 2023-24)

  • Iraqi political influence (contested, mixed results)

  • Houthi harassment of shipping (marginal impact)

  • Regional instability (success?) but not hegemony

Net Assessment: Hundreds of billions spent, thousands killed, decades invested—for regional position that collapsed in 14 months.

VII. HIS OWN WORDS INDICT HIM

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Supreme Leader of Iran, who invested tens of billions in the Axis of Resistance — a strategy that collapsed into ash by December 2024. Reuters

Throughout his rule, Khamenei justified foreign spending with grand strategic claims.

On Regional Influence (2019): “Our presence in Syria, Lebanon, Iraq isn’t aggression—it’s defense. We support oppressed Muslims resisting Zionist and American aggression. This is exporting the revolution’s values.”

Test: Syria lost. Hezbollah devastated. Hamas crushed. Where’s the successful “export”?

On Strategic Depth (2021): “Fighting enemies away from our borders protects Iran. Our allies provide strategic depth. Enemies know attacking Iran means facing our regional network.”

Test: June 2025, enemies attacked Iran directly. Regional network was irrelevant or couldn’t respond. Strategic depth proved illusory.

On Priorities (2017): “Some ask why we spend in Syria when Iranians face economic problems. This is shortsighted. Defending Syria defends Iran. Regional security enables domestic prosperity.”

Test: By 2024, Syria was lost and Iranians faced worse economic crisis than 2017. Foreign spending didn’t enable prosperity—it competed with domestic needs.

The Iranian People’s Verdict

Protest slogans across multiple years:

  • 2017: “Leave Syria, think about us!”

  • 2019: “No Gaza, no Lebanon, my life for Iran!”

  • 2025: “Leave Syria, think about us!” (updated version)

Iranians weren’t asking complex geopolitical questions. They were asking simple ones: Why spend billions abroad while we starve? Why die in Syria while Iranian cities have no water?

The Revolutionary Standard

The revolution promised to serve Iranians, especially the oppressed masses. Foreign policy should protect Iranian interests and people.

Test Khamenei’s foreign policy:

  • Did it protect Iranians? No—made them targets

  • Did it serve their interests? No—consumed resources they needed

  • Did it improve their lives? No—contributed to economic crisis

By revolutionary standards—serving the people—foreign adventurism was failure.

VIII. THE IRANIAN EXCEPTION: THE HOUTHIS

Houthi supporters shout slogans during a rally against Israel and the United States’ war on Iran, in Sanaa, Yemen, Friday, March 13, 2026. (AP Photo/Osamah Abdulrahman)

Notably, one Iranian proxy thrived: Yemen’s Houthis.

Throughout 2023-2025, Houthis:

  • Attacked shipping in Red Sea and Gulf of Aden

  • Launched drones and missiles toward Israel (mostly intercepted)

  • Survived U.S./UK airstrikes with capabilities largely intact

  • Expanded territorial control in Yemen

  • Demonstrated resilience despite military pressure

But Houthi “success” was marginal strategically. They harassed shipping and launched largely ineffective attacks on Israel, but they didn’t threaten Israel’s existence, didn’t provide Iran strategic depth, and didn’t offset losses elsewhere.

The exception proved the rule: Iran’s proxy strategy largely failed.

CLOSING: THE RING OF FIRE BECAME ASH

On December 31, 2024, as Assad settled into Moscow exile and rebel forces consolidated control in Damascus, the full scope of Iran’s regional collapse was undeniable.

The “Axis of Resistance”—four decades in building—had disintegrated:

  • Syria: Lost entirely

  • Hezbollah: Militarily devastated

  • Hamas: Organizationally shattered

  • Iraqi militias: Ineffective and reluctant

  • Houthis: Only remaining capable proxy, marginal impact

Four decades. Hundreds of billions of dollars. Thousands of Iranian lives. Countless proxy fighters.

All while ordinary Iranians faced:

  • 50%+ poverty

  • 60% malnourishment

  • 48% inflation

  • Currency collapse

  • Daily blackouts

  • Water shortages

The revolution promised to serve the Iranian people. Khamenei served regional ambitions while Iranians suffered.

By his own standards—protecting Iranian interests, exporting revolution successfully, establishing regional power—December 2024 marked comprehensive strategic failure.

The “Ring of Fire” rhetoric was always hyperbole. But the collapse wasn’t gradual decline—it was rapid disintegration exposing the hollowness beneath decades of propaganda.

Iranians watched billions go to Syria—then Syria fell. They watched billions go to Hezbollah—then Hezbollah was crushed. They watched billions go to Hamas—then Hamas was devastated.

And they asked the obvious question: What was it all for?

The Guardian had no answer. Because there was no answer that didn’t admit strategic catastrophe and policy failure.

The bunker beneath his compound wasn’t just physical protection. It was metaphor for a defensive crouch—hiding from the consequences of forty years of decisions that sacrificed Iranian welfare for regional ambitions that collapsed when tested.

The Ring of Fire burned out. Only the ash remains.
