Ukraine: The Crown Jewel of Putin’s Strategy

Ukraine represents the clearest test case of whose interests Trump serves. Russian and American strategic interests are diametrically opposed:

Russian Strategic Interest: Conquer Ukrainian territory, force regime change or territorial concessions, demonstrate that Western security commitments are meaningless, destroy Ukraine as an independent nation.

American Strategic Interest: Support allied democracy against authoritarian aggression, maintain international norms against territorial conquest, demonstrate credibility of American security commitments, strengthen European stability.

These interests cannot be reconciled. Any policy that advances one necessarily harms the other. So whose interests has Trump consistently advanced?

The First Impeachment: Withholding Military Aid

Trump’s first impeachment centered on his withholding of Congressionally-appropriated military aid to Ukraine—$391 million that Congress had specifically allocated to help Ukraine defend against Russian aggression.

The timeline is damning:

  • July 18, 2019: Office of Management and Budget places hold on Ukraine military aid

  • July 25, 2019: Trump calls Ukrainian President Zelensky, pressures him to announce investigations into Joe Biden

  • August-September 2019: Ukraine officials desperately seek explanation for why aid is frozen

  • September 9, 2019: House committees announce investigation

  • September 11, 2019: Aid is suddenly released

Trump didn’t withhold this aid because he thought Ukraine was corrupt or wanted better oversight. He withheld it to pressure Zelensky to announce investigations that would benefit Trump’s 2020 reelection campaign.

But look at who else benefited: Russia.

Every day that military aid was withheld, Ukraine’s defensive capabilities weakened. Every day Zelensky twisted in the wind wondering whether American support was real, Russia gained strategic advantage. Every day the world watched America treat Ukraine’s security as a bargaining chip for domestic political investigations, Putin’s message that American commitments are unreliable was reinforced.

The aid was eventually released—but only after the whistleblower complaint triggered investigation. Trump held Ukraine’s survival hostage to his personal political needs, giving Russia months of advantage.

Trump was impeached and acquitted along party lines. But the damage was done. Ukraine learned that American military aid comes with strings attached to the president’s personal interests. Russia learned that Trump could be pressured to undermine American commitments to Ukraine.

On February 22, 2022, Trump told a rally: “Putin declares a big portion of Ukraine as independent. Oh, that’s wonderful. So Putin is now saying it’s independent — a large section of Ukraine. I said, how smart is that? And he’s going to go in and be a peacekeeper. That’s the strongest peace force. We could use that on our southern border. That’s the strongest peace force I’ve ever seen. Here’s a guy who’s very savvy.”

Trump called an invasion—an act of war that would kill tens of thousands—”wonderful” and “genius.” He praised Putin’s transparent pretext of “peacekeeping” as something America should emulate.

Even after images of Bucha emerged—documented evidence of Russian forces massacring civilians, including women and children—Trump refused to clearly condemn Russian war crimes. When asked directly if Putin was a “war criminal,” Trump deflected: “I think it’s a terrible thing that’s happening.”

Not “yes, he’s a war criminal.” Not “Russia must be held accountable.” Just “it’s a terrible thing”—the kind of passive phrasing you’d use for bad weather, not systematic atrocities.

NATO: Destroying the Alliance Putin Has Always Hated

NATO is the most successful defensive alliance in history, preventing major European war for 75 years and serving as the primary obstacle to Russian expansion. NATO’s Article 5—the commitment that an attack on one member is an attack on all—is what keeps Putin from rolling tanks into the Baltics.

Putin has spent his entire career trying to weaken or destroy NATO. So what has Trump done?

Public Attacks and Threats to Withdraw

Trump has repeatedly called NATO “obsolete,” questioned its value, and suggested it’s a bad deal for America. In his first term, Trump told advisors he wanted to withdraw from NATO entirely. According to The New York Times, Trump told then-National Security Advisor John Bolton in 2018: “I don’t give a shit about NATO.”

The “fair share” argument is particularly insidious. Trump frames his attacks on NATO as concern about burden-sharing, making it sound like he’s standing up for American taxpayers. But the effect of his rhetoric is to undermine the alliance’s credibility and cohesion—which is exactly what Putin wants.

NATO doesn’t weaken because members don’t spend 2% of GDP on defense. NATO weakens when members doubt whether America will actually honor Article 5 commitments. Trump’s attacks create exactly that doubt.

In February 2025, Trump said at a rally: “One of the presidents of a big country stood up and said, ‘Well, sir, if we don’t pay, and we’re attacked by Russia, will you protect us?’ I said, ‘You didn’t pay, you’re delinquent?’ He said, ‘Yes, let’s say that happened.’ No, I would not protect you. In fact, I would encourage them to do whatever the hell they want. You got to pay. You got to pay your bills.”

“I would encourage them to do whatever the hell they want.”

The President of the United States is telling Putin that if Russia invades the Baltics, America might not respond—and in fact, Trump would “encourage” Russian aggression against American allies.

This isn’t burden-sharing negotiation. This is the collapse of collective security guarantees that have prevented major war in Europe for eight decades.

Syria: Handing Strategic Territory to Russia

In October 2019, Trump abruptly ordered the withdrawal of American forces from northern Syria. The decision came after a phone call with Turkish President Erdogan, without consulting military leadership or allied commanders.

The immediate result: Kurdish forces who had fought alongside Americans against ISIS were exposed to Turkish attacks. Strategic territory was abandoned. And Russian forces moved into former American bases within hours.

Turkey got to attack Kurds. ISIS got breathing room. Russia gained strategic military presence in the Middle East.

America gained nothing except international humiliation and the blood of betrayed Kurdish allies.

But Russia gained enormously:

  • Strategic territory in Syria

  • Military bases previously occupied by American forces

  • Demonstrated American unreliability to allies

  • Showed the world that American security commitments could be revoked on presidential whim

The decision was made so quickly that American forces had to scramble to evacuate. They left behind facilities that Russian forces occupied immediately—suggesting Russia knew the decision was coming before American commanders did.

Russia’s strategic interest in Syria: Maintain military bases, demonstrate American unreliability, gain regional influence, consolidate Assad’s regime.

Trump’s action: Abrupt withdrawal that handed territory to Russia and destroyed American credibility.

Once again, Trump’s decision served Russian interests while harming American ones.
