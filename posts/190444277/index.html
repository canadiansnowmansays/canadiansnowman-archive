He Didn’t Kill Himself

Part 27: What the DOJ Finally Released About His Death

March 10, 2026

March 10, 2026

On the morning of August 10, 2019, Jeffrey Epstein was found dead in his cell at the Metropolitan Correctional Center in Manhattan. The official ruling was suicide by hanging. The medical examiner signed off. The Justice Department closed the case. And the phrase “Epstein didn’t kill himself” became a meme, a joke, a conspiracy theory to be dismissed by serious people.

On March 7, 2026—nearly seven years later—the Department of Justice quietly released documents that suggest those serious people might owe the conspiracy theorists an apology.

The documents reveal that Tova Noel, one of two guards responsible for monitoring Epstein the night he died, googled “latest on Epstein in jail” twice in the 40 minutes before his body was discovered. That she made a mysterious $5,000 cash deposit ten days before his death. That the FBI believes she was the unidentified orange shape caught on surveillance video carrying linen toward Epstein’s cell around 10:40 PM—the last time any guard approached that tier.

And that an inmate told the FBI he heard guards shouting “Breathe! Breathe!” followed by “Dudes, you killed that dude,” and a female guard saying “If he is dead, we’re going to cover it up.”

This isn’t speculation. This is what’s in the Justice Department’s own files.

And it raises a question that won’t go away: If Jeffrey Epstein killed himself, why does every piece of evidence suggest he didn’t?

What Tova Noel Did That Night

Let’s start with the timeline.

Tova Noel was one of two correctional officers assigned to monitor the Special Housing Unit at the Metropolitan Correctional Center on the night of August 9-10, 2019. Her job was to check on inmates every 30 minutes. She didn’t do that. Instead, according to records released by the Justice Department, she spent the night napping and shopping online for furniture.

Around 10:40 PM, security cameras captured an orange flash moving toward the tier where Epstein’s cell was located. An internal FBI briefing concluded: “At approximately 10:40 pm, a correctional officer, believed to be Tova Noel, carried linen or inmate clothing up to the L-Tier, last time any correctional officer approached the only entrance to the SHU tier.”

That’s important because Epstein was found with strips of orange cloth around his neck.

When questioned by the DOJ in 2021, Noel denied providing any linen to Epstein. “I never gave out linen — ever,” she said. That was the responsibility of the previous shift, she claimed. She said she last saw Epstein alive “somewhere around after 10” PM.

But the FBI’s 66-page forensic examination of the Bureau of Prisons desktop computers tells a different story about what Noel was doing in the hours before Epstein’s death.

At 5:42 AM on August 10, she googled “latest on Epstein in jail.”

Eight minutes later, at 5:52 AM, she searched the same term again.

Less than 40 minutes later, at 6:30 AM, her colleague Michael Thomas found Jeffrey Epstein dead.

Why would a prison guard google an inmate she was supposed to be monitoring—twice—in the 40 minutes before discovering he was dead?

When the DOJ asked her about those searches in 2021, Noel said: “I don’t remember doing that.” She claimed the FBI records were not “accurate” and said she didn’t recall looking him up.

The FBI highlighted those searches in its forensic report. It was the only search activity they called out

The Money

Ten days before Epstein died, on July 30, 2019, Tova Noel deposited $5,000 in cash into her Chase Bank account.

It was the largest of twelve cash deposits that Chase Bank flagged in a “suspicious activity report” filed with the FBI in November 2019. The deposits began in April 2018—shortly after Noel started working at the Metropolitan Correctional Center—and continued through July 2019.

According to records reviewed by the New York Post, Noel’s bank account also showed “a lot of monthly ‘Quick Zelle’ cash payments” during this period, as investigative journalist Julie K. Brown noted.

By December 2018, Noel had made seven cash deposits totaling $11,880.

She was driving a $62,000 2019 Land Rover Range Rover.

On a correctional officer’s salary.

When the Justice Department deposed her in 2021, they didn’t ask about the cash.

They didn’t ask why Chase Bank had flagged the deposits as suspicious.

They didn’t ask how a prison guard who had been on the job for just over a year could afford a luxury SUV.

They asked about her Google searches. She denied them. And the interview moved on.

What the Inmates Heard

One of the FBI’s 302 reports—interview summaries—contains an account from an inmate housed in the Special Housing Unit the morning Epstein died.

Around 6:30 AM on August 10, 2019, he heard shouting.

“Breathe! Breathe!” officers were yelling.

Then he heard someone say: “Dudes, you killed that dude.”

According to the inmate’s account, a female guard replied: “If he is dead, we’re going to cover it up and he’s going to have an alibi — my officers.”

The inmate told the FBI that the whole wing heard the exchange.

Later, after learning Epstein had died, inmates were saying “Miss Noel killed Jeffrey.”

This account has not been independently corroborated. The inmate’s credibility hasn’t been established. But it’s consistent with other evidence showing that something happened in that cell that wasn’t a simple suicide.

When Michael Thomas discovered Epstein’s body, he reportedly said: “Breathe, Epstein, breathe” and “We’re going to be in so much trouble.”

He later defended Noel to a lieutenant: “It’s not her fault, we f***ed up.”

But what did they “f*** up”? Their rounds? Their paperwork? Or something else?

The Physical Evidence That Doesn’t Add Up

Dr. Michael Baden, the forensic pathologist hired by Epstein’s estate to attend the autopsy, reviewed the physical evidence and concluded that the injuries to Epstein’s neck were more consistent with strangulation than hanging.

Specifically, Epstein had three broken bones in his neck, including his hyoid bone.

While hyoid fractures can occur in hangings, they’re more common in older individuals and are much more typical of manual strangulation. Multiple fractures—as Epstein had—are even more suggestive of homicide rather than suicide.

The New York Medical Examiner ruled the death a suicide. But the physical evidence raised enough questions that Baden went public with his concerns.

And then there’s the cell itself.

Security footage from inside the Metropolitan Correctional Center shows Epstein’s cell. The bunk is approximately four feet high. There are no obvious anchor points for someone to hang themselves effectively. The cell was designed to be suicide-resistant.

As one observer noted watching the footage: “How are you hanging yourself from that?”

The official story is that Epstein used strips of orange cloth—bedding—to fashion a noose. But inmates in the Special Housing Unit aren’t supposed to have excess bedding. They’re given minimal materials precisely to prevent suicide attempts.

Epstein somehow had enough fabric to create a noose and hang himself. Noel says she never provided linen. The previous shift says it wasn’t their responsibility. Yet there it was.

The Surveillance That Failed

Security cameras at the Metropolitan Correctional Center were positioned throughout the facility. But the cameras that should have captured Epstein’s cell? They malfunctioned.

The footage that does exist—released by the Justice Department in 2025—shows the hallway outside the Special Housing Unit, not Epstein’s actual cell or even the tier where it was located. It’s across the unit, down stairs, up stairs, through a door, and down a hall from his cell.

CBS News commissioned multiple experts to review the footage. They found “several indications” that it might not be raw footage—that it could have been edited or manipulated.

Julie K. Brown, the Miami Herald journalist whose investigation led to Epstein’s arrest, put it bluntly: “The prison camera view released by the DOJ did not show Epstein’s cell or even the stairway to his cell.”

Every camera that mattered failed. Every camera that could have shown what actually happened in that cell either malfunctioned or was pointed in the wrong direction.

And a forensic audit of the scene found that every mistake that could have been made was made. Evidence was mishandled. The scene was contaminated. Footprints were everywhere. The guards weren’t interviewed for two years.

For the highest-profile federal prisoner in decades—a convicted sex offender with connections to presidents and princes and billionaires, who had already attempted suicide once and was supposedly on suicide watch—the Bureau of Prisons created conditions where his death would be impossible to investigate properly.

Unless, of course, that was the point.

Who Was Supposed to Be His Cellmate

Jeffrey Epstein wasn’t supposed to be alone in his cell the night he died.

High-profile inmates are typically given cellmates specifically to prevent suicide. Epstein had a cellmate until August 8 or 9—just days before his death. His name was Efrain “Stone” Reyes, a former NYPD officer and convicted murderer.

According to documents in the Epstein files, Reyes told prison officials that he advised Epstein to pay inmates and guards for protection. “You’ll be safer if you pay people,” he allegedly said.

Sources told the Miami Herald that Epstein did make protection payments.

Two days after Epstein’s death, Reyes updated his will.

Reyes has since died.

On August 8, Epstein was taken off suicide watch and placed in the Special Housing Unit, where he would be in solitary confinement without a cellmate. This decision came despite the fact that he had supposedly attempted suicide less than a month earlier, on July 23.

But here’s what makes it even stranger: according to internal documents, officials considered assigning Epstein a new cellmate after Reyes left.

The person they considered?

Cesar Sayoc.

Yes, that Cesar Sayoc. The man who was arrested in 2018 for mailing pipe bombs to prominent Democrats and Trump critics, including CNN. The man who had covered his van with Trump stickers and photos of the president’s political enemies with crosshairs over their faces.

The man who federal prosecutors described as radicalized by right-wing media and whose targets were chosen based on Trump’s rhetoric.

That’s who federal officials considered placing in a cell with Jeffrey Epstein.

Sayoc was never actually assigned as Epstein’s cellmate. But the fact that his name came up at all—that someone in the Bureau of Prisons thought it was appropriate to consider housing a convicted sex offender with a man who had tried to assassinate the president’s political enemies—tells you everything about how seriously they were taking Epstein’s safety.

Or weren’t.

Two Days Before: Bill Barr’s Visit

On August 8, 2019—two days before Epstein was found dead—Attorney General William Barr visited the Metropolitan Correctional Center.

The Attorney General of the United States personally went to the jail where the country’s most notorious prisoner was being held.

Barr has never explained why. The visit was not announced publicly at the time. It only came to light later through internal records and reporting.

Why would the Attorney General visit a prisoner?

It’s worth noting that Barr’s father, Donald Barr, hired Jeffrey Epstein to teach at the Dalton School in the 1970s despite Epstein having no college degree and no teaching credentials. The Barr family’s connection to Epstein goes back decades.

It’s also worth noting that Bill Barr was working for the CIA during the time Epstein was teaching at Dalton. Both men ended up with documented ties to intelligence agencies.

And it’s worth noting that Bill Barr, as Attorney General and head of the Department of Justice, was in charge of the Bureau of Prisons when Epstein died.

If you wanted to ensure that something happened—or didn’t happen—to a federal prisoner, being the head of the organization that controls his detention would be useful.

Barr announced Epstein’s death himself, hours after it happened. He called it a suicide and promised a full investigation.

Then he vowed there would be accountability.

Tova Noel and Michael Thomas were charged with falsifying records. Those charges were dropped. Both were fired. Neither has been charged with anything related to Epstein’s actual death.

No one has been held accountable.

Who Benefited

Stephen Hoffenberg knew Jeffrey Epstein for decades. They were business partners. Hoffenberg went to prison for their crimes; Epstein didn’t. In the final days of his life, Hoffenberg was cooperating with prosecutors and naming names.

On the morning of August 10, 2019, when Hoffenberg learned that Epstein had died, he was in a hospital in New Hampshire undergoing a medical procedure.

According to Zev Shalev, who spoke with Hoffenberg that morning, Hoffenberg “freaked out.” He demanded that all needles and medical equipment be removed from his body immediately. He called his doctor in. He refused to undergo any procedures that day.

He told Shalev to stay on the line. “I want you to hear all of this.”

Shalev asked why Hoffenberg was so worried.

“They killed Jeffrey,” Hoffenberg said. “They’re going to kill me.”

Who? Shalev asked.

“Donald Trump,” Hoffenberg replied.

Hoffenberg died in 2022. The cause was never fully explained.

Mark Epstein, Jeffrey’s brother, called the FBI after his brother’s death. According to documents in the Justice Department files, Mark said he suspected Donald Trump had killed Jeffrey Epstein.

So we have two people close to Epstein—his longtime business partner and his brother—both saying they suspected Trump was involved in his death.

We have a president who was mentioned more than any other person in the Epstein files.

We have a Justice Department that withheld documents about accusations against Trump for months, only releasing them under pressure and with 37 pages still missing.

We have an Attorney General who visited Epstein two days before his death and whose father gave Epstein his first foothold into elite society.

We have guards who received mysterious cash payments, googled Epstein minutes before finding him dead, and were allegedly heard by inmates discussing a cover-up.

We have surveillance footage that doesn’t show the cell, cameras that malfunctioned, physical evidence consistent with strangulation, and a crime scene so badly mishandled that no legitimate investigation could determine what happened.

And we have a man who was about to stand trial and had the potential to implicate some of the most powerful people in the world.

Who benefited from Jeffrey Epstein’s death?

The answer is obvious.

The Conspiracy That Isn’t

For years, saying “Epstein didn’t kill himself” was treated as a conspiracy theory. It was a meme. A joke. Something unserious people believed.

But look at what we now know from the Justice Department’s own documents:

A guard receiving suspicious cash deposits in the months before Epstein’s death.

The same guard googling Epstein twice in the 40 minutes before his body was discovered.

The FBI concluding that guard was likely the orange shape caught on camera carrying linen to Epstein’s tier—the same orange fabric found around his neck.

Inmates reporting they heard guards discussing a cover-up.

Physical evidence more consistent with strangulation than hanging.

Every camera that mattered failing.

Every forensic mistake that could be made being made.

An Attorney General with family ties to Epstein visiting him two days before his death.

Documents showing Trump was mentioned more than anyone else in the files.

Documents showing the Justice Department withheld files about Trump.

The people closest to Epstein—his business partner and his brother—saying they suspected Trump killed him.

At what point does a conspiracy theory become the most logical explanation for the available evidence?

At what point does the official story—that Epstein, a man with everything to lose and powerful people to expose, somehow hung himself in a suicide-proof cell while guards slept and cameras failed—become less credible than the alternative?

The phrase “Epstein didn’t kill himself” became a meme because millions of people looked at the official story and recognized it for what it was: a lie.

Not because they’re conspiracy theorists. But because they can see what’s right in front of them.

And now the Justice Department has released documents that confirm what those people suspected all along.

What We Still Don’t Have

There are 100 hours of recorded interviews between Jeffrey Epstein and journalist Michael Wolff. Wolff conducted these interviews in the months before Epstein’s arrest. Epstein talked openly about his relationship with Donald Trump, about their business dealings, about what he knew.

Those recordings have never been released.

Wolff claims “nobody wants them.” That’s demonstrably false. Journalists, researchers, prosecutors, and the American public would all benefit from hearing what Epstein said.

But Wolff, who was revealed in the Epstein files to have been much closer to Epstein than he publicly acknowledged, has kept those recordings to himself.

We don’t have the transcript of the final phone call between Epstein and his girlfriend, Karyna Shuliak, the night before he died. They spoke for 20 to 30 minutes. What did they discuss? What was Epstein’s state of mind?

That transcript would tell us whether he seemed suicidal or whether he expected to survive and fight the charges.

We don’t have it.

We don’t have a clear explanation for why Epstein updated his will two days before his death.

We don’t have any accountability for the failures that allowed his death—murder or suicide—to occur.

And we still don’t have answers to the most basic question: What actually happened in that cell?

The Pattern Continues

Part 25 of this series documented how the accountability calendar operated despite the distraction of a war.

Guards receiving suspicious payments weren’t asked about the money.

And the Attorney General whose father gave Epstein his start, who visited him days before his death, and who controlled the prison where he died, declared the case closed.

For forty-three years, Jeffrey Epstein got away with crimes that should have put him in prison for life.

And in death, the system that protected him for so long made sure we’d never know exactly what happened in that cell.

The conspiracy theorists were right.

Epstein didn’t kill himself.

And everyone who dismissed that as crazy needs to explain why every piece of evidence the Justice Department has finally released points in exactly that direction.

ADDENDUM: March 13, 2026

Six days after the New York Post and Miami Herald revealed the evidence about Tova Noel’s suspicious activities the night Epstein died, the House Oversight Committee subpoenaed her.

The committee announced on Friday, March 13, that Noel has been called to testify in a deposition scheduled for March 26, 2026. The committee is investigating “Epstein, the circumstances of his death and potential co-conspirators.”

The timeline speaks for itself:

March 7-8: DOJ documents revealed Noel googled Epstein twice minutes before his death, made a $5,000 deposit 10 days before he died, and was likely the orange figure captured on video bringing linen to his cell.

March 13: Congress moved to depose her.

March 26: Scheduled testimony.

This is the same pattern documented in Part 25 of this series, when the House Oversight Committee subpoenaed Attorney General Pam Bondi on March 4—during Day 5 of the Iran war—after the missing Trump files were discovered. Five Republicans joined Democrats in that vote.

The accountability calendar doesn’t care about timing. It doesn’t care about distractions. It doesn’t care about wars or political calculations.

When evidence surfaces that demands answers, Congress acts.

Tova Noel will testify under oath. She’ll be asked about the Google searches she denied making. She’ll be asked about the $5,000 deposit she was never questioned about. She’ll be asked about the orange linen she allegedly brought to Epstein’s cell despite claiming she “never gave out linen—ever.”

She’ll be asked what really happened in that cell on August 10, 2019.

And for the first time since Jeffrey Epstein died, someone who was there that night will have to answer those questions under penalty of perjury.

The conspiracy theory keeps looking more like the truth.

And the truth keeps demanding accountability.
