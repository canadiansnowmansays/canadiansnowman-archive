Trump’s Propaganda Playbook in Action

“Just Tell Them and They Believe It”—Trump’s Confession Becomes Policy

November 1, 2025

Previous essays examined how the Trump administration found billions everywhere except for feeding Americans, and how they blamed everyone but themselves for the crisis they created, this essay exposes something more sinister: the deliberate deployment of a propaganda strategy Trump himself had bragged about. This wasn’t accidental misinformation. This was a technique Trump had explicitly taught his staff.

The Smoking Gun: Trump Explains His Own Con

Before we examine the October 2025 shutdown, let’s establish what Trump himself said about lying.

In August 2023, Trump’s former Press Secretary Stephanie Grisham went on CNN and revealed something extraordinary. Trump had taught her what she called his “method of lying.” His exact words to her:

“As long as you keep repeating something, it doesn’t matter what you say.”

Sit with that for a moment. The President of the United States explicitly told his Press Secretary that truth doesn’t matter—only repetition does.

But it gets worse. In 2017, TV personality Billy Bush confronted Trump about inflating his “Apprentice” ratings. Trump’s response, captured by Bush and confirmed by multiple outlets including the Washington Post and MSNBC:

“Billy, look, you just tell them and they believe it. That’s it: you just tell them and they believe. They just do.”

This wasn’t a slip of the tongue. This was Trump explaining his worldview: people are gullible, repetition works, truth is optional.

Even Trump’s ghostwriter Tony Schwartz called him out. The “truthful hyperbole” concept from The Art of the Deal? Schwartz later admitted it was “a contradiction in terms. It’s a way of saying, ‘It’s a lie, but who cares?’”

The Academic Proof: Yes, Trump Really Does This

Before you dismiss this as political hyperbole, know that academics have studied Trump’s technique. A study in Public Opinion Quarterly found a direct correlation between how many times Trump repeated falsehoods and Republican misperceptions about those topics.

The Washington Post had to create a special category—the “Bottomless Pinocchio”—just for Trump. The criteria? Any politician who repeated the same lie at least 20 times.

Trump was the only one who qualified.

With 14 different lies.

Immediately.

The Post concluded Trump had “effectively engaged in disinformation,” exploiting what psychologists call the “illusory truth effect”—our unfortunate tendency to believe lies after hearing them repeatedly.

So when October 2025 rolled around, Trump had both a proven technique and a political crisis. What happened next was as predictable as it was despicable.

The Big Lie: “Democrats Want Healthcare for Illegal Aliens”

Here’s what actually happened: Democrats refused to vote for a Republican bill unless it addressed two healthcare crises affecting millions of Americans. They wanted to extend Affordable Care Act subsidies (preventing premiums from more than doubling) and reverse Medicaid cuts that Trump had enacted.

Nothing—absolutely nothing—in the Democratic demands involved undocumented immigrants. Federal law has prohibited them from receiving these benefits since 1996. Democrats weren’t trying to change that.

But Trump and the Republicans needed a narrative. So they invented one.

The Lie Factory Fires Up

On September 24, 2025, Trump kicked off the propaganda campaign on Truth Social: “The Democrats want Illegal Aliens, many of them VIOLENT CRIMINALS, to receive FREE Healthcare.”

By September 28, VP JD Vance was on Fox News claiming “Democrats are threatening to shut down the entire government because they want to give hundreds of billions of dollars of health care benefits to illegal aliens.”

Speaker Mike Johnson posted on October 2: “Democrats are holding the American government HOSTAGE so they can give FREE health care to ILLEGAL ALIENS.”

Notice the pattern? Same words. Same capitalization. Same lie.

The White House literally produced a memo titled “FACT: Democrats Shut Down Government Over Free Healthcare for Illegals” and distributed official talking points to every Republican official.

By our count, at least 20 Republican officials repeated this lie over 100 times throughout October. The coordination was explicit—and it was exactly what Trump had told Grisham and Bush: just keep repeating it.

What Democrats Actually Wanted (Since Facts Still Matter)

The Democratic position was boringly reasonable:

  • Extend ACA subsidies affecting 24 million Americans (92% of whom already receive subsidies)

  • Restore Medicaid for lawfully present immigrants (refugees, asylees, green card holders)

The Congressional Budget Office estimated that without these extensions, 4.2 million Americans would lose insurance and premiums would increase by 114%.

The average premium would jump from $888 to $1,904 annually.

Three-quarters of affected Americans lived in states Trump won.

But why let facts interfere with a good propaganda campaign?

The Fact-Checkers’ Unanimous Verdict: Total BS

Every single fact-checking organization reached the same conclusion. Not “mostly false” or “misleading”—just straight-up FALSE.

PolitiFact: “Their proposal would not grant federally supported health care benefits to people in the U.S. illegally, because they did not have access to them in the first place.”

FactCheck.org: “The Republican claim is highly misleading.”

NBC News: “GOP misleads with claim that Democrats shut down to give health care to ‘illegal immigrants.’”

NPR’s headline: “GOP blames government shutdown on health care for undocumented people. That’s false.”

MIT economist Jonathan Gruber told NPR the Republican claim was “false,” noting undocumented immigrants “do not qualify for Medicaid. They do not qualify for tax credits.”

Georgetown University’s Center for Children and Families was even blunter, saying the GOP claims had “no discernable factual basis.”

Yet the repetition continued. Because as Trump said: it doesn’t matter what you say, as long as you keep repeating it.

The Repetition Machine: Shamelessness as Strategy

Watch how Trump’s stated strategy played out in real time:

JD Vance repeated the lie at least 5 times in 4 days. When pressed on its accuracy, he doubled down: “It’s not something that we made up. It’s not a talking point.”

(Narrator: It was exactly something they made up. It was literally a talking point.)

Mike Johnson hit the lie at least 6-7 times across multiple platforms. By late October, even he acknowledged the messaging was getting “old”—but kept using it anyway.

Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt deployed it daily—at least 10 documented instances—in briefings, Fox appearances, and official White House videos. They even put it on the White House comment line’s hold message.

Trump himself used variations 3-4 times, always with “ILLEGAL ALIENS” in all caps, because apparently lies are more believable when shouted.

The language coordination revealed the top-down strategy:

  • Always say “ILLEGAL ALIENS” (caps preferred)

  • Always say “FREE healthcare”

  • Always frame it as Democrats “shutting down” for this purpose

  • Bonus points for adding “VIOLENT CRIMINALS”

When Repetition Meets Reality

The propaganda campaign had mixed results. Polling showed more Americans blamed Republicans (38%) than Democrats (27%) for the shutdown. Among independents, the gap was even wider.

But here’s the revealing part: when pollsters asked about the actual policy—extending ACA subsidies—78% of Americans supported it. Including majorities of Republicans.

This suggests something important: Trump’s repetition strategy could confuse the narrative but couldn’t overcome the underlying reality that Americans actually wanted what Democrats were fighting for.

The Georgetown analysis noted that of 10 million people who would lose coverage, “the vast majority will be U.S. citizens.”

The entire “illegal aliens” frame was a lie wrapped in a distraction inside a propaganda campaign.

The Perfect Hypocrisy: Theory Meets Practice

Let’s connect the dots:

  1. Trump tells Grisham: “As long as you keep repeating something, it doesn’t matter what you say.”

  2. Trump tells Bush: “You just tell them and they believe it.”

  3. Trump’s ghostwriter reveals: “Truthful hyperbole” means “It’s a lie, but who cares?”

  4. October 2025: Republicans repeat a demonstrable lie about “healthcare for illegal aliens” over 100 times in one month.

This isn’t hypocrisy in the traditional sense. This is something worse—it’s hypocrisy with a user manual. Trump didn’t just lie about the shutdown; he had previously explained exactly how and why he would lie about it.

He gave away the con, then ran it anyway.

The Deeper Horror: It Almost Worked

Despite universal fact-checking calling BS, despite Democrats having the more popular position, despite the obvious coordination, the repetition strategy created enough confusion to help Republicans politically survive a shutdown that should have destroyed them.

Trump proved his own thesis: you really can just keep repeating something, and some people will believe it. Truth becomes negotiable. Reality becomes partisan. Facts become opinions.

As one analysis noted, Trump “effectively engaged in disinformation” through repetition, exploiting our psychological vulnerabilities. We’re wired to believe things we hear repeatedly—it’s an evolutionary shortcut that worked fine when information was scarce. But in an era of mass media and social platforms, it becomes a weapon.

And Trump knew it. Explained it. Then did it.

Why This Matters More Than Money or Blame

The financial hypocrisy exposed in the first essay was enraging—billions for ballrooms but not for food. The blame-shifting documented in the second essay was shameless—demanding others meet standards Trump himself rejected.

But this? This is an assault on the very concept of shared reality.

When a leader openly admits that repetition matters more than truth, then demonstrates it by coordinating a propaganda campaign built entirely on lies, we’ve moved beyond normal political dishonesty. We’re in the realm of manufactured unreality.

Trump told us exactly what he was going to do. His staff revealed his “method of lying.” His ghostwriter exposed the philosophy. Academic research documented the technique.

Then, during the October 2025 shutdown, while millions of Americans faced hunger and healthcare crisis, Trump deployed that exact strategy. At least 20 Republican officials repeated the same lie over 100 times, despite every fact-checker in America calling BS.

They did it because Trump was right about one thing: repetition works. Not on everyone, not forever, but on enough people for long enough to survive a crisis that should have ended careers.

The Lesson We Must Learn

When someone tells you who they are, believe them.

When someone explains their con, expect them to run it.

When someone says truth doesn’t matter, only repetition does, prepare for an avalanche of lies.

Trump gave us the playbook. Then he used it. The only question now is whether we’ll remember this lesson the next time the repetition machine starts up.

Because it will start up again. That’s not speculation—that’s just Trump being Trump. He told us so himself.

November 2025 Update: The Lie Evolves But the Strategy Remains

As this trilogy publishes, the repetition machine continues. Trump has now expanded the false narrative, claiming Democrats want to give healthcare money to “prisoners,” “drug dealers,” and “millions of people that were allowed to come in.”

The same strategy Trump explained to Grisham - “just keep repeating something” - now includes new villains. But the truth remains unchanged: Democrats are fighting for healthcare subsidies for 24 million Americans who will see their premiums double without congressional action.

The propaganda playbook Trump described isn’t just history. It’s happening right now, in real time, as Americans suffer through Day 34 of the shutdown.

The facts in this essay are drawn from federal court rulings, Trump’s own recorded statements, academic research, fact-checking organizations, and contemporary reporting from the October 2025 government shutdown. The pattern, unfortunately, speaks for itself.
