When Satire Becomes Weaponized

Part IV: The LEGO Intifada — How Iran Out-Trolled the Troller-in-Chief

May 3, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays • April 2026

When this series launched in January, we were watching Venezuelan invasion memes — crowdsourced, AI-generated, delightfully savage — and marvelling at the speed with which ordinary people could now manufacture satirical counter-narratives using nothing but pattern recognition and a Grok subscription. Three months later, the game has changed completely. The amateurs have been replaced by professionals. The crowdsource has been weaponized by a state. And the results are, by any reasonable measure, devastating.

Iran’s Islamic Republic — a theocratic regime that hangs dissidents, blacks out its own internet, and crushed domestic protests with estimated casualties in the hundreds of thousands just months ago — has become the most effective satirist on the internet. Let that sentence sit for a moment. The mullahs are funnier than Trump. The ayatollahs out-meme the master of memes. The regime that invented “Death to America” as a national slogan has discovered that “L-O-S-E-R” set to an AI-generated rap beat travels further.

Welcome to the age of slopaganda. And if you think it’s just about LEGOs, you haven’t been paying attention.

I. The Opening Salvo: When the White House Started It

To understand how Iran won the meme war, you have to understand that America fired first.

Within days of Operation Epic Fury’s launch on February 28, the White House social media team began posting videos that blended real strike footage with clips from Call of Duty, complete with killstreak animations — the numerical scores that flash on screen when a gamer eliminates an enemy. One video, set to Childish Gambino’s “Bonfire,” racked up more than 58 million views on X. Another used footage from Braveheart, Top Gun, Iron Man, and Gladiator spliced with actual explosions. A TikTok clip captioned “OPERATION EPIC FURY” was set to a remix of “Macarena” — prompting Antonio Romero Monge, co-writer of the original song, to express what he called “profound discomfort” that a melody associated with joy was being used to soundtrack the destruction of a country. He joked that at this rate, they’d make the song fashionable in mortuaries.

The gamification of war. Real bombs, real casualties, real service members dying — rendered as content. Kill scores superimposed on missile strikes. Superman flying alongside F-35s. SpongeBob, inexplicably, making an appearance. Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth celebrated the campaign with the enthusiasm of a Twitch streamer narrating a speedrun. White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt would later boast that the administration’s videos had generated more than two billion impressions.

Former Bush-era diplomat James Glassman saw the problem immediately: the administration was communicating how it was bombing Iran rather than why. Roger Stahl, a propaganda scholar at the University of Georgia, was blunter — the White House was producing a “sizzle reel” of weapons footage, designed to distract from the war’s mounting costs: shipping lane disruptions, rising gas prices, dead troops, dead civilians, and the absence of any coherent strategic objective.

And then Iran responded. Not with “Death to America.” Not with solemn martyrdom videos. Not with the stale, predictable messaging that propaganda researchers had been cataloguing for decades.

With LEGOs.

II. The Brick That Launched a Thousand Memes

The videos arrived in early March, and they arrived with a cultural fluency that stunned the people who study this for a living.

A small team in Tehran calling itself Explosive Media — or, as they put it in their TikTok bio, the “Iranian Lego team” — began producing AI-generated animations depicting Trump as a sweating, cowering LEGO minifigure, manipulated by a Netanyahu figure who looms behind him like a puppet master. The aesthetic was instantly recognizable — the blocky figures, the bright plastic environments, the toy-world scale — but the content was razor-sharp. Iranian soldiers and civilians were cast as resolute underdogs. American troops marched into rivers of LEGO blood. Tiny coffins draped in Stars and Stripes were carried past plastic ruins. A LEGO backpack and miniature shoes sat near rubble — an unmistakable reference to the Shajareh Tayyebeh school bombing in Minab.

The videos were set to AI-generated rap tracks — terrible, catchy, impossible to get out of your head. Lyrics referenced Trump’s TACO trades, his Diet Coke habit, Hegseth’s drinking, and the Epstein files. One recurring message: this war was launched to distract from the disclosures in the Jeffrey Epstein documents. Another: Trump had been goaded into it by Netanyahu. A third, aimed squarely at American service members: “If he sends troops to slaughter, you’re the only one to blame.”

Georgetown’s Renée DiResta, one of the leading scholars of digital influence operations, attributed the videos’ success to their “incredible cultural fluency.” They used rap. They referenced familiar American tropes. They responded to events as they happened — sometimes within hours. When Vance’s trip to Pakistan for peace talks was postponed, a video appeared the same day. When Trump threatened that Iran’s “whole civilization will die tonight,” the LEGO team turned his words into a punchline before sunrise.

The Pratfall Effect, long studied in social psychology, explains part of the mechanism: visible mistakes alter perceptions of competence depending on how the individual is already perceived. Public ridicule exploits this dynamic ruthlessly. It doesn’t need to prove that a leader is incompetent. It simply needs to make them feel ridiculous. Strength starts to look like bluster. Confidence becomes arrogance. Threats become tantrums.

Iran didn’t debate Trump. Iran diminished him. And they did it with children’s toys.

III. The Numbers That Should Terrify the Pentagon

The Institute for Strategic Dialogue published its analysis on April 23, and the numbers are staggering.

Roughly 150 Iranian diplomatic and official accounts — embassies, consulates, foreign ministry spokespeople — collectively generated approximately 900 million views and 22 million likes in the first 50 days of the war. That represents a thirtyfold increase in engagement compared with the preceding 50 days. Total shares rose from 4.3 million to 76 million. Comments jumped from 191,000 to 870,000. Posts nearly quadrupled, from 10,500 to 40,000.

Six accounts — Foreign Minister Araghchi, Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf, and the embassies in South Africa, India, Zimbabwe, and the consulate in Hyderabad — individually received more likes during the war than all monitored accounts combined in the pre-war period. The Iranian embassy in South Africa saw its followers rise from roughly 4,000 to more than 150,000 in seven weeks.

But Explosive Media was only the tip of the iceberg. ISD identified two additional pro-Iranian networks — dubbed BRICS4CLICKS and Verified4War — whose combined posts received more than one billion views in the first month alone. At peak output, BRICS4CLICKS generated more than 63 million views in a single day across roughly 900 posts. The networks circulated a mix of AI-generated war scenes, antisemitic tropes, and content designed to exploit domestic American fissures — particularly the Epstein files, which were mentioned in approximately 200 posts generating more than 17.7 million views.

And then there were the embassies themselves, posting with what Bret Schafer of ISD called a “previously uncharacteristic edge.” Iran’s embassy in Kenya referenced Trump’s links to Epstein from its official government account. The Iranian embassy in South Africa called Trump a “psychopath” and posted, after the ceasefire, a picture of the Iranian flag captioned “Say hello to the new world superpower.” The embassy in Zimbabwe, responding to Trump’s threats to open the Strait of Hormuz, posted simply: “We’ve lost the keys.” An AI-generated video of Trump singing a parody of the 1980s pop ballad “Voyage, voyage” — renamed “Blockade, blockade,” with lyrics including “I thought this was easy-peasy” and “I think soon I get impeachie” — generated 8.8 million views from a single embassy post.

This is not amateur hour. This is a coordinated state information operation disguised as entertainment. And the disguise is working.

IV. Slopaganda: The Word We Didn’t Know We Needed

The term “slopaganda” — viral, low-cost, AI-generated content designed for maximum shareability — captures something the older vocabulary of propaganda studies doesn’t quite reach. Traditional propaganda was formal, centralized, and unmistakably political. You knew it when you saw it because it looked like propaganda. Posters. Speeches. State broadcasts. Heavy symbolism. Controlled distribution.

Slopaganda operates on an entirely different logic. It is fast, cheap, disposable, and engineered for the algorithmic engines of social media. It doesn’t need to convince anyone of anything in particular. It needs to entertain its way into the feed. As one RTE analysis put it, these videos function as Trojan horses — drawing viewers in with recognizable imagery, references, and music while delivering a narrative about American overreach, dysfunction, and corruption.

The Conversation’s Adam North identified the deepest irony: the cultural conditions that produced The Daily Show and Last Week Tonight — the erosion of trust in mainstream political communication, the demand for authenticity and humour over formal rhetoric — have created a media environment in which a foreign state can distribute propaganda to millions of Americans and have it indistinguishable from domestic political satire. The Iranians didn’t invent the grammar. They learned it. And then they spoke it better than the White House.

Propaganda scholar Nancy Snow crystallized the reversal: Iran is now “using popular culture against the No. 1 pop culture country, the United States.”

Information warfare analyst Tal Hagin was less diplomatic about the implications. When asked how prepared social media users are for this new landscape, he offered a two-word assessment: “We’re cooked.”

V. The Anthropic Connection (Because of Course There Is One)

One detail from the NYT’s reporting deserves its own section, because it speaks directly to the themes of the Asimov Gap series running parallel to this one.

Charlie Curran, a 35-year-old Hollywood filmmaker distressed by the shooting down of an F-15E jet over Iran, decided to make an American counter-video in the Iranian LEGO style. He used Anthropic’s Claude to write the script and ByteDance’s Seedance 2.0 to generate the animation. It took him 30 minutes. The 72-second video has been viewed more than 800,000 times and shared across platforms by millions more.

Thirty minutes. One person. One AI chatbot. One video generator. A piece of wartime counter-propaganda with a reach that would have required an entire government communications department a decade ago.

“It’s not inherently difficult,” Curran told the Times, “which is why I think you’ll see a lot more of this.”

This is the Asimov Gap made visible. The tools exist. The guardrails do not. The same technology that lets a concerned filmmaker in Hollywood produce a patriotic response video in half an hour lets a team of ten people in Tehran produce a global propaganda campaign that racks up a billion views. The asymmetry isn’t technological — everyone has access to the same AI tools. The asymmetry is strategic. Iran understood what the tools were for before the United States did.

VI. The Canadian View: What Leacock Would Have Made of This

Here is what strikes me, watching this from the Mariposa on the Don River — or rather, from the larger Mariposa that is Canada, perpetually observing its southern neighbour with a mixture of affection, alarm, and exhausted bemusement.

The United States invented the internet. Built the platforms. Created the AI. Exported the culture. Established the norms of online discourse — from the meme formats to the algorithmic reward structures to the very concept of going viral. America is the infrastructure of the global information space.

And now a sanctioned theocracy with a blacked-out domestic internet has weaponized every single piece of that infrastructure against its creator. Using American platforms. American cultural references. American pop music. American toys. American AI tools built by American companies.

There is a Stephen Leacock sketch in this somewhere — the grand inventor undone by his own invention, the man who built the megaphone discovering that anyone can shout into it. Leacock would have appreciated the absurdity of a White House that gamified war with Call of Duty killstreaks being out-gamed by an adversary using children’s building blocks. He would have noted, with his characteristic dry precision, that the nation which pioneered “soft power” as a strategic concept has just been defeated in soft power by a country whose previous best contribution to global discourse was “Death to America.”

And he would have observed — because Leacock always observed the thing underneath the thing — that the real story isn’t who won the meme war. The real story is that the meme war is the war. Or at least, it’s the war that most people will remember. The spectacle reaches more people than the reality. People encounter war first as content, and only later — if at all — as news.

The bombed school in Minab, where more than 160 girls died. The 13 American service members killed. The thousands of Iranian civilian casualties. The shipping lanes. The oil prices. The ceasefire that may or may not hold. All of it, processed through LEGOs and rap beats and killstreak animations and SpongeBob and the Macarena.

That’s the world we live in now. The digital resistance we celebrated in January — the crowdsourced, democratic, bottom-up satirical response to overreach — has been professionalized, state-captured, and scaled to a billion views. The tools haven’t changed. The wielders have. And the line between satire and propaganda, between resistance and manipulation, between laughing at power and serving power, has collapsed entirely.

Welcome to the slopaganda era. Bring your own AI. And try, if you can, to remember that behind every LEGO coffin draped in a plastic flag, there’s a real one draped in a real one.
