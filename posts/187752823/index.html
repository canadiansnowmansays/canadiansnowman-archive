The World According to Trump

The World According to Trump

Episode 1: The Nasty/Nice Binary — How Trump Sees the World Through a Mirror

February 13, 2026

A new series by CanadianSnowmanSays

On February 11, 2026, the New York Times published a remarkable piece of data journalism. Titled “How Trump Sees the World: It’s Personal,” the article analyzed more than 300 of the president’s public statements about foreign nations and distilled them into an interactive map. The result was both illuminating and disturbing — not because any individual quote was surprising, but because the aggregate pattern was so unmistakable.

New York Times, Feb. 11, 2026. Screenshots from NYT interactive used throughout this essay for commentary and analysis.

The world, as seen through the eyes of Donald J. Trump, is not organized by alliances, shared values, strategic interests, or historical partnerships. It is organized by a single question: Are you being nice to me?

This is the first essay in a new series — “The World According to Trump” — in which I’ll use the Times’ extraordinary data, alongside my own analysis, to explore what happens when the most powerful person on Earth filters the entire planet through the lens of personal grievance. For those who have followed my work on Trump’s accommodation psychology and the TACO framework (Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate), this series represents a geographic application of patterns I’ve been documenting for months. The map, it turns out, is just the ego made visible.

The Nasty/Nice Binary

Let’s start with what the Times found, because the simplicity of it is the point.

Trump divides the world into two categories. There are countries that have been “nasty” and countries that have been “nice.” There are leaders who are “friends of mine” and leaders who “always get it wrong.” This isn’t subtext. It’s not an analytical inference drawn from policy decisions. It’s the actual vocabulary the president uses, publicly, to describe sovereign nations and their leaders.

NYT Interactive

Canada is “very nasty to deal with.” Norway — a NATO ally that has fought alongside American soldiers in every major conflict since Korea — earns “I lost a lot of respect.” France’s Emmanuel Macron “always gets it wrong.” Spain is “doing very well off our backs.”

Notice the language. It’s not strategic. It’s not even political. It’s personal. Canada isn’t pursuing policies that harm American interests — it’s being “nasty.” Norway hasn’t failed to meet defense obligations — Trump has “lost respect” for it, the way you might describe a friend who forgot your birthday. These aren’t diplomatic assessments. They’re relationship status updates.

As a Canadian watching this from Toronto, I confess to a particular interest in being labeled “very nasty.” We’ve been called many things over the years — polite, boring, cold — but “nasty” is a new one. It tells you everything about the framework. Canada’s sin isn’t being adversarial. It’s being insufficiently deferential. In Trump’s world, an ally that maintains its own negotiating position is worse than an adversary who flatters him.

The Strongman Premium

Now compare the treatment of democratic allies with the treatment of authoritarian leaders, and the pattern becomes impossible to ignore.

NYT Interactive

China’s Xi Jinping: “We get along great with China; I have a great relationship with President Xi.” North Korea’s Kim Jong Un: “Kim Jong Un and I had a very good relationship, as you remember, and still do.” Hungary’s Viktor Orbán: “I stick up for Viktor Orbán. Not a lot of people do, because in many cases, they’re jealous.” Turkey’s Erdogan: “A friend of mine... He’s a tough cookie. I actually like him a lot.” Saudi Arabia: “Great. We have a great leader and a friend of mine.”

NYT Interactive

The contrast is breathtaking in its consistency. Democratic allies who push back get contempt. Authoritarian leaders who project strength and offer personal deference get warmth. This isn’t a coincidence, and it isn’t strategic realism. It’s a psychological pattern — one that has been extensively documented in studies of narcissistic personality structures.

The strongman premium works because authoritarian leaders understand something that democratic leaders, constrained by parliaments and electorates and the rule of law, often cannot deliver: personal loyalty. Xi can offer Trump a grand state visit. Kim can write him “beautiful letters.” Orbán can publicly praise him without qualification. MBS can roll out red carpets and sword dances. Democratic leaders, answerable to their own citizens, can’t compete in the flattery marketplace — and in Trump’s world, that makes them the enemy.

The Respect Obsession

Perhaps no theme recurs more frequently in Trump’s global commentary than “respect.” He claims, repeatedly and in increasingly emphatic terms, that America is now “respected again” — always, explicitly, because of him.

NYT Interactive

After visiting the Netherlands: “Everybody was coming up, ‘Mr. President, Mr. President.’ They didn’t do that a year ago or two years ago or three years ago.”

NYT Interactive

After an Asia trip: “I just got back from Asia where I met the Leaders of many Countries... It was a Great Honor to meet them but, more particularly, to see that America is respected again — RESPECTED LIKE NEVER BEFORE!”

On China and Russia: “The only Nation that China and Russia fear and respect is the DJT REBUILT U.S.A.”

The caps lock tells you something. This isn’t statecraft. It’s affirmation-seeking. Trump measures America’s standing in the world not by alliance cohesion, trade balances, deterrence credibility, or institutional influence, but by how people treat him personally when he enters a room. “Mr. President, Mr. President” is the evidence he cites for American renewal. The metric of national greatness is applause.

This conflation of personal reception with national respect is not merely embarrassing — it’s strategically dangerous. It means that any leader who understands the game can purchase American goodwill with ceremony. And any leader who refuses to perform deference, even while maintaining substantive cooperation, risks being cast as an adversary. The incentive structure is perverse: flatter Trump, and your country gets preferential treatment; maintain your dignity, and your country gets punished.

The Endorsement Delusion

One of the more extraordinary revelations in the Times data is Trump’s repeated claim that his personal endorsements have swung foreign elections.

NYT Interactive

On Poland: “The man who won the election, who’s fantastic, was not expected to even come close. He was one of many people running, and through a friend of his and a friend of mine, a mutual friend, I endorsed him and he won the election.”

On Latin America: “I endorsed, as you know, the winning president, the man who won in Honduras. I endorsed the man who won in Chile. I endorsed the man who won in Argentina.”

This is delusional in the clinical sense — not as an insult, but as a description. The idea that Donald Trump’s endorsement is the decisive factor in Polish, Honduran, Chilean, or Argentine elections requires a narcissism so complete that it distorts basic reality. These countries have their own political dynamics, their own media ecosystems, their own voter motivations. Trump’s endorsement, to the extent it was even noticed by most voters in these countries, was marginal at best. But in his telling, he is the kingmaker of global democracy — the man whose blessing determines who governs nations he could not find on a map.

The Nobel Grievance

Perhaps nothing crystallizes the self-referential nature of Trump’s worldview more perfectly than the Nobel Peace Prize thread that runs through his commentary on global conflicts.

He claims to have personally “settled” or ended at least eight conflicts — between Armenia and Azerbaijan, Rwanda and the Congo, Thailand and Cambodia, Serbia and Kosovo, India and Pakistan, Egypt and Ethiopia, plus the Abraham Accords and the Russia-Ukraine situation.

NYT Interactive

But here’s the revealing part: he doesn’t discuss these conflicts in terms of outcomes, humanitarian impact, or strategic significance. He discusses them exclusively in terms of the recognition he believes he’s been denied.

NYT Interactive

“I won’t get a Nobel Peace Prize for stopping the War between Serbia and Kosovo.”

“I won’t get a Nobel Peace Prize for stopping the War between India and Pakistan.”

“And I won’t get a Nobel Peace Prize for doing the Abraham Accords in the Middle East.”

“I won’t get a Nobel Peace Prize for keeping Peace between Egypt and Ethiopia.”

“No, I won’t get a Nobel Peace Prize no matter what I do, including Russia/Ukraine, and Israel/Iran, whatever those outcomes may be, but the people know, and that’s all that matters to me!”

The repetition is compulsive. The grievance is the point. He’s not claiming credit for peace because peace matters to him — he’s claiming credit because the lack of recognition for his role bothers him. The conflicts themselves are props in a narrative about personal injustice. Children are dying in Sudan, cities are being leveled in Ukraine, but the real tragedy, the thing that animates Donald Trump’s commentary, is that nobody gave him a prize.

And notice the tell at the end: “whatever those outcomes may be.” He doesn’t care how Russia/Ukraine or Israel/Iran resolve. He cares whether people acknowledge his involvement. The outcomes are irrelevant. The credit is everything.

Immigration as Racial Geography

The Times’ analysis reveals another dimension of Trump’s global mental map, and it’s the one most Americans prefer not to discuss directly. I have the advantage — or perhaps the obligation — of the outsider’s perspective.

NYT Interactive

Trump describes immigration-source countries in starkly racialized terms. Afghanistan, Haiti, Somalia, and the Congo are “hellholes.” He announced “a permanent pause on Third World migration” from these countries. The Congo, he claims, opens “their jails and they walk into our country.”

Meanwhile, his preferred immigrants come from a very specific geography.

NYT Interactive

“Why can’t we have some people from Norway, Sweden — just a few — let us have a few from Denmark. Do you mind sending us a few people? Send us some nice people, do you mind?”

The word “nice” here is doing extraordinary work. The countries producing “nice” people are the whitest countries in Europe. The countries producing “hellhole” people are overwhelmingly Black and Brown nations. This is not a coincidence, and pretending otherwise is an act of willful blindness.

NYT Interactive

His view of European nations further confirms the pattern. He evaluates them almost entirely through the immigration lens. Sweden was “crime-free” before immigration. Germany’s Angela Merkel made “two big mistakes: immigration and energy.” France is “in the midst of a major crime problem because of their absolutely horrendous handling of immigration.” Poland “doesn’t take people in” — and this is praised. Hungary’s Orbán “has not made a mistake on immigration” — and this earns admiration.

The consistent message: countries that keep out non-white immigrants are wise. Countries that accept them are failing. Countries that produce them are hellholes. This is not a border security policy. It is a racial worldview expressed through geographic categories.

The Counterfactual Presidency

The Times notes, almost as an aside, one of the most telling features of Trump’s global commentary: his insistence that catastrophic events would not have occurred had he been in power.

The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine? Wouldn’t have happened. The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel? Wouldn’t have happened.

These are, as the Times delicately puts it, “impossible-to-prove counterfactuals.” But their rhetorical function is clear: they establish Trump as the sole guarantor of global order. Without him, the world descends into chaos. With him, all catastrophes are averted. This is the strongman’s essential claim — not that he will manage problems well, but that his very presence prevents them from occurring. It is governance as divine aura, not competence.

What the Map Really Shows

Step back from the individual quotes and look at the aggregate picture the Times has assembled, and what you see is not a foreign policy. You don’t see a doctrine. You don’t see strategic calculation, historical awareness, or institutional understanding.

What you see is a single psychological pattern applied uniformly to 7.8 billion people across 195 countries: How does this make me feel about me?

Allies who challenge him are enemies. Dictators who flatter him are friends. Countries that show him “respect” — meaning personal deference — are proof of American greatness. Countries whose immigrants he finds undesirable are hellholes. Conflicts he can claim credit for are achievements; the same conflicts, when they deny him recognition, are proof of persecution. Foreign elections where his preferred candidate won are evidence of his global influence. And every disaster that occurred while he was out of office is proof that the world needs him specifically — not American leadership, not democratic values, not alliance structures, but him.

The final line of the Times analysis says it all, with the quiet understatement that is the paper of record’s signature move:

“It will always, somehow, be about him.”

That sentence could serve as the epigraph for this entire series. Because what we’ll explore in the essays ahead — when we turn this same lens on Trump’s view of American cities and states — is that the domestic map looks exactly the same as the global one. The same binary. The same grievance. The same mirror.

The world according to Trump isn’t a worldview at all. It’s a self-view, projected onto a globe.
