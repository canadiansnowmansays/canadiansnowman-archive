The Iran Crisis

The Theory Meets the Street

Part XIX: The First Day

February 28, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | February 28, 2026

Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

Donald Trump’s theory of this war can be stated simply: bomb enough, destroy enough, frighten enough — and a population that already despises its government will finish the job.

By mid-afternoon on the first day, the streets of Tehran had answered him.

Iranians came out in force — not to overthrow their government, but to defend their country. Photographs verified by the New York Times show crowds waving Iranian flags, hands pressed to chests, marching through the capital. In the background of one image, two signs are visible. One reads: “Welcome to Israel.” The other: “51st State of United States of America.”

Those signs were already there. They were not made for today. They were made by a government that has spent forty years telling its people that this moment was coming. And on the first day of the war that government was proved right.

Trump told Iranians their hour of freedom was at hand. The first mass street mobilization of the war was nationalist. Not revolutionary.

That is not the end of the story. It may not even be representative of the broader Iranian population, most of whom are sheltering, fleeing, or mourning. But it is the first visible answer to the question Trump asked from Mar-a-Lago at 2:30 in the morning. And it is not the answer he expected.

The School

Minab, Hormozgan Province, Iran — February 28, 2026. Residents gather outside the ruins of Shajareh Tayyebeh girls' elementary school following a strike that killed at least 85 people, most of them children. Approximately 170 students were in class when the building was hit during the first shift of the school day. Iran's state broadcaster IRIB confirmed 85 dead and 93 injured by Saturday evening. The school is adjacent to an IRGC naval base. CENTCOM said it was "looking into" reports of civilian harm. Credit: IRIB/AFP.

Before anything else, the school.

Shajareh Tayyebeh girls’ elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan Province. Saturday morning, first shift. Approximately 170 children in class. The first day of the workweek in Iran. Parents had already dropped their children off and gone to work.

A strike hit the school. By Saturday evening, Iran’s state broadcaster IRIB had confirmed 85 dead and 93 injured. Iran’s Red Crescent and multiple state news outlets confirmed the toll. The Times verified videos showing damage to the building.

In one video, black smoke billows from a crumbling structure whose walls are painted with pastel-colored flowers. Onlookers scream, wail, and embrace. In another, rescuers dig through rubble with cranes and shovels. Piles of bloodied, dusty backpacks.

A man holds up fistfuls of school papers and notebooks, raises his voice over the sound of drilling: “Under this rubble, students are buried. The blood of our loved ones, our students, which you can see on their schoolbooks.”

Hossein Kermanpour, Iran’s health ministry spokesman, wrote on social media: “God knows how many more children will be pulled out of the rubble.”

The Times also verified a strike on Hedayat High School in Tehran, near 72nd Square in the Narmak district. Two students died there. That strike appears to have been aimed at the nearby residence of former President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad — the same Ahmadinejad who Jaber Rajabi told The Atlantic just days ago was “the one figure with enough domestic support to lead Iran.” Three of his bodyguards were killed. He was reported unharmed.

The military’s position on the Minab school is documented. CENTCOM issued a statement: “We are aware of reports concerning civilian harm resulting from ongoing military operations. We take these reports seriously and are looking into them. The protection of civilians is of utmost importance.”

Videos verified by the Times show that the school is adjacent to an IRGC naval base. A separate verified video shows a strike hitting the same base.

That is the military’s justification. The reader can assess its moral weight alongside the image of the backpacks.

The Theory and Its First Refutation

This series has documented, across eighteen parts, the assumption at the center of this war: that the Iranian population’s hatred of its government would translate into revolution once the regime was sufficiently degraded.

Trump said it directly: “When we are finished, take over your government. This will be probably your only chance for generations.”

Reza Pahlavi echoed it from exile: “The time to return to the streets is approaching.”

The UN Special Rapporteur on human rights in Iran, Japanese legal scholar Mai Sato, offered a different frame: military intervention “does nothing to address why people took to the streets. It is for the Iranian people to shape their own future.”

She was speaking before the protest photographs arrived. They arrived anyway, and they made her point.

There is a documented distinction that this war’s architects appear to have missed. Iranians who despise their government — and millions do, as 7,000 deaths in January’s crackdown attest — do not necessarily experience foreign bombs as liberation. They may experience them as invasion. The two things are not the same, and conflating them is not a strategic assessment. It is a wish.

Danny Citrinowicz, the former head of the Iran branch of Israeli military intelligence quoted in Part XVI of this series, said on Saturday: “If they know it’s a fight for their life, then they have nothing to lose by continuing.”

He said this about the Iranian government. It applies equally to the Iranian people watching their schools burn.

The Strait

Cargo ships and oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz earlier this week.Credit...Giuseppe Cacace/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

The Strait of Hormuz is now effectively closed to commercial shipping — not by military blockade, but by voluntary curtailment.

Iran’s Revolutionary Guards warned all vessels that passage was “currently unsafe.” The US Maritime Administration issued a formal advisory for all American commercial ships to avoid the Persian Gulf and the strait entirely. Shipping companies stopped sending tankers without being ordered to. “Nobody’s going to enter right now,” said Angeliki Frangou, CEO of Navios Maritime Partners. “It’s just simply unfeasible for commercial ships to transit in a time of war.”

A US official said Saturday there was no evidence Iran was attempting a formal military blockade. Ships were still moving through the strait, according to S&P Global Energy’s tracking data — but at considerably reduced volume.

The distinction between a blockade and a voluntary shutdown matters less than it appears. The economic consequence is the same: oil stops moving. Global oil prices closed Friday at $73 a barrel, already up 20 percent this year. Analysts expect a significant jump when markets open Sunday evening.

The stakes are asymmetric. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have pipeline alternatives for some of their exports. Iraq and Qatar do not. For those countries, the Strait is the only route to market. Any sustained reduction in tanker traffic cuts their revenue, destabilizes their economies, and increases pressure on the Gulf coalition Iran inadvertently assembled this morning.

Elliott Abrams, who served as Trump’s special representative for Iran in the first term, framed the central question precisely: “What are the Iranians hitting today, and what will they hit tomorrow?” Iran has three escalation options for the Strait: mines, missile strikes on tankers, or attack boats. It has used all three in previous confrontations. Whether a government fighting for its survival will exercise restraint on the world’s most critical oil chokepoint is not a question any analyst is willing to answer confidently.

No country has successfully closed the Strait since large-scale oil production began in the Middle East. In past conflicts, tankers have typically resumed passage after pauses of hours or days. Peter Sand, chief analyst at Xeneta, said the strikes on Saturday “shatter hopes of a large-scale return of container shipping to the Red Sea in 2026.” The Red Sea was already disrupted by the Houthis. Both of the world’s critical Middle Eastern shipping lanes are now compromised simultaneously.

Daniel Yergin, the Pulitzer Prize-winning energy historian: “This will be a shock to the oil market.”

Iran’s Navy Is Burning

Satellite image ©2026 Vantor, via Associated Press

Trump said in his 2:30 AM video that the United States would “annihilate their navy.”

By mid-afternoon, a satellite image captured an Iranian military ship engulfed in flames at the Chabahar naval base in southern Iran. A large black plume of smoke. Debris scattered across the adjacent pier. Several base buildings damaged.

Chabahar sits on the Gulf of Oman, at the eastern approach to the Strait of Hormuz. It is not a peripheral facility. It is one of Iran’s primary naval installations on the waterway that the IRGC has now declared unsafe for commercial traffic.

Trump’s promise is being kept. The question Abrams asked — what will Iran hit tomorrow — is inseparable from this image. A navy that is burning has less to lose by using what remains.

500 Targets

Sources: Iranian state news agency, verified satellite images and video. Lazaro Gamio, Samuel Granados, Pablo Robles, Daniel Wood/The New York Times

Israel’s military confirmed it had struck approximately 500 targets across Iran since the opening of the campaign — air defense systems, missile launchers, ballistic missile sites, anti-aircraft batteries. A new wave of strikes on aerial defense and missile launch systems in central Iran began in the early afternoon.

This is no longer a decapitation strike. It is a sustained air campaign of a scale and ambition that has no modern precedent outside of full-scale declared wars.

The Israeli military said the strikes had allowed the Israeli Air Force to “expand its aerial superiority” in Iranian skies. That phrase deserves attention: aerial superiority in Iranian skies means Israeli and American jets can now fly over Iran with reduced risk. What comes next in a campaign with aerial superiority and a stated goal of regime change is not difficult to project.

Congress

The constitutional reckoning is now formally underway.

Senator Chuck Schumer called for the Senate to “quickly return to session and reassert its constitutional duty by passing our resolution to enforce the War Powers Act.” Senator Tim Kaine called the strikes a “colossal mistake” and said “every single senator needs to go on the record about this dangerous, unnecessary and idiotic action.” Senator Mark Warner stated: “The Constitution is clear. The decision to take this nation to war rests with Congress.”

Senator Rand Paul, co-sponsor of the bipartisan war powers resolution: “The Constitution conferred the power to declare or initiate war to Congress for a reason — to make war less likely.”

Representative Thomas Massie, Republican: “Acts of war unauthorized by Congress.”

Representative Ro Khanna called for Congress to convene Monday to vote on the war powers measure: “Trump has launched an illegal regime-change war in Iran with American lives at risk.”

Republicans largely praised the strikes while circumventing the authorization question entirely.

The New York Times published a careful legal analysis by Charlie Savage documenting the full constitutional history. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 requires unauthorized deployments to end after 60 days unless Congress votes to approve them. Every president since has exceeded the resolution’s purported limits. A 1983 Supreme Court ruling now requires override measures to pass both chambers with two-thirds majorities to survive a presidential veto. The mechanism that was supposed to restrain presidents has been systematically weakened by the same accumulation of unchallenged precedents it was designed to prevent.

Trump has pushed further at every step: the Suleimani assassination, the June Iran strikes, the Venezuela invasion. Each went unchallenged. Each became precedent.

This is the war that precedent built.

The Dissent Nobody Heard

The Atlantic has now published what happened inside the administration in the hours and days before the bombs fell. It belongs in the record.

Vice President Vance, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs General Dan Caine, and senior Pentagon official Elbridge Colby all expressed reservations about the operation. Caine in particular warned privately that air strikes alone would not be enough to undermine the regime and that any move against Iran would be extraordinarily complicated.

Chief of Staff Susie Wiles did not take a personal position on the wisdom of the strikes. But she organized a conversation with the president about the importance of following through on his campaign promises — specifically his promise to avoid new foreign wars. Republican midterm strategists gathering in Florida this weekend have grown anxious that Trump’s military adventures are turning off voters who want their leaders focused on the economy.

None of it stopped him.

The Omani foreign minister Badr Albusaidi visited Vice President Vance on Friday — the day before the strikes — in a last-ditch effort to buy more time for negotiations. This series documented Albusaidi’s role extensively: he was inside the room, mediating, and had said a deal was within reach. On Friday he told CBS News’s Face the Nation that the two sides had agreed to “a very important breakthrough that has never been achieved any time before” — that if the ultimate objective was ensuring Iran could never have a nuclear bomb, “I think we have cracked that problem through these negotiations.”

Hours after that interview, the first bombs fell.

Trump’s lead negotiator Steve Witkoff believed further talks were futile because Iran would not agree to the administration’s demands — which included destruction of Iran’s primary nuclear sites, delivery of all enriched uranium to the United States, and no sunset clauses. But Albusaidi was not describing Iranian intransigence on Friday afternoon. He was describing a breakthrough.

The Atlantic also documents the psychology behind the decision. A Trump adviser told their reporters that the president’s success in Venezuela last month, combined with the June Iran strikes, may have convinced Trump that the US military is “an almost biblical force that can accomplish anything.” That phrase — an almost biblical force — is the clearest window into the decision-making that produced today.

The Worst-Case Scenario

Military planners who rehearsed this operation for years concluded explicitly that strikes alone would not produce regime change. They projected weeks of sustained attacks. They anticipated Iran would move to close the Strait of Hormuz within 12 hours of the opening strikes — which it did. They war-gamed the escalatory cycle: every Iranian missile launch forces Washington to expend costly and limited air-defense interceptors, creating pressure to strike launchers, storage sites, and transportation routes in return.

Dana Stroul, the former deputy assistant secretary of defense for the Middle East, described the worst-case outcome in one sentence: “Complete chaos.” A failed state with enriched uranium. Destabilized oil routes. A refugee crisis. Disrupted global economy. Regional spillover.

The more hopeful scenario Stroul outlined: opposition leaders emerge from prison to unify the country, assuming sustained strikes dismantle the regime’s religious and security apparatus. That scenario requires weeks of bombing. It runs directly counter to the Trump administration’s stated pivot toward China and the Western Hemisphere.

The record on regime-change initiatives is sparse. No ground troops are expected. Trump wants to avoid a quagmire. But even with the supreme leader dead — and with 150 aircraft and drones, more than a dozen destroyers, and two aircraft carriers at Trump’s disposal — the planners concluded it may not be enough.

One former defense official framed the objective as he sees it: “The next time we sit down with Iran, it must not be as equals. It must be as victor and vanquished.”

MTG

Among the voices condemning the strike on the Minab school was one that will be quoted widely:

Former Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene, who broke with Trump and the MAGA movement last year, wrote on social media: “I did not campaign for this. I did not donate money for this. I did not vote for this, in elections or Congress. This is heartbreaking and tragic. And how many more innocent will die? What about our own military? This is not what we thought MAGA was supposed to be.”

Whatever one thinks of Marjorie Taylor Greene — and this series has documented her record extensively — that statement carries a specific weight that statements from Democrats do not. She is not arguing about war powers or constitutional process. She is saying: this is not what we were promised. The children in the rubble are not what MAGA was supposed to be.

That fracture, however early and however limited, is worth recording.

The Broader Picture

Iran fired missiles at a hotel on Palm Jumeirah in Dubai. Four people were injured. A drone struck Kuwait’s main airport. Residential buildings in Bahrain’s capital Manama were targeted — videos verified by the Times show a moving object striking an apartment building. Hundreds of Shiite Muslims marched in Pakistan in solidarity with Iran. The Houthis in Yemen declared solidarity and promised preparation “for all scenarios.”

The regional war that analysts warned about is not hypothetical. It is the current state of affairs.

Airlines have cancelled more than 37 percent of flights to Israel and roughly 7 percent of all flights to the Middle East. Dubai’s airports remain closed. The Strait of Hormuz is closed. The UN Security Council convened an emergency meeting. The UN Special Rapporteur called the strikes unlawful.

Trump monitored the assault overnight at Mar-a-Lago alongside members of his national security team, according to the White House press secretary. He also spoke by phone with Netanyahu.

Khamenei Is Dead

As this essay was being finalized, the question mark became a confirmation.

Reuters, CNN, and Axios — three independent Tier 1 sources — confirmed within minutes of each other that Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in the Israeli and US strikes on his compound in Tehran. Reuters cited a senior Israeli official. CNN cited two Israeli sources, one of whom said Israel had obtained a photograph of the body. Axios reported that Israel’s ambassador to Washington personally informed US officials. An official announcement was being prepared as of late afternoon.

Khamenei has not been seen in public or in video since the strikes began. His body was recovered from his destroyed compound.

Iran’s foreign ministry spokesman had told ABC News hours earlier that Khamenei was “safe and sound.” Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi told NBC that “as far as I know” Khamenei was alive, adding that losing “one or two commanders” was “not a big problem.” Those statements are now on the record as either deliberate disinformation or the genuine belief of officials who did not yet know their supreme leader was dead.

Also confirmed killed: Iran’s Defense Minister Amir Nasirzadeh, IRGC commander Mohammed Pakpour — who had taken over after Israel killed his predecessor in June 2025 — and four senior IRGC intelligence commanders including the head of the foreign intelligence unit, the head of the security unit, the head of the counterterrorism unit, and an adviser on the war with Israel. Khamenei’s son-in-law and daughter-in-law were also reported killed in the strikes.

Mojtaba Khamenei — the supreme leader’s son, flagged in Part XVI of this series as more dangerous and more apocalyptically obsessed than his father — was targeted. Israeli officials say he survived.

The succession mechanism is now activated. The Assembly of Experts, an 88-member clerical body vetted by a hardline watchdog aligned with Khamenei, formally selects the new supreme leader. Khamenei had delegated crisis authority to longtime loyalist Ali Larijani, who posted on X within hours of the reports: “We will make the Zionist criminals and the dishonorable Americans regret this.” The succession plan Khamenei put in place anticipating exactly this scenario is now in operation.

Secretary of State Rubio told lawmakers in January: “No one knows” who would take over if the regime falls. The US intelligence community assessed that the IRGC would likely fill the leadership void in the short term — but acknowledged the assessment was not definitive, particularly following the US assassination of Iran’s most powerful military commander in Trump’s first term.

Satellite imagery showed a black plume of smoke and extensive damage at the compound of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Iran’s supreme leader. The image, taken by Airbus on Saturday morning, showed collapsed buildings at the complex, which typically serves as Mr. Khamenei’s residence and main premises for hosting senior officials.

In Tehran, witnesses reported loud cheering, people at their windows, and celebratory music breaking out after news of Khamenei’s death spread. Elsewhere in the capital, the streets remained deserted and silent.

Both things are true simultaneously. That is Iran on the first day of the war.

This is the governance question Part XVI asked. The governance crisis Part XVI warned about. The succession vacuum Part XVI documented as the question nobody in Washington was publicly asking.

It is no longer a question. It is the present tense of this war.

Who Khamenei Was

Karim Sadjadpour, one of the foremost Western analysts of Iran, published his assessment of Khamenei’s life and death in The Atlantic this afternoon. Two passages belong in this record.

On the internal sociology of the regime: an Iranian academic told Sadjadpour that at the revolution’s beginning, the regime consisted of 80 percent true believers and 20 percent opportunists. By Khamenei’s final years, the ratio had inverted — 20 percent believers, 80 percent opportunists who had gathered around officials for wealth and privilege.

That is the regime that just lost its supreme leader. That is what the IRGC is now holding together. Not ideology. Opportunism.

On the man himself, Sadjadpour wrote the line that will define Khamenei’s epitaph: “He lived by ‘Death to America’ and ‘Death to Israel.’ He died by death from America and Israel.”

He was 86 years old. He led Iran for 35 years — longer than any other leader in the region. He inherited a revolution, preserved it through force of will and brutality, and in the end could not preserve it against the two nations he spent his entire tenure trying to destroy.

The governance crisis that follows is Part XX. But the man who created it, and the world he imposed on 93 million people for nearly four decades, deserves a moment in the record before the next chapter begins.

The Congressional Notification — A Correction to the Record

Earlier reporting in this series documented that congressional leaders were not notified before the strikes. The full picture is more nuanced and requires a correction.

The Gang of Eight — the top congressional leaders and the chairs and ranking members of the House and Senate Intelligence Committees — were notified on Friday evening, before the strikes began. Secretary of State Rubio contacted Speaker Johnson, Majority Leader Thune, and the top two Democrats, Jeffries and Schumer, in advance. The Pentagon notified Armed Services Committee members shortly after strikes began, though Senator Jack Reed said he personally was not contacted — his staff were.

Senator Mark Warner, top Democrat on the Intelligence Committee: “While I disagree with the action, I think there was better consultation this time.”

The constitutional argument, however, remains intact — and the Times reporting supplies the precise language that sustains it. Multiple members described the exchanges as “one-way discussions rather than consultations about an operation that had been in preparation for months.” Notification is not authorization. Being told what is about to happen is not the same as being asked whether it should. The Constitution’s war powers clause does not say Congress must be informed. It says Congress must declare.

The pattern is also documented: lawmakers were not notified before Venezuela. Before the June Iran strikes, Democrats received what they described as perfunctory notices with scant operational detail, too late for meaningful oversight. This time was, by Senator Warner’s own assessment, better. Better is not constitutional.

No US Casualties — Twelve Hours In

CENTCOM confirmed: no US casualties or combat-related deaths in the twelve hours since the strikes began. Hundreds of Iranian missiles and drones were successfully defended against. Damage to American bases described as “minimal and has not impacted operations.”

The US military used a one-way attack drone for the first time in this operation.

This is the military reality at the twelve-hour mark. It is also the reality Trump’s theory of the war depends on — a campaign that inflicts maximum damage on Iran while keeping American casualties at zero, long enough for the Iranian population to finish the job. Whether that calculus holds through day two, day ten, or day sixty is the question every military analyst quoted in this series has refused to answer with confidence.

What the First Day Revealed

Part XVI asked who governs Iran after. Part XVII documented how the war began. Part XVIII documented what the first hours revealed about the military balance and the coalition Iran built against itself.

Part XIX must record what the first day revealed about the theory of the war itself.

The theory was: bomb the regime, liberate the people.

The first day produced: 85 children dead in a school in Minab. Iranians in the streets with their flags and their hands on their hearts. A Strait closed to one-fifth of the world’s oil. Five hundred Israeli strikes and counting. A constitutional crisis in Washington. A fracture in MAGA. A region on fire.

The Iranian foreign minister called Trump’s regime change demand “Mission Impossible.”

The RAND analyst Shira Efron said the Americans and Israelis seemed to assume the Iranian government lacked resilience, that decapitating its leadership would topple it, and that opposition forces would rise up. “We would all be very happy if the ayatollahs would go away,” she said, “but those assumptions may not be robust.”

They are not robust. The first day has said so.

What comes on the second day is the question this series will continue to document.

“Peace Throughout The World”

As the bombs continued falling on Saturday evening, President Trump posted his confirmation on Truth Social.

“Khamenei, one of the most evil people in History, is dead.” He described the killing as justice for Americans, Iranians, and people across the world. He noted that the bombing “will continue, uninterrupted throughout the week or, as long as necessary.” He closed by declaring the objective: “PEACE THROUGHOUT THE MIDDLE EAST AND, INDEED, THE WORLD!”

He signed it: “Thank you for your attention to this matter. PRESIDENT DONALD J. TRUMP.”

That closing line deserves the same attention as everything else in this record. A president announces the killing of a foreign head of state — the leader of a nation of 93 million people, under sustained bombardment, with an unknown succession crisis now unfolding — and closes with the formal register of a man who believes the matter is settled.

This series noted weeks ago that Trump’s Nobel Prize fixation was never incidental. The Puppet Regime episode published the day before the bombs fell made the same observation in felt and foam. “PEACE THROUGHOUT THE MIDDLE EAST AND, INDEED, THE WORLD” is not a military assessment. It is a prize acceptance speech being written in real time, on the same day the war began, before the succession crisis has resolved, before the Strait has reopened, before a single Iranian protest has produced a governing coalition, before the IRGC has stood down, before anyone knows who leads Iran tomorrow morning.

Trump went to Venezuela and received a medal. He launched a war and declared world peace by evening.

The Nobel committee has not yet been contacted. Give it a week.
