Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Introduction: The Article That Made Me Ask “How Long?”

February 4, 2026

Opening: The Photograph That Stopped Me

I’ve been writing about American politics too long now, publishing 500+ essays across 50+ different series here on Substack. I’ve documented Trump’s accountability evasion, dissected Charlie Kirk’s debates, analyzed autonomous vehicle economics, and tracked fascist patterns in modern governance. I thought I’d developed a thick skin for bad news.

Then I opened The New York Times on February 4, 2026, and saw a photograph that stopped me cold.

An elderly woman sits in a blue plastic chair in the village of Badgor, her weathered face etched with exhaustion and worry. In her arms, wrapped in a vibrant patterned blanket, is a tiny child. They’re waiting to be seen by a doctor - one of the last mobile medical teams still operating in Afghanistan’s earthquake-devastated regions. Around them, other children hover, witnessing suffering that has become routine.

The woman’s expression haunts me. It’s not just grief or fear - it’s the weariness of someone who has endured too much, for too long, with no end in sight.

This is Badgor, in Kunar Province, months after a summer earthquake killed over 2,200 people. The families whose homes collapsed still live in tents battered by freezing winds. The International Rescue Committee team visiting that day was the last mobile health unit the organization kept operating after Trump’s aid cuts forced them to disband 33 others.

Four million Afghan children are now at risk of dying from malnutrition. The most in a quarter-century.

The Question I Couldn’t Stop Asking

As I read through the article - past Malika Ghullami losing her twins after the local midwife left, past the earthquake survivors still living in battered tents, past the “Welcome to your sweet country” sign greeting 2.8 million expelled refugees - I found myself asking a question that wouldn’t let go:

How long has Afghanistan been suffering like this?

Not just since Trump cut the aid. Not just since the American withdrawal in 2021. Not even since the Soviet invasion in 1979.

How far back does this go?

I knew about the Soviet occupation - that killed over a million Afghans. I remembered the Taliban’s first regime, the destruction of the Bamiyan Buddhas, the harboring of Al-Qaeda. I was aware of the “Great Game” between the British and Russian empires in the 19th century.

But something about this New York Times article - the casual cruelty of cutting aid to the world’s most vulnerable children, the specific details of mothers losing babies for want of a nearby clinic, the systematic dismantling of healthcare infrastructure - made me want to dig deeper.

What if Afghanistan’s tragedy wasn’t measured in decades, but in centuries?

What if those starving children aren’t just victims of Trump’s budget cuts, but the latest casualties in a pattern that stretches back further than anyone alive can remember?

What I Found Stunned Me

I started researching. At first, I thought I’d write a single essay contextualizing the current humanitarian crisis within Afghanistan’s modern history - the Soviet invasion, the civil war, the American occupation.

But the more I dug, the further back I had to go.

The American invasion in 2001? That came after decades of Soviet devastation. The Soviet invasion? That came after centuries of British imperial meddling. The British “Great Game”? That came after the Mughal and Persian empires fighting over Afghan territory. The Islamic conquest? That came after the Mongol apocalypse. The Mongols? They came after waves of Turkish dynasties. The Turks? After the Arab conquest. The Arabs? After the Sassanid Persians. The Persians? After Alexander the Great.

And Alexander?

Alexander the Great - perhaps history’s most successful military conqueror, the man who subdued the known world from Greece to India in just thirteen years - spent three years bogged down in Afghanistan (then Bactria and Arachosia), fighting guerrilla warfare in the mountains, losing men to tribal resistance, resorting to mass marriages and garrison cities because he couldn’t pacify the region by force alone.

That was in 329-327 BCE.

Almost 2,400 years ago.

The Pattern Nobody Talks About

Here’s what stunned me: Afghanistan has been invaded, occupied, fought over, or used as a battleground for empires almost continuously for at least 2,500 years.

Let me repeat that: Two thousand, five hundred years.

The brief periods of genuine Afghan independence and peace - when Afghans controlled their own destiny without foreign armies on their soil or foreign powers dictating their foreign policy - add up to perhaps 100-150 years total out of 2,500 years of recorded history.

That’s less than 6 percent.

For 94 percent of recorded history, Afghanistan has been somebody’s battlefield, somebody’s conquest, somebody’s “buffer state,” somebody’s problem to solve with military force.

The Achaemenid Persians, Alexander’s Macedonians, the Seleucids, the Mauryans, the Greco-Bactrians, the Parthians, the Kushans, the Sassanids, the Arabs, the Ghaznavids, the Seljuks, the Ghorids, the Mongols (twice - Genghis Khan and then Timur), the Mughals, the Safavids, the British (three times!), the Soviets, and finally the Americans.

Every single one came with confidence. Every single one underestimated Afghan resistance. Every single one eventually left. And every single one left Afghanistan worse than they found it.

A boy begging at a market in central Kabul.

Why This Matters Now

You might be wondering: Why does ancient history matter when children are starving today?

Because understanding the depth of Afghanistan’s suffering changes how one sees the present crisis.

When Trump cut nearly $1 billion in annual U.S. aid to Afghanistan - aid that was keeping 4 million children from starvation - it wasn’t just a cruel budget decision. It was the latest chapter in the longest-running tragedy in human civilization.

When those 450 health clinics closed, when Malika Ghullami’s twins died because she couldn’t reach a distant clinic for prenatal care, when little Muhammad Ali lay in a Kabul hospital too weak to ingest milk - these aren’t isolated incidents of suffering.

They’re the continuation of a pattern that has operated for millennia.

Every empire that has touched Afghanistan has:

  1. Invaded with certainty of success

  2. Faced unexpected resistance

  3. Caused massive civilian suffering

  4. Eventually withdrawn

  5. Left Afghans to deal with the aftermath

What makes America’s withdrawal uniquely cruel is that the United States didn’t just leave. It created dependency through 20 years of aid, then cut it off. Previous empires destroyed Afghanistan through war. America is destroying it through abandonment.

Each essay will stand alone, but together they’ll answer the question that the New York Times article forced me to ask: How long has this been going on?

A Pattern Empires Refuse to Learn From

The most frustrating part of this research wasn’t discovering that Afghanistan has suffered for 2,500 years. It was discovering that every empire makes the exact same mistakes.

The British learned nothing from the Mughals and Persians who fought over Afghanistan for centuries before them. The Soviets learned nothing from three British disasters in Afghanistan. The Americans learned nothing from the Soviet catastrophe.

Each invader believes they’re different. Each invader has better technology, better intentions, a better plan. Each invader discovers that technology doesn’t matter in the Hindu Kush mountains, that intentions don’t feed hungry children, and that plans collapse on contact with Afghan reality.

And each time, it’s Afghan children who pay the price.

Why I’m Writing This

I’m a 74-year-old retired businessman from Toronto. I’m not an academic historian or a foreign policy expert. I’m just someone who read a devastating New York Times article about children starving because of preventable policy choices, and couldn’t stop asking “Why?”

Why does this keep happening? Why does every empire think it can succeed where all others failed? Why do nations never learn?

The answer, I discovered, is that people don’t know their history. Or they’ve forgotten it. Or they assume that ancient history is irrelevant to modern problems.

But those children dying of malnutrition in Nalej and Kabul aren’t dying because of anything new. They’re dying because a 2,500-year pattern is repeating once again, and the world is too historically illiterate to recognize it.

What You’ll Learn

By the end of this series, you’ll understand:

  • Why Afghanistan’s geography made endless invasion almost inevitable

  • How even history’s greatest military minds (Alexander, Genghis Khan, the British Empire) failed to hold Afghanistan

  • Why the brief “golden age” of 1933-1978 was Afghanistan’s only extended period of peace in recorded history

  • How each invasion laid the groundwork for the next disaster

  • Why the current humanitarian crisis isn’t an aberration, but a continuation

  • And most importantly: Why empires keep making the same mistakes

A Note on Sources and Approach

I’m not a professional historian, so I’ve relied heavily on academic sources, contemporary accounts, and established historical scholarship. Where there’s scholarly debate (dates of invasions, casualty estimates, motivations of actors), I’ll note it.

My goal isn’t to write definitive history. It’s to provide context that most coverage of Afghanistan lacks - the long view that shows this isn’t a crisis of the last few decades, but a tragedy measured in millennia.

And I’m writing this because that New York Times article haunts me. Those mothers who lost children. Those babies too weak to eat. Those closed health clinics.

They deserve more than pity. They deserve understanding of why this keeps happening, so maybe - just maybe - the pattern can finally be broken.

Starting at the Beginning

So let’s go back to where it started. Not to 2001, or 1979, or even 1839.

Let’s go back to 550 BCE, when Afghanistan first appears in recorded history as part of the Achaemenid Persian Empire.

Let’s trace every invasion, every occupation, every empire that has treated Afghanistan as a prize to be won or a problem to be solved with military force.

Let’s count the rare years of peace and compare them to the endless years of war.

And let’s see if history can finally teach the lesson that 2,500 years have been demonstrating:

Afghanistan can be invaded. It cannot be conquered. And those who try leave behind nothing but grief.
