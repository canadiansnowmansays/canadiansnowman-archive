The Root Cause No One Will Name

Why Patriarchy Explains the Epstein Files Better Than Wealth or Elite Networks

March 2, 2026

Bill Gates, Jeffrey Epstein and friends. Source: Yahoo

The Question the Podcast Asked

I’ve spent the past year documenting how Jeffrey Epstein’s network operated. How Attorney General Bondi refused to turn around and look at survivors. How 50 pages of FBI interviews about Trump went missing. How Les Wexner testified under oath about Epstein’s Rothschild connections. How the United Kingdom arrested a prince while the United States gave its Commerce Secretary “full support.”

I’ve documented the accommodation pattern in exhaustive detail. The sweetheart plea deal. The continued relationships after conviction. The systematic protection of powerful men while exposing victims.

But last week, I listened to a podcast about why men rape. And it asked a question I hadn’t quite framed correctly in all my Epstein coverage:

Everyone is asking how these men got away with so much rape. Almost no one is asking what would cause so many men to want to rape in the first place.

That question reframes everything we’ve documented.

Because the Epstein files don’t just show us a protection racket for wealthy criminals. They show us what happens when a particular belief system about male entitlement meets hierarchical structures designed to protect the powerful.

What We’ve Been Missing

Mainstream media has converged on four explanations for the Epstein scandal:

The New York Times: “How JPMorgan Enabled the Crimes of Jeffrey Epstein” Real News Network: “Jeffrey Epstein: How Wealth Protected America’s Worst Child Sex Criminal” Jacobin: “Capitalism Enabled Jeffrey Epstein’s Crimes”

The Guardian: “Epstein Files Place Renewed Attention on US Authorities’ Failure to Stop Him” Helsinki Times: “Inside a Depraved Transatlantic Elite and the Institutions That Protected It”

TIME: “Commerce Secretary Calls Jeffrey Epstein ‘The Greatest Blackmailer Ever’” BBC: “Billionaire Les Wexner Tells US Lawmakers He Was Conned by Epstein”

Wealth. Elite networks. Institutional failure. Blackmail.

All true. All documented in the files. All important to understand.

But as writer Celeste Davis pointed out in a recent essay on her Matriarchal Blessing Substack, we’re hyperfocused on the fertilizer and water that made the weeds grow tall. We’re not looking at the seed that grew into a weed in the first place.

We’re acting as if rapists are just a given, and the only problem is that some rapists have enough money and power to avoid consequences.

But that framing treats the desire to rape as natural and inevitable. It suggests the problem is purely about punishment and deterrence, not about what creates that desire in the first place.

The French Town

Last year, a case in France revealed something most people don’t want to confront.

Dominique Pelicot drugged his wife Gisèle and invited men from online forums to rape her while she was unconscious. Over a decade, more than 90 men accepted his invitation.

They weren’t billionaires. They weren’t Epstein’s elite network. They were nurses, teachers, firefighters, fathers, grandfathers, farm workers.

At a conference on sex trafficking, journalist Lorraine de Foucher (who won a Pulitzer for covering the porn industry and child prostitution in France) was asked about the Pelicot case.

A man in his seventies asked carefully: “So in the fairly small town of Mazan, Dominique Pelicot easily found 90+ men willing to rape his wife while she was drugged and unconscious. Hundreds more saw the messages and not one decided to tell police. Does that mean that in every town, every village in our country, there are just as many men willing to rape an unconscious woman?”

De Foucher replied: “Yes.”

“But then that means there are thousands, tens of thousands, maybe hundreds of thousands!”

“Yes.”

“But… that’s abominable! It’s a catastrophe! It’s a national emergency!”

“Yes. It is.”

We cannot explain what happened in Mazan with wealth, elite networks, or blackmail. Those 90 men had none of those things.

They had something else: a belief that when given the opportunity and assurance they wouldn’t face consequences, raping an unconscious woman was acceptable.

What created that belief?

What the Research Actually Shows

Some will argue that men rape because of biology—testosterone makes them naturally aggressive and sexual.

The research completely debunks this.

A peer-reviewed meta-analysis published in the National Library of Medicine found that sex offenders do not have higher testosterone levels than non-sex offenders. Trans men who increase testosterone do not become more abusive. When researchers decreased testosterone in domestic violence perpetrators, it didn’t curb their abusive behavior.

What DID work to stop domestic abusers?

“Changing their deeply held beliefs about their sense of entitlement.”

If biology caused rape, rates would be consistent across all cultures. They’re not.

The World Health Organization has concluded that “gender inequality and norms on the acceptability of violence against women are a root cause of violence against women.”

UN Women’s comprehensive data shows that countries with greater gender equality have significantly lower rates of violence against women. The pattern holds across every measure of gender equality—education, economic opportunity, political representation, reproductive rights.

A CDC-published study examining all 50 US states found the same correlation: “States with a high degree of gender inequality also report higher prevalence estimates among women for completed or attempted rape using physical force.”

Additional research shows that it is not innate aggression that makes men violent, but the internalized belief that they fall short of society’s perceived standards for masculinity. This phenomenon—called “masculine discrepancy stress”—correlates with almost every type of violence, including sexual assault. As Ruth Whippman wrote in the New York Times: “the more acutely a man suffers from this, the more likely he is to commit almost every type of violence, including sexual assault, intimate partner violence and assault with a weapon.”

So: entitlement, gender inequality, and failing to meet masculine standards all correlate with sexual violence.

There’s a word for the system that creates these conditions. The system that teaches boys that dominance over women is essential to manhood. The system that makes gender inequality seem natural and inevitable. The system where being “emasculated” is more shameful than being immoral.

Patriarchy.

The $21 Million Proof of Concept

If you want to see patriarchy operating as an explicit business model—not hidden or subtle, but stated plainly as the product being sold—look at Andrew and Tristan Tate.

The New York Times published a bombshell investigation by Megan Twohey and Isabella Kwai revealing that Trump administration officials had overridden prosecutors to secure the Tates’ release from detention. The indictment documented alleged romance fraud, violence against women, and coercion into pornography that generated £21 million in profits.

How did the Tates frame this to their followers? “Defending masculinity.”

They didn’t hide the exploitation. They rebranded it. They turned patriarchal domination into a subscription service called “Hustler’s University,” teaching tens of thousands of paying customers that exploiting women is what real men do.

The message was explicit: dominance over women isn’t just acceptable—it’s essential to manhood. Any suggestion otherwise is “emasculation” by feminists trying to destroy masculine men.

Sound familiar? It’s the exact belief system that correlates with sexual violence in every research study.

But here’s what makes the Tate case particularly instructive for understanding the Epstein pattern:

The Tates got hierarchical protection the same way Epstein did—by making themselves useful to powerful people.

Epstein positioned himself within banking and political networks. The Tates positioned themselves within the MAGA political movement, delivering a voting bloc of young men radicalized by anti-feminist content.

When Romanian prosecutors tried to hold them accountable, Trump administration officials intervened.

Deliver a voting bloc. Play the culture-war martyr. Frame exploitation as “defending masculinity.” And justice becomes optional.

The Tates didn’t just exploit women. They monetized the instruction manual, teaching thousands of men the playbook: patriarchal entitlement as business model, political utility as protection mechanism.

As I documented in my six-part series “The Masculinity Con,” the Tate case is proof-of-concept for the global export of Trump’s accountability-evasion system.

How many men are already copying it?

The Word Twelve Major Articles Refused to Print

I decided to check something.

Twelve major articles from The New York Times, BBC, PBS, and TIME attempting to explain the Epstein files and the networks that enabled him.

How many times did they use the word “patriarchy”?

Zero.

Okay, but patriarchy is controversial. How many times did they mention “gender inequality”?

Also zero.

A Reuters poll asked Americans about their concerns regarding the Epstein files: 69% said the files show powerful people are rarely held accountable; 53% said the files lowered their trust in political and business leaders.

Valid concerns. Important findings.

But no one asked whether the files reveal a problem with gender inequality, male entitlement, or patriarchal systems.

Meanwhile, 22% of American men believe gender inequality doesn’t really exist, and a third believe feminism is making things worse.

We’ll talk endlessly about how to better punish rape. We refuse to discuss how to prevent the desire to rape in the first place.

Two Types of Impunity

Here’s what I’ve come to understand from a year of documenting the Epstein files:

There are two types of impunity that enable sexual violence.

Situational Impunity: The belief that “I won’t get caught.”

This is what the 90 men in Mazan, France relied on. This is what most rapists in most contexts believe. They’re counting on the victim’s vulnerability, their own anonymity, the low reporting rates, the even lower conviction rates.

They’re not wrong about the odds.

Structural Impunity: The belief that “Even if caught, systems will protect me.”

This is what Jeffrey Epstein had. This is what his network continues to have. This is what Andrew and Tristan Tate received when Trump administration officials overrode Romanian prosecutors.

They’re not just counting on not getting caught—they’re counting on hierarchical systems being designed to protect people who serve the powerful, even when the evidence is overwhelming.

They’re also not wrong about this.

The difference between the Pelicot rapists and the Epstein network isn’t the root cause. It’s the mechanism of protection.

Both groups share the foundational belief that exploiting women is acceptable. That their desires matter more than women’s humanity. That masculinity includes the right to dominate.

That’s patriarchy operating at both levels.

The difference is only in the infrastructure of impunity available to protect them when that patriarchal entitlement leads to criminal acts.

How Structural Impunity Actually Worked

Let me walk through what we’ve documented about how hierarchical protection functioned in Epstein’s case, because it shows a system working exactly as designed.

Elite Positioning

Epstein didn’t just accumulate wealth. He positioned himself within the most powerful networks in the world.

Les Wexner testified under oath before Congress that Epstein’s credentials came from “his personal work for the Rothschild family in France.” Documents show a $25 million contract between Epstein’s company and Edmond de Rothschild Holding in 2015—seven years after his conviction for soliciting a minor.

Ariane de Rothschild, CEO of the Edmond de Rothschild banking group, corresponded with Epstein dozens of times and met with him repeatedly after his conviction. Emails show her helping create custom candles for him with his “favorite mathematical formula.” When she was sick in 2016, he invited her to stay at his apartment: “it would be private. no one the wiser.”

Lynn Forester de Rothschild introduced Epstein to key figures including Alan Dershowitz (who became his lawyer and negotiated the 2008 plea deal) and Prince Andrew. These weren’t casual associations. This was strategic positioning within hierarchies that grant protection.

The 2008 Plea Deal

Federal prosecutors had evidence for sex trafficking charges involving dozens of underage girls. They had identified co-conspirators. The case supported decades in prison.

Epstein pleaded guilty to soliciting prostitution from a minor—a state charge. He served thirteen months in county jail with work release, spending most days at his office.

The plea agreement included unprecedented immunity for unnamed co-conspirators. The people who helped him weren’t just not prosecuted—they were given legal protection from future prosecution.

The deal was kept secret from victims in violation of federal law.

Notice the language: prosecutors called teenage trafficking victims “prostitutes.” That’s not a legal term. That’s patriarchal framing that blames the victims for their own exploitation.

Continued Access After Conviction

Banking relationships continued. Elite networks maintained contact. Powerful figures accommodated him.

World Economic Forum CEO Borge Brende received texts from Epstein in March 2019—months before his final arrest—containing his lawyers’ defense of his conduct. Brende responded with a thumbs-up emoji.

The conviction didn’t end his access because the hierarchical systems valued maintaining elite connections more than protecting girls and women.

Protection After Death

We’ve documented this extensively in the Epstein Files series:

  • Co-conspirators’ names redacted in the 2007 draft indictment

  • Victims’ names and faces exposed through “technical errors” while powerful men remained protected

  • 2.5 million pages withheld from the 6 million ordered released

  • Approximately 50 pages of FBI interviews containing accusations against President Trump missing from public files

  • DOJ calling missing files “improperly tagged” rather than admitting they were withheld

In twenty years, exactly two people have faced consequences: Jeffrey Epstein (died in custody) and Ghislaine Maxwell (serving 20 years).

Not the co-conspirators. Not the assistants. Not the powerful men who maintained relationships with a convicted sex offender for years.

The International Contrast

The clearest evidence that both patriarchy and hierarchical protection matter comes from comparing consequences across countries.

United Kingdom: Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor arrested. Peter Mandelson arrested. Both face potential criminal charges.

Norway — which consistently ranks among the world’s most gender-equal countries — saw a former Prime Minister charged with “gross corruption,” the Crown Princess under investigation and publicly expressing regret, and multiple diplomats resign or face investigation.

World Economic Forum: CEO Borge Brende resigned after investigation.

United States — which ranks #43 globally in gender equality — gave its Commerce Secretary “full support” despite documented visits to Epstein’s island after his conviction. The Olympics chairman remains in position. The NFL “investigates.”

Countries that have made progress on gender equality—that take patriarchy seriously as a root cause—are more willing to hold powerful men accountable.

Countries that ignore patriarchal entitlement also maintain hierarchical protection for elite abusers.

You need to address both to get accountability.

Why Both Systems Protect Abusers

I’ve spent a year documenting the accommodation pattern. How “good people” maintained relationships with Epstein after his conviction. How they accepted his money, flew on his plane, signed contracts with him.

Here’s what I’ve learned: hierarchical systems and patriarchal systems protect abusers for the same reason.

When powerful men face consequences for abusing women and girls, it threatens both systems simultaneously.

It challenges the hierarchy by suggesting that rules apply to everyone, not just to people without money and connections.

It challenges patriarchy by suggesting that women’s and girls’ rights matter more than men’s sexual entitlement.

Neither system can allow this without undermining its own legitimacy.

So they protect abusers through:

Preventing accountability initially: Sweetheart deals. Secret agreements. Immunity for co-conspirators. Language that frames victims as “prostitutes.”

Minimizing damage when prevention fails: Limiting consequences to individuals while protecting networks. Epstein and Maxwell prosecuted, but the banking relationships continue, the academic appointments remain, the political connections endure.

Punishing threats to the system: Marjorie Taylor Greene pushed for file release. Trump called her a “traitor” and primary-challenged her out of Congress. Victims who speak out face exposure. Prosecutors who refuse corrupt orders resign.

The Accommodation Pattern Explained

This explains something I’ve documented repeatedly but hadn’t fully understood: how accomplished, educated, progressive people maintained relationships with a convicted sex offender.

They weren’t all true believers in Epstein’s innocence. They weren’t all participants in abuse.

But they accommodated him because both hierarchical and patriarchal systems make that accommodation rational.

Speaking out about a powerful man’s exploitation of girls risks your position in the hierarchy. Maintaining relationships preserves access.

The cost of accommodation falls on women and girls. Their suffering doesn’t threaten your status in either system.

So good people make small compromises:

  • “I didn’t know” (despite public conviction)

  • “It was professional, not personal” (as if exploitation is ethically neutral when it’s business)

  • “Everyone knew but no one said anything” (the collective silence was a choice)

  • “Looking back, I wish I hadn’t” (the passive voice that erases agency)

Cindy McCain said it honestly: “We all knew.”

The silence was a choice rooted in both hierarchical protection of the powerful and patriarchal dismissal of girls’ suffering.

What Actually Breaking These Patterns Requires

The Epstein Files Transparency Act passed with overwhelming support. Sunlight was supposed to bring accountability.

We’ve now documented what happened instead:

Files released with victims exposed and powerful men protected. Some resignations, others staying with “full support.” Justice Department calling missing files “improperly tagged.” The Commerce Secretary keeping his job.

Breaking both patriarchal entitlement and hierarchical impunity requires attacking both systems.

On Patriarchy:

  • Taking gender inequality seriously as a public health crisis

  • Investment in women’s education, reproductive rights, economic empowerment (the interventions the UN identifies as reducing violence)

  • Challenging masculine norms that equate manhood with domination

  • Preventing rape by addressing entitlement, not just punishing after the fact

On Hierarchical Protection:

  • Actual criminal consequences for powerful people (prison time for elite enablers)

  • Institutional consequences for organizations that enabled abuse

  • Economic consequences for enablers (personal financial devastation)

  • Cultural rejection of wealth and power as exemptions from accountability

Countries making progress do both: The UK arrested a prince. Norway charged a former prime minister. The World Economic Forum accepted a resignation.

The United States gave its Commerce Secretary “full support.”

The Answer to Both Questions

After a year of documentation, here’s what the Epstein files actually reveal:

Why did so many men want to participate in or enable Epstein’s network?

Because patriarchal systems teach boys from birth that male entitlement to women’s bodies is natural. That masculinity includes the right to dominate. That women and girls exist for male pleasure. When those boys become men with power and opportunity, those beliefs don’t disappear—they find expression.

Why did the network operate for decades with minimal consequences?

Because hierarchical systems create structural impunity where powerful people face minimal risk even when caught. Because those hierarchies protect their own members more fiercely than they protect the vulnerable people those members exploit.

The Epstein files didn’t reveal an anomaly. They revealed the most thoroughly documented example of how patriarchy and hierarchy function together.

Every accommodation I’ve documented—every continued relationship, every redacted name, every missing file, every person still in position—is evidence of both systems working as designed.

Patriarchy creates the entitlement. Hierarchy provides the protection. Together, they ensure that elite men can exploit women and girls with near-total impunity.

And as long as we refuse to name patriarchy, we’ll keep missing the root cause.

We’ll keep acting surprised when the next Jeffrey Epstein emerges from the same soil that produced this one.

But here’s what should terrify us: the Tate Brothers aren’t waiting for the next one to emerge organically. They’re actively teaching the playbook to tens of thousands of paying customers.

Patriarchal entitlement as product. Exploitation rebranded as “defending masculinity.” Political utility as protection mechanism. The instruction manual is for sale, and thousands of men are buying it.

The research is clear: countries that address gender inequality see less violence against women. Countries that dismantle hierarchical protection hold powerful people accountable.

The question is whether we’re willing to do the work on both fronts.

The survivors deserve both. They deserve a world that prevents the desire to exploit them and dismantles the systems that protect their abusers.

But deserving it and getting it are two different things.

The United Kingdom arrested a prince. Norway charged a former prime minister. The World Economic Forum accepted a resignation.

The United States gave its Commerce Secretary “full support.”

The hierarchy is counting on us never understanding why.
