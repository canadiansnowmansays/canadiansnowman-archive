The Kash Patel Files

Last Call

Part I: The Bottle and the Bureau

April 20, 2026

"Last Call" — a CanadianSnowmanSays original. Not available at fightwithkash.com. Yet.

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

The Department of Justice’s ethics handbook contains a sentence that reads like it was written for a future no one wanted to arrive: “An employee is prohibited from habitually using alcohol or other intoxicants to excess.”

The department’s inspector general has elaborated on why, in language that belongs not in a human resources manual but in a counterintelligence briefing: off-duty alcohol consumption can not only impair employees’ judgment; it can make them vulnerable to exploitation or coercion by foreign adversaries.

That language was written for a GS-13 analyst with a drinking problem. It was not written for the Director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation. It did not need to be. No one imagined it would have to be.

And yet.

On April 17, 2026, Sarah Fitzpatrick of The Atlantic published a devastating investigation — sourced to more than two dozen current and former FBI officials, intelligence agency staff, hospitality-industry workers, members of Congress, political operatives, lobbyists, and former advisers — that painted a portrait of an FBI director whose personal conduct has become what the sources themselves called “a national-security vulnerability.”

The portrait is not subtle. It does not require interpretation. It requires only the ability to read.

Kash Patel, the man entrusted with the domestic security of 330 million Americans, is known to drink to the point of obvious intoxication — at Ned’s, a private club in Washington, and at the Poodle Room in Las Vegas, where he frequently spends parts of his weekends. He does this, according to the reporting, while in the presence of White House and other administration staff. Early in his tenure, meetings and briefings had to be rescheduled for later in the day as a result of his alcohol-fueled nights. Six current and former officials confirmed this to Fitzpatrick.

On multiple occasions in the past year, members of his security detail had difficulty waking Patel because he was seemingly intoxicated.

And then this: A request for “breaching equipment” — the tools normally used by SWAT and hostage-rescue teams to quickly gain entry into buildings — was made last year because Patel had been unreachable behind locked doors.

Read that again. The security detail assigned to protect the director of the FBI — the person who, in the event of a terrorist attack or a foreign intelligence operation on American soil, must be reachable at all times — considered deploying the same equipment used to enter buildings during hostage rescues. Because the director was behind a locked door and could not be roused.

This is not a management story. This is not a gossip story. This is not a story about a man who occasionally has one too many at a Washington dinner party. This is a story about a strategic vulnerability in the American national security apparatus, and it is a story that America’s adversaries can read as clearly as the rest of us.

The Iranians already know.

In March 2026, hackers claiming affiliation with Handala Team — a pro-Iranian hacktivist group associated with Iran’s Ministry of Intelligence and Security — posted more than 300 messages from Patel’s personal Gmail account on a website that cybersecurity tools flagged as being hosted on a Russian server. The emails were from 2010 to 2022, largely anodyne personal messages. But the hackers signalled they were holding back additional material — including a 2016 résumé claiming a classified CIA award.

The implications are stark. A foreign adversary — the same foreign adversary the United States is currently at war with — has demonstrated the ability to compromise the personal communications of the man responsible for defending America against that adversary’s intelligence operations. The same adversary whose Revolutionary Guard Corps has, according to FBI reporting, planned kidnappings and assassinations of Americans on U.S. soil. The same adversary whose Quds Force operatives tasked an asset with concocting a plan to assassinate Donald Trump himself.

And the man who is supposed to be coordinating America’s defence against these operations is, on some nights, unreachable behind a locked door.

Former FBI agent Jacqueline Maguire, who spent 25 years at the bureau and held multiple leadership roles overseeing counterterrorism and criminal investigations before being pushed out in 2025, wrote in The New York Times that Patel’s behaviour was distracting the FBI from the danger of terrorism at the worst possible moment. The Iran war, she wrote, “significantly elevates the regime’s threat to Americans at home and abroad.”

Peter Strzok, the former FBI counterintelligence official, told David Frum of The Atlantic that under Patel, “there is very much a greater vulnerability than there was prior to Kash Patel showing up.” He described the leadership vacuum in clinical terms: the people who spent 20 years building the expertise to advise the director on complex threat assessments have been forced out. The people below them — good agents, Strzok acknowledged — simply haven’t had the time to develop the institutional knowledge required to sort genuine threats from noise.

“This isn’t even the B team,” Strzok said. “These are the people. It’s like nobody in the first term wants to work for them again.”

The drinking is not new to the White House.

FBI Director Kash Patel celebrated with the US men's hockey team at the Winter Olympics in Italy after the team defeated Canada to take home a gold medal. Kash Patel / X

While on official travel to Italy in February 2026 for the Milan Olympics, Patel was filmed chugging beer with the U.S. men’s Olympic hockey team following their gold-medal victory. The video was public, celebratory, and seemingly harmless — except to the President of the United States, who does not drink and whose brother Fred died following a long struggle with alcoholism.

Trump called Patel to convey his unhappiness.

But officials told Fitzpatrick that Patel’s alcohol use goes “far beyond the occasional beer.” FBI officials and others in the administration have privately questioned whether alcohol played a role in the instances in which he shared inaccurate information about active law-enforcement investigations — including his premature social media announcements about suspects in the Charlie Kirk assassination and the Brown University shooting, and his blindsiding of Justice Department lawyers by tweeting about a Michigan terrorism case before charges had been filed.

This is the intersection that should alarm every American, regardless of partisan affiliation: a man whose judgment is professionally required to be sharp at all times, whose agency handles the most sensitive intelligence in the domestic security apparatus, whose decisions about deploying agents and allocating resources directly affect whether terrorist plots are detected and disrupted — and whose colleagues have watched him drink to the point of incapacitation, repeatedly, while holding that responsibility.

Let me be clear about what this essay is and is not.

This is not a morality lecture about drinking. I am Canadian. I am 74. I have had my share. Alcoholism is a disease, not a character flaw, and I have no interest in shaming anyone for struggling with it.

But there is a difference — an enormous, unbridgeable difference — between a private citizen struggling with alcohol and the director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation being unreachable during a war because his security detail cannot wake him up. The first is a personal tragedy deserving of compassion. The second is a national security emergency deserving of immediate action.

The DOJ’s own ethics handbook exists because the department understood, long before Patel arrived, that an impaired official is a vulnerable official. An official who drinks to excess is an official who might say something indiscreet to the wrong person at Ned’s. An official who is habitually incapacitated is an official whose routines, patterns, and vulnerabilities become predictable to adversaries. An official whose personal communications have already been compromised by Iranian-linked hackers is an official who cannot afford a single additional vector of vulnerability.

Patel has all of them.

The people who know are afraid to speak.

Exterior of FBI Headquarters at night. FBI

Fitzpatrick reported that many of her sources had been afraid to reveal their concerns about Patel publicly or through traditional whistleblower channels, because he has been aggressive in cracking down on anyone he deems insufficiently loyal. At Patel’s direction, FBI employees are polygraphed in an effort to identify leakers. In these sessions, according to The New York Times, agents have been asked whether they have ever said anything disparaging about the director or the president.

This is the trap. The man whose conduct most urgently requires whistleblower reporting has constructed a surveillance apparatus specifically designed to punish whistleblowing. The employees who could trigger the accountability mechanisms — the inspector general, the congressional oversight committees, the internal ethics review — are the same employees who have watched their colleagues fired for case assignments they did not choose, terminated for attending concerts with the wrong friends, pushed out for displaying a Pride flag.

The message is unmistakable: if you report what you see, you will be next.

And so the drinking continues. The absences continue. The meetings get rescheduled. The breaching equipment stays close at hand. And 38,000 FBI employees — including the ones still doing the work of protecting the country — carry the knowledge of their director’s incapacitation like a classified document they’re not permitted to discuss.

“That’s what keeps me up at night.”

That is what one official told Fitzpatrick about the prospect of a domestic terrorist attack while Patel is in office. The official said it before the full weight of the Iran war was felt. Before the counterintelligence agents devoted to Iran were fired. Before the breaching equipment story became public.

The remark deserves to sit with us for a moment.

An FBI official — someone inside the building, someone who understands the threat matrix, someone who sees the intelligence briefings that the director reportedly does not read — lies awake at night worrying about what happens when the next attack comes and the director cannot be reached.

Not will not be reached. Cannot.

The Iranians have a playbook for exactly this kind of operation. They have used Hezbollah to strike American military targets. They have used Quds Force assets to plan kidnappings and assassinations on American soil. They have used hackers to penetrate American infrastructure. They have demonstrated, repeatedly, the patience and the capability to wait for the right moment.

And the right moment, for an adversary studying the patterns of the man who leads America’s domestic counterintelligence effort, might be a Saturday night — when the director is at the Poodle Room in Las Vegas, or behind a locked door that requires breaching equipment to open.

How did the most pre-warned disaster in modern American governance come to pass?

The man behind the locked door did not arrive there by accident. He arrived there because every institutional guardrail that existed to prevent someone like him from reaching that position was dismantled, one by one, by the people who wanted him there precisely because he would do what no qualified person would.

That story — the warnings, the guardrails, and the capitulation that swept them all aside — begins long before Patel walked into the Hoover Building.

Patel’s response to Fitzpatrick’s 19 detailed questions was attributed to him in a statement: “Print it, all false, I’ll see you in court — bring your checkbook.”

He made good on the threat — or at least announced he would.

On Sunday morning, April 19, two days after Fitzpatrick’s report was published, Patel appeared on Fox News’s Sunday Morning Futures with Maria Bartiromo and announced that he would file a defamation lawsuit against The Atlantic on Monday. “Absolutely, it’s coming tomorrow,” he said. “You want to attack my character? Come at me. Bring it on. I’ll see you in court.” On Monday, he made good on it — filing a $250 million defamation suit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia against both The Atlantic and Fitzpatrick personally. A spokeswoman for the publication said they would "vigorously defend The Atlantic and our journalists against this meritless lawsuit

His lawyer, Jesse Binnall, posted a letter on social media that had been sent to The Atlantic before publication, claiming that most of the article’s 19 substantive assertions were “false” and warning that Patel would “have no choice but to take swift legal action to uphold his reputation.”

The Atlantic‘s editor-in-chief, Jeffrey Goldberg, responded with five words: “We stand by our reporting.”

Sarah Fitzpatrick, who wrote the article, was equally concise: “I stand by every word.”

But what was most revealing about Patel’s Sunday performance was not the lawsuit. It was the silence at its centre.

Bartiromo asked him directly about the drinking allegations. His response contained exactly zero sentences addressing the actual claims. Not one word about the breaching equipment. Not one word about the security detail unable to wake him. Not one word about Ned’s or the Poodle Room. Not one word about meetings rescheduled because he was incapacitated the night before. Instead, he delivered a pre-loaded statistics package — homicide rates, opioid deaths, child victims, fentanyl seizures, Most Wanted fugitives — that could have been read off a teleprompter for any appearance on any topic. “This FBI has the most prolific year in crime reduction in the United States history,” he said. “A 20-point percent reduction in the homicide rate. A 20-point drop in opioid overdose deaths.”

The allegations were not rebutted. They were simply displaced by volume.

And the volume was not directed at Bartiromo. It was not directed at her viewers. It was directed at one man — the man who, according to Fitzpatrick’s reporting, has already complained about Patel’s television appearances, who has active discussions underway about his replacement, and whose attention Patel has spent his entire career trying to capture and hold. This was not an FBI director defending his record to the American public. This was an FBI director defending his job to his employer. Every statistic was a plea: Look what I have done for you. Look at the numbers. Do not fire me. Nine days earlier, a login error had sent Patel into a panic, convinced the White House had already made the call. The Sunday show performance was the same panic, dressed in a suit and delivered to camera — a man who knows his position is precarious, reciting his résumé on national television because the only audience that matters might be watching.

Then came the second tell.

On the same Sunday morning broadcasts where he announced the lawsuit — on the same shows, in the same interviews — Patel claimed that the FBI had gathered evidence supporting Donald Trump’s long-standing assertion that the 2020 election was stolen. “We have the information that backs President Trump’s claim,” he told Fox News Sunday. He declined to provide specifics, citing an ongoing investigation being handled with DOJ prosecutors under Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche. “Stay tuned this week,” he said. “You might see a thing or two.”

He also promised arrests related to the 2016 Russia investigation. “I am never going to let this go,” he said.

Even Maria Bartiromo — not a journalist typically inclined to challenge Trump allies — pushed back. She noted that Trump says the election was rigged “all the time” and that “it’s almost getting lost because he says it so much.” She asked Patel directly: “You’ve been at the FBI now 14 months. Have you done anything about that?”

The question answers itself. Courts, election officials, and Trump’s own Justice Department during his first term rejected allegations of widespread fraud sufficient to change the outcome of the 2020 election. Patel has been making versions of these claims — about Chinese fake IDs, about suppressed intelligence reports, about hidden rooms in FBI headquarters — since he took office, and none of it has produced a single prosecution, a single indictment, or a single piece of verified evidence.

But evidence was never the point. The point was spectacle. The point was to change the subject.

This is TACO in its purest, most concentrated form. A devastating investigative report drops on Friday, sourced to more than two dozen current and former officials, documenting the FBI director’s incapacitation during a war. By Sunday morning, the director is on television threatening to sue the magazine (Threaten), validating the president’s debunked election conspiracy (Accommodate), promising arrests and revelations that may never materialise (Obfuscate), and wrapping himself in the flag of the “men and women of the FBI” he has been systematically purging for fourteen months (Obfuscate again). The drinking, the breaching equipment, the locked doors, the national security vulnerability — all of it displaced, at least for one news cycle, by the spectacle of a man in a suit on a Sunday morning talk show promising legal action and dark revelations.

It is the response of a man who believes that aggression is a substitute for argument, that threatening litigation is the same as providing evidence, that denial constitutes defence. It is the response of a man who has spent his career confusing bluster with competence. And it is the response of a man who, when confronted with the testimony of twenty-six sources describing his incapacitation, reaches not for a rebuttal but for a distraction.

The White House spokesperson, Karoline Leavitt, said that under Trump and Patel, “crime across the country has plummeted to the lowest level in more than 100 years.” Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche said that “anonymously sourced hit pieces do not constitute journalism.”

Twenty-six sources. Current and former FBI officials. Intelligence agency staff. Hospitality-industry workers. Members of Congress. Lobbyists. Former advisers. This is not an anonymously sourced hit piece. This is the institutional memory of the FBI itself, speaking through the only channels left to it because the official channels have been mined and booby-trapped by the man they’re trying to warn us about.

He should be worried. But the rest of us should be terrified.

Because somewhere tonight, the director of the FBI may be behind a locked door. And somewhere tonight, the people who mean this country harm are very much awake.

And as this essay goes to press, the man who cannot be reached by his own security detail is filing a defamation lawsuit against the journalists who told us so.

But the question remains: how did we get here? The answer begins not in 2026, not in 2025, but years earlier — with an attorney general who said “over my dead body,” a CIA director who threatened to resign, a chairman of the Joint Chiefs who warned a young political operative that life looks really shitty from behind bars, and a West Africa hostage rescue that nearly killed SEAL Team 6 because someone may have fabricated an approval that didn’t exist.

Every one of those people tried to stop this. Every one of them failed.
