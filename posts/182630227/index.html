Trump’s ISIS Theater

How Coercive Diplomacy Turned Nigeria's Sovereignty Into Political Theater

December 26, 2025

Introduction: The Christmas Day Calculation

On December 25, 2025, as American families gathered for Christmas celebrations, President Trump announced U.S. military strikes against ISIS targets in northwestern Nigeria. The timing wasn’t coincidental—it was calculated theater designed to send a specific message to a specific audience.

“Tonight, at my direction as Commander in Chief, the United States launched a powerful and deadly strike against ISIS Terrorist Scum in Northwest Nigeria,” Trump declared on Truth Social, “who have been targeting and viciously killing, primarily, innocent Christians, at levels not seen for many years, and even Centuries!”

The operation involved more than a dozen Tomahawk cruise missiles fired from a Navy ship in the Gulf of Guinea, hitting two ISIS camps in Sokoto State. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth followed with his own social media post: “The President was clear last month: the killing of innocent Christians in Nigeria (and elsewhere) must end. The @DeptofWar is always ready, so ISIS found out tonight — on Christmas.”

This marked the second ISIS-focused military action in a week. Days earlier, the U.S. had conducted dozens of airstrikes against Islamic State targets in Syria, fulfilling Trump’s vow to avenge two Army soldiers and a civilian interpreter killed in a terrorist attack.

Two strikes. One week. Both against ISIS affiliates. Both accompanied by inflammatory social media declarations. Both carefully choreographed for maximum political impact rather than strategic effectiveness.

The pattern reveals far more about Trump’s approach to military intervention than any single operation could: selective outrage calibrated for domestic political consumption, coercive diplomacy that forces accommodation from foreign governments, and theatrical displays that prioritize symbolism over substance—all while military officials privately acknowledge the limited strategic value of such actions.

The US defence department posted a short video that appears to show a missile being launched from a military vessel

The Accommodation Cascade: Coercive Diplomacy in Action

The path to the Christmas Day strikes demonstrates Trump’s approach to foreign policy: threaten unilateral action, force accommodation from sovereign governments, then claim the resulting cooperation as diplomatic success.

In November 2025, Trump ordered the Defense Department to “prepare to intervene militarily in Nigeria to protect Christians.” The threat was explicit and public. He warned he would “do things in Nigeria that Nigeria is not going to be happy about” and threatened to go into “that now disgraced country guns-a-blazing.”

U.S. Africa Command responded by drawing up targeting options and forwarding them to the Pentagon and White House. The options included airstrikes on known militant compounds in northern Nigeria.

But even as these plans were being developed, American military officials expressed skepticism. According to the New York Times, officials said “it was doubtful they would have much long-term impact because of the entrenched nature of the conflict.”

The strategic assessment was clear: tactical strikes might kill some militants, but they wouldn’t meaningfully alter the underlying dynamics driving violence across Nigeria. The insurgency, the banditry, the sectarian tensions—none of these would be resolved by Tomahawk missiles.

Yet the threat achieved its political purpose. Nigerian officials, facing the prospect of American military action on their territory without their approval, increased cooperation. By late November, Reuters reported that the U.S. had begun conducting “intelligence-gathering surveillance flights over large parts of Nigeria.”

When the strikes came on Christmas Day, Nigerian Foreign Minister Yusuf Tuggar revealed the extent of the coordination. He told Nigeria’s Channels TV that he spoke with Secretary of State Marco Rubio “for 19 minutes before the strike and then we spoke again for another five minutes.” President Bola Tinubu gave the green light for the operation.

The Nigerian Foreign Ministry issued a carefully worded statement emphasizing cooperation: “The precision strikes on terrorist targets in Nigeria were carried out in coordination with the Nigerian government.”

This represents classic accommodation psychology in action:

Phase 1 - Threaten: Trump publicly orders the Pentagon to prepare for unilateral military intervention, warning Nigeria he’ll go in “guns-a-blazing”

Phase 2 - Accommodate: Nigerian officials, facing potential violation of sovereignty, increase cooperation and provide intelligence for strikes

Phase 3 - Capitalize: Trump conducts strikes with Nigerian approval, claims both decisive action and international partnership

Phase 4 - Obfuscate: The coerced nature of the cooperation disappears behind declarations of mutual interest and shared values

The deleted AFRICOM post reveals the awkward dynamics. According to Axios and CNN, AFRICOM initially posted on X that it conducted the strike “at the request of Nigerian authorities”—a statement that was quickly deleted, suggesting sensitivity about who actually initiated the operation.

For Nigeria, the accommodation came at cost to sovereignty and diplomatic independence. The government had to publicly support military strikes framed around a religious persecution narrative it had explicitly rejected, timed for the American political calendar rather than Nigerian strategic needs.

Militants allied to IS have sought to establish a presence in two north-western states, while a separate IS-linked group has a stronghold in north-eastern Borno

The Christian Persecution Framework: Selective Narrative Construction

Trump’s emphasis on protecting Christians represents a carefully constructed narrative that serves domestic political purposes while distorting the complex reality on the ground in Nigeria.

“Primarily, innocent Christians at levels not seen for many years, and even Centuries!” Trump wrote, framing the violence as targeted religious persecution requiring American military intervention.

This narrative resonated powerfully with his evangelical base. Republican politicians and Christian advocacy groups had spent months amplifying allegations of “Christian genocide” in Nigeria, building pressure for U.S. action.

But Nigerian officials tell a fundamentally different story—and they did so repeatedly, even as they were forced to accommodate Trump’s demands.

Foreign Minister Tuggar was explicit in his rejection of the religious framing. In an interview with BBC, he stated the strike was a “joint operation” targeting “terrorists,” and “has nothing to do with a particular religion.”

Speaking to CNN, Tuggar emphasized: “This is not about religion. It is about Nigerians, innocent civilians, and the wider region as a whole.” He continued: “When you talk about the Sahel, (the) majority are Muslims. They’re not Christians.”

The Nigerian government’s position reflects the actual security situation. The web of violent armed groups operating across the country kills Muslims and Christians in roughly equal numbers. The insurgency has been ongoing for more than a decade, claiming thousands of lives across sectarian lines.

More than 12,000 people were killed by various violent groups in Nigeria in 2025 alone, according to Armed Conflict Location and Event Data. The violence in northwest Nigeria, where the Christmas Day strikes occurred, is driven primarily by armed bandits and kidnapping gangs seeking ransom—not religious ideology.

The most devastating recent attack received no mention in Trump’s Christmas declarations. On December 24—the day before Trump’s strikes—a suspected suicide bomber detonated an explosive device during evening prayers at a mosque in Maiduguri, killing at least 12 people. Nigerian Muslims mourning their dead on Christmas Day warranted no presidential sympathy.

Even more revealing: Canadian broadcaster CBC ran a fact-check segment titled “Do terror groups in Nigeria target Christians?” specifically to debunk the misinformation spreading about Nigeria’s terrorist threats.

A Nigerian government official wrote in an October Al Jazeera op-ed that while Christians had “suffered horrific attacks,” a “significant portion of the violence” mischaracterized as religious persecution stems from longstanding clashes between mostly Christian farmers and largely Muslim herders over land. Conflicts in the country “are multi-faceted, driven by ethnic rivalries, land disputes and criminality, with religion often secondary.”

Yet Trump persisted with the Christian persecution narrative because it served his political needs. The evangelical audience he was targeting didn’t need nuanced understanding of Nigerian security dynamics—they needed to see their president taking decisive action to protect persecuted Christians on Christmas Day.

The U.S. designated Nigeria a “country of particular concern” under the International Religious Freedom Act. Trump added Nigeria to the U.S. travel ban list. These diplomatic measures followed the political narrative rather than the security reality.

Nigerian President Tinubu, navigating the impossible position of accommodating American pressure while maintaining his own government’s credibility, issued a Christmas Eve message calling for peace “especially between individuals of differing religious beliefs” and pledging to do “everything within my power to enshrine religious freedom in Nigeria and to protect Christians, Muslims, and all Nigerians from violence.”

The contrast between Tinubu’s inclusive message and Trump’s exclusive focus on Christian victims reveals the fundamental disconnect: Nigerian leaders understand they govern a diverse nation where security threats affect all communities; Trump understands he needs evangelical support and frames military action accordingly.

The Theater of Military Action: Performance Over Strategy

The Christmas Day timing, the inflammatory social media posts, the celebration of “dead Terrorists”—all reveal military action instrumentalized for political messaging rather than strategic effectiveness.

Trump’s Truth Social post concluded: “May God Bless our Military, and MERRY CHRISTMAS to all, including the dead Terrorists, of which there will be many more if their slaughter of Christians continues.”

Wishing Merry Christmas to dead terrorists represents a particular brand of performative aggression—simultaneously celebrating the holiday while reveling in violence against enemies defined primarily by their religious targeting of Christians.

Defense Secretary Hegseth amplified the theatrical framing, posting “Merry Christmas!” after warning “More to come…” The rebranding of the Department of Defense as the “Department of War” adds another layer of aggressive symbolism.

Republican politicians joined the performance. UN Ambassador Mike Waltz posted: “Merry Christmas to the Christians in Nigeria and around the world who know tonight that the President of the United States will fight for them.”

Sen. Tom Cotton praised strikes against “bloodthirsty ISIS savages.” Rep. Riley Moore, whom Trump had specifically asked to examine additional measures against Nigeria, declared: “Do not test President Trump’s resolve in this matter. Tonight’s strike in coordination with the Nigerian government is just the first step.”

The theatrical approach serves multiple political purposes:

First, it demonstrates responsiveness to evangelical concerns about Christian persecution, reinforcing Trump’s image as a defender of religious freedom (for Christians specifically).

Second, it projects strength and decisiveness—qualities Trump consistently emphasizes as central to his leadership identity.

Third, it creates news cycles dominated by military action and presidential decisiveness rather than policy complexities or strategic questions.

Fourth, it allows Trump to claim credit for “protecting Christians” while conducting operations that military officials privately acknowledge will have limited long-term impact.

The gap between theatrical performance and strategic effectiveness becomes explicit in the military’s own assessment. General Dagvin Anderson, commander of U.S. Africa Command, issued a carefully worded statement: “U.S. Africa Command is working with our Nigerian and regional partners to increase counter terrorism cooperation efforts related to ongoing violence and threats against innocent lives.”

Note what’s missing: any claim that these strikes will meaningfully reduce the violence or alter the strategic situation. The emphasis is on “cooperation efforts” and “working with partners”—diplomatic language that acknowledges the limited impact of tactical military strikes against entrenched insurgencies.

According to CNN, security analysts suggested the actual target may have been Lakurawa, a lesser-known group prominent in northwestern states that Nigeria declared a terrorist organization in January. If true, this represents another disconnect between Trump’s ISIS framing and the actual security dynamics

The Forced Partnership: Sovereignty Under Coercion

Nigeria’s cooperation with the Christmas Day strikes tells its own story about how Trump’s coercive diplomacy transforms foreign relationships.

Nigerian officials initially rejected allegations of Christian genocide, correctly noting that armed groups kill Muslims and Christians across sectarian lines. This measured assessment disappeared after Trump’s November threat of unilateral military intervention.

Foreign Minister Tuggar, even while describing the extensive coordination with U.S. officials, made clear Nigeria’s position. He told CNN: “We’re not going to dwell or pour over forensically on what has been said – or what hasn’t been said,” stressing that Nigeria’s focus is “to fight against terrorism, to stop the terrorists from killing innocent Nigerians, be (they) Muslim, Christian, atheist, whatever religion.”

The Nigerian military’s statement revealed additional details. Major General Samaila Uba, director of defence information, said Nigeria’s armed forces “in conjunction with” the U.S. carried out strikes based on “credible intelligence and careful operational planning.” He emphasized the operation targeted “identified foreign ISIS-linked elements” and underscored “the resolve of the Federal Government of Nigeria, working with strategic partners, to confront transnational terrorism.”

The language throughout Nigerian statements attempts to maintain dignity and agency while acknowledging American military action on Nigerian soil. References to “structured security cooperation” and “strategic coordination” obscure the coercive dynamic that produced the cooperation.

This accommodation pattern extends across Trump’s foreign policy approach. Mexico accommodated on immigration enforcement after tariff threats. Central American countries accepted “Remain in Mexico” policies under threat of aid cutoffs. China made agricultural purchases after trade war threats. NATO allies increased defense spending after Trump questioned alliance commitments.

In each case, Trump defenders cite the accommodation as evidence his coercive approach works. Critics note that forcing cooperation through threats degrades relationships, creates resentment, and undermines sustainable partnership.

For Nigeria, the long-term implications extend beyond a single strike operation. Trump established precedent that American presidents can threaten military intervention in sovereign nations to address domestic political pressures, then claim resulting forced cooperation as diplomatic success.

This approach treats foreign governments as props in domestic political theater rather than partners in addressing shared security challenges. Nigerian officials provided intelligence, approved strikes, issued supportive statements—all while their own assessments of the security situation were publicly dismissed in favor of Trump’s Christian persecution narrative.

The Syria-Nigeria Pattern: When Tactics Replace Strategy

The rapid succession of strikes—Syria followed by Nigeria within a week—reveals how military action becomes instrumentalized for domestic political messaging rather than strategic counterterrorism operations.

In Syria, ISIS militants killed two Army soldiers and a civilian interpreter in early December. Trump’s response was swift: dozens of airstrikes across ISIS positions, demonstrating overwhelming force in retaliation for American deaths.

The military logic was straightforward: American service members were killed, therefore ISIS targets must be struck. The political logic was equally clear: Trump could demonstrate strength and decisiveness while honoring fallen soldiers.

But the strategic logic was absent. ISIS has been operating in Syria since 2013. American forces have been conducting operations against ISIS in Syria and Iraq since 2014. Periodic strikes in response to attacks haven’t eliminated the group or meaningfully altered its capacity to conduct operations.

Within a week, Trump ordered strikes in Nigeria—this time not in response to American casualties but in response to evangelical advocacy groups and Republican politicians demanding action to protect Nigerian Christians.

The pattern across both operations reveals a consistent approach:

Reactive rather than strategic: Strikes come in response to specific incidents (Syria) or political pressure (Nigeria) rather than as part of comprehensive counterterrorism strategy

Theatrical rather than effective: Operations are announced via inflammatory social media posts emphasizing strength and decisiveness rather than strategic objectives

Selective rather than consistent: Christian victims in Nigeria warrant Tomahawk missiles; Muslim victims in the same country warrant no mention in presidential statements

Political rather than military: The primary audience isn’t ISIS militants but American voters, particularly evangelical Christians who want to see their priorities reflected in foreign policy

Military officials understand these dynamics. When Africa Command drew up targeting options for Nigeria, they simultaneously assessed that strikes would likely have limited long-term impact given the entrenched nature of the conflict.

Yet the operations proceeded because their purpose wasn’t primarily military—it was political. Trump needed visible action that demonstrated responsiveness to evangelical concerns and projected strength against terrorist threats.

AFRICOM’s initial assessment concluded that “multiple” ISIS terrorists were killed in the strikes. But what was accomplished strategically? ISIS affiliates still operate across Nigeria. The group’s ability to conduct attacks remains intact. The broader insurgency continues killing thousands annually.

Tomahawk cruise missiles cost approximately $2 million each. The Christmas Day operation expended more than a dozen against two camps in northwest Nigeria. The militants who were killed will be replaced. The camps that were destroyed will be relocated. The violence that claimed 12,000 lives in 2025 will continue in 2026.

Effective counterterrorism requires intelligence networks, cooperation with local security forces, economic development, governance reforms, regional coordination, and long-term commitment measured in years or decades. Tomahawk strikes provide none of these elements.

Yet Trump’s approach treats each strike as decisive action rather than tactical operation within comprehensive strategy. His social media posts celebrate dead terrorists without acknowledging the new recruits already replacing them.

This represents counterterrorism as theater: visible operations generating headlines and political capital while underlying strategic challenges remain unaddressed.

Conclusion: The Price of Political Theater

The Christmas Day strikes in Nigeria reveal fundamental patterns in Trump’s approach to military intervention and foreign policy that extend across his entire second term.

Military action serves political messaging more than strategic objectives. The timing, framing, and public rollout of strikes prioritize domestic political impact over operational effectiveness or long-term strategy.

Evangelical political priorities drive foreign policy decisions. Trump’s emphasis on protecting Christians reflects responsiveness to key political coalition rather than assessment of global security threats or humanitarian crises affecting all religious communities.

Coercive diplomacy forces accommodation from sovereign nations. Threats of unilateral intervention compel cooperation that can then be presented as willing partnership, disguising the coercion that produced it and the costs imposed on foreign governments navigating American pressure.

Theatrical displays replace comprehensive strategy. Tomahawk strikes generate headlines and social media content while military officials privately acknowledge limited long-term impact on entrenched conflicts.

Selective outrage distorts complex realities. Framing Nigerian violence as Christian persecution ignores Muslim victims, overlooks sectarian dynamics, and instrumentalizes religious identity for political purposes.

For Nigerian officials forced to accommodate these dynamics, the calculus becomes managing American pressure rather than partnering on shared interests. They provided intelligence, approved strikes, issued supportive statements—all while maintaining that the violence affects all communities and has complex causes beyond religious targeting.

Foreign Minister Tuggar’s repeated emphasis that the strikes target “terrorists” regardless of religion, his reference to Muslim majorities in the Sahel region also facing violence, his insistence that Nigeria’s fight “has nothing to do with a particular religion”—all reveal a government maintaining its own narrative even as it accommodates American demands framed entirely differently.

The Christmas Day strikes will fade from news cycles quickly. ISIS affiliates will replace killed fighters and relocate destroyed camps. Armed groups will continue terrorizing communities across Nigeria. Similar numbers of Nigerians—Muslim and Christian alike—will likely die from political violence in 2026 as died in 2025.

But Trump achieved his political objectives: headlines celebrating decisive action on Christmas Day, evangelical supporters seeing their priorities reflected in presidential orders, social media posts projecting strength against terrorism, and forced cooperation from the Nigerian government presented as diplomatic partnership.

The Tomahawks became Christmas cards—delivered with maximum symbolic impact to a specific political audience, celebrating American military might while changing little on the ground, advancing presidential political objectives while leaving underlying conflicts unresolved.

As Defense Secretary Hegseth promised: “More to come…”

The pattern established through these strikes suggests he’s right—more theatrical military operations serving political messaging rather than strategic objectives, more accommodation forced from sovereign nations facing American coercion, more evangelical priorities driving foreign policy decisions, more complex conflicts reduced to simplistic narratives.

This is how military force becomes instrumentalized for domestic political theater—and why military officials privately express skepticism even as they execute the orders they receive.

ADDENDUM: December 27, 2025

New reporting from The New York Times confirms and extends the analysis above, revealing the strikes may have been even more theatrical—and less strategic—than initially apparent.

The Christmas Day strikes hit Sokoto State, which is “populated overwhelmingly by Muslims, who bear the brunt of terrorist attacks there,” according to NYT reporting. Bishop Matthew Hassan Kukah of Sokoto—the local Catholic bishop whose flock Trump claimed to be protecting—stated recently that the area does “not have a problem with persecution” of Christians.

Even more damaging: one target hit was “the outskirts of Jabo, a town in Sokoto that analysts said was not known to harbor any terrorist or bandit groups.” A local resident described hearing the strike in the middle of the night, finding pieces of ordnance on a farm where “a farmer’s hut had been set on fire, but nobody was hurt.” He added that “he did not know of any terrorist camps in the area.”

Analysts remain divided over whether the Lakurawa group operating in Sokoto even has ties to ISIS. “Evidence of those links is inconclusive, as the identity of the Lakurawa group remains very murky,” the Times reports. The terrorist group in Nigeria with the best documented ISIS links “is in northeastern Nigeria, on the other side of the country from Sokoto State.”

During the critical 19-minute phone call with Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Nigerian Foreign Minister Tuggar “emphasized that communications about the strikes should not ‘get bogged down on the issue of religion.’” Yet Trump’s entire public framing centered exclusively on Christian persecution—precisely what Nigeria’s foreign minister explicitly asked the U.S. to avoid.

Colin P. Clarke of the Soufan Center captured the fundamental question: “Is the attack against ISIS in Nigeria connected to a broader counterterrorism campaign? Or are these strikes intended to assuage Christians in the United States that form part of the president’s base?”

The evidence increasingly points to the latter: Tomahawk missiles expended against uncertain targets in Muslim-majority region where local Catholic bishop says Christians aren’t persecuted, possibly hitting a town with no known terrorist presence, all while Nigerian officials pleaded to keep religion out of it—but Trump’s evangelical base got their Christmas Day message of protecting Christians.

Military officials’ private skepticism about “long-term impact” appears increasingly validated. If strikes hit wrong targets in wrong part of country against groups without clear ISIS ties, the gap between political theater and strategic effectiveness becomes chasm.

These developments validate the core thesis of this analysis: the Christmas Day strikes served domestic political messaging rather than counterterrorism strategy, forcing Nigerian accommodation to American political needs while achieving minimal strategic impact against militant groups actually threatening Nigerian communities.
