The Machine That Never Stops

Conclusion: Six Decades, One Reflex, and the Architecture of American Authoritarianism

April 1, 2026

Roy Cohn and a young Donald Trump, circa 1980s. Cohn gave Trump three rules: attack, admit nothing, claim victory. He gave him something more dangerous — a worldview. The machine that runs America in 2026 was assembled in this relationship. Source: Getty Images / Bettmann Archive

By CanadianSnowmanSays

I began this series with a congressman from Colorado who voted his conscience and lost his endorsement before the day was over.

I end it with a question that has been building through every volume, every example, every arc of destruction and submission documented across these pages: What kind of country does this produce?

Not the country as it was. The country as it is becoming.

What We’ve Documented

Across six volumes and an introduction, this series has traced a single behavioral pattern from its origins to its current expression as governing philosophy.

In Volume 1, we went back to the beginning — to Fred Trump’s Senate testimony in 1954, to the 1973 DOJ housing discrimination suit, to the moment a twenty-seven-year-old developer walked into Le Club and met a disbarred McCarthy lawyer who would reshape his understanding of power. Roy Cohn gave Donald Trump three rules: attack, admit nothing, claim victory. He gave him something more dangerous than rules — a worldview. The world is hostile. Everyone is either a tool or an adversary. Loyalty is the only currency. Any challenge, no matter how legitimate, must be met with disproportionate aggression. Trump absorbed these lessons so completely that fifty years later, they operate as reflex rather than strategy.

In Volume 2, we tracked the Cohn playbook through three decades of escalation — from stiffing contractors in Atlantic City to suing journalists who wrote unflattering stories, from the birther campaign born of personal humiliation at the 2011 White House Correspondents’ Dinner to the 2016 primary destruction tour that eliminated every Republican who stood in Trump’s way. The weapons evolved. The instinct didn’t.

In Volume 3, we examined how the presidential pardon — historically an instrument of mercy — was converted into a loyalty test. Roger Stone lied to Congress and was pardoned. Paul Manafort kept quiet under enormous pressure and was pardoned. Michael Flynn stayed loyal and was pardoned. Michael Cohen cooperated with prosecutors and testified truthfully and was left to rot. My nine-part “Clemency Chronicles” documented this across four administrations, showing how exceptional Trump’s weaponization of clemency truly is. The Retribution Report positioned it as one tool in a much larger arsenal.

In Volume 4, we documented the Republican purge — the systematic destruction of anyone in Trump’s own party who challenged him. The seventeen members of Congress who voted to impeach or convict after January 6th: roughly ninety percent eliminated from political life. Liz Cheney, destroyed by 37 points. Adam Kinzinger, forced into retirement. Mitt Romney, retiring. And then the story that defines this entire series: Marjorie Taylor Greene — Trump’s fiercest defender for five years — branded “Marjorie Traitor Greene,” death threats targeting her son, driven from Congress because she pushed to release the Epstein files. Beside her, Thomas Massie — still fighting, still facing a dedicated super PAC, still posting that he is “not suicidal” and that the brakes on his car are in good shape. And the submitters — Rubio, Graham, Cruz, Vance — rewarded precisely because the humiliation of their reversal proved the submission was total.

In Volume 5, we documented the most dangerous phase: retribution wielded through the full power of the federal government. 250,000 federal employees terminated. 7,800 research grants cancelled across 600 universities. A federal judge ruling that mass layoffs were conducted “for the purpose of political retribution.” DOGE as the instrument. Schedule F as the mechanism. States punished. Media arrested. The cabinet operating — as I documented in my “Enforcers” series — as independent enforcement arms, each internalizing the retribution instinct and applying it to their own domains. Pete Hegseth purging generals. RFK Jr. firing the CDC director. Tulsi Gabbard revoking security clearances. Marco Rubio — who once called Trump a “con artist” — gutting 2,000 State Department employees.

Volodymyr Zelensky in the Oval Office, February 28, 2025 — the moment the retribution architecture went global. He maintained his position. He was expelled. He posted one line afterward: "Thank you, America." Source: AP Photo / Evan Vucci (standard White House pool)

In Volume 6, we traced the pattern to the international stage, where nations face the same three paths as individuals. Saudi Arabia and Hungary performing flattery and receiving protection. Zelensky humiliated on camera in the Oval Office for maintaining his dignity. Denmark forced to respond to absurd territorial claims. Canada subjected to tariffs and annexation rhetoric. The retribution playbook applied to the entire map — with the same binary psychology, the same escalation ladder, and stakes measured not in careers but in lives, jobs, and the stability of the international order.

Seven essays. One pattern. Fifty-three years.

The Architecture, Complete

What emerges from this documentation is not a collection of separate stories — an angry tweet here, a pulled endorsement there, a tariff, a prosecution, a firing. It is a single architecture of control, designed and refined over six decades, operating through every available tool, at every level of power, against every category of target.

The architecture has five components.

The Psychology. The binary worldview documented in my “World According to Trump” series. Every person, institution, and nation sorted into exactly two categories: loyal or disloyal. No middle ground. No “loyal but disagrees on this issue.” The sorting is instantaneous and total. Once you’re in the disloyal column, the retribution machinery activates.

The Trigger. Any act that Trump perceives as personal betrayal. The severity of retribution doesn’t correlate with the significance of the act — it correlates with how personally Trump takes it. Jeff Hurd voted against tariffs and lost an endorsement. Greene pushed the Epstein files — which implicate people Trump wants to protect — and was destroyed. The trigger is always personal.

The Escalation. The response is always disproportionate to the provocation. A congressman votes his conscience — endorsement pulled, primary opponent backed, public threat issued, all within hours. A president maintains his position — expelled from the Oval Office on camera, military aid suspended, intelligence sharing cut off. The disproportionality is the point. It’s Cohn’s $100 million countersuit against the DOJ for a housing discrimination case they clearly had evidence for. The goal is not to win the specific battle — it’s to make the cost of opposition so catastrophic that the next person doesn’t try.

The Tools. They scale with power. As a private citizen: litigation, media manipulation, personal intimidation. As a candidate: nicknames, rally attacks, primary challenges. As a first-term president: pardons, firings, Twitter. As a second-term president with a compliant Congress and packed courts: DOJ prosecutions, federal employee purges, university funding cuts, tariffs, military aid suspension, territorial claims. The instinct remains constant. The weapons grow.

The Audience. Every act of retribution has two targets — the specific victim and everyone watching. When Trump destroys Liz Cheney, the message isn’t just for Cheney. It’s for every Republican member of Congress considering a vote against him. When Trump humiliates Zelensky, the message isn’t just for Ukraine. It’s for every head of state calculating how to manage the relationship. The architecture of retribution is, fundamentally, an architecture of deterrence. It works not because Trump punishes every opponent — he can’t — but because he punishes enough of them, visibly enough, to make the calculation clear for everyone else.

The Three Paths — Revisited

Throughout this series, I’ve returned to the Three Paths framework because it captures the essential choice that Trump’s retribution architecture imposes on everyone in its orbit.

Path One: Never Oppose. Total alignment, maintained absolutely, on every issue, forever. This path works — until it doesn’t. Marjorie Taylor Greene followed it for five years and was destroyed the moment she deviated on a single issue. The lesson: perpetual submission is the price of survival, and even perpetual submission offers no guarantee.

Path Two: Oppose, Then Submit. The path of Rubio, Graham, Cruz, Vance — and at the international level, any nation that initially resists and then capitulates. The more dramatic the original opposition and the more humiliating the reversal, the more valuable the submission. This path leads to reward, but at the cost of everything you once claimed to believe. Vance went from “America’s Hitler” to Vice President. The hypocrisy is the proof of loyalty.

Path Three: Oppose and Stay Principled. The path of Cheney, Kinzinger, Massie, Merkel — and the path that leads to political destruction or sustained siege. Roughly ninety percent of the Republicans who voted to impeach or convict Trump after January 6th are gone from political life. The survival rate for principled opposition is, by design, catastrophically low.

The framework is not complex. It is not subtle. It is, in fact, the simplest possible political architecture: obey or be destroyed. What makes it extraordinary is not its sophistication but its comprehensiveness — the fact that it applies identically to a congressman from Colorado, a five-year loyalist from Georgia, a constitutional libertarian from Kentucky, a university in Massachusetts, a scientist at NIH, a journalist covering a protest, a foreign ally fighting for its survival, and a neighboring country that happens to share the world’s longest undefended border with the United States.

One framework. Every target. No exceptions.

What Kind of Country?

I said at the beginning of this conclusion that the series ends with a question. Here it is.

A system in which political opposition carries career-ending consequences is not a healthy democracy having a rough patch. It is something else.

I have been careful throughout these series — the Accountability Project, the Enemies List, the Enforcers, the Clemency Chronicles, and now the Retribution Report — to document rather than editorialize. To show the pattern through evidence rather than assertion. To let readers draw their own conclusions from the accumulation of facts.

But documentation demands honesty, and honesty demands this observation: when a federal judge rules that mass government layoffs were conducted “for the purpose of political retribution” — those are not my words, those are a federal judge’s words — the system being described has a name. When a president promises “retribution” as a campaign platform and then delivers it systematically across every branch of government, every institution, every level of society, and every nation on earth, the pattern has precedents — and those precedents are not found in the history of democratic governance.

And in case the documentation itself leaves any ambiguity, the president has provided his own summary. On January 13, 2026, he posted to Truth Social: "FEAR NOT, GREAT PEOPLE OF MINNESOTA, THE DAY OF RECKONING & RETRIBUTION IS COMING!" No analyst named this pattern. He did.

In my “Enforcers” series, I used the language of organized crime — the don, the lieutenants, the contract, omertà — as a satirical lens to make the machinery visible. Some readers found the metaphor clarifying. Others found it too generous. Because organized crime families, for all their brutality, don’t typically fire 250,000 people from their jobs, strip universities of research funding, or threaten to annex allied nations.

The retribution architecture documented in this series has exceeded the mob metaphor. It has exceeded political hardball. It has exceeded anything in the modern American political experience.

What it has not exceeded — yet — is the capacity of democratic institutions to resist. Courts have struck down tariffs. Judges have ruled layoffs unlawful. Thomas Massie is still fighting. Universities are filing lawsuits. Journalists who were arrested have been released. Allied nations are building alternative security frameworks. The architecture of retribution is powerful. It is not yet total.

The question is whether it will become total. Whether the deterrence works so thoroughly — the examples so devastating, the costs so visible — that opposition simply stops. That every congressman votes the party line. That every scientist self-censors. That every journalist calculates the risk before publishing. That every nation performs submission. That the machine wins not by destroying every opponent, but by making opposition itself unthinkable.

That is the question this series raises. I don’t have the answer. I don’t think anyone does yet.

A Note on This Project

This series sits at the center of what has become, over the past year, a body of investigative work that I believe represents one of the most comprehensive documentations of Trump’s authoritarian architecture available in long-form journalism. Seven hundred plus essays across more than fifty series, written from Toronto by a retired businessman who discovered in his seventies that investigative journalism is the most fulfilling work of his life.

I write with full transparency about my perspective. I am Canadian. I am watching from across the border. I have family diplomatic connections — my great uncle Hugh Keenleyside, worked alongside Lester Pearson and served as a Churchill-Roosevelt intermediary during World War II — that give me a particular appreciation for the institutions of international cooperation that Trump’s retribution architecture threatens. I have a stake in this, as every Canadian does, as every citizen of every democracy does.

But I write primarily because documentation matters. Because patterns only become visible when someone takes the time to connect the dots across decades, across categories, across borders. Because Roy Cohn and Fred Trump and the 1973 DOJ countersuit only illuminate the 2026 Oval Office ambush of Zelensky if someone draws the line between them. Because Marjorie Taylor Greene’s story only achieves its full power when placed beside Liz Cheney’s and Marco Rubio’s and Michael Cohen’s and Jeff Hurd’s and every other story in this series.

The Retribution Report is, in the end, one long argument made through evidence: that what we are witnessing is not new, not random, and not going to stop on its own.

The Machine Continues

As I publish this conclusion, the retribution machine is still running. Thomas Massie’s primary is months away, and the super PAC aligned against him is spending millions. Universities are still losing research funding. Federal employees are still being terminated. The Enemies List, documented in my parallel series, continues to grow. International relationships continue to be subordinated to the binary loyalty test.

There will be more volumes of this series. There will have to be. Because the defining feature of Trump’s retribution architecture — the thing Roy Cohn understood before anyone else, the thing that makes this system fundamentally different from normal political hardball — is that it never stops.

Normal political conflicts end. Elections are held. Opponents concede. Grievances fade. Policy disagreements are resolved or accepted. The system moves on.

Trump’s retribution doesn’t move on. The slight is never forgotten. The score is never settled. Liz Cheney voted to impeach in January 2021 and is still being targeted in 2026. Michael Cohen cooperated with prosecutors in 2018 and is still paying the price. Roy Cohn taught that you never let up, never forgive, never declare victory and go home — because the act of retribution itself is the victory. It is not a means to an end. It is the end.

A machine designed never to stop will not stop on its own. It stops only when something outside the machine — a court, a vote, a movement, an institution, a refusal to submit — proves stronger than the retribution instinct.

Whether that happens is not a question I can answer from Toronto.

It is a question that Americans — and increasingly, the rest of the world — will have to answer for themselves.
