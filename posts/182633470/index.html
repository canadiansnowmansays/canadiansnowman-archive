The Prince of Peace vs. The Tomahawk President

Competing Visions of Moral Leadership on Christmas Day 2025

December 26, 2025

Introduction: Two Americans, Two Christmas Messages

On Christmas Day 2025, two Americans offered competing visions of leadership to a watching world.

From the Vatican, Pope Leo XIV—the first American-born pope in history—delivered his first Christmas “Urbi et Orbi” blessing just eight months after his election. He urged “the parties involved” in Ukraine, “with the support and commitment of the international community,” to find “the courage to engage in sincere, direct and respectful dialogue.”

From Mar-a-Lago, President Trump posted on Truth Social: “The United States launched a powerful and deadly strike against ISIS Terrorist Scum in Northwest Nigeria... May God Bless our Military, and MERRY CHRISTMAS to all, including the dead Terrorists, of which there will be many more if their slaughter of Christians continues.”

Pope Leo XIV proclaimed: “Today, true peace has come down to us from heaven.” He called for “justice, peace and stability for Lebanon, Palestine, Israel and Syria,” asking that “the clamor of weapons cease.”

Trump launched Tomahawk missiles while celebrating “numerous perfect strikes, as only the United States is capable of doing.”

The contrast between two Americans on Christmas Day illuminates fundamentally different approaches to power, faith, and moral authority. One American calls for courage to pursue dialogue and reconciliation. The other celebrates military strikes and threatens more violence. One invokes Christ as “our peace.” The other positions himself as warrior-protector of Christians willing to launch missiles on Christianity’s holiest day.

These competing Christmas messages from two Americans reveal deeper questions about the relationship between power and morality, between military might and spiritual leadership, between theatrical displays of strength and substantive pursuit of peace.

Pope Leo XIV’s Message: Dialogue Over Division

Pope Leo XIV’s first Christmas message as pontiff centered on peace as both divine gift and human responsibility—a theme carrying particular weight coming from an American pope navigating global conflicts while his home country pursues military solutions.

Speaking to an estimated 26,000 people gathered in St. Peter’s Square—many standing in rain that had fallen during morning Mass—Leo delivered his “Urbi et Orbi” (to the city and the world) blessing with a message emphasizing dialogue over force.

“There will be peace when our monologues are interrupted and, enriched by listening, we fall to our knees before the humanity of the other,” Leo preached in his Christmas homily.

The specific call regarding Ukraine carried particular weight given American-led negotiations and Trump’s promised quick resolution: Leo prayed “in a particular way for the tormented people of Ukraine,” asking that “the clamor of weapons cease” and that the parties involved find “the courage to engage in sincere, direct and respectful dialogue.”

Note the emphasis: courage to engage in dialogue. Not courage to launch strikes. Not strength to impose terms. The transformative step of kneeling before the humanity of others rather than dominating them through force.

Leo’s Christmas message invoked his papal namesake, Saint Leo the Great: “The Lord’s birth is the birth of peace.” He reflected on the Nativity story’s meaning: “Jesus was born in a stable because there was no room for him in the inn.” The Son of God, “through whom all things were created,” was not welcomed, and “out of love” accepted “poverty and rejection, identifying himself with those who are discarded and excluded.”

This theological framework connected directly to geopolitical reality. Leo cited at length from “Wildpeace,” a poem by Israeli poet Yehuda Amichai, contrasting “the peace of a ceasefire” with deeper peace that arrives unexpectedly “like wildflowers” after exhaustion and conflict.

The universality of Leo’s concern mattered profoundly. His message addressed suffering regardless of victims’ nationality or religion:

“In becoming man, Jesus took upon himself our fragility, identifying with each one of us: with those who have nothing left and have lost everything, like the inhabitants of Gaza; with those who are prey to hunger and poverty, like the Yemeni people; with those who are fleeing their homeland to seek a future elsewhere, like the many refugees and migrants who cross the Mediterranean or traverse the American continent.”

Leo called for “justice, peace and stability for Lebanon, Palestine, Israel and Syria.” He remembered “the victims of all current wars in the world, especially those that are forgotten,” naming Sudan, South Sudan, Mali, Burkina Faso, and the Democratic Republic of Congo.

He prayed for those who have lost jobs, are seeking work (especially young people), underpaid workers, and prisoners. He called for dialogue to address challenges in Latin America, reconciliation in Myanmar, and restoration of friendship between Thailand and Cambodia.

The message was comprehensive moral witness: acknowledge suffering wherever it occurs, resist violence in all its forms, pursue dialogue over division, recognize humanity in all people regardless of religion or nationality.

Leo revived the tradition abandoned by his predecessor Pope Francis of offering Christmas greetings in multiple languages. Speaking in Italian, French, English, German, Spanish, Portuguese, Polish, Arabic, Chinese and Latin, the American pope told the world: “Merry Christmas! May the peace of Christ reign in your hearts and in your families.”

The multilingual gesture—receiving especially warm cheers when he spoke in his native English and Spanish (the language of his adopted Peru where he served as missionary and archbishop)—symbolized the universal reach of the peace message transcending national boundaries.

Trump’s Counter-Message: Force Over Dialogue

Trump’s Christmas message represented the opposite approach: celebration of military might, promise of more violence, exclusive focus on Christian victims, and zero interest in the dialogue Leo advocated.

“I have previously warned these Terrorists that if they did not stop the slaughtering of Christians, there would be hell to pay, and tonight, there was,” Trump wrote. “The Department of War executed numerous perfect strikes, as only the United States is capable of doing.”

The terminology alone reveals the orientation: “Department of War” rather than Department of Defense. “Terrorist Scum” rather than militants. “Perfect strikes” celebrating tactical precision over strategic wisdom or moral consideration.

“Under my leadership, our Country will not allow Radical Islamic Terrorism to prosper,” Trump continued. “May God Bless our Military, and MERRY CHRISTMAS to all, including the dead Terrorists, of which there will be many more if their slaughter of Christians continues.”

The juxtaposition is jarring: “Merry Christmas” to dead terrorists. Blessing the military while threatening more killings. Celebrating Christianity’s holiest day—the birth of the Prince of Peace—with promises of escalating violence.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth amplified the message: “The President was clear last month: the killing of innocent Christians in Nigeria (and elsewhere) must end. The @DeptofWar is always ready, so ISIS found out tonight — on Christmas. More to come… Grateful for Nigerian government support & cooperation. Merry Christmas!”

The “more to come” threat transforms Christmas from celebration of peace to promise of escalating violence. The sign-off “Merry Christmas!” after threatening more strikes creates cognitive dissonance—unless the audience understands Christmas as opportunity to demonstrate strength against enemies of Christians.

Republican politicians joined the chorus. UN Ambassador Mike Waltz posted: “Merry Christmas to the Christians in Nigeria and around the world who know tonight that the President of the United States will fight for them.”

The message to evangelical Americans was explicit: your president militarily defends Christians on Christmas Day. Not through dialogue Leo advocated. Not through diplomatic engagement. Through Tomahawk missiles launched from warships in the Gulf of Guinea.

Two Americans, Two Visions

The Christmas Day contrast between Pope Leo XIV and President Trump carries particular significance precisely because both are Americans offering competing visions of how American leadership engages with a troubled world.

Leo, elected just eight months earlier as the 267th pope and first American to hold the position, represents moral authority grounded in consistency with articulated universal values. His call for dialogue in Ukraine, his concern for suffering in Gaza and Yemen, his remembrance of forgotten conflicts in Africa—all reflect witness to peace and dignity transcending national interest.

The American pope can call for universal peace and concern for all suffering people because he claims no political power and owes allegiance to no nation. This independence provides moral authority that political leaders cannot match when they subordinate moral claims to political necessity.

Trump represents power grounded in selective application serving domestic political coalitions. His Christmas Day strikes targeted ISIS camps in Nigeria framed entirely around protecting Christians, with explicit indifference to Muslim victims of the same violence.

The Christmas Eve mosque bombing killed at least 12 Muslims during evening prayers. Trump’s Christmas Day post made no mention of these deaths. Nigerian Foreign Minister Tuggar noted that in the Sahel region where terrorism flourishes, “the majority are Muslims. They’re not Christians.” This reality disappears in Trump’s narrative.

Leo’s message explicitly recognized this universal suffering: his reference to “those who are prey to hunger and poverty, like the Yemeni people” and “those who have nothing left and have lost everything, like the inhabitants of Gaza” refused to distinguish between deserving and undeserving victims based on religion.

The competing visions extend to fundamental questions about peace and dialogue. Leo called for Ukraine and Russia to find courage for “sincere, direct and respectful dialogue.” His Christmas homily emphasized: “There will be peace when our monologues are interrupted and, enriched by listening, we fall to our knees before the humanity of the other.”

Trump’s approach has no place for such dialogue. His November threat to Nigeria promised to go in “guns-a-blazing.” His Christmas post celebrated “perfect strikes” and promised “there will be many more” killings if violence continues.

The papal vision recognizes that lasting peace requires courage to engage adversaries, wisdom to address root causes of conflict, and commitment to justice for all affected populations. Trump’s vision treats military force as solution: threaten intervention, conduct strikes, celebrate dead enemies, promise more violence if attacks continue.

One approach pursues transformation through dialogue and reconciliation. The other pursues compliance through coercion and violence.

The Instrumentalization of Faith

Trump’s Christmas Day strikes reveal how religious identity becomes instrumentalized for political purposes in ways that contradict the very faith being invoked—a contrast made sharper by Leo’s theological message.

Leo’s Christmas homily emphasized Christ’s identification with the excluded and rejected: “Out of love, he accepted poverty and rejection, identifying himself with those who are discarded and excluded.” The pope noted that already in Jesus’s birth, “we glimpse the fundamental decision that would guide the entire life of the Son of God... the decision not to leave us under the burden of sin, but to bear it Himself for us.”

This theological framework—God becoming vulnerable human, born in poverty, bringing peace to all humanity—stands in stark contrast to Trump’s Christmas celebration of military strikes and dead terrorists.

Leo restored the tradition of celebrating Christmas morning Mass publicly (abandoned for 30 years since 1995), using the liturgy to emphasize peace and dialogue. In his homily he asked: “The Word has pitched his fragile tent among us. How, then, can we not think of the tents in Gaza, exposed for weeks to rain, wind and cold; and of those of so many other refugees and displaced persons on every continent; or of the makeshift shelters of thousands of homeless people in our own cities?”

Trump’s Christmas message launched missiles, killed “Terrorist Scum,” and promised more violence. The disconnect between Christmas theology and Christmas military theater couldn’t be starker.

Yet evangelical political leaders embraced Trump’s message. They see him as defender of Christians willing to use American military might to protect believers worldwide. The theological tension between Christ’s teachings about peace, loving enemies, and turning the other cheek gets resolved in favor of Old Testament warrior God vanquishing enemies.

This represents particular strain of American evangelical political theology that synthesizes Christian identity with American nationalism and military power. In this framework, defending Christians justifies violence, American military might serves God’s purposes, and presidential willingness to launch strikes on Christmas Day demonstrates commitment to faith rather than contradiction of it.

Leo’s message offered different synthesis: Christ’s peace requires human responsibility, dialogue demands courage, and identifying with the suffering means recognizing humanity in all people regardless of religion. “Those who do not love are not saved; they are lost,” Leo preached. “And those who do not love their brother or sister whom they see, cannot love God whom they do not see.”

The Weight of American Identity

That both Leo and Trump are Americans adds profound dimension to their competing Christmas messages. The first American pope and the American president offered the world fundamentally different visions of how American leadership engages with global conflict.

Leo, born Robert Francis Prevost in Chicago’s Bronzeville neighborhood, spent decades as Augustinian missionary in Peru before becoming bishop of Chiclayo, then prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops under Pope Francis. His election in May 2025 was unexpected—Vatican insiders believed the prospect of an American pope unrealistic given the United States’ superpower status.

Yet Leo’s election reflects the global church recognizing that American leadership need not mean military dominance. An American can call for dialogue, advocate for peace, recognize suffering across all communities, and resist the instrumentalization of faith for political purposes.

Trump’s Christmas Day strikes demonstrate the opposite: American power serving domestic political coalitions, protection extending selectively based on religious identity, and Christmas becoming occasion for military theater rather than pursuit of peace.

For international observers, the contrast illuminates competing possibilities for American engagement with the world. Leo represents moral authority grounded in universal values consistently applied. Trump represents political power grounded in selective application serving particular interests.

For American evangelicals who celebrated Christmas Day strikes as defense of persecuted Christians, Trump’s approach resonates as leader willing to use American might to protect faith community. The American pope calling for dialogue and universal concern might seem weak or naive by comparison.

For observers who see 12 Muslims killed on Christmas Eve warranting equal concern as Christian victims, who understand Nigerian violence affects all communities, who believe Christmas should inspire pursuit of peace rather than celebration of military strikes—Trump’s approach represents instrumentalization of faith for political theater while Leo’s message represents authentic witness to Christmas’s meaning.

Historical Weight

Pope Leo XIV’s choice of papal name carries historical significance relevant to his Christmas message. He chose to honor Pope Leo XIII, who developed modern Catholic social teaching during the Second Industrial Revolution’s tumult—both to echo Leo XIII’s concern for workers and fairness, and as response to challenges of a new industrial revolution driven by artificial intelligence.

Leo XIII (1878-1903) issued Rerum Novarum, the groundbreaking encyclical addressing workers’ rights and economic justice. He advocated for dialogue between labor and capital, rejected both unfettered capitalism and socialism, and emphasized human dignity and the common good.

Leo XIV’s invocation of this legacy on Christmas Day 2025—calling for dialogue over force, emphasizing solidarity with the suffering regardless of religion, recognizing that peace requires addressing root causes of conflict—continues this tradition of Catholic social teaching engaging with contemporary challenges.

Trump’s Christmas Day strikes represent different tradition: American military might deployed for domestic political purposes, religious identity instrumentalized to justify violence, and complex conflicts reduced to simplistic narratives of good versus evil requiring military solutions.

The competing traditions reflect broader questions about American identity and leadership. Does American power serve universal values or particular interests? Does American leadership pursue peace through engagement and dialogue or impose compliance through military force?

Christmas Past: Historical Contrasts

The Christmas Day military strikes gain additional context when compared to historical examples of Christmas during wartime.

The famous Christmas Truce of 1914 during World War I saw British and German soldiers spontaneously cease hostilities, emerge from trenches, exchange gifts, sing carols, and play football together. The truce represented soldiers’ recognition of shared humanity transcending national conflict—a brief moment when Christmas message of peace overcame warfare’s logic.

Military commanders on both sides condemned the truce and worked to prevent its recurrence, understanding that soldiers seeing enemies as fellow humans undermines effectiveness in killing them.

During World War II, President Franklin Roosevelt’s 1944 Christmas message acknowledged the war but emphasized enduring faith: “It is not easy to say ‘Merry Christmas’ to you, my fellow Americans, in this time of destructive war. Nor can I say ‘Merry Christmas’ lightly tonight to our armed forces at their battle stations all over the world—or to our allies who fight by their side.”

Yet Roosevelt emphasized hope, sacrifice for peace, and vision of world where future Christmases wouldn’t require warfare.

President John F. Kennedy’s 1962 Christmas message, delivered during Cold War tensions, emphasized: “As we express our gratitude, we must never forget that the highest appreciation is not to utter words, but to live by them.” He called for pursuing peace as “a practical goal” requiring “practical methods.”

These historical examples share recognition that Christmas during wartime creates tension between holiday’s message and reality of combat. Leaders acknowledged this tension rather than celebrating it.

Trump’s approach inverts the formula: Christmas becomes occasion to celebrate military action, promise more violence, and position himself as warrior-protector willing to strike enemies on Christianity’s holiest day.

The historical contrast reveals how Trump’s Christmas military theater represents departure from American tradition of treating Christmas during conflict with at least rhetorical acknowledgment of the tension between Christian peace message and wartime necessity.

The Moral Cost

Leo’s Christmas message emphasized the moral responsibility accompanying claims of faith: “Without a heart freed from sin, a heart that has been forgiven, we cannot be men and women of peace or builders of peace.”

Trump’s Christmas message revealed the cost of instrumentalizing faith for political purposes: moral authority dissolves when religious language serves partisan ends rather than universal values.

Evangelical political leaders celebrating Christmas Day strikes may see Trump as defender of faith. But observers who don’t share that political-theological framework see president weaponizing Christianity’s holiest day for political theater while contradicting Christmas’s core message.

The American pope offering Christmas message of peace, dialogue, and universal concern provides alternative model of how Christian witness engages with global conflict. Leo’s first Christmas as pontiff demonstrated that American leadership can mean moral authority grounded in consistent values rather than military might serving political coalitions.

Conclusion: Two Paths Forward

The competing Christmas messages from Pope Leo XIV and President Trump—both Americans addressing the world on December 25, 2025—represent fundamentally different visions of leadership, power, and moral authority.

One path calls for courage to pursue dialogue, commitment to addressing suffering wherever it occurs regardless of victims’ religion or nationality, and recognition that lasting peace requires wisdom to address root causes rather than military strikes that kill individuals while leaving conflicts unresolved.

The other path celebrates military might, promises escalating violence, focuses exclusively on one religious community’s suffering, and treats Christmas as occasion to demonstrate strength through killing enemies.

One path derives moral authority from consistency with articulated values applied universally. The other derives political power from selective application of force serving domestic coalition’s priorities.

One path treats adversaries as humans who might be engaged through dialogue and reconciliation, kneeling “before the humanity of the other.” The other treats enemies as “scum” to be eliminated through “perfect strikes” celebrated on Christmas Day.

These competing visions raise fundamental questions about American leadership and identity:

Does American power serve universal values or particular interests? Does American military might defend the vulnerable everywhere or selectively based on religious and political calculations? Does American leadership pursue peace through diplomacy and engagement or impose compliance through coercion and violence?

Trump’s Christmas Day strikes provided clear answers: American military power serves domestic political purposes, protection extends to Christian communities prioritized by evangelical coalition, and peace comes through force rather than dialogue.

Pope Leo XIV’s alternative suggests different answers rooted in different values—answers that may lack political power in American electoral terms but claim moral authority through consistency and universality.

For evangelical Americans who celebrated Christmas Day strikes as defense of persecuted Christians, Trump’s approach resonates as leader willing to use American might to protect faith community. The American pope’s call for dialogue and universal concern might seem weak by comparison.

For observers who see 12 Muslims killed on Christmas Eve warranting equal concern as Christian victims, who understand Nigerian violence affects all communities, who believe Christmas should inspire pursuit of peace rather than celebration of military strikes—Trump’s approach represents instrumentalization of faith for political theater while Leo’s message represents authentic witness.

The choice between these visions ultimately reflects deeper questions about relationship between power and morality, between national interest and universal values, between military might and spiritual authority, between American nationalism and Christian universalism.

Pope Leo XIV closed his Christmas message by emphasizing both divine gift and human responsibility: “On this holy day, let us open our hearts to our brothers and sisters who are in need or in pain. In doing so, we open our hearts to the Child Jesus, who welcomes us with open arms and reveals his divinity to us.”

Trump closed his: “MERRY CHRISTMAS to all, including the dead Terrorists, of which there will be many more if their slaughter of Christians continues.”

One opens hearts to all suffering humanity. The other promises more killing.

One becomes pilgrim of peace through dialogue. The other becomes warrior wielding Tomahawks as Christmas cards.

The contrast between two Americans on Christmas Day 2025 illuminates not just competing approaches to foreign policy but competing visions of what American leadership and Christian witness mean in a world desperate for peace yet perpetually preparing for war.

The first American pope demonstrated that American identity need not mean military dominance, that Christian witness transcends national interest, and that moral authority comes from consistency with universal values rather than selective application of force.

The American president demonstrated how military theater serves domestic politics, how religious identity becomes instrumentalized for political purposes, and how Christmas becomes occasion for celebrating violence rather than pursuing peace.

History will judge which vision of American leadership better serves both American interests and human dignity. But on Christmas Day 2025, the contrast couldn’t have been clearer: two Americans, two messages, two fundamentally different understandings of power, faith, and the meaning of peace.

ADDENDUM: December 27, 2025

New reporting from The New York Times reveals how thoroughly Trump’s Christmas Day narrative diverged from reality on the ground—and how explicitly Nigerian officials tried to prevent the religious framing Trump imposed.

The strikes targeted Sokoto State, home to “the sultan of Sokoto, the spiritual leader of Nigeria’s Muslim population,” in a region “populated overwhelmingly by Muslims, who bear the brunt of terrorist attacks there.” This is the area Trump claimed required American intervention to protect Christians from genocide.

Most damaging to Trump’s religious framing: Bishop Matthew Hassan Kukah of Sokoto—the actual Catholic bishop whose Christian flock Trump purported to defend—stated recently that the area does “not have a problem with persecution” of Christians. The local religious leader contradicts the American president’s entire justification for military action.

During the crucial 19-minute phone call with Secretary of State Marco Rubio before the strikes, Nigerian Foreign Minister Tuggar “emphasized that communications about the strikes should not ‘get bogged down on the issue of religion.’” Nigeria’s top diplomat explicitly asked the United States to avoid religious framing. Trump ignored this request entirely, centering his entire Christmas Day message on protecting Christians and celebrating strikes against “Terrorist Scum” killing Christians.

The contrast with Pope Leo XIV’s Christmas message becomes even sharper. While the American pope called for universal concern—remembering “those who are prey to hunger and poverty, like the Yemeni people” and “those who have nothing left and have lost everything, like the inhabitants of Gaza”—the American president ignored the Muslim-majority population actually suffering the attacks in Sokoto.

One strike hit “the outskirts of Jabo, a town in Sokoto that analysts said was not known to harbor any terrorist or bandit groups.” A local resident found pieces of ordnance on a farm with a burned farmer’s hut but no casualties, adding “he did not know of any terrorist camps in the area.” Analysts note that Nigeria’s terrorist group with clearest ISIS links “is in northeastern Nigeria, on the other side of the country from Sokoto State.”

Colin P. Clarke of the Soufan Center posed the essential question: “Is the attack against ISIS in Nigeria connected to a broader counterterrorism campaign? Or are these strikes intended to assuage Christians in the United States that form part of the president’s base?”

The evidence increasingly supports the latter interpretation—and reveals the moral cost of instrumentalizing faith for political purposes. A Muslim-majority region suffering terrorist attacks gets Tomahawk missiles framed entirely as Christian protection, against the explicit wishes of Nigerian officials and despite the local Catholic bishop saying Christians there aren’t persecuted.

Pope Leo XIV’s call to recognize humanity in all suffering people—”those who do not love their brother or sister whom they see, cannot love God whom they do not see”—stands in even starker contrast to Trump’s selective concern calibrated for evangelical voters rather than actual victims of violence in Nigeria.

These revelations validate this analysis’s central argument: Trump’s Christmas Day strikes represented instrumentalization of faith for political theater, contradicting both the theological meaning of Christmas and the reality of suffering on the ground in Nigeria.
