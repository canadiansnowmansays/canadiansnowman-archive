The Intelligence Operation

Part 1: What Trump Really Fears in the Epstein Files

November 18, 2025

Source: Coffee & Tea with Lev & Dean: Breaking —Trump, Epstein Files & the Plan to Rig 2026, Lev Parnas, Dean Blundell, and Ellie Leonard, Nov 15, 2025

On Sunday night, November 16, 2025, Donald Trump boarded Air Force One in Florida having lost complete control of his own party on the Epstein files.

For months, he had fought with everything he had. He weaponized the Department of Justice. He personally called members of Congress from the Situation Room to threaten them. He ordered a pretextual investigation designed to block congressional oversight indefinitely. He called his own supporters “stupid” and “weaklings” for demanding transparency. He broke completely with Marjorie Taylor Greene—one of his most loyal attack dogs—calling her a “traitor” and withdrawing his endorsement. He warned Republicans not to fall into “the Epstein TRAP.”

None of it worked.

The discharge petition hit 218 signatures. The defections kept coming—not just the four Republicans who signed on, but projections of 50 to 100 more ready to vote yes on the House floor. Representative Thomas Massie warned his colleagues publicly: “In 2030, he’s not going to be the president. And you will have voted to protect pedophiles if you don’t vote to release these files, and the president can’t protect you then.”

Facing a veto-proof majority and the complete collapse of his obstruction strategy, Trump posted his capitulation from 30,000 feet:

“House Republicans should vote to release the Epstein files, because we have nothing to hide, and it’s time to move on from this Democrat Hoax.”

“The House Oversight Committee can have whatever they are legally entitled to. I DON’T CARE!”

The man who spent months fighting with existential desperation to block these files was now claiming he never cared about them at all.

The question isn’t why he capitulated. It’s why he fought so desperately, for so long, over what he now insists is nothing. Why weaponize the Justice Department over files he claims exonerate him? Why threaten members of Congress? Why break with his most loyal allies? Why risk fracturing his own party ten months into his second term?

The answer isn’t what we thought.

PART I: WHEN SEX SCANDAL MASKS INTELLIGENCE COMPROMISE

For eight episodes, we’ve documented Donald Trump’s increasingly desperate accommodation to block the release of Jeffrey Epstein’s files. We watched him progress through five stages of panic, weaponize the Department of Justice itself, and risk constitutional crisis to prevent congressional oversight. The pattern seemed clear: a powerful man trying to hide his connection to a sex trafficking operation, protecting himself and his elite friends from public exposure and potential prosecution.

We thought we understood the stakes. We were wrong.

The accommodation wasn’t about shame over knowing that Epstein trafficked underage girls through Mar-a-Lago. It wasn’t even primarily about protecting powerful donors whose names appear in those files. The desperation that drove Trump to weaponize the Justice Department, threaten members of Congress, and orchestrate a pretextual investigation went far beyond trying to hide embarrassing photographs or damaging associations.

Trump’s worst fear—according to sources close to the situation—isn’t the girls. It’s the documentation of how foreign intelligence services, particularly Russia, used Jeffrey Epstein’s operation to compromise American political leadership. As Lev Parnas put it bluntly on a recent podcast: “Trump’s trying to distance himself from Epstein, not because of the girls or the pedophile stuff. He’s trying to distance himself because it’s the link to the Russians.”

This reframes everything we’ve documented across eight episodes. The accommodation psychology, the bipartisan elite protection, the institutional resistance to transparency—none of it was really about sex crimes. It was about kompromat. About documented evidence of intelligence compromise. About proof that foreign powers had leverage over American leadership.

And that changes everything.

I. THE EPSTEIN-RUSSIA CONNECTION

Before we examine what Lev Parnas revealed about Trump’s worst fears, it’s essential to understand who he is—and why his testimony about intelligence operations carries weight that many observers might not initially recognize.

Lev Parnas is not an outside analyst speculating about Trump’s inner circle. He was inside it. For years, Parnas worked as Rudy Giuliani’s associate, operating at the center of Trump’s shadow foreign policy apparatus. He was physically present for meetings, phone calls, and strategic discussions that most Americans only learned about during the first Trump impeachment. When Parnas testified about the Ukraine pressure campaign—about what Trump knew, when he knew it, and how the operation actually worked—he wasn’t theorizing. He was reporting what he witnessed firsthand.

That testimony, dismissed at the time by Trump allies as lies from a disgruntled associate, has been systematically vindicated. The text messages Parnas turned over to Congress, the recordings he made, the documentary evidence he provided—all of it proved accurate. What Trump’s defenders called “conspiracy theories” in 2019 became confirmed facts by 2020. Parnas didn’t speculate. He documented.

This matters now because Parnas is making similarly explosive claims about Trump and the Epstein files—specifically, about Russian intelligence compromise and what the sealed DOJ files actually contain. MAGA media will inevitably dismiss these revelations as “conspiracy theories” from a convicted felon trying to take down Trump. That’s the predictable playbook.

But consider Parnas’s unique position to know what he claims to know:

On intelligence operations: Parnas didn’t just work on domestic political campaigns. He operated in the shadow diplomacy space where American foreign policy, Ukrainian politics, and Russian interests intersected. He worked directly with Giuliani—Trump’s personal attorney who maintained extensive contacts with Ukrainian and Russian figures. Parnas wasn’t theorizing about how intelligence operations work; he was participating in one.

On Trump’s fear patterns: Parnas watched Trump’s accommodation psychology up close during the Ukraine scandal. He saw how Trump reacted when evidence of his actions began surfacing. He witnessed the panic, the obstruction, the witness intimidation, the legal maneuvering. He knows what Trump looks like when he’s terrified of documentary evidence becoming public.

On insider sources: Parnas maintains contact with people still inside Trump’s orbit—people who have reason to cooperate with someone who’s already proven willing to go public with receipts. When Parnas claims to have sources telling him what Trump fears most in the Epstein files, he’s describing the same kind of insider network that allowed him to expose the Ukraine operation.

On foreign intelligence matters: The Ukraine affair wasn’t just a political scandal. It involved foreign intelligence services, kompromat, and attempts by both Russia and Ukraine to leverage American political figures. Parnas operated in that world. He understands how kompromat works not as academic theory but as observed practice.

Is Parnas bitter about being convicted while Trump walked free? Undoubtedly. Does he have an agenda to expose Trump? Absolutely—he’s stated it explicitly on his Substack and in his GoFundMe campaign to fund a “truth tour.” But having an agenda doesn’t make someone wrong. It makes their testimony predictable. The question isn’t whether Parnas wants to damage Trump. The question is whether what he’s saying is true.

And here’s what distinguishes Parnas from actual conspiracy theorists: He provides specific, falsifiable claims. Not vague insinuations about shadowy plots, but concrete allegations about specific documents (June 24, 2018 Epstein email referencing Churkin as past handler), specific people (Churkin as conduit to Putin before his death in 2017), specific locations (sealed DOJ files containing intelligence assessments), and specific patterns (Trump’s accommodation escalating after May 2025 Bondi briefing).

These aren’t claims designed to be unfalsifiable. They’re claims that can be proven or disproven if the files Parnas references ever become public. That’s not how conspiracy theorists operate. They traffic in claims that can never be tested. Parnas is making claims that congressional oversight, FOIA requests, or whistleblowers could verify.

Moreover, Parnas isn’t operating alone. On the November 15 “Coffee & Tea with Lev & Dean” podcast where he made these revelations, he appeared alongside host Dean Blundell and independent journalist Ellie Leonard—both of whom have been systematically reviewing the 20,000+ Epstein emails released so far. Leonard specifically confirmed details about Epstein’s intelligence-level access to multiple countries’ leadership. This isn’t one angry ex-associate making wild accusations. It’s corroborated reporting from people examining the documentary evidence.

When Parnas says Trump’s biggest fear isn’t the sex scandal but the intelligence compromise, he’s not speculating. He’s reporting what sources with direct knowledge have told him—the same kind of sources that proved accurate during Ukraine. When he says the DOJ files contain intelligence assessments connecting Epstein to foreign actors, he’s describing specific documents whose existence can be verified or refuted.

This doesn’t mean everything Parnas says is automatically true. It means his claims deserve serious examination rather than reflexive dismissal. During the Ukraine scandal, dismissing Parnas as a liar was politically convenient. It was also wrong. The documentary evidence he provided proved devastatingly accurate.

The question readers must answer: Are you willing to consider that Parnas might be right again? Or will you dismiss intelligence operation allegations the same way Trump allies dismissed Ukraine pressure campaign allegations—only to be proven wrong when the evidence eventually surfaced?

What follows is Parnas’s account of what Trump fears most in the Epstein files, corroborated where possible by independent journalists reviewing the released emails and by Parnas’s own track record of documentary evidence. Judge the claims on their specificity, their falsifiability, and their consistency with Trump’s observable behavior—not on whether they come from a friendly source.

Because the most valuable testimony often comes from people who were in the room when it happened, then decided they couldn’t stay silent anymore.

The June 24, 2018 email has now been confirmed by multiple news organizations including ABC News, the Washington Post, and Fox News. If Parnas is wrong about what the DOJ files contain, those files can eventually be declassified to show he fabricated the claim. If his sources about Trump’s fears are inventing things, the absence of corroborating evidence will become clear over time.

That’s not how conspiracy theorists operate. That’s how whistleblowers with documentary evidence operate—and why their claims, however politically convenient or inconvenient, deserve serious examination rather than reflexive dismissal based on the messenger’s motives.

The June 2018 Email and the 40-Year Operation

Vitaly Churkin had served as ambassador to the UN from 2006 until his death in 2017

On November 15, 2025, Lev Parnas appeared on the “Coffee & Tea with Lev & Dean” podcast to discuss newly released Epstein emails. What he revealed goes far beyond the estate document dump that congressional Republicans thought they could manage. Parnas disclosed information from sources with direct knowledge of what Trump fears most in those files.

The smoking gun came from the email releases now confirmed by multiple news organizations including ABC News, the Washington Post, and Politico. On June 24, 2018—more than a year after Vitaly Churkin’s death in February 2017—Jeffrey Epstein sent an email to Thørbjorn Jagland, former Norwegian Prime Minister and then-Secretary General of the Council of Europe. In it, Epstein referenced his past relationship with Churkin, Russia’s former UN Ambassador: “Churkin was great. He understood trump after our conversations. it is not complex. he must be seen to get something. its that simple.”

The email then showed Epstein positioning himself to meet with Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov’s assistant, writing: “I think you might suggest to putin, that lavrov, can get insight on talking to me. Vitaly churkin used to but he died.”

Jagland’s response was immediate: “I’ll meet Lavrovs assistant on Monday and will suggest. Thank you for a lovely evening.”

The November 15, 2025 podcast panel: Host Dean Blundell (left), former Trump insider Lev Parnas (center), and independent journalist Ellie Leonard (right)—who discovered the June 24, 2018 Epstein email referencing Churkin as his past handler and conduit to Russia.

Independent journalist Ellie Leonard, who has been systematically reviewing the 20,000+ Epstein emails, connected this exchange to intelligence officer assessments. According to her source—Jacob Carsborough, a Danish intelligence officer who served on the UN Security Council at the same time as Churkin—this email outlined “how he was Epstein’s handler and connection and conduit to Russia.” Carsborough’s assessment: “Holy shit, this stinks to high heaven.”

This wasn’t casual name-dropping. Epstein had direct access to major European leaders and was using them as intermediaries to reach Putin and Lavrov. The email documented Epstein functioning as what Leonard described as “Jeffrey Epstein’s conduit and agent, agent de faire to the Kremlin”—and showed him trying to maintain that role even after Churkin’s death by positioning himself as someone who could provide Putin insight on Trump through Lavrov.

The timing matters: this email was sent just one month before the infamous July 2018 Helsinki summit between Trump and Putin. On the day of that summit, former Treasury Secretary Larry Summers emailed Epstein asking, “Do the Russians have stuff on Trump? Today was appalling even by his standards.” Epstein replied that Trump was “totally predictable,” adding: “He thinks he has charmed his adversary. He has no idea of the symbolism. He has no idea of most things.”

Parnas connected this to the broader intelligence operation: The email demonstrated Epstein functioning as an intermediary in what Parnas characterized as “a 40-year operation” by Russian intelligence to compromise Trump. Churkin had been the handler. After Churkin died, Epstein was trying to establish new channels through Lavrov.

The implications are staggering. Jeffrey Epstein wasn’t just a financier who threw parties for powerful people. He wasn’t even just a sex trafficker who used underage girls to compromise the elite. If Parnas’s sources are accurate, Epstein operated as an intelligence asset with the kind of high-level foreign access that no legitimate private citizen could possibly obtain.

Independent journalist Ellie Leonard, who has spent months reviewing the 20,000+ Epstein emails released so far, confirmed the scale of Epstein’s intelligence-level operations: “He’s telling China what loans to take out, who to talk to, who not to listen to, how much to take out. He’s telling their Finance Minister... he’s meeting with Putin... meeting with Israel.”

The question Leonard posed is the right one: “How the hell did he get here? How does he have this Authority? Is he talking to someone higher than him, or does he literally have this much power as a singular person?”

No private financier, no matter how wealthy or connected, advises multiple countries’ finance ministers on state policy. No private citizen gets face-time with Vladimir Putin to discuss geopolitical strategy. That level of access indicates intelligence work—specifically, the kind of multi-state intelligence operation where an asset collects kompromat on powerful figures while simultaneously serving as a backchannel for foreign powers seeking to influence American policy.

This explains why Trump’s accommodation to block these files went so far beyond normal bounds. This explains why he weaponized the Department of Justice itself. This explains why he risked constitutional crisis. Because the files don’t just document sex crimes at Mar-a-Lago. They document a foreign intelligence operation that compromised him—and potentially many others across both political parties.

When Trump boarded Air Force One recently and a reporter asked about potentially pardoning Ghislaine Maxwell, he responded: “I’m looking at it.” But when the same reporter asked about releasing the Epstein files, Trump’s demeanor changed completely. “Quiet, quiet, quiet, quiet,” he said, refusing to answer.

The pattern continued throughout the weekend. On Friday night, Trump posted on Truth Social claiming House Republicans should “vote to release the Epstein files, because we have nothing to hide.” But in the same post, he revealed the actual strategy: his DOJ investigation would focus exclusively on “Democrat operatives (Bill Clinton, Reid Hoffman, Larry Summers)” while warning Republican members not to fall into “the Epstein ‘TRAP.’”

By Sunday night, facing mass defections, Trump escalated to full capitulation—claiming he didn’t care what Congress received. But the Friday investigation was already in place as the real defensive line.

The message was clear—public support for transparency, private mechanisms to control what’s actually released. Classic accommodation psychology: say one thing to manage public pressure while doing the opposite to block actual disclosure.

That fear isn’t about embarrassment. It’s about documented evidence of being controlled by a foreign power for decades. It’s about proof that the “America First” president spent his entire political career compromised by Russian intelligence. It’s about kompromat—the Russian term for compromising material used to control political figures—on an industrial scale.

According to Lev Parnas’s sources, there are two specific places where the full documentation of Epstein’s intelligence operations resides. Understanding these locations explains Trump’s specific actions over the past year—and why his panic intensified after what he learned in his May 2025 briefing from Attorney General Pam Bondi.

The briefing itself is significant. According to Parnas, Trump didn’t know the full extent of how Russia had compromised him through Epstein until Patel and Bondi opened the files and showed him. “Trump’s trying to now distance himself from Epstein, not because of the girls or the pedophile stuff,” Parnas explained. “He’s trying to distance himself from Epstein because it’s the link to the Russians in Cherkin and all of this stuff, because that’s what he found out. See, Trump did not know this stuff until... When he got into office and Patel and Bondi opened up and gave him that briefing. So truly defining the words useful idiot like he had no idea he was in work.”

Trump spent decades thinking he was a brilliant dealmaker getting favorable Russian financing. The briefing revealed he’d been an intelligence asset for 40 years.

The first location is inside the Department of Justice itself. Not the estate document dump that congressional Republicans thought they could spin. Not the flight logs that show who traveled when. The sealed DOJ files contain investigative memos, intelligence assessments, and documentary evidence connecting Epstein’s network to foreign actors, including Russia.

As Parnas wrote in his November 16 Substack article: “My sources are telling me there are two other places Trump knows the full story lives: Inside the Department of Justice – sealed files, investigative memos, and intelligence assessments connecting Epstein’s network to foreign actors, including Russia.”

These are the files that the discharge petition has now forced to a House vote—scheduled for November 18—despite Trump’s months-long obstruction campaign. These are the files that document not just who attended what party, but which foreign intelligence services were watching, recording, and collecting.

As Parnas explained on the podcast: “We’re just getting one part of the whole email structure. We’re getting Epstein’s emails from his trust. That’s the real juice.”

These files explain why Trump spent 40 years bowing to Vladimir Putin—because they contain the proof.

The second location is Ghislaine Maxwell herself. Maxwell wasn’t just Epstein’s accomplice in sex trafficking. She knows, according to Parnas’s sources, “not just who was where and when, but which foreign intelligence services were watching, recording, and collecting.”

This explains Trump’s increasingly serious consideration of pardoning Maxwell—something he has openly admitted to considering despite the political toxicity. Her two decades as Epstein’s closest associate means she knows not just who attended parties, but who was recording them, which governments were collecting leverage, and how the kompromat was distributed and deployed. Maxwell in custody, facing 20 years in prison, eventually starts looking at cooperation deals. Her knowledge of which intelligence services ran collection operations through Epstein’s network makes her potentially the most dangerous witness alive to Trump’s presidency.

This is why Trump’s accommodation crossed every constitutional boundary. Not because he’s afraid of photographs with young women. Because the DOJ files document decades of foreign intelligence compromise, and Maxwell’s testimony could confirm which foreign services ran the operations and exactly what they collected on Trump and others.

The stakes aren’t embarrassment. They’re treason.

III. WHY IT’S BIPARTISAN

The most puzzling aspect of Episodes 1-8 was the bipartisan silence. Yes, 218 members of Congress—mostly Democrats—signed the discharge petition. But Democratic leadership never made it a priority. Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer hasn’t committed to bringing a House-passed bill up for a vote. House Democratic leadership didn’t whip votes or make Epstein file transparency part of their legislative agenda when they controlled the chamber. Major Democratic donors with documented Epstein connections—including Reid Hoffman and others named in the emails—face no party pressure to address their associations. And crucially, judges appointed by both parties have sealed documents citing “national security,” suggesting the protection transcends partisan interest.

The Biden Justice Department’s four-year record on Epstein prosecutions is particularly telling. Despite inheriting extensive investigative files, despite victims and advocates pushing for accountability, despite campaign promises about restoring DOJ independence, the Biden administration prosecuted no one beyond Ghislaine Maxwell. No co-conspirators faced charges. No financial enablers were indicted. No intelligence connections were investigated. Attorney General Merrick Garland—celebrated as an institutionalist committed to law over politics—oversaw a DOJ that quietly closed the Epstein investigation with the same “nothing there” conclusion that Trump’s DOJ later echoed. This isn’t partisan protection. This is institutional consensus that certain investigations threaten too many powerful interests across party lines to be allowed to proceed.

The Epstein-Russia connection explains why. If multiple foreign intelligence services used Epstein’s operation to collect kompromat on American political, business, and cultural leaders—not just Trump, not just Republicans, but figures across the political spectrum—then elite protection becomes bipartisan by necessity.

Consider the evidence of multi-national intelligence involvement:

Israel: Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak appears extensively in the Epstein emails. Despite this documentation, The New York Times invited Barak to speak at their DealBook conference. When independent journalist Ellie Leonard immediately wrote to Andrew Ross Sorkin questioning this decision, he responded merely: “I hear what you’re saying. Thanks.” The event proceeded.

Why would the paper of record platform someone whose name appears throughout emails documenting an international sex trafficking and kompromat operation? Either because they don’t believe the emails are significant, or because powerful interests prefer normalizing Epstein’s network rather than investigating it.

Financial Infrastructure: JPMorgan Chase paid $290 million in settlements for enabling Epstein’s trafficking operation. Banks don’t pay nearly $300 million for “not knowing” what a client was doing. As Leonard noted, she is “still going through all the JPMorgan stuff”—meaning the full scope of how major financial institutions facilitated the operation remains under investigation.

Banks enabled the money flows. Foreign intelligence services collected the kompromat. And American political leaders across party lines now have incentive to ensure full transparency never happens.

The Dershowitz Smoking Gun: The emails also revealed how elite lawyers systematically silenced victims through legal and financial warfare. Independent journalist Ellie Leonard discovered the smoking gun just days before the podcast recording.

Virginia Giuffre had accused Alan Dershowitz—Epstein’s longtime attorney—of sexually abusing her when she was underage, as part of her civil lawsuit. Dershowitz has spent years using his reputation as Harvard Law Professor Emeritus to threaten anyone who questions him, insisting Giuffre later recanted. In a 2022 settlement, Giuffre indeed issued a statement saying she had been “mistaken” in identifying Dershowitz, that she was “young and traumatized,” and withdrew her accusation.

That statement never sat right with Leonard. As she explained on the podcast: “You don’t forget a face when somebody’s abusing you. And she’s never said that about anyone ever of anyone she’s ever accused.”

Then, reviewing the email releases at 1 AM, Leonard found it: An email from Alan Dershowitz to Virginia Giuffre’s lawyer, David Boies. The email contained the exact statement Giuffre would later issue—word for word. As Leonard put it: “It says, ‘this is what she has to say,’ and it’s the exact statement that she said.”

“There is the proof that she was threatened and forced to make that statement,” Leonard concluded. “And then he walked away with four million dollars.”

Parnas’s response connected the email evidence to Giuffre’s original sexual assault allegations against Dershowitz: “He’s a bad guy.” The implication was clear—the smoking gun email showed Dershowitz silencing a victim through legal and financial pressure.

This demonstrates how the elite protection network operates. Financial pressure. Legal warfare. Exhausting victims through prolonged litigation. Then paying settlements that require recantation statements—statements that were actually drafted by the defense lawyers years before the victims “voluntarily” agreed to say them.

If this is how the system protects individual lawyers accused of participation, imagine how the system protects political leaders when foreign intelligence agencies hold the leverage. The accommodation becomes institutional. Bipartisan. Systemic.

The files don’t just threaten Republicans. They threaten an entire class of American leadership that allowed foreign intelligence services to operate a kompromat collection operation on American soil for decades—and did nothing to stop it because too many powerful people were compromised.

That’s why both Trump’s DOJ and Biden’s DOJ refused to prosecute beyond Maxwell. That’s why judges seal documents for “national security.” That’s why media coverage remains muted. Not partisan protection. Systemic protection of a compromised American elite.

This systemic, bipartisan protection of Epstein’s network—and the foreign intelligence operations connected to it—seemed impenetrable. Both parties’ DOJ refused to prosecute beyond Maxwell. What follows isn’t speculation about future collapse—it’s documentation of a machine breaking down in real time.
