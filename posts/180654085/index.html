America's 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 1: Thank You, Senator Hawley — Now Let’s Finish What Harry Truman Started

December 3, 2025

Editor’s Note: Initial social media reports suggested Senator Hawley asked “why don’t we make health care free in America?” What Hawley actually announced was the “No Taxes on Healthcare Act,” eliminating taxes on healthcare expenses up to $25,000 per person. While this is meaningful tax relief, this series argues that America needs more fundamental reform to address the underlying cost crisis.


Senator Josh Hawley did something remarkable this week. The Missouri Republican looked at America’s healthcare crisis and announced legislation to provide relief from what he called “out-of-control medical costs that have skyrocketed in recent years.”

For that willingness to confront his party’s decades-long silence on healthcare, Senator Hawley deserves genuine praise.

His “No Taxes on Healthcare Act” would allow all taxpayers to deduct up to $25,000 per person in medical expenses, providing relief to the 90 percent of American families who currently get zero tax relief for healthcare costs Josh Hawley. He’s right that America faces a crisis. Healthcare costs have increased roughly 2100% since 1960. He’s right that working families are being crushed by medical bills. And most importantly, he’s right that this requires Congress to stop posturing and start problem-solving.

But here’s where we need to be direct: tax deductions on healthcare spending, while helpful to struggling families, don’t address WHY those costs exploded 2100% in the first place. The cost crisis predates the tax treatment of medical expenses. What America needs isn’t just relief from outrageous bills—Americans need to stop the bills from being outrageous in the first place.

Think of it this way: if your roof is leaking and destroying your home, a tax deduction on the water damage doesn’t fix the roof. You still need to repair the leak.

Still, Hawley has done something his colleagues won’t: he’s acknowledged the crisis exists and proposed concrete legislation to help families. That puts him ahead of most Republicans, and miles ahead of Donald Trump, who promised “concepts of a plan” in the 2024 presidential debate—nine years after promising to “repeal and replace” Obamacare.

So let’s accept Senator Hawley’s challenge. Let’s talk about real solutions. But first, Americans need to understand how their country arrived at this crisis—and why every previous attempt at fundamental reform has failed.

An Outsider’s Perspective

I write from the perspective of someone who has lived under universal healthcare in Canada for 74 years. Our system isn’t perfect—we have real problems with wait times for elective procedures and specialist access in rural areas. Americans are right to point these out.

But here’s the crucial difference: these are problems of system optimization, not system existence. Every developed nation debates how to improve their universal coverage. Only America debates whether universal coverage should exist at all.

And there isn’t just one model. Switzerland uses regulated private insurance, Canada uses single-payer provincial systems, Germany uses competing insurers with government-set prices, and the UK has government-owned healthcare Commonwealth Fund. They all have different strengths and weaknesses. But they all solved the fundamental problem America hasn’t: ensuring no one goes bankrupt from medical bills.

Before examining possible paths forward, Americans need to understand the 80-year pattern of failure on this issue.

The Ghosts of Reforms Past

The fight for universal healthcare in America didn’t begin with Obamacare, or even with Hillary Clinton. It began nearly 80 years ago with a plain-spoken president from Missouri—Senator Hawley’s own state.

Harry Truman: The Original Fight (1945-1953)

In 1945, just seven months into a presidency he inherited from Franklin Roosevelt, Harry Truman proposed universal national health insurance, declaring to Congress that “millions of our citizens do not now have a full measure of opportunity to achieve and enjoy good health” HISTORY.

Truman had seen the problem firsthand during World War I, when he was shocked by the number of men deemed unfit for military service due to poor health. He felt the middle class was left out when it came to healthcare coverage—the poor had charity care, the wealthy could afford private doctors, but working families had nothing HISTORY.

His solution? Healthcare for all, paid for through a payroll tax, similar to Social Security HISTORY. The Wagner-Murray-Dingell Bill proposed comprehensive coverage funded by a 3% payroll tax.

The opposition was swift and brutal. The American Medical Association denounced it as “socialized medicine” and played on growing fears of communism HISTORY. Sound familiar? The same scare tactics used against Truman in 1945 are still being deployed in 2025.

The bill never even made it to a vote, and Truman later called it the greatest disappointment of his presidency HISTORY.

The Partial Victory: Medicare and Medicaid (1965)

Twenty years after Truman’s attempt, President Lyndon Johnson finally achieved a breakthrough—but only a partial one. In 1965, Johnson signed the Medicare Act, providing healthcare to U.S. citizens age 65 and older, with Harry and Bess Truman present at the signing, where Johnson dubbed Truman “the real daddy of healthcare” Harry S. Truman Presidential Library and Museum.

Medicare and Medicaid were historic achievements. They proved that government could efficiently administer health insurance (Medicare has just 3% administrative overhead compared to 12% for private insurance). They demonstrated that Americans would accept and even love government health coverage (try taking Medicare away from seniors and see what happens).

But here’s the tragedy: Johnson and Congress couldn’t muster the political will to extend that same coverage to everyone. Instead, they created a two-tier system—government insurance for the elderly and poor, private insurance for working-age Americans. This compromise set up the dysfunctional system America has today, where the country spends $14,885 per capita on healthcare, nearly double the $7,371 average for wealthy OECD countries Peterson Foundation.

Notably, this was just as Canada was implementing its own universal system. Between 1966 and 1971, Canada’s provinces adopted universal coverage—proving it could be done in a large, geographically diverse, federal system with both French and English-speaking populations. If Canada could do it, why couldn’t America?

Hillary Clinton’s Doomed Task Force (1993-1994)

Nearly three decades after Medicare, the healthcare crisis had only worsened. When Bill Clinton took office in 1993, he moved quickly to address it. President Clinton appointed First Lady Hillary Clinton as leader of the Task Force on National Health Reform—”the most powerful official post ever assigned to a first lady” Ballotpedia.

Hillary Clinton and advisor Ira Magaziner organized more than 500 healthcare policy professionals into 15 cluster groups studying cost controls, coverage, benefits, and long-term care Ballotpedia. She testified before five congressional committees, met with governors and members of Congress, and traveled the country explaining the plan.

The Clinton plan sought to build on America’s mixed public-private system, calling for universal coverage with all employers required to contribute toward insurance premiums for their workers, with Americans choosing from multiple private insurance plans competing for their enrollment New England Journal of Medicine.

On September 23, 1993, President Clinton addressed a joint session of Congress to unveil the Health Security Act. Initial polling showed two-thirds approval, and Clinton spoke with tremendous force Princeton University. For a brief moment, comprehensive reform seemed achievable.

Then it all collapsed.

The administration underestimated the opposition and overestimated support for reform, failing to mobilize any organized constituency to counter the attacks from groups they had counted on as allies, such as big business New England Journal of Medicine. The insurance industry launched the devastating “Harry and Louise” ad campaign. Republicans, moderate Democrats, and key interest groups backpedaled from proposals they themselves had earlier co-sponsored or endorsed Princeton University.

Just a year after its introduction, the Clinton Health Security Act was dead in Congress Princeton University. It would stand as one of the great lost political opportunities in American history.

The Breakthrough: Obamacare (2010)

After so many failed attempts over seven decades, when the Affordable Care Act finally passed in 2010, it must have seemed like a godsend for millions of Americans.

And it was.

The ACA accomplished what Truman, Clinton, and countless others couldn’t: it dramatically expanded coverage. Millions of Americans who had been locked out of insurance due to pre-existing conditions could finally get coverage. Young adults could stay on their parents’ plans until age 26. Medicaid expansion provided coverage to millions of working poor families.

But here’s the painful truth: even this hard-won victory was a compromise that left the fundamental cost problem unresolved. President Obama and Congressional leaders concluded that more liberal plans, such as Medicare-for-all, could not win filibuster-proof support in the Senate, so they deliberately drew on bipartisan ideas—the same basic outline supported by former Senate Majority Leaders from both parties Wikipedia.

The result? America expanded access without controlling costs. The U.S. built on the existing employer-based system rather than replacing it. Americans left the insurance companies, pharmaceutical manufacturers, and hospital systems free to continue charging whatever the market would bear.

And the market has borne a lot. Healthcare spending reached $4.9 trillion in 2023, or $14,570 per person, accounting for 17.6% of GDP CMS. That figure is predicted to exceed 20% of GDP by 2035 Commonwealth Fund.

The Pattern Is Clear

Look at this 80-year pattern from an international perspective:

  • 1945: Truman proposes what Britain would achieve three years later and Canada twenty years later → AMA calls it “socialized medicine” → Dies in committee

  • 1965: Johnson achieves Medicare/Medicaid → Proves government can run healthcare efficiently → But only for elderly and poor, not universal

  • 1993: Clinton proposes managed competition → Insurance industry attacks → Dies without a vote

  • 2010: Obama achieves ACA → Expands access but not cost control → Costs continue to soar

Meanwhile, the rest of the developed world moved forward:

  • Britain implemented universal coverage in 1948

  • Canada achieved it between 1966-1971

  • Germany, France, Japan, Australia—all managed it

  • Even Switzerland, using a regulated private insurance model, achieved universal coverage

Every other developed nation found a way. America just... didn’t.

Each American attempt has been either completely blocked or watered down to the point of inadequacy. Each time, powerful interests—the AMA in Truman’s day, insurance companies in Clinton’s, pharmaceutical manufacturers today—have deployed the same tactics: fear-mongering about “socialized medicine,” dire warnings about “government takeover,” and massive lobbying campaigns.

Each time, Republicans have been the primary obstacle. Not always the only obstacle—conservative Democrats have played their part—but consistently, predictably, the main barrier to progress.

The Current Failure

Which brings us back to Senator Hawley and his party’s current abdication of responsibility.

Where is the Republican healthcare plan? After nine years of promising to “repeal and replace” Obamacare, where is the replacement?

Trump’s answer in the 2024 debate: “I have concepts of a plan.”

Concepts. Of. A. Plan.

After nearly a decade. After controlling the presidency, Senate, and House in 2017-2018. After countless committee hearings and policy proposals and campaign promises—concepts.

This isn’t governing. This is malpractice.

To his credit, Hawley wrote in the New York Times in May 2025 about an “identity crisis” within the Republican Party, questioning whether Republicans would be “a majority party of working people, or a permanent minority speaking only for the C suite” Josh Hawley. He warned that a “Wall Street wing” was urging Congress to slash health insurance for the working poor through Medicaid cuts Josh Hawley.

This is the right diagnosis. But diagnosis without treatment is just documentation of decline.

The Challenge

Senator Hawley, you’ve asked the right question: “Why don’t we make health care free in America?”

The answer is simple: because both American parties—but especially Republicans—have lacked the political courage to do what every other developed nation, including my own Canada, accomplished decades ago. They’ve regulated prices. They’ve negotiated drug costs. They’ve created universal coverage systems that, while varied in structure, all share one feature: government as the ultimate guarantor of access and controller of costs.

Most peer nations place a strong emphasis on cost containment through regulation of and negotiation with health providers, while in the U.S. the federal and state governments less directly control commercial health insurance prices KFF.

You’ve suggested Congress should lock itself in a room until it solves this. I agree. But let’s be clear about what “solving it” means:

  1. Universal coverage — Every American insured, no exceptions

  2. Cost controls — No more 2100% increases over 60 years

  3. Price regulation — What every other developed country does

  4. Medical bankruptcy eliminated — Nobody loses their home to pay for cancer treatment

Anything less isn’t a solution. It’s a press release.

Your “No Taxes on Healthcare Act” is a start—fine, make it easier for families drowning in medical debt. But it’s like offering a better interest rate on the Titanic’s deck chairs. America needs to plug the hole in the hull.

What Comes Next

In the coming installments of this series, I’m going to do what Congress hasn’t: actually examine the solutions that work. I’ll look at how other countries control costs while delivering better outcomes. I’ll analyze the specific mechanisms they use—price negotiations, administrative efficiency, global budgets. I’ll document the lobbying dollars that prevent reform. I’ll lay out a practical, phased implementation plan.

Most importantly, I’ll hold accountable the politicians—from both parties, but especially Republicans—who have blocked progress for 80 years.

Harry Truman fought for universal healthcare in 1945. He lost. But he paved the way for Medicare. Hillary Clinton fought in 1993. She lost. But she laid the groundwork for the ACA. Barack Obama fought in 2010. He won—partially. Enough to prove it could be done, but not enough to finish the job.

Senator Hawley, you’re from Missouri—Truman’s state. You’re asking Truman’s question. The difference is, you’re asking it as a Republican, from a party that has spent eight decades saying no.

This is your chance—and America’s chance—to finally join the rest of the developed world. From my vantage point in Canada, I can tell you: universal healthcare isn’t perfect. We have real challenges. But it’s infinitely better than a system where families choose between medication and food, where medical bills are the leading cause of bankruptcy, where people die rationing insulin.

The solutions exist. The models work—imperfectly, but they work. The question is whether American politicians have the courage to implement them.
