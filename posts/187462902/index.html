The Great Canadian Trade War

Part IX - The Art of No Deal

How Trump Threatened a Bridge He Endorsed, Lost a Nuclear Treaty He Could Have Saved, and Expects Canada to Trust Him With USMCA

February 9, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | February 09, 2026

Tonight, Donald Trump posted a 235-word tantrum on Truth Social about the Gordie Howe International Bridge — a $4.6 billion, six-lane crossing between Windsor, Ontario and Detroit, Michigan that is 98% complete and scheduled to open this year.

He claimed Canada “owns both sides.” False.

He claimed it was “built with virtually no U.S. content.” False.

He claimed Obama “stupidly” gave Canada a waiver to avoid using American steel. Misleading to the point of fabrication.

He claimed the United States gets “Absolutely NOTHING.” Spectacularly false.

And then — in what may be the single most unhinged sentence ever written by a sitting U.S. president — he claimed that if Canada makes a trade deal with China, “The first thing China will do is terminate ALL Ice Hockey being played in Canada, and permanently eliminate The Stanley Cup.”

I am not making this up. The President of the United States believes that the People’s Republic of China has the power — and apparently the motivation — to abolish the Stanley Cup.

Let that sink in while we do the math.

The $4.6 Billion Gift Horse That Trump Is Punching in the Mouth

Here’s what actually happened with the Gordie Howe International Bridge, stripped of Truth Social fiction and grounded in public record.

In 2012, Michigan’s Republican Governor Rick Snyder — not exactly a card-carrying member of the Canadian resistance — accepted an offer from the Canadian federal government to finance the entire bridge. Michigan’s legislature had refused to fund its share. Canada said: fine, we’ll pay for the whole thing, including the $250 million U.S. customs plaza, and recoup the costs through tolls.

Read that again. Canada offered to build Michigan a $4.6 billion bridge and pay for it entirely. Michigan said yes. Canada set up the Windsor-Detroit Bridge Authority, a Crown corporation, to manage the project. Construction began in 2018.

The bridge is jointly owned by Canada and the State of Michigan through the Crossing Agreement. Not “owned by Canada” on both sides, as Trump claims. Michigan is a co-owner of infrastructure it didn’t pay a dime to build.

Now let’s fact-check the rest.

“Built with virtually no U.S. content”

The Buy American waiver that Trump is so furious about — the one Obama “stupidly” granted — actually requires that all iron and steel for any component of the bridge be produced in the United States or Canada. Not China. Not overseas. North America. The waiver didn’t eliminate American content — it expanded the sourcing pool from “U.S. only” to “U.S. or Canada,” which, given that Canada was paying for the entire bridge, was a perfectly reasonable accommodation.

Michigan’s own Department of Transportation called claims that the bridge would use foreign steel “false” back in 2018. “Nothing could be further from the truth,” said MDOT spokesperson Jeff Fischer. The bridge employs American and Canadian workers. It used American and Canadian steel.

“What does the United States get? Absolutely NOTHING!”

Let’s count what the United States gets:

  • A $4.6 billion bridge it didn’t pay for

  • A second crossing to break the monopoly of the privately-owned Ambassador Bridge

  • Direct freeway access connecting I-75 and I-96 to Ontario’s Highway 401

  • An estimated 6,000 daily commuters from Ontario into Detroit

  • A U.S. Homeland Security-designated official port of entry (declared January 30, 2026)

  • Competition that will lower tolls at the existing Ambassador Bridge

  • American jobs in construction, customs, and ongoing operations

The United States gets “absolutely nothing” the way someone who inherits a house gets “absolutely nothing” because they didn’t pick the paint color.

“Ontario won’t even put U.S. spirits, beverages, and other alcoholic products on their shelves”

This one is my favourite, because Trump is complaining about the consequences of his own tariffs. Ontario’s ban on U.S. alcohol was a retaliatory measure — a direct response to the tariffs Trump imposed on Canadian goods in 2025. He punched Canada in the face and is now complaining that his knuckles hurt.

The Moroun Connection: Follow the Money Across the Ambassador Bridge

Here’s where it gets interesting. And by interesting, I mean suspicious.

Jason Kenney — the former Conservative premier of Alberta, hardly a Trudeau Liberal — posted this within minutes of Trump’s rant: “Trump’s latest unhinged threat to Canada is one part toddler level temper tantrum, and one part $ grift. My hunch is the Trump organization just got some kind of payoff from Detroit’s Maroun family, who own the competing Ambassador Bridge.”

Is Kenney right? We can’t prove a direct payoff. But consider the history.

The Moroun family of Detroit has owned the Ambassador Bridge since 1979. That single, privately-owned bridge carries roughly a quarter of all U.S.-Canada trade. The Gordie Howe Bridge, once open, will compete directly with the Ambassador — and the Morouns know it. Their own lawyers have estimated it could take away 75% of their commercial toll revenue.

For over 20 years, the Moroun family has fought the Gordie Howe Bridge with every tool available:

  • They sued the governments of Canada and Michigan. They lost.

  • They fought property expropriations in court. They lost.

  • They funded ballot measures in Michigan. They lost.

  • They deployed fake “eviction notices” on homes in Detroit’s Delray neighbourhood through Americans for Prosperity. That’s right — a billionaire family used a Koch-funded advocacy group to terrorize homeowners into opposing a bridge that would benefit their community.

  • In 2018, they aired a TV commercial on Fox & Friends — Trump’s favourite show — addressed directly to “Dear Mr. President,” asking him to revoke Obama’s presidential permit for the bridge.

And here’s the punchline: Trump said no.

In 2017, Trump and Justin Trudeau issued a joint statement calling the Gordie Howe Bridge “a vital economic link between our two countries.” In 2019, Trump signed the first U.S. funding for the bridge — $15 million for customs and border protection — into law.

Donald Trump endorsed this bridge. Donald Trump funded this bridge. And now, seven years later, he’s threatening to block it using talking points that sound like they were copy-pasted from a Moroun family press release.

The question Kenney is really asking isn’t whether Trump got a direct payment. It’s simpler than that: who benefits from blocking the Gordie Howe Bridge? Not Michigan. Not Detroit. Not the 6,000 daily commuters. Not the U.S. economy. The only entity that benefits from keeping the Gordie Howe Bridge closed is the family that owns the competing bridge.

The Art of No Deal: A One-Week Masterclass

Now zoom out. Because the bridge tantrum didn’t happen in a vacuum. It happened four days after Trump let the last nuclear arms control treaty on Earth expire.

On February 5, 2026, the New START treaty officially lapsed. For the first time in more than half a century, there are no binding limits on the strategic nuclear arsenals of the United States and Russia — the two countries that together hold nearly 90% of the world’s nuclear warheads.

The deal was there for the taking. In September 2025, Vladimir Putin offered to continue observing the treaty’s limits for one year past its expiration, until February 2027, to allow time for a replacement. Trump publicly called Putin’s offer “a good idea.” He then did absolutely nothing. No formal response. No counter-proposal. No negotiation.

“If it expires, it expires. We’ll do a better agreement,” Trump said in January.

It expired. There is no better agreement. There is no agreement at all.

As former Soviet negotiator Nikolai Sokov put it: “Everything I did in my life is now gone.”

UN Secretary-General António Guterres called it “a grave moment for international peace and security.” Pope Leo XIV called on both sides to prevent a new arms race. Trump called New START “a badly negotiated deal” and said he’d have his “Nuclear Experts work on a new, improved, and modernized Treaty.”

Sound familiar? It should. It’s the same pattern. Tear up the existing deal. Promise a better one. Deliver nothing.

And here’s the kicker: Trump says any new nuclear treaty must include China. China has shown zero interest in nuclear disarmament talks. So Trump’s precondition for a replacement treaty is something he knows won’t happen. It’s not a negotiating position — it’s an excuse to never negotiate.

TACO Tuesday: The Gordie Howe Bridge Edition

Regular readers of this series know the TACO framework: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. It’s the four-stage cycle that defines Trump’s approach to every confrontation where he doesn’t hold all the cards.

Let’s apply it to the bridge.

Stage 1: THREATEN ✅ (We are here)

“I will not allow this bridge to open.” Classic opening salvo. Maximum bluster, minimum legal authority. He’s demanding the U.S. own “at least one half” of a bridge that Canada paid for entirely.

Stage 2: ACCOMMODATE (Coming soon)

Michigan’s Democratic Senator Elissa Slotkin has already fired back: Trump is “punishing Michiganders for a trade war he started.” Windsor’s mayor called it “just insane.” The U.S. already declared the bridge an official port of entry on January 30. The political pressure from Michigan — a state Trump needs — will mount quickly. Expect the rhetoric to soften to “we’re in productive discussions.”

Stage 3: CAPITULATE (Weeks, not months)

The bridge will open. It has to. Michigan co-owns it. The legal framework is in place. The Homeland Security designation is published. Trump’s only real leverage is delaying staffing at the U.S. customs plaza, and even that faces legal challenge. When it opens, there will be no U.S. ownership stake. There will be no “compensation.”

Stage 4: OBFUSCATE (The victory lap)

“I’m pleased to announce that after tough negotiations, the Gordie Howe Bridge will open with tremendous benefits for American workers. This is a WIN for the United States, and it wouldn’t have happened without my strong leadership.”

You can set your watch by it.

The USMCA Problem: Would You Trust This Man With Your Trade Agreement?

And now we arrive at the question that actually matters.

By July 1, 2026 — less than five months from today — the United States, Mexico, and Canada must complete the first mandatory review of the USMCA. The three countries must decide whether to extend the agreement for another 16 years, or trigger a 10-year sunset countdown that would terminate the deal in 2036.

This is the most consequential trade negotiation in North American history. The USMCA governs over $1 trillion in annual Canada-U.S. trade. It’s the framework that determines whether Canadian goods face zero tariffs or punitive levies at the border. It’s the agreement that Trump himself negotiated during his first term — and bragged about incessantly.

And Canada is supposed to negotiate this with a man who, in the span of a single week:

  1. Let the last nuclear arms treaty lapse without lifting a finger

  2. Threatened to block a bridge he previously endorsed and funded

  3. Made factual claims about that bridge that are demonstrably, provably false on every point

  4. Speculated that China would “permanently eliminate The Stanley Cup”

Canada’s departing ambassador to the United States, Kirsten Hillman, was diplomatic about the USMCA timeline: “It’s very difficult to predict. This is the first time that we’re using this review process. And so there’s no history to go on.”

She’s right that there’s no procedural history. But there’s plenty of behavioural history. We know exactly how Trump negotiates. He threatens. He blusters. He makes claims that are factually wrong. He demands things he has no right to. And then — when the math doesn’t work — he capitulates and claims victory.

The USMCA review will follow the same pattern. Trump’s trade representative, Jamieson Greer, has already signaled the administration may seek to end the USMCA entirely in favour of bilateral deals. That’s the threat. The accommodation will come when American businesses — who overwhelmingly support the agreement — push back. The capitulation will be a deal that looks remarkably like the current one, possibly with tighter rules of origin. And the obfuscation will be a Rose Garden ceremony where Trump declares he’s negotiated “the greatest trade deal in the history of trade deals.”

Canada’s job is to survive the tantrum, keep the math in front of the cameras, and wait for Stage 3.

How Canada Deals With Trump Tantrums

So how does Canada actually deal with this?

Jason Kenney’s advice — “keep calm and carry on” — is more strategically sound than it might appear. Here’s why.

First, the legal position is strong. The Gordie Howe Bridge is governed by the Canada-Michigan Crossing Agreement, signed in 2012. Michigan is a co-owner. The U.S. Homeland Security Department has already designated the bridge as an official port of entry. If Trump tries to block the opening by withholding customs staffing, the governments of Canada and Michigan can — and should — challenge that in U.S. courts. The legal framework is in place. The bridge is built. The only question is whether one man’s tantrum can override international agreements, state co-ownership, and federal administrative law. History suggests it cannot.

Second, Michigan is Canada’s ally here — whether it wants to be or not. Senator Slotkin’s response was immediate and pointed: “canceling this project will have serious repercussions. Higher costs for Michigan businesses, less secure supply chains, and ultimately, fewer jobs.” She added that Canada is pursuing a trade deal with China precisely because “President Trump has kicked them in the teeth for a year.” Michigan’s economy depends on cross-border trade. Detroit needs this bridge. The political math runs against Trump in his own backyard.

Third, Canada has already demonstrated that it can outlast Trump’s tantrums. The 85% consumer boycott. The Buy Canadian movement. The 1.4 million Facebook members. The apps named “Buy Beaver” and “O SCANada.” The retaliatory tariffs. The China trade deal. None of these things happened because Canadians panicked. They happened because Canadians did the math, organized methodically, and applied sustained economic pressure.
And when math fails, there's always satire. Within an hour of Trump's bridge post, Canadian X was already turning his logic against him. One user pointed out the obvious parallel: the Alaska Highway — a 2,232-kilometre road running through the Yukon and British Columbia, originally built by the U.S. Army in 1942, and maintained entirely at Canadian expense since 1946. It primarily serves American military and civilian traffic to and from Alaska. Canada has never been compensated. Mimicking Trump's cadence perfectly, the poster wrote: "We should shut down the highway and start negotiations IMMEDIATELY until we are treated fairly. Thank you for your attention to this matter." If Trump's bridge logic holds, Canada is owed 80 years of highway maintenance back-payments. The math, as always, cuts both ways.

Fourth, and most importantly: the facts are on Canada’s side. Every single factual claim in Trump’s bridge post is wrong. That’s not a matter of interpretation — it’s a matter of public record. The bridge ownership, the steel sourcing, the Buy American waiver, the alcohol ban — all of it is documented, all of it contradicts what Trump said. When the facts are this clear, the best strategy is the simplest one: state them loudly, state them repeatedly, and let the audience do the math.

UPDATE — Wed, Feb 11: The House Just Voted Against Trump’s Tariffs on Canada

After publishing this essay, the U.S. House of Representatives voted 219-211 to roll back Trump’s tariffs on Canada. Six Republicans broke ranks and defied the president — while he was actively threatening them on Truth Social.

Let that timeline sink in.

On Monday, Trump threatened to block a $4.6 billion bridge he endorsed seven years ago. On Wednesday, his own party’s House members voted to dismantle his tariff regime against the country that paid for it.

The six Republicans who crossed the aisle: Don Bacon (NE), Brian Fitzpatrick (PA), Jeff Hurd (CO), Kevin Kiley (CA), Thomas Massie (KY), and Dan Newhouse (WA). Only one Democrat — Jared Golden of Maine — voted against.

Trump’s response, posted to Truth Social as the votes were being cast: “Any Republican, in the House or the Senate, that votes against TARIFFS will seriously suffer the consequences come Election time, and that includes Primaries!”

They voted anyway.

The resolution seeks to terminate the national emergency declaration Trump invoked in February 2025 — the claim that Canada’s “failure” to address fentanyl trafficking constituted an “unusual and extraordinary threat” to U.S. national security. That emergency declaration is the legal foundation for the 35% tariffs on Canadian goods.

Will it become law? Almost certainly not. Trump will veto it, and the vote fell well short of the two-thirds majority needed for an override. The Senate has already voted twice to block Trump’s Canada tariffs, with four Republicans joining Democrats each time. But even a veto-proof majority in both chambers wouldn’t change the policy if Trump refuses to sign.

So why does this matter?

Because it confirms the TACO cycle is accelerating. We’re watching Stage 2 — Accommodate — unfold in real time. When members of the president’s own party vote against his signature trade policy while he’s threatening them from the presidential social media account, the political math is shifting. Swing-district Republicans are calculating that the cost of supporting Trump’s tariffs now exceeds the cost of defying him. That calculation only moves in one direction.

Colorado’s Jeff Hurd explained it simply: “I’ve heard a lot from our farmers, agricultural producers, manufacturers of all sizes across the district that these tariffs are not good for Colorado’s 3rd District.”

The polling explains why. As CNN reported today, 66% of Canadians give Trump's first year a resounding F — a plurality across every single party, left to right. Only 6% gave him an A. But here's the number that should keep Republican strategists up at night: Americans actually like Canada. A net +46 favourability rating among all adults, +64 among Democrats, +46 among independents, and +30 even among Republicans — making Canada the most popular country tested by the Chicago Council on Global Affairs. Canada is more popular in America than Trump is. Canadian tourism to the U.S. is down 25% year-over-year while American visits to Canada are essentially flat — real dollars walking away from the American economy. And the prediction markets? Kalshi puts a 70% chance the Supreme Court rules Trump's tariffs illegal. Six Republicans didn't just read the polls. They read the room.

The Art of No Deal isn’t just a Canadian observation anymore. It’s a bipartisan American conclusion.

The Bottom Line

Donald Trump spent his career branding himself as the world’s greatest dealmaker. But the evidence tells a different story.

He couldn’t negotiate the continuation of New START — even when Putin offered him an extra year on a silver platter. He couldn’t negotiate a replacement for the Iran deal, the Paris Accord, the TPP, or the INF Treaty. He can’t even negotiate with his own first-term self, threatening to block a bridge he endorsed seven years ago.

And now he’s going to negotiate the most important trade agreement in North American history? With claims that are factually false? While speculating about China abolishing hockey?

The Art of the Deal was always more fiction than memoir. But The Art of No Deal? That’s the autobiography Trump is writing in real time, one abandoned treaty and one unhinged Truth Social post at a time.

Canada’s strategy is clear: survive the tantrum, trust the math, and wait for the TACO to complete its cycle.

The bridge will open. The USMCA will be reviewed. And somewhere in between, Donald Trump will claim he negotiated the greatest deal in history.

The math, as always, will tell a different story.
