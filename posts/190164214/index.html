The Iran Crisis

“MIGA”

Part XXXV: Day Seven March 6, 2026

March 6, 2026

Signed: MIGA. Make Iran Great Again. Day Seven.

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 6, 2026

At 9:18 on Friday morning, Donald Trump posted to Truth Social.

“There will be no deal with Iran except UNCONDITIONAL SURRENDER! After that, and the selection of a GREAT & ACCEPTABLE Leader(s), we, and many of our wonderful and very brave allies and partners, will work tirelessly to bring Iran back from the brink of destruction.”

He signed off: “MIGA!”

Make Iran Great Again.

The post went up approximately two hours after Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian had signaled publicly that “some countries have begun mediation efforts” and that Iran was committed to “lasting peace in the region.” It went up while Iran’s intelligence ministry was, according to officials briefed on the outreach, reaching through intermediaries to the CIA to discuss terms for ending the war. The back-channel was opening. It had been alive for perhaps two hours. And the president of the United States, in the span of a Truth Social post, slammed it shut, signed off with a campaign slogan borrowed from his own critics, and watched the price of Brent crude jump to $90 a barrel within the hour.

The irony requires a sentence of context. Marjorie Taylor Greene and the MAGA isolationist base have been using “MIGA” sardonically for days — sometimes rendered as “Make Israel Great Again” — to express their fury that Trump has abandoned anti-interventionism for a war most of his original base opposes. The cover of Time magazine this week is a red baseball cap emblazoned with “Make Iran Great Again.” Trump apparently absorbed the mockery and adopted it as his own rallying cry, apparently without registering that he was signing off on a war post with the language his own critics invented to ridicule him.

Karoline Leavitt, asked later to define what unconditional surrender would actually look like, provided what may be the most honest definition the administration has offered for anything in seven days: when Trump personally determines that Iran no longer poses a threat to the United States of America, Iran will essentially be in a state of unconditional surrender. No benchmarks. No timeline. No measurable criteria established in advance. The endpoint of the war is the interior judgment of one man, applied at a moment of his own choosing, to a condition he has declined to define.

This is the war, in one press briefing.

Iran showed no interest in surrendering. It continued to launch drone and missile salvos at Israel throughout Friday. Air-raid sirens went off in Tel Aviv in the predawn hours. The Israeli military confirmed fresh missile launches from Iran, though no major damage was reported from that specific wave. The war continued, as it has continued without pause, for seven full days.

I. Rationale Eleven, and the One Before It

Marie-Rose Sheinerman and Isabel Ruehl catalogued ten rationales for the war across six days in The Atlantic. By Friday afternoon the tally had reached eleven, and reviewing the complete sequence is worth the time because it tells you something essential about the nature of what is happening.

On the opening night, Trump cited imminent threats to US assets and allies (Rationale One), permanent destruction of Iran’s nuclear program (Two), ending support for regional terrorist proxies (Three), and regime change — the Iranian people should “seize control of your destiny” (Four). Before sunrise on Day One, he had also cited Iranian election interference in 2020 and 2024 (Five). By Saturday afternoon, with Khamenei reported dead, the stated objective had expanded to “PEACE THROUGHOUT THE MIDDLE EAST AND, INDEED, THE WORLD” (Six). Sunday brought the claim, offered on Fox News, that Iran would have had a nuclear weapon within two weeks had the US not struck — a timeline not included in the initial address and not corroborated by any intelligence agency (back to Two, with added urgency and no supporting evidence). A Sunday night ABC interview produced: “I got him before he got me” — the killing of Khamenei framed as preemptive personal self-defense against an alleged assassination plot (Eight).

On Monday came Rationale Nine.

A combat-unit commander briefed officers and told them that the war in Iran is part of God’s plan — that Donald Trump was “anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth.” A noncommissioned officer, apparently disturbed by what they heard, filed a formal complaint with the Military Religious Freedom Foundation, which circulated it publicly.

The complaint has not been denied. The officer has not been identified or publicly disciplined.

Also on Monday, Secretary of State Rubio said Israel forced America’s hand — the US had to strike preemptively or suffer higher casualties after Israel acted (Ten). That rationale lasted 24 hours before Trump reversed it entirely on Tuesday, saying if anything he had forced Israel’s hand. Rubio dutifully reversed himself too, insisting the decision had been made separate from and prior to Israel’s.

Friday morning: unconditional surrender. Rationale Eleven.

Sheinerman and Ruehl document the administration’s response to this cascade of contradictions. Hegseth, on Monday, said the administration was “very clear-eyed” and would avoid “foolish policies of the past” that lacked goals “tethered to actual, clear objectives.” The White House simultaneously posted: “Our objectives are clear. We will not stop until they are achieved.”

Eleven rationales. Clear objectives. The administration offered both of these statements with apparent sincerity.

But it is Rationale Nine that demands the most sustained attention, because it is categorically different from the others. Imminent threats, nuclear timelines, election interference, preemptive self-defense — these are policy rationales, subject to evidence, scrutiny, and counter-argument. The claim that a US military commander told troops this war is divinely ordained to trigger the Second Coming of Christ is not a policy rationale. It is a statement about the internal culture of the American military under Pete Hegseth’s leadership: a secretary who has purged officers he considers insufficiently warrior-ethos, who has blocked military personnel from elite university education, who called the adversary “lunatics” operating on “prophetic Islamist delusions” in a formal Pentagon briefing — apparently without noticing what that phrase sounds like when you rotate it 180 degrees and point it at a briefing room in Tampa.

A war with eleven rationales, one of which is Armageddon. Being fought at $1 billion per day. Against a country whose succession crisis has been paralyzed because naming a new leader marks him for assassination before he takes office. With an internal planning horizon of at minimum 100 days.

II. The Bunker Beneath the Bunker

Source: Satellite image from March 6 by Planet Labs. Daniel Wood/The New York Times

Khamenei’s compound in central Tehran was struck on Day One. The strike killed him. It was designed to do exactly that.

What the Day One strike could not do was penetrate the emergency command infrastructure built beneath the compound — the underground bunker designed specifically to allow Iranian state continuity after an above-ground strike destroyed the surface. After Khamenei was killed, senior Iranian officials moved underground and continued operating from that failsafe. The Iranian state did not stop functioning. It went deeper.

On Day Seven, 50 Israeli fighter jets struck the bunker.

The Israeli military released graphics showing the bunker’s layout: multiple streets in length, several entrances, meeting rooms for senior officials. They released video of the strike. The New York Times reviewed satellite imagery from Planet Labs showing fresh damage to buildings at the compound site — damage layered atop the Saturday craters, successive concentric circles of destruction. Whether anyone was killed in Friday’s strike: unknown. The symbolic communication is precise regardless. You built a failsafe for the day we destroyed your leadership above ground. We have now destroyed the failsafe.

There is no depth.

Simultaneously, Israel issued evacuation warnings for an industrial area in the Qom region — ten miles from the Fordo nuclear enrichment facility, buried deep inside a mountain that American B-2 bombers tried and may have failed to fully penetrate during the June 12-day war. Qom is among the holiest cities in Shia Islam. It is home to the shrine of Fatima Masoumeh, a revered figure in Shia tradition whose tomb draws pilgrims from Pakistan, Iraq, India, Lebanon, and every country where Shia Muslims live. It houses Iran’s most influential religious seminaries — the institutions that have trained the clerics governing Iran since 1979, and that will train the clerics, whoever they are, who govern Iran after this war.

The city has been largely spared in this round. The evacuation warning means it will not be spared much longer.

If Fordo is struck again — and this time more successfully — the nuclear rationale, Rationale Two, is satisfied in a visible and audible way. If Qom’s seminaries or the shrine of Fatima Masoumeh are damaged in the process, the reaction across the Shia world will be of a different order than anything this war has yet produced. The 25 people killed in Pakistani protests in the first week, the candle-lit vigils in Srinagar, the fury in Karachi — these will seem restrained by comparison to what striking the heart of Shia religious learning would generate.

Israel said it also struck more than 400 additional targets in western Iran on Friday, including missile launchers and drone storage sites. The cumulative totals since Saturday: over 2,500 Israeli strikes, over 6,000 munitions dropped. Israeli Chief of Staff Zamir claimed earlier this week that 80 percent of Iran’s air defenses and 60 percent of its ballistic missile launchers have been destroyed. The Friday strikes are pursuing what remains beneath the surface of what was already destroyed.

III. The Azadi and the Record of Civilian Infrastructure

Damage to Azadi Stadium/sports complex in Tehran — Strikes hit this major venue (home to Iran’s national soccer team and a cultural landmark). Photos show smoke rising from the site post-strike, damaged structures, and the complex amid the attacks.

On Day One, a sports hall in the town of Lamerd, in Fars province, was struck while children were playing volleyball inside. Eighteen boys and girls were killed. A video verified by the New York Times showed smoke rising from the blackened exterior of the building.

On Day Six, strikes hit the Azadi sports complex in Tehran. One of the largest stadiums in Asia. Home of Iran’s national soccer team. The stadium where Iran clinched its World Cup berth for the summer tournament coming to North America. The stadium where Frank Sinatra performed in 1974 in front of tens of thousands of Iranians, a concert now remembered as evidence of a pre-revolutionary Iran less isolated from the world — the Iran that the Islamic Revolution of 1979 closed off, the closure that produced the next 47 years of the history everyone is now living through.

The Azadi is also the stadium where Sahar Khodayari — Blue Girl — was arrested in 2019 for sneaking in to watch a match. Women had been banned from attending football matches under the Islamic Republic. She was caught. She was sentenced to prison. Before she could be imprisoned, she killed herself through immolation outside a courthouse. Her death ignited the women’s rights protest movement that culminated in the Mahsa Amini uprising of 2022 and has never fully extinguished, running as a current beneath Iranian society that the bombs now falling on Tehran will not simplify.

Iran’s sports minister called the Azadi strike a war crime. A CENTCOM spokesman: “Unlike Iran, we don’t target civilians but instead take all precautions available to minimize the risk of unintended harm.” Israel did not respond.

The ICRC definition of war crimes under the Geneva Conventions does not require a confessed order to target civilians. It requires documentation of a pattern of attacks on civilian infrastructure without demonstrated military necessity. The pattern across seven days now includes: a girls’ elementary school with 175 dead, most of them children; two boys’ schools in Parand with blown-out windows and collapsed classroom walls; a volleyball hall with 18 dead children; the Azadi stadium complex; a medical clinic; a gas station; two residential buildings; and a parking lot. The UN human rights chief has called for an investigation into the school strike. The record is being assembled in satellite imagery, verified videos, and survivor testimonies sent to journalists under bombardment.

Whether it will produce accountability — given that neither the United States nor Israel is a member of the International Criminal Court, and given that the Security Council contains a US veto — is a question history will answer on its own timeline. The documentation exists regardless of the accountability.

IV. Lebanon: 300,000

Shefgar Othman is staying in a tent by Beirut’s seaside promenade with his family after they were told to evacuate their home in the city’s southern suburbs earlier this week.Credit...Diego Ibarra Sanchez for The New York Times

While Tehran was being struck from the air, Lebanon was being emptied on the ground.

The Norwegian Refugee Council estimated 300,000 people displaced in Lebanon by Friday. It warned the number could reach one million. Lebanon’s death toll crossed 200, with more than 700 wounded. Israeli ground forces advanced in southern Lebanon simultaneously with the air campaign, turning the fifteen-month November 2024 ceasefire into a historical footnote. Israeli artillery shells struck a UNIFIL peacekeeping base occupied by a Ghanaian battalion — at least one peacekeeper seriously wounded and airlifted by helicopter, in what constitutes an attack on UN personnel.

In Martyrs’ Square in central Beirut, a 23-year-old pharmacist named Zeinab Srour stood with two pet carriers — her cats Tuti and Virus — waiting for a contact from a pet shelter. Her family had been displaced from Dahiya; she could no longer care for both animals, so she was trying to find them a safe place until she could come home. “The sounds of bombings disturb them as much as they do us,” she said. “We hope this war won’t last long.”

On the waterfront promenade, Mohamed Hjoula, 35, had taken refuge with about 40 family members, including his ailing parents. He learned on Friday afternoon that his home in Dahiya had been destroyed by airstrikes. Before the war, he had been saving to buy an apartment and find a bride. “We civilians are paying for the price of war,” he said. Then: “My life here has failed. They have taken us back a thousand years. They have taken us back to the Stone Age.” His anger was directed equally at Israel and at Hezbollah, which he said had drawn Dahiya into a war with little regard for the people living there.

Shefgar Othman, 32, sent his brother back into Dahiya on Friday to retrieve belongings from their home. The bombing restarted while the brother was inside. He fled empty-handed, returning to a family of ten stranded on the promenade with nothing.

Sameer Hjoula, 50, had fled with his four children. “Look at my eyes. I have not slept since I left home. We have nothing.”

These are the people paying for a war that has eleven rationales. None of the eleven mentions them.

Macron called Lebanese President Aoun and Parliament Speaker Berri on Friday — Berri being the conduit through whom foreign officials communicate with Hezbollah, which is designated a terrorist organization by most Western governments. Macron offered French military support including armored vehicles, reported receiving Lebanese commitments to assume control of Hezbollah positions, and called for a ceasefire. Israel continued striking. Whether commitments made while jets are bombing Dahiya into rubble can be operationalized is the question no one in Paris or Beirut can currently answer.

V. The Contractors, the September Horizon, and the Math

Trump hosted the six largest American defense contractors at the White House on Friday: Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, RTX Corporation, Boeing, Honeywell, and L3Harris. Leavitt’s framing: the US military has “more than enough munitions, ammo, and weapons stockpiles.” The meeting had been “scheduled weeks ago.” Trump asked the companies to build more weapons.

The framing and the action are irreconcilable. You do not summon the entire American defense industrial base to the Oval Office and ask them to accelerate production because you have more than enough of everything.

The math is visible without any classified information. Lockheed Martin produced 620 Patriot interceptors in all of 2025. Its plans call for scaling to 2,000 per year over the next several years. Iran has launched over 2,000 drones in seven days. The UAE alone has intercepted nearly 1,200 Iranian drones and over 200 ballistic missiles since Saturday. The war costs a preliminary Pentagon estimate of $1 billion per day. The projected supplemental funding request Congress will eventually see: up to $50 billion.

Buried in Vivian Salama and Jonathan Lemire’s Atlantic investigation published Friday afternoon is the detail that reframes the entire week: US Central Command is asking the Pentagon to send more military-intelligence officers to its headquarters to support operations against Iran for at least 100 days — and likely through September.

Not four to five weeks. Not a “very specific, clear mission.” One hundred days minimum. Through September. The midterms are November 3.

Trump told Ankara the war would last four days. Trump told the American public four to five weeks. CENTCOM is planning through September. Three separate timelines, none of which match, all simultaneously in operation.

The defense contractor meeting, the $50 billion supplemental on the horizon, and the September planning horizon together constitute the actual shape of this war as the people managing it understand it — not the version posted to Truth Social at 9:18 in the morning. The gap between those two versions of the war is the gap between the MIGA post and the CENTCOM briefing room in Tampa.

VI. “It’s Going to Work Like in Venezuela”

The succession crisis did not resolve on Day Seven. Mojtaba Khamenei remains the consensus choice. He remains unannounced because naming him makes him a target before he takes office.

Trump told CNN Friday: “It’s going to work very easily. It’s going to work like in Venezuela.” He said he was willing to keep a clerical government in Iran — “I don’t mind religious leaders. I deal with a lot of religious leaders” — provided they were “fair” to Israel and the United States. He said he expected Cuba to fall soon, giving him a trifecta of leadership change across three adversary nations. “MIGA,” he had posted that morning.

Iran’s intelligence ministry was reaching through intermediaries to the CIA to discuss terms. Iran’s president had publicly signaled mediation. The price of Brent crude, having absorbed all of this, settled Friday at $92 a barrel — up nearly 30 percent in a single week, the biggest weekly jump since April 2020 and the onset of the COVID pandemic. Qatar’s energy minister said oil could reach $150 within weeks and that the situation could “bring down the economies of the world.”

The Venezuela model requires a Delcy Rodríguez — someone inside the existing power structure with the institutional standing to deliver compliance, who has maintained a private back-channel to Washington and has the authority to pivot the entire state apparatus toward accommodation. Iran has no such person. Those who want to make amends cannot deliver. Those who can deliver do not want to make amends.

“MIGA,” the president signed off.

The man who cannot be named has still not been named. The bunker beneath the bunker has been struck. The volleyball hall and the Azadi stadium are in the record. Three hundred thousand Lebanese are displaced. CENTCOM is planning through September. Brent is at $92 and rising.

Day Eight begins tonight.
