Presidential Power as Corporate Shield

Part 29: When Americans Die for Corporate Profits (2025-2026)

March 17, 2026

On February 18, 2026, President Donald Trump signed an executive order containing a sentence that should have been unthinkable in American governance:

“The Secretary shall ensure that any order, rule, or regulation issued under this section does not place the corporate viability of any domestic producer of elemental phosphorus or glyphosate-based herbicides at risk.”

Corporate viability. Not public health. Not cancer prevention. Not protecting children from carcinogens.

Corporate viability.

Trump used the Defense Production Act—a law designed to protect America during wartime—to grant legal immunity to companies producing glyphosate, a herbicide the International Agency for Research on Cancer classified in 2015 as “probably carcinogenic to humans.” He explicitly mandated that federal policy prioritize corporate profits over the health consequences Americans would face.

But the glyphosate order was not an isolated decision. It was part of a systematic pattern spanning 2025-2026 in which Trump used every lever of presidential power to grant corporations immunity from accountability while Americans paid with their lives, their health, and their financial security.

Workers died because Trump gutted workplace safety enforcement. Coal miners suffocated from black lung because Trump delayed protections. Americans got sick because Trump destroyed food safety oversight. Elderly nursing home residents died of neglect after industry executives donated $4.8 million to Trump’s super PAC and received a private lunch where they successfully lobbied for deregulation.

This is not policy disagreement. This is corporate capture at its most lethal. And in every case, the corporations benefiting from Trump’s actions faced zero accountability—while the Americans harmed had no recourse.

Glyphosate: When Corporate Immunity Serves War Readiness

On February 18, 2026, Donald Trump issued an executive order declaring elemental phosphorus essential to “military readiness and national defense.” The order mandated continued production of glyphosate—the controversial weedkiller known as Roundup—citing the need to protect America’s food supply.

But the order wasn’t primarily about food security. It was about protecting Bayer, the German pharmaceutical giant, from billions in cancer lawsuits. And it was about ensuring America’s sole supply of white phosphorus for military munitions—the same chemical compound used to make Roundup and also used to fill smoke screens and incendiary devices that can violently burn property or people.

The timeline tells the story.

The Lobbying Campaign

Bayer spent more than $9 million in 2025 to pay 53 lobbyists registered to represent the company’s interests with the White House and federal agencies. Among them was Brian Ballard, who raised more than $50 million for Trump’s 2024 campaign. His former partners include White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles and Attorney General Pam Bondi.

In June 2025, Bayer CEO Bill Anderson met with EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin to request an update on glyphosate regulation and the company’s pending Supreme Court case. The case could block thousands of lawsuits from cancer patients who claim Roundup caused their non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma.

The World Health Organization’s International Agency for Research on Cancer has deemed glyphosate “probably carcinogenic to humans.” Bayer has already spent billions on settlements. In August 2025, the company announced it could be “forced to stop U.S. production” unless regulatory changes staved off the litigation.

The Protection Arrives

In October 2025, the federal government approved Bayer’s bid to open a new phosphate mine in Idaho. Then in late 2025, the Trump administration backed Bayer in the Supreme Court case.

On February 17, 2026, Bayer moved to end the bulk of its current Roundup litigation, proposing a $7.25 billion class-action settlement.

The next day—February 18, 2026—Trump issued his executive order. It didn’t just protect glyphosate production. It protected “elemental phosphorus” production, which the order declared crucial to “military readiness and national defense.”

The Military Connection

U.S. military personnel with a round of white phosphorus in Afghanistan in 2011.Credit...Tyler Hicks/The New York Times

Here’s what the order didn’t say prominently: Bayer, through its 2018 acquisition of Monsanto, operates the only facility in the United States that produces white phosphorus. The facility is in Soda Springs, Idaho, and uses phosphate rock the company mines locally.

Bayer uses most of that white phosphorus to make the glyphosate in Roundup. But the company also supplies some white phosphorus, via intermediaries, to the United States military, which uses it to fill white phosphorus munitions at the Pine Bluff Arsenal in Arkansas.

White phosphorus ignites spontaneously when it comes in contact with oxygen. It produces thick white smoke and can reach temperatures high enough to burn through metal. The military uses it as a smoke screen to mask troop movements, or to mark targets. But it can severely burn people who come into contact with it, causing wounds that reignite when bandages are removed.

Using white phosphorus isn’t illegal, though deploying it deliberately against civilians or in a civilian setting violates the laws of war.

The War Timing

A white phosphorus munition struck a target during a military exercise in Taiwan in 2022.Credit...Sam Yeh/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Ten days after Trump’s executive order—on February 28, 2026—the United States launched Operation Epic Fury against Iran. But military analysts noted that white phosphorus munitions are typically used in ground operations, not airstrikes.

As one defense analyst explained to the New York Times: If the administration were to “take action against drug cartels in Latin America or to launch a ground operation in Cuba, forces might be expected to use these types of white phosphorus munitions to disguise their movements.”

On March 3, 2026—just 13 days after the executive order—Trump announced the deployment of U.S. special forces to Ecuador to combat drug cartels Los Lobos and Los Choneros. This was Trump’s first announced land operation requiring ground forces.

The executive order had ensured America’s sole supply of white phosphorus munitions just in time.

The Corporate-Military-Political Loop

The pattern is clear:

Step 1: Bayer lobbyist who raised $50 million for Trump (and whose former partners now run the White House and Justice Department) meets with EPA Administrator about protecting the company from cancer lawsuits.

Step 2: Federal government approves Bayer’s new mine. Trump administration backs Bayer in Supreme Court case that could block cancer lawsuits.

Step 3: Bayer proposes $7.25 billion settlement to end current litigation.

Step 4: Trump issues executive order the next day protecting both glyphosate (Bayer’s product) and white phosphorus (Bayer’s monopoly, needed for military munitions).

Step 5: Ten days later, Trump launches unauthorized war against Iran. Thirteen days after the order, Trump deploys ground forces to Ecuador—exactly the type of operation requiring white phosphorus munitions.

When asked about the significance of munitions in the executive order, White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said: “The president made this decision based on national security priorities.”

She didn’t mention the $50 million Brian Ballard raised for Trump’s campaign. She didn’t mention that Ballard’s former partners now control the White House and Justice Department. She didn’t mention the February 17 settlement proposal that preceded the February 18 executive order by exactly one day.

The Dual Accountability Void

Here’s what makes this particularly insidious: Trump created an executive order that serves three purposes simultaneously:

  1. Corporate Protection: Shields Bayer from billions in cancer lawsuit liability

  2. Military Readiness: Ensures white phosphorus supply for unauthorized military operations

  3. Political Payback: Rewards a major campaign donor with regulatory protection

And because the order wraps corporate immunity in the flag of “national security,” it becomes virtually immune to challenge. Who wants to be accused of undermining America’s defense readiness by questioning why we’re protecting a German company from cancer lawsuits?

Roundup products lining the shelves in San Rafael, Calif.Credit...Josh Edelson/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

The accountability void is complete. Cancer patients can’t sue because the Supreme Court (backed by Trump) may block their lawsuits. Congress can’t investigate because it’s framed as national security. The public can’t know the full reasoning because military readiness is classified. And Bayer’s $50 million investment in Trump pays dividends in both lawsuit protection and guaranteed government contracts for white phosphorus.

When Representative Thomas Massie spoke on the House floor on February 20, he said: “All three branches of this government are under siege by lobbyists and lawyers from a German company named Bayer.”

He was right. But he missed the full picture: Those lobbyists didn’t just buy corporate immunity. They bought military supply chain protection. And they bought it just days before Trump launched ground operations that would require the very munitions Bayer uniquely produces.

The February 18 executive order wasn’t about food security or even just about protecting corporate donors. It was about ensuring the munitions supply for unauthorized wars that Trump was already planning to wage.

Thousands of cancer patients will continue to suffer. America’s sole white phosphorus supplier will continue to operate without meaningful accountability. And Trump’s unauthorized military operations will continue with the munitions they need.

All because $50 million bought an executive order that serves corporate immunity, military readiness, and political payback simultaneously.

The system isn’t broken. It’s working exactly as designed—for the people who paid for it.

Worker Safety Destroyed: When Americans Die on the Job

While Trump protected chemical companies from cancer lawsuits, he systematically destroyed the agencies protecting workers from dying on the job.

OSHA Gutted

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration—created in 1970 to ensure safe workplaces—has been gutted under Trump’s second term:

Inspections Collapsed: Workplace inspections dropped 20% in six months (29,229 to 23,531). Senator Elizabeth Warren’s analysis found the drop included a 42% decrease in “willful violations”—the category with highest penalties.

Workforce Slashed: OSHA lost nearly 300 employees between February 2024 and June 2025 (846 federal inspectors down to 736). Trump’s budget proposal cuts another 223 full-time positions.

Budget Cut: From $632.3 million to $582.4 million—an 8% reduction in the face of rising costs.

Rules Rolled Back: Over 60 workplace safety and wage regulations eliminated or under review, including safety rules in mines, construction lighting, and farmworker protections.

Penalties Reduced: Changed criteria to give companies “free passes” for first-time violations, removing what little deterrent existed.

Heat Safety Rule Delayed: A rule that would have protected 36 million workers from heat-related injuries was indefinitely delayed.

NIOSH Devastated: The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health—OSHA’s research arm—saw a 90% workforce cut (1,400 employees to fewer than 150) and 80% budget cut.

The human cost is measurable. In 2023, 5,300 workers died from fatal injuries—one worker every 99 minutes. Roughly 20 times that many die from occupational illnesses traced to workplace exposures. Nearly 100 workers die every week from traumatic injuries.

Those numbers will increase. With fewer inspectors, fewer inspections, reduced penalties, and eliminated safety rules, more Americans will die on the job.

Former OSHA Deputy Assistant Secretary Jordan Barab captured the absurdity with a devastating comparison:

“You used to get fined more for harassing a burro on federal land than for a serious OSHA violation.”

Rebecca Reindel, occupational safety director at the AFL-CIO, warned: “People are at very great risk of dying on the job already. This is something that is only going to make the problem worse.”

Coal Miners Suffocating: MSHA and Black Lung

Trump’s assault on worker safety extended underground, where coal miners face deadly silica dust that causes black lung disease.

On April 8, 2025—the same day Trump signed an executive order “reinvigorating America’s beautiful clean coal industry”—the Mine Safety and Health Administration quietly announced it was delaying enforcement of new silica safety standards. The rule, decades in the making, was scheduled to take effect six days later.

The Biden administration’s April 2024 regulatory impact analysis estimated the rule would prevent 1,067 lifetime deaths and 3,746 lifetime cases of silica-related illnesses.

Trump paused it. Then delayed it to August. Then October. Then eliminated it entirely.

The reason? NIOSH—the research agency that provides scientific support—had been gutted by Trump’s 90% workforce cut. The same cuts that destroyed OSHA’s research capacity also eliminated the scientific foundation for protecting miners from deadly dust.

In late 2025, NIOSH officials sent an email to facilities offering black lung screenings: halt all submissions for evaluation due to staffing cuts. “We have no further information about the future of [the screening program] at this time.”

Black lung—coal workers’ pneumoconiosis—is caused by inhaling coal and silica dust. It affects roughly 1 in 5 miners who have worked in Central Appalachian mines for 25 years or more. Complicated black lung is on the rise because miners are exposed to more silica than ever before.

Dr. Brandon Crum, a Kentucky doctor who has diagnosed over 600 cases, said: “The amount and severity of black lung is worse now than ever.”

Chris Williamson, former Assistant Secretary of Labor for Mine Safety and Health, said after Trump’s delay: “We just had the rug pulled out from under us.”

Dr. Crum warned: “The pausing of the new silica rule along with the primarily shutting down of NIOSH and cutbacks in MSHA will put the miners’ health back into the stone ages.”

Trump also attempted to close 34 MSHA field offices—offices that conducted nearly 17,000 safety inspections in 2024-2025. After massive pushback, he reversed the closures. But the budget cuts remain. The silica rule remains eliminated. The black lung screening program remains halted.

Workers will die. Miners will suffocate. And Trump faces no consequences for dismantling protections that prevent Americans from being killed at work or losing their lungs underground.

Food Safety Collapse: Americans Will Die

Trump’s destruction of food safety oversight guarantees Americans will die from preventable foodborne illness.

Foreign Inspections Plummeted: FDA inspections of foreign food facilities—which produce most of America’s seafood and much of its food supply—dropped nearly 50% in March 2025 and remained about 30% lower through July. ProPublica’s November 2025 investigation, based on interviews with two dozen current and former FDA officials, directly blames Trump’s staffing cuts.

Experts Fired: 89 food safety scientists and experts were “indiscriminately” fired from FDA’s Human Foods Program. The program’s director, Jim Jones, resigned in protest.

Support Staff Eliminated: Critical investigative support staff were cut, leaving inspectors to book their own travel, obtain passports and visas, and process their own expense reimbursements. A backlog of over $1 million in unfulfilled reimbursements accumulated, with inspectors responsible for paying off their own credit cards.

Compliance Delayed: The FSMA 204 Food Traceability Rule compliance date was delayed 30 months—from January 2026 to July 2028.

State Funding Cut: Federal backing for state and local food programs was reduced 30% ($117 million to $83 million).

Standards Eliminated: FDA revoked 52 food standards of identity for cheese, canned goods, dairy, and baked goods—standards that prevent manufacturers from cutting corners.

USDA Gutted: Salmonella enforcement in poultry was indefinitely delayed. CDC FoodNet surveillance was slashed from eight foodborne pathogens to two. Over 15,000 USDA employees took resignation offers.

The stakes are deadly. FDA investigators have found disturbing conditions at foreign facilities: workers hauling dough in soiled buckets, seafood sliding along cracked conveyor belts, crawling insects, dripping pipes, and fake testing data claiming products were pathogen-free.

Each year, roughly 48 million Americans get sick from foodborne diseases, 128,000 are hospitalized, and 3,000 die.

One former FDA official told ProPublica bluntly: “It’s only a matter of time before people die.”

The corporations cutting corners in food production face reduced oversight and reduced consequences. The Americans who will die from foodborne illness have no mechanism to hold anyone accountable. Trump faces no consequences for destroying the system that keeps food safe.

Elder Care Sold for $4.8 Million: The Smoking Gun

If there was any doubt that Trump’s corporate immunity campaign was driven by corruption rather than ideology, the nursing home industry removed it.

In early August 2025, nursing home executives faced a costly problem: Biden’s rule requiring increased staffing levels to reduce neglect and death among elderly residents. The rule mandated 24-hour registered nurse presence and minimum care hours per resident. A 2024 University of Pennsylvania analysis estimated the staffing standards would save 13,000 lives per year.

The industry hated it. They said it would cost too much and cut into profits.

So starting in early August, nursing home companies began making donations that would eventually total $4.8 million to MAGA Inc., Trump’s super PAC.

The donations bought access. Industry executives got a private lunch at Trump’s golf club with the president himself.

Less than a month after those meetings, the Department of Justice stopped defending the Biden-era staffing rule in court.

On December 3, 2025, the Trump administration published an interim final rule withdrawing the minimum staffing requirements entirely. The rule eliminated:

  • The 24/7 registered nurse requirement

  • Minimum staffing hours per resident

  • Protections that would have saved 13,000 lives yearly

The rule went into effect February 2, 2026—just 60 days after publication, with minimal public comment.

Industry groups celebrated. The American Health Care Association called it necessary to prevent facilities from closing.

Consumer advocates were horrified. Sam Brooks, director of public policy for the National Consumer Voice for Quality Long-Term Care, told reporters: “They’re repealing a regulation that could have saved 13,000 lives a year,” citing the University of Pennsylvania analysis.

The New York Times reported that HHS used industry talking points nearly verbatim in justifying the rollback, citing concerns about “disproportionate burden” on facilities—the exact language nursing home lobbyists had used.

This is not deregulation. This is not “cutting red tape.” This is legalized corruption: $4.8 million in donations bought a lunch meeting, which bought a 10-year congressional ban on staffing requirements, which bought 13,000 preventable deaths per year.

The elderly Americans who will die from neglect in understaffed nursing homes cannot hold anyone accountable. The nursing home companies pocketing the savings face no liability. Trump faces no consequences for selling 13,000 American lives per year for $4.8 million in campaign donations.

When corporate viability becomes the highest national priority, American lives become expendable.

The Pattern: Systematic Corporate Immunity

These five examples—glyphosate immunity, OSHA destruction, mining safety elimination, FDA collapse, nursing home corruption—are not isolated decisions. They reveal a systematic pattern:

Grant Explicit Immunity: The glyphosate order’s corporate viability mandate. OSHA penalty reductions giving “free passes.”

Gut Enforcement: FDA inspections down 50%. OSHA inspections down 20%. Black lung screening halted.

Slash Budgets: OSHA budget cut 8%. NIOSH cut 90%. FDA state funding cut 30%.

Fire Experts: 89 FDA scientists. 300 OSHA employees. 1,250 NIOSH workers.

Delay Compliance: FSMA 204 delayed 30 months. Silica safety rule eliminated. Heat safety indefinitely delayed.

Eliminate Standards: 52 food standards removed. 60+ OSHA rules rolled back. Nursing home staffing requirements eliminated.

Accept Bribes: $4.8 million = 13,000 deaths per year.

As documented in Parts 27-28, Trump also repealed the EPA’s endangerment finding, eliminating all federal climate regulation to protect fossil fuel companies. The pattern extends across every domain where corporate profit conflicts with public health.

The approach is systematic: use every lever of presidential power to shield corporations from accountability while Americans bear the costs in death, illness, injury, and financial harm.

The Accountability Void

Trump faces no consequences for granting corporate immunity while Americans die:

No Congressional Action: Republicans control Congress and have shown no interest in protecting workers, consumers, or public health over corporate interests.

No Judicial Relief: Courts have blocked some actions (CFPB mass firings), but litigation takes years. Meanwhile, inspections decline, workers die, miners suffocate, food safety deteriorates, nursing home residents die of neglect.

No Electoral Consequences: Trump’s base sees “deregulation” as economic freedom, not corporate capture. The deaths and illnesses are diffuse, hard to trace to specific policy changes.

No Public Shame: Trump proudly announces these actions as victories. The glyphosate order explicitly prioritizing corporate viability was issued as a White House fact sheet celebrating “national security.”

No Criminal Liability: Using executive power to benefit corporations isn’t prosecuted unless there’s direct quid pro quo—and even when there is (nursing homes), nobody faces charges.

The workers dying because OSHA can’t inspect their workplaces can’t hold Trump accountable. The miners suffocating from black lung can’t sue the president who eliminated silica protections. The cancer victims exposed to glyphosate can’t force Trump to prioritize health over corporate immunity. The Americans sickened by unsafe food can’t restore FDA inspections. The elderly dying in understaffed nursing homes can’t get their lives back or hold anyone responsible for the $4.8 million that bought their neglect.

The system has no mechanism to stop a president from weaponizing executive authority to protect corporate profits at the expense of American lives.

What It Means: The Price Americans Will Pay

These aren’t abstract policy debates. Real Americans will pay real costs:

Workers Will Die: 5,300 workplace deaths per year will increase as inspections decline and safety rules disappear. OSHA already has too few inspectors to cover 11.6 million work sites. With even fewer inspectors and weaker rules, more Americans will be killed on the job.

Miners Will Suffocate: 1,067 prevented deaths and 3,746 prevented illnesses from the silica rule will never happen. Black lung cases will continue rising. One in five long-term miners will lose their lungs.

Food Will Kill: 48 million Americans get foodborne illness yearly. 128,000 are hospitalized. 3,000 die. With foreign inspections down 50% and domestic oversight gutted, those numbers will rise. Former FDA officials warn: “It’s only a matter of time before people die.”

Elders Will Die of Neglect: 13,000 preventable deaths per year from nursing home neglect—deaths that would have been prevented by staffing requirements—will happen. Because $4.8 million bought a lunch, which bought deregulation, which bought 13,000 deaths annually.

Cancer Cases Will Increase: Glyphosate exposure continues with federal immunity protecting manufacturers. Americans will develop cancer. Companies will cite presidential protection.

The consequences are measurable. The deaths are preventable. Trump chose corporate immunity instead.

Conclusion: When Corporate Power Defeats Life Itself

Donald Trump spent his second presidency turning presidential power into a corporate shield.

He granted immunity to companies producing cancer-causing chemicals. He destroyed workplace safety enforcement, ensuring more workers die on the job. He eliminated protections against black lung, guaranteeing miners suffocate. He gutted food safety oversight, guaranteeing Americans die from preventable illness. He sold 13,000 elderly lives per year to nursing home executives for $4.8 million in campaign donations.

And in every case, the pattern was identical: use executive authority to protect corporate profits while Americans pay with their lives.

The glyphosate order’s explicit mandate—ensure corporate viability is not at risk—reveals everything about Trump’s priorities. Corporate survival trumps public health. Even when Americans die.

Trump faces no accountability for any of it. The system designed to stop presidents from weaponizing executive power for corporate benefit has completely failed.

Workers will die. Miners will suffocate. Food will kill. Elders will die of neglect.

And Trump will face zero consequences—because the corporations he protects are more powerful than the Americans he sacrifices.

When corporate viability becomes the highest national priority, American lives become expendable.
