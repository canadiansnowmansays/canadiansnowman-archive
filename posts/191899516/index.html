The Exception Clause

The Enemy Religion

Part V: How America Used Religion to Do the Work of Race

April 15, 2026

On February 19, 1942, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066. The order never mentioned race or religion—yet everyone understood it targeted 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry. When the racial argument felt too raw, the religious argument (Buddhist and Shinto ‘disloyalty’) provided constitutional cover. Zócalo Public Square / historical photo (National Archives collection)

On February 19, 1942, President Franklin Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, authorizing the Secretary of War to designate military areas from which any person could be excluded. The order did not mention Japanese Americans. It did not mention race. It did not mention religion. It did not need to. Everyone who signed it and everyone who read it understood precisely what it meant: that approximately 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry living on the West Coast of the United States — roughly two-thirds of them American citizens by birth — would be removed from their homes and confined in what the government called relocation centers and what they were: internment camps.

The order has been analyzed, litigated, and memorialized for more than eighty years, primarily as a story about race and wartime hysteria — about a government that sacrificed the constitutional rights of an ethnic minority to the panic that followed Pearl Harbor. That analysis is correct as far as it goes. What it tends to omit is the religious dimension of the internment argument: the extent to which Buddhist and Shinto practice was explicitly cited, by government officials and in public discourse, as evidence that Japanese Americans carried a loyalty incompatible with American citizenship.

The omission matters because it reveals something about how the template works at its most sophisticated. Race and religion can be deployed simultaneously, each reinforcing the other, each providing the justification that the other alone might not quite sustain. When the racial argument seemed too naked for constitutional comfort, the religious argument was available. When the religious argument seemed too abstract, the racial argument provided the visceral specificity that mobilized public support. Together, they were more powerful than either would have been alone.

The Religious Argument for Internment

The case against Japanese American loyalty was made on multiple grounds simultaneously, and the religious grounds were among the most prominent.

Shinto, in its state form, was the official religion of imperial Japan — the theological framework that undergirded the divine status of the Emperor and the spiritual mission of the Japanese nation. State Shinto was genuinely militaristic and genuinely implicated in Japanese imperial ideology. The problem was that State Shinto in Japan had essentially nothing to do with the religious practices of Japanese Americans in California, Oregon, and Washington, who practiced a range of Buddhist traditions, various forms of Christianity, and what anthropologists would describe as folk religious practices — none of which bore any operational relationship to the imperial ideology of the Japanese state.

This distinction was not made in the internment arguments. “Japanese religion” became a monolithic category, encompassing both the imperial theology of Tokyo and the meditation practices of a Buddhist temple in Los Angeles, and the entire category was labeled as evidence of unassimilable foreign loyalty. The fact that many Japanese Americans were Christians — some of them third-generation members of American Protestant denominations — did not substantially complicate the argument, because the argument was not really about religious practice. It was about race, using religion as its constitutional clothing.

Lieutenant General John DeWitt, the Western Defense Commander who administered the internment program and whose recommendation to the War Department was the proximate cause of Executive Order 9066, was explicit about the underlying logic: “A Jap’s a Jap,” he told a congressional committee in April 1943. “It makes no difference whether he is an American citizen or not... You can’t change him by giving him a piece of paper.” DeWitt’s recommendation to the Secretary of War had argued that the “racial strains” of Japanese Americans were “undiluted” and that “the Japanese race is an enemy race.”

The religious framing was the respectable version of DeWitt’s argument — the version that could appear in official documents without quite saying what DeWitt said out loud. Buddhist temples were described as centers of Japanese nationalist organization. Shinto shrines were characterized as sites of loyalty to the Emperor. Japanese-language schools, many of which were attached to Buddhist temples, were described as instruments of ideological programming. The argument required treating religious institutions as political ones — as outposts of a foreign power rather than as communities of worship — which is precisely the move that the template makes in every chapter of this series.

Korematsu and the Constitution

Fred Korematsu, 23-year-old American citizen born in Oakland, California, who refused the exclusion order. His case reached the Supreme Court as Korematsu v. United States (1944). Justice Hugo Black’s majority opinion declared racial classifications ‘immediately suspect’—then upheld them under the military necessity exception. The dissenters called it ‘legalization of racism / NPR / Code Switch

The constitutional challenge to the internment reached the Supreme Court in 1944, in Korematsu v. United States. Fred Korematsu was a twenty-three-year-old American citizen, born in Oakland, California, who had refused to report for relocation. He was convicted of violating the exclusion order and appealed on the ground that the order was an unconstitutional racial classification.

The Supreme Court ruled against him six to three.

Justice Hugo Black’s majority opinion acknowledged, for the first time in the court’s history, that racial classifications in law were “immediately suspect” and subject to the “most rigid scrutiny.” It then applied that scrutiny and found that the military necessity of the internment justified the racial classification. The language of security — the fourth element of the template — had found its constitutional formulation: when the government asserts military necessity, even the most suspect classification can survive.

The three dissenters — Justices Roberts, Murphy, and Jackson — were unsparing. Justice Murphy called the decision a “legalization of racism” and described the military’s argument as based on “an accumulation of much of the misinformation, half-truths and insinuations that for years have been directed against Japanese Americans by people with racial and economic prejudices.” Justice Jackson, in a passage that has become one of the most cited in constitutional law, warned that validating the military’s reasoning would convert the principle into “a loaded weapon ready for the hand of any authority that can bring forward a plausible claim of an urgent need.”

Jackson was right. Korematsu was not formally overturned until 2018, when Chief Justice Roberts, writing in Trump v. Hawaii — a case involving the Trump administration’s travel ban targeting Muslim-majority countries — acknowledged that Korematsu had been “gravely wrong the day it was decided.” The acknowledgment was significant and notably belated. It also came in an opinion that upheld the travel ban.

The loaded weapon that Jackson had warned about in 1944 was still being fired in 2018.

McCarthyism and the Godless Enemy

The Cold War introduced a different theological framing into American political life — one that did not target a specific religious minority but that structured the entire domestic persecution campaign of the early 1950s in explicitly religious terms.

Communism was “godless.” This was not merely a rhetorical epithet. It was a theological argument — the claim that Soviet Communism, by its atheist foundations, was incompatible with the spiritual basis of American civilization, and that Americans who were attracted to it had, in some sense, apostasized from the national faith. The phrase “one nation under God” was added to the Pledge of Allegiance in 1954, explicitly to distinguish the United States from the godless Soviet adversary. “In God We Trust” became the national motto in 1956 for the same reason.

The “godless communism” framework did several things simultaneously. It provided a theological legitimation for the persecution of political dissidents — not merely as political enemies but as spiritual traitors. It fused American national identity with Protestant Christianity more explicitly than at any point since the founding, constructing a civic religion in which belonging to the nation and belonging to a broadly Christian faith were effectively synonymous. And it created a category of religious suspicion that could be applied to anyone whose theological commitments were deemed insufficiently orthodox — not just Communists, but Jews whose political associations were suspect, Protestant ministers who had signed peace petitions, Catholics whose loyalty to Rome rather than to American anti-communism was questioned.

Senator Joseph McCarthy (left) with his chief counsel Roy Cohn (right) during the 1954 Army-McCarthy hearings. Cohn, the aggressive young lawyer who helped drive the “godless communism” purges and loyalty tests, later became Donald Trump’s personal attorney and mentor in the 1970s–80s, teaching him the combative style of attack and counterattack. The same theological and suspicion-based framing used against alleged communists was later repurposed in other contexts — showing how the template of fusing loyalty, ideology, and power persists and gets handed down / Source: Bettmann Archive

Senator Joseph McCarthy himself was a Catholic, which created a peculiar dynamic: a man whose religious community had spent a century being accused of foreign loyalty was now deploying the same accusation against others, using a different theological frame. The irony was structural — the template does not require its practitioners to have clean hands, only to have access to the mechanism.

The House Un-American Activities Committee’s investigations targeted, among others, a substantial number of Jewish intellectuals and artists — a targeting that was not officially framed in religious terms but that drew on the same reservoir of suspicion about Jewish loyalty and Jewish radicalism that had characterized American antisemitism for decades. The theological and the racial were, again, doing the same work from different angles.

The Persistence of the Fusion

The fusion of racial and religious targeting that the Japanese internment illustrates most clearly did not end with the internment. It is a structural feature of the template — available whenever the racial argument alone seems constitutionally or socially unsustainable, and whenever the religious argument alone lacks the visceral specificity needed to mobilize public support.

It appears in the targeting of Black Muslim communities from the 1950s onward — J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI surveilled the Nation of Islam not merely as a political organization but as a religious one, with considerable attention to the theological content of its teachings, from the 1950s through Hoover’s death in 1972. It appears in the post-9/11 period, when the religious framing — Islam as an ideology incompatible with American values — provided the constitutional language for what was simultaneously a racial targeting of Arab, South Asian, and Middle Eastern communities. It appears in the Trump-era travel bans, where the administration’s lawyers worked to find non-religious framing for policies that the president himself had publicly described as a “Muslim ban.”

The lesson of the internment is not simply that the government can confine people in camps when it decides their religion makes them enemies. It is that the government can do so while maintaining, with varying degrees of plausibility, that race has nothing to do with it — that the concern is theological, or security-related, or about the specific institutions of a foreign power, or about anything other than the racial classification that is actually doing the work.

The template, it should be noted, was not uniquely American. Canada interned approximately 22,000 Japanese Canadians under the War Measures Act — proportionally more than the American internment, and with harsher consequences: their homes and businesses were sold by the government while they were confined, and some were deported to Japan after the war ended.

Hugh Keenleyside, a senior official in Canada’s Department of External Affairs, argued against the internment from the beginning. He was not a marginal figure. He had served as secretary to the Canadian delegation at the 1930 London Naval Conference, was one of the principal architects of the 1940 Permanent Joint Board on Defence established with Roosevelt, and understood better than most what the wartime alliance required and what it did not. His argument against internment was both principled and practical: it served no genuine security purpose, it violated the rights of Canadian citizens, and it would compromise Canada’s postwar credibility as a nation that meant what it said about democratic values. He was overruled. The political pressure from British Columbia — driven by the same combination of racial anxiety and economic competition that had fueled anti-Asian sentiment on the West Coast for decades — proved more powerful than the constitutional argument.

The template does not respect borders. It respects opportunity.

Korematsu‘s “military necessity” exception is the most powerful legal tool the template has ever been given. It remains available. It has been used within the lifetime of most people reading this series.

Fred Korematsu’s Last Act

Fred Korematsu receives the Presidential Medal of Freedom from President Bill Clinton in 1998 — fifty-six years after his conviction under Executive Order 9066. In 2004, the year before his death, Korematsu filed an amicus brief warning that the ‘military necessity’ exception used to justify his internment was being revived at Guantánamo Bay. The loaded weapon Justice Jackson warned about in 1944 remained loaded.

Fred Korematsu spent most of his adult life trying to clear his name. In 1983, a legal team led by Peter Irons discovered that the government had suppressed evidence in the original Korematsu case — evidence that the military’s claims of Japanese American disloyalty had no factual basis. A federal district court vacated his conviction. The Supreme Court’s ruling was not touched.

In 2004, the year before his death, Korematsu filed an amicus brief in Rumsfeld v. Padilla and Rasul v. Bush, the cases involving the detention of enemy combatants at Guantanamo Bay. His brief argued that the military necessity exception the government was invoking to justify indefinite detention without charge was the same exception that had been used to justify his own internment sixty years earlier. He was asking the court to remember what it had blessed and to decline to bless it again.

The court’s decisions in those cases were partial and procedural. The military necessity exception was not categorically rejected. The loaded weapon remained loaded.

Korematsu received the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1998, from President Clinton, who noted that the Supreme Court’s ruling in his case had been “overruled in the court of history.” The court of law took another twenty years.

He died in 2005. He had spent sixty-three years as a convicted criminal for the act of remaining in the city where he was born.
