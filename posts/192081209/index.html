The Iran Crisis

“The Field Marshal and the Five-Day Clock”

Part LVII: Day Twenty-Eight: Pakistan Offers to Host Talks, the 82nd Airborne Deploys, and Iran Cannot Be Bombed Into a Deal It Cannot Communicate

March 25, 2026

An apartment shattered by an airstrike in Tehran on Monday.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

I. The Diplomat the War Produced

On Tuesday, Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif of Pakistan posted on X that his country “stands ready and honoured to be the host to facilitate meaningful and conclusive talks for a comprehensive settlement of the ongoing conflict.” President Trump shared a screenshot on Truth Social. The war, now in its fourth week, had found its unlikely mediator: Pakistan.

Adam Rasgon, Julian Barnes, and Farnaz Fassihi, writing in the New York Times, confirmed what the social media exchange had signaled: the United States has sent Iran a 15-point plan to end the war, delivered by way of Pakistan. The plan addresses Iran’s ballistic missile and nuclear programs — the issues that caused the breakdown of negotiations between Kushner, Witkoff, and Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi that led to the February 28 strikes — and discusses maritime routes, meaning the Strait of Hormuz. The Times did not see a copy of the plan. Israel’s position on it remains unclear.

The man carrying the plan is Pakistan’s Army Chief, Field Marshal Syed Asim Munir — described by Trump as his “favorite field marshal,” a man who met twice with Trump in 2025 and who is believed to maintain close ties to the IRGC, giving him the ability to pass messages between the warring sides that no Gulf state can currently provide. Saudi Arabia and Qatar expelled Iranian diplomats weeks ago. Egypt and Turkey are encouraging Iran to engage. Pakistan is the only country with credibility on both sides of this war.

Pakistan brings specific advantages as a mediator. It is a non-Arab Muslim country, like Iran, and does not host a US military base — sparing it from Iranian strikes that have hit Kuwait, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar. It shares a 560-mile border with Iran. Nearly a fifth of its 240 million people are Shiite Muslims. And it has a powerful economic incentive: 81 percent of Pakistan’s oil imports come from the Gulf, making it among the first countries to provide military escorts for its own ships through the Hormuz.

The Pakistani military chief, Field Marshal Syed Asim Munir, seen on a billboard in Lahore last year, has emerged as the main intermediary between Iran and the United States.Credit...Arif Ali/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Trump said on Tuesday that Vice President Vance and Secretary of State Rubio were now joining Witkoff and Kushner in the negotiations — a significant escalation of the diplomatic tier, suggesting the administration is treating the five-day window more seriously than Iranian public statements suggest.

There is, however, a structural problem that no mediator can solve. The Times reported that senior Iranian officials have been struggling to communicate internally, and they fear that Israel will bomb them if they attempt to meet in person. On the first day of the war, Israel struck an Iranian leadership compound, killing Khamenei and dozens of top officials. Who now holds the power to make decisions on diplomacy, war and peace remains uncertain. The regime that must accept or reject the 15-point plan may not have a functioning decision-making structure capable of doing so.

Iran’s public position remains unchanged: no negotiations are underway. Its practical position, as confirmed through intermediaries, is that it wants a sustainable peace deal with guarantees against future US-Israeli attacks, and specific sanctions relief — terms the US has historically said would only come after Iran delivered on nuclear commitments. The distance between the public positions and the private ones is the space in which Pakistan is working.

II. The Drone That Cannot Be Stopped

A missile and a Shahed drone on display during a military parade in Tehran in 2025.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

While diplomats negotiate, the weapons continue to be manufactured.

Nicholas Kulish, writing in the Times, has documented the operational reality that complicates every military objective in this war: Iran’s Shahed drones cannot be reliably eliminated through airstrikes, because the technology that produces them cannot be reliably destroyed.

The Shahed-136 — the primary weapon Iran has been using against Gulf states — is just over eight feet wide and approximately twelve feet long. It travels at 115 miles per hour, has a range of up to 1,500 miles, carries a 90-pound warhead, and costs an estimated $35,000 per unit. Against it, Gulf states are firing interceptors costing millions of dollars each and scrambling fighter jets that must slow nearly to stall speed to engage it. The arithmetic is Iran’s friend.

The deeper problem is production. The Shahed requires no specialized facility — bent aluminum, 3D-printed components, a basic motorcycle engine. US Central Command released before-and-after images of a drone factory near Isfahan University of Technology that it had destroyed. The images are real. The production problem is not solved. “If it’s relatively easy to do, to bend aluminum, to 3-D print, a basic motorcycle engine, then it’s harder to track where it’s coming from,” said Maximilian Bremer of the Stimson Center. Yasir Atalan of CSIS put it more directly: “Iran will be able to produce more if this war continues.”

Iran was launching more than 400 drones per day on March 1. It is now launching 70-90 per day — a significant degradation, but not elimination. Iran has focused its remaining capacity on the Gulf states, which are closer than Israel, giving air defenses less reaction time. Saudi Arabia intercepted at least 30 drones since Tuesday evening. Kuwait intercepted six more, and drones hit a fuel tank at Kuwait International Airport, starting a fire that took hours to extinguish. Bahrain’s air raid sirens sounded again Wednesday morning.

Russia’s involvement adds a dimension no US airstrike can address. Russia was potentially producing as many as 1,000 drones per day and has every incentive to ensure the Strait of Hormuz remains closed — its own oil revenues rise with global prices, and every interceptor fired at an Iranian drone is one less that can be shipped to Ukraine. Ukraine, for its part, has sent more than 200 drone experts to the Middle East to help Gulf states develop the cheap-interceptor tactics it developed against Russian Shahed attacks. The war in Iran has become a live testing ground for the same weapons ecosystem that is fighting in Eastern Europe.

The strategic implication is stark: the United States can degrade Iran’s drone capacity, but it cannot eliminate it without ending the industrial base that produces it — which is distributed, concealed, and partially reconstructable in a workshop. The promise to Gulf allies that the Shahed barrage will eventually stop may be a promise the United States cannot keep.

III. You Want to Leave Us Alone With Mojtaba?

Fatemeh Bahram / Andalou / Getty

Arash Azizi, writing in The Atlantic, has spoken with more than a dozen Iranians over the past three weeks — an act of reporting that requires noting: Iran’s internet blackout is now in its fourth week, the most severe digital shutdown ever recorded globally, and each contact carried risk for the people he reached.

What he found is a paradox that the war’s architects in Washington do not appear to have accounted for: Iranians increasingly fear not just the continuation of the war, but its end.

“I am shit-scared,” said Shaghayeh, a 32-year-old left-wing activist in Tehran. “But I won’t cheer if the war ends now. You want to leave us alone with Mojtaba?”

Mojtaba Khamenei — the new Supreme Leader, his father’s son, the man students are calling “Rat Ali” and whose toy-rat effigies hang from university trees — is what a negotiated peace leaves in place. Trump and Netanyahu have vacillated throughout this war on whether their demands include regime change. The 15-point plan, as the Times reported, suggests the administration “would be willing to leave the current regime in place, at least for now, albeit in a weakened and more pliant state.”

For Iranians who spent January watching their fellow citizens massacred in the streets, that outcome is not liberation. It is the regime, more brutal and more paranoid, having survived American bombs.

“I thought they had concluded that an Iran without the Islamic Republic was in their best interests too,” said Shahrzad, 29, who moved to Europe weeks before the war. “Now I know this isn’t their priority.” She pointed to America’s apparent willingness to negotiate with Khamenei’s inner circle: “I can now see that their intention is perhaps to make Iran weaker than before, even if the regime stays in power.”

Melika, 21, also now in Europe, captured the emotional arithmetic precisely: “When Khamenei died, I was happy, but only for a moment — like you get a hit from a drug. It didn’t even last a day. After that I’ve only felt one thing: fear, fear, fear.”

The uprising that was supposed to follow the opening strikes — the Mossad plan, the Trump promise, “take over your government, it will be yours to take” — never came. The bombs kept falling. The internet stayed dark. The regime mobilized its security forces. And now Iranians who despised the government are being asked to accept a peace that leaves it in power, having survived, having demonstrated that survival is enough.

“The war upsets me, of course,” Shahrzad said. “But I am worried that when it ends, the regime will be even worse.”

IV. The Knock

Marie-Rose Sheinerman, writing in The Atlantic, has documented what the numbers in this series’ daily tallies cannot: the 13 American families who received the knock.

Tyler Simmons wanted to be in the air from the time he was a child. He and his father would pull over on streets with a clear view of airport runways, watching planes take off and land. A Tuskegee Airman deacon at their Columbus, Ohio church took the family to an airfield when Tyler was in ninth grade. “The next thing we know, we’re up in the air, and Tyler’s flying the plane,” his father Mylo said. Tyler enlisted in 2017, worked up to technical sergeant, became a boom operator — the specialist who refuels tanker aircraft mid-flight, one of the most demanding jobs in the Air Force. He died earlier this month when his plane crashed over Iraq along with five other American service members.

Valerie Plesch / Bloomberg / Getty

“The doorbell rang, and when I opened the door and I saw the military, I said, Oh no. Oh no,” his father said. “The worst thing you can imagine.”

These are the first US service members lost in a foreign war since the withdrawal from Afghanistan in 2021. Taryn Davis, whose husband Army Corporal Michael Davis was killed in Baghdad in 2007, told Sheinerman: “I don’t think anyone who has lost a family member in war can look at any number of casualties and not feel like it’s already too much.”

The families process their losses in public, surrounded by obligations — flights to Dover, dignified transfers, funerals, paperwork — while the war continues and the news cycle moves on. Hegseth has announced at each press briefing that “today will be the largest strike package yet.” He has not spoken of the families who receive the knock while he speaks.

V. What the Markets Tell Trump

David McNew / Getty

Will Gottsegen, writing in The Atlantic, has confirmed through reporting what this series documented as a question on March 23: Trump’s major policy announcements are timed with trading hours in mind — and the Iran war has made that pattern more visible than ever.

John Bolton, who served as Trump’s national security adviser from 2018 to 2019, told The Atlantic that during Trump’s first term, foreign-policy announcements were sometimes timed with trading hours in mind. Those calls were made both by the president himself and by Treasury officials who advised him. The initial Liberation Day tariff announcement last April was deliberately delayed until the end of the trading day — “Atlantic reporting has confirmed” — to limit the immediate market damage.

Trump has alerted aides, according to Gottsegen’s reporting, when he thought an upcoming social media post would get a reaction from Wall Street — and then watched the market ticker move in real time.

Thomas Wright of the Brookings Institution told The Atlantic that the Iran war has clarified Trump’s sensitivity to oil prices specifically: “We’ve discovered in the Iran war that he’s also very sensitive to the price of oil — which we could have gathered from what he said over many years — but I think we have some evidence to it.”

Trump himself confirmed the connection after announcing the five-day pause: “The price of oil will drop like a rock as soon as a deal is done. I guess it already is today.”

Iran is watching. Gottsegen notes that the more Trump reacts to market pressure, the more predictable his reversal points become — and the more easily he can be exploited by a regime that has spent four decades studying American domestic politics. The living room strategy, as Sadjadpour documented, depends on making the economic cost too high for the American public. The bond market’s 4.40 percent threshold is now publicly documented as the trigger. The Strait of Hormuz is the lever. Iran does not need to win militarily. It needs to hold the lever long enough for the economics to do their work.

The New York Times has published the definitive visual accounting of what this war has severed — a flow diagram tracing every barrel of oil and cubic meter of gas from Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Iraq, and the UAE through the 35-mile strait to its destinations across Asia, Europe, and the rest of the world. Four weeks in, that flow is essentially zero. The graphic is worth a minute of your time. [See the full Strait of Hormuz visualization here.]

VI. The War Expands

The five-day pause covers US strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure. The war itself is not pausing.

The Pentagon has ordered approximately 2,000 soldiers from the 82nd Airborne Division’s Immediate Response Force to begin moving to the Middle East — including Major General Brandon Tegtmeier, the division commander, and dozens of his staff, plus two battalions of approximately 800 soldiers each. Combined with 4,500 Marines already en route, the total additional ground troops dispatched since the war began now approaches 7,000. The overall operation — code-named Epic Fury — involves approximately 50,000 US troops across the region. It is unclear where the paratroopers will be positioned, but officials confirmed the location would be “within striking distance of Iran.” The Kharg Island seizure option remains on the table.

Israel’s defense minister announced that Israel plans to expand the territory under its control in southern Lebanon — signaling a ramping-up of the ground offensive against Hezbollah. Lebanon responded by ordering the expulsion of Iran’s newly appointed ambassador, a rare and significant rebuke of Tehran over its backing of Hezbollah. Israeli warplanes roared low over Beirut overnight, striking Hezbollah command centers and gas stations, continuing until dawn.

Saudi Arabia’s MBS has been pressing Trump to continue the war, arguing the US-Israeli military campaign presents what he has characterized as “a historic opportunity” to remake the Middle East. This is the same Saudi Arabia that expelled Iran’s military attaché weeks ago, that has seen its energy infrastructure struck repeatedly, and whose foreign minister warned that “what little trust” existed with Iran had been “completely shattered.” MBS’s interest is not in ending the war quickly. It is in ensuring the war ends with Iran permanently weakened.

In western Iraq, a second deadly strike in two days hit military infrastructure belonging to the Popular Mobilization Forces — killing seven soldiers and wounding 13, striking near an army medical center in Anbar Province. Iraq’s Defense Ministry described the attack as “a dangerous escalation that requires a firm response” without naming the perpetrator. The expansion of the war’s ground footprint into Iraqi territory — whether acknowledged or not — carries its own escalatory logic.

China’s Foreign Minister Wang Yi spoke with Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi at Iran’s request on Tuesday, calling on “all sides to seize every opportunity” for peace. China has now publicly inserted itself into the diplomatic frame — a development worth watching as the 15-point plan works its way through Pakistani channels toward a regime struggling to communicate.

Iran sent a letter to the International Maritime Organization, dated March 22, stating that “non-hostile” ships — defined as those not tied to the US or Israel — may pass safely through the Strait of Hormuz. Around 800 tankers remain idling on either side of the strait. Shipping analysts were skeptical. The conditional nature of the letter, one analyst noted, “introduces uncertainty, which is typically enough for operators and insurers to stay cautious.” A small number of ships have passed through in recent days. A widespread return will not happen without a peace deal.

VII. The Missile in the Schoolyard

The fragment visible in the NYT’s “In One Image” feature is, according to Jeffrey Lewis of the Middlebury Institute for International Studies, the remnants of an Iranian Ghadr or Emad ballistic missile — variants of the Shahab-3, itself based on a North Korean medium-range missile. It landed on the grounds of the Shilat school at the Peduel settlement in the West Bank, approximately 15 miles east of Tel Aviv.

The girl in the photograph is Maayan Cohen, eight years old. Her family had been sleeping at the school’s shelter because their caravan home has no safe room. Phone warnings woke them around midnight. Her brother ran to close the shelter door. They heard a loud explosion.

When Maayan’s mother was texted an image of the fragment the next morning, she thought it was AI-generated. Maayan recognized the site immediately and became very frightened. Her mother let her and some friends go and look at the fragment — wanting them to understand what had happened rather than let their imaginations inflate their fears.

A number of the children asked: if this is shrapnel, then what counts as an entire missile?
