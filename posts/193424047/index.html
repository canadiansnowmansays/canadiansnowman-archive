The Iran Crisis

Top Ten Reasons to TACO

Or: Why the Art of the Deal Requires Knowing When to Fold

April 7, 2026

A satellite view of the Strait of Hormuz. Stocktrek/Photodisc/Getty Images

Tuesday, April 7, 2026

The deadline is tonight. Eight o’clock Eastern. Power Plant Day and Bridge Day.

Donald Trump has promised — four times now, across six weeks — to destroy every power plant and bridge in Iran if the Strait of Hormuz is not opened. Tonight is the night he either follows through or doesn’t. The world is watching. Markets are watching. Ninety-three million Iranians are watching. And one Iranian musician is sitting on a carpet in front of the Damavand power plant with a blanket and his instrument, because if there is no power, there is no water.

From my vantage point in Toronto, where we have been watching this war with the particular clarity that geographic distance provides, I want to offer the president something he clearly lacks: a case for why walking away — TACOing, in the framework this series has used since Part I — is not weakness. It is the only move that makes sense.

This is not a partisan argument. It is a strategic one.

Top Ten reasons. In ascending order of importance.

10. The deadline has already been extended four times. One more is not surrender. It is pattern.

March 21. March 23. March 26. April 6. Now April 8. If Trump extends again, he is not abandoning a principle. He is continuing a well-established pattern that the markets have already priced in, that Iran has already planned around, and that America’s allies have already built their diplomatic calendars around.

The fifth extension is not humiliation. It is Tuesday.

9. The colonel just came home. This is the best exit ramp you will ever have.

The rescue of the Air Force colonel from a 7,000-foot ridgeline in southwest Iran is the single most cinematically perfect event of the entire war. SEAL Team 6. CIA deception campaign. Bright orange bomb blasts lighting up mountain silhouettes. Two C-130s blown up in the Iranian desert. Three replacement planes lifting off as the sun rose on Easter Sunday. “WE GOT HIM.”

That is a victory. It is a real one. No one can take it away. It is the kind of story that becomes a movie, a book, a recruiting poster.

Use it. Declare a strategic pause — pending negotiations — and let the colonel’s homecoming be the image the week ends on. The exit ramp does not get more scenic than this.

8. The war crimes accounting is real, and it will follow you.

One hundred legal experts. The Geneva Conventions. The Nuremberg Principles. The UN Charter. Edward Wong’s accounting in the Times is meticulous and will be cited for decades.

You said in January, in the Oval Office, that you don’t need international law. You said your only limit is your own morality.

Your morality, then, is the only thing standing between 93 million Iranians and darkness. That is a sentence that will appear in every history of this war. It will appear in the accountability literature for the rest of your life.

There is still time to not be the president who bombed every power plant in a country simultaneously. That distinction, once claimed, cannot be unclaimed.

7. Iran’s 10-point proposal is actually something to work with.

Strip out the maximalist demands — the congressional approval requirement, the UN guarantee — and what remains is this: Iran will lift the blockade. Iran will allow ships through. Iran wants to use a $2 million per ship toll, split with Oman, to fund reconstruction. Iran wants sanctions lifted.

That is not nothing. That is, in fact, closer to a deal than anything that existed on February 27.

The art of the deal is not getting everything. It is getting enough to declare victory and leave. Steve Witkoff is in the room. Jared Kushner is in the room. JD Vance may be in Budapest but his phone works. Pakistan has been on the phone continuously for six weeks.

There is something here. “Not good enough” is a negotiating position. It does not have to be the final answer.

6. The interceptors are running out.

The UAE has expended more than three-quarters of its PAC-3 stockpile. Kuwait is counting shrapnel in residential areas. Bahrain’s petrochemical facilities are on fire. The defensive architecture that has kept the Gulf’s glittering towers standing is depleting faster than it can be replenished.

If you bomb the power plants and Iran retaliates — as it has promised to do “much more crushingly and extensively” — the interceptors may not be there. The desalination plants that give Gulf citizens water may not survive the response. The Saudi oil facilities that survived this far may not survive the next wave.

The allies whose bases you need, whose airspace you use, whose intelligence you depend on, are already at the edge. They are asking you to finish the job you started. But finishing it by triggering a retaliatory wave that they cannot defend against is not finishing the job. It is destroying the coalition.

5. China has been preparing for this for years. Every week the war continues, Beijing wins.

Xi Jinping does not need to fire a single missile. He needs the war to continue for another few months. China has stockpiled oil, shifted to coal-based petrochemicals, built a fortress economy designed to withstand severe commodity disruptions. Meanwhile Japan, South Korea, the Philippines, India — the democracies that anchor the Indo-Pacific alliance — are in crisis. Energy rationing. State of emergency declarations. Violence at gas stations.

Every week the Strait stays closed is a week China’s competitors weaken and China’s resilience advantage compounds. Robert Pape at the University of Chicago has already named it: Iran is becoming a fourth center of global power. Russia is recouping $40 billion in oil exports. Iran is earning more per day than before the war.

The people winning this war are not on your side.

4. The Bab al-Mandab is the next domino.

Iran’s new supreme leader’s advisor has already threatened to close the Red Sea chokepoint. The Houthis are already there. If the power plant strikes trigger the retaliation Iran has promised, and if that retaliation includes activating the Bab al-Mandab, the global shipping crisis stops being an energy crisis and becomes a civilizational one.

There is no shipping route around both straits simultaneously. There is no quick fix. There is no “two to three weeks.”

The domino you do not want to tip is not in Iran. It is at the southern end of the Red Sea.

3. The intelligence community was right about everything. You were told.

Shane Harris documented it definitively in The Atlantic this week. The Defense Intelligence Agency said Iran’s ICBM capability was 2035 at the earliest. The intelligence community said Iran would close Hormuz and strike Gulf neighbors. Your own officials briefed Arab governments on this likelihood before the war. Qatar told your team directly. A European intelligence partner concluded the same and shared it with Washington.

You said nobody expected that. The record shows otherwise.

The intelligence community cannot save you from Tuesday night’s decision. But it can tell you what happens next. And what it is telling you — that Iran’s new leadership is more hawkish, not less, that the IRGC is consolidating power, that the calculus on nuclear weapons has shifted — is not a case for escalation.

It is a case for the table.

3. The missing pilot scenario is still possible.

You mentioned Jimmy Carter. You mention him often. You know exactly what 444 days did to a presidency, to a country, to a man.

You got the colonel back. It was extraordinary. But there are more aircraft flying over Iran every day. There are more pilots. There are more weapons systems officers. The shoulder-fired missile that downed the F-15E cost a fraction of what the rescue mission cost. Iran has more of them.

The hostage scenario that ended Carter’s presidency did not happen this week. It can still happen. The probability of it happening increases with every new mission flown, every new deadline issued, every new escalation that keeps the war going.

You escaped Carter’s fate by thirty-eight hours and the extraordinary work of SEAL Team 6. That is not a reason to press further. It is the strongest possible argument for taking the exit ramp that just appeared.

2. Legacy is written tonight, not later.

You have spent your entire public life thinking about how history will remember you. The buildings with your name on them. The Forbes list. The ratings. The crowd size. The presidential portrait. The library. You have said repeatedly that you want to be remembered as the greatest president in American history. You have said it more times than any president since the republic was founded.

Here is what the legacy calculus looks like at 8 PM tonight.

If you bomb the power plants: you become the first American president in history to deliberately destroy the civilian infrastructure of a nation of 93 million people. One hundred legal experts have already said it. The International Court of Justice will eventually say it. The historians will say it. The schoolchildren of Iran — the ones whose schools you have already struck, the ones who grew up in darkness because the power went out and never came back — will say it for generations. “The man who bombed us back to the Stone Ages” is a sentence that will be translated into Farsi and taught in Iranian schools for a hundred years. It is not the legacy of the greatest president. It is the legacy of a war criminal. And the distinction between those two things, once the bombs fall, cannot be argued away by any lawyer, any press secretary, or any Truth Social post.

If you TACO: you are the president who launched the most complex special operations rescue mission in American history and brought a colonel home from a mountain crevice in Iran on Easter Sunday. You are the president who degraded Iran’s military more than any predecessor. You are the president who held the line on nuclear weapons. You are the president who, when the moment came, chose negotiation over annihilation — and can call that strength, because only a strong man walks away from a fight he has already won enough of.

Nixon went to China. Reagan negotiated with Gorbachev. Neither was called weak for it. Both are in the history books for it.

The legacy you want is still available. It requires walking away from the legacy you do not want, before 8 PM tonight.

A Note from the Analytical Record — Added Tuesday Morning

Before the deadline arrives, three things published this morning deserve to be part of this case.

Karim Sadjadpour, one of the foremost Iran scholars in the world, explained in The Atlantic this morning why every pressure tactic fails and will continue to fail. The regime’s hostility to America is not a bargaining chip. It is the foundation of their identity — what political scientists call ontological security. You cannot negotiate away the thing that justifies your existence. The carrots America offers the nation are sticks to the men who rule it. The sticks America wields against the regime are carrots to men whose power depends on isolation, conflict, and chaos.

This is not an argument for surrender. It is an argument for understanding your adversary before you bomb their power plants. The understanding that Sadjadpour offers makes the bombing less likely to work, not more.

David Graham, writing in The Atlantic, noted that Trump’s most inflammatory statements come when he feels cornered — and that a president who claims a former president privately told him he regretted not striking Iran, a claim all three living former Democratic presidents’ advisors denied, is beginning to sound like a different former president: Richard Nixon, who had conversations with portraits on the White House walls as his presidency collapsed.

And Barbara Perry, a presidential historian, placed the Easter Sunday post in its proper historical context. Every profane president before this one — Johnson, Nixon, Kennedy — confined their blue language to private. Trump posted it on a platform he owns on the holiest morning of the Christian calendar. Perry’s contrast: Pope Leo XIV on Easter Sunday said “Let those who have weapons lay them down.” The American-born pope. The response from the American president was “Open the Fuckin’ Strait, you crazy bastards.”

These three pieces, taken together, make the case that tonight’s deadline is not a moment of strength. It is a moment of a cornered man, facing an adversary that cannot be bombed into ideological surrender, threatening actions that no previous American president has threatened publicly, on a day that no previous American president would have chosen for such a threat.

The exit ramp is still there. The case for taking it has never been stronger.

1. The strait was open on February 27. It can be open again.

This is the simplest reason of all, and the most important.

The Strait of Hormuz was open before the war began. Ships moved freely. Oil flowed. The global economy functioned. Iran was under sanctions, contained, unpopular with its own people, and strategically weakened.

Six weeks later: the strait is closed. Iran controls the chokepoint. Oil is up 60 percent. Gas is at a record $4.12 nationally. 13 Americans are dead. 373 are wounded. 1,616 Iranian civilians are confirmed dead, including 244 children. Sharif University — Iran’s MIT — is rubble. The Mahshahr petrochemical complex that employed 300,000 people needs two years to rebuild. Bushehr nuclear plant was struck 75 meters from the reactor.

And Iran is a fourth global power.

The goal of the war — a free and open strait, a weakened Iran, a safer region — is further away than it was on February 27.

The art of the deal is knowing when the deal you are chasing has already cost more than it is worth. The art of the deal is knowing when the exit ramp is more valuable than the destination.

Tonight at 8 PM Eastern, you have a choice. You can bomb the power plants and own everything that follows. Or you can TACO — and let the colonel’s sunrise homecoming be the image history remembers.

One of those options has an exit. The other does not.

From Toronto, on a night when I am not sleeping either, I hope you find it.

Postscript

April 7, 8:29 PM Eastern:

He read it. Or someone did. At 6:35 PM Eastern, ninety minutes before his own deadline, Donald Trump announced a two-week ceasefire. The power plants are still standing. The bridges are still there. A French literature teacher and her partner are on their way home to Paris after four years in Evin Prison. The strait will open, with conditions. The civilization did not die tonight.

The exit ramp was there. He took it.

From Toronto, I am going to sleep.
