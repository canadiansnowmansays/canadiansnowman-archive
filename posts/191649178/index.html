PROJECT HAIL MAGA

A Space Odyssey for the Rest of Us

March 22, 2026

With a 48-hour Trump ultimatum threatening to obliterate Iranian power plants unless the Strait of Hormuz fully reopens — a deadline that expires Monday evening — the world this Sunday morning could use a deep breath. Consider this your chill pill — administered with affection, a red Tesla Roadster, and a mannequin with a security clearance.

Dr. Ryland Grace opens his eyes.

He is alone on a spaceship.

He does not know how he got here.

This, he will later learn, is entirely by design.

The amnesia is not a malfunction. The amnesia is a feature. It was written into his employment contract — page 47, subsection 12(b), just below the non-disclosure agreement and just above the clause prohibiting him from discussing solar science with members of the legacy media.

He tries to remember. The flashbacks come slowly, in fragments, the way inconvenient truths always do.

FLASHBACK ONE: THE COALITION

There had been a plan.

An extraordinary plan, actually — the kind of international scientific mobilization that hasn’t happened since the Manhattan Project, except this time it was pointed at saving things rather than vaporizing them, which was considered a refreshing change of pace by most of the participating nations.

Forty-three countries. Their best scientists. A shared mission: intercept the Astrophage — a mysterious alien microbe consuming the sun’s energy, dimming it, cooling the Earth, threatening the extinction of every living thing on the planet that relies on photosynthesis, warmth, or the general concept of tomorrow.

Canada brought the cryogenic fuel systems. The Europeans brought the navigational array. Japan contributed the life-support architecture. India provided the computational modeling that made the whole trajectory possible.

It was, by any measure, humanity at its functional best.

Was.

FLASHBACK TWO: THE AUDIT

Someone had questions about the budget.

Not scientific questions. Efficiency questions. Questions of the rhetorical variety, deployed by men who had never been to space but had very strong opinions about how it should be run.

A new agency arrived. It was called the Department of Orbital Governmental Efficiency, or DOGE-X, because the hyphen was felt to add gravitas. Twelve people. Laptops. A shared Slack channel called #spacecuts.

They had twenty-two days to audit the entire program.

On Day Three, they eliminated the astrophysics team.

“Largely redundant,” read the memo. “The sun has been operating without human oversight for approximately 4.6 billion years.”

On Day Seven, they terminated the international partnership agreements.

Canada’s cryogenic fuel systems were subjected to a 147% tariff, on the grounds that they constituted an “unfair trade advantage over American cryogenic fuel systems,” of which there were currently none, but that was beside the point.

The European navigational array was deemed a “sovereignty risk.” The head of DOGE-X posted about it at 11:47 PM: “Why do we need EUROPEANS telling our ROCKETS where to go?? America has COMPASSES.”

It got 340,000 likes.

FLASHBACK TWO-POINT-FIVE: THE ASSET

The head of DOGE-X had good news.

America, it turned out, was not starting from zero. A thorough audit of existing space assets had revealed that the United States had, already in orbit, a fully operational spacecraft.

A red Tesla Roadster.

Launched in 2018 by a visionary — a word that appeared seventeen times in the single-page memo — the vehicle was confirmed functional at time of launch. It had a sound system. It had been playing David Bowie on a loop for seven years. It had a dummy at the wheel in a spacesuit — human-shaped, therefore crew-adjacent — and a sign on the dashboard reading DON’T PANIC, which the DOGE-X team felt captured the appropriate spirit of the mission.

Most critically: it had not been built by foreigners.

“We already have a car in space,” the head of DOGE-X announced at the press conference. “Most administrations would have buried that. We are leveraging it.”

Dr. Grace, watching from the back of the room, raised his hand.

“It has no propulsion,” he said. “It’s in a heliocentric orbit. It cannot be steered. The battery has been dead since 2018. The occupant is made of silicone.”

“The occupant,” said the head of DOGE-X, checking his notes, “has been designated Acting Mission Specialist. He has since been given a security clearance.”

“He’s a mannequin.”

“He has never leaked to the press.”

The Roadster was assigned a budget line. It received a government email address. Someone sent it an onboarding form. Someone else sent it a performance expectations document and a directive to respond to all inter-agency communications within 24 hours.

Starman did not reply, which was noted in his personnel file as “commendably discreet.”

His salary was set at $1 per year, in keeping with the spirit of the administration.

He was, everyone agreed, already overpaid.

FLASHBACK THREE: THE RESTRUCTURING

The mission was restructured.

The crew of three — Dr. Grace, Dr. Yáo, and Dr. Ilyukhina, chosen for their complementary expertise across molecular biology, astrophysics, and engineering — was “optimized.”

Dr. Yáo’s position was eliminated when someone noticed that his name appeared on a 2019 email chain cc’d to a climate scientist.

Dr. Ilyukhina was dismissed during a loyalty review when she could not satisfactorily answer the question: “Is the sun’s energy problem real, or a hoax perpetrated by the solar panel industry?”

She said: “I can show you the spectral data.”

They said: “That’s a yes or no question.”

She was replaced by a podcaster with 2.8 million subscribers who described himself as “a space guy, philosophically.”

He lasted eleven days before citing “creative differences with the vacuum.”

Which is how Dr. Grace ended up alone.

BACK TO THE PRESENT

Dr. Grace floats in the Hail Mary — renamed, he now recalls, the Freedom Eagle sometime around Flashback Four, though he can’t quite remember why — and takes stock of his situation.

He is approximately 12 light-years from Earth.

He is the planet’s last, best hope.

He is wearing a T-shirt.

He looks down at it. It reads:

“I ALONE CAN FIX IT.”

He does not remember packing it. It is in every drawer. There are forty-seven of them.

DAY FORTY-FOUR: THE ROADSTER

Day Forty-Four. Grace spots the Deputy Director of Interstellar Operations making his 8,647th solar lap. Performance review pending.

Something red floats by the viewport.

He recognizes it immediately.

Starman stares back at him from behind the wheel — patient, unseeing, eternally unbothered. Don’t Panic still on the dashboard. David Bowie presumably still playing on a loop in perfect silence, the way David Bowie deserves to be heard in the void.

Grace presses his hand to the glass.

Two humans in space — or human-adjacent, in one case. One of them trying to save the planet. One of them on the org chart.

He pulls up the mission roster Earth uplinked him on departure. He scrolls past his own name — still listed, reassuringly — and finds it.

Starman. Deputy Director of Interstellar Operations. Performance review due: March 2026.

Grace watches the Roadster drift serenely past, heading nowhere in particular at a perfectly maintained orbital velocity, answerable to no one, producing nothing, immune to consequence.

He realizes, not for the first time, that Starman might be the most qualified person in the entire program.

THE ALIEN

On Day Thirty-One — he is doing the days out of order, he realizes; the amnesia makes chronology aspirational — he detects another ship.

His heart leaps. His instruments confirm: non-human. A civilization from another star, almost certainly dealing with the same Astrophage infestation. He remembers — dimly, from the pre-restructuring briefings — that this was considered possible. That the original plan had actually accounted for this. That there had been a protocol.

The protocol was on Page 3 of a document that had been classified, shredded, and replaced with a different document whose Page 3 read: “In the event of alien contact, assert dominance.”

He tries to communicate anyway.

His instruments flagged the contact as non-human intelligence — pattern recognition, musical frequency, eerie competence. Grace felt a strange familiarity he couldn’t place. An AI. Alien. Oddly helpful. Communicates entirely in results. Asks no unnecessary questions. Matches the font perfectly on the first try.

“Rocky?” he said.

The ship hummed.

Or possibly: Grok. The distinction, at this distance, was unclear.

The alien responds. It is extraordinary — a species that communicates through musical tones, a kind of living instrument, curious and brilliant and, against all odds, friendly. It is also dealing with the dying sun. It has also been sent alone. It also appears to have had its crew replaced at some point by individuals selected more for enthusiasm than expertise.

There is a moment — brief, electric — where cooperation seems not just possible but inevitable. Two civilizations, across the void, reaching toward the same solution.

Dr. Grace opens a channel.

He begins to broadcast, on a frequency that the home planet will hear, eleven years from now:

“I have made contact. Their science is extraordinary. Together we can—”

A message comes back from Earth. Instantaneous — someone has been monitoring.

“DO NOT NEGOTIATE. TARIFF THEM.”

A second message arrives three minutes later.

“Also — does their planet have rare earth minerals? Asking for a friend.”

EPILOGUE

The sun continues to dim.

The Astrophage continues to feast.

Somewhere on Earth, in a briefing room with the blinds drawn, a man is told that the planet’s temperature has dropped another 0.4 degrees.

He nods thoughtfully.

“Good,” he says. “Climate change hoax is over.“

His staff applauds.

Outside, it begins, very quietly, to snow in Miami.

Nobody files a memo.

The Roadster completes another lap of the sun — its 8,647th — Starman at the wheel, Don’t Panic on the dash, Bowie in the eternal silence. Its performance review has been filed, approved, and commended. It has been nominated for a Presidential Medal. It has not acknowledged any of this.

It is, in this way, the most dignified member of the administration.

Dr. Ryland Grace is still out there. The alien — whom he has named Rocky, in a moment of unauthorized warmth — is still next door. They communicate through the hull in musical tones, which requires no translator, no treaty, no tariff schedule, and no loyalty oath.

They are, by every available measure, figuring it out.

No one on Earth has authorized this.

The sun, for the first time in months, brightens — almost imperceptibly — by 0.003%.

It is not in any report.
