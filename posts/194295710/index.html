The Marble Was Always a Lie

Part XIX: The courts kept saying no. Markets may be the last word

April 15, 2026

President Trump, left, and Jerome Powell, the Federal Reserve Chairman, during a tour of the central bank’s headquarters last year. Credit...Haiyun Jiang/The New York Times

CanadianSnowmanSays | April 15, 2026

Three weeks ago, in Part XVIII of this series, I documented a federal judge’s conclusion that the Justice Department’s criminal investigation of Jerome Powell had produced “no evidence whatsoever that Powell committed any crime other than displeasing the president.”

Chief Judge James Boasberg quashed the subpoenas. He offered prosecutors a private opportunity to show him anything they actually had. They declined, because there was nothing. The case built around $2.5 billion worth of marble and cost overruns — the pretext Jeanine Pirro’s office had been peddling for months — collapsed under scrutiny, as pretexts tend to do.

I ended that essay with a question dressed as a statement: the pattern of judicial rejection is now so consistent it constitutes something close to a verdict on the strategy itself.

This morning, Donald Trump answered the question.

He didn’t appeal to a higher court. He didn’t produce new evidence. He didn’t construct a new pretext. He appeared on Fox Business and threatened to fire Jerome Powell if Powell did not resign.

Not for the marble. Not for the renovation. Not for any of the legal scaffolding that took months to build and weeks for the courts to demolish. Just: leave, or I will remove you.

The marble was always a lie. This morning, Trump said so himself.

What Dropping the Pretext Means

There is a specific kind of moment in authoritarian theatre when the prop falls over and reveals the stagehands. The audience already suspected the set was fake. But there is something different — something clarifying and dangerous at once — about watching the performance stop and the threat delivered plain.

For months, the official story offered a fig leaf. A building renovation. Cost overruns. Congressional testimony. There was a pretext, however threadbare, and the existence of the pretext meant that defenders of democratic norms could argue within the system — take it to court, quash the subpoenas, write the ruling, win on the merits. And they did, repeatedly, with increasing clarity and increasing contempt for the prosecution’s foundations.

The pretext is gone now. What remains is the demand itself, stripped of camouflage: the President of the United States wants the Federal Reserve to cut interest rates. The Federal Reserve Chair has declined. The president wants him gone.

That is the whole story. It always was. It just took until this morning for Trump to say it out loud on television.

The Legal Obstacle Trump Is Betting On

Powell’s term as Fed Chair expires on May 15 — thirty days from today. Under the law and established precedent, he can continue serving in an acting capacity until his nominated successor, Kevin Warsh, is confirmed by the Senate. He can also remain on the Federal Reserve’s board as a governor until 2028. There is no legal mechanism that removes Jerome Powell from the Federal Reserve on May 16. He can stay. He has said, in March, that he intends to stay until the investigation is resolved “with transparency and finality.”

The firing threat has a legal problem: the Federal Reserve Act permits removal of board members only for cause — meaning actual malfeasance or dereliction of duty, not policy disagreement, not personal animus, not the president’s preference for cheaper money.

Whether that protection is real is currently before the Supreme Court.

Trump tried to fire Fed governor Lisa Cook last year, alleging mortgage fraud in a case legal observers described, charitably, as thin. Cook sued. The case is now at the Supreme Court, awaiting a decision. When it comes, that ruling will determine whether a president can remove Fed officials by executive fiat — or whether the independence Congress wrote into the Federal Reserve Act actually means what it says.

Trump’s firing threat this morning is not a bluff, exactly. It is a bet. He is betting that SCOTUS rules in his favour on Cook, that the legal protection evaporates, and that Powell — facing certain removal — concludes that leaving voluntarily is better for the institution than the spectacle of a contested firing.

Powell, whose entire posture since January has been to call Trump’s bluffs rather than accommodate them, seems unlikely to find that argument persuasive.

The Warsh Trap

There is a further irony that deserves naming.

Trump needs Kevin Warsh confirmed to install his preferred Fed Chair. The Senate confirmation process has been complicated — in part because the criminal investigation Trump launched against Powell created procedural entanglements that slowed the committee process. Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina announced in January that he would block any Fed nominee until the investigation was dropped.

After Boasberg’s ruling last month, Tillis was characteristically direct: the decision confirmed the investigation was “nothing more than a failed attack on Fed independence,” and he called on Pirro’s office to “save itself further embarrassment and move on.”

The apparatus Trump built to pressure Powell out has become the obstacle to confirming Powell’s replacement. The investigation delayed Warsh. The appeal Pirro announced after the ruling delayed Warsh further. And now, with thirty days left on Powell’s term, the confirmation sits in limbo — which means that even if Trump’s firing threat is legally executable, he has no confirmed replacement to install. He would be removing a Fed Chair into a vacuum, with markets watching, and a Supreme Court case still undecided, and a senator from his own party publicly calling the whole enterprise an embarrassment.

This is what institutional sabotage looks like when conducted without a coherent plan.

Powell’s Answer

Jerome Powell has already told us what he intends to do.

In March, he told reporters he had no intention of leaving the Federal Reserve until the investigation was resolved with what he called “transparency and finality.” His decision about his future, he said, would be based on what he thought was best for the institution and the people it serves.

Not what was best for him. Not what was best for the president. What was best for the institution.

The investigation has not been resolved. There has been no transparency. There has been no finality — Pirro has appealed, the appeal is pending, the case remains legally live. By the terms Powell himself set in March, there is no condition under which he has committed to leave.

He is not going.

Which means the next thirty days will tell us one of two things: either the Supreme Court gives Trump the legal tool he needs to fire Powell, or Trump attempts a removal that will be challenged in court immediately, and the Federal Reserve enters a period of genuine constitutional crisis — a sitting chair who considers himself unlawfully removed, a nominated replacement unconfirmed, and markets that cannot wait for the courts to sort it out.

The Enemies List, Updated

Seventeen parts ago, this series began with a simple observation: a pattern was emerging. Each entry in the list followed the same script. A target is identified. A pretext is constructed. Prosecutorial or administrative machinery is deployed. The institution — the court, the grand jury, the judge — resists. And each time the institution resists, the escalation comes.

With Powell, we have now passed through every phase.

The pretext was constructed — the marble. The machinery was deployed — Pirro’s grand jury subpoenas. The institution resisted — Boasberg’s ruling, twice, with increasing contempt. And today came the escalation: the pretext abandoned, the threat made explicit, the mask fully off.

The question Part XIV asked — if they can come for the Fed, what’s left? — now has a sharper form. The courts stopped the criminal route. What happens when the president simply announces he will fire the independent institution’s head, pretext be damned, and dares the legal system to stop him in real time?

We are about to find out.

The TACO Scenario

There is, however, a fourth possibility that the constitutional analysts tend to overlook — because it has nothing to do with constitutions.

Donald Trump has a well-documented relationship with financial pressure. Readers of this series will recognize the framework: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate. It is the pattern that has governed his approach to tariffs, to NATO, to trade partners who called his bluff and watched him fold. The red lines dissolve. The threats evaporate. The capitulation arrives, usually dressed up as a strategic masterstroke.

The Federal Reserve exists, at its core, to maintain the confidence of financial markets. That confidence is not abstract. It is priced daily, hourly, in the bond market, in the dollar’s exchange rate, in the spread between Treasuries and everything else. A constitutional crisis over the removal of the Fed Chair — a sitting chair who considers himself unlawfully removed, a nominated replacement unconfirmed, a Supreme Court case mid-deliberation — is precisely the kind of event that markets do not wait for courts to resolve. They price it immediately. They price it badly.

Trump’s billionaire allies understand this better than most. They were not enriched by being slow to recognize when an asset is being destroyed in real time. And several of them — the ones with enough Mar-a-Lago access to get a phone call returned on a Tuesday — have already signalled, in the careful language of people who do not want to be the next entry on the enemies list, that Fed independence is a hill worth defending.

The most likely scenario in which Donald Trump does not fire Jerome Powell is not a SCOTUS ruling, though that may come. It is not a Senate revolt, though Tillis has laid the groundwork. It is a morning when the bond market opens badly, the dollar slides, the phones start ringing in Palm Beach, and someone whose net worth Trump respects says, plainly: this is going to cost you more than Powell is worth.

At which point the threat will be quietly shelved, the capitulation dressed up as magnanimity, and the president will take credit for the Fed’s independence as though he had been defending it all along.

The marble was always a lie. The TACO may be the ending.
