The $10 Billion Shakedown

Part 40: Trump Sues His Own Government While Controlling It (February 2026)

April 23, 2026

"What I would do is tell them to pay me." — Donald Trump, when asked by NBC News whether he would direct his subordinates to settle his $10 billion lawsuit in his favor. (Manuel Balce Ceneta / Associated Press)

In January 2026, President Donald Trump did something unprecedented in American history.

He sued the Internal Revenue Service and the Treasury Department—two agencies under his direct control—for $10 billion in taxpayer money.

This wasn’t a lawsuit filed by a citizen against the government. It was a president suing agencies he commands, to be defended by subordinates he appointed, with settlement authority held by officials who report to him.

The corruption isn’t that Trump can unilaterally seize $10 billion. Courts don’t work that way. A judge or jury must decide the case, or the parties must settle.

The corruption is that Trump’s own subordinates—people who owe their jobs to him, who can be fired by him, who serve at his pleasure—control whether to fight the lawsuit vigorously or settle it for a massive sum. They control the litigation strategy. They control whether to put up a strong defense or a weak one. They control whether to drag the case out or settle quickly and quietly.

And they report directly to the man suing the government.

As a Canadian watching this unfold, I find myself reaching for comparisons that don’t exist. Imagine if Justin Trudeau sued the Canada Revenue Agency for $10 billion while simultaneously controlling it. Imagine if the Finance Minister who would defend the case reported directly to the Prime Minister filing the lawsuit. Imagine if the Attorney General overseeing the defense was a former ally the PM had previously paid off with charity funds.

It would be a constitutional crisis that would bring down the government within hours. In Canada, in Britain, in Germany, in any functioning democracy, such a scheme would be impossible to even attempt. And if you then fired the Attorney General and replaced her with the Prime Minister's personal defense lawyer—two weeks before settlement talks began—the entire cabinet would resign in protest.

In Trump’s America, it’s just Tuesday.

But this isn’t just another Trump scandal lost in the noise. This is the most brazen presidential self-dealing in American history, and it creates a clear, prosecutable record of constitutional crimes that will remain actionable when Trump leaves office in 2029.

Let me show you how the scheme works, why it matters, and why Trump may have finally created evidence of corruption he cannot escape.

The Mechanics of the Shakedown

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and President Donald Trump look on during The White House Digital Assets Summit in the State Dining Room on March 7, 2025. Anna Moneymaker/Getty Images

The scheme relies on a conflict of interest so blatant it reads like satire.

Scott Bessent holds two positions simultaneously:

  1. Secretary of the Treasury - appointed by Trump, reports to Trump

  2. Acting Commissioner of the IRS - also appointed by Trump, also reports to Trump

When Trump sued the IRS and Treasury for $10 billion, both defendant agencies are headed by the same person—Trump’s own direct subordinate.

As former White House Press Secretary Jen Psaki explained on her MSNBC show: “Donald Trump is ordering his subordinates to give him your money. Scott Bessent has two jobs. He is simultaneously serving as both Trump’s Treasury Secretary and as the Acting Commissioner of the IRS. That means that when Trump sued the IRS and the Treasury for $10 billion, Trump was effectively asking Scott Bessent, his direct subordinate, to just give him that $10 billion.”

Now, to be precise: Bessent can’t literally hand Trump $10 billion. A court must decide the case or the parties must reach a settlement. But here’s where the conflict becomes corruption:

Bessent controls:

  • Whether to fight the case vigorously or mount a weak defense

  • Whether to settle the case (and for how much)

  • What resources to dedicate to defending against Trump’s claims

  • The litigation strategy and legal arguments

  • Whether to seek dismissal or let the case proceed

And Bessent reports to the plaintiff.

Think about what this means:

The plaintiff: Donald Trump, President of the United States

The defendants: The IRS and Treasury, both headed by Bessent

Defense strategy controlled by: Bessent, who serves at Trump’s pleasure

Settlement authority held by: Officials Trump appointed and can fire

DOJ attorney defending the case: Reports to Acting AG Todd Blanche—Trump's former personal criminal defense lawyer—who reports to Trump

The payment source: The Treasury general account—taxpayer money

The amount: $10,000,000,000

This isn’t a normal lawsuit. This is a lawsuit where the plaintiff controls the defendants’ entire litigation strategy and settlement authority.

How Corruption Works: Settlement and Weak Defense

Let me be clear about what legal corruption looks like in practice, because it’s more sophisticated than Trump simply ordering a check to be written.

Scenario 1: The Quiet Settlement

Bessent and the DOJ could settle the case for a substantial sum—say, $2 billion—without it ever going to trial. The settlement could be framed as “avoiding costly litigation” or “resolving the matter efficiently.” Trump doesn’t get the full $10 billion he demanded, so it looks like a compromise. But he gets $2 billion of taxpayer money based on a settlement negotiated by subordinates who report to him.

No judge rules on the merits. No jury weighs the evidence. The case just... goes away, with a massive payment to the president.

Scenario 2: The Weak Defense

The case could go to trial, but DOJ could mount a half-hearted defense. Assign inexperienced attorneys. Don’t challenge Trump’s claims aggressively. Don’t pursue favorable legal theories. Make procedural mistakes that help Trump’s case. Then, when a judge rules in Trump’s favor (or partially in his favor), the Attorney General can say “we fought hard, but the court decided.”

Trump gets a judicial imprimatur on extracting taxpayer money, even though the defense was deliberately weak.

Scenario 3: The Sweetheart Deal

Combine elements of both: settle for a large sum, but structure it in ways that benefit Trump beyond the headline number. Maybe the settlement includes favorable tax treatment. Maybe it resolves other disputes in Trump’s favor. Maybe it includes provisions that help Trump’s businesses.

The corruption isn’t crude—it’s sophisticated. And it’s all legal, as long as the subordinates make it look like they’re doing their jobs.

This is how kleptocracy works. Not through obvious theft, but through the manipulation of legal processes by people whose loyalty is to the leader, not to the public.

Update: April 17, 2026 — The Quiet Settlement Begins

I wrote those three scenarios in February. On April 17, 2026, Scenario 1 began playing out in real time.

Trump’s lawyers filed a motion requesting a 90-day pause in the case to “engage in discussions designed to resolve this matter and to avoid protracted litigation.” The filing’s language could have been lifted from the scenario above: “This limited pause will neither prejudice the parties nor delay ultimate resolution. Rather, the extension will promote judicial economy and allow the Parties to explore avenues that could narrow or resolve the issues efficiently.”

The Department of Justice consented.

Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche — Trump's former personal criminal defense lawyer through four prosecutions and 34 felony convictions. On April 2, 2026, Trump fired Pam Bondi and installed Blanche to lead the DOJ. Fifteen days later, Blanche's department consented to settlement negotiations in Trump's $10 billion lawsuit against his own govt.

Let that sink in. The DOJ—now headed by Todd Blanche, Trump’s former personal criminal defense lawyer, installed just fifteen days earlier—agreed to sit down and negotiate a settlement with the man who appointed him. The man he previously represented as a paid advocate. The man whose criminal cases he defended.

Both sides agreed. Because both sides are the same side.

And this wasn’t even the first time. In March 2026, the Justice Department agreed to pay Trump’s former national security adviser Michael Flynn more than $1 million to resolve his wrongful prosecution case — taxpayer money, paid through DOJ settlement, to a Trump ally. The Flynn settlement was the test run. The $10 billion IRS suit is the same mechanism, scaled up by a factor of ten thousand.

The watchdog group Democracy Forward had filed an amicus brief anticipating exactly this: “This case is extraordinary because the President controls both sides of the litigation, which raises the prospect of collusive litigation tactics. To treat this case like business as usual would threaten the integrity of the justice system and the important taxpayer and privacy protections at the heart of this case.”

The court has been warned. The collusive litigation tactics are no longer hypothetical. They are happening.

And this isn’t even the only shakedown in progress. Trump has also filed administrative complaints seeking an additional $230 million in damages—one related to the FBI’s Russia investigation and the other concerning the FBI’s search of Mar-a-Lago after he refused a subpoena to return classified documents.

The total extraction: potentially $10.23 billion, all from agencies he controls, all defended by subordinates he appointed.

There is one additional detail that makes the quiet settlement route even more attractive for Trump. On April 13 — four days before the settlement filing — a judge dismissed Trump’s separate $10 billion lawsuit against the Wall Street Journal over its Epstein coverage. Courts are pushing back on Trump’s litigation-as-profit strategy. Which means the IRS suit is more likely to be resolved through settlement than trial — exactly the scenario where the conflict of interest is most dangerous, because no judge ever rules on the merits.

The quiet settlement isn’t a scenario anymore. It’s a strategy.

“So Taxpayers?” “Yes.”

'Who pays for $10B Trump lawsuit - taxpayers?': Gallego pins Bessent down in brutal hearing exchange February 2026

During a Senate Finance Committee hearing, Senator Ruben Gallego of Arizona pressed Bessent on the most fundamental question: if Trump’s lawsuit succeeds or settles, where would the money come from?

The exchange was brief and devastating:

Gallego: “Where would that $10 billion come from?”

Bessent: “It would come from Treasury.”

Gallego: “Which comes from the general fund?”

Bessent: “The Treasury general account.”

Gallego: “So taxpayers?”

Bessent: “Yes.”

There it is. Confirmed under oath. On the record. In a Senate hearing.

Whether through settlement or judgment, if Trump gets money from this lawsuit, it comes from taxpayers. And the officials who will decide how hard to fight the case—or whether to settle it—all report to Trump.

Passing the Buck to the Attorney General

When pressed on this obvious conflict, Bessent attempted to deflect. He suggested that the Department of Justice—not Treasury—would actually handle the lawsuit, since DOJ represents federal agencies in court.

This defense creates an even more problematic conflict of interest.

When Trump filed the lawsuit in January 2026, the Attorney General was Pam Bondi. She reported directly to Trump. She served at Trump’s pleasure. And Trump and Bondi had history: in 2013, Trump’s personal charity—the Trump Foundation—donated $25,000 to Bondi’s re-election campaign while she was Florida’s Attorney General actively considering whether to investigate fraud allegations against Trump University. Bondi ultimately decided not to pursue the case. In 2019, Trump was forced to dissolve the Trump Foundation and pay a $2 million settlement after New York’s Attorney General found he had systematically misused charitable funds—including using foundation money to make that $25,000 donation to Bondi.

The woman Trump previously paid off with charity funds was overseeing the defense of a lawsuit where Trump sought $10 billion in taxpayer money.

That was bad enough.

Then, on April 2, 2026—just fifteen days before the settlement talks filing—Trump fired Bondi.

Her replacement? Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche—Trump’s former personal criminal defense lawyer.

Blanche represented Trump in the Manhattan felony case that resulted in 34 criminal convictions. He represented Trump in the classified documents case. He represented Trump in the January 6 federal indictment. He was Trump’s paid advocate across multiple criminal prosecutions.

Now he heads the Department of Justice—the department responsible for defending the government against Trump’s $10 billion lawsuit. Trump’s personal lawyer is now deciding:

  • How aggressively DOJ fights the case

  • What resources to dedicate to the defense

  • Whether to settle (and for how much)

  • What arguments to make in court

  • Whether to appeal adverse rulings

The man who spent years defending Trump against criminal charges is now in charge of defending the government against Trump’s demand for $10 billion in taxpayer money.

If the conflict of interest under Bondi was recursive, under Blanche it is farcical. This is not merely the fox guarding the henhouse. This is the fox’s personal attorney being appointed to guard the henhouse, two weeks before settlement negotiations begin.

The corruption is recursive. The conflicts compound.

And as of April 17, 2026, his department has consented to settlement negotiations. The fox is not merely guarding the henhouse. The fox’s personal lawyer has agreed to discuss, over the next 90 days, how many chickens to hand over.

“What I Would Do Is Tell Them to Pay Me”

When NBC News asked Trump directly whether he would tell his subordinates to settle the case in his favor, Trump didn’t even attempt to hide behind legal abstractions.

Reporter: “You’re going to tell them to pay you though, right?”

Trump: “Well, what I would do is tell them to pay me, but I’ll give 100% of the money to charity. I don’t want any of the money.”

“What I would do is tell them to pay me.”

Now, Trump can’t literally order his subordinates to “pay him”—courts don’t work that way. But what he can do is make clear his expectation that the case be resolved in his favor, either through settlement or weak defense.

And his subordinates understand that their jobs depend on keeping the president happy.

Trump’s statement reveals his understanding of how this works. He doesn’t need to issue formal orders. He just needs to make his expectations clear. His subordinates will figure out how to deliver.

His defense is that he’ll donate it all to charity.

Which brings us to the second-most important question: should anyone believe that?

The Charity Lie: A Documented Pattern

Donald Trump has a long, well-documented history of lying about charitable donations.

The 2016 Campaign Fraud

When Trump launched his presidential campaign in 2016, he claimed he had donated more than $102 million to charity in the previous five years. His campaign released a 93-page list of those donations.

The Washington Post investigated. Their finding: not a single donation on that list was a personal gift of Trump’s own money.

The Post dug deeper. In the 15 years before running for office, Trump had publicly promised to donate more than $8.5 million to various charities.

How much did he actually donate? About $2.8 million—and all of it through his personal charity, the Trump Foundation.

The foundation he would later be forced to dissolve for fraud.

The Trump Foundation Scandal

In November 2019, a New York judge ordered Trump to pay $2 million to settle a lawsuit alleging he misused his charitable foundation for personal and political gain.

What did Trump use Trump Foundation money—money donated by others for charitable purposes—to purchase?

Three portraits of himself. Not one. Not two. Three different portraits of Donald Trump, purchased with money people donated to charity.

A signed Tim Tebow football helmet. Charity funds used to buy Trump a sports collectible.

The $25,000 payment to Pam Bondi while she was considering whether to investigate Trump University.

The foundation also paid a $258,000 settlement to resolve a lawsuit over a hole-in-one contest at a Trump golf course. Charity money used to settle Trump’s business debts.

New York Attorney General Letitia James described a “shocking pattern of illegality” involving the Trump Foundation, including “unlawful coordination with the Trump presidential campaign, repeated and willful self-dealing, and much more.”

The judge ordered the foundation dissolved. Trump had to admit misuse of charitable funds. He paid $2 million in damages.

The Pattern

Promise to donate to charity → Don’t donate, or use someone else’s money, or use charity money to buy things for yourself → Get caught → Pay a settlement (using someone else’s money) → Do it again.

And now Trump is asking Americans to trust that if he extracts $10 billion through this lawsuit—whether by settlement or judgment—he’ll donate it all to charity.

After being forced to dissolve his foundation for charity fraud.

After using foundation money to buy portraits of himself.

After using charity funds to pay off Pam Bondi, who oversaw the defense of this very lawsuit until Trump fired her—and replaced her with his own criminal defense lawyer.

Trump himself has acknowledged the optics. "Nobody would care because it's going to go to numerous very good charities," he said in February. Even he understands that a $10 billion payout to the president from the Treasury would "never look good."

The audacity is breathtaking.

The “We’ll Catch Fraudsters” Excuse

Faced with the obvious problem—potentially extracting billions from taxpayers during a time of economic anxiety—Trump offered an even more audacious justification.

He told NBC News: “Do you know if we captured 50% of the fraud of this country right now? We would have better than a balanced budget.”

The argument appears to be: Don’t worry about this lawsuit potentially costing taxpayers billions, because my administration will catch so many fraudsters that it will more than make up for any payment.

This excuse fails on every level.

Trump Gutted the IRS

The agency responsible for catching tax fraud—the IRS—has lost more than 20% of its workforce under Trump.

Government Executive reported in early 2025 that the IRS has started pulling employees from Human Resources departments to process tax returns because there aren’t enough revenue agents left.

An agency that is pulling HR staff to handle basic tax processing is not going to catch record amounts of fraud. It’s going to miss massive amounts of fraud because it lacks the staff to investigate.

Trump is claiming he’ll catch fraudsters while simultaneously gutting the agency that catches fraudsters. The logic is absurd.

Trump Gutted Federal Prosecutors

Trump specifically cited fraud investigations in Minnesota as an example of the fraud his administration would recover.

But the same week he made that claim, news broke that four prosecutors who led Minnesota’s major fraud cases had resigned as part of a wave of departures following Trump’s immigration crackdown.

The Minnesota U.S. Attorney’s office went from 70 assistant U.S. attorneys to as few as 17—a loss of 75% of its prosecutorial capacity.

The Trump-appointed U.S. Attorney, Daniel Rosen, filed court documents stating that his short-staffed office has been forced to “abandon pressing and important priorities” because it is overwhelmed managing immigration cases.

An office that has lost three-quarters of its prosecutors and is abandoning important cases to handle immigration enforcement is not going to catch record amounts of fraud.

Trump is claiming his administration will recover billions from fraudsters while simultaneously gutting the offices that prosecute fraud. Again, the logic collapses.

The Kafkaesque Absurdity

Let’s be clear about what Trump is arguing:

“This lawsuit might result in taxpayers paying me billions, but don’t worry, I will find other people’s money—by catching fraudsters—to replace it, using agencies I have gutted of the staff needed to catch fraudsters.”

This is not a policy argument. This is a con man’s excuse. It’s the financial equivalent of “the check is in the mail” or “I’ll pay you back Tuesday for a hamburger today.”

It is an insult to anyone capable of basic arithmetic.

In which case, investigating Trump’s $10 billion lawsuit would be counterproductive.

Congress Tries to Close the Barn Door

On April 15 — two days before the settlement filing — Democratic lawmakers introduced the “Ban Presidential Plunder of Taxpayer Funds Act.” Sponsored by Senator Elizabeth Warren, Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, Representative Jamie Raskin, and Representative Dave Min, the bill would ban the president, vice president, their spouses, their children, and any entity they own or control from collecting settlement payments from the federal government.

Separately, Representative Mike Thompson introduced legislation imposing a 100 percent tax on any civil judgment or settlement paid by the United States to a sitting president or the president’s immediate family.

“While American families are getting flattened by skyrocketing costs, Donald Trump is trying to snatch up billions of taxpayer dollars to line his own pockets and settle personal scores,” Warren said.

The bills would allow a sitting president to collect compensatory damages only if a judge appoints independent counsel to represent the federal entity being sued and makes all proceedings public — precisely the safeguards that do not exist in Trump’s current lawsuit.

The legislation is, of course, dead on arrival in a Republican-controlled Congress. But its existence serves the historical record. Congress saw what was coming. Members introduced legislation to stop it. And the legislative branch chose not to act.

When prosecutors review the evidence after 2029, the Ban Presidential Plunder of Taxpayer Funds Act will be Exhibit A in establishing that the conflict of interest was not merely obvious in retrospect — it was obvious at the time, flagged by legislators, and ignored.

A Canadian’s Perspective: This Is Third World Corruption

I write this series as a Canadian looking south with a mixture of bewilderment, horror, and increasingly, pity.

Canada is not without corruption. We’ve had our scandals—SNC-Lavalin, the sponsorship scandal, senators caught with their hands in the till. But there are lines we do not cross. There are norms that hold. There are mechanisms that function.

If Justin Trudeau sued the Canada Revenue Agency for $10 billion while controlling the agency’s defense strategy and settlement authority, the government would fall within 24 hours. The opposition would call a vote of no confidence. The media would treat it as the constitutional crisis it is. The public would demand resignation.

This is not a partisan observation. It’s a structural one. Parliamentary systems have mechanisms—votes of no confidence, dissolution of parliament, coalition collapses—that prevent this kind of naked corruption from persisting.

The American system, designed with elaborate checks and balances, has somehow created a situation where a president can sue his own government for billions while controlling how vigorously the defense is mounted and whether to settle, and the primary response is... a Senate hearing where his subordinate admits any payment would come from taxpayers, followed by news coverage that treats it as just another controversy in an endless stream of controversies.

This is not normal. This is not how democracies function. This is how kleptocracies function.

When a leader sues government agencies he controls, when the officials defending the case owe their jobs to the plaintiff, when settlement authority rests with people who can be fired by the person suing, when the distinction between public funds and personal claims dissolves, when the mechanisms of oversight are gutted or captured or simply ignored—that is the operational definition of a kleptocracy.

I am Canadian. I have no vote in American elections. I have no standing to tell Americans how to govern themselves.

But I live next door to a country where the president is suing his own government for $10 billion while controlling the defense strategy, and I am watching—with the alarm of someone who lives downwind of a nuclear reactor—as the cooling systems fail one by one.

The Constitutional Violation

The U.S. Constitution contains two Emoluments Clauses designed to prevent exactly this:

The Domestic Emoluments Clause (Article II, Section 1): The president “shall not receive... any other Emolument from the United States, or any of them” beyond the presidential salary.

The purpose is explicit: prevent the president from using the office to enrich himself at taxpayers’ expense.

Trump’s $10 billion lawsuit violates this clause whether he wins in court or settles with his own subordinates. Either way, he would be receiving payment from the United States Treasury beyond his salary—not millions, but billions.

This makes Trump’s first-term emoluments violations—documented in Part 23 of this series—look quaint by comparison. Foreign governments paying for hotel rooms at Trump properties? Corporate lobbyists booking Mar-a-Lago for events? The Secret Service paying Trump’s company for protection?

Those were indirect, deniable, harder to prosecute.

This is direct. This is explicit. This is a lawsuit, filed in court, seeking $10 billion from the U.S. government, with the defense controlled by Trump’s subordinates, admitted openly by Trump on camera.

If this does not violate the Domestic Emoluments Clause, then the clause is meaningless.

But the $10 billion lawsuit creates something different: recent, undeniable, extensively documented evidence of constitutional crimes that cannot be pardoned and for which the statute of limitations won’t begin until Trump leaves office in January 2029.

Potential Criminal Charges

Bribery: If evidence shows Trump influenced Bessent or Blanche’s litigation decisions through his official position, prosecutors can charge bribery—using official power to obtain personal benefit.

Honest Services Fraud: Using his position to deprive citizens of honest services by manipulating litigation for personal gain.

Conspiracy to Defraud: If Trump coordinated with Bessent, Blanche, or others to structure the lawsuit or settlement in ways designed to extract taxpayer money.

Tax Fraud: If Trump receives money through settlement or judgment and doesn’t donate it as promised, prosecutors can charge tax evasion for unreported income.

False Statements: If Trump makes material false statements to Congress, in court filings, or to federal officials about the case, the charity donation, or the conflicts of interest.

Wire Fraud: Using interstate communications to conduct the fraudulent scheme.

Emoluments Violations: The most obvious charge—receiving payment from the United States beyond the presidential salary, whether through settlement or judgment.

Which means:

  • ✅ He cannot win another election to shield himself from prosecution

  • ✅ He loses presidential immunity when he becomes a private citizen

  • ✅ State prosecutors can bring charges he cannot pardon

For the first time in his life, Trump’s escape routes are foreclosed.

The question is whether prosecutors will have the courage to act.

Conclusion: The Shakedown in Plain Sight

Regardless of what happens in 2029, the historical record is already established.

In January 2026, the President of the United States filed a lawsuit against federal agencies under his control, seeking $10 billion in taxpayer money. The defense was assigned to subordinates he appointed. Congress introduced legislation to stop it — and didn’t pass it. The DOJ had already paid Michael Flynn $1 million through the same settlement mechanism. A judge threw out Trump’s other $10 billion lawsuit four days before his lawyers sat down to negotiate this one. And on April 17, the Department of Justice — now headed by the president’s former personal criminal defense attorney — consented to 90 days of settlement talks.

This happened. It is in the court record, in Senate testimony, on video, in the April 17 filing, and in the congressional record. No amount of spin can change it.

If this stands — if the case settles favorably and prosecutors decline to act after 2029 — then the Emoluments Clauses become dead letters. The distinction between public office and personal enrichment dissolves. The precedent is set: the presidency is a profit opportunity, the Treasury is a source of wealth to be extracted, and the government is a legal opponent you can sue while controlling its defense.

Once that norm is gone, it’s not coming back.

Trump has always operated in plain sight. But the $10 billion lawsuit is corruption distilled to its essence — so brazen, so direct, so unapologetic that it almost seems designed to test whether anything can stop him.

The 90-day clock is ticking. Somewhere in a federal courthouse in Florida, the most audacious presidential shakedown in American history is moving from theory to practice.

The shakedown is happening in plain sight.

The question is whether anyone will stop it.
