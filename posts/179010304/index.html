“The Investigation Trap - When Accommodation Weaponizes Due Process"

How Trump May Have Already Neutralized the Discharge Petition

November 15, 2025

Opening: The Victory That Wasn’t

Episode 6 concluded with optimism: Speaker Johnson admitted he couldn’t block the parade. The vote is coming next week. Constitutional mechanisms prevailed. 218 signatures defeated five stages of accommodation psychology.

Friday night, former federal prosecutor Glenn Kirschner explained why Trump’s investigation order might have already made that victory meaningless.

Not by stopping the vote. Not by preventing passage. Not even by vetoing successfully.

By creating legal justification to ignore the vote’s outcome entirely.

The discharge petition can force a vote. The House can pass it with a veto-proof majority. The Senate can follow. Trump can even let it become law without his signature.

And the files might never be released anyway.

Because there’s now an “active criminal investigation.”

The Prosecutor Who Sees The Trap

Glenn Kirschner’s Credentials

Glenn Kirschner spent 30 years as a federal prosecutor with the U.S. Attorney’s Office for the District of Columbia. He tried hundreds of criminal cases—from street crimes to complex fraud to homicides to public corruption. He served as chief of the Homicide Section and later as an Assistant U.S. Attorney supervising major trials.

This isn’t a political commentator offering hot takes. This isn’t a pundit with a law degree who never practiced. This is a career prosecutor who spent three decades in the courtroom putting criminals behind bars.

Kirschner knows how FOIA litigation works because he defended DOJ in FOIA cases. He knows how “pending investigation” claims function because he made those claims in legitimate investigations. He knows what real prosecutions look like because he conducted hundreds of them.

When Kirschner explains legal mechanisms, he’s explaining systems he operated within for 30 years. Thank you Glenn, for your learned insight.

The Contrast: Jay Clayton

Now consider who Trump assigned to lead the Epstein “investigation.”

Attorney General Pam Bondi called Jay Clayton “one of the most capable and trusted prosecutors in the country.”

Kirschner’s response: “If she were under oath, that would be perjury.”

Jay Clayton’s actual background:

  • Wall Street corporate lawyer

  • Chairman of the SEC during Trump’s first term

  • Partner at Sullivan & Cromwell (white-shoe law firm)

  • Zero prosecution experience

Kirschner’s challenge to Clayton: “Hey sport, have you ever prosecuted so much as a traffic violation? Ever?”

Why The Contrast Matters

Kirschner spent 30 years prosecuting crimes—following evidence wherever it leads, pursuing crimes regardless of defendant’s power, maintaining prosecutorial independence from political pressure.

Clayton spent his career representing corporations and serving Trump’s political interests. Now he’ll lead an “investigation” targeting only people Trump names while maintaining “pending investigation” status indefinitely and never bringing actual charges.

One man dedicated three decades to putting criminals in prison. The other man is being used to protect criminals from exposure.

Friday Night’s Warning

On November 15, 2025, Kirschner addressed his community in what he called a “therapy session”—his own, not theirs. His assessment was dark but professionally informed:

“I am beyond disheartened right now... This is nothing more than a ploy to try to resist any of the files being released publicly.”

Then he explained exactly how the ploy works.

The Legal Mechanism: How “Pending Investigation” Blocks Everything

The FOIA Battlefield

Freedom of Information Act litigation is how journalists, victims, and citizens demand government file release:

  1. Citizens/journalists file FOIA request demanding documents

  2. DOJ reviews and typically denies sensitive materials

  3. Requesters sue in federal court to compel release

  4. DOJ files opposition citing various exemptions

  5. Judge weighs public interest against government secrecy claims

DOJ’s Most Effective Defense:

“We cannot possibly release these materials. There is a pending criminal investigation.”

The Court’s Typical Response:

“That’s reasonable. We defer to DOJ’s judgment. Request denied.”

Why Judges Defer

Courts give enormous deference to DOJ claims about ongoing investigations for legitimate reasons: evidence integrity, prosecution strategy, witness safety, grand jury secrecy.

These are legitimate concerns in actual investigations.

The problem: This investigation isn’t actual.

The Timeline Proves It

July 2025 - Kash Patel and Pam Bondi:

Department of Justice memo: “Thoroughly scrutinized the Epstein files and found nothing in them that could sustain opening further inquiries into anyone.”

November 14, 2025 - Same Officials:

Trump orders investigation. Bondi instantly complies. Clayton assigned to lead probe of Clinton, Summers, Hoffman, JPMorgan, “and many other people and institutions.”

What Changed?

Not the files. Not the evidence. Not new victim testimony.

What changed: The discharge petition reached 218 signatures. Transparency became inevitable. Trump needed a legal mechanism to block it.

The only variable that changed was Trump’s political need to prevent file release.

The Sophistication: Accommodation Psychology’s Legal Evolution

Stage Six: Weaponizing Due Process Itself

Parts 1-5 documented five accommodation stages:

  1. Information manipulation (July): “No client list exists”

  2. Strategic silence (August): Let it fade from news

  3. Complexity overload (November 12): 23,000-page dump

  4. Institutional force (November 13): Situation Room intimidation

  5. Government weaponization (November 14): Investigate opponents

Part 8 reveals Stage Six: Legal process weaponization

The sophistication: Previous stages tried to prevent transparency. This stage renders transparency legally impossible even after congressional mandate.

How It Neutralizes The Discharge Petition

Scenario: House Passes With Veto-Proof Majority

House votes 310-125 for release. Senate passes 68-32. Trump doesn’t veto. It becomes law.

The law says: DOJ must release all Epstein investigative files.

DOJ Response:

“We’d love to comply with Congress. But we have active criminal investigations ongoing. We cannot release materials that might compromise those investigations. Here’s our legal brief explaining why the law’s timeline must be extended indefinitely pending investigation conclusion.”

What Courts Will Likely Do:

Accept it. Because courts always defer to DOJ on “pending investigation” claims.

The investigation doesn’t need to produce indictments. It doesn’t need grand jury proceedings. It just needs to exist—providing legal cover for non-compliance with congressional mandates.

The Three-Year Timeline—And The Pardon Question

Kirschner’s calculation: The investigation will last “until probably the day after Trump leaves office.”

Trump’s apparent strategy: Delay file release while controlling DOJ, then face consequences from private citizenship—potentially after issuing himself a blanket federal pardon on his last day in office.

But the self-pardon strategy has fatal flaws:

First, it’s constitutionally untested. The Supreme Court has never ruled on whether presidents can pardon themselves. If challenged, it might fail.

Second, it only covers federal crimes. State prosecutions in New York, Florida, or the U.S. Virgin Islands could proceed regardless of federal pardon.

Third, it doesn’t prevent civil liability. Epstein’s victims can sue Trump in civil court no matter what pardons he issues.

Fourth, it requires public admission. Issuing a self-pardon for Epstein-related conduct would be Trump publicly acknowledging there’s something pardonable—the very guilt he’s spent months claiming is a “hoax.”

Fifth, it doesn’t erase the evidence. Even with a pardon, the files would still document what Epstein wrote: “Of course he knew about the girls.”

The investigation’s real purpose isn’t to make Trump immune from future consequences. It’s to delay transparency while he maintains power—hoping years of obstruction exhaust public pressure, fragment victim advocacy, and give him time to build legal defenses (including self-pardon) before accountability arrives.

But victims don’t forget. Evidence doesn’t expire. And obstruction—no matter how sophisticated—doesn’t change what Epstein documented in his own hand.

First Trump Term:

Second Trump Term:

  • Now assigned to “investigate” Epstein network—but only Trump’s opponents

An actual federal prosecutor might follow evidence wherever it leads and investigate Republicans and Democrats equally.

A corporate lawyer with zero prosecution experience will do exactly what Trump tells him and maintain “pending investigation” status indefinitely without ever bringing charges.

Clayton’s lack of prosecution experience isn’t a bug—it’s the feature.

Why This Might Work

The Legal Precedent Is Strong

Courts genuinely defer to DOJ on pending investigation claims. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that grand jury secrecy is paramount, ongoing investigations merit protection, and public interest in transparency must yield to prosecution integrity.

These are good legal principles for actual investigations.

Trump is weaponizing good legal principles for illegitimate purposes.

The Bipartisan Protection Factor

Part 7 documented that Epstein accommodation wasn’t just Trump—Stacey Plaskett, Stephen Bannon, Larry Summers, Kathryn Ruemmler, Tom Barrack all appear in documents.

If files implicate members of both parties, both parties’ leadership may quietly accept the “pending investigation” excuse as preferable to full transparency.

Kirschner’s dark observation: “Might it be that the leadership of both parties have absolutely no interest in holding accountable rich, connected, powerful, influential people?”

Why Didn’t Biden’s DOJ Act?

2021-2025: Merrick Garland had the same files.

If they truly contain evidence of crimes by Clinton, Summers, Hoffman, and others as Trump now claims—why didn’t Biden’s Attorney General investigate?

Four years. Same documents. Same evidence. No prosecutions announced.

Either the files don’t contain prosecutable evidence (making Trump’s investigation pretextual) or Garland chose not to pursue powerful people (validating Kirschner’s theory).

Neither answer is good. Both suggest the investigation isn’t about justice.

Why This Might Not Work

Congress Has Constitutional Powers

The discharge petition doesn’t just request file release—it legally mandates it. If DOJ refuses citing “pending investigation,” Congress has options:

Contempt proceedings against Bondi and Clayton, appropriations power to cut SDNY funding until compliance, impeachment of Bondi for defying congressional mandate, and subpoena power to force testimony about investigation legitimacy.

Courts might defer to DOJ on FOIA requests but be less deferential when Congress exercises Article I authority.

The Investigation Must Appear Legitimate

For “pending investigation” defense to work, the investigation must be plausibly real—grand jury empaneled, witnesses subpoenaed, evidence gathered, prosecutorial resources committed.

If it’s obviously pretextual with no actual investigative activity, courts might reject the defense.

The risk: Clayton can make it look real without it being real. Empanel grand jury, issue subpoenas, drag it out for years, never bring charges.

The July Memo Is Documented Evidence

Kirschner identified the smoking gun: July memo saying “nothing in the files warrants further investigation.”

If DOJ claims in court they can’t release files due to “pending investigation,” challengers have Exhibit A: DOJ’s own statement four months ago that no investigation was warranted.

Courts might reject obvious pretextual investigation when documentary evidence proves DOJ already concluded no investigation was justified.

Victims Can Challenge The Excuse

Elizabeth Stein and Jessica Michaels—two victims who watched the 218th signature—can sue arguing: “We’re the crime victims. We WANT the files released. How does releasing evidence of crimes against us compromise investigation?”

That’s a compelling argument courts might credit over DOJ’s blanket “pending investigation” claim.

The Democracy Test Continues

What Part 8 Reveals

The discharge petition was never about just forcing a vote. It was about testing whether constitutional mechanisms can overcome elite protection of predators.

Part 6 concluded optimistically: 218 signatures proved constitutional mechanisms work.

Part 8 reveals: The test isn’t over. Trump may have found a legal mechanism to ignore congressional mandates.

But the counter-test emerges: Can Congress, courts, and public pressure overcome DOJ weaponization of due process?

The Sophisticated Trap

Previous accommodation stages were obvious obstruction. Legal process weaponization is different: It uses legitimate legal principles (protecting ongoing investigations) for illegitimate purposes (blocking transparency).

Courts must decide: Do we defer to DOJ’s “pending investigation” claim even when:

  • DOJ said four months ago no investigation was warranted?

  • Investigation targets only political opponents?

  • Investigation announced immediately after discharge petition succeeded?

  • Non-prosecutor installed to lead it?

  • Congressional mandate explicitly requires release?

Three Possible Outcomes

Outcome 1: The Trap Works

Courts defer to DOJ. “Pending investigation” blocks release for 3+ years. Files emerge in 2029 after Trump leaves office. Political moment passes. Accommodation succeeds through legal delay.

Outcome 2: Courts Reject Pretext

Judges recognize obvious pretextual investigation. July memo proves DOJ already concluded no investigation warranted. Courts order immediate compliance with congressional mandate. Constitutional mechanisms prevail.

Outcome 3: Political Pressure Forces Compliance

Veto-proof House majority + Senate passage + victim advocacy + public outrage = DOJ realizes “pending investigation” defense won’t survive. Bondi releases files to avoid contempt/impeachment. Democracy works messily but ultimately.

Conclusion: When Due Process Becomes A Weapon

What Friday Night Revealed

Glenn Kirschner explained that Trump’s investigation order wasn’t about finding crimes. It was about creating legal cover to ignore congressional mandates.

The sophistication: You can’t criticize it without sounding like you oppose legitimate law enforcement. “What’s wrong with investigating crimes?” sounds reasonable—until you examine the timing, the targets, the July memo, and Clayton’s installation.

The Fundamental Question

Is the investigation legitimate law enforcement that happens to provide legal basis for delay?

Or is it pretextual obstruction weaponizing due process to defeat constitutional mechanisms?

The answer determines whether accommodation psychology has found its most effective stage yet—or whether courts and Congress will recognize weaponized legal process as the sophisticated obstruction it represents.

Why This Matters Beyond Trump

If Trump succeeds in using “pending investigation” to ignore congressional mandates:

  • Future presidents learn the tactic

  • Discharge petitions become meaningless

  • DOJ independence dies completely

  • Constitutional oversight collapses

  • Elite protection becomes permanent

The stakes aren’t just Epstein files. The stakes are whether any congressional oversight mechanism can function when DOJ serves presidential rather than public interest.

What Next Week’s Vote Still Means

Even if the investigation trap might work, the vote must happen.

Because the alternative—accepting that Trump can prevent transparency through pretextual investigation—means accepting that accommodation psychology has evolved beyond constitutional checks.

218 signatures forced the vote. The vote will force confrontation with whether “pending investigation” blocks congressional mandates. That confrontation will force courts to decide whether DOJ can weaponize due process.

None of these confrontations happen without the discharge petition. None of these questions get answered without the vote.

The parade might reach a locked gate. But at least we’ll learn whether anyone has the key—or whether the gate was designed to be impassable all along.
