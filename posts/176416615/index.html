A Diary Becomes Espionage

18 Espionage Act Counts for Sharing Personal Notes with Family

October 17, 2025

BREAKING: John Bolton surrendered to federal authorities this morning, facing 18 counts under the Espionage Act. His crime? Sharing diary entries with his family.

Five days ago, it was predicted this indictment would come “as early as next week.” I documented the pattern: Trump demands prosecution, career prosecutors resist, they’re overruled, indictment follows.

Today, that pattern reached its grotesque conclusion.

The Charges: A Diary Becomes Espionage

The 26-page indictment filed Thursday in federal court accuses President Trump’s former National Security Advisor of “transmitting and retaining highly sensitive material.”

That material? Personal diary entries.

According to prosecutors, Bolton’s notes “described in detail Bolton’s daily activities as the National Security Advisor,” including classified settings and briefings. He allegedly shared these diary entries—over 1,000 pages of personal notes—with his wife and daughter.

Let that sink in: The United States government has charged a former National Security Advisor under the Espionage Act—the same law used against Edward Snowden and Reality Winner—for sharing his diary with his wife and daughter.

The Timeline That Tells Everything

2019-2020: Bolton keeps a diary while serving as National Security Advisor, as many officials do to later write memoirs or simply process the intensity of high-level government service.

2021: The FBI learns about Bolton’s diary entries. The Biden Justice Department investigates. Career prosecutors examine the evidence: personal notes shared with family. They close the case. No charges.

August 2025: Trump takes office. The FBI raids Bolton’s home and office, seizing documents and electronics.

September 20, 2025: Trump posts that Bolton is “guilty as hell.”

October 17, 2025: Bolton faces 18 federal charges for the same diary entries the Biden DOJ declined to prosecute.

The pattern is undeniable: A closed case reopened. Political pressure applied. Career prosecutors overruled. Charges filed.

The Breathtaking Disparity

To understand the weaponization here, consider two cases side by side:

Donald Trump’s Document Case:

  • Took hundreds of classified documents to Mar-a-Lago

  • Stored TOP SECRET materials in a bathroom

  • Documents included nuclear weapons information

  • Showed classified military plans to unauthorized people at his club

  • Refused to return documents when subpoenaed

  • Directed employees to move boxes to hide them from FBI

  • Instructed aide to delete security footage

  • Result: All 40 charges dismissed by Judge Aileen Cannon

John Bolton’s Document Case:

  • Kept personal diary entries from his time in government

  • Shared them with his wife and daughter

  • FBI knew about them since 2021

  • Biden DOJ investigated and found no crime

  • Result: 18 counts under the Espionage Act

One man hoarded nuclear secrets in his bathroom and walked free. The other shared his diary with his wife and daughter and faces potential decades in prison.

Three Critics, Three Weeks, Three Indictments

Bolton’s surrender this morning completes a pattern I’ve been documenting:

September 25: James Comey indicted for perjury—despite career prosecutors finding no probable cause and the Inspector General supporting Comey’s version of events.

October 9: Letitia James indicted for bank fraud—for saving $50 per month on a mortgage by paying it off early.

October 17: John Bolton indicted under the Espionage Act—for diary entries the Biden administration already investigated and cleared.

Each case follows the same trajectory:

  1. Trump publicly demands prosecution

  2. Career prosecutors evaluate and find insufficient evidence

  3. Political pressure intensifies

  4. Prosecutors who resist are marginalized or removed

  5. Charges are filed despite the lack of merit

The Shared Defense

In a telling detail, Bolton is now represented by Abbe Lowell—the same attorney defending Letitia James. Even the defense bar recognizes what’s happening here. These aren’t isolated cases; they’re a coordinated campaign.

Lowell called Bolton’s case “a misuse of prosecutorial discretion,” emphasizing that the diaries were “unclassified, shared only with his immediate family, and known to the FBI as far back as 2021.”

That last point deserves emphasis: This isn’t new information. The FBI has known about these diary entries for four years. The Biden administration knew. They investigated. They found an old man sharing his memories with his family. They closed the case.

What changed? Only who controls the Justice Department.

The Chilling Message

If you’ve ever served in government and kept notes, you should be terrified.

Bolton’s prosecution establishes that:

  • Your personal diary can retroactively become contraband

  • Sharing memories with your spouse or children can be “espionage”

  • A closed investigation can be reopened years later if you criticize the wrong person

  • The Espionage Act—designed for spies selling secrets to foreign powers—now applies to family conversations

Every former official who ever jotted down their thoughts, every staffer who kept a journal, every appointee who wanted to remember their service for their memoirs or their grandchildren—you’re all potential criminals now. All it takes is criticizing the president.

Why Bolton?

Bolton’s real crime wasn’t keeping a diary. It was writing a book.

In June 2020, Bolton published “The Room Where It Happened,” a devastating account of Trump’s first term. The book revealed Trump’s ignorance, his transactional foreign policy, his requests for foreign help with his reelection. It sold millions of copies months before the 2020 election.

Trump never forgave him. He called repeatedly for Bolton’s prosecution. And now, five years later, he’s getting his wish—using diary entries as the pretext.

The message to future tell-all authors couldn’t be clearer: Write about us, and we’ll find something to charge you with. If not your book, then your diary. If not your diary, then your taxes. If not your taxes, then a mortgage from 20 years ago.

We’ll find something. We always do.

The Espionage Act’s Dark Turn

The Espionage Act of 1917 was created to prosecute spies during World War I. It’s been controversially used against whistleblowers who leaked to journalists: Daniel Ellsberg, Chelsea Manning, Edward Snowden, Reality Winner.

But even in those controversial cases, the Act targeted people who deliberately disclosed national security information to the public or foreign powers.

Bolton shared his diary with his wife and daughter.

If that’s espionage, then the word has lost all meaning. If that’s espionage, then every candid conversation between spouses who work in government is a potential federal crime. If that’s espionage, then the Act has become nothing more than a tool for prosecuting political enemies.

Which, of course, is exactly the point.

What Happens Next

John R. Bolton, who served as a national security adviser during President Trump’s first term, leaving his home in Bethesda, Md., on Friday before a court appearance in Greenbelt.Credit...Alex Kent/Getty Images

Bolton pleaded not guilty this morning in federal court in Greenbelt, Maryland. He arrived at the courthouse “almost before it opened,” according to CNN, appearing in a navy suit and red tie. He didn’t speak to reporters. His statement, released through his lawyer, was defiant: He vowed to “defend my lawful conduct and to expose [Trump’s] abuse of power.”

But defiance only goes so far when you’re facing 18 federal counts.

The magistrate released Bolton with conditions: He must remain in the continental United States and surrender his passport. At 76 years old, John Bolton—who traveled the world as UN Ambassador and National Security Advisor—is now effectively a prisoner in his own country for sharing his diary with his wife and daughter.

His next court date is November 21. Because the case involves classified information, it could take “at least a year” to go to trial. Think about that: Even if Bolton is ultimately acquitted—even if a judge throws out these charges as the political persecution they clearly are—he’ll spend at least a year unable to leave the country, millions in legal fees mounting, his reputation forever linked to “Espionage Act violations.”

The process is the punishment.

And every other potential Trump critic will have received the message loud and clear: This could be you.

The Normalization of Persecution

Three weeks ago, the first indictment seemed shocking. Today, the third feels almost routine. That’s how democratic norms die—not in one dramatic moment, but through steady erosion until the unthinkable becomes mundane.

We’re watching the transformation of the Justice Department from an institution of law into an instrument of revenge. And it’s happening with breathtaking speed:

  • Week 1: Prosecute someone for testimony (Comey)

  • Week 2: Prosecute someone for a mortgage (James)

  • Week 3: Prosecute someone for a diary (Bolton)

What will Week 4 bring? Prosecution for a tweet? For attending a protest? For donating to the wrong candidate?

Once you’ve crossed the line into prosecuting diaries shared with wives and daughters, everything is possible.

The Pattern Is the Point

In my earlier essays in this series, I traced how authoritarian regimes use criminal prosecution to silence critics. Stalin’s show trials. Hitler’s People’s Court. Putin’s prosecution of Navalny. The pattern is always the same:

  1. Identify the enemy

  2. Find or fabricate a crime

  3. Use the machinery of law to destroy them

  4. Make the process public to deter others

We’re living through Step 4 right now. Bolton’s prosecution isn’t really about Bolton. It’s about you—anyone who might consider criticizing this administration. The message is clear: We can reach into your past, find anything you’ve ever written, and turn it into a federal crime.

Keep your diary if you dare.

Conclusion: The Weaponization Complete

When I began this series, I warned that we were watching the construction of an American enemies list. Critics called me alarmist. They said our institutions would hold. They said career prosecutors would resist. They said judges would intervene.

Today, John Bolton—a lifelong Republican, a conservative hawk who served four GOP presidents—cannot leave the United States because he shared his diary with his wife and daughter.

The institutions didn’t hold. The career prosecutors were overruled. The judges haven’t intervened.

The enemies list isn’t coming. It’s here. It’s operational. And it’s working exactly as designed.

Three weeks. Three critics. Three indictments.

The only question left is whether we’ll recognize this for what it is—the systematic weaponization of American justice—before it’s too late to stop it.

Or whether we’ll keep telling ourselves that this is normal, that this is fine, that this could never happen to us.

Until the day it does.
