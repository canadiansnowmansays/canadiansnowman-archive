The Rise and Fall of Matt Gaetz

The Rise and Fall (then Pivot) of Matt Gaetz

Part 2: The Survivor

November 19, 2025

Matt Gaetz, then a Florida congressman, outside the Capitol last year. A bipartisan House Ethics Committee investigation determined there was substantial evidence that he had sex with the 17-year-old in exchange for money.Credit...Haiyun Jiang for The New York Times

The Insurrection

On January 6, 2021, a mob of Trump supporters stormed the U.S. Capitol. That evening, when Congress reconvened to certify the election results, Gaetz rose to object—and to deflect.

“I don’t know if the reports are true,” he said from the House floor, “but the Washington Times has just reported some pretty compelling evidence from a facial recognition company showing that some of the people who breached the Capitol today were not Trump supporters. They were masquerading as Trump supporters and in fact were members of the violent terrorist group antifa.”

The Washington Times later retracted the story. The facial recognition company, XRVision, demanded the article be taken down because it was false—the individuals they had identified were actually affiliated with neo-Nazi organizations and QAnon, not antifa.

But Gaetz had already done his work: providing cover for Trump, muddying the waters, deflecting blame from the people who had just attacked the seat of American democracy at Trump’s urging.

He voted to overturn the election results anyway.

“States that do not run clean elections should be stripped of their electors,” Gaetz declared. “This fraud was systemic. It was repeated. It was the same system, and I dare say it was effective.”

There was no systemic fraud. Trump had lost. But Matt Gaetz had proven, once again, that he would say anything for the cause.

The Investigation Goes Public

Then, on March 30, 2021, the New York Times reported that Gaetz was under investigation by the Justice Department for sex trafficking—specifically, whether he’d had a sexual relationship with a seventeen-year-old girl and paid for her to travel with him across state lines.

That same night, Gaetz appeared on Tucker Carlson’s show to defend himself. What followed was, in Carlson’s own words, “one of the weirdest interviews I’ve ever conducted.”

Gaetz claimed it was all an extortion plot. He demanded the FBI release recordings. He named a former DOJ official as the mastermind. Then he tried to rope Carlson into the controversy, insisting the host would remember a woman Gaetz had brought to a dinner they’d shared years earlier—a woman Gaetz claimed was threatened by the FBI.

“I don’t remember the woman you are speaking of or the context at all, honestly,” Carlson replied.

When Gaetz argued he wasn’t the only person on screen who’d been falsely accused of a sex crime—referencing a decades-old allegation against Carlson himself—the host cut him off.

“You just referred to a mentally ill viewer who accused me of a sex crime 20 years ago,” Carlson said. “And of course, it was not true.”

After the segment ended, Carlson addressed his audience directly: “That was one of the weirdest interviews I’ve ever conducted. I don’t think that clarified much. But it certainly showed this is a deeply interesting story.”

The investigation was real. In April, the House Ethics Committee opened its own inquiry. In May, Joel Greenberg—Gaetz’s friend and the man at the center of the federal probe—pleaded guilty to sex trafficking charges and agreed to cooperate with prosecutors. Reports emerged that the FBI had seized Gaetz’s cell phone.

Fox News reportedly put Gaetz on an informal blacklist for a year after the disastrous Carlson interview. CNN reported that Carlson was livid about Gaetz’s attempt to implicate him.

And Matt Gaetz did what he always did: he survived.

In August 2021, he married Ginger Luckey, whose brother Palmer founded Oculus VR and was a major Republican donor. He continued appearing at Trump rallies. He remained a fixture on conservative media—just not Fox News.

By early 2023, the DOJ closed its investigation without filing charges. The Ethics Committee investigation continued quietly. But Gaetz was still standing.

The Peak

By October 2023, Gaetz had consolidated his position as Trump’s most reliable attack dog in Congress—and he was about to prove it in spectacular fashion.

On October 3, 2023, Gaetz filed a motion to vacate the chair, triggering a vote to remove Kevin McCarthy as Speaker of the House. McCarthy had committed the cardinal sin of working with Democrats to pass legislation keeping the government open.

With the support of only seven other House Republicans, Gaetz succeeded. McCarthy became the first Speaker in American history to be voted out of office.

“Has there been a politician both as broadly despised, including in his own party, and yet as improbably effective as Matt Gaetz?” Benjamin Wallace-Wells asked in The New Yorker.

McCarthy accused Gaetz of acting on a personal vendetta—specifically, that Gaetz wanted McCarthy to quash the House Ethics Committee investigation that had been quietly examining allegations against the Florida congressman since 2021.

Allegations involving sex. Drugs. And a seventeen-year-old girl.

At the moment of his greatest political triumph, Matt Gaetz was also a man under active investigation by the House Ethics Committee for crimes that would make the allegations against him public in ways he could not control.

The investigation had begun with his friend.

The Tax Collector

Joel Greenberg was elected tax collector of Seminole County, Florida, in 2016—the same year Gaetz won his congressional seat. The two men became close. They traveled together. They partied together. They shared a taste for young women, drugs, and the kind of excess that comes with the assumption of impunity.

In 2020, federal investigators began examining Greenberg for stalking a political opponent by mailing fake letters to the school where the opponent worked, claiming the man had sexual relations with a student. As they dug into Greenberg’s finances and communications, they discovered something else: evidence of a sex trafficking operation involving multiple women, some of them minors, paid through Venmo and other apps.

And they discovered that Greenberg had been introducing these women to other men.

Including Matt Gaetz.

The Girl

She was seventeen years old. A junior in high school. Her parents were divorced. One of them was so poor they lived in and out of a homeless shelter—and when the girl stayed with that parent, she lived in the shelter too.

She worked at McDonald’s, trying to save money for braces to fix her teeth.

In December 2016, hoping to earn more, she created a profile on a website that matched older men seeking “companionship” with young women looking for money. She listed her age as eighteen. She was lying.

In April 2017, she met Joel Greenberg through the site. He invited her to his boat. They didn’t have sex that day, but he paid her $400 and gave her ecstasy to try at home.

Shortly after, they met at a hotel. They had sex. He paid her another $400.

Over the following months, Greenberg would have sex with her seven times for money before she turned eighteen, often providing ecstasy, sometimes paying extra for taking the drugs during sex. He used both his personal credit card and one issued to the Seminole County tax collector’s office.

Then, on July 15, 2017, Greenberg invited her to a party at the home of Chris Dorworth, a former Republican state legislator working as a lobbyist for Ballard Partners—a firm run by a major Trump fundraiser.

Cocaine and ecstasy were offered. The girl took ecstasy and drank. She danced naked. She swam naked in the pool.

Matt Gaetz, then thirty-five, was at the party with a girlfriend.

According to testimony the girl would later provide to the House Ethics Committee, she had sex with Gaetz twice that evening—once on a pool table or air hockey table, while Dorworth watched and “laughed about it with other partygoers.”

Gaetz paid her $400.

That fall, she moved to Texas to live with a family member. She never finished high school. She continued working minimum-wage jobs in food service. Eventually, between the money from her “dates” and her legitimate work, she saved enough for braces.

The Investigation Expands

By 2021, the Justice Department’s investigation into Joel Greenberg had expanded to include Matt Gaetz. Federal prosecutors were examining whether Gaetz had sex with a minor and paid for her to travel across state lines—a federal sex trafficking offense.

Greenberg pleaded guilty in May 2021 to six federal charges, including sex trafficking of a minor, and agreed to provide “substantial assistance” to prosecutors. He gave investigators years of Venmo and Cash App transactions, thousands of photos and videos, and access to his personal social media accounts.

At his sentencing in December 2022, the judge noted that Greenberg’s cooperation was “more than I’ve seen in 22 years.” He was sentenced to eleven years in prison.

But the Justice Department never charged Gaetz. Prosecutors were reportedly concerned that the seventeen-year-old victim—now an adult—would not be seen as credible under cross-examination by defense lawyers.

The girl who had been paid for sex by powerful men was deemed too damaged by that experience to be trusted to testify about it.

Matt Gaetz had survived again.

The Ethics Committee investigation continued.
