Peace in Our Time (Please Allow Five Business Days)

How Washington Learned to Love the Deadline It Never Intends to Keep

March 29, 2026

"The microwave beeps. The Strait keeps burning." Image and caption: CanadianSnowmanSays

The five-day clock began ticking Thursday morning, which is either a coincidence or a scheduling miracle, because Thursday is also the day Americans begin mentally preparing for the weekend and are therefore least likely to notice that a countdown has been announced which nobody expects to reach zero.

“They better get serious soon,” the President posted on Truth Social, “before it is too late, because once that happens, there is NO TURNING BACK, and it won’t be pretty!”

There is a specific grammar that belongs to American presidential ultimatums in the social media age. The capital letters signal genuine feeling. The exclamation point signals urgency. The passive construction — there is NO TURNING BACK — signals that responsibility for whatever happens next has already been distributed to parties TBD. And the five-day window signals that someone, somewhere, has decided that the Strait of Hormuz crisis can be resolved in roughly the same timeframe as a mattress delivery.

This is what I have come to think of as performative time — the cousin of performative outrage and performative negotiation, which we have also watched at close range. Performative time looks exactly like a countdown but is never intended to reach zero. It is the microwave that beeps while the food continues to burn. It is the deadline that exists not to impose consequence but to impose the appearance of consequence — which, in this administration, is the closest approximation to governance we are likely to get.

The beauty of the five-day window, from a purely aesthetic standpoint, is that it requires almost no one to behave differently.

Benjamin Netanyahu, who understands the assignment, has ordered a 48-hour maximum-destruction sprint against Iranian arms infrastructure. This is not a violation of the pause. The pause has not started yet. Indeed, from a certain angle, it might be argued that he is helpfully clearing the table before negotiations begin — which is one way to enter talks, and is also, one notes, a method of making them unnecessary.

Iran, for its part, has submitted a five-point counter-proposal. The terms include war reparations, formal recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait, and protection guarantees for Hezbollah. These are not the terms of a country on its knees. These are the terms of a country that has read Brent crude at $106 a barrel, watched 800 tankers form the longest queue in the history of maritime commerce, and concluded that controlling the bottleneck is, at this particular moment, a form of leverage that no F-35 squadron can simply erase from a spreadsheet.

Meanwhile — and here the satire genuinely struggles to keep pace with the ledger — the USS Abraham Lincoln absorbed a cruise missile strike last Tuesday. First successful hit on an American carrier in this conflict. The Pentagon is preparing a $200 billion supplemental budget request. Congressional Republicans, emerging from classified briefings with the expressions of people who have just discovered the restaurant they booked has no tables, are beginning to say, carefully, that they are “not getting enough answers.” One Armed Services Committee chairman told reporters the situation was “concerning” — which, in the Senate, is the rough equivalent of standing in a burning building and describing the smoke as “atmospheric.”

Diesel is at $5.38 a gallon. The American strategic minesweeping capacity in the region currently consists of four aging vessels stationed, for reasons that now seem eccentric, in Japan.

I follow all of this from Toronto, where the greatest geopolitical controversy of the moment involves whether the federal election will produce a government capable of maintaining a position for more than one news cycle, and whether the carbon tax should apply to hockey rinks. Our absurdities are smaller, cheaper, and blessedly free of carrier strike groups. From this northern distance, the whole production has the quality of watching someone play a very expensive game of chicken from the passenger seat of their own limousine — the principles are clear, the exits are not, and the driver keeps posting about it.

What the other Stephen — Leacock, the one with the actual jokes — understood is that the finest political comedy lives in the gap between the announcement and the reality. I am doing something, says the announcement. Nothing has changed, says the reality. The laugh lives in that interval, and it ages poorly, because the interval keeps widening.

The five-day clock ticks. Netanyahu’s jets conduct their timed demolition derby. Pakistan’s envoy shuttles proposals that all parties have publicly dismissed and privately left on the table, because it is the nature of shuttle diplomacy that it must be seen to continue even when everyone has agreed, quietly, that it won’t succeed. Somewhere in Muscat or Doha, a diplomat checks his watch — not to know the time, but to calculate how many more five-day pauses a single war can sustain before the word “ceasefire” is replaced by something we haven’t coined yet.

The intermission, in other words, is not between acts. The intermission is the act. The pause is the policy. And the clock — God bless it — will expire Thursday morning, at which point another window will be announced, or the bombs will resume, or the price of Brent will make the decision that the diplomats couldn’t.

What is not performative: the thirteen Americans who are not coming home. The 800 tankers idling in actual water. The $200 billion supplemental request drawn on an actual Treasury. The four minesweepers in Japan, representing an actual absence.

Only the clock is performative. Only the clock was never meant to strike.

This is Day 29. Or Day 30. The difference, like so much else right now, is mostly a matter of how you count the pauses.
