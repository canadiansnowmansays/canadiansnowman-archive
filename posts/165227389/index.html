The Putin TACO

Episode 2: From "24 Hours" to Senate Delays - When Ukraine Promises Meet Washington Reality

June 10, 2025

When Ukraine Promises Meet Washington Reality

Welcome to Episode 2 of Trump TACO Tuesdays, where we track the gap between bold presidential promises and inevitable reality checks. Last week's Double TACO analysis proved remarkably prescient: China policy moderated from Trump's "TOTALLY VIOLATED" accusations to a "very positive" 90-minute Xi call and renewed London trade talks, while steel tariffs implemented exactly as predicted—targeted 50% increases that sent U.S. steel stocks surging 11-24% rather than broader economic disruption. This validation of the TACO framework makes this week's analysis even more significant: Trump's Ukraine promises colliding with the complex realities of foreign policy, congressional oversight, and diplomatic constraints.

The Putin TACO represents perhaps the most psychologically revealing example of the pattern. Unlike the China situation, which followed the classic TACO progression from confrontation to cooperation within days, the Ukraine situation exposes a darker dynamic—the gap between Trump's strongman rhetoric and his actual effectiveness when confronting a strategic adversary who understands his vulnerabilities. Where trade policy TACOs allow institutional constraints to provide face-saving explanations for moderation, the Putin TACO reveals personal psychology overriding national interests.

The Bold Promise: "24 Hours" Becomes Forever

The initial Trump promise was characteristically absolute: "I will have that war settled in one day – 24 hours." This wasn't hedged political rhetoric but a definitive declaration repeated throughout the 2024 campaign. Trump positioned himself as uniquely capable of what the entire international community had failed to achieve – immediate resolution of Europe's largest conflict since World War II.

The promise relied on classic Trump mythology: personal relationships trump institutional processes, deal-making skills overcome strategic complexity, and bold leadership succeeds where conventional diplomacy fails. Trump's emphasis on his "excellent relationship" with Putin suggested the conflict could be resolved through direct negotiation between strongmen, bypassing the messy realities of Ukrainian sovereignty, NATO coordination, and Russian strategic objectives.

This framing ignored fundamental realities that would inevitably force a TACO. Ukraine's government has consistently rejected territorial concessions. NATO allies have invested heavily in Ukrainian defense and expect consultation on any resolution. Congress has appropriated tens of billions in aid with specific conditions. Putin has shown no willingness to abandon territorial gains without massive concessions that would humiliate the West.

Early Warning Signs: The Pattern Emerges

The first signs of TACO development appeared within weeks of Trump's inauguration. The "24 hours" timeline quietly disappeared from presidential statements, replaced by vaguer references to "very soon" and "in the near future." This linguistic softening represents the classic early-stage TACO – the gradual introduction of caveats that weren't present in the original bold declaration.

More revealing was Trump's April 2025 social media plea: a desperate "Please stop" directed at Putin to halt the bombing of Ukraine. This public begging violated every principle of effective diplomacy and revealed Trump's complete lack of leverage over Russian decision-making. Putin's response was swift and unambiguous: not only did the bombing continue, but it intensified dramatically, culminating in a devastating 400-drone assault that preceded their May 19 phone call.

This sequence perfectly illustrates the TACO psychology: bold initial promises → reality check → desperate personal appeals → public humiliation → gradual retreat from original position. Trump's plea represented the moment when campaign confidence crashed into the hard reality that Putin views him as weak rather than formidable.

The May 19 Call: Strategic Humiliation Disguised as Diplomacy

Trump's two-hour call with Putin on May 19, 2025, epitomizes the TACO pattern in foreign policy. While Trump characterized the conversation as going "very well" and emphasized future "large scale TRADE" opportunities, the optics revealed a deeper power imbalance that chess grandmaster Gary Kasparov expertly analyzed.

Putin's decision to take the call from a music school in remote South Russia was a calculated display of disdain. By conducting this supposedly crucial conversation from such an informal, provincial setting, Putin simultaneously diminished the significance of speaking with the American president while projecting confidence in his dominance over the relationship. This wasn't diplomatic casualness – it was strategic humiliation designed to reinforce Trump's subordinate position.

The call's timing was equally revealing. It occurred just days after the massive Russian drone assault that followed Trump's public plea for peace. Putin had demonstrated his ability to ignore American demands with impunity, then graciously agreed to discuss "trade opportunities" from his position of strength. For Trump, desperate for any sign of diplomatic progress, this represented success. For Putin, it was masterful psychological manipulation.

Trump's post-call focus on future commercial opportunities while Ukraine burned exemplified the transactional mindset that Putin exploits so effectively. While a strategic leader would leverage economic incentives for immediate concessions, Trump offered future rewards in exchange for conversation – exactly the dynamic that enables continued Russian aggression.

The June 4 Capitulation: "Let Them Fight for a While"

The Putin TACO reached its most revealing moment on June 4, 2025, when Trump effectively abandoned his "24 hours" promise in the starkest terms possible. Following Ukraine's audacious "Operation Spiderweb"—a drone strike on Russian nuclear-capable bombers across five bases from Murmansk to Siberia—Putin called Trump to announce his intention to retaliate.

Rather than leverage this moment to pressure Putin toward peace, Trump passively accepted the escalation threat. In a 75-minute call, Putin told Trump "very strongly" that Russia "will have to respond to the recent attack on the airfields." Trump's response revealed the complete collapse of his Ukraine strategy: he acknowledged the conversation "would not yield an immediate peace" and made no mention of applying pressure for a ceasefire.

The psychological surrender became complete the following day when Trump compared Ukraine and Russia to "two young children fighting" during a meeting with German Chancellor Friedrich Merz. "Sometimes you see two young children fighting like crazy in a park, and you try and pull them apart. They don't want to be pulled, sometimes you're better off letting them fight for a while," Trump said, adding that he'd related this analogy to Putin during their call.

This represents the ultimate TACO moment: the leader who promised to end the war in "24 hours" now suggesting it might be better to "let them fight for a while." The linguistic evolution from decisive intervention to passive observation perfectly illustrates how Putin's psychological dominance has neutered Trump's initial bold promises. Where other presidents might have used Ukraine's successful operation as leverage against Russian aggression, Trump treated it as justification for American disengagement.

The most dramatic TACO development came with Trump's request for Senate delay of Russia sanctions consideration. According to Senate Armed Services Committee Chair Roger Wicker, Trump specifically asked lawmakers to postpone action on additional sanctions – a move that perfectly fits the pattern of initial tough rhetoric giving way to accommodation.

This request represents the institutional constraint phase of a classic TACO. Campaign promises of getting "tough on Putin" crashed into the legislative reality that Congress, not the president, controls sanctions policy. Rather than push for stronger measures that might give him leverage in Ukraine negotiations, Trump sought to reduce pressure on Russia – exactly the opposite of what his campaign rhetoric suggested.

The timing was particularly damaging. The request came as Putin was escalating attacks on Ukrainian infrastructure and threatening broader retaliation for Western support. Instead of responding with strength, Trump's first instinct was to reduce pressure – sending a signal of weakness that Putin undoubtedly noted for future reference.

Republican senators' resistance to this request highlights how even Trump's own party recognizes the strategic folly of accommodating Russian aggression. The institutional wisdom of Congress, in this case, serves as a check on presidential weakness rather than bureaucratic overreach.

The Psychological Dimension: Fear-Driven Decision Making

What distinguishes the Putin TACO from trade policy TACOs is the psychological component that multiple observers have identified. Mary Trump's clinical assessment of her uncle as a "frightened little boy" whose bravado masks deep insecurities perfectly explains his behavioral shift when confronting Putin versus other adversaries.

This fear-driven psychology manifests in several observable patterns:

Avoidance of Escalation: While Trump routinely threatens military action against Iran, North Korea, and even allied nations, he consistently avoids any rhetoric that might provoke Russian retaliation. His Ukraine diplomacy emphasizes de-escalation rather than leverage – exactly the opposite of his approach elsewhere.

Personal Appeals Over Strategic Pressure: Trump's preference for direct appeals to Putin, rather than coordinated pressure through allies and institutions, reflects his comfort with transactional relationships over strategic confrontation. This approach plays directly into Putin's strengths while abandoning American advantages.

Accommodation Disguised as Deal-Making: Trump's focus on "trade opportunities" and future cooperation allows him to frame accommodation as business acumen rather than strategic weakness. This psychological defense mechanism enables continued concessions while preserving his self-image as a successful negotiator.

The Kompromat Factor: Leverage Beyond Economics

The Putin TACO's most troubling aspect involves potential leverage that goes beyond normal diplomatic or economic pressure. Trump's extraordinary secrecy about his Putin interactions – including the confiscated interpreter notes from Helsinki 2018 – suggests vulnerabilities that traditional TACO analysis cannot fully capture.

Whether specific kompromat exists or not, Putin's behavior indicates confidence in his psychological dominance over Trump. The disdainful manner of their interactions, the timing of Russian escalations around American diplomatic initiatives, and Putin's ability to extract concessions without offering meaningful reciprocal measures all suggest leverage beyond normal state-to-state relations.

This dynamic fundamentally alters the TACO pattern. While trade policy moderation can be explained by institutional constraints and economic complexity, Trump's accommodation of Russian aggression requires psychological explanation. The "frightened little boy" assessment provides that framework – suggesting personal vulnerabilities that Putin exploits more effectively than any foreign adversary has managed with previous American presidents.

Market and Strategic Implications

Unlike trade TACOs that primarily affect economic markets—as we saw with last week's steel tariff increases benefiting domestic producers—the Putin TACO has profound implications for global security architecture. Each display of American weakness encourages aggression from other adversaries while undermining confidence among allies.

European allies increasingly question American reliability when Trump's Ukraine promises prove empty. NATO's Article 5 guarantees become less credible when the American president appears unable to influence Russian behavior despite overwhelming economic and military advantages. This erosion of deterrent credibility invites exactly the kind of aggression that American strength traditionally prevents.

For China, watching Trump's ineffectiveness with Putin provides valuable intelligence about American resolve in potential Taiwan scenarios. If the United States cannot effectively confront Russian aggression in Europe – where American interests are clearly defined and allied support is strong – it suggests fundamental weakness in American leadership that Beijing can exploit.

The economic implications extend beyond immediate market reactions. Trump's emphasis on future "trade opportunities" with Russia while sanctions remain in place creates uncertainty about long-term American policy coherence. Businesses cannot plan effectively when presidential promises contradict congressional mandates and alliance commitments.

The Strategic Pattern: Strength Through Accommodation?

Trump's approach to Putin reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of how power operates in international relations. His belief that personal relationships and commercial incentives can overcome strategic competition ignores the zero-sum nature of geopolitical conflict.

Putin's strategic brilliance lies in exploiting this misunderstanding. By offering the prospect of personal success (trade deals, diplomatic breakthroughs) while maintaining strategic pressure (military escalation, territorial expansion), Putin keeps Trump focused on potential rewards rather than actual Russian behavior.

This dynamic creates a perverse incentive structure where Russian aggression is rewarded with American accommodation. Trump's desire to appear successful as a negotiator makes him vulnerable to manipulation by adversaries who understand that offering him personal victories can secure strategic concessions.

The result is policy incoherence that damages American interests while enabling continued Russian aggression. Each cycle of escalation followed by accommodation teaches Putin that American red lines are negotiable and that persistence pays strategic dividends.

What to Watch Next: TACO Acceleration Indicators

The Putin TACO shows signs of potential acceleration as reality continues to diverge from initial promises. Key indicators include:

Linguistic Evolution: Watch for Trump's language about Ukraine timeline to become increasingly conditional. Phrases like "if both sides want peace" or "it's more complicated than people realize" signal classic TACO development.

Escalation Management: Monitor Trump's responses to Russian provocations. His pattern of de-escalatory rhetoric following Russian aggression indicates comfort with accommodation that Putin will continue testing.

Congressional Pushback: Senate resistance to sanctions delays represents institutional constraint that typically forces TACO moderation. Trump's ability to circumvent congressional oversight will determine how far this TACO can develop.

Allied Coordination: European frustration with Trump's bilateral approach to Russia creates pressure for more traditional multilateral responses. This tension between Trump's personal diplomacy and alliance coordination will force difficult choices.

Putin's Next Moves: Based on the established pattern, Putin will likely escalate military pressure while maintaining diplomatic contact. Watch for increasingly bold Russian actions designed to test American resolve while offering Trump face-saving diplomatic alternatives.

The Ultimate TACO Test

The Putin TACO represents the ultimate test of whether personal psychology can override institutional constraints and strategic interests in American foreign policy. Unlike trade disputes that can be resolved through compromise, the Ukraine conflict involves fundamental questions of sovereignty, territorial integrity, and international law that cannot be easily split.

Trump's "24 hours" promise has already proven impossible, but the question remains: how far will accommodation go before institutional resistance or allied pressure forces a course correction? The answer will determine not only Ukraine's fate but America's credibility as a global leader.

Putin understands this dynamic perfectly. By exploiting Trump's psychological vulnerabilities and transactional mindset, he has effectively neutralized American power in their bilateral relationship despite overwhelming American advantages. This represents perhaps the most successful psychological operation in modern international relations – turning American strength into strategic weakness through personal manipulation.

Conclusion: The Ultimate TACO Revealed

The Putin TACO has demonstrated the complete evolution of the pattern in its most consequential form. Bold promises ("24 hours") gave way to reality checks (ineffective appeals), which led to accommodation (sanctions delays) and ultimately to surrender ("let them fight for a while"). The June 4th Trump-Putin call and subsequent "children fighting" analogy represent the final stage of TACO development—where initial bravado transforms into explicit abandonment of responsibility.

Unlike trade TACOs that affect markets and economic policy, the Putin TACO threatens fundamental American credibility and global stability. When the world's most powerful military and economy can be neutralized through psychological manipulation, it creates dangerous precedents that other adversaries will seek to replicate. Putin's masterful exploitation of Trump's vulnerabilities has turned American strength into strategic weakness through pure psychological dominance.

The complete TACO cycle reveals how personal psychology can override institutional constraints when those constraints depend on presidential leadership. Congress can resist sanctions delays, allies can express frustration, but they cannot force a president to exercise American power effectively when he fundamentally misunderstands his position in a strategic relationship.

As Putin prepares his promised retaliation for Ukraine's drone strikes, he does so confident that Trump will frame any escalation as justification for further American disengagement. The frightened little boy has not just failed to confront the strategic strongman—he has handed him the psychological victory of American withdrawal from global leadership.

The international community must grapple with the reality that American power, while still formidable, can be compromised by leaders who understand how to exploit their counterpart's personal vulnerabilities. The Putin TACO demonstrates that some patterns transcend policy areas and reveal deeper truths about leadership, psychology, and power. When those patterns reach their ultimate conclusion, they represent a fundamental challenge to the international order that depends on predictable American leadership.
