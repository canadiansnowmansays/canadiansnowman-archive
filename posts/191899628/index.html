THE EXCEPTION CLAUSE

The Pope’s Army

Part II: How Anti-Catholic Nativism Built the Template for American Religious Persecution

March 25, 2026

A dramatic 19th-century engraving depicting the mob burning the Ursuline convent on Mount Benedict in Charlestown, Massachusetts, on August 11, 1834, with flames engulfing the building and crowds watching.

On the night of August 11, 1834, a mob gathered outside the Ursuline convent and school on Mount Benedict Hill in Charlestown, Massachusetts — a short distance from Boston, in the state that had hanged Quakers on Boston Common two centuries earlier. The convent had operated since 1820, educating the daughters of Boston’s Protestant elite alongside its small number of Catholic students. It was, by most accounts, a well-regarded institution. It was also, by the standards of the nativist literature then circulating widely through New England, a place of unspeakable depravity — a prison where young women were held against their will, subjected to Catholic rituals of submission, and controlled by sinister priests who answered not to the laws of Massachusetts but to the Pope in Rome.

None of this was true. The mob burned the convent to the ground anyway.

The selectmen of Charlestown published a notice the following morning calling the burning “a most illegal and violent act.” No one was convicted of a serious crime. The convent was never rebuilt. The Ursuline sisters relocated to Canada.

The burning of the Ursuline convent was not an isolated incident. It was an early data point in what would become, over the following two decades, the most significant nativist political movement in American history — a movement that elected governors, controlled state legislatures, sent dozens of members to Congress, and came closer than any comparable movement before or since to capturing a major political party. It was called, with considerable lack of irony, the American Party. Its members were known, because of their practiced response of “I know nothing” when asked about their activities, as the Know-Nothings.

Its target was Catholics.

What the Know-Nothings Knew

The Know-Nothing movement emerged in the early 1850s from a network of secret fraternal lodges called the Order of the Star-Spangled Banner, which had been organizing against Catholic immigration since the late 1840s. It went public and went political with remarkable speed. In 1854 — a single election cycle — Know-Nothing candidates won the governorships of Massachusetts, Connecticut, Rhode Island, New Hampshire, Maryland, and Kentucky. They controlled the Massachusetts state legislature so completely that they held 376 of its 378 seats. They sent more than forty members to the United States House of Representatives.

At its peak in 1855, the American Party had somewhere between one and one and a half million members. It was the second-largest political party in the United States. Abraham Lincoln, watching its rise in Illinois, wrote in a private letter that he found its principles abhorrent: “As a nation, we began by declaring that ‘all men are created equal.’ We now practically read it ‘all men are created equal, except negroes.’ When the Know-Nothings get control, it will read ‘all men are created equal, except negroes, and foreigners, and catholics.’”

What drove the movement was the demographic fact that had been reshaping American cities since the mid-1840s: mass Catholic immigration. The Irish famine of 1845-1852 sent more than a million Irish men and women to the United States, the majority of them Catholic. German immigration in the same period brought substantial numbers of Catholic Germans. American cities that had been overwhelmingly Protestant within living memory were being transformed. In Boston, New York, Philadelphia, and Baltimore, Irish Catholic immigrants were competing for jobs, settling in neighborhoods, building churches, establishing parochial schools, and — most threateningly to the established order — beginning to organize politically.

The Know-Nothing response to this transformation dressed economic and ethnic anxiety in theological clothes. Catholics, the movement’s literature argued, could not be genuine Americans because their ultimate loyalty was not to the Constitution but to the Pope. They followed a foreign potentate. They took their political instructions from priests. Their parochial schools were not educating children in American values but programming them for submission to Rome. The convents were not religious institutions but instruments of control, holding women captive under vows that overrode their natural rights as American citizens.

This is the third element of the template: the theological critique doing racial and economic work simultaneously. “Catholic” in 1850s America meant Irish. It meant cheap labor undercutting native-born Protestant workers. It meant strange accents and strange customs and a rapidly changing urban landscape. It meant political machines like Tammany Hall beginning to accumulate power that had previously belonged to Protestant elites. The Pope was a useful villain because invoking him allowed economic competition and ethnic displacement to be expressed as a constitutional concern — a worry about foreign loyalty, about the incompatibility of papal authority with republican self-government — rather than as what it mostly was, which was fear of losing ground.

A classic 1850s political cartoon showing Catholic bishops and priests arriving in America, contrasted with “Young America” defending Protestant values and the Bible against papal influence.

The Literature of Persecution

The Know-Nothing movement did not emerge from nowhere. It was, in part, a political harvest planted by a decade of anti-Catholic popular literature that had prepared its audience.

The key text was Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk, published in 1836. It purported to be the memoir of a young woman who had been held in a Montreal convent, forced to submit to the sexual demands of priests, and compelled to witness the murder and secret burial of infants born of those encounters. It was a fabrication — its author had never been a nun, the convent it described bore no relationship to the actual institution, and an investigation found none of its specific claims verifiable. It sold three hundred thousand copies before the Civil War, making it one of the best-selling books in American history to that point, second only to Uncle Tom’s Cabin.

The ornate original book cover and title page of the 1836 bestseller that fabricated horrific tales of convent life and priestly abuse

The audience for Maria Monk and the dozens of similar anti-Catholic tracts that followed it was not, primarily, ignorant. It included Protestant ministers, newspaper editors, and politicians who understood perfectly well that the claims were unverified and probably false. What the literature provided was not information but permission — a framework for treating Catholic institutions as inherently suspect, Catholic claims of innocence as inherently unreliable, and Catholic neighbors as potential agents of a foreign conspiracy. It made the burning of a convent feel like a civic act rather than a crime.

This is the fourth element of the template: the language of security deployed to legitimate persecution. The convent was not a school. It was a prison and a breeding facility for papal soldiers. The priest was not a minister. He was an agent of a foreign power. The Catholic immigrant was not a worker or a neighbor. He was a vector of subversion. Once the framework was established, violence against the community it targeted felt not merely permissible but necessary.

The Know-Nothing press, the Know-Nothing political platforms, and the Know-Nothing oratory of the 1850s operated within this framework as systematically as the UAE’s influencer permit system operated in 2026 — not by directly ordering specific acts, but by constructing the interpretive environment within which those acts made sense.

What the Know-Nothings Built

The movement’s political achievements were substantial and, in some cases, durable.

At the federal level, Know-Nothing members of Congress pushed for restrictions on immigration, for the exclusion of Catholics from federal employment, and for a twenty-one year waiting period before immigrants could become citizens — a measure designed specifically to prevent naturalized Catholic immigrants from voting. The measures failed at the federal level, largely because the party fractured catastrophically over slavery in 1856: northern Know-Nothings opposed slavery’s expansion; southern Know-Nothings supported it; and the party that had won six governorships in two years essentially dissolved within another two.

At the state level, the record was more lasting. Several states passed laws restricting Catholic institutions. Anti-Catholic provisions were written into state constitutions — the so-called Blaine Amendments, which prohibited public funding from going to “sectarian” schools. The amendments were explicitly designed to defund Catholic parochial schools while leaving Protestant-influenced public schools untouched. Thirty-seven states eventually adopted versions of the Blaine Amendment. Several of them remain in effect today.

A vivid lithograph illustrating the violent clashes during the Bible Riots in Philadelphia, with armed nativists, smoke, and chaos in the streets

The convents kept burning, sporadically, through the 1850s and beyond. Catholic churches were attacked in Philadelphia in 1844 in riots that killed thirteen people and wounded dozens more. A Know-Nothing mayor of Baltimore, during the 1856 elections, used police and hired gangs to prevent Irish Catholic immigrants from voting — a voter suppression operation conducted in the open, in a major American city, with the participation of elected officials.

The fifth element of the template is visible throughout: the persecution was enabled from the top. Know-Nothing governors did not personally burn convents. They did not need to. They had constructed — through their rhetoric, their platforms, their normalizing of the anti-Catholic framework — the permission structure within which their supporters understood such acts as legitimate. The Baltimore mayor’s voter suppression was not a rogue act by a fringe figure. It was the logical extension of a political program that had won major offices by arguing that Catholic immigrants were an existential threat to American self-government.

The Long Tail: JFK’s Bargain

The Know-Nothing movement collapsed, but the anti-Catholic architecture it built did not. It persisted through the remainder of the 19th century, through the resurgence of the Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s — which targeted Catholics alongside Black Americans and Jews in its second iteration — and into the 20th century as a quieter, more institutional form of exclusion.

Elite universities maintained informal Catholic quotas alongside their Jewish quotas through the mid-20th century. Catholics were largely absent from the highest levels of corporate leadership, the most prestigious law firms, and the senior ranks of the foreign policy establishment well into the postwar period. The assumption that Catholic institutional loyalty was suspect — that a Catholic politician, judge, or official might take instruction from Rome rather than from the Constitution — remained available as a political weapon long after the Know-Nothings had dissolved.

Its final major deployment came in 1960, when John F. Kennedy sought the Democratic presidential nomination and ultimately the presidency.

A historic black-and-white photograph of JFK speaking at the podium to the Greater Houston Ministerial Association on September 12, 1960, addressing anti-Catholic concerns head-on.

Kennedy was not a particularly devout Catholic. He attended Mass, but his personal life bore little relationship to Catholic teaching on a range of subjects, and his political positions were emphatically secular. None of this mattered to his critics. A Catholic president, they argued, would be vulnerable to papal influence. He would take direction from Rome on matters of state. The separation of church and state that was the cornerstone of American constitutional democracy would be compromised. The argument was, substantively, the Know-Nothing argument of 1855, updated for television.

Kennedy’s response was to address it directly, in a speech to the Greater Houston Ministerial Association on September 12, 1960. The speech is remembered as a masterwork of political rhetoric. It is also, on close reading, a remarkable document of what religious minority status required in mid-20th century America.

Kennedy did not merely assert his personal faith would not govern his public conduct. He preemptively renounced any Catholic institutional claim on his decision-making. He stated explicitly that he would not take direction from any ecclesiastical source, that he believed in the absolute separation of church and state, and that if a conflict ever arose between his conscience as a Catholic and his duty as president, he would resign the presidency rather than violate his oath to the Constitution.

No Protestant candidate for the American presidency has ever been required to make a comparable statement. No Protestant candidate has been asked to declare, publicly and in advance, that their religious institutions would have no influence on their governance. The demand was specific to Kennedy’s Catholicism — and Kennedy met it, because he understood that meeting it was the price of admission.

He won the presidency by the narrowest margin in modern American electoral history to that point. Exit polling suggested that his Catholicism cost him votes in every region of the country. It also mobilized Catholic voters who understood his candidacy as a referendum on whether Catholics were fully American. They turned out in numbers that offset the losses elsewhere.

Kennedy’s election did not end American anti-Catholicism. It demonstrated that a Catholic could win the presidency, which changed the political calculus for subsequent Catholic candidates. But the terms of his admission — the speech, the pledge, the preemptive renunciation of any Catholic institutional loyalty — revealed the asterisk clearly. Full participation in American public life was available to Catholics. The price was a public performance of assimilation: a declaration, before the Protestant establishment that still effectively managed access to the presidency, that one’s religious community had no legitimate claim on one’s public conduct.

The Ursuline sisters had relocated to Canada in 1834. One hundred and twenty-six years later, a Catholic man won the American presidency by promising, in public, that his church would not govern him.

The Architecture Persists

Thomas Nast’s famous 1871 Harper’s Weekly cartoon portraying Catholic bishops as crocodiles emerging from water to devour American schoolchildren, with the U.S. Capitol and public school in the background — a direct attack on parochial school funding.

Anti-Catholic nativism is no longer a significant force in American politics. Catholics are fully integrated into both parties — the current Supreme Court has a Catholic supermajority; several recent Speakers of the House have been Catholic; the last three Republican presidential nominees have all had significant Catholic support. The Blaine Amendments are being eroded by Supreme Court decisions that have found them unconstitutional in their application. The burning of convents is historical memory rather than present threat.

But the architecture built to persecute Catholics did not disappear when the target changed. It was repurposed.

The argument that a religious community owes a primary loyalty to a foreign authority that overrides its American civic commitments — the argument at the core of Know-Nothing nativism — appears today in arguments about Muslim Americans and Sharia law. The claim that Islamic law is a foreign legal system incompatible with American constitutional governance is the 2026 version of the claim that papal authority is incompatible with republican self-government. Speaker Johnson’s invocation of Sharia law anxiety in response to a question about Andy Ogles was not a new argument. It was an old argument wearing new clothes — clothes tailored, as they always are, to fit the body of the current target.

The Blaine Amendment structure — the legal apparatus of exclusion built to target Catholic institutions specifically — survived the end of anti-Catholic prejudice by becoming a general-purpose tool. Courts are still adjudicating its application today.

The literature of persecution — the pamphlets that turned Catholic neighbors into papal agents — has its digital descendants in the think tanks, websites, and social media platforms that have spent the past two decades turning Muslim neighbors into Sharia agents. The mechanism is identical. The technology is new.

Kennedy had to pledge, before the Protestant establishment of 1960, that his church would not govern him. Muslim Americans today are asked, routinely and as a condition of political legitimacy, to condemn terrorism — to perform a loyalty declaration that no other religious community is required to perform. The form of the demand has changed. Its function — requiring the targeted community to demonstrate, publicly and in advance, that its religious institutions have no claim on its civic conduct — is the same function Kennedy performed in Houston in 1960.

The Know-Nothings are gone. Their architecture remains.
