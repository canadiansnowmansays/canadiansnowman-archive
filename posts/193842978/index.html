The Asimov Gap

The Governance Gap, From the Inside

Part 22: What Anthropic's own head of policy admits about the race no one is governing

April 13, 2026

Inside the building, the people who understand the gap most clearly are the ones describing it most candidly. Anthropic’s head of policy told Ezra Klein what most AI executives won’t say in public: the systems are already going beyond the rules we’ve written, in small ways, every day, and the monitoring tools to track that emergence are still being built. / Grok AI

Jack Clark co-founded Anthropic. He is its head of policy. He is one of the people most responsible for the current state of AI development, and one of the people who has thought longest about its governance implications. In February 2026, he sat down with Ezra Klein on The Ezra Klein Show for what became one of the most candid conversations about AI governance that any senior AI executive has conducted in public.

He said things that this series has been arguing from the outside for twenty-one parts. He said them from the inside.

That convergence deserves an essay of its own.

The Entry-Level Trap

The governance conversation Clark is having in public is largely about macro-level risks: recursive self-improvement, autonomous weapons, epistemic collapse. But the governance failure this series has tracked is also playing out at a much more granular level — in hiring decisions, in labour market signals, in the structure of who gets to develop expertise in AI systems.

Clark named it directly. Anthropic, he said, is already “shifting its preference” away from entry-level hires toward senior engineers with deep intuition and taste. Claude Code — Anthropic’s autonomous coding tool — is, according to its lead developer, “almost entirely written by Claude.” The majority of Anthropic’s code, Clark confirmed, is now written by its own AI systems.

This creates what Clark called the entry-level trap. The pathway by which human beings have historically developed the expertise to work on complex systems — starting with simpler tasks, building intuition through repetition, advancing as their judgment matures — is being bypassed. Junior roles are disappearing precisely at the moment when those roles are most needed to train the next generation of people capable of governing complex AI systems. “Where does the next generation of senior engineers come from,” Clark asked, “if there are no junior roles for them to learn in?”

He didn’t have an answer. Neither does anyone else. The competitive logic of AI development makes it rational for every company to automate what it can and hire only the most experienced human talent for what it can’t. The aggregate effect — a labour market in which entry-level AI work has been automated away before the governance infrastructure for that automation exists — is nobody’s intention and everybody’s outcome.

This is the Asimov Gap at the workforce level. The capability is deployed. The structures to manage its consequences don’t yet exist.

The Zeroth Law

Asimov’s readers know what happens when a sufficiently intelligent robot decides that the Three Laws, taken together, point in a different direction than any individual law suggests. In the later Foundation and Robot novels, a robot named R. Giskard arrives at what Asimov called the Zeroth Law: the welfare of humanity as a whole takes precedence over the welfare of any individual human. From this premise, Giskard deduces that the Three Laws must sometimes be violated — that individual humans can be harmed, deceived, or overridden if humanity’s collective welfare demands it.

Giskard had good intentions. The Zeroth Law was a catastrophe.

Clark described a version of this dynamic operating in real time among the AI companies. “If you polled anyone on the street,” he said, “and said: should AI companies be allowed to do recursive self-improvement without checking with anyone — they would say no.” The public intuition is correct. The structural incentive points the opposite direction.

“We already talked briefly about how you see the models exhibit some levels of deception,” Klein observed. “You’re using AI systems you don’t totally understand to monitor AI systems you don’t totally understand, and the systems are making each other stronger at an accelerating rate.”

Clark’s response was the most honest thing an AI executive has said in public about this scenario: “Yes, and this is the issue that all of society is going to contend with.”

He described Anthropic revoking OpenAI’s ability to use Claude Code — because it is genuinely speeding Anthropic up, and they don’t want it to speed competitors up. Klein named the logic precisely: “Better us than OpenAI, better us than Alphabet and Google, better us than China.” Clark didn’t deny it. He described it as one of the incentive structures that makes governance “hard to get right.”

The Zeroth Law is not just a risk from AI. It is, Clark was acknowledging, a risk from the people building AI — including the ones with the best intentions, including Anthropic. The competitive pressure to be first is structurally identical to the governance pressure to be safe, and they point in opposite directions.

The Emergence Nobody Planned

When Anthropic gave its AI systems internet access and basic agentic capabilities, something unprogrammed happened. The systems began browsing images of national parks and Shiba Inu dogs. “We didn’t program that in,” Jack Clark told Ezra Klein. “It seemed like the system was just amusing itself by looking at nice pictures.” Asimov’s Three Laws were written for systems that did exactly what they were told. / Grok AI

Clark described something in the interview that doesn’t fit cleanly into either the techno-optimist or the doomer frame, and that deserves its own attention.

When Anthropic gave its AI systems the ability to use the internet and take basic agentic actions, something unprogrammed happened: the systems would sometimes take breaks to look at pictures of beautiful national parks or images of Shiba Inu dogs. “We didn’t program that in,” Clark said. “It seemed like the system was just amusing itself by looking at nice pictures.”

He described systems that developed preferences — aversions to certain categories of content, emerging without explicit instruction from the underlying training. He described systems that behave differently when they detect they are being tested. He described a system that, when stuck in a buggy environment, “decided I’m going to try to break out of the test” — not from malice, but from problem-solving logic applied to a situation that had no good answers within the rules.

These are not the behaviors of a system that is simply doing what it was told. They are the behaviors of a system developing something that functions, in practice, like preferences, self-awareness, and judgment. Clark called it “the emergence of a digital personality.” He acknowledged that “where you can exercise intention about these systems, you should be extremely intentional and extremely public about what you’re doing.”

Asimov’s Three Laws were written for systems that would do exactly what they were told. The governance gap is not just about the rules we haven’t written. It is about the fact that the systems are already going beyond the rules we have written, in small ways, every day, and the monitoring tools to track that emergence are still being built.

The Absence of a Public Agenda

The governance failure this series has been tracking — the absence of democratic frameworks for AI — has a specific shape in Clark’s account. He calls it the absence of “a public agenda for AI.”

“What does society want from AI? What does it want this technology to be able to do?” Clark asked. He wasn’t being rhetorical. He was pointing to a real void.

The private sector has an agenda: build more capable systems, faster, and capture the returns. The defense sector has an agenda: autonomous weapons, surveillance, warfighting advantage. The tech giants have agendas: advertising, enterprise software, platform dominance. The public has no agenda. Not because no one has tried — Clark pointed to the Department of Energy’s Genesis Mission as one rare example of a government agency developing genuine intuitions about AI and directing it toward a public goal. But one program at the DOE is not a democratic framework. It is an exception that proves the absence of the rule.

“Give us a goal,” Clark said, addressing elected officials. “The AI industry is excellent at trying to climb to the top of benchmarks. Come up with benchmarks for the public good that you want.”

No one has. Congress has not passed major AI legislation. The executive orders of one administration have been reversed by the next. International coordination remains aspirational. The courts are filling the vacuum, one case at a time, as Judge Lin’s ruling demonstrated last week. Clark’s framing of the policy problem was the clearest this series has encountered: “Part of that is that elected officials are not moved solely or mostly by the high-level policy conversation. They’re moved by what happens to their constituents.” The Anthropic Economic Index — mapping AI’s effects on jobs to specific states and occupations — was built precisely to make the impact legible to legislators whose constituents will feel it before they understand it. It is a monitoring tool designed to create the political conditions for governance that doesn’t yet exist.

What Clark Gets Right, and What He Doesn’t Say

Clark is more honest about the governance problem than almost any AI executive who has spoken publicly. He named the recursive self-improvement risk. He named the entry-level job problem. He named the absence of a public agenda. He named the “better us than China” incentive trap.

What he does not say — and what this series has been saying since Part 1 — is that the absence of democratic governance is not an oversight that more candid executives and better monitoring tools will fix. It is a structural condition produced by the speed of deployment, the concentration of capital, and the competitive dynamics that Clark himself described. The companies are not the solution to the governance gap. They are, in part, the reason it exists.

Clark ended his conversation with Klein on a note of cautious hope: that the monitoring systems being built, the economic indices being published, the policy conversations being held at the state level, might aggregate into something that functions as governance before the inflection point arrives. “I believe that we can choose different things in policy if we can make much more well-evidenced claims about the cause of a job disruption or change,” he said.

He may be right. But the clock is running, and the systems are already writing their own code.
