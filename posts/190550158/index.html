Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 7: The Golden Age: Afghanistan's Brief Peace (1919-1978)

April 1, 2026

Opening: The Photographs That Prove What Was Possible

Young Afghan woman on the streets of Kabul in the late 1960s, captured in everyday modern settings. Source LivingNomads.com

There’s a photograph from 1960s Kabul that circulates widely whenever Afghanistan is in the news. It shows young Afghan women walking through the city - in skirts, uncovered hair, books under their arms, heading to university. They look like they could be in Paris or London or New York.

Another photograph from the same era shows a mixed-gender classroom at Kabul University - men and women sitting together, studying medicine or engineering or literature.

These images shock people today because they seem impossible. Afghanistan? Women in miniskirts? Universities with female students?

But it happened.

Women in the Faculty of Medicine, Kabul University. Image credit: “Afghanistan: Cockpit in High Asia, 1966,” by Peter King

For roughly 59 years - from independence in 1919 through 1978 - Afghanistan experienced its longest period of sustained peace in recorded history. Not perfect. Not without problems. But a period when Afghans controlled their own destiny, when development was possible, when hope seemed reasonable.

This was Afghanistan’s golden age. The proof that with peace and resources, Afghanistan could modernize and prosper.

And then it was destroyed - by internal radicalism, Soviet invasion, and a chain of events that led directly to those starving children in the 2026 New York Times article.

That woman in Badgor holding her child - her grandmother might have attended university. Her mother grew up during the chaos that followed the golden age’s destruction. She herself has known nothing but war.

Three generations. Three completely different Afghanistans.

This is the story of what Afghanistan achieved with peace, and how tragically fragile that achievement proved to be.

Independence and Early Stumbles (1919-1933)

When King Amanullah Khan won independence from British control in 1919, he had ambitious plans. He’d traveled to Europe, seen modernization firsthand, and believed Afghanistan could follow Turkey’s path under Ataturk.

Amanullah’s Reforms (1919-1929)

Amanullah attempted rapid modernization:

  • Constitutional government with parliament

  • Secular education, including schools for girls

  • Abolition of the veil (or making it optional)

  • Western dress codes for officials

  • Legal reforms reducing power of religious courts

  • Infrastructure (roads, telegraph)

  • Modern military organization

The Backlash

These reforms were too much, too fast, too focused on urban elites while ignoring rural Afghanistan.

Rural Afghans - the vast majority - saw reforms as:

  • Attacks on Islam

  • Imposed by a king who’d abandoned Afghan values

  • Beneficial only to city dwellers

  • Threatening to tribal autonomy

In 1929, tribal rebellions forced Amanullah to abdicate and flee into exile. Chaos followed until Mohammad Nadir Shah seized power and was crowned king in 1929.

The Lesson

Even an independent Afghan king, ruling in the name of Afghan nationalism, couldn’t rapidly modernize without triggering resistance.

The same forces that resisted foreign invasion - tribal structure, geographic isolation, suspicion of central authority - also resisted Afghan modernizers.

But Nadir Shah and his successors would learn to move more slowly.

King Zahir Shah: The Long Peace (1933-1973)

Official portrait of King Mohammad Zahir Shah during his long reign

In 1933, Nadir Shah was assassinated. His 19-year-old son became King Zahir Shah.

He would rule for 40 years - the longest stable period in Afghan history.

The Strategy: Gradual, Voluntary Modernization

Unlike Amanullah’s forced reforms, Zahir Shah took a gradualist approach:

  1. Urban modernization without forcing change on rural areas

  2. Voluntary education - building schools but not mandating attendance

  3. Infrastructure first - roads, electricity, hospitals in cities

  4. Women’s rights - allowed in cities, not enforced rurally

  5. Economic development - accepting aid from both superpowers

  6. Light governance - letting rural areas largely self-govern

This created a bifurcated Afghanistan:

Urban Afghanistan:

  • Kabul, Herat, other cities modernized

  • Women attended universities

  • Cinemas, jazz clubs, theaters

  • Western fashion coexisted with traditional dress

  • Growing middle class

  • Professionalized bureaucracy

Rural Afghanistan:

  • Remained deeply traditional

  • Tribal structures intact

  • Islamic law dominant

  • Women rarely educated

  • Subsistence agriculture

  • Minimal government presence

The Cold War Advantage

Afghanistan’s neutrality proved financially lucrative. Both superpowers poured aid in to win influence:

  • USSR: Built roads in north, trained military officers, technical assistance

  • USA: Infrastructure in south, funded universities, agricultural aid

Afghanistan played them beautifully. American and Soviet projects existed side by side.

From 1950-1978, Afghanistan received approximately:

  • $2.5 billion in Soviet aid

  • $500 million in US aid

  • Significant aid from other countries

For a poor country, this was transformative.

The 1960s: Peak Prosperity

The 1960s represented peak prosperity. Those famous photographs date from this period:

  • Women in miniskirts in Kabul (mostly elite, urban)

  • Kabul University with 5,000 students, including women

  • Faculty of Medicine training female doctors

  • Cinemas showing international films

  • Hotels hosting foreign tourists

  • Peace Corps volunteers teaching

  • Hippies on the “hippie trail” stopping in Kabul

For urban, educated Afghans, it must have seemed Afghanistan had finally escaped its violent history.

The Critical Limitations

But this prosperity had severe limitations:

  1. Geographic: Limited to cities and major roads

  2. Class-based: Benefited urban elites, not rural majority

  3. Fragile: Dependent on foreign aid and Cold War competition

  4. Shallow: Modern institutions hadn’t deeply rooted

  5. Unequal: Growing gap between urban and rural Afghanistan

Thomas Barfield notes the paradox: “While the Afghans had recovered from many earlier periods of state collapse, the body politic was now afflicted with an autoimmune disorder in which the antibodies of resistance threatened to destroy any state structure.”

The Seeds of Future Catastrophe

Several developments would prove disastrous:

  1. Military officers trained in USSR - many became communists

  2. Growing inequality - between urban/rural, educated/traditional

  3. Weak institutions - modern surface, traditional underneath

  4. No industrial economy - dependent on agriculture and foreign aid

  5. Pashtun nationalism - ongoing disputes with Pakistan

But in the early 1970s, these seemed manageable. Afghanistan appeared stable, developing, peaceful.

The golden age seemed sustainable.

It wasn’t.

The Unraveling Begins (1973-1978)

By Unknown author / Government of Afghanistan; processing by Roman Kubanskiy - GettyImages

The 1973 Coup

In July 1973, while King Zahir Shah was in Italy for medical treatment, his cousin Mohammad Daoud Khan staged a bloodless coup.

Daoud abolished the monarchy and declared Afghanistan a republic with himself as president.

Daoud was a modernizer - perhaps more radical than Zahir Shah:

  • Promoted women’s rights aggressively

  • Pushed secular education

  • Cracked down on Islamic conservatives

  • Sought closer Soviet ties initially

The Communist Infiltration

The 1973 coup succeeded with help from Afghan communist parties, particularly the People’s Democratic Party of Afghanistan (PDPA).

The PDPA split into factions:

  • Khalq (Masses) - led by Taraki and Amin, radical, wanted revolution

  • Parcham (Banner) - led by Karmal, moderate, preferred gradual socialism

Daoud initially relied on communist support. But by 1975-77, seeing them as threats, he moved to reduce communist influence.

The communists decided to strike first.

The Saur Revolution (April 27, 1978)

Communist military forces and tanks in Kabul during/after the April 27–28, 1978 coup that ended the golden age.

On April 27, 1978, communist military officers launched a coup. In heavy Kabul fighting, they killed Daoud and his family.

The PDPA, under Nur Muhammad Taraki, took control. Afghanistan became the Democratic Republic of Afghanistan.

For the first time, avowed communists ruled the country.

The Radical Reforms

The new government immediately launched reforms that attacked every traditional power structure:

  1. Land redistribution - seizing from landowners

  2. Forced literacy programs - including for women

  3. Debt cancellation - undermining credit systems

  4. Marriage law reforms - banning bride price, setting minimum age

  5. Atheist propaganda - in a deeply Islamic country

  6. Purges - of Islamic leaders, tribal elders, potential opponents

These reforms were implemented with Soviet-style brutality - arrests, executions, torture.

The Rural Rebellion

Rural Afghanistan exploded almost immediately.

In March 1979, Herat rebelled. Army units mutinied and joined rebels. Soviet advisers were killed - some reportedly skinned alive.

The government sent troops to retake Herat. Thousands died.

But Herat was just the beginning. By summer 1979, armed resistance spread across rural Afghanistan. The mujahideen - “those who engage in jihad” - formed into organized guerrilla forces.

Communist Infighting

The PDPA was tearing itself apart. Taraki and his deputy Hafizullah Amin feuded. In September 1979, Amin arranged Taraki’s murder (possibly suffocation with a pillow).

Amin took full power - but he was erratic, paranoid, losing control.

The Soviet Union watched with alarm:

  • Armed rebellion spreading

  • Afghan army defecting to mujahideen

  • Amin’s government collapsing

  • Potential American influence in the vacuum

In December 1979, the Soviets made their fateful decision: They invaded.

The Golden Age: What It Proved

Before we continue to the Soviet catastrophe in Part 8, let’s understand what the golden age proved and what it couldn’t overcome.

What It Proved:

1. Afghans Can Build When Given Peace With 59 years of peace and foreign aid:

  • Universities trained doctors, engineers, teachers

  • Infrastructure connected the country

  • Cities became cultural centers

  • Women could be educated and work professionally

  • A middle class emerged

2. Modernization Is Possible - With Patience Zahir Shah’s gradual approach worked better than Amanullah’s forced reforms. Change was happening, slowly, without triggering mass resistance.

3. Afghanistan Can Navigate Great Powers Playing USSR and USA against each other brought resources without domination. Afghanistan maintained autonomy while accepting aid.

4. Geographic Limitations Can Be Overcome - Partially Roads, schools, and clinics reached some remote areas. Technology was slowly overcoming isolation.

What It Couldn’t Overcome:

1. The Urban-Rural Divide Modernization remained concentrated in cities. Rural Afghanistan stayed traditional. The gap widened, creating resentment.

2. The Fragility of Institutions Modern institutions were shallow - they looked like Western bureaucracies but operated on traditional patronage. When crisis came, they collapsed.

3. The Dependency on Foreign Aid Afghanistan’s economy depended on foreign money. When crisis came, aid stopped, and the economy collapsed.

4. The Tribal Structure Beneath the surface, tribal loyalties remained stronger than national identity. In crisis, people fell back on tribe and valley, not nation.

5. The Lack of Industrial Economy Afghanistan remained agricultural and aid-dependent. No industrial base meant no sustainable economy.

6. The Radicalization Vectors Both communist and Islamic radicalization threatened the moderate path. The center couldn’t hold against extremes.

The Historical Count: The Brief Window

From 1919 to 1978: 59 years of independence and relative peace.

This represents approximately:

  • 2.3% of the 2,500 years we’ve traced

  • The longest sustained peace in Afghan recorded history

  • The only extended period of genuine self-determination

What This Means:

For 97.7% of recorded history, Afghanistan has been:

  • Invaded

  • Occupied

  • Fought over

  • Recovering from invasion

  • Experiencing civil war

The golden age was the exception. War and foreign interference are the rule.

The Tragedy of What Was Lost

When the communist coup came in 1978, Afghanistan lost more than political stability. It lost:

Human Capital:

  • A generation of educated professionals (doctors, teachers, engineers)

  • Women who’d achieved professional status

  • Artists, poets, musicians

  • Civil servants who knew how to govern

Institutional Capital:

  • Universities that trained future leaders

  • Hospitals that served the poor

  • Schools that educated girls

  • Government agencies that functioned

Social Capital:

  • Trust between urban and rural

  • Moderate Islamic leadership

  • Civil society organizations

  • Cultural institutions

Economic Capital:

  • Foreign aid relationships

  • Trade networks

  • Agricultural systems

  • Infrastructure

All of this would be destroyed in the Soviet catastrophe to come.

And once destroyed, it proved nearly impossible to rebuild - because the next 40+ years would be continuous war.

The Bridge to Catastrophe

The golden age ended not with foreign invasion, but with internal radicalism. Afghan communists, trained in the Soviet Union, launched a coup that attacked every traditional power structure simultaneously.

This triggered rural rebellion. The rebellion threatened Soviet interests. The Soviets invaded in December 1979.

What followed would be Afghanistan’s worst catastrophe since the Mongol invasions - and would create the conditions for everything that came after: The Taliban, 9/11, American occupation, and finally, those starving children in 2026.

That three-month-old baby Zakia, vomiting with no medical care - her grandmother might have been one of those women in miniskirts at Kabul University. Her mother was born into Soviet devastation. She herself has never known peace.

The golden age proves what Afghanistan could be with peace.

The destruction that followed proves how fragile that progress was.

In Part 8, we’ll trace the Soviet catastrophe - ten years that killed over a million Afghans, created five million refugees, and destroyed what sixty years had built.
