The Middle Kingdom — A Reader's Guide

How a section found its architecture, and where to find everything.

April 15, 2026

When I published “The 141 Pages” on March 10, I did not know it was the first essay in a section. I knew it was the first essay in a series — The Quiet War was conceived as six parts from the beginning, each building toward the same conclusion: that China is winning a strategic competition the United States is too distracted to see.

What I did not know was that The Quiet War would need neighbours. That Formosa — an eleven-part series on Taiwan I had already written — belonged in the same house. That both of them pointed toward a history that needed telling. The section arrived after the first series was already underway, because the work has a way of telling you what it wants to become.

China calls itself Zhōngguó — the Middle Kingdom. This section examines the country that name describes: its strategic ambitions, its technological competition with the United States, the territories it says it cannot lose, and the history that explains why it behaves the way it does.

I have visited China multiple times, always with fascination. The scale impresses. The grip is real. What visitors do not see is the system that produces the bullet trains and the skylines: the silence of a friend who stops replying to political essays. This section holds both truths simultaneously.

The Middle Kingdom contains three series, published in reverse chronological order — the present first, then the disputed territory, then the history that explains both.

The Quiet War

The strategic competition the Iran war is obscuring. While the United States spends billions bombing Iran, China published a 141-page plan to dominate AI, robotics, quantum computing, rare earths, and semiconductors simultaneously. Nobody noticed. That was the point. Six parts, complete.

Formosa: The Beautiful Problem

The most dangerous unresolved territorial dispute in the world. Thirty-six thousand square kilometres of mountains, microchips, and unresolved legal status. Eleven parts, beginning May 2026. You may have seen the preface posted last week.

China: A History in Ten Parts

How China became the country that published those 141 pages. From the centre of the known world through the Century of Humiliation to Xi Jinping’s consolidation. The foundation everything else in this section rests on. Forthcoming.

The reverse chronology is deliberate. The Quiet War tells you what China is building. Formosa tells you what China says it cannot lose. The history tells you why. Readers who arrive at the history last will understand it differently — and more urgently — than if they had encountered it first.

Navigation footers at the bottom of each essay will guide you between parts and between series. This guide will be linked from all of them.

— Stephen Gordon, Toronto, April 2026
