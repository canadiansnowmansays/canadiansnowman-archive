America's Next Industrial Revolution

An 8-Part Series on EVs, AI, and Robotics

Episode 2: Economic Reality vs. The $1.98 Promise

June 19, 2025

The Current Reality: High Gas Prices and Oil Production Limits

President Trump's campaign promise to cut gas prices to $1.98 per gallon within 12 months reflected a genuine desire to help American families struggling with energy costs. However, the persistence of $3.13/gallon prices despite increased domestic production reveals fundamental limitations in the oil-only approach to energy affordability.

Understanding Global Oil Market Dynamics

The disconnect between Trump's promise and current reality isn't due to lack of effort or bad faith—it's due to the complex nature of global oil markets that even the world's largest producer cannot fully control:

Global Price Setting Mechanisms: Oil is a globally traded commodity. Even if the US produces every barrel it consumes, American prices still respond to worldwide supply and demand. When OPEC cuts production, when conflicts disrupt Middle Eastern supplies, or when China's economy grows and demands more energy, US gas prices rise regardless of domestic drilling activity.

Refining Bottlenecks: The US has sufficient crude oil but limited refining capacity. Many US refineries were designed for heavy crude from countries like Venezuela, not the light crude from Texas shale formations. Converting refineries is expensive and time-consuming, creating bottlenecks that keep gas prices high even when crude is abundant.

Infrastructure Constraints: Moving oil from production sites in Texas and North Dakota to refineries and then to gas stations requires massive pipeline and transportation networks. Infrastructure limitations mean that increased production doesn't immediately translate to lower prices at the pump.

Market Speculation Impact: Oil futures markets, where traders bet on future prices, can drive current costs up or down based on perceived risks. Even successful US production increases can be offset by speculation about future supply disruptions.

The $3.13 Reality and Regional Impact

At current gas prices, the average American household spending 12,000 miles annually (at 25 mpg combined city/highway) consumes 480 gallons of gasoline, costing $1,502 per year. For a family earning $60,000 annually, this represents 2.5% of their gross income—a significant burden affecting spending on housing, healthcare, education, and savings.

Regional Price Variations:

  • California: Often $4.50+ per gallon due to special fuel requirements and taxes

  • Texas: Closer to $2.90 per gallon due to proximity to refineries

  • Midwest: Around $3.10 per gallon, reflecting transportation costs

  • Northeast: $3.20+ per gallon due to transportation and environmental regulations

These variations show that even successful domestic production cannot create uniform, low prices across the country due to infrastructure and regulatory differences.

Economic Ripple Effects: High gas prices affect more than individual drivers:

  • Commercial Transportation: Higher fuel costs for trucking increase prices for all goods

  • Rural Communities: Longer commutes and limited public transportation make rural Americans particularly vulnerable

  • Economic Growth: Consumer spending on gasoline reduces discretionary income for other activities

Oil Industry Employment and Regional Republican Dependence

The American oil and gas industry employs approximately 600,000 workers directly, with millions more in supporting industries. These jobs are geographically concentrated in states crucial to Republican electoral success:

Texas: Energy Powerhouse

  • Direct employment: 230,000 oil and gas workers (38% of national total)

  • Economic integration: Houston's refining complex to Permian Basin drilling

  • Community impact: Small towns transformed by fracking boom

  • Political importance: Core Republican constituency in largest red state

North Dakota: Bakken Transformation

  • Direct employment: 35,000 workers in Bakken formation

  • Population impact: Williston grew from 12,000 to 35,000 residents during boom

  • Economic dependence: Oil drives state's entire economic model

  • Infrastructure strain: Housing, roads, services overwhelmed by rapid growth

Pennsylvania: Rust Belt Revival

  • Direct employment: 32,000 jobs in Marcellus Shale development

  • Regional focus: Rural counties struggling with manufacturing decline

  • Political significance: Swing state with energy-dependent communities

  • Union consideration: Both union and non-union energy workers

Oklahoma: Traditional Production

  • Direct employment: 28,000 workers in oil and gas

  • Historical significance: Long-standing oil economy since early 1900s

  • Diversification challenge: Economy heavily dependent on energy prices

  • Political alignment: Strong Republican base built on energy prosperity

Wyoming: Coal and Oil Combination

  • Direct employment: 18,000 workers in oil and coal production

  • Economic cornerstone: Energy represents primary state revenue source

  • Transition pressure: Coal decline increasing oil importance

  • Federal land issues: Significant portion on federal leases

These workers and their communities represent core Republican constituencies who understandably worry about policies that might threaten their livelihoods. Any transition to alternative energy sources must account for their economic security and provide pathways to equally good jobs in emerging industries.

The Geopolitical Oil Vulnerability

Despite increased domestic production, the US remains vulnerable to global oil market manipulation:

OPEC+ Influence: The Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, led by Saudi Arabia, can still influence global prices through production cuts. In 2023, OPEC+ (including Russia) reduced output by 2 million barrels per day, contributing to price increases worldwide despite US production increases.

Chinese Demand Impact: As the world's largest oil importer, China's economic growth directly affects global prices. When China's economy rebounds from COVID-19 slowdowns or implements stimulus measures, increased demand drives up costs for American consumers regardless of domestic supply.

Infrastructure Vulnerability Risks: Critical oil infrastructure remains vulnerable to cyberattacks, natural disasters, and geopolitical conflicts. The 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack caused gas shortages across the Southeast, demonstrating how quickly supply disruptions can affect American families even with abundant domestic production.

Strategic Reserve Limitations: While the US maintains emergency oil reserves, these are limited and primarily intended for short-term crises, not long-term price stability. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve can provide temporary relief but cannot control sustained price pressures.

Toronto's ULO Rate Model: Revolutionary EV Economics

Here in Toronto, we've implemented Ultra-Low Overnight (ULO) electricity rates that demonstrate how smart rate structures make EVs incredibly economical and should serve as a model for American utilities:

The EV Economics Revolution: For a Tesla Model 3 requiring 33 kWh per 100 miles:

  • Overnight charging cost: 33 kWh × $0.028 = $0.92 per 100 miles

  • Gasoline equivalent (30 mpg at $3.84/gallon): $12.80 per 100 miles

  • Savings: 93% lower fuel costs

Annual Impact for Toronto EV Owners:

  • Average annual mileage: 12,000 miles

  • Overnight charging cost: $111 annually

  • Gasoline equivalent cost: $1,536 annually

  • Annual savings: $1,425

Why Americans Should Demand Similar Programs: Anyone with an EV would be foolish not to optimize their rate plan. This model should be replicated across America to accelerate EV adoption. The 8-hour overnight window (11 p.m. to 7 a.m.) perfectly matches when most EVs charge at home, and the dramatic cost difference makes the economic case undeniable.

Implementation Strategy for US Utilities:

  • Grid Benefits: Overnight EV charging uses excess renewable energy

  • Infrastructure Efficiency: Better utilization of existing electrical capacity

  • Consumer Choice: Optional programs that benefit EV owners without penalizing others

  • Political Appeal: Market-based solution requiring no government mandates

Climate and Environmental Cost Reality

Beyond economic considerations, continued reliance on oil production faces mounting environmental and health challenges that create hidden costs for American families:

Air Quality Health Impacts: The American Lung Association estimates that fossil fuel air pollution causes 50,000 premature deaths annually in the US, with associated healthcare costs exceeding $600 billion. These health impacts disproportionately affect children, elderly Americans, and communities near refineries and high-traffic areas.

Climate-Related Economic Costs: Extreme weather events linked to climate change cost the US $150 billion annually, according to NOAA. Republican-heavy states like Texas, Florida, and Louisiana bear significant costs from hurricanes, while Midwest farming communities face increasing drought and flooding expenses.

Infrastructure Maintenance Burden: Oil and gas infrastructure requires constant maintenance and eventual replacement. The American Society of Civil Engineers estimates that upgrading petroleum infrastructure to modern standards would cost $78 billion over the next decade—costs ultimately passed to consumers.

The Innovation Investment Gap

While America leads in oil extraction technology, continued focus on fossil fuels risks falling behind in the technologies that will dominate the next economic era:

Patent Analysis Showing Decline: China filed 2.4 times more battery technology patents than the US in 2023, despite America's traditional innovation leadership. This patent gap translates to future manufacturing advantages and technology export opportunities that could employ millions of Americans.

Venture Capital Flow Analysis: Global investment in EV and battery technology reached $31 billion in 2024, with 60% flowing to Chinese companies and only 18% to American firms. This investment gap predicts future competitive disadvantages and job creation outside America.

Talent Migration Trends: Engineering graduates increasingly choose careers in renewable energy and EV technology over traditional oil and gas roles. The average starting salary for battery engineers ($95,000) now exceeds that of petroleum engineers ($87,000), reflecting market demand shifts that suggest where future opportunities lie.

Path Forward Recognition: Both/And Rather Than Either/Or

Acknowledging these limitations doesn't mean abandoning oil production or the workers who depend on it. Instead, it means recognizing that:

  1. Oil production alone cannot deliver $1.98 gas due to global market realities beyond any president's control

  2. American families need relief from transportation costs that consume increasing portions of their income

  3. Oil workers deserve security during any energy transition, with pathways to equally good jobs

  4. American manufacturers need new opportunities to compete globally and create the jobs Trump's base demands

  5. National security requires reducing dependence on volatile global energy markets

The solution isn't choosing between oil and alternatives—it's building a diversified energy economy that leverages America's strengths in both traditional energy and emerging technologies while creating the high-paying manufacturing jobs that align with Republican values of industrial strength and worker prosperity.

This recognition sets the stage for understanding why the convergence of electric vehicles, artificial intelligence, and robotics represents not just an environmental imperative, but an economic and strategic opportunity that aligns with core Republican values of job creation, manufacturing leadership, and national strength.

The economic case is clear, but skeptics ask: "What about the practical challenges?"
