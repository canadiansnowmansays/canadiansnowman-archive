The Iran Crisis

“No Victory”

Part XLVI: Days Fifteen and Sixteen: Isfahan Burns, the Interceptors Are Running Low, and Iran’s Foreign Minister Names the Only Off-Ramp

March 15, 2026

A satellite image shows a closer view of the destroyed tunnel entrances at Isfahan missile complex after reported airstrikes, amid the U.S.-Israeli conflict with Iran, in Isfahan, Isfahan Province, Iran, March 8, 2026. Vantor/Handout via REUTERS

I. Isfahan

In the early hours of Sunday morning, US and Israeli forces struck the Isfahan province for the second time in this war. At least 15 people were killed. Dozens more were killed in strikes across Tehran, Shiraz, Hamedan, Dezful Air Base, Khomein, and Zanjan. Video showed extensive damage at the Jask port in Hormozgan Province. Twenty explosions were heard near Shiraz alone. Heavy explosions rolled across southern Tehran before dawn.

Isfahan is not a random target. It is the hub of Iran’s ballistic missile programme — production plants, research facilities, and the underground tunnel complex where, the IAEA has confirmed, Iran stored a portion of its highly enriched uranium. It was struck during the June 2025 twelve-day war. It was struck again this week. The 441 kilograms of near-weapons-grade uranium that survived the first war is still there, still unmonitored, still in canisters the administration has described as “easily transportable.” Rubio has said “people are going to have to go and get it.” No one has said who, or when, or how.

Iranian deputy foreign minister Kazem Gharibabadi condemned the targeting of historical and cultural sites, calling it “an assault on part of Iran’s civilizational identity.” The count is now at least 56 significant sites seriously damaged or destroyed, including UNESCO World Heritage Sites — Golestan Palace, Naqsh-e Jahan Square, Chehel Sotoun, the Shah Mosque, the Jameh Mosque. Blue Shield International has called it a war crime. Tehran’s governor reported that at least 10,000 residential homes in the capital have been “damaged or completely destroyed” since February 28.

Debris litters the UNESCO-listed Chehel Sotoon Palace in Isfahan after nearby US-Israeli strikes [File: Social Media/Reuters]

Pete Hegseth, meanwhile, told reporters that US forces have now struck more than 15,000 enemy targets — more than 1,000 per day since the war began. IDF spokesman Brig. Gen. Effie Defrin told CNN on Sunday that the Israeli military has plans for at least three more weeks of operations in Iran, with thousands of targets remaining. “We have thousands of targets ahead,” he said. “We are ready, in coordination with our US allies, with plans through at least the Jewish holiday of Passover, about three weeks from now. And we have deeper plans for even three weeks beyond that.” The IDF, Defrin added, is “not working according to a stopwatch, or a timetable, but rather to achieve our goals.”

Phase two of four. The longest phase.

II. The Interceptor Question

On Saturday, the US news site Semafor reported, citing US officials familiar with the matter, that Israel had informed the United States this week that it was running “critically low” on ballistic missile interceptors.

By Sunday morning, the IDF had denied it. “There is currently no problem with interceptors,” military sources said. “We prepared for a lengthy war. We are monitoring the situation constantly.”

Both things may be simultaneously true. The IDF was designed to fight a war of this duration and has prepared accordingly. It is also burning through interceptors at rates that were not anticipated when the stockpiles were assembled. Iran has fired approximately 300 ballistic missiles at Israel, roughly half carrying cluster munitions — warheads that burst at altitude and scatter 20 to 80 bomblets across a radius of up to ten kilometres. Each bomblet is a separate threat. Each threat requires a response. The math is not static.

Aerial view of cluster munition remnants and unexploded submunitions scattered across terrain, illustrating the persistent threat from Iran’s cluster warhead barrages used to overwhelm Israeli defenses. Source: Human Rights Watch

The cluster munitions dimension of this war has received less coverage than it deserves. The IDF confirmed on Day 10 that Iran has been firing them “on a nearly daily basis” since February 28 — a dramatic escalation from the June 2025 war, when cluster munitions appeared only occasionally. The warheads are designed specifically to defeat the interception architecture: Iran fires a missile, Israel’s Arrow system may intercept the missile, but if the warheads release before interception the bomblets disperse and there is effectively nothing that can stop them. Iron Dome is designed for short-range rockets, not high-altitude submunitions. The design of Iran’s cluster warheads — built to scatter bomblets so widely across civilian areas — led N.R. Jenzen-Jones of Armament Research Services to tell CNN it appeared “designed purely as a weapon of terror.”

At least three people have been killed directly by Iranian cluster munitions. Two construction workers died in Yehud on March 10 when sirens failed to give adequate warning. A children’s playground in Rishon LeZion was hit on March 9. The IDF Home Front Command has distributed flyers warning Israelis not to approach unexploded submunitions — bomblets fail to detonate on impact more frequently than conventional warheads, meaning the ground is littered with delayed death after every cluster strike. The unexploded ordnance problem will persist for years after the shooting stops, as it has in every country where cluster munitions have been used.

Iran, Israel, and the United States are all non-signatories to the 2008 Convention on Cluster Munitions. Amnesty International condemned Iran’s cluster munition use during the June 2025 war as a “flagrant violation of international humanitarian law.” The same condemnation applies here, multiplied by a factor of thirty. There is no enforcement mechanism.

The interceptor question and the cluster munition question are connected. Iran’s strategy, as multiple analysts have now confirmed, is deliberate attrition — using cheap cluster munitions to force expensive interceptor expenditure, while simultaneously depleting the interceptor stocks of Israel, Qatar, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE across the entire Gulf theatre. Every $3 million interceptor fired at a $200,000 missile is a favourable exchange for Iran. Multiply it by thousands.

III. No Ceasefire, No Negotiation

Abbas Araghchi, Iran’s foreign minister, appeared on CBS News’ Face the Nation on Sunday and said something that should be read carefully.

“No, we never asked for a ceasefire, and we have never asked even for negotiation,” he said. “We are ready to defend ourselves as long as it takes. And this is what we have done so far, and we continue to do that.”

He defended Iran’s strikes on Gulf states: “They have given their soil to American forces to attack us. What can we do? We just sit and watch as American forces are attacking us from their soil? Everything belongs to the Americans.”

On when the war might end, Araghchi named a single condition. The war ends, he said, when Trump “comes to the point that this is an illegal war with no victory.”

No victory. Not unconditional surrender — the formulation the White House has used since Day One. Not the destruction of Iran’s nuclear program — which Hegseth says remains a war objective even as CENTCOM’s target list cites no new nuclear damage. Not regime change — which has slipped from the administration’s public statements even as Netanyahu continues to advocate for it privately. Iran’s foreign minister has named the only off-ramp that Iran will accept: the acknowledgment, by Trump, that the war cannot be won.

Trump, the same Sunday, told NBC News that Iran “wants to make a deal” but that he is not ready for one because “the terms aren’t good enough yet.” He has not specified what terms would be good enough. His Energy Secretary, Chris Wright, gave two Sunday morning television interviews that are worth comparing. On ABC he said the war would “certainly come to an end in the next few weeks.” On NBC’s Meet the Press, asked whether the Strait was safe for tanker passage, Wright said simply: “No, it is not.” When pressed on oil prices, he acknowledged there were “no guarantees” they would fall. Wright is the same official who told CNBC on Thursday that the US was “simply not ready” to begin tanker escorts. The distance between his optimism and his candour is the distance between the war the administration planned and the war it is fighting.

Araghchi offered a separate statement Sunday to Arabic-language outlet Al-Araby Al-Jadeed that contained a small but notable signal: countries in the region were putting forward ideas to end the war as part of mediation efforts, and Iran was “ready to study any proposals.” This is not a ceasefire offer. It is a crack in the otherwise total defiance of his CBS appearance. The EU, watching the Strait of Hormuz chokehold tighten, is now discussing expanding its Aspides naval mission to the Strait. Egypt’s foreign minister and Qatar’s emir met Sunday to discuss regional de-escalation. The diplomatic perimeter is being built. The question is whether there is anyone on the American side authorised to stand inside it.

IV. Mojtaba

US intelligence shared with Trump showed that Ali Khamenei was wary about his own son replacing him — sources described the late supreme leader’s assessment as believing Mojtaba was “not very bright, and was viewed as unqualified to be leader,” and that he had “issues in his personal life.” Trump told Fox News in a Friday interview that Mojtaba “is not somebody that the father even wanted.”

Iran’s foreign minister on Sunday said there is “no problem” with the new supreme leader. “He is performing his duties according to the constitution and he will continue to do that.”

The $10 million question is now also literal. The US State Department has offered a $10 million reward for information about Mojtaba Khamenei and other top Iranian officials. Netanyahu told his Thursday press conference he would not “sign off on life insurance” for Mojtaba. The man issuing Iran’s military orders from an undisclosed location — injured, hidden, not seen on video since before the war — has become the object of both a bounty and a denial, simultaneously.

The IRGC, for its part, announced on Sunday it had launched at least 10 hypersonic missiles at Israel, along with an unspecified number of drones, targeting US military installations. Drone and missile strikes continued across the Gulf — Kuwait’s Ahmed Al-Jaber Air Base struck, with three Kuwaiti soldiers lightly wounded. Iran’s Revolutionary Guards separately vowed to “pursue and kill” Netanyahu.

The war is now in its third week. The supreme leader who did not want his son in power is dead. The son is issuing orders. The orders are being followed.

V. What Baghdad Means

This video grab taken from UGC images posted on social media shows smoke billowing from the US embassy in Baghdad's fortified "Green Zone" following a reported drone strike on March 14, 2026. - / AFP

The US Embassy in Baghdad is under evacuation. All American citizens in Iraq have been urged to leave immediately by overland routes — commercial flights are not operating. The warning marks a significant escalation from the “keep a low profile” guidance issued just 24 hours earlier. The embassy compound itself has been attacked twice. Kataib Hezbollah struck the roof on Friday night. A US counterstrike hit their Baghdad headquarters, killing three including a commander known as Abu Ali al-Amiri.

The domestic dimension of the war deepened on Sunday with new detail about the West Bloomfield Township synagogue attack. The Israeli military confirmed that Ayman Ghazali’s brother Ibrahim was a Hezbollah commander killed in an Israeli strike in Mashgharah, Lebanon on March 5. Also killed in that strike: Ibrahim’s two children and another brother, Qassem. Ayman attended a memorial for his slain family at a mosque in Dearborn Heights on March 8. Four days later he drove into Temple Israel. His vehicle was loaded with fireworks. One guard was injured. Roughly 140 children and preschool staff were safely evacuated. Hezbollah denied that Ibrahim or his family were affiliated with the group and said the attack was motivated by personal revenge. The Israeli military did not link Ayman to Hezbollah. The chain of causation — an Israeli airstrike in Lebanon, a memorial in Michigan, an attack on a synagogue — does not require ideological alignment to be real.

The geographic footprint of this war has now expanded to include: Iran, Lebanon, Israel, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, the UAE (Dubai airport and hotels struck), Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman, Turkey’s airspace (three Iranian missiles intercepted), Britain’s Akrotiri base on Cyprus, Italian and French military installations in Iraq. The USS Gerald R. Ford and its carrier group. Six KC-135 crew members in the western Iraqi desert.

This is what phase two of four looks like from the outside. From the inside, according to a poet in Tehran who spoke anonymously to the New York Times on Saturday, it sounds like explosions at 5 in the morning. The streets are empty. The security forces own the night. Some people cling to hope that the government will fall. Others are simply on “the edge between depression and hope,” he said. “No one knows what will happen.”

VI. The Boom That Wasn’t

Real-world photo of a US gas pump showing prices in the $3.30–$3.70 range in March 2026, symbolizing the 26% national increase that’s eroded consumer spending and fiscal tailwinds like tax refunds. Source: Reuters

Before the war, Trump’s economic team was projecting 4 percent growth for 2026. The stock market was buoyant. Inflation had levelled. White House officials saw ample evidence for a robust year. Joseph Lavorgna, the chief economist at SMBC Nikko Securities America, who until recently served as a top Treasury adviser, said the case was genuine: “Prior to the initiation of the conflict, a strong case could be made that 2026 was going to be a strong year for growth.”

That was before February 28.

One day after Trump proclaimed at a Kentucky rally that “inflation is plummeting, incomes are rising, the economy is roaring back and America is respected again,” the government released a set of economic indicators that told a different story. Inflation ticked up to 2.8 percent in January — before the war. The economy grew more slowly in the final quarter of 2025 than initially estimated. Employers shed 92,000 jobs in February. Futures markets, which priced in two Federal Reserve rate cuts at the start of the year, now price in zero cuts for all of 2026. The 10-year Treasury yield rose above 4.25 percent, pushing mortgage rates back above 6 percent for homebuyers who had just seen them briefly fall below for the first time in years.

Goldman Sachs, in a forecast released Wednesday, projected Brent crude averaging around $98 per barrel through March and April, returning toward pre-war levels only by the fourth quarter — if the war ends. It estimated that inflation would reach 2.9 percent by year end and could jump to 3.3 percent if oil stays well above $100. It projected a hit to GDP and an unemployment rate peaking at 4.6 percent by the third quarter.

Tom Porcelli, chief economist at Wells Fargo, put it plainly: “We are treading water at best.” He noted that US hiring was “not in good shape” even before the war began, and that the oil price spike “comes at an inopportune time.” The fiscal tailwind that was supposed to drive consumer spending — the tax refunds, the deregulation dividend — has been eaten by gas prices. Gas hit $3.70 per gallon nationally on Sunday, up from $2.93 a month ago, a 26 percent increase.

On CBS Sunday, Kevin Hassett, director of the White House National Economic Council, predicted a “big positive shock” for the global economy shortly after the war ended. Trump on social media has been demanding that Fed chair Jerome Powell cut rates “IMMEDIATELY.” Powell has not responded.

Steve Witkoff, the president’s special envoy, reportedly told associates before talks with Iran’s foreign minister that he was “just really surprised the Iranians won’t surrender.” That comment, reported by Politico’s Felicia Schwartz on Washington Week, illuminates the economic miscalculation as much as the military one. The surprise at Iranian resilience is the same surprise now playing out in the bond market, at the gas pump, and in the Goldman Sachs forecast for unemployment in Q3.

VII. The Alliance and the Press

The war is testing two things simultaneously that rarely get tested at the same time: the durability of European military capacity, and the durability of press freedom during a war the administration cannot justify to the public.

On the first: France has deployed approximately a dozen vessels including its sole nuclear-powered aircraft carrier to the Mediterranean and Persian Gulf — roughly 60 percent of its entire combat fleet, the largest European naval deployment since the Gulf War. Britain sent a destroyer to Cyprus, though it arrived more than a week after an Iranian drone hit the Akrotiri base, prompting analysts at the Royal United Services Institute to describe Britain’s contribution as “pinpricks.” Italy transferred one of its three operational SAMP/T air defense systems from the Baltic region to the Gulf, leaving it with one battery at home producing approximately 200 interceptor missiles per year. “Our capabilities are very, very low right now,” Senator Carlo Calenda told the New York Times. Ed Arnold of the Royal United Services Institute identified the deeper strategic cost: “If the U.S. is firing off so much ordnance against Iran, then they can’t use it against the Chinese in, say, two years, and it’s not going to be available for the Europeans against Russia.”

The figure that clarifies the scale: in the first week of the Iran war, US allies burned through approximately 800 Patriot missile interceptors — more than Ukraine has received in over four years of war against Russia. Ukraine, for its part, has deployed drone experts to the Gulf states to advise on countering Iranian drones, using expertise developed under Russian bombardment. The administration that spent the past year scaling back support for Ukraine now requires Ukrainian military knowledge to fight Iran.

Poland declined to contribute. “We currently have a war on our borders,” the foreign minister said.

On the second: FCC Chairman Brendan Carr threatened on Saturday to revoke broadcast licenses over coverage of the war with Iran. He accused broadcasters of “running hoaxes and news distortions” and warned them to “correct course before their license renewals come up.” The threat followed a Truth Social post by Trump attacking the Wall Street Journal’s reporting on refueling planes struck in Saudi Arabia. Defense Secretary Hegseth, at a Friday press conference, delivered a lengthy complaint about CNN’s war coverage and said he looked forward to the network being controlled by billionaire David Ellison — whose acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery, if completed, would bring CNN under his purview. Ellison has already installed more conservative leadership at CBS News.

Senator Elizabeth Warren called the FCC threat “straight out of the authoritarian playbook.” Senator Mark Kelly said that “when our nation is at war, it is critical that the press is free to report without government interference.” The Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression called Carr’s post “shocking — and dangerous.” Legal experts noted that the process for revoking broadcast licenses is deliberately onerous and that communications law prohibits the government from using regulations to censor.

The administration cannot sell the war to the public through normal persuasion. It launched the war with a midnight Truth Social video in a baseball cap. It has not addressed the nation in a formal speech. It has shifted rationale ten times. It is now threatening the institutions responsible for covering what it cannot explain.
