The Iran Crisis

The Flying Ace Goes to Iran

When the Pentagon runs out of options, it runs out of options in style

March 22, 2026

With a 48-hour Trump ultimatum threatening to obliterate Iranian power plants — a deadline expiring Monday evening — and the Strait of Hormuz closed to all but Tehran’s friends, the world needs a pilot it can trust. Fortunately, one has volunteered. He sleeps on his roof. He has history with the Red Baron. He has never once been deterred by a strongly worded Truth Social post.

The Flying Ace is wheels up.

Consider this your second chill pill of the day.

An AI-generated advertisement — fake product, fake website, real audacity — circulated on Facebook in February promoting a Snoopy windmill that no one would ever receive. The image, however, was so charming and the metaphor so precise that it demanded a second life. Consider this its honest one.

There is a photograph circulating in certain classified channels — or possibly in someone’s garden in Kent, the sourcing is disputed — that captures the precise moment American foreign policy achieved its logical terminus.

A beagle. A vintage biplane. RAF roundels. A red scarf streaming in the wind above the Strait of Hormuz.

The Flying Ace has answered the call.

Pentagon officials, speaking on background because they retain at least vestigial shame, confirmed that the legendary aviator — registration B7270, last active deployment somewhere over the Somme, circa 1917 — had been integrated into Carrier Air Wing Eight following what one senior officer described as “a reassessment of available assets.” A second officer, who asked not to be named, described it more accurately as “a Tuesday.”

The F-35 pilots assigned to fly formation with the Ace have filed a joint memo requesting clarification on several points of protocol: specifically, whether the red scarf constitutes a flight safety hazard, whether “Curse you, Red Baron!” qualifies as authorised radio communication, and whether suppertime — referenced repeatedly in the Ace’s personal log — is a recognised operational concept under current Rules of Engagement.

The memo has not been answered. The Pentagon is busy.

How We Got Here

It is worth pausing, as good satire requires, to consider the straight line between cause and effect.

The United States and Israel conducted joint strikes on February 28th that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, an event so consequential and so poorly theorised in advance that the administration spent the following three weeks doing what it does best: announcing victory, disputing the announcement, and scheduling the announcement of a subsequent victory to be named later.

Iran, meanwhile, entered a succession crisis of the kind that normally takes decades to resolve and was given approximately eleven days before Washington began demanding to know who was in charge and whether they were sufficiently grateful.

The answer, to both questions, was unclear.

Into this vacuum — political, strategic, moral — the United States deployed additional carrier groups, additional F-35 squadrons, additional rhetoric, and, according to sources who cannot be entirely discounted, the Flying Ace.

To be fair to the Ace, he did not ask for this. He was minding his own business, sitting atop his Sopwith Camel in a garden in the English countryside, dreaming of suppertime, when the orders came through. He is a professional. He does not complain. He simply adjusts his goggles, tightens his scarf, and banks into the wind.

These are qualities the current situation desperately needs and almost entirely lacks.

The Strategic Contribution

Military analysts — the ones who appear on cable television at two in the morning when the regular analysts have gone to bed — have begun to assess the Ace’s contribution to regional deterrence.

Their conclusions are mixed, though trending positive.

On the negative side: the Sopwith Camel has a top speed of approximately 185 kilometres per hour, which is substantially below the threat envelope of Iranian surface-to-air missile batteries. The aircraft is also constructed of wood and canvas, which degrades somewhat under radar-guided fire. And the Ace’s navigational system, which appears to rely on instinct, optimism, and an extremely good nose, has not been certified under current FAA or DoD standards.

On the positive side: he has never once been shot down. Not by the Red Baron. Not by von Richthofen’s entire squadron. Not by anybody. There is a case to be made that the Flying Ace represents the single most resilient airframe in the history of powered flight, and that the Pentagon, which has spent four hundred billion dollars on the F-35 program, might usefully study his maintenance record.

There is also the matter of morale. Three Iranian radar operators, working the night shift at a facility outside Bandar Abbas, filed incident reports last week describing an unidentified slow-moving contact that their systems could neither classify nor engage. Two of the operators noted that the contact appeared to be smiling. The third requested a mental health day.

This is, defence analysts note, a form of deterrence.

The TACO Protocol

In keeping with established patterns, the administration has now moved through the standard response cycle.

It Threatened Iran with consequences too severe to specify.

It Accommodated Iranian requests for clarification by clarifying nothing.

It Capitulated to domestic pressure to appear decisive by appearing decisive in ways that committed to nothing.

It is currently in the Obfuscation phase, which is the longest phase, and from which no administration has yet successfully emerged.

The Flying Ace, by contrast, operates on a simpler protocol. He sees the enemy. He engages. He returns for suppertime. The strategic objective is clear. The metrics are uncomplicated. The scarf is red.

Washington has seventeen inter-agency working groups. The Ace has a dog bowl and a purpose.

History, one suspects, will note the contrast.

Epilogue: Over the Strait

The last confirmed sighting of the Flying Ace placed him banking northeast over the Strait of Hormuz at approximately four hundred feet, which is either a combat altitude or a very low garden ornament, depending on your perspective.

His yellow associate — small, spherical, of indeterminate species — was not present. Some operations, it appears, a beagle must undertake alone.

The F-35s on his wing held formation. The roundels caught the morning sun. The red scarf streamed behind him into a sky the colour of something about to happen.

It was a dark and stormy night.

It was, in other words, perfect flying weather.

The Flying Ace was not afraid.

Editorial Disclosure: No beagles were harmed in the production of this essay. One small yellow bird of indeterminate species filed a workplace grievance citing "insufficient consultation prior to deployment" and "unreasonable expectations regarding suppertime logistics." The matter has been referred to inter-agency working group seventeen, where it joins forty-three other unresolved items dating to 2003.
