‘Abolish ICE’ Returns—And The Numbers Have Shifted

Support for dismantling ICE surged from 20% to 46% in 18 months. Even Joe Rogan is asking “Are we gonna be the Gestapo?” But Democrats are paralyzed between moral outrage and electoral fear.

January 26, 2026

On January 7, 2026, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot and killed Renee Nicole Good in Minneapolis. On January 24—seventeen days later—Border Patrol agents shot and killed Alex Jeffrey Pretti. Both were 37 years old. Both were American citizens. Both were Minneapolis residents. Both were killed by federal agents who immediately lied about what happened.

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem called Renee Good a “domestic terrorist.” DHS claimed Alex Pretti “wanted to massacre law enforcement.” Video evidence proved both claims were lies.

Within days, the calls began:

Graham Platner, U.S. Senate candidate in Maine: “Dismantling ICE is the moderate position.”

Jack Schlossberg, running for Congress in New York: “If Trump’s ICE is shooting and kidnapping people, then abolish it.”

Even Bill Kristol—the neoconservative pundit, Iraq War supporter, and former Republican strategist who spent decades defending American security agencies—logged onto X: “Abolish ICE.”

The slogan is back. Eight years after “Abolish ICE” became a progressive litmus test during Trump’s family separation crisis, it’s resurging with new force—and making Democratic strategists break out in cold sweats.

“Oh my God. Déjà vu all over again,” Lis Smith, national party strategist, told The Atlantic when asked about the slogan.

“Sigh,” texted Lanae Erickson, senior vice president at the center-left think tank Third Way. “Can that be my on the record quote?”

The question haunting Democrats: Is this 2018 redux—a politically toxic slogan that will hand Republicans easy attack ads and cost them winnable races? Or is this time different, a genuine response to state violence that Americans are actually ready to support?

The answer matters enormously for 2026 and beyond. Because whether Democrats like it or not, ICE is killing American citizens on video, conducting paramilitary raids in Target stores, and threatening military occupation of states whose leaders criticize them. The demand to abolish ICE isn’t coming from the activist fringe anymore—it’s coming from Senate candidates, members of Congress, and yes, even Joe Rogan.

The Democrats’ dilemma: How do you respond to genuine outrages without handing Republicans the political weapon that could doom your electoral prospects?

The Numbers Are Shifting—But Is It Enough?

The public opinion landscape has changed dramatically since 2019, when “Abolish ICE” last dominated political discourse.

A January 2026 Economist/YouGov poll conducted shortly after Renee Good’s killing found 46% of Americans support abolishing ICE, while 43% oppose—a dramatic shift from August 2024 when support was just 20% with 66% opposed. That’s a 26-point swing in support for abolition in less than two years.

A separate poll by The 19th found that half of American women now support abolishing ICE.

Even more striking: Roughly 57% of Americans now disapprove of how ICE is handling immigration enforcement. About 52% believe ICE is making cities less safe—not more safe.

The shift crosses traditional partisan lines. Even cultural figures not typically associated with progressive immigration politics are expressing alarm. Joe Rogan, the massively popular podcast host who endorsed Trump in 2024, watched videos of ICE operations and asked his millions of listeners: “Are we really gonna be the Gestapo? ‘Where’s your papers?’ Is that what we’ve come to?”

The Gestapo comparison from Rogan—a Trump supporter—signals something has shifted in public consciousness. When ICE operations provoke “secret police” comparisons from non-progressives, the agency has a legitimacy crisis.

But here’s the political reality that terrifies moderate Democrats: Public disapproval of ICE’s tactics doesn’t automatically translate into support for eliminating the agency. Americans can simultaneously think ICE is operating badly while still believing immigration laws should be enforced.

And more importantly: The same Americans who disapprove of ICE in polls might still vote against Democrats who campaign on abolishing it, because Republicans will spend hundreds of millions of dollars convincing them that “Abolish ICE” means “open borders.”

The 2018 Trauma Still Haunts

Democratic strategists remember 2018 vividly. “Abolish ICE” emerged during public outrage over Trump’s family separation policy. Progressive candidates embraced it as a litmus test. The movement went mainstream.

It was also deeply unclear. Did “abolish” mean eliminate ICE entirely? Replace it with something else? Reform it dramatically? Advocates couldn’t agree, and that ambiguity became a weapon Republicans used ruthlessly.

The slogan was never popular with broader electorates. It energized progressive bases but alienated moderate voters. And by 2020, even many activists had quietly abandoned it, recognizing it had become political poison.

Now Democrats are experiencing PTSD watching the cycle repeat. They see the viral videos of ICE shooting people. They understand the public outrage is genuine. But they also remember how “Defund the Police” in 2020 and “Abolish ICE” in 2018 became Republican attack lines that damaged Democrats for years afterward.

The fear is visceral and specific: In 2022, former Wisconsin Lieutenant Governor Mandela Barnes lost his Senate challenge to Ron Johnson by barely one percentage point. Republicans successfully weaponized Barnes’s previous support for abolishing ICE and defunding police. Barnes, now running for governor, has carefully avoided the issue this time.

Vice President Kamala Harris’s mixed record on immigration became fodder for relentless Republican attacks during the 2024 campaign. Republicans spent $741 million on immigration-related ads, according to America Forward. They will spend more in 2026 if Democrats give them ammunition.

“Unless you truly believe that the United States should not have an agency that enforces immigration and customs laws,” warns a memo from the Searchlight Institute, “you should not say you want to abolish ICE.”

The message to Democrats: Think very carefully before embracing this slogan, because Republicans will use your own words to destroy you.

The Current Adopters: Who’s Saying It Now

Despite the warnings, a growing number of Democrats—including some who previously opposed abolishing ICE—are now embracing the call.

Representative Adriano Espaillat (New York), chair of the 43-member Congressional Hispanic Caucus: “I was of the belief that perhaps we could reform ICE. Now I am of the belief that it has to be dismantled as an entity.”

Espaillat went further after Alex Pretti’s killing, characterizing Immigration and Customs Enforcement as “an agency that has become a paramilitary force terrorizing American communities.”

In a recent interview, Espaillat described ICE as “a runaway train that has become an aggressive and violent weapon. In fact, a deadly weapon.” He cited specific data: “The level of arrests of immigrants has gone up 2,450% and 92% of those in ICE custody today are folks that have no criminal record.”

Espaillat has personally visited detention centers and spoken with people held in custody. “The president lied to us,” he said. “He said he was going to arrest violent criminals. And in fact, what he’s doing is he’s stopping and arresting people that have no criminal conviction. In fact, within those folks, over a hundred people that have been stopped, questioned, detained are US citizens.”

The Congressional Hispanic Caucus, which includes four senators, has developed a multi-front strategy: challenging ICE budgetarily and legislatively, filing court cases across the country, and shaping public sentiment. Senator Alex Padilla was allegedly assaulted by ICE agents in California early in the operations—”thrown up against the wall, thrown slammed on the floor with the entire country saw what happened.”

The caucus is working on court cases to gain access to detention centers, prevent the IRS from sharing confidential tax information with ICE, and hold airlines accountable for selling passenger data to immigration enforcement. They’re also preparing comprehensive ICE legislation to be introduced “in the next couple of weeks that will be a road map for the rest of the democratic conference to follow.”

When asked about dismantling ICE, Espaillat was direct: “Anything that’s not working should be taken apart and should be made better. Right now, the violent behavior and culture that is gripping ICE is unacceptable.”

He raised fundamental questions about ICE operations: “Should officers be masked? Should they be anonymous without a first name and a last name, no shield number? Will you open your door to someone that’s masked and you don’t know whether they are in fact an ICE agent or somebody that may want to harm you? Where are they recruiting these officers? Are they being trained properly? Do they have the mental fitness to do the job? Why do they have to carry high caliber weapons? Immigration is a civil law issue. It’s not a criminal law matter.”

Congresswoman Delia Ramirez (IL-3) addressed a large crowd yesterday in Chicago

Representative Delia Ramirez (Illinois), a member of the Homeland Security Committee, has become one of the loudest voices calling for abolition. “Fine! Abolish ICE and DHS,” Ramirez wrote on X on January 13 in response to Third Way’s warning memo. She described ICE agents as “criminals in masks.”

Illinois statewide officials have joined the chorus. Lieutenant Governor Juliana Stratton, a Democrat running for U.S. Senate, declared ICE is “out of control and beyond reform.” “A new leadership or a reduced budget cannot change the fact that ICE exists to terrorize communities and execute the authoritarian agenda of Trump,” Stratton said.

Senate candidate Raja Krishnamoorthi and multiple Congressional candidates including Daniel Biss, Kat Abughazaleh, Laura Fine, and Patty Garcia have all endorsed abolishing ICE.

State Rep. James Talarico; Rep. Jasmine Crockett.Getty Images

Texas Senate candidates Jasmine Crockett and James Talarico both called for overhauling or dismantling ICE during their January 24 debate—hours after Alex Pretti’s killing.

“We absolutely have to clean house, whatever that looks like,” Crockett, who supports impeaching Noem, said when pressed on whether she would support defunding or abolishing ICE.

Talarico went further: “ICE shot a mother in the face. ICE kidnapped a 5-year-old boy. ICE executed a man in broad daylight on our streets just this morning. It’s time to tear down this secret police force and replace it with an agency that actually is going to focus on public safety.”

Rep Shri Thanedar has called for ICE to be defunded “ US Senate must stop all funding to ICE, even if that means shutting down this oppressive government”

Representative Shri Thanedar (Michigan): In June 2025, Thanedar was one of 75 Democrats who signed legislation expressing “gratitude” for ICE. By January 2026, he introduced the Abolish ICE Act.

That’s a remarkable evolution: From expressing gratitude to calling for abolition in seven months. The shift suggests Thanedar—and potentially others—believe something fundamental has changed.

Representative Joaquin Castro (Texas) told The Bulwark: “ICE should be disbanded. It has become a completely rogue operation arresting U.S. citizens, engaged in racial profiling, and deporting people out of the country without telling their families where they’re sending them.”

Rep Ilhan Omar on hearing the news of the death of Alex Pretti “ “This appears to be an execution by immigration enforcement. I am absolutely heartbroken, horrified, and appalled that federal agents murdered another member of our community. It is beyond shameful these federal agents are targeting our residents instead of protecting them.”

Representative Ilhan Omar (Minnesota) led nearly 100 Congressional Progressive Caucus members in pledging to vote against all future funding for immigration enforcement. “We cannot and we should not continue to fund agencies that operate with impunity, that escalate violence and that undermine the very freedoms this country claims to uphold,” Omar told reporters. “ICE has no place in terrorizing Minneapolis or any American community.”

Progressive stalwarts including Representatives Rashida Tlaib and Ayanna Pressley have reiterated their long-standing demands to dismantle the agency. For them, this isn’t new—they’ve been consistent since 2018.

Senate candidates like Abdul El-Sayed in Michigan and Graham Platner in Maine are making abolition central to their campaigns. El-Sayed told The Atlantic: “I made this call back in 2018, and that’s because I saw where this agency was going. Trump is trying to create an independent, domestic source of state power answerable only to him.” ICE is “corrupted beyond repair.”

Usamah Andrabi, communications director at Justice Democrats (the group that works to unseat insufficiently progressive Democratic incumbents), frames this as moral imperative: “This is the baseline at a certain point. Democrats need to heed the calls” of constituents “demanding that we abolish ICE.”

Even some who aren’t explicitly calling for abolition are reframing the issue in ways that amount to the same thing. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, who ran for Congress in 2018 on a promise to abolish ICE, has pivoted to framing it as a funding question: “The cuts to your health care are what’s paying for this. You get screwed over to pay a bunch of thugs in the street that are shooting mothers in the face.”

That interview, according to Adam Jentleson of the Searchlight Institute, demonstrates “highly effective messaging on ICE WITHOUT saying ‘abolish ICE.’” (Ocasio-Cortez still supports abolishing the agency, but she’s not leading with that language.)

The pattern: Some Democrats who’ve long supported abolition are getting louder. Others who opposed it are reconsidering. A few are finding ways to capture the outrage without using the specific word “abolish.”

The Anxious Opposition: “Please Don’t Say It”

If progressive Democrats and activists see abolition as moral necessity, moderate Democrats and party strategists see it as electoral suicide.

Tré Easton, vice president for public policy at the Searchlight Institute, draws explicit parallels to “Defund the Police”: “When you say ‘Abolish ICE,’ that means you’re abolishing the agency that’s in charge of enforcing immigration law in this country. Especially in a time when Democrats don’t have a lot of credibility on the immigration issue, it’s really, really, a fraught path that they should not go down.”

The argument is strategic, not moral. No one is defending ICE’s current operations. The question is whether “Abolish ICE” as a slogan helps or hurts Democrats’ ability to actually reform or constrain the agency.

Lanae Erickson of Third Way makes the same argument: Both “Abolish ICE” and “Defund the Police” appeared in response to terrible abuses of power. Both were overcorrections. “The question is not whether ICE should exist; the question is whether they should follow the laws and be held accountable.”

The distinction matters politically: “Reform and accountability” polls much better than “abolish” even when describing identical policy outcomes. Americans support holding ICE accountable, firing bad agents, establishing oversight, ending abusive practices. But “abolish” sounds like eliminating immigration enforcement entirely—and most Americans, even those critical of ICE, don’t support that.

Senator Ruben Gallego of Arizona is held up as an example of effective messaging. ICE is “out of control,” Gallego said. “People want a slimmed-down ICE that is truly focused on security.” That’s criticism without the “abolish” framing that Republicans can weaponize.

The moderate Democratic position, essentially: Say everything progressives want to say about ICE’s abuses, support every reform progressives want, but don’t use the word “abolish” because Republicans will use it to destroy you.

It’s tactical advice rooted in hard experience. And it’s driving progressives crazy.

The Republican Playbook: Already In Motion

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt wasted no time. When asked about renewed “Abolish ICE” calls, she framed it exactly as Democratic strategists feared: Democrats “don’t want our border secured or criminal illegal aliens being removed” and want to defund “federal law-enforcement agencies who are protecting public safety.”

House Majority Leader Steve Scalise echoed: Democrats “have been for open borders” for years.

The framing is dishonest—opposing ICE’s current operations isn’t supporting “open borders”—but it’s politically effective. Republicans will run hundreds of millions of dollars in ads making exactly these claims. They’ll use Democrats’ own words: “Abolish ICE” becomes “Democrats want open borders and no immigration enforcement.”

Moderate Democrats look at this playbook and see political disaster. They remember how Republicans used “Defund the Police” to damage Democrats in 2020 and 2022. They see the same strategy about to repeat, and they’re begging their colleagues: Please don’t give Republicans this weapon.

But progressives look at the same situation and see moral cowardice. ICE is killing American citizens. ICE is conducting paramilitary raids in retail stores. ICE is operating a deportation machine that targets people with no criminal records. And moderate Democrats’ response is... worry about polling and messaging?

The gap between these perspectives feels unbridgeable.

The 2028 Contenders Stay Silent

Notably, none of the leading 2028 Democratic presidential contenders has demanded ICE be abolished. Governors, senators positioning for future runs—they’re all sticking with calls for “reform,” “investigation,” and “accountability.”

This silence is strategic. Anyone considering a national campaign knows “Abolish ICE” would become an albatross. Republicans would run the clip in every swing state. Moderate voters would flee. The primary base might love it, but general election voters would reject it.

Democratic Senate candidates (with exceptions like Platner and El-Sayed) have also mostly avoided the phrase. They’ll criticize ICE operations. They’ll demand investigations into Renee Good’s killing and Alex Pretti’s killing. They’ll support oversight and accountability. But “abolish”? Too risky.

This creates a dynamic where the Democratic Party’s most visible figures stay silent while activists, some members of Congress, and a few challengers embrace the call. The silence from leadership sends its own message: We agree ICE is out of control, but we don’t trust Americans to support abolishing it.

This Time Is Different—Or Is It?

Progressive advocates argue 2026 isn’t 2018. The conditions are fundamentally different:

Then: Family separation was the precipitating crisis. Horrible, but people weren’t dying.

Now: ICE is killing U.S. citizens on video. Renee Nicole Good, shot three times while trying to leave, called a “domestic terrorist” by DHS. Alex Jeffrey Pretti, shot ten times in five seconds, accused of wanting to “massacre law enforcement.” Seventeen days apart.

Then: ICE operations were mostly at borders or through cooperation with local jails.

Now: Paramilitary raids in Target stores while families shop. Home Depot parking lots. Car washes. Residential streets. 3,000 federal agents deployed to Minneapolis in what DHS calls “the largest immigration operation ever.”

Then: Trump threatened to deploy military to cities; he didn’t follow through.

Now: Insurrection Act invoked. National Guard activated to protect federal buildings from DHS. Government shutdown threatened because Democrats refuse to fund an agency that kills citizens.

Then: Critics faced rhetorical attacks.

Now: Governor Walz ended his reelection campaign, overwhelmed by federal occupation. Governor Hochul demands Noem resign. Senators refuse to fund DHS.

Progressives argue: This IS different. This is worse. This is the moment when “Abolish ICE” stops being political liability and becomes moral imperative that voters understand.

Abdul El-Sayed frames it starkly: Trump is creating “an independent, domestic source of state power answerable only to him.” By now, ICE is “corrupted beyond repair.”

That’s the authoritarian argument: When an agency becomes a tool of presidential authoritarianism, reform isn’t sufficient. You dismantle it.

But moderate Democrats look at the same facts and reach different conclusions: Yes, this is worse than 2018. Yes, reforms are necessary. But Americans mostly support immigration enforcement, and “Abolish ICE” still sounds like “eliminate immigration enforcement” regardless of how activists define it.

The gulf: Progressives believe conditions have changed enough that voters will now support what they rejected in 2018. Moderates believe voters’ fundamental positions on immigration enforcement haven’t shifted enough to make “Abolish ICE” politically viable.

Who’s right matters enormously for 2026.

The Primary Pressure Builds

As the 2026 midterm primaries intensify, progressive challengers will pressure Democratic incumbents on this issue. Justice Democrats—the organization that backed Ocasio-Cortez and other Squad members—is explicitly focused on unseating incumbents deemed insufficiently progressive.

“Democrats need to heed the calls” of constituents demanding abolition, Usamah Andrabi told The Atlantic.

This creates a bind for Democratic incumbents in blue districts: Face primary challenges from the left if you don’t support abolishing ICE, face Republican attacks in the general if you do.

The incentive structure pushes Democrats toward symbolic gestures: Support investigations. Demand accountability. Call for reforms. But avoid the word “abolish” to minimize Republican attack ad effectiveness.

Except that strategy only works if progressive activists accept it. And increasingly, they don’t. Not after watching ICE kill Renee Good and lie about it. Not after Alex Pretti. Not after Target raids. Not after military threats against Minnesota.

At some point, the pressure from the base overwhelms the electoral calculations. And then Democrats face the question they’ve been avoiding: Do you actually support abolishing ICE, or just reforming it?

Because “reform” assumes the institution is salvageable. “Abolish” means it’s not.

Conclusion: The Party Divided

The Democratic Party faces an impossible choice on “Abolish ICE”:

Embrace it: Energize the progressive base, take a clear moral stand against state violence, distinguish themselves from Republicans. Risk: Lose swing voters, hand Republicans devastating attack ads, potentially doom electoral prospects in purple states.

Reject it: Maintain viability in swing states, avoid handing Republicans weapons, focus on “reform and accountability” messaging that polls better. Risk: Demoralize progressive base, look cowardly in face of state violence, fail to distinguish from Republicans on fundamental question of ICE’s legitimacy.

Split the difference: Let progressives say “abolish” while leadership says “reform.” Acknowledge different constituencies have different needs. Risk: Look divided and weak, confuse voters about party position, satisfy no one.

There’s no good answer. Every option carries enormous risks.

The 2026 Democratic primaries will test whether “Abolish ICE” is political poison or moral imperative. If candidates who embrace abolition win primaries in swing districts and then lose general elections to Republicans, moderate Democrats will say: “We told you so.” If they win both primaries and generals, progressives will say: “See? Voters understand.”

The most likely outcome: Mixed results that both sides interpret as validating their position, leading to continued internal Democratic warfare over immigration messaging heading into 2028.

But there’s another possibility: Conditions deteriorate so dramatically between now and November 2026 that the political calculations change entirely. If ICE kills more U.S. citizens. If paramilitary raids become weekly occurrences—at what point does public opinion shift decisively against ICE’s existence, not just its tactics?

Democrats are gambling that point hasn’t arrived yet. Progressives increasingly believe it has.

And while Democrats debate slogans and polling and electoral calculations, ICE continues killing people, conducting raids, operating with impunity. The question isn’t whether “Abolish ICE” polls well. The question is whether an agency that kills American citizens and lies about it can be reformed—or whether it must be dismantled.

Democrats are terrified to answer that question honestly. Because either answer carries political risk they’re not sure they can survive.

Some Democrats, however, have stopped debating and started acting. They’re not arguing about slogans anymore. They’re using the actual power they have—and that’s where the real fight begins.
