The Mortality Test

What Three Adversaries Reveal About the Man Who Cannot Let Go

March 21, 2026

Photo by Archie Carpenter/UPI/Shutterstock.

There is a test of character so simple it requires no committee, no investigation, no special counsel. It is the mortality test: how does a person respond when a political adversary faces mortality?

Robert Mueller died yesterday. He was 81 years old. He was a decorated Vietnam War veteran, a Bronze Star recipient, an FBI director who served under four presidents, a man who drafted his resignation letter at 1:30 in the morning rather than permit an unconstitutional surveillance program to continue. He was, by every credible account, an exemplar of institutional duty in an age that has largely forgotten what that looks like.

Donald Trump’s response, posted to Truth Social: “Good, I’m glad he’s dead. He can no longer hurt innocent people.”

This was not a surprise. It was a data point in a pattern — and the pattern is worth examining carefully, because it illuminates something that formal accountability mechanisms never quite capture: the architecture of the man’s psychology.

Act One: John McCain, August 2018

August 18, 2018 Photograph by Ludovic / REA / Redux

When Senator John McCain died — a prisoner of war for five and a half years, a man who refused early release rather than dishonour his fellow prisoners — Trump issued a tweet of eleven words: “My deepest sympathies and respect go out to the family of Senator John McCain.”

Behind the scenes, his staff had drafted a proper presidential statement calling McCain a hero. Trump personally rejected it. He would not say the word. The White House flag was lowered to half-staff and then quietly raised again within 48 hours — a breach of protocol so obvious that veterans’ groups and even Trump’s own staff pushed back until he relented. He was the only living president not invited to, nor attending, McCain’s funeral at the National Cathedral.

Within seven months, Trump was attacking McCain at rallies again — complaining about his thumbs-down vote on Obamacare, questioning his military record, mocking his legacy while surrounded by cheering crowds. The minimum of decorum observed at the moment of death had evaporated entirely.

The McCain case established the template: performative minimum at the moment of death, followed by resumed contempt once the news cycle moved on.

Act Two: Joe Biden, May 2025

Former President Joe Biden's office said the treatment lasted several weeks.Alex Wroblewski / AFP via Getty Images file

Biden was not yet dead — he had been diagnosed with Stage 4 prostate cancer, aggressive and metastatic. The diagnosis carried the weight of probable mortality. The entire political world, across party lines, responded with genuine expressions of sympathy. Even Marjorie Taylor Greene wrote that cancer is “truly awful.”

Trump’s initial Truth Social post was textbook: “Melania and I are saddened to hear about Joe Biden’s recent medical diagnosis. We extend our warmest and best wishes to Jill and the family, and we wish Joe a fast and successful recovery.”

Within twenty-four hours, the mask slipped. Speaking to reporters, Trump repeatedly conflated Biden’s Gleason score with his cancer stage — announcing that “to get to Stage 9, that’s a long time” — and pivoted immediately to suggesting concealment, cover-up, and that Biden’s doctors had been lying to the public. His son Donald Jr. demanded criminal charges against Jill Biden. The sympathy had lasted exactly one news cycle before becoming a weapon.

The Biden case established a refinement of the template: even the performative minimum had shrunk. The period of feigned decency before the attack now measured in hours, not months.

Act Three: Robert Mueller, March 2026

Robert S. Mueller III in 2019, while serving as special counsel investigating Russia’s attack on the 2016 presidential election.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

No performative minimum. No tweet of condolence. No period of calculated decorum. Just: “Good, I’m glad he’s dead.”

The progression is not subtle. From McCain — where Trump at least understood that he needed to perform the ritual — to Biden’s cancer — where the performance lasted one day — to Mueller, where he dispensed with the performance entirely. The inhibitions that political calculation once imposed have been stripped away, one adversary at a time. Whatever the explanation, the trajectory is unmistakable.

What I observe here is not simply cruelty, though it is cruel. It is the logic of a man who has never processed defeat, never metabolized grievance, never found in himself the psychological architecture that allows most people to eventually let go. Trump said, when Mueller was first appointed, “This is the end of my presidency.” That statement was made in 2017. Mueller has been dead for less than a day and Trump is still relitigating it. Nearly a decade of legal survival, political triumph, and electoral vindication was not enough to close the wound.

Mueller, for his part, understood this perfectly. He drafted a resignation letter. He confronted a president over an unconstitutional surveillance program. He conducted an investigation of rigorous integrity and then — in what history may judge his most consequential failure of nerve — declined to subpoena the man he was investigating, allowed written answers to go largely unanswered, and trusted Bill Barr to faithfully represent his conclusions to the public. He was sorely disappointed. He never spoke publicly of any of it until Trump commuted Roger Stone’s sentence in 2020, at which point he wrote a measured op-ed in the Washington Post asserting, simply, that his team had acted with integrity.

A man of that restraint is greeted in death by “Good, I’m glad he’s dead.”

The Mirror

I cannot leave this unexamined: Trump does not appear to understand that he is writing his own epitaph.

The number of people who will one day read his own obituary with relief — with the specific sentiment he expressed about Mueller, whether or not they say it aloud — will be measured in the tens of millions, distributed across dozens of countries, encompassing people who have never once voted in an American election but whose lives were materially affected by his governance. The Ukrainians. The Afghans. The immigrant families separated at borders. The scientists whose work was defunded. The allies who watched the architecture of the postwar order dismantled in real time.

Mueller was known primarily to the politically engaged. Trump has made himself globally, indelibly, personally consequential — in a way that ensures the ledger of relief at his passing will dwarf anything he could have imagined inflicting on any adversary.

The mortality test has one final irony: the man who fails it most publicly is writing his own worst review.

Mueller kept his silence for years. He operated on the assumption that institutions would hold, that procedural restraint was its own form of integrity, that history would sort it out. Whether that was wisdom or capitulation, historians will argue for decades.

What is not arguable is this: Robert Mueller died yesterday, and the president of the United States responded by saying he was glad.

That sentence will be in the footnote. It may also be in the headline.
