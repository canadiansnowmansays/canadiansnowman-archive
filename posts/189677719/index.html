The Democratic Promise

The Fallen Visionary

Part 5: Elon Musk

April 6, 2026

Stephen Gordon - CanadianSnowmanSays March 2026

The Most Complicated Figure in This Series

A note on transparency before we begin. I own several hundred shares of Tesla. I have owned them through the extraordinary gains and the gut-wrenching declines. I have watched the stock price respond to Musk’s tweets, his political entanglements, and his increasingly erratic public behavior. I have a financial interest in the success of a company whose founder’s political activities I find deeply troubling.

I mention this because this essay attempts something difficult: an honest accounting of a man who has done more to advance the democratic promise of technology than almost anyone alive — and who has simultaneously done more to undermine the democratic institutions that the promise depends on. Both things are true. If this essay were easy to write, it would not be worth reading.

The Achievements Are Real

Tesla Gigafactory Texas, one of the massive facilities that scaled electric vehicle and battery production worldwide. Public aerial photography

Any honest assessment of Elon Musk must begin with what he has actually accomplished, because the scale of it is historically extraordinary.

Tesla did not merely build electric cars. It forced the entire global automotive industry to abandon a century of commitment to the internal combustion engine. Before Tesla, every major automaker treated electric vehicles as compliance projects — ugly, underpowered, short-range vehicles built in small numbers to satisfy regulators. Tesla proved that an electric car could be desirable, fast, beautiful, and commercially viable. The result is that every major automaker in the world is now investing billions in electric vehicle development. The global energy transition in transportation — arguably the most important industrial transformation since electrification itself — was catalyzed by one company and one man’s refusal to accept that it could not be done.

Tesla Supercharger stations enabling practical long-distance electric vehicle travel and supporting the global energy transition.

This is the democratic promise in action. Jobs said technology should serve civilization, not just shareholders. Tesla’s mission — “accelerate the world’s transition to sustainable energy” — is a civilizational mission. And unlike many corporate mission statements, it was real. The Supercharger network, the Gigafactories, the battery technology, the solar roof, the Powerwall — these are not vaporware. They are physical infrastructure that is materially accelerating the decarbonization of the global economy.

SpaceX is, by any objective measure, the most successful aerospace company since the dawn of the space age. It reduced launch costs by a factor of ten through reusable rockets — a technology that NASA and every legacy aerospace contractor had dismissed as impossible. Starlink is providing internet connectivity to remote and underserved communities worldwide — and provided critical communications infrastructure to Ukraine during the Russian invasion. Distributing internet access to people who had none is about as literal an expression of the democratic promise as technology can produce.

The glass is not half full. The glass, measured by achievement, is overflowing.

SpaceX Falcon 9 booster on an autonomous drone ship after successful landing at sea, demonstrating the reusability that dramatically lowered space access costs

The Democratic Commitments Were Real Too

This is the detail that elevates Musk’s story from a business narrative to a moral one.

In 2014, Musk released Tesla’s patents to the public, writing that hoarding intellectual property was “contrary to the goal” of accelerating sustainable transport. Read that alongside Berners-Lee’s decision to release the Web into the public domain. The logic is identical: the mission is bigger than the company, and enclosing the tools undermines the mission. In 2014, Musk sounded like a builder.

In 2015, he co-founded OpenAI as a nonprofit artificial intelligence research organization explicitly designed to prevent the concentration of AI power in the hands of a few corporations. The founding charter could have been written by Steve Jobs: ensure that artificial general intelligence benefits all of humanity, not just the shareholders of whoever builds it first.

These were not gestures. They were structural commitments — patents surrendered, millions invested, institutions created — in service of the principle that transformative technologies should benefit everyone.

Which is why what followed is not merely disappointing. It is the most significant individual reversal in the history of the democratic promise.

The Turn

The transition from Twitter’s blue bird logo to X, marking Musk’s ownership and transformation of the public square

The acquisition of Twitter in October 2022 for forty-four billion dollars is the clearest inflection point.

Musk framed the purchase as a defense of free speech — itself a democratic value. And in fairness, the question of content moderation on social media is genuinely difficult. The pre-Musk Twitter had made decisions that reasonable people could criticize. The line between protecting speech and preventing harm has no clean answer, and honest observers should acknowledge this complexity.

But Musk did not engage with the complexity. He gutted the content moderation infrastructure wholesale. He reinstated accounts banned for harassment and incitement without case-by-case review. He amplified conspiracy theories from his personal account — the most followed account on the platform. He transformed X from a flawed but functional public square into something closer to a personal megaphone.

The man who had released Tesla’s patents to the public now owned the public square and was reshaping it in his image. The man who had co-founded OpenAI to prevent AI concentration was now building his own AI company, xAI, with no nonprofit charter, no safety commitments, and no democratic governance structure. The man who had said he wanted to accelerate the world’s transition to sustainable energy was now the closest advisor to an administration that openly mocked climate science and dismantled environmental regulations.

And then came the political entanglement that dwarfed everything else. Hundreds of millions in campaign spending. A quasi-governmental role restructuring federal agencies. Proximity to executive power that no unelected private citizen in a democracy should possess, regardless of their achievements.

Why Musk Matters More Than Zuckerberg

Zuckerberg’s betrayal, documented in Part 4, was cynical but simple. The democratic rhetoric was always marketing. When it stopped being useful, he discarded it. There is nothing to mourn in Zuckerberg’s reversal because there was nothing genuine to reverse.

Musk is different, and the difference is what makes his story the most important in this series. He really did believe. The patent release was real. The OpenAI founding was real. The civilizational missions were not branding exercises — they were expressions of a worldview that Musk held sincerely and acted upon concretely, at personal financial risk, for years.

What changed him? The honest answer is that no one outside Musk’s inner circle can know for certain. But the pattern suggests an answer that is relevant to the larger argument of this series.

Musk accumulated more power — financial, industrial, political, cultural — than perhaps any private citizen in modern democratic history. He did so through genuinely extraordinary achievement. The wealth was not inherited. The companies were not acquired through financial engineering. He built real things that solve real problems.

And the accumulation of that much power changed his relationship to democratic governance. When you are one person among many, democratic distribution of power serves your interests — you benefit from open systems, shared standards, collective action. When you are the most powerful person in the room, democratic distribution of power constrains you. The rules that protect everyone else become the rules that limit you.

This is not primarily a criticism of Musk. It is a criticism of the system. A democracy that allows any individual — however brilliant, however accomplished — to spend unlimited amounts on elections, to own the public square, to leverage government contracts across multiple companies into political influence, and to accept quasi-governmental authority without electoral accountability, has a structural problem that transcends the character of any particular individual.

The Tragedy

I use the word “tragedy” deliberately, in its classical sense — not as a synonym for “sad event” but as a narrative in which a figure of genuine greatness is undone by the very qualities that made them great.

Musk’s audacity, his refusal to accept conventional limits, his willingness to bet everything on visions that the establishment dismissed — these are the qualities that built Tesla and SpaceX. They are also the qualities that led him to believe he could reshape American governance through sheer force of will, that rules and norms that apply to others need not apply to him, and that his judgment should override democratic processes because he has been right before when others were wrong.

Elon Musk in close proximity to political leadership, highlighting the shift toward concentrated influence in democratic processes.

The tragedy has a practical dimension that Tesla shareholders understand viscerally. Musk’s political activities may ultimately damage the very companies whose achievements constitute his strongest claim on history. Tesla’s brand, built on environmental idealism, is now associated with political polarization. The stock has suffered. The customer base has fractured. The mission — accelerate the world’s transition to sustainable energy — has been complicated by the founder’s alignment with political forces that deny climate science and have launched a trade war that disrupts the global supply chains Tesla depends on.

A man who did more to advance the democratic promise than almost any living person is now doing more to undermine democratic institutions than almost any living person. Both halves of that sentence are true. Neither cancels the other.

The Lesson

Jobs warned about this in 1983, although he framed it differently. The fractional horsepower motor analogy assumed that distributing computing power would distribute political power. It did not account for the possibility that someone could build the motors, sell the motors, own the platform the motors connect to, and use the resulting wealth to reshape the government that is supposed to regulate the motors.

The builders in Part 2 solved this problem by removing themselves from the equation — giving away their creations so that no one, including themselves, could capture them. Gates partially solved it by redirecting his wealth toward global public goods. Amodei, in Part 7, will solve it by embedding constraints in his company’s governance and holding those constraints under extreme pressure.

Musk solved nothing. He accumulated without constraint, and when his priorities shifted, his power shifted with him — from democratic to oligarchic, from distributed to concentrated, from civilizational to personal.

If the democratic promise cannot survive in the hands of someone who genuinely believed in it, who actively pursued it, who built world-changing companies around it — then the promise has a structural vulnerability that no amount of good intention can address. Musk did not fail the democratic promise because he was a bad person. He may have failed it because the system allowed one person to accumulate so much power that their personal evolution became a civilizational risk.

The democratic promise requires more than brilliant individuals building world-changing technology. It requires systems that ensure the technology serves democracy even when the individuals who built it no longer do.

That is not a lesson about Elon Musk. It is a lesson about democracy.
