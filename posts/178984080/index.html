“The Accommodation Was Never Just Trump”

How Elite Power Protected Epstein Across Every Boundary

November 15, 2025

Clockwise from top left: Stephen K. Bannon, Michael Wolff, Kathryn Ruemmler, Ehud Barak, Tom Barrack and Larry Summers. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post, AP; Getty Images)

Opening: The Selective Investigation

Episode 5 documented Trump’s Friday order directing Attorney General Pam Bondi to investigate Jeffrey Epstein’s relationships with Bill Clinton, Larry Summers, Reid Hoffman, and JPMorgan Chase.

Notice who’s missing from that list: Donald Trump.

Also missing: Stephen Bannon. Tom Barrack. Anyone from Trump’s circle who appears extensively in the same Epstein emails.

Trump framed the investigation as exposing “Democrats’” Epstein problem. Hours later, the Washington Post published “Jeffrey Epstein’s vast web of powerful friends”—revealing that elite accommodation of Epstein crossed every boundary: party, profession, ideology, and decency.

The documents show something far more disturbing than partisan scandal: systematic protection of a predator by people who held or sought the highest positions of power in American government, business, and media. Democrats and Republicans. Obama officials and Trump advisors. Prosecutors and journalists.

This is why Trump’s selective investigation confirms rather than refutes guilt. Full transparency would expose not just him, but the entire system that accommodated Epstein for decades.

The Real-Time Coordination: When Congress Texted a Trafficker

February 27, 2019: The Cohen Hearing

Michael Cohen—Trump’s former personal attorney and fixer—testified before the House Oversight Committee, alleging Trump was racist, manipulated financial records, and directed hush money payments to cover up extramarital affairs.

The hearing was televised. Millions watched. And according to newly released documents, Jeffrey Epstein was watching too—while texting with someone in the hearing room.

The Text Exchange

The timestamps and content match up precisely with video from the hearing:

Epstein: “Cohen brought up RONA - keeper of the secrets” (misspelling Rhona Graff’s first name)

Recipient: “RONA??”

Recipient: “Quick I’m up next is that an acronym”

Epstein: “Thats his assistant”

Minutes later, when Del. Stacey Plaskett (D-Virgin Islands) questioned Cohen:

Plaskett: “Are there other people that we should be meeting with?”

Cohen: “So Allen Weisselberg is the chief financial officer in The Trump Organization...”

Plaskett: “You’ve got to quickly give us as many names as you can so we can get to them. Is Ms. Rhona, what is Ms. Rhona’s—...?”

Cohen: “Rhona Graff is the — Mr. Trump’s executive assistant... She was — her office is directly next to his, and she’s involved in a lot that went on.”

The Identity

Though the recipient’s name is redacted, the timestamps match Plaskett’s questioning exactly. The content matches her questions. The urgency—”Quick I’m up next”—matches a congressional member about to question a witness.

Plaskett’s office confirmed Friday that during the hearing, she “received texts from staff, constituents and the public at large offering advice, support and in some cases partisan vitriol, including from Epstein.”

What This Reveals

A sitting member of Congress—during a congressional hearing about potential presidential crimes—received and apparently acted on information provided in real-time by a convicted sex trafficker.

Not past communication. Not archived emails. Real-time texting during official congressional proceedings.

The accommodation: Epstein maintained relationships with people in power so normalized that a House delegate saw nothing wrong with accepting his input during one of the most significant congressional hearings of Trump’s first presidency.

Plaskett’s defense—that she “welcomes information that helps her get at the truth”—misses the fundamental question: Why was a convicted sex offender watching congressional hearings and offering tactical advice to sitting members of Congress in 2019?

The Media Weaponization: Trading Information for Influence

Michael Wolff’s Strategic Opportunism

Michael Wolff—author of “Fire and Fury: Inside the Trump White House”—had previously acknowledged Epstein was a source for his Trump tell-all book.

The newly released emails reveal something far more disturbing than journalistic sourcing: Wolff wasn’t just gathering information. He was coordinating strategy with Epstein about how to weaponize that information.

December 2015: Advance Warning

Wolff to Epstein: CNN is planning to ask Trump about his relationship with you.

Wolff: “If we were able to craft an answer for him, what do you think it should be?”

Epstein: [Awaiting Wolff’s strategic advice]

Wolff: “I think you should let him hang himself.”

The calculation: If Trump denies being on Epstein’s plane or at his house, “that gives you valuable PR and political currency. You can hang him in a way that potentially generates a positive benefit for you, or, if it really looks like he could win, you could save him, generating a debt.”

October 2016: Post-Access Hollywood Opportunity

After the “Access Hollywood” tape emerged showing Trump bragging about groping women, Wolff saw another opportunity:

Wolff to Epstein: There’s an “opportunity to come forward this week and talk about Trump in such a way that could garner you great sympathy and help finish him.”

The Accommodation

Wolff defended his methods in a Wednesday podcast: “I’m a writer who manages to make relationships that let me tell a story in the ways that the New York Times or other, very reputable, journalistic organizations are unable to tell.”

Translation: I accommodated a sex trafficker—offering strategic advice about when to expose or protect Trump—in exchange for information other journalists couldn’t access.

The confession: Wolff described coordinating with Epstein as “playacting” and being “nice” to get information. But the emails show calculation, not performance. He was advising Epstein on how to use Trump knowledge for “political currency” and offering to help “finish him” if strategically beneficial.

This wasn’t journalism. This was collaboration.

The Political Consultancy: When Populism Sought Predator Advice

Stephen Bannon’s European Operation

Stephen Bannon—Trump’s former White House strategist—appears extensively in Epstein’s 2018-2019 communications, revealing a relationship that extended far beyond casual social contact.

The Frequency

  • Regular text message exchanges in months before Epstein’s July 2019 arrest

  • Epstein’s phone number and email stored with Bannon’s contact information

  • Multiple offers from Epstein to send private jet or charter planes to ferry Bannon to Paris

  • Bannon stayed overnight at Epstein’s home in spring 2019

  • Bannon regularly sent links to his media appearances; Epstein offered feedback

The Advisory Role

July 2018—Epstein to Bannon on European populism:

“If you are going to play here, you’ll have to spend time, europe by remote doesn’t work. Lots and lots of face time and hand holding. Its doable, but time consuming, there are many leaders of countries we can organize for you to have one on. ... Europe can be a wife not a mistress.”

Epstein was advising Trump’s former chief strategist on how to export political populism to Europe—offering to arrange meetings with European leaders.

The Warning

Summer 2018—Bannon to Epstein:

“But let’s discuss — their is a crazed jihad against u — ive never seen anything like it — and I’ve seen a lot.”

Epstein: Don’t worry, don’t risk diversion from midterm elections.

Bannon: “Exactly. But somebody big has u in the gunsights.”

What This Reveals

By 2018, Epstein had been convicted of soliciting prostitution with a minor for a decade. He’d served 13 months in county jail with work-release privileges. Every major news organization had documented his crimes and controversial plea deal.

Yet Trump’s chief political strategist was:

  • Staying overnight at Epstein’s home

  • Accepting strategic advice on European operations

  • Warning him about enemies

  • Maintaining extensive communication through the 2018 midterms

The accommodation: Bannon’s relationship with Epstein wasn’t historical or social. It was active, advisory, and ongoing while Bannon was shaping American and European political movements.

The Bipartisan Elite: When Party Lines Disappeared

Larry Summers: Two Years of Communication

The documents reveal two years of exchanges between Epstein and Larry Summers—former Treasury Secretary under Clinton, former Harvard president, former Obama National Economic Council director.

The topics: Ranked-choice voting, economic policy, and Summers’s “romantic problems.”

June 2019 exchange—just days before Epstein’s arrest on federal sex trafficking charges:

Summers again seeks advice seemingly about a woman. In earlier exchanges, Epstein referred to himself as a “good wing man” while discussing Summers’s attempts to attract a woman, though Summers said he would focus on his marriage.

The accommodation: A former Treasury Secretary and Harvard president maintained friendly, advisory communications with a convicted sex offender through June 2019—exchanging romantic advice with someone he knew had trafficked underage girls.

Kathryn Ruemmler: Attorney General Ambitions

Kathryn Ruemmler—Obama’s White House counsel, now Goldman Sachs chief legal officer—appears repeatedly in Epstein’s messages.

September 2014—As Attorney General Eric Holder announced his resignation:

Ruemmler: Considering whether to replace Holder at Justice Department, “but I need to be prepared to say yes before I talk to him [boss]”

Epstein: Urged her to “talk to boss,” then listed “professional, emotional, and financial” risks and rewards

Ruemmler: “Most girls do not have to worry about this crap”

Epstein: “’girls?’ careful i will renew an old habit”

Then Epstein listed influential people he was meeting that week during the UN General Assembly, adding: “you are a welcome guest at any.”

August 2018—After Michael Cohen pleaded guilty to campaign finance crimes:

Epstein to Ruemmler: “You see, I know how dirty Donald is. my guess is that non-lawyers NY biz people have no idea what it means to have your fixer flip.”

The Pattern

Obama’s White House counsel was:

  • Discussing career decisions with Epstein

  • Receiving invitations to his gatherings with influential people

  • Exchanging views about Trump’s legal troubles

All while Epstein was a registered sex offender.

Tom Barrack: The Creepiest Exchange

Tom Barrack—now U.S. Ambassador to Turkey, former top Trump fundraiser—appears in several exchanges.

March 2016:

Epstein: Received calls from reporters asking about Trump but refrained from commenting

Barrack: “Hope ur good. Let’s catch up”

Epstein: “Send photos of you and child. — makes me smile”

This isn’t policy discussion. This isn’t social pleasantries. This is a convicted sex offender asking a Trump confidant to send photos of a child.

The Systemic Protection: Why Accommodation Crossed Every Line

The Bipartisan Reality

Trump’s Friday investigation order targeted:

  • Bill Clinton (Democrat)

  • Larry Summers (Democrat)

  • Reid Hoffman (Democrat)

  • JPMorgan Chase (corporate)

Not mentioned:

  • Stephen Bannon (Trump advisor)

  • Tom Barrack (Trump fundraiser, ambassador)

  • Michael Wolff (Trump chronicler)

  • Any Republican in Epstein’s network

Why Selective Investigation Proves Guilt

If Trump genuinely wanted accountability for Epstein’s network, the investigation would include everyone—Democrats and Republicans, Obama officials and Trump advisors.

Instead, Trump weaponized DOJ against political opponents while protecting political allies who appear in the exact same documents.

The accommodation confession: Trump doesn’t want transparency. He wants selective exposure that damages opponents while protecting himself and his circle.

The Power Structure

The Washington Post documents reveal why Epstein maintained access and protection for so long:

Political Power: Plaskett coordinating during congressional hearing

Media Power: Wolff offering strategic advice on when to expose or protect Trump

Strategic Power: Bannon receiving European operation guidance

Economic Power: Summers, Goldman Sachs counsel maintaining relationships

Institutional Power: People considering or holding Attorney General positions

Epstein didn’t just know powerful people. He provided services to powerful people—advice, connections, intelligence, strategic guidance. And they accommodated him because he was useful.

The “Everyone Knew Him” Defense Collapse

Trump’s defense: Everyone in elite circles knew Epstein, so his relationship means nothing.

The documents prove: Not everyone maintained active advisory relationships through 2019. Not everyone texted during congressional hearings. Not everyone received strategic guidance on political operations. Not everyone asked for child photos.

The problem: Trump “knew about the girls” according to Epstein’s 2011 email and merely asked Maxwell “to stop” recruiting at Mar-a-Lago rather than reporting crimes.

That’s not “everyone knew him socially.” That’s documented knowledge of child trafficking without reporting to law enforcement.

Why Full Transparency Threatens Everyone

The Prosecutorial Problem

Attorney General Pam Bondi’s July memo stated DOJ had “thoroughly scrutinized the Epstein files and found nothing in them that could sustain opening further inquiries into anyone.”

The Washington Post documents reveal why that conclusion is suspicious:

If Plaskett’s congressional coordination isn’t worth investigation... If Bannon’s overnight stays and advisory relationship isn’t worth investigation... If Barrack’s child photo request isn’t worth investigation... If Summers’s two-year friendly communications isn’t worth investigation... If Ruemmler’s career advice exchanges aren’t worth investigation...

Then what standard is DOJ actually applying?

The Bipartisan Incentive

Full transparency threatens:

  • Democrats who maintained relationships (Plaskett, Summers, Ruemmler)

  • Republicans who maintained relationships (Bannon, Barrack, Trump)

  • Media figures who accommodated him (Wolff)

  • Institutions that profited from him (JPMorgan, Harvard)

Both parties have members implicated. Both parties have incentive to limit exposure. Both parties have accommodation to answer for.

Trump’s selective investigation is strategic: Target only Democrats, hope Republicans stay quiet about Republican accommodation, prevent full transparency that exposes everyone.

The Constitutional Mechanism’s Power

This is why the discharge petition matters: 218 signatures backed by constituent moral clarity can overcome bipartisan elite incentives to protect each other.

When the issue is framed as “protecting children versus protecting predators,” party loyalty becomes irrelevant. Republican and Democratic constituents both demand transparency regardless of which party members face exposure.

Conclusion: The Investigation That Won’t Happen

Trump’s Selective Outrage

Friday’s DOJ investigation order deliberately excludes:

  • Everyone in Trump’s circle who appears in Epstein documents

  • The question of why Epstein maintained these relationships

  • The systemic failure that allowed a convicted sex offender continued access to power

  • Trump’s own documented knowledge per Epstein’s emails

The confession: If the goal were justice for victims, the investigation would include everyone. If the goal were understanding how the system failed, the investigation would examine bipartisan accommodation.

But the goal is deflection. So the investigation targets only political opponents.

What Full Transparency Would Reveal

The complete Epstein files—currently held by Trump’s Justice Department—would document:

  • Why law enforcement didn’t investigate congressional coordination

  • Why FBI didn’t examine ongoing relationships with convicted offender

  • What warnings were ignored and by whom

  • Which institutions facilitated continued access to power

  • Who knew what and when across both parties

Every person in the Washington Post article has one thing in common: They maintained relationships with Epstein after his 2008 conviction. Some through his July 2019 arrest.

The question isn’t whether Democrats or Republicans failed. The question is why American power structures—across party, profession, and institution—accommodated a convicted sex offender for over a decade.

The Vote Next Week

When the House votes on releasing DOJ’s complete Epstein files, every member faces a choice:

Vote yes: Expose bipartisan accommodation, systematic failures, institutional capture—including members of their own party

Vote no: Protect party members while abandoning moral clarity and victims’ demands for accountability

Trump’s selective investigation proves he’s terrified of option one. Not because it exposes Democrats. Because it exposes everyone—including the systematic elite accommodation that allowed Epstein access to congressional hearings, White House advisors, media figures, and people considering the highest law enforcement positions in government.

Democracy’s Test

The discharge petition isn’t about Trump versus Democrats. It’s about whether constitutional mechanisms can overcome elite protection of predators when that protection crosses party lines.

218 signatures say yes. Next week’s vote will determine if they’re right.
