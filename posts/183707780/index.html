The Venezuela Invasion Series

The Pattern Accelerates

48 Hours After Capturing Maduro, Trump Threatens Colombia and Denmark—Venezuela as Proof of Concept

January 6, 2026

January 6, 2026

Saturday: Trump invades Venezuela, captures Maduro. Sunday: Trump threatens Colombia—”It sounds good to me” when asked about invasion. Monday: Trump demands Greenland from Denmark, a NATO ally. Denmark warns: Attack would “end NATO.” Trump advisor Stephen Miller: “Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily over the future of Greenland.” Three countries in three days. Venezuela wasn’t an endpoint. It was proof of concept.

SUNDAY NIGHT: COLOMBIA THREATENED

Air Force One, January 4, 2026—Trump returning from Florida, surrounded by reporters.

Venezuela was 36 hours old. Maduro sat in a Brooklyn detention center. The world was still processing Saturday’s invasion.

Trump was already moving to the next target.

A reporter asked about Colombia. Its president, Gustavo Petro, had criticized the Venezuela invasion. He’d blocked deportation flights. He’d stood in Manhattan urging U.S. soldiers to disobey orders.

Trump’s response:

“Colombia is run by a sick man who likes making cocaine and selling it to the United States. He’s not going to be doing it for very long. He has cocaine mills and cocaine factories.”

The framing was identical to Venezuela: Narco-terrorism. Drug production. A leader who “likes making cocaine.”

Then came the threat:

Reporter: “Would the United States conduct an operation against Colombia?”

Trump: “It sounds good to me.”

Not “we’re considering it.” Not “all options on the table.” Simply: “It sounds good to me.”

Like ordering from a menu.

WHY COLOMBIA? THE RESOURCE CALCULUS

In Part 9 of this series, I documented Florida Republicans’ predictions about Trump’s next targets after Venezuela.

They said: Cuba and Nicaragua. Ideological targets. Communist holdouts. Finishing the Cold War.

They were wrong.

Trump threatened Colombia.

Why? Because Colombia has what Trump wants:

Oil: Approximately 800,000 barrels per day production. Significant reserves. Infrastructure that works (unlike Venezuela’s degraded facilities).

Strategic location: Borders Venezuela, Panama Canal access, Pacific and Caribbean coasts. Control Colombia, control northern South America.

Larger economy: Colombia’s GDP is roughly $380 billion. Venezuela’s is under $100 billion (collapsed from $482 billion in 2014). More to extract.

Leftist president: Gustavo Petro is former M-19 guerrilla, first leftist president in Colombian history. He criticized Trump, blocked deportations, opposed Venezuela invasion. Personal animosity plus ideological justification.

The pattern from Venezuela: Trump doesn’t invade for ideology. He invades for resources.

Cuba has no oil. Venezuela took its subsidy away. Cuba is bankrupt. Trump said Sunday: “Cuba looks like it is ready to fall. I don’t know if they’re going to hold out, but Cuba now has no income.”

Why invade a country that’s already collapsing and has nothing you want? Just wait. Cuba will fall on its own.

Nicaragua has minimal oil. Some production, nothing significant. Small economy. Limited strategic value beyond symbolism.

Why waste military resources on symbolic victories?

But Colombia? Oil, economy, strategic position, leftist president who insulted you. That’s worth invading.

And Trump just proved he could.

MONDAY MORNING: GREENLAND DEMANDED

Less than 24 hours after threatening Colombia, Trump escalated further.

The Atlantic magazine interview published Monday morning:

Trump: “We need Greenland from the standpoint of national security.”

Not “we’d like.” Not “we’re interested.” We need.

The justification: “Greenland is covered with Russian and Chinese ships all over the place.”

The insult: Asked about Denmark’s defense of Greenland, Trump mocked: “You know what Denmark did recently to boost up security on Greenland? They added one more dog sled.”

Denmark’s response—Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen:

Sunday evening: “It makes absolutely no sense to talk about the U.S. needing to take over Greenland. The U.S. has no right to annex any of the three countries in the Danish Kingdom.”

Monday morning, interviews with Danish media: Trump “should be taken seriously when he says he wants Greenland.” If the U.S. attacked Greenland, “everything would come to an end. The international community as we know it; democratic rules of the game; NATO, the world’s strongest defensive alliance—all of that would collapse if one NATO country chose to attack another.”

She said it three times in different interviews: An attack on Greenland would END NATO.

Stephen Miller went on CNN Monday morning:

Miller: “Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily over the future of Greenland.”

Read that sentence again.

Not “we won’t attack a NATO ally.” Not “we respect Denmark’s sovereignty.” Instead: Nobody can stop us.

The doctrine, explicit: Might makes right.

EUROPE RESPONDS: SEVEN LEADERS ISSUE JOINT STATEMENT

Monday evening, January 6—hours after Miller’s CNN appearance—seven European leaders issued an unprecedented joint statement.

The signatories:

  • President Emmanuel Macron (France)

  • Chancellor Friedrich Merz (Germany)

  • Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni (Italy)

  • Prime Minister Donald Tusk (Poland)

  • Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez (Spain)

  • Prime Minister Keir Starmer (United Kingdom)

  • Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen (Denmark)

The statement, titled “Joint Statement on Greenland”:

“Arctic security remains a key priority for Europe and it is critical for international and transatlantic security.”

“NATO has made clear that the Arctic region is a priority and European Allies are stepping up. We and many other Allies have increased our presence, activities and investments, to keep the Arctic safe and to deter adversaries. The Kingdom of Denmark – including Greenland – is part of NATO.”

“Security in the Arctic must therefore be achieved collectively, in conjunction with NATO allies including the United States, by upholding the principles of the UN Charter, including sovereignty, territorial integrity and the inviolability of borders. These are universal principles, and we will not stop defending them.”

“Greenland belongs to its people. It is for Denmark and Greenland, and them only, to decide on matters concerning Denmark and Greenland.”

This is extraordinary.

Seven leaders—representing Europe’s major powers—issuing a same-day response to Trump’s Greenland threat.

The signatories represent:

  • Three of Europe’s five largest economies (Germany, France, UK)

  • NATO’s nuclear powers in Europe (France, UK)

  • The EU’s most populous country (Germany)

  • A fellow populist who typically aligns with Trump (Meloni, Italy)

  • Reagan’s ally against Soviet communism (Tusk, Poland)

Poland’s presence is particularly significant. Donald Tusk, leading the country Reagan supported against Soviet tyranny, is now joining a statement defending another nation against American threats.

The statement directly rebukes Miller’s doctrine:

Miller said: “Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily over the future of Greenland.”

Europe responded: “These are universal principles, and we will not stop defending them.”

The implications:

This isn’t Denmark standing alone. This is Europe united—including countries Trump considers allies (Poland, Italy) and countries he needs for trade/security (Germany, France, UK).

If Trump invades Greenland, he doesn’t just fight Denmark. He fights Europe.

And Europe just said: We’re not backing down.

The statement invokes the UN Charter: sovereignty, territorial integrity, inviolability of borders. The same principles Russia violated in Ukraine. The same principles Trump violated in Venezuela.

Now Trump threatens to violate them against a NATO ally. And Europe is drawing a line.

The question Miller posed—”Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily”—just got an answer: Maybe not militarily. But Europe will defend these principles. Which means economic sanctions, diplomatic isolation, NATO crisis, transatlantic rupture.

All for Greenland’s rare earths.

THE PATTERN ACCELERATES FURTHER: ESTABLISHMENT ALARM

CNN’s Fareed Zakaria, Monday evening interview with Anderson Cooper:

Zakaria—establishment voice, not progressive firebrand—sounded alarms about what Venezuela means for Trump’s next moves.

On Venezuela’s outcome, Zakaria predicted two options:

Option 1 (most likely): “The Trump administration is going to leave the entire regime in place. All it’s done essentially is decapitated, gotten rid of Maduro and then leave Delcy Rodríguez and the interior minister and the defense minister who have all the guns under them in place... This will not be a regime change. This is really the most expensive arrest in human history.“

Option 2 (regime change): “That would look a lot like Iraq. These guys have guns, they have arms. By the way, there are a lot of militias in Venezuela because of the drugs, because of the oil.”

On Trump’s oil obsession:

“Trump has a very old-fashioned view of economics. ‘We extract these minerals, we extract the oil.’ In point of fact, unless you did something like 19th century colonialism... we don’t get any special deal. We don’t need the oil. We’re the largest producer of liquid hydrocarbons in the world.”

Cooper: “I remember interviewing him back when he was running in 2015, 2016. He would talk about ‘We should have taken the oil from Iraq. We should have literally surrounded the oil fields with American troops, sucked out the oil and taken it.’”

Zakaria: “It shows oil is at the heart of certainly his thought.”

On threatening Colombia and Mexico:

“The Colombian one and the Mexican one are bizarre. Because these are our allies, they cooperate with American law enforcement... Colombia, for 15 years was the second largest recipient of US aid after Israel. Because they were fighting all these drug cartels, and we were helping them.”

On threatening democracies vs. dictatorships:

“These are democracies. It’s one thing to get rid of a nasty dictatorship like Maduro, who the people hate. But that’s not the case in Mexico, that’s not the case in Colombia. These are elected leaders, and you’re suddenly galvanizing nationalism in each of these countries against America.“

On squandering goodwill:

“All these countries are now feeling resentful of America. You’re squandering all of the goodwill that America built up in Latin America over the last 50 years, by partnering with these countries, by providing aid, by helping them with their drug problems... for what? For braggadocio? For the ability to sound like you’re a tough guy, you’re squandering 50 years of goodwill.“

This matters because Zakaria isn’t a progressive critic. He’s CNN’s establishment foreign policy voice. Council on Foreign Relations. Harvard PhD. Not prone to hyperbole.

And he’s saying: Venezuela is “the most expensive arrest in human history.” Threatening Colombia/Mexico is “bizarre.” Trump is practicing “19th century colonialism.” He’s “squandering 50 years of goodwill” for “braggadocio.”

The establishment is alarmed. Europe is united in opposition. And Trump threatened three more countries in 48 hours.

The pattern isn’t slowing. It’s accelerating.

THE GREENLAND CALCULUS: RESOURCES AND POSITIONING

Why Greenland?

Not ideology. Denmark is a democracy, U.S. ally, NATO member since 1949.

Resources and strategic positioning:

Rare earth minerals: Greenland potentially has massive deposits of elements crucial for batteries, electronics, weapons systems. China currently dominates global rare earth markets. Control Greenland, break Chinese monopoly.

Arctic positioning: Climate change is opening Arctic shipping routes and making previously inaccessible resources extractable. Greenland sits at the center of Arctic competition between U.S., Russia, China.

Military base: U.S. already has Pituffik Space Base (formerly Thule Air Base) in Greenland, critical for missile defense and Arctic surveillance. But Denmark controls access, sets terms. U.S. ownership means unconstrained military use.

Oil and gas: Parts of Greenland’s continental shelf may hold significant undiscovered deposits. Greenland’s government abandoned oil ambitions in 2021 for environmental reasons. U.S. control overturns that.

The Trump National Security Strategy (November 2025) foreshadowed this:

The “Trump Corollary” to the Monroe Doctrine asserts U.S. right to “restore American pre-eminence” and deny “non-Hemispheric competitors” the ability to “own or control strategically vital assets.”

“Strategically vital assets.”

Venezuela’s oil: Strategically vital. U.S. invaded.

Greenland’s rare earths and Arctic position: Strategically vital. U.S. demands.

The doctrine: If it’s strategically vital, America takes it.

THE DENMARK PROBLEM: ATTACKING A NATO ALLY

Greenland is fundamentally different from Venezuela or Colombia:

Denmark is a NATO founding member. Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty states: “an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or North America shall be considered an attack against them all.”

If the U.S. invades Greenland, it triggers Article 5.

Which means:

NATO must either defend Denmark against the United States (impossible—U.S. is NATO’s military backbone)

Or NATO dissolves (as Frederiksen warned—”everything would come to an end”)

The implications:

European security collapses: Without NATO, Russia faces no collective defense. Putin can pressure Baltic states, Poland, Finland without triggering alliance response.

U.S. credibility destroyed: Every alliance commitment becomes worthless. Japan, South Korea, Philippines, Australia—why trust U.S. defense guarantees if America attacks its own allies?

Global order shattered: If NATO—the most successful military alliance in history—can’t survive U.S. aggression against a member state, what international institution means anything?

Miller’s response: “Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily.”

Translation: Yes, invading Greenland ends NATO. So what? Who’s going to stop us?

This is the pattern Venezuela established:

International law says you can’t invade sovereign nations and kidnap leaders. Trump invaded Venezuela. The world condemned it. And nothing happened.

So why not Greenland? NATO says you can’t attack allies. Trump might anyway. The world will condemn it. And probably nothing will happen.

Because—as Miller said—nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily.

THE ACCELERATION: THE WEEKEND’S TARGETS

Saturday, January 3: Venezuela invaded, Maduro captured

Sunday, January 4: Colombia threatened (”It sounds good to me”)

Monday, January 5: Greenland demanded, Denmark warned

Also mentioned over the weekend: Mexico (”We’re going to have to do something” about cartels), Iran (”Going to get hit very hard”), Cuba (”Ready to fall”)

The pattern isn’t slowing. It’s accelerating.

Why?

Because Venezuela worked. Military operation succeeded, economic leverage achieved, oil access secured, no retaliation, no serious punishment, no political cost at home.

From Trump’s perspective: The formula works. Why stop?

WHY FLORIDA REPUBLICANS WERE WRONG: OIL BEATS IDEOLOGY

In Part 9, I documented predictions from Florida Republicans about Trump’s next targets after Venezuela.

Marco Rubio, Rick Scott, Mario Diaz-Balart—they all said: Cuba and Nicaragua next. Finish what Reagan started. Roll back communism. Free the last oppressed peoples of the Western Hemisphere.

They were thinking ideologically. Trump thinks transactionally.

Cuba has no oil. Its economy is collapsing. Trump said it’s “ready to fall” on its own. Why waste military resources on a country that’s already dying and has nothing to extract?

Nicaragua has minimal oil. Daniel Ortega is a leftist dictator, yes. But Nicaragua’s economy is tiny. Its oil production is negligible. Its strategic value is limited.

Invading for ideology is expensive. You have to occupy, govern, rebuild. Iraq and Afghanistan cost trillions because Bush wanted to spread democracy.

Invading for resources is profitable. You extract oil, leave the government to locals (as long as they comply), collect revenue.

Colombia has oil. Greenland has rare earths.

That’s why Trump threatened Colombia, not Nicaragua.

That’s why Trump demands Greenland, not Iceland (which has no significant resources).

The pattern is clear: Trump invades where there’s something to take.

Florida Republicans were predicting Cold War 2.0. Trump is conducting Resource War 1.0.

THE COLOMBIA WILDCARD: A COUNTRY THAT COULD FIGHT BACK

Colombia is different from Venezuela in crucial ways:

Military capacity: Colombia has 293,000 active military personnel, battle-tested from decades fighting FARC guerrillas and drug cartels. Venezuela had perhaps 123,000 (with questionable loyalty).

U.S. support legacy: Colombia received billions in U.S. military aid over decades through Plan Colombia. Much of its military is U.S.-trained, uses U.S. equipment. Officers have relationships with U.S. counterparts.

Economic integration: Colombia is a major non-NATO ally. Trade partner. Cooperates on counter-narcotics. Not isolated like Venezuela.

Regional support: Colombia has better relations with neighbors than Venezuela did. Brazil, Chile, Peru would be more sympathetic to Colombia than they were to Maduro.

Geography: Colombia has two coasts (Caribbean and Pacific), shares border with Panama. Harder to blockade than Venezuela.

The problem for Trump:

Invading Colombia is significantly harder than invading Venezuela. The military would actually fight. U.S. casualties would be higher. Regional reaction would be stronger.

But Trump threatened it anyway.

Why?

Because Venezuela established the precedent. And in Trump’s calculation, even if Colombia is harder, the formula still works: frame as narco-terrorism, invoke law enforcement, ignore international law.

Colombia’s President Petro responded Monday:

“If you detain a president whom much of my people want and respect, you will unleash the people’s jaguar.”

He ordered Colombian military: “Any commander of the security forces who prefers the U.S. flag over the Colombian flag will be immediately removed from the institution.”

He said he “asked the people to defend the president against any illegitimate violent act.”

But Colombia’s Defense Minister, Pedro Sánchez, immediately walked it back:

He emphasized continued cooperation with U.S. on counter-narcotics. Said he had regular communication with American officials. Noted they hadn’t discussed any U.S. operation in recent conversations.

Translation: Petro is defiant. But his own Defense Minister is already signaling accommodation.

The pattern from Venezuela: Defiance lasts until the blockade tightens. Then accommodation.

THE PERMISSION STRUCTURE ACTIVATES

In Part 8 of this series, I documented how Trump’s Venezuela invasion creates a “permission structure” for autocrats worldwide.

If the U.S. can invade Venezuela, then:

  • China can cite that precedent for Taiwan

  • Russia can cite it for Ukraine

  • Any regional power can cite it for neighbors

I wrote Part 8 before Trump threatened Colombia and Greenland.

Now the permission structure is activating in real-time—but not the way I expected.

It’s not other countries using Venezuela as precedent. It’s Trump using Venezuela as precedent.

Venezuela proved: Military invasion works. International condemnation is meaningless. “Law enforcement” framing provides cover. Resource extraction is achievable.

So Trump immediately applied the formula to:

  • Colombia (”narco-terrorism” framing, oil extraction objective)

  • Greenland (”national security” framing, rare earth/Arctic objective)

  • And threatened: Mexico, Iran, Cuba

The permission structure isn’t external. It’s internal.

Trump gave himself permission. Venezuela was the proof of concept. Everything after is applying the template.

CONCLUSION: VENEZUELA AS PROOF OF CONCEPT

Saturday’s Venezuela invasion wasn’t an endpoint. It was a beginning.

It proved U.S. military can invade, capture leaders, face condemnation—and suffer zero consequences.

Within 48 hours: Colombia threatened. Greenland demanded.

The template works. Why not apply it everywhere?

Denmark’s Prime Minister Frederiksen warned: A U.S. attack on Greenland would “end NATO.”

Stephen Miller responded: “Nobody’s going to fight the United States militarily.”

That’s the doctrine. Explicitly stated. Unapologetic.

International law is optional. Sovereignty is conditional. Alliances are transactional. Might makes right.

Venezuela proved the concept. The acceleration is real.

And nothing stops it until something does: A miscalculation triggering unwinnable war. Casualties mounting past tolerance. Occupied countries becoming ungovernable money pits. Allies abandoning America. Empire collapsing from overextension.

That’s how empires end: Not with deciding to stop, but discovering they can’t continue.

POSTSCRIPT: THE PATTERN ACCELERATES FURTHER (January 7, 2026)

Soon after this essay went to press, the pattern accelerated beyond even these projections.

Tuesday night: Trump announced Venezuela would hand over 30-50 million barrels of oil—$1.8 to $3 billion worth. Energy Secretary Chris Wright clarified Wednesday: The U.S. will control Venezuelan oil sales “indefinitely.”

Wednesday morning: U.S. Coast Guard seized a Russian-flagged oil tanker in the North Atlantic. The Marinera (formerly Bella 1) had evaded the Venezuela blockade. Russia sent a naval vessel to escort it. The U.S. boarded anyway.

The blockade isn’t limited to Venezuela. It’s global.

Meanwhile, repression in Venezuela intensifies: Armed colectivos search phones at checkpoints for keywords: “invasion,” “Maduro,” “Trump.” Anyone celebrating Maduro’s capture faces arrest. A 56-year-old produce vendor shouted celebration—police arrested him, demanded $1,000 bribe plus fruit and vegetables for release. Fourteen journalists detained. The 90-day emergency decree authorizes “immediate search and capture” of anyone supporting “the armed attack by the United States.”

Cuba spirals toward collapse: Economist Omar Everleny Pérez, 64, who has lived in Cuba his entire life: “It’s never been as bad as it is now.” Blackouts last 14-15 hours daily. Ration cards worthless. Gasoline queue: one Havanan is 5,052nd in line after signing up three months ago. 2.75 million Cubans have fled since 2020—nearly 30% of the population.

Europe panics privately but stays silent publicly: New York Times correspondent Steven Erlanger reports “a massive gap between public and private reactions from European leaders. Privately, they are panicking about what happens next, especially in Greenland. But publicly on Venezuela, they are desperate not to say anything critical or invoke international law on Trump at a time of maximum peril for Ukraine.”

Latin America condemns at OAS: Brazil, Mexico, Colombia strongly condemned the invasion. Mexico’s representative: “Intervention has never brought lasting stability.” A protester interrupted the U.S. ambassador, shouting: “Imperial oil grab.” Only El Salvador backed the U.S.

And Trump’s demands to Venezuela’s interim government? Secretary of State Marco Rubio told lawmakers: Expel all Chinese, Russian, Iranian, and Cuban advisers. Reopen oil trade to the U.S. Relax or end nationalization.

Democracy? Political prisoners? Elections? Trump was asked Sunday whether those had been discussed. His response: “We haven’t gotten to that yet. What we want to do now is fix up the oil.”

Four days after Venezuela, the pattern is clear:

Saturday: Maduro captured
Sunday: Colombia threatened
Monday: Greenland demanded
Tuesday: Venezuela hands over billions in oil
Wednesday: Russian tanker seized in North Atlantic

The blockade is global. The repression intensifies. Europe panics. Cuba collapses. Latin America condemns. And Trump wants the oil.

Venezuela wasn’t an endpoint. It wasn’t even the beginning. It was proof the formula works.

And the pattern accelerates.
