The Iran Crisis

They Told Us So

Part XXII: What Was Known Before the First Bomb Fell

March 1, 2026

The risks of this war were not unknowable.

They were known. They were named. They were published — in some cases on the same morning the bombs fell, in others weeks before the 10-day deadline expired. The analysts who understood Iran best looked at the strategic situation, the internal dynamics, the historical precedents, and the specific personalities involved, and they told us what would happen.

This essay documents what they said. Not as a vindication exercise. As an accountability record.

The Trump Accountability Project — which this series has maintained across twenty-one parts — is not only about what presidents do. It is about what was knowable at the time they acted. When consequences that were predicted in print come true in real time, the decision to proceed anyway is not a miscalculation. It is a choice. Choices have owners.

The Kissinger Asymmetry: America Cannot Win This War By Its Own Definition

Henry Kissinger in a classic 1970s portrait, whose guerrilla warfare insight—”The insurgent wins by not losing, while the conventional power loses by not winning”—Karim Sadjadpour applied to the U.S.-Iran asymmetry before the strikes (source: National Security Archive historical photo).

On the day the bombs fell, Karim Sadjadpour published the strategic line that will define this conflict’s historical assessment. Writing in The Atlantic, he quoted Henry Kissinger on guerrilla warfare:

“The insurgent wins by not losing, while the conventional power loses by not winning.”

America’s military superiority is overwhelming. Sadjadpour was explicit about that. But military superiority, in this particular contest, is not necessarily decisive. The two sides are playing for different stakes. Washington seeks a transformative victory: regime change, a post-theocratic Iran, the Nobel Prize framing Trump announced by Saturday evening. Tehran seeks only to survive.

Under Kissinger’s framework — which Sadjadpour applied directly to this conflict before the first strike — America loses if it doesn’t achieve regime change. Iran wins if it doesn’t collapse. That asymmetry does not favor the side with the aircraft carriers.

Pezeshkian’s son said it plainly on Saturday, while confirming his father survived the assassination attempt: “We will probably experience a longer conflict and this will be a war of attrition. Therefore, determination and patience are essential to get through these days.”

That is not the language of a losing side. That is a government that has read Kissinger and knows the game it is playing. The war has been underway for 36 hours. Iran is already winning by the only definition that matters to it: it has not collapsed.

The Venezuela Hubris: Petraeus Warned Us

General David Petraeus in full dress uniform during his retirement ceremony, the decorated commander whose pre-strike warnings highlighted how Venezuela’s quick transition fueled unrealistic expectations for Iran (source: U.S. Army official photo.

General David Petraeus, one of the most decorated American commanders of the post-Cold War era, offered a specific warning before the strikes that Sadjadpour documented and that has received almost no coverage in the 36 hours since.

Just as the swift fall of the Taliban in 2001 fueled unrealistic expectations for regime change in Iraq, Petraeus cautioned, the lightning capture of Maduro — and the seemingly seamless transition to Delcy Rodríguez in Venezuela last month — may have led Trump to believe that such a scenario is easily replicable in Iran.

This series has documented the Venezuela parallel extensively. What Petraeus added was the Iraq connection: a rapid early victory producing assumptions that proved catastrophically wrong when applied to a harder, more complex target.

Venezuela had 30 million people, a broken economy, a military that had largely stopped believing in Maduro, and a pre-identified successor with an established relationship with the Trump administration. The transition took days. It was visible, coordinated, and planned in advance with both sides at the table.

Iran has 93 million people, an economy that has survived four decades of maximum-pressure sanctions, an IRGC that has spent 35 years preparing for exactly this assault, an internet blackout already operational, a succession plan pre-delegated to Larijani, and three overlapping military command structures. The Delcy of Iran has been named. He is currently posting revenge threats on X while forming an interim council under bombardment.

This is not Venezuela. Petraeus said so. In print. Before the bombs fell.

What Iranians Actually Wanted — And What They Got

Crowds in Tehran during recent anti-regime protests, waving Iranian flags and portraits—illustrating the pre-war street momentum that analysts like Arash Azizi said would be militarized and disrupted by all-out war (source: Axios/Reuters coverage).

The January Azizi piece for The Atlantic documented something that has been entirely lost in the week of escalation leading to Saturday: what Iranians who opposed the regime actually asked for.

The consensus was striking in its specificity. Activists, academics, Nobel laureates, filmmakers, left-wing party leaders, and ordinary protesters — across the political spectrum of the Iranian opposition — largely agreed on one thing. They wanted Khamenei taken out. Targeted. A scalpel, not a sledgehammer.

A Vancouver-based leader of a prominent left-wing Iranian party told Azizi directly: “A broader military attack will help the Islamic Republic and hurt the revolution. An all-out war will surely benefit the Islamic Republic. They will militarize the atmosphere.”

He pointed to the June 2025 twelve-day war as evidence: it paused a nationwide truck-driver strike that had been building domestic pressure on the regime. The bombs stopped the protests.

The same pattern is visible now. The January crackdown killed as many as 30,000 Iranians. Massive street protests were building. The regime was, by every internal account, experiencing what Arash Azizi called “acute existential anxiety.” And then the bombs fell — and the streets of Tehran that were protesting against the regime now have something else to process: foreign jets overhead, schools destroyed in Minab, a navy burning in Chabahar.

Some Iranians celebrated Khamenei’s death. That is documented and real, and Part XX reported it in full. But the analyst who warned that all-out war “will militarize the atmosphere” was also right. Both things can be true simultaneously. The question is which dynamic is larger — and whether the celebrating Tehran is armed and organized enough to act, while the mourning Tehran and the IRGC are still standing.

The Iranians who opposed the regime asked for a scalpel. They got 500 Israeli strikes on Day One, a school in Minab with 85 dead children, and a week of sustained bombing with no ground troops, no transition plan, and no governance structure waiting on the other side.

The Internal Opposition Was Destroyed Three Weeks Before the War

Azar Mansouri (Persian: آذر منصوری; born c. 1964) is an Iranian reformist politician. She is a founding member of the Union of Islamic Iran People Party and the party's secretary-general. She is also a senior member of the Islamic Iran Participation Front.

This is the detail that deserves more attention than it has received, and Azizi documented it on February 20 — eight days before the strikes.

On February 8 and 9, the Iranian regime arrested Azar Mansouri — the leader of the Iranian Reformist Front, the same political organization that formed the base of President Pezeshkian’s electoral coalition. Several of her colleagues were arrested simultaneously: Javad Emam, Ebrahim Asgharzadeh, and others. They were charged with “undermining national unity” and “coordinating with enemy propaganda.”

What had they done? They had joined an initiative called the National Assembly for the Salvation of Iran — a domestic framework for a transition to democratic governance. The initiative had barely gotten off the ground. The regime shut it down anyway, and arrested its participants, before it could take shape.

Three weeks later, the bombs fell and Trump told Iranians to take over their government.

The governance bridge that might have caught Iran on the other side of the strikes — a functioning domestic opposition with a transition framework, with legitimacy inside the country, with relationships across political factions — was systematically dismantled by the regime in the three weeks before the bombing began. Whether that timing was coincidental or deliberate preparation for exactly the scenario now unfolding is a question this series will continue to investigate. What is documented is the sequence: the internal opposition neutralized in February, the bombs in March, and now a succession process happening entirely inside the IRGC and the Assembly of Experts with no domestic opposition structure in a position to offer an alternative.

Anahita Hosseini-Lewis, an Iran expert at Birkbeck University of London, described the Islamic Republic’s “long-standing strategy of preventing domestically rooted alternatives from taking shape.” That strategy was executed with particular thoroughness in the three weeks before the war it was preparing for.

Trump told the Iranian people their only chance for generations was at hand. The organized domestic infrastructure that might have translated that chance into a governing coalition had been in prison since February 9.

The Fox and the Hedgehog: A Fight With No Negotiating Table

Sadjadpour’s January essay — published before the strikes, before the deadline, before the Geneva talks collapsed — identified the philosophical asymmetry at the center of this conflict with precision that reads, in hindsight, as prophetic.

Trump is the fox: no fixed beliefs, transactional, convinced that everyone has a price and every nation has a breaking point. Khamenei was the hedgehog: one big thing, resistance, immovable because to move was ideological suicide.

“Trump cannot understand why pressure and threats don’t break Khamenei,” Sadjadpour wrote. “He assumes that every man can be bought and every nation has a breaking point. But Khamenei, who espouses resistance and martyrdom, and who believes that ceding to pressure projects weakness, cannot be persuaded with material blandishments.”

Now Khamenei is dead. The hedgehog is gone. What replaces him is the question of Part XX — but the answer, based on everything this series has documented, is that the IRGC is 80% opportunists who are not hedgehogs. They are a different kind of fox: transactional, economic, calculating their personal survival. That is actually the most hopeful sign in the entire situation. A regime of opportunists is a regime that can deal.

But the fox who launched this war has not demonstrated the patience or the strategic clarity to recognize a dealing partner when one emerges. Trump sent Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff to negotiate with a theocracy that had been hardening its positions for 47 years. He dismantled Voice of America and Radio Farda the year before he needed them most. He attended a fundraising dinner while the war entered its second day. He declared world peace while ordering bombing to continue throughout the week.

Sadjadpour described Trump’s approach to the Iranian forces on the night of the strikes as sounding “like a closing argument across a negotiating table: ‘Lay down your weapons and have complete immunity. Or, in the alternative, face certain death.’” That is not the language of someone who has thought carefully about what comes after compliance. It is the language of someone who believes the deal closes itself once the other side understands the terms.

The deal, in Iran’s case, does not close itself.

The Liar’s Dividend: The Fog Is A Weapon

Mahsa Alimardani, writing for The Atlantic in January, documented a mechanism that is now operating at full scale inside Iran: the liar’s dividend.

The concept is simple. When bad actors sow uncertainty about whether any documentation is real — whether videos are AI-generated, whether death tolls are accurate, whether protest footage is authentic — the benefit accrues to the party with the most to hide. In Iran’s case, that is the regime.

The Iranian regime has spent four decades undermining the credibility of dissent. AI has given it a new tool — not just to create fakes, but to weaponize the suspicion that everything might be fake. Every blurry video, every enhanced image, every contested footage clip becomes “evidence” of a foreign psyop.

The internet is now at 4% of normal capacity. The digital wall is fully operational. State media is the primary information source for most Iranians inside the country. The liar’s dividend is being collected at scale.

What this means for the uprising Trump predicted: even if Iranians want to organize, they cannot coordinate in real time. Even if opposition leaders want to communicate, they cannot reach their base. Even if the celebrating Tehran outnumbers the mourning Tehran, the celebrating Tehran does not know that — because the information infrastructure that would allow it to know has been shut off.

The regime that is losing its supreme leader, its IRGC commander, its defense minister, and its National Security Council is still operating its internet blackout. That is the capability the bombs haven’t touched. And it may be the most important capability in the succession crisis now unfolding.

The 1956 Warning: Blood Guilt and the Hungarian Precedent

A Hungarian revolutionary with rifle amid rubble and a Soviet tank in Budapest, 1956—Eliot Cohen’s pre-strike warning drew direct parallels to the moral hazard of U.S. encouragement without follow-through (source: TIME historical archive)

The most arresting warning came not from a liberal critic but from Eliot Cohen — a Johns Hopkins professor, former counselor to the State Department under Condoleezza Rice, and one of the architects of serious Republican national security thinking for three decades. Cohen is not someone who reflexively opposes American military action. He supported the Iraq war. He is not the left.

Writing for The Atlantic on the night of the strikes, Cohen named something that no other analyst had put directly on the table: the 1956 Hungarian precedent.

In 1956, the United States — through Radio Free Europe and Voice of America — encouraged Hungarians to rise against Soviet occupation. They rose. America did not come. Soviet tanks crushed the uprising. As many as 2,500 Hungarians were killed in the fighting, and 200,000 fled the country. The blood guilt followed American foreign policy for a generation. It remains one of the defining moral failures of the Cold War.

Cohen applied it directly to Iran: “Trump has created a substantial moral hazard for the United States of a kind not seen since the Hungarian Revolution, in 1956. By encouraging the Iranian people to rise up against a regime that was willing to openly massacre them in the thousands or tens of thousands, it has incurred a profound obligation to see them through this. If such uprisings are attempted and fail, the blood guilt will be American, incurring even more damage to America’s credibility, reputation, and honor than has already been suffered.”

The Iranians who rose in January are already documented. The state killed as many as 30,000 of them. Trump cited that massacre as his red line. He then launched the war. He told the Iranian people their only chance for generations was at hand. He told them to take over their government.

If they try, and the IRGC crushes them — as it crushed the January protesters, as Soviet tanks crushed Budapest — the 1956 question arrives in full: who incited them, and who failed to follow through?

Cohen also named a problem the series has not yet fully documented: the arsenal question. “One may hope, but should not expect, that there are enough precision missiles and interceptors in Israeli and US arsenals to let this war roll on for weeks or longer, and that there are plans for refilling empty bins after some intense fighting.” Weeks of sustained strikes at the intensity of Day One depletes munitions at a rate that neither Israel nor the US has publicly addressed. The planners built in weeks. Whether the weapons to execute those weeks exist in sufficient quantity is a classified question with unclassified consequences.

Cohen’s closing line: “With such uncertainties, a queasy doubt that Trump knows what he is doing is entirely in order.”

He wrote that the night of the strikes. He is a conservative. He is on the record.

The Accountability Record

This series does not celebrate being right. Prescient analysis is worthless if the predictions come true. The point is not that the analysts were smart. The point is that the decision makers had access to the same information and chose to proceed anyway.

General Petraeus warned about Venezuela hubris. Sadjadpour identified the Kissinger asymmetry. Azizi documented that Iranians wanted a scalpel and warned that all-out war would militarize the atmosphere. The internal opposition transition framework was arrested three weeks before the bombs fell. The Voice of America transmitter that was supposed to reach Iranians during exactly this crisis had been shut down for months.

None of this was secret. All of it was published. The New York Times, The Atlantic, academic journals, policy think tanks — the entire analytical infrastructure of the American foreign policy establishment spent months documenting the risks of exactly this operation. The Atlantic’s Shane Harris and colleagues documented that even Trump’s own senior advisers — Vance, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Caine, Elbridge Colby — expressed reservations before the strikes. That the president proceeded anyway is the accountability question.

Trump told confidants that his legacy could be defined by overthrowing the regimes in Venezuela, Iran, and potentially Cuba. He was drawn, The Atlantic reported, to the idea of redrawing global maps and doing what his predecessors could not.

The question the Trump Accountability Project asks is not whether the risks were known. They were. The question is what it means for a president to know the risks, to have them documented in his own intelligence community’s assessments and in the newspapers he reportedly monitors, and to proceed regardless — because the prize is legacy, and legacy is worth the gamble.

That gamble is now being settled in real time. 85 children in Minab. A first Israeli fatality in Tel Aviv. 8 protesters dead outside the US consulate in Karachi. Dubai’s safe haven shattered. The Strait of Hormuz at 30% capacity. An IRGC making private calculations in hardened bunkers.

They told us this could happen. It is happening. The record is clear.
