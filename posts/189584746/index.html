The Iran Crisis

Grand Strategy or Grand Accident?

Part XXV: The China Thesis

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

There is a version of everything you have read in this series in which none of it was recklessness.

Not the tieless speech from Mar-a-Lago. Not the fundraising dinner on Day Two. Not the AM radio transmitter that had to be emergency-restored on the day it was needed most. Not the congressional war powers gap. Not the three Americans dead without a declaration of war. Not the 175 children in Minab.

In this version, all of it — the chaos, the improvisation, the apparent absence of a day-after plan — was either misdirection or irrelevant noise around a strategy so large and so long in its time horizon that the tactical disorder simply didn’t matter. Because the actual target was never Iran.

The actual target was China.

This argument was circulating on social media on Day Two in rough form. By Sunday afternoon it had been picked up and developed by named analysts in Newsweek, independently arriving at the same conclusion with sourced data. It deserves to be taken seriously — and stress-tested.

The China Energy Thesis

Global map of oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz in recent data (pre-2026 escalation), showing dense routes converging on the chokepoint—China as primary destination for Gulf crude, underscoring existential dependency on flows now disrupted (source: EIA/US Energy Information Administration interactive).

The argument has now been articulated by named analysts. Newsweek published it on Sunday under the headline “Trump’s Iran Strike Is a Bigger Play That Also Cuts at China.” The analysts are worth quoting directly because they are not anonymous social media accounts — they are institutional researchers with named affiliations.

Madi Kapparov, Head of Research at the Centre for Information Defence and Strategies in London: “The objective is to choke out the primary source of cheap oil to China.” He noted that China bought over 80% of Iran’s shipped oil in 2025, according to Kpler data, and that Beijing had squeezed down the price it paid for that oil for years precisely because Iran had no other buyers under sanctions. That arrangement is now over.

Ross Babbage, CEO of Strategic Forum and fellow at the Center for Strategic and Budgetary Assessments in Washington: “There appears to be elements of a strategy in the US, and it seems to be making real progress.” He framed Venezuela and Iran as sequential steps in a pattern: “The countries that have been authoritarian strongholds are falling over. The partners that Beijing and Moscow have are reducing in number and in strategic weight.” He predicted Cuba could be next.

Babbage offered the honest caveat the thesis requires: “I’d be the first to say the evidence is scattered. There are some people in the administration who appear to have a fairly cohesive view, but in terms of implementation there are a lot of other factors to take into account.”

The logic of the thesis, assembled from these and other analysts, runs as follows.

China imports over 10 million barrels of oil per day — Bloomberg puts Iran’s share at 13.4% of China’s offshore oil imports. It cannot fuel its factories, its heavy industry, its military, or its rapidly expanding AI infrastructure without continuous external energy supply. Unlike the United States — now effectively energy independent — China is existentially dependent on supply chains it does not control, moving through chokepoints it cannot defend.

The Trump administration, on this reading, set about systematically placing those supply chains under American strategic leverage.

Venezuela first. By isolating and constraining Venezuelan oil flows, Washington disrupted a major external energy channel that China had spent years cultivating. Maduro’s Venezuela had been selling oil to Beijing through back channels that circumvented American sanctions. After Maduro’s removal and Venezuela’s realignment toward Washington, those flows came under American-aligned control. One Chinese energy lifeline compromised.

Iran next. China is the primary buyer of Iran’s seaborne oil exports — over 80% in 2025. Nearly one-fifth of global oil supply moves through the Strait of Hormuz — a single narrow chokepoint that, as of Sunday, is effectively closed, with only Iranian tankers transiting and a sinking Iranian warship on the bottom of the Gulf of Oman at Chah Bahar pier.

In geopolitics, geography is destiny. American and Israeli military dominance over Iran means effective leverage over Hormuz. And Hormuz is the jugular of the Chinese industrial economy. Kapparov makes the military dimension explicit: “A military cannot run on coal or renewables.” Whatever China has invested in energy transition, its armed forces run on oil. Control the oil, and you constrain the military.

The strategic picture, assembled: Venezuelan supply under American-aligned control. Iranian supply disrupted. Middle Eastern flow monitored by overwhelming US naval power. The chokepoint through which one-fifth of the world’s daily oil moves effectively under American leverage — without a single direct confrontation with China.

Pressure without direct war. Energy leverage without conflict. Bloomberg notes that China has been restrained in its response precisely because Xi and Trump have a March meeting coming and Beijing wants to maintain the trade truce. The world’s most powerful authoritarian government is watching its energy jugular threatened and responding with foreign ministry statements — because it has calculated that confrontation costs more than accommodation right now.

One day, historians may write that this was one of the most consequential grand strategies of the 21st century.

Aerial view of the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway linking Persian Gulf oil producers to global markets—now effectively closed to most non-Iranian traffic in March 2026, per Kpler/MarineTraffic data (source: Getty/NYT archive aerial).

Why The Thesis Is Not Crazy

Before stress-testing this argument, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what makes it genuinely compelling.

The China-energy dependency is real and severe. China’s 10+ million barrel daily import requirement is not a talking point — it is a structural fact of its industrial economy. The Strait of Hormuz closure that has been documented across this series is not an abstraction. It is happening. The economic consequence for China — whose factories, whose Belt and Road infrastructure projects, whose military modernization all run on oil it does not produce — is significant and not fully priced.

The Venezuela-Iran sequencing is also real. Whatever the stated justifications for each operation, the effect of both has been to place two of China’s major non-Russian energy suppliers under American strategic pressure within the span of months. That could be coincidence. It could also be design.

Some people in the Trump administration do think in these terms. Elbridge Colby, the Under Secretary of Defense for Policy, has been explicit throughout his career that China is the primary strategic challenge and that American resources should be allocated accordingly. Marco Rubio has a long record of viewing Middle Eastern and Latin American policy through a China competition lens. The NSC is not uniformly composed of Mar-a-Lago loyalists — it contains people who think seriously about 100-year strategic competition.

And the historical precedent this thesis invokes is sound: controlling an adversary’s energy supply lines has been a decisive factor in every major industrial conflict of the 20th century. The American oil embargo on Japan preceded Pearl Harbor. The Allied control of Romanian oil shaped the Eastern Front. Energy geography is strategic geography.

Where The Thesis Breaks Down

The China energy argument is the strongest possible steelman of Trump’s Iran strategy. It deserves to be presented at its strongest before being examined critically. Having done so, here is what the evidence from this series does not support.

The design problem. The China energy thesis requires that the chaos be misdirection or irrelevant noise. But Eliot Cohen — a conservative Republican national security hawk, not a Trump critic from the left — wrote on the night of the strikes that “a queasy doubt that Trump knows what he is doing is entirely in order.” Cohen documented specifically that Trump did not prepare Congress, did not address the nation from the Oval Office, did not work to unify his own base, and antagonized the opposition party that will likely control at least one house after the midterms. A strategist executing a multigenerational China containment play does not leave those flanks exposed. He secures the domestic political architecture first.

The tools problem. If the actual target is China and the actual weapon is energy leverage, the logical preparation would include maximizing the capacity to communicate with the Iranian population — because regime change is more durable leverage than bombing, and a pro-American Iran is infinitely more powerful than a closed Strait. Instead, the Trump administration spent 2025 systematically dismantling Voice of America and nearly shuttering Radio Farda — the Persian-language broadcast service built specifically to reach Iranians through internet blackouts. On the day the war began, the United States had to emergency-restore the AM transmitter it had spent a year trying to close. A strategist who understood that Iran was the key to China’s energy vulnerability would not have dismantled the tools needed to consolidate the Iran play.

The Vahidi problem. If the goal is durable American leverage over Hormuz through regime change in Iran, the appointment of Ahmad Vahidi — Interpol-wanted, AMIA bombing architect, ideological hardliner — as IRGC commander on Day Two is a problem for the thesis. A collapsing regime executing a strategic handoff to American-aligned successors does not appoint the founding commander of the Quds Force to run its military. The Iranian state’s response to Day One suggests an institution fighting for survival, not one arranging a managed transition to a more favorable successor government.

The Netanyahu problem. “What I’ve yearned to be carrying out for 40 years.” That is not the language of a partner executing an American strategic vision. That is the language of a man fulfilling a personal historical mission. If Trump is the 100-year strategist and Netanyahu is the instrument, Netanyahu’s 40-year framing suggests the instrument has its own agenda — which means the strategic coherence of the operation depends on two governments with different ultimate objectives remaining aligned through a prolonged, costly, uncertain military campaign.

The simplest explanation problem. Everything this thesis attributes to grand strategy can be explained more simply. Trump launched the war because he was convinced by Netanyahu and hardliners in his own administration that the moment was right, because the Venezuela success primed him to believe rapid regime change was achievable, because Khamenei was genuinely one of his most hated adversaries, and because the legacy of “redrawing global maps” was irresistible. The China energy leverage is a real consequence of these actions. The question is whether consequence equals intention. History is full of consequential grand strategies that were recognized as such only in retrospect by analysts who worked backward from the outcome.

The Honest Verdict

Grand strategy and catastrophic improvisation are not mutually exclusive. This is the thing the binary framing of the China energy thesis versus the Cohen critique both miss.

It is entirely possible that some people in the Trump administration — Colby, perhaps, or elements of the NSC staff — genuinely understood the China energy leverage dimension and saw this as a generational strategic opportunity. It is simultaneously entirely possible that Trump himself was motivated primarily by personal animus, legacy hunger, and Netanyahu’s 40-year vision, and that the China dimension was a secondary benefit that sophisticated advisers recognized and privately valued without it being the driving logic of the decision.

Outcomes can be strategically significant without being strategically intended. The American oil embargo on Japan in 1941 was not designed to produce Pearl Harbor — it was designed to constrain Japanese expansion. The strategic consequence was catastrophic and generational. The designers were not wrong that energy leverage was powerful. They were wrong about how Japan would respond to it.

The more useful question for this series is not whether Trump is a 100-year strategist or a reckless improviser. It is: what does China do now?

China is watching the Strait close. China is watching its primary Iranian oil supplier’s leadership structure collapse. China is watching the Russia-China-Iran axis’s Iranian partner absorb 500+ strikes and lose its supreme leader. China has publicly condemned the operation and has done nothing beyond statements. Beijing has not moved naval assets. It has not threatened counter-measures. It has not offered Iran anything beyond condolences.

If the China energy thesis is right — if this is a deliberate energy pressure campaign against China — then China’s non-response is the most important data point of the war so far. A power that watches its energy jugular threatened and responds with a foreign ministry statement is either calculating a longer game, or it has concluded that the cost of defending Iran exceeds the benefit, or it has decided that its interests are better served by positioning for the post-war energy architecture than by preventing the war’s outcome.

Any of those conclusions, if correct, would suggest that the China energy thesis — whatever its limitations as a description of Trump’s intentions — may be accurate as a description of the strategic consequence. America may have just demonstrated that it can close China’s most important energy chokepoint, kill China’s most important oil supplier’s head of state, and watch China issue a press release.

If that is what happened, historians will indeed write about it. Whether they write about it as genius or as catastrophic luck will depend on what comes next.

What Would Have To Be True

For the China energy thesis to hold in its strongest form — deliberate, coherent, multigenerational — the following would have to be true in the coming weeks and months.

A successor Iranian government, however constituted, would have to be more amenable to American strategic interests than the one that existed on Friday morning. Not necessarily pro-American — that is too high a bar — but dealable. The Trump back-channel signal (”they want to talk”) would have to produce a framework that includes some form of Hormuz stability under conditions favorable to American naval oversight.

The IRGC under Vahidi would have to be brought to heel by the political leadership — Arafi, Pezeshkian, Larijani — rather than the reverse. An IRGC that continues fighting indefinitely produces the Libya scenario, not the Venezuela scenario, and the Libya scenario does not produce durable energy leverage. It produces a failed state sitting on top of the world’s most important oil chokepoint, which is strategically worse for everyone including the United States.

China would have to make the calculation that resisting American leverage in the Gulf is more costly than accommodating the new energy architecture — and that calculation would have to hold long enough to actually constrain Chinese strategic ambition in the ways this thesis predicts.

None of those outcomes is certain. Some are unlikely. All of them are outside American control, dependent on decisions being made right now in Tehran bunkers, Beijing strategy sessions, and Emirati boardrooms that Washington cannot see or direct.

Grand strategy requires not just the vision to set events in motion. It requires the capacity to control their trajectory. The bombs have been dropped. The Strait is closed. The supreme leader is dead. What happens next will determine whether this was grand strategy or grand accident — and that determination will be made not in Washington, but in the places this series has been documenting since Saturday morning: the dark rooms where the answer is being written, and the world that will live with it either way.
