Let's Talk Texas

The Primary

An Addendum to the Series — March 4, 2026

March 4, 2026

By Stephen Gordon — CanadianSnowmanSays

When we outlined this series two weeks ago, we laid out five threads: the Rehmet earthquake, the Colbert ban, the seminarian’s gambit, the farm team problem, and Talarico vs. Crockett. Last night, three of those threads converged in a single evening — and the results were more dramatic than any of us predicted.

Let’s talk about what happened.

Talarico Wins — and It Wasn’t Close

Eric Gay / AP

James Talarico defeated Jasmine Crockett in the Democratic Senate primary, avoiding a runoff entirely. With 85% of counties reporting, Talarico took 53.1% to Crockett’s 45.6%. Ahmad Hassan collected 1.3%.

Think about what that means. Two weeks ago, the University of Texas had Crockett up by 12 points. UT-Tyler had her up by 21. The Emerson poll that showed Talarico ahead at 52-47 was treated as an outlier. It turned out to be the most accurate survey in the race.

Talarico’s coalition was exactly what he promised. He carried white voters 71-29, Hispanic voters 60-39, voters under 40 by ten points, and voters over 70 by 13. Crockett dominated among Black voters (80-18) and led narrowly among women (51-48). But the number that should make Democrats sit up straight is this: among voters who identify as independent, Talarico won 62-35. In a state where a Democrat hasn’t won statewide since 1988, that number is the whole ballgame.

“Tonight our campaign is shocking the nation,” Talarico told supporters around midnight. “This is proof that there is something happening in Texas.”

He’s not wrong. And the proof didn’t come from nowhere.

The Colbert Effect

Remember Part 2 of this series? The Colbert ban? CBS lawyers pulled Talarico’s interview from the broadcast over FCC fears, the segment went to YouTube instead, Colbert made the censorship itself the story, and the video went viral. We called it a textbook Streisand effect. We said the censorship was the advertisement.

We understated it.

Talarico’s appearance — or rather, his non-appearance on CBS — landed on the first day of early voting. His fundraising surged. His name recognition jumped outside Texas. And among early voters, Talarico led Crockett 58-41. That early vote margin is where the race was decided. Crockett led among Election Day voters 50-39, but by then the math was already against her.

The party of free speech handed a Democratic Senate candidate the most effective campaign ad of the cycle — for free.

The Dallas County Disgrace

Now for the part that should make your blood boil regardless of who you supported.

In Dallas County — Crockett’s home base, the second-most-populous county in the state — hundreds of voters showed up at polling locations on Election Day and were turned away. The reason? The Dallas County Republican Party, led by former Florida congressman Allen West, abandoned countywide vote centers and forced both parties back to precinct-level voting. During early voting, any voter could go to any of 73 locations across the county. On Election Day, you had to know your exact precinct assignment.

The switch was rooted in election conspiracy theories about voting machines. Dallas Republicans originally wanted to hand-count ballots — a process election experts warn leads to errors and delayed results. They ultimately abandoned hand-counting because of the cost, but the precinct-based voting went forward anyway.

The result was chaos. County election “navigators” stationed outside at least 75 polling locations reported redirecting between 50 and 100 percent of arriving voters. The county elections website crashed under the traffic. A Dallas County judge ordered polls open until 9 PM. Then the Texas Supreme Court intervened, blocking the extension and ordering all ballots cast by voters who joined the line after 7 PM to be separated. Those ballots remain in limbo.

The same thing happened in Williamson County, north of Austin — Talarico’s home turf. Same Republican-driven precinct switch. Same voter confusion.

Let me be clear about what this is. Both Dallas and Williamson counties had spent a combined million dollars of taxpayer money trying to inform voters about the change. Democrats had pushed for a joint election. Republicans said no. And the mid-decade redistricting that redrew every district line made the confusion worse — voters who might have known their old precinct had no idea where their new one was.

Crockett’s campaign said she planned to sue. “This may be a very close election, and it may hinge on who was allowed to vote or who wasn’t allowed to vote in Dallas County,” Crockett said. “Regardless as to whether or not it’s close or not, this is wrong.”

She’s right. It is wrong. And it connects directly to Part 1 of this series, to the redistricting thread, and to everything we’ve been documenting in “When Democracy Fights Back.” The party that screams about election integrity created the conditions for election dysfunction — in its own primary.

The GOP Civil War Goes to Overtime

On the Republican side, the primary delivered exactly what the polls predicted: a bruising runoff. Senator John Cornyn finished first with approximately 42% of the vote. Attorney General Ken Paxton took 41%. Congressman Wesley Hunt, the third major candidate, came in at 13%. Nobody cleared the 50% threshold. They’ll fight it out on May 26.

The margin between Cornyn and Paxton is functionally a tie. And the dynamics of a runoff may favor Paxton. As political consultant Bill Miller put it, “The runoff will be the hardcore primary voters, and that’s his base.” Paxton’s pitch — that Cornyn is a soft, out-of-touch career politician — plays better in a low-turnout contest where the most ideologically committed voters dominate.

Cornyn came out swinging, calling Paxton “a flawed, self-centered and shameless candidate.” Paxton predicted he’d win the runoff by 30 points. The race has already burned through nearly $100 million in advertising — the most expensive Senate primary in Texas history and the second most expensive in U.S. history after Arizona’s 2022 contest.

The wild card is Trump, who declined to endorse anyone during the primary, saying he supported “all three.” NBC News reported that Trump is likely to back Cornyn if the race stays close, viewing him as the lower-risk choice for holding the seat. But if Paxton builds a double-digit lead, Trump’s calculus could shift. He doesn’t like endorsing losers.

For Democrats, the Republican civil war is the best possible setup. Cornyn has argued — correctly — that Paxton’s legal baggage (impeachment, federal investigations, a public divorce featuring accusations of infidelity from his ex-wife, state Senator Angela Paxton) could make the seat competitive in November. If Paxton wins the runoff, Talarico’s big-tent pitch becomes significantly more potent.

Bonus: The Crenshaw Casualty

One more result worth noting. In Texas’s 2nd Congressional District, state Representative Steve Toth defeated four-term incumbent Dan Crenshaw by a staggering 57-39 margin. Crenshaw was the only sitting House Republican in Texas who ran without Trump’s endorsement. Ted Cruz endorsed Toth in the final week.

Crenshaw — a Navy SEAL who lost his right eye in Afghanistan — was punished for the sins of occasional independence: supporting Ukraine aid, certifying the 2020 election results, and clashing with the party’s hard-right caucus. In today’s Republican Party, a Purple Heart and a glass eye aren’t enough to survive without a MAGA permission slip.

What It Means

The November preview: James Talarico, 36, a seminarian who won without corporate PAC money, and Ken Paxton, an impeached attorney general who appealed the Dallas County voting extension on his own ballot. (Source: New York Times)

Stand back and look at what happened in a single night in Texas.

A 36-year-old Presbyterian seminarian who flipped a Republican state House seat in 2018, who led a Democratic walkout against gerrymandering, and whose network television interview was killed by corporate lawyers afraid of the FCC — that man just won a statewide Democratic primary in Texas by seven points, without a runoff, while drawing record numbers of young voters, first-time voters, and independents.

His opponent in November will be either a four-term senator hemorrhaging support from his own base, or an impeached attorney general with a federal investigation and a tabloid divorce. Both of them will have spent the spring tearing each other apart.

And the election itself was marred by voter suppression rooted in conspiracy theories — suppression that Republicans inflicted on their own voters as much as anyone else.

“We’re about to take back Texas,” Talarico said.

I’m Canadian. I don’t have a vote in this. But I’ve been watching American politics for a long time, and I know what the first cracks in a dam look like. Texas isn’t turning blue overnight. Nobody serious is saying that. But something is shifting — structurally, demographically, and now electorally.

The Rehmet earthquake was a 31-point swing in a Fort Worth district. The Colbert ban was a Streisand effect that elected a senator. The Dallas County disgrace was the party of election integrity sabotaging its own election. And James Talarico just put himself on the map as the most interesting Democratic candidate in the country.

Let’s keep talking Texas.
