The Counterfactual President

American Diplomacy

Part 2: The State Department Restored

March 27, 2026

Hollowed out by design: Empty seats where ambassadors and career diplomats once represented American interests, symbolizing the gutted State Department in 2025

In the first weeks of 2025, approximately thirty career ambassadors were recalled from their posts simultaneously. Not because of performance failures. Not because of strategic realignment. Because the new administration needed to determine their loyalty before deciding whether they could stay.

Many couldn’t. They were replaced — where they were replaced at all — by political donors, loyalists, and personal associates whose qualification was proximity to Trump rather than knowledge of the regions they would represent. Key posts sat vacant for months. In capitals where crises were developing, where relationships needed tending, where American interests required experienced advocacy — America simply had no one.

This is what the deliberate destruction of diplomatic capacity looks like. Not dramatic. Just empty chairs where expertise used to sit.

To understand why this happened so quickly and so completely, it helps to know that the template was established in the first term. Rex Tillerson gutted the State Department’s budget and workforce in 2017-2018. Mike Pompeo weaponized diplomatic resources for Trump’s political purposes. Career Foreign Service Officers learned what was expected: not expertise, not strategic thinking, not honest analysis — just loyalty.

By the time Trump returned in 2025, the institutional memory of what independent, professional diplomacy looks like had already been damaged. The second term finished what the first term started.

The message to anyone who remained in the Foreign Service became crystal clear over twelve months: Your expertise doesn’t matter. Your relationships don’t matter. Your understanding of complex regional dynamics doesn’t matter. Only your willingness to defend whatever Trump decides — including threats against Canada, Denmark, and Panama — matters.

This is what we watched American diplomacy become over the course of a year.

But there’s a counterfactual. One where the State Department was actually allowed to do its job for those twelve months. Where expertise was valued rather than purged. Where alliances were strengthened rather than extorted. Where American leadership meant something other than chaos, betrayal, and territorial aggression against friends.

That’s not idealism. That’s how American diplomacy functioned for 75 years — building the postwar order, containing Soviet expansion, creating prosperity through cooperation, leading the democratic world.

Let’s examine what that alternative first year would have looked like.

The Harry S. Truman Building, headquarters of the State Department—once the hub of professional diplomacy, now emblematic of institutional decline and purge under the real timeline. Source: DC Historic Sites

The Diplomatic Corpse: What Year One Produced

Start with what we actually watched during Trump’s first year back:

Embassy staffing remained gutted. Ambassadorships stayed unfilled or went to political donors throughout the year. America lacked diplomatic representation in key capitals precisely when representation mattered most.

Career expertise was systematically dismissed. Foreign Service Officers who’d spent decades building relationships, understanding cultures, mastering languages — pushed out or ignored over twelve months. Their replacements: political appointees whose qualification was loyalty to Trump, not knowledge of the regions they represented.

Policy coherence was nonexistent. Trump announced major policy shifts via social media without consulting State Department professionals throughout the year. Allies learned about American positions from Truth Social rather than through diplomatic channels. Strategic planning was impossible when the President reversed course based on Fox News segments.

Alliances were treated as protection rackets. NATO members faced constant threats of abandonment throughout the year. Japan and South Korea received demands for increased cost-sharing. Trade relationships were weaponized for political gain month after month.

Adversaries were emboldened. Putin received deference all year. Xi received flattery. Kim Jong Un maintained legitimacy through continued engagement without substance. Meanwhile, democratic allies were insulted, threatened, and treated as adversaries — or worse.

This wasn’t incompetence. It was intentional destruction sustained across twelve months. Trump views diplomacy as weakness and transactional relationships as strength. He doesn’t want a functioning State Department — he wants personal control over foreign policy, unconstrained by institutional knowledge, strategic thinking, or basic respect for allied sovereignty.

The Territorial Threat: When Allies Became Targets

Something unprecedented happened during Trump’s first year back that demands its own examination — because no previous American president, Democrat or Republican, hawk or dove, isolationist or interventionist, ever did this:

He threatened sovereign democratic allies with territorial annexation.

Trump threatened to seize Greenland from Denmark — a NATO member — by “economic force or military force.” He threatened to retake the Panama Canal, built by American labor but ceded to Panama through solemn treaty in 1999. And most shockingly, most personally for me as a Canadian writing from Toronto: he repeatedly, insistently, and without apparent irony declared that Canada should become the 51st state of the United States.

Not as a joke. Not as idle bluster. As a stated policy position, reinforced month after month by tariff warfare, economic coercion, and consistent public messaging. When pressed on whether he would rule out military or economic force against Canada, he declined to rule it out.

Let me be precise about what this means. The United States threatening to absorb Canada — your largest trading partner, your closest ally, the nation with which you share NORAD and the longest undefended border in the world, the country that has fought alongside you in every major conflict of the past century — is not diplomacy. It is aggression.

A moment in time, Carney meets Trump in the Oval Office May 6, 2025 AP

The same aggression Trump publicly condemned Putin for in Ukraine — violating sovereign borders, threatening territorial absorption by force — was now directed against democratic allies.

This created a dissonance that no diplomat could paper over. How do you maintain an alliance with a nation whose president has declared his intention to annex you? How do you coordinate on defense with a partner who treats your sovereignty as negotiable? How do you plan trade relationships when your closest neighbor views USMCA/CUSMA not as a binding agreement but as leverage to be discarded?

Canada’s response during Trump’s first year back was a masterclass in dignified resistance: Prime Ministers refusing to take the bait publicly while privately recalibrating every assumption about American partnership. Trade diversification accelerating toward Europe. Military procurement shifting toward European suppliers. The political consensus in Canada — left, right, and center — hardened around a single principle: Canadian sovereignty is not negotiable.

But the damage to the diplomatic relationship was profound and will outlast Trump’s presidency. Trust, once broken at this fundamental level, does not restore itself between election cycles.

Would Kamala Harris have threatened to annex Greenland? Would she have called Canada the 51st state? Would she have suggested economic or military coercion against Panama?

The question answers itself. The counterfactual here isn’t about policy nuance or competing approaches to burden-sharing. It’s about whether America’s president respects the sovereignty of democratic allies or treats them as territories to be absorbed. Under Harris, Canada, Denmark, and Panama would have been partners. Under Trump, they became targets.

The Counterfactual: What Year One Under Harris Would Have Looked Like

President Harris would likely have appointed someone like Wendy Sherman or William Burns as Secretary of State — career diplomats who understand that American power depends on credibility, that alliances multiply strength, that expertise matters.

What would that first year have looked like?

Embassies would have been staffed during year one. Ambassadorships filled by qualified diplomats — career professionals in complex posts, political appointees only in friendly capitals. America would have had actual representation in the world throughout those twelve months.

Career Foreign Service Officers would have been empowered from day one. The hundreds who left or were pushed out would have been welcomed back. Their expertise would have informed policy rather than being dismissed.

Policy would have been coherent and strategic throughout the year. Major announcements coordinated with allies, planned through diplomatic channels, designed to achieve specific objectives. Allied governments wouldn’t have learned about American positions through social media.

Alliances would have been valued, not extorted — and certainly not threatened with annexation. NATO Article 5 would have been reaffirmed without conditions. Canada would have been treated as a partner, not a colony in waiting.

But Harris would have faced significant challenges throughout her first year: Rebuilding diplomatic credibility after Trump would have taken the entire year and beyond. Allies burned once would have remained cautious. International partners would have hedged their commitments, uncertain whether the next election would bring another Trump. And Harris would have faced Republican opposition to every diplomatic initiative, with Senate Republicans potentially blocking ambassadorial appointments and treaty ratifications out of pure obstruction.

This isn’t revolutionary. It’s how American diplomacy worked under every president from Truman through Obama. It’s normalcy, not utopianism.

And we spent a year watching what abnormal looks like.

NATO: What Twelve Months of Uncertainty Produced

Let me be blunt about what happened to NATO during Trump’s first year back: He spent twelve months trying to destroy it.

Not through formal withdrawal — too complicated legally. But through systematic sabotage: questioning Article 5 commitments repeatedly, encouraging Putin’s aggression, suggesting America might not defend allies under attack, demanding impossible financial contributions, and — crucially — demonstrating through the Greenland and Canada threats that America now views even its closest allies as potential targets rather than partners.

European allies spent the year conducting urgent conversations about what security means without reliable American commitment. They increased defense spending not because Trump bullied them into burden-sharing, but because they couldn’t count on American protection. They explored European defense integration as insurance against American abandonment — not just abandonment through inaction, but potentially abandonment through active hostility.

The Nordic nations were particularly shaken. When America threatens to seize Greenland by force from Denmark — a NATO founding member — every other NATO member is forced to ask: Are we next? Is NATO a collective defense arrangement or a vehicle for American expansion?

These questions hadn’t been asked since NATO’s founding. Trump made them necessary again.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, NATO Article 5 would have been unequivocal from day one. No conditions. No extortion. No uncertainty. No threats against member states’ territory. Alliance burden-sharing would have been addressed through traditional diplomatic channels — coordination, negotiation, mutual accommodation.

The result after one year: A stronger NATO, more confident European allies, clearer deterrence against Russian aggression, maintained American leadership.

This isn’t about spending more money. It’s about maintaining the structure that has prevented great power war for 75 years. That structure spent a year fracturing under Trump. It would have been reinforced under Harris.

Ukraine: What a Year of Betrayal Produced

President Donald Trump and Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy during their confrontational Oval Office meeting at the White House, February 28, 2025. Photo credit: Saul Loeb / AFP via Getty Images

Trump’s approach to Ukraine during his first year back was exactly what he promised: systematic betrayal in service of a deal with Putin that rewards Russian aggression.

Military assistance was cut or conditioned throughout the year. Ukrainian defense needs mattered less than Trump’s relationship with Putin. Professional military assessments were overridden by political considerations month after month. The administration pushed Kyiv toward negotiations on terms that required ceding significant territory — essentially demanding that Ukraine ratify Russian conquest.

The message to Ukraine and allies became clear: Don’t count on American military commitments. And beyond that: Democratic resistance to authoritarian aggression will eventually be abandoned by its democratic sponsors.

This affected more than Ukraine throughout the year. Every nation depending on American security guarantees spent those twelve months recalculating. If America abandons Ukraine to please Putin, if America threatens to absorb Canada, if America treats allies as either vassals or targets — what does an American security guarantee actually mean?

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, American support for Ukraine would have continued based on strategic logic: Allowing Russian territorial conquest by force undermines international order. Supporting Ukrainian defense is cheaper than fighting the wars that follow if aggression succeeds.

Harris would have faced enormous competing pressures throughout that first year: growing progressive opposition, burden-sharing tensions with European allies, Republican accusations of endless war, Ukrainian demands for more weapons systems. None of that would have been easy. But there is a categorical difference between the difficulty of sustaining a defensive alliance and the moral catastrophe of abandoning one.

We spent a year watching the moral catastrophe.

China: What a Year of Chaos Produced

Trump’s China policy during his first year back was the incoherent chaos we expected: trade wars announced via tweet, tariffs imposed then suspended then reimposed, personal flattery of Xi alternating with accusations, policy driven by whether Chinese decisions benefited Trump businesses.

Meanwhile, China advanced systematically throughout the year. Belt and Road expanding. Military capabilities growing. Technology leadership advancing. Relationships strengthening with countries exhausted by American volatility.

Asian allies experienced impossible strategic choices all year. They needed American partnership to balance Chinese power. But American policy was chaos. They hedged constantly — maintaining relationships with both powers, reducing trust in American leadership, quietly preparing for a world where America is less dominant.

The counterfactual: Harris’s first year would have maintained the strategic competition framework — compete where necessary, cooperate where possible, coordinate with allies, invest in American competitiveness. Consistent policy. Systematic technology competition. Predictable military presence.

Not perfect — China remains formidable. But strategy beats improvisation over twelve months. Allied coordination beats solo flailing.

Trade: What a Year of Economic Warfare Against Canada Produced

As a Canadian, I need to be specific about what Trump’s trade policy meant during his first year back, because it moved from bad policy into something closer to economic warfare against a democratic neighbor being told it should cease to exist as an independent nation.

The tariffs came. They were substantial. They were designed not to address trade imbalances or protect American industries — the economic justifications shifted weekly — but to create sufficient economic pain that Canada would capitulate to whatever Trump demanded next.

$2 billion crosses the Canada-US border daily. Our economies are integrated at every level — auto parts manufactured across multiple border crossings, energy systems interconnected, agricultural supply chains intertwined. Tariff warfare against Canada doesn’t just hurt Canadians. It hurts American workers, American businesses, American consumers.

But the economic damage, significant as it is, isn’t the deepest wound. The deepest wound is what it revealed about how Trump views relationships: as opportunities for extraction rather than foundations for mutual benefit.

Canadian businesses spent the year factoring “Trump risk” into every American investment decision. Canadian politicians who had spent careers building closer North American integration found themselves instead building European alternatives. Canadian military planners who had assumed continental defense cooperation now had to consider scenarios their predecessors never imagined.

The counterfactual is almost painfully simple: Under Harris, Canada is a partner. USMCA is honored. Disputes are resolved through agreed mechanisms. The relationship returns to the productive normalcy that serves both nations.

That’s not utopia. That’s Tuesday in a functional alliance.

And we spent a year watching economic warfare instead.

Climate: What a Year of Sabotage Produced

Activists participate in the Stand Up for Science 2025 rally at the Lincoln Memorial on March 7, 2025, in Washington, D.C. Stand Up for Science, a grassroots organization, held the rally calling on “policymakers, institutions, and the scientific community to uphold the integrity of science, protect its accessibility, and ensure its benefits serve all people.” (Image credit: Alex Wong/Getty Images)

Trump withdrew from the Paris Agreement again, gutted environmental regulations, promoted fossil fuel production, and mocked climate science throughout his first year back.

What international climate negotiators experienced over twelve months: American sabotage made global goals harder to reach. American refusal of technology sharing meant developing nations couldn’t transition as quickly. American opposition to climate finance eliminated adaptation funding. American denial encouraged denial globally.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, America would have led climate action. Paris Agreement honored and strengthened. Clean energy technology developed and shared. Climate finance provided. Adaptation investments protecting vulnerable populations.

For Canada specifically: our climate action was undermined throughout Trump’s first year back. We needed American partnership on Great Lakes management, forest fire response, Arctic policy, continental energy grid integration. Trump made that partnership impossible for twelve months. Harris would have made it functional.

The Democratic Coalition: What a Year of American Collapse Taught

For decades, America inspired democratic movements worldwide. American success demonstrated that democratic governance could produce prosperity, freedom, and stability.

What we watched die during year one: When the world watched American democracy collapse further over twelve months — coup attempts pardoned, institutions captured, rule of law abandoned, allies threatened with annexation — the message to global democrats was complete: Democracy doesn’t work. Even in America, democracy produces authoritarianism and imperial aggression against neighbors.

The demoralization spread throughout the year. America was proof that democracy succeeds. After twelve months back under Trump, America was proof that democracy either fails — or transforms into something that resembles the authoritarianism it claimed to oppose.

The counterfactual: Harris’s first year would have demonstrated that January 6th was a failed coup, not a successful revolution. That democratic resistance worked. That institutions held. That the fever broke.

Instead, Trump’s first year back proved the opposite: Authoritarians win. Coups need only wait. Democratic resistance fails eventually.

And we spent a year proving that to the world.

What My Family History Makes Me Mourn After This Year

Hugh Llewellyn Keenleyside (7 July 1898 – September 27, 1992) Wikipedia

My relative Hugh Keenleyside served alongside Lester Pearson in Canada's Department of External Affairs and played a senior role in Canadian-American wartime diplomacy during World War II — serving as Canadian Secretary of the Permanent Joint Board on Defence and as a key advisor to Prime Minister Mackenzie King during the Churchill-Roosevelt negotiations

In the 1940s, American leadership tipped scales toward democracy. The postwar order, NATO, Marshall Plan — imperfect institutions that created prosperity, prevented war, enabled democratic expansion.

What I watched this past year: American leadership tipping scales toward authoritarianism and imperial aggression. Putin emboldened. Xi empowered. Democratic allies threatened. The institutions America built weakening because America no longer supported them — and in some cases actively undermining the sovereignty those institutions were designed to protect.

The men who built the postwar order understood something Trump does not: American power is most durable when it is exercised in service of shared principles rather than naked self-interest. When America leads through respect for sovereignty, it builds the coalitions that amplify its power. When America threatens allied sovereignty, it drives those allies toward the alternatives that diminish American influence.

Keenleyside’s generation built something remarkable. Trump spent a year dismantling it.

The counterfactual asks: What would allied leaders have felt during Harris’s first year if American leadership returned? If commitments were reliable? If sovereignty was respected? If strategy replaced chaos?

The answer from every democratic capital: Relief. Hope. Renewed confidence.

Instead, we spent a year watching the opposite.

The Quiet Conversations in Ottawa Throughout the Year

Canadian security policy for 75 years assumed American partnership: NORAD, NATO, integrated continental defense.

Trump made that assumption invalid over twelve months. We were forced to consider constantly: What if America abandons NATO? What if American elections produce chaos? What if partnerships with America create vulnerability? What if America itself becomes the threat we need to plan for?

That last question — what if America becomes the threat — was not asked quietly or theoretically. It was asked in strategic planning sessions. It was asked when a sitting American president refused to rule out economic or military coercion against Canadian sovereignty. It was asked when we calculated whether NORAD, which requires sharing our airspace and defense systems with the United States, remained an arrangement that served Canadian security.

These questions devastated Canadian strategy throughout the year. We’re not large enough for full military independence. We depend on American partnership. But if that partnership is unreliable — or worse, if the partner is hostile — we need alternatives we can barely afford to imagine.

In the counterfactual: These conversations don’t happen during Harris’s first year. Her election would have signaled: American commitments are reliable. Canadian sovereignty is respected. Alliance partnerships continue. The 51st state rhetoric disappears because the person generating it is gone.

The relief in Ottawa — and every allied capital — would have been profound.

Instead, we spent twelve months in strategic crisis planning and national sovereignty defense.

The Exhaustion of a Year of Contingency Planning

Here’s what killed allied strategists during Trump’s first year back: endless contingency planning for American unreliability — and now, for American hostility.

Military planners had to consider all year: What if America withdraws from NATO? What if America pressures us to cede territory? Diplomats planned constantly: What if American policy reverses tomorrow? Economic ministers factored monthly: What if Trump imposes tariffs without warning?

This exhaustion was universal across democratic allies throughout the year. We all wanted American leadership. We all benefited from American partnership. We all desperately wanted to trust American commitments.

But Trump made trust impossible for twelve months — and replaced it with something worse than uncertainty: the knowledge that alliance with America now comes with the risk of being declared the next target.

The counterfactual: Under Harris’s first year, there would have been no endless contingency planning. American commitments would have been reliable. Policy would have been predictable. Partnerships would have functioned normally. No allied leader would have spent a moment considering whether America was about to claim their territory.

That’s not utopia. That’s basic alliance function. It’s how relationships worked for 75 years.

And we spent a year mourning its loss — and defending against its replacement.
