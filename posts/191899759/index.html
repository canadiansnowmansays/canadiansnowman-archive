The Exception Clause

The Ship That Was Turned Away

Part IV: How the United States Turned Away 937 Jewish Refugees and Called It Policy

April 8, 2026

On Board The SS St. Louis

On May 13, 1939, the MS St. Louis departed Hamburg, Germany, carrying 937 passengers. All but a handful were Jewish. Most had been waiting for years for American immigration visas — waiting through the escalating persecution of Germany’s Jews that had accelerated since the Nuremberg Laws of 1935 stripped them of citizenship, since Kristallnacht in November 1938 had destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues and sent thirty thousand Jewish men to concentration camps. They had Cuban landing certificates, purchased at considerable expense, that were supposed to allow them temporary refuge while their American visa applications were processed. They were not fleeing a rumor. They were fleeing a documented, systematic, escalating campaign of state violence that the world’s governments had observed for six years.

The MS St. Louis arrived in Havana harbor on May 27. Cuban authorities, responding to political pressure from domestic groups hostile to Jewish immigration, refused to honor the landing certificates for all but twenty-eight passengers. The ship waited in Havana harbor for days while its passengers’ representatives negotiated desperately with Cuban officials, with the American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee, with anyone who might intervene. The negotiations failed.

The captain, Gustav Schroeder, sailed the ship toward the Florida coast. He came close enough that passengers could see the lights of Miami. He sent appeals to President Franklin Roosevelt. Roosevelt did not respond. The State Department informed the passengers that they would have to wait their turn on the German quota, which was years from being cleared.

The St. Louis sailed back to Europe. Four countries — Britain, France, Belgium, and the Netherlands — agreed to accept its passengers. Within a year, Germany had occupied France, Belgium, and the Netherlands. Of the 937 passengers who had sailed from Hamburg, 254 were killed in the Holocaust.

The Architecture of Exclusion

The turning away of the MS St. Louis was not an accident or an oversight. It was the product of an immigration system that had been deliberately constructed, over the preceding two decades, to minimize Jewish immigration to the United States — constructed through a combination of legislation, administrative practice, and institutional culture at the State Department that operated with quiet, bureaucratic efficiency to ensure that the legal mechanisms available to refugees were as difficult to navigate as possible.

The Immigration Act of 1924 — the Johnson-Reed Act — established national origin quotas that severely restricted immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, the regions where the largest concentrations of Jewish populations lived. The quota for Germany was set at approximately 25,000 per year. It sounds substantial until you note that the State Department, through the 1930s, consistently used less than half of the available quota — in some years, substantially less — by applying its discretionary authority to find grounds to deny applications, by requiring documentation that refugees fleeing persecution could not possibly obtain, and by invoking a provision of immigration law that allowed consular officers to deny visas to anyone “likely to become a public charge.”

The likely-to-become-a-public-charge provision was applied with particular thoroughness to Jewish applicants during the Depression years, on the reasoning that refugees fleeing with few assets were financial risks. It was applied even to applicants who had documented financial sponsors in the United States. It was applied in ways that made the denial rate for Jewish applicants from Germany and Austria substantially higher than the rate for other applicants from the same countries.

This was not rogue behavior by individual consular officers. It was policy, encouraged and sustained by Breckinridge Long, the Assistant Secretary of State responsible for the visa division from 1940 to 1944. Long was, by the documentary record, an antisemite who regarded Jewish immigration as a threat to the United States and who used his administrative authority systematically to obstruct the processing of refugee applications. He testified to Congress in 1943 — falsely — that the United States had admitted 580,000 refugees since 1933, a figure that overstated actual admissions by a factor of several times. His testimony was used to defeat proposed legislation that would have created a specific refugee rescue program.

The State Department’s obstruction was the bureaucratic expression of a broader cultural reality: that American antisemitism in the 1930s and 1940s, while rarely manifesting in the open violence of European models, had constructed an institutional architecture of exclusion that was, in its practical effect, lethal.

The American Flavor

American antisemitism has a specific character that distinguishes it from its European variants — a character that makes it harder to see clearly and therefore harder to address.

European antisemitism, in its most virulent forms, was eliminationist: it sought the removal or destruction of Jewish populations as an explicit goal. American antisemitism, with exceptions, was exclusionary: it sought to limit Jewish participation in the institutions of public and civic life without necessarily seeking physical harm. It operated through quotas rather than pogroms, through gentlemen’s agreements rather than laws, through the unwritten understanding of who belonged at a particular club, in a particular firm, at a particular university, in a particular neighborhood.

Harvard University implemented a formal quota limiting Jewish enrollment in 1922, responding to the increase in Jewish students that had resulted from the university’s meritocratic admissions policies. President Abbott Lawrence Lowell argued explicitly that too many Jewish students would damage Harvard’s character and drive away the Protestant elite families whose sons the university primarily served. Columbia, Yale, Princeton, and most other elite institutions followed with their own informal or formal quota systems. The quotas persisted, with varying degrees of rigidity, into the 1950s.

Elite law firms maintained their own exclusions. The major Wall Street firms did not hire Jewish lawyers — or hired them only in subordinate positions — well into the postwar period. Jewish lawyers founded their own firms, which became, in several cases, among the most distinguished in the country. The exclusion produced a parallel institutional structure that is sometimes misread as evidence that antisemitism was not a serious barrier. It was evidence that Jewish Americans built around the barrier because they had no alternative.

Father Charles Coughlin delivers a radio broadcast. Detroit, United States, March 11, 1935. | Credit: US Holocaust Memorial Museum

Father Charles Coughlin broadcast antisemitic content to an audience of thirty million American radio listeners in the late 1930s, explicitly defending Kristallnacht and distributing content drawn directly from Nazi propaganda sources. He was not a fringe figure. He was the most listened-to radio broadcaster in the United States. The Catholic hierarchy eventually silenced him in 1942, under pressure from the Roosevelt administration during wartime. Before that, he had operated for years with institutional tolerance.

Henry Ford published The International Jew in four volumes between 1920 and 1922, drawing on the fabricated Protocols of the Elders of Zion to argue that Jewish financiers controlled American and global institutions. The series was reprinted in Germany. Adolf Hitler kept a copy on his desk and cited Ford approvingly in Mein Kampf. Ford received the Grand Cross of the German Eagle, Nazi Germany’s highest honor for foreigners, in 1938, on his seventy-fifth birthday.

Ford was not prosecuted. He was not significantly sanctioned. He was one of the most celebrated industrialists in American history, the creator of the assembly line, the man who had put America on wheels. His antisemitism was known, documented, and largely tolerated — not because it was approved of by everyone, but because the mechanism for withdrawing permission for it did not exist or was not deployed.

The St. Louis and the Exception Clause

The MS St. Louis episode is, for the purposes of this series, the clearest single illustration of what happens when the exception clause and the bureaucratic architecture of exclusion operate in combination with catastrophic external circumstances.

The passengers of the St. Louis were not asking for special treatment. They were asking for the application of existing immigration law — for their quota numbers to be processed, for their legal right to seek refuge to be honored. They were denied not by an explicit decision that they should die — no American official made that calculation openly — but by the accumulated effect of a system designed to keep them out, administered by officials who had spent a decade ensuring that the available legal mechanisms were as difficult to navigate as possible, and presided over by a president who chose not to intervene.

Of the 937 passengers who sailed from Hamburg, 254 were killed in the Holocaust after Germany occupied the European countries that had accepted them. They did not ask America to go to war. They asked it to process their visa applications.

Roosevelt’s silence on the St. Louis is the hardest part of the story for admirers of the New Deal to absorb. Roosevelt was not an antisemite in the Ford or Coughlin mode. He had Jewish friends, Jewish advisors, Jewish members of his cabinet. He was aware of what was happening in Germany. He was also aware that polls consistently showed that a majority of Americans opposed increasing immigration to help Jewish refugees, that any move to admit the St. Louis’s passengers outside the quota system would be politically costly, and that his primary foreign policy objective in 1939 was to keep the United States out of a war that he feared the American public would not support entering.

He made a political calculation. The calculation was rational given his constraints. Its consequence — for 254 people who were returned to Europe and subsequently killed — was lethal.

This is the fifth element of the template at its starkest: the permission to exclude, to obstruct, to turn away, came from the top — not through explicit order, but through the choice not to act, not to respond, not to override the system that was doing the work of exclusion on his behalf.

The Fifth Element does not require malice. It requires only inaction at the moment when action was the only thing that could interrupt the machine.

What Survived the War

The Holocaust did not end American antisemitism. It changed its expression.

The postwar period brought the gradual dismantling of the most overt institutional exclusions — the university quotas loosened through the 1950s and 1960s, the restricted neighborhoods and clubs began to open, the formal barriers to professional advancement came down. Jewish Americans moved into the mainstream of American public life with a speed and completeness that had no parallel in the history of any other previously excluded group.

But the institutional antisemitism that had made the St. Louis episode possible did not simply evaporate. It transformed. The explicit quota became the implicit standard. The formal exclusion became the glass ceiling. The gentlemen’s agreement against Jewish neighbors became the realtor’s steering practice. These forms were less lethal than the ones that had preceded them, and less lethal than the contemporary versions of the template being applied to other communities. But they were continuous with what had come before — the same mechanism operating with reduced voltage.

The deeper legacy of the St. Louis is not the specific policy failure. It is the demonstration that a society can observe, in real time, the persecution of a religious and ethnic minority, and can choose — through inaction, through bureaucratic obstruction, through political calculation — to do nothing that would meaningfully interrupt it. The 937 passengers of the St. Louis did not ask the United States to go to war. They asked it to process their visa applications. The system that had been built, quietly and over decades, to prevent exactly that processing worked as designed.

That is what institutional antisemitism looks like at the moment of its maximum consequence. Not a rally, not a pogrom, not a burning building. A ship, turned away at sea, its passengers visible from the shore.

A Note on This Chapter’s Scope

This essay has examined a specific, bounded episode — the MS St. Louis and the bureaucratic architecture that produced it — rather than attempting to survey American antisemitism comprehensively. That choice is deliberate.

American antisemitism is too large and too complex a subject to be adequately addressed in a single essay in a series that is examining a broader pattern. Jewish Americans have experienced every form of persecution this series documents: theological exclusion, racial targeting, institutional discrimination, political violence, and the surveillance and suspicion that comes with being identified as a community whose loyalty is in question. The full history deserves its own series.

What the St. Louis episode provides, in the context of this series, is a precise illustration of the exception clause operating at the level of life and death — of the fine print in the American promise producing, through inaction and institutional obstruction, a direct contribution to the deaths of hundreds of people who had asked for nothing more than what the law said they were entitled to.

The asterisk, in 1939, was lethal.

It was lethal without anyone giving an order that it be so.

That is the most important thing this chapter has to say.
