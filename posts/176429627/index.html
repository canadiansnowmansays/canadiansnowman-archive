Digital Tribalism (2000s-2010s)

From Polite Silences to Screaming Matches

October 17, 2025

That polite fiction I witnessed as a wide-eyed teen on our 1967 band tour—sitting in a Greenwood, Mississippi home, listening to a white mother describe her Black housekeeper as “part of the family”? Our Canadian chaperones had warned us not to discuss race during stops in Cincinnati, Memphis, Greenwood, and New Orleans. It was a directive that speaks volumes about how Americans once managed their deepest divisions.

This scene, from the hometown of Bobbie Gentry as “Ode to Billie Joe” topped the charts, captures a lost art: maintaining surface civility while navigating profound disagreement. The white family’s “part of the family” narrative was, of course, a comfortable fiction that masked systemic inequality. But it was also a social mechanism that allowed daily life to function in a deeply divided society.

Canadian teenagers were warned away from the topic entirely. Americans, meanwhile, had developed elaborate codes, euphemisms, and polite fictions to discuss race without discussing it. These weren’t solutions—they were bandages over infected wounds—but they prevented constant confrontation. Digital tools did empower voices long silenced, like #MeToo or BLM—but at the cost of nuance.

The Digital Destruction of Polite Fictions

Fast forward to the 2000s. Those careful social mechanisms that allowed a Canadian teenager and a Mississippi family to have a civil, if deeply problematic, conversation about race? Digital media obliterated them.

Facebook launched in 2004. Twitter in 2006. YouTube in 2005. Suddenly, every American had a platform and an audience. The polite silences, geographic distance, and social barriers that had contained American divisions for decades evaporated.

That Mississippi family’s “part of the family” narrative? It would now be eviscerated in real-time by thousands of Twitter users explaining how it perpetuated racist power structures. The Canadian teenager’s culture shock? It would be livestreamed, hashtagged, and transformed into content.

Algorithms of Division

Social media platforms discovered a dark truth: anger drives engagement. Facebook’s algorithm, refined through billions of interactions, learned to surface content that provoked strong emotions. A post about racial injustice that might have prompted quiet reflection in 1967 now triggered cascade effects:

  • Original post gets angry reactions

  • Algorithm shows it to more people

  • Comments escalate

  • Post goes viral

  • Tribal lines harden

Unlike that controlled conversation in Greenwood, digital discussions had no natural boundaries. A discussion between neighbors became a battleground for millions.

The Death of Gatekeepers

In 1967, American media was controlled by a few networks and newspapers. These gatekeepers, whatever their flaws, enforced certain norms. Walter Cronkite wouldn’t amplify every extreme voice or conspiracy theory.

Digital media democratized information—but also disinformation. The same platforms that allowed Black Lives Matter to document police violence also let white supremacists recruit and organize. QAnon could never have existed in the three-network era.

From Local to National

That Mississippi family’s racial attitudes were once a local matter, discussed in hushed tones or careful euphemisms. Social media nationalized every local conflict. A racist incident in a Georgia diner could trend nationwide within hours. A progressive professor’s comment at an Oregon college could become Fox News fodder for weeks.

Americans lost the ability to have contained disagreements. Every conflict became totalizing, with millions choosing sides based on incomplete information and tribal loyalty.

The Monetization of Outrage

Traditional media adapted to the digital ecosystem by embracing outrage. Cable news became Twitter in real-time, with hosts reading tweets on air and crafting segments to go viral. “Owning the libs” or “destroying MAGA” became business models.

Thoughtful voices were drowned out by whoever could generate the most engagement. A Canadian watching American media would see a country in constant crisis, because crisis sells ads and drives clicks. And let’s be real, Canada’s not immune—our Twitter pile-ons during the Freedom Convoy showed how fast algorithms can turn polite neighbors into keyboard warriors.

Echo Chambers and Filter Bubbles

By 2010, Americans inhabited completely different information universes. Conservatives had Fox News, Breitbart, talk radio, and Facebook groups sharing increasingly extreme content. Progressives had MSNBC, Vox, podcast networks, and Twitter communities enforcing ever-stricter ideological purity.

That 1967 conversation in Greenwood required both parties to inhabit the same physical space and maintain social courtesy. Online, Americans could simply block, mute, or unfriend anyone who challenged their worldview.

The Pandemic Effect

COVID-19 supercharged digital tribalism. Locked at home, Americans lived online. Every aspect of pandemic response—masks, vaccines, school closures—became tribal markers. “Following the science” meant different things in different tribes.

The Canadian teenager from 1967 witnessed polite fictions masking deep divisions. By 2020, Americans had abandoned even the pretense of shared reality. They couldn’t agree on basic facts about disease transmission, election results, or whether January 6th was a tourist visit or insurrection.

The Lost Art of Productive Discomfort

That conversation in Greenwood was undoubtedly uncomfortable. The racial dynamics were problematic, the power imbalances real. But it was also human—two people navigating difficult terrain with some attempt at courtesy and understanding.

Digital media stripped away the humanity. Behind screens, Americans forgot they were talking to real people with complex lives and motivations. The Mississippi mother became a caricature—either a racist villain or a misunderstood traditionalist, depending on your tribe.

The teenager was instructed to avoid discussing race entirely—an imperfect solution that maintained surface peace while leaving injustice unchallenged. Digital America swung to the opposite extreme: constant confrontation that generates heat but little light.

It makes me think of my American cousins—we have millions of relatives across our borders. Growing up, we’d visit back and forth, and as kids we’d joke about how “pretentious” they seemed (all in good family fun, of course). But we were kin, all the same. No partisan thoughts divided us at the dinner table. That easy familiarity between Canadian and American families, built on shared BBQs and summer vacations, feels increasingly fragile in the digital age. Would those same cousins unfollow each other on Facebook today over political posts? The algorithms don’t care about family bonds—they care about engagement.

The question facing both our countries: If you’re reading this on your phone, ask yourself: What’s one conversation you’ve muted lately? Unmute it.

Thoughts for my Next Essay

But these digital divisions don’t exist in a vacuum. They feed on real pain—economic pain. While Americans sorted themselves into online tribes, the ground beneath their feet was shifting. Good jobs disappeared, entire regions hollowed out, and the American Dream became a luxury item. The same algorithms amplifying cultural warfare were about to meet economic desperation, creating a combustible mix that would reshape American politics forever...
