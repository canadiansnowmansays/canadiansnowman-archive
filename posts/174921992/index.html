The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 3 - What About the Left?

The Other Side of the Coin and Final Verdict

October 2, 2025

In Part 1, we established what fascism actually is based on how Mussolini created it and Hitler perfected it. In Part 2, we measured Trump and the MAGA movement against the ten defining characteristics of historical fascism and found that they align substantially with six characteristics completely, three substantially, and one partially.

Now we need to apply the same rigorous standard to the contemporary American left. If we’re going to call Trump fascistic, we need to honestly assess whether the left shows similar tendencies. This is where intellectual honesty matters most—we can’t use a harsh standard for one side and a generous standard for the other.

What About the Left?

Let’s apply the same ten characteristics to progressive politics and the Democratic Party.

1. Ultranationalism and “Blood and Soil” Ideology

The left does not exhibit this characteristic. Progressive politics is generally internationalist, supportive of international cooperation, critical of American exceptionalism, and explicitly opposed to defining American identity in ethnic or racial terms. The left’s vision of American identity is civic and inclusive—based on shared values rather than shared blood or heritage.

Where the right says “real Americans” look and think a certain way, the left argues that anyone committed to democratic values and equal rights can be American. This is the opposite of blood and soil ideology.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left’s entire ideological framework opposes this kind of nationalism.

2. Violent Suppression of Opposition

The left has not deployed paramilitary forces, systematically assaulted political opponents, or attempted to violently overturn election results. There is no left-wing equivalent to the Proud Boys, Oath Keepers, or Three Percenters—armed groups that have committed violence in service of right-wing politics.

Critics will immediately point to antifa (short for anti-fascist). So let’s address it directly: antifa is a decentralized movement of activists who oppose fascism and white supremacy, sometimes using violent tactics like property destruction and street fighting with right-wing groups. There have been instances of antifa violence, and it should be condemned when it occurs. The 2020 protests that included antifa participation resulted in over $2 billion in property damage nationwide—a scale of destruction that cannot be dismissed and that fueled legitimate concerns about public safety and anarchic violence.

However, there are crucial differences between antifa and fascist paramilitary forces. Antifa is not an organization with leadership, membership, or coordinated strategy—it’s a loose collection of local groups and individuals. They don’t operate at the direction of Democratic politicians or progressive leaders. When antifa violence occurs, Democratic officials consistently condemn it. Most importantly, antifa’s targets are not democratic institutions or processes—they oppose specific far-right groups they view as fascist threats. They’re not trying to overturn elections, install a dictator, or suppress democratic opposition. They’re engaging in street-level confrontation with groups they see as dangerous, not attempting to seize state power.

This doesn’t excuse violence, but it’s categorically different from organized paramilitary groups working to help a political leader maintain power through force. The Proud Boys showed up on January 6th to help Trump overturn an election. Antifa has never attempted anything remotely similar.

The 2020 protests after George Floyd’s murder included some property damage and violence, but these were not organized campaigns to suppress opposition—they were protests against police violence that sometimes turned chaotic. There’s no evidence of systematic Democratic Party encouragement of violence, and Democratic leaders consistently called for peaceful protest.

Some on the left have engaged in deplatforming, shouting down speakers, and aggressive protest tactics. These can be illiberal and counterproductive, but they’re not fascist violence. They’re not paramilitary suppression of opposition.

Does this characteristic fit? No. Individual acts of violence or property damage during protests are not equivalent to systematic political violence organized to maintain authoritarian power.

3. Scapegoating of Minorities and “Others”

The left does not scapegoat racial, ethnic, or religious minorities. Its politics are built on protecting those groups from discrimination and expanding their rights.

However, there is a version of this on the left—not against minorities, but against “white people,” “men,” or “the rich” as monolithic categories responsible for all social ills. Some left rhetoric does essentialize and blame these groups in ways that can be counterproductive and divisive.

But this isn’t equivalent to fascist scapegoating. Fascism targets the vulnerable and powerless, using state violence to persecute them. Left rhetoric may sometimes be reductive about privileged groups, but it doesn’t advocate state violence against white people or men, and it doesn’t seek to strip them of rights. Criticizing systems of privilege isn’t the same as scapegoating minorities for genocide.

Does this characteristic fit? No. Critiquing privilege and power is not equivalent to fascist scapegoating of vulnerable minorities.

4. Fusion of State and Corporate Power

The left does not seek this. Progressive politics generally advocates for stronger regulation of corporations, higher taxes on the wealthy, more robust worker protections, and in some cases, breaking up monopolies or nationalizing certain industries for public benefit.

The left’s economic vision is more power to workers and consumers, less to corporations—the opposite of fascism’s marriage of state and corporate power serving elite interests.

Some on the left critique “woke capitalism”—corporations performatively supporting social justice while maintaining exploitative practices—but this is criticism of corporate power, not alliance with it.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left’s economic agenda opposes the fusion of state and corporate power.

5. Control of Information and Attack on Press

The left does not systematically attack the press or attempt to control information through state power. Democratic politicians generally respect press freedom and don’t threaten to revoke licenses or jail journalists.

However, there are concerning tendencies around information control on the left. Some progressives have pushed for deplatforming, content moderation, and fact-checking in ways that can feel like censorship to those affected. Universities and institutions have sometimes suppressed speech deemed offensive or harmful.

But there’s a crucial difference: these are mostly private actors (social media companies, universities) making decisions about their own platforms, not state power being used to silence critics. When progressives argue that misinformation should be countered or harmful speech shouldn’t be platformed, they’re generally not calling for government censorship—they’re calling for private consequences or corporate policy changes.

The debate about where to draw lines around hate speech, misinformation, and harmful content is legitimate. But it’s not equivalent to a leader calling the press “the enemy of the people” and threatening journalists with state power.

Does this characteristic fit? No. Debates about content moderation and speech norms are not equivalent to authoritarian control of information.

6. Rewriting of Education and History

This is where the left faces legitimate criticism. Some progressive approaches to education and history do resemble ideological control, though in the opposite direction from fascism.

Some on the left have pushed for curriculum that emphasizes America’s historical injustices—slavery, genocide of Native Americans, imperialism, systemic racism—to such an extent that it can feel like teaching that America is fundamentally evil. Some diversity training has veered into ideology rather than education, mandating specific views about race, gender, and power.

On college campuses, Title IX has been expanded under progressive administrations to include increasingly broad definitions of harassment and discriminatory speech, leading to investigations and sanctions for speech that would be protected in other contexts. Campus speech codes at many universities have restricted expression deemed offensive or harmful, with students and faculty facing consequences for violating evolving norms around identity and social justice. These represent attempts at ideological control through institutional power, even if not through federal mandate.

However, there’s a crucial distinction: the goal is not to create nationalist mythology but to complicate it. Teaching about slavery and racism isn’t rewriting history—it’s teaching history that was previously whitewashed. The question is one of balance and age-appropriateness, not truth versus lies.

Fascism rewrites history to glorify the nation and justify violence. The left’s approach to education seeks to reveal uncomfortable truths and center marginalized voices. These are not morally equivalent, even if both involve contested curriculum and both can become ideologically rigid.

Importantly, the most aggressive pushback against teaching certain aspects of American history (like critical race theory bans, book bans) actually comes from the right, not the left. The left is arguing for more complexity in education, not less—even when that argument sometimes becomes heavy-handed.

Does this characteristic fit? Partially, but in the opposite direction. Progressive approaches to education can be ideological and sometimes suppress dissenting views, but they’re not creating nationalist mythology to justify violence or ethnic exclusion.

7. Cult of Personality and the Strongman

The left does not exhibit this. There is no Democratic equivalent to Trump worship. Biden is not treated as infallible or above criticism by his supporters. Neither was Obama, despite his charisma and popularity. Sanders and AOC have passionate supporters, but not cults of personality that view criticism as heresy.

Democratic politics remains organized around policies, coalitions, and competing visions—not worship of a strongman leader. When Democratic politicians fail or disappoint, their supporters criticize them. This is how democracy should work.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left does not engage in strongman worship.

8. Rejection of Democratic Norms and Institutions

The left does not systematically reject democratic norms. Democrats have not attempted to overturn election results, incited mobs to attack the Capitol, or refused to accept electoral defeats.

Some on the left have questioned the legitimacy of certain institutions—the Electoral College, the Senate’s structure, the Supreme Court’s composition—but this is democratic debate about reforming systems, not rejection of democracy itself. Arguing that institutions should be changed through constitutional processes is not the same as attempting coups.

There are concerning instances where progressives have pushed to limit democratic processes in the name of justice—prioritizing correct outcomes over democratic procedures. But these are marginal voices, not the Democratic Party’s position.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left engages in normal democratic politics, including vigorous debate about institutional reform.

9. Glorification of Violence and Militarism

The left does not glorify violence or militarism. Progressive politics generally advocates for reduced military spending, diplomatic rather than military solutions, and skepticism of American military interventions.

Some on the far left romanticize revolutionary violence or make excuses for violent protest, but this is not mainstream Democratic politics, and it’s not glorification of military power or authoritarian strength.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left generally opposes militarism and violence.

10. Nostalgia for a Mythical Golden Age

The left does not engage in this kind of nostalgia. Progressive politics is future-oriented, arguing that America’s best days lie ahead if we continue expanding rights, equality, and justice. The left’s view of the past is generally critical—seeing it as something to learn from and move beyond, not something to restore.

Some on the left romanticize past progressive movements or leaders, but this isn’t nostalgia for a time when America was “greater”—it’s admiration for specific struggles and achievements that moved the country toward justice.

Does this characteristic fit? No. The left’s politics are progressive, not reactionary.

The Left’s Scorecard

The contemporary American left, measured against the ten defining characteristics of historical fascism:

  1. Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology: No

  2. Violent suppression of opposition: No

  3. Scapegoating of minorities: No

  4. Fusion of state and corporate power: No

  5. Control of information and attack on press: No

  6. Rewriting of education and history: Partial, but opposite direction

  7. Cult of personality and the strongman: No

  8. Rejection of democratic norms and institutions: No

  9. Glorification of violence and militarism: No

  10. Nostalgia for a mythical golden age: No

That’s one characteristic with partial alignment (and in the opposite ideological direction), and nine that don’t fit at all.

The Comparative Verdict

Here’s what the evidence shows when we measure both sides against the ten defining characteristics of historical fascism:

This is not a “both sides” situation. The accusation that Trump and MAGA represent fascist tendencies is substantially supported by evidence when measured against historical fascism. The accusation that the left is fascist is almost entirely without merit.

The pattern of behavior, rhetoric, and political strategy aligns with fascist movements in their rise to power. Mussolini and Hitler didn’t start with death camps—they started with rallies, scapegoating, nationalist mythology, attacks on the press, cults of personality, and attempted coups. The question is whether Trump is following the same playbook and whether American institutions are strong enough to stop him.

Why Precision Matters

Some will argue that calling Trump fascist is still too strong, that it exaggerates the threat and alienates potential allies. Others will argue that not calling him fascist normalizes the abnormal and fails to sound the alarm loudly enough.

Here’s why precision matters: If we call everything fascism, we lose the ability to distinguish between authoritarianism and normal politics. If we refuse to call anything fascism until it reaches the level of Nazi Germany, we’ll always be too late.

The value of having clear criteria is that we can make honest assessments. Based on the historical definition of fascism—as Mussolini created it and Hitler perfected it—Trump and the MAGA movement exhibit enough of the core characteristics to warrant the label, or at least to warrant “fascistic” or “proto-fascist.”

The left, despite its flaws and illiberal tendencies in some quarters, does not resemble historical fascism in any meaningful way. Calling the left fascist is either ignorance or bad faith.

This doesn’t mean the left is perfect. Progressive movements have their own authoritarian tendencies, their own blind spots, their own capacity for illiberalism. But these are not fascism, and pretending they are prevents us from having honest conversations about the actual threats we face.

When Stephen Miller turns Charlie Kirk’s memorial into a call for an “army” to rise against political enemies, using rhetoric that echoes Goebbels’ speeches about martyred Nazi comrades, it reveals how grief itself can be weaponized for authoritarian purposes. When Governor Newsom called Miller a fascist, he wasn’t engaging in empty name-calling. Based on the evidence, measured against historical fascism’s defining characteristics, the label fits. What matters is whether enough Americans understand what that means and whether they care enough to stop it.

What This Means Going Forward

Understanding what fascism actually is gives us tools to resist it:

Don’t wait for death camps to sound the alarm. Fascism doesn’t start with genocide—it starts with the rhetoric and tactics we’re seeing now. By the time you have death camps, it’s too late. The warning signs are here. We’re watching them in real time.

Protect democratic institutions. The Electoral College, the courts, free press, independent law enforcement, civil service, voting rights—these are the barriers between democracy and authoritarianism. They’re not perfect, but they’re essential. Every time Trump or his allies attack these institutions, they’re removing another barrier to authoritarian control.

Resist the normalization of abnormal behavior. When a political leader attempts a coup, calls the press enemies, glorifies violence, or incites hatred against minorities, this cannot become normal. Each capitulation makes the next one easier. Each time we shrug and say “that’s just Trump being Trump,” we move the line of acceptability closer to authoritarianism.

Maintain commitment to truth. Fascism requires destroying shared reality. When leaders can lie without consequence and supporters will believe anything, democracy cannot function. This means supporting independent journalism, fact-checking, and holding leaders accountable for falsehoods. It means refusing to accept “alternative facts” as legitimate.

Build coalitions across differences. Mussolini rose to power partly because the left was divided—socialists fighting with communists while fascists united behind a strongman. Marx and Mazzini couldn’t unite their visions, and both lost to Mussolini. Unity against authoritarianism doesn’t mean agreeing on everything—it means recognizing the greater threat and working together despite differences.

Don’t lose sight of real people. It’s easy to dismiss Trump supporters as fascists or deplorables. But people are drawn to authoritarian movements for real reasons—economic anxiety, cultural displacement, desire for meaning and belonging, feeling unheard by elites. Addressing those underlying conditions is essential to preventing fascism’s appeal. You can’t defeat authoritarianism by simply condemning the people who support it—you have to offer them something better.

Understand the stakes. Democracy is fragile. It can be lost. It has been lost before, in countries that thought it couldn’t happen to them. Germany was a modern, educated, cultured nation. Italy had a rich history of art, philosophy, and civic ideals. Both fell to fascism because enough people were willing to trade freedom for the promise of greatness, to trade truth for the comfort of simple answers, to trade democracy for a strongman who said he alone could fix everything.

The Historical Pattern

History shows us how this works. Fascism doesn’t announce itself with dramatic music and villainous speeches about world domination. It arrives wrapped in flags, promising greatness, blaming others for your problems, and asking for your loyalty to a strong leader who will make everything right again.

It starts with rallies where the leader mocks the vulnerable and the crowd laughs. It starts with calling the press enemies and truth whatever the leader says. It starts with identifying scapegoats—immigrants, minorities, “the other”—and blaming them for complex problems. It starts with a myth of a better past that must be restored.

It continues with attacks on democratic institutions that constrain the leader. With attempts to override election results. With deployment of federal forces to punish opposition. With loyalty tests for government employees. With demands that corporations choose sides. With rewriting education to serve nationalist mythology.

It escalates when the leader faces accountability and chooses to fight rather than accept democratic constraints. When he attempts to overturn an election. When he incites violence and then refuses to stop it. When he describes his opponents as vermin to be eliminated. When he promises to be a dictator, even “just for one day.”

And it succeeds when enough people who should know better decide it’s not really that bad, or that both sides are equally problematic, or that the threat is exaggerated, or that their policy preferences matter more than preserving democracy.

The Choice Ahead

We are at a choice point, and the evidence is mounting in real time. Trump has shown us who he is through his actions, not just his words. He attempted to overturn an election through fraud, pressure, and violence. He has promised to purge the government of anyone not personally loyal to him. He has threatened to prosecute political opponents. He has described immigrants as “poisoning the blood of our country.” In September 2025, as I write this, federal troops are being deployed to Democratic-led cities over the objections of local officials—Oregon is literally suing to stop unconstitutional Guard deployments. He has attacked every institution that constrains him.

The question is not whether Trump exhibits fascistic tendencies—the evidence is overwhelming that he does. The question is whether American democracy is strong enough to survive another Trump presidency, and whether enough Americans understand the stakes to prevent it.

Mussolini and Hitler showed us what happens when fascism wins. They started with rhetoric and rallies. They built cults of personality. They scapegoated minorities. They attacked the press. They attempted coups. They succeeded when democratic institutions failed to stop them and when enough people decided the threat wasn’t serious enough to overcome their other political preferences.

The warning signs are all there. The playbook is being followed. The only question is whether we’ve learned anything from history.

A Final Word on Language

The word “fascist” gets thrown around carelessly because it’s a powerful insult. But it’s also a specific historical phenomenon with identifiable characteristics. When we measure Trump and MAGA against those characteristics using the same standard we apply to the left, the comparison holds up far more than many would wish.

This doesn’t make everyone who votes for Trump a fascist. Many are motivated by legitimate policy preferences, economic concerns, or simple partisan loyalty. But it does mean they’re supporting a leader and a movement that exhibits the defining characteristics of fascism as Mussolini created it and Hitler perfected it.

Governor Newsom wasn’t wrong to call Stephen Miller a fascist. The evidence supports it. Those who respond by calling the left fascist are either genuinely confused about what the word means or deliberately muddying the waters to neutralize a legitimate criticism.

Precision in language matters because it allows us to see threats clearly. When we’re precise about what fascism is and honest about measuring both sides against the same standard, the conclusion is unavoidable: one side of American politics is following the fascist playbook, and it’s not the left.

The question now is what we do with that information. Do we treat it as just another partisan talking point, something to argue about on social media and then forget? Or do we recognize it as a genuine warning about the fragility of democracy and act accordingly?

History suggests that by the time everyone agrees something is fascism, it’s too late to stop it. The time to act is when the warning signs are clear but the outcome is not yet certain. That time is now.

We know how this story ends when people ignore the warning signs. The question is whether we’re paying attention.
