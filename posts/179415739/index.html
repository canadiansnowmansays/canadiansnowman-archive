When Democracy Fights Back

The Backfire

Part 6: When Trump’s Own Judge Destroys Trump’s Own Map

November 19, 2025

A Trump appointee uses DOJ’s letter as evidence that Texas engaged in racial gerrymandering—exactly what we warned would happen

In Part 5, I wrote: “Sometimes the most partisan actors create the tools for their own destruction.”

I didn’t expect the proof to arrive so soon.

Yesterday, a federal court blocked Texas from using its newly drawn congressional map in the 2026 midterms, ruling that the map is likely an unconstitutional racial gerrymander.

The judge who wrote the majority opinion? Jeffrey V. Brown—appointed by Donald Trump in 2019.

The evidence he cited as proof of racial intent? The July letter from Trump’s own Justice Department.

The irony is almost too perfect.

The Man Who Destroyed the “Big Beautiful Map”

When Trump nominated Jeffrey Brown to the federal bench in 2019, Governor Greg Abbott celebrated the appointment. Abbott noted that he had “witnessed firsthand” Brown’s “sharp legal mind and commitment to the Constitution and the rule of law.”

On Tuesday, that same judge used Abbott’s own words against him, writing that when Abbott cited the DOJ letter as justification for redistricting, he “explicitly directed the Legislature to redistrict based on race.”

Brown didn’t mince words about the DOJ letter that was supposed to provide Texas legal cover. He called it “challenging to unpack” because it “contains so many factual, legal, and typographical errors.”

Even better: Brown noted that attorneys employed by Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton—”who professes to be a political ally of the Trump Administration”—described the DOJ letter as “legally unsound,” “baseless,” “erroneous,” “ham-fisted,” and “a mess.”

When your own allies are calling your legal strategy “ham-fisted” and “a mess,” you might have a problem.

The Trap Sprung Exactly as Predicted

In Part 5, I outlined three possible outcomes for the redistricting battle. Outcome B—”The Accidental Reform”—described a scenario where courts establish precedent that applies equally to all mid-decade redistricting, accidentally creating national standards that would threaten Republican gerrymanders everywhere.

That’s precisely what happened.

The DOJ letter claimed that Texas’s 2021 map was unconstitutional because it contained “coalition districts”—districts where minorities form a majority but no single racial group dominates. The letter demanded Texas eliminate these districts.

But as Judge Brown ruled, this was “a legally incorrect assertion.” States aren’t required to draw coalition districts under federal civil rights law—but they’re also not prohibited from doing so.

Here’s the critical legal finding: “Nor is it lawful for a legislature to purposefully target such districts for destruction.”

Read that again. If targeting coalition districts for destruction is illegal, then every Republican redistricting effort that dismantled minority-influence districts is now legally vulnerable.

The DOJ handed civil rights groups a weapon they can use nationwide.

How Abbott Destroyed His Own Defense

Texas Republicans had one defense: they were engaged in partisan gerrymandering (legal under Rucho v. Common Cause), not racial gerrymandering.

Abbott destroyed that defense himself.

When he called the special legislative session, Abbott explicitly cited the DOJ letter about the racial makeup of districts. As Brown wrote: “In doing so, the Governor explicitly directed the Legislature to redistrict based on race.”

The court found that the final map “achieved all but one of the racial objectives that DOJ demanded.” The Legislature didn’t just dismantle the districts DOJ identified—they went further, targeting “several other ‘coalition districts’ around the State.”

You can’t claim you were doing partisan gerrymandering when you literally cited racial concerns as your justification and then achieved racial objectives.

This is what happens when you try to have it both ways. DOJ wanted to claim they were protecting against racial gerrymandering in Texas while prosecuting California for the same thing. Texas wanted to claim they were just doing partisan gerrymandering while explicitly responding to DOJ’s racial concerns.

The contradictions collapsed under their own weight.

The Scoreboard Flips

A voter looking in August at a proposed redistricting map that was approved by Texas lawmakers for the 2026 midterm elections. Credit...Moises Avila/Agence France-Presse — Getty

If this ruling holds, Republicans will have lost the redistricting battle they started.

Consider where we are:

Texas’s map is blocked. The state must use its 2021 map for the 2026 midterms—meaning Republicans lose the five Democratic seats they were targeting.

California’s map stands. Democrats gained five Republican seats, and while DOJ has sued, that case doesn’t go to court until next month.

Indiana backed out. Republicans there declined Trump’s request to redistrict, drawing blowback from Trump on social media over the weekend.

The math is brutal. Republicans currently hold just a three-seat majority in the House. They launched this redistricting effort to pad that majority. Instead, they may have handed Democrats the advantage.

As Governor Newsom put it: “Trump and Greg Abbott played with fire, got burned—and democracy won. This ruling is a win for Texas, and for every American who fights for free and fair elections.”

The SCOTUS Wildcard

Texas is appealing to the Supreme Court. The case will likely go first to Justice Samuel Alito, who handles emergency matters from that region.

The timing is brutal for Texas. The filing deadline for candidates is early December. Primaries are in March. If SCOTUS doesn’t act quickly, Texas may have to run its 2026 elections under the 2021 map regardless of the ultimate legal outcome.

But even if SCOTUS intervenes, they face an uncomfortable choice.

If they uphold Brown’s ruling, they’ve established that DOJ letters claiming racial concerns create evidence of racial intent when states respond to them. Every future DOJ letter becomes a potential trap.

If they reverse Brown’s ruling, they have to explain why targeting coalition districts for destruction isn’t racial gerrymandering—a ruling that would gut what remains of the Voting Rights Act.

And lurking in the background is Louisiana v. Callais, which could strike down Section 2 of the VRA entirely. The court’s 6-3 conservative majority signaled deep reservations with Louisiana’s Black-majority district when they heard oral arguments in October.

If they gut Section 2, it might actually help California’s defense against DOJ’s lawsuit while making Texas’s racial defense irrelevant. The legal chess game has become impossibly complex.

The Real Story: 40% White, 73% Control

Beyond the legal maneuvering, there’s a fundamental injustice that this ruling begins to address.

As NAACP President Derrick Johnson noted: “The state of Texas is only 40 percent white, but white voters control over 73 percent of the state’s congressional seats.”

That’s not partisan gerrymandering. That’s racial disenfranchisement dressed up in partisan clothing.

The Republican strategy has been to claim that any map that advantages Republicans is merely “partisan”—even when it systematically dilutes minority voting power. But when your “partisan” gerrymander consistently targets districts held by Black and Latino Democrats, when the racial math is this lopsided, the distinction collapses.

Judge Brown saw through it. A Trump appointee looked at the evidence and concluded that “substantial evidence shows that Texas racially gerrymandered the 2025 Map.”

The Texas Democrats Who Made This Possible

Credit where it’s due: Texas House Democrats, led by Representative Gene Wu, bought the time that made this legal victory possible.

When Republicans tried to ram through the redistricting in August, Democrats fled the state, denying Republicans the quorum needed to pass the map. The move drew national attention and—critically—spurred California Democrats to launch their counter-gerrymander.

“Greg Abbott and his Republican cronies tried to silence Texans’ voices to placate Donald Trump, but now have delivered him absolutely nothing,” Wu said after Tuesday’s ruling.

The walkout looked like a futile gesture at the time. Republicans eventually passed their map anyway. But by delaying the process and drawing national attention, Texas Democrats helped create the conditions for this legal challenge to succeed.

Sometimes the futile gesture turns out to be the decisive one.

What Happens Next

The legal battle is far from over. Texas will push for an emergency stay from SCOTUS. The California lawsuit proceeds next month. Louisiana v. Callais threatens to reshape the entire legal landscape.

But something important happened on Tuesday. A Trump-appointed judge looked at the evidence, applied the law, and concluded that Trump’s redistricting scheme was likely unconstitutional.

The rule of law bent, but it didn’t break.

In Part 4, I documented how DOJ had been weaponized as a partisan tool—giving legal cover to Republican gerrymanders while prosecuting Democratic ones. That weaponization continues. But courts remain a check on that abuse, at least for now.

Conclusion: When You Build a Trap, Make Sure You’re Not Standing in It

Ken Paxton called the Texas map “the Big Beautiful Map”—Trump’s nickname for the redistricting effort.

A Trump-appointed judge just called the legal strategy behind it “ham-fisted” and “a mess.”

The DOJ letter that was supposed to provide legal cover instead provided evidence of racial intent. The explicit citations to that letter by Abbott and legislative leaders gave plaintiffs exactly what they needed to prove their case.

The most partisan actors created the tools for their own destruction.

Will SCOTUS bail them out? Perhaps. The conservative majority has shown willingness to intervene on behalf of Republican interests. But even a SCOTUS reversal would come with costs—it would require either blessing obviously pretextual racial justifications or gutting the VRA protections that remain.

For now, though, we have a federal court ruling that says what many of us have been saying all along: Texas’s mid-decade redistricting was racial gerrymandering disguised as partisan gerrymandering, launched at Trump’s command and justified with a legally incoherent DOJ letter.

Democracy fought back. And on Tuesday, democracy won a round.
