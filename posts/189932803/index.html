The Iran Crisis

“Death and Destruction All Day Long”

Day Five: The War Behind the War

March 4, 2026

The rubble of a police station in Tehran this week after the U.S.-Israeli airstrikes. Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

On Tuesday, hours before the Pentagon released the names of four dead Iowa Army Reserve soldiers, Representative Zach Nunn of Iowa’s third congressional district announced on social media that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth would be attending a political fundraiser in his honor.

The event is scheduled for March 14 in Ankeny, Iowa. It is billed as “Operation Top Nunn — A Salute to the Troops!” and themed after the 1986 action-adventure film Top Gun. Tickets start at $26. Those willing to pay $1,000 or $3,500 gain entry to a VIP reception. Nunn posted: “WINGMAN CONFIRMED.”

When he posted those words, the four soldiers from his district were still unnamed. By Tuesday evening, they had names. Sergeant First Class Nicole Amor, 39, White Bear Lake, Minnesota. Captain Cody Khork, 35, Winter Haven, Florida. Sergeant First Class Noah Tietjens, 42, Bellevue, Nebraska. Sergeant Declan Coady, 20, Des Moines, Iowa. All assigned to the 103rd Sustainment Command — headquartered in Nunn’s district. All trained and drilled in the communities where “Operation Top Nunn” will charge $3,500 for a VIP reception with the man who dispatched them to Kuwait.

The fundraiser is still on.

When Hegseth was asked Wednesday about media coverage of the deaths of American service members, he did not express condolences. He complained.

“We’ve taken control of Iran’s airspace and waterways without boots on the ground,” he told reporters at the Pentagon. “We control their fate. But when a few drones get through, or tragic things happen, it’s front page news. I get it. The press only wants to make the president look bad; but try, for once, to report the reality.”

Senator Mark Warner of Virginia, the top Democrat on the Intelligence Committee, said the fundraiser was “disrespectful and unseemly when he and the president acknowledge there’s going to be probably many more victims to come.”

Several Republicans declined to comment.

This is Day Five of Operation Epic Fury. Let us report the reality.

I. The Back-Channel Iran Killed

The most consequential new fact of Day Five was not in any press conference. Michael Crowley, Julian Barnes, and Ronen Bergman reported it Wednesday for the New York Times, sourced to Middle Eastern officials and a Western country official speaking on condition of anonymity.

A day after Operation Epic Fury began — Sunday, March 1 — operatives from Iran’s Ministry of Intelligence reached out indirectly to the CIA through another country’s spy agency. They offered to discuss terms for ending the conflict.

The offer was not taken seriously in Washington. Israel, which wants a campaign of several weeks to inflict maximum damage on Iran’s military and possibly cause the government to collapse, urged the United States to ignore it. For now, the offer is considered not serious.

On Tuesday morning, before his Oval Office meeting with Chancellor Merz, Trump posted on Truth Social that it was now “too late” for talks with Iran.

Parse what just happened. Iran’s intelligence apparatus — not its foreign ministry making public statements, but its spy agency reaching out through a third country — opened a back-channel within 24 hours of the bombs starting to fall. That is not the behavior of a government certain of its ability to outlast the assault. That is the behavior of a government testing whether there is an exit before the walls close in.

Israel said no. Trump said too late.

Araghchi, the Iranian foreign minister who spent weeks negotiating in Geneva and believed until the end that a deal was possible, posted on social media Wednesday that Trump had treated the nuclear talks as “a real estate transaction” and bombed “the negotiation table out of spite.”

The back-channel offer also exposed the central problem the Trump administration has declined to address publicly: with the Iranian leadership being systematically killed, it is becoming harder by the day to find anyone with enough authority to make a deal stick. Trump himself said it Wednesday afternoon, with what seemed like genuine bewilderment: “Their leadership is just rapidly going. Everybody that seems to want to be a leader, they end up dead. It’s an amazing, an amazing thing that’s taking place before your eyes.”

He rated the American war effort a 15 out of 10.

The CIA, Crowley reported, produced an intelligence assessment before the war examining various scenarios of what Iranian leadership might emerge after a US-Israeli attack. None of the scenarios had a high degree of confidence — there were simply too many unknown variables. Some policymakers concluded the most likely outcome was that IRGC figures would emerge as the most influential voice in whatever government survived. The question now is whether any of those officials will emerge alive.

Trump said Wednesday he would now prefer “somebody that’s there that’s currently popular, if there’s such a person.” He seemed uncertain whether such a person existed. He appeared to be searching for an answer in real time, in public, during a war he launched five days ago.

II. Forty-Six Years in the Making

Yair Rosenberg, writing in The Atlantic, documented Wednesday what this series has been tracking since Part XXI: the shifting justifications for the war are not a communications failure. They are a smoke screen for a decision that was made long before any of the stated pretexts existed.

In 1980, a 34-year-old Donald Trump told NBC that America “sits back and allows a country such as Iran to hold our hostages” and that he “absolutely” advocated sending in troops, adding that had America done so, “I think right now we’d be an oil-rich nation.” In 1987, he gave a New Hampshire speech calling for the United States to “attack Iran and seize some of its oil fields.” In 1988, he told The Guardian: “I’d be harsh on Iran. They’ve been beating us psychologically, making us look a bunch of fools.”

Netanyahu recently said he had dreamed of being able to destroy the Iranian regime for 40 years. Trump had him beat by a decade.

On Wednesday, Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz confirmed what Rosenberg argued: Israel had originally planned to launch the campaign this summer, but “events — including the recent mass protests in Iran — forced Israel’s hand.” The protests. The ones where Khamenei’s security forces killed at least seven thousand people in January. The massacre that produced the January chaos accelerated the timeline for the war.

The imminent threat — the various formulations of nuclear weapons, ballistic missiles, Iranian retaliation against Israeli strikes — were always pretexts for a decision that preceded them. As Rosenberg summarized CNN reporter Andrew Kaczynski’s observation: Trump “never actually opposed” regime change wars at the time. He only opposed them after they went bad.

Trump himself underlined this Wednesday. When Hegseth announced that the leader of the Iranian covert unit that planned to assassinate Trump in 2024 had been killed in Tuesday’s strikes, the personal stakes of the operation came into full view. Trump had said Sunday, confirming Khamenei’s death: “I got him before he got me. They tried twice. Well I got him first.”

The president of the United States publicly framed the launch of the largest American military operation since the 2003 Iraq invasion as personal revenge. He said it out loud. It is in the record.

III. Operational Success, Strategic Void

Hegseth and General Caine held their second press conference in two days Wednesday morning, and the metrics were genuinely significant. Iran’s ballistic missile strikes are down 86 percent from Day One, with a 23 percent decrease in the last 24 hours alone. Drone strikes are down 73 percent. Iran’s air defenses have been sufficiently degraded that the US and Israel are now shifting from expensive standoff munitions to cheap precision gravity bombs — 500-pound, 1,000-pound, and 2,000-pound weapons “of which we have a nearly unlimited stockpile,” Hegseth said. Iranian command and control is “in a bad way.” Airspace control expected within 48 hours. More bombers arriving Wednesday. The US submarine torpedo that sank the IRIS Dena — confirmed Wednesday as the first American submarine torpedo fired in combat since World War II — illustrated the scope and reach of the campaign.

“Death and destruction from the sky, all day long,” Hegseth told Iran’s surviving leaders.

These are real achievements. The US military operationally is extraordinarily capable, and it is performing accordingly.

Tom Nichols, writing for The Atlantic and speaking Wednesday in the magazine’s panel discussion, offered the counterweight that the Pentagon briefing did not: “The problem is that without strategic guidance or vision, operational successes don’t start mounting up into a strategic success.” Nichols spent years teaching this at the Naval War College. He was watching Elbridge Colby — the undersecretary of defense for policy who built his entire career around “forget all this stuff, pivot to China” — struggle to answer questions about Iran. “If the undersecretary for policy doesn’t know where we’re supposed to be going,” Nichols said, “has anybody else supposed to know?”

Hegseth’s press conference was a catalogue of operational metrics. It contained no strategic vision. No theory of what comes after the airspace is controlled. No description of what Iran looks like in six weeks, six months, six years. No answer to what the US does when a new supreme leader is installed, the IRGC consolidates power, and the regime — battered, martyred, and narratively vindicated in the eyes of its remaining supporters — survives.

A Western official told the Times Wednesday that Iran can continue firing missiles at current rates for “several more days.” Tehran may decide to hold some munitions in reserve, extending that window. The operational success is real. The endpoint is not.

IV. The Information War — Fighting on a Second Front

While the US and Israel dominated Iranian airspace, Iran was fighting a second war that required no aircraft at all.

On Iranian state television and through a network of affiliated social media accounts, the country presented a completely different picture of the conflict. In Tehran’s telling, Iranian missiles had ravaged Tel Aviv, Iranian jets had decimated an American aircraft carrier, and hundreds of Americans had been killed at bases across the region.

None of it was true. But the sophistication and speed of the operation was notable. When an Iranian state television anchor read a statement claiming the USS Abraham Lincoln had been hit by four ballistic missiles, the claim spread across pro-Iranian social media accounts within minutes, was picked up by TASS and RT, and reached millions of users before US Central Command posted a flat-out rebuttal on X: “LIE. The Lincoln was not hit. The missiles launched didn’t even come close.”

AI-generated videos showed US soldiers crying after missile strikes, always with the same caption — a coordinated propaganda format designed, according to researchers at the Institute for Strategic Dialogue, to “visually communicate psychological defeat.” Videos from a 2017 Ukrainian ammunition depot fire were recirculated as footage of Iranian strikes on Israeli nuclear facilities. A 2015 residential tower fire became a CIA building in Dubai ablaze.

Moustafa Ayad of the Institute for Strategic Dialogue: “It’s flooding the zone with content that projects strength in the wake of attacks on Iran — and it’s similarly distorting the picture of what is actually happening inside the country.”

The information war operates on a strategic logic the military campaign cannot resolve through airpower. Iran is not trying to convince Americans that it is winning. It is trying to convince the watching world — the Global South, the Muslim world, the fence-sitters — that America is not winning as decisively as it claims, that the costs are higher than reported, and that any post-war settlement will be imposed by force on a population that resisted. The goal is to undermine the political legitimacy of whatever comes after.

Nancy Youssef, speaking in the Atlantic panel discussion, made a point that deserves to be stated plainly: this is the first US military conflict of its scope that she can cover only from her office. No embeds. Most of the press corps not allowed into the Pentagon. No journalists on ships or in bases or riding in Humvees with the soldiers whose deaths fill the front pages. “It’s different when you’re in the Humvee with them and feeling the fears the same way that they are,” she said. “It’s all in these cool details that are the difference between really being able to understand and communicate the war to the American public, and it remaining something sort of distant and confusing.”

Into that information vacuum, Iran’s AI-powered propaganda machine is operating at full speed. Russia is amplifying every claim. CENTCOM is fact-checking on social media. The American public is getting the war filtered through official briefings, official metrics, and official framings — and through whatever gets through on X before it is debunked.

V. The Drone Economics — What the Math Actually Says

Farah Stockman reported Wednesday the numbers that explain why Iran’s strategy of asymmetric endurance is not irrational even as its ballistic missile capability degrades.

Each Iranian Shahed drone costs between $20,000 and $50,000 to manufacture, built from off-the-shelf commercial electronics, small enough to launch from the back of a truck. The gold standard American interceptor — the Patriot PAC-3 — costs more than $3 million per shot and Lockheed Martin delivered just 620 of them in all of 2025, a production record. The Raytheon Coyote counter-drone interceptor costs $126,500 per shot. Cheaper, but still multiple times the cost of the target.

Arthur Erickson, the CEO of Texas drone manufacturer Hylio: “It is definitely more expensive to shoot down a drone than to put a drone in the sky. It’s a money game. The cost ratio per shot, per interception, is at best 10 to one. But it could be more like 60 or 70 to one in terms of cost, in favor of Iran.”

Iran has fired more than 2,000 one-way drones since Saturday. Some got through billion-dollar air defense systems and hit their targets. The IRGC spy cells arrested in Qatar — ten suspects with coordinates of sensitive facilities and drone sabotage training — suggest that not all Iranian drone operations depend on missiles fired from launchers that US airstrikes can target.

When Hegseth was asked about munitions supply Wednesday, he dismissed concerns by pointing to the shift toward cheap gravity bombs. Caine’s answer was more careful: “We have sufficient precision munitions for the task at hand — but I want to tell you, teammates, as a matter of practice, I don’t want to be talking about quantities.”

The hedge inside the reassurance is the tell.

VI. The Gerald Ford Problem — And Everything It Represents

Nancy Youssef, in the Atlantic panel discussion, traced the biography of one ship to illustrate the strategic overextension the official briefings do not mention.

The USS Gerald Ford was scheduled to deploy to Europe last June, to be America’s visible symbol of support for Ukraine. It was diverted to the Caribbean for the Venezuela operation. It is now in the Middle East. It has been extended multiple times and will nearly double its original deployment duration. To conduct the Iran mission, the United States pulled munitions from the Western Hemisphere and from the Asia-Pacific — the two regions Trump identified just a month ago as the priority focus of his national defense strategy.

“It doesn’t come without cost,” Youssef said. “Let’s think about the forward. When you extend a unit, it not only affects personnel — divorce rates, stress on the family — but the ship itself is not designed to go that long on these kinds of deployments.”

The Gerald Ford is a metaphor, but it is also a real ship with a real crew that has been away from home far longer than planned, carrying munitions that were pulled from other theaters that still exist and still have adversaries.

Youssef also raised a point that no official briefing has addressed: more than two dozen admirals and generals were pushed out in the run-up to this operation. The positions that needed filling were staffed two or three rungs below normal qualification level. The result is not just a loss of expertise but, more critically, a climate in which people are afraid to raise concerns. “There’s not the environment to sort of raise concerns,” Youssef said. “And so we’re seeing the lack of precision manifested in real-time in this conflict.”

The evacuation chaos — Americans told to leave while airspace was closed, a State Department hotline telling callers for hours not to rely on the US government for assistance, the recorded message corrected only after public pressure — is the practical result of that environment. The British, who had less advance notice of the strikes than the Americans, managed to organize military flights for their citizens more efficiently than the country that launched the war. That is not an accident. It is what happens when the people responsible for planning logistics are afraid to tell the secretary of defense that the plan has gaps.

Olorunnipa added a dimension that received almost no coverage Wednesday: Trump launched this war while the Department of Homeland Security is partially shut down, in a dispute over immigration funding. There has already been one mass shooting in Texas that authorities believe may be connected to the Iran strikes. “It only really takes one mass shooting or one action back here at home to make this very real for the American public,” Olorunnipa said. The president who launched a war to protect the homeland did so while the agency responsible for protecting the homeland was not fully operational.

VII. The Lie About Spain

Pedro Sánchez, Spain’s prime minister, has condemned the attack on Iran as a violation of international law.Credit...Violeta Santos Moura/Reuters

Karoline Leavitt told reporters Wednesday afternoon that Spain had “heard the president’s message loud and clear” and that “over the past several hours they’ve agreed to cooperate with the US military.”

Spain’s Foreign Minister José Manuel Albares went on radio within hours. “That’s not true. We categorically deny it. Spain’s position has not changed.”

The White House fabricated allied support in real time, on the record, and was immediately and publicly contradicted by the ally in question.

Prime Minister Sánchez, in his national address Wednesday, was explicit: “We are not going to be accomplices to something that is bad for the world and contrary to our values and interests, simply because of fear of reprisals from some.” He invoked 2003: “Another American administration dragged us into a war in the Middle East.” He called for the US, Israel, and Iran to “stop before it’s too late.”

The broader European picture is a study in calibrated distance. Britain, France, and Germany issued a joint statement promising defensive support. France deployed its Charles de Gaulle aircraft carrier to the Mediterranean, with Macron calling the strikes “outside of international law, which we cannot approve” while simultaneously deploying Rafale jets to neutralize drones over the UAE. Greece sent F-16s and warships to Cyprus. Britain allowed the US to use its bases “to strike Iranian missiles and launchers” — a carefully chosen formulation — while Starmer told Parliament: “What I was not prepared to do on Saturday was for the UK to join a war unless I was satisfied there was a lawful basis and a viable thought-through plan.”

Every European ally is threading the same needle: defending against Iranian retaliation without endorsing the American decision to launch the war. The gap between those two positions is where American diplomatic credibility used to live.

VIII. The Widening — What Is Now on Fire

The conflict that began Saturday over Iran is now burning across a geography that was not on the original target list.

Turkey and NATO: An Iranian ballistic missile — targeted, US officials confirmed, at Incirlik Air Base, where American troops are stationed — was shot down by a US Navy SM-3 interceptor in the eastern Mediterranean as it headed toward Turkish airspace. Remnants fell in Turkey’s Hatay province. NATO condemned the targeting of a member state. Turkey’s defense ministry said it would “take all necessary steps to defend our territory and airspace.” Turkey’s foreign minister spoke with Araghchi and urged restraint. Turkey shares a 300-mile border with Iran and has longstanding trade and diplomatic relations with Tehran. It is now a country whose airspace was violated by an Iranian ballistic missile.

Damage from an Israeli strike in the southern suburbs of Beirut. Syrian officials said that more than 25,000 Syrians have returned to the country from Lebanon over the past three days.Credit...Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Lebanon: The death toll from Israeli strikes reached 72 Wednesday, with more than 80,000 Lebanese displaced. Israel issued mass evacuation orders for southern Lebanon, urging civilians to move north of the Litani River. Hezbollah head Naim Qassem: “We will fight to the death and will not surrender.” Israel struck more than 200 targets in Beirut alone over five days. A hotel in the upscale Baabda suburb — less than a mile from Lebanon’s presidential palace — was hit at 4am Wednesday. The 2024 ceasefire is functionally dead.

Pakistan: Pakistan’s foreign minister warned Iran that Islamabad has a mutual defense pact with Saudi Arabia — an attack on one is an attack on both. “The Iranian leadership should keep that in mind,” he told the Pakistani Senate. Ten people died when crowds stormed the US Consulate in Karachi on Sunday. The State Department ordered non-essential staff to leave Lahore and Karachi consulates.

Syria: Forty thousand Syrians living in Lebanon have fled back to Syria over four days — back to a country still in fragile post-Assad transition, because Lebanon under Israeli bombardment now feels more dangerous than Syria under reconstruction. At the Masnaa border crossing, suitcases were tied to the roofs of cars heading east. “I don’t have a house,” said Kheder Shaabo, 22, his face and neck cut by glass from the windows of his Tyre apartment, shattered by a nearby airstrike. “But I’m going back to my family.”

China: Beijing announced Wednesday it would send a special envoy to the Middle East for conflict mediation. China is Iran’s largest oil buyer and has consistently condemned the strikes as “unacceptable.” Its entry into the diplomatic arena is not neutral. It signals that the world’s second superpower has decided the conflict is consequential enough to require its direct involvement — and that it does not intend to let the United States shape the post-war order uncontested.

Grand Ayatollah Sistani in Iraq — the supreme religious authority for Shia Muslims worldwide, a figure who almost never speaks on political matters — issued a statement condemning the attacks and calling on Muslims and “free people of the world” to work for the war’s immediate cessation. When the most cautious and respected Shia cleric on earth breaks his political silence, the war has acquired a religious dimension that airpower cannot resolve.

IX. The Price — In Every Sense

Qatar suspended LNG production after its facilities were struck. The main Asian LNG benchmark is up 91 percent since last Friday. European benchmark up 44 percent. Qatar supplies 20 percent of the world’s LNG, and its customers include China, India, and South Korea. European gas stores are already low from a cold winter.

The beneficiaries of the supply disruption are American energy companies. Shell, TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, and Cheniere — the dominant players in US LNG export capacity — can now sell fuel at roughly double their cost of delivery. “It’s a real windfall,” said Jason Feer of Poten & Partners consulting. “They get the benefit of this big jump.”

The president who launched the war told the American public that oil prices would drop “as soon as this ends.” The energy companies making record margins from the disruption might be in less of a hurry for it to end than the people paying $3.20 at the pump.

Gas was $2.97 last Wednesday. It was $3.20 Wednesday. Diesel crossed $4.00 for the first time since April 2024. A Cornell supply chain professor noted the speed was faster than the post-Ukraine invasion spike in 2022 — “A major supply is getting throttled, that’s going to get the prices soaring right away.”

The S&P 500 stabilized somewhat Wednesday after three days of decline. Markets are beginning to price in the war as a sustained condition rather than a shock event. That stabilization is not reassurance. It is accommodation.

X. What Day Five Has Established

The US military is winning tactically. Iran’s ballistic missile capacity has been significantly degraded. Airspace control is days away. The campaign is accelerating. These things are true.

The back-channel that Iran opened on Day Two has been rejected. The diplomatic door that the Geneva talks represented is officially closed — “too late,” in Trump’s own words. The succession deliberations continue, with Mojtaba Khamenei still the frontrunner and Israel’s defense minister having publicly threatened to kill whoever is chosen. The CIA’s worst-case scenario for Iranian leadership is materializing in real time.

The War Powers 60-day clock continues running. The Senate blocked the resolution 53-47, almost entirely along party lines, with Rand Paul the sole Republican crossover. The House votes Thursday. It will also fail. But Hawley has drawn his line at ground troops. Mace has drawn hers at “a few weeks.” Senator Curtis of Utah: “Yes, I wish I would have been consulted. I wish my vote would have been asked for before this.”

The strategic question Nichols asked at the Naval War College — when operational successes fail to accumulate into strategic success, what then? — has not been answered by any official in any briefing in five days.

Hegseth is going to a Top Gun-themed fundraiser in Iowa in ten days. The families of Noah Tietjens, Cody Khork, Nicole Amor, and Declan Coady live in that district. Trump will attend the dignified transfer of their remains.

“Everybody that seems to want to be a leader, they end up dead,” Trump said Wednesday, of Iran. “It’s an amazing, an amazing thing that’s taking place before your eyes.”

It is. Watch carefully.
