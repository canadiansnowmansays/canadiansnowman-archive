The Trump Accountability Project

Three Failed Mechanisms

Part 35: Legal, Electoral, and Party Accountability (1973-2026)

April 7, 2026

Donald Trump has engaged in racist behavior for fifty years. The pattern documented in Part 33 is undeniable: housing discrimination, calling for the execution of innocent teenagers, birther conspiracies, “shithole countries,” false claims about immigrants eating pets, calling refugees “garbage,” racist caricatures of Black leaders, and depicting the Obamas as apes.

In any normal organization, this pattern would result in immediate termination. But Donald Trump is not subject to normal accountability. To understand why fifty years of racist behavior has produced zero consequences, we must examine the mechanisms that would normally enforce accountability and why each has failed.

This part examines the first three mechanisms: legal, electoral, and party accountability.

Mechanism 1: Legal Accountability

Donald Trump stands with New York Mayor Ed Koch, Gov. Hugh Carey and Robert T. Dormer of the Urban Development Corp. at the launching ceremony of the New York Hyatt Hotel in June 1978. AP

The law is supposed to constrain racist behavior. Housing discrimination is illegal. Employment discrimination is illegal. Civil rights violations are illegal. When individuals or organizations engage in discriminatory practices, they face legal consequences: lawsuits, fines, consent decrees, injunctions, even criminal prosecution.

This mechanism has partially functioned but ultimately failed to create accountability.

In 1973, the Department of Justice sued Trump Management Corporation for housing discrimination. The evidence was overwhelming: Black applicants marked with “C” for colored, told no apartments were available when apartments were vacant, quoted higher rents than white applicants with identical qualifications. The DOJ won.

But the consent decree that resolved the lawsuit did not require admission of wrongdoing. Trump Management signed an agreement promising not to discriminate, but Trump characterized the settlement as a victory. He had been forced to change practices but admitted nothing. And three years later, in 1978, the DOJ found the company was still discriminating—violating the very consent decree it had signed.

What were the consequences? Another round of monitoring. No criminal prosecution. No significant fines that would genuinely hurt the business. Trump’s father continued to run the company. Donald Trump continued to work in it. The discrimination was documented, legally condemned, and functionally unpunished.

In 1992, Trump’s Atlantic City casino paid a $200,000 fine for removing Black dealers at a patron’s request. The fine was the largest civil rights penalty levied against an Atlantic City casino at that time. But for Trump’s business empire, $200,000 was negligible—a cost of doing business. The fine did not change Trump’s behavior or attitudes. It did not prevent him from later admitting he didn’t want “Black guys counting my money.”

Legal accountability requires enforcement robust enough to deter future misconduct and consequences severe enough to matter. For Trump, legal accountability has been neither.

The problem is that the consequences were never severe enough. Fines that represent a minor cost of doing business do not deter. Consent decrees that allow denial of wrongdoing do not create accountability. Settlements that impose monitoring but not punishment do not change behavior.

Trump learned early that legal consequences for racism were manageable. Pay the fine. Sign the agreement. Deny wrongdoing. Continue as before. The legal system could document his racism, could force technical compliance, could extract settlements—but it could not impose consequences severe enough to change his fundamental behavior.

By the time Trump became a political figure, legal accountability became even less effective. Presidents enjoy broad immunity from civil litigation while in office. The Justice Department’s policy against indicting sitting presidents shields them from criminal prosecution. And even after leaving office, the statute of limitations had expired on most of Trump’s earlier misconduct.

Legal accountability has failed because penalties were too small to matter and because political power eventually placed him beyond the law’s reach.

Mechanism 2: Electoral Accountability

In a functioning democracy, voters are supposed to hold elected officials accountable for their behavior. Officials who engage in racist conduct should lose elections. The threat of electoral defeat should constrain behavior.

This mechanism has completely failed.

Donald Trump in 2011 speaking to reporters in New Hampshire after President Obama released his long-form birth certificate. Trump said he was “very proud” to have forced the White House’s decision. Jim Cole/AP

Trump launched his political career with the birther conspiracy against Barack Obama. For five years, from 2011 to 2016, he promoted a racist lie designed to delegitimize the first Black president by claiming he wasn’t really American. This would, in normal political circumstances, be disqualifying.

Trump won the presidency anyway.

During the 2016 campaign, voters knew about the housing discrimination lawsuits. They knew about the Central Park Five. They knew about the birther conspiracy. The information was public, reported, and discussed. Trump won anyway.

During the 2024 campaign, the list had grown substantially. Voters knew about “shithole countries.” They watched him falsely claim Haitian immigrants were eating pets during a nationally televised debate. They saw him call Somali immigrants “garbage.” They witnessed him post racist caricatures of Black Democratic leaders.

Trump won reelection anyway.

More remarkably, Trump actually made gains with minority voters in 2024 compared to 2020. He won approximately 15 percent of the Black vote, roughly doubling his share from four years earlier—the largest share of the Black vote for any Republican presidential candidate in at least a quarter-century. He made gains among Latino voters as well.

How is this possible? Several factors explain the complete failure of electoral accountability:

Single-issue voting: Some voters care more about other issues—the economy, immigration enforcement, foreign policy—than they care about Trump’s racism. They may acknowledge his racist behavior but consider it less important than policies they support.

Rationalization: Some voters do not believe Trump’s behavior is racist. They accept his explanations. They believe the media exaggerates controversies. They trust Trump’s denials more than documented evidence.

Shared attitudes: Some voters share Trump’s racist views. They appreciate that he says what they think. His racism is not a bug; it’s a feature.

Tribalism: Some voters support Trump simply because he is the Republican candidate. Party loyalty overrides other considerations.

Misinformation: Some voters are unaware of the extent of Trump’s racist behavior because they consume media that doesn’t report it comprehensively or dismisses incidents as “fake news.”

Economic self-interest: Some minority voters supported Trump despite his racism because they believed his economic policies would benefit them personally.

The result is that electoral accountability does not function. The assumption that voters will punish racist behavior has proven false. A politician can engage in overt, documented racism spanning five decades and still win national elections with increasing support from the very communities he has denigrated.

Electoral accountability only works if enough voters make racism a disqualifying characteristic. In contemporary America, they do not.

Mechanism 3: Party Accountability

Political parties are supposed to enforce norms and standards. When a party leader engages in behavior that violates the party’s stated values or threatens its electoral prospects, other party leaders should speak out, pressure the leader to change, or in extreme cases withdraw support.

This mechanism has largely failed, with brief and ineffective exceptions.

For most of the fifty years, Trump’s racist behavior prompted little response from Republicans. When he was a businessman discriminating in housing or casinos, Republicans said nothing—he was not yet a political figure. When he promoted birtherism from 2011 to 2016, few prominent Republicans challenged him directly. Some quietly encouraged him because the conspiracy theory energized the base against Obama.

When Trump called African nations “shitholes” in 2018, Republican senators who were present claimed not to remember hearing it. When he called Somali immigrants “garbage,” when he posted racist AI-generated images, when he falsely claimed Haitian immigrants were eating pets, the response was muted.

The ape video in February 2026 produced a different response—briefly.

On February 5, 2026 U.S. President Donald Trump posts a video depicting former President Obama and his spouse as monkeys. /Truth Social

Senator Tim Scott of South Carolina, the only Black Republican in the Senate, publicly called it “the most racist thing I’ve seen out of this White House.” Other Republicans joined him. Representative Mike Lawler said it was “wrong and incredibly offensive.” Representative Michael Turner called it “offensive, heart breaking, and unacceptable.” Senator Roger Wicker said it was “totally unacceptable.”

For approximately forty-eight hours, it appeared party accountability might function. Republicans were speaking out. The post was deleted. Surely consequences would follow.

But the window closed as quickly as it opened.

Trump spoke with Tim Scott on the phone. Trump said Scott was “great” and called him “a great guy.” The confrontation was over. Scott did not push further. He did not threaten to resign. He did not withdraw his support. Within days, all the Republicans who had condemned the video moved on to other topics. Trump never apologized. No one was disciplined. There were no consequences.

Why did Republicans briefly speak out against the ape video but remain silent for most of Trump’s other racist behavior? Two factors:

Degree of offense: The ape imagery was so blatant, so historically loaded, that even Republicans who had tolerated Trump’s other racist rhetoric felt compelled to object.

Electoral timing: The incident occurred eight months before midterm elections. Republicans were concerned about political fallout. Scott, as chair of the National Republican Senatorial Committee, had to worry about Senate races.

But electoral concerns prompted criticism, not consequences. Republicans condemned the post and suggested Trump should apologize. But they did not threaten to withdraw support. They did not question his fitness for office. They expressed disapproval but took no action.

Party accountability requires party leaders to be willing to pay a price for confronting the party’s standard-bearer. For Republicans in 2026, that price is too high.

Trump controls the Republican base. Challenging him risks a primary challenge, loss of donor support, and the end of a political career. So Republican leaders express mild disapproval when Trump’s racism becomes too blatant to ignore, then quickly move on.

The party does not constrain Trump’s behavior. Trump constrains the party’s response. Party accountability has inverted: instead of the party holding Trump accountable, Trump holds the party accountable for any disloyalty.

The Pattern Across Three Mechanisms

Legal accountability imposed minimal penalties that didn’t deter future behavior and became largely unavailable once Trump gained political power.

Electoral accountability failed because enough voters tolerate, dismiss, rationalize, or actively support Trump’s racism—and because single-issue voting, tribalism, and misinformation insulate him from consequences.

Party accountability failed because Republicans fear Trump more than they fear the consequences of enabling racism, and because Trump has inverted the relationship—controlling the party rather than being constrained by it.

Each mechanism has failed for different reasons, but they share a common thread: accountability requires both the power to impose consequences and the will to use that power. The power often exists, but the will does not.

Conclusion

Three major accountability mechanisms have failed when applied to Trump’s fifty-year pattern of racism. The law documented violations but imposed minimal penalties. Elections rewarded rather than punished racism. Political parties protected rather than constrained him.

But these are not the only mechanisms that should enforce accountability. In Part 28, we examine three more: institutional accountability, media accountability, and social accountability—and why they have failed just as completely.
