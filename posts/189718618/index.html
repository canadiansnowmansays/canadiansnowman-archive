The Department of War Got Its War

Part 2 of 2: The Breakthrough, the Betrayal, and the Fracture

March 2, 2026

Photo Illustration by Damon Winter/The New York Times

Trump’s Foreign Policy Series CanadianSnowmanSays — March 2, 2026

In Part 1 of this essay, I laid out the timeline from the September 2025 DOW rename to the February 28, 2026 launch of Operation Epic Fury — a trajectory from branding to bombs conducted almost entirely in public. The rename didn’t cause the war. It branded an intention that was already in motion.

Part 2 examines what may ultimately prove more consequential: the diplomatic breakthrough that was ignored, the intelligence that contradicted the public rationale, the congressional authority that was bypassed, and the fracture now tearing through the MAGA coalition over a war its members were explicitly promised would never happen.

The Breakthrough Nobody Wanted

Posted in IG 4 days ago by Iran state-controlled media

Tehran, IRNA – Foreign Minister of Oman, Sayyid Badr bin Hamad Al Busaidi has announced a "significant progress" in third round of indirect nuclear talks between Iran and the United States, adding that the discussions will continue at the technical level next week in Vienna.

The most damning evidence that this war was predetermined isn’t found in the military buildup or the DOW rebranding. It’s found in a CBS News interview that aired on February 27, 2026 — less than 24 hours before the first bombs fell.

Omani Foreign Minister Badr Al Busaidi, who had been mediating indirect talks between the U.S. and Iran in Geneva, sat down with Margaret Brennan on Face the Nation and described what he called a historic breakthrough. Iran, he said, had agreed to three things that no previous negotiation had ever achieved: a commitment to never stockpile enriched uranium, full and comprehensive verification by the International Atomic Energy Agency, and an agreement to degrade its current stockpiles to the lowest possible level and convert them irreversibly into fuel.

“If the ultimate objective is to ensure forever that Iran cannot have a nuclear bomb, I think we have cracked that problem through these negotiations by agreeing a very important breakthrough that has never been achieved any time before,” Al Busaidi told Brennan. He estimated that all remaining issues could be resolved “amicably and comprehensively” within three months.

Read that again. The mediator — a trusted intermediary who had been conducting shuttle diplomacy between the two sides for months — was publicly stating that the core objective of U.S. policy toward Iran had been achieved through negotiation. Iran had agreed to the thing America said it wanted.

Then Brennan asked the question that will define this moment historically: “To be clear, you were helping with the diplomacy last summer, and in the middle of that diplomacy is when the United States and Israel carried out the bombing of the nuclear sites. The U.S. hit those three nuclear sites. Are you concerned that that’s what’s happening now?”

Al Busaidi’s answer: “I’m very concerned, like you and like everyone else, the whole world is concerned about this.”

He was right to be concerned. The strikes began the next morning.

The Dismissal

The day after this breakthrough announcement, Trump told reporters he was “not exactly happy” with the way negotiations were going. Iran, he said, was “not willing to give us what we have to have.”

But what, exactly, did the U.S. “have to have” that Iran hadn’t offered? Zero stockpiling of enriched uranium. Full IAEA verification. Irreversible degradation of existing materials. If the stated objective was to ensure Iran could never build a nuclear bomb, the Omani mediator had just described exactly that achievement.

The disconnect between the mediator’s assessment and Washington’s characterization is stark. Al Busaidi described a breakthrough. Bloomberg reported that the Americans left Geneva disappointed. Both cannot be true in the way they were presented. Either Iran had made unprecedented concessions that addressed the core American demand, or it hadn’t. The mediator, who was in the room, said it had.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio had insisted that any deal cover not just the nuclear program but also Iran’s missiles — a demand that effectively moved the goalposts beyond what any nuclear negotiation could achieve. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi had said the talks focused specifically on the nuclear program and that broader issues could be addressed subsequently. The question of whether missile coverage was a legitimate prerequisite or a deliberate poison pill designed to ensure negotiations would fail is one that historians will debate. The timing suggests the latter.

The June Precedent

What makes the diplomatic sabotage particularly significant is that it had happened before — in almost identical circumstances.

In June 2025, Oman was mediating talks between the U.S. and Iran when the 60-day deadline Trump had set expired. Israel attacked Iran, followed by U.S. strikes on Iran’s three major nuclear facilities. The negotiations were blown up mid-process.

Margaret Brennan’s question to Al Busaidi wasn’t hypothetical. She was asking whether history was repeating itself, with the same mediator, in the same diplomatic framework. It was. The pattern is now established: the Trump administration enters negotiations, maintains military pressure throughout, and strikes while talks are still producing results — then claims diplomacy was “exhausted.”

This is not what exhausting diplomacy looks like. This is what using diplomacy as cover looks like.

The Intelligence That Didn’t Support the War

If the diplomatic record is damning, the intelligence record may be worse.

Trump’s public case for war rested on the claim that Iran posed an imminent nuclear and missile threat to the United States. In his State of the Union address in late February, he alleged Iran was developing missiles capable of striking American soil.

This claim was disputed by his own intelligence community. The Defense Intelligence Agency had previously assessed that it would be at least a decade before Iran could develop the technical capability to produce missiles that could reach the United States. The DIA had also assessed that Iran’s nuclear program was set back by two years following the June 2025 strikes — the strikes Trump himself said had “obliterated” Iran’s nuclear capabilities.

So which was it? Was the nuclear program obliterated in June 2025, or was it an imminent threat in February 2026? Both claims came from the same president. They cannot both be true.

Perhaps most critically, PBS reported that in private congressional briefings after the strikes, administration officials acknowledged that U.S. intelligence did not suggest Iran was preparing to launch a preemptive strike against the United States. The administration instead pointed to a “more general threat” from Iran’s missiles and proxy forces — a framing significantly different from the “imminent threat” language used publicly.

The parallels to Iraq in 2003 are unavoidable and have been noted by multiple analysts. In both cases, the administration made claims about weapons capabilities that were disputed by the intelligence community, launched military operations while diplomacy was actively producing results, presented shifting justifications for action, and bypassed congressional authorization. The difference is that in 2003, the intelligence failures took years to become public. In 2026, the contradictions were visible within 48 hours.

The Constitutional Crisis Nobody Is Calling a Crisis

Operation Epic Fury was launched without congressional authorization. This is not a matter of interpretation — it is a matter of constitutional text.

Article I of the U.S. Constitution gives Congress, not the president, the power to declare war. There is no existing Authorization for Use of Military Force that covers Iran. The 2001 AUMF targeted al Qaeda and associated forces in the aftermath of September 11. The 2002 AUMF authorized the invasion of Iraq. Neither applies to a regime-change war against a sovereign nation that the president’s own intelligence community says posed no imminent threat of attack.

Legal scholar Ilya Somin of George Mason University put it bluntly: “This is very obviously a war. You don’t have to take my word for that — Trump himself says it’s a war.” And that’s the constitutional trap the administration has set for itself. Trump used the word “war” repeatedly in his announcements. Hegseth calls himself the “Secretary of War.” Constitutional scholars across the political spectrum agree that when the president launches what he himself describes as a war, Congress’s exclusive authority to declare war is triggered.

The War Powers Resolution of 1973 requires the president to notify Congress within 48 hours of committing forces to hostilities and to cease operations within 60 days without authorization. Secretary of State Rubio notified seven of the eight Gang of Eight members before the strikes — but they were not given a full accounting of the legal justification. The full Congress received no advance briefing.

The House and Senate are set to vote on war powers resolutions as early as March 4 — the Massie-Khanna resolution in the House and the Paul-Kaine resolution in the Senate. Previous attempts to pass similar measures after the June 2025 strikes and the Venezuela raid both failed. The odds of success remain low given Republican majority loyalty, and even if passed, no war powers resolution veto has ever been overridden.

But the constitutional question extends beyond the immediate votes. The pattern is now established: drug boats in the Caribbean, no congressional authorization. Venezuela raid, no congressional authorization. Iran nuclear strikes in June 2025, no congressional authorization. Full-scale regime-change war in February 2026, no congressional authorization. Each operation was larger than the last. Each further eroded the constitutional requirement of congressional consent. Each made the next unilateral action easier.

Representative Jim Himes, the ranking Democrat on the House Intelligence Committee, called the upcoming vote “the Iraq war vote” of this generation. He may be right — but the Iraq war vote at least happened before the invasion.

The MAGA Fracture

The Iran war has produced something that seemed impossible six months ago: a genuine split in the MAGA coalition over the foundational question of what “America First” means.

The speed of the fracture has been remarkable. Tucker Carlson — who visited the White House just the previous week — told ABC News that Trump’s decision to attack Iran was “absolutely disgusting and evil” and predicted it would “shuffle the deck in a profound way.”

Former Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene, who had already broken with Trump over the Epstein files, unleashed a blistering statement: “The Trump admin actually asked in a poll how many casualties voters were willing to accept in a war with Iran??? How about ZERO you bunch of sick f***ing liars. We voted for America First and ZERO wars. We said ‘No More Foreign Wars, No More Regime Change!’ We said it on rally stage after rally stage, speech after speech. Trump, Vance, basically the entire admin campaigned on it.”

Senator Rand Paul quoted James Madison: “The Executive Branch is the branch most prone to war, therefore, the Constitution, with studied care, delegated the war power to the legislature.” Representative Thomas Massie posted: “I am opposed to this War. This is not ‘America First.’” Representative Warren Davidson, a former Army Ranger, was asked if he supported the strikes. His answer: “No. War requires Congressional authorization.”

Perhaps most haunting is the resurrection of Charlie Kirk’s voice. The late conservative activist and Turning Point USA founder, who was assassinated in September 2025, had warned in June 2025 that regime change in Iran was “pathologically insane” and would result in “a bloody civil war.” MAGA influencers circulated his clips widely over the weekend, invoking a dead man’s warnings against the war his movement’s leader chose to launch.

On the other side, the pro-war faction was equally vehement. Fox News host Mark Levin called Carlson a “deranged traitor” and a “disgusting Woke Reich lowlife” for criticizing the strikes during an active military operation. Dinesh D’Souza sneered that “Tucker’s one-man diplomacy on behalf of the Iranian regime appears to have failed.” Sean Hannity compared Iran’s regime to Nazi Germany and asked: “If you knew after what Hitler did, you could stop it ahead of time, wouldn’t you want to do it?”

The Wall Street Journal editorial board, most GOP congressional leadership, and the FDD Action network backed the strikes enthusiastically.

But the numbers tell a different story than the pro-war commentariat. A Reuters/Ipsos poll released March 1 found that only 25% of Americans approve of the Iran attack. Roughly half believe Trump uses “too much” military force. 43% disapprove of the strikes outright.

As Axios reported, some of the Trump officials now executing or tacitly supporting the Iran war spent years arguing it should never happen. The article noted what may be the most telling indicator of where this is heading politically: “Whether launching a regime-change war in Iran was worth American lives is a question that will dominate debate inside MAGA, Congress and the country for the rest of Trump’s presidency.”

The TACO Framework

For readers familiar with this series, the DOW-to-Epic Fury trajectory maps cleanly onto the TACO framework I’ve been using to analyze Trump’s behavioral patterns: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate.

Threaten: The 10-day deadline. The largest carrier buildup since 2003. The State of the Union claims about Iranian missiles reaching America. “We are locked and loaded and ready to go.”

Accommodate: Enter negotiations through Oman. Hold three rounds of talks. Express public desire for a deal. Create the appearance of having exhausted diplomacy while simultaneously deploying a massive military force to the region. The accommodation phase isn’t about achieving peace — it’s about inoculating the administration against the charge of not having tried.

Capitulate: Congressional Republicans defer to presidential war authority despite the anti-war MAGA base. Media coverage focuses on the strikes rather than the diplomatic breakthrough. The public receives a fait accompli at 2 AM on a Saturday. The Overton window, moved incrementally from drug boats to Venezuela to Iran, has done its work.

Obfuscate: The justifications shift in real time. First it was the Iranian protesters. Then the nuclear program — the same one “obliterated” eight months earlier. Then regime change — except “this is not a so-called regime change war, but the regime sure did change.” Private intelligence briefings contradict public claims. No public legal justification is offered. Hegseth refuses basic operational details at the Pentagon podium and snaps at reporters for asking.

The TACO cycle depends on each phase creating cover for the next. The threats establish that action is possible. The accommodation establishes that diplomacy was tried. The capitulation of institutional checks removes obstacles. The obfuscation ensures no single justification can be pinned down long enough to be evaluated.

What’s in a Name?

Let me return to where we started: the Department of War.

The original 1947 rename from the Department of War to the Department of Defense was not, as Trump claimed, an act of political correctness. It was a deliberate choice by a generation that had just witnessed the most destructive conflict in human history. They understood something that seems to have been forgotten: that the words a nation uses to describe its military institutions shape how those institutions are understood, both by the nation and by the world. Calling it the Department of Defense was a statement of purpose. It said: our military exists to prevent war, not to wage it.

The 2025 reversal rejected that principle. And here is where I’ll refine the framing from my original question about whether the rename “incentivized” war. That was the wrong word. The rename didn’t incentivize anything. It normalized what was coming.

When the Department of War goes to war, it is fulfilling its stated mission. When the Department of Defense goes to war, it represents a failure of its stated mission. That linguistic shift matters enormously in how the public processes military action. The DOW brand conditioned Americans to think of their defense establishment as a war-fighting institution — so that when it fought a war, the response was not “how did deterrence fail?” but “the Department of War is doing what it does.”

The name didn’t cause the war. But when you rename the Department of Defense to the Department of War, tell Norway you’re no longer bound by obligations of peace, ignore a diplomatic breakthrough that achieved your stated objectives, override your own intelligence community, bypass Congress at 2 AM on a Saturday, and then refuse to explain what you’re doing or why — at what point does the branding become indistinguishable from the strategy?

The Department of War has its war now. The Secretary of War has his war. The question — the one Hegseth won’t answer and the American public hasn’t been given the information to assess — is whether anyone planned for what comes after.

Because the people who renamed the department to the Department of War don’t appear to have spent much time thinking about what happens when the war is over. Then again, the original Department of War existed for 158 years. This new one needed only five months to fulfill its promise.
