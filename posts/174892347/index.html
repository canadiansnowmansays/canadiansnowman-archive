The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 2 - Trump, MAGA, and the Fascism Checklist

Measuring the Right Against History

October 1, 2025

In Part 1, we established what fascism actually is—not as an insult, but as a specific historical phenomenon with identifiable characteristics. We saw how Mussolini perverted a democratic unification movement into violent authoritarianism, and how Hitler refined that model into industrialized genocide.

Now comes the harder work: honestly measuring contemporary American politics against that historical reality. We have our checklist of fascism’s defining characteristics. Let’s see where Trump and the MAGA movement actually line up with Mussolini and Hitler.

This won’t be comfortable. But if we’re serious about preventing authoritarianism, we need to be honest about what we’re seeing.

The Checklist, Revisited

As a reminder, here are the defining characteristics of historical fascism:

  1. Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology

  2. Violent suppression of opposition

  3. Scapegoating of minorities and “others”

  4. Fusion of state and corporate power

  5. Control of information and attack on press

  6. Rewriting of education and history

  7. Cult of personality and the strongman

  8. Rejection of democratic norms and institutions

  9. Glorification of violence and militarism

  10. Nostalgia for a mythical golden age

Let’s examine Trump and the MAGA movement against each of these.

Trump and MAGA: A Characteristic-by-Characteristic Analysis

1. Ultranationalism and “Blood and Soil” Ideology

This is perhaps the clearest parallel. Trump’s entire political brand is built on “America First”—not as a policy preference for prioritizing American interests (which every nation does), but as an explicit rejection of international cooperation and multilateral institutions. His 2017 inaugural address painted America as a victim of the world: “For many decades, we’ve enriched foreign industry at the expense of American industry... One by one, the factories shuttered and left our shores.”

But it goes deeper than economic nationalism. Trump has consistently defined who counts as a “real American” in ways that echo Mussolini’s approach. His political rise began with birtherism—the insistence that Barack Obama, despite being born in Hawaii, couldn’t really be American because of his father’s Kenyan heritage and his middle name. The subtext was clear: real Americans look a certain way, have certain backgrounds.

His campaign announcement in 2015 framed Mexican immigrants as rapists and criminals. Throughout his presidency, he described majority-Black cities like Baltimore as “rat and rodent infested” and “disgusting.” He told four congresswomen of color, three of whom were born in the United States, to “go back” to the countries they came from. When white supremacists marched in Charlottesville chanting “Jews will not replace us,” Trump said there were “very fine people on both sides.”

The “replacement” rhetoric is particularly telling. Trump has amplified claims that Democrats are intentionally importing immigrants to “replace” native-born Americans and dilute their political power. This is the exact same narrative Mussolini used about foreign races weakening Italian culture. It’s not about immigration policy—it’s about defining the nation in ethnic and racial terms.

Does this characteristic fit? Yes, substantially. While Trump hasn’t explicitly embraced racial pseudo-science the way Hitler did, his political movement is built on defining American identity in exclusionary terms and positioning non-white immigrants and citizens as threats to the “real” America.

2. Violent Suppression of Opposition

This is where we need to be precise, but also where recent developments demand attention. Trump has consistently encouraged violence against opponents and critics. At his rallies, he has said of protesters: “I’d like to punch him in the face,” “knock the crap out of them,” and “in the old days,” they’d “be carried out on stretchers.” He offered to pay legal fees for supporters who assaulted protesters. When his supporters chanted “send her back” about Congresswoman Ilhan Omar, he let it continue.

Most significantly, on January 6, 2021, after months of falsely claiming the election was stolen, Trump directed his supporters to march on the Capitol. What followed was a violent assault on the seat of American democracy, with his supporters attacking police officers, breaking into the building, and attempting to stop the constitutional process of certifying electoral votes. Trump watched it unfold on television for hours before telling them to go home, and even then, he told them “we love you, you’re very special.”

But there’s a more direct parallel to Mussolini’s Blackshirts that deserves examination: Trump’s use of federal forces as a tool of political intimidation and enforcement. During the George Floyd protests in 2020, Trump deployed federal agents to Portland over the objections of local officials. These agents, wearing military-style gear but lacking clear identification, detained protesters in unmarked vehicles and used violent tactics against crowds. Trump threatened to send federal forces to other Democratic-led cities, framing urban areas as war zones that local officials were too weak to control.

More recently, Trump has deployed federal troops and agents to cities like Washington DC, Los Angeles, and Portland, ostensibly to assist ICE with immigration enforcement. As recently as September 2025, National Guard troops were sent to Portland, Chicago, and Memphis over the objections of Democratic mayors—a pattern of federal force overriding local democratic authority. Oregon has filed lawsuits challenging these deployments as unconstitutional overreach. But the pattern is clear: federal force is being used in cities that didn’t vote for him, over the objections of local officials, targeting populations he has scapegoated throughout his political career. ICE raids under Trump have increasingly resembled paramilitary operations—armed agents conducting mass arrests, separating families, detaining people in conditions that international observers have compared to concentration camps.

The detention facilities at the border, where migrant children were separated from parents and held in cages, where people lack basic sanitation and medical care, where deaths have occurred—these are detention camps with conditions that international observers have compared to concentration camps. They’re places where people are concentrated based on their ethnicity and nationality, held in dehumanizing conditions as a deliberate deterrent. When Trump describes immigrants as “animals” who are “poisoning the blood of our country,” and then deploys federal forces to round them up, the historical parallel becomes harder to ignore.

This isn’t yet Mussolini’s Blackshirts beating opposition politicians in the streets, but it’s the use of federal force as a political weapon, targeting populations that have been scapegoated and dehumanized, in defiance of local democratic authority. It’s state violence used to terrorize vulnerable populations and send a message about who has power.

Does this characteristic fit? Substantially. The deployment of federal forces to Democratic cities, the ICE raids, the detention camps, and the deliberate cruelty of family separation all represent state violence used as a political tool in ways that echo fascist tactics.

3. Scapegoating of Minorities and “Others”

This is unambiguous. Trump’s political strategy has consistently relied on identifying groups to blame for America’s problems, presenting them as both inferior and threatening.

Immigrants, particularly from Latin America and Muslim-majority countries, have been his primary targets. Beyond calling Mexican immigrants rapists and criminals, he implemented a “Muslim ban” that targeted travelers from several Muslim-majority nations. He described asylum seekers as an “invasion” and claimed without evidence that they included “Middle Eastern” terrorists. He separated migrant children from their parents as a deliberate deterrent policy. He described immigrants as “poisoning the blood of our country”—language that directly echoes Nazi rhetoric about racial purity.

He has falsely claimed that Haitian immigrants in Ohio were eating people’s pets, amplifying racist stereotypes to stoke fear and outrage. He has described predominantly Black urban areas as hellscapes and routinely conflates Blackness with criminality and disorder.

He has attacked transgender people, describing them as threats to children and women, proposing to ban them from sports and bathrooms, and making their existence a central culture war issue to mobilize his base.

When protests against police brutality erupted after George Floyd’s murder, Trump described protesters as “thugs” and threatened to deploy the military against them. He staged a photo op in which peaceful protesters were violently cleared from Lafayette Square so he could hold up a Bible in front of a church.

The pattern is consistent: identify a minority group, exaggerate or fabricate threats they pose, use them as an explanation for social problems, and promise to protect “real Americans” from them.

Does this characteristic fit? Yes, completely. This is textbook fascist scapegoating.

4. Fusion of State and Corporate Power

This is more complicated. Trump did not nationalize industries or create the kind of formal corporatist structure Mussolini built. However, he consistently used state power to reward corporate allies and punish corporate critics.

He delivered a massive tax cut that overwhelmingly benefited corporations and the wealthy. He gutted regulations on businesses across sectors. He appointed corporate executives and lobbyists to lead agencies meant to regulate their industries—the Environmental Protection Agency was led by a former coal lobbyist, the Labor Department by a corporate lawyer who fought worker protections.

More telling is how he wielded power against corporations that crossed him. He threatened to revoke NBC’s broadcasting license over critical coverage. He tried to block the AT&T-Time Warner merger because CNN (owned by Time Warner) covered him negatively. He attacked Amazon because its CEO Jeff Bezos owned the Washington Post, which investigated him. He threatened John Deere with tariffs if they moved manufacturing to Mexico.

Trump’s economic vision isn’t about free markets or worker power—it’s about using state authority to reward loyalty and punish disloyalty. The measure of a corporation isn’t its innovation or service to consumers, but its relationship to Trump.

Does this characteristic fit? Partially. Not the formal corporatist structure of Mussolini’s Italy, but a clear pattern of using state power to serve Trump-aligned business interests and punish critics.

5. Control of Information and Attack on Press

Trump has not shut down newspapers or imprisoned journalists. The American press remains free in the legal sense. But he has waged an unprecedented assault on the legitimacy of independent journalism.

He has called the press “the enemy of the people”—language borrowed directly from Stalin. He has described negative coverage as “fake news,” training his supporters to reject any information that contradicts him. He has threatened to revoke broadcasting licenses and change libel laws to make it easier to sue news organizations. He has encouraged violence against journalists, and at his rallies, supporters have harassed and threatened reporters in attendance.

His attacks extend beyond news coverage to entertainment that mocks him. He has threatened to investigate NBC over Saturday Night Live’s satirical portrayal of him, suggesting the network should face consequences for comedy sketches. He has similarly attacked late-night hosts like Jimmy Fallon and Stephen Colbert, suggesting that their shows amount to illegal campaign contributions or should face FCC scrutiny. The message is clear: not just news coverage, but any public mockery or criticism could bring federal retaliation. This is the mindset of an authoritarian who cannot tolerate criticism in any form.

He has lied at a scale unprecedented in American politics—the Washington Post documented more than 30,000 false or misleading claims during his presidency. When fact-checked, he simply doubles down and attacks the fact-checkers. Truth becomes whatever he says it is.

His relationship with Fox News during his presidency resembled state media more than independent journalism—the network frequently coordinated with his administration, promoted his narratives uncritically, and its hosts advised him on policy and political strategy.

Most significantly, he has created an alternative information ecosystem where his supporters trust nothing except sources that affirm what Trump says. Not because he controls all media—he doesn’t—but because he’s successfully delegitimized everything else.

Does this characteristic fit? Substantially. Not full state control of media, but a systematic effort to destroy the public’s ability to distinguish truth from lies, making independent journalism ineffective even where it remains free.

6. Rewriting of Education and History

Trump created the “1776 Commission” explicitly to counter teaching about racism in American history. He threatened to cut federal funding to schools that used the 1619 Project, a historical examination of slavery’s central role in American development. He banned diversity training in federal agencies, calling it “divisive” anti-American propaganda.

His attacks on higher education have been even more direct and threatening. He has repeatedly targeted Harvard and other elite universities, threatening to revoke their tax-exempt status and cut federal funding over how they handle campus protests, particularly regarding Israel and Palestine. He has demanded universities eliminate diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, framing them as discrimination against white students. He has threatened funding cuts to universities that allow certain speakers or curriculum he deems anti-American. He has also threatened to ban or severely restrict Chinese students from American universities, framing them as national security threats and potential spies—a sweeping ethnic generalization that would deny educational access based on nationality. Some universities have capitulated to these threats, shutting down DEI offices and restricting campus speech. Others have resisted, but the pressure is constant and escalating.

This represents direct federal interference in university autonomy—using the threat of financial ruin to dictate what can be taught, who can speak, who can study, and what values institutions can promote. It’s not just about individual curriculum decisions; it’s about bringing higher education under ideological control through economic coercion and ethnic exclusion.

Republican officials aligned with Trump have pushed laws banning “critical race theory” in schools—a term they’ve expanded to mean virtually any teaching about systemic racism or historical injustice. Books about LGBTQ people and racism have been banned from school libraries across Republican-controlled states. Florida’s “Don’t Say Gay” law and its restrictions on teaching about racism are examples of this trend.

Trump has promoted a nationalist mythology of American history—one where the nation’s founding was purely noble, westward expansion was manifest destiny rather than genocide, and systemic racism is a lie invented by the left. He has described attempts to teach accurate history as “child abuse” and “a form of child abuse.”

This isn’t yet the total control of education that Mussolini achieved, but the direction is clear: suppress teaching that complicates nationalist mythology, ban materials that humanize marginalized groups, use federal funding as a weapon to enforce ideological conformity, exclude students based on ethnicity and nationality, and frame education as indoctrination whenever it contradicts Trump’s narrative.

Does this characteristic fit? Substantially. Not total control yet, but systematic and escalating efforts to bring education under ideological control through federal coercion.

7. Cult of Personality and the Strongman

This might be the single clearest parallel. Trump’s entire political identity is built on the idea that only he can fix America’s problems. “I alone can fix it,” he declared at the 2016 Republican Convention. His supporters wear his name on hats and shirts, fly flags with his image, display golden statues of him at political conferences, and describe him in quasi-religious terms.

He demands absolute loyalty from everyone around him. Officials who criticize him or refuse to carry out his wishes—no matter how illegal—are labeled traitors and driven out. He has gone through an unprecedented number of cabinet officials and advisors because he cannot tolerate anyone who won’t treat him as infallible.

He describes himself as above the law. He claimed he could shoot someone on Fifth Avenue and not lose supporters. He argued in court that a president could not be prosecuted for any crime committed while in office. He claimed absolute immunity for any action taken as president. He has refused to comply with subpoenas, blocked witnesses from testifying, and described all investigations of him as illegitimate “witch hunts.”

His supporters have internalized this completely. At rallies, they chant his name. They believe his claims that any negative information about him is fabricated by enemies. They view criticism of Trump as criticism of themselves. The MAGA movement isn’t organized around principles or policies that could outlive him—it’s organized around Trump personally.

When he lost the 2020 election, millions of his supporters believed his claims that it was impossible for him to lose legitimately. The cognitive dissonance of Trump losing was so unbearable that they convinced themselves democracy itself must have failed.

Does this characteristic fit? Yes, completely. This is pure cult of personality, indistinguishable from the strongman worship of Mussolini and Hitler.

8. Rejection of Democratic Norms and Institutions

This is the most dangerous parallel. Trump has demonstrated consistent contempt for democratic processes whenever they constrain him.

He refused to commit to accepting the election results in 2016 (which he won). After winning, he falsely claimed he actually won the popular vote and that millions voted illegally. He created a commission to investigate voter fraud, which disbanded without finding evidence because there was none.

In 2020, he spent months before the election claiming it would be rigged. After losing, he insisted he had actually won in a landslide. He pressured state officials to “find” votes. He pressured the Justice Department to declare the election corrupt. He pressured his Vice President to illegally reject electoral votes. When all of that failed, he incited a mob to attack the Capitol and try to stop the certification by force.

He has been indicted for attempting to overturn election results in Georgia and federally. The indictments detail a conspiracy to submit false electors, pressure state officials, and use violence to remain in power despite losing.

He has said he would be a “dictator” only on “day one” if re-elected—a joke that reveals the authoritarian fantasy. He has threatened to prosecute political opponents, saying he would appoint a special prosecutor to investigate the Bidens. He has said that if re-elected, he would purge the federal government of anyone not personally loyal to him.

This isn’t a politician stretching norms or playing hardball. This is someone who views democratic constraints as illegitimate obstacles to his power.

Does this characteristic fit? Yes, completely. Trump attempted a coup. He tried to overturn an election he lost. This is disqualifying in any democracy.

9. Glorification of Violence and Militarism

Trump has consistently expressed admiration for violence and military displays of power. He wanted a military parade like he saw in France. He wanted generals who were loyal to him personally the way he believed (incorrectly) Nazi generals were loyal to Hitler. He complained that his generals weren’t like Hitler’s generals.

He has praised authoritarian leaders who use violence—Putin, Xi, Kim Jong Un, Duterte, Erdogan. He said of China’s crackdown on Tiananmen Square protesters: “they were vicious, they were horrible, but they put it down with strength.” He admired Kim Jong Un’s ability to make his people “sit up at attention.”

He pardoned war criminals convicted of murdering civilians. He loosened rules of engagement and reduced oversight of military operations. He assassinated an Iranian general without congressional authorization, nearly starting a war.

His rhetoric glorifies strength and dominance. He describes himself as a “tough guy” and his opponents as weak. He mocks disabled people, prisoners of war, and anyone he perceives as vulnerable. He admires strength for its own sake, regardless of how it’s used.

Does this characteristic fit? Substantially. Not the militarism of starting major wars, but a clear pattern of admiring and encouraging violence as a demonstration of power.

10. Nostalgia for a Mythical Golden Age

“Make America Great Again” is built entirely on this. The slogan itself demands return to a past that was greater—though Trump has never been specific about when exactly America was greater or for whom.

The implication is clear: America was greater before civil rights, before women’s liberation, before immigration changed the country’s demographics, before LGBTQ rights, before political correctness supposedly silenced “real Americans.” It was greater when hierarchies were clearer and enforced, when white Christian men faced no challenge to their dominance.

This is the same nostalgia Mussolini weaponized with his appeals to the glory of Rome. A mythical past when the nation was pure, strong, and respected becomes the justification for purging everything that’s changed since then.

Does this characteristic fit? Yes, completely. MAGA is fundamentally about restoring a mythical past through rejecting the present.

The MAGA Movement Beyond Trump

It’s important to note that Trump didn’t create these tendencies from nothing—he activated and empowered them. The Republican Party has been moving in this direction for years: questioning Obama’s legitimacy, obstructing democratic processes, stoking racial resentment, building alternative media ecosystems, and prioritizing power over principles.

Trump accelerated and crystallized these tendencies. Elected Republicans who initially criticized him as unfit have almost entirely capitulated. Those who resist—Liz Cheney, Adam Kinzinger, Mitt Romney—are driven from the party. What remains is a movement organized around Trump’s personality and willing to embrace authoritarian tactics to maintain power.

Republican-controlled states have passed laws restricting voting access, gerrymandering districts to entrench minority rule, and giving legislatures power to override election results. The party has moved from normal conservative politics to something more dangerous.

The Scorecard

Let’s be clear about what we’ve found. Trump and the MAGA movement, measured against the ten defining characteristics of historical fascism:

  1. Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology: Yes, substantially

  2. Violent suppression of opposition: Substantially

  3. Scapegoating of minorities: Yes, completely

  4. Fusion of state and corporate power: Partially

  5. Control of information and attack on press: Substantially

  6. Rewriting of education and history: Substantially

  7. Cult of personality and the strongman: Yes, completely

  8. Rejection of democratic norms and institutions: Yes, completely

  9. Glorification of violence and militarism: Substantially

  10. Nostalgia for a mythical golden age: Yes, completely

That’s six characteristics that fit completely, three that fit substantially, and one that fits partially.

The pattern of behavior, rhetoric, and political strategy aligns with fascist movements in their rise to power. Mussolini and Hitler didn’t start with death camps—they started with rallies, scapegoating, nationalist mythology, attacks on the press, cults of personality, and attempted coups.
