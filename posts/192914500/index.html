The Pardons Come Due

Addendum: When “Freedom” Became a Crime Wave

April 3, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays

When this series concluded, I wrote that presidential clemency had been transformed from constitutional mercy into political currency. I suggested the reckoning would come. I did not expect it to arrive quite this fast, or quite this ugly.

The New York Times Editorial Board — not a publication given to hyperbole — published a piece on March 31, 2026 with a headline that would have seemed unimaginable in any previous administration: “The People Trump Pardoned Are on a Crime Spree.”

This addendum exists because the story has moved, the numbers have grown, and the cases have gotten worse. Much worse.

What the Times Got Right

The editorial board documented twelve pardoned January 6 rioters who have since been charged with serious new crimes: child molestation, assault, harassment, murder plots, vandalism, child pornography, stalking, burglary, copper wire theft, death threats against a sitting congressman, and a dog attack that put four people in hospital.

Twelve is a damning number. It is also significantly too low.

By the time the Times went to press, the watchdog organization Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) had documented at least 33 pardoned insurrectionists who had been rearrested, charged, or sentenced for additional crimes since January 6, 2021. House Judiciary Democrats, in their January 2026 report — “One Year Later: Assessing the Public Safety Implications of President Trump’s Mass Pardons of 1,600 January 6 Rioters and Insurrectionists” — confirmed the same figure, with a charge list that reads like a criminologist’s nightmare: child sexual assault, production of child pornography, rape, conspiracy to murder FBI agents, kidnapping, aggravated robbery, reckless homicide, illegal firearms possession, domestic violence by strangulation, and drug trafficking.

Thirty-three. Fourteen months in. The pattern, as CREW’s researchers noted, is not slowing down.

The Cases the Times Didn’t Include

Beyond the twelve the editorial board profiled, several additional cases deserve the attention they haven’t received.

Andrew Taake was sentenced to more than six years for his Jan. 6 role, after the FBI documented him assaulting Capitol Police officers with pepper spray and a metal whip. Trump pardoned him. He was then rearrested on pre-existing charges of online solicitation of a minor — a 2016 incident in which he allegedly sent sexually explicit messages to an undercover law enforcement officer while the case had been suspended during his federal prosecution.

Edward Kelley was one of the first insurrectionists to breach the Capitol. After his pardon, he was convicted of plotting to murder the FBI agents who had investigated his January 6 case. His sentencing, initially scheduled for July 2025, was one of the most chilling post-pardon outcomes of the entire episode: a man freed by presidential clemency, immediately planning to kill the law enforcement officers who had put him there.

John Banuelos had displayed remarkable candor at his original sentencing. “President Trump’s going to be in office six months from now, so I’m not worried about it,” he told the court. He was correct about the pardon. In October 2025, he was arrested on charges of kidnapping and sexual assault related to a 2018 incident in which he had allegedly trapped a woman in his home, beating, strangling, and sexually assaulting her. One presumes he is somewhat more worried now.

Taylor Taranto had been convicted of illegal gun possession and making a threat to blow up the National Institute of Standards and Technology. His Jan. 6 case was dismissed along with everything else. The bomb threat conviction proceeded separately.

Theodore Middendorf’s January 6 case was dismissed by the Trump administration. He had already been sentenced to nineteen years in prison in May 2024 for the sexual assault of a seven-year-old child. The pardon machinery apparently did not pause to notice.

“Colton” (withheld pending full verification) argued during his trial that Trump’s pardon should extend to his child pornography charges, since the material had been discovered during a search related to his January 6 investigation. The court disagreed. He was convicted of receiving child pornography and sentenced to 80 months in federal prison.

In total, six pardoned insurrectionists have been charged with child sex crimes. Six. A number that ought to end careers, terminate political alliances, and prompt a national conversation about what it means when a president calls violent insurrectionists “unbelievable patriots” and “hostages.”

The Restitution Grotesquerie

One detail in the Andrew Paul Johnson case deserves particular attention, because it captures — in a single, stomach-turning detail — the full moral catastrophe of what has been done.

Johnson, you will recall, was serving a one-year federal sentence for his Jan. 6 role when Trump pardoned him. Seven months later, he was back in custody for sexually abusing two children in Florida.

What kept the children quiet? Johnson told them he was expecting millions of dollars in restitution money from the Trump administration, in connection with his January 6 case. He used the pardon itself as a grooming tool. Presidential clemency, transformed into leverage over child victims.

The Trump administration — through pardon attorney Ed Martin and Justice Department official Jonathan Gross — has been actively promoting the idea of financial restitution for pardoned January 6 defendants. The program has not yet been implemented. But Johnson used the promise of it to silence two children.

There is no framework in which this is acceptable. There is no version of “looking backward” that makes this irrelevant. There is no Republican talking point that addresses it.

The Flood the Zone Defense

The Times editorial noted, correctly, that Trump’s self-serving pardons are “so numerous that public attention cannot keep up with them.” Steve Bannon’s “flood the zone” strategy, applied to clemency: do so much, so fast, that the consequences cannot be tracked.

But they can be tracked. CREW tracked them. House Democrats tracked them. The Times tracked twelve of them. This addendum adds more.

And the tracking reveals something beyond individual cases: a pattern. The House Judiciary Democrats’ report identified at least 159 pardon recipients who had already been convicted of violent offences before January 6. One individual, Peter Schwartz, had 38 prior convictions dating back to 1991, including assault with a deadly weapon, battery, and domestic violence. These were not first-time offenders who had a bad day at the Capitol. Many were career criminals who had a bad day at the Capitol while also being career criminals.

The former Pardon Attorney, Liz Oyer — whom this series quoted at length in its final installment — had warned that the traditional 40-lawyer vetting process had been “neutered” under Ed Martin. This is why. The process exists precisely to catch the Andrew Taakes, the Edward Kelleys, the John Banuelos of the world before they receive presidential clemency. Remove the process; receive the consequences.

What the Victims Received

The pardons erased federal criminal accountability. They did not erase everything.

Civil litigation has proceeded. In 2026, a federal jury awarded $500,000 to the widow and estate of Metropolitan Police Officer Jeffrey Smith, who took his own life following the trauma he suffered on January 6. The jury ordered David Walls-Kaufman — a chiropractor who had pleaded guilty to a misdemeanour riot charge — to pay $380,000 in punitive damages and $60,000 in compensation.

The pardon did not protect him from that verdict.

One victim of a pardoned insurrectionist told CBS News in February 2026: “I’m just afraid that I’m going to come home from work one night and he’s going to be right there.”

She is not looking backward. She is looking over her shoulder.

The Political Arithmetic

The Times editorial board concluded with a prescription: the only accountability available is electoral. The midterms. Public opinion. Vote.

They are not wrong, as far as they go. But there is something worth noting from a Canadian vantage point, where we have been watching this with the particular clarity that geographic distance sometimes provides.

The United States is a nation that arrests people at the border for carrying legally purchased cannabis from a neighbouring jurisdiction. It maintains a sex offender registry that follows individuals for life. It sends teenagers to adult prison. It executes people for crimes committed when they were children. It is, by any metric, among the most punitive criminal justice systems in the developed world.

And the same political movement that insists on all of that has released 1,500 violent offenders — including men who were in prison for child sexual abuse — because they stormed a building in service of one man’s refusal to accept an election result.

The contradiction is not subtle. It is not a matter of interpretation. It is documented. It is empirical. It is, at this point, simply a fact about what the Republican Party has chosen to be.

Speaker Mike Johnson: “I stand with him on it.” Representative Jim Jordan: Complained about prison conditions for the rioters. Representative Lauren Boebert: Wanted to give them a guided tour. Senator John Thune: Declined to answer. “Looking backward.”

Thirty-three arrested. Six charged with child sex crimes. Two charged with rape. One convicted of plotting to murder FBI agents. One dead at a traffic stop, armed and resisting.

This is not looking backward. This is the present tense.

A Final Note on Accountability

When this series began, with David Gentile walking out of prison after thirteen days for a $1.6 billion fraud, I asked a simple question: Why should the guilty be pardoned?

The Trump answer, as documented across nine parts, was: because they’re loyal. Because they paid. Because they’re MAGA.

The Jan. 6 pardons added a fourth answer: because they stormed a building on my behalf.

The consequences are now in. The answer the rest of us receive is: thirty-three arrests, six child sex crime charges, two rapes, one murder plot against federal agents, one rioter dead at a traffic stop, one child victim told to stay quiet with the promise of presidential restitution money.

The Constitution grants the pardon power without review. It trusts the president’s decency. The framers, as Liz Oyer noted when she left the office she had dedicated her career to protecting, “were lacking in imagination.”

We no longer have that excuse.
