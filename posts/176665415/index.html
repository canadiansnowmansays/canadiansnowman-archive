#7: "Empathy is a Made-Up Concept and Damaging to Society" - Part II: The Challenge

November 5, 2025

Charlie Kirk dismissed empathy as a “made-up, New Age term” that “does a lot of damage,” favoring sympathy’s detached clarity. Let’s test this against neuroscience, Christian theology, evolutionary biology, and basic human morality—using Kirk’s prized tools of evidence, logic, and faith.

Empathy Exists (Neuroscience)

Kirk called empathy a fabricated psyop, but brain science proves it’s as real as the prefrontal cortex.

The Science of Shared Pain

Research by neuroscientist Jean Decety at the University of Chicago shows empathy activates mirror neurons—brain cells that fire both when we act and when we observe others’ actions or emotions. A 2023 meta-analysis in Nature Neuroscience confirmed that observing suffering triggers the anterior insula and cingulate cortex, simulating the pain in the observer and significantly reducing aggressive responses in controlled studies.

Even critics like Paul Bloom, in Against Empathy (2016), acknowledge its existence—they debate its limits, not its reality. Dismissing it as “invented” ignores over a century of empirical validation, contradicting Kirk’s “facts over feelings” mantra. The term dates to 1909, but the capacity is evident in infant distress syncing, predating any “New Age” influence.

It Evolved for Good Reasons (Biology)

If empathy “clouds truth,” why did it evolve as a survival mechanism?

Cooperation’s Edge

Charles Darwin noted empathy’s role in The Descent of Man (1871): It binds groups, enabling altruism beyond kin. Modern evolutionary biology, including Frans de Waal’s primate studies (e.g., chimps consoling distressed peers in Annual Review of Psychology, 2008), backs this: Empathetic cultures show correlations with lower conflict rates and stronger economies.

Kirk’s preferred “sympathy” (pity from afar) is evolutionarily weaker—non-human animals demonstrate full empathy, like rats freeing trapped companions without reward. Societies lacking it devolve into zero-sum brutality. Kirk’s view inverts biology: Empathy forges civilizations; its absence dooms them.

It’s Biblically Mandated (Theology)

Kirk invoked faith to elevate sympathy over empathy, but Scripture demands immersive compassion.

Biblical Empathy in Action

Jesus didn’t sympathize from a distance—he empathized:

  • Matthew 9:36: “When he saw the crowds, he had compassion on them, because they were harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd” (Greek splanchnizomai: gut-wrenching empathy)

  • The Good Samaritan (Luke 10:33-35) binds wounds and pays costs—not abstract pity

  • Golden Rule (Matthew 7:12): “Do to others what you would have them do to you”—requires feeling their shoes

Hebrews 4:15 describes Jesus as our high priest who “empathizes with our weaknesses,” having been tempted as we are. 2 Corinthians 1:3-4 calls God “the Father of compassion and the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our troubles, so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God.”

Theologians like C.S. Lewis (Mere Christianity) affirm: Empathy is God’s image in us, fueling agape love. Kirk’s “toxic empathy” echoes Pharisees’ legalism, not Christ’s tears for Jerusalem (Luke 19:41).

Pro-Life Parallel

Kirk championed fetal empathy as sacred—yet rejected it for migrants or the marginalized? This selective approach violates Leviticus 19:34: “The foreigner residing among you must be treated as your native-born. Love them as yourself.”

It Enables Morality (Philosophy)

Kirk saw empathy as a leftist “extortion,” but philosophers prove it’s morality’s foundation.

From Kant to Rawls

Immanuel Kant’s categorical imperative demands universalizing actions—requiring empathetic imagination. John Rawls’ “veil of ignorance” (1971) tests policies by asking: “Would you accept this without knowing your position?” Empathy makes this possible.

Research in personality psychology consistently shows high empathy correlates with lower crime rates, stronger social cohesion, and better parenting. Even Bloom notes “empathy fatigue” in helping professions as a dosage issue, not grounds for denial—cognitive empathy (understanding without overwhelm) balances it, enhancing judgment rather than blurring it.

Kirk’s Alternative Fails His Own Tests (Logical Consistency)

Kirk’s sympathy-only lens presents a false dichotomy: Emotional immersion (damage) vs. detached pity (clarity). This ignores bounded empathy—compassion guided by wisdom—which aligns with his evidence standard. His inconsistent application (empathy for fetuses, sympathy for migrants) contradicts the principled consistency he demanded.

In immigration rants, Kirk decried “empathy for criminals” while quoting Musk on “deep empathy” for victims—selective deployment that undermines his position.

The Ultimate Test: Kirk’s Own Standards

“Prove Me Wrong”

Evidence proves Kirk wrong: Empathy is ancient, adaptive, and essential—no “New Age” hoax.

Evidence-Based Reasoning

He valued facts, yet dismissed neuroscience as irrelevant. This undermines his position.

Christian Values

Kirk’s sympathy elevates detachment over Incarnation—Jesus became us in empathy (Philippians 2:7). True faith demands both.

Protecting Society

Empathy didn’t damage civilization; its erosion (via echo chambers) did. Kirk’s stance risked the division he fought. His sympathy for like-minded groups (e.g., 2023 posts on Chick-fil-A franchisees) showed selective empathy in action—compassion for “his side,” detachment for others. True empathy, per de Waal, transcends teams.

The Tragic Irony

Kirk’s sympathy for franchisees (e.g., his 2023 Chick-fil-A post) showed selective empathy in action—compassion for ‘his side,’ detachment for others. True empathy, per de Waal, transcends teams.

Conclusion: Empathy’s Enduring Power

Kirk warned empathy would unravel society, but evidence—from neurons to Nazareth—shows it weaves us together. We needn’t choose between truth and compassion; empathy sharpens both. Even critics like Bloom affirm its value in measured doses, distinguishing cognitive understanding from unchecked emotion.

The path forward: Bounded empathy, Christian clarity, evidence-guided mercy. Kirk challenged us to prove him wrong. Science, Scripture, and survival do exactly that. Feelings aren’t fiction—they’re the fabric of freedom.
