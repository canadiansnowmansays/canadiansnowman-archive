The Oakland Transcript

The Witness

Day 1 — April 28, 2026

April 29, 2026

Ronald V. Dellums Federal Courthouse, Oakland, California

The Ronald V. Dellums Federal Building and U.S. Courthouse, Oakland, California. Named for a Democratic Socialist who said corporate power was the primary obstacle to human flourishing. Currently hosting the richest lawsuit in the history of artificial intelligence.

A companion series to The Asimov Gap, CanadianSnowmanSays

On May 25, 2015, Sam Altman sent Elon Musk an email proposing a “Manhattan Project for AI” — a Silicon Valley research lab that would build enormously powerful artificial intelligence and share it with the rest of the world via some sort of nonprofit. Musk replied that evening. The idea was “probably worth a conversation.” Before the end of the year, they had founded OpenAI.

Eleven years later, Musk arrived at the Ronald V. Dellums Federal Courthouse through the basement. So did Altman. Security concerns required it — a man believed to be angry about AI had recently been arrested after throwing a firebomb at Altman’s San Francisco home. A gaggle of photographers rushed to the front doors to snap shots through the glass. Lawyers and clerks rolled carts of documents inside. It was 51 degrees.

This is where the governance of artificial intelligence has arrived: two men entering a federal courthouse through the basement, in a city named for a Democratic Socialist who spent his career arguing that corporate power was the primary obstacle to human flourishing, to argue about who broke a promise they made to humanity in an email.

The microphones mostly worked.

What Musk Said

Elon Musk arrives at the federal courthouse as opening statements begin in the trial over Elon Musk’s lawsuit against OpenAI in Oakland, California, on April 28, 2026. Karl Mondon/AFP/Getty Images

Elon Musk took the witness stand as the first witness in his own case, called by his own counsel. He was sworn in at approximately 3 p.m. Pacific time.

His lawyers had a strategy and they executed it with discipline. Before getting to OpenAI, they walked Musk through his entire career — from a stint as a young lumberjack, which surprised many in the courtroom, through PayPal, SpaceX, Tesla, Neuralink, and X. The message to the jury was clear: this is a man who has spent his life trying to save humanity, and what OpenAI did to him was a betrayal of that mission.

“Life just can’t be about solving one miserable problem after another,” Musk told the jury. “There have to be reasons to be excited and inspired about the future.”

He described SpaceX as “life insurance for life as we know it.” He described Neuralink — the company that implants computer chips in people’s skulls — as an AI safety project. “If we can achieve an AI-human symbiosis,” he said, “we can achieve an AI that is better for humanity.”

He explained why he helped found OpenAI. It began with a conversation with Google co-founder Larry Page, who called Musk a “specieist” — a person who favours humans over the digital life-forms of the future. Page believed AI would bring utopia. Musk believed Page was not paying proper attention to the existential risks. “The reason OpenAI exists is because Larry Page called me a specieist,” Musk said. He wanted a counterweight to Google. A company without a profit motive that would freely share its technology with the world.

On the stakes of the case, Musk was unambiguous. “It is not OK to steal a charity,” he said. If Altman and OpenAI win, he added, it would “give license to looting every charity in America.” The consequences, he said, “go far beyond me or everyone here. The entire foundation of charitable giving in America will be destroyed.”

He ventured into science fiction to illustrate the AI stakes. “It could kill us all,” he said. “We don’t want to have a Terminator outcome. We want to be in a Gene Roddenberry outcome, like Star Trek. Not so much a James Cameron movie like Terminator.”

He acknowledged that he was part of early discussions about creating a for-profit arm for OpenAI. But he wanted to keep it small. “A for-profit was fine,” he said, “as long as the tail did not wag the dog.” He painted himself as the driving force behind the nonprofit before it was taken from him. “I came up with the name, recruited the key people, and raised the funding.”

He ultimately said he left because the other founders demanded too much equity in the proposed for-profit arm and the process had become “very annoying.”

The judge brought Day 1 to a close with Musk still on the stand. He will return Wednesday morning.

What OpenAI Said

William Savitt, OpenAI’s lead counsel / Brennan Smart for The New York Times

OpenAI’s lead counsel William Savitt had a simpler argument and he stated it early and often.

“We are here because Musk didn’t get his way at OpenAI. My clients had the nerve to go on and succeed without him. Mr. Musk did not like that.”

Savitt showed the jury emails from the summer of 2017 — from Musk’s own chiefs of staff, Shivon Zilis and Sam Teller — discussing ways to transform OpenAI’s nonprofit into a traditional company or attach a for-profit operation to it. Those emails discussed giving Musk a 55 percent stake in the for-profit entity while giving Altman 7.5 percent. Musk’s own people were designing the commercial structure he now claims was a betrayal.

Savitt also pointed to the Tesla merger email — already entered into evidence — in which Musk told OpenAI leadership in February 2018 that merging with Tesla was “the only path that could even hope to hold a candle to Google.” When Altman and the other founders refused to give Musk control, he quit. He is now suing them for the commercial transformation he himself proposed.

“That’s when the sour grapes kicked in,” Savitt said, referring to the moment ChatGPT launched in 2022 and OpenAI became a global phenomenon. Musk had not objected when Microsoft invested $1 billion in the lab in 2019. He did not object through OpenAI’s years of growth. He objected when it succeeded without him.

Savitt added, with economy: Musk “didn’t understand artificial intelligence very well.”

On OpenAI’s structure, Savitt argued that the original nonprofit foundation “remains in control of the organisation” and is “doing leading edge work to cure diseases and promote economic diversity.” The for-profit arm, he said, was always contemplated — the issue is whether Musk’s vision of strict nonprofit oversight was honoured, not whether a for-profit existed at all.

Microsoft’s lead counsel Russell Cohen made compatible arguments. He noted that Musk continued to donate to OpenAI as it created its for-profit structure and never raised an issue when the Microsoft partnership was announced. “Microsoft knew nothing about this in advance,” Cohen said of the 2023 board firing, describing the situation as “chaotic” for Satya Nadella. Microsoft, he said, was not pulling strings — it was facing its own crisis.

What the Courtroom Revealed

Several details from Day 1 deserve attention beyond the formal testimony.

Before opening statements, Judge Gonzalez Rogers called Musk to the bench to discuss a potential gag order. “How can we get things done without you making things worse outside the courtroom?” she asked. Musk said he was responding to things OpenAI had posted online. The judge asked both men to start with a “clean slate” and keep social media to a minimum. They agreed. Musk had already posted more than two dozen times about the trial on the platform he owns, including calling Altman “Scam Altman” and Brockman “Greg Stockman.” The agreement was made at approximately 11:22 a.m. Pacific time.

Musk’s lead counsel opened by acknowledging that Altman does not have a direct equity stake in OpenAI — a fact that creates an obvious problem for the claim that Altman enriched himself through the nonprofit. Molo argued that Altman enriched himself through companies that do business with OpenAI, and that Altman has said he might take equity in the future. This is a significant vulnerability in Musk’s case and the fact that his own lawyer addressed it in the opening statement suggests the defence will press it hard.

OpenAI’s founding counsel, William Savitt, had previously represented Twitter — now X — against Musk when Musk tried to withdraw from his $44 billion acquisition. He is arguing against his former client’s former adversary. The irony of the assignment was not noted in the courtroom.

Greg Brockman, OpenAI’s president and a named defendant, sat calmly throughout the day, occasionally writing on a small reporter’s notepad and looking up to examine the old emails displayed as evidence. Sam Altman, before the jury was welcomed on Monday, approached a New York Times reporter and said: “I hope you enjoy this.”

Ari Emanuel — Hollywood superagent, Musk friend, member of the investor group that attempted to purchase OpenAI’s nonprofit assets last year in a bid Altman soundly rejected — sat in the gallery behind Musk’s legal team.

Meanwhile, at SpaceX’s facilities in Hawthorne, California, the company was making its case to existing and prospective investors for a public offering expected this summer at a valuation of up to $1.5 trillion. Musk was in Oakland.

One irony noted by Ryan Mac of the New York Times: as Musk argued in court about the sanctity of charitable giving, his own Musk Foundation had failed to give away the legally required minimum of five percent of its assets for four consecutive years. He is also a signatory of the Giving Pledge — the agreement among billionaires to give away at least half their wealth by death — with few details provided about how he intends to honour it. Theodore Schleifer, covering Silicon Valley wealth for the Times, noted that Musk has long argued on X that charitable efforts are often ineffective or riddled with fraud and that the real path to social impact is through for-profit ventures.

The man who founded a nonprofit to save humanity from AI has spent years publicly arguing that nonprofits are a fraud. This came up on Day 1. It will come up again.

What This Confirms

The AI boom's founding feud arrives in federal court. Between the two men at the table, technology companies worth many trillions of dollars. Between them and democratic accountability — a nineteenth century legal doctrine and a nine-person jury. Visual by Grok AI.

Cross-references to The Asimov Gap

The founding promise was real and contemporaneous. The May 25, 2015 Altman email — “Manhattan Project for AI,” via “some sort of nonprofit” — is now in the trial record. So is Musk’s reply. So are the subsequent emails in which all the founders agreed a nonprofit was the right structure. Whatever happened afterward, the promise was made consciously, in writing, by people who understood what they were committing to. This is the factual foundation of the ultra vires argument examined in Part 31.

The Tesla merger email confirms the key irony. Musk’s February 2018 email telling OpenAI to merge with Tesla — “the only path that could even hope to hold a candle to Google” — is now in the trial record under oath. He is suing Altman for the commercial transformation he himself proposed when he thought he would control it. This was flagged in Part 31 as the most significant exhibit of the trial’s opening. Day 1 confirms it is central to OpenAI’s defence.

The 55 percent stake email is new and significant. Musk’s own chiefs of staff were designing a for-profit structure that would give him majority control. This was not in the Farrow-Marantz investigation examined in Part 28. It is now in the trial record. The argument that Musk wanted a nonprofit and Altman wanted a for-profit is considerably more complicated than either side’s opening statement suggested.

The governance vacuum is visible in the room. Ari Emanuel in the gallery. Shivon Zilis on the witness list. Altman and Musk entering through the basement. The world’s richest man on the stand describing a brain-chip company as an AI safety project. OpenAI’s counsel arguing its nonprofit foundation is doing “leading edge work to cure diseases.” The Asimov Gap is not an abstraction. It is in this courtroom, under fluorescent lights, with broken microphones and rolling carts of documents. This is what governance improvised after the fact looks like.

Watch For Wednesday

Musk returns to the stand Wednesday morning for what is expected to be the beginning of OpenAI’s cross-examination. This is the session that matters most for the trial’s first week.

OpenAI’s lawyers will have the 2018 Tesla merger email. They will have the 55 percent stake emails from Musk’s own chiefs of staff. They will have the record of Musk’s donations to OpenAI continuing after the for-profit structure was created. They will have the record of his silence when Microsoft invested $1 billion. They will have his public statements about the inefficacy of nonprofits. And they will have his own for-profit AI company, xAI, which is now part of SpaceX and heading toward a $1.5 trillion IPO.

“My clients had the nerve to go on and succeed without him. Mr. Musk did not like that.”

That sentence is the cross-examination, previewed in the opening statement. Wednesday will test whether it holds.
