The Dynasty They Always Denied

Part XXXIII: Day Six: March 5, 2026

March 5, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 5, 2026

The man in the portrait is dead. The system he built is choosing his son.

They called it a theocracy, not a monarchy. They insisted the Islamic Republic was a system of governance, not a family business, that the supreme leader was chosen by God’s representatives on earth — the 88 clerics of the Assembly of Experts — not by blood. For 47 years, they drew the distinction carefully, knowing that the Shah’s dynasty was the thing the revolution had destroyed, and that installing another one would expose the regime’s founding lie.

On Day Six, Iran International reported that the Assembly of Experts, under direct pressure from the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, has elected Mojtaba Khamenei — the 56-year-old son of the assassinated supreme leader — as Iran’s new supreme leader.

No official announcement has yet come from Tehran. The deliberations are being conducted under wartime conditions, virtually, with the Assembly’s physical offices bombed and its members dispersed. But the reporting is sourced, specific, and consistent with everything that has been known about the IRGC’s preference since Day One.

If confirmed, the Islamic Republic has done the one thing it swore it would never do. It has become a dynasty.

I. The Son the System Chose

Mojtaba Khamenei - File photo

Mojtaba Khamenei has spent decades being invisible by design. He has never run for office, never delivered a Friday sermon, never made a public political address. Most Iranians have never heard his voice. He cultivated this obscurity deliberately, understanding that his power was maximized by proximity to his father rather than visibility to the public.

What he was doing while invisible was building an empire. He forged deep ties to the IRGC from his youth, when he served in the Habib Battalion during the Iran-Iraq War. His comrades from those years went on to lead the security and intelligence apparatus of the Islamic Republic. Mojtaba followed a different path — one through financial networks, political influence, and the Basij paramilitary forces that crushed the Green Movement in 2009, the 2019 protests, and the January 2026 uprising that killed at least seven thousand people.

Western media has linked his name to the movement of billions of dollars through a network of associates connected to the Iranian establishment. His name does not appear directly in the transactions. It never does. That is the point.

His mother, wife, and one of his sisters were killed in the strike that killed his father on February 28. He was reportedly not present. He survived. The IRGC, which had been lobbying for his succession for months before the war began, now has the supreme leader it wanted — battle-hardened by grief, ideologically uncompromising, and owing his position entirely to the military force that put him there.

Trump, who admitted on Tuesday that “most of the people we had in mind are dead,” had suggested he was looking for “somebody that’s there that’s currently popular, if there’s such a person.” Mojtaba Khamenei is not that person. He is the opposite of that person. He is a man the Iranian public did not know existed, chosen by an institution that answers to no public, elevated to absolute power by a revolutionary guard corps that has just watched American bombs kill its previous patron’s family.

Israel’s defense minister Israel Katz had already answered the question of what comes next. If Mojtaba follows his father’s ideology, Katz vowed, he would be “an unequivocal target for elimination.”

The United States has now potentially killed one supreme leader and is pre-threatening to kill the next one before he has officially been announced. This is not a policy. It is an escalation ladder with no visible top rung.

II. The 1953 They Cannot Escape

On Thursday, Donald Trump told Reuters and Axios in separate interviews that the United States should have a role in choosing Iran’s new leader, and that Mojtaba Khamenei is an “unacceptable” choice.

These were not casual remarks. They were the most explicit Trump has been about his vision of what this war is ultimately for. His model, as he told the New York Times on Sunday and has repeated in conversations with world leaders and members of Congress, is Venezuela — where a Delta team seized Nicolás Maduro and sent him to a federal prison in Brooklyn. Trump wants to replicate that operation in a country of 92 million people run by a complex interlocking system of clerics, elected officials, and a revolutionary guard corps that has been preparing for exactly this scenario for forty years.

The analysts noting “huge differences between Iran and Venezuela” are being diplomatic. The differences are total. Venezuela is a collapsing petrostate whose military melted away. Iran is a civilization with a two-thousand-year history, a nuclear program, proxy forces across seven countries, ballistic missiles that have been hitting US allies for six days, and a population that regardless of its feelings about the Islamic Republic does not want an American president to choose its government.

David Sanger’s observation in the Times is the one that will echo: Trump’s comments “are bound to remind Iranians of a period in their history taught in the country’s schools.”

He means 1953.

In August of that year, the CIA — operating under the codename TPAJAX, in coordination with British intelligence — orchestrated the overthrow of Mohammad Mossadegh, Iran’s democratically elected prime minister. Mossadegh had nationalized Iranian oil, cutting off British Petroleum’s access to Iranian reserves. That made him, in Washington’s Cold War calculus, a liability. The coup took three days. It installed Mohammed Reza Pahlavi as the Shah — an absolute monarch backed by American power, whose secret police, SAVAK, became a byword for torture and repression.

The Shah ruled for 26 years. His overthrow in 1979 produced the Islamic Republic, which has governed Iran for the 47 years since. The Islamic Republic’s founding legitimacy rests entirely on the argument that Iran’s sovereignty was stolen by the United States in 1953 and that the revolution reclaimed it. Every child in Iran is taught this history. It is not a grievance from the past. It is the organizing principle of the present.

Barack Obama acknowledged the CIA’s role in the coup in his 2009 Cairo speech, calling it a significant US role in “the overthrow of a democratically elected Iranian government.” Republicans criticized him at the time for appearing to apologize.

Trump’s Thursday interviews have now rendered that controversy quaint. Obama acknowledged a coup that happened 70 years ago. Trump is announcing, in real time, that the United States intends to choose Iran’s next government — while American bombs are still falling on Iranian cities, while the CIA is arming Kurdish forces to cross the border, and while the Assembly of Experts is meeting virtually because its physical offices have been destroyed.

The Iranians who heard Trump’s comments on Thursday did not need Sanger’s historical gloss to understand what they were hearing. They knew immediately. Every Iranian knows 1953 the way Canadians know 1867 or Americans know 1776. It is the year their sovereignty was taken from them by the country that is currently bombing them.

Iran’s foreign minister Abbas Araghchi, in an interview with NBC Nightly News on Thursday, said Iran sees “no reason” to come to the negotiating table, is prepared to confront a US ground invasion, and has had zero communication with Trump’s special envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner since the Oman talks were interrupted last Saturday by the bombs.

The back-channel that Iran opened on Day Two — reaching out through a third country to the CIA — was already closed. Trump said “too late.” Now Trump is saying he wants to pick the next leader. Araghchi is saying there is nothing to discuss.

Araghchi’s framing of the earlier Geneva nuclear talks — that Trump treated them “like a real estate transaction” and bombed “the negotiation table out of spite” — has acquired new precision. The man across the table from Iran’s negotiators is now publicly announcing he wants the power to select their government’s leadership.

1953 is not history in Iran. It is a template. Trump has now confirmed, on the record, that he intends to use it.

III. The Boots — Just Not American Ones

Tom Nichols said it plainly in the Atlantic panel discussion: regime change requires boots on the ground. They don’t have to be American boots, but somebody’s boots have to be involved. On Day Six, the shape of whose boots those might be came into full view — and the picture is considerably more dangerous than the administration has publicly acknowledged.

CNN confirmed Wednesday that the CIA is actively working to arm Kurdish forces with the explicit aim of fomenting a popular uprising inside Iran. The plan originated with Netanyahu and the Mossad, which has spent years cultivating intelligence networks among Kurdish groups in Iran, Iraq, and Syria. The CIA joined the effort at a later stage. Israeli officials have reportedly promised Kurdish factions not only military support but political support for a Kurdish autonomous region in a post-regime Iran.

PJAK and allied Iranian Kurdish militia fighters on alert in rugged border terrain, as reports confirm CIA arming and positioning inside Iran to stretch regime forces and potentially spark uprisings. (Credit: Al Jazeera / Reuters)

PJAK fighters — the Kurdistan Free Life Party — began taking combat positions inside Iranian territory from midnight on March 2, Day Three of the war. A coalition of five dissident Kurdish Iranian groups had already formed the Coalition of Political Forces of Iranian Kurdistan six days before the war began, suggesting the organizing predated the bombs by at least a week.

Trump’s position on the Kurdish ground offensive is now on the record. He told Reuters on Thursday he is “all for” Kurdish forces waging an offensive against Tehran’s government, while declining to say whether the US would provide military support. “I think it’s wonderful that they want to do that,” he said.

The Kurdistan Regional Government — the official governing body of Iraqi Kurdistan — issued a categorical denial on Thursday, saying it is not part of “any campaign to expand the war and tensions in the region” and that reports linking it to plans to arm and send Kurdish opposition parties into Iran are “completely unfounded.” The denial is precise in its scope: it covers the KRG itself, not the Iranian Kurdish factions — PJAK, PDKI, PAK — that are based in Iraqi Kurdistan but are not subject to its authority. Those factions remain a different matter entirely, and the denial leaves them unaddressed.

Trump spoke separately with Mustafa Hijri, head of the Democratic Party of Iranian Kurdistan, and with Iraqi Kurdish leaders Masoud Barzani and Bafel Talabani. The conversations with Barzani and Talabani went poorly — both expressed serious reservations about getting the Peshmerga involved, citing the catastrophic risk of Iranian retaliation against Iraqi Kurdistan itself. The Governor of Erbil reported that his city had already been attacked by more than 100 Iranian drones and missiles.

When Rubio was asked about arming the Kurds in congressional briefings, he said the US was not doing so. Then he added: “You never know with the Israelis.”

Iraqi Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani told Iran’s foreign minister directly that Iraq would “under no circumstances” allow its territory to be used to attack Iran. The Iranians struck Kurdish positions in Iraqi Kurdistan anyway, killing at least one PAK fighter, signaling they would treat the Kurdish regions of Iraq as a hostile staging ground regardless of Baghdad’s assurances.

Representative Andy Kim of New Jersey, who has worked on Kurdish issues and is generally not given to hyperbole, described what he was seeing plainly: “It very much feels like they are fomenting potentially different groups on the ground to start to get engaged in what will essentially be what happened in Syria before, what happened in Libya before. If they are trying to start a civil war, this is a very good way to go about it.”

A Kurdish official told i24NEWS that a future Kurdish state could serve as “a strategic corridor linking the Persian Gulf to Israel,” and that “Kurds and Jews are natural allies in the Middle East.” The promise of a Kurdish homeland has been dangled before. It was dangled in Syria in 2017, when the US armed and trained Kurdish YPG fighters, used them to defeat ISIS, and then withdrew support when Turkey objected. Those Kurds signed a deal with the Syrian government and integrated into the forces of a new regime that had ISIS ties. The previous betrayal has not been forgotten. Kurdish sources say factions are waiting for the American “green light” before committing fully — because they have learned, at considerable cost, that the green light can turn red without warning.

This is the ground war that the administration says is not happening, while Kurdish forces are already inside Iranian territory and the CIA is negotiating with multiple factions about the terms of their involvement.

IV. The Near-Miss Nobody Briefed

In a CNN exclusive that received far less attention than it deserved, two sources briefed on the operation revealed that Iranian bombers came within minutes of striking Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar — the largest American military installation in the Middle East.

Qatari fighter jets intercepted them before they reached their target.

Al Udeid houses tens of thousands of US personnel, serves as the forward headquarters of US Central Command, and is the primary staging base for the air campaign against Iran. Had those bombers broken through, Day Six would have a completely different character. The press conference metrics about Iranian strikes being “down 86 percent” would be irrelevant. The war would have escalated into territory that no official briefing has prepared the public for.

This was not mentioned by Hegseth. It was not mentioned by Caine. It surfaced through reporting, sourced to people briefed on the operation, days after the event.

The press access blackout that Nancy Youssef described in the Atlantic panel — the first war of this scope she can cover only from her office — has a concrete consequence: the American public learns that its largest Middle East military base nearly took a direct bomber strike from a CNN exclusive, days later, rather than from the people responsible for the war.

V. The Strait That Won’t Open

Marine tracking map showing clustered oil tankers anchored and stalled outside the Strait of Hormuz—commercial traffic near zero due to insurance withdrawal and escalating conflict risks. (credit NBC News)

The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed.

It was not officially declared closed by any binding legal mechanism. Iran’s closure is not legally enforceable under international maritime law. None of that matters. On March 5, protection and indemnity insurance — the coverage that allows ships to operate commercially — was withdrawn for Strait transit. Without it, no commercial ship owner will send a vessel through regardless of military escorts. Over 150 ships are anchored outside the strait, waiting. Traffic is near zero.

Twenty percent of the world’s daily oil supply transits the Strait. Brent crude reached $82.76 a barrel Wednesday, up from around $70 before the war. US crude hit $78.80 intraday — the highest level since January 16, 2025, the last full day of the Biden administration. The Asian LNG benchmark is up 91 percent in one week.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told CNBC Wednesday that the administration would make “a series of announcements on energy transport in the region.” The White House would not provide a timeline for when the Strait would be safe for commercial traffic. Leavitt said the Navy would escort tankers “if and when necessary.”

By Thursday afternoon, Brent crude had reached $85 a barrel — up nearly 20 percent in one week, and up more than 40 percent for the year from $60 at year-end 2025. West Texas Intermediate crossed $80, its highest level since July 2024. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.14 percent, up from 3.93 percent before the war began — the equivalent of one full Federal Reserve interest rate hike absorbed in six days. Gas at the pump hit $3.25. Diesel crossed $4.00. The S&P 500 fell more than one percent Thursday and is now negative for the year.

“If and when necessary” is not a policy. It is a placeholder for a problem that has no good solution. The US Navy can escort military vessels. It cannot simultaneously escort the volume of commercial tanker traffic that 20 percent of global oil supply requires. The Strait has never before been commercially closed during a US military operation in the region. This is new territory.

The economic clock is running independently of the military one. Gas in the United States is at $3.20 and rising. Diesel is over $4.00. A Cornell supply chain professor noted last week that the price spike is moving faster than the post-Ukraine invasion spike in 2022, which ultimately pushed gas to over $5.00 a gallon by June. That June is now a plausible destination, and the midterms are in November.

An LNG tanker bound for Europe was diverted to Asia this week — the first ship to reverse course during the current price spike. Markets are beginning to price in not a temporary shock but a sustained disruption. Oxford Economics is modeling potential global recession scenarios. The gap between what the administration says about energy markets and what is actually happening to them is widening by the day.

VI. Amazon as a Military Target

Iran’s Fars News Agency announced explicitly this week that Amazon’s Bahrain data center was targeted because of the company’s support for “US military and intelligence activities.” Two additional Amazon Web Services data centers in the United Arab Emirates were directly struck by drones. All three remain offline per the AWS health dashboard.

This is a threshold moment that has received insufficient attention. For the first time in a major conflict, a private American technology company’s cloud infrastructure has been explicitly named and publicly targeted as a military objective by an adversary state. Not collateral damage. Not a misidentified target. A deliberate, named strike on commercial infrastructure specifically because of its relationship with the US military and intelligence community.

The implications extend well beyond the current conflict. AWS hosts significant portions of the US government’s classified and unclassified computing infrastructure, along with the data operations of thousands of commercial enterprises worldwide. The physical data centers that make cloud computing possible are buildings in countries that can be hit with drones. Iran just demonstrated that it knows this and is willing to act on it.

The three affected facilities remain offline. No timeline for restoration has been provided. The companies and governments whose data they process are operating on backup or degraded capability. The technology sector’s assumption that its physical infrastructure in the Gulf was insulated from military targeting has been operationally refuted.

VII. Ukraine’s Unexpected Alliance

Volodymyr Zelenskyy confirmed Wednesday that Ukraine has sent drone warfare experts to Gulf states to help defend against Iranian drone attacks. On Thursday, he went further — confirming the United States has formally requested “specific support” from Ukraine for the Middle East theater. “I gave instructions to provide the necessary means and ensure the presence of Ukrainian specialists who can guarantee the required security,” Zelenskyy wrote. “Ukraine helps partners who help ensure our security and protect the lives of our people.”

Britain’s defense ministry confirmed separately on Thursday that British drone specialists who have worked in Ukraine have been sent to the Middle East. Their expertise, the ministry said, could help nations “fending off attacks from drones and ballistic missiles.” One British unit destroyed additional drones overnight in Iraqi airspace that were heading toward coalition forces.

Let the geography of that sentence settle. Ukrainian experts — people who have spent years under attack by Russian drones built using Iranian Shahed technology, developing tactics and countermeasures through brutal trial and error — are now operating in Qatar and other Gulf states, helping American allies defend against those same drones in a completely different theater.

Zelenskyy said more experts would follow, “in a shape and form which would not be detrimental to home security.” He is managing a war on his own territory while seconding specialized personnel to a conflict his country is not party to, because the technology being used against his allies is technology his country knows better than anyone.

The circle is precise and bitter. Iran supplied Russia with the drones that have been killing Ukrainians for years. The US supplied Ukraine with weapons to defend against them. Now Ukrainian expertise is defending US allies against Iranian drones in the Gulf, while Russia amplifies Iranian propaganda about the war. The proxy conflict architecture of the last decade has collapsed into a single interconnected military system in which everyone is fighting everyone through everyone else.

VIII. The First Time for Everything

The war has now produced a series of historical firsts that the official briefings present as metrics of success and that the historical record will examine as evidence of escalation.

The first American submarine torpedo fired in combat since World War II — the IRIS Dena, 180 crew, 87 confirmed dead, 32 rescued, the rest unaccounted for in the Indian Ocean off Sri Lanka.

The first time an F-35 stealth fighter has shot down a manned aircraft in air-to-air combat in history — an Israeli F-35I “Adir” killing an Iranian Yak-130 over Tehran. Also the first Israeli air-to-air kill since 1985.

The first time a major American technology company’s cloud infrastructure has been explicitly named and targeted as a military objective.

The first time Iranian bombers attempted a direct strike on the largest US military base in the Middle East — and were stopped minutes from their target.

The first time the CIA has actively worked to arm Kurdish forces for operations inside Iranian territory during an active American military campaign.

Each of these firsts would, in any previous conflict, have been the subject of extensive congressional debate, public disclosure, legal review, and strategic assessment. In this war, they are mentioned in press briefings, confirmed in reporting, and move on within the news cycle. The pace of escalation is itself a form of normalization. By the time the public registers what has happened, something else has already happened.

IX. The Canadian Angle — Canberra

Mark Carney, in Canberra for a trade meeting with Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese, called Wednesday for “broader de-escalation” of the war — but added the qualification that it “cannot be achieved unless Iran’s ability to acquire a nuclear weapon and export terrorism is ended.”

That formulation is careful to the point of being architectural. Carney is calling for de-escalation while simultaneously endorsing the stated military objectives that the current operation is meant to achieve. It is the position of a government that has “supported with regret,” that has criticized the US and Israel for acting “without engaging the United Nations or consulting allies including Canada,” and that now finds itself watching the war widen into territory that no one briefed Ottawa on before it started.

Wikipedia’s compilation of international responses includes the note that Canada said it “can’t rule out participation.” That phrase — the double negative that stops short of commitment while keeping the door open — is doing considerable diplomatic work. It is the language of a government that is watching, calculating, and not yet decided.

France has gone further. French broadcaster BFMTV reported Thursday that France has authorized American armed forces to use French bases amid the war. The Charles de Gaulle carrier is en route to the Mediterranean. French Rafales are already operating over the UAE, neutralizing drones targeting French installations. Macron called the strikes “outside of international law, which we cannot approve” — and then authorized military cooperation anyway.

That is the European position in a single sentence. Outside international law, but here is our carrier group.

The European Union’s foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas called the situation “a dangerous moment” for Europe and said Iran was “trying to sow chaos and set the region on fire.” EU and Gulf Cooperation Council officials are meeting Thursday to “discuss the way forward.” The phrase “the way forward” is diplomatic language for the fact that no one has a plan.

X. “The Dahiya Will Look Like Khan Younis”

On Thursday morning, Israel’s military issued evacuation orders for the entire Dahiya — the densely populated cluster of southern Beirut neighborhoods where Hezbollah has long held sway. Roads immediately clogged with honking cars. Hospitals began moving ICU patients. Al-Sahel Hospital ordered staff and patients out entirely; Bahman Hospital closed its emergency department and kept only a skeleton crew for patients on life support.

“I just saw the message, but I have no place to go,” said Amir Hattoum, navigating the chaos on his moped.

“There’s no safe place in Lebanon anymore,” said Fatima Ibrahim, who had been preparing lunch at a shelter when the orders came. “Where shall we go?”

These sweeping evacuation warnings for southern Beirut were never issued during Israel and Hezbollah’s most recent war, which ended with a fragile ceasefire in November 2024. Their issuance now signals something qualitatively different from what came before. Israel’s far-right finance minister Bezalel Smotrich removed any ambiguity about the intended outcome. In a video released Thursday on social media, he said: “The Dahiya will look like Khan Younis. You wanted to give us hell, but you’ve brought hell upon yourself.”

Khan Younis is a city in Gaza. It was decimated.

Lebanon’s death toll reached 102 on Thursday. At least seven of those killed in the last 24 hours were children, per the Lebanese Health Ministry. Lebanese President Joseph Aoun called Emmanuel Macron directly after the evacuation order, urging him to intervene to prevent the targeting of southern Beirut. Macron, in a separate statement, said he had received commitments from Lebanon’s “highest authorities” that the government would take control of Hezbollah positions, and called on Hezbollah to “immediately cease its fire toward Israel” while also telling Israel to “refrain from any ground intervention or large-scale operation on Lebanese territory.”

The gap between what Macron is asking for and what Smotrich is promising is the entirety of southern Beirut.

The UN refugee agency reported Thursday that roughly 100,000 people fled Tehran in the first two days after the war began, and that more than 84,000 people in Lebanon have moved into collective shelters. Some have been forced to sleep in cars or on the street.

Lebanon was supposed to be a secondary front. A total of 150,000 to 200,000 Americans live across the 14 countries now under State Department evacuation advisory — a majority of them Israeli dual citizens, but tens of thousands scattered across the Gulf states, Lebanon, and Cyprus, where protection and indemnity insurance has also been withdrawn and NATO has raised alliance-wide alert levels following the Iranian missile intercept over Turkey.

Dubai International — normally one of the world’s busiest airports, handling hundreds of thousands of passengers daily — had a few hundred stranded travelers on Thursday. Emirates is operating a “reduced schedule.” At Terminal 3, Ellen Den, 37, a Greek tourist with her husband and twin toddlers, stood at a queue for canceled bookings. “If it was just two of us we wouldn’t be too worried,” she said, “but we have very small children and they are our priority.”

The UN Security Council has not met in emergency session. The 60-day War Powers clock is running. And on Thursday, an Israeli finance minister promised a Lebanese neighborhood the fate of a Gazan city.

XI. The Word They Won’t Say

There is, on Capitol Hill, a word that has become the Republican Lord Voldemort — He Who Must Not Be Named. That word is “war.”

The avoidance is not accidental. It is structural. Under the Constitution, only Congress can declare war. If Republicans acknowledge that a war is happening, they must acknowledge that it began without congressional authorization. If they acknowledge that, they must either act to authorize it or admit they have surrendered their constitutional function to a president who did not ask for their consent.

And so the gymnastics began.

Speaker Mike Johnson on Wednesday: “We’re not at war right now. We’re four days into a very specific, clear mission — an operation.” Representative Randy Fine of Florida: “It’s not a war. The way you are officially at war is Congress declares war, and we haven’t declared war.” Representative Ken Calvert, chairman of the defense appropriations subcommittee: “This has been about 72 hours. I would call it an operation at this point.” Senator Josh Hawley: “If I’m going to commit ground troops into combat, that constitutes war in the constitutional sense.” He then voted to block the war powers measure, ensuring Congress had no say.

Senator Markwayne Mullin of Oklahoma briefly escaped the agreed script on Tuesday. “This is war, and we’re taking out the threat,” he said. He was immediately asked to account for the word. He backtracked. “We haven’t declared war. They called it war. I was saying they declared war on us, but war is ugly.” Reminded that he had, in fact, used the word himself, Mullin replied: “That was a misspoke.”

David Axelrod, watching from the Obama orbit: “You find yourself wondering, how many ways can they bend?”

The ridicule came from the right as well. Sean Davis, co-founder of The Federalist — a conservative publication that has generally supported the operation — wrote: “It’s not a war unless it comes from the war region of France, otherwise it’s just sparkling combat.”

The person who made the gymnastics most difficult was the president himself. Trump has called it a war repeatedly and freely. In his first public comments after the strikes began, he said the mission could result in American casualties, “as often happens in war.” On Wednesday, wrapping up an event in the Indian Treaty Room, he noted: “I have to go back and look at the war.” Defense Secretary Hegseth, at the Wednesday briefing: “death and destruction from the sky, all day long.” Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, on the floor: “To anyone around here who thinks we’re not at war, listen to Hegseth’s press conference. What he is saying loud and clear is this: We’re at war, and this administration is signaling more escalation ahead.”

The constitutional question underneath the semantics is not complicated. Six Americans are dead. The military has conducted more than 2,500 strikes and dropped more than 6,000 munitions. The CIA is arming Kurdish forces for ground operations inside a sovereign country. The president has publicly stated he intends to determine who governs Iran. The Strait of Hormuz is closed. The war has spread to Lebanon, Iraq, the Gulf states, and the waters off Sri Lanka.

Representative Fine’s reference point: “I haven’t seen the Grand Army of the Republic storm the beaches yet. We’ll see what happens.”

Senator Brian Schatz of Hawaii, on social media: “This war is costing a billion dollars a day. They literally are taking away your food and your healthcare for this regime-change war of choice.”

It costs a billion dollars a day. Six Americans are dead. The president is choosing Iran’s next leader. Congress, by a vote of 53-47, has decided this is not its business.

XII. Twenty-Five Percent

Reuters/Ipsos published the polling Wednesday. Twenty-five percent of Americans support the US-Israeli attacks on Iran. Forty-three percent oppose. Twenty-nine percent are uncertain.

Trump’s response: “I don’t care about polling.”

Tom Nichols, in the Atlantic panel, noted that the absence of a rally-around-the-flag effect is historically unprecedented. American presidents always get a bump from using military force. George H.W. Bush reached 91 percent approval during the Gulf War. Trump has not gotten a bump. He has gotten a decline. Nichols’ explanation: the only people left to persuade are independents who don’t want this kind of engagement, and they are moving against it.

The Brookings Institution’s Suzanne Maloney: “What we’re seeing is going to be more complicated than the White House may have hoped.” A former US Ambassador to Bahrain: “He hasn’t really made the case in the United States for the cause of going into Iran. They’ve been a bit all over the place in the rationales that they’ve offered, and polling indicates that only one in four Americans really get the rationale and supports it. It’s a bit risky to his base for those reasons.”

One in four. Not a majority of Republicans. Not a plurality of the country. One in four Americans supports this war that the president launched without congressional authorization, without a consistent explanation, and without a visible exit strategy — and is now expanding through Kurdish proxy forces, CIA arming operations, and pre-emptive threats against a successor leader who has not yet been officially announced.

The House votes today on the War Powers resolution. It will fail, as the Senate’s did, 53-47. The 60-day clock started ticking on Day One. Day 60 is April 28.

Between now and then: the succession of Mojtaba Khamenei, if confirmed. The Kurdish ground offensive, if the green light comes. The Strait of Hormuz, if it reopens. The gas prices, as they climb. The midterms, as they approach.

“I don’t care about polling,” said the man whose party faces those midterms with one in four Americans behind this war.

XIII. What Day Six Has Established

The worst-case succession scenario has arrived. Mojtaba Khamenei — IRGC-backed, dynasty-born, ideologically uncompromising — is reported to be Iran’s new supreme leader. Israel has already pre-threatened to kill him. The United States president has publicly declared his choice “unacceptable” and announced that America should have a role in selecting who governs Iran.

That statement will be taught in Iranian schools. It already is, in a sense — Iranians did not need a historian to explain what Trump was describing. They recognized 1953 immediately. The CIA removed their government once before, installed a Shah, watched him fall, and spent 47 years dealing with what replaced him. Trump is proposing to do it again, in public, on the record, while the bombs are still falling.

The CIA is arming Kurdish forces for ground operations inside Iran. Kurdish fighters are already across the border. The administration denies a plan exists. Trump says he is “all for” the offensive. Rubio told Congress the US isn’t arming the Kurds and immediately added “you never know with the Israelis.” The Kurdistan Regional Government denies involvement. The Iranian Kurdish factions are waiting for the green light. Iran has already struck Iraqi Kurdistan in preemptive retaliation.

The Strait of Hormuz is commercially closed. Brent crude at $85. WTI above $80. Gas at $3.25 and climbing. Ten-year Treasury yield up the equivalent of a full Fed rate hike in six days. The S&P 500 negative for the year. No timeline for reopening.

Southern Beirut is being evacuated. Israel’s finance minister has promised it will look like Khan Younis. 102 dead in Lebanon. Seven of the last 24 hours’ dead are children. 100,000 fled Tehran in the first two days. 84,000 Lebanese in collective shelters.

The largest US military base in the Middle East nearly took a direct bomber strike on Day Five. The public found out through a CNN exclusive, days later. Amazon’s data centers in the Gulf were explicitly named and bombed as military objectives. Three remain offline.

Congressional Republicans cannot say the word “war.” The president says it freely. Hegseth said “death and destruction all day long.” Six Americans are dead. The 60-day clock started on Day One.

One in four Americans supports it.

The president does not care about polling.

Seventy-two years ago, the United States chose Iran’s government. What followed was the Shah, SAVAK, 1979, the hostage crisis, 241 Marines in Beirut, three decades of proxy war, and the Islamic Republic that stands — battered, leaderless, defiant — on the other side of this bombing campaign. Trump now proposes to do it again, having announced it openly, having removed the back-channel, having pre-threatened the successor, and having told Reuters he should be involved in the selection.

Day 60 is April 28.

Iran’s foreign minister said Thursday there is no reason to negotiate.

On this, at least, both sides appear to agree.
