ABBA Against the Odds

Against the Odds

Part 3: The Video Revolution: How They Pioneered Virtual Touring Before Anyone Understood What That Meant

January 23, 2026

In 1977, most successful bands measured their commitment to fans by how much they toured. More cities, more countries, more nights on the road—that was the metric of dedication and the path to building audience loyalty. The biggest acts played hundreds of dates annually. The Rolling Stones, Led Zeppelin, Elton John—they toured relentlessly because that’s what successful bands did.

ABBA looked at this conventional wisdom and made a radical decision: What if we don’t?

Iconic Lasse Hallström-Directed Music Video Stills (1970s-1980s – the core “video tour” strategy)

Not because they couldn’t tour. They had the global demand. They had the production capability. They certainly had material worth performing live. But Björn, Benny, Agnetha, and Frida—guided by Stig Anderson’s strategic vision—recognized something most of their contemporaries missed: touring was a 19th-century solution to a 20th-century problem. You couldn’t be everywhere at once, so you traveled to where audiences were.

But what if you could be everywhere at once?

What if you could perform in Tokyo and London and New York simultaneously without leaving Stockholm? What if you could reach global audiences without the physical, emotional, and relationship costs of constant travel?

The answer was music videos—not as promotional afterthoughts or marketing supplements, but as the core strategy for global presence. ABBA didn’t just make videos. They made videos their primary performance medium years before MTV existed, before the music industry understood what music videos could be, before “music video” was even a standard term.

This wasn’t intuition or lucky timing. This was strategic innovation driven by clear-eyed assessment of costs, benefits, and alternatives. And it worked so well that 45 years later, at age 75-79, they’d apply the same principle through avatar technology, creating virtual performances that generate over $100 million annually without any of them setting foot on stage.

The video revolution wasn’t just clever marketing. It was ABBA recognizing that the medium was changing and being willing to embrace that change while their competitors clung to traditional touring models.

The Conventional Touring Trap

To understand why ABBA’s video strategy was revolutionary, you need to understand what touring meant in the 1970s.

The Beatles stopped touring in 1966 because it was killing them. The screaming made it impossible to hear themselves play. The constant travel, the lack of creative time, the pressure—it became unsustainable. They retreated to the studio and produced some of their best work, but they also dissolved as a band partly due to the strain of divergent creative visions and personal tensions that touring might have managed differently.

Elton John played hundreds of shows annually through the 1970s and into the 1980s. The schedule was punishing. The drugs and alcohol that made it tolerable created their own problems. He’s spoken openly about how touring nearly destroyed him.

The Rolling Stones are still touring in their 80s, which is either admirable dedication or evidence of what happens when you build an entire business model around live performance and can’t imagine an alternative.

These weren’t aberrations. This was the standard model: record albums, tour to promote them, tour some more to capitalize on hit singles, keep touring because that’s where the money and audience connection came from.

The costs were substantial:

Physical exhaustion. Performing night after night in different cities, dealing with travel delays, eating poorly, sleeping in hotels or on buses, maintaining vocal health—it ground people down.

Creative stagnation. Tour schedules left little time for writing, recording, or musical development. You performed the same set night after night, which was great for audiences but limiting for artists.

Relationship strain. Touring meant months away from partners, families, normal life. For bands with internal romantic relationships—like ABBA’s two couples—the strain multiplied.

Limited reach. No matter how much you toured, you still couldn’t be everywhere. Playing Tokyo meant not playing London. There were only so many hours in a day and only so many nights you could perform.

Most bands accepted these costs as unavoidable. If you wanted to be a successful global act, you toured. There was no alternative.

ABBA saw an alternative.

“Tour the Video”

Outrageous Sequin/Costume Shots from Videos

The quote that captures ABBA’s strategic insight comes from music journalist Paul Gambaccini: “ABBA were the first artists who realized you can’t be everywhere at once. Tour the video, let the video be your tour. Sure, do some live dates. But the video can be everywhere.”

Read that again: “Tour the video.”

Not “make promotional videos.” Not “supplement touring with videos.” Tour. The. Video.

This was radical reframing. Videos weren’t marketing materials supporting the real work of touring. Videos were the touring—the primary means of reaching global audiences.

Consider what this meant in practical terms:

Global simultaneous presence. A video could air in twenty countries the same week. You couldn’t tour twenty countries simultaneously. The video could.

Consistent quality. Every audience saw the same performance, filmed under optimal conditions with the best audio and visual quality. Live performances varied based on venue acoustics, the band’s energy that night, technical issues. Videos didn’t.

Permanent availability. A tour ended. A video kept working. TV stations could show it repeatedly. It could be rebroadcast. In the emerging home video market, people could own it.

Creative control. ABBA worked with director Lasse Hallström, who later became a successful Hollywood director. They controlled the visual presentation, the costumes, the lighting, the editing. Live touring meant dealing with varying venue conditions and production capabilities.

Cost efficiency. Producing videos was expensive, but cheaper than mounting global tours with equipment, crew, transportation, and all the logistical overhead touring required.

Relationship preservation. This might have been the most important factor for ABBA specifically. Making videos meant staying in or near Stockholm. It meant going home at night. It meant not subjecting two fragile romantic relationships to the additional strain of constant travel. The ability to create professional output while maintaining personal boundaries proved crucial as relationships deteriorated.

Enhanced mystique. Paradoxically, being less available in person may have made them more compelling. When you couldn’t just see ABBA on tour whenever they came through town, each video became an event. Scarcity created value.

Controlled image. Live performances involve risk. A bad night happens. Technical problems occur. Someone’s sick or tired or having an off day. Videos captured them at their best, always. The global audience never saw ABBA having an off night because there were no off nights in edited video content.

The strategic logic was overwhelming. The only question was whether it would actually work.

Before MTV Changed Everything

Outrageous Sequin/Costume Shots from Videos

Here’s the crucial timing issue: ABBA pioneered video-based global presence before MTV existed.

MTV launched in August 1981. By then, ABBA had already established video as their primary performance medium for years. They weren’t responding to MTV’s demand for video content. They created the demand that MTV would eventually institutionalize.

In the late 1970s, music videos existed but were marginal. Some artists made promotional films for TV shows. Some created visual content for specific markets. But there was no infrastructure supporting music videos as a primary medium. No MTV, no VH1, no dedicated video programming that could justify significant production investment.

ABBA made that investment anyway because they understood something crucial: infrastructure follows demand, not the other way around. If they created compelling video content, distribution channels would emerge to monetize it.

They were right.

Their 1977 tour of Europe and Australia was filmed for a television movie—ABBA: The Movie. This wasn’t just concert footage. It had a storyline, character development, narrative structure. As Björn explained: “At the beginning that was just meant to be a documentary... for ourselves. Actually, to have some document from where we were in Australia. But it just grew bigger and bigger, and all of a sudden, there was a television movie for 2 hours.”

What started as documentation became product. The movie aired on television. It was released on home video. It generated revenue and reach that touring alone couldn’t match.

The Australian tour also featured what Benny described as “a little part... where we wrote a little mini musical within the show. ‘The Girl with the Golden Hair.’ These 2 girls playing two different sides of the same woman.”

Notice the progression: They weren’t just performing songs. They were creating theatrical experiences, narrative content, visual storytelling. They were thinking like filmmakers and TV producers, not just musicians on tour.

This investment in visual storytelling paid off when MTV launched and suddenly needed content. ABBA’s video archive became valuable assets in a new medium. While other artists scrambled to produce videos for MTV, ABBA had a catalog ready to go.

But even before MTV, their videos were working through traditional TV variety shows, through television specials, through the emerging home video market. They were building global presence through screens rather than stages.

The Iconic Visual Style

ABBA’s videos became so distinctive that they could be parodied and everyone would get the reference.

As Paul Gambaccini noted: “That’s why someone like French and Saunders could send up the ABBA video style, and everybody would get it, because everybody had seen them videos.”

What made the style so recognizable?

The costumes. ABBA’s outfits were deliberately outrageous—sequins, platform boots, jumpsuits in colors that shouldn’t exist in nature, designs that tested the boundaries of taste. These weren’t costumes they personally loved. As one account noted, “they thought they were ridiculous, but wore them anyway, because they knew it was of the time.”

That phrase—”they knew it was of the time”—captures ABBA’s pragmatic professionalism. Personal preference didn’t matter. What worked for the visual medium mattered. So they “wisely delegated their costumes to someone who knew costumes” and wore whatever created maximum visual impact.

The choreography. Simple, synchronized movements that looked good on camera. Not complex dance routines requiring extensive rehearsal, just coordinated positioning and gesture that created visual interest.

The production quality. Working with Lasse Hallström meant professional cinematography, proper lighting, thoughtful editing. These weren’t cheaply made promotional clips. They were professionally produced short films.

The accessibility. The videos weren’t avant-garde art pieces requiring interpretation. They were straightforward visual presentations of the songs—sometimes with light narrative elements, always with clear focus on the band performing.

This combination created videos that were immediately recognizable, visually compelling, and appropriate for mainstream television. They weren’t challenging or difficult. They were pop perfection translated into visual medium.

The Australia Tour: Proof of Concept

The 1977 Australian tour demonstrated both the value of selective live performance and the power of documenting it through video.

ABBA toured Australia and Europe in 1977—the most extensive touring they’d do. The 1977 tour was their only major world tour—they did smaller European legs in 1979-1980, but never again mounted a global touring campaign. The Australian leg was particularly significant. They were huge in Australia, where “Fernando” had been an enormous hit. The tour attracted massive audiences and demonstrated their live performance capability.

But rather than treating this as the beginning of continuous global touring—the standard model—they treated it as an event worth documenting. Hence ABBA: The Movie, which captured the energy and spectacle while allowing them to monetize it repeatedly through television and home video.

A few hundred thousand people saw ABBA live in Australia. Millions saw ABBA: The Movie.

After 1977, their touring diminished dramatically. Some sources note that after becoming “really big,” they “didn’t have the time” to tour extensively. But this frames it wrong. They had time. They chose to allocate it differently. They chose to focus on writing, recording, and video production rather than touring because those activities generated better returns on their creative and emotional investment.

By 1979, ABBA’s net profits were $15 million (equivalent to $68 million in 2024 dollars). This came from record sales, merchandise, and video/television revenue—not primarily from touring. They were generating enormous revenue while doing a fraction of the touring their competitors required to maintain similar visibility.

From Video to Avatars: The Same Strategy, Different Technology

ABBA Voyage Avatars on Stage

Fast forward to 2021. ABBA, now in their mid-70s, could have done a traditional reunion tour. The demand existed. The ticket prices would have been astronomical. It would have generated enormous revenue.

Instead, they created ABBA Voyage—a virtual concert featuring digital avatars of themselves as they appeared in 1979, backed by a live band, performing in a purpose-built London arena.

The critics were skeptical. Would audiences accept digital representations? Would it feel authentic? Would anyone care?

The show has sold over 3 million tickets across three years, generating over $350 million in revenue. It’s sold out consistently at 90%+ capacity. It contributed £2 billion to the UK economy. It shows no signs of slowing.

The principle is identical to the video strategy: You don’t need physical presence to create emotional connection. You need high-quality content presented effectively through appropriate media.

In the 1970s, that medium was video. In the 2020s, it’s avatar technology with live musical accompaniment. The technology evolved. The understanding remained constant.

As Björn explained about Voyage: “Someone came to say that, you know you can go on the road as holograms on tour, and you don’t even have to be there. And we said... the idea came and we thought it was irresistible. And because it’s something that is pushing boundaries and something nobody has ever seen before.”

“Nobody has ever seen before”—that was the video strategy in 1977. That was the avatar strategy in 2021. ABBA keeps finding ways to be everywhere at once without actually being anywhere.

The Revolution Nobody Noticed

ABBA’s video revolution happened so smoothly that many people didn’t recognize it as revolutionary. They just noticed ABBA was everywhere—on television, in video, globally present—without touring the way comparable acts toured.

The revolution wasn’t loud. It was strategic, methodical, and executed so professionally that it looked easy.

But it was radical. ABBA fundamentally reimagined what it meant to be a global music act in the era of emerging visual media. They demonstrated that physical presence was optional, that touring was a choice not a requirement, that video could be primary rather than supplementary.

Forty-five years later, when Gen Z discovers ABBA through TikTok videos featuring their songs, they’re experiencing the latest iteration of the video strategy ABBA pioneered: visual media spreading their music globally without their physical presence.

The technology changes. The principle endures: Create excellent content, distribute it through the most effective available media, and understand that being everywhere at once is more valuable than being somewhere specific repeatedly.

That’s not just clever marketing. That’s strategic innovation that created competitive advantage sustaining across five decades.
