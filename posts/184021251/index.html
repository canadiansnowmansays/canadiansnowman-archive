Part 12: The Dismissal (2024)

January 15, 2026

On July 15, 2024—the first day of the Republican National Convention where Trump would officially receive his party’s nomination—Judge Aileen Cannon dropped her bombshell.

In a 93-page order, Cannon dismissed the entire case against Trump, holding that Jack Smith’s appointment violated the Appointments Clause and that Smith’s office was unconstitutionally funded.

“Upon careful study of the foundational challenges raised in the Motion, the Court is convinced that Special Counsel Smith’s prosecution of this action breaches two structural cornerstones of our constitutional scheme,” Cannon wrote.

She ruled that Smith’s appointment “effectively usurps” Congress’s legislative authority and that his office’s funding violated the Appropriations Clause.

The ruling was:

Legally Radical: It contradicted decades of precedent upholding special counsel appointments under identical statutory provisions.

Factually Questionable: Cannon claimed Smith’s funding came without congressional authorization, but Attorney General Garland noted the relevant statutes had been on the books for decades with congressional approval.

Procedurally Unusual: Cannon relied heavily on Justice Thomas’s solo concurrence in a different case rather than binding precedent.

Practically Devastating: The order didn’t just pause the case—it killed it entirely, including charges against Trump’s co-defendants Walt Nauta and Carlos De Oliveira.

Cannon’s order was so far outside mainstream legal analysis that even conservative legal scholars expressed shock. “I don’t think that, before Justice Thomas wrote his concurrence, this argument had a snowball’s chance,” one attorney told CNN.

The Legal Community Responds: “Shocking” and “Unprecedented”

The reaction was immediate and intense.

Attorney General Merrick Garland told NBC News: “For more than 20 years, I was a federal judge. Do I look like somebody who would make that basic mistake about the law? I don’t think so.”

Jack Smith’s office announced it would appeal the dismissal, calling the ruling contrary to “the uniform conclusion of all previous courts to have considered the issue.”

Former federal judge Nancy Gertner and two legal scholars filed motions asking the 11th Circuit to reassign the case to a new judge, citing “well-founded concerns that she may be biased against the Government’s case.”

Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) joined the call for Cannon’s removal, noting this would be the third time the 11th Circuit had reversed her in “a seemingly straightforward case.”

The timing couldn’t have been more perfect for Trump: dismissed on the first day of the Republican convention, just two days after surviving an assassination attempt, and weeks before he would win the presidency.

Trump’s Reaction: “Big Win for the Rule of Law”

Trump spokesman Steven Cheung declared: “Judge Cannon today restored the rule of law and made the right call for America. Jack Smith is not above the law and must be held accountable under the Constitution.”

Christopher Kise, Trump’s attorney, added: “From the outset, the Attorney General and Special Counsel have ignored critical constitutional restrictions on the exercise of the prosecutorial power of the United States.”

House Republicans celebrated. Rep. Elise Stefanik applauded Cannon’s “courage and wisdom.” Rep. Clay Higgins posted: “Winning. More to come. MAGA.”

The Orwellian framing—calling the end of prosecution a “victory for the rule of law”—captured Trump’s complete success in using delay and judicial favoritism to escape accountability.

What Happened to the Secrets?

Lost in the legal wrangling was a chilling question: What happened to the national security secrets?

The documents reportedly included:

  • Intelligence on foreign nuclear capabilities

  • Military contingency plans

  • Communications intercepts

  • Information about U.S. intelligence sources and methods

Trump had shown classified war plans to people without security clearances. He stored TOP SECRET documents in a ballroom used for parties. He potentially exposed America’s most sensitive intelligence.

Intelligence officials expressed deep concern about potential damage. But with the case dismissed and Trump president again, there would never be a full accounting of:

  • What secrets were exposed

  • Who accessed them

  • What damage was done to intelligence sources

  • Whether foreign adversaries obtained classified information

The national security implications—the entire reason the Espionage Act was passed in 1917—were subordinated to constitutional technicalities.

The Appeal and Electoral Victory

The 11th Circuit ordered briefing on Smith’s appeal. Legal experts believed Cannon’s ruling would be reversed—she had been reversed twice before in the same case.

But Trump’s campaign surged forward. On November 5, 2024, he won the presidency.

Trump’s victory changed everything. On November 25, 2024, Jack Smith filed motions to drop the classified documents appeal against Trump and to dismiss the January 6th case entirely.

Smith cited Department of Justice policy prohibiting prosecution of a sitting president. “The prohibition is categorical,” Smith wrote, “and does not turn on the gravity of the crimes charged, the strength of the Government’s proof, or the merits of the prosecution, which the Government stands fully behind.”

The 11th Circuit dismissed the appeal on November 25, 2024.

The documents case against Trump was over.

The Secrets Return to Mar-a-Lago

After Trump won the election and Smith dropped the case, something remarkable happened: the Justice Department returned the seized documents to Trump.

On February 28, 2025, Trump brought the boxes back to Mar-a-Lago.

The classified secrets that Trump illegally retained, showed to unauthorized persons, stored in bathrooms and ballrooms, and lied about returning—are back at Mar-a-Lago.

Some of America’s most sensitive national security information is once again at an active social club where foreign nationals attend events and hundreds of people have access.

Intelligence officials are powerless to prevent it. Trump is president. The case is dead. The investigation is over.

The national security disaster continues.

The Suppressed Report

In January 2025, Jack Smith filed his final report on his Trump investigations.

For the January 6th case, Volume I was released publicly. But Volume II—covering the classified documents case—was never released.

Judge Cannon entered an order on January 21, 2025, barring public release of Volume II, saying it was necessary to protect defendants’ due process rights—even though the case had been dismissed and would never go to trial.

The Knight First Amendment Institute has filed legal actions seeking release, arguing the public has a right to know how DOJ approached “unprecedented allegations against a president.”

Cannon has blocked these efforts. Smith’s detailed evidence and legal analysis—the full accounting of Trump’s classified documents crimes—remains sealed.

Even in defeat, Cannon continues protecting Trump by suppressing the documentary record.

Cannon’s Legacy: Breaking the System

Judge Cannon’s handling of the classified documents case will be studied for decades as an example of how a single judge can derail prosecution of even the most serious crimes.

Her tactics:

1. The Indefinite Delay: Postponing trial indefinitely ensured Trump wouldn’t face trial before the election.

2. Entertaining Frivolous Defenses: Giving serious consideration to legally dubious arguments consumed time and gave Trump’s delay strategy legitimacy.

3. The Radical Dismissal: Embracing an unprecedented constitutional theory rejected by every other court killed the case entirely.

4. Ignoring Precedent: Relying on Justice Thomas’s solo concurrence rather than binding appellate precedent placed ideology over jurisprudence.

5. Suppressing Evidence: Blocking release of Smith’s final report prevents the public from knowing what Trump did.

The 11th Circuit had twice reversed Cannon’s decisions. Legal scholars who filed amicus briefs believed her dismissal would be reversed again.

But Trump’s electoral victory made the appeal moot before the 11th Circuit could act.

The Co-Defendants: They Walk Too

Jack Smith initially planned to continue prosecution of Trump’s co-defendants Walt Nauta and Carlos De Oliveira, since DOJ policy doesn’t apply to them.

But in January 2025, DOJ asked the court to dismiss their cases too. The court granted this request on January 29, 2025.

Trump’s subordinates who allegedly helped obstruct justice—moving boxes, suggesting deletion of security footage, lying to investigators—also face no consequences.

Everyone walks.

What Should Have Happened

The classified documents case should have resulted in Trump’s conviction and imprisonment. The evidence was overwhelming:

Physical Evidence: Hundreds of classified documents in unauthorized locations, photographed in situ by FBI agents.

Video Evidence: Security footage showing Trump’s aides moving boxes to hide them from Trump’s own lawyer.

Documentary Evidence: Text messages, attorney notes, subpoenas, false certifications creating a paper trail of obstruction.

Witness Testimony: Mar-a-Lago employees, FBI agents, National Archives officials, Trump’s own lawyers describing Trump’s refusal to comply.

Trump’s Own Words: Audio recordings of Trump showing classified documents to unauthorized persons and acknowledging he couldn’t declassify them.

Former federal prosecutors called it a “slam dunk.” National security lawyers said anyone else would be in prison.

Instead:

  • A Trump-appointed judge entertained frivolous defenses for over a year

  • She dismissed the entire case on an unprecedented constitutional theory

  • Trump won the election before appeals could proceed

  • The case was dropped under DOJ policy

  • Evidence of Trump’s crimes was sealed from public view

  • Trump got his classified documents back

  • Nobody was held accountable

The Systemic Failures

The collapse represents multiple systemic failures:

Random Case Assignment: The system’s random assignment produced a uniquely favorable judge for Trump. Any of dozens of other judges would likely have managed the case differently.

No Recusal Standards: Despite Cannon’s prior unusual rulings that were reversed, she had no obligation to recuse herself.

No Removal Mechanism: Even when Cannon’s bias became apparent, there was no practical way to remove her without an appellate reversal—and she structured delays to prevent that.

DOJ’s Sitting President Policy: The policy against prosecuting sitting presidents, while defensible, created an escape hatch Trump could reach through delay and electoral victory.

Electoral Victory as Immunity: Trump’s ability to run for president while under indictment, and use the presidency to end prosecutions, creates perverse incentives.

Most fundamentally: Trump committed serious crimes against national security. The evidence was overwhelming. The prosecutor was competent. The charges were appropriate.

And one judge—appointed by the defendant—ensured he never faced trial.

Conclusion: “A Major Victory for the Rule of Law”?

Trump’s spokesperson called the dismissal “a major victory for the rule of law.”

It was the opposite.

The rule of law means:

  • Evidence matters

  • Crime has consequences

  • No one is above the law

  • Courts adjudicate facts fairly

  • Justice is blind to status and power

The classified documents case’s collapse demonstrated:

  • A well-connected defendant can get a favorable judge

  • That judge can delay proceedings indefinitely

  • Delay can prevent trial until electoral victory

  • Electoral victory provides immunity from prosecution

  • Evidence of serious crimes goes unevaluated

  • National security violations go unpunished

Trump took classified national defense information. He refused to return it. He showed it to unauthorized persons. He obstructed federal investigations. He orchestrated a cover-up documented on video.

And he faced zero consequences.

This isn’t the rule of law. It’s the rule of power, wealth, and judicial favoritism.

Judge Cannon broke the system to protect a president who broke the law. Trump’s legacy includes adding “successful obstruction of espionage prosecution” to his long list of accountability evasions.

And the classified secrets? They’re back at Mar-a-Lago, stored somewhere between the ballroom and the bathroom, available to anyone with access to America’s most insecure presidential resort.

“Major victory for the rule of law” indeed.

Postscript: Smith Confirms What We Already Knew (December 17, 2024)

Three months after the case’s dismissal, Special Counsel Jack Smith testified before Congress. Under oath, he confirmed what the evidence showed:

“Our investigation developed powerful evidence that showed President Trump willfully retained highly classified documents after he left office... storing them at his social club, including in a bathroom and a ballroom. He then repeatedly tried to obstruct justice to conceal his continued retention of those documents.”

Smith’s testimony made explicit what Judge Cannon’s dismissal obscured: the evidence of Trump’s guilt was overwhelming. “Powerful evidence” of willful retention. “Repeated” obstruction. Specific confirmation of the bathroom and ballroom storage.

But there was no trial to present that evidence to a jury. Judge Cannon’s unprecedented ruling prevented it. Trump’s delay tactics worked. His electoral victory ended the case.

Smith’s congressional testimony now stands as the permanent record: Trump committed serious federal crimes involving national security, the Special Counsel had powerful evidence proving it, but Trump escaped accountability through judicial favoritism, procedural delays, and electoral victory.

The rule of law didn’t prevail. Trump did.
