Now This is Fake News

Part 3: The Ministry’s First Month: When Propaganda Meets the Work

October 30, 2025

George Orwell gave us “War is Peace.” The Pentagon gave us the Department of War. Now they’re giving us the Ministry of Truth—where propaganda calls itself journalism and asking questions is a crime.

The Ministry’s First Month: When Propaganda Meets the Work

Two weeks after the Pentagon walkout and one week since Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced his “next generation” press corps, something curious is emerging from the limited information available: the outlets that replaced independent journalists don’t seem particularly interested in doing journalism.

Over 60 journalists from outlets like Gateway Pundit, LindellTV, and Tim Pool’s Timcast received credentials to replace the reporters who walked out rather than sign away their independence. The Pentagon threw open its doors to its hand-picked replacements, ready to demonstrate how “honest journalism” works when you agree not to ask unauthorized questions.

What’s happened since is instructive—even if the full picture remains incomplete.

ABC News reported that the first briefing under the new system drew only a handful of the newly-credentialed journalists. Tim Pool himself posted on X that his team would attend “when feasible” but skip “in-depth investigative reporting”—an announcement that raises the obvious question: what exactly did he think Pentagon credentials were for?

Meanwhile, CNN reported that the Pentagon has held “practically zero press briefings and gaggles” for months, conducting mostly “one-way communication with the public through social media.” So the new press corps isn’t just skipping briefings—there aren’t many briefings to skip.

The reporters who refused to compromise—who packed up their desks and walked out together on October 15—are doing something remarkable: they’re still working. Harder than ever, actually.

This is the month when the Pentagon’s propaganda experiment collided with the stubborn persistence of actual journalism. Turns out, you can’t replace the truth with talking points just because you changed who gets to sit in the briefing room.

The Question of Commitment

Let’s be clear about what we know, and what we can reasonably infer from it.

We know: Tim Pool announced publicly that Timcast won’t maintain “a significant presence” at the Pentagon or engage in investigative reporting. This is documented—he posted it himself.

We know: Pool’s Timcast, according to Justice Department documents, received payments from Tenet Media, a company operated by Russian government-backed media, to create divisive content designed to weaken the U.S. government.

We know: Gateway Pundit recently settled a defamation lawsuit with Georgia election workers it falsely accused of rigging the 2020 election, and filed for bankruptcy after facing multiple lawsuits over its content.

We know: LindellTV is run by Mike Lindell, whose primary business is selling pillows and whose primary political activity has been promoting election conspiracy theories.

We know: Just the News was posting job advertisements seeking a Pentagon reporter—suggesting they didn’t actually have one when they received credentials.

We know: Even defense trade publications refused to sign. Valerie Insinna from Breaking Defense noted on X that “all of the defense trade publications refused to sign the Pentagon’s media access policy, and we write about budget and military technology–not exactly what you think of when you envision ‘activists who masquerade as journalists.’” These are the technical publications that cover weapons systems and procurement—hardly the “activists” Hegseth’s team claims to be fighting.

We know: NPR’s Tom Bowman, who held his Pentagon press pass for 28 years, turned it in rather than compromise his journalistic independence.

What we can reasonably infer: These are not outlets positioned to conduct aggressive investigative journalism into defense spending, military operations, or strategic failures. They sought credentials, but seeking credentials and using them for accountability journalism are very different things.

The outlets that signed Hegseth’s pledge clearly wanted something—the legitimacy that comes with Pentagon access, perhaps, or the ability to claim they’re “real journalists” with official credentials. What’s less clear is whether they wanted the tedious, unglamorous, often thankless work that actual Pentagon reporting requires.

That work requires showing up. It requires persistence. It requires caring more about getting the story right than keeping officials happy.

Whether the new press corps is doing that work is difficult to assess from outside—which is, in itself, telling. What we’re not seeing is the kind of aggressive, independent reporting that characterized the Pentagon press corps for decades. What we’re not seeing is tough questions that lead to uncomfortable revelations.

Maybe that’s coming. Or maybe the outlets that signed Hegseth’s pledge got exactly what they wanted: credentials to point to, without the burden of actual accountability journalism.

What Real Journalism Looks Like From the Outside

Barbara Starr, who covered the Pentagon for CNN for 21 years before retiring in 2022, put it perfectly in response to the purge: “Reporters are going to continue to report whether they’re inside the building or not.”

She wasn’t being metaphorical. And the evidence suggests she was right.

What we know for certain: The journalists who walked out didn’t stop working. The Washington Post, New York Times, CNN, Associated Press, Reuters, and others are still covering the Pentagon—just from outside the building now.

What we’re seeing in coverage: Stories are still breaking about military operations, defense spending, and Pentagon controversies. Questions are still being asked. The journalism is still happening—it’s just harder and more expensive than before.

This is the difference between journalism and propaganda: journalism doesn’t need permission. It doesn’t require official access or government blessing. It just requires persistence, integrity, and a commitment to telling the truth even when powerful people would prefer you didn’t.

The Pentagon thought it could control the narrative by controlling access. What it seems to be discovering is that independent journalism doesn’t need their building—just their information, which turns out to be harder to hide than anticipated.

The reporters who walked out are adapting because that’s what actual journalists do when you try to shut them down: they find another way.

The Crackdown Intensifies

While the press corps purge made headlines, Hegseth has been tightening control over information in other ways.

Just yesterday (October 28), The Hill reported that the Pentagon issued new rules making it easier to fire civilian workers, directing managers to move with “speed and conviction” to dismiss underperforming personnel. The memo from Anthony Tata, acting Under Secretary of Defense for Personnel and Readiness, tells office heads to cite “Douglas Factors” that give managers “flexibility to address performance issues swiftly and effectively.”

This comes after Hegseth earlier this year ordered the Pentagon’s workforce cut by nearly 8 percent—about 60,000 personnel—via voluntary buyouts and attrition. As Hegseth told hundreds of generals and admirals last month at Quantico: “The sooner we have the right people, the sooner we can advance the right policies. Personnel is policy.”

The Washington Post reported in September that the Pentagon is also pushing strict nondisclosure agreements and random polygraph tests for workers, including many top officials—all part of what the Post described as Hegseth’s “war on leakers and internal dissent.”

The pattern is clear: silence the press corps that covers you, intimidate the employees who might talk to remaining reporters, fire those who don’t fall in line, and threaten anyone left with polygraphs and NDAs. This isn’t transparency. This is information lockdown.

Meanwhile, Hegseth remains on message. Currently on a tour of Asia with President Trump, he’s maintaining his “warrior ethos” focus—recently barring troops with medical shaver waivers from attending his event in South Korea, and continuing his crusade against facial hair despite the fact that such waivers predominantly benefit Black service members with pseudofolliculitis barbae.

It’s all theater, of course. But theater designed to distract from the real story: a Defense Department increasingly operating in darkness, accountable to no one but itself.

What Propaganda Can’t Hide

Here’s what the Ministry of Truth’s first month suggests, based on available evidence and reasonable inference:

Propaganda only works if people believe it. Propaganda dressed up as journalism only works if the propagandists actually do journalism.

The Pentagon’s new press corps—based on their own public statements and early reporting—doesn’t appear to be doing the kind of aggressive accountability journalism that characterized the Pentagon press corps for decades. They have credentials. What they’re doing with those credentials remains to be seen.

Meanwhile, actual journalism continues. Military families are still learning about deployments and casualties. Defense spending is still being investigated. Strategic failures are still being reported. The stories keep breaking.

This reveals something fundamental about the difference between journalism and propaganda: propaganda requires cooperation from those being covered. It requires official channels, approved narratives, controlled information flow.

Journalism just requires stubbornness. The willingness to keep asking questions even when doors close. The commitment to finding information even when officials try to hide it. The persistence to keep reporting even when you lose building access.

The reporters who walked out are proving that every day.

Conclusion: The Ministry Is Operational

We now have a Pentagon press corps that includes outlets funded by Russian propaganda operations (documented by DOJ), outlets that settled defamation lawsuits (public record), and outlets whose representatives have announced they won’t do investigative reporting (their own words).

We have journalists who have covered the Pentagon for decades working from outside the building, doing the journalism the credentialed outlets seem less interested in pursuing.

We have a policy that multiple First Amendment organizations have called unconstitutional, that legal experts describe as textbook prior restraint and viewpoint discrimination, and that even Fox News—Pete Hegseth’s former employer—refused to accept.

We have a Defense Secretary simultaneously purging the press corps, firing civilian workers, imposing NDAs and polygraph tests, and cracking down on any potential source of unauthorized information.

What we know for certain: The Ministry of Truth is built. The propagandists are credentialed. The official channels are established. The information lockdown is intensifying.

What we can reasonably assess: The tough questions aren’t being asked by those with official access. The stories that need investigation aren’t being pursued by the new press corps. The accountability journalism that should happen inside the building is instead happening outside it.

What we can observe: You can credential propagandists, but you can’t make them do journalism. You can expel adversarial reporters, but you can’t stop them from reporting. You can build a Ministry of Truth, but you can’t make truth stop mattering.

The outlets that walked out proved something important: their journalism doesn’t depend on Pete Hegseth’s permission. The outlets that stayed will prove something equally important over the coming months: whether they’re actually interested in journalism at all, or just the credentials.

Two weeks in, the evidence suggests credentials were the goal. The journalism remains optional.

The reporters who walked out are still working. The stories are still breaking. The truth is still getting out.

And that, ultimately, is why propaganda always struggles against persistent journalism. Not because propaganda is dishonest—everyone expects that. But because it’s optional. And journalism—real journalism, the stubborn, adversarial, unglamorous pursuit of truth—isn’t optional for those who practice it.

It’s just who they are. With or without building access.

But there’s a question the Ministry of Truth can’t answer, no matter how many propagandists it credentials: what happens when nobody’s watching? What stories go untold? What operations proceed without scrutiny?

In Part 4, we’ll examine the most urgent consequence of the press purge: a military campaign that has killed at least 57 people since September, with minimal oversight and zero public evidence—exactly the kind of story that demands the accountability journalism the Pentagon just eliminated.
