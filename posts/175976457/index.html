Indictment Imminent

The Third Prosecution in Three Weeks—And the Most Ironic

October 12, 2025

As this essay goes to press, federal prosecutors are meeting over the weekend to finalize criminal charges against John Bolton, President Trump’s former National Security Adviser. According to two federal officials, Bolton could be indicted as early as next week.

If charges are filed, Bolton will become the third Trump critic to face federal prosecution in less than three weeks—following James Comey (indicted September 25) and Letitia James (indicted October 9).

The pattern is no longer emerging. It’s established.

Who Is John Bolton?

John Bolton is a foreign policy hawk who served in multiple Republican administrations spanning four decades. He was:

  • Assistant Attorney General under Ronald Reagan (1985-1989)

  • Assistant Secretary of State under George H.W. Bush (1989-1993)

  • Under Secretary of State for Arms Control and International Security under George W. Bush (2001-2005)

  • U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations under George W. Bush (2005-2006)

  • National Security Adviser under Donald Trump (April 2018 - September 2019)

Bolton is a lifelong Republican with impeccable conservative credentials. He’s known for his aggressive foreign policy positions, particularly regarding Iran, North Korea, and Russia. He’s been called a neoconservative, a hawk, and worse—but never, until recently, unpatriotic or a criminal.

The Falling Out

Bolton served as Trump’s National Security Adviser for 17 months before Trump fired him in September 2019. The two had clashed repeatedly over foreign policy, particularly regarding:

  • Russia: Bolton favored a harder line; Trump sought warmer relations with Putin

  • North Korea: Bolton was skeptical of Trump’s outreach to Kim Jong Un

  • Ukraine: Bolton opposed Trump’s pressure campaign on Ukraine (the subject of Trump’s first impeachment)

  • Syria: They disagreed on troop withdrawal timing

After his firing, Bolton considered testifying in Trump’s first impeachment inquiry but ultimately did not appear. Instead, he wrote a book.

The Book That Changed Everything

In June 2020, Bolton published “The Room Where It Happened: A White House Memoir.” The book was devastating for Trump, revealing:

  • Trump asking China’s Xi Jinping to help him win reelection by purchasing American agricultural products

  • Trump’s ignorance of basic geography and history

  • Trump’s transactional approach to foreign policy

  • Trump’s willingness to help authoritarian leaders in exchange for personal favors

  • Detailed accounts of Trump’s interactions with Putin, Xi, Kim Jong Un, and other foreign leaders

The book became a bestseller and provided damaging insights into Trump’s first term just months before the 2020 election.

Trump was furious. He called Bolton “incompetent,” “a dope,” “a liar,” and “a washed-up guy.” But most tellingly, Trump said Bolton should face criminal prosecution for the book.

The First Investigation: 2020-2021

Before Bolton published his book, he was required to submit his manuscript to the National Security Council for prepublication review to ensure it contained no classified information.

In January 2020, Ellen Knight, the NSC’s Senior Director for Records, Access and Information Security Management, wrote to Bolton warning that the manuscript contained “significant amounts of classified information, to include information classified at the TOP SECRET level.”

Bolton made revisions. Knight later indicated the revisions appeared to remove classified content, but Bolton proceeded with publication before receiving final written authorization from the NSC.

The Trump Justice Department sued to block publication in June 2020. Federal Judge Royce Lamberth denied the injunction but issued a scathing opinion: “Bolton has gambled with the national security of the United States. He has exposed his country to harm and himself to civil (and potentially criminal) liability.”

However, the judge also noted the book was already being distributed, making an injunction pointless.

The civil case continued, but in June 2021, the Biden Justice Department dropped both the civil lawsuit and a related criminal inquiry. The case was closed.

Bolton had obtained a letter from an NSC official in 2020 stating the book contained no classified material. He maintained he had fulfilled his legal obligations.

The case was over. Until it wasn’t.

The FBI Raids: August 2025

On August 22, 2025—more than five years after the book’s publication and four years after the investigation was closed—the FBI conducted simultaneous raids on Bolton’s home in Bethesda, Maryland and his office in downtown Washington, D.C.

The searches were court-authorized, based on information provided by the CIA. Federal agents seized:

From Bolton’s office:

  • Documents labeled “secret,” “confidential,” and “classified”

  • Materials about weapons of mass destruction

  • Documents about U.S. Mission to the United Nations

  • Travel memos marked “secret”

  • A binder about State Department security briefing for 2000-2001 transition

  • Four computers and a USB flash drive

From Bolton’s home:

  • Phones, computers, and drives

  • A binder labeled “statements and reflections to allied strikes”

  • Folders labeled “Trump I-IV”

  • Various documents with classification markings

FBI Director Kash Patel, a Trump loyalist, posted on social media during the raids: “NO ONE is above the law... @FBI agents on mission.”

The timing was notable. The raids occurred in late August, just weeks before Trump would publicly demand prosecutions of Comey and James on September 20.

Bolton’s Defense

Bolton’s attorney, Abbe Lowell (who also represents Letitia James), issued a strong defense:

Lowell emphasized that these were “the kinds of ordinary records, many of which are 20 years old or more, that would be kept by a longtime career official who served at the State Department, as an assistant attorney general, the U.S. ambassador to the United Nations and the National Security adviser.”

The argument is straightforward: Bolton served in sensitive government positions for decades. Like many former officials, he retained documents—some with classification markings—from his years of service. These aren’t classified documents he stole or mishandled; they’re materials from his own government work spanning multiple administrations.

The Investigation Accelerates

For weeks after the August raids, the case proceeded at a normal pace. A grand jury in Maryland heard evidence. Career prosecutors evaluated whether charges were warranted.

Then everything changed.

The Pressure Intensifies

According to CNN, in late September, “senior Justice Department leaders” began “advocating for a charge against” Bolton. An attorney from the Deputy Attorney General’s office started “pressing the Maryland US attorney’s office this week to charge Bolton on or before Friday.”

Career prosecutors in Maryland pushed back. They wanted more time to build a stronger case by year’s end. They believed rushing to charges was inappropriate.

But according to sources familiar with the investigation, “some political leadership at the Justice Department see the Bolton case as a way to charge a criminal case Trump would like to see to placate the president.”

The pattern repeats: Career prosecutors say wait. Political leadership says charge now.

The Weekend Meeting

On Saturday, October 11, federal prosecutors met to finalize details of a potential indictment. According to sources, the Maryland-based team had “lifted their objections” to charging Bolton and were “working on the charges this weekend.”

Kelly Hayes, the acting U.S. Attorney for Maryland, reportedly told Justice Department colleagues “she believes there is a reasonable basis to pursue charges against Bolton.”

According to two people familiar with the case, “a complaint or an indictment could come as early as next week.” The sources said Hayes and her team were weighing whether to file charges in a “rushed complaint”—a filing that allows lodging criminal allegations quickly with a grand jury indictment to follow later.

The Irony: Trump’s Documents vs. Bolton’s Documents

The Bolton prosecution presents an almost comical irony that would be funny if the implications weren’t so serious.

What Trump Did

In 2023, Special Counsel Jack Smith charged Trump with 40 counts related to classified documents, including:

  • Willful retention of national defense information (31 counts)

  • Conspiracy to obstruct justice

  • Withholding documents and records

  • Corruptly concealing documents

  • Concealing documents in a federal investigation

  • Scheme to conceal

  • False statements and representations

The indictment alleged Trump:

  • Retained hundreds of classified documents after leaving office

  • Stored them in unsecure locations (ballroom, bathroom, storage rooms at Mar-a-Lago)

  • Showed classified documents to people without security clearances

  • Refused to return them when requested

  • Obstructed efforts to recover them

  • Directed staff to hide documents from his own lawyers

  • Had staff move boxes to conceal them from the FBI

The FBI recovered approximately 13,000 items from Mar-a-Lago, including:

  • Documents marked “TOP SECRET/SCI” (highest classification)

  • Information about nuclear programs

  • Defense and weapons systems

  • Intelligence methods and capabilities

What Happened to Trump’s Case

In July 2024, U.S. District Judge Aileen Cannon—a Trump appointee—dismissed all charges on the grounds that Special Counsel Jack Smith’s appointment was unconstitutional.

Trump faced no consequences for:

  • Keeping hundreds of classified documents after leaving office

  • Storing them in a bathroom

  • Showing them to unauthorized people

  • Refusing to return them

  • Obstructing justice

The case was thrown out entirely.

What Bolton Did

Bolton, according to investigators:

  • Retained documents with classification markings from his decades of government service

  • Kept them in his private office and home

  • Most date from 1998-2006 (George W. Bush administration)

  • Some relate to his 2020 book manuscript

  • No evidence he showed them to unauthorized people

  • No evidence he refused to return them when requested

  • No evidence of obstruction

His attorney argues these are “ordinary records” that would be kept by a career official who served in multiple senior positions over 40 years.

The Disparity

The message is clear: what matters isn’t the conduct. It’s who you are and whose side you’re on.

The Foreign Government Complication

The Bolton case has an additional wrinkle that makes the prosecution even more problematic: how investigators learned about Bolton’s documents may have compromised national security more than anything Bolton allegedly did.

According to two people familiar with the matter, U.S. intelligence discovered Bolton’s AOL emails during intelligence collection operations directed at a foreign government (identity redacted in court documents).

The U.S. intelligence community believes Bolton’s emails may have been intercepted by China, Russia, or Iran years ago. That hack was one basis for the search warrant.

But here’s the problem: By using this intelligence to justify the Bolton prosecution, the U.S. has now revealed to that foreign government that American intelligence has access to its specific systems.

As sources told MSNBC, this revelation “is likely more damaging to U.S. national security than any secret Bolton may have revealed.”

Think about that: To prosecute Bolton for potentially mishandling classified information, the government compromised intelligence methods and capabilities that are likely far more sensitive than anything in Bolton’s office.

The cure is worse than the disease—unless, of course, the point isn’t actually protecting national security.

Trump’s Secret Service Retaliation

There’s another element to the Bolton story that reveals Trump’s mindset.

In 2022, an Iranian national—a member of Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps—was charged with plotting to murder Bolton in retaliation for the U.S. drone strike that killed Iranian General Qasem Soleimani in January 2020.

The murder-for-hire plot was serious enough that Bolton received Secret Service protection. The threat was real and ongoing.

In January 2025, days into Trump’s second term, the President canceled Bolton’s Secret Service detail—even though the Iranian threat remained active.

Bolton lost his security protection while facing a credible assassination threat from a hostile foreign government.

Then, in August, the FBI raided Bolton’s home and office.

In October, indictment is reportedly imminent.

The sequence tells its own story.

Why DOJ Says This Case Is Different

Multiple sources tell CNN that prosecutors within the Justice Department view the Bolton case as “different than the Comey and James cases.”

The differences cited:

  • Federal judges approved searches: Judges in both Maryland and Washington, D.C. approved search warrants, indicating probable cause

  • Physical evidence seized: Unlike Comey (testimony-based) and James (document-based investigation), investigators actually seized classified documents from Bolton’s office and home

  • Ongoing investigation: The Bolton probe began under Biden, wasn’t started by Trump (though it was closed and then reopened)

These distinctions matter for understanding how prosecutors justify the case internally. This isn’t purely based on testimony or old mortgage documents—there are actual classified documents that were seized.

But several factors undercut the “this is different” argument:

  • Career prosecutors initially wanted more time: They asked to build the case through year’s end. Political pressure forced acceleration.

  • The documents are decades old: Most date from 1998-2006, during Bolton’s service in the Bush administration. These aren’t recently stolen classified secrets.

  • Biden DOJ closed this case: The prior administration investigated and decided not to prosecute. Reopening closed investigations of political enemies is itself concerning.

  • The timing is suspicious: Raids in August, acceleration in September-October as other Trump enemies are indicted, weekend meetings to rush charges—the timeline suggests political motivation.

  • Trump publicly demanded Bolton face consequences: For years, Trump has called for Bolton’s prosecution over the book. Now it’s happening.

The Pattern Completes

With Bolton, the pattern that began with Comey and continued with James reaches its logical conclusion:

Three weeks. Three indictments. Three Trump critics.

All after Trump’s September 20 post demanding prosecutions.

All brought despite prosecutorial concerns about evidence.

All following the same pattern of political pressure overriding professional judgment.

What Happens Next

If Bolton is indicted next week as expected, he will face:

  • Years of legal proceedings

  • Enormous legal costs

  • Potential decades in prison if convicted

  • Damage to his reputation and legacy

  • The stress and uncertainty of federal criminal prosecution

Even if he’s ultimately acquitted, the process serves Trump’s purposes:

  • Bolton is neutralized as a critic

  • Other potential critics see what happens when you cross Trump

  • The “enemies list” becomes operational reality

  • The justice system is weaponized successfully

Bolton’s case differs from Comey’s and James’s in one crucial respect: there are actual classified documents that were seized. That gives the prosecution more substance than a he-said/he-said about testimony or a $50-per-month mortgage discrepancy.

But the context remains damning:

  • A closed investigation reopened

  • Career prosecutors pressured to rush charges

  • Political leadership pushing for indictment “to placate the president”

  • Trump’s years of publicly demanding Bolton face prosecution

  • The disparity between how Bolton and Trump are treated for similar conduct

The Broader Message

The Bolton indictment—when it comes—will complete a trifecta that sends unmistakable messages:

To former officials: Keep your mouth shut about what you witnessed. Writing tell-all books will result in prosecution.

To federal prosecutors: We can reopen any closed investigation. We can re-examine any document retention. Everyone is potentially vulnerable.

To Trump critics: The president’s enemies list is operational. Cross him, and we’ll find something to charge you with.

To civil society: The justice system is now a weapon. Fifty years of post-Watergate norms are dead.

Bolton’s 60 Minutes Warning

In August 2025, Bolton appeared on 60 Minutes—after the FBI raids but before his expected indictment. He was asked about Trump’s second term.

Bolton’s response: “The historical verdict on Trump will be devastating. It’s rare to see one person being so destructive. He’s more like a vandal warlord than a constructive president. That’s what history will judge him on very severely.”

Those words will likely be cited in the indictment or by prosecutors as evidence of Bolton’s bias against Trump—proof that he’s a political enemy deserving of prosecution.

But read another way, Bolton’s statement becomes prophetic. A president who uses the Justice Department to prosecute critics is, indeed, “a vandal warlord” rather than a president operating within constitutional bounds.

History may well judge Trump severely. But first, Trump is judging his enemies—and the verdicts are being delivered through federal indictments.

The Case for Prosecution: What DOJ and Trump Allies Would Say

The Department of Justice would argue the Bolton case is fundamentally different from Comey and James—and they’d have some legitimate points. Federal judges in both Maryland and Washington, D.C. approved search warrants based on probable cause that classified documents were improperly retained. Unlike the other cases, investigators actually seized physical evidence: documents marked “classified,” “secret,” and “confidential” from Bolton’s office and home. The Biden DOJ had opened the investigation, not Trump—it wasn’t manufactured from whole cloth. Bolton himself served in sensitive positions where he had access to highly classified material. And unlike testimony-based perjury or decade-old mortgage applications, these are recent documents with current classification markings.

Here’s why that defense is insufficient: The Biden Justice Department investigated and closed the case in 2021, deciding not to prosecute. Reopening closed investigations of political enemies is itself concerning. Most of the seized documents date from Bolton’s Bush administration service (1998-2006)—decades-old materials that former officials commonly retain. Career prosecutors in Maryland wanted more time to build a proper case; political appointees at Main Justice pressed for immediate charges to “placate the president,” according to sources. The Trump administration compromised sensitive intelligence methods by revealing how they learned about Bolton’s emails (foreign government intercepts)—doing more damage to national security than Bolton’s document retention. Most damningly, the disparity: Trump hoarded hundreds of highly classified documents at Mar-a-Lago, showed them to unauthorized people, and obstructed recovery—yet Judge Aileen Cannon dismissed all charges. Bolton kept old documents from his own government service and faces decades in prison. The selective application and political timing reveal weaponization.

Conclusion: The Third Act

The Bolton indictment, when it comes, will mark the completion of the pattern’s first cycle:

  • Act One (Comey): Establish the template

  • Act Two (James): Prove it’s systematic

  • Act Three (Bolton): Demonstrate acceleration and reach

Each prosecution was more brazen than the last:

Comey’s case rested on contradictory testimony where the key witness lacked credibility and the Inspector General supported Comey’s version.

James’s case involved $50 per month in mortgage savings—a prosecution so petty it bordered on absurd.

Bolton’s case prosecutes conduct (retaining old classified documents from government service) that Trump engaged in on a far more egregious scale—and faced no consequences for.

Three weeks. Three critics. Three indictments.

The enemies list isn’t a threat anymore. It’s operational.

And Bolton—the consummate Washington insider, the Republican foreign policy hawk, the man who served four presidents over four decades—is about to become its third victim.

Unless career prosecutors somehow prevail. Unless judges reject the charges. Unless there’s institutional resistance we haven’t seen yet.

But based on what we’ve witnessed over the past three weeks, I wouldn’t bet on it.

What You Can Do

This isn’t just about John Bolton. It’s about whether writing tell-all books critical of a president can result in federal prosecution years later, and whether former officials can retain documents from their own government service without facing charges while the president who hoarded far more sensitive materials faces none. Here’s how to engage:

Stay Informed

  • Follow Bolton’s case when charges are filed (expected week of October 13)

  • Compare charging documents to Trump’s dismissed Mar-a-Lago case

  • Monitor whether Bolton receives adequate time to prepare a defense or faces rushed proceedings

Make Your Voice Heard

  • Contact representatives about the Espionage Act’s application to former officials

  • Support organizations defending whistleblowers and government accountability: Government Accountability Project, Project On Government Oversight

  • Follow national security law experts analyzing the case: Bradley Moss, Kel McClanahan, Mark Zaid

Support Institutional Resistance

  • Track whether career prosecutors in Maryland continue to object publicly

  • Watch for judges who scrutinize selective prosecution claims

  • Document the chilling effect: Are other former officials destroying documents or self-censoring memoirs?

Three weeks. Three critics. Three indictments (expected).

The pattern that began with Comey and continued with James completes with Bolton—unless institutional resistance emerges that hasn’t appeared yet.

The enemies list is operational. The only question is how far it will extend.
