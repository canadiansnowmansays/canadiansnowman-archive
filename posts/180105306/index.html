The Trump Accountability Project

Introduction: A Thanksgiving Reflection on Accountability

November 27, 2025

The Rules That Bind Us

As you sit down for Thanksgiving dinner this year with your family, friends, and loved ones, take a moment to reflect on something profound. Think about how you live your life. You hold yourself accountable for your actions. You apologize when you’ve wronged someone. You pay your debts. You fulfill your obligations. When you look in the mirror each morning, you can do so knowing you try to live with morals, integrity, and empathy. You understand that your choices have consequences, and you accept responsibility for them.

These aren’t extraordinary virtues—they’re the basic expectations of human decency. We teach them to our children. We demand them of our neighbors. We build our communities on them. The entire fabric of civilized society depends on the understanding that our actions matter, that we owe duties to one another, and that we must face consequences when we fail to meet those duties.

Now think about Donald John Trump, the 45th and 47th President of the United States.

Have you normalized his behavior? Have you accepted as inevitable—or worse, admirable—the fact that he operates by an entirely different set of rules? That he has spent nearly six decades systematically evading every form of accountability that would touch the rest of us?

The Monopoly Game Where Only One Player Wins

Imagine sitting down to play Monopoly with your family on Thanksgiving evening. But this isn’t a normal game. One player—let’s call him Donald—operates under different rules than everyone else.

Donald always lands on Free Parking and collects a windfall. He rolls the dice, passes GO, and collects $200—every single time. When he lands on your property, he negotiates the rent down to nothing or simply refuses to pay. When he lands on “Go to Jail,” he pulls out another “Get Out of Jail Free” card from an apparently infinite stack.

Meanwhile, you and your family follow the rules. You pay rent when you land on his properties. You go to jail and miss turns. You mortgage your properties and eventually go bankrupt. The game continues, but only one player ever really had a chance of winning.

Everyone at the table can see what’s happening. It’s not fair. It’s not right. But Donald keeps playing, keeps winning, and keeps insisting he’s just better at the game than everyone else.

This isn’t a metaphor. This is Donald Trump’s actual life story.

For 58 years—from 1968 to 2026—Trump has played by different rules than everyone else. He has evaded consequences that would have destroyed any other American’s career, freedom, and reputation. He has broken laws, betrayed trusts, defrauded partners, assaulted women, obstructed justice, incited violence, and enriched himself at every turn.

And he has never been held accountable. Not once.

The Record We Cannot Ignore

Consider what we know, documented in court records, investigative journalism, and Trump’s own admissions:

He evaded military service through a fabricated medical diagnosis—a favor exchanged for rent concessions—while working-class Americans died in Vietnam.

He discriminated against Black families seeking housing in his properties, fought the Justice Department for years, signed a consent decree promising to comply with civil rights law, then continued discriminating anyway.

He bankrupted three Atlantic City casinos through reckless debt and incompetent management, stiffed hundreds of contractors and small businesses out of $70 million in payments, extracted millions for himself, and then claimed tax deductions on the debts he never paid—making contractors’ losses his personal tax advantage.

He defrauded 7,000 students through Trump University, using high-pressure tactics to extract up to $35,000 from vulnerable people seeking financial success, delivering worthless “education,” then settled for $25 million only after winning the presidency made a trial politically untenable.

He turned his charitable foundation into a personal slush fund, using donated money to buy portraits of himself, settle his businesses’ legal obligations, and coordinate illegally with his presidential campaign. When caught, he paid $2 million in damages and his foundation was permanently shuttered.

He structured his business affairs to avoid paying federal income taxes for years—possibly decades—claiming a $916 million loss that turned financial catastrophe into personal tax advantage, all while contractors went unpaid and workers faced layoffs.

He systematically inflated and deflated asset values to defraud banks and insurance companies while reducing tax obligations. New York Attorney General Letitia James proved decades of fraud. A judge imposed a $454 million judgment. Trump appealed, won the presidency, and the judgment was eliminated.

He was convicted on 34 felony counts for falsifying business records to conceal hush money payments that violated campaign finance law. He became the first former president ever convicted of crimes. Then he won the election, and the judge granted him an unconditional discharge—conviction remains, but zero punishment.

He assaulted E. Jean Carroll in a department store dressing room. A jury found him liable. A judge clarified his conduct met the definition of rape. He was ordered to pay $88.3 million in damages across two trials for sexual abuse and defamation. He has never faced criminal charges for sexual assault.

He maintained a 15-year friendship with convicted sex trafficker Jeffrey Epstein, whose operation recruited a minor from Trump’s own property. When Epstein died under impossible circumstances in federal custody—under the watch of Trump’s Attorney General—every avenue to the full truth was extinguished. No investigation into Trump’s knowledge of Epstein’s crimes has ever been conducted.

He obstructed justice at least ten times during the Mueller investigation—pressuring the FBI director, firing him when that failed, attempting to fire the Special Counsel, tampering with witnesses through pardon dangles, and creating false records. Mueller documented it all but couldn’t charge a sitting president. Congress failed to impeach on these grounds. No charges came after he left office.

He mishandled classified national security documents, taking hundreds to Mar-a-Lago, storing them in a bathroom and ballroom, showing them to visitors, refusing to return them, and directing staff to hide them from his own lawyer. When the case was dismissed by a judge he appointed, he declared victory.

He attempted to overturn the 2020 presidential election through a coordinated scheme involving fake electors, pressure on state officials to “find” votes, and pressure on his own Vice President to refuse certification. When those schemes failed, he incited a mob to attack the Capitol. When prosecuted federally and in Georgia, he used delay tactics until winning re-election made prosecution impossible.

He was impeached twice—once for extorting Ukraine to manufacture dirt on Joe Biden, once for inciting the January 6th insurrection. Both times the evidence was overwhelming. Both times the Senate acquitted him because Republican senators valued party loyalty over constitutional duty.

He systematically violated the Hatch Act, using federal resources and the White House itself for campaign purposes. He held the Republican National Convention at the White House. Cabinet officials campaigned for him using their official positions. The Office of Special Counsel documented dozens of violations. No one was disciplined.

He overrode security clearance denials for at least 25 people, including son-in-law Jared Kushner, whom the CIA believed posed security risks. After leaving office, Kushner received $2 billion from Saudi Arabia—exactly the foreign influence security professionals had warned about.

He lied about COVID-19’s severity, telling Americans it would “disappear like a miracle” while privately admitting to Bob Woodward it was “deadly stuff.” He promoted dangerous quack cures, politicized mask-wearing, attacked Dr. Fauci, and held super-spreader rallies. By the time he left office, more than 400,000 Americans were dead—studies estimate 40% were preventable deaths with competent federal leadership.

He enabled a 16-year conspiracy by four conservative activists to destroy the federal government’s ability to regulate greenhouse gases—revoking the scientific endangerment finding that underpinned every federal climate regulation, sabotaging the renewable energy technologies that could replace fossil fuels, and locking in atmospheric damage that will be paid for by generations not yet born, including his own grandchildren.

He weaponized presidential power to grant corporate immunity across every regulatory domain—protecting glyphosate producers with an explicit “corporate viability” mandate, gutting workplace safety enforcement that allows 5,300 workers to die yearly, eliminating mining protections causing black lung deaths, destroying food safety oversight, selling nursing home deregulation for $4.8 million that costs 13,000 elderly lives per year, and handing regulatory agencies to the industries they regulate, costing consumers $19 billion while transportation safety enforcement collapses by 50-83%.

He poisoned communities and silenced their voices—rolling back drinking water protections that affected 73 million Americans, gutting PFAS reporting by 97% to hide forever chemicals from public disclosure, delaying toxic coal ash cleanup at 300+ sites, slashing EPA’s budget 54% while touting “massive accomplishments,” eliminating 2,000 environmental protection staff in one year, cutting civil enforcement by 69%, and destroying NEPA environmental review that gave communities a legal voice when polluters threatened their health.

He launched seven military operations without a single congressional war powers vote—waging undeclared wars while Congress abdicated its constitutional authority to check presidential military power. He ordered a bombing campaign against Iran whose objectives shifted from nuclear disarmament to unconditional surrender in the space of a single week, using rhetoric that mirrored, almost word for word, the justifications Vladimir Putin used to invade Ukraine. He used the Iran war to bury politically damaging Epstein media coverage, and his aggression strengthened the very hardliners he claimed to oppose, handing Iran’s theocratic regime a gift of legitimacy. The country that spent seventy years defining itself against Soviet authoritarianism was now borrowing its language, its logic, and its playbook.

He turned his social media account into the most profitable trading desk in history, using presidential announcements to manipulate Truth Social stock prices, generating $400 million in a single hour while his followers lost fortunes buying at peaks he created and sold at valleys he engineered—transforming the presidency itself into an insider trading operation that would send any other American to prison.

And in every single instance—every single one—he faced no meaningful accountability.

No criminal convictions that stuck. No significant financial penalties he couldn’t absorb or eliminate. No admission of wrongdoing. No genuine consequences that altered his trajectory. Instead, through wealth, legal maneuvering, political power, and shameless defiance, Trump has demonstrated something profoundly disturbing about American society: that accountability is effectively optional for those with sufficient resources and shamelessness.

How Trump Plays the Game: Six Strategies for Evading Accountability

Examining Trump’s 58-year record reveals consistent patterns in how he evades consequences:

Delay, Delay, Delay

Trump’s primary legal strategy is delay. He files motion after motion, appeals every ruling, and drags cases out for years or decades. Trump University took six years to settle. The classified documents case was deliberately slow-walked by a favorable judge. The Georgia case was bogged down in procedural fights.

Trump understands that justice delayed is justice denied. If he can postpone accountability long enough, witnesses die, evidence disappears, public attention wanes, and political circumstances change. His 2024 election victory demonstrated the ultimate success of this strategy—by delaying criminal cases until after the election, he regained power and ended the prosecutions.

Settle Without Admitting Wrongdoing

When delay doesn’t work, Trump settles. Trump University: $25 million settlement, no admission of guilt. Housing discrimination: consent decree, no admission of guilt. Trump Foundation: $2 million payment, no admission of guilt.

These settlements allow Trump to claim he never did anything wrong. He paid money to make the cases go away, but he frames it as buying peace, not as compensation for victims of his misconduct.

Use Power to Fire Investigators

When Trump gained presidential power, he weaponized it against those investigating him. He fired FBI Director James Comey for refusing to drop the Flynn investigation. He attempted to fire Special Counsel Mueller. He pressured Attorney General Sessions to unrecuse from the Russia investigation. He pardoned allies who might have testified against him.

Trump demonstrated that presidential power can be used to obstruct justice if you’re willing to abuse it—and if the Senate won’t convict you for doing so.

Exploit Partisan Protection

Trump’s Republican allies in Congress and the Senate protected him from accountability at every turn. They refused to call witnesses during his impeachment trials. They voted to acquit despite overwhelming evidence. They blocked investigations and subpoenas. They abdicated their constitutional war powers authority rather than challenge a president of their own party.

Partisan loyalty trumped constitutional duty. Trump understood this and exploited it ruthlessly.

Win Elections to End Prosecutions

The ultimate accountability evasion: Trump ran for president again and won. His 2024 victory ended the federal criminal cases against him. The classified documents case was dismissed. The January 6th prosecution was dropped. The Georgia case collapsed.

Trump proved that if you can delay criminal accountability long enough to win an election, you can end the prosecutions entirely.

Shamelessness as Strategy

Perhaps Trump’s most effective tool is his complete lack of shame. Normal politicians resign when caught in scandals. Normal businesspeople settle quietly. Normal people feel embarrassment when exposed as frauds.

Trump doubles down. He attacks his accusers. He lies about the evidence. He claims persecution. And his supporters believe him because they can’t conceive that someone would lie so brazenly and consistently.

Shamelessness breaks the accountability system because the system assumes that exposure and public criticism will create pressure for consequences. Trump is immune to shame, so exposure doesn’t matter.

Why This Matters Now

“Why dwell on the past?” some will ask. “Trump won. He’s president again. Move on.”

But this isn’t about dwelling on the past—it’s about understanding the present and safeguarding the future.

Every time Trump escaped accountability, he learned he could push further. The bone spurs fraud taught him that wealth and connections could secure exemptions from shared sacrifice. The housing discrimination case taught him that aggressive legal tactics could blunt federal enforcement. The Atlantic City bankruptcies taught him that corporate structures could shield him from personal responsibility even as he enriched himself from others’ losses. Trump University taught him that he could settle massive fraud claims without admitting guilt. The Trump Foundation taught him that illegal use of charity funds brought only civil penalties he could afford. The classified documents case taught him that judicial appointments could shield him from prosecution. The January 6th case taught him that presidential immunity—even for criminal acts—was the ultimate get-out-of-jail-free card. And the Venezuela seizure taught him that he could use military force abroad without consequence, paving the way for an undeclared war against Iran waged with Putin’s rhetoric and without Congress’s vote.

Each unpunished violation was a lesson: accountability is for other people.

And now he’s president again, armed with three more years of power and the knowledge that he has never faced real consequences for anything. He’s also actively profiting from the office RIGHT NOW—directing federal money to his properties, accepting foreign government payments, systematically violating the Constitution’s Emoluments Clause, and using his social media account to manipulate stock prices for personal gain. He is waging war without congressional authorization, using military force to distract from domestic scandals, and borrowing the language of authoritarian aggressors to justify open-ended conflict. This ongoing conduct from 2025-2029 might finally create accountability he cannot evade, because after January 20, 2029, he can never run for president again.

CONCLUSION: The Weight of the Record: A Final Accounting

A note on numbers: this series contains forty-seven parts spanning both Trump presidencies — from the forty-fifth to the forty-seventh. The forty-seven parts matching his presidential number was not designed. It arrived that way. It documents fifty-eight years of accountability evasion, from 1968 through 2026. The Woman Who Defeated One Polluter, Then Watched Trump Protect Them All

In 1996, a legal clerk named Erin Brockovich exposed Pacific Gas & Electric’s contamination of drinking water in Hinkley, California. Her investigation led to a $333 million settlement—the largest direct-action lawsuit in U.S. history at the time. The case became legendary. She became a symbol of how persistent individuals could hold powerful corporations accountable for poisoning communities.

In July 2024, Brockovich published an essay in The New York Times warning that if Trump won the election, “Americans’ health will be at risk” as corporations challenged or ignored weakened environmental protections.

Trump won.

And in 2025-2026, his administration systematically dismantled drinking water protections for 73 million Americans, gutted reporting requirements by 97% to hide toxic chemicals from public disclosure, delayed cleanup of contaminated sites, slashed the EPA’s budget by 54%, eliminated 2,000 environmental protection staff, and cut enforcement of pollution laws by 69%.

Erin Brockovich spent decades fighting to hold one company accountable for contaminating one community’s water. Trump used presidential power to protect every company contaminating every community’s water.

She defeated one polluter. He protected them all. And he faces no accountability for it.

This is the pattern that defines Trump’s presidency: the systematic use of executive power to grant immunity to those who harm Americans while those Americans have no recourse.

Workers die—5,300 every year—because Trump gutted OSHA enforcement and eliminated mining safety protections. No accountability.

Elderly nursing home residents die—13,000 preventable deaths annually—because nursing home executives donated $4.8 million and Trump eliminated staffing requirements. No accountability.

Consumers lose $19 billion to predatory lenders because Trump attempted to eliminate the CFPB and slashed enforcement. No accountability.

Communities drink contaminated water—73 million people exposed to cancer-causing chemicals—because Trump weakened drinking water standards and hid toxic chemicals from public view. No accountability.

Future generations inherit a poisoned atmosphere because four conservative activists spent 16 years plotting to destroy climate regulations, and Trump executed their plan. No accountability.

A president wages seven wars without a single congressional vote, borrows Vladimir Putin’s rhetoric to justify bombing a nation of eighty-eight million people, and uses the conflict to bury politically damaging media coverage. No accountability.

A president turns his social media account into a trading desk, manipulating stock prices for personal profit while his supporters lose fortunes buying at the peaks he creates. No accountability.

The Architecture of Impunity: How Every System Failed

This series documents 47 case studies across 58 years. What emerges is not a collection of isolated failures, but a comprehensive architecture of impunity—a system where accountability mechanisms exist on paper but fail in practice.

Legal accountability failed. Trump could afford endless litigation. His victims couldn’t. Justice delayed became justice denied.

Electoral accountability failed. Voters knew about the fraud, the sexual assault, the corruption. They elected him anyway. Twice.

Political accountability failed. Senate Republicans acquitted him during two impeachments despite overwhelming evidence. Party loyalty trumped constitutional duty.

Judicial accountability failed. Trump’s appointed judges ruled in his favor. The Supreme Court granted him immunity and delayed trials until he could win re-election and end them.

Institutional accountability failed. Federal agencies became tools of the industries they were supposed to regulate. The EPA that should protect communities now protects polluters. The DOT that should ensure transportation safety now serves railroad executives. The CFPB that should defend consumers was gutted to protect predatory lenders.

Constitutional war powers accountability failed. Congress refused to assert its exclusive authority to declare war, allowing a president to launch seven military operations—including a major bombing campaign against Iran—without a single vote. The War Powers Resolution of 1973, designed to prevent exactly this, proved toothless when the legislators responsible for enforcing it chose party loyalty over constitutional duty.

Financial market accountability failed. When a president uses his social media account to manipulate stock prices, generating hundreds of millions in personal profit while his supporters lose fortunes, no securities regulator investigates. No prosecutor files charges. No congressional committee holds hearings. The SEC that should police insider trading looks the other way when the insider is the president.

Scientific accountability failed. Climate science was ignored. Public health expertise was dismissed. Environmental research was defunded. The endangerment finding—a scientific determination that greenhouse gases threaten human health—was revoked not because the science changed, but because Trump chose to ignore it.

Social accountability failed. Shame, embarrassment, and public disapproval—the informal mechanisms that enforce norms in a functioning society—simply don’t apply to someone who feels no shame and whose supporters reward shamelessness.

Media accountability failed. “Both-sidesism” treated Trump’s lies as one legitimate perspective among many. Normalization replaced alarm. The sheer volume of misconduct overwhelmed the capacity to maintain sustained attention on any single violation.

Every mechanism that should create accountability—legal, electoral, political, judicial, institutional, constitutional, financial, scientific, social, media—failed. Not because they’re inherently flawed, but because they depend on people in power choosing to enforce them. And when those people value power over principle, the entire architecture collapses.

What We’ve Normalized

Perhaps the most disturbing aspect is what we’ve come to accept as normal.

We’ve normalized that a president can be convicted of 34 felonies and face zero punishment.

We’ve normalized that a president can be found liable for sexual abuse—conduct a judge confirmed meets the definition of rape—and face no criminal charges.

We’ve normalized that a president can attempt to overturn an election through a coordinated scheme involving fake electors and mob violence, and escape accountability by winning the next election.

We’ve normalized that a president can mishandle classified national security documents, show them to visitors, hide them from investigators, and have the case dismissed by a judge he appointed.

We’ve normalized that a president can accept $4.8 million in donations from nursing home executives and then eliminate staffing requirements that would have saved 13,000 elderly lives per year.

We’ve normalized that a president can use executive power to protect glyphosate manufacturers with an explicit “corporate viability” mandate while exposing Americans to a probable carcinogen.

We’ve normalized that a president can weaken drinking water standards affecting 73 million Americans, gut toxic chemical reporting by 97%, and slash the EPA budget by 54%—all while claiming to make America healthy again.

We’ve normalized that a president can enable a 16-year conspiracy to destroy federal climate regulations, locking in atmospheric damage that will harm generations not yet born, including his own grandchildren.

We’ve normalized that contractors go unpaid, students get defrauded, charitable donations fund personal purchases, casino workers lose jobs while Trump extracts millions, and COVID-19 deaths reach 400,000 with 40% preventable through competent leadership.

We’ve normalized that a president can wage seven wars without a single congressional vote, echo Vladimir Putin’s invasion rhetoric almost word for word, demand unconditional surrender from a nation of eighty-eight million people, and use the conflict to bury damaging media coverage—all while the Speaker of the House calls it “an operation” to avoid the word that would trigger constitutional accountability.

We’ve normalized that a president can use his social media account as a trading desk, announcing policies and making statements timed to move stock prices, selling at peaks and buying at valleys, generating $400 million in a single hour while his supporters—who bought at the peaks he created—lose fortunes. We call it “just business” instead of what it is: the president running an insider trading operation from the Oval Office.

We’ve normalized all of this. We shrug. We move on. We accept it as the price of doing politics.

But this isn’t politics. This is the systematic dismantling of the principle that our actions have consequences.

We’ve accepted that there are two Americas: one where a single mother faces jail time for welfare fraud involving hundreds of dollars, and another where a president’s foundation can be shut down for “shocking patterns of illegality” involving millions and he simply pays a fine and continues. One where a worker dying on the job triggers an OSHA investigation and potential penalties, and another where a president can gut OSHA enforcement causing thousands of additional deaths and face no consequences. One where a teenager caught with marijuana faces years in prison, and another where a president can attempt to overturn an election and escape prosecution by winning the next one. One where a community contaminated by illegal pollution can demand cleanup, and another where a president can delay that cleanup, hide the contamination data, and eliminate the agency responsible for protecting them. One where an ordinary citizen who uses insider information to trade stocks goes to prison, and another where a president can manipulate markets with his social media account for hundreds of millions in personal profit and face no investigation.

This normalization is dangerous not because of Trump alone, but because of what it teaches every ambitious, unscrupulous person watching: if you have enough money, enough shamelessness, and enough willingness to break norms, you can get away with nearly anything in America.

A Question for Our Time

So as you consider this record—47 parts documenting 58 years of accountability evasion—ask yourself:

Have we accepted that accountability is no longer a universal expectation in America? Have we normalized a two-tier system where consequences are for the powerless but optional for the powerful?

What does it mean when a president can poison communities, gut agencies meant to protect them, and face no consequences?

What does it mean when workers die, consumers are defrauded, elderly residents are neglected, and communities are contaminated—all because a president used executive power to protect the corporations responsible?

What does it mean when a president can wage undeclared wars using an autocrat’s rhetoric while the Speaker of the House calls it “an operation”?

What does it mean when a president can manipulate financial markets for personal profit while his supporters lose their savings, and no one investigates?

What does it mean when every mechanism designed to create accountability—legal, electoral, political, judicial, institutional, constitutional, financial, scientific, social—fails simultaneously?

And most importantly: What does it say about us that we elected him anyway?

Erin Brockovich defeated Pacific Gas & Electric because the system, however imperfectly, eventually held PG&E accountable. The company paid $333 million. The contaminated community got recognition and compensation. The precedent established that corporations couldn’t poison people’s water without consequences.

Trump systematically destroyed that principle. He used presidential power to ensure corporations can poison water without consequences. He gutted the agencies that would investigate. He eliminated the reporting that would expose. He cut the enforcement that would penalize. He silenced the communities that would object.

And he faced no accountability for any of it.

The Promise and the Warning

This series doesn’t claim to have all the answers. But it does aim to preserve the record. Because if we don’t document how we got here—how decades of accountability evasion became a pathway to the presidency, and how presidential power became a tool to grant immunity to those who harm Americans, to manipulate markets for personal gain, and to wage undeclared wars with an autocrat’s playbook—we’ll never understand how to ensure it doesn’t happen again.

We’ll never restore the principle that should unite every American, regardless of politics: that our actions have consequences, that accountability matters, and that no one—no matter how rich, how famous, or how powerful—should be above the law.

The Monopoly game continues. Trump still has a stack of “Get Out of Jail Free” cards. And he’s just landed on Free Parking again.

But we don’t have to accept this as inevitable. We don’t have to normalize it. And we don’t have to forget.

This series is the record of what happened—and what didn’t happen afterward.

Forty-seven case studies. Fifty-eight years. Zero meaningful accountability.

Read it. Remember it. And ask yourself what we’re going to do about it.

Because the next person watching this—the next ambitious, unscrupulous person learning that accountability is optional if you have enough power and shamelessness—is taking notes.
