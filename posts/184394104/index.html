The Asimov Gap

Breaking the Pattern

Part 4: Why Oligarchs Aren’t Invincible

January 16, 2026

The Feeling of Inevitability

When you look at the five billionaires racing to build AGI—their wealth, their influence, their captured regulators, their ability to shape policy—it’s easy to feel hopeless.

Elon Musk spent $270 million to help elect a president. He now has a government position with power to gut the regulatory agencies that might oversee his companies. Mark Zuckerberg controls the information diet of 3 billion people. Sam Altman has the attention of every major government. These men have resources and power beyond what most people can comprehend.

Concentrated power always feels permanent. Until suddenly it doesn’t.

This essay is about pattern recognition: We’ve been here before. Different technology, different oligarchs, but same basic structure—concentrated wealth using its power to resist democratic accountability.

And we’ve won before.

Not easily. Not quickly. Not without setbacks and sacrifices. But democratic movements have repeatedly defeated concentrated power that looked invincible.

The pattern is consistent: Oligarchs consolidate power. Democratic opposition organizes. Long struggle. Oligarchs predict disaster if they lose control. Oligarchs lose. Society prospers.

Let’s examine the pattern so we can apply it to AI oligarchy.

The Progressive Era: Breaking the Robber Barons

A historical cartoon of the John D. Rockefeller Standard Oil octopus

Late 1800s America: Industrial revolution creates unprecedented wealth concentration.

The oligarchs:

  • John D. Rockefeller (Standard Oil): Controlled 90% of US oil refining

  • J.P. Morgan (Finance): Dominated banking, railroads, steel

  • Andrew Carnegie (Steel): Near monopoly on steel production

  • Cornelius Vanderbilt (Railroads): Controlled much of rail transport

  • Combined wealth: Equivalent to hundreds of billions today

Their power:

  • Bought politicians directly (Senate was “Millionaire’s Club”)

  • Owned newspapers that shaped public opinion

  • Controlled prices, wages, working conditions

  • Crushed unions with private armies and government help

  • Democratic accountability virtually nonexistent

Their argument:

  • “We built this economy. You want to destroy it?”

  • “Competition is natural selection. Efficiency requires consolidation.”

  • “Regulation will destroy innovation and prosperity.”

  • “We know better than government how to run these industries.”

  • “Trust us. We’re the experts. Government interference will ruin everything.”

Sound familiar?

The response took decades:

1890: Sherman Antitrust Act passed (weak, rarely enforced initially)

1901: Theodore Roosevelt becomes president, begins “trust-busting”

  • Northern Securities (railroad monopoly) broken up (1904)

  • Standard Oil broken up (1911)

  • American Tobacco broken up (1911)

1913:

  • 16th Amendment (income tax) enables government to tax concentrated wealth

  • 17th Amendment (direct election of senators) reduces oligarchic control of Senate

  • Federal Reserve created to regulate banking

1914: Clayton Antitrust Act strengthens government power

The oligarchs predicted disaster: “Breaking up our companies will destroy the economy. Innovation will stop. America will fall behind.”

What actually happened:

  • Economy boomed in 1920s

  • Innovation accelerated (automobiles, electricity, aviation, radio)

  • More companies meant more competition, faster progress

  • Breaking concentration of power enabled broader prosperity

  • Middle class emerged

The lesson: Oligarchs always predict disaster when they lose control. They’re always wrong.

How It Was Done: The Organizing Strategy

The Progressive Era victory didn’t happen automatically. It required:

1. Exposure

Muckraking journalists documented oligarchic abuses:

  • Ida Tarbell exposed Standard Oil’s predatory practices (1904)

  • Upton Sinclair revealed meatpacking industry horrors (The Jungle, 1906)

  • Lincoln Steffens documented political corruption (The Shame of the Cities, 1904)

Made abuses visible and undeniable.

2. Coalition Building

Diverse groups united against concentrated power:

  • Workers (unions organizing for rights)

  • Farmers (hurt by railroad monopolies)

  • Small business (crushed by trusts)

  • Middle class (concerned about democracy)

  • Intellectuals (documenting problems)

Different motivations, common enemy.

3. Political Pressure

  • Mass demonstrations

  • Strikes

  • Boycotts

  • Electoral organizing

  • Made supporting oligarchs politically costly

4. Legal Action

  • Antitrust lawsuits

  • State-level reforms

  • Constitutional amendments

  • Used democratic institutions to constrain oligarchic power

5. Persistence

From Sherman Act (1890) to breaking up Standard Oil (1911): 21 years.

From Roosevelt becoming president (1901) to major reforms (1913-1914): 12-13 years.

Took decades. But won.

Pattern for today: Same strategy can work against AI oligarchs. Exposure (document harms), coalition (unite affected groups), political pressure (make supporting oligarchs costly), legal action (democratic oversight), persistence (decades-long commitment).

How Cheap Corruption Actually Is

When Geoffrey Hinton arrived in the United States as a postdoc in 1978, he received an education in American democracy that shocked him.

At Georgetown, he recalled: “There was a US government organization that protected consumers that said that used car dealers should be required to tell you about a fault in the car if they had used that fault in the car to buy it cheap from the previous owner.”

Reasonable regulation. If dealer bought car cheap because of known defect, dealer must disclose that defect to buyer. Basic consumer protection.

The Used Car Dealers of America didn’t like that law.

Hinton continued: “They paid every senator $10,000. 100 senators, that’s just a million dollars, and the Senate voted against it.”

Let that sink in: The entire United States Senate for $1 million.

“That just totally shocked me that you could buy the Senate for a million dollars.”

This was 1978. Adjusted for inflation, about $4.7 million today. Still absurdly cheap.

Now consider: Elon Musk spent $270 million to help elect Trump. That’s 54 times what bought the entire Senate in 1978.

The AI oligarchs have vastly more money than the used car dealers did. And corruption has only gotten easier.

Citizens United (2010) removed limits on corporate political spending. Super PACs allow unlimited contributions. Dark money hides donors. Lobbying has become sophisticated industry.

Hinton’s assessment at Georgetown: “I think the US has a problem which I put down to the Supreme Court and judgments they’ve made that you can just buy politicians—not Bernie but the other ones—because Bernie gets his money from ordinary people. And there’s no limit to how much you can contribute to politicians via these sort of super PACs that pretend to be independent. That means the rich can just buy the political system.“

If the entire Senate cost $1 million in 1978, what does it cost now?

More, certainly. But not proportionally. Political spending has increased. But so has oligarchic wealth. The used car dealers pooled resources to buy 100 senators for $10,000 each.

Tech oligarchs have billions. Individually. They can outspend the used car dealers by orders of magnitude.

And they are:

  • Meta spending millions lobbying on AI regulation

  • OpenAI hiring Washington insiders

  • Amazon funding think tanks producing favorable research

  • Google employing former regulators as consultants

  • Collective spending dwarfs what citizen groups can marshal

Pattern is clear: Democracy isn’t expensive to buy. Never has been. Just requires concentrated wealth and willingness to use it.

That’s what makes oligarchic AI control so dangerous: The people developing AI have the wealth to buy protection from democratic oversight. And incentive to do it.

Hinton’s 1978 shock was prescient. If democracy was that cheap then, it’s not more expensive now—just more sophisticated in how money flows.

The solution Sanders advocates isn’t complicated: Get money out of politics. Public financing of elections. Overturn Citizens United. Ban corporate political spending.

But achieving that requires overcoming the oligarchs who benefit from current system. Which requires popular movement strong enough to demand change despite oligarchic resistance.

History says it’s possible. The used car dealers won in 1978. But robber barons lost in 1900s. Tobacco companies lost in 1990s.

Oligarchs always look invincible. Until they’re not.

The question is whether we organize before AI oligarchs consolidate power that truly is irreversible—because they control technology more powerful than human intelligence.

The Tobacco Fight: Forty Years to Victory

1950s: Evidence emerges that smoking causes cancer.

The oligarchs: Tobacco companies (Philip Morris, RJ Reynolds, etc.)

  • Massive industry, deeply embedded in American culture

  • Huge advertising budgets shaping public perception

  • Millions of addicted customers

  • Deep political connections

Their strategy:

  • Deny the science (”Not proven”)

  • Fund contrary research (create doubt)

  • Attack critics as anti-freedom

  • Lobby heavily against regulation

  • “Personal choice. Government overreach. Economic disaster if regulated.”

The fight took four decades:

1964: Surgeon General’s report definitively links smoking to cancer

1965: Warning labels required on cigarette packages (weak, ineffective)

1971: Cigarette advertising banned on TV and radio

1980s-1990s: Mounting evidence, increasing restrictions, growing public awareness

1998: Master Settlement Agreement—tobacco companies agree to pay $206 billion over 25 years, accept advertising restrictions, fund anti-smoking campaigns

From first evidence (1950s) to major victory (1998): About 40 years.

The tobacco companies predicted disaster: “Regulation will destroy industry. Jobs will vanish. Government tyranny. Slippery slope to banning everything.”

What actually happened:

  • Smoking rates dropped dramatically (42% of adults in 1965 → 16% in 2020)

  • Millions of lives saved

  • Healthcare costs reduced

  • Tobacco companies still exist (smaller but profitable)

  • Public health won without economic catastrophe

How it was done:

Exposure: Scientists documented harms. Whistleblowers revealed company lies. Media covered dying smokers.

Coalition: Public health advocates, cancer survivors, parents, state attorneys general, insurance companies (tired of costs).

Political pressure: Lawsuits. Ballot initiatives. Electoral campaigns making tobacco support toxic.

Legal action: State lawsuits. Federal regulations. Master Settlement Agreement.

Persistence: Forty years. Multiple setbacks. Kept fighting.

The lesson: Even deeply embedded, culturally normalized oligarchic industries can be defeated through sustained democratic organizing.

The Civil Rights Movement: Overturning “Natural Order”

1950s America: Racial segregation was law in South, practice in North. Backed by:

  • State governments

  • Local police

  • Federal inaction

  • Supreme Court precedent (Plessy v. Ferguson, 1896)

  • Cultural norms accepted by white majority

  • Economic interests (cheap labor, divided working class)

The oligarchic argument:

  • “This is natural order”

  • “Separate but equal”

  • “States’ rights”

  • “Change will destroy social fabric”

  • “You’re moving too fast”

  • “Gradual progress, not disruptive change”

The power structure was total: Government, courts, police, culture, economy all supported segregation.

Looked utterly impossible to change.

The movement took 15 years to major victories:

1954: Brown v. Board of Education (Supreme Court reverses Plessy)

1955-1956: Montgomery Bus Boycott

1960: Sit-ins spread across South

1961: Freedom Rides

1963: Birmingham campaign, March on Washington

1964: Civil Rights Act

1965: Voting Rights Act

From Montgomery (1955) to Voting Rights Act (1965): 10 years to transform American society.

The segregationists predicted disaster: “Destroying our way of life. Racial mixing will cause chaos. Economic ruin. Federal tyranny.”

What actually happened:

  • South didn’t collapse

  • Democracy expanded

  • Economy grew

  • Social fabric strengthened (despite resistance)

  • Justice advanced without apocalypse

How it was done:

Exposure: Photographs of police dogs attacking children. Media coverage of violence against peaceful protesters. Made injustice undeniable.

Coalition: Black churches, students, labor unions, white allies, religious groups, eventually business leaders tired of instability.

Political pressure: Boycotts (Montgomery buses, lunch counters). Sit-ins. Marches. Made segregation economically and politically costly.

Legal action: Lawsuits challenging segregation. Federal intervention. Congressional legislation.

Persistence: Years of beatings, arrests, murders. Kept organizing despite tremendous personal cost.

The lesson: Even power structures that seem woven into society’s fabric can be dismantled through sustained democratic organizing—if people refuse to accept injustice as permanent.

The Environmental Movement: Stopping “Progress”

1960s America: Industrial pollution was accepted cost of progress.

  • Rivers caught fire (Cuyahoga River, Cleveland, 1969)

  • Air in cities barely breathable

  • Toxic waste dumped freely

  • No environmental regulations

  • Economic growth > environmental protection

The oligarchs: Industrial corporations

  • “Pollution is progress”

  • “Jobs vs. environment”

  • “Regulation will destroy competitiveness”

  • “Trust us to self-regulate”

  • “Environmentalists are radicals threatening prosperity”

The movement mobilized rapidly:

1962: Rachel Carson’s Silent Spring exposes pesticide dangers

1969: Cuyahoga River fire galvanizes public opinion

1970: First Earth Day (20 million participants)

1970: Environmental Protection Agency created

1970: Clean Air Act

1972: Clean Water Act

1973: Endangered Species Act

From Carson’s book (1962) to major legislation (1970-1973): About 10 years to transform policy.

The polluters predicted disaster: “Regulation will destroy economy. Manufacturing will move overseas. America will lose competitiveness. Poverty will increase.”

What actually happened:

  • Rivers stopped catching fire

  • Air quality improved dramatically

  • Public health enhanced

  • Economy continued growing (actually grew faster with cleaner environment)

  • Innovation increased (clean tech)

  • Environmental protection and prosperity coexisted

How it was done:

Exposure: Scientists documented harms. Journalists showed pollution’s impacts. Public saw rivers burning on TV.

Coalition: Scientists, outdoor enthusiasts, parents concerned about children’s health, fishing/hunting groups, eventually businesses seeing opportunity in clean tech.

Political pressure: Earth Day demonstrated massive public support. Made opposing environment politically toxic.

Legal action: Lawsuits against polluters. Federal legislation. EPA enforcement.

Persistence: Continued organizing despite industry resistance and claims of economic harm.

The lesson: Industries claiming regulation will cause economic catastrophe are consistently wrong. Environmental protection strengthened economy.

The Common Pattern

Looking across these victories, pattern emerges:

Stage 1: Oligarchic Consolidation

  • Power concentrates

  • Democratic accountability erodes

  • Oligarchs normalize their dominance

  • Alternatives seem impossible

Stage 2: Exposure

  • Abuses documented

  • Harms made visible

  • Lies revealed

  • Public awareness grows

Stage 3: Organization

  • Diverse groups unite around common cause

  • Coalitions form across traditional divisions

  • Movement infrastructure develops

Stage 4: Political Pressure

  • Demonstrations, boycotts, strikes

  • Electoral organizing

  • Making oligarchic support politically costly

  • Shifting power dynamics

Stage 5: Legal Action

  • Lawsuits

  • Legislation

  • Regulatory action

  • Democratic institutions assert control

Stage 6: Oligarchic Resistance

  • Predict disaster if they lose power

  • Massively fund opposition

  • Delay and obstruct

  • Fight desperately to preserve control

Stage 7: Victory (Eventually)

  • Democratic governance asserted

  • Oligarchic power constrained

  • Predicted disaster doesn’t materialize

  • Society prospers with accountable power

Stage 8: Normalization

  • New system becomes accepted

  • Younger generation can’t imagine old system

  • Former oligarchic control seems absurd in retrospect

This pattern has repeated consistently across American history.

Current moment with AI: We’re at Stage 2 (Exposure) moving into Stage 3 (Organization).

Sanders convening Hinton at Georgetown = Exposure (making AI oligarchy visible)

Next stage: Organization (building coalition, creating political pressure)

History suggests this is winnable. But requires work.

Why AI Oligarchs Are Vulnerable

Despite their wealth and power, AI oligarchs have weaknesses:

1. They need public trust

Unlike robber barons (sold oil, steel—products people needed regardless of trust), AI companies need people to voluntarily use their products.

If public distrusts AI, adoption slows, revenues decline.

This creates leverage: Public backlash threatens business model.

2. They need government legitimacy

AI companies need:

  • Legal protection (Section 230, etc.)

  • Government data and contracts

  • Infrastructure support (electricity, data centers)

  • Favorable tax treatment

Government can withdraw these. This creates dependency.

3. They need workers

AI requires highly skilled workers. Unlike industrial era (replaceable factory workers), AI talent is scarce and mobile.

Workers organizing can threaten AI development. Google’s Project Maven rebellion (2018) showed this—workers forced company to drop military AI contract.

4. They’re competing against each other

Unlike Progressive Era’s trusts (coordinated to suppress competition), AI companies fiercely compete.

This prevents unified oligarchic resistance to regulation. Can’t all agree on lobbying strategy because they’re racing each other.

5. They need data

AI requires training data. Much of it comes from users who could withhold it or demand compensation.

Regulatory requirement to pay for training data would transform economics.

6. International markets matter

Europe is regulating. If European standards become global (Brussels Effect), US companies must comply regardless of US policy.

This reduces US oligarchs’ ability to escape regulation.

7. The technology itself creates vulnerabilities

AI makes mistakes. Causes harms. Creates scandals. Each failure builds case for regulation.

Pattern: Unlike robber barons (controlled physical resources) or tobacco (had addicted customers), AI oligarchs depend on trust, legitimacy, talent, data, and international acceptance. All vulnerable to democratic pressure.

The Cracks Are Already Showing

We’re seeing early signs of oligarchic vulnerability:

Internal dissent:

  • AI researchers leaving companies over safety concerns

  • Workers organizing (Google, Microsoft, OpenAI)

  • Whistleblowers exposing problems

  • Companies not monolithic

Regulatory action:

  • EU passed AI Act

  • US states proposing regulation

  • Investigations into AI impacts

  • Democratic institutions beginning to act

Public backlash:

  • Growing concern about job displacement

  • Distrust of AI decision-making

  • Resistance to surveillance

  • Popular opinion shifting

Technical failures:

  • AI mistakes generating scandals

  • Bias in AI systems exposed

  • Security vulnerabilities revealed

  • Building case for oversight

International coordination:

  • G7 discussing AI governance

  • UN examining AI regulation

  • Democratic countries comparing approaches

  • Potential for coordinated action

Elite division:

  • Some tech leaders calling for regulation

  • Some investors worried about risks

  • Some government officials taking seriously

  • Oligarchs not united

These are early cracks. Not victory. But vulnerabilities exist.

What Has to Happen: The Organizing Agenda

Based on historical pattern, here’s what democratic victory requires:

1. Continue Exposure

  • Document AI harms (job displacement, bias, manipulation, surveillance)

  • Expose oligarchic capture (lobbying, regulatory evasion)

  • Make visible what’s hidden

  • Sanders-Hinton conversation is model

2. Build Coalition

  • Workers threatened by displacement

  • Small businesses crushed by AI monopolies

  • Civil rights groups concerned about bias

  • Privacy advocates fighting surveillance

  • Parents worried about children

  • Artists whose work feeds AI

  • Diverse groups, common enemy

3. Generate Political Pressure

  • Make supporting AI oligarchs politically toxic

  • Organize voters around AI accountability

  • Primary politicians captured by AI money

  • Electoral consequences for oligarchic support

4. Legal Action

  • Antitrust lawsuits (break up AI monopolies)

  • Worker protections (transition support, unemployment insurance)

  • Privacy legislation (limit surveillance)

  • Copyright enforcement (pay for training data)

  • Use democratic institutions to constrain oligarchic power

5. International Coordination

  • Support EU AI Act

  • Encourage other democracies to regulate

  • Create democratic coalition on AI governance

  • Isolate authoritarians and oligarchs

  • Brussels Effect can spread standards globally

6. Sustain Pressure

  • This takes years, probably decades

  • Setbacks inevitable

  • Oligarchs will fight hard

  • Persistence is essential

This is the formula. It’s worked before. It can work again.

The Optimistic Case: We’ve Done Harder Things

Breaking AI oligarchy seems impossible. But consider what seemed impossible and got done anyway:

Ending slavery (1865): Entrenched for centuries. Economically foundational. Backed by Constitutional protections. Abolished.

Women’s suffrage (1920): “Natural order” denied women voting. Took 70+ years of organizing. Won.

Defeating fascism (1945): Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan had massive military power. Defeated by democracies.

Civil Rights (1960s): Segregation woven into American life. Backed by law, culture, violence. Dismantled.

Clean air and water (1970s): Industry said regulation impossible. Achieved.

Defeating tobacco (1998): Addictive product, massive industry, cultural norm. Constrained.

Marriage equality (2015): “Defense of marriage” seemed permanent. Changed in one generation. Won.

Pattern: Things that seem impossible become inevitable once movements build sufficient power.

AI oligarchy looks permanent. It’s not. It’s vulnerable. It can be defeated.

Requires:

  • Exposure (document harms)

  • Organization (build coalitions)

  • Pressure (political and economic)

  • Legal action (democratic governance)

  • Persistence (decades-long commitment)

We’ve done this before. We can do it again.

Closing: They’re Not Invincible

When you look at Musk’s wealth, Zuckerberg’s control of information, the capture of regulators, the Supreme Court’s Citizens United decision—it’s easy to despair.

But concentrated power always looks permanent until it’s not.

Rockefeller looked invincible until broken up. Tobacco companies looked unstoppable until constrained. Segregation seemed eternal until dismantled.

The pattern is consistent: Oligarchic power feels overwhelming. Democratic organizing defeats it.

Not easily. Not quickly. Requires:

  • Years or decades of sustained effort

  • Coalition building across diverse groups

  • Political organizing that makes supporting oligarchs costly

  • Legal action using democratic institutions

  • International coordination

  • Persistence despite setbacks

But it works. It’s worked repeatedly. And the AI oligarchs have vulnerabilities previous oligarchs didn’t:

They need public trust. They need government legitimacy. They need workers. They need data. They compete with each other. International regulation can force compliance.

These are exploitable weaknesses.

Sanders convening Hinton at Georgetown was exposure stage. Now comes organization. Building coalitions. Creating political pressure. Using democratic institutions to constrain oligarchic power.

History says this is winnable.

The robber barons looked invincible. Progressive Era broke them. Tobacco companies looked unstoppable. Public health movement defeated them. Segregation seemed permanent. Civil Rights movement dismantled it.

AI oligarchs look unbeatable. They’re not.

They’re vulnerable. They can be defeated. Democracy can govern AI.

Just requires enough people refusing to accept oligarchy as inevitable and organizing for democratic alternative.

The pattern is clear. The path is known. The question is whether we walk it.
