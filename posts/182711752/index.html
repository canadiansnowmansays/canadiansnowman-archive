Trump 2025-2026: Scorecards State of the Union and Reality Checks

A First-Year Scorecard

Part 1 of 3: Domestic Policy Foundations

January 1, 2026

An Unofficial State of the Union: Annual Review and Resolutions for 2026

Introduction: A Time for Assessment and Course Correction

As America collectively looks back at 2025 and forward to the upcoming year, it’s a natural time for reflection—both personal and national. Just as individuals assess their progress and set resolutions for improvement, so too should America examine their government’s performance and identify areas requiring course correction in 2026.

Donald Trump’s return to the White House marked the first time since Grover Cleveland that a president secured non-consecutive terms. He promised nothing less than a fundamental transformation of American governance, policy, and society. One year later, any honest assessment must weigh promises against results, intentions against outcomes, and rhetoric against documented reality.

This three-part review examines ten critical policy areas not through a partisan lens, but through the objective framework of: What was promised? What was delivered? What were the costs? And most importantly—what lessons should inform better governance in 2026?

The evidence presents a complex picture. Some policies achieved their stated objectives; others failed entirely. Some methods proved legally questionable; others set important precedents. The humanitarian consequences in several areas demand immediate attention regardless of one’s political affiliation.

This is not an exercise in partisan criticism. It’s an evidence-based assessment identifying both successes and failures, examining constitutional questions that transcend politics, and proposing concrete improvements for the year ahead. Good governance requires honest accounting—not to assign blame, but to chart better courses forward.

Think of this as an unofficial State of the Union—not from the White House, but assessed from outside, where distance sometimes provides clarity that proximity obscures. As a Canadian observer who has tracked American politics extensively, I bring neither the defensive patriotism that might soften critique nor the partisan animosity that might exaggerate it. Just evidence, analysis, and concern for governance that affects all.

Part 1 examines domestic policy fundamentals: immigration enforcement, federal workforce transformation, foreign aid elimination, and trade policy.

Part 2 will cover economic outcomes, drug policy, diplomacy, and domestic military use.

Part 3 will assess justice system weaponization, civil rights policies, constitutional questions, and forward-looking resolutions.

1. Immigration: Record Lows Through Brutal Means

The Promise: Trump vowed to impose the most comprehensive immigration crackdown in American history, promising mass deportations and an end to what he characterized as Biden’s border crisis.

The Reality: Trump achieved his goal of driving illegal border crossings to historic lows—under 10,000 per month at the southwest border, numbers not seen in decades. However, this came through a combination of asylum restrictions, coercive pressure on Mexico, and aggressive enforcement that raised serious legal and humanitarian concerns.

Despite high-profile deportation raids, the administration fell dramatically short of its stated goal. Through the year, approximately 500,000 immigrants were deported—half the administration’s target of one million. More troubling, government data through October showed that most arrested immigrants in urban operations lacked criminal records, contradicting claims about targeting “the worst of the worst.”

The administration also deported migrants to war-torn nations they had never been from, a practice designed to intimidate voluntary returns. Trump’s expansion to legal immigration proved equally extreme: refugee admissions were slashed to the lowest levels in program history, with most slots reserved for white South African Afrikaners. The Laken Riley Act and tripled ICE budget provide long-term infrastructure for ongoing enforcement.

The human cost extended beyond those deported. Immigration attorney Diana Buttu characterized the approach as “normalizing genocide” through policies that treat entire populations as security threats. The use of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798—a wartime law applied in peacetime—drew condemnation from legal scholars as constitutional overreach.

Verdict: Achieved stated goals through legally questionable means with severe humanitarian costs. The infrastructure created ensures these policies will outlast this administration.

2026 Resolution: Maintain border security gains while restoring due process. Implement humane enforcement that distinguishes between security threats and families seeking opportunity. Restore refugee program to historical levels with proper vetting. End deportations to third countries unrelated to migrants’ origins.

2. Federal Workforce: Dismantling the “Deep State”

The Promise: Trump vowed to “demolish the deep state,” promising to purge the civil service of what he characterized as disloyal bureaucrats.

The Reality: The administration achieved massive workforce reductions through a combination of purges, buyouts, and intimidation. By November 2025, the Office of Personnel Management estimated 317,000 federal workers had left government employment, with only 68,000 new hires—a net loss of 249,000 employees.

The Justice Department and FBI saw the most dramatic purges. Trump dismissed en masse the senior leaders and prosecutors involved in criminal cases against him. FBI Director Kash Patel redirected the bureau’s focus toward immigration enforcement while curtailing public corruption work.

Working with Elon Musk’s Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE), the administration unilaterally gutted agencies created by Congress, including USAID and the Education Department’s core functions. Independent inspectors general—Congress’s mechanism for policing agencies for waste, fraud, and abuse—were purged, eliminating oversight.

The consequences proved catastrophic for public services. Disease control, disaster response, and cancer research all suffered severe degradation. While immigration enforcement expanded, the government’s ability to perform basic functions collapsed. The administration’s elimination of civil service protections transformed the federal workforce into a spoils system, reversing reforms enacted after the abuses of the 19th century.

Verdict: Succeeded in workforce reduction but created a government unable to perform basic functions. Constitutional violations are extensive and precedent-setting.

2026 Resolution: Right-size government through strategic assessment rather than mass purges. Restore inspector general independence. Rebuild capacity for disease control, disaster response, and essential services. Reform civil service thoughtfully rather than eliminate protections entirely.

3. USAID Elimination: “Public Man-Made Death”

The Promise: Eliminate wasteful foreign aid that doesn’t serve “core national interests.”

The Reality: This represents Trump’s most catastrophic policy failure, with death tolls that dwarf any military conflict. The systematic dismantling of USAID has resulted in between 500,000 and 1,000,000 preventable deaths in 2025 alone, according to multiple independent analyses.

The Lancet study documented that USAID programs had saved 92 million lives over two decades through disease prevention, malnutrition programs, and health services. The agency’s elimination cut off HIV treatment for 176,000 people, tuberculosis prevention programs, malaria bed nets, nutritional packets for starving children, and chlorine tablets for water purification.

Harvard’s Atul Gawande, former USAID assistant administrator, wrote that the agency’s closure has “already caused the deaths of six hundred thousand people, two-thirds of them children.” Boston University epidemiologist Brooke Nichols’ real-time tracking shows an adult dying from HIV every 3 minutes and a child every 31 minutes due to PEPFAR funding cuts.

The Lancet projects 14 million additional deaths by 2030 if cuts continue, including 4.5 million children under age 5. Senator Brian Schatz noted that 360,000 people had died from lack of food and medication by mid-July, characterizing it as transforming America from “the good guys to being a conduit for death and sickness and hunger.”

The administration produced no evidence of the fraud or waste that ostensibly justified the elimination. Instead, Elon Musk’s ideological opposition to foreign aid drove policy, with DOGE officials manually blocking even life-saving expenditures that Secretary of State Rubio had authorized.

Verdict: The single most devastating policy failure, with death tolls approaching those of major armed conflicts. No legitimate national interest was served—only ideological preference.

2026 Resolution: Immediately restore life-saving programs for HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, malaria, and malnutrition. Rebuild humanitarian capacity under whatever administrative structure achieves results. Acknowledge deaths occurred and commit to preventing further loss. Reform aid delivery for better accountability without eliminating effective programs.

4. Trade and Tariffs: Economic Whiplash Without Results

The Promise: Use sweeping tariffs to rebalance global trade, bring manufacturing jobs back, and strengthen American industry.

The Reality: Trump enacted the most chaotic and economically damaging trade policy in modern American history. His “Liberation Day” tariffs in April triggered financial market meltdowns so severe he was forced to partially reverse course. The effective tariff rate reached 18 percent—the highest since 1934—through a whipsaw-like approach that created massive uncertainty.

The trade war with China proved particularly destructive. China imposed retaliatory restrictions on mineral flows and halted American soybean purchases, requiring Trump to bail out farmers. Despite promises of a factory boom, the manufacturing sector lost more than 50,000 workers since Trump took office, continuing a two-year decline.

The trade deficit for 2025 remained significantly higher than the previous year, contradicting Trump’s stated goals. Companies initially stockpiled goods before tariffs took effect, but as supplies dwindled, price increases began passing to consumers. The Supreme Court appeared skeptical of Trump’s use of emergency powers to impose tariffs, suggesting many could be invalidated.

California bore disproportionate costs, with firms incurring $11.3 billion in tariff expenses from January through May. The Port of Los Angeles operated at only 70% capacity, and Southern California saw a 40% decline in trade and logistics job postings. Governor Newsom characterized Trump’s policies as economic “betrayal.”

Verdict: Failed on stated objectives while creating economic disruption and uncertainty. Legal foundations remain questionable, and promised manufacturing revival never materialized.

2026 Resolution: Replace chaotic tariff approach with strategic trade policy developed through Congressional input. Focus protection on genuinely strategic industries. Negotiate seriously rather than threaten. Accept that some manufacturing jobs won’t return and invest in workforce retraining instead.

Part 1 Conclusion: Foundation Failures

The four policy areas examined in Part 1 reveal a pattern: achievement of some stated objectives through legally questionable means, with severe unintended consequences that undermine overall success.

Immigration enforcement achieved historic border crossing reductions but at humanitarian costs and through methods that may not survive judicial review. Federal workforce reductions succeeded numerically but crippled government’s ability to perform essential functions. USAID elimination produced no measurable benefit while causing between 500,000 and 1,000,000 preventable deaths. Trade policy created economic chaos without achieving stated objectives.

Each area demands course correction in 2026—not through partisan reversal, but through evidence-based adjustment that maintains successes while addressing catastrophic failures.
