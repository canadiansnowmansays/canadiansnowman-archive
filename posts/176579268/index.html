The Charlie Kirk Debates

#5: Gun Deaths Are "Worth It" - Part I: The Case

October 21, 2025

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to the scales of sanctity and safety. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to your statement that gun deaths are “worth it” to preserve the Second Amendment. On April 5, 2023, just one week after the Nashville Covenant School shooting that killed three children and three adults, you told a TPUSA Faith audience: “I think it’s worth it. I think it’s worth to have a cost of, unfortunately, some gun deaths every single year so that we can have the Second Amendment to protect our other God-given rights. That is a prudent deal. It is rational.” Prove me wrong.

Kirk’s Position on Gun Deaths and the Second Amendment

At a TPUSA Faith event in April 2023, Charlie Kirk made one of his most controversial statements when responding to an audience question about Second Amendment rights following a recent school shooting. The questioner asked how to convince people who want to take away gun rights that “it’s important to have the right to defend ourselves.”

Kirk’s response was unequivocal:

“The Second Amendment is not about hunting. I love hunting. The Second Amendment is not even about personal defense. That is important. The Second Amendment is there, God forbid, so that you can defend yourself against a tyrannical government. And if that talk scares you—wow, that’s radical, Charlie, I don’t know about that. Well, then you have not really read any of the literature of our Founding Fathers. Number two, you’ve not read any 20th century history.”

The “Prudent Deal” Argument

Kirk went further, making an argument that few politicians would dare articulate:

“We must be honest with the population. Having an armed citizenry comes with a price, and that is part of liberty. Driving comes with a price—50,000, 50,000, 50,000 people die on the road every year. That’s a price. You get rid of driving, you’d have 50,000 less auto fatalities. But we have decided that the benefit of driving—speed, accessibility, mobility, having products, services is worth the cost of 50,000 people dying on the road.”

This automotive analogy set up his central claim:

“So we need to be very clear that you’re not going to get gun deaths to zero. It will not happen. You could significantly reduce them through having more fathers in the home, by having more armed guards in front of schools. We should have a honest and clear reductionist view of gun violence, but we should not have a utopian one. You will never live in a society when you have an armed citizenry and you won’t have a single gun death. That is nonsense. It’s drivel.”

And then came the statement that would resurface after his own death:

“But I am—I think it’s worth it. I think it’s worth to have a cost of, unfortunately, some gun deaths every single year so that we can have the Second Amendment to protect our other God-given rights. That is a prudent deal. It is rational. Nobody talks like this. They live in a complete alternate universe.”

Kirk’s Solutions

Rather than comprehensive gun control, Kirk proposed specific solutions:

“People say, oh, Charlie, how do you stop school shootings? I don’t know. How did we stop shootings at baseball games? Because we have armed guards outside of baseball games. That’s why. How did we stop all the shootings at airports? We have armed guards outside of airports. How do we stop all the shootings at banks? We have armed guards outside of banks. How did we stop all the shootings at gun shows? Notice there’s not a lot of mass shootings at gun shows, there’s all these guns. Because everyone’s armed.”

His conclusion was direct: “If our money and our sporting events and our airplanes have armed guards, why don’t our children?”

Kirk also suggested social solutions: “having more fathers in the home” as a way to “significantly reduce” gun deaths, though he provided no evidence for this claim. He extended this cultural critique to the perpetrators themselves, frequently attributing mass shootings to leftist ideology rather than access to firearms. In a 2022 response to the Uvalde tragedy, he posted on X: “Another mass shooter raised in a Democrat-run state, influenced by the godless, fatherless culture the left promotes. This isn’t about guns—it’s about broken homes and leftist indoctrination destroying our youth.” He echoed this at CPAC in 2018 after Parkland: “The vast majority of mass shooters are Democrats or left-leaning. They come from environments where traditional values are attacked. Prove me wrong.” For Kirk, this reinforced his “prudent deal”: Gun deaths weren’t a Second Amendment flaw but a symptom of moral decay under “tyrannical” progressive policies, justifying armed resistance as the cure.

Kirk pinpointed mental health and family breakdown as the true drivers, blaming “leftist policies” for exacerbating them. Following the 2023 Nashville shooting—the very context of his “worth it” remarks—he tweeted: “Mental health crisis exploding because Dems defunded asylums, glorified victimhood, and destroyed the nuclear family. Guns don’t kill—ungodly ideology does.” At a 2024 TPUSA summit, he added: “Every school shooter has severe mental illness untreated due to leftist policies. We need more dads and therapy, not disarming law-abiding Christians.” This framed gun deaths as a societal failing, not a rights trade-off.

The Constitutional Framework

Kirk grounded his argument in a specific interpretation of the Second Amendment:

  • Primary Purpose: Protection against tyrannical government, not hunting or personal defense

  • Historical Precedent: Founding Fathers’ intent and 20th century examples of government tyranny

  • Absolute Right: The right to bear arms as essential for protecting “other God-given rights”

  • Acceptable Trade-off: Some deaths are the unavoidable price of liberty

Kirk’s Consistency on the Issue

This wasn’t an isolated statement. In multiple venues, Kirk maintained this position:

  • He regularly spoke about receiving death threats, saying in 2024: “We get death threats all the time. They send stuff to our homes and all that. That’s just par for the course.”

  • On “The George Janko Show,” he claimed: “If you are not getting active death threats, you’re not really doing much for the country.”

  • He saw armed citizenry as the ultimate check on government power

The Theological Component

Speaking at a faith-based event, Kirk attempted to frame this as compatible with Christian values by:

  • Calling Second Amendment rights “God-given”

  • Suggesting that defending against tyranny was a moral imperative

  • Implying that Christians who disagreed hadn’t properly studied history or the Founding Fathers

Public Reaction

Kirk’s statement immediately drew sharp criticism:

  • Representative Mike Thompson called his statements “asinine,” asking: “Who chooses which lives Charlie Kirk wants to sacrifice?”

  • Gun violence prevention advocates challenged him to “ask the parents and family of a gun violence victim if they believe their child’s life was worth an extremist view of the Second Amendment”

  • The comments circulated widely after Kirk’s own death by gunfire, highlighting what many saw as tragic irony

Kirk’s Framework

Kirk established several key premises:

  • Inevitability: Gun deaths cannot be eliminated in an armed society

  • Liberty Has Costs: Freedom requires accepting certain risks

  • Practical Solutions: Armed guards and social changes, not gun restrictions

  • Historical Necessity: The Second Amendment protects against tyranny

  • Rational Calculus: The benefits outweigh the costs

This position represented one of Kirk’s most controversial stances, articulating what many Second Amendment advocates might believe but rarely state publicly: that preserving gun rights is worth accepting thousands of annual gun deaths, including those of children.

Part II: The Challenge will systematically examine this position using Kirk’s own standards of evidence, Christian theology, constitutional scholarship, and proven alternatives to reduce gun violence while respecting rights.
