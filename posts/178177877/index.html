The Race for Full Self-Driving

Episode 5.5: The Execution Phase Begins: From FSD Validation to 5 Million Vehicles

November 8, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays

November 8, 2025

Tesla’s November 6 shareholder meeting delivered the executive compensation approval Elon Musk demanded, creating clear alignment for the critical 2026-2028 scaling phase. The market celebrated—until Musk’s response to questions about xAI collaboration revealed something investors hadn’t expected: uncertainty.

With 75% of shareholders approving Musk’s path to 25% ownership while giving qualified support to a Tesla-xAI investment with “significant abstentions,” the meeting provided clear executive alignment. But when asked about xAI partnership specifics, Musk delivered a rambling, non-committal response that stood in stark contrast to his detailed clarity on every other topic that day.

The market’s real-time reaction told the story: enthusiasm when compensation passed, immediate selloff when Musk’s xAI response revealed no clear plan, then net flat as investors processed what just happened. The concern wasn’t about xAI collaboration itself—it was about why Musk didn’t seem to have a coherent answer ready for something shareholders had just voted on.

On FSD timeline, chip architecture, production targets, and regulatory strategy, Musk was characteristically specific and confident. On xAI, he said “whatever... it’s complicated... has to go through a lot of hoops.” That contrast—not opposition to collaboration—is what spooked investors.

The Vote That Worked: Executive Compensation

Result: Over 75% approval

The compensation package passed with strong support, creating what may be the largest performance-based equity award in corporate history:

Structure:

  • Pathway to ~25% ownership (from current ~13%)

  • 12 tranches of performance-based equity

  • 423+ million additional shares if all milestones hit

  • Potential value: $878 billion to $1+ trillion

Performance Triggers:

  • $2 trillion market cap (first tranche)

  • Up to $8.5 trillion market cap (final tranches)

  • 20 million vehicle deliveries annually

  • 10 million active FSD subscriptions

  • 1 million Optimus robots delivered

  • 1 million robotaxis in commercial operation

  • $50 billion to $400 billion in adjusted annual profit

Market Reaction:

Stock surged approximately $11 per share in after-hours trading immediately following the compensation announcement. Investors clearly viewed this as alignment achieved—Musk now has massive personal incentive ($25 billion per $100 billion in market cap) tied specifically to robotaxi and Optimus success.

The Vote That Confused: xAI Investment Authorization

Result: “More votes in favor than against, but significant abstentions”

This was the surprise of the meeting—not that it passed (barely), but how differently the market reacted compared to the compensation vote.

What Was Actually Voted On:

The proposal, submitted by retail shareholder Stephen Hawk, was advisory—meaning it doesn’t mandate action, only authorizes the board to “examine next steps in light of this level of shareholder support.”

Hawk’s Pitch:

His presentation made the strategic case clearly:

  • xAI is “maximally truth seeking” and aligned with Tesla’s mission

  • Enables bidirectional talent flow for retention

  • Integration of Grok models could “supercharge FSD” and “power Optimus”

  • Vertical integration creates “neurolink of AI platforms”

  • xAI’s explosive growth (now valued at $200 billion) could yield massive returns

The Board’s Non-Position:

Critically, Tesla’s board took no official position on this proposal—unlike every other shareholder proposal where they recommended against. This neutral stance was presumably to avoid legal exposure on conflict-of-interest grounds.

Board Chair Robyn Denholm explained: “It is a shareholder proposal, and what that means is that the company didn’t put the proposal on the proxy. It was actually submitted by shareholders. We received a number of them, and actually this was the most common proposal around xAI to take a minority stake, or at least vote on that.”

The High Abstention Rate:

General Counsel Brandon Ehrhart’s announcement was notably cautious:

“With respect to proposal seven regarding xAI, while we have received more votes in favor than we did against, there were significant number of abstentions. Since this is an advisory vote, the board will examine the next steps in light of this level of shareholder support.”

Translation: This barely passed, lots of shareholders actively declined to take a position, and we’re not committing to anything.

The Market Reaction That Told the Real Story

Here’s where it gets interesting:

The stock’s after-hours trajectory during the meeting itself revealed what investors actually think:

  1. Compensation announced → +~$11 surge (celebration)

  2. xAI discussion begins → Immediate selloff, giving back gains (concern)

  3. Meeting ends → Net roughly flat from where it started

Note: This analysis focuses on the real-time market reaction during the November 6 meeting itself, which isolates investor response to specific announcements. Subsequent market moves reflect broader macro factors beyond the scope of this analysis.

Musk’s Response: The Clarity Gap That Spooked Investors

When asked later in the Q&A whether xAI partnership could help accelerate Optimus training, Musk gave a response that stands in stark contrast to his clarity on every other topic:

“Yeah, actually. I invested to get approved. No, yes, Okay, Okay, okay, okay, Um, You know, whatever? Like, it’s, it’s, like, uh, you know, Tesla, some other company that I have interested in, and it’s like, always quite complicated to, Uh, you know, do things. Has to go through a lot of Hoops to happen, Uh, you know? I do think there’s a lot of professional collaboration with xai in the future, And with SpaceX.”

Compare this to his other responses that day:

On AI4 sufficiency: “Robo taxi will start to roll out in scale on hardware 4.”

On Cybercab timeline: “Production is happening right here in this Factory, and we’ll be starting production in April next year.”

On terra fab: Detailed technical specifications, capacity numbers (1 billion chips annually), strategic rationale about consumer electronics scale versus data center scale.

On FSD timeline: “Autonomy is solved... in the next month or two, we should be able to allow you to text and drive essentially.”

On production targets: Specific run rates—2.7M (2026), 4M (2027), 5M (2028)

The xAI response contained:

  • “Whatever” - Dismissive language about something shareholders just voted on

  • “Always quite complicated” - No clarity on governance structure

  • “Has to go through a lot of hoops” - Suggests no clear execution plan exists

  • “Professional collaboration” - Vague term that could mean anything

  • “And with SpaceX” - Wait, now a third company is involved?

This wasn’t uncertainty about technology. It was uncertainty about whether there’s actually a plan.

Why the Uncertainty Matters More Than Opposition

The market’s negative reaction wasn’t necessarily about opposing xAI collaboration on strategic grounds. The problem was uncertainty about what this actually means in practice:

Unresolved Questions:

  1. Investment Structure: How much? When? What terms? Board seats? IP rights?

  2. Governance: How are conflicts managed? Who decides technology transfer pricing? What oversight exists?

  3. Value Flow: Does Tesla pay xAI for services? Does xAI license Tesla data? How is value split?

  4. Capital Allocation: Is this $1 billion? $5 billion? Musk mentioned $5B previously but nothing was specified in the vote

  5. Strategic Priority: Does this accelerate autonomy or create distraction during critical scaling phase?

  6. Competitive Dynamics: Does Grok integration give Tesla an advantage, or does it add complexity competitors can exploit?

The Governance Red Flags:

  • Musk owns significant stakes in both companies (massive conflict of interest)

  • No independent committee structure announced

  • No clear arm’s-length pricing methodology discussed

  • Board explicitly took “no position” to avoid legal liability

  • Previous court ruling on 2018 compensation cited concerns about board independence

What Institutional Investors Likely Think:

The high abstention rate and immediate market selloff suggest sophisticated investors are thinking:

“We approve paying Musk to focus on Tesla. We don’t approve creating complex cross-company arrangements without clear governance when he’s already spread across SpaceX, X, Neuralink, Boring Company, and now DOGE.”

The Strategic Logic vs. The Execution Risk

Let’s separate the two issues:

The Strategic Case FOR xAI Integration (Strong)

Training Compute:

  • xAI’s Colossus cluster (100,000 H100s) could supplement Tesla’s AI training

  • Potential for more efficient training algorithms reducing Tesla’s compute needs

  • Faster iteration cycles on FSD improvements

Model Architecture:

  • Techniques from large language models might transfer to vision models

  • Grok’s reasoning capabilities could enhance FSD’s edge case handling

  • Natural language interface for robotaxi passengers (customer experience improvement)

Talent Pooling:

  • Bidirectional employee flow between companies

  • Attract AI researchers with multi-company opportunities

  • Retain top talent by offering exposure to diverse problems

Data Synergies:

  • Tesla’s real-world driving data trains better AI

  • xAI’s synthetic data generation could create better training scenarios

  • Combined approach might unlock breakthroughs neither achieves alone

Competitive Positioning:

  • Waymo can’t direct DeepMind’s priorities (different parent priorities)

  • Chinese competitors have state AI resources but less agility

  • Tesla-xAI could be more focused partnership than Waymo-DeepMind

The Execution Risk AGAINST Formal Integration (Also Strong)

Capital Allocation:

  • Every dollar to xAI is a dollar not funding additional training clusters, Gigafactory expansion, or Cybercab tooling

  • xAI is already valued at $200 billion—expensive entry point for Tesla

  • Dilution of focus during critical 2026-2028 scaling phase

Governance Complexity:

  • Managing two AI organizations vs. unified focus

  • Potential culture clash between teams

  • Transfer pricing disputes inevitable

  • Conflicts over IP ownership

Execution Bandwidth:

  • Musk already leads Tesla, SpaceX, X, Neuralink, Boring Company, and served in Trump administration

  • Adding formal xAI integration creates more meetings, more coordination, more overhead

  • Risk of slowing decision-making when speed is competitive advantage

Legal/Regulatory Exposure:

  • Delaware court already skeptical of Tesla board independence regarding Musk

  • Formal investment opens new legal attack vectors

  • Potential shareholder lawsuits over conflict of interest

  • SEC scrutiny of related-party transactions

Competitor Response:

  • Formal Tesla-xAI partnership might trigger more aggressive Waymo-DeepMind integration

  • Could accelerate Chinese government support for domestic competitors

  • Might create urgency that helps competitors rather than hurts them

What’s Actually Likely to Happen

Based on Musk’s non-committal response and the board’s cautious language, here’s the probable path forward:

Most Likely Scenario: Informal Collaboration Without Major Investment

  • No large capital investment from Tesla to xAI in next 12-18 months

  • Continued informal knowledge sharing and talent flow

  • Musk personally facilitates collaboration without formal structures

  • Board revisits after robotaxi deployment success/failure becomes clearer

  • Keeps optionality open without triggering governance battles

This is probably the smart play given:

  • It preserves Musk’s attention for critical 2026-2028 scaling

  • It avoids creating legal attack surface during compensation litigation

  • It allows natural collaboration without forcing formal structure

  • It gives board cover (”we’re examining next steps”)

  • It doesn’t alienate shareholders who abstained

Less Likely: Formal Investment with Clear Governance

For this to happen, Tesla would need to announce:

  • Specific dollar amount and terms

  • Independent committee oversight

  • Arm’s-length pricing methodology

  • Clear IP ownership rules

  • Limited scope (e.g., only FSD training, not full integration)

The meeting provided none of this, suggesting it’s not imminent.

Least Likely: Large-Scale Integration

The $5 billion investment Musk previously mentioned seems off the table given:

  • Shareholder ambivalence (high abstentions)

  • Market negative reaction

  • Musk’s own uncertainty in response

  • Board’s non-position

What This Means for the Autonomous Vehicle Timeline

The Good News:

  1. Compensation alignment is real - 75% approval with strong retail and institutional support gives Musk massive incentive to execute on robotaxi

  2. AI collaboration can happen anyway - Even without formal investment, Musk facilitates knowledge sharing between companies

  3. Focus may improve - Not pursuing complex xAI integration keeps attention on Tesla’s core mission

  4. Capital preserved - Money not spent on xAI investment can fund Cybercab production, additional training compute, and Gigafactory expansion

The Concerning News:

  1. No AI accelerant - Tesla won’t get formal access to xAI’s Colossus cluster or Grok capabilities in near term

  2. Uncertainty overhang - Markets don’t like ambiguity; this remains unresolved question

  3. Governance questions persist - Board independence concerns didn’t get resolved, they got postponed

  4. Distraction risk - Even informal collaboration takes Musk’s time and attention

The Actual Meeting Developments: What We Learned About Autonomous Timeline

While xAI dominated market psychology, the meeting actually contained more important autonomous vehicle updates:

FSD v14.3: The “Autonomy Solved” Declaration

Musk’s Key Statement:

“Autonomy is solved. We’re actually getting to the point where we almost feel comfortable allowing people to text and drive, which is kind of a killer app, because that’s really what people want to do. I’m confident that in the next month or two, we should be able to allow you to text and drive essentially.”

Why This Matters:

This isn’t marketing hyperbole when you understand Musk’s decision framework. As analyst Phil Bal explained:

“His view of FSD is 3 months ahead of ours because he can see the advancements that are coming. When he spoke of ‘the next version will be almost sentient,’ you have to ask yourself how would you know there is no next version? Of course there’s a next version. It’s sitting there in the lab getting the final wraps on its training and validation.”

The Convergence Principle:

Bal introduced a critical framework for understanding Musk’s timeline statements: “When Elon’s talking about these types of things, he’s talking about a principle that I call convergence. When he says the word ‘is,’ we think of it as a present tense word like ‘is solved.’ But from a technology point of view, you have to think of it as he sees it converging... there’s nothing in the way to get that convergence.”

Current FSD v14.14 Capabilities:

  • 36 inference cycles per second (faster than human reaction)

  • 360-degree vision (no blind spots)

  • Predictive pedestrian intent detection

  • End-to-end neural network with mixture-of-experts

  • Real-time adaptation without operator intervention

Bal’s field testing experience: “This car through 360-degree vision is doing this at 36 times a second. There’s no way that we could be hyperfocused on one area in our field of view about something that’s happening, but this car is going to see stuff way before us.”

The AI4 vs AI5 Clarification: Definitive Sufficiency

The meeting provided definitive clarity on a critical question: AI4 hardware is fully sufficient for unsupervised robotaxi operations.

Phil Bal’s unequivocal assessment: “Can you solve a problem on the current hardware 4? Yeah, I think so. I think robo taxi will start to roll out in scale on hardware 4.”

What AI5 adds:

  • Interactive features (Grok voice interface for passengers)

  • Enhanced user preference customization

  • More computational headroom for multiple AI models

  • Better edge case handling through larger networks

What AI5 doesn’t add: Requirements for basic Level 5 autonomy. That works on AI4.

The Technical Advantage:

Tesla’s use of integer-based inference—rather than the floating-point operations used by competitors—provides fundamental efficiency:

“Integer operations are fundamentally more efficient than floating point operations... much more power efficient, much more silicon efficient. But you have to actually train for integer inference, which everyone else is training for floating point.”

This architectural choice, embedded in AI4, gives Tesla efficiency advantages that compound with scale.

Why AI5 is Being Delayed: A Sign of Strength, Not Weakness

The most revealing technical insight wasn’t about what AI5 will add—it was about why the timeline is being pushed out.

Phil Bal: “I don’t think the timeline for AI5 is being pushed out because there’s some manufacturing or design issue with the chip. What I think is that Elon wants more in that chip... they wouldn’t be pushing that chip out if they thought that AI4 was not capable.”

Tesla’s approach was confirmed in the meeting when discussing their integer-based inference: “We’re doing very very fast operations on AI4 and we’re getting what we want out of that chip... there’s more legs to that chip than we thought.”

Translation: AI5 is being delayed because AI4 is performing better than expected, giving engineers time to pack more features into AI5 rather than rushing it to market to compensate for AI4 limitations.

This is the opposite of the narrative many assumed—that delays meant problems. Instead, it signals Tesla has more strategic flexibility than anticipated.

Production Targets That Rewrite Deployment Speed

Vehicle Production Run Rates Announced:

  • End of 2026: 2.7 million vehicles annually

  • End of 2027: 4.0 million vehicles annually

  • End of 2028: 5.0 million vehicles annually

Actual Annual Production (calculated from run rates):

  • 2026: ~2.25 million vehicles

  • 2027: ~3.35 million vehicles

  • 2028: ~4.5 million vehicles

The Conviction Behind the Numbers:

When Musk announced these targets, they weren’t aspirational goals—they reflected production system confidence.

Analyst CERN Basher, who toured Giga Texas: “With the unbox process, you put the four parts of the car together in different parts and bring them all together. The space savings is tremendous... It really truly will be a revolutionary change in production of automobiles.”

Manufacturing Cycle Time Targets:

  • 10-second cycle time = 2-3 million vehicles annually per line

  • 5-second cycle time = 5 million vehicles annually per line (aspirational)

Musk’s framing: “The manufacturing system of the cyber Cab is closer to a high volume consumer electronics device than it is a car manufacturing line.”

Critical Question: Where Do These Vehicles Go?

Current Tesla sales are ~1.8 million annually. The additional 2.7 million vehicles by 2028 create a massive deployment question.

Analyst CERN Basher’s analysis: “Where are those 1.1 million vehicles going to go in 2027? I don’t think it’s a 50/50 split at that point. I think it’s mostly robotaxi.”

Conservative Robotaxi Fleet Projections:

  • 2026: 250,000 vehicles

  • 2027: 1,000,000 vehicles (cumulative fleet)

  • 2028: 2-2.5 million vehicles (cumulative fleet)

Valuation Implications:

CERN Basher: “I have market cap at about three trillion for robotaxi with a million vehicles. If we double that, we’re close to six trillion. If we triple it, we’re close to 9 trillion just on robotaxi.”

Cybercab Production: April 2026 Start

Specific Commitment: Production begins April 2026 at Giga Texas

This is significant acceleration from vague “2026” guidance. April start means:

  • 9 months of 2026 production

  • 50,000-100,000 Cybercabs possible before year-end

  • Ramp to 500,000+ in 2027 if “unbox” manufacturing works

The Manufacturing Advantage:

The “unbox” process—building vehicle in sections and assembling together rather than linear production—enables:

  • 10-second cycle time = 2-3 million annual per line

  • 5-second cycle time = 5 million annual per line (aspirational)

This is consumer electronics production speed applied to vehicles.

Regulatory Confidence: The Barrier That Isn’t?

Musk’s Statement on Regulation:

When asked if regulation might require steering wheels on Cybercabs: “We believe that regulation is going to be basically stairstepped along with production.”

His Safety Argument:

“If we have the data to show that it is safer, then it’s almost criminal for politicians to stop this. We can prove to them that we’re saving lives. So why would you not let a technology save lives?”

Current Safety Data:

“In 2024, our Global Fleet of vehicles experienced one crash after driving more than 6.8 million miles. That’s 10 times better than the average driver in the U.S. And nearly double the safety performance of our autopilot technology in 2018.”

With v14.3’s improvements, Tesla expects “orders of magnitude” better performance.

The Terra Fab: Solving the Chip Bottleneck

Announcement: Tesla plans chip fabrication facility (”terra fab”) producing 1 billion chips per year

Why This Is Necessary:

Phil Bal captured the scale of Musk’s thinking: “His brain he’s thinking a lot like iPhone a lot, not like a lot like Nvidia data center a lot.”

This distinction matters. Nvidia thinks in terms of thousands of high-end data center chips. Tesla thinks in terms of tens of millions of edge inference chips for consumer devices. The terra fab isn’t just about supply chain security—it’s about achieving consumer electronics manufacturing scale for AI hardware.

Scale of demand:

  • 2 chips per Optimus × 10 million annual production = 20 million chips

  • 2 chips per vehicle × 4-5 million annual production = 8-10 million chips

  • Starlink edge inference (amount undisclosed)

  • Data center training (ongoing expansion)

Total: 30-50 million chips annually by 2028, with growth accelerating beyond

AI5 Specifications:

  • Roughly equivalent performance to Nvidia Blackwell

  • One-third the power consumption

  • Less than 10% of Nvidia chip cost

  • Purpose-built for Tesla’s AI inference workloads

Multi-Sourcing Strategy:

  1. Maximum capacity from TSMC (primary)

  2. Samsung (secondary)

  3. Potential Intel partnership

  4. Proprietary terra fab for remaining demand

Analyst Phil Bal’s Assessment:

“He’s negotiating out loud. He’s saying ‘you guys got to put up for me or I have to do this.’ But even when we extrapolate the best case scenario for chip production from suppliers, it’s still not enough. I think we may have to do a Tesla terra fab. It’s like giga but way bigger.”

The Architectural Advantage: Why Tesla’s Chips Are Different

Phil Bal explained why Tesla’s purpose-built chips create a moat competitors can’t easily replicate:

“These chips are designed to execute their software. And nothing is better than that marriage between hardware and software. It is not a horizontally applicable chip like throwing just sort of your average Nvidia whether it’s training or inference chip at the problem.”

The organizational secret: While chip design and software teams are separated by discipline, “they spend a lot of time talking... they are practically separated by domain but not necessarily by space in the office.”

Result: Nvidia designs chips that must serve the superset of all past and future customers’ requirements. Tesla designs chips optimized for exactly one customer’s needs—Tesla’s AI stack. Every transistor serves a purpose; nothing is wasted on general-purpose flexibility.

“The hardware doesn’t spend its time or doesn’t have wasted silicon. It’s all about solving the problem that they want.”

This isn’t just cost efficiency—it’s architectural advantage that compounds with each generation. As Phil Bal noted about the efficiency gains:

“If you took like if you tried to put a Blackwell chip in the vehicle on the inference side, a huge amount of that chip would be underutilized, not touched, and beyond that, it would just be inefficient as a chip for operating that way.”

The Two Futures: With vs. Without xAI Integration

Scenario A: xAI Remains Informal (Most Likely - 70% probability)

Timeline:

  • FSD v14.3 releases December 2025 / January 2026

  • Limited unsupervised operation begins Austin Q1 2026

  • Cybercab production starts April 2026

  • 5-10 city expansion throughout 2026

  • 250,000 robotaxi fleet by end of 2026

  • Nationwide scaling 2027-2028

Competitive Position:

  • Tesla maintains focus advantage over Waymo

  • Chip independence creates cost advantage (AI5 at <10% cost of Nvidia equivalents)

  • Manufacturing scale enables winner-take-most dynamics

  • But no formal AI accelerant from xAI collaboration

Valuation Range: $3-6 trillion by 2028 (robotaxi alone, per CERN Basher analysis)

Scenario B: Formal xAI Integration (Unlikely Near-Term - 15% probability)

If Tesla actually pursues this in 2026-2027:

  • Could accelerate FSD improvements 6-12 months through Colossus cluster access

  • Grok integration enhances robotaxi experience with natural language interface

  • Training compute access speeds iteration on edge cases

  • BUT governance complexity slows decision-making during critical scaling

  • AND capital diverted from core business (Cybercab, terra fab)

  • AND increased legal/regulatory scrutiny on conflict of interest

Net Effect: Possibly neutral or even negative in near-term

Scenario C: Post-2027 Integration (Moderate Probability - 15% probability)

More Likely After Cybercab Deployment Proven:

  • Once robotaxi deployed and cash flow improves, formal partnership makes more sense

  • Technology integration when pressure is lower

  • Clearer governance framework after compensation litigation resolved

  • Strategic collaboration from position of strength rather than desperation

Conclusion: The Alignment Tesla Got vs. The Clarity It Didn’t

November 6, 2025 gave Tesla the executive compensation alignment it needed. With 75% approval, Musk now has $25 billion of personal incentive for every $100 billion in Tesla market cap—directly tied to robotaxi and Optimus success.

But the meeting revealed something else: uncertainty about how Tesla’s AI development strategy actually works. The xAI vote passed with high abstentions, and Musk’s response was so vague that the market immediately sold off after celebrating the compensation approval.

This tells us what investors actually want:

✅ Musk focused on Tesla (hence celebrating compensation)
❌ Complex cross-company arrangements (hence selling on xAI discussion)
✅ Clear execution plans (hence enthusiasm for Cybercab/production targets)
❌ Ambiguous governance (hence concern about “it’s complicated”)

The central question for the autonomous vehicle race isn’t whether Tesla has the technology—FSD v14.3, AI4 sufficiency, and the production targets suggest they do. The question is whether Musk can maintain focus through the critical 2026-2028 scaling phase while managing SpaceX, X, Neuralink, Boring Company, DOGE, and now potential xAI integration.

The compensation alignment helps. The xAI uncertainty hurts. The market’s real-time reaction during the meeting showed which one matters more.

For investors analyzing the autonomous vehicle race, November 6 clarified one thing: Tesla’s path to dominance runs through manufacturing scale and chip independence, not through complex AI partnerships. The company that makes 5 million vehicles annually with proprietary chips costing 10% of competitors’ will win through execution, not through synergy.

Whether that’s the right strategy depends on one assumption: that Tesla’s internal AI development is sufficient. Phil Bal’s technical analysis suggests it is—AI4 works for robotaxi, the architecture is sound, and the software-hardware co-design creates compounding advantages.

If xAI collaboration would have accelerated the timeline 12-18 months, then avoiding the governance complexity might prove costly. If Tesla’s AI team can achieve the same results independently—which the “autonomy solved” declaration suggests they can—then simplicity wins.

We’ll know which bet was correct by mid-2026, when FSD v14.3 either delivers unsupervised capability or doesn’t, and when Cybercab either starts production in April or slips.

The autonomous vehicle race just became a manufacturing and execution race. Tesla bet on focus over synergy. The market approved—nervously.
