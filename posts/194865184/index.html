The Rise and Fall of Lori Chavez-DeRemer

Third cabinet secretary down, and the Teamsters' gamble with it

April 21, 2026

Labor Secretary Lori Chavez-DeRemer stepped down from her role on Monday.Credit...Al Drago for The New York Times

There is a song by Queen — you know the one, the strutting bassline, Freddie Mercury’s insouciant delivery — that has become something of an unofficial anthem for the Trump administration’s approach to personnel management. Another one gone. Another one gone. The rhythm section never stops.

In Donald Trump’s first term, fourteen cabinet secretaries departed — more than under any president since the Brookings Institution began tracking the data in 1981. Senior White House staff turnover hit ninety-two percent. Only four of his original fifteen cabinet picks survived the full four years. The administration went through four Homeland Security secretaries, four chiefs of staff, three attorneys general, and two secretaries of state. The revolving door spun so fast it generated its own weather system.

The second term was supposed to be different. Trump’s team spent the transition vetting for loyalty with the zeal of a medieval inquisition. For all of 2025, not a single cabinet secretary left. The Brookings scholars took note. The political press observed a calmer ship. Then 2026 arrived, and the bassline kicked in again.

Kristi Noem was fired from Homeland Security in March, after congressional hearings about her department's enforcement failures became indistinguishable from a roast — that, and a $220 million ad campaign featuring the secretary on horseback. Pam Bondi was ousted as Attorney General in early April, amid frustration over the Epstein files and her broader leadership of the Justice Department. And now, on April 20th, Lori Chavez-DeRemer has resigned as Labor Secretary, the third cabinet member out in seven weeks. The next verse may already be queued up: FBI Director Kash Patel is skating on ice so thin that on April 10th, when a routine computer login failed, he panicked and called aides to announce he'd been fired — a story he is now suing The Atlantic $250 million to suppress, in a lawsuit that helpfully confirms the login problem it claims never happened. Polymarket gives him an eighty-three percent chance of being ousted by 2027. The betting markets, at least, can hear the bassline.

The Brookings Institution’s “A Team” tracker puts senior staff turnover in the second term at thirty-two percent as of mid-April 2026 — lower than the first term’s rate at the same point, but still more than triple the average of other modern presidencies. The cabinet departures are accelerating. The deputies are running the departments. And the song keeps playing.

Lori Chavez-DeRemer’s particular verse, however, is worth hearing in full, because hers is not merely a story of another appointee washing out. It is a parable about what happens when a cabinet appointment is treated as a transaction rather than a responsibility — and what the parties to that transaction are left holding when the deal collapses.

The rise was, by any fair measure, remarkable. Born in Santa Clara, California in 1968, the daughter of a Teamster, the first in her family to attend college, she married her high school sweetheart, Shawn DeRemer, and together they built an anesthesia management company and a string of medical clinics across the Pacific Northwest. She entered public life modestly — the Happy Valley Parks Committee in 2002, a city council seat, and then, in 2010, the mayor’s office. She was the first woman and the first Latina to serve as Happy Valley’s mayor. Under her watch, the suburb of Portland became Oregon’s fastest-growing community.

In 2022, she won one of the most competitive House races in the country, becoming the first Republican woman Oregon had ever sent to Congress. She co-sponsored the PRO Act — one of only three House Republicans willing to back organized labour’s top legislative priority. Georgetown’s Lugar Center rated her the second-most bipartisan member of Congress. She was exactly the sort of politician who makes both parties nervous and both editorial boards swoon.

Then she lost. In November 2024, Janelle Bynum beat her. A one-term congresswoman from a swing district, defeated. In most political careers, this is where the story ends — a footnote in the Congressional Directory, a future lobbying gig, a quiet descent into the anonymity from which most legislators emerge only briefly.

But Donald Trump had other plans. Or rather, Sean O’Brien did.

Sean O’Brien, president of the Teamsters union, spoke on the second night of the R.N.C. in Milwaukee.Credit...Kenny Holston/The New York Times

To understand the appointment, you have to understand the deal.

O’Brien, the combative fourth-generation Teamster from Boston who had clawed his way to the presidency of the International Brotherhood of Teamsters, had made a bet that stunned the labour movement: he would align the Teamsters with Donald Trump. He spoke at the Republican National Convention in prime time — the first Teamsters president ever to do so. He declined to endorse Kamala Harris, even after the Biden administration had broken a Senate tie to bail out a Teamster pension fund covering nearly 400,000 members. He began appearing at the White House, on podcasts with Republican senators, in photographs with men who had, until very recently, been trying to gut every workplace protection his members depended on.

O’Brien justified all of this with a poll: sixty-five percent of Teamsters rank and file, he said, planned to vote for Trump. The membership wanted a seat at the table. He would get them one.

The price of that seat — or, more precisely, the token payment Trump offered in return — was Chavez-DeRemer. A Teamster’s daughter. A PRO Act co-sponsor. A signal, however faint, that this Republican administration might not treat organized labour as the enemy. Trump announced the pick with characteristic grandiosity: “Lori’s strong support from both the Business and Labor communities will ensure that the Labor Department can unite Americans of all backgrounds.”

The Senate agreed, or at least enough of it did. She was confirmed 67-32 in March 2025, with seventeen Democrats voting yes. O’Brien was ecstatic. “There is no doubt Chavez-DeRemer is the best person for the job. We were proud to stand with her from the start.”

It is worth pausing to note who voted against her. Rand Paul and Mitch McConnell, both of Kentucky, objected to her union sympathies. Ted Budd of North Carolina joined them. The Republican dissenters were, in hindsight, wrong about the reason but right about the outcome. They worried she would be too pro-worker. The actual problem turned out to be rather different.

The fall, when it came, was not a single event but an accumulation — a slow-motion institutional collapse that began with whispers in January 2026 and ended with a terse post from Steven Cheung’s X account on April 20th.

The New York Post reported first: the Labor Department’s inspector general was investigating complaints that the secretary was having an affair with a member of her security detail, drinking alcohol during the workday, and using taxpayer-funded travel as cover for personal trips. The White House initially dismissed the reports as “baseless.” The Labor Department issued similar denials. Then the denials got quieter. Then they stopped altogether.

What investigators found, according to the New York Times, was a department in a state of institutional rot. Chavez-DeRemer’s chief of staff and deputy chief of staff were placed on leave in January and resigned in March. A third senior aide, Melissa Robey, was fired in late March — days after giving a four-hour interview to the Office of the Inspector General. The message to the remaining staff was unmistakable: cooperate with investigators at the cost of your career.

The inspector general’s team spoke with several dozen witnesses. They uncovered evidence that Chavez-DeRemer and her staff had abused federal spending limits on personal trips — fancy hotels, SUV rentals, restaurant bills. They reviewed text messages between the secretary, her former deputy chief of staff, her husband, and her father, all sent to young female staffers. The texts suggested daytime drinking and raised what investigators described, with characteristic bureaucratic understatement, as “questions about professionalism.”

Allegations against Dr. Shawn DeRemer, the husband of Labor Secretary Lori Chavez-DeRemer, were raised as part of an investigation by the Labor Department’s inspector general.Credit...Kent Nishimura/Reuters

And then there was Shawn DeRemer. The high school sweetheart. The anesthesiologist from Portland. The co-founder of the medical clinics. At least two female Labor Department employees accused him of unwanted sexual advances at the department’s headquarters. A police report was filed. He was barred from the building. D.C. police and federal prosecutors declined to bring charges, but three hostile work environment claims were subsequently filed against the secretary herself.

Meanwhile, the department’s social media operation was conducting its own parallel descent. In January 2026, the official X account posted an eleven-second video captioned “One Homeland. One People. One Heritage. Remember who you are, American.” Historians, union leaders, and the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum noted the uncomfortable resemblance to a notorious Nazi-era slogan. The department’s headquarters was also among the federal buildings decorated with a massive banner of Trump’s face. It was a curious aesthetic for an agency named after Frances Perkins.

By the time Senator Charles Grassley, the Republican chair of the Judiciary Committee, demanded internal records and statements, the end was inevitable. Senator Thom Tillis, Republican of North Carolina, told reporters he believed Chavez-DeRemer was resigning “because the allegations are true.” Senator John Kennedy of Louisiana offered the gentler but no less devastating observation that the secretary had “demonstrated a lot of wisdom in resigning” and “read the room.” Tillis added, with the sort of candour that emerges only after the damage is done, that senators needed to do a better job vetting Trump nominees: “Anywhere where benefit of the doubt was given in the past, you’ve got to doubt.”

Keith Sonderling, the deputy secretary who had been effectively running the department the entire time, was named acting secretary. The announcement came from Cheung, not from Trump — a tell, in this administration, that the departure was not a source of presidential pride. Her own attorney told NBC News the resignation was “not the result of legal wrongdoings” but rather “a personal decision.” Chavez-DeRemer issued a statement thanking Trump as “the greatest President of my lifetime” and promising to continue fighting for American workers from the private sector. She did not acknowledge a single allegation.

Her departure has consequences that extend well beyond the usual cabinet musical chairs. The appointment of Chavez-DeRemer was the tangible evidence of the O’Brien-Trump alliance, the physical proof that the Teamsters’ gamble had purchased something real. With her gone, what exactly did Sean O’Brien buy with his convention speech, his refusal to endorse Harris, his rupture with the broader labour movement?

The answer, fourteen months into the arrangement, is becoming painfully clear: not much. The administration has appointed a former UPS and Amazon safety compliance officer to head OSHA — despite both companies’ dismal safety records. A union-busting lawyer was nominated as chief counsel for the National Labor Relations Board. Federal employee unions have been decertified under national security pretexts. ICE agents have appeared at Teamster picket lines to intimidate strikers. The UPS contract that O’Brien touted as historic is unravelling amid hub closures and tens of thousands of layoffs. As one writer for Current Affairs put it: labour’s birthright has been sold for a mess of very bad pottage.

And now the Labor Secretary that O’Brien personally championed — the Teamster’s daughter, the PRO Act co-sponsor, the proof of concept — has resigned under a cloud so dense that Republican senators are publicly calling the allegations credible. The seat at the table O’Brien bargained for has been occupied by someone else entirely. Sonderling, a labour lawyer with no particular union sympathies, is now running the department he was already running.

O’Brien faces his own reckoning. The Teamsters’ 2026 election is scheduled for November, and the Fearless slate — led by Richard Hooker Jr. of Local 623 in Philadelphia and John Palmer, a current vice president at large — is running explicitly against O’Brien’s embrace of Trump. Hooker’s position is blunt: instead of putting the White House on speed dial, the Teamsters “should be in the mud with everybody else fighting tooth and nail against Trump and his regime.” Whether O’Brien can survive the optics of his investment’s collapse — with challengers organizing, rank-and-file anger simmering, and his hand-picked cabinet appointment now a punchline — is, at minimum, an open question.

From the Canadian side of the border, there is something grimly instructive about the arc of Lori Chavez-DeRemer. She was, by all accounts, exactly the kind of person a functioning government would want in public service — a first-generation college graduate, a successful businesswoman, a mayor who grew her community, a legislator who worked across the aisle. She was chosen not for those qualities but as a transaction, a token of an alliance between a president who views labour as a prop and a union leader who views the presidency as a patron. She was confirmed by senators who did not vet her adequately, praised by a White House that did not supervise her, and defended by spokespeople who stopped defending her the moment the defence became inconvenient.

The tragedy — if it is a tragedy, and not merely a farce — is that the Department of Labour’s sixteen thousand employees and its mission to protect American workers were, at every stage of this process, an afterthought. The appointment was about a deal. The confirmation was about a vote count. The tenure was about the absence of leadership. And the resignation was about the accumulation of scandals that flourish when nobody with actual authority is paying attention.

Fourteen cabinet secretaries departed in Trump’s first term. Three are already gone in his second. Ninety-two percent senior staff turnover the first time around, thirty-two percent and climbing now. The revolving door has been installed as a permanent architectural feature.

“She read the room,” Senator Kennedy said. Perhaps. But someone should have read it a good deal earlier. The bassline, after all, never really stopped.
