George W. Bush: The Restraint Doctrine

December 2, 2025

When a President Refused His Own Vice President

George W. Bush used clemency so sparingly that he granted fewer pardons than any modern two-term president. His record is remarkable not for what it contains, but for what it doesn’t—and for a single case that reveals his willingness to maintain boundaries even when politically costly.

The Numbers: Unprecedented Restraint

George W. Bush’s eight-year record: 189 pardons and 11 commutations—200 total clemency actions.

To put this in perspective:

  • Bill Clinton (1993-2001): 459 total

  • Ronald Reagan (1981-1989): 406 total

  • Jimmy Carter (1977-1981): 563 total

Bush averaged fewer than 25 clemency actions per year—the lowest rate of any modern two-term president. This wasn’t oversight. It reflected a philosophical approach: consequences matter, and clemency should be truly exceptional.

Most of Bush’s pardons went to individuals who had completed their sentences years earlier and demonstrated rehabilitation. Bush granted almost no pardons in his first term, waiting until his final years to exercise this power.

But one case towers above all others: I. Lewis “Scooter” Libby.

The Scooter Libby Case: When the Vice President Lost

Scooter Libby served as Chief of Staff to Vice President Dick Cheney—one of the most powerful non-elected officials in American history. The case emerged from an investigation into how CIA operative Valerie Plame’s identity was leaked to the press, apparent political retaliation against her husband, Ambassador Joseph Wilson, who had challenged Bush administration claims about Iraq seeking uranium.

The Crime: In October 2005, Libby was indicted on five counts: perjury (two counts), making false statements (two counts), and obstruction of justice. Special Prosecutor Patrick Fitzgerald concluded that Libby lied about his conversations with journalists regarding Plame.

Fitzgerald told the jury that Libby’s actions left “a cloud over the White House” by obstructing the investigation. In March 2007, a jury convicted Libby on four of five counts. Judge Reggie Walton sentenced him to 30 months in prison, a $250,000 fine, and two years probation.

The Pressure: Vice President Cheney immediately launched what observers described as a “crusade” to secure a full pardon. Conservative commentators rallied. Republican officials called for clemency.

But opposition was fierce. Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid urged Bush not to pardon Libby. Major newspapers editorialized against it. A CNN poll found 69% of Americans opposed to a Libby pardon, with only 18% in favor.

Bush’s Decision: On July 2, 2007—hours after an appeals court denied Libby’s request to remain free during appeal—Bush acted. But he didn’t grant what Cheney wanted.

Bush commuted Libby’s prison sentence, eliminating jail time. But critically, Bush left everything else intact: the conviction, the $250,000 fine, the two years probation. Libby would not go to prison, but he remained a convicted felon who would be automatically disbarred and likely never practice law again.

Bush’s statement:

“I respect the jury’s verdict. But I have concluded that the prison sentence given to Mr. Libby is excessive. Therefore, I am commuting the portion of Mr. Libby’s sentence that required him to spend thirty months in prison.”

Note what Bush did not say: He didn’t claim Libby was innocent. He didn’t attack the prosecutor. He didn’t question the jury. He specifically stated he “respected the jury’s verdict” while finding only the prison time excessive.

The Aftermath: Cheney was not satisfied. According to Time magazine reporting and later memoirs, Cheney continued lobbying Bush for a full pardon throughout Bush’s presidency. The issue strained their relationship nearly to breaking.

In the final hours before leaving office in January 2009, Cheney made one last push. Bush refused. He had decided the week before that Libby was undeserving of a full pardon. A top adviser later said he had never seen Cheney focused so single-mindedly on anything over two terms.

Two days after their term expired, Cheney publicly expressed regret that Bush hadn’t pardoned Libby.

Applying Our Framework to Libby

1. Connection to President: Extremely strong—senior White House official, Cheney’s Chief of Staff, at center of administration policy.

2. Nature of Crime: Perjury, false statements, obstruction of justice in investigation of whether White House officials leaked CIA operative’s identity for political retaliation. Serious crimes striking at integrity of justice system.

3. Victim Impact: The justice system, public’s right to accountability, and potentially endangered CIA operative and contacts. Crimes obstructed ability to determine who actually leaked Plame’s identity and why.

4. Stage of Justice: Early—before Libby reported to prison, immediately after appeals court ruling.

5. Process: Highly irregular—Bush didn’t consult Justice Department, bypassing normal Office of Pardon Attorney review.

6. Timing: Mid-presidency (July 2007, 18 months before term ended), suggesting response to immediate pressure rather than traditional end-of-term clemency.

7. Justification: Bush provided substantive explanation—sentence excessive compared to similar cases—but notably didn’t question conviction or attack prosecution.

The Critical Restraint

Here’s what makes the Libby case revealing: Despite enormous pressure from his own Vice President, despite the conviction involving a senior White House official, despite conservative media demands, and despite political logic of protecting a loyal aide—Bush showed restraint.

He didn’t grant what was requested. He didn’t fully exonerate Libby. He left conviction, fine, and probation intact. When Cheney continued pressing for full pardon, Bush refused, even at cost of seriously damaging his relationship with his Vice President.

Fred Fielding, Bush’s White House Counsel, deliberately worded the commutation “in a way that would make it harder for Bush to revisit it in the future.” The language was “intended to send an unmistakable message, internally as well as externally: No one is above the law.”

This restraint is meaningful because Bush had power to do more and chose not to. When the most powerful person you work for repeatedly begs you to pardon his Chief of Staff, and you refuse, that reveals commitment to boundaries even facing personal and political pressure.

Was It Justified?

The Libby commutation was and remains controversial. House Judiciary Chairman John Conyers argued Bush abused his power to protect himself and Cheney from oversight. The Washington Times—which had supported Bush—called his decision “neither wise nor just,” emphasizing that “perjury is a serious crime.”

Public opinion was decisively negative (69% opposed). And substantively, the argument that Libby’s sentence was excessive is debatable—obstruction of justice in high-profile national security investigation by senior White House official seems precisely the case warranting serious punishment.

But here’s the critical distinction: Bush’s intervention, while controversial, was limited. He didn’t erase the crime. He didn’t attack the justice system. He didn’t pardon multiple associates. He didn’t create a pattern. And when pressed to go further, he refused.

This becomes crucial when comparing to what came later.

Other Bush Pardons: Traditional Patterns

Beyond Libby, Bush’s pardon record followed traditional patterns. Most went to individuals who had:

  • Completed sentences years or decades earlier

  • Demonstrated rehabilitation through community service and clean records

  • Committed non-violent offenses, often decades past

Notable examples:

  • Isaac Toussie (Brooklyn developer, false statements to HUD)—Bush pardoned him then revoked it one day later when questions arose about his father’s Republican donations. This rare reversal shows Bush’s sensitivity to appearance of impropriety.

  • Ignacio Ramos and Jose Compean (Border Patrol agents convicted of shooting Mexican drug smuggler, then covering it up)—controversial case championed by conservatives

  • Various individuals convicted of tax offenses, drug possession, other non-violent crimes many years earlier

Bush granted almost no pardons in his first term—suggesting he took the power seriously. The bulk came in his final year, following traditional end-of-term practice.

What Bush’s Record Reveals

Bush’s clemency approach reveals several principles:

Extreme Parsimony: Bush granted fewer pardons than virtually any modern president, suggesting philosophical belief that consequences matter and clemency should be truly exceptional.

Traditional Process: With rare exceptions (like Libby), Bush generally followed Justice Department review processes and granted clemency through proper channels after substantial time served.

Resistance to Pressure: The Libby case shows Bush willing to disappoint his closest ally rather than fully pardon a convicted felon, even when it damaged personal relationships.

No Self-Protection Pattern: Bush didn’t systematically pardon people who could implicate him. He didn’t pardon multiple associates. He didn’t create a clemency system serving his political interests.

Minimal Victim Erasure: Because Bush granted so few pardons and generally waited until people completed sentences, his clemency didn’t systematically erase restitution or leave victims uncompensated.

Meaningful Boundaries: When Trump later granted Libby a full pardon in 2018 (ten years after Bush left office), it highlighted Bush’s restraint—Bush could have done what Trump did, but chose not to.

Conservative Restraint

Bush’s record matters because it demonstrates that a conservative Republican president can use clemency sparingly, resist pressure from powerful allies, and maintain meaningful boundaries.

This isn’t about whether Bush’s presidency was successful. It’s about how he approached the pardon power specifically. And on this measure, Bush established an important precedent:

Even when protecting a senior White House official convicted in an investigation touching the administration itself, even when the Vice President pleaded repeatedly, even when conservative media demanded action—Bush drew a line.

He commuted a sentence he deemed excessive but left the conviction intact. He refused to grant a full pardon. He accepted damage to a crucial relationship rather than fully exonerate a convicted felon.

Was this perfect? No. The Libby commutation itself was controversial and arguably inappropriate. Bush’s overall stinginess meant many deserving cases went unaddressed. His bypassing of Justice Department review for Libby was irregular.

But Bush’s approach was bounded. It recognized limits. It showed restraint when restraint was costly.

The Baseline for Comparison

When we examine Obama, Biden, and Trump, the Libby case provides a measuring stick:

  • Did the president protect people more connected than Libby was to Bush?

  • Did the president go further than Bush went, granting full pardons where Bush granted only commutations?

  • Did the president systematically pardon associates rather than as isolated incident?

  • Did the president create systems that monetized or politicized clemency?

Bush set a low bar for overall clemency use—his record was minimal. But for political ally pardons specifically, he set a relatively high bar: he showed you could resist pressure and maintain limits even when politically costly.

Whether his successors maintained or abandoned these boundaries tells us something crucial about how they understood presidential power and accountability.
