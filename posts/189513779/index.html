The Iran Crisis

The Invisible Succession

Part XX: Day Two

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

When Nicolás Maduro was removed from power in Venezuela last month, the world watched. There were cameras. There was an arrest. There was a transfer. The process, however chaotic, was visible.

Iran will not work that way.

Whatever is happening inside the Iranian state today — in the bunkers, in the IRGC command structure, in the Assembly of Experts, in the offices of men who served Khamenei for decades and are now calculating their next move — is happening in the dark. The internet is at 4% of normal capacity. State media is broadcasting revolutionary songs. The IRGC does not hold press conferences. The Assembly of Experts does not livestream its deliberations.

The West will not see the succession as it happens. It will see the result — when a result exists — and work backward to understand how it was reached. By then, the facts on the ground will already be set.

This is the central reality of Day Two. Not the bombs, which continue. Not the markets, which open tonight. Not the diplomacy, which is paralyzed. The central reality is that the most consequential political transition of the 21st century is occurring entirely out of sight, inside a state that has spent 47 years perfecting the art of opacity.

What The Succession Mechanism Says — And Doesn’t

Iran’s constitution is clear on process. The Assembly of Experts — 88 clerics, vetted by the Guardian Council, formally empowered to select and supervise the supreme leader — is the body that names Khamenei’s successor. It has done this once before, in 1989, when Ali Khamenei was selected following the death of the revolution’s founder, Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini.

That process took days. It was contested. It required Rafsanjani to claim, plausibly or not, that Khomeini’s dying wish was for Khamenei — a man of limited clerical credentials — to lead. The result was not destiny. It was maneuver.

The constitution assumes the theocracy is intact. It assumes the Assembly of Experts can convene. It assumes the Guardian Council that vetted those 88 clerics still functions. It assumes the IRGC, which Khamenei spent decades cultivating as his praetorian force, accepts the outcome rather than shapes it.

None of those assumptions can be verified today.

Khamenei: Confirmed

Iran’s state news agency IRNA confirmed the supreme leader’s death. Iranian state television announced it. The denial phase — foreign ministry officials saying he was “safe and sound,” claims he had been evacuated to a secure location — collapsed under the weight of the confirmation the regime itself could no longer withhold.

The CIA intelligence story now documented by the New York Times explains the precision of the strike. The agency had been tracking Khamenei for months, building confidence about his locations and movement patterns. Then it learned that a leadership meeting would take place Saturday morning at the compound in the heart of Tehran — and that Khamenei would be there. The United States and Israel adjusted the timing of their entire operation, shifting from a planned nighttime strike to a daylight attack, specifically to exploit this intelligence. The CIA passed what officials described as “high fidelity” information on Khamenei’s position to Israel.

Two hours and five minutes after Israeli jets took off, at 9:40 AM Tehran time, long-range missiles struck the compound. Khamenei was in his office at home. Senior national security officials were in a separate nearby building. Both buildings were hit.

The Iranian government’s own announcement framed his death as martyrdom: state television’s anchor read a statement from the National Supreme Council saying his death “will spark a massive uprising in the fight against oppressors.” That is the regime’s public narrative. The streets of Tehran told a different story simultaneously.

Pezeshkian Survived — And That Changes The Picture

President Masoud Pezeshkian was targeted. He survived.

President Masoud Pezeshkian was targeted. He survived.

His son Yusuf confirmed it publicly: “The assassination attempts failed and other officials are also healthy.” Iranian state media IRNA, Tasnim, and Mehr all confirmed the president is alive and in full health. Two Israeli sources told CNN that Pezeshkian was on the target list alongside Khamenei. The presidential palace was among the strikes. The attempt failed.

Pezeshkian is now the highest-ranking surviving official of the Iranian state with a formal constitutional role. The Iranian president, under the Islamic Republic’s constitution, is the second most powerful figure in the state — subordinate to the supreme leader, but with executive authority over the government. In a succession crisis, the president’s survival is not incidental. It is a potential anchor for continuity.

Pezeshkian is a reformist by Iranian standards. He was elected in 2024 on a platform of engagement with the West and economic recovery. He is not an ideologue. He is precisely the kind of figure — in Sadjadpour’s framework — from the 80% opportunist majority rather than the 20% true believer core.

His son added a sentence that belongs in the record: “We will probably experience a longer conflict and this will be a war of attrition. Therefore, determination and patience are essential to get through these days.”

That is not the language of a government preparing to collapse. That is the language of a government preparing to endure.

The IRGC: Opportunists With Guns — But Can Someone Step Forward?

The question being asked in every capital tonight is whether the IRGC is strong enough and coherent enough for someone to step forward and assume control of Iran. The answer is yes — and it’s complicated in ways that will define the next phase of this war.

The IRGC is not a conventional military. It is a parallel state that happens to have an army. It controls an estimated one-third of Iran’s economy — construction, telecommunications, energy, import/export, smuggling networks. It has its own navy, air force, and intelligence service, with the Basij paramilitary beneath it. It answers to the supreme leader — not the president, not parliament. With the supreme leader gone, it answers to itself. It has 125,000 to 150,000 military personnel and millions in the Basij. It has missile capability, naval capability, and proxy forces across the region. It is the most institutionally coherent force remaining in Iran right now.

But the IRGC has never governed alone. It has always operated as the enforcement arm of clerical authority — not as the clerical authority itself. Stepping from enforcer to ruler is a different thing. It requires legitimacy the IRGC doesn’t have constitutionally and has never needed before.

There is also the leadership crater to consider. Hossein Salami — the previous IRGC commander — was killed by Israel in June 2025. His replacement Mohammed Pakpour was confirmed killed on Saturday along with Armed Forces Chief of Staff Abdolrahim Mousavi and Defense Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh. Iranian state media confirmed 201 killed and 747 injured in the strikes overall — figures that cannot be independently verified but represent the regime’s own accounting. Khamenei’s daughter, grandchild, daughter-in-law, and son-in-law were also killed in the compound strike, according to Israeli reports.

That unknown person — whoever was third in the IRGC command line — has now been identified. His name is Ahmad Vahidi. And his appointment changes the succession calculus in ways that are not encouraging for a negotiated outcome.

Vahidi is not an opportunist in the Sadjadpour sense. He is a founding member of the IRGC’s Quds Force — the same position Qassem Soleimani eventually succeeded him in. He later served as defense minister and interior minister. He is under US sanctions for his role in the lethal suppression of the 2022 protests. He is wanted by Interpol on an Argentine arrest warrant for his alleged role in the 1994 AMIA bombing of the Buenos Aires Jewish community center, which killed 85 people and wounded 330. Argentina’s judiciary concluded the attack was “agreed upon at the highest levels of the Iranian government.” He has previously warned that Israel would “draw its final breath” if it dared strike Iran’s nuclear sites. He is, by any analytical measure, from the 20% true believer core — not the 80% opportunist majority.

The opportunist calculus this series has documented — men calculating whether fighting or dealing preserves their economic holdings — does not apply to Vahidi in the same way it applies to the construction empire builders and telecom operators. He is an ideologue with a record of violence and an Interpol notice. His appointment as IRGC commander on Day Two is a signal from whatever remains of the Iranian command structure that the institution is not preparing to deal. It is preparing to fight.

The historical parallel that fits best is the Soviet Union in August 1991, when hardliners attempted a coup against Gorbachev. The coup plotters had the military, the KGB, and the full apparatus of state coercion. What they didn’t have was the will to use it decisively when the moment came — because enough of them had calculated that their personal futures were better served by not firing on the crowds. That calculus held for the Soviet opportunists. Whether it holds for an institution now commanded by the man allegedly responsible for the Buenos Aires bombing is a different question.

Iran's military command structure. Three parallel forces — the Artesh (conventional military), the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps (IRGC), and the Law Enforcement Command (LEC) — sit beneath the Armed Forces General Staff and the Khatam ol Anbia Central Headquarters, the IRGC's economic conglomerate. Whoever controls all three branches simultaneously controls Iran. As of March 1, 2026, the IRGC commander is dead. The supreme leader is reported dead. The question of unified command is unanswered.

A structural point most Western coverage is missing: the IRGC does not sit alone at the top of Iran’s military hierarchy. Iran’s command structure has three parallel pillars beneath the Armed Forces General Staff and the Khatam ol Anbia Central Headquarters — the IRGC, the Artesh (Iran’s conventional military, distinct from and historically rivalrous with the IRGC), and the Law Enforcement Command with its Special Units (the internal security apparatus that crushed the January protests and currently maintains the internet blackout). Whoever steps forward to claim authority would need to control not just the IRGC but all three branches simultaneously to actually govern Iran. The IRGC commander is dead. The supreme leader is dead. The question of who holds unified command across all three pillars is, as of this writing, unanswered.

The Leadership Council: Arafi, Pezeshkian, Mohseni-Ejei

The interim council is now formally constituted under Article 111 of Iran’s constitution. Three members: President Pezeshkian, Chief Justice Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Ejei, and Ayatollah Alireza Arafi as the Guardian Council cleric. The Expediency Discernment Council confirmed Arafi’s appointment on Sunday morning via its spokesman on X.

Arafi is not a name that was circulating in Western coverage before Saturday, but his profile is analytically significant. He is 67, born in Yazd province, moved to Qom for religious study at age 11, and spent decades ascending through the clerical structure as a Khamenei loyalist. Before Sunday he simultaneously held three of the most powerful non-supreme-leader positions in the Iranian state: director of the nationwide seminary system, member of the Assembly of Experts, and member of the Guardian Council. He also heads the Basij paramilitary. He received Pope Francis in a private audience at the Vatican in 2022 — a rare gesture of interfaith diplomacy for a senior Iranian cleric. He speaks fluent Arabic and English. He has advocated for the regime to adopt artificial intelligence to spread its ideological message globally.

He is not a reformist. He is a Khamenei continuity figure. But he is more sophisticated, more internationally engaged, and more technologically minded than the standard hardliner profile. The clerical establishment’s choice of Arafi signals that it wants continuity and stability, not a sudden rupture — and that it wants someone capable of functioning in a diplomatic environment if that moment arrives.

Ali Larijani is a veteran politician and principlist in Iran, having previously served in various senior government positions, including the presidency of the Islamic Consultative Assembly, Secretary of the Supreme National Security Council, chief nuclear negotiator, and head of the Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB), among others.

Ali Larijani remains the crucial figure outside the formal council. He was the man Khamenei pre-designated as crisis manager — the Delcy of Iran, as the New York Times reported from Iranian officials’ own pre-war discussions. His public posture is defiant: “Today we will hit them with a force they have never experienced before.” His private role is to hold the IRGC in line and determine whether this war ends in negotiation or prolonged attrition. He is a pragmatist who has survived every faction of the Islamic Republic across four decades, and he has no formal seat on the Leadership Council — which means his power is entirely informal, entirely dependent on the relationships he holds and the credibility he commands with Vahidi’s IRGC.

That is a precarious position. Larijani is the Delcy of Iran governing alongside an IRGC commander who is wanted by Interpol and has warned Israel it will “draw its final breath” for attacking Iran. The tension between those two postures — deal or fight — is the central unresolved question of the Iranian state as Day Two closes.

Will Mojtaba Khamenei’s Transition Alter Power Dynamics in Tehran?

Mojtaba Khamenei’s status remains formally unresolved, though new information has narrowed it. Israeli state media reports that Khamenei’s daughter, grandchild, daughter-in-law, and son-in-law were killed in the compound strike. The family deaths are now on record. Mojtaba himself was listed as “killed, Israel said” by the New York Times, while separate Israeli officials said he survived. That conflict has not been officially resolved as of this writing. Given the confirmed family fatalities in the same strike, confirmation of Mojtaba’s death would not be surprising. If confirmed, the hardline succession threat from the true believer faction loses its most dangerous potential figurehead — though Vahidi’s appointment to the IRGC suggests that faction retains institutional power regardless.

The West will not see the conversation that resolves these questions. It will see the outcome.

The Succession: Three Candidates, One Already Dead

Khamenei had named three preferred successors before the strikes. They are now documented.

Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Ejei, head of the judiciary — the hardliner who declared during the January protests that authorities would use all resources to “crush savage armed terrorists.” Ali Asghar Hejazi, Khamenei’s chief of staff — confirmed killed in the strikes. And Hassan Khomeini — a moderate cleric, grandson of the revolution’s founder Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, from the reformist faction.

Of Khamenei’s three named preferences, at least one is confirmed dead. The pragmatist and the reformist remain. The hardliner remains. The Assembly of Experts — 88 clerics, unable to convene safely under bombardment — must ultimately make the choice. In the meantime, Larijani’s interim council governs.

Hassan Khomeini’s presence on the list is the detail that will receive the least attention and deserves the most. A moderate, a reformist, a grandson of the man who made the revolution — and Khamenei named him as a possible successor. That is either a genuine opening toward pragmatism built into the succession plan, or Khamenei’s final act of managing factions by giving the reformists a name without giving them power. The Assembly of Experts will decide which.

Two Tehrans

The Iranian government’s framing of Khamenei’s death as martyrdom that will “spark a massive uprising against oppressors” collided Saturday night with what actually happened in the streets.

Large crowds in Tehran, Shiraz, Isfahan, and other cities poured into the streets to celebrate. Fireworks. Persian dance music. Drivers honking. People on balconies chanting “freedom, freedom.” A man in Galleh Dar tearing down a monument bearing Khamenei’s silhouette as fires burned around him. “Am I dreaming?” screamed one man on verified video. “Hello to the new world.”

Sara, a 53-year-old Tehran resident who was beaten by security forces during the January protests, said she screamed and jumped when she heard the news. “Then we bolted outside and shouted from the top of our lungs and laughed and danced with our neighbors.”

Simultaneously, on Sunday morning, large crowds gathered in Enghelab Square in Tehran for a mass mourning ceremony — tearful people waving Iranian flags, holding photos of Khamenei, dirges over loudspeakers. In Kashmir, thousands marched with black flags. Iraq declared three days of national mourning.

Both things are true. Two Tehrans exist simultaneously. The war’s outcome depends on which one is larger — and whether the celebrating Tehran is armed, organized, and connected enough to do anything about it.

The Iranian people are unarmed. The state killed as many as 30,000 civilians in the January crackdown. The internet is effectively down. State media is the primary information source for most Iranians inside the country. Trump told them to take over their government via a platform most of them cannot currently access. That means any uprising will not be coordinated by social media in real time. It will be spontaneous, neighborhood by neighborhood, city by city, without central organization, without arms, against a security force that has spent decades preparing for exactly this scenario.

History does not offer many examples of unarmed populations successfully overthrowing governments that have just lost their leader but retained their security apparatus. It offers some. They are not the majority.

The Iranian People: Unarmed, Offline, and Waiting

The Iranian diaspora can see everything. The Iranian population inside Iran can see almost nothing.

This is not an accident. Khamenei built the digital wall deliberately. The IRGC administers it. Shutting down the internet in a crisis is not a panic measure — it is a rehearsed protocol, executed within hours of the first strikes. The regime’s information control apparatus is one of the few parts of the state that appears to be functioning normally on Day Two.

Trump told them to take over their government. Reza Pahlavi told them the time to return to the streets was approaching and that he would communicate when. Both messages were sent via platforms that most Iranians inside Iran cannot currently access.

The uprising Trump predicted — if it comes — will therefore not be coordinated by social media in real time. It will be spontaneous, neighborhood by neighborhood, city by city, without central organization, without arms, against a security force that has spent decades preparing for exactly this scenario. The digital wall the regime built is still standing while everything above it burns. That may be the most consequential capability the bombs have not yet touched.

Reza Pahlavi: The Face Looks For a Body

Reza Pahlavi — son of the last Shah, 45 years in exile, the face this series documented emerging within hours of the strikes — faces his defining moment on Day Two.

He has name recognition. He has diaspora support. He has no military force, no institutional base inside Iran, no party, no coalition. He told Iranians to wait for his signal. He told the IRGC to stand down or sink with Khamenei’s ship.

On Day Two, with Khamenei confirmed dead, the signal question becomes urgent. Does he call for the streets? Does he reach out to IRGC commanders? Does he coordinate with Washington, which created the conditions he called “humanitarian intervention” but has offered no formal recognition or support structure?

The governance question from Part XVI just became the governance crisis of Part XX. Pahlavi is a symbol. Symbols require institutions to become governments. The institutions that could support him are either destroyed, in hiding, or calculating their own survival.

He is the most visible Iranian opposition figure in the world tonight. Visibility without structure is not power. It is exposure.

The Bombing Continues

Trump was explicit: the strikes will continue “throughout the week or as long as necessary.” Day Two is not the end of the campaign. It may be the most dangerous phase.

The military logic is documented in The Atlantic’s reporting: planners projected a decisive phase in the days following the initial bombardment. 500 Israeli strikes on Day One cleared Iranian air defenses sufficiently to establish aerial superiority. Day Two and beyond focuses on what that superiority enables — sustained strikes on IRGC infrastructure, missile storage, and the economic empire the IRGC has built over decades.

Destroying the IRGC’s economic assets is not the same as destroying the IRGC’s will or its command structure. It may accelerate the opportunist calculus — men with nothing left to lose economically have less reason to deal and more reason to fight. Or it may break the financial incentive that holds the force together. The planners did not know which. They built weeks of strikes into the plan because they did not know which.

Every day the bombing continues is a day the succession conversation inside Iran happens under fire. Negotiations under bombardment produce different outcomes than negotiations in silence. The US is betting that continued pressure accelerates the regime’s collapse. The alternative — that it hardens the IRGC’s resolve and produces the failed state scenario — is the risk Dana Stroul described as “complete chaos.”

Israel’s defense minister confirmed the frame on Sunday: the operation will continue “for as long as necessary” and one of its stated objectives is to “create the conditions” for a change of government in Iran. What those conditions look like, and whether the bombing creates or forecloses them, is the central unanswered question of Day Two.

What The West Cannot See

Venezuela was visible. The arrest was on camera. The transfer was tracked. The new government took its first steps in public.

Iran will not work that way. It never has. The Islamic Republic was built on the principle that its internal workings are not the world’s business. That principle is intact even as the regime that enforced it has lost its leader.

What the West will see: missile launches, bomb damage assessments, satellite imagery, social media from the diaspora, statements from officials who may or may not represent coherent authority. What the West will not see: the actual conversations that determine whether this war ends in transition, in chaos, or in a prolonged insurgency that looks nothing like Venezuela and everything like Iraq.

Trump told the Iranian people their only chance for generations was at hand. He may be right. He may also have launched a war whose outcome is now in the hands of 80% opportunists making private calculations in hardened bunkers, with no internet, no press, and no obligation to inform the world of their decisions until those decisions are irreversible.

The governance question Part XVI asked has arrived. The answer is being written in the dark.
