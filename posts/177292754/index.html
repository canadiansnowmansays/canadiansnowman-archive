The Great Gayermandering Goes National

Part 3 The Reckoning: What Happens When State-by-State Madness Becomes a National Crisis

October 27, 2025

Previously, in Part 2: Fifty Shades of Gerrymander...

Texas’s pioneering “gayermandering” scheme spread like wildfire across America. Missouri drew districts based on Anthropologie shopping patterns. North Carolina created “The Spite Snake.” California countered with kombucha-based redistricting. Kansas unveiled the “Goodbye Sharice” district. Indiana proposed “Real America Corridors.” Florida’s maps went underwater at high tide. Ohio let AI draw districts based on Skyline Chili consumption. And New York, Illinois, and Maryland decided “if you can’t beat ‘em, join ‘em.”

Now, as the 2026 midterms approach, the national consequences of this redistricting arms race become impossible to ignore...

The National Impact: A Patchwork of Absurdity

As of late October 2025, America faces an unprecedented redistricting crisis where:

  • 17 states are actively considering or implementing mid-decade redistricting

  • Republicans could gain 6-9 House seats through these efforts

  • Democrats are pursuing up to 8 additional seats in response

  • Zero voters asked for any of this

Political scientist Dr. Wanda Representative-Democracy at We’re-All-Doomed University explains: “What we’re witnessing is the complete abandonment of even the pretense that elections should reflect the will of the people. Both parties have looked at democracy and said, ‘You know what this needs? More graphic design.’”

The Cook Political Report has given up trying to rate competitive districts, instead issuing statements like “Who knows anymore” and “Ask us after the lawsuits are resolved.”

Independent analysis suggests that if all proposed redistricting efforts succeed, approximately 11 House seats would change parties without a single voter changing their mind. Democracy!

The Technology Arms Race

The situation escalated further when both parties began employing increasingly sophisticated—and ridiculous—technology to identify target voters.

Republicans deployed “PatriotMap AI™,” which analyzes:

  • Netflix viewing histories (rom-coms = liberal, action movies = conservative)

  • Grocery store purchase patterns (arugula vs. iceberg lettuce)

  • Vehicle type and number of bumper stickers

  • Font choices in emails (Comic Sans = conservative, Helvetica = dangerous radical)

  • Social media posting times (early morning = farmer = conservative)

Democrats countered with “VoteBlue Analytics,” which tracks:

  • Reusable shopping bag ownership

  • Attendance at farmer’s markets

  • Number of houseplants owned

  • Whether someone’s ever used the word “intersectionality” in conversation

  • Tote bag to backpack ratio

Both systems are approximately 34% accurate, which hasn’t stopped anyone from using them.

The technology has led to bizarre outcomes. One Texas neighborhood found itself split into four different districts because the AI determined that one side of the street prefers Starbucks while the other prefers local coffee shops—a distinction the AI deemed “electorally determinative.”

The Legal Challenges Mount

Lawsuits have been filed in every state attempting redistricting. The cases have names like:

  • People Who Like Good Coffee v. State of Missouri

  • Coalition for Districts That Don’t Look Like Drunk Snakes v. North Carolina

  • Literally Everyone With Common Sense v. Florida

  • Kansas City Residents Who Know Geography v. Kansas Legislature

  • The Concept of Representative Democracy v. Everyone Involved in This Mess

Voting rights organizations are working overtime. Marc Elias of Democracy Docket has filed so many lawsuits he’s had to hire a logistics coordinator just to keep track of which courthouse he’s supposed to be in.

“I’ve been fighting voter suppression for years,” Elias said at a press conference, looking exhausted, “but I never imagined I’d be arguing that you can’t exclude neighborhoods based on avocado toast consumption. Yet here we are.”

The Brennan Center for Justice has published seventeen reports since August, each more alarmed than the last. Their most recent report is simply titled “Seriously, Everyone Stop.”

The ACLU has filed so many amicus briefs that they’ve started numbering them like software versions. They’re currently on “Amicus Brief 47.3: The Madness Continues.”

The Unexpected Coalition

In a twist no one saw coming, LGBTQ+ advocacy groups have joined forces with rural farming communities, traditional Republicans uncomfortable with the extremism, and basically everyone who thinks maybe democracy shouldn’t be decided by graphic designers with partisan agendas.

The coalition, calling itself “Americans for Districts That Don’t Look Completely Insane,” has a simple platform: “Maybe just draw normal squares?”

This radical proposal has been dismissed by both parties as “dangerously naive” and “not understanding how politics really works.”

The coalition’s spokesperson, retired Army General James “Just-Draw-Normal-Districts” Thompson, has become an unlikely folk hero. His testimony before various state legislatures—featuring phrases like “I fought for this country so y’all could draw districts that look like abstract art?”—has gone viral.

The coalition has proposed what they call “The Common Sense Redistricting Act,” which would require:

  • Districts that don’t require a GPS to understand

  • No corridors thinner than one mile

  • A maximum of six sides per district (they’re willing to compromise)

  • Districts that don’t primarily consist of highways

Both parties rejected the proposal within hours.

International Reaction

The international community has responded with a mixture of concern, confusion, and schadenfreude.

The British Parliament passed a resolution expressing “deep worry about the state of American democracy, coming from people who only recently figured out their own voting system.” One MP added: “We have literal lords in our legislature and even we think this is undemocratic.”

The European Union issued a statement: “We have observers monitoring elections in developing democracies worldwide. Should we... should we send some to America?”

The UN briefly considered sending election monitors but decided “there’s too much chaos to monitor effectively.”

Even Russia’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs weighed in: “We may interfere in elections, but at least we don’t pretend we’re not doing it. This is just sad.”

A Canadian official, speaking on condition of anonymity, said: “We’re watching this with the same horrified fascination usually reserved for reality TV shows about hoarders.”

The Media Coverage

Major news networks have struggled to cover the story because explaining gerrymandering requires maps, and the maps are now so complicated they cause vertigo.

CNN attempted to display North Carolina’s proposed districts on screen and had to issue a motion sickness warning.

MSNBC devoted an entire hour to showing Missouri’s “Bowtie Corridor” from various angles. Viewers reported feeling “geographically nauseated.”

Fox News declared the entire thing a “triumph of innovative governance” while showing a map that looked like a Rorschach test designed by someone having a breakdown.

The New York Times created an interactive tool allowing readers to see if they’ve been gerrymandered. The tool crashed within hours because too many people tried to use it simultaneously, united in their fury.

Local newspapers have been reduced to running articles with headlines like:

  • “Your District Is Where Now?”

  • “New Map Means Voting Location Is In Different County”

  • “Cartographers Admit They Have No Idea Anymore”

  • “Local Woman Discovers She Lives In Four Districts Depending On Which Map You Use”

The Economic Impact

The redistricting mania has created an unexpected economic boom in certain sectors:

Map printing companies are experiencing record sales. One Cincinnati-based printer reported: “We can’t keep up with demand. Every week there’s a new map and everyone needs hundreds of copies.”

GPS companies are scrambling to update their systems to reflect new district boundaries. Garmin released a statement: “We can tell you how to get somewhere, but we have no idea what district you’ll be in when you arrive.”

Moving companies report increased business from confused voters attempting to move into more convenient districts, only to discover the maps changed again.

Therapists specializing in civic frustration are booking months in advance.

Political cartographers have formed a support group called “Lines Anonymous” where they meet weekly to discuss the trauma of drawing districts that violate every principle of their profession.

The 2026 Midterms Approach

As the redistricting battle continues, several facts have become clear:

  1. Both parties have decided that winning through map manipulation is easier than winning through good governance

  2. Voter turnout is expected to be either incredibly high (people angry about being gerrymandered) or incredibly low (people convinced their vote doesn’t matter anyway)

  3. Court challenges will likely continue through 2026, 2027, and possibly until the heat death of the universe

  4. Democracy is having what therapists call “a moment”

  5. Cartographers are drinking heavily

The Bottom Line

What started as Texas’s desperate attempt to maintain Republican control through cartographic creativity has become a nationwide race to see which party can make democracy look more ridiculous.

Voters across America are learning that their representation is being decided not by their votes, but by whoever controls the state legislature and has access to mapping software. It’s democracy, but make it graphic design.

As one weary political observer put it: “We used to say ‘every vote counts.’ Now it’s more like ‘every vote counts, but only if it’s in a district we bothered to make competitive, which it probably isn’t, so good luck with that.’”

Meanwhile, independent voters—the people both parties claim to desperately want—are looking at this entire circus and thinking: “You know what? Maybe I’ll just stay home in 2026. You folks seem to have already decided everything anyway.”

And perhaps that’s the real goal of gayermandering all along: make the system so absurd, so obviously rigged, so geometrically nonsensical that people just give up on the whole idea of representative democracy.

Mission accomplished, America. Mission accomplished.

Where We Go From Here

The Supreme Court has already ruled that partisan gerrymandering doesn’t violate the Constitution. But surely there’s a limit, right? Surely at some point, someone in power will look at a map that looks like a Rorschach test designed by someone having a breakdown and say, “This is too far.”

Right?

Checks notes.

Right?

Lower courts are currently wrestling with questions like:

  • Can a district be 600 miles long and 50 feet wide?

  • Is it constitutional to require voters to take boats to reach their polling places?

  • At what point does a district become so absurd it violates due process?

  • Can we all just agree this has gotten out of hand?

Legal experts predict these questions will take years to resolve, by which time the maps will have been redrawn again anyway.

This is a work of satirical fiction. Unfortunately, the real gerrymandering is almost as absurd as what we’ve described here. Which should concern everyone.

A Final Word: What You Can Actually Do

While this has been satirical, the real gerrymandering crisis is no joke. Here’s how to fight back:

Get Informed:

  • Democracy Docket (democracydocket.com) - Latest on voting rights lawsuits

  • Brennan Center for Justice (brennancenter.org) - Nonpartisan redistricting research

  • FairVote (fairvote.org) - Electoral reform advocacy

  • Common Cause (commoncause.org) - Government accountability

Take Action:

  • Contact your state legislators about redistricting reform

  • Support ballot initiatives for independent redistricting commissions

  • Volunteer as a poll worker in 2026

  • Donate to voting rights organizations

  • Most importantly: VOTE in every election, especially local ones

Stay Engaged:

  • Attend state legislature hearings on redistricting

  • Join local advocacy groups

  • Share accurate information about redistricting with friends and family

  • Hold elected officials accountable

The real gerrymandering happening right now is almost as absurd as our satire suggests. The difference is, we can actually do something about it.

Democracy is a participation sport. Time to suit up.
