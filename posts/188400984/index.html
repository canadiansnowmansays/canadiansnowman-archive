The Colbert Ban

Part 2: How the FCC Tried to Silence a Senate Candidate and Accidentally Made Him Famous

February 19, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays · Let’s Talk Texas · February 2026

On his Monday night broadcast, Stephen Colbert, left, said CBS lawyers informed him he could not interview Democratic politician James Talarico due to proposed regulatory guidance from the FCC. (Talia Sprague/The Associated Press, Jae C. Hong/AP

Here’s what happened. Texas state representative James Talarico was booked on The Late Show with Stephen Colbert. He showed up at the studio. He did the interview. And then CBS’s lawyers called the show directly and told them they could not air it.

Not should not. Could not.

Colbert, to his credit, told his audience exactly what had happened. “You know who is not one of my guests tonight?” he said on air. “And because my network clearly doesn’t want us to talk about this, let’s talk about this.” He then posted the full interview to YouTube, where it promptly became one of the most-watched political interviews of the week.

The censorship was the advertisement.

The FCC Weapon

This didn’t come out of nowhere. Last month, the Federal Communications Commission — now led by Trump appointees — issued new guidance declaring that entertainment-oriented talk shows carried on local broadcast stations must offer candidates equal airtime if they’re vying for the same office.

Media companies had long considered late-night talk shows exempt from that rule. The FCC said that was no longer the case. The guidance was transparently aimed at hosts who anger the president: Jimmy Kimmel, Seth Meyers, and Colbert. The FCC had already opened a probe into The View after a Talarico appearance there.

Follow the logic. Talarico is running in a Democratic Senate primary. If CBS airs his interview on broadcast television, the FCC’s new interpretation says every other Democratic primary candidate is entitled to equal time. CBS would either have to offer that time — expensive and logistically complicated — or face regulatory consequences from an FCC that has made no secret of its hostility toward networks it considers insufficiently friendly to the administration.

So CBS folded. Their official statement tried to split the difference, claiming they didn’t prohibit the interview but merely “provided legal guidance that broadcasting it could trigger the FCC rule.” That’s a distinction without a difference, and everyone involved knows it.

What Talarico Actually Said

The interview CBS didn’t want you to see on television wasn’t radical. It wasn’t incendiary. It was a Texas state legislator talking about his faith, his family, and why he’s running for Senate.

Talarico, an eighth-generation Texan and Presbyterian seminarian, made an argument that is quietly devastating to the religious right: that for fifty years, a political movement has convinced American Christians that the most important issues were abortion and gay marriage — “two issues that aren’t mentioned in the Bible, two issues that Jesus never talked about.” What Jesus did talk about in Matthew 25, Talarico said, was feeding the hungry, healing the sick, and welcoming the stranger.

“Don’t tell me what you believe,” he said. “Show me how you treat other people, and I’ll tell you what you believe.”

This is the argument that terrifies Republican strategists in Texas more than any policy position. Texas is deeply religious. The evangelical vote is a cornerstone of GOP dominance. And here is a seminarian — not attacking faith, but reclaiming it — telling voters that Christian nationalism “is the worship of power in the name of Christ” and “a betrayal of Jesus of Nazareth.”

Colbert, himself a practicing Catholic, pushed the conversation further. “How must it diminish God,” he asked, “to be associated with something so small as a present political party?” And then he landed the sharpest line of the interview: “If you managed to co-opt Christianity, if you convinced everybody that Jesus was on your side — if you then lose the election, sounds like you’ve got a pretty weak Jesus.”

You can see why the FCC didn’t want this on broadcast television. Not because it violated any rule. Because it was effective.

The Streisand Effect, Texas Edition

James Talarico and Stephen Colbert on CBS’s The Late Show with Stephen Colbert on Monday. Photograph: CBS Photo Archive/CBS/Getty Images

The term comes from 2003, when Barbra Streisand tried to suppress aerial photographs of her Malibu mansion. The lawsuit drew attention to photos that almost nobody had seen, and they were downloaded 420,000 times in the month after she filed suit.

The Colbert ban is the same mechanism, amplified by social media. If Talarico had simply appeared on CBS at 11:35 PM on a Tuesday, it would have been one segment among many, seen by Colbert’s audience and forgotten by morning. Instead, the banning of the interview became a national news story. The New York Times covered it. The YouTube posting became an event. And the content of the interview — Talarico’s argument about faith, democracy, and the culture war smokescreen — reached an audience orders of magnitude larger than a standard late-night segment would have delivered.

Talarico himself made the point on air: “This is the party that ran against cancel culture and now they’re trying to control what we watch, what we say, what we read. And this is the most dangerous kind of cancel culture — the kind that comes from the top.”

The irony is structural. Trump’s FCC targeted late-night shows to suppress Democratic messaging. CBS complied. And the compliance itself became the most powerful Democratic message of the week — a real-time demonstration that the administration is using regulatory power to silence political opponents on television.

The Bigger Pattern

This isn’t just about one interview. The FCC’s new posture is part of the same architecture I’ve been documenting in the redistricting series: a coordinated effort to tilt the playing field before voters ever get to the ballot box.

Redraw the maps to choose your voters. Pass the SAVE Act to restrict who can register. Use the MEGA Act to eliminate mail voting and ban ballot counting after Election Day. Send the FBI to seize election records in Georgia. And now, use the FCC to control which candidates can appear on broadcast television.

Each mechanism operates independently. Together, they form a system. And the system has a single purpose: to narrow the space in which democratic accountability can function.

Canada doesn’t have an FCC equivalent with this kind of power over political speech. Our broadcast regulator, the CRTC, operates under rules that explicitly protect political coverage during elections. The idea that a government agency would pressure a network to pull an interview with a Senate candidate because the ruling party didn’t like what he might say — it’s not just unlikely in Canada. It’s structurally impossible under our Broadcasting Act.

I don’t say this to be smug. I say it because it’s useful to see what’s normal and what isn’t. What’s happening with the FCC right now is not normal. It is the use of regulatory power to suppress political speech, and it’s happening in plain sight.

The Race to Watch

Talarico’s Senate primary is shaping up to be one of the most consequential races of 2026. He’s running against U.S. Representative Jasmine Crockett, and the choice between them reflects a genuine strategic debate within the Democratic Party.

Crockett is a national figure, a skilled communicator, and a favourite of the party’s progressive base. Talarico is a state legislator with seminary training, a grassroots operation that’s recruited 14,000 volunteers, and a fundraising machine built entirely without corporate PAC money. The Cottle column in the Times noted that Texas Republicans she spoke with were more concerned about facing Talarico in the general election — though none seemed especially worried about either.

That last detail matters. Republicans are confident because Texas is Texas. But Texas was Texas when Taylor Rehmet flipped a Fort Worth Senate seat by 14 points, too.

The primary is March 3. Early voting begins this week.
