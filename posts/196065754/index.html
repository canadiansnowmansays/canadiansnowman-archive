The Oakland Transcript

The Dissection

Day 3 — April 30, 2026

May 1, 2026

A companion series to The Asimov Gap, CanadianSnowmanSays

Elon Musk is questioned by Russell Cohen, a lawyer for Microsoft, during Musk’s lawsuit over OpenAI for-profit conversion at a federal courthouse in Oakland, California, U.S., April 30, 2026 in a courtroom sketch. Vicki Behringer | Reuters

By the third morning, the ritual had settled into something almost theatrical. Elon Musk enters from the basement. The photographers rush the glass. The wooden benches fill with reporters, lawyers, and the curious. The Honorable Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers surveys her courtroom with the patient authority of someone who has seen rich men misbehave before and intends to remain unimpressed by it.

What followed over two days was the kind of legal theatre that reveals not just the claims on the record, but the essential contradictions in the man making them. By Thursday morning, when Musk stepped down from the stand after roughly two hours of final questioning, the overflow room — which had held a hundred people on Wednesday — had dwindled to about thirty. The crowd had come for Musk. With Musk gone, so had the crowd.

The Term Sheet: The Escalation

Day 2 ended with the term sheet unresolved. William Savitt had pressed Musk on whether he had actually read the 2018 document — four pages that explicitly projected OpenAI raising $10 billion — and Musk had claimed he got the gist from the opening. The exchange was combative; Savitt eventually withdrew; the gallery laughed. But he had not finished.

Thursday morning, he returned with the deposition reel. Video of Musk’s pre-trial testimony played in the courtroom. In none of those sessions had Musk mentioned reading the term sheet in any capacity — not the opening, not the gist. His courtroom claim now sat against his own sworn record. Musk fired back: “Your questions are definitionally complex, not simple. It is a lie to say they are simple.” The jury had seen both versions of the story.

Savitt then pressed the statute of limitations directly. Musk’s team needs the jury to believe he didn’t fully comprehend OpenAI’s transformation until the $10 billion Microsoft investment closed — a date that falls conveniently within the three-year window. Musk himself had framed his awakening in three phases: Phase I, full confidence; Phase II, “where I’m like, wait a second… they might be stealing a charity?”; Phase III, “where they stole a charity.” Under questioning, Musk estimated Phase II lasted from approximately 2017 to 2022. That is not a statute of limitations window. That is five years of suspicion before filing suit. Savitt noted it. The jury did too.

“I Contributed My Reputation”

The exchange that will be quoted longest from Musk’s three days on the stand came when Savitt turned to the money.

Musk had promised early on that he could be good for a billion-dollar investment if needed. He ultimately donated $38 million. Savitt asked whether he had contributed “anywhere near a billion dollars” to OpenAI. Musk grew visibly annoyed. “I contributed my reputation!” he stressed. “These things have value! Without me, OpenAI wouldn’t exist! I came up with the name!”

Savitt waited. “My question was simple. You didn’t contribute anywhere near a billion dollars to OpenAI: yes or no?”

“In strict monetary terms,” Musk finally said, “I contributed $38 million.”

Savitt also probed Musk’s coverage of rent at the Pioneer Building in San Francisco — OpenAI’s early headquarters. He noted that Musk’s company Neuralink was also located there, suggesting the rent wasn’t an act of charity so much as an overhead cost already on the books. “I would have sublet it!” Musk said. The gallery laughed.

The exchange on donations had a third dimension. Musk affirmed under cross-examination that there was no written document specifying what his $38 million was for — no contract, no conditions, no terms — beyond a corporate charter stating OpenAI was a nonprofit. The man suing OpenAI for misusing his money had no document saying how the money was to be used.

The Cross-Examination’s Sharpest Angles

Savitt built his case around a methodical accumulation of contradictions — each individually manageable, collectively damaging.

Internal emails from 2017 showed Musk directing Birchall to register a for-profit public benefit corporation in OpenAI’s name. Musk’s explanation: he did it “in case it was needed.” His courtroom framing: “I needed to make sure it would go in the right direction and I was providing almost all the money.”

Savitt raised Musk’s 2023 bid to acquire OpenAI outright through a group of for-profit investors — a maneuver that sits uneasily alongside the lawsuit’s framing of Musk as guardian of nonprofit purity. Musk’s response: “There’s nothing wrong with having a for-profit organization, you just can’t steal a charity.”

Savitt also asked Musk whether he had ever directed the algorithm controlling X to promote his own account. Musk said he had not. Savitt had a ready response: in February 2023, shortly after Musk bought the company, he grew upset that a Biden Super Bowl post outperformed his own. He demanded his engineers investigate. At one point, they made an adjustment that caused the site’s timeline to feature Musk’s posts almost exclusively before switching back. Musk had just testified, under oath, that he had not directed this.

Russell Cohen, the lead lawyer for Microsoft — also named in the suit — then took his turn. He tried to show that Musk was well aware of the OpenAI-Microsoft relationship as far back as 2020 but chose not to sue until four years later, only after ChatGPT became a global phenomenon and the stakes of the AI race had escalated beyond anything the 2019 partnership had suggested. The statute of limitations argument, pressed from two sides simultaneously, is the trial’s sharpest legal edge.

Musk’s own lawyer, Molo, then returned for redirect to address one key point: why Musk had not filed suit years earlier. Musk repeated his position that his awareness of the full extent of the conversion came only later. As he flatly denied that Tesla was pursuing AGI — this from a man who recently posted on social media that Tesla would be one of the companies creating it — Greg Brockman, seated behind OpenAI’s legal team, scribbled a note on a small piece of yellow paper and passed it to his lawyers.

The trial’s most significant new disclosure came almost in passing. Savitt asked Musk about xAI’s development — specifically whether it had used OpenAI’s technology to train its own models, a process known as distillation. Musk acknowledged it was “partly” true. xAI used OpenAI’s models to help build the company. That practice appears to violate OpenAI’s terms of service. The plaintiff’s own AI company had been training on the defendant’s technology.

And the week’s most unexpected exhibit: text messages between Musk and Mark Zuckerberg, now part of the public record. In December 2024, Zuckerberg told Musk that someone had leaked Meta’s letter to the California attorney general — a letter that supported Musk’s lawsuit against OpenAI. In February 2025, Zuckerberg praised Musk’s DOGE project and offered to have Meta teams take down content targeting Musk’s staff. Musk then asked whether Zuckerberg would be open to bidding on OpenAI’s intellectual property together. Zuckerberg wanted to discuss it live. He ultimately declined to join the $97.4 billion bid that was rebuffed by OpenAI. The picture that emerges is not a lone guardian of nonprofit principle — it is a coordinated network of tech billionaires with overlapping competitive interests in OpenAI’s dismemberment.

The Judge’s Best Lines

Before the jury was seated Thursday morning, Musk’s lead attorney Steven Molo again tried to put AI existential risk before the court. Judge Gonzalez Rogers shut it down with a ruling that may prove consequential to Musk’s case: “We are not going to get into issues of catastrophe and extinction.” When Molo kept arguing with OpenAI’s lawyer, Rogers raised her voice and delivered the line of the trial: “I suspect that there are a number of people who do not want to put the future of humanity in Mr. Musk’s hands. But we’re not going to get into that. We just are not going to have this whole thing explode for the world to view it.”

This matters strategically. Musk’s lawyers have argued throughout that his existential fears about AI prove he is protecting humanity, not harming a competitor. The judge’s ruling removes that argument from the jury’s deliberations. What remains is the contractual and financial record — which, as the cross-examination showed, is considerably less favourable to Musk than the cosmic framing.

When Musk kept slipping in Terminator references during his testimony anyway, Rogers cut it off. “They got it,” she said. “It’s enough.”

When Musk protested that Savitt was asking a leading question — which Savitt is fully entitled to do during cross-examination — the judge reminded the courtroom that Musk is “not a lawyer.” “I did take Law 101,” Musk replied. The courtroom laughed.

Rogers has now cut off Musk’s wife-beating analogy; told the courtroom to calm down; capped the Terminator testimony; and corrected Musk on the basic rules of cross-examination. Her record in Epic v. Apple — where she found Apple had “outright lied under oath” — suggests technology principals receive no special deference in her courtroom. Musk is not her first difficult witness.

Birchall at the Lectern

Jared Birchall is questioned by Robert Kry, Elon Musk’s lawyer, during Musk’s lawsuit over OpenAI for-profit conversion at a federal courthouse in Oakland, California, U.S., April 30, 2026, in a courtroom sketch. Vicki Behringer | Reuters

With Musk’s testimony concluded, the stand passed to Jared Birchall — head of Musk’s family office Excession LLC, executive at xAI, CEO of Neuralink. He is the operational shadow of the Musk organization. As Musk left the courtroom and Birchall took the stand, the two men passed each other without acknowledgment.

His testimony did not help his employer’s cause.

Musk’s lawyer Robert Kry walked Birchall through roughly sixty donations Musk made to OpenAI from 2016 to 2020 — including rent payments made for OpenAI’s San Francisco offices even after Musk had left the board. The intent was to establish Musk as OpenAI’s most significant financial contributor. Birchall also testified about the $97.4 billion bid to purchase OpenAI’s assets, framing it as an attempt to “prevent the diminishment of the value of the foundation” by establishing a market value for it.

OpenAI’s attorney Bradley Wilson focused on Musk’s donations to donor-advised funds managed by Vanguard and Fidelity. Once Musk contributed those funds, he had no legal right to direct how they were used. Birchall acknowledged he was not a lawyer and could not say otherwise.

Wilson also pressed Birchall on his belief that Altman had been negotiating “on both sides of the table” during OpenAI’s restructuring. Birchall conceded that most of what he knew came from public news sources and conversations with lawyers — not from OpenAI’s board, bankers, or leadership.

The sharpest moment came when Wilson asked Birchall whether he had coordinated the $97.4 billion bid with one of the lawyers who brought Musk’s lawsuit against OpenAI. Birchall acknowledged that he had. OpenAI’s team immediately moved to strike his bid testimony from the record — arguing that the bid was not an independent market valuation but an extension of the litigation strategy. The judge said she would decide the matter on Friday.

What Three Days on the Stand Produced

Savitt’s closing line of cross-examination had a blunt elegance. He asked Musk about OpenAI’s nonprofit initiatives — Alzheimer’s research, safety commitments, public work. Musk, who has positioned himself as guardian of OpenAI’s founding soul, replied: “I don’t know everything they’ve done. I don’t know what’s going on at OpenAI.”

The cumulative portrait Savitt built over three days is this: a plaintiff who promised a billion and gave $38 million with no written conditions; who claimed to have read a document he never mentioned in deposition; who sought majority control of the entity he says was stolen; who bid to acquire it commercially while suing to protect its nonprofit status; whose own AI company trained on the defendant’s technology; and who was texting Zuckerberg about carving up OpenAI’s intellectual property while the trial was being prepared.

None of this necessarily means Musk loses. OpenAI’s conversion from nonprofit to for-profit is a genuine governance question, and the founding mission documents are in evidence. But Savitt has made it substantially harder for the jury to see Musk as what he claims to be: a disinterested philanthropist who simply wants humanity’s interests protected.

On that foundational question, the jury will have three days to think it over. There are no proceedings Friday. Court resumes Monday morning.
