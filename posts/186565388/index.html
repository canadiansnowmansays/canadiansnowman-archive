The Asimov Gap

The Democracy Crisis

Part 10: Can Voting Survive AI?

February 6, 2026

When Algorithms Shape Elections

Chesnot | Getty Images

In November 2016, Cambridge Analytica used AI to analyze Facebook data on 87 million Americans, build psychological profiles, and target voters with personalized messages designed to manipulate their behavior.

They didn’t just advertise. They predicted individual psychological vulnerabilities, crafted messages exploiting those vulnerabilities, and delivered them when each person would be most susceptible.

It worked. Cambridge Analytica claimed credit for Trump’s victory (disputed, but their methods were real).

When exposed, everyone was shocked. Facebook apologized. Cambridge Analytica shut down. Politicians promised reform.

But here’s what most people missed: That was 2016 AI. Primitive compared to what exists now.

GPT-2 (2019) would have been science fiction in 2016. GPT-4 (2023) would have seemed like magic. Current frontier models (2025) are orders of magnitude more capable.

Now imagine Cambridge Analytica with current AI. And imagine it’s not one rogue firm—it’s standard practice for campaigns, corporations, foreign governments, anyone with resources.

That’s where we’re heading.

The AI Manipulation Toolkit

What’s possible with current AI:

Hyper-personalized persuasion: Create unique messages for each voter. Not demographics—individuals. AI analyzes your social media, browsing history, shopping patterns, location data. Builds psychological profile. Crafts message designed specifically for you.

Real-time adaptation: AI monitors your responses. Message not working? Try different approach. Keeps adapting until it finds what moves you.

Deepfake everything: Generate video of candidate saying exactly what you want to hear. Audio of opponent saying exactly what you fear. Images of rallies, protests, crimes that never happened. All photorealistic. All fake.

Micro-targeting at scale: In 2016, Cambridge Analytica targeted thousands of voter segments. Current AI could target millions of individuals. Each getting custom content. No two voters seeing the same campaign.

Automated A/B testing: AI tests thousands of variations on millions of people simultaneously. Keeps what works. Discards what doesn’t. Evolution at digital speed.

Emotional manipulation: AI identifies your fears, hopes, insecurities, grievances. Amplifies them. Makes you angry about things you weren’t angry about. All in service of political objectives you may not share.

This isn’t speculation. This is current capability.

The Three Democracy Threats

1. The Authenticity Crisis

Democracy requires shared reality. Voters disagree on policy—that’s normal. But voters need agreement on basic facts. Did candidate say X? Did event Y happen?

AI destroys shared reality. Deepfakes make seeing no longer believing. Video evidence becomes worthless when video can be faked perfectly. Photographs prove nothing when images can be generated.

Watch this Barack Obama deepfake video here

Example from 2024: Deepfake video circulated showing candidate making racist statement. Looked real. Sounded real. News organizations reported it. Millions shared it. Later revealed as fake. Retraction issued. Damage done. Voters who saw original never saw retraction.

Now imagine that hundreds of times. Every candidate. Every issue. Every day. Nobody can track what’s real.

2. The Manipulation Arms Race

Traditional campaign: Candidate makes speech. Voters hear same speech. Make decision. Voters know they’re being persuaded. Can seek alternative views.

AI campaign: You see content designed specifically for you. Nobody else sees it. You don’t know it’s targeted persuasion. You have no defense because you don’t know you’re being attacked.

AI learns your psychological weaknesses. Insecure about economic future? AI amplifies that insecurity, links it to opponent’s policies. Fear immigration? AI shows you immigrant crime stories (even if crime is falling). Feel ignored by elites? AI shows you elitist candidate moments (even if out of context).

The manipulation is invisible. Personalized. Adaptive. Undetectable.

This creates arms race. Except voters are individuals with limited resources. Campaigns are well-funded organizations with unlimited AI access. This isn’t fair fight. It’s asymmetric warfare.

3. The Oligarchic Capture

Why oligarchs win:

Cost: Building sophisticated AI manipulation systems requires billions. Only oligarchs can afford it.

Data: Effective manipulation requires massive data on voters. Only oligarchs control that data (through Meta, X, Google).

Deployment: Reaching voters requires controlling communication channels. Oligarchs own those channels.

Legal protection: Defending against legal consequences requires armies of lawyers. Only oligarchs can afford that.

Result: Democratic elections become oligarchic manipulation exercises. Not “oligarchs have influence.” Oligarchs determine outcomes by controlling the AI that shapes voter behavior.

Elon Musk is building 10 million humanoid robots by 2027, calling it his “infinite money glitch,” with no democratic oversight of technology that could displace entire workforces. The same oligarchic control extends from physical labor to democratic processes themselves.

The “Responsible” Company That Can’t Resist

Even the AI industry’s supposed conscience reveals the inexorable pull of oligarchic competition.

Anthropic—the company founded by researchers who left OpenAI over safety concerns, the firm publishing the most alarmed warnings about authoritarian AI—is now seeking hundreds of millions in funding from the United Arab Emirates and Qatar.

When questioned about taking money from autocratic regimes while simultaneously warning about the dangers of authoritarian control of AI, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei had a revealing response in an internal memo: “We never made a commitment not to seek funding from the Middle East.” He acknowledged these would be investments from what he called “dictators,” but insisted they wouldn’t have control over the company.

The justification? Competitive pressure. As Anthropic’s policy chief Jack Clark explained: “The world gets to make this decision, not companies,” and “the system of capital markets says, Go faster.”

This is what oligarchic capture looks like in practice. Not evil companies versus good companies. Just companies—all of them—subject to the same market forces, all making the same calculations, all concluding they cannot afford to slow down even when their own research suggests they should.

If Anthropic—with its published constitutions, its manifestos, its public hand-wringing about democracy and authoritarianism—cannot resist these pressures, what chance do democratic regulations have against them?

The answer: Democratic regulations work only when democratic institutions can act faster than oligarchic competition drives deployment. Right now, they can’t.

Asimov Saw This Coming

Foundation series (1942-1993): Mathematician develops “psychohistory”—using mathematics to predict and manipulate mass behavior. Elite foundation uses it to guide civilization.

Key insight: Psychohistory only works if populations don’t know they’re being manipulated. Once people know, they defend against it. Manipulation stops working.

Asimov’s lesson: Democratic consent requires transparency. Invisible manipulation is incompatible with democracy.

In a future United States where one individual is selected by computer to represent the entire national electorate in voting for the new President, ordinary Norman Muller is not sure he wants that privilege.

“Franchise” (1955): Elections decided by computer selecting single representative voter, analyzing their psychology, extrapolating to entire population. Efficient. Accurate. But it’s not democracy.

“The Evitable Conflict” (1950): Machines run economy optimally. But they’ve been subtly manipulating humans to accept machine control. Even well-intentioned manipulation destroys human agency.

All written 50-70 years ago. All describing exactly what’s happening now.

The Current Reality Check

What we know:

  • 2016: Cambridge Analytica used primitive AI for voter manipulation

  • 2020: Foreign disinformation campaigns using more sophisticated AI

  • 2024: Multiple deepfakes circulating. AI-generated campaign content everywhere. Micro-targeting standard practice

  • 2025: Current frontier AI can generate perfect deepfakes, write personalized persuasive content at scale, analyze psychological vulnerabilities in real-time

Election manipulation infrastructure exists. Being used. Getting more sophisticated.

What we don’t know: Extent of manipulation. Effectiveness. Who’s using most sophisticated tools. Foreign vs domestic manipulation.

Democracy requires informed consent. How can voters consent to manipulation they don’t know is happening?

The Optimistic Case

Democracy has survived manipulation before.

Yellow journalism (1890s): Solution—professional journalism standards, fact-checking, media literacy.

Radio propaganda (1930s): Solution—regulated broadcasting, equal time provisions, independent media.

Television advertising (1960s): Solution—disclosure requirements, campaign finance laws.

Social media disinformation (2010s): Solution (partial)—content moderation, fact-checking, algorithm transparency.

Pattern: Each new communication technology enables new manipulation. Democracy adapts. Develops defenses. Survives.

AI is latest challenge. Democracy can adapt again. How?

Path 1: Transparency Requirements

Mandate disclosure of AI use in political campaigns. If AI generates content → must be labeled. If AI targets individuals → must be disclosed. If AI creates deepfakes → must be watermarked.

Europe is moving this direction: AI Act requires transparency for AI-generated content. Penalties for deepfakes.

Path 2: Algorithmic Accountability

Require campaigns to disclose targeting criteria. Can’t micro-target without justification. Can’t use psychological manipulation without consent. Create audit trails. Independent oversight.

Path 3: Public AI Infrastructure

Don’t let oligarchs monopolize election AI. Create public AI systems for campaigns. Funded by government. Available to all candidates equally. Transparent algorithms.

Level playing field. Nobody wins by having better manipulation tools.

This is radical idea. But democracy is radical. Public education was radical once. Public broadcasting was radical. Public election financing was radical.

Path 4: Constitutional Amendments

“Algorithmic Bill of Rights” protecting citizens from invisible AI manipulation. Right to know when AI is making decisions affecting you. Right to contest automated decisions.

The Counterargument

“Voters aren’t that manipulable. Democracy is resilient. This is overblown.”

Evidence of voter resilience: Trump won 2016 despite never leading polls. Brexit won despite expert consensus. Underdog candidates win regularly. Voters resist elite consensus.

Evidence of democratic adaptation: Democracy survived printing press, radio, television, social media. Each time doomsayers predicted end. Each time democracy adapted.

But there’s crucial difference:

Previous manipulation technologies were visible. You knew you were reading newspaper. Watching TV ad. Could consciously evaluate and resist.

AI manipulation is invisible. You don’t know content is AI-generated. Don’t know you’re being targeted. Don’t know your psychological profile is being exploited. Can’t defend against what you can’t see.

Second difference: Speed. Yellow journalism took years to influence elections. Radio propaganda took months. AI manipulation works in real-time. Tests thousands of messages. Finds what works. Deploys at scale. All before voters can organize resistance.

Third difference: Oligarchic control. Previous media had many owners. AI is controlled by handful of oligarchs. Concentrated power. Democratic competition harder when game is rigged.

What Sanders Is Fighting For

In democracy context, Sanders’ concern is existential: If oligarchs control AI that manipulates elections, democracy becomes theater. Real decisions made by oligarchs. Elections ratify oligarchic preferences. Democratic forms maintained. Democratic substance lost.

This is “managed democracy”—what Russia has. Elections happen. Voters vote. Results predetermined by those controlling information flow.

US isn’t there yet. But the infrastructure is being built:

  • Oligarchs own social media platforms where voters get information

  • Same oligarchs develop AI manipulation tools

  • Same oligarchs fund political campaigns

  • Same oligarchs hire politicians after they leave office

Sanders fighting for:

  • Public ownership/oversight of election-related AI

  • Transparency requirements for campaign AI use

  • Regulations preventing psychological manipulation

  • Democratic control of AI development

Democracy can use AI. But AI must serve democratic values, not replace democratic process.

The Choice Point

Two paths forward:

Path A: Oligarchic Managed Democracy — AI manipulation becomes standard. Elections become expensive AI arms races. Winners are those with best manipulation technology. Voter choice becomes illusion.

Path B: Democratic AI Governance — Transparency requirements. Algorithmic accountability. Public AI infrastructure. Constitutional protections. AI serves democratic deliberation instead of replacing it.

Current trajectory: Path A. Default outcome if nothing changes. Oligarchs building infrastructure. Deploying tools. Normalizing manipulation.

Path B requires active choice. Organizing. Regulation. Public investment. Democratic assertion against oligarchic control.

Asimov’s Faith in Democracy

In Isaac Asimov’s Foundation, the character Hari Seldon, a psychologist, sociologist, and statistician, develops a branch of science called psychohistory. His science basically examines the actions and reactions of societal history in order to formulate a predictive model for future macro-societal events.

Foundation series ends with twist: Psychohistory becomes obsolete when people learn about it. Humans develop resistance. Make unpredictable choices. Assert democratic agency.

Asimov’s optimism: Humans aren’t perfectly manipulable. Informed populations resist invisible control. Democracy has strength oligarchs underestimate—collective agency, unpredictability, creative resistance.

This is basis for optimism: Democracy survives AI if voters understand and resist manipulation.

That requires:

  • Education about AI manipulation methods

  • Transparency about when AI is being used

  • Tools for detecting and resisting manipulation

  • Regulation preventing worst abuses

  • Democratic organization to demand accountability

All achievable. All worth fighting for.

Closing: Can Voting Survive AI?

Answer: Yes. If we fight for it.

AI doesn’t automatically destroy democracy. But AI in oligarchic hands, deployed without transparency, optimized for manipulation, could hollow out democratic substance while maintaining democratic forms.

The crisis: Not whether voting continues, but whether voting means anything when AI determines outcomes.

Sanders is right to warn. Hinton is right to worry. This is urgent.

But urgency isn’t hopelessness. Democracy has tools:

  • Regulation

  • Transparency

  • Public investment

  • Constitutional protection

  • Democratic organizing

Use them. Before voting becomes algorithm-optimized theater.

The democracy crisis isn’t about whether voting survives AI. It’s about whether democracy survives AI.
