Insatiable

Essay #20: What AI’s Energy Crisis Smells Like on the Ground

March 13, 2026

Colossus, Elon Musk’s data center in Memphis, can consume as much electricity over the course of a year as 200,000 American homes. (Landon Speers for The Atlantic)

Last week, in Essay #19, I told you about a ceremony at the White House. Tech CEOs in suits, signing a voluntary pledge, cameras rolling. The political story. The numbers story. The $47.2 billion in PJM capacity costs, the eleven states filing moratorium bills, the 6.9 percent price increase landing on ratepayers while the companies driving the demand got discounts.

Today I want to tell you what it smells like.

Matteo Wong, writing in The Atlantic’s April 2026 issue, drove through southwest Memphis with KeShaun Pearson, who directs a local nonprofit called Memphis Community Against Pollution. Wong kept his window down because, as Pearson told him, their destination was best tasted, not viewed. They passed an abandoned coal plant, then an active gas plant, then a forest of electrical towers — and then, from behind a patch of trees, a white-walled hangar bigger than a dozen football fields.

This is Colossus. Elon Musk’s data center. The facility where xAI trains Grok. And the air around it smelled of soot, gasoline, and asphalt, with something else — a tickle sliding up Wong’s nostrils and down into his throat, like the onset of a cold.

Welcome to the physical reality of the AI revolution. It has a taste.

The Neighbourhood That Nobody Told

Colossus sits in Boxtown, Memphis. The neighbourhood is named for the railway boxcars that formerly enslaved people used to build homes. It is still almost entirely Black. Virtually every heavy industry in Memphis has set up nearby — a wastewater facility, an oil refinery, a coal-fired power plant. Colossus itself occupies the hull of an old oven factory, next to a steel mill and a trucking yard. Life expectancy in and around Boxtown is more than five years below the national average. Cancer risk is four times higher.

Into this neighbourhood, xAI dropped one of the most energy-intensive facilities on earth. Construction began in May 2024. The project was announced the following month. Most residents and elected officials found out that day. By Labour Day weekend — less than three months later — Colossus was running.

To get it operational fast, xAI built its own power plant: up to 35 natural-gas turbines, railcar-size engines that are major sources of smog, installed without an air quality permit. The company argued it didn’t need one because the turbines would operate for less than a year. The Southern Environmental Law Center, representing the NAACP, threatened to sue. Researchers at the University of Tennessee found that nitrogen dioxide levels near Colossus have been substantially elevated since its announcement.

In Boxtown, a neighborhood in southwest Memphis, many residents and elected officials were unaware that Colossus was being built until the project was well under way. (Landon Speers for The Atlantic)

Behind her one-story brick home, a resident named Sarah Gladney grows tomatoes. When the vines wilted early last summer, she had a suspect in mind. One of her neighbours told Wong the turbines have made her uncertain about whether she should let her grandchildren visit.

I’ve spent 19 essays in this series talking about gigawatts and capacity auctions and policy frameworks. This is what those numbers look like when they land on someone’s tomato garden.

To be fair to xAI: the company says it will install technology to reduce turbine emissions and plans to recycle greywater to ease aquifer strain. Perhaps it will. But promises of mitigation after the fact are not the same as consultation before it. And Boxtown is not unique — it is emblematic. Similar siting battles are playing out in Virginia’s Data Centre Alley, in Georgia, in Michigan, in every community where a hyperscaler arrives faster than the local governance structure can respond. I’m using Memphis as the opening because Wong’s reporting makes it the most vivid case. But the pattern is industry-wide.

The Numbers Behind the Smell

Let me give you the scale, because Wong’s reporting puts it in terms this series hasn’t used before.

Run Colossus at full strength for a year and it consumes as much electricity as 200,000 American homes. When fully operational, Colossus and two other xAI data centres nearby will require nearly two gigawatts — roughly twice the electricity consumption of Seattle. And Musk’s operation is not the outlier. It’s the template.

Amazon and Meta are each building data centres in Indiana and Louisiana requiring more than two gigawatts apiece. OpenAI has announced plans for facilities requiring more than 30 gigawatts of power in total — more than the largest recorded demand for all of New England. Since ChatGPT launched in November 2022, the capital expenditures of Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, and Google have exceeded $600 billion, much of it on data centres. Adjusted for inflation, that is more than the United States spent to build the entire interstate highway system.

Read that again. The AI industry has spent more on data centres in three years than America spent building 48,000 miles of highway.

Jesse Jenkins, a climate modeller at Princeton, told The Atlantic that these are “the largest single points of consumption of electricity in history.” The International Energy Agency projects that by 2030, U.S. data centres will consume more electricity than all of the country’s heavy industries combined — more than cement, steel, chemical, automotive, and every other industrial facility put together. Roughly half of that demand will come from generative AI specifically.

Conservative forecasts say the tech industry will drop the equivalent of 40 Seattles onto America’s grid within a decade. Aggressive scenarios predict more than 60 in half that time.

From Toronto, I keep trying to find the right analogy for what’s happening, and I keep failing, because there is no precedent. This isn’t the industrial revolution playing out over decades. This is the industrial revolution compressed into a few years, concentrated in a few hundred facilities, financed by a few dozen companies, and landing disproportionately on communities that were already bearing more than their share.

Gas Now, Nuclear Later (Trust Us)

Three Mile Island’s cooling towers have until recently served as grave markers for America’s nuclear-power industry.

Here is the part of Wong’s reporting that should trouble anyone who has followed this series.

The industry knows what clean energy looks like. Microsoft is paying $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island’s Unit One — the undamaged reactor that operated safely for decades after the 1979 partial meltdown at Unit Two. Google, Amazon, and Meta are buying nuclear power. Nearly every major data centre company is investing in small modular reactors. Google is even exploring building a data centre in space.

And yet. When asked where the energy for data centres should come from, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has repeatedly said the same thing: “Short-term: natural gas.”

That short-term answer is becoming the long-term reality. Chevron and Exxon are angling to hook natural-gas facilities directly into data centres. All three major gas turbine manufacturers now advertise their products specifically for data centre use. A Louisiana utility is building three gas plants for a single Meta facility. Coal plant lifespans are being extended. xAI’s second Memphis facility, Colossus II, has filed to operate 41 additional gas turbines on a former Duke Energy site — turbines that could emit more carbon dioxide annually than the city of San Jose.

The IEA estimates that data centre emissions could more than double by 2030, making them one of the fastest-growing sources of greenhouse gases in the world.

The industry’s position, as Jenkins summarized it for The Atlantic, is: “The market has converged on ‘Add gas now, and then add nuclear later.’” The question nobody can answer is when “later” arrives. Three Mile Island’s restart countdown clock reads 650 days. Small modular reactors remain experimental. Meanwhile, gas turbines are so back-ordered that acquiring one in the next few years will be virtually impossible — which tells you everything about the trajectory.

Wong has been building toward this story for over a year. In September 2024, he wrote about Microsoft’s deal to restart Three Mile Island — the ultimate bellwether of the AI age: clean nuclear power finally getting its moment, not to decarbonize the existing grid but to offset new emissions from a single company. And the deeper question is why nuclear always remains “later.” Jonathan Rauch, also writing in The Atlantic, documented the answer in devastating detail back in 2023: it’s not environmentalists blocking nuclear. It’s the nuclear industry itself. The two big reactors under construction in Georgia were licensed in 2008, broke ground in 2012 with a $14 billion price tag and a 2017 completion date. The cost doubled to $30 billion. The company building them, Westinghouse, went bankrupt. France started a new reactor in 2007 planning to open it in five; it still wasn’t ready when Rauch wrote sixteen years later.

The innovators exist. Kairos Power is building molten-salt reactors using SpaceX’s “build a little, test a little, fix a little” methodology. NuScale, X-energy, Ultra Safe Nuclear — nearly 150 companies worldwide are working on small advanced reactors. And to be fair, the momentum is real and accelerating — Microsoft’s Three Mile Island deal represents genuine capital commitment, not just a press release, and Google, Amazon, and Meta are all pursuing nuclear power purchase agreements. But as Kairos co-founder Mike Laufer told Rauch: “Nuclear as it exists today is clean, it’s reliable, it’s safe. But it’s not affordable.” Not one of the SMR companies has delivered a commercial product at scale. And so the industry’s promise — gas now, nuclear later — keeps compounding the same way. The gas arrives on schedule. The nuclear doesn’t.

And here’s the geopolitical kicker that should keep American policymakers up at night: China’s energy advantage in the AI race comes not from coal and oil but from nuclear reactors and solar panels. China is building nearly two-thirds of the world’s new solar and wind capacity. In 2024, it produced nearly as much electricity as the U.S., Europe, and India combined. The country America is supposedly racing against is winning with clean energy while America defaults to gas.

I covered this dynamic in Essay #18 — Washington handing out coal trophies while the world builds the future. Wong’s reporting confirms it from the ground up.

Data Centre Alley: A Glimpse of Everywhere

If Memphis shows you the environmental justice cost, Loudoun County, Virginia shows you the sheer physical saturation of what’s coming.

Wong drove through Data Centre Alley with Julie Bolthouse of the Piedmont Environmental Council, who taught him the visual grammar: cooling towers on the roof, dozens of backup diesel generators to one side, no windows — or false ones, decorative glass panels backed by a wall of concrete. Wong gave up counting data centres within minutes, unable to tell where one ended and the next began.

Loudoun County now hosts 199 data centres with approximately 30 more under construction. According to one report, 13 percent of global data centre capacity is squeezed into 520 square miles. Construction north of Dulles International Airport has created hills of freshly dug dirt looming over roads, with orange dust tinting the air. Within a 1.5-mile radius of the economic development office, there are 12 electrical substations. High-voltage transmission lines hang over the county like a cobweb.

Transmission lines, like these in Memphis, carry electricity throughout the grid—including to data centers. (Landon Speers for The Atlantic)

This is what Loudoun’s longtime economic development director, Buddy Rizer, built — courting data centres with tax incentives for nearly two decades. They provide almost 40 percent of the county’s budget. But Loudoun is running out of space, and the model is spreading. Phoenix. Atlanta. Dallas. Indiana. Louisiana. OpenAI has proposed that the U.S. establish “AI Economic Zones” — little Loudouns, as Wong puts it, everywhere.

For readers of this series, that phrase should chill you. Because we’ve now documented what Loudoun looks like: the moratorium wave from Essay #19 is the political reaction to communities watching their landscape transform into an industrial zone that employs a few dozen people per facility while consuming electricity at a rate that dwarfs residential demand.

The Bubble Question (Because Honesty Demands It)

Throughout this series, I’ve treated the AI energy crisis as real, accelerating, and likely to intensify. I stand by that assessment. But intellectual honesty demands I acknowledge what Wong surfaces in The Atlantic: this has happened before, and it ended badly.

In 1999, as fibre-optic cables were being laid in Loudoun County, Forbes ran a headline that now reads like prophecy: “Dig More Coal — The PCs Are Coming.” Energy companies built gas and coal plants in anticipation of a demand surge that never materialized. The result was a glut of surplus plants and multiple bankrupt energy companies.

The generative AI boom could prove to be a bubble. The technology remains extraordinarily expensive, largely because of the cost of advanced computer chips, and no AI firm has yet presented a convincing business model. More efficient algorithms could reduce the need for new power plants. And if AI doesn’t prove as transformative as predicted, swaths of data centres could be left unused or unfinished — ruins from a future that never arrived.

But here’s what makes the bubble scenario almost as troubling as the non-bubble scenario: the gas plants get built either way. The permits get issued either way. The emissions happen either way. Sarah Gladney’s tomatoes wilt either way. Communities absorb the infrastructure costs and the health costs regardless of whether Grok 5 achieves AGI or quietly disappoints.

The risk isn’t symmetrical. If AI succeeds, America has an energy crisis. If AI fails, America has stranded assets and communities stuck with industrial infrastructure they never asked for. Neither outcome is good for Boxtown.

Indoor Rain

I want to close with an image from Wong’s reporting that I haven’t been able to shake.

When a data centre’s cooling equipment malfunctions, spiraling heat combined with humid air can produce what he describes as “that rarest of meteorological events: indoor rain.” The machines generate so much heat that it literally rains inside the building.

There’s something almost too on-the-nose about this as a metaphor. The AI industry is generating so much heat — literal and political — that the weather is changing. Inside the data centres, yes. But also inside PJM’s capacity markets. Inside state legislatures filing moratorium bills. Inside utility bills arriving in 65 million mailboxes. Inside the lungs of a grandmother in Boxtown wondering whether her grandchildren should visit.

In 18 previous essays, I documented the technologies that could solve this: solar, wind, nuclear, fusion, batteries, space-based solutions. In Essay #19, I documented the political failure to deploy them coherently. Today, thanks to Matteo Wong’s extraordinary reporting, I’m documenting what that failure smells like on the ground.

It smells like soot, gasoline, and asphalt. With a tickle that slides down your throat like the onset of a cold.

The AI revolution promised us the future. What it’s delivering, for now, is natural gas turbines in Black neighbourhoods, diesel generators behind false windows, and indoor rain.

The industry says nuclear is coming. Clean energy is coming. Patience.

But patience is easy to counsel from a White House signing ceremony or a Silicon Valley boardroom. It is harder to counsel from a tomato garden in Boxtown, Memphis. And it is impossible to counsel from Canada, where we watch the richest industry in human history choose the dirtiest path to the future because it is three months faster.

This essay draws extensively on Matteo Wong’s reporting in The Atlantic, “Inside the Dirty, Dystopian World of AI Data Centers” (March 13, 2026) and “For Now, There’s Only One Good Way to Power AI” (September 21, 2024), as well as Jonathan Rauch’s “The Real Obstacle to Nuclear Power” (February 7, 2023). I commend all three pieces to every reader of this series.
