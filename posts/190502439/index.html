The Quiet War

“What America Is Not Building”

Part V: The CHIPS Act Rifle Versus the Arsenal. The Accountability Essay.

April 7, 2026


I. The Inventory

Let us conduct a simple inventory of the strategic competition between the United States and China, sector by sector, as it stands in March 2026.

Semiconductors: The CHIPS and Science Act appropriated $52.7 billion. It has catalysed over $640 billion in private investment. The 25 percent investment tax credit expired. China’s 15th Five-Year Plan commits to “extraordinary measures” for semiconductor self-reliance, with an estimated $70 billion in incentive funds targeting domestic firms. The United States simultaneously sold China one million Nvidia H200 chips — increasing China’s installed AI compute by an estimated 250 percent compared to relying solely on domestic chips.

Rare earths: China processes 90 percent of the world’s supply and manufactures 94 percent of permanent magnets. The Pentagon’s DFARS 2027 deadline bans Chinese-sourced rare earths from defence procurement. Non-Chinese processing capacity at defence-relevant scale does not yet exist. The United States is consuming rare-earth-dependent interceptors in the Iran war at rates that exceed annual production capacity.

Humanoid robotics: China has over 150 companies, $20 billion in state subsidies, 54 percent of global industrial robot installations, and robots deploying into factory production lines at $16,000 per unit. The United States has a handful of well-funded startups competing for venture capital returns.

Quantum communication: China operates the world’s only long-distance quantum communication network, has launched multiple quantum satellites, and demonstrated a 12,900-kilometre intercontinental quantum link. The United States has no operational quantum communication satellite and no deployed quantum network.

Artificial intelligence: China’s AI+ action plan targets 70 percent AI penetration across the economy by 2027 and 90 percent by 2030. DeepSeek built frontier-competitive models on restricted hardware. The Bank of China pledged $137 billion over five years for the AI supply chain. The United States leads in foundational AI research but has no equivalent economy-wide integration mandate.

6G telecommunications: Named as a Five-Year Plan priority. No equivalent US programme at comparable scale.

Brain-machine interfaces: Named as a Five-Year Plan priority. US research exists primarily in private-sector initiatives without coordinated federal investment at the Chinese level.

Nuclear fusion: Named as a Five-Year Plan priority with specific timelines. US fusion investment exists but lacks the coordinated state-directed acceleration the Five-Year Plan provides.

The pattern is consistent across every sector. China has a coordinated plan covering the full technology stack. The United States has a single major legislative response — the CHIPS Act — covering a single sector, and a patchwork of agency-level programmes across others. This is the rifle and the arsenal.

II. The CHIPS Act: What It Is and What It Is Not

The CHIPS Act allocated $52 billion to rebuild American semiconductor manufacturing. The rifle has been loaded. The arsenal — quantum networks, robotics, battery supply chains — remains unbuilt.

The CHIPS and Science Act deserves genuine credit. It represents the first significant American industrial policy in a generation. It broke the bipartisan consensus against government direction of private investment. It has produced real results: new fabrication facilities, hundreds of thousands of jobs, and a meaningful increase in domestic semiconductor manufacturing capacity.

It is also insufficient by design. The Act was conceived as a semiconductor response to a semiconductor problem. It was not designed to address rare earth processing, humanoid robotics, quantum communications, AI integration across the economy, 6G infrastructure, or any of the other sectors the Five-Year Plan covers simultaneously.

The Science portion of the Act — which was intended to authorise broader research investment — has been substantially unfunded. The original legislation authorised significant new spending on research and development across multiple agencies. The appropriations have not matched the authorisations. The gap between what the CHIPS and Science Act promised and what it has delivered outside of semiconductors is the gap between an industrial policy and a procurement programme.

There is no American equivalent of the Five-Year Plan. This is not a criticism — the American system is not designed to produce one. The separation of powers, the annual appropriations process, the two- and four-year electoral cycles, the constitutional limits on executive authority over private enterprise — all of these are features of a democratic system that has produced extraordinary innovation and wealth. They are also structural impediments to the kind of coordinated, multi-sector, multi-decade industrial mobilisation that the Five-Year Plan represents.

The United States cannot and should not replicate China’s model. But it must contend with the consequences of competing against it with structurally different tools. The CHIPS Act is a rifle — precise, powerful, effective within its range. It does not cover the range of the competition.

III. The H200 Contradiction

The world's most advanced semiconductors are still made in Taiwan. The CHIPS Act is closing that gap — but closing a gap is not the same as building an arsenal TSMC/Reuters

In December 2025, Trump announced the sale of Nvidia H200 chips to China. The Commerce Department codified it in a January 2026 regulation. The Council on Foreign Relations described the policy as “strategically incoherent.”

The incoherence is worth pausing on. The United States spent years constructing an elaborate export control architecture — chip restrictions, entity lists, foreign direct product rules — designed to slow Chinese AI development by limiting access to advanced computing hardware. This architecture imposed significant costs on American companies, strained relations with allies who were pressured to adopt similar restrictions, and became a centrepiece of the bilateral technology competition.

Then the United States sold China the chips.

One million H200 units would increase China’s total installed AI compute by an estimated 250 percent compared to relying solely on domestic chips. The export controls did not prevent Chinese innovation — DeepSeek demonstrated frontier-competitive AI development despite chip restrictions, triggering a global technology sell-off and proving that constraints on hardware could be partially offset by software ingenuity. But the controls did impose real costs and real delays. Reversing them while the Five-Year Plan’s AI+ action plan was being finalised sent a signal that the United States was not serious about the technology competition it had initiated.

The H200 sale is not an isolated policy failure. It is symptomatic of a strategic competition being conducted without strategic coherence — where different agencies, different political priorities, and different economic interests produce actions that contradict each other in real time.

The Five-Year Plan does not contain contradictions of this kind. It cannot. The document is reviewed, reconciled, and approved as a unified directive before it is published. Ministerial budgets are aligned to it. Provincial targets are derived from it. State-owned enterprises adjust their capital plans accordingly. The system that produces the plan has many flaws — rigidity, information suppression, political distortion — but internal contradiction is not among them.

The FY2026 supplemental funding request prioritises interceptors and munitions. Quantum networks and advanced manufacturing do not appear as line items.

IV. The Attention Deficit

There is a cost to the Iran war that does not appear on any budget line: the consumption of political attention.

Congressional hearings are dominated by the war. Media coverage is dominated by the war. The president’s public communications are dominated by the war. The policy bandwidth of the executive branch — the finite number of hours that senior officials can devote to any issue — is consumed by operational decisions, supplemental funding negotiations, and crisis management.

During the same week that China published the 15th Five-Year Plan, the United States Congress was debating the first supplemental funding request for Operation Epic Fury. One document reshapes the technological foundations of the world’s second-largest economy for the next fifteen years. The other pays for one more week of Tomahawk missiles.

This is not an argument that the Iran war is unnecessary or that its costs are unjustified. Reasonable people can disagree about the strategic merits of Operation Epic Fury. This is an observation that the war is consuming the one resource that cannot be supplemented: the attention of the people and institutions that would otherwise be formulating the American response to the 141 pages.

Nobody in the White House is reading the Five-Year Plan this week. Nobody in Congress is holding hearings on the quantum communication gap. Nobody on cable news is explaining that the rare earth chokehold is tightening faster because of the interceptor burn rate. The war is not just costing money. It is costing the cognitive bandwidth required to recognise and respond to the strategic competition that will matter more than the war itself.

The 141 pages were published while the bombs were falling. The timing, as Part I of this series argued, was not an accident. The bombs provide the distraction. The 141 pages provide the strategy. Both are proceeding according to plan — but only one of them is China’s.

Executive orders are not Five-Year Plans. Statements of intent require congressional action to become investments. Congressional attention is currently elsewhere.

V. The Question This Series Has Been Building Toward

What is the United States building?

It is building interceptors — at rates that cannot match consumption. It is building semiconductor fabs — in one sector of a multi-sector competition. It is building AI models — that it then sells to its competitor. It is researching quantum computing — without deploying quantum communication networks. It is studying the rare earth problem — while consuming rare-earth-dependent weapons at historic rates.

What is China building?

Everything. Simultaneously. On a fifteen-year timeline. With a 141-page plan that translates into ministerial budgets this spring, provincial targets by summer, and state-owned enterprise capital plans by autumn.

The question is not whether the United States can compete with China in any individual technology. It can — American innovation remains extraordinary. The question is whether a country that responds to a multi-sector, multi-decade strategic challenge with a single-sector legislative instrument and a foreign policy that consumes its attention, its budget, and its industrial output in a Middle Eastern war can sustain that competition over time.

The CHIPS Act is a rifle. It is a good rifle. But you do not bring a rifle to a theatre-wide campaign.
