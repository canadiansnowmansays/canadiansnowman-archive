The Maze

Introduction: How Big Money Ate American Democracy

April 14, 2026

Stephen Gordon Apr 13, 2026

There is a video game that most people of a certain age have played, or at least watched someone play, in an arcade or a basement or on a phone screen during a flight. The character is a yellow disc with a mouth. The maze is full of dots. The character moves through the maze eating the dots, eating everything in its path, pursued by ghosts it cannot quite outrun. When it dies, the maze resets. The dots reappear. The character starts again, from the same position, eating the same dots, in the same maze, indefinitely.

I have been thinking about this game while following American campaign finance law.

The maze is the American political system. The dots are reform efforts — the Tillman Act of 1907, the Federal Election Campaign Act of 1974, the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002. The character eating them is not a cartoon. It is money: concentrated, patient, legally innovative, and completely indifferent to the intentions of the people who built the maze. Every time reformers close a loophole, the money finds a new path. Every time a law passes, a court case follows. Every time a court case closes one door, two more open. The maze resets. The dots disappear.

This series is about how that happened, and who made it happen, and why it keeps happening, and what — if anything — can be done about it.

It is called Super PACman. The “PAC” is an acronym: Political Action Committee, the legal vehicle through which organized money has moved through American elections since 1943. The “Super” arrived in 2010, when the Supreme Court’s Citizens United decision and a follow-on ruling from a lower court created a new vehicle — the super PAC — that could accept unlimited contributions from any source, spend unlimited amounts, and operate with a degree of transparency that makes “minimal” seem generous. The “man” is the game. The game never ends.

I am writing this from Toronto. I want to name that at the outset, because it matters to what follows. I am not an American. I have no vote in this system. I have no personal stake in any particular electoral outcome, and no party allegiance that I am trying to dress up as analysis. What I have is a ringside seat, a newspaper archive, and the particular clarity that sometimes comes from watching a problem from a distance close enough to see its details but far enough to see its shape.

The shape is this: the United States has spent more than a century attempting to prevent concentrated wealth from purchasing political outcomes, and has failed, systematically and repeatedly, not because the goal was wrong but because the tools were always weaker than the problem. Every significant reform in American campaign finance history has been followed by a legal challenge, and most of those legal challenges have succeeded, because the courts — particularly since 1976 — have chosen to interpret political spending as protected speech, which means that limiting it requires justifications the law has rarely been willing to provide.

The result is a system that calls itself democratic and functions, increasingly, as something else: a mechanism by which the preferences of a very small number of very wealthy people are amplified to a volume that drowns out everyone else. In the 2022 midterm elections, twenty-one donor families contributed $783 million to political causes. The millions of ordinary Americans who gave small amounts to candidates they believed in were outspent, collectively, by those twenty-one families. This is not a rumour. It is a documented fact, and it is the logical outcome of decisions made over decades by legislators, judges, and strategists who understood exactly what they were building.

Super PACman is the story of those decisions. It proceeds in ten parts.

Part I begins in 1976, with a Supreme Court decision that declared money to be speech and thereby made everything that followed not only possible but, in a sense, inevitable. Part X ends with the question that the history earns: what does reform look like when it actually works, and what would it take to build it in the country that needs it most?

In between, we will meet the union organizer who accidentally invented the political action committee. The senator who spent a decade trying to repair the damage from a scandal that had his name on it. The five justices who decided a film about Hillary Clinton was the appropriate occasion for dismantling a century of campaign finance law. The billionaires who understood immediately what that decision meant and moved faster than any regulatory response could follow.

We will also meet the people who have tried to stop this — the reformers, the litigators, the public financing advocates, the constitutional amendment campaigners — because their story is part of the record too, and because understanding what has been attempted is necessary to understanding what might still be possible.

The maze is real. The money is real. The dots keep disappearing.

Let’s follow the money.
