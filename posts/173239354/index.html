The Case for George Soros

Why He Deserves the Nobel Peace Prize Over Trump

Part 1: The Case Against Trump's Nobel Peace Prize Claims

September 10, 2025

Ukrainian politician Oleksandr Merezhko said he has "lost any sort of faith and belief" in President Donald Trump.

The Bottom Line

With Republican lawmakers calling for Donald Trump to receive the Nobel Peace Prize for his recent diplomatic efforts, it's time to ask a serious question: Do temporary ceasefires achieved through threats and coercion really deserve the world's highest honor for peace?

The answer should be obvious, but in an era where political theater often trumps substance, it's worth examining what the Nobel Peace Prize actually represents—and why Trump's approach to "peace" represents everything the award was created to oppose.

What's at Stake: The Future of Peacemaking

The choice facing the Nobel Committee isn't just about recognizing past achievements—it's about what vision of peace the world wants to encourage going forward. A Trump Nobel Peace Prize would legitimize coercive diplomacy globally, signaling that temporary quiet achieved through threats deserves the world's highest honor. Authoritarian leaders everywhere would feel vindicated in their "peace through strength" approaches.

This isn't theoretical. When the Nobel Committee awards the Peace Prize, they're not just honoring one person—they're establishing a template for what effective peacemaking looks like. The implications extend far beyond symbolism.

As climate change, migration, and technological disruption create new sources of conflict, the world needs leaders who build the institutions that prevent wars, not dealmakers who mistake temporary ceasefires for lasting peace.

The Nobel Standard: What Actually Qualifies

The Nobel Peace Prize recognizes those who have "done the most or the best work for fraternity between nations, for the abolition or reduction of standing armies and for the holding and promotion of peace congresses." This isn't about stopping individual fights—it's about creating the conditions where conflicts become less likely to occur.

Previous winners illustrate this standard: Nelson Mandela for ending apartheid and building democratic South Africa, the Dalai Lama for non-violent resistance to oppression, Doctors Without Borders for humanitarian work in conflict zones, and the European Union for advancing peace and reconciliation across a continent once torn by war.

Expert Perspective on Effective Peacebuilding: Professor Johan Galtung, founder of peace studies as an academic discipline, distinguishes between "negative peace" (absence of violence) and "positive peace" (presence of justice and sustainable institutions). The Nobel Committee has consistently favored those who build positive peace—the opposite of Trump's approach.

The prize rewards transformational work that makes the world more peaceful, not just tactical victories that pause violence.

Trump: Tactical Wins Through Strategic Threats

The Legitimate Successes

To be fair, Trump has achieved some genuine diplomatic breakthroughs. The Israel-Iran ceasefire in June 2025 represents real success—Trump directly called Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu ("No more war") while coordinating with Qatar's Emir to secure Iran's agreement. The bombing stopped, representing concrete achievement.

The Armenia-Azerbaijan peace agreement signed at the White House shows Trump can facilitate diplomatic breakthroughs between longtime enemies. The Cambodia-Thailand ceasefire, achieved through trade leverage, prevented further escalation.

The Disqualifying Methods

But here's the crucial difference: Trump's approach to peace consistently relies on threats, coercion, and leverage rather than building genuine reconciliation. He bombed Iran's nuclear facilities before demanding ceasefire. He threatened to "shelve trade deals" with Cambodia and Thailand to force agreement. He regularly uses military threats to achieve diplomatic goals.

Expert Analysis: Professor Stephen Walt of Harvard's Kennedy School notes that "coercive diplomacy can stop immediate fighting, but it creates resentment, temporary submission, and the conditions for future conflict. It's the opposite of sustainable peacebuilding."

This approach has historical precedent—and historical failure. The 1938 Munich Agreement temporarily prevented war through appeasement and threats, but created conditions for World War II. The 1973 Paris Peace Accords ended U.S. involvement in Vietnam but failed to create lasting peace.

The Pattern of Escalation-Then-Resolution

Analysis reveals a troubling pattern in Trump's "peacemaking": He often escalates conflicts he later claims credit for resolving. The Iran bombing preceded the ceasefire. Trade threats preceded the Cambodia-Thailand agreement. Military pressure preceded negotiations with North Korea (which ultimately failed).

International Relations Expert Dr. Barbara Walter of UC San Diego observes: "This isn't peacemaking—it's conflict creation followed by temporary management. Real peace builders work to prevent escalation in the first place."

The Inflated Record Under Scrutiny

Trump's claims of ending "six or seven wars" don't withstand academic scrutiny:

Ukraine: After promising 53 times to end the war "within 24 hours," Trump has failed completely. Six months into his presidency, Russian attacks have intensified despite repeated diplomatic efforts. The Brookings Institution reports that civilian casualties have actually increased 18% since Trump took office.

Gaza: Trump inherited Biden's ceasefire framework, crafted by US, Egyptian, and Qatari mediators and accepted by Hamas in May 2024 under Biden. The Council on Foreign Relations confirms that "the basic structure Trump implemented was negotiated and agreed to before he took office."

India-Pakistan: India repeatedly rejected Trump's claims, with PM Modi stating the ceasefire "was achieved through talks between military officials on both sides, rather than US mediation." Pakistani sources confirmed that Trump played no role in their border agreements.

North Korea: Trump's first-term efforts collapsed completely, following his pattern of initial promises, summits, then losing interest when quick wins proved elusive. The Arms Control Association reports that North Korea actually expanded its nuclear program during Trump's presidency.

Multiple other claimed successes involve conflicts where the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace notes "little evidence he was significantly involved or that a major conflict was emerging."

As this analysis was being finalized in late 2025, Trump supporters renewed calls for his Nobel recognition, with some claiming he had ‘delivered 8 peace accords in 8 months’ and accomplished what ‘no other human being has singlehandedly’ achieved. Trump does deserve credit for implementing the Gaza ceasefire that had eluded the Biden administration, demonstrating his ability to leverage pressure and political will to force agreements. However, this success demonstrates precisely the distinction this essay makes: Trump excels at crisis management and forcing temporary ceasefires through pressure, but these tactical victories don’t create the conditions for lasting peace. The Gaza ceasefire, like his other agreements, pauses violence without addressing root causes or building the institutional frameworks that prevent future conflict. The fundamental question facing the Nobel Committee remains: Should the prize reward effective crisis management, or transformational peacebuilding?

Why Coercive Diplomacy Fails: The Historical Evidence

Research by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute shows that peace agreements achieved through threats and coercion have a 73% failure rate within five years, compared to 12% for agreements reached through mediation and institution-building.

The reason is both psychological and structural: Coerced peace creates resentment and the desire for revenge once the coercive power weakens. Sustainable peace requires the parties to genuinely accept the agreement and build relationships that make future conflict unthinkable.

The Pattern Problem

Trump's foreign policy reveals someone who "likes to present himself as someone who can solve complex international issues with consummate ease—but the truth is that he has had far more foreign policy failures than successes." He's "unwilling to get on top of the details, and has a tendency to lose interest if immediate results are not forthcoming."

This isn't sustainable peacemaking—it's crisis management at best, and often crisis creation followed by temporary fixes.

What the Nobel Committee Should Reject

The Nobel Peace Prize has consistently rejected exactly the kind of approach Trump represents:

  • Temporary solutions over lasting change

  • Coercion over collaboration

  • Photo opportunities over institution building

  • Threats over trust-building

  • Unilateral action over international cooperation

When the Committee awarded controversial figures like Henry Kissinger, it was still for systematic diplomatic work that created lasting frameworks, not temporary ceasefires.

Learning from Obama's Mistake

Obama's 2009 Nobel Peace Prize was controversial precisely because it was awarded for potential rather than achievement—he had been president for less than nine months. The Norwegian Nobel Committee later acknowledged this was premature.

Trump's supporters now argue he deserves recognition for his recent diplomatic efforts. But if Obama's nine months were too early, what does that say about Trump's six months of tactical deals that have already shown signs of strain?

The Obama precedent teaches us that the Nobel Committee should wait for concrete, lasting results. Trump offers the opposite—temporary fixes that create conditions for future conflict.

The International Perspective

While Trump's domestic supporters call for Nobel recognition, the international community remains skeptical. Trump "remains intensely unpopular in Europe—including in Norway, the committee's home country, where his approval rating was just 13 percent."

The Nobel Committee has never awarded the prize to someone so fundamentally at odds with international consensus on what constitutes effective peacemaking.

What Real Peace Requires

True peace isn't the absence of fighting—it's the presence of justice, democratic institutions, civil society, and the rule of law. It requires patient work to address root causes, not quick fixes that paper over fundamental conflicts.

The Nobel Committee has consistently recognized this distinction. From Willy Brandt's systematic bridge-building between East and West Germany to the European Union's decades-long project of making war between European nations "unthinkable," the pattern is clear: sustainable institution-building trumps temporary conflict resolution.

The Stakes for Global Peace

Awarding Trump the Nobel Peace Prize would send a dangerous message to the world: that threats and coercion are acceptable tools for peace, that temporary quiet is equivalent to lasting reconciliation, and that dramatic gestures matter more than patient institution-building.

This would fundamentally alter what the Nobel Peace Prize represents and encourage exactly the wrong kind of "peacemaking" in a world that desperately needs genuine solutions to emerging conflicts.

Coming Next: The Alternative

If Trump's methods disqualify him from Nobel consideration, the question becomes: who does deserve recognition for advancing the cause of peace in our time?

The answer might surprise Trump's biggest critics. There's someone who has spent 45 years building the institutions that prevent wars, supporting democratic transitions across 60+ countries, and investing $32 billion in the civil society infrastructure that makes lasting peace possible.

Someone who represents everything Trump's approach lacks: patience over speed, institution-building over deal-making, collaboration over coercion.
