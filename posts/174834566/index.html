Inside Trump’s Government Mob

Episode 4: The Comey Hit - When the Don Finally Orders the Contract on His Most Dangerous Snitch

September 29, 2025

Satirical image from Charlie Angus’s reporting on Trump’s authoritarian governance—the medals may be fictional, but the protection racket is real.

By CanadianSnowmanSays | September 29, 2025

Bottom Line Up Front: On September 25, 2025, former FBI Director James Comey was indicted on charges of lying to Congress—just 48 hours after Trump demanded it at an Ohio rally. The charges are weak, the timing is blatant, and career prosecutors objected. But this isn’t about justice. This is the ultimate enforcer contract: eliminating the witness who documented Trump’s 2017 loyalty demands, who warned Trump would weaponize the DOJ, and who threatened to expose the Russian money trail that built Trump’s empire. Every warning Comey issued is now being proven correct—through his own persecution.

The Hit Order

September 23, 2025. Trump’s rally in Ohio. The crowd roaring, the don comfortable at his podium. And then the order, delivered with characteristic bluntness: “Pam, go after Comey now!”

Two days later, a federal grand jury in Alexandria, Virginia indicted James Comey. Two counts. Two pages. Career prosecutors at DOJ had called the case “exceptionally weak” and memoed their objections. The grand jury rejected a proposed third count. But Attorney General Pam Bondi—who received Trump campaign donations when she was Florida AG—and Deputy AG Todd Blanche—Trump’s former personal attorney—pushed it through anyway.

This is how the mob completes a contract: the boss gives the order, the enforcers execute, and the process becomes the punishment even if the case never survives trial.

Eight Years from Loyalty Oath to Indictment

To understand why this hit was inevitable, you need to go back to March 2017. A private dinner in the Oval Office. Trump looks across the table at FBI Director Comey and says the words that would seal both their fates:

“I need loyalty. I expect loyalty.”

Comey recognized mob tactics when he saw them. In organized crime, this is called “the sit-down”—when the boss tests whether you’re a made man or a rat. Comey refused the oath. He documented the meeting immediately, knowing Trump would lie about it later.

In May 2017, Trump fired him. In June, Comey testified before Congress, calling Trump’s behavior “like a Cosa Nostra induction ceremony.” He wrote books warning that Trump would corrupt institutions and weaponize the Justice Department. He became the most dangerous kind of witness: credible, documented, and unwilling to shut up.

The mob has a saying: You can survive defying the boss. But you can’t survive knowing where the bodies are buried.

Comey knew where the bodies—and the money—came from.

The Russian Money Trail Comey Threatened to Expose

This is where Charlie Angus’s reporting connects the dots that explain why Comey was always the ultimate target. The indictment isn’t just revenge for refusing loyalty in 2017—it’s about protecting the money trail that made Trump possible.

As Angus documents, Trump’s empire was built on Russian oligarch cash. In the mid-1980s, Soviet gangsters identified Trump as the perfect mark and began funneling money into his real estate operations. With the Soviet Union’s fall came massive asset stripping of public infrastructure, and Trump’s properties became the perfect vehicle for laundering stolen wealth.

By the late 1990s, a third of luxury apartments in Trump Tower had been purchased with Russian money. When Trump owed over $4 billion and no American bank would touch him, Russian “banks”—notorious for laundering kleptocrat funds—kept him afloat. Donald Trump Jr. wasn’t subtle about it: “Russians make up a pretty disproportionate cross-section of our assets. We are seeing a lot of money pouring in from Russia.”

The Russians built Trump SoHo and gave Trump 18% of profits without him investing a dollar. The 2013 Miss Universe Competition in Moscow paid him $20 million—money he desperately needed. That’s when Trump saw his political future and tweeted about hoping to become Putin’s “best friend.”

While Trump was schmoozing at Miss Universe, the FBI was breaking up a major gambling and money laundering operation in Trump Tower. They arrested 29 men. The operation’s leader, Alimzhan Tokhtakhounov, was running the scheme in a condo directly under Trump’s residence. While the FBI searched for him, he attended Trump’s Miss Universe Pageant in Moscow, sitting seats away from Trump.

The U.S. Attorney who led that investigation? Preet Bharara. Trump fired him immediately upon taking office.

What Comey’s Investigation Threatened

When Comey authorized Crossfire Hurricane in July 2016, he wasn’t just investigating election interference—he was following money trails that led back decades:

Paul Manafort, Trump’s campaign manager, had worked for Russian oligarchs and served as chief political advisor to Viktor Yanukovych, Putin’s puppet leader in Ukraine. After Ukrainian protesters forced Yanukovych out, Manafort jumped to the top spot on Team Trump. He was later convicted on eight felony counts—until Trump pardoned him.

Felix Sater, a convicted felon who worked with Russian mafia, lived in Trump Tower and pushed a massive Moscow real estate project. His promise in emails: “I will get Putin on this and we will get Donald elected.”

Michael Flynn, Trump’s National Security Advisor, was so compromised with Russia that Acting Attorney General Sally Yates warned he was prime blackmail material. Trump fired Yates, then pressured Comey to “let go” of the Flynn investigation. When Comey refused, Trump fired him too. Flynn was eventually convicted—until Trump pardoned him.

Jared Kushner, Trump’s son-in-law, had extensive Russian contacts and participated in the June 2016 Trump Tower meeting with Russian operatives offering dirt on Hillary Clinton.

George Papadopoulos and Carter Page, Trump’s foreign policy advisors, both had documented ties to Russian operatives and were involved in efforts to soften Republican policy on Ukraine.

Comey’s investigation mapped the entire network connecting Trump’s financial dependency on Russian money to the 2016 election interference. Mueller’s 2019 report confirmed “sweeping and systematic” Russian efforts to help Trump, documenting over 140 contacts between the Trump campaign and Russian nationals.

The investigation didn’t find criminal conspiracy charges that would stick, but it documented the connections. And those connections led back to the money—the decades of Russian oligarch cash that built Trump’s empire and bought his loyalty.

That’s what Comey threatened to expose. Not just the 2016 hacking, but the entire financial foundation of Trump’s political rise.

How the Enforcers Cleared the Field for This Hit

This indictment didn’t happen in a vacuum—it’s the logical endpoint of the enforcement network we’ve documented across this series. Trump’s cabinet spent months systematically eliminating anyone who might provide institutional resistance to weak prosecutions:

Each purge eliminated potential witnesses, experts, and officials who might publicly defend Comey’s decision to investigate. By the time Bondi and Blanche pushed the indictment through, there was minimal institutional resistance left.

The enforcers spent months clearing the field so this hit could go down clean.

The Indictment: Flimsy by Design

The charges center on Comey’s September 2020 testimony before the Senate Judiciary Committee. Count one alleges false statements about FBI leaks. Count two alleges obstruction through testimony about surveillance.

But legal experts immediately identified fatal flaws. The indictment quotes Senator Ted Cruz, not Comey, then claims Comey perjured himself about that quote. Comey testified he shared memos with a Columbia professor for media relay—which wasn’t an FBI policy breach according to multiple legal analysts. The grand jury rejected a third count entirely.

Ruth Marcus called it “flimsy and dangerous”—a reputational hit even if it collapses at trial. Career prosecutors memoed that it was “too weak” to pursue. Some Republicans privately expressed concerns about the timing and strength of evidence.

But that’s the point. In mob prosecutions, the process is the punishment. Legal fees, stress, destroyed reputation, years of litigation—even if Comey wins at trial, he’s been broken on the rack as a warning to everyone else.

U.S. Attorney Erik Siebert reportedly balked at the case’s weakness. Trump removed him and installed Lindsey Halligan, a loyalist who pushed it through. When your prosecutor won’t execute the hit, you find one who will.

What Comey Got Right

Comey’s 2017 testimony warned that Trump would corrupt institutions and demand personal loyalty over constitutional duty. His 2018 book A Higher Loyalty predicted Trump would weaponize the Justice Department against critics. His speeches invoked Timothy Snyder’s warnings about how democracies die—through the slow boil of institutional erosion.

Now Trump’s enforcers are proving every warning correct by prosecuting the man who issued them.

The irony is perfect: Comey predicted Trump would abuse DOJ for personal vendettas, and Trump is using DOJ to punish Comey for that prediction.

Comey’s response on X was characteristically defiant: “Let’s have a trial.” He’s ready to air the entire sordid history—the loyalty demands, the Russian money trails, the systematic corruption he documented. Trump’s enforcers might regret giving him that platform.

The Message to Every Other Potential Snitch

This indictment completes Trump’s transformation of federal law enforcement into personal vendetta machinery. If they can indict a former FBI Director for testifying to Congress about investigations he authorized, they can indict anyone.

Every whistleblower watching understands the message: Document presidential misconduct and you might face prosecution eight years later. The family never forgets, and loyalty is the only protection that matters.

Inspector General Michael Horowitz, who might investigate abuse? Trump tried to fire him with 16 others in a single night. Federal prosecutors who might refuse weak cases? They get replaced by loyalists. Intelligence officials who might provide inconvenient context? Their clearances get revoked.

The systematic enforcement creates omertà—the code of silence—not through violence but through prosecution. It’s mob tactics filtered through legal mechanisms, which makes them more insidious because they carry the veneer of legitimacy.

What Happens Next

Trump’s enforcers have perfected the prosecution playbook through the Comey hit. They’ve demonstrated that weak cases don’t matter if the goal is intimidation. They’ve shown that career prosecutor objections can be overruled by loyalist appointments. They’ve proven that even the most credible witnesses can be indicted if they cross the don.

This creates the template for systematic enemy prosecution. Trump has publicly named other targets: New York Attorney General Letitia James faces federal investigation after successfully prosecuting Trump for fraud. Anyone who crossed Trump during either term now calculates whether they’re next.

The enforcement network we’ve documented—cabinet members weaponizing their agencies, systematic purges of expertise, loyalty tests replacing constitutional oaths—has achieved its purpose. The institutions that might resist authoritarian prosecution have been captured or cowed.

The Comey Contract: Democracy’s Canary

In mob operations, you don’t kill the snitch just to eliminate him. You kill him to terrify everyone else. Comey’s indictment serves that exact function—not justice, but demonstration.

The former FBI Director who documented Trump’s loyalty demands, who warned about institutional corruption, who threatened to expose the Russian money trail that built Trump’s empire, now faces prosecution for the crime of being right about everything.

This is Episode 4 of Trump’s government mob: the ultimate contract, on the ultimate witness, proving the ultimate warning. When senior law enforcement officials can be indicted for investigating presidential misconduct, the transformation from democracy to protection racket is complete.

The don is watching. The enforcers are executing. And the message to every other potential whistleblower couldn’t be clearer: This is what happens when you don’t kiss the ring.
