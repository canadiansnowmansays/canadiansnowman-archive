The Original Sin

Part I: The Court That Decided Money Was Speech and Called It Freedom

April 29, 2026

The Supreme Court of the United States, where on January 30, 1976, the majority decided that spending money to influence elections was constitutionally protected speech. The building's inscription reads "Equal Justice Under Law." (Library of Congress)

Every system has a moment where the foundational mistake is made. Not the mistake that breaks the system — those come later, and they come often — but the mistake that makes all the subsequent breaking possible. The load-bearing error. The crack in the foundation that the architects either didn’t see or chose not to mention in the brochure.

For American campaign finance law, that moment arrived on January 30, 1976, when the Supreme Court of the United States handed down its decision in Buckley v. Valeo.

The decision runs to 294 pages. The sentence that matters fits in a single line.

Spending money to influence elections, the Court declared, is a form of protected speech under the First Amendment.

That is not, in fairness, the whole story of Buckley v. Valeo — and intellectual honesty requires saying so before the argument proceeds. The Court did not simply tear up the 1974 reforms and send Congress home. It upheld contribution limits to candidates, finding that direct donations created a genuine risk of quid pro quo corruption that the government had standing to prevent. It upheld disclosure requirements. It preserved the public financing system for presidential elections. The reformers did not lose everything on January 30, 1976.

What they lost was the load-bearing wall.

The Court struck down limits on independent expenditures — money spent to support a candidate without direct coordination with that candidate’s campaign. The majority’s reasoning was, within its own logic, defensible: if no transaction occurs between spender and candidate, no corruption occurs. And if spending money to express a political view is a form of speech — which the Court held it was — then limiting that spending means limiting that speech, which the First Amendment does not permit without a compelling justification the Court was unwilling to find.

Justice Byron White, dissenting in part, understood where that logic led even if the majority did not follow it there. He wrote that the framework would “allow the wealthy few to exercise a disproportionate influence on election outcomes.” The majority was not composed of cynics. Several justices genuinely believed they were protecting political expression from government overreach. They were also, whether they understood it or not, constitutionalizing a system in which the volume of your speech is determined by the size of your bank account. That is the original sin — not that the reasoning was indefensible, but that the Court chose a premise whose inevitable conclusion was the one Justice White identified, and called it freedom.

To understand what Buckley broke, you need to understand what it was breaking.

Two years earlier, in the wreckage of Watergate, Congress had done something genuinely serious. The Federal Election Campaign Act of 1974 was the most comprehensive attempt to regulate money in American politics since the Progressive Era. It imposed limits on what individuals could contribute to candidates. It imposed limits on what candidates could spend. It created the Federal Election Commission — an independent agency whose entire purpose was to watch the money, track the money, and stop the money when it crossed lines the law had drawn. It established public financing for presidential elections, so that candidates who accepted federal matching funds would be insulated, at least partially, from the relentless pressure to raise unlimited sums from people who wanted something in return.

The Watergate complex, Washington, D.C. The scandal that began here led to the most comprehensive campaign finance reform in American history. Two years later, the Supreme Court began dismantling it. (Wikimedia Commons)

It was, by the standards of what American politics normally produces, a serious piece of legislation written by serious people who had just watched a president of the United States collect more than $60 million in illegal campaign contributions — much of it in cash, much of it from corporations legally forbidden from donating, much of it deployed to fund a criminal enterprise operating out of the White House.

The ink was barely dry.

In 1975, a coalition sued. The plaintiffs were, on paper, an ideologically improbable alliance: Senator James Buckley of New York, a Conservative Party senator with strong libertarian instincts; former Senator Eugene McCarthy of Minnesota, the antiwar Democrat whose 1968 presidential campaign had been funded largely by a small number of very wealthy liberal donors who would have been constrained by the new limits; the American Civil Liberties Union, which had decided that campaign finance restrictions were a free speech problem; and a collection of conservative organizations that had concluded, correctly, that unlimited money was more likely to flow rightward than leftward if the restrictions were removed.

Strange bedfellows, as the expression goes. Unified by a shared interest in the outcome, as strange bedfellows always are.

The Court’s opinion, written per curiam — meaning collectively, without a single named author — drew a distinction that has organized American campaign finance law ever since, and that has caused more damage than almost any other legal distinction in modern constitutional history.

Contributions to candidates, the Court said, could be limited. The government had a legitimate interest in preventing the direct exchange of money for political favors — what the opinion called “quid pro quo corruption.” If you hand a candidate $100,000 and the candidate then does something that benefits you, the transaction looks enough like a bribe that the law can regulate it. Contribution limits upheld.

But expenditures — money spent independently, not given directly to a candidate — were different. If you spend your own money to run advertisements supporting a candidate, without coordinating with that candidate, no transaction has occurred. No quid pro quo. No corruption, or at least no corruption the Court was prepared to recognize. And since spending money to express political views is, the Court said, a form of political speech, limiting that spending means limiting that speech, which the First Amendment does not permit without a compelling government interest, which the Court did not find.

Expenditure limits struck down.

The distinction is not irrational on its face. Bribery and advocacy are genuinely different things, and a legal system that cannot distinguish between them has a problem. The Court was not wrong that there is a difference between handing a politician cash and buying a television advertisement.

What the Court failed to reckon with — or chose not to reckon with — was the practical reality that unlimited independent spending would not remain independent for long, and that a politician who knows a billionaire is spending nine figures to ensure their election has entered into a relationship with that billionaire that is, for all functional purposes, indistinguishable from the corrupt one the contribution limits were designed to prevent. The corruption doesn’t require a meeting. It doesn’t require a handshake. It doesn’t require anyone to say anything out loud. It operates through the perfectly legal mechanism of mutual understanding, and it is invisible to a legal framework that defines corruption exclusively as an explicit transaction.

Justice Byron White, dissenting in part, understood this. He wrote that the Court’s framework would “allow the wealthy few to exercise a disproportionate influence on election outcomes.” He was not making a prediction. He was reading a blueprint.

There is a particular kind of political naivety that presents itself as principled idealism and functions, in practice, as permission for the powerful. The Buckley majority was not, for the most part, composed of cynics. Several of the justices who joined the opinion were genuinely committed to First Amendment values and genuinely persuaded that political speech — including the spending of money to amplify that speech — deserved the highest constitutional protection.

They were also, whether they knew it or not, making a choice about whose speech would be amplified.

The First Amendment protects everyone’s right to speak. It does not guarantee that everyone’s voice will be heard equally. In a system where speaking louder requires spending more money, and spending more money is constitutionally protected, the people who can spend the most money are the people whose speech the Constitution most effectively protects. This is not a paradox. It is arithmetic.

A billionaire and a schoolteacher both have First Amendment rights. The billionaire has $500 million. The schoolteacher has $200 in discretionary income per month. In the world before Buckley, the law at least attempted to close some of that gap by limiting how much either of them could spend to influence an election. In the world after Buckley, the gap is not only preserved but constitutionalized. The schoolteacher’s right to speak is equal. The billionaire’s capacity to be heard is unlimited.

The Court called this freedom.

The immediate practical consequences were visible within a single election cycle.

The 1976 presidential election — the first conducted under post-Buckley rules — saw the beginning of what political scientists would come to call the “permanent campaign”: the relentless, year-round fundraising apparatus that consumes candidates’ time, distorts their priorities, and systematically advantages those who can write large checks over those who cannot. The public financing system that Watergate reformers had built provided some counterweight for presidential candidates who accepted it. But the logic Buckley had established — money is speech, spending is protected, independent expenditures cannot be limited — was already eating at the edges.

The arc of outside political spending from Buckley (1976) through Citizens United (2010) and beyond. What began as a legal distinction between contributions and expenditures became a multibillion-dollar industry.

By the 1980s, the parties had discovered soft money: contributions technically given to party organizations for “party building activities,” theoretically distinct from direct candidate support, practically indistinguishable from it. Soft money was Buckley’s first child. It would not be the last.

The reformers who had built the 1974 framework spent the next three decades trying to patch the holes that Buckley had opened. The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 — McCain-Feingold — was the most serious of those patches, and the one that came closest to actually working. It lasted eight years before Citizens United finished what Buckley had started.

We will get to Citizens United. It has its own essay, because it deserves its own reckoning. But Citizens United did not come from nowhere. It came from Buckley. It applied Buckley’s logic, extended Buckley’s premise, and arrived at Buckley’s conclusion with greater force and fewer restraints.

The original sin is not that the Court got it wrong in some technical legal sense — reasonable people have argued about the First Amendment implications of campaign finance restrictions for fifty years and will argue about them for fifty more. The original sin is that the Court chose to resolve a genuinely difficult question in a way that systematically advantaged concentrated wealth over diffuse democratic participation, and then described that choice as the only constitutionally available one.

It was not the only choice. It was a choice.

James Buckley, for what it is worth, later expressed reservations about what the decision bearing his name had produced. He had not anticipated super PACs. He had not anticipated dark money. He had not anticipated the billion-dollar election cycles, or the twenty-one families, or the undisclosed donors running civil rights imagery in Virginia referendums. He had anticipated, he said, a somewhat freer political marketplace.

The marketplace got very free indeed.

For some people.
