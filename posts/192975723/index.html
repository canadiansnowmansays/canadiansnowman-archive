He Is Risen (Again)

A Theological Inquiry Into America’s Most Durable Miracle

Sunday Satire Series | Easter Special, Part One of Two

April 5, 2026

“On Palm Sunday, Jesus entered Jerusalem as crowds welcomed him with praise, honoring him as a king. They call me king now. Can you believe it?” — Donald J. Trump, White House Easter Lunch, April 1, 2026

Let us begin with the date.

April 1, 2026. April Fools’ Day. The day the 47th President of the United States gathered more than one hundred evangelical and Catholic leaders in the East Room of the White House, reflected upon the Passion of Christ, and arrived — with what can only be described as the humility of a man who owns his own airline — at the conclusion that he and Jesus had quite a lot in common.

The video was briefly posted to the White House YouTube channel before being taken down, which is what happens when the Holy Spirit has second thoughts.

I. A Brief History of the Miracle

We must be precise about the chronology, because the theological implications are staggering.

Donald Trump has now risen from the dead so many times that the event has ceased to be miraculous and become, instead, a scheduling matter.

He rose from the Access Hollywood tape in 2016. He rose from two impeachments. He rose from January 6th. He rose from four indictments across three jurisdictions. He rose from 91 criminal counts. He rose from a civil fraud judgment of $454 million. He rose from a conviction on 34 felony counts in a Manhattan courtroom — the first former president in American history to be found guilty of crimes. He rose from a bullet that grazed his ear in Butler, Pennsylvania, stood up, raised a bloodied fist to the crowd, and turned a near-assassination into a campaign poster before the ambulance arrived. He rose from a second attempt on a golf course in Florida, which, given the location, may say something about his handicap. And then he promptly won the presidency again by seven million votes.

At some point, theologians must concede: this is not luck. This is eschatology.

The early Christians needed one resurrection to build a faith that lasted two millennia. Donald Trump has managed, conservatively, thirteen, and his approval rating remains in the mid-forties. If the early Church had access to Fox News and Truth Social, Christianity would look considerably different today, and the Nicene Creed would be substantially longer.

II. The Scriptural Evidence

The comparison, to be fair, did not originate with Trump on April 1st. It has been building, responsibly and with considerable citation, for years.

In 2019, Trump retweeted a commentator describing him as “the second coming” and “the King of Israel.” He has told supporters at the National Religious Broadcasters annual meeting that he takes “the bullets” and “the arrows” for them — “I’m being indicted for you“ — which is either a claim of substitutionary atonement or the most creative use of a preposition in the history of American politics.

During Holy Week 2024, facing a $454 million civil fraud judgment, Trump shared a message on Truth Social from a supporter who observed that “it’s ironic that Christ walked through His greatest persecution the very week they are trying to steal your property from you.” Trump called this “Beautiful.” He then shared Psalm 109, a plea for judgment against false accusers, which he found both personally relevant and, apparently, a perfectly normal thing to circulate while sitting in a Manhattan courtroom facing charges related to an adult film actress.

The Palm Sunday comparison arrived this week, fully formed and delivered with the cadence of a man who has rehearsed it but also sort of hasn’t. Jesus was hailed as king. Trump is also called king. Therefore — and the logic here is Thomistic in its rigour — they are alike. “I’m such a king,” Trump added, “I can’t get a ballroom approved.”

This is the only recorded instance in the history of Christendom of the Messiah complaining about permitting delays.

III. The Passion (As Revised)

Paula White-Cain, Senior Adviser to the White House Faith Office and Trump’s spiritual adviser since 2016, completed the theological arc. Taking the stage in the East Room after Trump finished speaking, she addressed him directly and drew a line from Gethsemane to his various indictments, from the Via Dolorosa to the Southern District of New York, from the crown of thorns to the E. Jean Carroll verdict. Jesus, she implied, would have understood the hush money. The Garden of Gethsemane, after all, was also a place where difficult arrangements were made in the dark, and not everyone present could be trusted.

A Catholic theologian watching the live feed called it “blasphemous.” Jesuit priest James Martin, with the precision of a man who has read both the Gospels and the First Amendment, noted the distinction between praying for a leader to make wise decisions and “comparing a political leader to the sinless Son of God during Holy Week.” Pastor Benjamin Cremer was more direct: “This is what it sounds like to take Jesus’ name in vain.”

The Pope — Pope Leo XIV, it should be noted, who has already criticized Trump’s war in Iran by observing that Jesus “does not listen” to the prayers of those who wage war — was unavailable for comment, being occupied with the traditional burdens of Holy Week and, one suspects, a certain weariness.

The White House gave no indication it viewed any of this as out of the ordinary. This is, by now, the expected posture. When the extraordinary becomes routine, the routine becomes doctrine.

IV. The Theological Problem

There is, for students of comparative religion, a structural difficulty with the Trump-as-Christ framework that its proponents have not fully resolved.

Jesus was betrayed for thirty pieces of silver. Trump charges $500,000 for a photo opportunity and recently launched a cryptocurrency. Jesus drove the money changers from the temple. Trump’s cabinet is largely composed of billionaires, and the temple has not been inconvenienced.

Jesus said the meek would inherit the earth. Trump’s first executive order on meekness remains unissued. Jesus fed five thousand people with five loaves and two fish. Trump has imposed tariffs on fish. Jesus said render unto Caesar what is Caesar’s; Trump is currently litigating what belongs to Caesar and what belongs to Trump, and the courts are backed up.

On Holy Thursday, Trump reflected on the betrayal of Judas and said: “We know the feeling.” He did not specify who, in his telling, plays Judas. The list of candidates is long enough to fill the East Room twice over, and most of them have memoirs in progress.

The most theologically curious element, however, is the resurrection itself. Because unlike the original, which happened once and, by most accounts, settled the matter, Trump’s resurrections keep coming. He does not stay risen. He rises, achieves something improbable, then faces a fresh crucifixion — a new indictment, a new scandal, a new deposition — and rises again.

This is not resurrection theology. This is a subscription service.

V. The Canadian Observation

From this side of the 49th parallel — where we have our own spiritual crises but have not yet merged them with the tax code — the spectacle invites a certain anthropological wonder.

A man who has spent his public life in casinos, courtrooms, and boardrooms, who has been sued by contractors, deposed by attorneys general, convicted by juries, and described under oath by associates in terms that would not appear in any gospel, has become, for tens of millions of Americans, a Christ figure.

This is not, in the end, about theology. It is about the human appetite for a story in which the protagonist cannot be defeated, in which every setback is a setup for the comeback, in which persecution is proof of righteousness rather than evidence of wrongdoing.

Trump did not invent this story. He simply recognized it, stepped into it, and has been riding it ever since — indicted for you, persecuted for you, risen for you, tariffed for you.

The miracle, when you look at it this way, is not Donald Trump. The miracle is the audience.

VI. A Closing Benediction

He is risen.

He was always going to rise. The 91 counts could not hold him. The $454 million could not hold him. The Access Hollywood tape, the E. Jean Carroll verdict, the two impeachments, the January 6th committee, the four jurisdictions — none of it could hold him.

On April 1st, in the East Room, surrounded by bishops and evangelists and one very committed spiritual adviser, he told us what we should have known all along: Palm Sunday was about him. Holy Thursday was about him. The king who rides into Jerusalem, the one who is betrayed — it is all, in the end, about him.

Whether this constitutes a miracle or an April Fools’ joke is a question best left to the cardinals.

They call him king now.

Can you believe it?
