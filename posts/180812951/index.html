When Democracy Fights Back

The Shadow Docket Strikes

Part 7: How SCOTUS Overturned Democracy Over a Holiday Weekend

December 5, 2025

A Trump judge found racial gerrymandering. SCOTUS reversed him in five paragraphs over Thanksgiving.

I need to start Part 7 with an apology.

When I published Part 6 three weeks ago, I celebrated Judge Jeffrey Brown’s 160-page ruling blocking Texas’s redistricting map. I called it a victory for democracy. I wrote: “Sometimes the most partisan actors create the tools for their own destruction.”

I was right about the trap. I was wrong about who would fall into it.

On Thursday, the Supreme Court reversed Brown’s ruling with a five-paragraph unsigned order. No oral arguments. No full briefing. No detailed reasoning. Just a swift emergency intervention that allowed Texas to use its gerrymandered map for the 2026 midterms.

This is what the shadow docket looks like when it’s used to override democracy.

What Just Happened

Let me walk you through the timeline, because the speed of this reversal is part of the story:

November 18: Three-judge panel (2-1) blocks Texas map. Judge Brown—a Trump appointee—writes 160-page opinion documenting how “substantial evidence shows that Texas racially gerrymandered the 2025 Map.”

November 21: Texas files emergency appeal to Supreme Court, asking for immediate action because candidate filing deadline is December 8.

December 4: Supreme Court grants Texas’s request, reversing the lower court in a brief unsigned order.

Total elapsed time: 16 days.

Compare that to the lower court process: 10 days of hearings, extensive fact-finding, detailed examination of legislative intent, 160 pages of analysis.

SCOTUS reversed all of that work in two weeks with a five-paragraph order.

The Shadow Docket Explained

If you’re not familiar with the term “shadow docket,” here’s what you need to know.

The Supreme Court traditionally decides cases through an extensive process: full briefing, oral arguments, detailed written opinions explaining the reasoning. This is the “merits docket”—the transparent process that builds public trust in the judiciary.

But the Court also has an emergency docket for cases requiring immediate action—historically used for things like last-minute stays of execution or genuine emergencies.

The “shadow docket” is what happens when that emergency process gets weaponized. Cases get decided without full briefing, without oral arguments, often without signed opinions or detailed reasoning. The public doesn’t know who voted for what or why.

As University of Chicago law professor William Baude (who coined the term) wrote: “In important cases, [the Court] granted stays and injunctions that were both debatable and mysterious. The Court has not explained their legal basis and it is not even clear to what extent individual Justices agree with those decisions.”

During the 16 years of the Bush and Obama administrations combined, the Department of Justice filed just 8 emergency applications total—once every two years on average.

During Trump’s first four years, DOJ filed 41 emergency applications. The Court granted 28 of them.

The second Trump administration is on pace to shatter even that record. As of December 2025, Trump’s DOJ has already filed 23 emergency applications in less than a year.

The shadow docket has become a parallel Supreme Court—one where decisions get made quickly, with minimal explanation, overwhelmingly favoring the Trump administration.

What the Majority Said (Or Didn’t Say)

The unsigned majority opinion is remarkable for what it doesn’t contain.

The entire legal reasoning fits in two sentences:

“Texas is likely to succeed on the merits of its claim that the District Court committed at least two serious errors. First, the District Court failed to honor the presumption of legislative good faith by construing ambiguous direct and circumstantial evidence against the legislature.”

The Court then invoked its 2024 decision in Alexander v. South Carolina State Conference of the NAACP, arguing that the challengers’ failure to “produce a viable alternative map that met the State’s avowedly partisan goals” should have led to a “dispositive or near-dispositive adverse inference” against them.

That’s it. No detailed explanation of what those “two serious errors” were. No engagement with the extensive factual record Brown compiled. No discussion of the DOJ letter that Brown found so problematic he called it “ham-fisted” and “a mess.”

Just an assertion that the lower court was wrong, followed by the real justification: “The District Court improperly inserted itself into an active primary campaign, causing much confusion and upsetting the delicate federal-state balance in elections.”

This is the Purcell Principle on steroids. The idea is that courts shouldn’t change election rules close to an election. But here’s the thing: Texas changed its own rules by redistricting mid-decade. The lower court was trying to prevent that change. SCOTUS invoked Purcell to protect the disruptor, not the status quo.

Alito’s Concurrence: “Partisan Advantage Pure and Simple”

Justice Alito wrote a separate concurrence, joined by Thomas and Gorsuch, making two points.

First, he argued it’s “indisputable” that Texas’s motivation was “partisan advantage pure and simple”—not racial gerrymandering.

This is legally significant because if redistricting is purely partisan, federal courts have no jurisdiction (per Rucho v. Common Cause). But it requires ignoring all the evidence Brown documented: Abbott explicitly citing the DOJ letter about race, the Legislature achieving “all but one of the racial objectives that DOJ demanded,” the targeted dismantling of coalition districts.

Second, Alito argued that under the Court’s 2024 decision in Alexander v. South Carolina, “when the asserted reason for a map is political, it is critical for challengers to produce an alternative map”—showing that partisan goals could be achieved without racial gerrymandering. The plaintiffs didn’t do this, so according to Alito, there’s “a strong inference that the state’s map was indeed based on partisanship, not race.”

This is circular reasoning dressed up as legal analysis. The plaintiffs don’t have access to the Legislature’s internal deliberations or modeling. They presented extensive evidence of racial intent—Abbott’s explicit citation of the DOJ letter, legislative testimony about race, the systematic dismantling of minority coalition districts. Alito wants them to prove a negative—that there’s no possible partisan map that would produce identical results—while giving Texas’s bare assertions the benefit of the doubt.

Kagan’s Dissent: “We Are Not a Better Court”

Justice Kagan’s 17-page dissent, joined by Sotomayor and Jackson, is devastating.

Her core argument: “We are a higher court than the District Court, but we are not a better one when it comes to making such a fact-based decision.”

The lower court’s task, she explained, “was a singularly factual one: It had to choose between the plaintiffs’ ‘race predominated’ account and the State’s ‘race never entered the picture’ story.”

The lower court held 10 days of hearings. It examined thousands of pages of evidence. It heard testimony from legislators, map drawers, and experts. It analyzed Abbott’s statements, legislative debate, the DOJ letter, and the final maps.

Then it issued a 160-page opinion carefully documenting its findings.

As Kagan writes in her dissent: “Yet this Court reverses that judgment based on its perusal, over a holiday weekend, of a cold paper record.”

She continues: “Today’s order disrespects the work of a District Court that did everything one could ask to carry out its charge. Today’s order disserves the millions of Texans whom the District Court found were assigned to their new districts based on their race.”

A holiday weekend. That’s how much time the conservative majority gave to reviewing a case that will determine the composition of Congress and affect millions of voters.

Kagan also demolished the majority’s reliance on the Purcell Principle. “Election Day is eleven months from now,” she wrote, and “even the primary election (which Texas could change) is in March.”

“Except to the extent all of us live in election season all the time, the 2026 congressional election is not well underway.” But more importantly: “the District Court ruled as ‘late’ as it did because the legislature enacted a new map less than three months before.”

In other words: Texas created the time crunch. The lower court moved expeditiously. And SCOTUS used that manufactured emergency to invoke Purcell.

The Lower Court Civil War

The controversy wasn’t just at the Supreme Court level. On the original three-judge panel, Judge Jerry Smith—a Reagan appointee on the Fifth Circuit—issued a blistering 104-page dissent.

Smith called Brown’s conduct “the most outrageous … by a judge that I have ever encountered in a case in which I have been involved.” He described the majority’s opinion as “the most blatant exercise of judicial activism that I have ever witnessed.”

Smith’s dissent became fodder for Texas’s appeal, with the state using his inflammatory language to argue the lower court had engaged in partisan overreach.

But here’s what Smith’s dissent actually reveals: even when a Trump-appointed judge meticulously documents racial gerrymandering, when the evidence is overwhelming, when the legal analysis is thorough—Republican-appointed judges will accuse him of “judicial activism” if the ruling doesn’t favor Republican interests.

Brown did everything right. He followed Supreme Court precedent. He conducted extensive hearings. He carefully weighed the evidence. And for that, he got accused of “the most outrageous” conduct Smith had “ever encountered.”

This is what good-faith judging looks like in 2025: you follow the law, document the facts, and get savaged by your colleagues for political reasons.

The Timing Manipulation

Here’s the most insidious part of what just happened: Texas deliberately created the emergency.

Think about the timeline:

  • Trump called for redistricting in February/March 2025

  • DOJ sent its letter about “coalition districts” in July

  • Two days later, Abbott directed the Legislature to redraw the map

  • Abbott waited until summer to call a special session

  • Legislature passed the map in August

  • Legal challenge was filed immediately

  • Lower court moved quickly—10 days of hearings, ruling in November

  • Candidate filing deadline: December 8

  • Primary election: March 3, 2026

Texas chose to redistrict mid-decade at the last possible moment. Then they rushed to SCOTUS claiming the lower court was disrupting the election.

But the disruption was Texas’s doing. They created the time crunch. They manufactured the emergency.

And SCOTUS rewarded that manipulation by invoking Purcell to protect Texas’s last-minute change.

As Thomas Saenz, president of MALDEF (one of the groups challenging the map), said: this incentivizes “guerrilla tactics” in redistricting. If states wait until late in the cycle to draw new maps, they can effectively prevent courts from intervening even if the maps violate the law.

This is how you rig the system: you break the rules at the last minute, then claim any attempt to enforce those rules would cause “confusion” and “disrupt” the election.

The Doom Loop Accelerates

In Part 3 of this series, I warned about a doom loop: both parties gerrymander, courts can’t keep up, voter cynicism grows, democracy erodes.

This SCOTUS ruling proves the loop is worse than I thought.

It’s not just that courts can’t keep up—it’s that SCOTUS actively protects partisan actors who game the timing.

Texas’s map stands. California’s map (which DOJ is challenging) now looks even safer—because if Texas’s explicit race-based justifications don’t invalidate a map, what would?

The scoreboard:

Texas: Redistricted mid-decade, explicitly citing racial concerns, targeting coalition districts → Map upheld by SCOTUS

California: Redistricted in response, passed by 65% voter referendum, maintaining similar racial composition → DOJ suing, but after this ruling, California’s legal position looks stronger

Missouri, North Carolina: Also redistricting mid-decade → Likely emboldened by this ruling

The message is clear: redistrict late, move fast, and SCOTUS will protect you if you can create enough of a time crunch.

What This Means for 2026

If this ruling stands (and it likely will—Texas can now use this map for 2026), Republicans could gain 5 House seats in Texas alone.

But California’s map could flip 5 Republican seats Democratic. North Carolina and Missouri add 2 more GOP seats.

The net effect is unclear. What’s certain is that the 2026 House will be determined more by gerrymandering than by voters’ actual preferences.

And that’s before we account for Louisiana v. Callais, the case currently before SCOTUS that threatens to gut what remains of the Voting Rights Act. If the Court strikes down Section 2 of the VRA (as oral arguments suggest they might), it would give states across the South license to tear up existing maps and eliminate minority-majority districts.

We’re heading toward an election where the maps matter more than the votes.

The Rule of Law Is Dying in Real Time

Let me be blunt about what just happened: The Supreme Court used its shadow docket to override detailed fact-finding by a lower court in order to protect a partisan gerrymander enacted at the president’s behest.

Judge Brown—a Trump appointee celebrated by Abbott for his “commitment to the Constitution and the rule of law”—documented extensive evidence of racial gerrymandering.

SCOTUS didn’t disprove that evidence. They didn’t explain why Brown was wrong. They just asserted he made “serious errors” and moved on.

This isn’t judicial review. It’s judicial override.

And it’s part of a pattern. The Brennan Center tracks shadow docket cases involving Trump administration actions. Since January 2025, SCOTUS has issued 23 decisions on the shadow docket concerning administration actions.

The pattern is unmistakable: The Court grants Trump’s requests quickly, with minimal reasoning, often reversing lower courts that ruled against the administration.

As Justice Kagan wrote in an earlier shadow docket dissent: “Our emergency docket should never be used, as it has been this year, to permit what our own precedent bars. Still more, it should not be used, as it also has been, to transfer government authority from Congress to the President, and thus to reshape the Nation’s separation of powers.”

The Uncomfortable Truth

In Part 5, I wrote about how DOJ’s partisan lawsuit against California might backfire. In Part 6, I celebrated when Judge Brown’s ruling seemed to prove that theory correct.

I was naive.

The truth is darker: When you have a Supreme Court willing to use its shadow docket to protect partisan interests, legal precedent doesn’t matter. Careful fact-finding doesn’t matter. Detailed documentation of violations doesn’t matter.

What matters is power. And right now, the Supreme Court is using its power to enable partisan gerrymandering while claiming to be neutral arbiters of the law.

Abbott’s response to the ruling says everything: “We won! Texas is officially—and legally—more red.”

Not “We won because we followed the law.” Not “We won because the evidence supported our position.”

Just: We won. We’re more red. Power secured.

What Happens Next

The lower court case continues—this was just an emergency stay. Theoretically, the case could return to SCOTUS on the merits after full briefing and arguments.

But realistically? The 2026 primaries are in March. The general election is in November. By the time a full merits hearing could happen, the election will be over.

The shadow docket ruling is the effective final word. Texas gets to use its gerrymandered map. Millions of voters will be placed in districts designed to dilute their political power.

Conclusion: When the Emergency Docket Becomes the Partisan Docket

The shadow docket exists for genuine emergencies—death row inmates facing execution with new evidence, government actions causing immediate irreparable harm. Not to override careful district court fact-finding, reward manufactured time crunches, or protect political actors who explicitly cite racial concerns.

But that’s what it’s become: a fast-track mechanism for the Supreme Court to intervene on behalf of the Trump administration and Republican-led states, without having to explain its reasoning or engage with inconvenient facts.

As University of Texas law professor Stephen Vladeck argues, the shadow docket “has none of” the principled reasoning that historically gave the Court its legitimacy. “We may not agree with the specific principles the justices are articulating” in major cases on abortion or guns, “but at least we have some sense that these decisions are based on legal principles.”

The shadow docket has no such anchor. Just raw assertions of power, dressed up as emergency relief.

Judge Brown did everything right. He held extensive hearings. He carefully documented the evidence. He wrote a thorough opinion explaining his reasoning.

And the Supreme Court reversed him with a five-paragraph order.

That’s not the rule of law. That’s the rule of whoever controls the Court’s emergency docket.

In Part 6, I ended with optimism: “Democracy fought back. And on Tuesday, democracy won a round.”

Two weeks later, I have to admit: Democracy didn’t win. It lost. Because when carefully documented facts don’t matter, when detailed judicial opinions can be overturned with cursory emergency orders, when the highest court uses procedural shortcuts to protect partisan actors—the rule of law is already dead.

It’s just taking a while for everyone to notice.
