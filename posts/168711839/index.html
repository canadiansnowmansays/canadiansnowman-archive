How Tulsi Gabbard May Have Just Pulled Off the Ultimate Political Distraction

When Trump's base revolted over Epstein files, suddenly documents about Obama appeared. Coincidence? This one is way too convenient.

July 19, 2025

“Baseless accusations of treason are unfortunately par for the course for this Director of National Intelligence," Rep. Jim Himes said of Tulsi Gabbard. | Andrew Caballero-Reynolds/AFP via Getty Images

Do you believe in coincidences? This one is way too convenient.

Yesterday, Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard dropped what she called a bombshell: over 100 pages of declassified documents that she claims prove Barack Obama's administration "manufactured" the entire Russia interference narrative to destroy Trump.

But here's what makes this story really interesting—the timing.

Just eleven days earlier, Trump's own Justice Department had quietly announced they wouldn't be releasing any more Jeffrey Epstein files, breaking a major campaign promise. Trump's base was furious. Conservative conferences erupted in criticism. Even loyal MAGA influencers were calling for Attorney General Pam Bondi to be fired.

Then suddenly—Russia documents! Obama conspiracy! Treasonous plot!

The same day Gabbard released these files, Trump filed a lawsuit against The Wall Street Journal over their Epstein reporting. Quite a coincidence.

What Gabbard Claims She Found

According to Gabbard, the newly declassified documents show that:

  • Before the 2016 election, U.S. intelligence consistently said Russia was "probably not trying to influence the election"

  • After Trump won, Obama ordered intelligence agencies to create a new assessment claiming the opposite

  • The whole Russia interference story was basically a "years-long coup" against Trump

  • She's referring Obama officials to the Justice Department for prosecution

If true, this would be the political scandal of the century. But there's a problem with this narrative—we've already investigated these exact claims extensively.

The Inconvenient Truth About Previous Investigations

Here's what Gabbard either doesn't know or hopes you'll forget: we already had a massive, three-year investigation into Russian interference. It wasn't led by Democrats or "deep state" officials—it was led by Republicans.

The Senate Intelligence Committee, chaired by Republican Richard Burr, spent three years reviewing over a million documents and interviewing hundreds of witnesses. Their conclusion? Russia absolutely interfered in the 2016 election to help Trump.

This wasn't a partisan hit job. Even Marco Rubio, the Republican acting chairman, stated: "We found irrefutable evidence of Russian meddling." The committee's nearly 1,000-page report was endorsed by both parties.

Think about that for a moment. If Obama officials really "manufactured" evidence of Russian interference, how did a Republican-led investigation with access to all the same intelligence agencies reach the same conclusion?

The Documents That May Not Change Anything

Now, it's possible Gabbard has found some FBI internal emails that weren't available to the Senate committee. Some of these documents were classified under "Crossfire Hurricane" (the FBI's Trump-Russia investigation) and only recently declassified after Trump's executive orders.

But here's the key question: even if some FBI officials sent problematic emails or showed bias in their internal communications, does that invalidate all the other evidence of Russian interference?

The Senate investigation didn't just rely on FBI assessments. They had:

  • Technical forensics of Russian hacking operations

  • Social media influence campaign documentation

  • International intelligence corroboration

  • Private cybersecurity firm analysis

  • Financial records and communications intercepts

Russian interference wasn't just an FBI opinion—it was documented by multiple independent sources using different types of evidence.

The Art of Political Distraction

Let's talk about that timing again, because it's almost too perfect to be coincidental.

July 7: Justice Department announces no more Epstein files

July 7-17: Trump faces rare revolt from his own base

July 18: Gabbard releases Obama conspiracy documents

July 18: Trump sues Wall Street Journal over Epstein story

This follows a classic crisis management playbook:

  1. Acknowledge the crisis (Trump said "credible" Epstein files should be released)

  2. Deflect with bigger story (Obama committed treason!)

  3. Give your base something to rally around (Attack Democrats instead of criticizing Trump)

  4. Flood the zone (Multiple actions on same day)

Whether this was coordinated or just opportunistic timing, it's politically brilliant. Instead of Trump supporters demanding Epstein transparency, they're now focused on prosecuting Obama officials.

The Bigger Picture

Here's what's really concerning about this whole episode: it shows how intelligence documents can be weaponized for political purposes.

Think about the irony here. Gabbard is accusing Obama officials of politicizing intelligence, while potentially doing exactly that herself—releasing documents not based on national security considerations, but to help Trump escape political criticism.

As DNI, Gabbard oversees 18 intelligence agencies. If she's timing declassifications to manage political crises rather than serve national security, that's a problem regardless of what you think about Trump or Obama.

What We Should Actually Focus On

Instead of getting distracted by this latest shiny object, here are the questions that actually matter:

On Russia: We have extensive bipartisan evidence that Russia interfered in 2016. Some FBI officials may have been biased or made mistakes, but that doesn't erase the underlying facts documented by multiple sources.

On Epstein: Why won't the Trump administration release more files about an international sex trafficking operation? What's in those Treasury Department records showing $1.1 billion in wire transfers that Senator Wyden says could provide "4,725 potential lines of investigation"?

On Intelligence: Should the DNI be making declassification decisions based on political calendars? How do we maintain intelligence community independence when documents become tools for narrative management?

The Bottom Line

Gabbard may have found some problematic FBI emails or internal communications that add context to how the Russia investigation began. That would be worth examining.

But the broader claim of a "treasonous conspiracy" by Obama officials appears to be political theater designed to distract from current controversies. The evidence of Russian interference has been validated by too many independent sources to be dismissed as a Democratic plot.

More importantly, the timing suggests we may have just witnessed a masterclass in political distraction. While Trump's base was revolting over Epstein files, suddenly they're focused on prosecuting Obama instead.

I don't believe in coincidences—especially not in politics. This wasn't lucky timing. It was brilliantly orchestrated, and it worked perfectly. Trump's base stopped demanding Epstein transparency and started demanding Obama prosecutions.

In politics, sometimes the most important story isn't what's being revealed—it's what's being hidden while everyone's looking the other way.
