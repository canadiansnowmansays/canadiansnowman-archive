The Iran Crisis

“The Hunger and the Law”

Part L: Days Twenty and Twenty-One: South Pars Burns, Riyadh Intercepts Missiles, and the War Has No Legal Basis

March 19, 2026

I. South Pars

A natural gas refinery is visible at the South Pars gas field on the northern coast of the Persian Gulf, in Asaluyeh, Iran on March 16, 2019. Vahid Salemi/AP/File

On Day Twenty-One, Israel crossed a threshold the war had not previously reached. Strikes hit petrochemical facilities at Asaluyeh, the southern Iranian city that processes gas from the South Pars field — the largest natural gas reservoir in the world, shared between Iran and Qatar, containing enough gas to supply global needs for roughly 13 years. South Pars accounts for 70 to 75 percent of Iran’s domestic natural gas production. It is not primarily an export facility — most of what it produces heats Iranian homes, runs Iranian stoves, generates Iranian electricity. Striking it does not hurt the world’s gas supply directly. It hurts 90 million Iranians.

Iranian state media reported fires at multiple refinery units. The governor of Asaluyeh said the fires had been contained and “the situation is fully under control.” Iran’s oil ministry confirmed airstrikes had damaged facilities connected to South Pars. Brent crude surged more than 6 percent to above $108-109 a barrel on the news — investors calculating not what the South Pars strike itself meant for supply, but what Iran’s retaliation against Gulf energy infrastructure might mean. The IRGC obliged by issuing explicit warnings to civilians in Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar to stay away from major oil and gas facilities.

Qatar condemned the strikes in the sharpest terms any Gulf state has used since the war began. Majed al-Ansari, a spokesman for Qatar’s foreign ministry, accused Israel of taking a “dangerous and irresponsible step” that could threaten global energy security. Qatar’s North Dome installations — its name for its section of the same reservoir — did not appear to have been targeted. Qatar hosts the largest US military base in the Middle East. Qatar’s LNG production had already been shut down by Iranian attacks on energy installations earlier in the war.

Israel did not immediately comment.

The Larijani funeral proceeded Wednesday in Tehran. Large crowds. His coffin draped in the Iranian flag. State media broadcast the mourners chanting “Death to America” and “Death to Israel.” State media also broadcast pro-government rallies. The regime’s choreography of grief as defiance, perfected over decades, on display again.

Iran confirmed the killing of Intelligence Minister Esmaeil Khatib — President Pezeshkian called it a “cowardly assassination.” In three consecutive nights, Israel had killed the Basij commander, the de facto ruler of Iran, and the intelligence minister. The elite-thinning continues. The layer beneath each killed leader is harder.

II. Riyadh, Ramat Gan, Ben-Gurion

Saudi Arabia intercepted four ballistic missiles over Riyadh on Wednesday afternoon. Debris fell across the capital. No injuries, no significant damage. The Saudi interior minister confirmed the “danger has passed.” A New York Times reporter in Riyadh watched an interceptor shoot up into the sky and heard multiple loud booms before residents received sudden alerts: “the area is under a hostile aerial threat.”

Four ballistic missiles over the Saudi capital. Not a fringe target. Not a Gulf outpost. Riyadh.

In Israel, Yaron and Ilana Moshe were killed in their apartment in Ramat Gan, a suburb of Tel Aviv, in the early hours of Wednesday morning. An Iranian cluster missile — a warhead that breaks apart in the air, dispersing dozens of small grenade-like munitions over several miles — struck their building. Yaron Moshe was disabled. The couple did not make it to their safe room. A metal walker lay in the rubble. The front of their apartment was blown away, walls pockmarked from shrapnel. They were the fourteenth and fifteenth civilian deaths in Israel since the war began. Israel’s President Herzog visited the site: “Innocent people were murdered last night by a vicious ballistic missile from Iran — a cluster missile.”

Iran targeted Tel Aviv with missiles carrying cluster warheads in what it said was retaliation for Israel’s assassination of Iran’s security chief Ali Larijani, Iranian state television reported on Wednesday.

The cluster warhead also caused extensive damage in and around Tel Aviv, hit a train station in central Tel Aviv — suspending national rail service for several hours — and damaged three private planes at Ben-Gurion Airport. The airport itself has not been closed, but its infrastructure is taking hits. Central Israel came under missile fire again Wednesday afternoon. Three more people injured.

III. The Senate Intelligence Hearing

Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard testifies during a Senate Intelligence Committee hearing on worldwide threats on Capitol Hill in Washington, on March 18, 2026.Francis Chung / Politico via AP

Tulsi Gabbard, Director of National Intelligence, testified before the Senate Intelligence Committee Wednesday alongside CIA Director John Ratcliffe, FBI Director Kash Patel, and senior officials from the NSA and Defense Intelligence Agency. The hearing was dominated by two questions: what did the intelligence say before the war, and how quickly can Iran rebuild its missile program.

Gabbard said Iran’s leadership has been “largely degraded” by US and Israeli attacks — but the government “appears to be intact.” A precise and carefully hedged statement. She avoided answering with any specificity when Democrats pressed her on pre-war threat assessments. She neither confirmed nor denied the core of Joe Kent’s claim.

Ratcliffe contradicted Kent directly: “I think Iran has been a constant threat to the United States for an extended period of time and posed an immediate threat at this time.” He called the war an effort to “address a 47-year problem” and said it would take four to six weeks. He did not provide a timeline for when Iran might develop a missile capable of striking the United States but said Iran was “gaining experience” in longer-range missiles and that degrading Iran’s missile production capability “is so important to our national security.”

Representative Jim Himes, the top Democrat on the House Intelligence Committee, speaking outside the hearing: “I’ve seen absolutely no evidence that there was an imminent threat of attack by Iran. There is no evidence, at least that was provided to the committees or that I’m aware of produced by the intelligence community to suggest that they were close to producing a nuclear weapon, or had the intent to produce a nuclear weapon.”

On Gabbard’s position: she backed Trump while carefully not mentioning Kent by name, and she did not say the intelligence had shown imminent threat. She said Trump “concluded that the terrorist Islamist regime in Iran posed an imminent threat and he took action based on that conclusion.” A description of what Trump decided. Not a confirmation of what the intelligence showed.

IV. The Food and the Water

The dimension of this war that has received the least coverage relative to its severity is the one Vivian Salama documents in The Atlantic: the war is threatening the food and water supply of 60 million people in the Gulf states, and through fertilizer disruption, threatening crop yields worldwide.

The numbers are stark. The World Economic Forum found that about 85 percent of all food consumed in GCC member countries — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain — is imported. The equivalent figure in the United States is 15 percent. Gulf dependency on imports rises to nearly 100 percent for rice and 90 percent for cereals. Seventy percent of all food imports for those six countries pass through the Strait of Hormuz. Vessel traffic through the Strait is down at least 90 percent since February 28. On March 12, Lloyd’s List Intelligence recorded zero transits for the first time since the war began.

Saudi Arabia has roughly four months of wheat supply. The UAE says its strategic reserves cover four to six months. The UAE has already urged residents to report price gouging through a dedicated hotline. These are not reassuring numbers. Four months is not a long time if the war shows no signs of ending and the Strait shows no signs of reopening.

Iran has already struck desalination plants — the primary source of drinking water for millions in the Gulf. Bahrain’s Interior Ministry reported an Iranian drone attack damaged a desalination plant on March 8. Kuwait reported fragments from an intercepted drone struck a fuel tank at the Doha West power and water station. The GCC countries are among the most water-stressed globally. They cannot grow their way out of this. The aquifers Saudi Arabia once used for wheat have already been depleted. The sovereign wealth funds that bought farmland in Eastern Europe and South America still need safe shipping to get the food home.

The fertilizer dimension reaches beyond the Gulf. The region provides nearly half of the world’s urea — a key ingredient in nitrogen-based fertilizers. The shipping standstill has pushed global fertilizer component prices up roughly 25 percent. American spring planting accelerates in March and April, and farmers are already dealing with high borrowing costs and volatile commodity prices. “We’re up for a food disaster and all we talk about is gas prices,” Michael Werz of the Council on Foreign Relations told Salama. The World Food Programme has already warned that the war threatens to push 45 million additional people into acute hunger — a potential all-time global record.

The Arab Spring comparison is not alarmist. In 2010, a heat wave destroyed a third of Russia’s wheat harvest. Global wheat prices surged 50 percent. Egypt’s bread-subsidy program — the world’s largest wheat importer — was pushed to breaking point. The protests began shortly after. Gulf governments remember this. The officials Salama spoke to were reluctant to discuss their vulnerability publicly, precisely because they didn’t want to call attention to it. “Another said only that he hoped the war would end soon.” That is the diplomatic language of genuine fear.

V. The Constitutional Crisis

David Frum, writing and speaking in The Atlantic, makes an argument that has been underplayed since the war began: this is the first major American war since 1945 that has no legal basis whatsoever.

Frum catalogues the precedents. Korea: authorized by UN Security Council resolution, which Truman argued Congress had pre-authorized by joining the UN Charter. The Gulf War: explicit congressional authorization. Afghanistan: authorized by Congress. Iraq: authorized by Congress. Kosovo, Libya, Grenada: limited enough in scope that presidents could argue executive authority. The ISIS campaign: argued as a continuation of the Iraq authorization. Even the wars fought without explicit congressional authorization had a legal pretext — a treaty, a UN resolution, a previous authorization still in force.

Iran has none of this. No treaty. No UN resolution. No congressional authorization. No legal pretext. No request for authorization. Nothing.

The war is costing $1 to $2 billion a day. By the administration’s own account, more than $11 billion in the first six days. At some point — when? — Congress will be asked to appropriate money for it. At that point, legislators who have spent weeks saying nothing, taking no votes, assuming no responsibility, will be asked to pay. “Since they’re going to do that,” Frum argues, “if they’re going to be present at the funding stage, they need to be present at the war-fighting stage.”

The deeper problem Frum identifies: many in Congress, including Democrats, privately support the war’s goals and don’t want to cast a vote either for or against. They are “becoming increasingly compliant and even complicit with President Trump’s preference to just do everything on his own.” The progressive demand to stop the war is, in Frum’s view, “meaningless and irresponsible and impossible.” But the answer to an impossible demand is not silence. It is oversight. “No emoluments, no atrocities. They have to be present.”

Frum’s specific concerns: Pete Hegseth has publicly invoked “no quarter” — which, if true, is a war crime. Congressional oversight of prisoner treatment is not optional. And Trump has already taken a plane from Qatar, a country the United States is currently protecting, and his family has received major UAE investments. In 1991, Gulf state contributions to the cost of the Gulf War went into the US Treasury, as required by congressional provisions. There is currently no mechanism ensuring that any contributions from allies in this war go to the Treasury rather than to the Trump family.

VI. Why Britain Said No — and What Blair’s Adviser Thinks

Alastair Campbell, Tony Blair’s closest aide during the Iraq War, spoke to The Atlantic this week. His framing of Starmer’s decision is the most precise external account yet of why Britain stayed out.

Starmer is an international lawyer. When the Americans requested British participation, he and the UK Attorney General assessed the legal basis and found none. Twenty-four hours later, when Iranian responses appeared to approach UK targets in Cyprus, Starmer allowed two British bases to be used for limited defensive operations. That was the limit. Trump has attacked him repeatedly since.

Campbell’s central observation: “There’s never been a successful regime change from the air. There has to be a follow-through plan. There has to be something. You can’t just leave the whole place in complete chaos and then move on to the next adventure.”

He draws the parallel to Iraq with precision. The Blair team did not ask hard enough about what happened after Saddam. They assumed the post-war planning would be handled. They were the junior partner. “Most of the military hardware was American; most of the leadership was American.” The lesson he takes: when you are the junior partner, you must demand the answers to the questions the senior partner refuses to ask itself.

On Trump’s theory of the war — the Venezuela template, the expectation of a swift result, the dismissal of post-conflict planning — Campbell is exact: “He takes out the ayatollah, and then they’re just into this sort of day by day.” And: “He’s got his characters, and he gives them all a personality. In his head, I think this is sort of good versus evil, and he sees himself as good.”

On Russia: Campbell visited Ukraine the week before the war began. He said it is “very hard to escape the notion that, basically, when it comes to Ukraine, the Americans are on Putin’s side, the way they talk about it.” This is a senior European political figure saying plainly what diplomatic language has been dancing around for months.

VII. The Jones Act Waiver

The Trump administration announced a 60-day waiver of the Jones Act — the 1920 maritime law requiring that goods shipped between US ports travel on American-built, American-crewed vessels. The waiver applies to oil, natural gas, fertilizer, and coal. Karoline Leavitt called it an effort “to mitigate the short-term disruptions to the oil market.”

The expected impact: approximately 5 cents per gallon of gasoline. Gas is at $3.84 per gallon nationally, up 29 percent since February 28. The largest component of the price increase is the cost of crude oil, which has surged roughly 40 percent. American tankers cost about $50,000 per day more to hire than foreign vessels. Switching saves pennies. The administration knows this. The Jones Act waiver is a political signal — evidence of action — rather than a solution. Previous waivers have been granted after Hurricane Maria and the Colonial Pipeline cyberattack. Those were genuine supply disruptions of a domestic character. This one is a war.

VIII. The Sweden Execution

Iran, more than three weeks into a war in which it is being bombed daily, executed a Swedish citizen on Wednesday. Sweden’s Foreign Minister Maria Malmer Stenergard confirmed the execution, saying the Swedish citizen did not receive a fair trial. Iran accused him of spying for Israel. The Swedish government had repeatedly advocated for a fair process since his arrest in June 2025.

Iran executed a foreign national for alleged espionage, on the eve of Nowruz, in the third week of a war, while its de facto ruler was being buried and its intelligence minister had just been killed. The regime’s domestic repression apparatus continues to function. The elite-thinning is real. The institutional machinery beneath the elites is also real.
