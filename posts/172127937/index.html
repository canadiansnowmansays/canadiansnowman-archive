The Case for George Soros

What exactly Triggers Trump regarding George Soros

A deep dive into the psychological and political dynamics behind Trump's decade-long obsession

August 27, 2025

The Bottom Line Up Front

Donald Trump called for billionaire George Soros and his son to face federal racketeering charges over their support of "violent protests" on Truth Social Wednesday morning, marking the latest escalation in what has become a near-decade-long fixation. But Trump's obsession with the 95-year-old financier goes far deeper than typical political opposition—it reveals a complex web of envy, insecurity, and strategic scapegoating that speaks to something fundamental about Trump's worldview and vulnerabilities.

The pattern is unmistakable: Trump targets Soros with an intensity and persistence that suggests something deeply personal, going far beyond normal political disagreement into the realm of what can only be described as pathological preoccupation. Understanding why reveals as much about Trump's psychology as it does about America's political moment.

The Contrast That Cuts Deep

The most striking aspect of Trump's Soros obsession may be the stark contrast in how these two men accumulated their wealth and wielded their influence. It's a comparison that likely gnaws at Trump in ways he'd never publicly admit.

George Soros made his fortune primarily through currency speculation and hedge fund management, most famously "breaking the Bank of England" in 1992 through a massive short position against the British pound, netting him a profit of about $1 billion. His firm averaged a 20% annual rate of return over four decades, making it one of the most profitable hedge funds in history. While aggressive, Soros's methods operated within financial markets through legitimate, if high-stakes, trading strategies.

Born into a prosperous Jewish family that was disrupted by the Nazis' arrival in Hungary in 1944, Soros survived by using false papers to avoid concentration camps before moving to London in 1947. He studied economics at the London School of Economics, moved to New York City in 1956, and built his fortune through pure financial acumen.

Trump's wealth accumulation tells a very different story. From questions about tax strategies and property valuations to various civil lawsuits and recent criminal convictions related to business practices, Trump's financial empire has been shadowed by legal scrutiny in ways that Soros's legitimate trading operations never were. The 2024 conviction for covering up hush money payments represents just the latest in a long line of legal challenges that have dogged Trump's business dealings.

This legitimacy gap may be the core wound—Trump built a brand around being a successful businessman, yet here's someone who actually achieved what Trump only claimed to have done: building vast wealth through pure skill and intellect, then using it for causes bigger than himself.

The Philanthropy That Stings

Perhaps even more galling to Trump is what Soros did with his fortune. Since 1984, Soros has used his profits to fund the Open Society Foundations, distributing more than $8 billion of his earnings to promote democracy, freedom of information, and help for the underprivileged. By 2017, his charities had assets of more than $18 billion, representing roughly 73% of his family office's total assets.

The scope is breathtaking: funding disaster relief to Sarajevo under attack in 1993, providing funding to build low-cost housing in South Africa for over three million people in 1995, starting the Central European University in Prague and Budapest in 1990, and donating hundreds of millions for humanitarian aid to Bosnia and scientists in the former Soviet Union by 1992.

Compare this to Trump's charitable record—or lack thereof. The Trump Foundation was shut down by New York's attorney general amid allegations of misuse, and Trump's promises to donate to charity have repeatedly failed to materialize without media pressure. For someone who craves adulation and wants to be seen as generous, watching Soros deploy billions for humanitarian causes while earning global respect for it must be particularly painful.

The Perfect Scapegoat Strategy

But Trump's Soros fixation isn't just personal—it's also coldly strategic. Soros serves as "a perfect foil to try to convince people that Trump stands for the average American. What better scapegoat than a wealthy, uber-liberal currency speculator who helped fund Obama and Hillary Clinton, supports abortion, gay and trans rights and gives generously to the electoral campaigns of liberal public defenders and prosecutors?"

Soros has become "a magnet for conspiracy theories, with opponents claiming he is behind such diverse events as the 2017 Women's March, the fact-checking website Snopes, the gun-control activism engaged in by the survivors of the Stoneman Douglas High School shooting, the October 2018 Central American immigrant caravans, and protests against the Brett Kavanaugh Supreme Court nomination".

The pattern reveals Trump's media savvy: by consistently invoking Soros, he taps into a rich vein of existing conspiracy theories while deflecting attention from his own problems. Every Trump scandal becomes about "Soros-backed prosecutors." Every protest against his policies becomes "Soros-funded agitators." It's masterful misdirection.

The Antisemitic Undertones

Professor Armin Langer has noted that Soros is "the perfect code word" for conspiracy theories that unite antisemitism and Islamophobia. Soros, a Jewish Holocaust survivor, "has featured prominently in anti-Semitic conspiracy theories on the far right", making him an ideal target for Trump's base while allowing Trump plausible deniability about the antisemitic elements of his attacks.

Trump has repeatedly called Manhattan District Attorney Alvin Bragg, who is Black, a "Soros backed animal" even though "George Soros, the Hungarian American and Jewish billionaire who conservatives frequently invoke as a boogeyman, doesn't know and didn't directly donate to Bragg". The false connection serves to racialize the attacks while invoking antisemitic tropes about Jewish puppet masters.

This isn't accidental. Trump's political instincts tell him that attacking Soros allows him to activate multiple forms of resentment—against wealth, against Jews, against immigrants, against globalism—all wrapped in one convenient target.

The Democracy vs. Authoritarianism Frame

Soros announced in 2023 that he would hand over control of his philanthropic empire, the Open Society Foundations, to his son Alex, ensuring continuity in work that Trump sees as directly threatening to his authoritarian project. The Open Society Foundations' core mission—promoting democracy, human rights, and civil society—represents everything Trump seeks to undermine.

Various right-wing figures, including Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orban and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi, have embraced conspiracy theories that the Soros family secretly funded riots and politically destabilizing activity in their countries. Trump fits comfortably into this global authoritarian playbook that uses Soros as a boogeyman for democratic opposition.

What Trump understands intuitively is that Soros represents the antithesis of his worldview: someone who uses wealth to strengthen democratic institutions rather than corrupt them, who promotes transparency rather than secrecy, who empowers civil society rather than crushing it.

The Latest Escalation

Trump's Wednesday morning Truth Social post demanding that "George Soros, and his wonderful Radical Left son, should be charged with RICO because of their support of Violent Protests, and much more, all throughout the United States of America" represents a dangerous new level of escalation. Legal experts note there is "no legal basis" for Soros to be prosecuted under the RICO Act, as "RICO requires a pattern of racketeering activity" that doesn't apply to legitimate political donations and activism.

The Open Society Foundation responded that "these accusations are outrageous and false. The Open Society Foundations do not support or fund violent protests. Our mission is to advance human rights, justice, and democratic principles at home and around the world".

But facts have never mattered in Trump's Soros obsession. The accusations "echo Trump's pattern of calling to punish or imprison his perceived political opponents as organizers mobilize nationwide against the president for his immigration policies and threats to send federal troops into American cities".

What This Reveals About Trump

Trump's decade-long Soros fixation ultimately reveals three key aspects of his psychology:

1. Deep Insecurity About Legitimacy: Every attack on Soros is an implicit acknowledgment that Trump knows his own wealth and influence lack the legitimacy that comes from actual achievement and ethical use of power.

2. Authoritarian Instincts: Trump's inability to accept that democratic opposition might be organic rather than manipulated reveals his fundamentally authoritarian worldview—in his mind, resistance to his agenda must be artificially created by puppet masters rather than arising naturally from citizens.

3. Strategic Scapegoating: Trump understands that Soros serves as a perfect lightning rod, allowing him to channel multiple forms of resentment while deflecting attention from his own failures and corruption.

The Broader Pattern

Trump's Soros obsession fits into a broader pattern of conspiracy thinking that includes claiming "antifa activists were being funded by Democrats, George Soros or 'other people,'" alleging "that Soros was backing the protests against Kavanaugh's Supreme Court nomination," and claiming "that Soros funded the Central American migrant caravans heading toward the United States".

This reflects what some observers call "Soros Derangement Syndrome"—a belief in "a nonexistent opposition" that "reveals a dangerous delusion that could lead to their downfall". Trump's refusal to acknowledge organic opposition to his agenda forces him into increasingly elaborate conspiracy theories that disconnect him from political reality.

The Ultimate Psychological Warfare: A Nobel Peace Prize for Soros?

Here's a thought that would truly expose the depth of Trump's Soros obsession: George Soros should be nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize—and he absolutely qualifies.

Anyone meeting specific criteria can nominate candidates for the Nobel Peace Prize, including university professors in relevant fields, directors of peace research institutes, members of national assemblies, and former Peace Prize laureates. All living persons and active organizations are eligible candidates, with nominations accepted until January 31 each year.

Soros's credentials are actually impressive by Nobel standards:

The Peace-Building Record: Soros played a crucial role in the peaceful transition from communism to democracy in Hungary (1984-89) and provided substantial endowment to Central European University in Budapest. A 2017 study found that his grant program which awarded funding to over 28,000 scientists in former Soviet republics "more than doubled publications on the margin, significantly induced scientists to remain in the science sector, and had long-lasting beneficial impacts". His funding was considered crucial to the success of Georgia's peaceful Rose Revolution.

The Humanitarian Impact: Since 1979, he has distributed over $32 billion to advance democracy, human rights, and civil society worldwide. His early work began with scholarships for Black South African students under apartheid, and he pioneered working directly with local communities. During the 1990s Balkan conflicts, he provided disaster relief to besieged Sarajevo and humanitarian aid throughout the region.

Already Nominated: In 2017, five professors from Hungary's John Wesley Theological College actually did nominate Soros for the Nobel Peace Prize, citing his "relentless and systematic support offered to the Hungarian, Czech, and Polish opposition in the 1980s" and his "major contribution to the creation of democracy and to the break-up of the Warsaw Pact".

Now contrast this with Trump's self-proclaimed peace credentials, which grow more grandiose with each telling. Trump has claimed to have ended "six wars" or even "seven wars" depending on the day, boasting about preventing conflicts and brokering peace deals around the globe.

But the reality is far less impressive:

Ukraine/Russia - The Biggest Failure: Trump promised 53 separate times during his campaign that he would end the Ukraine war "within 24 hours" or before taking office, speaking "in an entirely serious tone, manner and context". When reminded of these promises, he now falsely claims he was speaking "in jest"—a demonstrable lie. After six months in office, "peace appears further away than ever" with "repeated diplomatic approaches answered by Russia with its largest air attacks to date". Trump has made dramatic gestures—bringing Putin to Alaska for talks, then summoning Zelensky to DC with European leaders "tailing behind"—that create the appearance of serious diplomacy. But the reality is that Putin is making a mockery of Trump's efforts, with every diplomatic overture met by Russian escalation. Trump has "TACO'd too much"—given away his negotiating position by being so desperate to claim victory that Putin knows he can extract maximum concessions while offering minimal genuine compromise.

Israel-Iran - Legitimate Success: Trump deserves full credit here. He directly brokered this ceasefire by calling Netanyahu ("No more war") while coordinating with Qatar's Emir to secure Iran's agreement. The bombing did stop after the initial exchange, though both sides have since been accused of violations.

Gaza - Inherited Biden's Deal: The Gaza ceasefire "proposal was first drafted by mediators from the United States, Egypt, and Qatar, accepted by Hamas on 5 May 2024, and presented by U.S. president Joe Biden on 31 May". Trump joined Biden in pressuring Israel to accept it, but this was largely Biden's framework implemented during Trump's term.

India-Pakistan - India Disputes Trump's Role: India "repeatedly rejected the idea that Trump negotiated the ceasefire" and Indian PM Modi told Trump "the ceasefire was achieved through talks between military officials on both sides, rather than US mediation".

North Korea - Past Failure: Trump's record is "blotted by the failure of his first-term peace efforts with North Korea" which followed the same pattern of initial promises, summits, then losing interest.

Armenia-Azerbaijan - Shared Success: Trump hosted the leaders who signed a peace agreement, representing legitimate diplomatic achievement.

Cambodia-Thailand - Leveraged Trade Threats: Trump "threatened to shelve trade deals with both Thailand and Cambodia to halt a border war" and "the leverage was effective", though "the agreement was brokered by the Association of Southeast Asian Nations."

Rwanda-DRC - Temporary and Fragile: Trump brokered a deal but "the peace agreement between Democratic Republic of Congo and Rwanda is temporary and shaky".

Egypt-Ethiopia - No Deal: "Egypt's Foreign Minister announced June 29 that talks with Ethiopia over the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam have officially stalled" and "with Egypt and Ethiopia, there is no deal on the table".

Serbia-Kosovo - Minimal Evidence: "With Kosovo and Serbia, there's little evidence he was significantly involved or that a major conflict was emerging".

Overall Assessment: Foreign policy experts said "the others strike me as major exaggerations or very premature" and while Trump "deserves some credit for recent peace agreements in several conflicts," many claims are inflated.

So Trump has 2-3 legitimate successes (Israel-Iran, Armenia-Azerbaijan, and possibly Cambodia-Thailand), several exaggerated claims, and his biggest promises (Ukraine, Gaza as original architect) remain unfulfilled or were inherited from Biden.

The Psychological Impact: Imagine Trump's reaction to Soros winning the Nobel Peace Prize—the ultimate global recognition for promoting democracy and human rights, everything Trump undermines. It would be the final confirmation that Soros achieved the moral authority and international respect that Trump desperately craves but will never possess.

The sting would be particularly acute given Trump's well-documented obsession with Barack Obama, who won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2009. Trump spent years promoting birtherism and undermining Obama's legitimacy, yet Obama achieved the international recognition that perpetually eludes Trump. A Soros Nobel Prize would mean both Obama—Trump's political nemesis—and Soros—his conspiracy obsession—would hold the award Trump desperately wants but will never deserve.

US President Joe Biden, right, presents the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest US civilian honor, to Alex Soros on behalf of his father George Soros, in the East Room of the White House, January 4, 2025, in Washington. (AP Photo/Manuel Balce Ceneta)

Soros already received the Presidential Medal of Freedom in January 2025, "the highest civilian honor in the United States," for his "lifelong commitment to promoting human rights, equity, and justice". A Nobel Peace Prize would complete the transformation of Trump's greatest boogeyman into his generation's most honored humanitarian, joining the exclusive club that includes Obama but will forever exclude Trump.

The irony would be perfect: the man Trump attacks as a threat to democracy winning history's most prestigious award for advancing it, while Trump—despite his grand claims about ending wars—remains "intensely unpopular in Europe—including in Norway, the committee's home country, where his approval rating was just 13 percent".

So Does This Qualify Trump for the Nobel Peace Prize Over Soros?

Trump's supporters will certainly argue that brokering the Israel-Iran ceasefire and facilitating the Armenia-Azerbaijan agreement represent concrete peace achievements that should put him ahead of Soros for Nobel consideration. After all, these involve stopping active warfare, not just philanthropic work.

But here's why Trump still shouldn't be considered:

The Disqualifying Pattern: Trump's approach to "peace" consistently involves threats, leverage, and coercion rather than genuine reconciliation. He "threatened to shelve trade deals" with Cambodia and Thailand, bombed Iran's nuclear facilities before demanding ceasefire, and regularly threatens military action to achieve diplomatic goals. This is the opposite of the Nobel Peace Prize's foundational principles.

Undermining Democratic Peace: While Trump may stop individual conflicts, he simultaneously undermines the democratic institutions and international law that create lasting peace. His attacks on NATO, embrace of authoritarians like Putin and Orbán, and systematic destruction of democratic norms represent the antithesis of sustainable peace-building.

Track Record of Escalation: Trump's "peace" deals often follow conflicts he helped escalate or create. The Israel-Iran war intensified significantly under his policies, and his withdrawal from the Iran nuclear deal contributed to regional instability.

Temporary vs. Transformational: Soros's work has created lasting institutional change—building civil society, strengthening democracy, and creating educational infrastructure that prevents conflicts. Trump's deals are tactical ceasefires that pause fighting without addressing root causes.

The Nobel Standard: The Peace Prize recognizes those who advance "fraternity between nations," not just stop shooting. Soros has "contributed more than $32 billion to advancing freedom and justice across the world" and "pioneered a new approach to philanthropy of working directly with local communities"—building the foundations of peace, not just managing its absence.

Trump may be a dealmaker, but Soros is a peacebuilder. Only one of those deserves a Nobel Peace Prize.

Conclusion: The Mirror Effect

In the end, Trump's obsession with George Soros functions like a funhouse mirror, distorting reality while revealing uncomfortable truths about the obsessed party. Every attack on Soros implicitly acknowledges what Trump lacks: legitimate business success, ethical use of wealth, genuine philanthropic impact, and respect from global democratic institutions.

Soros represents the path not taken—what Trump might have been if he had actual business acumen, ethical principles, and a commitment to something larger than himself. That Trump will never admit this publicly only makes his private awareness of it more psychologically damaging.

The tragedy is that Trump's Soros fixation isn't just personal pathology—it's become a template for American authoritarianism, teaching his followers to see democratic opposition as illegitimate manipulation rather than citizen engagement. In attacking Soros, Trump isn't just revealing his own insecurities—he's undermining the very foundations of democratic discourse.

The real question isn't what triggers Trump about Soros—it's whether American democracy can survive a leader so threatened by legitimate success and ethical wealth that he's willing to destroy democratic norms to avoid the comparison.
