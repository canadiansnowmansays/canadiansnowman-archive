The Iran Crisis

The Ratification

Part LVI: Day 42. Islamabad. The highest-level meeting between American and Iranian officials since the Islamic Revolution. Four hours and counting.

April 13, 2026

Vice President JD Vance with Pakistani officials after arriving in Islamabad, Pakistan, on Saturday.Credit...Pool photo by Jacquelyn Martin

On the morning of Saturday, April 11, 2026, Pakistani workers were still painting the curbs yellow and black near the Serena Hotel when the delegations began to arrive. Islamabad had been on lockdown since Thursday — roads blocked with shipping containers, hiking trails closed, a two-mile security perimeter around a five-star hotel whose previous guests had been abruptly instructed to collect their belongings and leave. The authorities had affixed signs to lampposts throughout the city: three flags, one message. Islamabad Talks. April 2026.

A young boy handed JD Vance a bouquet of flowers on a red carpet. Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner met him at the end of the honour guard. The vice president of the United States had flown seventeen hours to conduct the most consequential American diplomacy since the hostage crisis — to end a war he had privately opposed from the start, negotiating on behalf of a president who had already moved on and was live-posting on Truth Social while the talks were underway.

Iran’s Supreme National Security Council had already described what was happening in Islamabad in an official statement, with a candour that should have ended the suspense: the talks were “a ratification of a victory rather than a negotiation.” The cease-fire, it added, “does not mean the end of the war.”

Vance was not flying to make a deal. He was flying to hear Iran’s terms.

The Plane Told the Story

A photograph released by Pakistan’s government showing the Iranian delegation led by the speaker of Iran’s parliament, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, second from right; and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi, second from left. They were welcomed by Pakistan’s army chief, Asim Munir, left; and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar, right, in Islamabad, Pakistan, on Friday.Credit...Pakistan's Ministry of Foreign Affairs, via Reuters

Before the first word was spoken in the Serena Hotel, the composition of Iran’s delegation had already communicated what Tehran intended. Seventy people. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi. The governor of the Central Bank. A former chief of staff of the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps. Legal advisers. Sanctions experts. Military commanders. Three Iranian officials confirmed to the New York Times that the delegation had full authority to make decisions without consulting Tehran — that the new Supreme Leader, Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei, had given Ghalibaf personal authority to make a deal or walk away.

Vali Nasr, the Johns Hopkins Iran expert who also served in the Obama State Department, was unambiguous: a delegation of this composition and size is deployed only when negotiations are in their final stages, not for preliminary testing of the waters. “What we can read from Iran’s delegation,” he said, “is that they have not come to stonewall.”

On the plane, photographs showed backpacks and framed photos of children occupying empty seats — a representation of the nearly 170 children killed when an American Tomahawk missile struck an elementary school in Minab. The delegation wore head-to-toe black, the colour of mourning. Every element was deliberate. Iran had come to Islamabad as a country that believed it had won the war, and intended to negotiate accordingly.

The American team — Vance, Witkoff, Kushner — had come to negotiate an end to a war the president had already declared won in a different sense. These are not the same victory.

The Toll Booth With Landmines in the Floor

Mark Landler’s image of the Strait of Hormuz as toll booth — Iran collecting passage fees on a case-by-case basis, rewarding the neutral and punishing the aligned — was already the defining metaphor of the ceasefire’s first week. France got a ship through the day after Macron broke with Trump. Turkey got three vessels through under Panama, Belize and St. Kitts flags. India bought its first Iranian oil in seven years. Pakistan, the mediator, was exempted. Britain, Germany, and the US-aligned Europeans: stuck behind 1,000 ships waiting in the Persian Gulf.

What nobody had yet publicly acknowledged, until a quiet intelligence disclosure on Friday, was that the toll booth has landmines in the floor that Iran itself cannot locate.

A billboard that appeared over the weekend in Tehran shows Iranian soldiers with American military planes and ships caught in a net, with the message “The Strait of Hormuz will stay closed.”Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

Iran mined the strait haphazardly. It did not record all positions. Some mines were placed in ways that allowed them to drift. Even when locations were known, Iran lacks the capability to remove them quickly. The United States, which destroyed Iran’s surface navy, also lacks robust mine removal capability. On Friday, two ships passed through the strait. Two. On Tuesday, Trump had demanded its “COMPLETE, IMMEDIATE, and SAFE OPENING.” On Saturday, he posted that the US had begun “clearing out the Strait of Hormuz” — a claim that could not be confirmed.

When Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi said on Wednesday that the strait would open “with due consideration of technical limitations,” American officials confirmed he was using diplomatic language for a simpler truth: we cannot open it fully even if we wanted to. The physical oil market, trading at $145 a barrel while futures sat at $97, was pricing this reality accurately. Futures traders were betting on resolution. Physical oil buyers were betting on the water. The physical market was right.

European airports warned that without significant, stable passage through the strait within three weeks, systemic jet fuel shortages would become reality. Ireland called in the army to clear fuel protest blockades from its motorways. South Korea dispatched a special envoy. Vietnam and Thailand had already turned away customers at petrol stations. Sri Lanka declared every Wednesday a public holiday. The cascade had become structural — a civilisation logistics problem, not an energy market story.

The Incompatibility Inventory

Karim Sadjadpour, whose analysis for The Atlantic is the sharpest single piece produced by this war’s journalism, constructed the negotiating table’s actual geometry with surgical precision. It is worth reproducing in full, because it names what the series has been approaching for sixty-four parts.

On nuclear enrichment, Trump demands zero. Iran calls it a sovereign right. On regional proxies, Trump demands abandonment. Iran calls them “the heroic Islamic Resistance.” On ballistic missiles, Trump demands suspension. Iran does not mention them, because they are nonnegotiable. On sanctions, Trump offers relief as a reward. Iran demands full removal as a baseline. On reparations, Trump offers nothing. Iran demands payment for the war.

“Not a negotiation over terms,” Sadjadpour writes. “A negotiation over incompatible realities.”

The Obama administration took nearly two years to negotiate a deal limited solely to Iran’s nuclear program. The Trump administration has two weeks to resolve the Strait of Hormuz, plus Tehran’s nuclear, missile, and regional ambitions, simultaneously. The Obama deal is the one Trump then tore up. The precedent is not encouraging.

The Vance Trap

There is a political geometry to Vance’s presence in Islamabad that Sadjadpour articulates with a precision the vice president’s own allies would recognise as accurate. Iran views Vance as the anti-war voice in MAGA — less sympathetic to Israel than Witkoff or Kushner, and highly motivated to resolve the conflict given his presidential ambitions. If he reaches the White House, he will be bound by whatever he signs in Pakistan. Iran has every reason to prefer him as the signatory of a deal that advantages Tehran.

Trump, meanwhile, has already completed the political transfer. “If it doesn’t happen, I’m blaming JD Vance,” the president recently told associates. “If it does happen, I’m taking full credit.” The joke lands as threat. A bad deal damages Vance. No deal damages him. A good deal — from an American perspective — is the only outcome that helps him. And Iran has no intention of offering one.

Pakistan asked for Vance specifically because his pre-war opposition made him credible to Tehran. He arrives as the man Trump sent to end the war Trump started over Vance’s objections. In the irony is the structural condition. The vice president who privately warned that regime-change war would be a disaster is now in charge of managing the disaster’s aftermath.

His predecessor in this particular role is instructive. In 2019, Mike Pence flew to Ankara and emerged with a cease-fire that Erdogan ignored, gaining territory and displacing tens of thousands of Kurds without paying a diplomatic price. Pence claimed it as a victory. The Kurds remember it differently.

Lebanon as the Second War Inside the First

Smoke rises from an explosion following an Israeli strike on Tyre, Lebanon / Adnan Abidi/Reuters

In the forty-eight hours before Vance landed in Islamabad, Israel struck more than two hundred targets in Lebanon affiliated with Hezbollah. The death toll in Lebanon from the renewed conflict stands at 1,953, including 357 killed in a single day of strikes on Wednesday — the day after the ceasefire was announced.

Lebanon and Israel do not maintain diplomatic relations. Their ambassadors to Washington agreed on Friday to meet in person on Tuesday — the first such direct engagement in half a century. They immediately issued contradictory statements about what they had agreed to. Lebanon said they were meeting to discuss announcing a ceasefire. Israel said it had agreed to begin peace negotiations and had refused to discuss a ceasefire. Same phone call. Same outcome. Two incompatible characterisations.

Netanyahu has vowed to continue striking Hezbollah “with full force.” Trump said Netanyahu would be “scaling back” operations. There has been no evidence of that. The Lebanese government has no control over Hezbollah. Hezbollah has dismissed the idea of talks between Beirut and Jerusalem. A Hezbollah official called the Lebanese government incapable of speaking for the group.

Nasr’s framing is exact: from Iran’s perspective, if Trump cannot make Netanyahu stand down in Lebanon, “at best that means the US is unable to control Bibi, which doesn’t give Iran much confidence. At worst, that means they can control him, and they have something else up their sleeve.” There is no reading of the Lebanon situation that augurs well for Islamabad.

What China Learned

While the delegations were assembling at the Serena Hotel, American intelligence agencies were tracking a separate development. China may have sent a shipment of shoulder-fired MANPADS to Iran — the assessment is not yet definitive, but the debate in Beijing about whether to send them is itself significant. Russia has already been providing Iranian forces with satellite targeting intelligence for strikes on American ships and military installations throughout the Middle East.

Simon Shuster’s analysis of what China absorbed from this war deserves to be read in full in any serious account of its consequences. A Taiwan blockade would hit global supply chains harder than Hormuz — 37 percent of world chip production against 20 percent of seaborne oil. The “silicon shield” argument, which held that American economic dependence on Taiwanese semiconductors would deter Chinese aggression, has been inverted. What Iran demonstrated is that an adversary far weaker than China can use supply chain leverage to force an American climbdown. The pain threshold has been measured. Beijing now has a model.

China is Iran’s largest trading partner. Chinese companies account for roughly 90 percent of Iranian oil exports. China has been supplying chemicals, fuel and dual-use components throughout the war. Its public posture is studious neutrality. Its private posture, increasingly, is something else.

The Psychotic State Negotiates

Jonathan Rauch and Peter Wehner, writing in the Times, offered the analytical frame that unlocks the Iran series in retrospect. The administration as a whole, they argue, lacks a consistent attachment to reality and the ability to organise its thinking coherently. This is not a clinical diagnosis of the president. It is a structural observation about governance. The policy review process — what they call “the government’s equivalent of the prefrontal cortex” — can be disrupted, circumvented, or abandoned at any moment on the president’s say-so. The result is institutional psychosis: not individual instability, but collective detachment from coherent decision-making.

The Iran war is their proof case. Goals changed by the sentence. Deadlines were issued and erased. Iran’s nuclear program was a casus belli in February, despite Trump having declared it “obliterated” last June. He called for an international coalition to open the strait, then said America could go it alone, then said it would “open itself.” Area experts were shocked when the administration proved unprepared for Hormuz’s closure — a contingency that experts had anticipated for decades, and that the administration’s own gutted State Department Iran desk and eliminated oil and gas expertise had left it unable to model.

On Saturday, while Vance sat in a room with Ghalibaf for the highest-level American-Iranian meeting since 1979, Trump posted on Truth Social that Iran was “LOSING, and LOSING BIG,” that their mine-dropper boats were “lying at the bottom of the sea,” and that the strait was being cleared — none of which could be confirmed.

When asked about a backup plan if talks collapsed, the president said: “You don’t need a backup plan. Their military is defeated. Their military is gone.”

Iran’s delegation in the next room numbered seventy people.

The Vietnam Frame, Honestly Applied

Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam (Thanh Hue / Getty)

Graeme Wood, writing for The Atlantic, extends Hasan Azizi’s Vietnam parallel into territory that is genuinely useful rather than merely provocative. The prediction that Iran will be America’s next Vietnam is, he argues, already circulating freely — including from the Iranian embassy in Hanoi, whose X account posted an AI-generated image of a Vietnamese soldier lecturing an American GI on the anniversary of Saigon’s fall.

The embassy’s choice of historical moment is revealing in ways it did not intend. Modern Saigon — Ho Chi Minh City — is a monument to consumer capitalism, American brands, and a comprehensive strategic partnership with the United States signed in 2023. Vietnam won the war and then ran toward America as fast as it could. The embassy is invoking a metaphor whose next fifty years disprove the point it is making.

But Wood’s serious argument is this: if the Vietnam parallel holds, it is less a curse than a relatively optimistic scenario. The sequence — American attack, American stymie, American withdrawal in denial, enemy celebration followed by confrontation with rubble — tracks the Iranian case in abbreviated form. What comes after the celebration, if Vietnam is the model, is isolation, poverty, abandonment by patrons, and eventual normalisation. Iran’s pre-existing conditions — economic catastrophe, ecological collapse, diplomatic isolation, social unrest — mean it may already have bottomed out faster than Vietnam did.

The crucial caveat is one that Wood makes honestly. Vietnam’s communism was the means to national independence. If Iran’s revolutionary Shiite Islamism is the end, and everything else is means, then the Vietnamese exit from the pit is unavailable. They would be, in Wood’s phrase, “unwilling to take the W.” That is the Parsi scenario by another name — not a negotiated resolution, but an ideology that cannot afford to let the war end on any terms that look like compromise.

What Monday Holds

Four hours. No joint statement as of Saturday night in Islamabad. The frozen assets question unresolved — Iranian state media reported an American concession; a US official denied it in careful language that did not close the door. The Strait of Hormuz is not open. Lebanon is still burning. Netanyahu has not scaled back. China may be arming Iran. Russia is providing targeting intelligence. The cease-fire clock has twelve days left.

Parsi’s non-negotiated status quo remains the baseline. Azizi’s Vietnam frame remains the best realistic outcome — grinding, partial, asymmetric, with the real work measured in years, not days. Sadjadpour’s incompatibility inventory has not been negotiated away. What Landler called the toll booth is operational, even if the floor is mined and the operator cannot find all the charges.

What happened in the Serena Hotel on Saturday afternoon is this: two countries that have not spoken face to face at this level in forty-seven years sat in the same room, with a Pakistani prime minister between them, and talked for four hours. That is not nothing. It is not resolution. It is, in the strictest sense, a beginning — which was also supposed to be true of every previous beginning, right up to the one in February that ended with American bombs falling on Tehran.

“We have goodwill,” Ghalibaf said at the Islamabad airport. “But we do not have trust.”

On that much, at least, both sides agree.

Twenty-one hours later, it was over — without an agreement.

Vance emerged from the Serena Hotel shortly before dawn on Sunday and held a brief press conference. “They have chosen not to accept our terms,” he said. He left Pakistan carrying what he called a “final and best offer” — a take-it-or-leave-it proposal whose contents he declined to specify. Air Force Two rolled for takeoff as the Islamabad Peace Talks billboards that had dotted the city were already being removed, as if, in Peltier’s dispatch from the scene, “tangible prospects of peace and Pakistan’s association with them are fading already.”

Vice President JD Vance arriving for a meeting with Pakistan’s prime minister, Shehbaz Sharif, in Islamabad, Pakistan, for peace talks about the Iran war on Saturday.Credit...Pool photo by Jacquelyn Martin

Three sticking points remained at the end, confirmed by Iranian officials: the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz; the fate of nearly 900 pounds of highly enriched uranium stored at Isfahan; and Iran’s demand that approximately $27 billion in frozen revenues held across seven countries be released for reconstruction. The nuclear gap, as Sanger of the Times laid out, was identical to the one that derailed Geneva in late February — which led Trump to order 38 days of bombing. Thirty-eight days of war appear to have hardened that position, not loosened it.

Iran’s supreme leader’s adviser posted that the key to the strait “is firmly in our hands.” Ghalibaf addressed American consumers directly: “Enjoy the current pump figures. Soon you’ll be nostalgic for $4–$5 gas.” As markets opened Sunday evening, Brent crude climbed back above $100. By Monday morning the blockade clock was running.

In the hours after the talks collapsed, Trump announced on Truth Social that the United States Navy would begin blockading the Strait of Hormuz — stopping all ships entering or leaving. CENTCOM then clarified that the actual blockade would apply to Iranian ports and coastal areas only, leaving non-Iranian transit unimpeded. Two different blockades, announced simultaneously by the same government. The Rogers note from the White House was understated but exact: a naval blockade is an act of war. Britain immediately said it would not join. Spain’s defense minister said it “makes no sense” and added, with weary precision: “Since this war started, nothing makes sense.” France and Britain announced they would organize their own separate 35-nation coalition for the strait — explicitly distinct from the American operation.

NATO has not fractured. It is fracturing, in real time, over a waterway whose floor is mined and whose mines nobody can locate.

Trump, watching UFC in Miami with Marco Rubio while Vance announced the impasse, told Fox News that gas prices “should be around the same” in November and might be “a little bit higher.” This was the political confession that ended the “short-term disruption” framing permanently. Republican strategists responded with the language of men watching a ceiling collapse. Then, Sunday night, the president turned his attention to Pope Leo XIV — accusing the first American pontiff of being “weak on crime,” of “catering to the radical left,” and of taking his position only because the Church needed someone to manage Trump. “If I wasn’t in the White House, Leo wouldn’t be in the Vatican,” the president wrote.

Leo, aboard a flight to Algeria on Monday morning, was asked about the post. “I have no fear of the Trump administration,” he said, “or speaking out loudly of the message of the Gospel, which is what I believe I am here to do.” Asked directly about Truth Social, he paused. “It’s ironic — the name of the site itself. Say no more.”

Parsi’s non-negotiated status quo is now the operating condition. The ceasefire holds — for eight more days. The Strait is blockaded by one side, mined by the other, and effectively closed to both. Lebanon’s death toll passed 2,020. Netanyahu’s thirteen-minute address did not mention Islamabad once. The Iranian foreign ministry issued its own statement as Vance headed for the airfield: “The heavy loss of our great elders, dear ones, and fellow countrymen has made our response to pursue the Iranian nation’s interests and rights firmer than ever before.”

The ratification did not happen. The blockade begins at 10 AM Eastern. The next eight days will determine whether Azizi’s Vietnam frame holds — a grinding, partial, asymmetric path toward something — or whether the toll booth, the mines, and the incompatible realities produce something none of the frameworks anticipated.

We will be here either way.
