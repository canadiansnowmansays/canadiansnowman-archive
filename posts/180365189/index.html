The Clemency Chronicles

Part 4

Joe Biden: When Family Becomes Policy

December 16, 2025

How a President with Sound Criminal Justice Instincts Crossed the Line

Joe Biden’s clemency record presents a paradox. On one hand, he followed Obama’s model—using presidential mercy primarily for criminal justice reform, granting commutations to thousands of non-violent offenders, and exercising clemency at historic scale for legitimate policy purposes.

On the other hand, Biden did something neither Bush nor Obama ever did: He pardoned his own son after repeatedly, explicitly promising he would not. Then, in his final minutes in office, he issued preemptive pardons to five family members who had never been charged with any crime.

This isn’t a case of “both sides do it.” Biden’s family pardons represent a categorical boundary violation that undermines his entire presidency’s commitment to restoring norms and respecting the rule of law.

The Numbers: Following Obama’s Lead

Joe Biden granted 4,245 clemency actions across his four years—the most of any president in history:

  • Approximately 4,165 commutations

  • 80+ pardons

The vast majority went to individuals convicted of non-violent offenses. On December 12, 2024, Biden commuted the sentences of 1,499 people and pardoned 39 convicted of non-violent offenses who had been released to home incarceration during COVID. On January 17, 2025, Biden commuted the sentences of 2,490 additional people.

This follows Obama’s model: systematic clemency for drug offenses and other non-violent crimes where sentences were disproportionate. Biden also issued a blanket pardon for all federal marijuana possession convictions, totaling approximately 6,500 people.

By these measures, Biden’s clemency record is defensible. He used presidential mercy for its traditional purpose: addressing unjust sentences, promoting rehabilitation, and implementing criminal justice reform policy.

But then came the family pardons.

The Hunter Biden Pardon: Breaking Explicit Promises

The Background

Hunter Biden, the President’s son, faced two federal prosecutions:

  • Gun charges (Delaware): Convicted in June 2024 of lying on a federal form when purchasing a gun in 2018 by falsely stating he wasn’t a drug user. He faced up to 25 years in prison.

  • Tax charges (California): Pleaded guilty in September 2024 to nine tax-related charges including three felonies, for failing to pay at least $1.4 million in federal taxes. He faced up to 17 years in prison and $1.35 million in fines.

Both prosecutions stemmed from a period when Hunter struggled with crack cocaine addiction following his brother Beau’s death. Both were brought by Special Counsel David Weiss, appointed during the Trump administration.

Hunter was scheduled for sentencing in December 2024. Federal sentencing guidelines would likely have resulted in far less than the maximum sentences, and it was possible he could have avoided prison entirely given his rehabilitation, cooperation, and lack of prior criminal record.

The Promises

Throughout his presidency, Biden repeatedly and explicitly promised he would not pardon his son:

  • June 2024 (after Hunter’s gun conviction): Asked if he would pardon Hunter, Biden said: “I will not pardon him.”

  • Multiple other occasions: Biden and White House officials repeatedly stated the President would not interfere with the Justice Department’s decisions regarding his son.

These weren’t vague statements subject to interpretation. They were categorical promises made to the American public.

The Pardon

On December 1, 2024—less than a month before leaving office—Biden announced he had signed a full and unconditional pardon for Hunter Biden.

The pardon wasn’t limited to the gun and tax convictions. It covered “any offenses against the United States which he has committed or may have committed or taken part in during the period from January 1, 2014 through December 1, 2024”—an extraordinary eleven-year blanket coverage.

Biden’s justification:

“I believe in the justice system, but as I have wrestled with this, I also believe raw politics has infected this process and it led to a miscarriage of justice... In trying to break Hunter, they’ve tried to break me—and there’s no reason to believe it will stop here. Enough is enough.”

Biden claimed Hunter was “treated differently” and “selectively and unfairly prosecuted.” He argued that without aggravating factors, people are “almost never” charged with felonies solely for how they filled out a gun form, and those late paying taxes due to addiction typically receive “noncriminal resolutions.”

The Reality Check

CNN conducted a comprehensive fact-check of Biden’s claims and found them false and inaccurate:

Biden’s claim about gun charges: Biden said “no one’s ever been tried on that. Nobody.” This is false. Hunter’s own legal team only argued “no one has ever been charged in Delaware” under these circumstances—a much narrower claim. Legal experts noted charges are usually brought alongside more serious offenses, but Hunter’s case wasn’t unprecedented.

Biden’s claim about tax charges: Biden insisted Hunter “had paid all his taxes,” suggesting this was new information he discovered. But this was widely known long before the pardon. Hunter hadn’t paid all taxes at the time of his crimes—he only paid them back after prosecution began, and only after pressure from investigators.

The timing claim: Biden said these factors were things “I found out” recently. But both issues were public knowledge throughout the prosecution.

Public reaction was overwhelmingly negative. An AP-NORC poll found only 2 in 10 Americans supported the pardon.

Applying Our Framework to Hunter

1. Connection to President: The strongest possible—his son.

2. Nature of Crime: Gun charges (lying on federal form) and tax evasion. Serious but non-violent crimes. However, the tax evasion involved $1.4 million over multiple years while living lavishly—not a momentary lapse.

3. Victim Impact:

  • Gun charges: No direct victim; Hunter never used the gun in a crime

  • Tax charges: The government/taxpayers—though restitution was eventually paid

4. Stage of Justice: Before sentencing. Hunter had been convicted/pleaded guilty but not yet received his punishment.

5. Process: Presidential initiative without DOJ consultation. Biden made the decision personally, reportedly during Thanksgiving weekend with family.

6. Timing: December 2024, one month before leaving office, after losing the 2024 election—when politically unaccountable.

7. Justification: Claims of selective prosecution and disproportionate treatment—claims that were demonstrably false or misleading.

Why the Hunter Pardon Is Indefensible

This isn’t a close call:

1. Biden Broke His Word: He made explicit, repeated promises not to pardon Hunter, then did exactly what he promised not to do. This wasn’t a change in circumstances—it was a broken promise.

2. The Breadth Is Unprecedented: An eleven-year blanket pardon covering any federal crimes Hunter “may have committed” goes far beyond addressing the specific convictions. It suggests concern about potential undiscovered crimes.

3. The Justifications Were False: Biden’s claims about why Hunter was treated differently don’t withstand scrutiny. This undermines the entire rationale.

4. The Timing Was Calculated: Biden waited until after losing the election, eliminating any political accountability for the decision.

5. It’s Naked Nepotism: There’s no principled distinction here. If Hunter’s name were “John Smith,” he wouldn’t have received this pardon.

Compare this to Bush refusing Cheney’s pleas to fully pardon Libby—his own Vice President’s Chief of Staff. Bush maintained a boundary even when it damaged a critical relationship. Biden obliterated the boundary for his son.

The Self-Protective Element

The 11-year blanket coverage reveals another uncomfortable dimension beyond simple nepotism.

The pardon doesn’t just shield Hunter from the gun and tax charges he faced—it explicitly protects him from any potential charges related to his foreign business dealings during 2014-2024. This period encompasses:

  • Hunter’s board position at Burisma (Ukrainian energy company) while Joe Biden was vice president

  • Hunter’s business relationships in China

  • The laptop contents Republicans were investigating

  • Potential FARA (Foreign Agents Registration Act) violations

  • Potential influence-peddling charges

Republicans were actively investigating whether Hunter’s foreign business dealings involved Joe Biden or traded on his vice presidency. The 11-year blanket preemptively ends those investigations by making Hunter immune from any charges that might emerge.

This makes Biden’s pardon both nepotistic AND self-protective. Biden wasn’t just protecting his son from consequences—he was protecting himself and his family from potential investigations into conduct that occurred while he held the second-highest office in the land.

This is a more serious corruption than simple family favoritism. It’s using presidential power to close ongoing investigations into your own family’s conduct during your prior government service.

The Family Pardons: An Unprecedented Expansion

If the Hunter pardon was indefensible, what came next was astonishing.

On January 20, 2025—Biden’s final minutes in office, literally as Trump was being inaugurated—Biden issued preemptive pardons for:

  • His brother James Biden and James’s wife Sara

  • His sister Valerie Biden Owens and her husband John Owens

  • His brother Francis Biden

These weren’t pardons for crimes they’d been convicted of or even charged with. They were preemptive pardons—protection against potential future prosecution.

Biden’s justification:

“My family has been subjected to unrelenting attacks and threats, motivated solely by a desire to hurt me—the worst kind of partisan politics. Unfortunately, I have no reason to believe these attacks will end.”

He argued that “baseless and politically motivated investigations wreak havoc on the lives, safety, and financial security of targeted individuals and their families” even when people “have done nothing wrong.”

The Context

House Republicans had investigated Biden family business dealings and sent a letter to the Justice Department in June 2024 recommending prosecution of Hunter and James Biden for allegedly making false statements to Congress during the impeachment inquiry.

James Biden’s lawyer called it a “baseless partisan action.” James Biden had testified that his brother Joe “never had any involvement” in family business dealings.

But Republicans pointed to various financial transactions and business relationships they claimed warranted investigation.

Why This Is Unprecedented

Presidential family pardons aren’t entirely new:

  • Bill Clinton pardoned his half-brother Roger Clinton in 2001, after Roger had served his sentence for drug charges roughly a decade earlier

  • Trump pardoned Charles Kushner (his son-in-law’s father) for crimes Kushner had already been convicted of and served time for

But Biden’s family pardons are different in kind:

1. They’re Preemptive: These family members hadn’t been charged with anything. The pardons prevent potential future prosecutions.

2. There Are Five of Them: This isn’t one family member—it’s wholesale protection for multiple relatives.

3. The Timing: Literally in the final minutes before leaving office, as Trump was being inaugurated.

4. They Follow the Hunter Pardon: Coming after breaking his promise on Hunter, these pardons establish a pattern: Biden will use clemency power to protect his family, period.

The Other Protective Pardons

On January 20, 2025—his final day in office—Biden issued preemptive pardons to:

  • Dr. Anthony Fauci (former director of NIAID)

  • General Mark Milley (former Chairman of Joint Chiefs of Staff)

  • Members of the January 6 Committee (Representatives Liz Cheney, Bennie Thompson, Adam Kinzinger, and others)

Biden framed these as “protective” pardons—shielding public servants from threatened prosecution by an incoming Trump administration.

The justification had merit: Trump and his allies had explicitly threatened to prosecute these individuals for their roles investigating January 6 or managing COVID-19 policy. The pardons could be seen as defensive—protecting people who served the public from politically motivated revenge.

But let’s be clear about what they are: These are political pardons protecting Biden administration allies and Trump critics from future prosecution. They establish a precedent that a president can preemptively pardon anyone they believe might be politically targeted—before any charges, before any investigation, before any evidence.

Apply the same framework we use for Trump:

  1. Connection: All Biden allies, all Trump critics, all served Biden’s political interests

  2. Nature of “Crime”: None—no charges filed, pure preemptive protection

  3. Victim Impact: N/A (no crime committed)

  4. Stage of Justice: Before any investigation or charges

  5. Process: Presidential decree, no DOJ review

  6. Timing: Last day in office, maximum political protection

  7. Justification: “Protecting public servants from political revenge”

The distinction from Trump’s fake-elector pardons:

  • Biden’s protect people who testified against Trump about real crimes

  • Trump’s protect people who conspired with Trump to overturn an election

But both are using clemency to protect political allies preemptively. Biden’s have more sympathetic recipients, but the principle—preemptive political protection—is the same.

If Trump can point to Biden’s Fauci/Milley/Cheney pardons to justify his own preemptive pardons, Biden handed him that precedent.

Applying Our Framework to Family Pardons

1. Connection to President: Immediate family—the strongest possible connection.

2. Nature of Crime: None. They haven’t been charged with anything.

3. Victim Impact: N/A—no convictions, though investigations were examining business dealings.

4. Stage of Justice: Before any charges, before any investigation completed—the earliest possible intervention.

5. Process: Presidential initiative in final minutes of presidency.

6. Timing: Literally as Trump was being inaugurated—maximum political unaccountability.

7. Justification: Protecting family from “politically motivated” investigations—but this assumes investigations would be baseless before they’ve even begun.

What Biden’s Record Reveals

Biden’s clemency record reveals a troubling split:

The Good:

  • Systematic Criminal Justice Reform: 4,000+ commutations primarily for drug offenses and COVID-related releases

  • Policy Consistency: Following Obama’s model of addressing sentencing disparities

  • Marijuana Pardons: Blanket pardon for federal possession convictions

  • Process for Non-Family Cases: Generally followed traditional DOJ review for non-family clemency

The Indefensible:

  • Hunter Pardon: Broke explicit promises, used false justifications, covered eleven years of potential crimes

  • Family Pardons: Preemptive protection for five family members never charged

  • Timing: Waited until politically unaccountable (after election loss, final hours in office)

  • Pattern: Established that family protection trumps all other considerations

The Comparison

Let’s apply our measuring stick from Bush and Obama:

Did Biden protect people more connected than Libby was to Bush? Yes—he pardoned his son and five other family members. Libby was Cheney’s Chief of Staff; Hunter is Biden’s son. There is no closer connection.

Did Biden go further than Bush went? Yes—Bush commuted Libby’s sentence but left conviction intact. Biden granted full pardons with sweeping language.

Did Biden systematically pardon associates? For family, yes—six family members. For others (Fauci, Milley, Jan 6 Committee), arguably yes, though these are more defensible as protective measures.

Did Biden create systems that monetized clemency? No—there’s no evidence of cash-for-pardons.

Did Biden’s clemency erase victim restitution? For the drug offense commutations, generally no—most had served substantial time. For Hunter, the tax restitution was paid (though only after prosecution began).

Where Biden Fits

Biden’s record is paradoxical:

  • Better than Trump (as we’ll document in the next essays) because he didn’t systematize corruption, didn’t create pardons-for-profit, didn’t pardon thousands of violent criminals

  • Worse than Bush and Obama because he violated the family boundary they respected

Biden had legitimate criminal justice reform accomplishments. His bulk clemency for drug offenders was appropriate. His marijuana pardons were sound policy.

But the family pardons are a stain that undermines everything else. They demonstrate that when family interests conflicted with principles, Biden chose family.

This matters because Biden campaigned on restoring norms and respecting the rule of law. He positioned himself as the antidote to Trump’s norm-breaking. Yet when it came to his own family, Biden engaged in precisely the kind of self-serving clemency he implicitly criticized in others.

The Uncomfortable Truth

A president who claims the justice system is fair and equal, who says “no one is above the law,” who promises repeatedly not to pardon his son—and then does exactly that—has told the American people their principles are for other people’s children, not his own.

Biden could have let the justice system work. Hunter likely would have received relatively lenient sentences given his rehabilitation, cooperation, and lack of prior record. Instead, Biden ensured Hunter faced no punishment at all and protected him from any potential future charges for an eleven-year period.

And then Biden extended that protection to five other family members.

This isn’t criminal justice reform. This is nepotism.

In the next two essays, we’ll examine whether Trump’s clemency record is better, worse, or comparable. But Biden’s family pardons have already established this much: When a president protects family from accountability, regardless of party, it corrupts the clemency power.

The question now is: If Biden—a president with generally sound instincts about justice—couldn’t resist using clemency to protect his family, what happens when a president with no such instincts has the power?
