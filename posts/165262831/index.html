The Erosion of Checks and Balances

Recent Actions of the Three Branches and the Looming Threat of a Constitutional Crisis

June 5, 2025

Constitutional crisis visualized: Executive dominance overshadows weakened institutional representatives against a backdrop of foundational documents, with cracking facades and underlying fire symbolizing the systematic breakdown of checks and balances and the erosion of democratic governance

Abstract

The U.S. Constitution established a delicate balance of power among the legislative, executive, and judicial branches through an intricate system of checks and balances designed to prevent any single branch from dominating governance. However, recent developments under the Trump administration's second term—including unprecedented assertions of executive prosecutorial immunity, systematic defiance of judicial orders, the weaponization of emergency powers for funding freezes, and Congress's alarming inability to fulfill its constitutional oversight role—have brought the nation to the precipice of a full constitutional crisis. This paper examines how these coordinated attacks on institutional guardrails threaten the fundamental principles of democratic governance and the rule of law. Drawing on current events, legal precedents, and constitutional scholarship, this analysis demonstrates that we are witnessing not merely isolated instances of executive overreach, but a systematic dismantling of the constitutional order that the Founding Fathers carefully constructed to preserve liberty and prevent tyranny.

Introduction

The framers of the Constitution, deeply informed by their experience under British rule and their study of failed republics throughout history, designed a government structure explicitly intended to prevent the concentration of power in any single institution. "The framers were acutely aware of competing interests, and they had great distrust of concentrated authority," explains Dartmouth professor John Carey, an expert on American democracy. James Madison's warning in Federalist No. 51 that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" reflected their understanding that only through institutional rivalry could liberty be preserved.

Yet today, scholars and constitutional experts are observing what David Gans of the Constitutional Accountability Center describes as the Trump administration's systematic violation of constitutional principles, creating what many view as an active constitutional crisis. Harvard Kennedy School faculty members have characterized the first weeks of Trump's second administration as potentially "the most severe attack on the rule of law in the United States since confederate armed forces began lobbing artillery shells into Fort Sumter in 1861."

The constitutional crisis now unfolding is not theoretical—it is measurable, observable, and accelerating. According to a February 2025 Quinnipiac University poll, 54% of U.S. voters expressed dissatisfaction with the current checks and balances system, while AP News has tracked nearly 180 lawsuits from states and national organizations challenging the Trump administration's various executive orders on constitutional grounds. This unprecedented level of legal resistance signals not routine political disagreement, but fundamental challenges to constitutional governance itself.

Section 1: Executive Branch Overreach - The Systematic Concentration of Power

1.1 The Assertion of Prosecutorial Immunity: Placing the Executive Above the Law

The most direct assault on constitutional governance has come through the Justice Department's unprecedented claim of prosecutorial immunity from judicial oversight. In April 2025, Attorney General Pam Bondi filed court documents asserting that only the Executive Branch can prosecute its own criminal contempt, effectively placing the administration above judicial review. This position represents a fundamental inversion of constitutional principles established in landmark cases like United States v. Nixon (1974), which explicitly rejected claims of unlimited executive privilege.

Bondi's legal theory goes beyond traditional executive privilege claims. It asserts that the executive branch can ignore judicial rulings by refusing to enforce them—a position that constitutional scholars describe as authoritarian. As legal experts note, this interpretation "guts accountability and the Constitution's balance of power" by removing any external check on executive behavior.

This isn't merely abstract legal theory. The practical implications became starkly apparent in the administration's systematic defiance of court orders across multiple policy areas, from immigration enforcement to federal spending. The message is clear: the executive will comply with judicial orders only when it chooses to do so.

1.2 The Human Face of Constitutional Breakdown: Kilmar Ábrego García

The constitutional crisis isn't abstract—it has a human face, and that face belongs to Kilmar Ábrego García, a Maryland construction worker whose case has become the defining symbol of executive power run completely amok.

On March 12, 2025, García was driving with his 5-year-old disabled son when ICE agents arrested him outside a Baltimore IKEA. They gave his wife Jennifer—a U.S. citizen—exactly ten minutes to pick up their child before threatening to turn the boy over to Child Protective Services. Three days later, García was on a plane to El Salvador's notorious CECOT mega-prison, despite a 2019 federal court order explicitly prohibiting his deportation.

García had fled El Salvador as a teenager when gang members threatened to kill his family and force him to join their ranks. A U.S. immigration judge granted him "withholding of removal" status after determining his life would be threatened if he returned. For six years, he lived legally in Maryland, learned English, became a skilled sheet metal worker, and helped raise three children—including two with special needs.

The Trump administration admits García's deportation was "an administrative error." But here's where it gets constitutionally terrifying: they refuse to bring him back.

On April 10, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that García's deportation was illegal and ordered the administration to "facilitate" his return. Justice Sonia Sotomayor warned that the government's position means "it could deport and incarcerate any person, including U.S. citizens, without legal consequence, so long as it does so before a court can intervene."

The administration's response? They filed motions to dismiss the case, argued that federal courts have no jurisdiction once someone is deported, and claimed they have no obligation to fix their own "error." Meanwhile, García remains locked in isolation in a Salvadoran prison, separated from his wife and children.

The Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals issued a scathing rebuke: "The government is asserting a right to stash away residents of this country in foreign prisons without the semblance of due process that is the foundation of our constitutional order."

García's case exemplifies Bondi's prosecutorial immunity theory in action: when the executive makes an "error" that violates court orders, it claims the exclusive right to decide whether and how to remedy that error, effectively placing itself above judicial review.

1.3 The $430 Billion Hostage Crisis: Weaponizing Congressional Spending Authority

What may be the most devastating assault on constitutional governance is happening in plain sight: Trump has blocked, frozen, or cancelled at least $430 billion in federal funding that Congress specifically allocated to American communities. To put this in perspective, that's more money than the entire GDP of Ireland—money that was supposed to fund cancer research, disaster relief, infrastructure projects, and healthcare for millions of Americans.

This represents a frontal attack on Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution, which explicitly grants Congress the "power of the purse." The administration's actions violate the 1974 Impoundment Control Act, which was passed specifically to prevent presidents from withholding congressionally appropriated funds for policy reasons after President Nixon's similar abuse of power.

Russell Vought, Trump's OMB director and a key architect of Project 2025, has openly declared the Impoundment Control Act unconstitutional. The administration's message is clear: We will decide what Congress meant when it passed spending bills, regardless of what Congress actually said.

The human cost is staggering. The administration has frozen funding for:

  • Critical medical research into Alzheimer's disease, cancer, and diabetes, literally throwing completed research "into the shredder"

  • Disaster relief for communities still recovering from hurricanes and wildfires

  • School meal programs feeding hungry children

  • Infrastructure projects creating jobs in every state

  • Healthcare programs serving 70 million low-income Americans through Medicaid

When the administration first attempted its sweeping funding freeze in January 2025, the immediate chaos was staggering. Doctors in all 50 states couldn't access Medicaid payments. Public housing authorities were locked out of their funding portals. Head Start programs serving 800,000 at-risk children faced immediate shutdown.

Even after federal courts blocked the freeze, the administration has used creative workarounds to maintain control. In Phoenix, healthcare provider Jessyca Leach had to lay off three employees and cut leadership salaries when her clinic serving LGBTQ+ and low-income patients was cut off from federal funding—even after court orders supposedly restored the money.

1.4 The Project 2025 Blueprint: Systematic Dismantling of Constitutional Constraints

These actions aren't random policy disputes—they're part of a coordinated plan. Project 2025, largely authored by figures now serving in the Trump administration, explicitly calls for dismantling the constitutional constraints on executive power. The project advocates for "restoring the President's authority to impound funds" and eliminating what it calls "unaccountable federal spending."

As constitutional scholars at the Center for American Progress warn, Project 2025 seeks to "quickly shatter laws, norms, guardrails, and other components that comprise the United States' traditional system of checks and balances and separation of powers without any reasonable structural methods to constrain a president."

The blueprint includes:

  • Reviving presidential impoundment to override Congressional spending decisions

  • Dismantling the career civil service through Schedule F reinstatement

  • Eliminating independent agency protections by asserting "unitary executive" theory

  • Weaponizing emergency powers to bypass normal constitutional constraints

This isn't governance—it's the systematic construction of what scholars call an "imperial presidency" that operates outside constitutional constraints.

Section 2: Congressional Abdication - The Collapse of Legislative Authority

2.1 From Separation of Powers to Separation of Parties

Harvard Kennedy School professor Benjamin Schneer identifies the current crisis as reflecting a fundamental shift from the Constitution's intended "separation of powers" to what he calls "separation of parties." This transformation has "been amplified since Trump's return to the White House, as Congress has largely relinquished its role as a meaningful check on the executive."

The Constitution assumed that institutional self-interest would drive each branch to defend its prerogatives against encroachment. But when partisan loyalty trumps institutional loyalty, the system breaks down. "What they weren't counting on was lockstep partisanship," explains Barbara Perry, a presidential historian at the University of Virginia's Miller Center.

2.2 Republican Congressional Complicity

The constitutional crisis has been exacerbated by what scholars describe as "lockstep partisanship" in Congress, where Republican majorities have largely abandoned their constitutional oversight responsibilities in favor of partisan loyalty to Trump. Even when presented with clear constitutional violations, such as Trump's attempts to eliminate birthright citizenship through executive order or his systematic defiance of court orders, Republican Congressional leadership has largely remained silent.

Georgetown University law professor David Super observes that Trump is "clearly making a very aggressive move to expand presidential powers at the expense of the other two branches of government," while Congress fails to respond.

The few instances of bipartisan resistance reveal both the potential for constitutional defense and its current weakness. A Senate vote on tariff reduction failed 49-49, demonstrating that even clear economic harm from executive overreach cannot overcome partisan divisions. As one Republican representative noted, "We have got to follow the law," but such voices remain marginalized within their own party.

2.3 The Consequences of Legislative Paralysis

Congress's failure to exercise its constitutional powers has created a cascading effect that undermines the entire system. When the legislative branch cannot fulfill its oversight role, the executive operates without meaningful constraint, and the judiciary becomes the sole check on executive power—a role it was never designed to play alone.

The Constitution gives Congress multiple tools to constrain executive overreach:

  • Impeachment for high crimes and misdemeanors

  • Budget authority to control federal spending

  • Oversight powers including subpoenas and investigations

  • Legislative nullification of executive policies

Congress's refusal to use these tools in the face of systematic constitutional violations represents what scholars call "institutional abdication"—the voluntary surrender of constitutional authority that the Framers never anticipated.

Section 3: The Judiciary Under Siege - Courts as the Last Stand

3.1 Judicial Authority Under Attack

The federal judiciary finds itself in an impossible position: serving as the sole remaining check on executive power while facing systematic defiance and political attacks. President Trump has called for the impeachment of judges who rule against him, claiming authority to determine which judicial orders his administration will follow. This prompted Chief Justice John Roberts to issue a rare public rebuke of presidential attacks on federal judges.

The administration's legal philosophy appears to be, as Harvard scholars note, that "if the administration does something, that alone makes it legal." This represents a fundamental rejection of the rule of law in favor of what legal scholars recognize as authoritarian governance.

3.2 The Limits of Judicial Power

Even when federal courts issue clear orders, they face the fundamental limitation that they "have few tools to actually make a president do something." As NPR's constitutional analysis notes, "if a president succeeds in defying the court, the whole system of government, and of checks and balances, would be in peril."

The García case exemplifies this vulnerability. Despite unanimous Supreme Court rulings, the administration continues to resist compliance through legal technicalities and procedural delays. The Fourth Circuit's plea reveals judicial awareness that constitutional governance now depends not on institutional structures, but on executive self-restraint—a dependency the Framers specifically sought to avoid.

3.3 Institutional Capture and Political Compromise

The Supreme Court itself has become part of the crisis through political capture. The Court has "lurched sharply rightward, now controlled by six extreme justices whose judicial philosophy often seems dictated by what would most empower the radically conservative, pro-corporate, pro-Christian agenda."

This creates a scenario where "the conservative majority of the Supreme Court could deflect the charge of being a Republican rubber stamp while also avoiding a full-fledged confrontation with the executive branch" by selectively enforcing constitutional principles. Such selective enforcement could "permanently diminish the power of Congress" while appearing to maintain judicial independence.

Section 4: States Fighting Back - The Constitutional Resistance

While Congress has largely abdicated its constitutional role, something remarkable is happening at the state level: a massive legal resistance movement that represents the broadest challenge to federal executive power in modern American history.

4.1 180 Lawsuits: A Historic Constitutional Rebellion

According to AP News tracking, nearly 180 separate legal challenges have been filed by states and national organizations against Trump administration actions on constitutional grounds. This isn't normal political disagreement—this is states essentially declaring that the federal executive has gone rogue.

California led the charge early, with Governor Gavin Newsom directing his administration to pursue legal action challenging federal tariffs that economists predict will cost the average American household $3,800 annually. Newsom's legal team is fighting to exempt California-made products from retaliatory measures, arguing the federal government is exceeding its constitutional authority.

Texas and Florida—typically Trump-friendly states—have found themselves in court opposing federal overreach when it affects their own programs and residents. Even in red states, federal funding cuts to disaster relief and infrastructure have prompted bipartisan opposition.

New York and the Northeast Coalition have filed some of the most aggressive challenges, particularly around federal spending freezes that have devastated healthcare systems and social services in urban areas.

The scope is staggering: states are challenging everything from birthright citizenship executive orders to federal funding freezes to immigration enforcement tactics. What makes this historically significant is that it cuts across traditional red-blue lines when constitutional principles are at stake.

4.2 Federalism as Constitutional Firewall

This level of state-federal legal conflict hasn't been seen since the Civil Rights era—or arguably since the lead-up to the Civil War itself. When states start systematically challenging federal executive authority in court, it signals a breakdown in normal constitutional governance.

Legal scholars note that the diversity of challenges—spanning immigration, healthcare, education, environmental policy, and fiscal authority—suggests the problem isn't any single policy disagreement, but a fundamental breach of constitutional norms by the federal executive.

The state resistance may be the last institutional check standing between American democracy and complete executive supremacy.

Section 5: What Happens Next - Scenarios for Constitutional Collapse or Recovery

The question isn't whether we're in a constitutional crisis anymore—it's how much deeper we're going to fall and whether the system can recover. Harvard Kennedy School analysis suggests that "if we are not yet in a constitutional crisis (one in which there are no constitutional rules to guide the resolution to a conflict), we're close enough to feel its approaching headwinds."

5.1 The Authoritarian Scenario

If current trends continue unchecked, constitutional scholars warn of a progression toward executive supremacy that would fundamentally end American democracy as we know it:

Phase 1: Complete Judicial Nullification - The administration continues defying court orders until federal judges lose all practical authority. Courts become advisory bodies with no enforcement power.

Phase 2: Congressional Irrelevance - With control over spending and the elimination of meaningful oversight, Congress becomes a ceremonial body. The executive governs through emergency declarations and executive orders.

Phase 3: Emergency Powers Expansion - Using claims of "invasion" and national emergency, the administration could potentially suspend habeas corpus, impose martial law in selected areas, and eliminate remaining civil liberties protections.

The administration has already laid groundwork for this: Trump has called immigrants an "invasion," claimed authority to ignore spending laws, and demanded impeachment of judges who rule against him. Legal scholars warn that each step makes the next one easier.

5.2 The Constitutional Resistance Scenario

Alternatively, the unprecedented state and civil society pushback could force a constitutional reckoning:

State Nullification of Federal Overreach - If enough states refuse to cooperate with unconstitutional federal demands, it could create a "federalism firewall" that limits executive power.

Congressional Awakening - The 2026 midterm elections could produce a Congress willing to use its constitutional powers, including impeachment, budget authority, and oversight.

Judicial Backbone - Federal courts could begin using contempt powers and other enforcement mechanisms more aggressively, potentially leading to a direct confrontation that forces resolution.

5.3 The Economic Pressure Scenario

The $430 billion spending freeze and arbitrary policy decisions could trigger economic consequences that force political change:

Market Panic - Continued uncertainty over basic government functions and policy could spark investor flight and economic instability.

State Budget Crises - As federal funding disappears, states face impossible choices between raising taxes dramatically or cutting essential services.

Public Backlash - When cancer research stops, disaster relief disappears, and school meal programs end, public opinion could shift dramatically against executive overreach.

5.4 The Point of No Return

Constitutional scholars warn there may be a "point of no return" where democratic norms are so thoroughly shattered that peaceful restoration becomes impossible. Harvard scholars suggest we may be approaching that point, warning of "the most obvious form of constitutional crisis that could arise—in which the president refused to respect the decisions of the court."

The next six months could determine whether America remains a constitutional republic or becomes something else entirely.

Conclusion

The United States stands at a constitutional crossroads. The systematic erosion of checks and balances under the current administration represents not merely aggressive governance, but a fundamental challenge to the constitutional order itself. As Harvard scholars warn, this "may well constitute the most severe attack on the rule of law in the United States since" the Civil War.

The evidence is overwhelming:

  • Executive supremacy through prosecutorial immunity claims and systematic defiance of judicial orders

  • Congressional abdication due to partisan loyalty overriding institutional responsibility

  • Judicial isolation as courts become the sole remaining check on unlimited executive power

  • $430 billion in blocked funding representing the largest seizure of congressional spending authority in modern history

  • 180 lawsuits from states indicating unprecedented federal-state constitutional conflict

The question is no longer whether we are approaching a constitutional crisis, but how deep into one we have already fallen. The Framers' system of checks and balances was designed to be self-correcting, but only when institutions and actors operate within constitutional constraints. When those constraints are systematically rejected, as they are being today, the system cannot function as designed.

The preservation of American democracy now depends on the willingness of courts to enforce their orders, of Congress to reclaim its constitutional role, and of the American people to demand accountability from their representatives. As the Fourth Circuit observed in the García ruling, this crisis "may present an opportunity as well" to "vindicate the rule of law" and "summon the best that is within us while there is still time."

The choice before the nation is stark: restore constitutional governance through institutional resistance and democratic accountability, or accept the transformation of the American system from a constitutional republic into something far more dangerous to liberty and democratic values. The window for peaceful, legal resolution of this crisis remains open, but it will not remain so indefinitely.

As constitutional scholar David Gans emphasizes, "We're in a period of conflict and struggle over our constitutional system, and the question is not only for lawyers and for courts, but for everyone. All people have an important say in this fight over the meaning, the enforcement, whether our constitutional promises will stand firm."

The stakes could not be higher. The Constitution, democracy, and the rule of law itself hang in the balance.
