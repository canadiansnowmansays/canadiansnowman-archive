The Counterfactual President

The Truman Show Presidency

Introduction: When Reality Becomes Unwatchable

January 20, 2026

January 20, 2026

There’s a moment in The Truman Show when Jim Carrey’s character realizes something is profoundly wrong. The world around him is too scripted, too controlled, too immune to normal cause and effect. He’s not living in reality—he’s trapped in someone else’s production. And the most unsettling part isn’t the deception itself. It’s that everyone else seems fine with it.

Welcome to year one of Trump’s second term.

Somewhere between the classified documents case dismissed, the January 6th pardons issued, the career prosecutors purged, the NATO allies threatened, the Ukraine aid cut, and the systematic dismantling of every democratic institution—we’ve spent a year watching the threshold crossed. We’ve left the world where actions have consequences and fully entered a reality show where the villain not only doesn’t get caught, he’s been given four more years to ensure he never will be.

Ernst Stavro Blofeld (Glenn Wrage)

If you feel like you’re watching a bad James Bond movie where Blofeld explains his scheme, commits the crimes on camera, admits to them proudly, and then wins—you’re not losing your mind. You’re experiencing clarity in an insane situation.

And here’s what I’ve discovered over this first year: This feeling is universal.

It’s not just Democrats asking the question. It’s Republicans in boardrooms navigating the economic chaos Trump’s tariff wars have created. Allied leaders in NATO capitals who’ve spent a year watching alliance commitments crumble. Business executives worldwide who’ve spent twelve months unable to plan when rules change based on Truth Social posts. Billions of ordinary people watching the supposed leader of the free world accelerate democratic collapse.

They’re all asking the same two questions:

How can Trump be stopped from the aggression and bullying we’ve watched for a year—domestically and internationally?

And more quietly, almost like a prayer:

What would the world look like if Kamala Harris had been elected President a year ago?

The Methodology of the Counterfactual

This is not “wouldn’t everything be perfect if only...” Harris would have faced enormous challenges—divided Congress, entrenched interests, global instability. She would have made mistakes, disappointed supporters, faced legitimate criticism.

But here’s what we can examine with rigor now that we have a year of Trump’s governance to assess:

What we know: Trump’s actual first-year record. The policies implemented. The institutions damaged. The alliances strained. The norms shattered. The consequences playing out.

What we can reasonably project: How Harris’s documented record—her voting as Senator, her positions as Vice President, her stated policy commitments, her approach to governance—would have produced different outcomes across these same twelve months.

What remains speculative: The precise unfolding of events under Harris. Specific crisis responses. Unforeseen challenges unique to her presidency.

What Harris would have struggled with in her first year: Progressive versus moderate tensions within her own party producing legislative gridlock. Congressional Republicans obstructing every initiative. Business and conservative opposition mobilizing against any progressive agenda. International crises forcing impossible tradeoffs. The inevitable gap between campaign promises and political reality. Media criticism from all sides.

The goal isn’t fantasy where Harris solves all problems in year one. The goal is examining—with evidence from the actual year we’ve just experienced—what specific governance patterns would have been absent under different leadership, and what different patterns would have emerged instead. Better versus worse, not perfect versus flawed.

Think of it as forensic analysis of the divergence point. On November 5, 2024, reality split. We took one path and have now lived a full year down that road. We can examine what the other path would have looked like by comparing Trump’s documented first-year record against Harris’s documented philosophy and approach to governance.

Why This Question Transcends Partisanship After One Year

I’m Canadian, writing from Toronto with a vantage point outside American tribal warfare. My family history runs through Canadian-American diplomatic cooperation—my relative Hugh Keenleyside served alongside Lester Pearson and acted as intermediary between Churchill and Roosevelt during World War II. I understand how profoundly American leadership choices affect the entire democratic world.

What I’ve watched this past year terrifies me—not as a partisan, but as someone who understands how quickly institutional norms collapse when the wrong person gains power.

Republican business leaders have spent a year navigating an economy where tariffs have been imposed based on personal grudges, where regulatory certainty has been replaced by imperial whim, where planning beyond the next quarter is impossible.

NATO allies have spent a year conducting contingency planning for American withdrawal from alliance commitments while Trump has openly questioned Article 5 and praised Putin.

Career intelligence professionals worldwide have spent a year recalibrating what information they can safely share after watching American intelligence leadership purged for insufficient loyalty.

Judges and prosecutors in democracies everywhere have watched a year of systematic destruction of American judicial independence and are wondering how to maintain rule of law when the global hegemon has abandoned it.

Ordinary citizens in every nation with skin in the democratic game have spent a year asking: If America fell to authoritarian capture, what happens to the rest of us?

This isn’t about Democratic versus Republican policy preferences after one year of governance. This is about whether democratic governance as a model can survive when its most powerful exemplar has spent a year being recaptured by someone who views democracy itself as an obstacle to personal power.

The Two Questions Driving This Series

Question 1: How can Trump be stopped?

After a full year back in power, this question has only become more urgent. We’ve watched the accommodation cascade accelerate. We’ve seen institutions capitulate. We’ve witnessed the TACO pattern (Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate) play out across every sector of government.

The damage compounds daily. Every norm broken this year makes the next violation easier. Every capitulation invites the next threat. Every act of obfuscation erodes what little institutional credibility remains.

Question 2: What would this past year have looked like under President Harris?

This is the counterfactual that millions have been privately contemplating throughout Trump’s first year back. Not because Harris would have been perfect, but because the alternative governance model she represents would have produced fundamentally different outcomes in measurable ways.

What would Justice Department independence have looked like this past year? What would NATO cohesion have felt like without twelve months of threats? What would economic policy have looked like when driven by analysis rather than rage? What would institutional function have looked like when career professionals weren’t being purged for insufficient loyalty?

What would this year have felt like if normal still meant something?

The Series Structure

Over eight parts, I’ll examine specific dimensions of this counterfactual with analytical rigor, grounded in the actual year we’ve just experienced:

Part 1: The Justice Department — What independence looks like versus the weaponization we’ve watched for twelve months.

Part 2: American Diplomacy — What the State Department could have been versus the hollowed institution we’ve watched for a year.

Part 3: Defense and Intelligence — What competence looks like versus the loyalty purges we’ve witnessed.

Part 4: Domestic Policy Divergence — What the first year of healthcare, climate, and economic policy could have been versus what we got.

Part 5: Fiscal Policy — Who paid and who benefited in Trump’s first year versus who would have under Harris.

Part 6: The Accountability Question — What happened to January 6th prosecutions, Trump’s cases, and rule of law in year one.

Part 7: The International Order — What allies experienced this past year versus what they could have had.

Part 8: The Global Democratic Project — What the world learned from America’s first year back under Trump versus what it could have learned.

Each part will ground analysis in the evidence of what actually happened this past year while acknowledging the surreal nature of living through it. Because part of what makes this unbearable is the gaslighting—the insistence that what we’ve watched for twelve months is normal, that our exhaustion is partisan bias, that our longing for cause-and-effect governance is naive idealism.

It’s not naive to want crimes to have consequences.

It’s not partisan to want institutional norms to hold.

It’s not idealistic to believe democracy should function.

Living in Someone Else’s Reality Show

The hardest part of the Truman Show isn’t the deception. It’s the isolation of knowing you’re being deceived while everyone around you pretends everything is fine. It’s the exhaustion of pointing at obvious manipulation for an entire year while others shrug and say “that’s just how things are now.”

But here’s what gives me hope after this first year: millions of people see it. They’re exhausted too. They’ve been trapped in the same unwatchable show for twelve months. And they’re all asking the same questions.

This series is for them. For everyone who’s felt gaslit by a year of normalized abnormality. For everyone who’s tired of being told they’re overreacting after watching a twelve-month constitutional crisis unfold. For everyone who remembers when reality had rules and has been mourning what we’ve lost.

We can’t exit the Truman Show. The cameras have been rolling for a year. The villain is still winning.

But we can document what this past year could have looked like. We can examine the road not taken with clear eyes and rigorous analysis, grounded in what actually happened. We can answer the question billions have been asking for twelve months:

What would this year have looked like if we’d chosen differently?

Let’s find out.
