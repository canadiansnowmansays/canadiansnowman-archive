When Democracy Fights Back

The Courts Choose Sides

Part 13: How the Supreme Court Blessed the Arms Race — And the Battlegrounds Nobody Saw Coming

February 18, 2026

February 4, the Supreme Court cleared California’s Proposition 50 maps for the 2026 midterms. Last Friday, the Virginia Supreme Court cleared a redistricting referendum for an April ballot. And as of last Saturday morning, a New York Republican is begging the Supreme Court to save her seat before petitioning starts on February 24.

If you’ve been following this series — from the Texas trigger through the shadow docket through Indiana’s revolt — you know the redistricting wars have been escalating since last summer. But in the two weeks since Part 11, the battlefield has shifted in ways that could fundamentally change the math for November.

Let’s take stock.

California: The Other Shoe Drops

On February 4, the Supreme Court denied an emergency request by the California Republican Party to block the state’s Prop 50 maps. A three-judge federal panel had already rejected the racial gerrymandering challenge in January, and the high court let that stand.

This was the mirror image of the Texas ruling we covered in Part 7. In December, the Court’s conservative majority cleared Texas’s maps despite a lower court finding of racial gerrymandering. Now the Court cleared California’s counter-maps too. Justice Alito, concurring in the Texas case alongside Thomas and Gorsuch, had said the quiet part out loud: the motivation behind both maps was “partisan advantage pure and simple.”

The net effect? Texas and California roughly cancel each other out. Five seats shift red, five shift blue. The arsonists burned down two houses and called it even.

But the real significance is the precedent. The Supreme Court has now told every state legislature in America that mid-decade partisan gerrymandering is consequence-free — as long as you can frame it as politics, not race. That’s the new rulebook. And states are reading it carefully.

Virginia: Democracy Goes to the Voters

Friday’s Virginia ruling is the most significant redistricting development since the SCOTUS California decision.

The Virginia Supreme Court cleared an April 21 referendum to go forward — even as it continues hearing a Republican legal challenge to the process. Early voting begins in the first week of March. The court laid out a briefing schedule extending past the vote itself, making clear that a final ruling won’t come until after Virginians have already decided.

House Speaker Don Scott told reporters he’s confident the court wouldn’t have green-lighted a $5 million referendum if it planned to kill it afterward. Republicans disagree. Senate Minority Leader Ryan McDougle, one of the plaintiffs, insists they’ll “win both — win the case and win the referendum.”

But the proposed maps tell the real story.

On February 5, Democratic leaders released their proposed congressional map. It creates one deep-red 9th District in Virginia’s southwest corner and ten districts that lean blue — carving heavily Democratic Northern Virginia counties into parts of five different districts.

Cardinal News’s analysis is worth reading in full. Political analyst Chaz Nuttycombe projects the map would produce a 10-1 Democratic delegation in a wave year like 2026, but structurally it’s really an 8-3 map — and in a Republican wave could go 7-4. Cardinal News calls it “the most extreme of any of the gerrymanders we’ve seen across the country” on a percentage basis.

Here’s the irony Cardinal News flags: Democrats could likely have won 8-3 under the current maps in a strong year, by defeating Rob Wittman in the 1st and Jen Kiggans in the 2nd. The whole constitutional amendment gambit — the referendums, the lawsuits, the $5 million special election — may net them only two seats beyond what a good election night would have delivered anyway.

Governor Abigail Spanberger, who initially said she believed Democrats could pick up seats without new maps, has now signaled she’s on board and is expected to sign the enabling legislation.

The Virginia story is the redistricting wars in miniature. A party that built guardrails against its own power (the bipartisan commission voters approved in 2020) is now desperately trying to unbuild them — temporarily, they promise — to counter the other side’s refusal to have guardrails at all.

New York: The Battleground Nobody Saw Coming

While everyone was watching Virginia and Florida, New York quietly became the most legally chaotic redistricting story in the country.

On January 21, a Manhattan judge struck down the boundaries of New York’s 11th Congressional District — the only Republican-held House seat in New York City, represented by Nicole Malliotakis. Four Staten Island residents had sued, arguing the district diluted Black and Latino voting power in violation of the state constitution. The judge agreed and ordered the state’s Independent Redistricting Commission to draw a new map.

House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries celebrated: “This ruling is the first step toward ensuring communities of interest remain intact from Staten Island to Lower Manhattan.”

Republicans appealed — and lost. New York’s Court of Appeals ruled it lacked jurisdiction for an immediate intervention. So Malliotakis escalated to the U.S. Supreme Court this week, filing an emergency application arguing that redrawing the district based on race would violate the Equal Protection Clause.

The Trump administration jumped in with a friend-of-the-court brief supporting Malliotakis. Solicitor General D. John Sauer argued this is different from Texas and California — calling New York’s situation “an open and unabashed racial gerrymander.”

The clock is extremely tight. Nominating petitions start circulating February 24. The Court has directed the challengers to respond by February 19 at 4 p.m. The request goes initially to Justice Sotomayor, who can act alone or refer it to the full court.

This matters beyond one seat. If the Court blocks the redraw, it reinforces the emerging precedent that racial considerations in redistricting are suspect — the same logic driving Louisiana v. Callais. If the Court allows it, New York adds another Democratic pickup opportunity to the national math.

Florida: The Lawsuit Nobody Expected

We’ve been tracking DeSantis’s April 20-24 special session since he announced it in January. But on February 5, the National Redistricting Foundation filed suit directly with the Florida Supreme Court, arguing DeSantis doesn’t have the constitutional authority to force the legislature into redistricting.

The lawsuit makes a simple separation-of-powers argument: the governor can call a special session, but he can’t dictate what the legislature does with it. Redistricting is a legislative function, not an executive one.

It’s a clever legal maneuver. Even if it fails, it burns time — and time is the scarcest resource in the redistricting wars. Florida’s primary is in August. The later DeSantis starts, the harder it becomes to implement new maps before filing deadlines.

Meanwhile, DeSantis has been explicit that he’s waiting for the Supreme Court’s ruling in Louisiana v. Callais before drawing maps. If Callais weakens Voting Rights Act protections, DeSantis could target majority-minority districts in addition to pursuing straight partisan advantage — potentially picking up three to five seats.

But Florida also has something Texas, Missouri, and North Carolina don’t: a voter-approved Fair Districts amendment that explicitly bans partisan gerrymandering. Over 60% of Floridians approved it in 2010. Whether that constitutional provision survives the current political environment is another question entirely.

Maryland: Principles vs. Survival

The Maryland story hasn’t changed much — but it remains one of the most interesting subplots in the redistricting wars.

Governor Wes Moore’s redistricting commission recommended a new map in January that would make the 1st District — the state’s only Republican-held seat — more favorable for Democrats. The House of Delegates passed the bill 99-37 on February 2.

And there it sits. Senate President Bill Ferguson continues to block a vote, telling NBC News earlier this year: “If we want to protect this democracy in the future, we have got to chart a different path.”

It’s one seat. It’s not going to change the national math. But Ferguson’s stand is notable because it’s the argument that should be winning everywhere and is winning almost nowhere: that the answer to gerrymandering isn’t more gerrymandering. That guardrails matter even when the other side has abandoned them.

Whether that’s principled or naive depends on whether you think the system can still be saved. Ask me again in November.

Utah: The Court-Ordered Surprise

One more battleground to add to the board: Utah.

After the state’s Supreme Court ruled in 2024 that the legislature’s congressional map was an unlawful partisan gerrymander — overriding a voter-approved anti-gerrymandering initiative — a state judge ordered a new map that creates one competitive, likely Democratic district comprising most of Salt Lake County.

Now two Republican House members, Celeste Maloy and Burgess Owens, have filed a federal lawsuit to block the court-ordered map. A three-judge panel will hear the case on February 18 — four days from now.

If the court-ordered map stands, it likely flips one Utah seat to Democrats.

The Updated Scorecard

Here’s where everything stands as of February 14, 2026:

Maps Locked In:

  • Texas: +5 R (SCOTUS cleared)

  • California: +5 D (SCOTUS cleared)

  • North Carolina: +1 R (Court allowed)

  • Ohio: +2 R (Commission map)

  • Locked-in subtotal: R +8 / D +5 → Net R +3

Likely But Not Final:

  • Missouri: +1 R (Referendum threat could suspend map)

  • Virginia: +3 to +4 D (Referendum April 21; Supreme Court cleared it to proceed)

Wildcards:

  • Florida: +2 to +5 R (Special session April 20; Fair Districts ban; DeSantis authority lawsuit; awaiting Callais)

  • Maryland: 0 to +1 D (House passed; Senate blocking)

  • Louisiana: 0 to +1 R (Entirely dependent on Callais; filing window closing)

  • New York: 0 to +1 D (Malliotakis district struck down; at SCOTUS now, response due Feb 19)

  • Utah: 0 to +1 D (Court-ordered map; federal hearing Feb 18)

Failed:

  • Indiana (21 Rs joined all Ds to kill it)

  • Kansas (No traction)

  • New Hampshire (No traction)

The New York Times published its own redistricting tracker this morning. Their net range: D+2 to R+3. That's almost exactly where our scorecard lands.

The math:

In the most likely scenario, redistricting nets Republicans somewhere between 3 and 5 additional seats — enough to significantly raise the bar for Democrats hoping to flip the House. Democrats currently need a net gain of just 3 seats. After redistricting, they likely need a wave of 7 to 9 seats to break even.

That’s the whole point of the exercise. Republicans aren’t trying to guarantee they hold the House through gerrymandering alone. They’re trying to raise the floor — to survive the midterm headwinds that historically punish the president’s party. In Trump’s first term, Democrats gained 40 House seats. The redistricting cushion is designed to ensure that even a significant blue wave falls short.

But there’s a nuclear option still sitting on the Supreme Court’s desk that could change everything.

While we wait for the Callais ruling we covered in Part 12, the battlefield keeps shifting.
