To the Moon, Alice

Part III: Stable One, Four Green Crew Members

April 10, 2026

April 10, 2026 — Splashdown Day

At 8:07 and 47 seconds Eastern time, a capsule the size of a campervan hit the Pacific Ocean off San Diego under three red and white parachutes and the voice of Reid Wiseman came through from the other side of fourteen minutes of fire.

“What a journey. We are stable one, four green crew members.”

Stable one means upright. Green means healthy. Ten days, 695,000 miles, the farthest any human beings have ever travelled from Earth, and the commander of the mission reduced it to nine words of pilot’s shorthand. Which is exactly right. Which is exactly what you would want.

Welcome home, Reid, Victor, Christina, and Jeremy. You went to the moon and came back, and the world is measurably better for it — even if it will take us a while to understand precisely how.

The Fireball

Victor Glover called it riding a fireball through the atmosphere, and he was not being dramatic.

At 7:53 p.m. Eastern, Integrity hit what NASA calls the entry interface — the altitude at which Earth’s atmosphere is thick enough to begin pushing back. The capsule was travelling at just under 25,000 miles per hour. In the next thirteen minutes, it had to decelerate to zero.

The compression of air molecules against the heat shield generated temperatures between 4,000 and 5,000 degrees Fahrenheit — at least twice as hot as molten lava. A cloud of charged particles formed around the capsule, blocking all radio communication for six minutes. Houston went quiet. The screens kept running. There was nothing to do but trust the physics and the engineering and the Avcoat heat shield and the forty years of accumulated knowledge that went into building the thing.

At 8:02 p.m., Wiseman’s voice returned: “Houston, Integrity, we have you loud and clear.”

At 8:03, the drogue parachutes deployed. At 8:04, the main parachutes. At 8:06, mission control reported: “We see three good-looking parachutes.” At 8:07 and 47 seconds, the water.

Thirteen minutes of things that had to go right. They went right. CBC’s Nicole Mortillaro had described it perfectly beforehand: the crew would be “riding a fireball through the atmosphere.” Glover confirmed it.

What Ten Days Proved

This was a test mission, and it is worth being precise about what was tested and what passed. NASA’s full mission summary tells the technical story.

The life support systems worked. The propulsion worked. The navigation worked. The heat shield, which had caused anxiety after Artemis I when portions of its ablative material broke away unexpectedly, held. The modified re-entry profile — a shorter skip manoeuvre, sharper heating for less time — protected it. The eleven parachutes deployed in sequence. The crew’s orange pressure suits, equipped with a new life support system capable of sustaining astronauts for up to six days in an emergency, performed without incident.

The toilet did not work especially well. This is noted not for comedy but for record. The Universal Waste Management System — NASA’s name for the first toilet ever used on a deep space mission — required troubleshooting throughout the ten days. Christina Koch, who has soldered electronics at minus-40 degrees hanging off an Antarctic tower, fixed it by removing and clearing the urine hose and repriming the system. “I’m the space plumber,” she said. The toilet was officially turned off as part of re-entry preparations on Day 10, which is either poignant or practical depending on your perspective.

The broader point is this: Artemis II was designed to find out what doesn’t work before astronauts attempt to actually land on the moon. The communications glitches, the toilet, the false cabin pressure alarm — these are the mission succeeding at its actual purpose. You want to discover problems in a test flight. The alternative is discovering them during Artemis IV, 250,000 miles from home with no way back except gravity.

The Moon They Brought Home

Of the more than 100 billion humans thought to have ever lived, twenty-four had seen the far side of the moon before this week. That number is now twenty-eight.

What those twenty-eight saw — and what the cameras captured, and what the science teams will spend months processing — is not the moon of mythology or the silver disc of the poets or even the grey pumice ball of the Apollo photographs. It is something stranger and more interesting than any of those.

Writing in The Atlantic, Sally Jenkins called the crew simply “studs” — and then documented why. Koch has soldered electronics at minus-40 degrees hanging off an Antarctic tower. Glover earned three advanced degrees while flying Hornets and Growlers as a military test pilot. Jeremy Hansen got his pilot wings at sixteen. Jenkins put it precisely: people of accomplishment bringing expertise — not bravado — to difficult problems.

Also in The Atlantic, Rebecca Boyle described the far side as brown — mushroom, chestnut, hazel, tea-stained, russet, the colour of earth rather than the colour of the sky. Whole regions appeared in hues that human eyes had never seen illuminated before, because the orbital geometry of this mission placed sunlight on surfaces that were in shadow during every Apollo pass. Victor Glover described a beam of sunlight emerging through a crater as what the Luxor spotlight in Las Vegas “wants to be when it grows up.” Christina Koch described fresh impact craters as lampshades with pinprick holes, light shining through from behind.

And Koch, after a long session of precise scientific observation — flash cards memorized, features catalogued, photographs taken in choreographed rotation with her crewmates — paused and said something that contained more scientific content than it might appear to:

“It’s a real place.”

Not a poster. Not a symbol. Not a strategic asset or a helium-3 repository or a potential orbital data centre anchor point. A real place, with colour and depth and shadows that move and craters that formed three point eight billion years ago and are still there, still sharp, still exactly as the impact left them because nothing erodes on the moon and nothing heals and everything that has ever happened is still written on the surface.

Carroll Crater is written there now. It will be there when the sun expands and swallows the inner solar system in five billion years. In the long run, the moon is the most permanent address humanity has ever been able to assign a name to. The New York Times captured every moment of this final day.

Guess We’ll Have To Go Back

On the way home, Wiseman looked out the window and reported that the moon was visible, but smaller than it had been yesterday.

Mission control replied: “Guess we’ll have to go back.”

This was delivered casually, in the tone of someone noting that the restaurant was closed and they’d try again next week. Which is precisely the point. The moon is no longer a destination. It is becoming, in the language of people who plan these things, a location — a place that human beings have now demonstrated they can reach, navigate around, observe scientifically, and return from safely, in a capsule named Integrity with a broken toilet and four extraordinary people inside.

What comes next is where the series gets harder.

Artemis III launches in 2027, but it will not land — it has been restructured as a test of the lunar landers being developed by SpaceX and Blue Origin, practicing rendezvous and docking in Earth orbit. Artemis IV and V are the planned landing attempts, targeting 2028, aimed at the lunar south pole. President Trump has made the 2028 landing a political priority, which is both an argument for and against its likelihood.

Meanwhile, China’s Chang’e 7 launches this year to scout the same south pole. China’s Mengzhou spacecraft conducts its first robotic test flight this year. The Long March 10 rocket is in development. Beijing has stated clearly, repeatedly, and with supporting hardware, that Chinese astronauts will stand on the moon by 2030. NASA administrator Jared Isaacman said last week that China “may be early” and that “recent history suggests we might be late.”

The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 prohibits national appropriation of the moon. It says nothing about who owns the water ice in the permanently shadowed craters at the south pole once it has been extracted. The Artemis Accords — signed by 61 countries and not signed by China or Russia — assert that extraction does not constitute appropriation. China’s state media has called this “akin to European colonial enclosure land-taking methods.” Nobody has a binding answer. Nobody has an enforcement mechanism. The first nation to establish durable infrastructure at the south pole will write the rules by the simple fact of being there first.

This is where we are going in the essays that follow. The land grab, the legal vacuum, the billionaire infrastructure dreams, the orbital data centres, the helium-3 economy, the two competing governance frameworks dividing the world into space blocs the way the Cold War divided it into political ones.

All of that is coming. But not today.

What The Atlantic Knew in 1963

In 1963, two NASA scientists made the case in The Atlantic for why human beings should go to the moon. As Stephanie Bai recently noted in that same magazine, their reasoning was simple: Their reasoning was simple: scientific and technological advancement, political clout, and an appeal “to the imagination of the student.”

Sixty-three years later, a Canadian farm boy who got his pilot wings at sixteen watched a beam of sunlight emerge through a lunar crater and said “it is blowing my mind what you can see with the naked eye from the moon right now.”

The imagination of the student. Still the best answer.

The View From Here

I want to end with the two images that define this mission for me.

The first is Integrity under three parachutes against the deep Pacific night — red and white canopies against a blue so dark it is almost black, the capsule hitting the water in a burst of white spray. It is simultaneously the most engineered and the most human image of the decade. Thirteen minutes earlier, that capsule was a fireball. Now it is floating.

The second is Earth’s curvature from just above the atmosphere on the way down — that impossibly thin blue line against the black of space. The whole atmosphere, every molecule of air any human being has ever breathed, visible as a suggestion, a membrane, a rumour of protection between the living planet and the void.

Christina Koch looked at that line from 252,000 miles away and said: “We will always choose Earth. We will always choose each other.”

She was right. She is right. And the reason we keep going back to the moon — the reason we name craters for the people we have lost and send dead men’s voices as wake-up calls and fling four human beings around the far side of a world that has never seen a living thing — is precisely because we choose Earth. Because we want to understand it. Because we want to protect it. Because we want to know what it looks like from far enough away to see it whole.

Because, as Johannes Kepler understood four centuries ago, there is a difference between seeing and perceiving. Robots see. Humans perceive.

And because Jim Lovell, in a message recorded before his death and played to four astronauts more than 200,000 miles from home, said simply: “Don’t forget to enjoy the view.”

They did not forget.

Neither should we.
