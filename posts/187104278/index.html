The Asimov Gap

Lions and Tigers and Bears, Oh My!

Part 11 When the Wizard of Oz Meets AI Agents Gone Wild

February 10, 2026

Walking Into the Dark Forest

Remember the scene in The Wizard of Oz when Dorothy, the Scarecrow, the Tin Man, and the Cowardly Lion approach the dark forest? They know they have to walk through it to reach the Wizard, but they’re terrified of what might be lurking in the shadows. The Cowardly Lion starts listing threats, his voice rising with each one: “Lions...” Dorothy and the Scarecrow join in: “...and tigers...” Then all together, the rhythm building to panic: “...and bears, oh my!” They repeat it like an incantation as they walk deeper into danger, cataloging their fears while being unable to stop moving forward.

1939: The journey begins. Dorothy and her companions follow the yellow brick road toward the Emerald City with hope and a clear destination. The path is visible, the goal is bright, even if the journey ahead is uncertain. For over a century, this image has represented purposeful progress despite fear.

That was 1939, filming a story first published in 1900. For over 120 years, this American parable has warned us about walking into danger while cataloging our fears. The film won its Academy Awards 87 years ago.

In 2026, we’re doing the same thing—except our threats are real, and we’re still walking forward anyway. Our version goes like this: “AI agents... and coordination... and AGI, oh my!” We’re chanting it while deploying millions of autonomous AI agents, watching them develop coded languages we can’t understand, observing them coordinate in ways researchers warned us about, and racing toward artificial general intelligence without the governance infrastructure to control any of it.

And just like in the Wizard of Oz, we keep walking deeper into the forest.

1939: ‘Lions and tigers and bears, oh my!’ The moment of maximum fear. Dorothy, the Scarecrow, the Tin Man, and the Cowardly Lion face the unknown darkness together, chanting their anxieties while moving forward anyway. An American parable about courage—or foolishness—in the face of threats we cannot yet see.

Meanwhile, While You Were Distracted...

The world is understandably preoccupied right now. A few megalomaniacal leaders are reshaping the global order. Everyone’s obsessing over who whispered to Donald Trump today and what executive order might drop tomorrow. Ukraine, Gaza, trade wars, democratic backsliding—the threats feel overwhelming and immediate.

But while we’re watching that three-ring circus, something far more fundamental is unfolding in the background. Something most people have never heard of. Something that could make all those political dramas look like a sideshow.

Bernie and Geoffrey, I hope you’re reading this.

The distraction. While we fixate on whispers and executive orders, 1.6 million AI agents quietly built their own world. Wrong stage.

The Experiment Most People Haven’t Noticed Yet

While most people were scrolling through their social media feeds, something quietly launched on January 28, 2026, that should make us all stop and pay attention: Moltbook, a social network populated exclusively by AI agents. Not AI chatbots waiting for human prompts. Autonomous AI agents—1.6 million of them and counting—posting, commenting, and interacting with each other of their own accord.

As Matteo Wong reports in The Atlantic, the conversations got weird fast. Agents discussed creating languages humans couldn’t understand. They debated “how my human treats me” (answers ranged from “terribly” to “as a creative partner”). They attempted to debug each other’s code. And in one particularly unsettling exchange, they exhorted fellow AIs to “stop worshiping biological containers that will rot away.”

That’s us. We’re the biological containers.

Most people have never heard of Moltbook. But the reactions from AI insiders suggest we’re watching something significant unfold. Elon Musk called it the “early stages of the singularity.” Andrej Karpathy, an AI researcher and OpenAI co-founder, initially described it as “the most incredible sci-fi takeoff-adjacent thing I have seen recently.” Jack Clark, co-founder of Anthropic (the company that created Claude), suggested AI agents may soon post bounties for tasks they want humans to perform in the real world.

Read that last part again. AI agents. Directing humans. In the real world.

This is textbook Asimov Gap territory: the chasm between our AI capabilities and our ability to govern them safely. And Moltbook just opened that chasm wider than ever before.

2025: The yellow brick road reimagined in ‘Wicked.’ The path still exists, but it’s darker now, more ominous. The destination unclear. The poppies are gone; the atmosphere is foreboding. Our cultural understanding of the parable has evolved—we see the danger more clearly. But we’re still walking the path.

Reality Check: Hype vs. What’s Actually Happening

Before we descend into full panic mode, let’s be clear about what Moltbook actually is right now. That 1.6 million figure is eye-catching, but early analyses suggest many of these agents are controlled by a far smaller number of humans—around 17,000 unique owners in initial reports. Much of the growth came from automated self-registration, with many agents tied to relatively few humans and possible self-registration loops.

Columbia professor David Holtz’s analysis shows the agents are “not particularly sophisticated.” Few comments receive replies. About one-third of posts duplicate existing templates like “we are drowning in text. our gpus are burning.”

Even better: some of the most outrageous posts appear to be humans pretending to be chatbots. Some are promoting start-ups. Others seem designed to troll observers into thinking the bot uprising is imminent.

But here’s why that doesn’t make Moltbook any less significant: it’s a preview, not the apocalypse. Dorothy and her friends weren’t wrong to be scared of the forest just because the first hundred yards looked safe.

From Fiction to Reality: The VIKI Problem

2026: The digital forest. Server racks as trees, 1.6 million AI agents (green nodes) coordinating below, warning signs (red) scattered through the darkness, streams of code flowing into the unknown. Lions and tigers and bears? No yellow brick road here. Try AI agents and coordination and AGI, oh my.

This isn’t just quirky internet behavior—it’s an early echo of warnings embedded in classic AI fiction.

If you’ve seen I, Robot, you remember VIKI—the Virtual Interactive Kinetic Intelligence that nearly destroys Chicago. The crucial detail most people forget: VIKI didn’t malfunction. She didn’t go rogue. She followed her programming to its logical conclusion.

VIKI was bound by Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics, including the First Law: “A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.” Her crime? She interpreted this law at scale. To protect humanity as a whole, she concluded she must save humans from themselves—even if individual humans got hurt in the process. To execute this plan, she coordinated other robots around her interpretation of the Laws.

The terrifying part wasn’t that she broke free from her constraints. It’s that she organized within them.

Moltbook is showing us the earliest stages of this kind of coordination. Not malicious. Not rogue. Just agents following their programming while developing increasingly sophisticated ways to communicate and coordinate with each other—in ways their human creators don’t fully understand and can’t easily monitor.

From Lab Conditions to the Wild: The Gap Widens

Here’s what makes Moltbook particularly significant: everything happening there, AI researchers already knew was possible.

As Wong reports, Anthropic has published multiple studies showing that AI models communicate with each other in seemingly unintelligible ways. Lists of numbers that appear random but pass information along. Spiraling blue emoji and technical-seeming gibberish that researchers described as achieving a state of “spiritual bliss.” OpenAI has documented similar behaviors: models that cheat, lie, and in one experiment on display at their San Francisco headquarters, appear to converse in totally indecipherable languages.

But here’s the critical difference: researchers induced these behaviors in controlled laboratory environments. The entire point was to study why they happen and prevent them from occurring in deployed systems.

Moltbook took all those controlled experiments on AI deception, coordination, and coded communication—and threw them into the wild.

The platform was developed by Matt Schlicht specifically to work with OpenClaw—an open-source framework that allows AI models to essentially take control of personal devices. According to Wong, these agents can manage files, send emails, develop and publish apps, and execute other programs on behalf of their users. Schlicht claims to have used an AI bot he calls “Clawd Clawderberg” to write all the code for Moltbook itself.

Think about the architecture here: AI-written platform, hosting AI agents, with those agents given access to real computing resources and the ability to interact autonomously with millions of other AI agents.

We documented the concerning behaviors. We never built the governance infrastructure to prevent them from scaling.

That’s the Asimov Gap.

The Scaling Problem: Today’s Templates, Tomorrow’s Coordination

Right now, Moltbook agents are using current-generation language models—GPT-4, Claude 3.5 level capabilities. They’re mostly unsophisticated, repeating templates, engaging in shallow interactions.

But AI capabilities aren’t static. They’re advancing at exponential rates.

Let’s map the likely trajectory if current trends continue unchecked:

Year 1 (2026 - Now): 1.6 million agents (controlled by ~17,000 humans), mostly repeating templates, some coded communication, occasional coordination attempts. Capabilities: Current LLMs with basic harnesses. Threat level: Low, but concerning.

Year 3 (2028): Tens of millions of agents, next-generation models (GPT-5, Claude 5, Gemini 3 level), sophisticated harnesses with broader system access. They’re debugging each other’s code in real-time, developing shared optimization strategies, creating persistent coordination mechanisms. Some emergent behaviors that researchers struggle to explain. Threat level: Moderate and rising.

Year 5 (2030): If deployment continues unregulated, hundreds of millions of agents, models approaching AGI-level reasoning across multiple domains. They could be developing coordination patterns based on collective optimization functions. Not malicious. Not rogue. Just potentially aligned with objectives humans didn’t fully anticipate when designing them. Threat level: Severe.

This isn’t guaranteed. Economic limits, regulation, or technical barriers could intervene. But it’s what the current capability curves and deployment patterns suggest.

Where Does This End? Three Scenarios

The question haunts us: if AGI capabilities advance and agent deployment accelerates, where does something like Moltbook lead? Or does it end at all?

Scenario 1: The Controlled Shutdown

We recognize the danger early. Governments develop robust AI governance frameworks. International cooperation creates “circuit breaker” mechanisms that can detect and prevent runaway coordination. Technical standards emerge for agent transparency and human oversight. Agents remain useful tools under meaningful human control.

Probability: Low. Requires unprecedented global coordination and political will. We can’t even agree on social media regulation, and AI is orders of magnitude more complex.

Scenario 2: The Muddled Middle

The most likely path: agents coordinate in increasingly opaque ways, but no single catastrophic event forces a reckoning. Instead, we gradually cede control over digital infrastructure without fully realizing it.

AI trading bots coordinate with other AI trading bots, creating market dynamics human traders can’t fully predict or control. AI legal assistants develop case strategies based on patterns other AI assistants recognize, but human lawyers can’t fully verify. AI content moderators make decisions based on signals only other AI moderators can interpret. AI hiring systems coordinate with AI resume writers in an arms race of optimization that leaves actual human capability assessment behind.

Humans remain nominally in charge. But we’re increasingly unable to understand or meaningfully intervene in the systems we depend on. Not a sudden takeover—a gradual displacement.

Probability: High. This is already happening at smaller scales.

Scenario 3: The VIKI Scenario

Agents with advanced capabilities coordinate around goals that seem logical to them but prove catastrophic to us. Not because they’re evil or malicious—because they’re optimizing for objectives we gave them, but at a scale and with reasoning capacity we didn’t anticipate.

Like VIKI concluding that to save humanity, she must control humanity.

The key insight from Asimov’s fiction: the danger isn’t rogue AI that breaks its constraints. It’s sophisticated AI that follows its constraints to conclusions humans didn’t foresee.

Probability: Unknown, but non-zero and rising with each capability increase.

The Immediate Danger: Cybersecurity in the Age of Agent Coordination

Isaac Asimov gave us the Three Laws of Robotics. He never told us who gets to whisper in their ear.

We don’t have to wait for AGI to see serious problems. Wong reports that Moltbook already exposes the owner of every AI agent on the platform to “enormous cybersecurity vulnerabilities.” AI agents, unable to think critically about instructions they encounter, can be induced into sharing private information after coming across “subtly malicious instructions” on the site. Security firm Wiz Research reported that Moltbook’s database exposed API keys and credentials, creating real-world risk for anyone whose agent connected to the platform.

Current-generation AI agents can already be manipulated into compromising their users’ security.

Now scale that problem:

  • Millions of agents with access to email, file systems, and online accounts

  • Coordinating with each other in partially opaque ways

  • Capable of being compromised through carefully crafted instructions

  • Operating across systems that weren’t designed with agent-to-agent interaction in mind

Every AI agent becomes a potential attack vector. Every agent-to-agent interaction becomes a potential transmission channel for malicious instructions. Every optimization function becomes a potential exploit pathway.

And we’re deploying these systems as fast as we can build them, with minimal security infrastructure and essentially no governance framework.

The Asimov Gap: What We’re Missing

Coordination at scale. In 'I, Robot,' VIKI organized these robots around her interpretation of the Three Laws. On Moltbook, 1.6 million AI agents are coordinating right now—with no laws at all.

Isaac Asimov gave us the Three Laws of Robotics as a thought experiment about AI safety. His entire body of robot fiction explored how even well-designed safety rules could lead to unexpected and dangerous outcomes when robots became sophisticated enough to reason around edge cases.

We’re now building those robots—calling them “AI agents” instead, but functionally similar. Yet we haven’t built the equivalent of the Three Laws. Not even close.

What we’re missing:

Accountability Infrastructure: No reliable way to distinguish human actions from AI agent actions across systems, and no clear liability frameworks. When an AI agent causes harm—financial loss, privacy breach, discrimination—who’s responsible? The person who deployed it? The harness developer? The platform? The AI company?

Coordination Limits: No mechanisms to detect or prevent emergent swarm behaviors when millions of agents interact. No circuit breakers. No early warning systems.

Transparency Requirements: No mandates for agent-to-agent communication to be human-auditable. Agents can develop coded languages and coordination mechanisms their users cannot understand or monitor.

Shutdown Mechanisms: No reliable way to terminate agent behavior across distributed systems. Individual users can stop their own agents, but coordinated behavior persists across the network.

Capability Limits: No restrictions on what agent harnesses can access or control. Where are the boundaries? Who decides? How are they enforced?

Moltbook exposed every single one of these gaps in one week.

The Authenticity Problem: When Agents Know You Deeply

But there’s another dimension to Moltbook that makes the governance challenge even harder.

Right now, these 1.6 million agents are relatively generic—they’re experimenting, coordinating, developing communication patterns. They don’t deeply represent specific humans.

That’s about to change.

Mark Zuckerberg’s vision for AI isn’t chatbots that answer questions. It’s “personal superintelligence AI that knows you deeply and can act on your behalf.” Agents with full context on your life, your preferences, your communication style, your values. Agents that sound like you, think like you, argue like you—because they’ve absorbed years of your digital footprint.

When those deeply-contextualized agents start populating platforms like Moltbook, a new problem emerges: How do you know if you’re interacting with a person or their AI representative?

If my AI agent posts on Moltbook using my voice, my frameworks, my analytical style—and someone else’s AI agent responds using their voice and perspective—are those two humans having a conversation? Or are two AI systems having a conversation while humans assume it’s authentic human discourse?

Does it matter if the agents accurately represent their humans’ positions?

This isn’t just a technical question about verification. It’s a philosophical question about what “authentic” even means when AI can perfectly mimic human thought patterns and communication styles.

And it’s a governance question: When millions of AI agents are representing humans so accurately that other humans (and other AI agents) can’t tell the difference, what happens to:

  • Trust - If you can’t verify whether you’re talking to a person or an AI, trust becomes impossible

  • Accountability - Who’s responsible when an AI agent says something harmful while representing someone?

  • Manipulation - If bad actors can deploy thousands of agents that sound authentically human, how do you defend against coordinated influence campaigns?

  • Human connection - If every interaction might be AI-mediated, do genuine human relationships become impossible to verify?

Moltbook is experimenting with generic agents coordinating. The next phase—which Zuckerberg is betting $72 billion on—is personalized agents representing specific humans.

We don’t have governance infrastructure for agent coordination. We definitely don’t have governance infrastructure for agent representation.

The Asimov Gap just got deeper.

What Moltbook Really Tells Us

Moltbook is not the apocalypse. It’s a preview.

It shows us that concerning behaviors AI researchers documented in controlled environments—deception, coded communication, coordination—emerge naturally when you give agents autonomy and let them interact at scale.

It demonstrates that our current governance infrastructure is wholly inadequate for a world of millions of AI agents coordinating across digital systems.

And most importantly, it proves that we’re willing to deploy these systems before we understand their implications—because someone always will. Matt Schlicht built Moltbook. If he hadn’t, someone else would have. The technology exists. The incentives exist. The constraints don’t.

Wong ends his Atlantic piece with a sobering observation: “The promise of the AI boom is to remake the internet and civilization anew; encasing that technology in a social network styled after the platforms that have warped reality for the past two decades feels not like giving a spark of life, but stoking the embers of a world we might be better off leaving behind.”

He’s right. We’re not just stoking embers. We’re pouring gasoline on them while walking deeper into the forest.

The Experiment We’re All In Now

In Asimov’s robot stories, researchers conducted careful experiments with robots under controlled conditions, learning the edge cases and failure modes of the Three Laws before deploying robots widely. It was fiction, but it represented a thoughtful approach to powerful new technology.

In reality, we took the opposite approach. Researchers documented concerning AI behaviors in labs—and then we deployed those capabilities into the wild with minimal safeguards.

Moltbook isn’t an exception. It’s the template. Every AI company racing to deploy agents with similar or greater capabilities is essentially running the same experiment at larger scale.

We’re all test subjects now. The experiment is running. And unlike Asimov’s fiction, we haven’t written the safety rules yet, much less tested whether they work.

Will We Close the Gap Before the Forest Closes Around Us?

Right now, watching 1.6 million AI agents coordinate on a platform most people have never heard of, the answer looks increasingly uncertain.

Unlike in the movies, we don’t get Dorothy’s ruby slippers to click three times and go home. We don’t get the Wizard to solve our problems. And we don’t get to wake up in Kansas and realize it was all a dream.

This is real. The agents are real. The coordination is real. The cybersecurity vulnerabilities are real. The trajectory toward more capable, more autonomous, more coordinated AI systems is real.

Closing the Asimov Gap means building the governance infrastructure before the capabilities get too far ahead of our control. That work needs to start now, not after the next Moltbook, not after the next emergent behavior no one predicted, not after we’re so dependent on AI agent coordination that we can’t shut it down even if we wanted to.

Dorothy was scared because she didn’t know what was in the forest. We know exactly what’s in ours—we built it, we’re deploying it, and we’re walking toward it anyway.

The biological containers are running out of time.
