When Democracy Fights Back

The Last Guardrail

Part 12: Louisiana v. Callais and the Supreme Court’s Slow-Motion Demolition of the Voting Rights Act

February 16, 2026

Alabama state troopers attack voting rights demonstrators in Selma, Ala., in this file photo from March 7, 1965. AP Photo/File

On March 7, 1965, Alabama state troopers beat peaceful marchers with clubs and tear gas as they crossed the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma. The footage shocked a nation. Five months later, President Lyndon Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act into law, calling it “a triumph for freedom as huge as any victory that has ever been won on any battlefield.”

Sixty years later — almost to the day — the Supreme Court of the United States is deliberating whether to gut the law’s last meaningful protection against racial discrimination in how Americans choose their representatives.

The case is called Louisiana v. Callais. You probably haven’t heard of it. But it may be the most important voting rights case of our lifetime.

How We Got Here

To understand Callais, you need to understand what’s left of the Voting Rights Act — and how little that is.

The VRA originally had two major enforcement mechanisms. Section 5 required states with histories of racial discrimination to get federal approval — called “preclearance” — before changing their voting laws. Section 2 prohibited voting practices that discriminated on the basis of race, including the drawing of electoral districts that diluted minority voting power.

Section 5 is already dead. The Roberts Court killed it in Shelby County v. Holder in 2013, ruling that the formula used to determine which states needed preclearance was outdated. Chief Justice Roberts wrote that things had changed in the South. Within hours of the decision, Texas and other states began implementing voter restrictions that had been blocked under preclearance.

That left Section 2 as the last remaining federal tool to combat racial gerrymandering in redistricting. Under Section 2’s “results standard,” courts could strike down district maps that had the effect of diluting minority voting power — even without proof of discriminatory intent.

Louisiana v. Callais targets that standard directly.

The Louisiana Story

The story began in 2022, when Louisiana’s Republican-controlled legislature drew a congressional map that gave Black voters the ability to elect their preferred candidate in just one of the state’s six districts — despite Black residents making up roughly one-third of the state’s population.

Civil rights groups sued under Section 2, and federal courts agreed. Louisiana was ordered to create a second majority-Black district. The state complied under protest, and two Black-majority districts were used in the 2024 elections.

But Louisiana wasn’t done fighting. State officials appealed to the Supreme Court, arguing that the lower court’s interpretation of Section 2 was itself unconstitutional — that requiring states to consider race when drawing districts violated the Equal Protection Clause. In other words: the remedy for racial discrimination is racial discrimination.

It’s a breathtaking argument. And based on two rounds of oral arguments — the Court took the unusual step of scheduling a second hearing in October 2025 after broadening its review — the conservative majority appears ready to embrace some version of it.

The question isn’t whether Section 2 will be weakened. It’s by how much.

Three Scenarios

Legal scholars have identified three possible outcomes, ranging from bad to catastrophic for minority representation:

Scenario 1: Narrowing. The Court could make it significantly harder to bring Section 2 claims by raising the evidentiary bar — requiring proof of discriminatory intent rather than just discriminatory results, or imposing stricter standards for what constitutes a “compact” minority population that warrants its own district. This would gut most pending redistricting challenges while technically leaving Section 2 on the books.

Scenario 2: The Trump Administration Position. The DOJ under Attorney General Pam Bondi has argued for scaling back Section 2’s coverage substantially. This would align with the Roberts Court’s broader “colorblind” philosophy — the same reasoning that struck down affirmative action in college admissions in 2023. Under this approach, states would have wide latitude to dismantle majority-minority districts as long as they claimed partisan rather than racial motivation.

Scenario 3: Full Invalidation. Louisiana’s maximalist argument: Section 2’s results standard is flatly unconstitutional. If the Court goes this far, every majority-minority district in America drawn under VRA protections would become vulnerable to elimination.

Most observers expect something between Scenarios 1 and 2. But even the “moderate” outcome would fundamentally reshape American politics.

The Dominoes Waiting to Fall

Here’s why Callais matters beyond Louisiana: a significant weakening of Section 2 wouldn’t just affect one state’s map. It would send a signal to every state legislature in the country that majority-minority districts are now optional.

The states watching most closely are the ones where minority representation hangs by a legal thread:

Louisiana itself has already pushed back its entire 2026 election calendar in anticipation of a favorable ruling. The filing deadline for candidates just passed on February 14, and the justices still haven’t ruled. If the Court strikes down the two-district mandate, Republicans could potentially redraw a map with zero Black-majority districts — what local redistricting expert John Landry has called the “6-0 nightmare scenario.”

Alabama has a congressional map that is virtually identical to Louisiana’s — two majority-Black districts drawn under court order. A Callais ruling weakening Section 2 would almost certainly trigger an immediate challenge.

Florida is the biggest prize. As we covered in Part 12, DeSantis has explicitly said he’s waiting for the Callais decision before drawing new maps. His April 20-24 special session is timed accordingly. If the VRA protections fall, DeSantis could target majority-minority districts in addition to pursuing partisan advantage — potentially picking up five or more seats.

Georgia, Mississippi, and South Carolina — all states with significant Black populations and Republican-controlled legislatures — would face similar pressure.

The Southern Poverty Law Center has warned that the effects would extend far beyond Congress. State legislatures, county commissions, city councils, school boards — every level of government where districts are drawn to ensure minority representation would be affected. There are ongoing congressional redistricting lawsuits in at least twelve states as of late 2025

The Timing Trap

The Court’s delay in ruling has created its own kind of crisis.

Louisiana delayed its election calendar specifically to take advantage of a ruling. But with the justices not returning to the courtroom until February 20 and filing deadlines passing, the window for 2026 map changes is closing fast. CNN reported Saturday that every week without a decision makes it harder for states to redistrict in time for November.

But here’s the thing: even if the ruling comes too late for some states to act before 2026, it will reshape every election from 2028 forward. The conservative Court has been steadily erasing the racial remedies of the Voting Rights Act for two decades. Whatever the justices decide in Callais will give state legislatures more latitude than they’ve had since before the civil rights era.

As William and Mary law professor Rebecca Green told CNN, the current majority is pursuing a “colorblind” approach to redistricting — attempting to eliminate racial considerations from the map-drawing process entirely. In theory, that sounds like equality. In practice, in a country where residential segregation, racially polarized voting, and generations of structural inequality remain facts of life, “colorblindness” is a formula for minority exclusion.

It’s the legal equivalent of removing the ramps from a building and calling it equal because everyone has to use the stairs.

A Canadian Perspective on an American Tragedy

I write about American politics from Toronto, which gives me a certain distance. Sometimes that distance is clarifying. Sometimes it’s just sad.

Here’s what I see from the outside: the Voting Rights Act was one of the great moral achievements of the 20th century. It was passed because Americans watched their fellow citizens get beaten for trying to vote and decided that was intolerable. It has been renewed multiple times with overwhelming bipartisan support — including by Republican presidents.

And now it’s being dismantled, piece by piece, by a Court that was largely appointed by presidents who lost the popular vote, confirmed by senators representing a minority of the American population, interpreting a Constitution whose framers never imagined that Black people would vote at all.

There is something profoundly broken about a system in which the people most affected by voting discrimination have the least say over whether voting protections survive.

The redistricting arms race that we’ve been tracking throughout this series — from Texas to California, from Virginia to Florida — is ultimately a symptom. The disease is a political system that allows the rules of democracy itself to be rewritten by the people who benefit from rigging them.

Louisiana v. Callais is the test. Not of whether the Supreme Court will protect minority voting rights — we already know the answer to that. The test is whether Americans will notice before the last guardrail falls.

What to Watch

The justices return to the bench on February 20. Decisions could come at any time after that — typically on opinion days, usually Mondays and occasionally Thursdays. Key dates to watch:

  • February 19 — SCOTUS response deadline in New York Malliotakis case

  • February 20 — Court reconvenes

  • March (first week) — Virginia early voting begins for the April 21 redistricting referendum

  • April 20-24 — Florida’s special redistricting session

  • April 21 — Virginia redistricting referendum

  • June/July — Traditional end-of-term window for major decisions

If Callais drops before Florida’s April session, expect DeSantis to use it as a blueprint. If it drops before Virginia’s referendum, expect it to dominate the campaign. And whenever it drops, expect a new wave of redistricting challenges — or the disappearance of the legal basis for them.

The clock is ticking. The guardrail is cracking. And sixty years after Selma, the fight for fair representation is far from over.
