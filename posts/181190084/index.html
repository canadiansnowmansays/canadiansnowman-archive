America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 6: “The Political Economy of Healthcare — How $700 Million in Lobbying Buys Failure”

December 12, 2025

Want to know why American healthcare reform fails despite overwhelming public support, proven international models, and bipartisan polling showing Americans want universal coverage?

Follow the money.

Not the money Americans spend on healthcare — we covered that in Part 3 ($14,885 per capita, double the OECD average). This is about the money that flows from healthcare industry to politicians, from Congress to lobbying firms, from government service to private sector windfalls.

This is about the revolving door that makes healthcare reform politically impossible even when it’s economically necessary and publicly popular.

This is Part 6 of Code Blue, our investigation into America’s 80-year healthcare failure. We’ve documented broken promises (Parts 1-2), followed patient dollars (Part 3), examined working international models (Part 4), and described the bipartisan compromise nobody wants (Part 5).

Now we show you why nobody wants it: because failure is more profitable than success.

The $700 Million Question

Between 2017 and 2024, the healthcare industry spent over $700 million on federal lobbying. Not campaign contributions — that’s separate. This is just lobbying: paying professionals to wine, dine, and persuade members of Congress.

Annual lobbying spending (2023):

  • Pharmaceutical/Health Products: $372 million

  • Hospitals/Nursing Homes: $126 million

  • Health Insurance: $89 million

  • Health Professionals: $98 million

Total: $685 million in one year alone.

For context, the entire gun rights lobby spends about $15 million annually. Big Tech (including Amazon, Google, Meta, Apple combined) spends roughly $70 million. The healthcare industry outspends them by 10-to-1.

What does $700 million buy?

It buys Congress’s attention. It buys access to committee chairs. It buys staffers who draft legislation. It buys think tank reports that justify industry positions. It buys academic studies that question reform proposals.

And most importantly, it buys time — endless delays, procedural obstacles, and watered-down compromises that preserve profits while appearing to address problems.

The Human Conveyor Belt

Lobbying isn’t just money. It’s people. Specifically, it’s the same people rotating between writing laws in Congress and lobbying against those laws from K Street.

Let’s meet some of them.

Billy Tauzin: The Godfather of Healthcare Lobbying

Background: Republican Congressman from Louisiana (1980-2005), Chairman of House Energy and Commerce Committee (2001-2004)

What he did in Congress: Co-authored Medicare Part D (prescription drug benefit for seniors) in 2003. The law explicitly prohibited Medicare from negotiating drug prices — a provision worth billions annually to pharmaceutical companies.

Where he went next: PhRMA (Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America) president and CEO, 2005-2010. Starting salary: $2 million annually.

Translation: Tauzin wrote the law prohibiting Medicare from negotiating drug prices, then immediately took a $2 million/year job with the industry that benefited from that prohibition.

This isn’t corruption in the legal sense — it’s perfectly legal. That’s the problem.

Liz Fowler: The ACA Architect Turned Insurance Executive

Background: Vice President of Public Policy at WellPoint (now Anthem, one of America’s largest insurers) before joining Senate

What she did in Congress: Chief health counsel to Senator Max Baucus (D-MT), primary author of the Affordable Care Act (2009-2010)

Where she went next: Vice President at Johnson & Johnson (2012-2015), then Executive Vice President at Anthem (2021-present)

What this means: The person who wrote the ACA was an insurance industry executive before and after writing it. The ACA preserved private insurance, maintained high administrative costs, and avoided strict price controls.

Coincidence?

Liz Fowler Part II: Max Baucus’s Entire Staff

Senator Baucus wasn’t just influential — he chaired the Senate Finance Committee during ACA negotiations. His staff didn’t just write the law. They became the healthcare industry.

Former Baucus staffers who became healthcare lobbyists:

  • Jeff Forbes: Chief of Staff to Baucus → Senior VP, Pharmaceutical Care Management Association

  • David Schwartz: Tax Counsel to Baucus → Lobbyist for healthcare companies at Alston & Bird

  • Russell Sullivan: Professional Staff Member → Health policy lobbyist at Prime Policy Group

  • Jon Selib: Communications Director → Healthcare lobbying at Alston & Bird

Pattern: Write the law → Immediately profit from the law by helping companies navigate the law you wrote.

Republican Side: Orrin Hatch’s Pipeline

Senator Orrin Hatch (R-UT) served on Senate Finance Committee for decades and chaired it multiple times. His staff had similar career trajectories.

Former Hatch staffers who became healthcare lobbyists:

  • Chris Campbell: Chief Economist → Healthcare lobbyist at Alpine Group

  • Mark Prater: Senior Tax Counsel → Pharmaceutical lobbyist at Pfizer

  • Pete Matheson: Chief of Staff → Healthcare lobbyist at Faegre Drinker

This isn’t partisan. Both parties feed the revolving door. Both parties benefit from it. Both parties refuse to close it.

Tom Daschle: The Almost-HHS Secretary

Background: Senate Majority Leader (D-SD, 1987-2005), lost re-election in 2004

What he did next: Special Policy Advisor to lobbying firm Alston & Bird, reportedly earning $2 million annually lobbying for healthcare companies including UnitedHealth Group and CVS Caremark

2009: President Obama nominated Daschle for Secretary of Health and Human Services. Daschle withdrew after disclosure of his lobbying income and tax issues.

Current status: Still lobbying for healthcare companies at Baker Donelson, still earning millions representing the industry he was supposed to regulate.

The Medicare Part D Gold Rush

The 2003 Medicare Part D legislation wasn’t just about Billy Tauzin. It created an entire industry of lobbyists who wrote the law, then profited from it.

Key staffers who wrote Medicare Part D, then joined the industry:

  • Cybele Bjorklund: House Republican policy director → Lobbyist for pharmaceutical companies at Patton Boggs

  • Jay Khosla: Senate Finance Committee health counsel → Vice President at Health Policy Source, representing drug companies

  • Jeremy Benson: Legislative assistant to Senator Grassley → Healthcare lobbyist at Fierce Government Relations

The pattern is consistent: Write industry-friendly legislation. Leave Congress. Get hired by the industry you helped. Earn 5-10 times your government salary.

Ratio of healthcare lobbyists to members of Congress: 5.5 to 1

For every senator and representative, there are more than five healthcare lobbyists working to influence them. Not counting the unregistered “consultants,” “advisors,” and “strategic counselors” who technically aren’t lobbyists but do the same work.

What Lobbying Buys: Case Studies

Let’s see how this money translates into policy outcomes.

Case Study 1: The Medicare Part D “Donut Hole” (2003-2010)

The Setup: Medicare Part D created prescription drug coverage for seniors. Good idea. But it included a bizarre coverage gap — the “donut hole.”

How it worked:

  • Medicare covered drugs up to $2,250 in annual costs

  • Then coverage STOPPED — seniors paid 100% out-of-pocket from $2,250 to $5,100

  • Then coverage resumed at 95%

Why? To keep costs down for the government while maintaining drug company profits. Seniors paid the difference.

Who designed this? Billy Tauzin and his staff, working closely with PhRMA lobbyists.

Who benefited? Pharmaceutical companies. Seniors couldn’t negotiate prices. Medicare couldn’t negotiate prices. Drug companies set prices.

Cost to seniors: Estimated $42 billion in out-of-pocket costs from 2006-2010, before the donut hole was gradually closed by the ACA.

Lobbying money spent defending the donut hole (2006-2010): Over $1 billion from pharmaceutical industry.

Case Study 2: The ACA’s Individual Mandate Penalty (2010-2017)

The Setup: The ACA required everyone to buy health insurance or pay a penalty. This mandate was essential to making the system work — you can’t ban pre-existing condition exclusions without requiring healthy people to buy insurance.

What happened: Republicans campaigned against the mandate for 8 years. Insurance companies quietly supported repealing it.

Why would insurance companies oppose a mandate that forces people to buy their product? Because the mandate came with price controls, coverage requirements, and medical loss ratio rules (80-85% of premiums must go to medical care, not profit/overhead).

Insurance companies preferred the pre-ACA system: deny people with pre-existing conditions, charge whatever they wanted, keep 20-30% of premiums as profit/overhead.

2017: Republicans eliminated the individual mandate penalty (while keeping the rest of the ACA). Insurance companies didn’t fight to save it. They just raised premiums.

Lobbying money spent by insurance industry 2010-2017: Over $650 million

Result: The mandate is gone. Insurance companies still profit. Premiums rose 25-30%. Healthy people dropped coverage. Sick people kept it. Insurers made more money.

Case Study 3: The Inflation Reduction Act Drug Pricing (2022) — When Lobbying Lost

Here’s the twist: Sometimes, lobbying fails.

2022: Democrats passed the Inflation Reduction Act, including Medicare drug price negotiation for 10 drugs (expanding to 20 by 2029). This was the first time Medicare gained any drug pricing negotiation power since Part D passed in 2003.

PhRMA response: All-out war.

Lobbying spending by pharmaceutical industry in 2021-2022: Over $750 million across two years.

Number of lobbyists deployed: 1,600+ registered pharmaceutical lobbyists.

Campaign contributions: Over $100 million to candidates in 2020-2022 cycle.

Result: They lost.

Why this matters: It proves that even $750 million in lobbying and 1,600 lobbyists can’t always stop reform when public anger is high enough and political will exists.

The drug pricing negotiation passed. It’s working. The first 10 drugs are being negotiated now. Prices are dropping 40-60% on negotiated medications.

This is the exception that proves the rule: Reform is possible. But it requires overcoming $750 million in lobbying, 1,600 industry representatives, and decades of captured politicians.

Campaign Contributions: The Other Half

Lobbying is just direct persuasion. Campaign contributions are indirect persuasion: “Here’s $50,000 for your re-election. Remember that when you vote on our industry.”

Healthcare industry campaign contributions (2020-2024 cycle):

  • Pharmaceuticals/Health Products: $176 million

  • Health Professionals: $167 million

  • Hospitals/Nursing Homes: $73 million

  • Health Insurance: $52 million

Total: $468 million over four years

Top recipients (2020-2024):

  • Senate candidates received $142 million

  • House candidates received $237 million

  • Presidential candidates received $89 million

Split by party:

  • Democrats: 55% ($257 million)

  • Republicans: 45% ($211 million)

Translation: The healthcare industry hedges its bets. They donate to both parties. They don’t care who wins. They care that whoever wins owes them.

The Think Tank Ecosystem

Lobbying isn’t just Congress. It’s the entire intellectual infrastructure that shapes policy debates.

Healthcare industry funding of think tanks and research centers:

American Enterprise Institute: Receives funding from PhRMA, insurance companies, and hospital associations. Consistently publishes research questioning universal healthcare feasibility.

Heritage Foundation: Receives healthcare industry funding. Authored the individual mandate proposal in 1989 (later adopted by Romney and Obama), then opposed it after Democrats embraced it.

Brookings Institution: Receives funding from pharmaceutical companies and health insurers. Publishes more balanced research but rarely advocates for single-payer systems.

Urban Institute: Receives healthcare industry funding. Produces detailed cost analyses of reform proposals — often emphasizing costs and downplaying savings.

The pattern: Industry doesn’t just lobby politicians. They fund the research that politicians cite. They shape the intellectual debate before bills are even drafted.

When a senator says “studies show Medicare for All would cost $32 trillion,” they’re often citing studies funded by the industries that would lose profits under Medicare for All.

The Revolving Door Numbers

Between 2000-2024:

Former members of Congress who became healthcare lobbyists: 47

Former Congressional staffers (committee staff, leadership staff) who became healthcare lobbyists: 584+

Former HHS officials who joined healthcare companies: 127

Former FDA officials who joined pharmaceutical companies: 219

Former CMS officials who joined insurance companies: 83

This is the conveyor belt. Write the regulations. Enforce the regulations. Then get hired by the companies you regulated to help them navigate the regulations you wrote.

Average salary increase when moving from government to healthcare lobbying: 300-400%. A $180,000 Senate staffer becomes a $600,000-$900,000 lobbyist overnight.

Why This System Persists

Because everyone benefits except patients.

Politicians benefit: Campaign contributions fund re-election. Lobbyists provide research, talking points, and draft legislation. Post-Congress lobbying careers await.

Lobbyists benefit: $300,000-$1,200,000 annual salaries. Access to power. Prestigious careers.

Healthcare companies benefit: Laws written to protect profits. Regulations that create barriers to entry. Price controls avoided. Administrative complexity maintained.

Think tanks benefit: Industry funding keeps doors open, staff employed, and influence strong.

Who loses? Americans paying $14,885 per capita for healthcare that costs $7,371 elsewhere. Americans going bankrupt from medical bills. Americans dying from inability to afford medications.

But those Americans don’t have lobbyists. They don’t write $31 million checks. They don’t offer $600,000 jobs to former senators.

So the system continues.

The International Comparison

Other countries have lobbying. Other countries have pharmaceutical companies, insurance companies, and hospitals. Other countries have campaign contributions and revolving doors.

But other countries have stronger restrictions:

Canada: Lobbyists must register publicly. Five-year cooling-off period before former officials can lobby. Strict gift limits.

Germany: Two-year cooling-off period. Lobbying spending publicly disclosed. Industry contributions to political campaigns heavily restricted.

Switzerland: Direct democracy referendum system allows citizens to overturn industry-friendly legislation. Strict financial disclosure requirements.

United Kingdom: Independent regulatory bodies insulated from political pressure. NHS purchasing power makes it harder for drug companies to charge excessive prices.

United States: Minimal cooling-off periods (1-2 years, often waived). Unlimited lobbying spending. Campaign contributions limited per-donor but unlimited through PACs and Super PACs. Regulatory capture is policy, not scandal.

But Here’s the Hope: The IRA Drug Pricing Victory

Remember: In 2022, despite $750 million in lobbying, despite 1,600 pharmaceutical lobbyists, despite decades of Medicare being prohibited from negotiating drug prices — reform passed.

How?

  1. Public anger was high enough: Insulin prices at $300/vial became a national scandal. Americans were dying rationing insulin. The stories were everywhere.

  2. Democrats unified: For once, moderate and progressive Democrats agreed drug pricing negotiation was necessary.

  3. Budget reconciliation: Only 50 votes needed (plus VP Harris tiebreaker). No Republican support required.

  4. Immediate implementation: First 10 drugs being negotiated now. Results visible quickly.

Current results (early 2025):

  • Negotiated drug prices 40-79% lower than previous prices

  • Estimated savings: $6 billion in first year

  • Pharmaceutical companies still profitable

  • No drug development stopped

  • Every dire industry prediction proved false

This proves reform is possible. The money doesn’t always win. The lobbyists don’t always prevail. The revolving door can be bypassed.

But it requires political courage, public pressure, and unified action.
