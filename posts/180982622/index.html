What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 2: Atlantic City — The Art of the Bankruptcy (1980s-2009)

December 11, 2025

The Castle Built on Other People’s Money

By Stephen Gordon
CanadianSnowmanSays

In April 1990, Donald Trump stood before cameras at the grand opening of the Trump Taj Mahal in Atlantic City, describing it as “the eighth wonder of the world.” Built at a cost of $1.1 billion — making it the most expensive casino ever constructed — the Taj Mahal represented Trump’s vision realized: a massive monument to excess, financed almost entirely with other people’s money.

Thirteen months later, it was in bankruptcy.

The Taj Mahal’s collapse was just the beginning. Over the next two decades, Trump’s casino empire would file for bankruptcy protection six times, leaving a trail of devastated small businesses, unpaid contractors, unemployed workers, and burned investors. Yet through it all, Trump himself prospered, extracting millions in salary, bonuses, and management fees while shifting his personal debts onto the casino companies.

“Atlantic City fueled a lot of growth for me,” Trump later boasted to The New York Times. “The money I took out of there was incredible.”

This is the story of how Trump perfected the art of corporate bankruptcy as personal enrichment — and how he faced no meaningful accountability for the financial devastation he left behind.

Building an Empire on Borrowed Time (1982-1990)

Trump’s entry into Atlantic City gambling began in 1982, when he received a casino license from the New Jersey Casino Control Commission. His first property, Trump Plaza (originally Harrah’s at Trump Plaza), opened in 1984 through a partnership with Harrah’s Entertainment. Crucially, Trump put up no money of his own. Harrah’s provided $220 million in financing and paid Trump a $24 million construction management fee plus $22 million for the land.

From the start, Trump was extracting value from projects financed by others.

The partnership with Harrah’s quickly soured. “It wasn’t a well-designed partnership,” recalled Philip G. Satre, retired chairman of Harrah’s. “We were a big company with an institutional approach to running a business, and he was a real estate guy who liked to do it by the seat of his pants.” Trump bought out Harrah’s stake, renaming it Trump Plaza.

In 1985, Trump acquired Trump’s Castle (later Trump Marina) for $320 million. According to investigative reporting by The New York Times, Trump borrowed more than the purchase price, putting little or none of his own money into the deal. In 1988, he purchased the unfinished Taj Mahal casino from Resorts International for $230 million, again using borrowed funds.

The Taj Mahal’s financing was particularly aggressive: $675 million in junk bonds at a 14% interest rate. Trump was now personally guaranteeing $832.5 million in loans, while his casino companies carried $1.3 billion in debt. New Jersey regulators warned in August 1990 that “the possibility of a complete financial collapse of the Trump Organization was not out of the question.”

They were right to worry. Trump had created an unsustainable debt structure that required the casinos to generate massive profits just to cover interest payments. When the U.S. economy entered recession in 1990, the house of cards collapsed.

The First Wave of Bankruptcies (1991-1992)

The Taj Mahal missed its first debt payment in November 1990, just months after opening. Trump’s Castle was also late on payments. By early 1991, Trump was negotiating with bankers who faced the prospect of seizing his assets and getting back pennies on the dollar.

Trump’s solution: bankruptcy protection.

1991: Trump Taj Mahal filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Trump ceded 50% ownership to bondholders in exchange for lowered interest rates and more time to pay debt. He also sold his Trump Shuttle airline and his 282-foot yacht, the Trump Princess.

1992: Trump Plaza and Trump’s Castle both filed for bankruptcy protection as Trump struggled to service the massive debts.

1992: Plaza Hotel (in Manhattan, not a casino) also filed for bankruptcy after Trump had purchased it for $393 million in 1988.

Through these bankruptcies, Trump learned a crucial lesson: Chapter 11 protection allowed him to restructure debt, force creditors to accept losses, and emerge with reduced obligations — all while maintaining control and continuing to draw salary.

But there was another category of victims: the contractors and small businesses who had built Trump’s empire.

The Contractors Who Got Stiffed

When the Taj Mahal filed for bankruptcy in 1991, Trump still owed $70 million to contractors and small businesses that had provided services to the casino. These weren’t wealthy bondholders who could absorb losses — they were working-class entrepreneurs whose livelihoods depended on being paid for work they’d completed.

Forest Jenkins and Triad Building Specialties had a $231,000 contract to install bathroom partitions in the Taj Mahal. Jenkins and his team spent months on the job in 1988 and 1989. The work was completed and approved. But the payment never came.

After three years of fighting through bankruptcy court, Jenkins received just $70,000 — 30 cents on the dollar. His company nearly collapsed.

“Trump crawled his way to the top on the back of little guys, one of them being my father,” Beth Rosser, Jenkins’s daughter, told The New York Times in 2016. “He had no regard for the thousands of men and women who worked on these projects.” Twenty-seven years after her father was stiffed, Rosser said: “I grit my teeth every time I see him on television blustering about what a wonderful businessman he is.”

Jenkins’s company was one of 253 subcontractors that performed work on the Taj Mahal. Trump’s bankruptcy meant mom-and-pop shops that had installed walls, plumbing, and lighting were left holding $69.5 million in unpaid bills.

The Edward J. Friel Company built cabinets for Trump Plaza in the early 1980s under a $400,000 contract. After the work was completed and approved, Trump bought out the construction contract from the general contractor and then refused to make the final payment of $84,000 — which represented all of Friel’s profit on the job.

“We had already worked for three [general contractors], and every single one of them lived up to their word until Donald Trump came to Atlantic City,” Edward Friel Jr. told CNN. Friel’s father tried to recoup the money but eventually gave up. Friel believes Trump used his influence to block his company from working on any future Atlantic City projects. The company filed for bankruptcy a few years later.

Marty Rosenberg of Atlantic Plate Glass told USA Today that Trump offered small businessmen “less than a third of what he owed them.” Trump’s breach of contract and the long wait for payment cost some tradesmen their companies — everything they’d built in their lives.

Steven P. Perskie, who was New Jersey’s top casino regulator in the early 1990s, summed up the damage: “He put a number of local contractors and suppliers out of business when he didn’t pay them. So when he left Atlantic City, it wasn’t, ‘Sorry to see you go.’ It was, ‘How fast can you get the hell out of here?’”

The Double Dip: Tax Deductions for Unpaid Debts

The contractors’ losses became Trump’s tax advantage through what experts call “a double dip.” When Trump’s casinos went bankrupt and he avoided paying contractors, the IRS allowed him to claim those unpaid obligations as business losses — losses he could then write off against future income.

Under rules in effect until 2002, the IRS counted cancelled debt or unpaid liabilities as if the business owner had invested that money into the business. This raised the bar on what could be written off if the business failed, allowing Trump to use losses as a deduction against years of future income — even though he had invested little or no cash of his own.

When Trump claimed a $916 million loss on his 1995 tax return (later revealed by The New York Times), a significant portion came from debt he never paid back and obligations to contractors he never fulfilled. The contractors lost their money and sometimes their businesses. Trump got a tax deduction that potentially allowed him to avoid paying federal income taxes for nearly two decades.

“It’s legalized theft,” one tax expert told Fortune magazine.Plaza Hotel.

Trump Gets Rich While His Casinos Fail (1990s-2000s)

Even as Trump’s casinos hemorrhaged money and filed for repeated bankruptcies, Trump himself prospered. A detailed investigation by The New York Times found that Trump:

Put up little of his own money: Most financing came from junk bonds and loans

Shifted personal debts to the casinos: He transferred his personal obligations onto the casino companies’ balance sheets

Collected millions in salary and fees: He received regular payments as bonuses, salary, and management fees even as the businesses failed

Extracted cash through refinancing: “Early on, I took a lot of money out of the casinos with the financings and the things we do,” Trump told the Times

The pattern was consistent: over-leverage the properties with unsustainable debt, extract personal wealth through fees and salary, let the companies fail, and use bankruptcy to force creditors to accept losses.

“Even as his companies did poorly, Mr. Trump did well,” the Times reported. “The burden of his failures fell on investors and others who had bet on his business acumen.”

H. Steven Norton, a casino consultant and former casino executive, summarized the problem: “He helped expand Atlantic City, but he just did not put the equity into the projects he should have to keep them solvent. When he went bankrupt, he not only cost bondholders money, but he hurt a lot of small businesses that helped him construct the Taj Mahal.”

The Second Wave: Taking It Public (2004)

In 1995, Trump shifted ownership of the Plaza casino to a new publicly traded company: Trump Hotels and Casino Resorts. The initial public offering sold 10 million shares at $14 each, and the company also sold $155 million in junk bonds at a 15.5% interest rate. Becoming a public company meant Trump could shift risk to shareholders while maintaining control as chairman and largest shareholder.

It didn’t help. In 2004, Trump Hotels & Casino Resorts filed for bankruptcy with $1.8 billion in debt. Trump’s ownership was reduced from 56% to 27%, and the company was renamed Trump Entertainment Resorts. Trump stepped back from day-to-day operations but continued collecting fees for use of his name.

Investment bank lawyers were blunt in their assessment. In court filings, they wrote: “The Trump name does not connote high-quality amenities and first-class service in the casino industry. Rather, the Trump name is associated with the failure to pay one’s debts, a company that has lost money every year, and properties in need of significant deferred maintenance and lagging behind their competitors.”

The Third Wave: Complete Collapse (2009-2014)

Trump Entertainment Resorts filed for bankruptcy again in February 2009, owing $1.2 billion. The 2008 financial crisis had devastated Atlantic City gambling revenues, but Trump’s casinos had been struggling long before the recession hit.

In 2009, Trump resigned as chairman just before the company filed for bankruptcy protection. This was a familiar pattern: distance himself from failure at the critical moment. Carl Icahn, the billionaire investor, eventually took control of the properties.

In 2014, Trump Entertainment filed for bankruptcy a fourth time. Trump sued to have his name removed from the casinos, claiming they had been “allowed to fall into disrepair” in breach of the licensing agreement. The Trump Taj Mahal finally closed in October 2016. Trump Plaza was demolished in 2021 using 3,000 sticks of dynamite — a fitting end to an empire built on explosive debt.

The Human Cost: Beyond the Numbers

The bankruptcies eliminated nearly 3,000 jobs at the Taj Mahal alone — 37% higher employee losses than comparable Atlantic City casinos. The Trump Marina closure cost additional thousands of jobs. Beyond job losses, the economic damage rippled through Atlantic City:

Small contractors went out of business

Suppliers filed for bankruptcy after non-payment

The local business ecosystem was damaged for years

Property values collapsed around the failed casinos

Tax revenue disappeared, hurting city services

Juan Carlos Enriquez, owner of The Paint Spot, did work for Trump’s Doral golf resort in Florida but was never paid the final $32,000. After a three-year legal fight, a court found in Enriquez’s favor in 2017 and ordered Trump’s company to pay not just the $32,000 but $300,000 in legal fees.

Noel Cintron worked as Trump’s chauffeur for 25 years. According to his lawsuit, Trump only gave him two raises after 2003: to $68,000 in 2006, and then to $75,000 in 2010 — but the second raise required Cintron to give up his health benefits. Cintron claimed 3,300 hours of unpaid overtime in just the last six years and sued for at least $350,000 in damages.

48 servers at Trump National Doral had to sue for unpaid overtime after working long hours for a special event. Settlements averaged around $800 per worker, with some as high as $3,000.

A USA Today investigation found Trump was involved in over 3,500 lawsuits during three decades. “At least 60 lawsuits, along with hundreds of liens, judgments, and other government filings” involved contractors claiming they got stiffed. The investigation also found “24 violations of the Fair Labor Standards Act since 2005 for failing to pay overtime or minimum wage.”

Trump’s Defense: “That’s Just Business”

When confronted with the contractor non-payments, Trump’s response was consistent: blame the victims and claim it was justified.

“Let’s say that they do a job that’s not good, or a job that they didn’t finish, or a job that was way late,” Trump said. “I’ll deduct from their contract, absolutely.”

But the contractors tell a different story. Their work was completed, approved by inspectors, and accepted by general contractors. Trump simply refused to pay after the work was done, using his leverage and deep pockets for legal fees to force small businesses to accept pennies on the dollar.

As for the bankruptcies, Trump framed them as savvy business tactics. “I’ve used the laws of this country to pare debt,” he said. “We’ll have the company. We’ll throw it into a chapter. We’ll negotiate with the banks. We’ll make a fantastic deal. You know, it’s like on The Apprentice. It’s not personal. It’s just business.”

But it was personal to Forest Jenkins, whose business nearly collapsed. It was personal to Edward Friel, whose family company went bankrupt. It was personal to the 3,000 workers who lost their jobs when the Taj Mahal closed.

Trump saw bankruptcy as a tool for personal enrichment. The people whose livelihoods he destroyed saw it differently.

Why No Accountability?

The Atlantic City catastrophe should have resulted in serious legal and financial consequences for Trump:

Criminal Investigation for Fraud: There’s a strong case that Trump’s practices constituted fraud — accepting work from contractors with no intention of paying them in full, misrepresenting his financial condition to obtain loans, and systematically breaching contracts. But no criminal charges were ever filed.

Civil RICO Claims: The pattern of systematic non-payment could have supported civil racketeering charges. But while individual contractors sued, no coordinated legal effort emerged to hold Trump accountable under federal racketeering statutes.

Personal Bankruptcy: Given his personal guarantees of over $800 million in loans, Trump should have been forced into personal bankruptcy. But he negotiated deals with banks that allowed him to avoid personal bankruptcy while his companies failed.

Bar from Future Casino Operations: New Jersey gaming regulators could have revoked Trump’s casino license or barred him from future operations. They didn’t. Trump maintained his license even as his casinos serially failed.

Securities Fraud Charges: As chairman of a public company, Trump had fiduciary duties to shareholders. His extraction of personal wealth while the company failed could have supported securities fraud charges. None were filed.

Why did Trump escape accountability? The same factors from Part 1 remained in play:

Wealth and Legal Resources: Trump could afford armies of lawyers to fight every contractor, delay every case, and exhaust opponents’ resources.

Corporate Bankruptcy Protection: Chapter 11 is designed to allow businesses to restructure. Trump weaponized it to dump debts while maintaining control.

Sophisticated Tax Planning: Trump’s accountants found every loophole to convert business failures into personal tax advantages.

Political Connections: Trump cultivated relationships with regulators and politicians who might otherwise have taken action.

Victim Fragmentation: Contractors sued individually rather than mounting coordinated litigation. This allowed Trump to pick them off one by one.

System Design: The legal system treats corporate and personal liability separately. Trump used this to shield his personal assets while his companies failed.

The Larger Pattern

The Atlantic City disasters reveal Trump’s core business model: leverage other people’s money, extract personal wealth through fees and salary, and use bankruptcy to avoid accountability when the inevitable collapse occurs. The pattern established in the 1990s would repeat throughout his career.

More fundamentally, the Atlantic City experience demonstrates how American capitalism’s rules allow wealthy individuals to privatize gains while socializing losses. Trump’s creditors, contractors, and workers bore the costs of his failures. Trump walked away rich.

In a functioning accountability system, Trump’s business career would have ended in Atlantic City. Banks wouldn’t lend to someone with four bankruptcies. Contractors wouldn’t work for someone with a documented history of non-payment. Investors wouldn’t trust someone who had destroyed billions in shareholder value.

Instead, Trump leveraged his notoriety into celebrity, hosting The Apprentice and building a brand around the very business failures that should have destroyed him. He successfully reframed six bankruptcies as evidence of business savvy rather than spectacular failure.

The contractors who lost their businesses had no such opportunity for reinvention. They simply bore the costs of Trump’s “success.”

Looking Ahead

In Part 3, we’ll examine Trump University — a fraudulent scheme that bilked thousands of people out of millions of dollars with false promises of real estate riches. Trump eventually settled for $25 million, but only after being elected president and only after fighting the case for years. Once again, the pattern held: delay, deny, attack, and ultimately settle without admitting wrongdoing.

The throughline from the bone spurs to Atlantic City to Trump University is unmistakable: consequences are for other people. As long as you have money for lawyers and no shame about leaving devastation in your wake, American institutions will allow you to evade accountability indefinitely.
