What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 6: The Mueller Report – Ten Instances of Obstruction, (2017-2019)

December 24, 2025

The Report That Should Have Changed Everything

On March 22, 2019, Special Counsel Robert Mueller delivered his report to Attorney General William Barr. The investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election and potential obstruction of justice had consumed twenty-two months, involved 500 witness interviews, executed nearly 500 search warrants, and cost approximately $32 million.

What Mueller found should have ended Donald Trump’s presidency.

Instead, it became another chapter in the story of how Trump evaded accountability—this time through a combination of constitutional constraints, political cowardice, and deliberate misrepresentation of the findings.

Mueller’s team documented ten separate instances where President Trump potentially obstructed justice. The Special Counsel’s careful language reflected an impossible situation: Department of Justice policy prohibited indicting a sitting president, but the evidence demanded documentation. Mueller’s solution was to lay out the facts and leave the judgment to Congress.

“If we had confidence after a thorough investigation of the facts that the President clearly did not commit obstruction of justice,” Mueller wrote, “we would so state. Based on the facts and the applicable legal standards, however, we are unable to reach that judgment.”

This was not exoneration. This was a prosecutor saying: I found substantial evidence of crimes, but I’m constitutionally prohibited from charging him. Congress, this is your job now.

Congress failed. This essay examines the first five instances of obstruction Mueller documented, revealing a president willing to abuse every lever of power to derail an investigation into his campaign and presidency.

Instance 1: The Campaign’s Response to Reports About Russian Support (June-November 2016)

The Context

In June 2016, news organizations began reporting that Russian government hackers had penetrated the Democratic National Committee’s computer networks. By July, WikiLeaks was publishing stolen emails in coordinated releases designed to damage Hillary Clinton’s campaign. U.S. intelligence agencies would later conclude with high confidence that Russia was conducting these operations specifically to help Trump win.

Trump’s public response was consistent and aggressive: denial, deflection, and ridicule of the very idea that Russia was helping him.

“I don’t know Putin,” Trump said repeatedly, despite having bragged about their relationship in the past. At a July 2016 press conference, he invited Russian hackers to find Clinton’s missing emails: “Russia, if you’re listening, I hope you’re able to find the 30,000 emails that are missing.”

The Obstruction

Mueller’s team found that Trump’s campaign was deeply concerned about public perception of the Russian assistance. Campaign officials recognized that the Russia story could undermine Trump’s electoral victory and create legal jeopardy. Rather than cooperating with investigations or distancing the campaign from Russian interference, Trump and his team chose obstruction through public deception.

Trump’s repeated false statements about Russian interference served multiple obstructive purposes. They discouraged witnesses from cooperating with investigators by creating a narrative that the entire inquiry was illegitimate. They signaled to potential witnesses what story Trump wanted told. And they laid groundwork for later obstruction by establishing the “witch hunt” narrative that would justify interfering with investigations.

The pattern was clear: whenever evidence of Russian interference surfaced, Trump attacked the investigators, attacked the intelligence community, and attacked the legitimacy of the inquiry itself. This wasn’t merely political spin—it was a calculated strategy to prevent Americans from understanding what Russia had done and to discourage any serious investigation.

Instance 2: The President’s Conduct Concerning Michael Flynn (February 2017)

The Setup

Michael Flynn served as Trump’s National Security Advisor for just 24 days. During the presidential transition, Flynn had multiple conversations with Russian Ambassador Sergey Kislyak about U.S. sanctions against Russia. When FBI agents interviewed Flynn about these contacts on January 24, 2017, he lied.

Flynn’s lies created immediate legal jeopardy for him and potential problems for Trump. If Flynn cooperated with investigators, what might he reveal about the campaign’s relationship with Russia? About Trump’s own knowledge of Flynn’s Russian contacts?

On February 13, 2017, after media reports exposed Flynn’s lies, Trump forced Flynn to resign. The official reason was that Flynn had misled Vice President Pence about the Kislyak conversations. But Trump’s private actions the next day revealed different concerns.

The Obstruction

February 14, 2017, began with a terrorism briefing in the Oval Office. When it concluded, Trump asked everyone to leave except FBI Director James Comey. Vice President Pence, Attorney General Jeff Sessions, Jared Kushner—all were dismissed. Trump wanted Comey alone.

According to Comey’s contemporaneous memo written immediately after the meeting, Trump said: “I hope you can see your way clear to letting this go, to letting Flynn go. He is a good guy. I hope you can let this go.”

Trump’s defenders later claimed this was an innocent expression of personal affection for Flynn, not a directive. But context matters. The President of the United States cleared the Oval Office of his most senior advisors to have a private conversation with the FBI Director about an ongoing investigation. Trump knew Flynn had lied to the FBI. And Trump was asking the FBI Director to “let it go.”

This wasn’t a suggestion. It was the President directing the FBI Director to drop a criminal investigation into someone who could implicate Trump.

Comey understood this immediately. He didn’t let it go. Instead, he documented the conversation and continued the investigation. Trump’s attempt had failed, but the attempt itself constituted obstruction—an obstructive act (the directive to Comey), connected to an official proceeding (the Flynn investigation), with corrupt intent (protecting Flynn and potentially himself).

Trump’s Consciousness of Guilt

Trump’s conduct after this meeting revealed his awareness that he had crossed a line. When Attorney General Sessions tried to remain in the Oval Office after the briefing, Trump sent him away. Trump knew he needed privacy for what he was about to do.

Later, when Comey testified publicly about these events, Trump’s fury demonstrated his understanding of the meeting’s significance. An innocent person comfortable with his conduct would welcome public scrutiny. Trump instead attacked Comey, called him a liar, and eventually used this episode as partial justification for firing him.

Instance 3: The President’s Reaction to Public Confirmation of the Investigation (March-April 2017)

The Public Confirmation

On March 20, 2017, FBI Director Comey testified before the House Intelligence Committee and publicly confirmed for the first time that the FBI was investigating Russian interference in the 2016 election, including possible coordination with the Trump campaign.

Trump’s reaction was immediate and extreme. While the investigation was ongoing, Trump had been able to maintain public ambiguity about whether he personally was under investigation. Comey’s testimony ended that ambiguity and created enormous political pressure on the new administration.

Trump became obsessed with getting government officials to make public statements that he was not personally under investigation.

The Obstruction Through DNI and CIA

Trump’s first approach was to enlist the intelligence community leadership. Shortly after Comey’s testimony, Trump spoke separately with Director of National Intelligence Dan Coats and CIA Director Mike Pompeo, asking them to publicly state that no evidence existed of coordination between the Trump campaign and Russia.

Both Coats and Pompeo felt uncomfortable with these requests. They viewed them as inappropriate attempts to involve the intelligence community in the Russia investigation. According to testimony later provided to Mueller’s team, Coats told associates that Trump had asked him if he could do anything to “get past” the Russia investigation. Pompeo had similar conversations where Trump sought public statements helpful to the President.

These requests placed the intelligence chiefs in an impossible position. If they complied, they would be making public statements about an ongoing FBI investigation—statements designed to benefit the President politically. If they refused, they risked the President’s anger.

Both declined. But Trump’s willingness to pressure intelligence officials to intervene in the Russia investigation demonstrated his determination to obstruct the inquiry through any means available.

The Direct Appeals to Comey

When the intelligence chiefs wouldn’t help, Trump turned directly back to Comey. Trump called Comey on March 30, 2017, and again on April 11, 2017, asking Comey to publicly announce that Trump was not personally under investigation.

These calls were extraordinary. Presidents don’t call FBI Directors about ongoing investigations. They don’t ask for public statements designed to benefit themselves politically. The separation between the White House and federal law enforcement exists precisely to prevent this kind of interference.

Comey refused both requests, explaining that making such a statement would create a duty to correct if circumstances changed. But Trump’s persistence revealed his corrupt intent—he wanted to use the FBI to create public statements that would undermine or limit the investigation’s scope.

Mueller’s team found that Trump’s efforts to enlist Coats, Pompeo, and Comey constituted potential obstruction because they were attempts to use government officials to make public statements that would interfere with the investigation’s credibility and momentum.

Instance 4: The President’s Termination of Comey (May 2017)

FBI Director James Comey testifies on Capitol Hill in Washington, Monday, March 20, 2017, before the House Intelligence Committee hearing on allegations of Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. presidential election. (AP Photo/J. Scott Applewhite)

The Firing

On May 9, 2017, Trump fired FBI Director James Comey. The termination letter cited Deputy Attorney General Rod Rosenstein’s memo criticizing Comey’s handling of the Hillary Clinton email investigation during the 2016 campaign.

This justification immediately fell apart.

On May 11, Trump gave an interview to NBC’s Lester Holt. Asked about the firing, Trump abandoned the official story: “I was going to fire Comey—my decision... I was going to fire regardless of recommendation... And in fact, when I decided to just do it, I said to myself, I said, you know, this Russia thing with Trump and Russia is a made-up story.”

Trump had just admitted on television that he fired the FBI Director because of the Russia investigation.

The next day, May 12, Trump met with Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov and Ambassador Sergey Kislyak in the Oval Office. According to a document read to the New York Times, Trump told the Russian officials: “I just fired the head of the F.B.I. He was crazy, a real nut job. I faced great pressure because of Russia. That’s taken off.”

These admissions—to NBC and to Russian officials—destroyed any pretense that Comey’s firing was about the Clinton investigation. Trump fired Comey because of Russia, and he expected the firing to relieve pressure on him.

The Pattern Before the Firing

Mueller’s investigation revealed that Trump had been contemplating Comey’s firing since at least February 2017, shortly after Comey refused to pledge personal loyalty and declined to “let Flynn go.”

Throughout March and April 2017, Trump grew increasingly angry at Comey for refusing to publicly state that Trump was not under investigation. Multiple witnesses described Trump’s fury when Comey’s March 20 testimony confirmed the FBI investigation. Trump complained repeatedly that Comey was not “loyal” and was not protecting him the way he believed Attorney General Bobby Kennedy had protected President John F. Kennedy.

This pattern of complaints established that Trump’s real grievance with Comey was his refusal to end or limit the Russia investigation. When pressure tactics failed, Trump resorted to termination.

Consciousness of Guilt

The manner of Comey’s firing revealed Trump’s awareness that his conduct was problematic. Rather than a face-to-face conversation or even a phone call, Comey learned he had been fired from television news while he was in Los Angeles for a speech.

Trump also attempted to justify the firing by soliciting a letter from Rosenstein criticizing Comey’s Clinton investigation handling—trying to create documentary cover for his decision. When that pretext collapsed under scrutiny, Trump’s admission to Lester Holt revealed the real reason.

An innocent president confident in his conduct doesn’t need pretexts. Trump’s elaborate justifications and subsequent admissions demonstrated his understanding that firing the FBI Director investigating him crossed a clear line into obstruction.

Instance 5: The Appointment of Special Counsel and Trump’s Efforts to Remove Him (May-June 2017)

“Oh My God. This Is Terrible.”

Eight days after firing Comey, on May 17, 2017, Deputy Attorney General Rod Rosenstein appointed Robert Mueller as Special Counsel to investigate Russian interference and potential obstruction of justice.

Trump’s reaction, according to multiple witnesses, was visceral and revealing. When then-Attorney General Jeff Sessions arrived at the White House to inform Trump of Mueller’s appointment, Trump slumped back in his chair and said: “Oh my God. This is terrible. This is the end of my Presidency. I’m fucked.”

This response is damning. An innocent president confident that investigation will find nothing doesn’t respond this way to appointment of an independent prosecutor. Trump’s immediate assumption that Mueller’s appointment meant “the end of my Presidency” revealed his consciousness that the investigation threatened to expose criminal conduct.

Trump continued: “Everyone tells me if you get one of these independent counsels it ruins your presidency. It takes years and years and I won’t be able to do anything. This is the worst thing that ever happened to me.”

Note what Trump was worried about: not that investigation would find wrongdoing, but that it would prevent him from doing what he wanted to do as president. His concern was entirely self-interested and political, not about justice or truth.

The Order to Fire Mueller

Within weeks of Mueller’s appointment, Trump decided the Special Counsel needed to be removed. In June 2017, Trump called White House Counsel Don McGahn at home and directed him to call Deputy Attorney General Rosenstein to have Mueller terminated.

Trump gave McGahn three alleged conflicts of interest that supposedly disqualified Mueller: a golf club fee dispute, Mueller’s former law firm representing people in the investigation, and Mueller interviewing for FBI Director the day before his Special Counsel appointment.

None of these alleged conflicts met the legal standard for disqualification, and all were pretextual. Trump was manufacturing reasons to justify firing the person investigating him.

McGahn’s Refusal

Don McGahn, The lawyer who said no.

Don McGahn refused to carry out Trump’s directive. He told Trump he would resign rather than execute the order. McGahn recognized that firing Mueller would be a clear obstruction of justice and would create a “Saturday Night Massacre” situation—a reference to President Nixon’s firing of the Watergate Special Prosecutor that led to multiple Justice Department resignations and accelerated Nixon’s downfall.

McGahn’s refusal prevented what would have been the most blatant obstruction of justice in modern American history: a president directly ordering the termination of the prosecutor investigating him.

But Trump’s attempt itself constituted obstruction. The fact that McGahn refused doesn’t erase Trump’s corrupt intent or his obstructive act. Trump tried to fire Mueller, and only staff resistance prevented it.

The Later Denial and Cover-Up Attempt

In 2018, when news reports revealed Trump’s order to McGahn to fire Mueller, Trump attempted to create a false record. He called McGahn and told him to issue a statement denying the reports. When McGahn refused, Trump told McGahn to write a letter to the file “for our records” stating that Trump never ordered Mueller’s termination.

McGahn again refused. He told Trump’s staff that the news reports were accurate—Trump had ordered Mueller fired, and McGahn had threatened to resign rather than do it.

This later effort to get McGahn to lie constituted additional obstruction—an attempt to create false documentation to cover up the earlier obstruction attempt.

The Pattern Emerges

These first five instances establish a clear pattern:

Escalation: Trump began with pressure tactics (asking Comey to drop Flynn investigation) and escalated to termination (firing Comey) and attempted termination (ordering McGahn to fire Mueller).

Corrupt Intent: Every action was motivated by Trump’s desire to stop, limit, or control the Russia investigation—not by legitimate policy concerns.

Abuse of Power: Trump used his presidential authority to pressure law enforcement and intelligence officials, attempting to turn federal agencies into tools for his personal protection.

Consciousness of Guilt: Trump’s reactions—clearing the Oval Office for private conversations, creating false pretexts for Comey’s firing, his panicked response to Mueller’s appointment—all revealed his awareness that he had something to hide.

Multiple Means: When one obstruction tactic failed, Trump tried another. When Comey wouldn’t pledge loyalty or drop Flynn, Trump fired him. When intelligence chiefs wouldn’t make helpful public statements, Trump tried direct appeals. When Mueller was appointed, Trump tried to fire him.

Mueller documented five more instances of potential obstruction. Part 7 will examine instances 6-10: Trump’s efforts to curtail Mueller’s investigation, his attempts to prevent testimony, and his campaign to influence witness cooperation through promises of pardons and threats of exposure. Together, these ten instances paint a portrait of a president willing to subvert American law enforcement to protect himself—and of a system that ultimately failed to hold him accountable for it.
