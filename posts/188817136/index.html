The Retribution Report — The Roy Cohn Rules

Volume 1: How a McCarthy Lawyer Taught a Young Developer That Revenge Is a Business Model (1973–1986)

March 2, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Roy Cohn watches as his most famous student addresses the press. Within a few years, Cohn would be disbarred, dying of AIDS he refused to acknowledge, and abandoned by the man he'd made. Trump learned every lesson Cohn taught — including, perhaps, the most important one: loyalty flows upward, never down. (Getty Images)

In October 1973, the United States Department of Justice filed one of the largest housing discrimination lawsuits of the era. The defendants were Fred Trump, his twenty-seven-year-old son Donald, and their company, Trump Management Inc. The complaint alleged systematic violations of the Fair Housing Act across 39 properties in Brooklyn, Queens, and Staten Island.

The evidence was devastating. Federal investigators had documented a coordinated scheme to exclude Black and Puerto Rican applicants from Trump-owned apartments. Employees marked applications from Black renters with a “C” for “colored.” Some applications were coded with “No. 9” — internal shorthand for undesirable applicants. Black families were told apartments were unavailable, only to have white testers shown the same units minutes later. A superintendent testified that Trump Management instructed him to attach a separate sheet with a large “C” to any application filed by a Black apartment-seeker.

The Trump family’s regular lawyers advised the obvious: settle the case and move on. The evidence was overwhelming. Fighting would only generate more negative publicity.

Donald Trump had a different instinct. He went looking for a different kind of lawyer.

He found Roy Cohn.

The Man Who Made McCarthy

Roy Cohn and Senator Joseph McCarthy during the Army-McCarthy hearings, 1954. Two decades before he met Donald Trump, Cohn was already perfecting the playbook: attack the accuser, question their loyalty, destroy their reputation. (Getty Images)

To understand what Roy Cohn taught Donald Trump, you need to understand who Cohn was before they met.

Born in 1927 to a politically connected Bronx family, Cohn was a prodigy of ruthlessness. He graduated from Columbia Law School at twenty and was admitted to the New York bar at twenty-one — technically too young to practice, so a special waiver was arranged. By twenty-three, he was a federal prosecutor helping send Julius and Ethel Rosenberg to the electric chair for espionage. The case made him famous. It also revealed the trait that would define his career: a willingness to manipulate legal processes for desired outcomes, regardless of ethical constraints. Decades later, evidence emerged that Cohn had improperly communicated with the judge during the Rosenberg trial — conduct that, had it been known at the time, might have changed the outcome.

His next act was more consequential. In 1953, Cohn became chief counsel to Senator Joseph McCarthy’s Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations — the engine of the Red Scare. At twenty-six years old, Cohn helped orchestrate the hearings that destroyed careers, shattered reputations, and turned accusation into conviction in the court of public opinion. The Army-McCarthy hearings of 1954 ultimately brought McCarthy down, but Cohn survived. He always survived.

For the next three decades, Cohn reinvented himself as New York’s most feared legal fixer. His client list was a masterclass in power without principle: Mafia bosses Tony Salerno, Carmine Galante, and John Gotti. Media mogul Rupert Murdoch. Studio 54 owners Steve Rubell and Ian Schrager. The Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New York. Politicians, socialites, criminals — anyone who needed problems solved by someone who didn’t care about the rules.

Cohn’s cousin David Marcus described his philosophy simply: “When they met, Roy said to him, ‘You might be guilty; it doesn’t matter. Go after the Justice Department. Don’t ever admit guilt.’”

Biographer Wayne Barrett called Cohn “the single greatest influence on Donald Trump’s life for a critical thirteen-year period.”

That period began the night they met at Le Club, an exclusive Manhattan disco, in 1973. Trump told Cohn about the Justice Department lawsuit. His regular lawyers wanted to settle.

Cohn’s response would become the founding document of Trumpism, decades before the word existed: “Tell them to go to hell and fight the thing in court.”

The $100 Million Countersuit: Retribution as First Instinct

What happened next established the template that Donald Trump has followed for over fifty years.

Cohn didn’t just defend the Trumps against housing discrimination charges. He counterattacked. He filed a $100 million countersuit against the United States government, calling the Justice Department’s complaint “irresponsible and baseless.” He accused federal investigators of employing “Gestapo-like tactics.” He called FBI agents “storm troopers.” He transformed a straightforward civil rights enforcement action into a public spectacle in which the Trumps were cast as the victims.

The countersuit was, by any legal measure, absurd. The judge dismissed it, calling it a waste of “time and paper.” The Justice Department had employees who testified under oath about receiving direct instructions to discriminate. Multiple rental agents described the coding system. Federal testers had documented the discrimination in real time. There was no defense.

But Cohn understood something that conventional lawyers didn’t: the countersuit wasn’t about winning in court. It was about winning in the press. It was about transforming defense into offense. It was about making the process so aggressive, so exhausting, and so personally threatening to the opposition that the next person who considered challenging the Trumps would think twice.

The case dragged on for two years. The Trumps eventually signed a consent decree in 1975 — one of the most comprehensive fair housing agreements of its era. They were required to advertise vacancies in minority newspapers, provide preferential access to civil rights organizations, and demonstrate compliance with the Fair Housing Act.

But there was no admission of guilt.

Cohn’s response was immediate: he declared victory. He told the press Trump had been vindicated. He framed a consent decree — the government forcing the Trumps to stop discriminating — as a triumph. “Roy went on the offensive and said this is a victory; Trump was vindicated,” his cousin recalled. “He knew before anybody else did that the court of public opinion is often more important than a court of law.”

Donald Trump absorbed this lesson at twenty-nine years old, and it has never left him. Not once in the subsequent fifty years has he approached any confrontation differently.

The Three Rules

Cohn’s mentorship of Trump has been distilled by observers into three rules. They are simple, and they explain virtually everything Trump has done since 1973.

Rule One: Attack, attack, attack. Never defend. When challenged, the response is always aggression — disproportionate, immediate, and personal. The $100 million countersuit was the prototype. The government filed a carefully documented civil rights case; Cohn responded with accusations of Nazi tactics. The disproportion was the point. As Village Voice investigative reporter Wayne Barrett observed, “The counterattack has to be always about a hundred times worse than the initial attack on you.”

Rule Two: Admit nothing, deny everything. “You might be guilty; it doesn’t matter.” The factual record is irrelevant. Multiple employees testified they were instructed to discriminate. Trump’s response: “I have never, nor has anyone in our organization ever, to the best of my knowledge, discriminated or shown bias in renting our apartments.” The evidence said otherwise. It didn’t matter.

Rule Three: No matter what happens, claim victory and never admit defeat. The consent decree required the Trumps to fundamentally change their rental practices and submit to ongoing oversight. Cohn declared it a vindication. Trump learned that if you say you won loudly enough and often enough, a significant portion of the audience will believe you — and the rest will eventually stop correcting you.

These three rules — attack, deny, declare victory — are the DNA of Trump’s retribution instinct. They explain the pardon loyalty tests documented in my Clemency Chronicles. They explain the destruction of Liz Cheney. They explain “Marjorie Traitor Greene.” They explain Jeff Hurd losing his endorsement within hours of a policy vote. Every act of retribution Trump has committed in six decades follows the template Roy Cohn established in a Brooklyn courtroom in 1973.

Trump himself acknowledged the debt. “All I can tell you,” he told Vanity Fair, “is he’s been vicious to others in his protection of me.”

Vicious to others in his protection of me. That sentence is the Rosetta Stone of Trumpism.

The Apprenticeship: Litigation as a Weapon (1973–1986)

Donald Trump and his father, Fred, in 1973 at Trump Village, Fred's last project, in Queens. Donald was an apprentice to his father at the time. (Barton Silverman/NYT/Redux)

The housing discrimination case was the beginning, not the end, of Trump’s education under Cohn. For thirteen years — from 1973 until Cohn’s disbarment and death in 1986 — Trump absorbed a comprehensive curriculum in using legal processes as instruments of retribution and control.

Cohn taught Trump how to dominate the media. He introduced Trump to the New York tabloid ecosystem and showed him how planted stories, strategic leaks, and cultivated relationships with editors could shape narratives more effectively than any courtroom verdict. Cohn introduced Trump to Rupert Murdoch — a connection that would pay dividends for decades.

Cohn taught Trump how to use litigation as intimidation. The lesson wasn’t about winning cases — it was about making the fight so expensive, so exhausting, and so personally threatening that opponents would capitulate before trial. Trump internalized this completely. Over the following decades, USA Today documented that Trump and his companies were involved in more than 3,500 lawsuits — an astonishing number even for a large real estate operation. The volume wasn’t incidental; it was strategic. Every lawsuit sent a message: cross Trump and you’ll spend years in court.

Cohn taught Trump how to exploit the gap between legal processes and justice. “I don’t want to know what the law is,” Cohn would say. “I want to know who the judge is.” The law was a tool, not a constraint. The question was never whether something was right — it was whether you could get away with it, and whether the process itself could be weaponized against your enemies.

And Cohn taught Trump the most important lesson of all: loyalty is everything, and betrayal is unforgivable. Cohn demanded absolute loyalty from his clients and associates. He rewarded it lavishly and punished disloyalty with total destruction. Trump watched this for thirteen years and absorbed it completely.

When Attorney General Jeff Sessions recused himself from the Russia investigation in 2017, exposing Trump to legal risk, Trump’s response was revealing. He didn’t ask for a legal strategy. He asked his aides a question that echoed across four decades: “Where’s my Roy Cohn?”

The Contractor Wars: Retribution Against the Powerless

Donald Trump in his Trump Tower office, circa late 1980s. Roy Cohn had been dead for only a few years. The wall behind him tells the story Cohn taught him to build — a shrine of press clippings, magazine covers, and self-congratulation. By the time this photo was taken, Trump had already stiffed hundreds of contractors, threatened dozens of journalists, and weaponized litigation against anyone who challenged him. The mentor was gone. The playbook was permanent. (Getty Images)

While Cohn taught Trump the philosophy of retribution, Atlantic City and Manhattan provided the laboratory.

By the mid-1980s, Trump was building at enormous scale — Trump Tower in Manhattan, the Taj Mahal casino in Atlantic City, hotels, resorts, and golf courses across the country. Each project employed hundreds of contractors, subcontractors, and small businesses. And a pattern emerged that would foreshadow everything that followed.

Trump systematically stiffed the people who built his empire.

Edward Friel Jr. owned a family cabinetry business founded by his father in the 1940s. In the early 1980s, he won a $400,000 contract to build desks, bars, and slot-machine bases at Trump Plaza in Atlantic City. His company completed the work. The general contractor approved it. Friel submitted his final bill: $83,600.

Then he and his father were summoned to a meeting with Donald and Robert Trump. They were told the work was inferior. The final payment would not be made. But — and this detail matters — Donald Trump offered to hire the Friel company for future projects. The offer undercut the claim of inferior work and revealed the true nature of the dispute: this wasn’t about quality. It was about power.

Paul Friel was working for his father’s company when he says a dispute with one of Donald Trump’s companies in Atlantic City caused his company to be blacklisted.

Edward Friel’s son Paul later told reporters that Trump also used his influence to block the company from getting other Atlantic City contracts. After years of struggle, the Edward J. Friel Company filed for bankruptcy.

Forest Jenkins’ company won a $200,000 contract to install toilet partitions at the Taj Mahal in 1988. The work was completed. The payment never came. After years of fighting in bankruptcy court, Jenkins received $70,000 — thirty cents on the dollar. His daughter remembered: “I grit my teeth every time I see him on television blustering about what a wonderful businessman he is. He stepped on a lot of people.”

Nat Hyman, a jewelry store owner who secured a kiosk in Trump Tower, spent fourteen years in litigation with Trump after being strong-armed. “When he’s nice, he’s very, very nice,” Hyman said. “And when he’s nasty, he’s immensely nasty. To him, it’s a sport. To him, it’s fun.”

The numbers are staggering. On the Taj Mahal alone, casino regulators documented that at least 253 subcontractors were not paid in full or on time. USA Today found more than 200 mechanic’s liens filed against Trump or his businesses by contractors and employees since the 1980s. At least 60 lawsuits were filed by contractors claiming they were stiffed. And when small businesses tried to collect what they were owed, Trump gave them an untenable choice: sue — despite the cost of litigation against a billionaire — or accept a fraction of what was promised.

Some of them went bankrupt. Some of them lost family businesses that had operated for decades. Trump’s assessment? “The money I took out of there was incredible.”

The contractor wars established something crucial: retribution wasn’t reserved for powerful enemies. It was deployed against anyone — including the working-class tradesmen who built Trump’s buildings with their hands. The cabinet maker, the plumber, the electrician — if Trump decided not to pay, the process of trying to collect became its own punishment. Litigation was the weapon. Delay was the strategy. And the asymmetry of power — a billionaire against a small business owner — made resistance functionally impossible.

Roy Cohn’s lesson, perfected: the process is the punishment.

The Mob Connection: Retribution’s Other Mentor

There is a dimension to Trump’s retribution education that goes beyond Cohn’s legal philosophy — and it connects directly to the organized crime world that Cohn inhabited.

Cohn’s client list included John Gotti, Tony Salerno, and Carmine Galante — bosses of the Gambino, Genovese, and Bonanno crime families. These were not casual associations. Cohn represented the most powerful figures in American organized crime for decades. And through Cohn, Trump entered a world where loyalty tests, retribution for disloyalty, and the systematic use of fear as a governance mechanism were not metaphors. They were business practices.

Trump’s own construction projects relied on relationships with mob-connected entities. In the 1980s, building anything at scale in New York City required navigating a construction industry in which organized crime controlled concrete suppliers, labor unions, and key contractors. Trump navigated it. Whether this constituted complicity or pragmatism is debatable, but the exposure was undeniable.

What matters for this series is the behavioral template. In my “Enforcers” series, I used the language of organized crime — the don, the lieutenants, the protection racket, omertà — as a satirical lens to describe Trump’s governance. But the satire works because the parallels are structural, not superficial. The loyalty demands, the retribution for betrayal, the use of intermediaries to carry out punishment, the code of silence — these aren’t just metaphors borrowed from mob movies. They’re patterns Trump observed directly, through his lawyer’s clients, during the most formative period of his professional life.

When Michael Cohen later described Trump as “a mobster, plain and simple,” and when James Comey compared Trump’s loyalty demands to “a Cosa Nostra induction ceremony,” they weren’t reaching for colorful language. They were describing behavior they had witnessed firsthand — behavior rooted in the world Cohn introduced to a young developer in the 1970s.

The End of Cohn, the Beginning of Trump

In 1986, a five-judge panel of the Appellate Division of the New York State Supreme Court disbarred Roy Cohn for unethical and unprofessional conduct — including misappropriation of client funds, lying on a bar application, and forcing a pen into a dying man’s hand to falsify a will. Among the character witnesses who showed up to defend him: Donald Trump.

Five weeks after his disbarment, Cohn died of AIDS-related complications. He had spent his final years denying his diagnosis, just as he had spent his career denying inconvenient truths. He was fifty-nine.

Trump’s reaction to Cohn’s decline was itself revealing. According to multiple accounts, Trump began distancing himself from Cohn as his mentor’s power waned — as AIDS ravaged his body, as legal troubles mounted, as the social invitations dried up. When Cohn was no longer useful, Trump’s loyalty evaporated.

Even Cohn noticed. Near the end, he reportedly told friends that Trump was treating him the way he had seen Trump treat others who were no longer useful. The mentor had become a case study in his own student’s curriculum.

But the lessons survived their teacher. By 1986, Donald Trump had absorbed thirteen years of education in a philosophy that can be summarized in a single sentence: The world is divided into people who submit and people who get destroyed.

That philosophy would drive Trump’s behavior for the next four decades — through Atlantic City’s collapse, through the birther campaign, through two presidential campaigns, through two terms in office, and through the systematic destruction of anyone who stood in his way.

What Cohn Built

President Donald Trump during a cabinet meeting in the Cabinet Room of the White House in Washington, D.C. on Jan. 29, 2026.Brendan Smialowski—Getty Images

Roy Cohn died in 1986. But his fingerprints are on everything that followed.

When Trump countersued the DOJ for $100 million in 1973, he was following Cohn’s instructions. When he declared the consent decree a “victory” despite being forced to change his practices, he was following Cohn’s playbook. When he stiffed contractors and dared them to sue, he was applying Cohn’s principle that the process is the punishment.

And in January 2026 — fifty-three years after that first countersuit — Trump filed a $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS and the Treasury Department over the leak of his tax returns during his first term. The leak was committed by a contractor named Charles Littlejohn, who is already serving a five-year prison sentence. The tax returns were ones Trump should have released voluntarily, as every president before him had done. The agencies he is suing are agencies he currently controls. He is, in the most literal sense imaginable, suing himself — and the American taxpayer is expected to write the check. Senator Ron Wyden called it “a shameless, disgusting act of corruption.” Ethics experts warned of “the most collusive lawsuit of all time, because it is ultimately the president suing a defendant whom he says has to do whatever he directs.” Trump’s response, aboard Air Force One: “I’m supposed to work out a settlement with myself.”

From $100 million in 1973 to $10 billion in 2026. The playbook hasn’t changed. The zeroes have.

But Cohn also gave Trump something larger than tactics. He gave him a worldview: the world is hostile, everyone is an adversary or a tool, loyalty is the only currency that matters, and any challenge — no matter how legitimate — must be met with disproportionate aggression.

In my “World According to Trump” series, I documented the binary psychology that drives Trump’s behavior: loyal versus disloyal, winners versus losers, with him or against him. That binary didn’t emerge from nowhere. It was forged in the thirteen years Trump spent at the side of a man who represented mob bosses, destroyed careers for McCarthy, and taught his protégé that the only response to any challenge is to hit back ten times harder.

In the next volume of this series, we’ll trace how Trump applied the Cohn playbook after his mentor’s death — from the destruction of Atlantic City competitors to the birther campaign against Barack Obama to the annihilation of his Republican primary rivals in 2016. The weapons evolved. The instinct never changed.

Roy Cohn taught Donald Trump how to fight. Trump spent the next forty years teaching America what that means.
