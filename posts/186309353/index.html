The Two Courts That Said No

Let’s be very clear about what happened here.

January 18, 2026: Protesters entered Cities Church in St. Paul during a service to demonstrate against ICE operations in Minnesota. The church’s pastor is an ICE official. Protesters chanted “ICE out” and disrupted the service.

Court Rejection #1: The Magistrate Judge

A federal magistrate judge reviewed the evidence.

The judge approved charges against 3 people.

The judge REJECTED charges against 5 people, including Don Lemon, finding the evidence insufficient.

Translation: The judge looked at what DOJ presented and said, “You don’t have probable cause to charge these people.”

This is a critical safeguard in the American justice system. Before you can arrest someone, a neutral judge reviews the evidence to ensure there’s a legitimate basis for charges. It protects against arbitrary arrests.

The judge said no to Lemon’s arrest.

Court Rejection #2: The Federal Appeals Court

DOJ didn’t accept this.

They petitioned a federal appeals court to force the magistrate judge to issue warrants for the remaining 5 people, including Lemon.

Think about that: DOJ went to a higher court and said, “Make the judge arrest these people even though the judge says there’s not enough evidence.”

The appeals court DENIED the petition.

Two courts. Two rejections. Two judicial determinations that there wasn’t sufficient evidence to charge Don Lemon.

The Indictment vs. The Video: How DOJ’s Claims Were Contradicted

February 13, 2026: The Washington Post published a forensic analysis of video footage from the church protest, comparing what actually happened to what DOJ claimed in the indictment.

The results are devastating to DOJ’s case.

The indictment calls Lemon and the other defendants “agitators” who “entered the Church in a coordinated takeover-style attack.” It describes their conduct collectively—treating journalists and protesters as the same.

But the video tells a different story.

Claim 1: Lemon “Oppressed, Threatened, and Intimidated” Parishioners

The indictment alleges:

Lemon “oppressed, threatened, and intimidated” people in the church “by physically occupying most of the main aisle and rows of chairs near the front of the Church, engaging in menacing and threatening behavior.”

What the video shows:

  • Lemon entered the church alone, shortly before 11 a.m.

  • He stood at the back when the protest began

  • He moved forward to where demonstrators gathered, narrating for his audience

  • At no point does video show Lemon threatening church congregants or chanting or yelling

  • Lemon repeatedly identified himself as a journalist

  • Three minutes after protest started, Lemon said: “I’m not part of the group; I’m just here photographing. I’m a journalist”

The video contains no evidence of threatening, menacing, or intimidating behavior by Lemon.

Claim 2: Lemon “Largely Surrounded” Pastor to “Oppress and Intimidate”

The indictment alleges:

Lemon, Fort and Richardson “largely surrounded” a pastor, standing close to him “in an attempt to oppress and intimidate him, and physically obstructed his freedom of movement,” while Lemon “peppered him with questions to promote the operation’s message.”

What the video shows:

  • Lemon interviewed Pastor Jonathan Parnell for less than two minutes

  • Lemon stood to the pastor’s right with microphone

  • Fort approached and stood to pastor’s left, also filming

  • Richardson was nearby

  • Lemon asked the pastor questions about the protest

  • Pastor answered, called it “shameful”

  • When pastor said he needed to “take care of my flock,” Lemon asked another question

  • Pastor walked away freely, ending the interview

  • At no point in video does Lemon appear to obstruct the pastor’s movement

Claim 3: Lemon “Caused” Pastor’s Hand to Graze Him

The indictment alleges:

Lemon “caused the pastor’s right hand to graze Lemon.”

What the video shows:

  • Pastor Jonathan Parnell placed his right hand on Lemon’s side (pastor initiated contact)

  • Lemon responded: “I want to be respectful, but please don’t push me”

  • Pastor lifted his hand

  • The pastor touched Lemon, not the other way around

  • DOJ’s claim is the exact opposite of what video shows

This is a particularly egregious misrepresentation. The video clearly shows the pastor initiating contact, and Lemon asking him not to push. DOJ claims Lemon “caused” this contact. This is provably false.

Claim 4: Lemon “Physically Obstructed” Congregants from Leaving

The indictment alleges:

Lemon “confronted some congregants” and “physically obstructed them as they tried to exit the Church building to challenge them with ‘facts’ about U.S. immigration policy.”

What the video shows:

  • Lemon held the door open for people leaving

  • One parishioner answered brief questions, then said “I don’t want to interview right now”

  • Lemon responded: “Okay, thank you, I appreciate it”

  • Lemon approached another man leaving, identified himself as journalist

  • Man disagreed with protesters’ approach but agreed with right to protest

  • Lemon tried to discuss immigration facts

  • Man said “You’re not a journalist” and walked away

  • Lemon followed briefly, attempting to continue conversation

  • Man walked away. Lemon did not physically prevent him from leaving.

Expert Analysis:

Judy Appelbaum reviewed the footage for the Washington Post. She was counsel to Senator Edward Kennedy and the lead congressional staffer who drafted the Face Act that Lemon is charged under.

Her assessment:

“There is clearly no physical obstruction of that exit. And I don’t see any use of force, nor any threat of force.”

The person who wrote the law says the video doesn’t show a violation of that law.

When Courts Become Irrelevant

Here’s the fundamental question: What happens when the executive branch ignores the judiciary?

The American system has three branches:

  • Legislative (makes laws)

  • Executive (enforces laws)

  • Judicial (interprets laws, checks other branches)

The judiciary’s role in arrests:

Before you can arrest someone, you need probable cause. A neutral magistrate reviews the evidence and determines if there’s enough to justify an arrest.

This is a check on executive power.

It prevents arbitrary arrests. It ensures someone besides the police/prosecutors looks at the evidence first.

In Lemon’s case:

  • Magistrate reviewed evidence: NO probable cause

  • Appeals court reviewed: NO, don’t force the warrant

  • DOJ: We’re arresting him anyway

Once executive branch can arrest people despite judicial determinations, what’s the point of judicial review?

If DOJ can say, “We don’t care what the magistrate decided, we’re arresting anyway,” then magistrates serve no function.

The implications:

  1. Judicial oversight becomes meaningless

    • Why review evidence if DOJ ignores the ruling?

  2. Probable cause becomes optional

    • DOJ decides who to arrest, courts be damned

  3. Executive power becomes unchecked

    • If judicial branch can’t stop arrests, what can it do?

  4. Political arrests become routine

    • When courts can’t stop DOJ, anyone can be arrested

This is how authoritarian systems work.

Courts exist. They issue rulings. The executive ignores them. Eventually, people stop appealing to courts because everyone knows they’re powerless.

We’re watching this happen in real-time with Don Lemon.
