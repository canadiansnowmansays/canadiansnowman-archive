The Insider’s Account: When the Pardon Attorney Speaks Out

January 20, 2026

Illustration by The New York Times; photograph by Andrew Harnik/Getty Images

“No MAGA Left Behind” - The New Standard for Presidential Clemency

Editor's Note: This essay publishes on January 20, 2026 -- exactly one year after President Trump's second inauguration, when he issued 1,500+ pardons on his first day in office without consulting the Office of the Pardon Attorney. Today we hear from the woman who led that office, tried to maintain its integrity, and lost her job for it.

On December 26, 2025, Liz Oyer published an op-ed in The New York Times with a headline that should alarm every American: “I Was the President’s Pardon Attorney. He’s Making a Mockery of the Pardon Process.”

For readers who have followed this series from its beginning--from David Gentile’s 13 days in prison for $1.6 billion fraud through systematic analysis of 6,000+ clemency decisions across four administrations--Oyer’s account provides something extraordinary: insider validation from the person who actually ran the Office of the Pardon Attorney.

Everything this series has documented about the corruption, systematization, and political weaponization of presidential clemency? The person whose job was to maintain the integrity of that system just confirmed it all.

And she revealed something even more damning: it’s worse than we knew.

The Woman Who Held the Line

Liz Oyer served as the Department of Justice Pardon Attorney from 2022 to 2025. She led a team of approximately 40 lawyers and staff members whose sole job was to scrutinize applications for clemency. Her tenure spanned the end of Biden’s administration and the beginning of Trump’s second term.

She witnessed both presidents’ use of the pardon power. She saw Biden’s process firsthand--and his ultimate abandonment of it. She saw Trump’s first actions in office--and her own subsequent dismissal when she became inconvenient to the new regime.

Now she’s speaking out. And her account is devastating.

The Process That Was Destroyed

Oyer describes the traditional pardon review process with precision:

Initial Review:

  • Applications face “exacting” scrutiny by the Office of the Pardon Attorney

  • Staff assess past criminal conduct, evidence of atonement, remorse, rehabilitation steps

  • Detailed guidelines from the Justice Manual are applied

FBI Investigation:

  • Cases that clear initial review trigger full background investigations

  • Comprehensive vetting of applicants and their circumstances

Stakeholder Input:

  • Prosecutors given opportunity to weigh in

  • Judges consulted for their perspective

  • Victims allowed to provide statements

The Numbers: In four years as president, Biden granted more than 4,000 commutations (sentence reductions) but only 80 pardons.

That ratio--50:1 commutations to pardons--reflects the difference between mercy (reducing harsh sentences) and erasure (eliminating consequences entirely). Biden leaned heavily toward mercy, rarely toward erasure.

Until he didn’t.

Inauguration Day: The System Implodes

Oyer learned about Trump’s first clemency decisions the same way most Americans did: by reading the news.

On Inauguration Day 2025, Trump pardoned approximately 1,500 people accused of crimes connected to the January 6 Capitol riot. The Office of the Pardon Attorney--the office Oyer led--was not consulted. At all.

But her staff was still expected to facilitate the releases of all those incarcerated.

Over the next three days, 27 more pardons were granted. Again, without “even a nod to the traditional role of the Justice Department in advising the president on pardons.”

The process wasn’t bypassed occasionally for exceptional circumstances. It was obliterated as standard operating procedure.

March 2025: The Replacement

White House Staff Secretary Will Scharf, Ed Martin and Donald Trump X/EagleEdMartin

After being sidelined for weeks, Oyer was dismissed in March 2025.

Her replacement? Ed Martin--an organizer in the “Stop the Steal” movement.

Martin described his pardon priorities unambiguously: “No MAGA left behind.”

Read that again. The person now running the Office of the Pardon Attorney--the office created to provide nonpartisan, merit-based review of clemency applications--has openly declared that political loyalty to Trump is the qualifying criterion.

Not rehabilitation. Not remorse. Not justice. Not mercy.

MAGA loyalty.

Under Martin’s leadership, Oyer writes, “the traditional application review process has been neutered.”

The New Cases: Validation of the Pattern

Oyer’s op-ed reveals clemency decisions that perfectly validate the patterns documented throughout this series. Each case illustrates a different avenue to Trump’s mercy:

The Pay-to-Play Cases

Michele Fiore - Nevada politician convicted of using money from a police memorial fund for personal expenses, including plastic surgery. Trump supporter. Pardoned.

Scott Jenkins - Former Virginia sheriff who took bribes in exchange for badges. Trump supporter. Pardoned.

Todd and Julie Chrisley - Reality TV personalities imprisoned for tax evasion and defrauding banks. Their daughter campaigned for Trump. Pardoned.

Tim Leiweke - Developer accused of bid rigging. Pardoned after Trump played a round of golf with one of Leiweke’s lawyers.

One round of golf. That’s the review process.

Paul Walczak - Former nursing home executive who pleaded guilty to tax crimes. Pardoned after Walczak’s mother attended a Mar-a-Lago dinner.

His mother went to dinner at Trump’s club. That’s the FBI background investigation.

The “I Don’t Even Know Him” Case

Changpeng Zhao - Billionaire founder of cryptocurrency exchange Binance, convicted of money laundering violations.

Trump’s own words after pardoning him: “I don’t know who he is.”

The President of the United States openly admits he doesn’t know the person whose federal conviction he just erased. He doesn’t know what the person did. He doesn’t know whether the person is remorseful or rehabilitated.

He just... pardoned him anyway.

This case appeared in Part 7 of this series, published before Oyer’s op-ed. Her account confirms every detail--and adds the crucial context that this pardon, like all the others, bypassed the entire review process.

The Human Cost: Recidivism

Oyer reveals data that should terrify every American: Trump’s pardons are already resulting in new crimes.

Among the January 6 defendants pardoned on Trump’s first day in office, several have already been charged with or convicted of new offenses:

  • Sexual exploitation of children

  • Threats against public officials

  • A plot to kill federal employees

And from Trump’s first term--when vetting was “similarly casual”--several clemency recipients have returned to prison:

  • At least two recidivist fraudsters

  • A drug trafficker subsequently accused of violent assaults

This is what happens when the review process is destroyed. When political loyalty replaces merit evaluation. When access to Trump replaces assessment of rehabilitation.

People get hurt.

The 40-lawyer team that Oyer led existed for a reason: to protect the public by ensuring that clemency goes to people who deserve mercy, not to people who can afford Mar-a-Lago dinners or play golf with Trump’s lawyers.

That protection is gone.

The False Equivalence: Biden’s Pardons

Oyer directly addresses Biden’s last-minute pardons--and her assessment is damning:

“Mr. Biden diminished the legitimacy of the pardon power at the end of his presidency when he issued a slew of last-minute, pre-emptive pardons to family members and allies. Those pardons, like Mr. Trump’s, bypassed ordinary channels of review; in those cases, my office was not consulted.”

She identifies two damaging messages Biden sent:

  1. That the pardon power is the president’s to use for personal gain

  2. That the justice system cannot be relied on to adjudicate cases fairly

Her conclusion: “Mr. Biden’s late-term pardons leave an unfortunate legacy.”

But then comes the crucial distinction:

“But no cleareyed American could conclude that they justify the current administration’s abuse of this power. Both quantitatively and qualitatively, what Mr. Trump is doing with pardons is far more damaging to the American ideal of delivering justice evenhandedly.”

This is the person who saw both presidents’ actions firsthand. Who had access to all the internal processes, all the case files, all the deliberations. Who knows exactly what reviews were conducted and what were bypassed.

Her verdict: Quantitatively and qualitatively different.

Biden’s pardons were wrong. Trump’s pardons are “far more damaging.”

The Constitutional Crisis

Oyer quotes the Supreme Court from a century ago, reaffirming the president’s vast pardon discretion:

“Executive clemency exists to afford relief from undue harshness or evident mistake in the operation or enforcement of the criminal law.”

But the Court added a crucial caveat: “Our Constitution confers this discretion on the highest officer in the nation in confidence that he will not abuse it.“

Oyer’s assessment: “Evidently, the Constitution’s framers were lacking in imagination, as we are now well past the point of abuse.”

We are now well past the point of abuse.

The person whose job was to maintain the integrity of the pardon process is telling us that the system has collapsed. That the constitutional assumption--that presidents would use this power responsibly--has been shattered.

The $3.5 Million Pardon

The Timeline:

  • Late 2024: Herrera’s daughter Isabela donates $2.5 million to MAGA Inc. (Trump super PAC)

  • July 2025: Isabela Herrera donates another $1 million to MAGA Inc.

  • January 2026: Trump pardons Herrera, Vázquez, and Mark Rossini (all had pleaded guilty to misdemeanor campaign finance charges)

Total donations before pardon: $3.5 million

This is textbook pay-to-play:

  1. Daughter donates $2.5 million

  2. Trump’s former lawyer negotiates lenient deal

  3. Daughter donates another $1 million

  4. Full pardon erases even the misdemeanor

The White House denies the donations played any role. The timeline speaks for itself.

Supporters claimed she was “targeted by prosecutors because Trump had wiped away her earlier sentence.” So Trump’s first improper clemency justified the second improper clemency when she proved his mercy was misplaced by committing new fraud.
