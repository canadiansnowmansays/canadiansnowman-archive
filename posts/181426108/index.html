The Masculinity Con

Part 1: The Masculinity Smokescreen: How the Tate Brothers Franchised Trump’s Accountability Evasion System

December 12, 2025

Author’s Note: This six-part series builds on groundbreaking investigative reporting by Megan Twohey and Isabella Kwai of The New York Times, published December 10, 2024. Their investigation obtained the sealed 360-page Romanian indictment, interviewed dozens of sources across Romania, the United States and Britain, and documented the diplomatic interventions that led to the Tates’ release from travel restrictions. Their meticulous reporting provided the factual foundation that made this analytical series possible. What follows is my effort to synthesize their findings through the lens of accountability evasion frameworks I’ve developed while documenting Trump’s 57-year pattern of escaping consequences. The Tate case demonstrates how that system has been successfully franchised internationally.

Prostitution, they say, is the world’s oldest profession. Which makes pimping just as old. For millennia, societies have found ways to rationalize, regulate, or romanticize the sale of women’s bodies - from temple priestesses to the Wild West saloon to the modern “sex work” euphemism. We’ve created elaborate linguistic constructs to make exploitation palatable: escort services, adult entertainment, content creation, sugar dating.

The Tate brothers understood something fundamental about this ancient transaction: if you can rebrand it as something else - as entrepreneurship, as liberation, as defending masculinity itself - you can not only avoid accountability, you can become celebrated for it.

But their real innovation wasn’t the exploitation. That’s as old as civilization. Their innovation was recognizing that Donald Trump had perfected a system for evading accountability, and that system could be franchised. Wrap alleged criminality in political victimhood, attach yourself to the right people, deliver a useful voting demographic, and accountability becomes optional.

The result: Trump administration officials intervened with Romanian authorities to secure the Tates’ release from a 360-page criminal indictment involving over 30 alleged victims across multiple countries. The accountability evasion system documented across 57 years of Trump’s life has now been exported internationally, complete with diplomatic intervention.

The Tates are proof of concept: the system works, and it’s for sale.

The Legitimate Movements: What Real Liberation Actually Looked Like

To understand the con, we need to remember what genuine liberation movements actually demanded and how they operated.

Gloria Steinem never exploited men to advance feminism. The suffragettes didn’t build their movement by coercing men into servitude. Betty Friedan didn’t fund women’s liberation by trafficking men. These movements fought from positions of profound structural disadvantage - women lacked the vote, economic power, legal rights, even the right to their own property or children in many cases. Yet they never sought liberation through exploitation of the opposite sex.

What united these movements across their differences and decades was a fundamental premise: liberation isn’t zero-sum. Expanding freedom for one group doesn’t require restricting freedom for another. The suffragettes didn’t need to enslave men to win the vote. Steinem didn’t need to monetize male bodies to fund Ms. Magazine. Even from positions of relative powerlessness, these movements understood that real liberation seeks equality, not domination.

Now consider the Tate model: they claim to defend masculinity - already the structurally advantaged position - while allegedly building a £21 million operation by systematically exploiting women. Their “masculinity movement” requires victims. Their “liberation” requires coercion. Their philosophy explicitly states women “aren’t built to be completely independent creatures” and need male control.

That asymmetry reveals the con. Women’s movements fought FROM disadvantage WITHOUT exploiting the advantaged. The Tates operate FROM advantage BY exploiting the disadvantaged. They’re not defending masculinity from oppression - they’re monetizing a power imbalance that already exists while claiming victimhood. They’ve inverted the language of liberation to justify exploitation.

When someone operating from a position of structural power claims they need to exploit those with less power in order to defend themselves, that’s not a liberation movement. That’s a protection racket with better marketing.

Now consider how this protection racket actually operated.

The Tate Model: Pimping with a Thesaurus

Andrew Tate has never hidden what he did - he just reframed it. In a 2019 interview posted to YouTube, he explained his business model with remarkable candor:

“It’s not just about picking up girls. It’s about converting them into really loving you enough to moving in with you and working for you and giving you all the money.”

Read that again. “Converting them.” “Working for you.” “Giving you all the money.”

This isn’t masculinity under siege. It’s pimping with a thesaurus.

But Tate went further, providing the ideological justification in a 2021 interview:

“It doesn’t matter whether a woman wants to be a lawyer, or a house maker, or webcam girl. Unless she has a man directing her, she’s gonna [expletive] it up. They’re just not built to be completely independent creatures.”

The architecture of the con: First, establish that women need male direction. Second, position yourself as providing that direction. Third, extract all economic value from their labor. Fourth, describe this extraction as helping them.

The Romanian indictment - a 360-page document obtained by The New York Times - alleges the brothers formed an organized criminal group that trafficked women through “Lover Boy” methods: creating the illusion of romantic relationships to coerce women into pornography production. The operation included surveillance systems, movement restrictions, financial control, and violence.

A text message exchange captures the philosophy:

Woman: “do you want me to be your slave or your wife?”

Andrew Tate: “you’ll do whatever I tell you / which is the same thing”

Over eight years, British courts determined, the brothers earned at least £21 million (nearly $28 million) from this operation. They taught others how to replicate it through courses with names like “PHD” - their acronym for “pimping hoes degree.”

The oldest profession, now with online courses and affiliate marketing.

The Trump Formula: Perfected and Exported

But the Tates’ most important insight wasn’t about exploitation - it was about accountability evasion. They recognized that Donald Trump had pioneered something revolutionary: a system for reframing every allegation as persecution, every accuser as a conspirator, every prosecutor as politically motivated.

For 57 years, Trump has evaded accountability through a consistent pattern:

Four bankruptcies? The banks conspired against me.
Multiple sexual assault allegations? Political hit job.
Two impeachments? Witch hunt.
Criminal indictments? Election interference.

The formula is consistent: deny, attack, claim victimhood, rally supporters against the “conspiracy.” What makes it work is wrapping alleged criminality in political grievance. Once you’re not a criminal defendant but a political prisoner, your supporters will defend you reflexively.

The Tates studied this blueprint and applied it internationally. They even made the parallel explicit. In September 2023, Trump shared on Truth Social a video of Tristan defending him. “Thank you!” Trump wrote. When Andrew spoke to reporters after the assassination attempt on Trump, he said: “I’m very close to the Trump family. I look forward to, once I am free, being with Donald Trump in person and reminding him that he’s a bulletproof badass.”

The message was clear: we’re fighting the same enemies, facing the same persecution, both victims of the same system.

When Romanian authorities arrested them in December 2022 and charged them with rape, trafficking, and forming an organized criminal group, the brothers didn’t retreat. They attacked, wrapping themselves in culture war rhetoric that conservative media eagerly amplified.

Tucker Carlson flew to Romania for a 2.5-hour interview that has drawn 112 million views. They discussed their shared beliefs about a “left-wing war on masculinity.” What they didn’t discuss: the substance of the criminal allegations. Carlson misrepresented the charges, falsely claiming Tate wasn’t accused of rape or sex crimes.

Charlie Kirk watched and had an epiphany: “Now I see why he’s popular.” While expressing disgust over the pornography business, Kirk agreed masculinity was under siege. Candace Owens flew to Romania for her own sympathetic interview, telling her audience not to judge Tate for his past comments.

Each validator made it easier for the next. The criminal allegations became evidence of persecution, not wrongdoing.

The Accountability Evasion Franchise

Here’s what makes the Tate case so revealing: Trump officials didn’t just express sympathy - they intervened.

In January 2025, six days before Trump’s inauguration, Andrew Tate sent a text message: “I had word from The Trump admin that theyre on top of things. Ive been told ill be free soon but Trump needs to see me in Miami.”

Six weeks later, the brothers were on a private plane to Florida.

Between those two points lies a story of systematic political pressure. Richard Grenell, Trump’s special presidential envoy, discussed the Tates’ case with Romanian officials twice - first at Mar-a-Lago in December 2024, then at a security conference in Munich in February 2025. Paul Ingrassia, a former Tates’ legal team member, became White House liaison to Homeland Security. Alina Habba told Andrew on a January podcast: “I got your back over here.”

In late February, an order came down from “the highest levels of the Romanian government.” Prosecutors - who had long argued the brothers were public dangers and flight risks - were told to find a compromise. Within days, the travel restrictions were lifted. The prosecutors were outraged.

On February 27, the brothers posted a video titled “The Tate Escape” as their plane took off for Florida.

When asked if the Trump administration aided the release, their lawyer Joseph McBride told The Times: “Do the math. These guys are on the plane.”

This is accountability evasion as foreign policy. It’s the Trump protection system, now available to anyone who positions themselves as a culture war ally and delivers a useful voting demographic. The Tates brought millions of young male followers. Trump world brought diplomatic pressure to free them from criminal charges in a sovereign nation.

The franchise model works like this:

Step 1: Accumulate allegations. Trump: multiple sexual assault allegations, fraud investigations, tax evasion. Tates: allegations across multiple countries, 30+ women, including minors.

Step 2: When charges come, claim political persecution. Trump: “witch hunt,” “weaponized justice.” Tates: “war on masculinity,” “political prosecution.”

Step 3: Wrap yourself in culture war rhetoric. Trump: defending “real America” against “elites.” Tates: defending “masculinity” against “woke culture.”

Step 4: Build political alliances. Trump: MAGA movement, loyal media. Tates: Tucker Carlson, Charlie Kirk, Trump family members including Barron.

Step 5: Deliver value to protectors. Trump: votes, rally attendance, donations. Tates: millions of young male followers, culture war content, a crucial voting demographic.

Step 6: Activate protection when needed. Trump: Political pressure, pardons, delays. Tates: Diplomatic intervention, pressure on foreign government, release despite prosecutorial opposition.

The system works because it creates mutual dependency. The Tates needed protection from criminal charges. Trump world needed access to young male voters. The transaction was explicit, documented in text messages and diplomatic encounters.

What Defending Masculinity Actually Means

There are legitimate conversations to be had about masculinity in modern society. Male suicide rates remain catastrophically high - men die by suicide at nearly four times the rate of women in the United States. Men face stigma around mental health. Economic changes have disrupted traditional male identities without providing clear alternatives. Young men report increasing feelings of purposelessness and isolation.

These are real issues deserving serious attention. Therapists are developing men’s mental health programs. Educators are rethinking how we socialize boys. Community organizers are creating spaces for men to process emotions constructively.

None of these efforts require trafficking women. None involve teaching young men to “convert girls into really loving you enough to moving in with you and working for you and giving you all the money.” None include courses called “pimping hoes degree.”

The tell is always in the economics. If your masculinity movement generates £21 million from exploiting women’s sexuality, it’s not about masculinity. It’s about money. If your solution to men’s problems involves systematic coercion of women, you’re not solving men’s problems - you’re creating women’s problems for profit.

True masculinity movements would focus on building men up, not tearing women down. They would address male mental health, economic opportunity, healthy relationships, and emotional development without requiring the exploitation of anyone.

The Tates’ version requires not just exploitation but organization of that exploitation, monetization of it, and teaching others to replicate it. That’s not a masculinity movement. That’s a criminal enterprise with good branding and political protection.

The Comparison That Reveals the Con

When suffragettes were arrested, it was for picketing, for civil disobedience, for demanding the vote. They weren’t arrested for exploiting men. There were no male victims alleging the suffragettes had trafficked them, coerced them into sex work, or beaten them.

When Gloria Steinem faced criticism, it was for her ideas, her advocacy, her challenge to patriarchal structures. No men came forward saying she’d raped them, strangled them, or taken their money through fraud.

The Tate brothers claim they’re facing the same persecution as any liberation movement. But liberation movements don’t leave trails of victims. They don’t generate 360-page criminal indictments detailing organized exploitation. They don’t flee countries when authorities start investigating. They don’t text about whether women will be slaves or wives and say “it’s the same thing.”

The comparison isn’t just false - it’s the con. It’s how you get from “alleged criminal enterprise” to “political martyr.” It’s how you turn accountability into persecution, victims into conspirators, and evidence into fake news.

This is the Trump formula, perfected and exported. It trivializes actual liberation movements while ennobling alleged criminal enterprise. It suggests that anyone facing accountability is being persecuted, that anyone accused of crimes is a political prisoner, that any pattern of victims is evidence of conspiracy rather than conduct.

When you can turn criminal accountability into political persecution, and when you have political power to enforce that framing internationally, the rule of law becomes optional for the politically connected.

Conclusion: The System Works

The masculinity crisis may be real. Young men may face genuine challenges in modern society. Male suicide rates, economic displacement, social isolation - these deserve serious attention and serious solutions.

The Tate brothers’ solution - coercing women into pornography through romance fraud, systematic surveillance, financial control, and violence - isn’t a solution to men’s problems. It’s a cause of women’s problems. And it generated £21 million in profits, political alliances with Trump world, and ultimately, freedom from Romanian prosecutors through diplomatic intervention.

This isn’t about masculinity under siege. It’s about monetizing exploitation while hiding behind culture war rhetoric. It’s about franchising Trump’s accountability evasion system internationally.

When suffragettes were arrested, they were fighting for rights. When the Tates were arrested, they had allegations from over 30 women across multiple countries. When Steinem faced criticism, it was for challenging power. When the Tates face charges, they activate political connections to pressure foreign governments.

The comparison isn’t just wrong - it’s the smokescreen. And we need to stop pretending we can’t see through it.

Prostitution may be the world’s oldest profession. Pimping may be just as old. But the Tates found something newer: Trump’s accountability evasion system, which turns alleged criminals into culture war martyrs and foreign prosecutors into obstacles that can be overcome through informal diplomacy.

The Tates aren’t an aberration. They’re the franchise model. And the fact that it worked - that Trump officials intervened, that Romanian authorities capitulated, that the brothers flew to Florida six weeks after that text message - proves the system is operational and exportable.

When someone tells you they’re defending masculinity while allegedly trafficking women, believe the actions, not the marketing.

And when they text “I had word from The Trump admin that theyre on top of things” and six weeks later they’re free despite prosecutorial opposition, believe that the accountability evasion system you’ve documented across Trump’s 57-year career has now been successfully franchised internationally.

That’s the real story. The masculinity rhetoric is just the smokescreen.
