The Gas Station and the Destination

Part V: The Question That Won’t Stay Dead

April 22, 2026

April 22, 2026


There is a literary tradition that has been running parallel to this series without acknowledgment, and it is time to name it.

In my Asimov Gap series, I spent considerable time with Isaac Asimov — his Three Laws, his robots, his vision of technology as something that could be systematised, governed, made rational. Asimov believed in order. He believed that if you thought carefully enough about consequence, you could design rules that would hold. His robots were logical. His futures were architecturally sound. His Mars, when he wrote about it at all, was a problem to be solved.

The appropriate companion to Asimov, when the subject turns to Mars, is Ray Bradbury.

Where Asimov systematised, Bradbury mourned. Where Asimov built logical architectures, Bradbury built elegies. And where Asimov’s robots were the product of human ingenuity applied to the future, Bradbury’s Mars was something older — a world that had already been, already dreamed, already grieved its own passing before we ever arrived.

The Martian Chronicles, published in 1950 — twenty-six years before Viking touched down on the Martian surface — imagined a Mars of crystal pillars and wine-coloured seas, of an ancient telepathic people who did not fight the arriving humans with weapons but simply absorbed them, became them, looked back from the canals and saw human faces where Martian faces had been. Bradbury’s Mars was not conquered. It was haunted.

“We Earth Men have a talent,” Bradbury wrote, “for ruining big, beautiful things.”

That line was written about Mars as an act of imagination. It applies, with uncomfortable precision, to what we are now planning to do there.

Bradbury wrote his chronicles from pure intuition — no Viking data, no orbital reconnaissance, no gas chromatographs. He conjured a living Mars from the same ancient impulse that made Percival Lowell see canals at the turn of the century: the deep human suspicion, persistent across cultures and centuries, that the red light in the night sky is not just a rock. That something looks back.

Twenty-six years after The Martian Chronicles, a science writer named David Chandler sat down at The Atlantic and wrote something that Bradbury, reading it, might have recognised as the moment the poetry became data.

The poet got there first. The journalist arrived in 1977 with instruments that rhymed.

What Viking Actually Found

An artist's depiction of Mariner 9's journey from Earth to Mars (Associated Press)

Chandler’s essay began with a careful, almost lawyerly hedge and then detonated an extraordinary claim in the reader’s lap.

The hedge: space scientists, he wrote, were being “extremely cautious” in interpreting the results of the Viking landers’ biology experiments. Scientific reputations were at stake. Nobody wanted to be wrong about something this consequential.

The claim: “Short of seeing something wiggling on the end of a pin, the case for life on Mars is now as complete as the Viking experiments could make it.”

That was Dr. Robert Jastrow, director of NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies, speaking in 1977 about data returned by the Viking landers that had touched down on Mars the previous year.

Nearly fifty years later, the case has not been closed. The data has never been fully explained. The scientists who ran the experiments have aged and died, some of them still arguing the results showed life. And humanity is now building the infrastructure — the rockets, the moon bases, the orbital fuel depots — to send human beings to the planet where those experiments were conducted.

If the moon is the gas station, Mars is the destination. And the destination may not be dead.

What Viking Actually Found

This is worth understanding precisely, because the popular memory of Viking is wrong. The popular memory is that Viking looked for life on Mars and found nothing. That is not what happened.

Viking carried three biology experiments. All three produced positive results.

The labeled-release experiment fed radioactive sugar solution to Martian soil and looked for carbon dioxide — the gas a microorganism would release while digesting the sugar. The result, in the words of the experiment’s designer, Dr. Gilbert Levin, was a response that “if we had seen it on Earth, we would unhesitatingly have described as biological.” Carbon dioxide poured out of the sample, then slowed — consistent, Levin argued, with what you would expect if the microorganisms drowned in the unexpectedly abundant water of the nutrient solution.

The gas exchange experiment fed a rich nutrient broth — nicknamed “chicken soup” by the scientists — to the soil and monitored the atmosphere. It produced immediate, substantial oxygen release followed by a levelling off. Again: consistent with biology, explicable as chemistry, definitively resolved as neither.

The pyrolytic release experiment was the cleanest of the three. It sealed Martian soil with Martian air and simulated Martian sunlight, looking for photosynthesis — the most fundamental biological process, the one that drives almost all life on Earth. It produced a response more than 500 percent above the background level. When the experiment was repeated in the dark, the response was eliminated — exactly what you would expect if the process were light-dependent, as photosynthesis is. When the soil was sterilised by heating, the response was eliminated — exactly what you would expect if the process were biological.

The GCMS — the gas chromatograph mass spectrometer, designed to detect organic molecules in the soil — found none. This is what collapsed the case for life in the public mind. If there are organisms, where are the organic molecules?

Astronomer Carl Sagan stands next to a model of a Viking lander to provide scale

Dr. Carl Sagan offered one answer: the organisms might be eating the organics as fast as they are produced. In a harsh, nutrient-scarce environment, efficient scavengers leave no waste. Others suggested organisms might be concentrated in microenvironments — pockets of viable conditions in an otherwise hostile landscape — with only spores transported elsewhere by wind. The GCMS landed in the wrong field, and found only the occasional scrap blown over.

Dr. Levin spent the rest of his career — he died in 2021 — arguing that the labeled-release experiment had detected life, and that the GCMS result was not the refutation it appeared to be. His argument was never definitively disproved. It was never definitively proved either.

NASA’s official position on Viking, nearly fifty years later, is that the results were “inconclusive.” Not negative. Not positive. Inconclusive.

What Happened in the Fifty Years After

Viking was not the end of the investigation. It was the beginning of a much longer, stranger story.

In 1996, NASA announced that a Martian meteorite — ALH84001, found in Antarctica — contained what appeared to be fossilised microbial structures, carbonate minerals consistent with biological processes, and organic compounds called polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. President Clinton held a press conference. The world briefly lost its mind. Then the scientific community spent the next decade arguing about contamination, alternative chemical explanations, and the limits of what the evidence could actually support. The meteorite question was never resolved either.

Martian meteorite

The Mars Odyssey orbiter, launched in 2001, detected hydrogen — a proxy for water — near the poles. The Phoenix lander, in 2008, confirmed water ice just below the Martian surface. The Curiosity rover, which landed in 2012 and is still operating, detected methane plumes — periodic surges of the gas that on Earth is produced primarily by biological processes, although it can also be produced geologically. Curiosity found the methane was seasonal, rising in Martian summer and falling in winter. The seasonal pattern is consistent with biological activity. It is also consistent with the thermal expansion and contraction of subsurface geological formations.

The Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter observed recurring slope lineae — dark streaks appearing on Martian slopes in warm seasons and fading in cold ones, initially interpreted as briny liquid water flowing on the surface. Later analysis suggested they might be dry granular flows. The debate continues.

In 2018, ground-penetrating radar aboard the Mars Express orbiter detected what appeared to be a subglacial lake — liquid water beneath the southern polar ice cap, kept liquid by the pressure of the ice above and possibly the heat of geothermal activity below. On Earth, such subglacial lakes support entire ecosystems in permanent darkness.

Each discovery is tantalising. Each is disputed. Each adds a data point to a picture that is coherent but not conclusive.

The picture is this: Mars had liquid water on its surface for perhaps a billion years. It had a thicker atmosphere. It had warmth. It had the conditions, for a very long time, under which life as we know it could have originated. Then something happened — the magnetic field weakened, the atmosphere was stripped by the solar wind, the water froze or escaped — and Mars became the cold, thin-aired desert it is today.

Life, if it originated, had two options: extinction, or adaptation. Drive deeper. Go underground. Find the liquid water beneath the ice. Slow down. Survive.

The permanently shadowed craters of Mars’s poles, the subsurface brine lakes, the deep geological formations where geothermal heat might persist — these are not obviously hostile to life. They are obviously hostile to human beings. They are not obviously hostile to microorganisms that have had three billion years to adapt to exactly these conditions.

The Question Musk Doesn’t Ask

Elon Musk wants to go to Mars. He has wanted to go to Mars for most of his adult life. He built SpaceX because he wanted to go to Mars, and concluded that no existing rocket could get him there. Starship is the Mars rocket. The moon is the practice run.

Musk frames Mars almost entirely as a survival question — a backup copy of humanity, insurance against the extinction event that he believes, with geological inevitability, will eventually arrive on Earth. Multi-planetary species or mortal species. Those are the options, in his framing.

What he does not discuss, at least not publicly, is the question that Viking raised and never answered: what if we are not going to a dead planet?

This is not an idle philosophical question. It has immediate, practical, and legally complex implications.

The 1967 Outer Space Treaty requires that space exploration be conducted “so as to avoid their harmful contamination.” NASA has a planetary protection office with strict protocols governing what biological material any Mars mission can carry, precisely because the consequence of contaminating Mars with Earth organisms — and then claiming to find Martian life — would be a scientific catastrophe of the first order.

But those protocols were designed around robotic missions. A crewed Mars mission — one that lands humans on the surface, has them breathe recycled air and produce biological waste and walk around in suits that cannot be perfectly sterilised — is a contamination event of a different order of magnitude entirely. Once humans are on Mars, the planet is irreversibly changed. Any subsequent attempt to determine whether life is genuinely Martian or whether it descended from Earth organisms that arrived with the crew becomes, in the most literal sense, impossible.

If the Viking experiments detected life — if Levin was right, if there are microorganisms in the Martian soil today — then a human landing on Mars is not just an exploration event. It is first contact. And we will have conducted it without knowing it, leaving behind evidence that will confound every future scientist who tries to sort out what was there before we arrived.

The Chandler Principle

David Chandler wrote in 1977 that Viking’s scientists were being cautious because “the discovery of any kind of extraterrestrial life will have profound and far-reaching effects; it is something which no scientist can afford to be wrong about.”

Forty-nine years later, the caution has hardened into something that looks less like scientific rigour and more like institutional paralysis. The Mars question is too big, too consequential, and too career-threatening to be answered definitively by anyone who has to keep their job at a government agency. So it is not answered. It is filed under “inconclusive” and left for the next generation.

Meanwhile, the next generation is planning to land people there.

The moon landings proved something important about the relationship between exploration and knowledge: you learn more from being there than from any amount of orbital observation. The Artemis II crew saw things in four days of lunar observation that fifty years of robotic imaging had not revealed. Human eyes, human perception, human curiosity operating in real time — these are not replaceable by instruments, however sophisticated.

The same will be true of Mars. The question that Viking raised but could not answer will be answerable, eventually, by human beings on the surface with the right instruments and the right mandate. But only if the question is asked honestly before the mission lands. Only if planetary protection protocols for crewed missions are taken seriously. Only if the scientific community is willing to say, publicly and clearly, what the Viking data actually suggested.

Which brings us back to where this essay began: with a cautious hedge and an explosive claim.

“If life does exist on Mars,” Dr. Levin said, “this is what it should be doing.”

He was talking about the labeled-release results. He spent forty-four years saying it. He died saying it.

He may have been wrong. The chemistry of Martian soil is strange and poorly understood, and exotic reactions involving peroxides and superoxides have been proposed as explanations. None of them have been demonstrated to reproduce all of the Viking results in laboratory conditions. None of them have been proved. The chemical explanations remain unproven alternatives to the biological explanation, which also remains unproven.

We are, in other words, still exactly where Viking left us in 1977. Clues up to the eyebrows. No conclusion.

The Gas Station Serves a Purpose

The moon is being built into a gas station for a reason. The water ice in the permanently shadowed craters, the helium-3 in the regolith, the infrastructure being planned by NASA, SpaceX, Blue Origin, China’s CNSA, and an expanding cast of commercial operators — all of it is aimed, ultimately, at making the Mars journey possible. The economics of deep space exploration are brutal without in-situ fuel production. With it, they become merely difficult.

The rockets that will leave the moon for Mars will carry instruments. They will carry human beings. They will carry, inevitably, the biological contamination that makes the planetary protection question acute.

They will also carry, if we are thoughtful, a genuine scientific mandate: to answer the question that Viking raised and no one has been willing to close. Not to look for life as a secondary objective between sample collection and equipment tests, but as the primary mission — the thing we go to Mars to determine before we do anything else that makes the question unanswerable.

Because if the question is answerable — if the permanently shadowed craters of Mars contain something alive, however simple, however ancient, however different from anything we have catalogued — then everything changes.

Not just the science. The ethics. The governance. The entire framework within which we have been planning human expansion beyond Earth.

You cannot build a gas station on a living planet without asking who the planet belongs to.

What Comes Next

The moon is the next essay in this series in practical terms — Chang’e 7 launches in August, the south pole land grab is accelerating, the governance crisis is real and immediate. I will follow those developments as they happen.

But Mars is the shadow that falls across all of it. The gas station exists to serve the destination. And the destination is asking a question that nobody in the room wants to answer.

In 1977, David Chandler ended his Atlantic essay with this:

“The discovery of any kind of life on Mars, however primitive it may prove to be, would profoundly increase the probability of finding other living beings elsewhere in the universe. As long as Earth was the only life-bearing planet that we knew of, the chance existed that life might have been produced by some fluke. But if the process is not unique, the universe may be teeming with all manner of beings at every imaginable stage of development.”

We are, forty-nine years later, still in the business of finding out.

The moon is how we get there.

The question is what we find when we arrive.
