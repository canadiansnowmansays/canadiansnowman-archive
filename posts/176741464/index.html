The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 10 – When Americans Said No Kings

October 18, 2025 and Beyond

October 22, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
October 20, 2025

In 1940, Charlie Chaplin released The Great Dictator, a direct challenge to fascism when most of Hollywood was too afraid to take sides. Chaplin understood something crucial: dictators demand obedience, but democracy requires defiance. His film ended with a simple plea from a barber mistaken for a tyrant: “I’m sorry, but I don’t want to be an emperor. I don’t want to rule or conquer anyone.”

On October 18, 2025—Day 18 of the government shutdown—millions of Americans demonstrated what that defiance looks like in practice.

This is Part 10: Documentation of what happened when the framework from Parts 1-9 met organized action. Not a victory declaration—South Korea’s Candlelight Revolution needed 20 weeks, Poland needed 8 years—but evidence that the international playbook from Part 9 can work in America when citizens commit to sustained action.

Let’s examine what October 18 showed us, what it didn’t, and what comes next.

The Numbers: Larger Than Expected, Smaller Than Needed

The Numbers: Historic Scale, But Is It Sustainable? According to organizers from Indivisible, ACLU, and No Kings Action, approximately 2,700 events occurred nationwide on October 18, with organizers reporting nearly 7 million participants. Independent crowdsourced analysis by data journalist G. Elliott Morris estimated 5.0-6.5 million participants, noting the organizers’ 7 million count “may be a bit optimistic (but is not impossible).” Major concentrations included:

  • New York City: ~200,000 (various estimates)

  • Chicago ~100,000+ (organizer estimates)

  • Los Angeles: ~150,000+

  • Washington D.C.: ~200,000 (organizer estimates)

  • Smaller cities and towns: 500-25,000 each

For context: South Korea’s first Candlelight Revolution protest on October 29, 2016 drew 30,000 people in Seoul. By Week 5, that had grown to 2.3 million. By Week 20, President Park Geun-hye was removed from office.

America’s Week 1 dramatically exceeded South Korea’s—by over 150 times if using the conservative 5 million estimate, or over 230 times if using organizers’ 7 million figure. This likely represents the largest single-day protest in American history, surpassing even the 2017 Women’s March. But as Part 9 documented, one protest—however massive—changes nothing without sustained commitment.

The 3.5% Threshold

Political scientist Erica Chenoweth’s research on 323 nonviolent campaigns from 1900-2006 found that movements reaching 3.5% of the population in active sustained engagement had a 100% success rate. For the United States, that’s approximately 11.5 million people.

October 18’s 5-7 million represents 1.5-2.1% of the U.S. population—significant and approaching the threshold, but not quite there. More importantly, Chenoweth’s rule requires sustained engagement, not a single event. The question isn’t whether 7 million showed up once, but whether they’ll show up twenty times.

The Coalition: Elected Officials Join Citizen Action

One notable aspect of October 18 was the direct participation of elected officials—something that distinguishes this from typical protests. Three governors spoke at major rallies:

Illinois Governor JB Pritzker (Chicago)

Pritzker, whose state has been targeted with $2.1 billion in frozen federal funds (as documented in Part 4), addressed an estimated 100,000 people:

“Donald Trump, stay the hell out of Chicago. We’re not your subjects—we’re citizens, and this city won’t kneel to a wannabe king.”

Pritzker explicitly framed his opposition in constitutional terms—defending federalism and the separation of powers—rather than purely partisan terms. His presence validated the constitutional concerns this series has documented about executive overreach.

Senator Chris Murphy (Washington D.C.)

Murphy, speaking to approximately 200,000 on the National Mall, connected current events to the historical framework from Parts 1-2:

“We are in the middle of an authoritarian takeover. Trump’s not just ignoring courts—he’s building a machine to bury them. Vought’s firings? Miller’s terror lists? This is Mussolini’s blueprint.”

Murphy’s remarks demonstrate that the comparison to historical fascism—which some dismissed as hyperbole when this series began—is now being made by elected U.S. senators in public speeches.

California Governor Gavin Newsom (Sacramento)

Newsom addressed approximately 7,000 in Sacramento while his speech was livestreamed to larger crowds in Los Angeles:

“I urge our nation to use this weekend’s No Kings marches as a declaration of independence. Trump’s not a president—he’s a petty tyrant.”

Newsom has been particularly vocal in calling Stephen Miller a “fascist”—the same assessment this series reached through systematic analysis in Parts 2 and 5-6.

Coalition Breadth

According to NBC exit polling conducted at multiple sites, approximately 20% of attendees identified as political independents or moderate Republicans. This suggests the movement is achieving the cross-ideological coalition that Part 9 identified as essential to successful democratic resistance.

Veterans groups were particularly visible, with Marine veteran Kevin Ryan (featured in Part 6) leading a contingent in Chicago chanting “No troops on our streets.”

Trump’s Response: Characteristic #7 in Action

On October 19, Trump posted an AI-generated video to Truth Social showing himself as “King Trump” in a crown, piloting an F-35 fighter jet that defecates on crowds of protesters. The video, which accumulated over 5 million views within 24 hours, included a voiceover: “Enjoy the shower, losers—real winners clean up!”

This warrants documentation because it exemplifies Characteristic #7 from our fascism framework: Cult of personality and the strongman.

When millions of citizens exercise their constitutional right to protest, a democratic leader might ignore them, disagree with them, or engage substantively with their concerns. Trump instead:

  1. Depicted himself as royalty (the exact imagery protesters opposed)

  2. Portrayed citizens as deserving of literal excrement

  3. Framed defiance of his authority as “losing”

  4. Demonstrated the thin-skinned authoritarian’s inability to tolerate dissent

The response is consistent with the pattern documented throughout this series: Trump views loyalty to him personally as loyalty to the nation, and therefore views opposition to him as illegitimate.

Immediate Political Impact: Measurable but Not Decisive

Post-protest polling from YouGov/Washington Post (October 19-20) showed:

  • Republican responsibility for shutdown: 48% (up from 38% pre-protest)

  • Democratic responsibility: 30% (relatively unchanged)

  • Public approval of protests: 70%

This represents a significant 10-point shift in a 48-hour period. However:

  • 48% still falls short of the 55%+ that typically forces political capitulation

  • The shutdown continues as of October 20 (Day 20)

  • No indication yet that House Speaker Mike Johnson will bring forward any compromise legislation

The protests created political pressure, but not yet enough to break the deadlock. Week 2 will determine whether this pressure intensifies or dissipates.

What Worked and What Didn’t

What Worked:

✓ Scale exceeded all expectations - 7 million claimed by organizers (5-6.5 million by independent analysis)

✓ Largest single-day protest in American history - surpassing even 2017 Women’s March

✓ Maintained peaceful character - Zero major arrests nationwide (per AP reporting)

✓ Generated significant media coverage - Top story on all major networks

✓ Created measurable political shift - 10-point movement in polling

✓ Built cross-ideological coalition - 20% independent/moderate participation

✓ Established infrastructure for Week 2 - Organizers retained contact information for follow-up mobilization

✓ Massive geographic reach - 2,700+ events across all 50 states

What Didn’t Work:

✗ Rural/small-town participation lagged - Events in Montana, Wyoming, and similar states drew only hundreds compared to urban tens of thousands
✗ Didn’t immediately break the shutdown - Political impact insufficient to force resolution
✗ Digital engagement didn’t match physical turnout - Only 1.2 million #NoKingsDay posts despite 3-5 million attendees
✗ Limited participation from key demographics - Working-class turnout in targeted communities remained below organizer goals

The Shutdown Status: Day 20

As of October 20, the government shutdown continues with escalating consequences:

Human Impact:

  • 750,000 federal employees furloughed without pay

  • 700,000 “essential” workers working without paychecks

  • 50,000 workers have received Reduction in Force (RIF) notices, per Vought’s September 24 memo

  • 1.3 million military service members missed their October 15 paycheck

Legal Resistance:

  • Judge April Perry’s restraining order blocking Chicago troop deployment remains in effect

  • Judge Karin Immergut’s order blocking Portland deployment remains in effect

  • Federal courts have extended temporary restraining orders on Vought’s funding freezes

  • GAO has opened its 50th investigation into potential Impoundment Control Act violations

Political Status:

  • House Speaker Johnson has extended recess through October 13, then again through October 20

  • Senate Democrats have held firm through seven failed CR votes

  • No indication of imminent compromise

  • Economic cost: Estimated $15 billion per week in total impact

The institutional firewall from Part 7 is holding, but barely. Courts can only block so much, and Congress remains deadlocked.

Week 2 and Beyond: Updated Action Blueprint

October 18 proved that millions of Americans are willing to show up. The question is whether they’ll show up repeatedly. Based on the international playbook from Part 9, here’s what sustained resistance requires:

Immediate: Week 2 (October 25) - “Hands Off Our Purse”

Focus: Federal buildings nationwide, demanding:

  • State Attorney General lawsuits challenging partisan funding freezes

  • Congressional oversight hearings on Vought’s illegal impoundments

  • Restoration of frozen funds

  • Protection for furloughed workers

Goal: Maintain or exceed Week 1 turnout (3-5 million)

How to participate:

  • Find your local event at NoKingsAction.org

  • If no event exists in your area, organize one (toolkit available through Indivisible)

  • Bring someone new who didn’t attend Week 1

Electoral Armor (Refreshed from Part 8):

Register Voters Relentlessly:

  • Goal: One new voter per week through November 2026

  • Priority targets: Furloughed federal workers (now 800,000 affected), families impacted by frozen infrastructure projects, veterans

  • Tools: Vote.org (5 minutes on phone), TurboVote, League of Women Voters

Join Organizing Groups:

  • Indivisible chapters report 15% membership growth since October 18

  • Swing Left is targeting 50 competitive districts—add “Vought’s partisan freezes” to voter contact scripts

  • Commit to one volunteer shift per week: phone banking, canvassing, voter registration

Support Furloughed Workers:

  • GoFundMe campaigns for impacted workers have raised $2 million

  • Organize local “Furlough Fridays”—community fundraisers for affected families

  • Church/synagogue/mosque pantries can provide direct assistance

Build Local Watchdogs:

  • Document how funding freezes impact your specific community

  • Create #NoKingsArchive tracking projects: Which infrastructure delayed? How many jobs lost? What communities most affected?

  • Share findings with local media and state legislators—this becomes evidence for lawsuits and oversight hearings

Bridge Coalition Divides:

  • October 18’s 20% independent/moderate participation proves this works

  • Reach out to someone you disagree with on policy: “We can debate taxes later—can we agree that presidents shouldn’t weaponize federal funding against political opponents?”

Measuring Success: The Long View

South Korea’s timeline:

  • Week 1: 30,000

  • Week 5: 2.3 million

  • Week 20: President removed

Poland’s timeline:

  • Year 1: Opposition formed coalition

  • Year 8: Democracy restored through elections

Chile’s timeline:

  • Years of organizing

  • Single referendum: 56% voted to end dictatorship

Brazil’s timeline:

  • 4 years of organizing between Bolsonaro’s election and defeat

  • 2022: Voted him out despite fraud claims

The pattern: Sustained commitment over time, not single spectacular events.

October 18 was Week 1. If turnout drops significantly by Week 3, the movement fails regardless of how impressive Week 1 was. If turnout sustains or grows, the machinery documented in Parts 1-4 becomes increasingly vulnerable.

The Uncomfortable Question

This series began by asking: What if we stopped using “fascist” carelessly and actually measured today’s politics against historical reality?

The verdict from Parts 1-4: Trump and MAGA align with 9 out of 10 historical characteristics of fascism.

The question from Part 9: Has organized resistance ever defeated authoritarianism?

The answer: Yes, multiple times, when citizens sustained the effort.

The question now is simpler and harder:

Will Americans show up for Week 2? Week 5? Week 20?

South Korea did. Brazil did. Poland did. Chile did.

October 18 proved Americans can. Whether Americans will is the only question that matters now.
