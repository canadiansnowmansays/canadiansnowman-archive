The Enemies List

A History Part 3

Putin's Russia and the Warning for America

October 12, 2025

In Part 1, we examined Stalin’s Moscow Trials and Hitler’s People’s Court—totalitarian regimes that weaponized justice to eliminate enemies. In Part 2, we saw how McCarthy demonstrated that political persecution can happen in American democracy through congressional power alone. Now we examine modern Russia to understand how these historical patterns persist—and what they mean for America today.

The patterns established by Stalin didn’t end with the Soviet Union. They persist in Putin’s Russia today, adapted for the 21st century but following the same fundamental playbook. And the case of Alexei Navalny provides a chilling contemporary example of how authoritarian regimes use judicial systems wrapped in legal procedures to eliminate political opponents.

Who Was Alexei Navalny?

Alexei Navalny was Russia’s most prominent opposition leader and anti-corruption activist. He founded the Anti-Corruption Foundation, which exposed corruption among Russian officials and oligarchs through detailed investigations posted on YouTube and social media. His videos reached millions of Russians, documenting billion-dollar palaces, offshore accounts, and systematic theft by those in power.

He organized street protests that brought tens of thousands into the streets. He ran for office despite systematic obstruction. He used every tool available—media, courts, elections, public demonstrations—to challenge Putin’s grip on power.

He was exactly the kind of threat that autocrats eliminate—and Putin tried. Multiple times.

The Assassination Attempt

On August 20, 2020, Navalny was poisoned with Novichok—a Soviet-era military-grade nerve agent that only Russian state authorities could access. The poisoning occurred during a domestic flight from Tomsk to Moscow. Navalny became violently ill, lost consciousness, and nearly died.

After an emergency landing in Omsk, Russian doctors initially claimed he was suffering from low blood sugar, alcohol use, fatigue, or poor diet. They refused to allow his evacuation to Germany, insisting there was no poisoning. Only after intense international pressure—and after his wife Yulia made desperate public pleas—was Navalny finally allowed to be airlifted to Germany two days later.

German laboratory technicians at Charité Hospital in Berlin confirmed Navalny had been poisoned with Novichok. Independent labs in Sweden, Finland, and France confirmed the findings. The Organization for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons (OPCW) certified the results. The evidence was incontrovertible.

The Evidence of State Involvement

Novichok nerve agents were created by the Soviet Union’s secret chemical weapons program. Russia is the only known country to have developed, stored, and used these weapons. The same type of nerve agent was used in the 2018 attempted assassination of former Russian spy Sergei Skripal and his daughter in Salisbury, United Kingdom.

An investigation by Bellingcat, The Insider, Der Spiegel, and CNN implicated Russia’s Federal Security Service (FSB) in the poisoning. The investigation identified specific FSB agents who had surveilled Navalny for years, broke into his hotel room in Tomsk, and applied the chemical agent to his belongings.

In a remarkable piece of investigative journalism, reporters called one of the FSB agents involved—Konstantin Kudryavtsev. Thinking he was speaking to a senior Russian security official, Kudryavtsev admitted details of the operation in a recorded phone call. He discussed how the poison was applied to Navalny’s underwear and how the team had been surprised that Navalny survived.

The evidence was overwhelming:

  • International labs certified the poison

  • Multiple investigations identified the perpetrators by name

  • The method—Novichok—pointed directly to Russian state actors

  • An FSB agent confessed on a recorded call

Putin’s response? Complete denial and massive disinformation. Pro-Kremlin media published over 200 false items about the poisoning between August 2020 and January 2021, claiming everything from “Navalny poisoned himself for publicity” to “the West fabricated the evidence” to “German doctors are lying.”

The obvious truth was simply denied—and domestically, for those who relied on Russian state media, that denial was sufficient.

The Audacity of Returning

After recovering in Germany, Navalny did something remarkable: he decided to return to Russia.

Everyone—his family, his supporters, international observers—knew what would happen. The Russian state had just tried to murder him with a military nerve agent. Returning meant arrest, imprisonment, or worse.

Navalny knew this too. In his final sequence filmed for the documentary “Navalny,” he recorded a message to be released if he was killed:

On January 17, 2021, Navalny’s plane touched down at Moscow’s Sheremetyevo Airport. He was arrested by Russian federal police before he could even leave the terminal. His lawyer and supporters were pushed aside. He was taken into custody immediately.

The Pretext: A Masterpiece of Cynicism

The charge that justified Navalny’s arrest? Violating the terms of his parole—while he was in Germany recovering from being poisoned by the Russian state.

Navalny had a suspended sentence from a 2014 case—the “Yves Rocher case”—involving allegations of fraud related to a cosmetics company. The European Court of Human Rights had already examined this case and ruled that it was arbitrary and politically motivated, finding violations of Navalny’s rights to a fair trial.

But Russian authorities claimed that by leaving Russia for medical treatment in Germany, Navalny had violated his parole conditions by not regularly checking in with authorities.

Think about the logic for a moment:

  1. The Russian state poisons Navalny with a military-grade nerve agent

  2. He is evacuated to Germany to save his life

  3. While recovering from attempted murder by the state, the state accuses him of violating parole by not checking in with authorities

  4. The violation: seeking medical treatment after the state tried to kill him

The cynicism was breathtaking. But it provided the legal pretext needed to imprison Putin’s most prominent critic.

The Show Trials

On February 2, 2021, a Moscow court sentenced Navalny to two and a half years in prison for the parole violation. The trial was a formality—the verdict was predetermined. The European Court of Human Rights immediately called for his release, saying the ruling was made “in regard to the nature and extent of risk to the applicant’s life.”

Russia ignored the ruling. In December 2020, Russia had passed laws giving its constitution precedence over international court rulings. The ECHR’s order meant nothing.

But Putin wasn’t done. As long as Navalny lived and could speak from prison—making statements through his lawyers, posting on social media through his team, denouncing Russian corruption—he remained a threat. The regime needed him silenced permanently.

The Escalating Sentences

March 2022: Navalny was sentenced to an additional nine years in a maximum-security prison on charges of fraud and contempt of court. Amnesty International called it a “sham trial.” The charges claimed he had stolen $4.7 million in donations to his political organizations—funds that had been used for their stated purpose of fighting corruption.

August 2023: Another 19 years were added on “extremism” charges. The court found him guilty of creating and leading an extremist organization. His total sentence now exceeded 30 years. He would be 77 years old before being eligible for release.

Navalny was officially designated a “terrorist” and “extremist” by Russian authorities. His Anti-Corruption Foundation and all associated organizations were liquidated and banned. His supporters faced prosecution simply for association with him. Even sharing his social media posts could result in criminal charges.

The Prison Conditions: Designed to Break

Navalny was held in conditions designed to destroy him physically and psychologically:

  • Constant transfers between facilities, making it difficult for lawyers and family to maintain contact

  • Extended solitary confinement, sometimes for months at a time

  • Denial of adequate medical care despite ongoing health issues from the poisoning

  • Sleep deprivation through constant surveillance and middle-of-the-night “checks”

  • Forced stress positions and physically demanding labor despite health conditions

  • Punishment cells for minor infractions, where conditions were deliberately harsh

The European Court of Human Rights and UN human rights experts repeatedly called for Navalny’s release and improved prison conditions. Russia ignored every request, every ruling, every appeal.

In December 2023, Navalny disappeared from prison. For nearly three weeks, his lawyers and family had no idea where he was. Communication was cut off completely. When he finally re-emerged, he had been transferred to IK-3—an Arctic Circle penal colony in the Yamalo-Nenets Autonomous Okrug, one of Russia’s harshest prisons.

The facility is known as “Polar Wolf.” Winter temperatures regularly drop below -40°F. It’s as remote as a prison can be—hours from any major city, deliberately isolated to make visits nearly impossible and to ensure that any health crisis would be fatal before medical help could arrive.

The Death

On February 16, 2024, Russian prison authorities announced that Alexei Navalny had died at age 47.

The official story: Navalny had taken a walk in the exercise yard, returned to his cell, and “felt unwell.” He lost consciousness and could not be revived. Just like that, Putin’s most prominent critic was gone.

His mother Lyudmila traveled to the Arctic colony, where she was told her son died from “sudden death syndrome”—an umbrella term for various cardiac conditions. His lawyer was told the cause of death was “still unclear” and that more tests were needed.

No credible autopsy was allowed. International investigators were denied access. Navalny’s personal physician, who had treated him after the Novichok poisoning, noted that Navalny had no underlying conditions that would put him at risk of sudden cardiac death.

UN human rights experts issued a statement: “If someone dies in the custody of the State, the presumption is that the State is responsible—a responsibility that can only be rebutted through an impartial, thorough and transparent investigation carried out by an independent body.”

No such investigation occurred. Within hours of announcing his death, Russian authorities had already embalmed Navalny’s body—destroying any evidence of what had actually killed him.

The timing was notable. Navalny died just weeks before a Russian presidential “election” that would extend Putin’s rule. His death removed the most visible symbol of opposition at a moment when Putin wanted to project total control.

The Pattern: From Stalin to Putin

The Navalny case demonstrates how modern authoritarian regimes adapt Stalin’s methods for the 21st century:

Elimination attempts using state resources: Stalin used firing squads and gulags. Putin used Novichok—a nerve agent that could only have come from state laboratories—and then Arctic imprisonment with denial of medical care.

Fabricated legal charges: Stalin charged old Bolsheviks with impossible conspiracies dating back decades. Putin charged Navalny with parole violations for seeking medical treatment after being poisoned by the state.

Show trials with predetermined outcomes: Stalin’s Moscow Trials featured forced confessions extracted through torture. Navalny’s trials featured absurd charges, denial of adequate defense, and guilty verdicts decided before proceedings began.

Escalating sentences: When initial sentences didn’t silence the target, both regimes simply added more years. Stalin executed his victims quickly. Putin kept adding prison time—2.5 years, then 9 more, then 19 more—until Navalny died in custody under suspicious circumstances.

International condemnation dismissed: Stalin ignored Western criticism during the Great Purge. Putin ignored the European Court of Human Rights, UN human rights experts, Western governments, and international sanctions. External pressure without consequences is just noise.

The facade of legality: Both regimes maintained the appearance of judicial process—courts, trials, prosecutors, verdicts, appeals, parole boards. The forms were followed even as the substance was completely corrupted. This facade served to legitimize persecution domestically and provide deniability internationally.

The Six Characteristics: A Pattern Across Time

Despite occurring in different countries, different political systems, and different eras, Stalin’s Moscow Trials, Hitler’s People’s Court, McCarthy’s congressional hearings, and Putin’s persecution of Navalny share fundamental characteristics:

1. Political objectives override legal standards. The purpose isn’t to determine guilt or innocence. It’s to eliminate enemies, intimidate opposition, and demonstrate power. Legal procedures become theater masking predetermined outcomes.

2. Removal of institutional barriers. Independent officials who might object are removed and replaced with loyalists. Stalin replaced prosecutors when needed. Hitler created new courts. McCarthy removed resistant officials. Putin controls all Russian institutions.

3. Process as punishment. Even without final conviction, the process inflicts damage. The arrest, public humiliation, years in harsh conditions, ruined reputation, enormous costs—these serve the regime’s purposes regardless of the ultimate verdict.

4. Chilling effect on opposition. The public nature of political prosecutions creates fear far beyond the individual case. Anyone who might consider opposing the regime sees what happens to those who do and adjusts their behavior accordingly.

5. Gradual normalization. What seems shocking at first becomes routine. Stalin’s first show trial generated controversy; his last was barely noticed. Hitler’s People’s Court started cautiously; within years it was executing thousands. McCarthy’s accusations seemed outrageous initially; by 1952 they were normal. Putin’s actions have become progressively more brazen as each previous outrage was absorbed.

6. Facade of legality. All these systems maintained the appearance of legal process. Courts, judges, prosecutors, trials, verdicts, appeals. Documents filed. Procedures followed (however corrupted). This facade provides domestic legitimacy, international cover, psychological justification for participants, and historical deniability.

Why This Matters for America Now

Some will object that comparing anything in modern America to Stalin’s Russia, Hitler’s Germany, McCarthy’s witch hunts, or Putin’s Russia is hyperbolic or inflammatory.

I understand the objection. And it’s true—we haven’t reached those extremes yet.

But historians of authoritarianism emphasize that we must recognize warning signs early, not wait until we’ve reached the worst-case scenario. As scholars note: “Democracy does not collapse overnight. It erodes gradually—and then suddenly—with the normalization of undemocratic practices that are cloaked in legality yet corrosive to constitutional governance.”

The Point Isn’t Equivalence—It’s Pattern Recognition

I’m not saying Trump is Stalin or Putin. The United States in 2025 is not the Soviet Union in 1937 or Putin’s Russia today.

What I am saying is that weaponizing justice systems against political enemies follows a recognizable pattern—and that pattern is appearing in America right now.

The point of studying history isn’t to wait until we’ve reached the absolute worst case before we’re allowed to notice similarities. The point is to recognize warning signs while there’s still time to change course.

Modern America has stronger democratic institutions than any of these historical examples:

  • A free press (for now)

  • Multiple centers of power (federal, state, local)

  • Strong civil society organizations

  • Economic resources for opposition

  • Constitutional protections

  • International scrutiny

These are real advantages. They make authoritarian consolidation harder.

But “harder” doesn’t mean “impossible.” And the pattern we’re seeing—presidential pressure on prosecutors, removal of resistant officials, installation of loyalists, rushed prosecutions over professional objections—follows the authoritarian playbook with disturbing precision.

What History Teaches

Historical precedent reveals several crucial truths:

It happens gradually. No autocrat announces their full intentions at the start. They test boundaries. They normalize the abnormal. They move incrementally. Each step builds on the last. Each outrage that goes unchallenged becomes the new baseline.

Institutions don’t save themselves. Courts, prosecutors, and officials who might resist need to actually resist. Historical precedent shows they usually don’t—or can’t effectively. The judges who might have stopped Stalin were replaced or intimidated. German judges largely complied with Nazi demands. American institutions mostly acquiesced to McCarthy until he attacked the Army. Russian institutions offer zero resistance to Putin.

Opposition must organize early. By the time the system is fully captured, it’s too late. Resistance must happen while institutions still have some independence. Navalny understood this—that’s why he returned to Russia despite knowing he’d be arrested.

The process is the punishment. Even if prosecutions ultimately fail, the chilling effect remains. Even if institutions eventually push back, the precedent has been set. The years Navalny spent in brutal conditions broke him physically even before his death. McCarthy’s blacklists destroyed careers even when convictions failed. The fear remains even when the prosecution ends.

Normalization is the goal. Each new outrage that goes unchallenged becomes the new baseline. What’s shocking today becomes routine tomorrow. Stalin’s first show trial generated internal criticism; his last was barely noticed. Hitler’s first People’s Court verdicts drew objections; by 1943 it was a killing machine. McCarthy’s first accusations seemed outrageous; by 1952 they were normal political discourse.

The Question Before Us

The question isn’t whether we’ve reached the level of Stalinist Russia, Nazi Germany, McCarthy’s America, or Putin’s Russia. We haven’t.

The question is whether we’re on a path toward weaponizing the justice system against political enemies—and whether American institutions designed to prevent this abuse can hold against presidential pressure.

History suggests that institutions alone don’t protect democracy. They require people willing to say “no” despite consequences. They require officials who will resign rather than comply with corrupt orders. They require prosecutors who will refuse politically motivated charges even if it costs them their careers. They require judges who will stand firm against executive pressure.

Do we have enough such people in positions of power today? History suggests we’re about to find out.
