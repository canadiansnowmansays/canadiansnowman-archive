Historical Roots of Division (1790s-1960s)

America's Eternal Struggle Between Unity and Fracture

October 17, 2025

As I watch American friends and family gear up for tomorrow’s “No Kings” rallies—echoing Jefferson’s revolutionary fire from my shared post this morning—I’m reminded that this fervor isn’t new. It’s baked into America’s DNA from the start.

Where Canada evolved gradually from British colonial rule, America was born in revolution—and that revolutionary spirit, for better or worse, never quite left. Our 1867 Confederation was no picnic either (Nova Scotia nearly bailed), but it set a tone of negotiated truce over all-out war.

The Original Fault Line (1790s)

From its very inception, the United States contained the seeds of its current polarization. The Federalist versus Anti-Federalist debate wasn’t merely about governmental structure—it was a fundamental disagreement about what America should be. Alexander Hamilton envisioned a strong central government, urban commerce, and elite leadership. Thomas Jefferson championed rural virtue, state sovereignty, and populist democracy.

Sound familiar? Today’s urban-rural divide, debates over federal power, and populist movements echo these 230-year-old arguments. Both our countries have wrestled with central versus local power—Canada’s Quebec tensions come to mind—but America’s founding set a particularly contentious tone.

The Ultimate Division (1850s-1865)

The Civil War remains America’s deepest trauma, one without a direct Canadian parallel, though our own divides (like the conscription crises) give a faint echo. Over 600,000 Americans killed each other over slavery, states’ rights, and competing visions of American identity. The war’s end didn’t resolve these tensions—it drove them underground.

Reconstruction’s failure created a century of Jim Crow laws, establishing a pattern: American political conflicts often involve fundamental questions of who belongs, who has rights, and whose vision of America is legitimate. The defeated South created a “Lost Cause” mythology that persists today, visible in Confederate flag controversies and debates over “heritage.”

Industrial Age Class Warfare (1870s-1930s)

As America industrialized, new divisions emerged. The Gilded Age saw extreme inequality, violent labor disputes, and the rise of both socialist movements and robber baron capitalism. Events like the Haymarket Affair, Pullman Strike, and Battle of Blair Mountain involved actual warfare between workers and capital.

Canada experienced labor strife too—think the Winnipeg General Strike—but America’s was notably more violent and ideologically charged. Both nations’ unions battled for wages and visions of society, highlighting how economic pain can fracture any democracy.

The Deceptive Consensus (1940s-1960s)

World War II and the early Cold War created an unusual period of American unity. Facing existential external threats, Americans temporarily suppressed internal divisions. The booming postwar economy allowed broad prosperity that masked underlying tensions.

But this “consensus” was always partial—it excluded Black Americans, women, and others from full participation. The Civil Rights movement would shatter this false unity, forcing America to confront its founding contradictions.

The Civil Rights Realignment (1954-1968)

The Civil Rights era fundamentally scrambled American politics. The Democratic Party’s support for civil rights legislation caused a massive regional realignment, with the “Solid South” switching from Democrat to Republican over subsequent decades. This wasn’t just a policy shift—it was about identity, history, and belonging.

That 1967 tour memory of mine, billeted in Greenwood, Mississippi—home to Bobbie Gentry as “Ode to Billie Joe” climbed the charts—captures the era’s tightrope. Our chaperones warned us Canadian teens not to broach race, yet there I was, hearing a white upper-class mom call her Black housekeeper “part of the family.” It was a polite fiction masking deep inequality, but it let the conversation happen without explosion.

Unlike Canada’s quieter evolution on minority rights, America’s progress came through dramatic confrontation: federal troops in Little Rock, burning buses in Alabama, the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. The period established the template for modern culture wars, where social change provokes fierce backlash.

Five Enduring Fault Lines

Looking at this history from north of the border, several patterns emerge that help explain today’s divisions:

  1. Revolutionary DNA: America’s default to absolutist positions mirrors our own passionate referendums, but both could learn from more cooling-off periods.

  2. Unresolved Traumas: From the Civil War to civil rights, conflicts simmer and resurface—much like our Indigenous reconciliation debates.

  3. Politics as Warfare: Treating opposition as enemies feels familiar in heated election seasons on both sides of the border.

  4. Identity Politics: Long before the modern term, American politics revolved around questions of identity and belonging, echoing our multicultural growing pains.

  5. Weak Institutional Buffers: America’s system, like ours, offers imperfect checks—Westminster parliaments have their own filibuster-like stalls—but both could use more grease for the gears.

As Americans prepare for tomorrow’s rallies invoking founders’ language about tyranny, they’re participating in a very American tradition—viewing political opponents not just as wrong, but as threats to the republic itself.

Thoughts for my Next Essay

But if these historical divisions set the stage, the 1970s began a new act entirely. Americans didn’t just disagree anymore—they started physically sorting themselves into separate communities, separate media bubbles, separate realities. The polite disagreements of the past were about to become impossible when neighbors no longer shared the same facts, let alone the same values.
