Trump's Week of Weakness

How Epstein Capitulation Led to Ukraine Betrayal

Trump’s Foreign Policy: An Analysis of America’s Autocrat Affinity Disorder

November 21, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
November 21, 2025

The Pattern Revealed: Petulance Masquerading as Policy

Donald, just because you capitulated this week over the Epstein files, why do you feel the necessity to throw Ukraine under the bus? Just because you’re in a bad mood? Sure seems that way when you also threaten lawmakers to death with sedition charges.

The week of November 17-20, 2025 revealed something more disturbing than strategic accommodation of authoritarian aggression. It exposed how Donald Trump’s foreign policy operates not through coherent doctrine but through wounded ego and autocrat affinity. When forced to back down domestically, Trump lashes out at democratic allies and retreats to the comfort of his strongman friends.

The timing is not coincidental. It’s revelatory.

Monday-Wednesday: The Epstein Humiliation

On Wednesday, November 20, Trump signed a measure compelling release of the Epstein files after losing a “monthslong, tooth-and-nail fight to prevent their disclosure,” representing a “stunning reversal” aimed at “quelling a brewing GOP revolt” CNN.

This wasn’t just another policy setback. CNN’s analysis described it as puncturing “Trump’s aura of invincibility within the MAGA movement in a way few, if any, things have before,” noting that “the man who has for a decade dominated his base and told it what to care about has backed down when that base decided its priorities didn’t align with his own” CNN.

Trump “threw his support behind a congressional effort to release all the files tied to convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein after it became clear that he could not halt its momentum and that he risked suffering an embarrassing blow on the House floor” CNN. Just 48 hours before reversing course, he had attacked Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene “in bitterly personal terms,” withdrawing his endorsement and labeling her a “traitor.”

The capitulation was complete. The humiliation was public. And Trump’s response pattern was predictable: when wounded domestically, accommodate authoritarians abroad while threatening democrats at home.

Thursday Morning: The Death Threats

Less than 24 hours after signing the Epstein files bill, Trump exploded.

On Thursday morning, Trump accused several Democratic lawmakers of “seditious behavior,” calling for them to “be arrested and put on trial” for behavior that could be “punishable by death” NBC News. His targets? Six Democratic lawmakers—all military veterans and former intelligence officials—who had posted a video reminding service members of their constitutional duty to refuse illegal orders.

Trump wrote “SEDITIOUS BEHAVIOR, punishable by DEATH!” and reposted multiple messages including one stating “HANG THEM GEORGE WASHINGTON WOULD !!” Al Jazeera

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer responded: “The president of the United States is calling for the execution of elected officials. This is an outright threat and it’s deadly serious. He is lighting a match in a country soaked with political gasoline” CBS News.

The lawmakers’ offense? Stating the law: “You can refuse illegal orders. You must refuse illegal orders. No one has to carry out orders that violate the law or our Constitution” NPR.

This is the behavior pattern of a wounded autocrat, not a democratic leader. Faced with domestic defeat, Trump threatens democratic opposition with execution while simultaneously accommodating actual authoritarians who oppress their own people.

The Autocrat Affinity: A Week of Strongman Celebrations

While threatening American lawmakers with death, Trump was busy feting actual authoritarians.

Viktor Orban: November 7

Two weeks earlier, Trump welcomed Hungary’s autocratic leader Viktor Orban to the White House, “praising him repeatedly as a ‘great leader’” and calling it “an honor to have a friend of mine here” ABC News.

The substance mattered more than the ceremony. Orban received “an exemption from US sanctions on Russia after meeting with Trump,” with Hungary’s Foreign Minister declaring the exemption “guarantees Hungary’s energy security” Al Jazeera.

During their meeting, Trump asked Orban, “So you would say that Ukraine cannot win that war?” Orban responded affirmatively, perfectly setting up Trump’s subsequent betrayal of Kyiv ABC News.

Mohammed bin Salman: November 18

On Tuesday, November 18, Trump welcomed Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman with “a lavish arrival ceremony, complete with red carpet with flags and a horse-mounted U.S. military honor guard” for his first visit to the U.S. since ordering the murder and dismemberment of Washington Post columnist Jamal Khashoggi The Washington PostABC News.

When asked about Khashoggi’s murder, Trump defended MBS: “Things happen, but he knew nothing about it, and we can leave it at that. You don’t have to embarrass our guest by asking a question like that” ABC News.

Beyond the Oval Office theatrics, “the substance of the administration’s announcements tells the real story of bin Salman’s remarkable rehabilitation in Washington,” including AI cooperation, advanced chips, critical minerals agreements, nuclear energy expansion, and F-35 fighter jet sales CNN.

The day ended with “a black-tie dinner at the White House” where “several tech CEOs, stars, and business and administration leaders attended the candlelit gathering in the East Room, including Elon Musk, Tim Cook and Cristiano Ronaldo” The Washington Post.

A man who ordered the murder and dismemberment of a U.S. resident journalist gets champagne and Cristiano Ronaldo. American lawmakers who cite the Constitution get death threats.

The Ukraine Betrayal: Putin’s Plan, Trump’s Signature

Into this week of autocrat accommodation dropped the revelation that shocked even seasoned observers.

President Trump’s 28-point plan for peace in Ukraine “would force Kyiv to give up additional territory in the east, cap the size of its military, and agree it will never join NATO,” with the plan including “de facto” U.S. recognition of Crimea and other Ukrainian territory as Russian Axios.

The plan’s origins expose everything wrong with Trump’s foreign policy:

Trump’s envoy Steve Witkoff “discussed it extensively with Russian envoy Kirill Dmitriev,” with the two spending “three days huddled” together in Miami from October 24-26. Dmitriev “expressed optimism about the deal’s chances of success because, unlike past efforts, ‘we feel the Russian position is really being heard’” Axios.

Let that sink in: Russia feels its position is “really being heard” in an American peace plan for ending Russian aggression.

European partners and Ukraine were “largely excluded from the process” of drafting the plan, though “President Volodymyr Zelensky has received the draft” The Kyiv Independent.

The Terms: Putin’s Wish List

The 28-point plan reads like a Russian maximalist fantasy:

Ukraine would be forced to withdraw from territories it currently controls in Donetsk, creating “a neutral demilitarized buffer zone, internationally recognized as territory belonging to the Russian Federation.” Crimea, Luhansk and Donetsk would be “recognized as de facto Russian, including by the United States.” Ukraine’s military would be capped, it would renounce NATO membership, and all parties would receive “full amnesty for their actions during the war” Axios.

A senior Ukrainian official told AFP the plan “calls for Ukraine to recognize Russia’s control over annexed Crimea and other occupied regions, reduce its armed forces to 400,000 personnel and relinquish all long-range weapons” The Moscow Times.

European Rejection: “No Plan At All”

The response from America’s actual allies was swift and damning.

European officials rejected the proposal as “Putin’s wish list,” with one European official saying the American plan is “a blow to the upset Europeans” who “recently thought that Trump had finally realized that Putin could not be trusted” Pravda USA.

European Commission foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas stated bluntly: “For any peace plan to succeed, it must be supported by Ukraine and Europe.”

Oleksandr Merezhko, head of the Ukrainian parliament’s foreign affairs committee, called the idea “completely detached from reality” and “absolutely senseless,” suggesting it looks “less like a serious proposal and more like ‘a provocation from Putin’s side’” The Kyiv Independent.

Keir Giles, a Russian military expert at London think tank Chatham House, described “the way news of the plan had emerged as a ‘Russian information operation rather than the basis of reality, which Western media has willingly bought into yet again’” Al Jazeera.

September’s “Paper Tiger” to November’s Capitulation: The TACO Returns

My September 23 analysis documented Trump’s extraordinary reversal from accommodation to confrontation. After the Alaska summit debacle, Putin’s continued aggression had finally exceeded Trump’s accommodation threshold. Trump declared Russia a “paper tiger” and said Ukraine could “win back all territory” with NATO support—the opposite of every previous pattern.

I concluded that analysis by noting the critical question: “Whether it survives Putin’s next test of American resolve—likely within the next two weeks, following Trump’s established timeline patterns—will determine whether this represents strategic awakening or the most predictable TACO episode yet.”

We have our answer. It took eight weeks, not two, but the pattern held: Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate.

The “paper tiger” rhetoric? Theater. The support for Ukrainian territorial integrity? Abandoned. The alliance pressure that briefly constrained Trump? Ignored when drafting peace terms with Putin’s envoy in Miami.

And the timing—coming immediately after Trump’s Epstein humiliation—reveals the psychological mechanism: when forced to back down on domestic issues, Trump seeks to reassert dominance by accommodating foreign authoritarians while abandoning democratic allies CNN.

The Nobel Prize Delusion: Policy as Performance Art

Running through all of this is Trump’s transparent pursuit of a Nobel Peace Prize—a quest that has become “something of a joke in foreign capitals,” according to diplomatic sources.

House Republicans nominated Trump for the Nobel Peace Prize in August, “hailing Trump as a peacemaker on several fronts, the most recent being his summit with Russian President Vladimir Putin and subsequent meeting with European leaders” Fox News.

White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt stated in August: “President Trump has brokered on average about one peace deal or ceasefire per month during his six months in office. It’s well past time that President Trump was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize” ABC News.

But as MSNBC analysis noted: “Trump is lobbying for a Nobel Peace Prize even while rolling out the red carpet for Putin and backing genocide in Gaza. It’s surreal” MSNBC.

The Ukraine peace plan fits this pattern perfectly: sacrifice Ukrainian sovereignty and European security for a “peace” deal that Trump can tout while campaigning for recognition, regardless of whether it serves American interests or democratic values.

The Defining Pattern: Autocrat Affinity Disorder

What we’re witnessing isn’t strategic realism or transactional diplomacy. It’s a consistent pattern of autocrat affinity combined with democratic ally abandonment, all filtered through Trump’s personal psychological needs.

The Evidence:

  • Putin’s envoy drafts the Ukraine peace plan and declares Russian positions are “really being heard”

  • Orban gets sanctions exemptions, praise as a “great leader,” and Trump asking him to confirm Ukraine cannot win

  • MBS receives red carpet ceremony, F-35s, advanced chips, and defense of his ordering Khashoggi’s murder

  • Democratic lawmakers who cite the Constitution get threatened with execution

  • Ukraine gets forced to cede territory, abandon NATO ambitions, and accept permanent insecurity

  • European allies are excluded from peace plan negotiations and dismissed when they object

This isn’t “America First.” It’s “Autocrats First, Democrats Last.”

The Credibility Crisis: Why Putin Won’t Believe This Either

The fundamental problem remains: Putin has heard this movie before.

Throughout 2025, Trump issued a relentless series of ultimatums that Putin systematically ignored:

  • May 8: Threatened sanctions if Russia didn’t agree to 30-day ceasefire. Russia rejected it.

  • May 28: Said he would know “within two weeks” if Putin was serious. Putin skipped Istanbul talks.

  • July: Set deadlines for “immediate progress.” Putin got Alaska summit instead.

  • August 15: Sanctions deadline became summit hospitality without Russian policy changes.

Each empty threat makes the next one less credible CNN. The international community now views Trump’s deadlines as performance art.

But this 28-point plan is different: it’s not a threat Putin needs to outlast. It’s a gift Putin can accept whenever convenient. The harder question is whether Europe and Ukraine will acquiesce to their own betrayal.

Conclusion: The Week That Revealed the Pattern

The week of November 17-20, 2025 crystallized everything wrong with Trump’s foreign policy in a five-day sequence:

Sunday-Wednesday: Forced capitulation on Epstein files punctures invincibility
Thursday Morning: Threatened Democratic lawmakers with execution
Throughout: Celebrated autocrats (Orban, MBS) while drafting Putin’s peace plan
Result: American credibility shattered, democratic allies abandoned, authoritarians empowered

This isn’t the sophisticated TACO pattern I documented in September—where Trump at least waited for Putin’s next provocation before capitulating. This is something cruder: a wounded president lashing out at democratic institutions while seeking validation from the strongmen he admires.

The pattern is clear:

  • Domestic defeat → Autocrat accommodation → Democratic ally betrayal

  • Personal humiliation → Death threats to critics → Capitulation to authoritarians

  • Forced transparency → Retreat to strongman comfort → Abandonment of democratic partners

My September analysis asked whether Trump’s “paper tiger” declaration represented “strategic awakening or tactical theater.” We now know: it was theater. The accommodation pattern never broke. It just waited for Trump’s next moment of domestic weakness to reassert itself.

The question now isn’t whether Trump will abandon Ukraine—the 28-point plan proves he already has. The question is whether Europe possesses the strategic autonomy to defend its own security interests when America has an autocrat affinity disorder masquerading as foreign policy.

From a Canadian perspective, watching America’s foreign policy operate through the lens of one man’s psychological needs rather than strategic doctrine is genuinely alarming. We share a continent with a superpower whose president threatens democratic lawmakers with execution while celebrating the murderers of journalists. We watch as American credibility evaporates in real-time, measured in the gap between “paper tiger” rhetoric and “Putin’s wish list” peace plans.

The Alaska summit’s accommodation was predictable. September’s reversal was surprising. But November’s capitulation—coming immediately after domestic defeat and accompanied by death threats to critics—reveals the true pattern: Trump’s foreign policy is not strategy. It’s psychology. It’s not doctrine. It’s disorder.

And the world’s authoritarians are taking notes.
