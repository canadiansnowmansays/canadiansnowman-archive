The Iran Crisis

The Morning After

Part LXIV: Days Forty Through Forty-Two: The Ceasefire That Is Already Coming Apart

April 10, 2026

The Blue Marble — Earth from space, Arabian Peninsula upper right NASA / ASU / Apollo Remastered

Friday, April 10, 2026

Here is what the morning after looks like.

Pakistan’s prime minister pre-approved his own “last-ditch public plea” with the White House before posting it. The exit ramp was being engineered while the threats were still being posted. The “Draft — Pakistan’s PM Message on X“ header that briefly appeared on Shehbaz Sharif’s Tuesday afternoon post was not a clerical error. It was coordination. The most powerful government on earth was simultaneously threatening to wipe out a civilization and quietly asking a Pakistani intermediary to hand it a ladder.

The ceasefire is now three days old. The Strait of Hormuz is not open. Iran is threatening to boycott the Islamabad talks unless the ceasefire is extended to Lebanon. Israel killed more than 200 people in Beirut on Wednesday — the deadliest single day of the Lebanon war — and has pledged to continue. Trump posted Thursday night that Iran is doing “a very poor job, dishonorable some would say” of allowing oil through the strait. Oil spot prices for immediate delivery are at $145 a barrel. Gas in America peaked at $4.16 a gallon this week — the largest monthly percentage increase since the Energy Information Administration began tracking such data in 1990. Bigger than Ukraine 2022. Bigger than the 1970s shocks.

The civilization did not die on Tuesday night. Something else may be dying instead.

I. The Draft

The Pakistan pre-approval is the most important single piece of reporting from the entire ceasefire period, and it rewrites the origin story of this war’s ending.

Tyler Pager and Katie Rogers of the Times reported Thursday that the White House had seen and signed off on Sharif’s public statement before he posted it. The statement appeared on his social media with the header “Draft — Pakistan’s PM Message on X“ briefly visible before it was corrected. The White House was engineering its own exit while Trump was posting about civilizations dying. A senior administration official confirmed the coordination, speaking on condition of anonymity.

This is not a revelation that changes the ceasefire. It is a revelation that explains it. Trump’s entire negotiating style — the threat, the deadline, the escalation, the walk-back — has always been theater over a predetermined script. What is new is the documentation. We now know that on the same Tuesday morning Trump posted “a whole civilization will die tonight,” his administration was already drafting the Pakistani statement that would give him a way out.

Haberman and Swan’s Times reporting from their forthcoming book established the February 11 origin: Netanyahu’s hour-long Situation Room presentation, the video montage of post-regime leaders including Reza Pahlavi, Trump’s “sounds good to me,” and the CIA’s overnight verdict — “farcical” (Ratcliffe), “bullshit” (Rubio) — that Trump absorbed and moved past. The war began with a president saying “sounds good to me.” It ended, for now, with a president approving the terms of his own ceasefire announcement and letting someone else post them.

Trump and Netanyahu — the handshake that launched a war. "Sounds good to me," Trump told Netanyahu after an hour-long Situation Room pitch for regime change in Iran. The CIA called the plan "farcical." The war began seventeen days later / Credit: Doug Mills / NYT

Forty-two days. From “sounds good to me” to the pre-drafted exit. Everything in between — the 1,701 dead Iranians including 254 children, the 1,500 Lebanese killed, the 13 American service members, Sharif University reduced to rubble, Mahshahr devastated, Bushehr struck 75 meters from the reactor, the F-15E downed over Iran, the colonel on the 7,000-foot ridgeline — everything in between was the cost of that distance.

II. The Suez Moment

In October 1956, Britain, France and Israel attacked Egypt to force open the Suez Canal. President Eisenhower ordered them to stop. Prime Minister Eden resigned. Gamal Abdel Nasser became a hero of anti-colonialism. Suez became shorthand for the moment Britain gave way as a global power to the United States.

The Ezra Klein/Fareed Zakaria conversation, published Friday in the Times, is the most comprehensive accounting of what has just happened to American power. Zakaria’s verdict, delivered without qualification:

The war gave Iran “a far more usable weapon than nuclear weapons.” Iran discovered it can destroy the global economy, block the Strait of Hormuz, and cause cataclysmic follow-on effects. It will now monetize this position — “presumably giving it $70 billion to $90 billion of revenue every year, which is about twice as much as it makes selling oil.” That revenue will be collected in cryptocurrency and yuan. The dollar’s role as the global reserve currency takes a structural hit with each transaction that bypasses it.

“It has strengthened Russia,” Zakaria continues, “because Russia is now making something on the order of $4 billion to $5 billion extra per month because of the price of oil.” It has brought China into the Gulf — Beijing pressed Iran to accept the ceasefire while vetoing the UN Security Council resolution. “It has almost wrecked the Western alliance.”

On the negotiating failure: “If this is a workable basis for negotiation, why the hell didn’t we negotiate on this basis two months ago? The Iranians would have been comfortable with seven of those demands. We could have easily negotiated — with no war.”

Steven Erlanger, writing in the Times from the perspective of European diplomacy, gathered the sharpest verdicts. Bruno Maçães, former Portuguese secretary of state for European affairs: “The myth of America as all-powerful is important, and it’s the basic requirement of a global hegemon to keep the oil flowing, to open up the strait and keep it open. This belief in an all-powerful America that can solve anything is disappearing.”

Stephen Wertheim of the Carnegie Endowment told Erlanger the war “accelerates an existing worry shared by countries around the world about what America’s declining quality of governance means for what they can expect from the United States.”

Francis Fukuyama, writing separately: “There has never been a time when the United States was more distrusted, by both traditional friends and by rivals.”

Anatol Lieven of the Quincy Institute: “By engaging in a war of choice in a critical region for global trade and utterly ignoring the probable consequences for the economies of its closest allies, the Trump administration has destroyed the legitimacy of American power.”

Rajan Menon of City University of New York, also in the Erlanger piece: “While we look crazed and talk about bombing a country back to the stone age, China looks like a peacemaker and agent of stability.”

Poland’s foreign minister Radoslaw Sikorski, asked directly by Erlanger if American hegemony has been diminished: “We hope not, but we fear it might be.”

Jim Tankersley, reporting in the Times from Berlin, documented the world leaders’ reactions as relief curdled into frustration. The British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, who rarely criticizes Trump by name in public, told ITV Thursday that he was “fed up” with Trump and Putin for causing energy prices to jump. That sentence represents something. Starmer has spent his entire prime ministership trying not to say that sentence.

The Suez analogy holds in the ways that matter: a war of choice launched without allied consultation, a strategic objective unachieved, a regional power emerging from the conflict in a stronger position than it entered, and the principal architect of the action diminished before the world that was watching.

The differences from Suez: there is no other global power capable of ordering Trump around the way Eisenhower ordered Eden. The United States is still the hegemon. It is becoming, as foreign policy scholar Stephen Walt described it, a predatory hegemon — one that has decided to monetize its power rather than sustain the rules-based system that made its power worth having.

Anton Troianovski, also in the Times, documented Trump’s NATO escalation — the post-Rutte-meeting posts, the Greenland pivot, the Wall Street Journal report that US troops may be moved from “unhelpful” European countries to Poland and Romania. Jeremy Shapiro of the European Council on Foreign Relations gave Troianovski the most precise diagnosis: “Beating up on Europe and NATO has really no domestic cost. It’s quite typical for Trump: When things are going wrong, he finds the weakest person in the room and blames them.”

The United States’ first military action, in 1798, was a quasi war with France over freedom of navigation. The war with the Barbary pirates was about freedom of navigation. Fareed Zakaria reminded Ezra Klein of this history in their Times conversation: “The U.S. has, literally, for its entire existence, stood for the freedom of navigation.” Trump proposed a joint venture with Iran to collect tolls on the Strait of Hormuz. His allies rejected it within hours. The proposal itself, however, is now on the record.

III. The Gap

Vice President JD Vance in the Oval Office with President Trump last month. Mr. Vance is to lead a U.S. delegation to Islamabad for a meeting on Saturday with Iranian officials.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Vance arrives in Islamabad Friday. He may be arriving to talks that Iran has already announced it will not attend.

Iran’s foreign ministry said Thursday that no Iranian delegates would participate if the ceasefire was not extended to Lebanon. Israel has confirmed it will not extend the ceasefire to Lebanon. Trump asked Netanyahu to scale back operations Thursday. Netanyahu said there is “no ceasefire in Lebanon” and vowed to continue striking Hezbollah until it is disarmed. Within hours of Trump’s request, Israel launched more strikes. The ceasefire’s central structural contradiction — that the US and Iran agreed to stop fighting while Israel continued fighting Iran’s primary proxy — has not been resolved. It is getting worse.

Robert Malley, Biden’s Iran envoy, told Michael Crowley of the Times: “It’s hard to know not just where you go from here, but where you are to begin with. The talks are starting on very weak grounds.”

The gap between the two formal positions remains unbridgeable on its face. Iran’s 10 points include control of the Strait of Hormuz, the right to enrich uranium, withdrawal of all US forces from the region, lifting of all primary and secondary sanctions, and reparations. Trump’s 15 points — never made public — address Iran’s nuclear and missile programs and maritime trade, and require zero enrichment. When Iran publicly released its 10 points Wednesday, a White House official said immediately that they did not match what Trump was referring to. Both sides are negotiating publicly for domestic audiences and privately for survival.

Trita Parsi of the Quincy Institute, speaking on The Left Hook podcast with Wajahat Ali, offered the most honest framework. He does not think a formal agreement is likely. His prediction: a non-negotiated status quo in which the US disengages from the war without promises, without lifting sanctions, without meeting Iranian demands — and the Iranians do not shoot at the US because the US is no longer shooting at Iran. The Israelis, unable to sustain a war with Iran without active American support, eventually pull back. What remains is a dirty war — Israeli assassinations, intelligence attacks, destabilization operations — but no open warfare. Both sides have a common interest in avoiding a return to open conflict. Neither side has enough political will to produce a real agreement. The non-negotiated status quo becomes the new normal.

Parsi’s most penetrating observation: “As a result of this failed war, the threat of American military force has lost its potency in any negotiation order.” Iran withstood the best the most powerful military in the world could deliver. The credibility of American threats — the foundation of five decades of Middle East policy — has been materially degraded. This is not an opinion. It is a strategic calculation every government in the region and Asia is now making simultaneously.

R. Nicholas Burns, who negotiated with Iran under Bush, told Crowley: “The fact that these senior career diplomats were excluded from the Witkoff-led talks with the Iranian foreign minister before the war was diplomatic malpractice.” Burns on the Islamabad talks: “Iran’s interest will be to drag out the talks, sticking to demanding U.S. concessions that President Trump cannot meet.”

Philip Gordon, who served as national security adviser to Vice President Harris, also speaking to Crowley: “For all the presentation of the cease-fire as an agreement, it was a very narrow agreement on a cease-fire with everything else T.B.D. It’s going to be an ugly, messy and incomplete process.”

IV. The Strait That Is Not Open

On Thursday morning, Sultan Ahmed Al Jaber — chief executive of Abu Dhabi National Oil Company, the state oil company of America’s closest Gulf ally — posted on LinkedIn: “Let’s be clear: the Strait of Hormuz is not open. Access is being restricted, conditioned and controlled.”

That is the verdict of the man who runs the oil infrastructure of the country that depends most on free passage through that strait. He is not a critic of the United States. He is a senior official of a government that has hosted American military forces for decades, that was struck by 537 Iranian ballistic missiles, 26 cruise missiles, and 2,256 drones during the war, and that was told this ceasefire would reopen the waterway.

The Hormuz billboard that appeared in Tehran over the weekend depicted Iranian soldiers holding a net full of American jets and a submarine, with the message: “The Strait of Hormuz will stay closed.” Iran’s internal messaging to its own population is unambiguous. The toll booth is the victory.

A billboard in Tehran, April 2026, showing Iranian soldiers holding a fishing net containing American warships and military aircraft. The Farsi text reads: “The Strait of Hormuz will stay closed. The Persian Gulf, from top to bottom, is our hunting ground.” Iran’s domestic messaging on the ceasefire has been unambiguous: the toll booth is the victory.Credit: Arash Khamooshi / The New York Times

Iran is now rewarding alignment and punishing hostility on a case-by-case basis. France’s CMA CGM was the first Western European-owned vessel to transit the strait — the day after Macron publicly criticized Trump’s management of the war. Turkey, which maintained active neutrality, got three ships through. India confirmed its first Iranian oil purchase in seven years. Pakistan, the mediator, gets passage. Countries that supported the US — Britain, Germany, the Gulf states — are being told to negotiate directly with the IRGC for transit rights, because there is no other option.

Mark Landler, reporting for the Times from Paris, documented how Iran is rewarding neutrality and punishing alignment. Bridget Diakun of Lloyd’s List Intelligence told Landler: “The Trump administration is forcing its allies to negotiate with Iran because there is no other option.”

Martin Kelly of EOS Risk Group, also speaking to Landler: “This is the most effective bargaining chip that Iran has got, and will always have. This is going to have a huge impact on global trade and the global economy.”

Trita Parsi, speaking on The Left Hook podcast with Wajahat Ali, asked the question that answers itself: “If the US is not going to open it up militarily, then who will? The Iranians are going to charge very low prices. For each ship, charging $1 million adds like 1% to each barrel. The insurance cost during the war was above 10%. That will come down. If you’re one of these countries, you don’t like it. But it’s either paying 1% or go to war. What do you think most countries are going to choose?”

They will pay the 1%. They are already paying. And the United States, which for 228 years has maintained that freedom of navigation is a right and not a privilege, has a president who proposed splitting the toll revenue as a joint venture.

As of Friday morning, no oil or gas tankers have transited the strait since the ceasefire was announced. Only a handful of bulk carriers. Nearly 1,000 vessels are waiting on both sides. The spot market for immediate oil delivery is at $145 a barrel. The futures market, for June delivery, is at $98 — the gap between those two numbers is the cost of uncertainty, measured in real time.

V. The Hillbilly Chutzpah Problem

JD Vance was the only person in the Situation Room on February 26 who looked at the plan and said: you know I think this is a bad idea. He was right about Hormuz. He was right about gasoline prices. He was right about the coalition fracture. He was right about the MAGA base. He was right about everything.

He was in Hungary stumping for Viktor Orban on Tuesday when Trump was threatening to wipe out a civilization.

He is now leading the peace negotiation. Pakistani officials specifically asked for him by name — they told Witkoff to get Vance involved. His anti-war record is his credential with the mediating party. Iran may view him with less contempt than Witkoff and Kushner, who led two previous rounds of talks that Trump then made irrelevant by launching the war the day after the second round concluded. That is the team’s track record: two rounds of talks, one war.

What Vance needs at the table in Islamabad is chutzpah — the unbothered, strategic audacity of someone who has nothing to prove and everything to gain. What he has spent his career cultivating is something different: hillbilly chutzpah, which is loud where the real thing is quiet, performative where the real thing is purposeful, and works beautifully in Middletown, Ohio and nowhere else on earth.

He has this weekend to find out which one he actually has.

Katie Rogers and Tyler Pager of the Times reported that Philip Gordon, who served as national security adviser to Vice President Harris, offered the sharpest caution about Vance’s position: “This reduces any opportunity he might have to distance himself from the policy if he’s going to be the lead negotiator.” If Islamabad fails, Vance owns the failure. If it succeeds — which Parsi considers unlikely — Vance owns the success and Marco Rubio recedes further into the background of Trump’s second term. The stakes for Vance personally are as significant as the stakes for the ceasefire.

Meanwhile, Trump spent Thursday posting a 482-word Truth Social screed attacking Tucker Carlson (”broken man”), Megyn Kelly (reviving their 2015 feud), Alex Jones (”says some of the dumbest things”), and Candace Owens (commenting on her appearance unfavorably compared to Brigitte Macron). He called Marjorie Taylor Greene “Marjorie ‘Traitor’ Brown.” He attacked the Wall Street Journal editorial board. He called all four podcasters “LOSERS.”

Owens responded in 11 words: “It may be time to put Grandpa up in a home.”

A president fighting his own coalition while his vice president tries to negotiate peace with Iran on the morning of Day Forty-Two. The Baseline Problem series is watching.

VI. The Political Calculation

David Sanger of the Times identified the essential political constraint on Thursday: even if the ceasefire expires on April 21 with no agreement, resuming combat operations is not a viable political choice for Trump.

Suzanne Maloney of the Brookings Institution told Sanger: “There is a bit of mutually assured economic destruction between the US and Iran right now. The US can resume military operations at any moment. But the Iranians have shown they can turn around and threaten attacks on the Strait of Hormuz, with all the predictable effects on the price of oil and fertilizer. And these are all the levers that the president is acutely aware of.”

The economic accounting is now complete for Week Six. Gasoline up 25% from February to March — the largest monthly percentage increase in EIA history since 1990. Gas prices forecast to peak at $4.30 a gallon in April. OECD projects US inflation at 4.2% this year, up from 2.6% in 2025. The Fed is holding rates at 3.5-3.75% and increasingly open to increases. Delta raised baggage fees. Amazon imposed a fuel surcharge. FedEx surcharge: 26.5%. USPS surcharge runs to January 17, 2027 — the economic consequences of this war are already being priced into next year’s calendars.

“You go into the grocery store, you buy the things you normally would, and then all of a sudden it’s $20 or $30 more there,” said Angie Howard, who lives in Portland, Ore.Credit...Amanda Lucier for The New York Times

Lydia DePillis, reporting in the Times on consumer spending, found the human cost in two voices. Angie Howard in Portland: “You go into the grocery store, you buy the things you normally would, and then all of a sudden it’s $20 or $30 more there. And at the end of the week, where you would normally have two nickels to rub together, now they’re not there.”

Dakota Wylde in Denver, a former federal contractor laid off in government cuts and now living on savings, told DePillis: “When we get to that point, we have to start thinking about the hard stuff. Like do we need to have a third meal a day?”

The midterms are in November. Marc Short, former chief of staff to Pence: “Knowing that this is a midterm election year, that is the biggest leverage point that the Iranians have, and they know that.” The China summit is scheduled for mid-May. Every week the war extends is a week of evidence against Trump in the midterm argument, a week of economic damage that feeds Democratic candidates, a week of gasoline prices that voters feel every time they fill their tank.

Parsi on why Trump will not go back to war: “He’s understood that this was a huge mistake. He won’t admit it, but he’s understood it. I find it extremely unlikely that he would go back into this war.”

Tom Nichols on Trump’s body language since Easter: “Get me out of this.”

The exit ramp was always the plan. The pre-approved Pakistani post was the mechanism. The ceasefire is the cover. What is left is managing the retreat without acknowledging it.

VII. The Fortieth Day

Thursday was the fortieth day since Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in the opening strikes of the war on February 28. In Shia Islam, the fortieth day after death — Chehelom — is an important milestone in the mourning process. Crowds gathered in streets across Iran, in Tehran, Mashhad, and Qom, for mourning processions. Photos on state media showed large crowds walking through the capital.

Khamenei has not been formally buried. No date for a public funeral has been announced.

The war began on the day he died. The ceasefire arrived on his fortieth day. He has not yet been buried.

His son Mojtaba — 56, IRGC-connected, more hawkish than his father, named supreme leader by a committee of senior Shiite clerics — is running the country through written statements. He has not been seen in public since gaining the post. Yeganeh Torbati’s reporting for the Times documented what is becoming increasingly apparent: the country is likely being run by the IRGC, with the new supreme leader serving largely as a ceremonial figurehead. The war that was meant to end the Islamic Republic has handed it to the IRGC.

Ali Alfoneh, Arab Gulf States Institute: “Survival is victory.”

Fareed Zakaria: “Just as Saddam Hussein did in 1980 when he invaded Iran at a time when Iran was weak, at a time when Khomeini had not completely consolidated his power. Because of the war, he managed to do so. Forty-seven years later, the Islamic Republic is still there.”

Parsi’s verdict, delivered to Wajahat Ali on The Left Hook in response to the direct question — looking back at six weeks, what are the wins for the United States of America? — was unambiguous: “There are no wins whatsoever.”

The one argument available: “At least now we know this was done and hopefully, as a result, we won’t do it again. We will be more humble. We will focus on diplomacy, we will focus on our core interests. We will not go into other countries’ wars. We will not be led into wars by other countries. One can hope.”

Arash Azizi, writing in The Atlantic, offered the only honest case for cautious optimism. The cease-fire’s national unity statement from Iran’s Supreme National Security Council read as a warning to hard-liners not to derail talks. Ghalibaf — former Tehran mayor, Davos attendee, technocrat as well as IRGC veteran — has Rouhani’s backing and the support of Pezeshkian’s aides. The work-arounds exist: Iran formally reserves the right to enrich without actually doing it; missile limits in exchange for air defense access and a no-strike pledge. The 2015 JCPOA was built on exactly this constructive ambiguity.

Azizi’s closing frame: not regime change, not democracy, not a US ally. But more Vietnam than North Korea. Economically open, diplomatically reintegrated, no longer pointing everything at the strait. That outcome — still authoritarian, still repressive, but no longer isolated and no longer throttling the world’s energy supply — is better than the non-negotiated status quo Parsi describes, and vastly better than the war continuing.

The distance between those two outcomes will be determined in Islamabad this weekend, if the talks happen at all.

The record remains open.
