Trump's Foreign Policy

Episode 2: From Ukraine to Iran.......The Acceleration of American Decline

June 18, 2025

Stephen Lawton June 18, 2025

Bottom Line Up Front: In just one month since my initial analysis, Trump's foreign policy has lurched from abandoning Ukraine to potentially dragging America into a new Middle East war. This whiplash demonstrates not strategic pivoting but the dangerous absence of any coherent doctrine—validating every concern raised about personality-driven chaos masquerading as statecraft.

When I wrote in May about Trump's foreign policy representing "reactive chaos" rather than strategic vision, I expected the pattern to continue. I didn't expect it to accelerate so dramatically that within weeks, America would pivot from pressuring Ukraine to surrender to Russia to contemplating military action against Iran alongside Israel. This isn't strategic flexibility—it's the foreign policy equivalent of a pinball careening between bumpers, with global stability hanging in the balance.

The Ukraine Abandonment: Mission Accomplished for Putin

My May analysis detailed how Trump's February confrontation with Ukrainian President Zelensky—where he threatened "we're out" unless Ukraine accepted a ceasefire—represented "the symbolic crucifixion of democracy." The subsequent months have only validated this assessment, with consequences now becoming clear.

Ambassador Bridget Brink's resignation captured the moral bankruptcy perfectly: "For the first time in 28 years of service under five different presidents, I felt we were on the wrong side of history." Her critique of the administration's "policy to put pressure on Ukraine, the victim, rather than on Russia, the aggressor" after witnessing three years of Russian atrocities represents the judgment of America's finest diplomatic minds.

The strategic implications extend far beyond moral considerations. Recent U.S.-Russia talks in Saudi Arabia excluded Ukraine entirely, with "Ukraine not invited to the talks" despite the war being fought on Ukrainian territory. This exclusion of the victim while accommodating the aggressor has emboldened Putin in ways that were predictable but apparently not anticipated by Trump's team.

Putin now understands that Trump will abandon democratic allies under pressure. This lesson won't be limited to Ukraine—every ally from Taiwan to South Korea is watching and drawing conclusions about American reliability under Trump's leadership.

The Iran Pivot: Stumbling Into Strategic Incoherence

The speed of Trump's pivot from Ukraine abandonment to potential Iran military action reveals the absence of any organizing principle beyond reaction to immediate pressures. When asked about potential U.S. military action against Iran, Trump told reporters: "I may do it. I may not do it. I mean, nobody knows what I'm going to do"—a response that epitomizes the unpredictability I identified as fundamentally damaging to American credibility.

What makes this pivot particularly concerning is how Israel has effectively manipulated Trump toward its hawkish Iran position. Initial reports suggest Trump "didn't want them going in because, I mean, that would blow it" referring to his diplomatic efforts. Yet Israel proceeded anyway, and Trump gradually shifted from opposition to "reluctant support" after briefings that convinced him "Israel had reason to act and that the U.S. would have to lend some support."

This represents exactly the kind of tail-wagging-the-dog scenario that undermines American strategic autonomy. Netanyahu presented Trump with intelligence that could have allowed targeting Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei, which Trump rejected—but only after being drawn into a conflict he initially opposed. The pattern shows how Trump's reactive decision-making creates openings for allies to manipulate American policy toward their preferred outcomes.

Trump's threat to Iran—"Why didn't you negotiate with me two weeks ago? You could have done fine... You would have had a country"—combined with his ominous conclusion represents the same maximalist rhetoric that has characterized his approach throughout. Yet this tough talk comes from the same president who just forced Ukraine into negotiations with its aggressor, creating a cognitive dissonance that undermines the credibility of his threats.

The contradiction is stark: Trump pressured Ukraine, a democratic ally under unprovoked attack, to negotiate with Russia while simultaneously threatening Iran with destruction for pursuing nuclear capabilities that Israel and the United States oppose. The inconsistency suggests decisions driven by domestic political calculations rather than strategic coherence.

Alliance Fractures in Real Time

The domestic response to potential Iran military action has been telling, with protests recorded in San Jose, New York City, and Milwaukee featuring demonstrators holding signs reading "hands off Iran". More significant is the opposition from Trump's own base, with "prominent conservative commentators Tucker Carlson and Steve Bannon, Trump's White House chief strategist in 2017, speaking out against the prospect" of American military involvement.

Tucker Carlson's network declared bluntly: "If Israel wants to wage this war, it has every right to do so. It is a sovereign country, and it can do as it pleases. But not with America's backing... there is another option: drop Israel. Let them fight their own wars." When Trump's most influential media ally advocates abandoning a key strategic partner, it reveals how the Iran escalation has fractured his own coalition.

This internal opposition validates my earlier observation about the contradictions in Trump's approach creating fissures within his own coalition. The June G7 summit provided perfect visual confirmation, with Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni caught on camera giving a dramatic eye roll during whispered conversations with French President Macron, while Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney cut off questions to Trump during their bilateral meeting.

Meanwhile, European allies who watched Trump abandon Ukraine are now observing America potentially stumble into another Middle East conflict. The pattern reinforces every concern about American reliability and strategic judgment that has emerged since Trump's return to office.

The Authoritarian Dividend: Putin and Xi Capitalize

My May analysis predicted that "Trump's approach appears likely to accelerate American decline by alienating allies, emboldening adversaries, and abandoning the moral leadership that made American hegemony legitimate." The Ukraine-to-Iran pivot provides textbook validation of this prediction.

Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov's warning that direct U.S. military support for Israel "would radically destabilize the entire situation" positions Russia as the voice of restraint while America contemplates military escalation. This represents a complete inversion of traditional roles—Russia, the aggressor in Ukraine, now lectures America about the dangers of military intervention.

Putin has achieved something remarkable: he's watching America potentially repeat the Middle East military overextension that weakened U.S. global position for decades, all while consolidating Russian gains in Ukraine without meaningful American opposition. Xi Jinping is undoubtedly taking notes about American fickleness and the opportunities it creates for patient adversaries.

The Transactional Trap: No Strategy, Only Tactics

The Ukraine-Iran sequence perfectly illustrates the fundamental flaw in Trump's transactional approach: without strategic framework, each crisis becomes an isolated negotiation divorced from broader consequences. Trump's complaint that Iran should have negotiated "two weeks ago" because now "you would have had a country" reveals his belief that every international relationship can be reduced to a business deal with clear winners and losers.

This reductive worldview ignores how abandoning Ukraine affects Iran calculations, how threatening Iran impacts European allies, and how both decisions influence Chinese assessments of American resolve in the Pacific. Each transaction exists in Trump's mind as a separate negotiation, but adversaries and allies understand that credibility and reliability are global commodities that can't be compartmentalized.

The result is a foreign policy that achieves tactical victories—getting Ukraine to negotiate, pressuring Iran—while suffering strategic defeats through the systematic erosion of American credibility and alliance cohesion.

Economic and Domestic Consequences

The whiplash from Ukraine to Iran comes as Trump's broader foreign policy approach continues generating negative economic consequences. Economists say his tariff policies "were a significant factor" in the U.S. economy shrinking "0.3% in the first three months of this year", while polling shows Trump "underwater on both tariffs (33 percent to 58 percent) and foreign policy (40 percent versus 54 percent)".

Potential military involvement in Iran threatens to compound these economic headwinds while creating new domestic political vulnerabilities. As one Milwaukee protester warned: "I feel like we are being dragged into what could be a World War III"—a sentiment that resonates beyond traditional anti-war constituencies to include Trump's own base.

The Moral Inversion Accelerates

UC Berkeley professor M. Steven Fish's characterization of Trump's Ukraine policy as representing "the symbolic crucifixion of democracy, decency and our commitment to our democratic allies" becomes even more damning when contrasted with potential Iran military action. Trump abandoned a democracy under attack while contemplating military support for actions against an authoritarian regime.

This moral inversion—pressuring victims while threatening aggressors—represents exactly the kind of values abandonment that has historically undermined American soft power and legitimacy. Allies notice when America applies different standards based on domestic political convenience rather than consistent principles.

Acceleration Toward Irrelevance

Fish's prediction that "it's hard to see the United States ever recovering its power and prestige" because "the power and prestige and influence that it has enjoyed over the last 80 years have all been squandered by Trump" feels less hyperbolic with each passing week. The Ukraine-Iran pivot demonstrates how quickly credibility can erode when foreign policy becomes reactive rather than strategic.

His forecast that Putin "will be able to terrorize our West European allies" while "Xi Jinping will get Taiwan and will be able to terrorize or pressure South Korea, Japan and Australia" seems increasingly plausible as American attention and credibility fragment across multiple crisis theaters without coherent strategic vision.

Looking Forward: The TACO Pattern and Strategic Incoherence

The month between my initial analysis and today has validated every concern about Trump's foreign policy representing personality-driven chaos rather than strategic innovation. The Ukraine-Iran sequence demonstrates how quickly American leadership can collapse when decisions flow from domestic political calculations rather than sustained strategic thinking.

Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei's defiant response to Trump's threats—"Intelligent people who know Iran, the nation and the history of Iran will never speak to this nation in the language of threats, because the Iranian nation cannot be surrendered"—shows how Trump's maximalist rhetoric increasingly falls on deaf ears among adversaries who have learned to discount American credibility.

Yet there remains the distinct possibility that Trump will execute what has become known as a "TACO"—Trump Abandons Course Of action—and back away from Iran involvement just as suddenly as he pivoted toward it. The domestic pressure from his own base (Carlson, Bannon), international allied concerns (visible at the G7), and his own instinct to avoid "endless wars" could combine to force another policy reversal.

If Trump does TACO on Iran, some might argue this vindicates his approach—that the threat of escalation brought Iran to negotiations while actual restraint avoided military disaster. But this would miss the fundamental point: whether Trump ultimately escalates or reverses course on Iran, the pattern of reactive decision-making based on immediate pressures rather than strategic consistency remains the defining characteristic of his foreign policy approach.

The damage to American credibility occurs regardless of the final outcome. Allies have already witnessed him abandon Ukraine, threaten Iran, and potentially abandon Iran—whiplash that reinforces unreliability and makes future crisis management exponentially harder. Adversaries learn that sustained pressure can force Trump reversals, while allies conclude that American commitments vary based on Trump's momentary calculations rather than enduring strategic principles.

The pattern will continue: crisis emerges, Trump makes maximalist threats, domestic and international pressure mounts, Trump either backs down or stumbles forward, credibility erodes further. Each cycle makes the next crisis harder to manage and America's position weaker—whether Trump TACOs or not.

The question is no longer whether Trump's foreign policy represents strategic vision or reactive chaos—the evidence is overwhelming. The question is whether American democracy and global stability can survive the acceleration of American decline that each new crisis reveals, regardless of how any individual crisis ultimately resolves.
