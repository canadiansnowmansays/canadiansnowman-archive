This is NOT Another Chapter of Mr. Smith Goes to Washington

Weekend Update

December 29, 2025

Introduction: Democracy Inverted

In Frank Capra’s 1939 classic “Mr. Smith Goes to Washington,” an idealistic young senator travels to the nation’s capital to serve democracy, armed with nothing but principles and faith in the American system. The film’s enduring power comes from its vision of Washington as the seat of democratic ideals, where even the humblest citizen can stand against corruption and speak truth to power.

On December 28 and 29, 2025, two foreign leaders made pilgrimages to meet President Trump. But they didn’t go to Washington. They went to Mar-a-Lago, Trump’s private club in Palm Beach, Florida—the sixth such visit for one leader, the fourth this year for the other.

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky arrived early Sunday morning for his latest audience with Trump. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu arrives Monday for his own Mar-a-Lago audience. Both come as supplicants, not partners. Both need Trump more than he needs them. Both are discovering that access to American power now runs through a private resort, not the halls of democratic government.

This is the opposite of Mr. Smith Goes to Washington. Foreign leaders don’t travel to the capital to engage with American democracy. They journey to a private club to court a president who treats diplomacy as transactional business, sovereignty as leverage, and foreign policy as reality television performed for domestic political consumption.

The contrast reveals how thoroughly Trump has inverted traditional American diplomatic practice—and what it means when the world’s most powerful democracy conducts foreign policy from a private club rather than the institutions designed to serve the public interest.

Zelensky’s Accommodation: The Fourth Visit

Volodymyr Zelensky, the wartime president of Ukraine fighting for his nation’s survival, arrived at Mar-a-Lago early Sunday for his fourth meeting with Trump in 2025. The optics alone told the story: a leader defending his country from Russian invasion must travel to Trump’s private resort to secure American support.

Trump announced the meeting via Truth Social, noting it would take place “in the main dining room of Mar-a-Lago,” with media present. The setting—Trump’s private club, cameras rolling, the president controlling the stage—transforms diplomacy into performance.

The substance revealed the accommodation dynamics at play. Zelensky came with a 20-point peace plan that he claimed was “90% agreed.” But agreed on whose terms? The territorial concessions Russia demands? The “demilitarized free economic zone” in Donetsk that would legitimize conquest? The referendum on ceding disputed territory that would ask Ukrainians to vote away their own land?

Trump’s framing made the power dynamic explicit. Asked about the “thorniest issues,” Trump said “it was ‘the land,’ referring to the Donbas region, which Russia has demanded Ukraine cede.” He added: “I think the land you’re talking about, some of that land has been taken. Some of that land is maybe up for grabs.”

“Maybe up for grabs.” Sovereign Ukrainian territory becomes negotiable real estate in Trump’s transactional framework. Zelensky, standing beside him, could only insist Ukraine’s position is “very clear” while Trump acknowledged “this is a very tough question.”

The meeting came shortly after Trump spoke with Russian President Vladimir Putin for over two hours—a call Trump described as “good and very productive.” The sequence matters: Trump consults with the aggressor, then receives the victim. Putin gets phone access; Zelensky must travel to Florida.

This wasn’t Zelensky’s first accommodation to Trump’s demands. In February, a meeting between the two leaders “devolved into public spectacle, with Trump and Vice President JD Vance accusing Zelensky of being ‘disrespectful’ to the U.S.” The Ukrainian president, fighting for his nation’s survival, was publicly chastised by American leaders for insufficient deference.

Zelensky has learned the lesson. Sunday’s meeting produced effusive praise for Trump and optimistic declarations about peace prospects. “We discussed all aspects of the peace framework,” Zelensky said, emphasizing areas of agreement. The accommodation was complete: travel to Mar-a-Lago, praise Trump, accept his framing, hope for continued American support.

The alternative—pushing back, demanding respect for Ukrainian sovereignty, rejecting territorial concessions—risks losing the military aid Ukraine desperately needs. So Zelensky accommodates, even as Russian missiles continue striking Ukrainian cities and Trump describes sovereign territory as “maybe up for grabs.”

Netanyahu’s Supplication: The Sixth Meeting

Twenty-four hours after Zelensky departed, Benjamin Netanyahu arrives for his own Mar-a-Lago audience—the sixth meeting between the two leaders since Trump returned to office in January.

The Israeli prime minister comes with his own accommodation challenges. Trump’s team—particularly special envoy Steve Witkoff and advisor/son-in-law Jared Kushner—has been pushing for faster implementation of the Gaza ceasefire and transition to Phase Two. Netanyahu has been resisting, skeptical of demilitarization plans, the composition of the proposed technocratic government, and roles for Turkey and Qatar in post-war Gaza.

White House officials believe Netanyahu is “slow-walking the peace process” and fear he will resume war with Hamas. As one senior Israeli official told the press: “Bibi is trying to convince a one-man audience. The question is whether Trump will side with him or with his top advisers when it comes to Gaza.”

Netanyahu’s supplication extends beyond policy disagreements. CNN reported the visit marks “more than a diplomatic reunion” but rather “the opening act of his 2026 reelection bid, in which the US President is positioned to play a starring role.” Trump’s approval rating among Israelis stands at 76%, compared to the Israeli government’s 40%. Netanyahu needs Trump’s political star power for his own electoral survival.

The accommodations Netanyahu has already made reveal the asymmetric power relationship. In September, when Israel struck Hamas leaders inside Qatar—a key U.S. ally—Trump was furious. During an Oval Office meeting, Trump insisted Netanyahu apologize to Qatari leaders, “even holding the telephone on his lap as the prime minister placed the call.” The image captures the dynamic: Trump literally controlling the phone while Netanyahu performs the required apology.

Netanyahu even considered opening the Rafah crossing between Gaza and Egypt as a “goodwill gesture” for the Trump meeting, but abandoned the plan after opposition from his right-wing coalition partners. The Israeli prime minister was prepared to make policy concessions specifically for Trump’s approval—then couldn’t deliver because of domestic political constraints.

Trump has publicly backed Netanyahu’s request for a presidential pardon on corruption charges. During an October address to the Knesset, Trump asked Israeli President Isaac Herzog: “Hey, I have an idea, Mr. President – why don’t you give him a pardon?” He dismissed the charges as trivial matters of “cigars and champagne.”

The quid pro quo is barely implicit: Trump advocates for Netanyahu’s legal relief while Netanyahu must accommodate Trump’s policy preferences in Gaza, Syria, and Lebanon. Israeli officials have already discussed hosting Trump in Israel during Netanyahu’s 2026 campaign—the American president as campaign asset for the Israeli prime minister.

Mar-a-Lago Diplomacy: Where Democracy Goes to Die

The shift from Washington to Mar-a-Lago represents more than geographic convenience. It symbolizes the transformation of American diplomacy from public service to private transaction, from institutional process to personal deal-making.

Traditional diplomacy occurs in government buildings designed to represent the people’s interests. These spaces embody the principle that American power serves democratic purposes and operates through constitutional structures. Mar-a-Lago is a private club where membership costs hundreds of thousands of dollars.

The choice to conduct high-stakes foreign policy negotiations at a private resort reflects Trump’s broader approach to governance. He treats the presidency as personal property rather than public trust, American power as his to wield rather than the people’s to direct through constitutional process.

Foreign leaders must accommodate this reality. They travel to Trump’s resort, perform for cameras, accept his framing of complex geopolitical conflicts, and hope their deference produces favorable outcomes.

European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen participated in an hour-long call with Trump and Zelensky after their Mar-a-Lago meeting. European leaders must coordinate through Trump’s resort rather than through established diplomatic channels or international institutions.

This approach serves Trump’s political needs while degrading diplomatic effectiveness. Complex negotiations about war, peace, territorial integrity, and security guarantees become photo opportunities and sound bites. “Some of that land is maybe up for grabs” plays well for Trump’s base but trivializes Ukrainian sovereignty. Making Netanyahu apologize on speakerphone demonstrates dominance but humiliates an ally.

These questions don’t trouble Trump because he rejects the premise that American power should be constrained by democratic norms, institutional process, or public accountability. Mar-a-Lago diplomacy perfectly expresses his view: the presidency belongs to him, American power serves his interests, and foreign leaders must seek his favor.

The Inversion: Power Without Democracy

Frank Capra’s Mr. Smith Goes to Washington presents an idealized vision of American democracy: an ordinary citizen goes to the capital, confronts corruption, and vindicates democratic principles through passionate advocacy and moral clarity. The film’s power comes from its faith that American institutions, properly functioning, serve justice and the common good.

Trump’s Mar-a-Lago diplomacy inverts every element of that vision.

Instead of citizens traveling to the capital to participate in democracy, foreign leaders travel to a private club to court personal favor.

Instead of moral principles and constitutional process guiding decisions, transactional calculations and personal relationships determine outcomes.

Instead of public institutions serving the common good, a private resort hosts negotiations serving the president’s political interests.

Instead of transparency and accountability, cameras capture performances choreographed for domestic political consumption while substance remains opaque.

Instead of democratic allies engaging as partners, sovereign nations accommodate as supplicants.

The inversion extends beyond symbolism to substance. Democratic diplomacy operates through established institutions, treaty obligations, alliance structures, and international law. These frameworks constrain arbitrary power and provide predictability for international relations.

Trump’s approach rejects these constraints. Alliances become transactional. Treaties are renegotiable. International law is disposable. NATO commitments depend on satisfaction with other members’ contributions. Ukrainian sovereignty is “maybe up for grabs.” Israeli actions receive support regardless of international law violations.

The Mar-a-Lago setting embodies this rejection of institutional constraint. Conducting diplomacy at a private club signals that Trump’s personal preferences, not democratic institutions or international norms, determine American foreign policy.

For foreign leaders, this creates impossible dilemmas. Traditional diplomatic engagement through official channels proves insufficient because Trump circumvents those channels. But accommodating Trump’s preferences often requires compromising their own nations’ interests or violating their own democratic principles.

Zelensky must balance Ukrainian sovereignty against desperate need for American weapons. Netanyahu must balance Israeli security concerns against Trump team preferences for faster Gaza transitions. Both must perform deference to maintain access to American power, even when that deference undermines their substantive positions.

The accommodations accumulate: accept meetings at private club rather than White House, endure public rebukes when insufficiently deferential, allow Trump to frame complex issues in simplistic transactional terms, coordinate with Trump’s family members rather than professional diplomats, time visits for Trump’s political convenience rather than strategic necessity.

Each accommodation individually might seem minor. Collectively, they transform the relationship from democratic partnership to patron-client dependency. The United States becomes not a democratic ally operating through predictable institutional processes but an erratic patron whose favor must be courted and maintained through personal relationship with the president.

The Domestic Audience: Foreign Policy as Reality TV

The media presence at Mar-a-Lago meetings reveals another inversion of democratic diplomacy: foreign policy as performance for domestic political consumption rather than substantive negotiation serving national interests.

The performance serves Trump’s domestic political needs. His base sees strong leadership through foreign leaders coming to his private club. His supporters hear him describing Ukrainian territory as negotiable real estate without considering the implications for sovereignty or alliance credibility. His evangelical coalition sees him courting Netanyahu and backing Israel without examining the costs of unconditional support.

The spectacle obscures substance. Complex questions about security guarantees, territorial integrity, war crimes accountability, and international law disappear behind images of Trump receiving foreign leaders and declaring progress toward peace.

For Trump, this represents optimal outcome: domestic political benefit from appearing decisive without accountability for consequences. If Ukraine “peace” produces Russian territorial gains through forced concessions, Trump claims credit for ending the war. If Gaza ceasefire collapses, Trump blames Netanyahu or Hamas.

The result is diplomacy as performance rather than genuine negotiation. Trump demonstrates strength for American voters. Foreign leaders demonstrate deference to maintain his favor. Policy outcomes become secondary.

This explains why meetings produce statements about progress while details remain vague. “90% agreed” sounds impressive until examining which 10% remains unresolved—territorial concessions, security guarantees, timelines, verification mechanisms.

Conclusion: When Washington Goes to Mar-a-Lago

The contrast between Frank Capra’s vision and Trump’s reality couldn’t be starker. Mr. Smith went to Washington to serve democracy. Zelensky and Netanyahu go to Mar-a-Lago to court a president who treats diplomacy as reality television, sovereignty as negotiable, and American power as personal property.

The inversion reveals profound questions about American democracy and global leadership:

Can democratic diplomacy function when conducted from private clubs rather than public institutions? The shift from Washington to Mar-a-Lago symbolizes broader rejection of institutional constraint and democratic accountability. Foreign policy becomes personal transaction rather than public service.

What happens to alliances when partners become supplicants? Zelensky’s fourth visit and Netanyahu’s sixth demonstrate dependency relationships replacing genuine partnerships. Democratic allies must perform deference to maintain access to American power, even when that deference undermines their own interests.

How does performance replace substance in foreign policy? The media-present Mar-a-Lago meetings prioritize spectacle over serious negotiation. Trump demonstrates power through summoning foreign leaders; they demonstrate accommodation through effusive praise and optimistic declarations. Actual policy outcomes become secondary to the performance.

What are the costs of accommodation? Zelensky accepts framing of Ukrainian territory as “maybe up for grabs.” Netanyahu must hope Trump sides with him over his own advisers. Both compromise substantive positions to maintain Trump’s favor.

Where does democratic accountability exist? Conducting diplomacy at a resort removes democratic institutions from the process. No congressional oversight, no State Department coordination, no transparency about who influences decisions.

The pattern established across Trump’s foreign policy approach extends beyond Ukraine and Israel. Nigerian officials accommodated threats of military intervention by providing intelligence for Christmas Day strikes framed around religious persecution they explicitly rejected. Mexican leaders accommodated tariff threats through immigration enforcement. NATO allies accommodated demands for increased defense spending. Trade partners accommodated threats through concessions.

In each case, Trump’s coercive approach forces accommodation from foreign governments, then presents that forced cooperation as diplomatic success. The Mar-a-Lago meetings with Zelensky and Netanyahu represent the culmination of this pattern: foreign leaders traveling to private resort, performing for domestic American audience, accommodating Trump’s framing even when it undermines their interests, hoping their deference produces favorable outcomes.

This is not another chapter of Mr. Smith Goes to Washington. It is the inversion of that democratic ideal: foreign policy as private transaction, diplomacy as reality television, alliances as dependency relationships, American power as personal property rather than public trust.

The idealistic young senator in Capra’s film believed American democratic institutions, properly functioning, would serve justice and the common good. He went to Washington armed with principles and faith in the system.

Modern foreign leaders go to Mar-a-Lago armed with accommodation strategies and desperate hope that personal relationship with Trump will secure the support their nations need. They discover that access to American power requires performance of deference, not appeals to democratic principles or alliance obligations.

The senators in Mr. Smith Goes to Washington eventually vindicate democracy by standing against corruption and defending principles over personal interest. The foreign leaders at Mar-a-Lago discover that principles must be compromised, interests must be negotiated, and sovereignty must be treated as “maybe up for grabs” to maintain favor with a president who views diplomacy as transactional business.

Washington represented the ideal of democratic governance serving the public good. Mar-a-Lago represents the reality of personal power serving private interests.

The distance between the two measures how far American democracy and diplomatic practice have fallen from the ideals Capra celebrated—and how thoroughly Trump has inverted the relationship between American power and democratic accountability.

Breaking News Postscript: The Accommodation Trap Springs

As this essay was being prepared for publication on Monday, December 29, Russia threatened to “revise” agreements reached in the Trump-mediated peace talks, citing an alleged Ukrainian drone attack on a Putin residence that Ukraine immediately called a “complete fabrication.”

Within 24 hours of Zelensky’s Mar-a-Lago accommodation, the predicted pattern played out with devastating precision: Putin called Trump Monday morning to frame the narrative. Trump told reporters he was “very angry” about the alleged attack despite having “no independent confirmation it had occurred.” Russia announced plans to capture the Ukrainian city of Zaporizhzhia “in the near future” and threatened to expand territorial demands to the “entirety” of Zaporizhzhia and Kherson regions.

The sequence validates this essay’s core argument: Zelensky’s performance of deference at Mar-a-Lago purchased no security against Russian aggression—only exposure to Russia’s next fabricated grievance and Trump’s willingness to believe Putin over his Ukrainian counterpart. Putin gets phone access to shape Trump’s perception. Zelensky gets scolded for allegedly “attacking his house.” Russia escalates its offensive and expands its demands.

This is precisely the accommodation trap that transforms democratic allies into supplicants. The Mar-a-Lago meeting produced no deterrence, no security guarantees, no protection against Russian fabrications. It produced only the illusion of progress—and 24 hours later, Russia demonstrated what that accommodation actually purchased: nothing.
