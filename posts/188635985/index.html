Constitutional Reckoning

Part 42: The Emperor Has No Tariffs — When the Supreme Court Finally Said No (2025-2026)

April 30, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Forty-seven parts. Forty-seven entries documenting fraud, obstruction, sexual misconduct, criminal conviction, constitutional violations, corporate immunity, racism, and grift on a scale American politics has never before produced. Forty-seven case studies in a single recurring theme: the man never pays.

And then, on February 20, 2026, something happened that this series was not designed to document.

He paid.

Not fully. Not finally. Not in the way that justice would demand after a lifetime of consequence-free wrongdoing. But for the first time in this series — across nearly sixty years of documented impunity — a co-equal branch of the United States government looked at Donald Trump’s assertion of extraordinary power and said, simply and unambiguously: no.

The Supreme Court, 6-3, struck down the legal foundation for approximately 80% of Trump’s tariff regime. Chief Justice John Roberts, writing for the majority, delivered the sentence that will define this chapter of American constitutional history: “Those words cannot bear such weight.”

This entry is different from every other in this project. It is the only one that documents accountability delivered. Which makes the question it ultimately asks all the more important: does it matter?

The Power He Claimed

To understand what the Court took away, you must first understand what Trump claimed to have.

In February 2025, Trump invoked the International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 — a law designed to freeze terrorist bank accounts and impose sanctions on hostile regimes — to impose sweeping tariffs on imports from nearly every trading partner the United States has. He declared a “national emergency” over trade deficits that had existed, in some cases, for decades. He used this declaration to impose tariffs of unlimited amount, on unlimited goods, from unlimited countries, for unlimited duration — all by executive fiat, without congressional authorization, without formal investigation, without the procedural safeguards Congress built into every legitimate tariff authority.

No president had ever used IEEPA this way in the 48 years since its passage.

The breadth of the claim was staggering. By Trump’s reading, a president could impose 1,000% tariffs on every good from every country on earth simply by declaring an emergency — and Congress, the courts, and the American public would have no recourse. His own Solicitor General warned the Supreme Court that a ruling against the administration would cause economic damage “akin to the Great Depression.” The implicit message: we’ve collected $250 billion already. Try and stop us.

This fits the pattern documented throughout this series. Trump has always governed by assertion — claiming powers he doesn’t have, spending money Congress didn’t appropriate, firing officials he couldn’t legally fire, declassifying documents through thought alone. The tariff regime was simply the largest and most economically consequential version of a governing philosophy built on the premise that power belongs to whoever seizes it.

Reciprocal tariffs unveiled — the framework that just lost its legal foundation.

The Legal Framework He Destroyed

The Constitution is explicit on this point, which is why Trump’s lawyers had to argue around it rather than through it.

Article I, Section 8 grants Congress — not the president — the power to “lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises” and to “regulate Commerce with foreign Nations.” This is not ambiguous. The Founders, having lived under a Crown that taxed without representation, were deliberate about placing the taxing power in the legislative branch.

Congress has, over time, delegated specific and limited tariff authorities to the president:

Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 allows tariffs when the Commerce Secretary finds imports threaten national security — but requires a formal investigation and report completed within 270 days.

Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 permits retaliation against specific unfair trade practices — but requires formal investigations by the U.S. Trade Representative taking 7-12 months.

Section 201 provides temporary relief for industries injured by imports — with strict time limits and congressional oversight.

Each of these authorities comes with procedural requirements, evidentiary standards, and time constraints. Each represents Congress carefully calibrating how much tariff authority it was willing to delegate and under what conditions.

Trump bypassed all of them. IEEPA contains the word “regulate” and the word “importation” — separated by sixteen other words. His administration argued those two words, in a sanctions statute that never mentions tariffs, taxes, or duties, granted the president unlimited power to tax all imports from all countries at any rate for any duration.

Three lower courts rejected this argument. The Federal Circuit rejected it 7-4 in August 2025. And on February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court rejected it 6-3.

The Ruling: What the Court Said

Chief Justice John Roberts

Chief Justice Roberts, joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, Jackson, Barrett, and Gorsuch, applied what lawyers call the Major Questions Doctrine — a principle requiring that when the executive branch claims authority over decisions of vast economic and political significance, it must point to clear congressional authorization.

The ruling’s central passage deserves to be read in full:

“The president asserts the extraordinary power to unilaterally impose tariffs of unlimited amount, duration, and scope. In light of the breadth, history, and constitutional context of that asserted authority, he must identify clear congressional authorization to exercise it.”

He could not. The statute says nothing about tariffs. Congress never delegated this power. The emergency Trump declared — trade deficits that had existed for decades with dozens of countries simultaneously — failed every legal definition of the sudden, unexpected crises IEEPA was designed to address.

“Those words cannot bear such weight,” Roberts wrote of the administration’s two-word textual argument. It is as clean a judicial rebuke as American constitutional law produces.

The vote itself warrants scrutiny. Six justices — three appointed by Democrats, three by Republicans — agreed. The three dissenters were Clarence Thomas, Samuel Alito, and Brett Kavanaugh — the last of whom Trump appointed. Even this was not the unified conservative shield many assumed would protect a Republican president’s power claims. The institutional integrity of the Court, battered by years of controversy, held on this day.

A technical note for the record: the Major Questions Doctrine analysis in Roberts's opinion was joined only by Gorsuch and Barrett, making it a three-justice plurality rather than a majority holding on that point. Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson joined the statutory analysis — the reading of the text, the constitutional backdrop, the conclusion that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs — but reached their conclusion without needing the Major Questions Doctrine to get there. The result was the same. The six-justice coalition held. But the methodological fracture within the majority is worth noting: even justices who agreed on the outcome disagreed on how to explain why.

Trade war on the ground: Surviving Section 232 tariffs keep the pressure on borders.

What the Ruling Means: The $166 Billion Question

The practical consequences are historic in scale.

Total IEEPA duties collected from April 2025 through the ruling in February 2026 amounted to approximately $166 billion, drawn from over 330,000 importers across 53 million shipments. Every dollar of it was collected under an assertion of presidential authority that courts at every level found constitutionally illegitimate.

Companies from Costco to small family importers had begun filing legal claims in anticipation of the ruling, racing to establish their place in what has become the largest tariff refund proceeding in American history. Nearly 2,000 importers filed cases at the Court of International Trade challenging the IEEPA tariffs while the Supreme Court deliberated.

What happened next was, by the standards of this series, genuinely surprising.

In March 2026, Court of International Trade Judge Richard Eaton ordered the administration to refund all IEEPA tariffs collected — not just for the companies that had sued, but for every importer who paid. A universal refund order. The administration did not appeal.

Legal experts were stunned. The administration that contests everything, appeals everything, delays everything — accepted a universal order to return $166 billion without challenge. Federal litigator Matthew Seligman called it “genuinely surprising.” Trade attorneys who had spent months preparing for years of individual litigation found themselves, instead, preparing for a government-built refund portal.

On April 20, 2026, U.S. Customs and Border Protection launched the Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries — the CAPE portal — inside the existing ACE Secure Data Portal. Phase 1 covers approximately 82% of eligible duties, roughly $127 billion including statutory interest, for unliquidated entries and entries within 80 days of liquidation. Importers file a CSV spreadsheet listing their entry numbers. CBP recalculates duties owed without the IEEPA codes. Refunds are expected within 60 to 90 days of approval.

It is, by the standards of federal bureaucracy, efficient. It is also, by the standards of justice, incomplete. The refunds go to importers of record — the companies that paid the duties at the border. Consumers who absorbed higher prices on everything from groceries to electronics will see no direct refunds. Any consumer-level benefit will arrive as gradual pricing adjustments over 2026 and 2027, if it arrives at all. The remaining 18% of entries — those already liquidated or currently under protest — are excluded from Phase 1 and await future phases or direct litigation through the CIT.

As of late April, fewer than 10% of eligible importers had completed the ACH enrollment required to receive electronic refunds. Portal congestion has been reported. CBP has signaled that claims may be subject to review, documentation requests, and post-refund audits. The process works. It also works slowly, unevenly, and with the burden falling entirely on the businesses that were illegally taxed in the first place.

This fits the pattern — but with a twist. From the Trump Foundation to Trump University to the Trump Organization, the methodology has always been the same: collect, claim legitimacy, and force victims to spend years in court to recover what was taken. The scale here is simply larger by several orders of magnitude. The twist is that, this time, the court imposed a universal remedy and the administration accepted it. The money is coming back. Not all of it. Not quickly. Not painlessly. But it is coming back.

The Defiance Question: Answered in Real Time

Every entry in this series has documented a version of the same escape. The fraud settled. The indictment dismissed. The conviction discharged. The penalty eliminated.

The escape mechanism this time announced itself within hours of the ruling.

Trump held a press conference from the White House briefing room, flanked by Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick. He called the ruling “deeply disappointing.” He said he was “absolutely ashamed” of certain members of the Court. He called the majority justices “a disgrace to our nation,” “fools,” and “lap dogs.” He called them “very unpatriotic and disloyal to our Constitution.”

He singled out Barrett and Gorsuch — justices he himself appointed — by name. “I think it’s an embarrassment to their families, if you want to know the truth, the two of them,” he said. He said their decision was “terrible” and “almost like not written by smart people.” He accused the Court of being “swayed by foreign interests and a political movement that is far smaller than people would ever think” — but could not name a single foreign interest or identify the movement.

He praised the three dissenters — Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh — for their “strength and wisdom and love of our country,” framing the entire judicial process as a loyalty test he administers and the Court failed.

On Truth Social, he wrote: “What happened today with the two United States Supreme Court Justices that I appointed against great opposition, Neil Gorsuch and Amy Coney Barrett, whether people like it or not, never seems to happen with Democrats. They vote against the Republicans, and never against themselves, almost every single time, no matter how good a case we have.” He added: “At least I didn’t appoint Roberts.”

Asked whether the justices who ruled against him should attend the upcoming State of the Union address, Trump answered: “They are barely invited.”

These were not impulsive remarks. A governor present at a closed-door White House breakfast earlier that morning reported that an aide entered the room and handed Trump a note about the ruling. He commented on the outcome, called it “a disgrace,” and left. The press conference that followed was organized within hours, with Bessent and Lutnick pre-positioned. This was the official, considered response of the President of the United States to a ruling by the Supreme Court.

He then announced his pivot: a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, a provision no president had ever used, taking effect February 24. Section 301 investigations. Section 232 expansions. He said he didn’t need Congress — a statement made while standing in the White House briefing room, minutes after the Supreme Court told him he does, in an opinion explicitly requiring “clear congressional authorization.”

On refunds: “I guess it has to get litigated for the next two years.” Asked directly if he planned to honor refunds, he told reporters: “I just told you the answer, right?”

The next morning, Saturday, February 21, Trump posted on Truth Social that he would raise the Section 122 tariff from 10% to 15% — the statutory ceiling — effective immediately.

He never did. Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed the intent on March 4. But as of this writing, no formal proclamation raising the rate to 15% has been issued. The tariff remains at 10%. The overnight escalation announced to the world on Truth Social was, in the end, a bluff — or a negotiating posture — or simply a statement made and then abandoned, which is its own kind of governing pathology.

The Next Door: Section 122 and the 150-Day Clock

The Court said he claimed extraordinary power without statutory authorization. His response, within hours, was to find the next statute and use it.

Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 authorizes the president to impose a temporary import surcharge of up to 15% for a maximum of 150 days to address “fundamental international payments problems.” It was written during the collapse of the Bretton Woods fixed-exchange-rate system in the early 1970s, designed for a specific kind of currency crisis that no longer exists in the era of floating exchange rates. No president had ever invoked it.

Trump invoked it for trade deficits — a distinct economic concept from the balance-of-payments crises the statute was written to address. The proclamation imposed a uniform 10% tariff on imports from all countries, effective February 24, 2026, with exemptions mirroring many of those from the IEEPA regime: USMCA-qualifying goods from Canada and Mexico, goods already subject to Section 232 tariffs, critical minerals, pharmaceuticals, certain electronics.

The legal challenges arrived within two weeks.

On March 5, 2026, twenty-four state attorneys general, led by Oregon, New York, Arizona, California, and Vermont, filed suit in the Court of International Trade. Their argument: the president is “contorting” the meaning of “balance of payments,” that a trade deficit is not the kind of fundamental international payments problem Section 122 was designed to address, and that the tariff’s numerous exemptions and carve-outs violate the statute’s requirement that such measures be applied “consistently” and with “broad and uniform application.”

On March 9, a separate business coalition lawsuit was filed by Burlap & Barrel, a New York spice importer, and Basic Fun, a Florida toy company, backed by the Liberty Justice Center — the same organization that led the IEEPA challenge. Their argument parallels the states’: Section 122 was written for a monetary crisis tied to the gold standard transition, the balance-of-payments trigger no longer applies in an era of floating exchange rates, and the administration has conflated “trade deficits” with “balance-of-payments deficits” — two distinct economic concepts.

Oral arguments in both cases were held on April 10, 2026. A ruling is pending.

The Section 122 tariffs expire on July 24, 2026 — 150 days from their effective date. The president cannot extend them unilaterally. Congress has not passed extension legislation, and bipartisan appetite for doing so is uncertain at best. Some members have introduced the “Reclaim Trade Powers Act,” aimed at constraining presidential tariff authority rather than extending it.

The Door After That: Section 301 and the Permanent Replacement

Section 122 was always the bridge. The administration said so openly. Section 301 is the destination.

On March 11, 2026, the U.S. Trade Representative initiated Section 301 investigations into “structural excess capacity or production in certain manufacturing sectors” across sixteen economies: China, the European Union, Singapore, Switzerland, Norway, Indonesia, Malaysia, Cambodia, Thailand, Korea, Vietnam, Taiwan, Bangladesh, Mexico, Japan, and India.

The following day, USTR initiated a second set of Section 301 investigations into forced labor enforcement failures across sixty countries — collectively representing more than 99% of U.S. imports.

The compressed timeline is the tell. Written public comments were due by April 15. Public hearings began April 28. USTR Ambassador Greer has publicly signaled an “accelerated timeline” to complete findings before Section 122 expires on July 24.

Unlike Section 122, Section 301 has no rate cap. Unlike Section 122, Section 301 has no time limit. Unlike IEEPA, Section 301 specifically authorizes tariffs as a remedy. Unlike any of the improvised authorities Trump has reached for since February 20, Section 301 comes with the procedural safeguards — investigations, public comment, evidentiary standards — that the Supreme Court found missing from the IEEPA regime.

Which makes it, paradoxically, both the most legally defensible and the most strategically aggressive authority available to the administration. Legal analysts expect the resulting tariffs, if and when they arrive, to approach or exceed the country-specific rates of the invalidated IEEPA regime — potentially reaching 20-40% or higher for countries like Vietnam, Cambodia, and Bangladesh, which previously faced rates in the 35-46% range.

The Supreme Court struck down 80% of his tariffs. Within three weeks, he had a temporary replacement in effect and the machinery for a permanent one under construction.

This is the escape mechanism for Chapter 42: not dramatic defiance of the ruling itself, but procedural replication of its effects through different authorities, combined with delegitimization of the Court that issued it. He cannot ignore the ruling directly. So he complies with its letter while rebuilding its substance through new statutes, attacks the institution that delivered it, and pivots to untested authorities that will generate new legal challenges in new courts.

The man who never pays has found the exit again. This time it is labeled Section 301. It does not expire.

Why This Entry Exists in This Series

This project has documented, across 41 prior entries, a man who built his life on a single operating premise: that rules apply to other people.

He avoided Vietnam through a disputed bone spur diagnosis while others died. He bankrupted Atlantic City casinos while creditors absorbed the losses. He defrauded students at Trump University while regulators looked away. He ran a charity as a personal slush fund while donors believed otherwise. He obstructed a federal investigation ten documented times while his party protected him. He incited an insurrection and was acquitted by the Senate he had terrorized. He was convicted of 34 felonies and sentenced to nothing. He sued the government he controls for $10 billion while controlling it.

In each case, the accountability mechanism that should have fired — legal, electoral, institutional, social — either misfired or was deliberately disabled.

February 20, 2026 is different. Six Supreme Court justices, including three appointed by Republican presidents, told Donald Trump he claimed a power the Constitution does not grant him and the law does not authorize. They said it clearly, on the record, in an opinion that will stand as long as the Republic does.

That matters. It matters for the rule of law. It matters for the principle that even presidents must find their authority in actual text rather than convenient assertion. It matters that the institutional guardrails the series has documented failing, repeatedly and catastrophically, held on this occasion.

And what followed — the universal refund order, the CAPE portal, the $127 billion in Phase 1 refunds now processing — represents something this series has rarely had occasion to document: a mechanism of restitution that is actually functioning. Slowly, imperfectly, with the burden falling on the wrong people — but functioning.

But accountability delivered is not accountability completed. The man who never pays has survived thirty-eight prior chapters of this story. The Section 122 tariffs are still collecting duties at 10% while courts deliberate. The Section 301 investigations are accelerating toward a July deadline designed to ensure that when the temporary tariff expires, its permanent replacement is ready. The administration has already replaced the legal authority the Court struck down — not once but twice over, with a third in construction.

Whether he pays now — in power actually constrained, in behavior actually modified, in the tariff wall actually dismantled rather than rebuilt from different materials — will be determined not by what the Court said on February 20, 2026, but by what happens in the months that follow.

The Verdict

The violation: Systematic abuse of emergency powers to impose sweeping, unlimited tariffs without congressional authorization, collecting $166 billion in duties from American businesses and consumers under an assertion of presidential authority that courts at every level found constitutionally illegitimate.

The accountability delivered: 6-3 Supreme Court ruling striking down the legal foundation for 80% of Trump’s tariff regime. Universal refund order from the Court of International Trade, accepted without appeal. CAPE refund portal launched April 20, 2026, with $127 billion in Phase 1 refunds now processing. The most significant judicial check on executive power in a generation.

The accountability still pending: $39 billion in Phase 2 refunds not yet addressed. Section 122 replacement tariffs collecting at 10%, facing legal challenge with a ruling pending. Section 301 investigations targeting 16 economies for excess capacity and 60 for forced labor, designed to produce permanent replacement tariffs by July 24. Section 232 investigations into pharmaceuticals, copper, semiconductors, and lumber. A documented pattern of treating court orders as obstacles to be routed around rather than commands to be obeyed.

The series verdict: For the first time in sixty years of documented impunity, a co-equal branch of government said no and made it stick — in law and, to a degree unprecedented in this series, in practice. The refunds are processing. The illegal tariffs are gone. The institutional guardrail held.

But the man who never pays has already built the next wall. It’s made of different legal materials. It goes by different statutory names. And it is designed to produce the same result the Supreme Court told him he could not achieve through IEEPA: a trade barrier of unlimited scope, unlimited duration, and unlimited rate, imposed by presidential will.

The man who never pays lost in the Supreme Court of the United States.

Whether he pays — truly, finally, in the way this series has spent forty-two chapters waiting to document — is still another matter entirely.
