How DHS Killed Alex Pretti and Triggered a Constitutional Crisis

After killing Renee Good, DHS deployed 3,000 agents to Minneapolis in “the largest immigration operation ever.” Then they killed again—and Democrats said: not one more dollar.

January 26, 2026

Video still: Alex Jeffrey Pretti (center, holding phone) 25 seconds before Border Patrol agents killed him, January 24, 2026, Minneapolis.

Seventeen days after killing Renee Nicole Good, they did it again.

Saturday morning, January 24, 2026, at 9:05 a.m., in 10-degree-below-zero cold, Border Patrol agents shot and killed Alex Jeffrey Pretti on a Minneapolis sidewalk. He was 37 years old—the same age as Renee Good. He was an American citizen—like Renee Good. He was a Minneapolis resident—like Renee Good.

And like Renee Good, federal agents killed him and immediately lied about what happened.

Alex was an ICU nurse at the Minneapolis Veterans Affairs hospital. He cared for America’s veterans in their most vulnerable moments. He had no criminal record. He was a lawful gun owner with a permit to carry. His colleagues described him as “sweet, kind, nonviolent”—someone whose “default look on his face was a smile.”

He was working extra shifts to save for a home and a new car. “He was happy, and I was happy for him because his life was just starting,” said Dr. Aasma Shaukat, who had hired him to his first job at the VA hospital.

On Saturday morning, Alex Jeffrey Pretti approached Border Patrol agents with his phone to record their actions—exercising his First Amendment right to observe law enforcement.

Seven agents wrestled him to the ground. They pepper-sprayed him while he was disoriented. They struck him with an object. They fired ten shots in five seconds while he lay motionless on the sidewalk.

Then the Department of Homeland Security lied.

DHS claimed Alex “approached US Border Patrol officers with a 9 mm semi-automatic handgun.” They claimed he “violently resisted” attempts to disarm him. They claimed he “wanted to do maximum damage and massacre law enforcement.”

Video shows Alex approached with his phone out, appearing to record the agents’ actions.

Not a gun. A phone.

This is the story of how DHS killed two American citizens in seventeen days, used the same lies about both, and triggered a constitutional crisis that may shut down the federal government.

“The Largest Immigration Operation Ever”

Between Renee Good’s killing on January 7 and Alex Pretti’s killing on January 24, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem deployed what the department itself called “the largest immigration operation ever taking place right now.”

The numbers were staggering:

Personnel deployed to Twin Cities:

  • 3,000 federal agents (per DHS statements)

  • ICE tactical units

  • Border Patrol special operations

  • Federal protective services

  • Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons personally supervising

  • Border Patrol Chief Mike Banks deployed to Minnesota

  • Secretary Noem making multiple operational visits

Operational tempo:

  • 1,000+ arrests by mid-January (per DHS claims)

  • 3,000+ arrests by January 19 during “Operation Metro Surge”

  • 150+ arrests in Minneapolis in a single day

  • Daily raids across Twin Cities metro area

Technology deployed:

  • Surveillance drones over neighborhoods

  • Facial recognition at checkpoints

  • License plate readers

  • Phone data extraction equipment

  • Armored vehicles

  • Tactical gear and weapons

On January 12, 2026, DHS announced a new “Program Executive Office for Unmanned Aircraft Systems and Counter-Unmanned Aircraft Systems”—a $115 million investment in drone technology. Secretary Noem said the new office would “defend our air superiority to protect our borders and the interior of the United States.”

The interior of the United States. Drones designed for border security, now explicitly deployed against American citizens in American cities.

This wasn’t immigration enforcement. This was military occupation of a state whose governor had criticized the president.

Noem’s Personal Involvement

Secretary Noem didn’t just direct the operation from Washington. She participated personally, bringing camera crews to document her involvement in arrests.

On January 6, 2026—one day before Jonathan Ross killed Renee Good—Noem rode with ICE agents to arrest Tomas Espin Tapia, a man from Ecuador wanted on murder and sexual assault warrants. DHS cameras filmed the entire operation.

As Tapia’s wife and child stood nearby, an agent translated while Noem addressed him: “You will be held accountable for your crimes. We’re taking you away for processing.”

The footage was posted to DHS social media. Noem appeared on Fox News to tout the arrest. “Another murderer and sexual predator off of Minneapolis’s streets thanks to ICE,” she said.

The next day, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot Renee Good three times. Within hours, Noem called Good a “domestic terrorist.”

Noem returned to Minneapolis multiple times throughout January, participating in operations, holding press conferences, defending the agents who killed Good, and announcing plans to send hundreds more federal agents to Minnesota.

“We will not let rioters slow us down from making Minnesota safe again—something Governor Walz and Mayor Frey REFUSED to do,” DHS spokesperson Tricia McLaughlin told Fox News.

By January 24, when Border Patrol agents killed Alex Pretti, the operation had become what DHS proudly called “the largest immigration enforcement operation in U.S. history.”

And it had killed two American citizens in seventeen days.

Who Alex Pretti Was

Alex Jeffrey Pretti graduated from high school in Green Bay, Wisconsin, in 2006, making the honor roll. He started working at the Minneapolis VA hospital in his early 20s as a research assistant—delivering pizzas at night to make ends meet while pursuing his dream of helping people.

Dr. Aasma Shaukat, who hired him to his first job in the hospital’s research department, remembered: “He was your typical struggling young person with a lot of ambition, but no direction yet. But he knew he wanted to help people in some way or another.”

Alex spent years working on medical studies and enrolling patients. Ruth Anway, a nurse colleague who encouraged him to pursue nursing, said: “He really thrived in that environment. He wanted to be helpful, to help humanity and have a career that was a force of good in the world.”

By Saturday morning, Alex was an ICU nurse—the job he’d worked so hard to achieve. Dr. Dimitri Drekonja worked with him for years: “He was capable, competent and friendly, the kind of person who cared deeply about his work and his patients. He was a really great colleague and a really great friend. The default look on his face was a smile.”

In his free time, Alex loved mountain biking on trails around Minneapolis. He lived in an apartment with his dog, Joule. His next-door neighbor, Jeanne Wiener, talked with him over the fence several times a week: “He’s the sweetest, kindest, most unoffensive, most nonviolent person you’d ever want to meet.”

Alex followed the news closely and cared deeply about social justice and fighting for fairness. Ruth Anway said: “I’m not surprised he was out there protesting and observing.”

Two weeks before his death, Alex’s father—Michael Pretti, now living in Colorado—warned him to be careful in Minneapolis. “Go ahead and protest, but do not engage, do not do anything stupid, basically,” Michael told him. Alex assured his father he understood.

He knew to be careful.

On Saturday morning, Alex Jeffrey Pretti was observing federal agents conducting an operation in his neighborhood. He approached them with his phone to record their actions—exactly what citizens are supposed to do to hold law enforcement accountable.

Seven Border Patrol agents killed him for it.

Alex Jeffrey Pretti, who spent his career caring for American veterans, was killed by federal agents—many of them veterans themselves.

The Immediate Aftermath: “Boo Hoo”

Hundreds of protesters gathered at the scene within an hour. Federal agents blocked off the intersection with barricades. Protesters chanted “ICE out now” and “They’re killing my neighbors!”

Minneapolis resident Josh Koskie, watching federal agents control the scene of another killing, said simply: “They’re killing my neighbors!”

When protesters shouted at federal agents, calling them “cowards” and telling them to go home, one officer responded mockingly as he walked away: “Boo hoo.”

That’s the response from a federal agent after killing an American citizen: Mockery of grieving neighbors.

Federal agents then deployed:

  • Tear gas

  • Pepper spray

  • Flash bangs

  • Stun grenades

  • Batons

Protesters dragged dumpsters into streets to create barricades. Federal agents shoved protesters, bloodying at least one who was then handcuffed and arrested. Two protesters were arrested for attempting to cross police tape.

All of this violated Judge Kate M. Menendez’s Friday injunction explicitly ordering federal agents not to use chemical irritants against peaceful protesters or retaliate against those exercising First Amendment rights.

DHS called the protesters “rioters” and claimed 200 people “began to obstruct and assault law enforcement” necessitating “crowd control measures for the safety of the public.”

The video shows peaceful protesters horrified by another killing. It shows federal agents deploying chemical weapons against citizens. It shows the government treating Minneapolis residents as enemy combatants.

Again.

Minneapolis Police Shut Out—Again

Minneapolis Police Chief Brian O’Hara revealed a crucial detail: Federal agents killed someone in Minneapolis and refuse to tell the city’s police chief what happened.

O’Hara also revealed that federal officials tried to dismiss Minneapolis police from the scene entirely. He refused. The standoff required intervention from Hennepin County Attorney Mary Moriarty, who demanded the scene be secured by local law enforcement for evidence collection.

Hours after the shooting, three armored FBI vehicles appeared at the scene. O’Hara told CNN that federal officials were blocking local police from accessing the scene.

This is the same pattern as Renee Good: Kill American citizen, control the scene, lock out local investigators, conduct self-investigation, declare shooting justified.

Secretary Noem announced on Sunday that DHS would lead the investigation into Alex Pretti’s killing. The same agency that called him a would-be cop killer within hours of his death will investigate whether the killing was justified.

The Minnesota Bureau of Criminal Apprehension is also investigating—but only after Governor Walz demanded it. And only if federal agents don’t obstruct them the way they obstructed investigations into Renee Good’s killing.

The Political Crisis Erupts

By Sunday, January 25, the political fallout reached crisis levels.

New York Governor Kathy Hochul called for Noem’s resignation or removal:

“Kristi Noem has forfeited her right to lead, and I’m calling on her to resign as Secretary of Homeland Security, or Donald Trump to do the right thing and just fire her. And if not, she must be removed or impeached. She has shown a profound disregard for human life.”

Hochul also called for the firing of Border Patrol Commander Gregory Bovino, who led operations in Minneapolis.

Representative Adriano Espaillat called for ICE to be dismantled:

“This is just an agency that has become a violent and deadly weapon. ICE should be dismantled. And border patrol, which is also involved in violent behavior, should also be looked at and potentially dismantled.”

44 Democratic members of Congress sent a letter to Noem on January 9 demanding answers about Renee Good’s killing. The letter called DHS’s immediate characterization of Good as a terrorist “outrageous” and demanded suspension of operations in Minneapolis.

DHS ignored the letter. The operations continued. The arrests continued. The lies continued.

Seventeen days later, they killed Alex Pretti.

As I write this on January 25, 2026, the federal government faces partial shutdown by Friday, January 31. The cause: Senate Democrats refuse to vote for a $1.2 trillion funding package if it includes appropriations for DHS.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and dozens of Democratic senators have drawn a line: Not one more dollar for an agency that kills American citizens.

Senator Patty Murray (D-WA), ranking member of the Senate Appropriations Committee:

“Federal agents cannot murder people in broad daylight and face zero consequences.”

Senator Amy Klobuchar (D-MN), appearing on “Meet the Press”:

“No, I am not voting for this funding. Our Republican colleagues have to stand up and stop this.”

Senate Republicans, however, refuse to remove DHS funding from the package.

“Government funding expires at the end of the week, and Republicans are determined to not have another government shutdown,” a person familiar with Republican leadership told CNBC. “We will move forward as planned and hope Democrats can find a path forward to join us.”

The standoff is simple: Democrats watched DHS kill two American citizens in seventeen days, lie about both killings, call both victims terrorists, and face zero accountability. They watched Governor Hochul demand Noem’s resignation. They watched protests spread to Los Angeles, Portland, and New York. They watched the NBA postpone a game in Minneapolis due to safety concerns.

And they said: Not one more dollar until this stops.

The House already passed the funding package Thursday. Without Senate approval by Friday, the federal government begins a partial shutdown.

The question is no longer whether DHS can be reformed. The question is whether DHS can be funded when it operates as a paramilitary force killing American citizens.

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, April 2025.

Noem regularly poses with firearms. Where is her ID?

Noem’s defense reduced to: A legal gun owner exercising his Second Amendment rights deserved to die because he didn’t have his physical ID card on him when federal agents shot him ten times.

This is the Secretary of Homeland Security justifying the killing of an American citizen.

The Constitutional Question

On Friday, January 31, 2026, the federal government may partially shut down because Democrats refuse to fund an agency that murders American citizens.

Republicans will call this partisan obstruction. They’ll claim Democrats are playing politics with government funding. They’ll argue that DHS serves essential functions that shouldn’t be held hostage to disagreements over specific operations.

But this isn’t partisan obstruction. This is the Constitution working exactly as designed.

DHS killed Renee Nicole Good, a 37-year-old American mother, and called her a terrorist.

DHS killed Alex Jeffrey Pretti, a 37-year-old VA nurse who cared for veterans, and claimed he wanted to “massacre law enforcement.”

Both were American citizens. Both were killed by federal agents. Both were lied about immediately. Both video contradicted the official narrative. Neither agent faced consequences.

Seventeen days apart.

When an agency kills American citizens, lies about it, and faces no accountability, the question isn’t whether Congress should fund it. The question is whether Congress can constitutionally fund it.

Article I, Section 9 of the Constitution: “No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law.”

The Appropriations Clause gives Congress the power—and the responsibility—to decide what gets funded. It’s not just a power. It’s a check on executive overreach. It’s how Congress prevents the executive branch from operating agencies that violate the Constitution.

When DHS kills American citizens exercising their First Amendment rights and faces no consequences, Congress has a constitutional duty to withhold funding until accountability is restored.

That’s not obstruction. That’s oversight.

That’s not partisan politics. That’s the separation of powers.

That’s not a constitutional crisis. That’s the Constitution finally working.
