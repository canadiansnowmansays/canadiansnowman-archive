The Iran Crisis

Nowruz Under Fire

Part LII: Day Twenty-Three The Ancient New Year Arrives Under Bombardment, the Marines Approach, and America Has No Plan B

March 20, 2026

The symbolic centerpiece of Nowruz, with seven items (starting with ‘S’ in Persian) representing renewal, health, prosperity, and spring. This evokes the holiday’s deep roots predating the current regime and conflict.

I. The New Year

Nowruz falls on the first day of spring. It has been celebrated in Iran since at least the fifth century BC — predating Islam, predating the Islamic Republic, predating every government that has ever tried to suppress or co-opt it. Iranians set out tables adorned with seven symbolic items representing renewal, health, and prosperity. They buy new clothes, spring-clean their homes, visit family. In significance and mood, it resembles a combination of Christmas, New Year’s Eve, and Independence Day.

This year it falls on Day Twenty-Three of the most intensive bombing campaign in the country’s history.

In Tehran’s Tajrish Bazaar on Thursday, the stalls were open but the crowds were thin. A vendor told a local newspaper his business was seeing 20 percent of its usual Nowruz volume. Armed men patrolled the streets and manned checkpoints, frightening people into staying home. Many Iranian businesses have had no revenue in what should be the busiest shopping period of the year. “Today Tajrish was alive and tired, full of Nowruz and full of fear, full of hope and sorrow,” wrote Golshan Fathi, an activist in Tehran, on Instagram. “How strange to want the new year to begin and to fear it too.”

On the last Tuesday of the old year, Iranians traditionally jump over small fires — a ritual of purification, of shedding the year’s misfortunes. Iranian police warned the public this year that “the enemy” could infiltrate the celebration through “spies and agents.” In the Chitgar neighborhood of Tehran, a group gathered anyway. Law enforcement arrived and broke it up.

The pre-Nowruz ritual of leaping over flames for purification and shedding misfortune. In the story, police disrupted gatherings, yet the tradition persists as an act of resilience and subtle protest

Eid al-Fitr also falls on Day Twenty-Three — the end of Ramadan, coinciding with Nowruz and the war’s fourth week. Two of the region’s most significant holidays arriving simultaneously under bombardment. In Beirut, a Sudanese mother named Sumeya Babaker marked Eid sheltering at St. Joseph Church in east Beirut, which has opened its doors to 190 displaced migrants from Sudan, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, and Sierra Leone. She fled her village of Kafra on a borrowed motorcycle with her three children, left behind everything her husband had built on daily farm wages, and forgot her diabetes medication in the rush. “Now we have no work, no money and that means the children cannot have any fun today,” she said. They were safe. For now, that was enough. “We are at zero,” said Indrani Manike, a Sri Lankan Christian married to a Sudanese Muslim, sheltering in the same church. Two holidays. One church. Every faith. No home.

Outside Iran, Nowruz celebrations in Iraqi Kurdistan were canceled. In New York, the Iranian-American Society canceled its annual gala. At a Brooklyn gathering, an organizer named Hedi Yousefi had typed out one by one the names of 3,000 people killed in Iran this year and displayed them on a wall. At Technowruz in Queens, the annual electronic party was reimagined as a community gathering for collective grief. People sat on the floor under a red glow and listened to ambient music.

“Nowruz isn’t something that we would put aside,” said Maryam, an activist in Tehran who communicated through an intermediary. “Iranians have learned to endure during these years. One of the ways to be resilient is collective participation in collective rituals.”

“Celebration is also a part of protesting,” said Shahin Mirshekari, founder of a New York group called Khooneh — which means home. “It shows that you cannot beat us. We are still here. We are still living.”

The Islamic Republic has spent decades trying to suppress Nowruz — a holiday whose emphasis on joy and renewal sits uneasily with a government whose ethos is martyrdom and mourning. It has never succeeded. The holiday that predates the regime will likely outlast it. Whether that matters to the 90 million Iranians spending this Nowruz in bomb shelters is a different question.

II. No Plan B

Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic, has produced the most direct strategic autopsy of the war yet written — and it arrives, with devastating timing, on Day Twenty-Three.

The core argument: Trump had a Plan A. Hit Iran hard, watch the theocrats flee, hand power to a government of his choosing. Plan B did not exist.

The intelligence community reportedly issued an assessment that the Iranian regime “will remain intact and possibly even emboldened, believing it stood up to Trump and survived.” According to the Wall Street Journal, when General Dan Caine warned Trump before the war that an attack would prompt Iran to close the Strait of Hormuz, Trump “told his team that Tehran would likely capitulate before closing the strait — and even if Iran tried, the U.S. military could handle it.” He was wrong on both counts.

Nichols draws the parallel to Putin’s invasion of Ukraine with precision: both leaders engaged in what analysts call “scriptwriting” — deciding what they wanted to happen and writing a script in which adversaries would dutifully play their part. Putin was certain Ukraine would collapse in days. Trump was certain Iran would collapse in hours. Neither planned for what their adversary could actually do, as opposed to what they preferred it to do. “Trump now appears lost,” Nichols writes, “unable to comprehend how a blockbuster movie that he scripted out, one in which he cast himself as the Liberator of Iran, has turned into a poorly received miniseries that might yet be renewed for another dreary season.”

The analogy is not exact, Nichols notes — and the distinction matters. Putin wages a war of conquest. Trump, however ineptly, acted against a regime that is a genuine malignancy. Had Plan A succeeded, Trump would deserve some credit. It did not succeed. And without a Plan B, the war has entered its fourth week with the United States being driven by events rather than controlling them. “Like a gambler chasing his losses,” Nichols writes, Trump “keeps investing new money to stay at the table.”

Pegah Banihashemi and Paul Poast, also writing in The Atlantic, explain precisely why Plan A was always unlikely to work — and why the endgame is not what either Washington or Tehran has publicly described.

The Islamic Republic, they argue, is not simply a government that can be removed. It is a deeply embedded system of political, military, and economic power built over decades of crisis. The IRGC — whose commanders now occupy key positions across the state apparatus and whose institutional reach extends across energy, infrastructure, and construction — was forged and strengthened by the eight-year war with Iraq that began in 1980. External threats have historically consolidated rather than fractured the regime’s internal cohesion. Mojtaba Khamenei’s succession as supreme leader, they note, suggests continuity rather than disruption — he has long served as a key intermediary between the clerical leadership and the IRGC.

The Iranian regime’s winning condition is simple: survive. As long as it believes it retains the capacity to resist, it will not view the current conflict as a decisive defeat. What comes next, Banihashemi and Poast suggest, may not be victory or defeat but transformation — overt fighting giving way to a subterranean conflict of deniable attacks, covert retaliation, pressure through regional proxies, and shadow operations designed to impose costs without triggering a new round of full-scale war. The battlefield would not disappear. It would simply move underground.

The Marines are now days from the Gulf. The $200 billion request is on its way to Congress. The options under active consideration — Kharg Island, Isfahan — are improvisations, not strategy. The war the United States cannot finish, Iran cannot lose merely by continuing to exist.

III. One War, Two Strategies

Steven Erlanger, writing in the New York Times, has provided the structural explanation for the South Pars fracture this series documented in Part LI.

The United States and Israel are fighting the same war with different wars in mind. As a global superpower, the United States cares about global energy markets, the Strait of Hormuz, Gulf ally stability, munitions expenditure, naval deployment duration, Ukraine readiness, and Asia-Pacific posture. As a regional power, Israel has its own natural gas, no Hormuz dependency, no responsibility for global trade, and an existential stake in Iran’s permanent military degradation that Washington simply does not share.

“We are a global power and they are a regional one,” said Aaron David Miller of the Carnegie Endowment. “So their threat assessments create a different set of objectives than ours.”

Israel wants state collapse and is willing to weather the economic storm to get it. The United States wants a manageable outcome and is watching its gas prices climb 31 percent. Israel struck South Pars because it was a logical move toward collapsing Iranian revenue. Trump said “I told him don’t do that” — because every Iranian energy strike drives Brent higher and the S&P lower, metrics that reach the Oval Office faster than any battlefield report.

At some point, Erlanger’s sources say, Netanyahu’s drive to create “some different Iranian reality” will collide with Trump’s need to stop. “And when Trump needs to say ‘stop,’” said Miller, “Netanyahu will reluctantly do so.” Netanyahu must face elections by October. He needs the American president and the American military on his side. That dependency is Trump’s only real leverage — and he has now used it publicly, on South Pars, with the world watching.

The deeper divergence: Trump has spoken of a Venezuela-style solution, with the regime intact but controlled. Israel wants a different leadership in Tehran entirely. They started with what appeared to be common goals. Twenty-three days in, the gap between those goals is now the central strategic fact of the war.

IV. The Jet Fuel Canary

Three weeks into the war, Asia has produced the first clear preview of what a sustained oil shock looks like at the human level — and it arrives not in financial data but in a Thai shrimp boat that cannot afford to leave the dock.

Wittaya Lekdee has worked at sea for more than 30 years. His boat, the Lucky Blessings, has been tied up all month in Samut Sakhon. Boat fuel is up 75 percent. He took a Times reporter out on a liter of diesel carried in a plastic water bottle — the government has capped his purchases at roughly $15, less than a fifth of his daily operating cost. There was no catch. He turned around and went home.

Jet fuel tells the macro story. It has hit $200 a barrel — more than double the pre-war price, a record that has never previously been seen. Jet fuel runs short first because it is held to stricter quality standards than other fuels, must be stored in specialized tanks, and cannot sit for long without degrading. Air New Zealand has canceled approximately 1,100 flights through early May. China restricted refined fuel exports within days of the war starting. Thailand and South Korea have followed. Vietnam, which imports nearly three-quarters of its aviation fuel, faces potential shortages by April. In Laos, more than 40 percent of gas stations have closed. In Cambodia, nearly a third. The Philippines has moved to four-day workweeks. Myanmar has imposed alternate-day driving mandates. Several Thai temples say they are struggling to cremate bodies because of diesel shortages.

“Jet is kind of a canary in the coal mine,” said James Noel-Beswick of Sparta Commodities. “We’re peering into what our gasoline and diesel future is going to look like if this doesn’t get resolved.”

Indonesia and Vietnam face potential sovereign rating downgrades. Thailand’s state fuel fund is back in deficit. “I’m not happy and am running out of ideas,” Thailand’s deputy prime minister told reporters Wednesday. Singapore, which got 47 percent of its LNG from Qatar last year, has warned of higher electricity prices. Its president told citizens to “brace ourselves for a long storm of global political instability.”

The war that Trump says will be over soon has already closed 40 percent of Laos’s gas stations. The people waiting in line with jerrycans outside Thai gas stations at midnight did not vote for this war, were not consulted about it, and have no mechanism to end it.

V. The Hormuz Offensive

The military picture sharpened significantly on Friday. American commanders confirmed they have escalated operations inside the strait itself — the most specific operational picture this war has produced.

The “Warthog” aircraft now repurposed for hunting IRGC fast-attack boats in the Strait of Hormuz — a key escalation in the Hormuz offensive, with Marines approaching.

A-10 Warthog attack aircraft, designed and built for close air support of ground troops, have been repurposed for naval combat. General Dan Caine confirmed they are now “hunting and killing fast-attack watercraft” operated by the IRGC in the contested sea lanes. Apache helicopter gunships — operated by unnamed regional allies — are hitting one-way attack drones. Earlier this week, fighter-bombers dropped multiple 5,000-pound GBU-72/B bunker-busting bombs on underground missile silos near the strait, targeting cruise-missile storage sites along the Iranian coastline.

The 2,200 Marines on three warships — a more precise figure than earlier estimates — have cut short their Indo-Pacific patrol and are steaming toward the Gulf. Their likely missions: helping clear the strait, or seizing Kharg Island, Iran’s primary oil loading hub in the northern Gulf. Either operation would put American forces in direct contact with Iranian territory.

Iran’s response to the escalation was characteristically asymmetric and deliberately threatening on a personal level. A statement from Iran’s armed forces published by the Fars news agency Friday said that Iran had information on US and Israeli military personnel and officials, and warned that “tourist attractions, resorts, and entertainment centers around the world will not be safe for you.” The threat is directed not at infrastructure or military assets — but at individuals, wherever they travel.

Iran’s foreign minister simultaneously told his British counterpart that the UK’s provision of military bases constitutes “participation in aggression” and demanded Britain stop cooperating with the United States and Israel in both military and media fields. Britain, which has sent a small team of military planners to US Central Command in Tampa to help develop Hormuz options, is walking a line that Iran has now formally declared it will no longer accept.

NATO, meanwhile, received its most direct public rebuke yet. Trump called the alliance a “paper tiger” on social media Friday, labeled its members “COWARDS,” and insisted joining the Hormuz effort would involve “so little risk.” NATO’s mutual defense pact has been invoked exactly once in its history — after September 11, 2001. It does not require member nations to join pre-emptive strikes launched by other members. Trump appears either unaware of this or indifferent to it.

VI. The Paper Tiger and the Cowards

The GOP oversight story is, in its own way, as significant as anything happening in the strait.

Nearly three weeks into an unauthorized war with a $200 billion price tag, no exit strategy, and shifting justifications, Republican lawmakers are actively resisting public congressional hearings. Their reasoning, stated openly, is that hearings would reveal divisions — and Iran might notice.

“You don’t want to show that kind of division to your enemy when you’re in the midst of a war,” said Senator Ron Johnson of Wisconsin. Senator Thune suggested that press conferences constitute sufficient oversight. Speaker Johnson told reporters he had relayed “the summary of the nonclassified information” — and that members were “out talking about it around the clock.”

Meanwhile Hegseth has restricted Pentagon press access and replaced national media organizations that declined to accept his reporting restrictions with pro-Trump outlets. Secretary of State Rubio has not held a press conference at the State Department since December. No public hearings are planned before Congress returns from a two-week recess in mid-April.

Democrats have noted that Colin Powell made the case for Iraq to Congress under oath before the war began — and Iraq had congressional authorization. This war has neither. “Unprecedented in American history to launch a war of this scale without open hearings,” said Senator Christopher Murphy of Connecticut. Representative DeLauro: “We cannot get any accounting. No specificity for what we will need going forward.” Senator Murkowski, one of the few Republicans pressing for hearings: “As a sitting United States senator, I think I deserve to have honest answers.”

The constitutional crisis David Frum described in Part L — a war with no legal basis, no congressional authorization, and no oversight mechanism — has now acquired its clearest institutional form. The branch of government constitutionally responsible for declaring war has decided that watching press conferences is close enough.

VII. NATO Exits Iraq

On Friday, NATO withdrew its last military trainers from Iraq, relocating to Naples. The departure came 23 years to the day after the American-led coalition invaded Iraq in 2003 to topple Saddam Hussein — triggering a civil war, the rise of ISIS, and a regional instability from which the Middle East has never fully recovered.

The NATO mission in Iraq was created in 2018 to train and advise Iraqi security forces. Its departure was driven by the same force driving every other allied calculation right now: the war with Iran has made the region too dangerous for noncombat personnel, and Iranian-backed militias have been targeting American diplomatic posts in Iraq as recently as last week.

About 500 US troops remain in Iraq, down from a peak of 165,000 in 2008. The alliance’s training mission will continue — from Naples.

The symmetry is not subtle. The war that began in Iraq in 2003, which Nichols and others have invoked as the template for strategic overconfidence and absent post-war planning, has now claimed its latest casualty: the multinational training mission that was itself a consequence of that original failure. The circle closes on the day Nowruz begins.

VIII. The Executions

On Nowruz eve, Iran hanged three men convicted of killing police officers during the January protests — including Saleh Mohammadi, a decorated 19-year-old wrestler from Qom. He was arrested on January 15. He was sentenced to death by early February. He was executed Thursday. Amnesty International reported he told the court he had confessed under torture. Human rights groups cited a fast-tracked trial and forced confession.

Iran executed more than 2,000 people in 2025 — the most since 1989. State forces killed over 6,700 people, including more than 200 children, during the January protests, according to the Human Rights Activists News Agency. The regime that cannot stop Israeli strikes against its leadership is still fully operational as a killing machine against its own citizens.

The Abdorrahman Boroumand Center for Human Rights said of Mohammadi’s execution: “It amounts to cruel, inhuman and degrading punishment designed to instill terror.”

It was Nowruz eve. The ancient festival of renewal. The fires were supposed to be lit for purification — the old year’s sorrows burned away. Instead the police broke up the fire-jumping in Chitgar. And in Qom, a 19-year-old wrestler was hanged.

IX. The War Expands

Israel struck Syrian government infrastructure in the country’s south on Friday — citing attacks on Druze civilians in Sweida province. Syria has stayed on the sidelines of the war since February 28. The strikes raised immediate fears of further expansion. Israeli operations in Syria had been on a downward trend. That trend reversed Friday.

The war that began as a US-Israeli operation against Iran’s nuclear and missile programs now has active fronts in Iran, Lebanon, Iraq, the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, the Caspian Sea, the West Bank, Jerusalem, and — as of Day Twenty-Three — southern Syria. The A-10s are hunting boats in the strait. The Marines are steaming in. NATO has left Iraq. The IRGC is threatening vacation resorts.

In Tehran, families marked Nowruz as best they could. Under bombardment. Under internet blackout. Under a government run by a committee whose wounded supreme leader has not appeared in public in twenty-three days. Yousef Pezeshkian, the president’s son, wrote in his diary that he visited his grandmother, who was entirely unaware her country was at war. He broke down and cried.

The new year has arrived. The war arrived first.
