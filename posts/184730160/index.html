Who’s Whispering “Take Greenland” in Trump’s Ear?

Following the Pattern Back to Stephen Miller

January 15, 2026

January 15, 2026

After days of breathless reporting about Trump’s Greenland obsession, the question that matters most has been largely ignored: Who put this idea in his head?

Trump doesn’t spontaneously develop coherent foreign policy positions. He responds to whisperers—advisers who understand his psychology and know how to frame ideas in terms of dominance, strength, and winning. So when Trump suddenly starts talking about acquiring Greenland “by any means necessary,” complete with threats to use military force against a NATO ally, someone planted that seed.

The evidence points overwhelmingly to one person: Stephen Miller.

The Katie Miller Tell

The clearest evidence came before Trump even started his public Greenland push. On January 3, 2026, Katie Miller—Stephen Miller’s wife and a significant political figure in her own right—posted on social media an image showing an American flag superimposed on a map of Greenland, with one word: SOON.

This wasn’t a random meme. Katie Miller doesn’t preview administration foreign policy on social media unless her husband has already sold the president on it. She was signaling what was coming—because Stephen had already whispered it into Trump’s ear and gotten the green light.

Within days, Trump began publicly discussing Greenland acquisition. On January 5th, Stephen Miller went on CNN to explicitly declare that the U.S. military could seize Greenland without a fight.

That’s not coincidence. That’s coordination. Katie’s meme was the tell that Stephen was driving this policy.

The “Iron Laws” Framework

Miller’s January 5, 2026 CNN appearance with Jake Tapper revealed the philosophical framework driving the Greenland push. Discussing Venezuela and Greenland in the same breath, Miller articulated his worldview:

“We live in a world, in the real world, that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power.”

This is Miller’s “iron laws” philosophy—the Hobbesian might-makes-right worldview I examined in Part 11 of my fascism series. In Miller’s framework, international law, treaties, alliances, and “international niceties” are irrelevant. What matters is whether you have the power to take something and the will to use that power.

Greenland fits this perfectly. It’s valuable (strategic Arctic position, rare earth minerals, military bases). Denmark is weaker than the United States. Therefore, in Miller’s logic, we should take it. The fact that Denmark is a NATO ally, that Greenland’s people have their own wishes, that this would shatter the post-WWII international order—none of that matters in a world governed only by strength.

This is the same philosophy that drove Miller’s Venezuela policy, where Trump has already claimed 30 to 50 million barrels of oil. It’s pure domination: we can, therefore we will.

Miller’s Expanded “Prime Minister” Role

A recent Atlantic profile documented that Miller is no longer just Trump’s immigration czar. He’s become what Steve Bannon calls Trump’s “prime minister”—with influence over “nearly every area about which he cares deeply: immigration and border security, yes, but also national security, foreign policy, trade, military action, and policing.”

The Atlantic revealed that during “Signalgate”—when the Trump administration accidentally included the magazine’s editor in chief on a private Signal chat about bombing Yemen—it was Miller, not the national security adviser, Pentagon chief, or vice president, who ended debate and directed the strikes forward.

Trump himself has described Miller as sitting “at the top of the totem pole” inside the White House. When Trump complains about something—homeless encampments, beef prices, Greenland—he turns to Miller and says “Get it done.”

Miller has successfully expanded his purview from immigration to everything. Greenland is just the latest target.

The Pattern: From Immigration to Empire

Understanding Miller’s trajectory reveals how Greenland became a target. His worldview started with immigration—defining America in ethnic terms, treating migrants as invaders, demanding military responses to humanitarian situations. But the underlying philosophy was always bigger.

If you believe power is the only reality, then every interaction becomes a question of domination. Immigrants aren’t people seeking better lives—they’re threats to be crushed. Venezuela isn’t a sovereign nation—it’s an oil reserve to be seized. Greenland isn’t a Danish territory with its own population—it’s strategic real estate to be taken.

Miller told CNN: “We are a superpower, and under President Trump, we are going to conduct ourselves as a superpower.” This is the logical expansion of Hobbesian philosophy. Once you reject moral constraints on power, once you dismiss international law as weakness, there’s no limiting principle.

The September 2025 murder of Charlie Kirk—close to Miller and others in the administration—appears to have accelerated this maximalist push. The Atlantic notes it “plunged Trump’s already single-minded martinet into a maximalist frenzy.” Kirk’s death seems to have removed whatever remaining restraints Miller felt, pushing him toward ever more aggressive assertions of raw power.

How Miller Whispers

Miller has mastered the art of translating his philosophical worldview into language Trump understands. He doesn’t lecture Trump about Hobbesian political theory or the decline of the liberal international order. He frames issues in terms Trump responds to instinctively: strength vs. weakness, winning vs. losing, domination vs. submission.

For Greenland, the whisper probably went something like this:

“Mr. President, Greenland is incredibly valuable—rare earth minerals, strategic position for controlling Arctic shipping routes, military bases to counter China and Russia. Denmark is weak. They can’t defend it. If we don’t take it, China will get access. You’d go down in history as the president who expanded American territory for the first time in generations. Teddy Roosevelt would be jealous.”

That’s how you sell Greenland to Trump. Not through careful geopolitical analysis, but through appeals to dominance, legacy, and the chance to do something “historic.” Miller understands Trump’s psychology better than almost anyone, and he’s ruthlessly effective at channeling the president’s impulses into policy.

Other Possible Whisperers

Miller is almost certainly the primary voice, but he likely has support:

Steve Bannon: The nationalist ideologue has always dreamed of an America that throws its weight around without apology. Territorial expansion fits his vision of civilizational conflict and American greatness.

Marco Rubio: As Secretary of State, Rubio would need to be involved in any Greenland strategy. He’s proven himself adept at framing aggressive policies in terms of countering China—a rationale that would work for Arctic control.

Pentagon hawks: Some military strategists genuinely believe controlling Greenland has strategic value in great power competition with Russia and China. They might be providing the “serious” policy rationale that gives cover to Miller’s power politics.

Trump’s 2019 Greenland interest predates Miller’s current expanded role, and genuine Arctic security concerns exist—Russia and China are increasingly active in the region, rare earth minerals matter strategically, and the Thule Air Base provides early warning capabilities. But Miller has transformed vague strategic interest into aggressive policy backed by threats of force.

But here’s the key difference: those other voices might support Greenland acquisition through negotiation, purchase, or long-term diplomatic pressure. Miller is the one whispering “take it by force if necessary.” He’s the one who’s comfortable declaring publicly that the U.S. military could seize it without a fight.

The “by any means necessary” part—that’s pure Miller.

The Danger of the Greenland Whisper

Why does it matter who’s whispering about Greenland? Because it reveals how far Miller’s influence has expanded and how dangerous his philosophy becomes when applied beyond immigration.

When Miller was just pushing immigrant deportations, Americans could tell themselves this was about border security or law enforcement. When he moved to deploying troops in American cities, it was concerning but still arguably domestic policy. When he orchestrated the Venezuela coup and oil grab, it crossed into aggressive foreign policy but at least targeted a hostile government.

Greenland is different. This is threatening military action against a NATO ally—Denmark—to seize territory from a peaceful democratic nation. It’s not countering a threat. It’s not responding to aggression. It’s pure expansion through force, the kind of 19th-century imperialism that the post-WWII order was designed to prevent.

If Miller can whisper this into Trump’s ear and have the president seriously consider it—even threaten it publicly—then there’s no limiting principle anymore. Any territory, any resource, any strategic position becomes fair game if America has the power to take it.

This is Miller’s Hobbesian philosophy applied to geopolitics: the world is governed by strength, force, and power, and “international niceties” are for the weak. Once that framework is accepted, once that whisper is heeded, the old rules don’t just bend—they shatter.

Why Trump Listens

Trump listens to Miller on Greenland for the same reason he listens to Miller on everything else: Miller has mastered the art of making Trump’s worst impulses sound like strategic genius.

When Trump wanted to separate families at the border, Miller told him it would be a “deterrent.” When Trump wanted to deploy troops to American cities, Miller told him it was necessary to counter “domestic terrorism.” When Trump wanted to overthrow Venezuela, Miller told him it would show American strength.

Now Trump wants Greenland—or more accurately, Miller has made Trump want Greenland by framing it as the ultimate power move. And Miller has spent years building credibility with Trump by never moderating, never backing down, never showing doubt. Trump trusts Miller because Miller is dogmatically consistent: might makes right, power does what it wants, weakness deserves what it gets.

The Atlantic profile notes that even Trump has joked about Miller’s extremism: “I’d love to have him come up and explain his true feelings—maybe not his truest feelings.” Trump knows Miller is more radical than even he is comfortable with publicly. But he keeps listening to the whispers.

The Broader Pattern

Greenland isn’t an isolated case. It’s part of a pattern:

  • Immigration: Treat vulnerable people as invaders, use military force against them

  • Domestic opposition: Deploy federal troops to Democratic cities over local objections

  • Venezuela: Overthrow a government and seize their oil

  • Greenland: Threaten a NATO ally and prepare to seize their territory

Each step follows the same logic: identify something you want, determine you have the power to take it, ignore all constraints—legal, moral, diplomatic—and just take it. This is the Hobbesian state of nature applied as foreign policy.

Miller’s genius—if you can call it that—is recognizing that Trump’s psychology aligns perfectly with this worldview. Trump already thinks in terms of dominance and submission. Miller just had to give him a philosophical framework that made those instincts sound like strategic vision.

What Comes After Greenland? The NATO Collapse and the Canada Question

If Trump seriously pursues Greenland—whether through coercive “negotiation” or military threats—the implications are staggering. Once you’ve established that the United States will ignore international law, threaten allies, and seize territory through force, where do you stop?

The NATO Implication

Here’s the terrifying reality: if the U.S. seizes Greenland from Denmark, NATO’s credibility as a defensive alliance would be shattered. Article 5—the mutual defense commitment that has kept peace in Europe since 1949—becomes worthless if America itself is the aggressor against a member state.

Putin must be celebrating. He’s spent years trying to fracture NATO, and now Miller is doing it for him. Once America demonstrates that alliance commitments mean nothing against raw power, why would any NATO member trust Article 5? The liberal international order that has prevented great power conflict for decades collapses if the world’s most powerful nation decides that might makes right.

For years, Putin has worked to undermine NATO, fracture the Western alliance, and establish that only power matters. Miller is accomplishing Putin’s strategic goals without Putin firing a shot.

If America seizes Greenland, Putin gets:

  • NATO’s defensive credibility destroyed

  • Proof that the post-WWII order is dead

  • Validation of his own might-makes-right philosophy

  • Freedom to pursue territorial expansion without American moral authority stopping him

  • A world where his invasion of Ukraine looks less like aberration and more like the new normal

Is Canada Next?

And here’s what keeps me up at night: once this precedent is set, what protects Canada?

This isn’t idle speculation. Trump spent much of 2025 making “51st state” jokes about Canada—rhetoric that Canadians found increasingly threatening as it persisted. As a Canadian watching from Toronto, those jokes stopped being funny months ago. Combined with Miller’s might-makes-right philosophy and the Greenland threats, what seemed like trolling now looks like testing the waters.

I’m not predicting imminent invasion. I’m following Miller’s stated philosophy to its logical conclusion to show why constraining principles matter. Think about Canada through Miller’s Hobbesian framework:

  • Vast natural resources (oil, minerals, fresh water that will become increasingly valuable with climate change)

  • Strategic Arctic territory for controlling northern shipping routes

  • A military that couldn’t resist American force

  • A shared border making occupation logistically simple

  • English as primary language, easing “integration”

In Miller’s worldview, Canada checks every box. The only thing protecting it is the same “international niceties” Miller has explicitly dismissed.

The Hobbesian Logic Points North

Follow Miller’s stated logic:

  1. Power is the only reality

  2. International law is irrelevant

  3. America should act as a superpower

  4. Valuable resources should be taken if you can

  5. Weak nations can’t defend what they have

  6. Alliance commitments don’t constrain power

Now apply that to Canada. Massive Alberta oil reserves. Fresh water. Arctic territory. Minerals and rare earths. A population 10% of America’s size. A military that couldn’t resist for days.

In Miller’s framework, combined with Trump’s persistent “51st state” rhetoric, the question becomes: why wouldn’t we take it?

The answer used to be: because we’re allies, because we respect sovereignty, because we believe in international law, because we value relationships and institutions that delivered unprecedented peace and prosperity.

But Miller has explicitly rejected every one of those constraints. Once you accept his philosophy, there’s no principle that protects Canada except American self-restraint.

And Miller’s entire career has been about removing restraints.

This is Why Philosophy Matters

This is why I spent time examining Miller’s Hobbesian worldview. It’s not academic abstraction. It’s the difference between a world where Canada can trust its southern border and a world where it needs to contemplate defense against American aggression.

The Founders chose Locke over Hobbes because they understood this danger. A government based purely on power, without moral constraints or respect for rights, inevitably becomes tyrannical. “Might makes right” destroys peaceful coexistence.

Miller wants to reverse that choice. And if Greenland falls, if NATO collapses, if the message becomes “America takes what it wants,” then every nation within reach of American power—including Canada, America’s closest ally sharing the world’s longest undefended border—has to reconsider its security.

Miller’s philosophy isn’t just wrong—it’s geopolitically disastrous. It destroys the alliance system that has amplified American power for 75 years. It trades moral leadership for brute force, and actually makes America weaker by isolating it from allies.

But Miller doesn’t care about alliances. He cares about domination. And he’s found a president willing to listen.

Following the Whispers

The next time Trump threatens to seize territory, overthrow a government, or deploy military force in defiance of law and allies, the answer to “who’s whispering this?” is always the same: the one who’s been turning Trump’s impulses into policy since the beginning, whose wife posts “SOON” before the president makes his move public, who told us explicitly that power is the only reality.

Stephen Miller isn’t just whispering about Greenland. He’s whispering a vision of America unbound by any constraint except its own power. And Trump is listening.

Q: What’s the difference between a Hobbesian and a hobbit?

A: One sees life as “nasty, brutish, and short” requiring authoritarian control. The other believes in gardening and elevenses.

Most days I wish Stephen Miller was more of a hobbit.
