The World Pays Tribute

Volume 6: How Trump Applied the Retribution Playbook to Every Nation on Earth

March 25, 2026

The February 28, 2025, Oval Office meeting where President Trump and Vice President Vance berated Ukrainian President Zelensky, a defining humiliation on Path Two: Oppose, Then Submit. Source: Wikipedia – 2025

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

On February 28, 2025, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky walked into the Oval Office to sign a minerals deal that his country desperately needed. He walked out with nothing — publicly humiliated on camera, berated by both the President and Vice President of the United States, and told to leave the White House.

Brazil’s president called it the most “grotesque” and “disrespectful” scene in the history of diplomacy. Russian state media celebrated. Former Russian president Dmitry Medvedev called it “a well-deserved slap in the face.” CNN noted that no American president had ever verbally attacked a visiting head of state the way Trump attacked Zelensky. Trump, for his part, seemed pleased. “This,” he told the cameras, “is going to be great television.”

It was great television. It was also a perfect demonstration of everything this series has documented — the retribution playbook that Roy Cohn taught a young developer in a Manhattan law office, now deployed against the leader of a nation fighting for its survival.

The Three Paths framework I established in the Introduction to this series — Never Oppose, Oppose Then Submit, or Oppose and Stay Principled — was designed to explain what happens to individuals who enter Trump’s orbit. Members of Congress. Federal employees. Journalists. Political rivals.

But the framework scales. It applies to nations the same way it applies to people. And when you map Trump’s treatment of every major country and its leader onto those three paths, the pattern is identical. The binary psychology I documented in “The World According to Trump” doesn’t stop at America’s borders. It extends to every head of state, every alliance, every trade relationship on earth.

The only difference is the weapons. Instead of pulled endorsements and primary challengers, it’s tariffs. Instead of Truth Social attacks, it’s territorial claims. Instead of super PACs, it’s the withdrawal of military aid. The tools are bigger. The instinct is the same.

Path One: Never Oppose — The Tribute States

Some nations learned early that the simplest route to survival in Trump’s world is total alignment. These are the countries that never challenge him, that flatter him publicly, that offer him the spectacle of respect he demands — and are rewarded for it.

Saudi Arabia and Mohammed bin Salman represent the purest example. When Trump took office the first time, his very first foreign trip was to Riyadh — an unprecedented break with the tradition of visiting Canada or Mexico first. The Saudis understood the assignment. They projected Trump’s face, stories tall, onto the side of a building. They gave him a gold medal. They laid on a sword dance. The message was clear: we will give you the pageantry you crave, and in return, you will give us what we want.

Trump participating in the ceremonial sword dance in Saudi Arabia during his first foreign trip, exemplifying Path One: Never Oppose, with lavish flattery from MBS rewarded with protection and silence. Source: The Guardian

What MBS wanted was American silence on the murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi. He got it. Despite the CIA’s assessment that MBS personally ordered the killing, Trump stood by him. “Maybe he did and maybe he didn’t,” Trump said — and then continued selling Saudi Arabia weapons. The Saudi sovereign wealth fund later invested $2 billion in a fund managed by Jared Kushner. In Trump’s second term, MBS was among the first foreign leaders to call. The relationship has never wavered, because MBS has never wavered in his performance of loyalty.

Hungary’s Viktor Orbán plays the same game from Europe. Orbán was the first European leader to endorse Trump’s 2024 campaign. He has modeled his own government on what he openly calls “illiberal democracy” — dismantling independent courts, controlling media, suppressing opposition. Trump has praised him repeatedly, calling him “a strong man” and “a great leader.” Orbán never criticizes Trump. Trump never criticizes Orbán’s democratic backsliding. The arrangement is simple: flattery for cover.

Trump shaking hands with North Korea’s Kim Jong Un at the 2018 Singapore summit, where pageantry and “beautiful letters” granted legitimacy in exchange for flattery on Path One. Source: CNN

North Korea’s Kim Jong Un represents the most dramatic example. Trump went from threatening “fire and fury” to exchanging what he called “beautiful letters” with a dictator who starves his own people and operates concentration camps. The Singapore summit, the walk across the DMZ — these were spectacles designed to project Trump’s self-image as a historic dealmaker. No substantive agreement was ever reached. North Korea’s nuclear program continued. But Kim gave Trump the pageantry, and Trump gave Kim legitimacy on the world stage. “We fell in love,” Trump said.

Israel under Benjamin Netanyahu follows the same pattern, though with added complexity. Trump moved the U.S. embassy to Jerusalem, recognized Israeli sovereignty over the Golan Heights, and brokered the Abraham Accords. Netanyahu reciprocated with constant public praise and named a settlement after Trump. The relationship held as long as Netanyahu maintained absolute alignment. In Trump’s second term, as Israel’s military operations in Gaza escalated, Trump provided unwavering cover — until Marjorie Taylor Greene called the actions genocide, at which point Trump’s fury shifted to her rather than to any policy change.

The lesson for nations on Path One is the same as for individuals: total alignment works, but only as long as it is maintained absolutely, on every issue, forever. MBS understood this. Orbán understood this. The moment any of them deviate — as MTG’s story proves at the individual level — the retribution machinery activates.

Path Two: Oppose, Then Submit — The Humiliation Ritual

For nations that initially resist and then capitulate, Trump demands the same thing he demands of Marco Rubio or Lindsey Graham: visible, public submission. The more dramatic the reversal, the more valuable it becomes as a signal to everyone watching.

Zelensky and Ukraine are the defining case study. During Trump’s first impeachment, Zelensky was the man Trump tried to extort — withholding military aid to pressure Ukraine into investigating Joe Biden. Zelensky navigated that crisis carefully, maintaining relationships with both parties. When Trump returned to power, Zelensky attempted diplomacy. He traveled to Washington. He offered the minerals deal. He praised Trump publicly and repeatedly — fact-checkers documented that Zelensky thanked the United States at least 33 times in English since January 2022.

None of it was enough. The February 28 Oval Office ambush demonstrated that Trump requires not just gratitude but complete deference to his framing — including the demand that Zelensky accept Putin’s terms without security guarantees. When Zelensky pushed back on the idea that diplomacy with Putin could work given Russia’s history of breaking agreements, Vice President Vance accused him of being “disrespectful” for raising the point. Trump told him, “You’re either going to make a deal or we’re out.”

The humiliation was deliberate. The cameras stayed on. Trump wanted the world to see what happens when a leader — even a wartime leader of an allied democracy — fails to perform adequate submission. Within hours, the administration suspended intelligence sharing and military aid to Ukraine. The message to every nation watching was unmistakable: defy Trump publicly and he will abandon you, even to the benefit of your invader.

Denmark and Greenland provide a smaller but instructive example. When Trump first floated the idea of purchasing Greenland in 2019, Danish Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen called it “absurd.” Trump cancelled a planned state visit to Denmark in retaliation — punishment for a single word. In his second term, Trump revived the Greenland claim with greater intensity, describing it as essential to American security and suggesting he might not rule out military force. Denmark found itself in the extraordinary position of a NATO ally being pressured with quasi-territorial threats by the alliance’s leading member.

Mexico under both Andrés Manuel López Obrador and Claudia Sheinbaum illustrates the submit-or-suffer dynamic through economic leverage. Trump’s tariff threats — 25% on all Mexican goods — forced both administrations into immigration enforcement concessions that served Trump’s domestic political narrative. AMLO, despite his leftist politics, maintained a remarkably warm personal relationship with Trump by performing public deference and cooperating on border enforcement. Sheinbaum initially took a more assertive posture, then found herself navigating the same impossible calculus: resist and face economic devastation, or submit and maintain access to the American market. The tariffs weren’t about trade policy. They were about compliance.

Canada — my own country — is a case I’ve documented extensively in “The Great Canadian Trade War” series. The pattern is textbook Path Two. Trump imposed 25% tariffs, called our prime minister weak, floated the idea of making Canada the 51st state, and deployed economic warfare against our most important trading relationship. Prime Minister Trudeau initially responded with countermeasures and sharp rhetoric. The subsequent political upheaval in Canada — Trudeau’s resignation, the leadership crisis, Mark Carney’s emergence — all played out in the shadow of Trump’s tariff threats. The message to Canadian voters and politicians was clear: your relationship with the United States depends on your relationship with Donald Trump, and resistance has a price.

What distinguishes Path Two at the international level is the scale of suffering that accompanies the submission demand. When Marco Rubio submitted, the cost was personal dignity. When nations submit, the cost is measured in jobs, GDP, and sometimes lives.

Path Three: Oppose and Stay Principled — The Permanent Target

For nations and leaders who refuse to submit, Trump’s response mirrors what he does to Liz Cheney or Thomas Massie — sustained, disproportionate hostility designed not just to punish the specific leader but to warn everyone watching.

Angela Merkel leaning in at the G7 summit while Trump stands with arms crossed in apparent contempt, emblematic of Path Three: Oppose and Stay Principled, met with sustained hostility. Source: TIME

Angela Merkel was the defining case in Trump’s first term. The German chancellor treated Trump with professional courtesy but never offered the flattery he craved and never abandoned her positions on NATO spending, climate, or Russian sanctions. Trump responded with open contempt. The handshake refusals in the Oval Office. The public statements that Germany was “controlled by Russia” because of the Nord Stream pipeline. The reported incident at a G7 summit where Trump tossed Starburst candies at Merkel, saying “Don’t say I never give you anything.” The NATO summit where Trump reportedly told European leaders that the U.S. would “go it alone” if they didn’t increase defense spending immediately.

Merkel never submitted. She maintained her positions, managed the relationship professionally, and outlasted Trump’s first term. But the treatment she received served its purpose: it told every other European leader what would happen to them if they challenged Trump publicly. When Merkel left office, Europe’s approach to Trump had already shifted from principled resistance to cautious accommodation.

The famous extended-grip handshake duel between Trump and Macron in 2017, a performative display of dominance in international relations. Source: The Guardian

Emmanuel Macron of France has oscillated between paths. His initial “bromance” with Trump — the extended handshake, the state dinner — was a deliberate attempt to build personal rapport that could moderate Trump’s behavior. It didn’t work. When Macron publicly disagreed with Trump on Iran, climate, and NATO, the relationship cooled. In Trump’s second term, Macron has attempted to position himself as Europe’s leading voice on Ukraine, which has put him directly at odds with Trump’s push for a deal on Putin’s terms. The Paris meeting before the Zelensky blowup was cordial but achieved little — Trump performed friendliness with Macron while simultaneously preparing to humiliate Zelensky the following day.

China presents a unique case — too powerful to bully through tariffs alone, too strategically important to fully alienate, and led by a leader, Xi Jinping, who is himself an autocrat comfortable with power dynamics. Trump’s relationship with Xi has cycled through threats and flattery, trade wars and trade deals, with neither leader fully submitting to the other. What distinguishes the China relationship is that Trump appears to recognize — perhaps uniquely among his international relationships — that the retribution playbook has limits when the adversary has comparable economic leverage. Xi doesn’t need Trump’s endorsement. He doesn’t depend on American voters. He can’t be primaried.

The Handshake and the Shove: Retribution as Physical Performance

What makes Trump’s international retribution distinctive is its performative quality. Domestic retribution operates through institutions — endorsements pulled, primaries funded, agencies gutted. International retribution operates through spectacle — physical dominance displays that humiliate leaders on camera for a global audience.

The catalog is extensive and well-documented. The extended-grip handshake with Macron in 2017, designed as a public dominance display — Macron later said he’d prepared specifically for it because he understood Trump saw handshakes as power contests. The moment at the 2017 NATO summit when Trump physically pushed aside Montenegrin Prime Minister Duško Marković to position himself at the front of a group photo — a literal shoving of a smaller nation’s leader out of the way. The pulling of Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe’s hand during their White House meeting, jerking him forward in a 19-second handshake that left Abe visibly uncomfortable. The repeated refusal to shake Merkel’s hand in the Oval Office, ignoring her offers while cameras clicked.

Trump physically pushing aside Montenegrin Prime Minister Duško Marković at the 2017 NATO summit, a literal shove symbolizing the “handshake and the shove” dynamic of retribution as physical performance. Source: NBC News

These aren’t diplomatic gaffes. They’re the international version of the pointed finger from the White House lawn — the image that opens this series. Each one sends the same message: I am dominant, you are subordinate, and the whole world is watching.

The Zelensky confrontation was this dynamic taken to its logical extreme. Not just a handshake power play but a full televised humiliation — the president and vice president of the United States tag-teaming a wartime leader, demanding he perform gratitude, then expelling him from the White House when he maintained his dignity. Trump’s comment — “This is going to be great television” — revealed the purpose. The audience wasn’t Zelensky. The audience was every other leader on earth.

Tariffs as the International Endorsement

In domestic politics, Trump’s primary weapon against disloyal Republicans is the endorsement — its bestowal for loyalty, its withdrawal for defiance. Tariffs serve the identical function in international relations.

The pattern is consistent. Tariffs are announced or threatened not in response to genuine trade imbalances but in response to perceived disloyalty or insufficient submission. Canada’s 25% tariffs followed years of Trump treating the bilateral relationship as a dominance contest. Mexico’s tariff threats were explicitly linked to immigration enforcement — a domestic political issue, not a trade issue. European tariffs were threatened in conjunction with NATO spending disputes. In each case, the tariff was the international equivalent of telling a Republican congressman, “You will seriously suffer the consequences come Election time.”

The Supreme Court’s 6-3 ruling striking down Trump’s tariffs in February 2026 — the event that triggered Jeff Hurd’s vote and this entire series — illustrated how thoroughly the retribution instinct has overtaken trade policy. Trump’s response to the Court ruling was not to accept a legal defeat but to attack the justices who voted against him, sort them into loyal and disloyal categories, and threaten consequences for any legislator who upheld the ruling. The international dimension and the domestic dimension collapsed into one: the tariffs were retribution against countries, and defending the tariffs became a loyalty test for American politicians.

My “Great Canadian Trade War” series documents this dynamic extensively from the Canadian perspective. But seen from the vantage point of The Retribution Report, Canada is just one example of a global pattern: trade policy subordinated to the binary psychology of loyalty and disloyalty, with every nation on earth subjected to the same sorting mechanism that Trump applies to members of his own party.

The Territorial Claims: Retribution Becomes Domination

Perhaps the most extraordinary escalation of international retribution in Trump’s second term has been the deployment of territorial claims — Greenland, the Panama Canal, Canada as the “51st state” — as dominance displays.

These claims are not serious policy proposals. No one in the administration has presented a realistic pathway to acquiring Greenland, seizing the Panama Canal, or annexing Canada. What they are, instead, is the international equivalent of Trump branding Marjorie Taylor Greene a “traitor” or calling Thomas Massie a “moron” at the National Prayer Breakfast. They are assertions of dominance designed to humiliate — to force a nation to publicly respond to an absurd demand, thereby demonstrating who sets the terms of the conversation.

When Denmark’s prime minister must stand before cameras and explain why Greenland is not for sale, Trump has already won the exchange. The mere fact that she must respond to the demand — that her nation’s sovereignty over its own territory becomes a topic of international discussion because an American president raised it — is itself the retribution. She called his previous proposal “absurd.” He cancelled a state visit. Now the claim is back, louder, with suggestions of military force. The escalation is the point.

The “51st state” rhetoric toward Canada serves the same function. No one believes the United States is going to annex Canada. But by saying it — repeatedly, publicly, to world leaders — Trump forces Canadian politicians to respond, keeps the Canadian public anxious, and establishes that he will say anything to any nation without consequence. It is the international version of the Roy Cohn playbook: the outrageous demand is not meant to be fulfilled. It is meant to establish that there are no rules, no norms, no boundaries that Trump will respect. And if there are no boundaries, then every nation must calculate its relationship with the United States based not on treaties, alliances, or shared values, but on one question: is Donald Trump happy with us right now?

What the World Learned

The international retribution pattern reveals something that domestic coverage often misses: Trump’s behavior is not chaos. It is not random. It is not the product of ignorance about how diplomacy works.

It is a system.

The same system documented in every previous volume of this series. The same binary sorting. The same escalation ladder. The same three paths: submit and be rewarded, resist and be destroyed, or grovel your way back after initial defiance and accept the humiliation as the price of re-entry.

MBS understood this and projected Trump’s face onto a building. Zelensky didn’t fully submit and was expelled from the Oval Office on camera. Merkel maintained her dignity and endured four years of contempt. Orbán performed loyalty and received protection. Every nation has been sorted.

The difference between domestic and international retribution is not the psychology — it’s the stakes. When Trump pulls an endorsement from Jeff Hurd, a congressman’s career is at risk. When Trump withdraws military aid from Ukraine, people die. When Trump imposes tariffs on Canada, thousands of jobs disappear. When Trump floats territorial claims against allied nations, the post-World War II international order — the system that has prevented great-power conflict for eighty years — erodes a little further.

In my Introduction to this series, I noted that I write from Canada — and that what happens in America doesn’t stay in America. Six volumes later, the evidence is overwhelming. The retribution architecture that Roy Cohn built in a Manhattan law office doesn’t just operate through the White House, the DOJ, and federal agencies. It operates through the State Department, through trade policy, through military aid, through NATO, and through the daily calculations of every government on earth about how to manage its relationship with a superpower led by a man who treats nations the way he treats disloyal Republicans.

The world has three paths. Submit, resist, or submit after resisting.

The playbook hasn’t changed. The map has gotten bigger.
