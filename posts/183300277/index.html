ABBA Against the Odds

Against the Odds

Part 8: The Avatar Gamble Innovation at 75

February 27, 2026

The ABBA Arena, East London. Purpose-built. £141 million. Not a single band member inside.

In 2018, ABBA announced they were reuniting. After 35 years of silence, they’d recorded two new songs. Fans worldwide celebrated—finally, after decades of speculation and multi-million dollar offers declined, ABBA was back.

Then came the curious part: they weren’t touring. Instead, they were creating something called “ABBA Voyage”—a concert featuring digital avatars of themselves as they appeared in 1979, performing alongside a live band in a purpose-built London arena.

The skepticism was immediate. Holograms? Digital representations? Would anyone pay to see fake ABBA when they could watch concert footage on YouTube for free? Would it feel authentic or like a gimmick? Was this just a cash grab by aging artists unwilling to do the work of actual touring?

Music industry analysts questioned the economics. Building a custom arena in London reportedly cost £141 million ($185 million). The avatar technology cost tens of millions more. For that investment to make sense, the show would need to run for years, selling out consistently at premium prices. How many people really wanted to see digital versions of a band that stopped performing in 1982?

Three years later, the skeptics have their answer: ABBA Voyage has sold over 3 million tickets, generated over $350 million in direct revenue, contributed £2 billion to the UK economy, and shows no signs of slowing. At 90%+ capacity, it’s one of London’s most in-demand productions. Extensions continue into 2026 and beyond.

Björn Ulvaeus is now 79 years old. Benny Andersson is 78. Agnetha Fältskog is 75. Anni-Frid Lyngstad is 79.

None of them set foot on stage for any of those 3 million performances.

This wasn’t a reunion tour. This was ABBA—at an age when most artists are either retired or doing greatest-hits nostalgia circuits—pioneering a completely new performance medium that could generate revenue indefinitely without their physical presence.

The avatar gamble wasn’t just commercially successful. It was the logical culmination of strategic thinking they’d demonstrated for 50 years: find better ways to achieve goals rather than accepting traditional methods as inevitable.

The Decision Point

After Mamma Mia!’s extraordinary success (Part 6) and the catalog’s continued strength through streaming and compilation sales (Part 7), ABBA faced a choice.

The easiest, most lucrative path was obvious: traditional reunion tour. The Rolling Stones, in their 70s and 80s, were still touring and earning hundreds of millions. Eagles, Fleetwood Mac, countless other 1970s acts had done profitable reunion tours. The model was proven. The demand existed. Promoters would pay astronomical sums.

Conservative estimates suggested ABBA could earn $500 million or more from a global reunion tour hitting major markets. Perhaps $1 billion if they really committed to an extended run.

But touring at 75-79 meant:

Physical strain. Performing night after night is exhausting at 30. At 75+, it’s potentially dangerous. Vocal health, stamina, mobility—aging bodies struggle with performance demands.

Repetition and boredom. Performing the same set repeatedly in different cities. The Rolling Stones have played “Satisfaction” thousands of times. Would ABBA want to spend their late 70s doing that?

Limited reach. No matter how extensive the tour, they could only play so many cities. Health and stamina would limit schedule. They’d reach millions, but not tens of millions.

Time-bounded revenue. Tour revenue stops when touring stops. Once they couldn’t perform anymore—whether due to health, exhaustion, or death—the income stream ended.

Personal dynamics. Two divorced couples, now in their late 70s. Did they want to spend months together on tour? Relationships had evolved to friendly but not intimate. Touring would test those boundaries.

As Björn later reflected, someone approached with a proposition: “You can go on the road as holograms on tour, and you don’t even have to be there.”

Their response: “The idea came and we thought it was irresistible. And because it’s something that is pushing boundaries and something nobody has ever seen before.”

“Nobody has ever seen before”—that phrase echoes their video strategy from the 1970s (Part 3). They’d pioneered using video as virtual touring then. Now they were pioneering avatar technology for the same purpose: being everywhere without actually being anywhere.

The Technology

1979 ABBA, performing in 2025. Digital avatars driven by motion capture of the real band members — now in their late 70s. Image courtesy of ABBA Voyage.

Creating convincing digital avatars of four people, capturing their 1979 appearance and movement, then having those avatars perform 90-minute concerts required cutting-edge technology.

ABBA partnered with Industrial Light & Magic—George Lucas’s visual effects company famous for Star Wars—to create the “ABBAtars.”

The process involved:

Motion capture sessions. All four members, now in their mid-70s, performed full concerts in motion-capture suits. Multiple cameras captured every movement, facial expression, gesture. This data would drive the avatars’ movements.

All four members performed full concerts in motion-capture suits. Their movements at 75 drive avatars that look 35.

Digital de-aging. The avatars weren’t 75-year-old ABBA—they were ABBA as they appeared in 1979, at the height of their fame and physical prime. Extensive digital work recreated their 40-years-younger appearances.

Real-time rendering. The avatars perform in real-time, synchronized with the live band. This wasn’t pre-recorded video—it was dynamic performance responsive to the music.

Custom venue. The ABBA Arena was purpose-built in East London specifically for Voyage. The 3,000-capacity venue featured advanced projection, sound, and lighting systems integrated to make the avatars feel present.

Live band integration. A 10-piece live band performs alongside the avatars, providing authentic musical energy. This hybrid approach—digital performers with live musicians—created something neither fully virtual nor fully traditional.

The investment was massive: £141 million for the arena, tens of millions more for technology development and production. This wasn’t safe investment in proven concept. This was bet on unproven technology creating experience audiences would accept and pay for.

The Creative Team

Like Mamma Mia! (Part 6), ABBA Voyage succeeded partly because they assembled the right creative team.

Producers Svana Gisla and Ludvig Andersson (Benny’s son) brought production expertise and family connection to the project.

Director Baillie Walsh created the staging and visual direction, ensuring the avatars performed with energy and personality rather than feeling robotic.

Industrial Light & Magic provided technical capability that made the avatars convincing rather than uncanny-valley creepy.

The ABBA members themselves were intimately involved. This wasn’t licensing their image to someone else’s production. They performed the motion capture. They approved the avatar appearances. They ensured the show met their standards.

As Anni-Frid described it: “It’s a bit odd because you can recognize yourself in the Avatar at the same time sitting there. I mean, as a person, you are watching it and it’s a bit... How can you say what’s the right expression would be good for it. It’s a bit... It’s weird and wonderful.”

That phrase—”weird and wonderful”—captures the audience reaction too.

May 2022: Opening Night

ABBA Voyage premiered May 26, 2022 at the ABBA Arena in London.

The pre-show skepticism was intense. Would it feel like watching screens? Would the avatars be convincing? Would audiences accept digital performers?

Björn Ulvaeus himself expressed amazement: “Sometimes I get that illusion completely. I mean, there are live musicians and you, I get the illusion f***, there must be someone up there. There is. They’re there, you know.”

Reviews were overwhelmingly positive. Critics praised the technology’s sophistication, the show’s energy, and the emotional connection despite the digital medium.

Audiences loved it. The show sold out. Extensions were announced. More extensions. The initial run was planned for limited engagement. It’s now booking years ahead.

What made it work?

The avatars were convincing. Industrial Light & Magic’s technology crossed the uncanny valley. The ABBAtars felt present, energetic, alive—not creepy or obviously artificial.

The live band added authenticity. Having real musicians performing live alongside digital avatars created hybrid energy—the best of both approaches.

The production quality was exceptional. Lighting, sound, staging, visual effects—everything was top-tier. This wasn’t budget virtual concert. This was premium theatrical experience.

The venue was intimate. At 3,000 capacity, the ABBA Arena feels personal. You’re not watching avatars on stadium screens from 200 yards away. You’re in a theater designed specifically for this experience.

The song selection was perfect. Greatest hits everyone wanted to hear, performed with energy and precision. No filler, no obscure album tracks—just hit after hit.

The emotional authenticity surprised people. Reviewers repeatedly noted they felt genuine emotional connection despite knowing they were watching digital representations. The music and performance transcended the medium.

The nostalgia was handled perfectly. The show celebrated ABBA’s legacy without drowning in it. The avatars were 1979 ABBA, but the production felt contemporary, not dated.

The Financial Performance

The math that made the gamble obvious

The numbers validate the gamble:

2022 (partial year, May-December): £97.1 million turnover ($128.7 million), 675,600 visitors, 228 performances

2023: £103.7 million turnover ($129 million), 1.1 million visitors, 374 performances

2024: £104.3 million turnover ($113 million), 1.06 million visitors, 374 performances

Total through 2025: Over 3 million tickets sold, over $350 million in direct revenue

Economic impact: £2 billion contributed to UK economy through visitor spending, employment, tourism

The show consistently sells at 90%+ capacity. Extensions are booked into 2026 and beyond. There’s no sign of demand declining.

This means ABBA Voyage generates roughly $110-130 million annually—comparable to what major touring acts earn, but without anyone touring.

The £141 million arena investment has been recouped. The technology costs are covered. Now it’s generating pure profit for as long as it runs.

And here’s the crucial point: it can run indefinitely. ABBA members don’t need to show up. They don’t age out of performing. They don’t get sick or tired. The avatars can perform eight shows a week, fifty weeks a year, for as long as demand exists.

Compare this to traditional touring:

  • The Rolling Stones earned approximately $400 million from their 2021-2022 tour, but Mick Jagger is 81 and the physical toll is obvious

  • Madonna’s 2023-2024 tour grossed over $400 million but required her to perform 80+ shows at age 65

  • ABBA Voyage generates $110-130 million annually and requires zero physical presence from band members in their late 70s

The avatar model is more sustainable, more scalable, and ultimately more profitable over time.

Why It Worked: The Throughline

ABBA Voyage’s success makes perfect sense when you trace the throughline from their earliest strategic decisions:

From Part 3 (Video Revolution): They pioneered using video as touring substitute in the 1970s. They understood that physical presence wasn’t necessary for emotional connection—high-quality content presented through appropriate media worked just as well. Voyage is the same principle with 2020s technology instead of 1970s video.

From Part 4 (Business Model): Their ownership structure meant they controlled rights to create Voyage and captured all the value it generated. No label executives needed to approve. No complex negotiations across multiple rights holders. They decided, invested, and profited.

From Part 7 (Wilderness Years): Their 17-year absence created scarcity and mystique that made Voyage feel special. Had they been touring constantly since 1982, Voyage would have been just another format. But after decades of not performing, the avatars felt like miracle—a way to see ABBA that shouldn’t be possible.

From their entire career: The quality of the music was high enough that people wanted to experience it live, even through avatars. Mediocre songs wouldn’t have sustained Voyage. ABBA’s catalog strength made the technology worth developing.

Voyage wasn’t a departure from ABBA’s strategic thinking. It was the logical evolution of patterns they’d demonstrated for 50 years.

The Broader Implications

ABBA Voyage represents more than successful commercial venture. It’s proof-of-concept for entirely new performance model.

For aging artists: Why tour physically when avatar technology can create sustainable performance medium that doesn’t require your presence? Madonna, Elton John, the Rolling Stones—all will eventually be unable to tour. Avatar technology offers alternative.

For deceased artists: Pophouse Entertainment—co-founded by Björn Ulvaeus and involved in Voyage—acquired KISS’s catalog and likeness rights. KISS avatars are planned. Imagine avatar shows for Elvis, Michael Jackson, Prince, David Bowie—artists who can’t tour because they’re dead.

For current artists: Why wait until you’re too old or dead? Create avatar show at your physical peak, then let it run while you do other things. Capture your prime forever and monetize it indefinitely.

For live entertainment: Virtual performers never cancel due to illness. Never have bad nights. Never age out of roles. The technology creates certainty that human performers can’t match.

The implications are profound and unsettling. What does it mean for live performance when the performers aren’t alive or present? What does it mean for audiences’ relationship with artists? What does it mean for the economics of music when catalog plus technology can generate endless value without new creation?

ABBA didn’t invent these questions, but Voyage forces the industry to confront them.

The Personal Element

For the four ABBA members—now in their late 70s—Voyage solved problems traditional reunion couldn’t.

They could collaborate on recording the two new songs (”I Still Have Faith in You” and “Don’t Shut Me Down”) released alongside Voyage. That happened in comfortable Stockholm studio environment over time they controlled.

They could participate in motion-capture performances—physically demanding but time-limited. A few weeks of intense work rather than months of touring.

They could then step back and let the avatars perform. They attended the opening night. They did some press. But they don’t need to show up eight times a week.

As Anni-Frid noted, there was something “weird and wonderful” about watching your younger self perform. It’s simultaneously you and not you. Your actual performance captured and preserved, but freed from your current physical limitations.

For two divorced couples navigating complex history, Voyage offered collaboration without the intensity of touring together. They could celebrate their legacy without having to live in each other’s pockets for months.

And practically, at 75-79, they could generate substantial income without the physical toll of touring. The avatars do the work. They collect the revenue.

The Longevity Multiplication

Here’s what Voyage means for ABBA’s longevity:

Indefinite revenue generation. As long as Voyage runs—potentially decades—it generates income without new creative input.

Preservation of legacy at peak. The avatars are 1979 ABBA—their physical and creative prime. This image is what future generations will associate with them, not elderly performers struggling through nostalgic sets.

Proof their music transcends presence. The show’s success confirms what their video strategy suggested 50 years ago: the music is strong enough to work without them present.

Template for future exploitation. If Voyage works in London, why not other cities? Why not touring avatar production visiting multiple venues? The format is infinitely replicable.

Intergenerational appeal. Gen Z discovering ABBA through TikTok can see them “perform live” through Voyage, even though ABBA last performed together in 1982—decades before Gen Z was born.

Cultural relevance maintenance. Voyage keeps ABBA in current conversation as innovators, not just nostalgia. They’re not the band that was big 50 years ago. They’re the band pioneering new performance technology now.

Financial security extension. The revenue provides security for remaining decades and creates assets that can be passed to heirs or continue generating value for decades after they’re gone.

ABBA won’t tour again. But through Voyage, they’ll keep performing indefinitely.

The Gamble That Wasn’t

In retrospect, calling Voyage a “gamble” underestimates ABBA’s strategic consistency.

Yes, the technology was unproven. Yes, the investment was massive. Yes, the audience acceptance was uncertain.

But ABBA had been making similar bets for 50 years:

  • Betting on video over touring in the 1970s (Part 3)

  • Betting on ownership and long-term value over short-term income (Part 4)

  • Betting that their music would find new audiences during wilderness years (Part 7)

Each bet paid off because they understood something fundamental: if the music is excellent and you control the rights, new technologies and opportunities will emerge to monetize that quality over time.

Voyage wasn’t random gamble. It was logical application of principles they’d demonstrated for half a century: find innovative ways to reach audiences, maintain quality standards, capture long-term value.

At 75, they innovated again. And it worked. Again.
