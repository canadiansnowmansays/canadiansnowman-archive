The Contribution Index

India

Part XII: The Sleeping Giant

April 17, 2026

India's Chandrayaan moon mission — the same civilization that reaches the lunar surface cannot reliably supply its rural health clinics. Source: ISRO / Press Trust of India

Underperformer #5

India is the world’s most populous nation, the world’s largest democracy by headcount, and a civilization older than most of the institutions that presume to measure it. It possesses nuclear weapons, a space programme that has reached Mars, a technology sector that powers global IT infrastructure, and a cultural heritage of extraordinary depth.

It also ranks 176th out of 180 nations on the Environmental Performance Index. Its democratic trajectory is moving in the wrong direction. And the gap between what India could contribute to humanity — given the scale of its talent, its institutions, and its civilizational resources — and what it currently contributes is vast.

The sleeping giant. Not because India lacks energy — it has more human energy than almost any nation on Earth — but because the choices its government makes channel that energy inward and downward rather than outward and upward.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 24 out of 50

India’s poverty reduction trajectory earns its highest human development score — a 6. The reduction of extreme poverty over the past two decades is genuine and significant. Hundreds of millions of Indians now live above subsistence, and programmes like Ayushman Bharat have expanded healthcare access to hundreds of millions previously uncovered. This is not trivial. In absolute numbers, India’s poverty reduction since 2000 is second only to China’s, and it was achieved within a democratic framework rather than an authoritarian one — which makes the achievement more remarkable, even as it makes the remaining failures less excusable.

But the baseline remains punishing. Life expectancy is approximately 70 years — well below global averages for nations at India’s level of economic development and more than a decade behind China, which started from a comparable position. Healthcare access scores a 4 because the gap between Ayushman Bharat’s promise and the reality of rural healthcare delivery remains enormous. Government hospitals in many states are understaffed, undersupplied, and overwhelmed. Private healthcare is excellent for those who can afford it and nonexistent for those who cannot. The two-tier system mirrors India’s broader inequality — a nation of extremes where world-class cardiac surgeons practise in the same country where children die of preventable diarrhoea.

Educational attainment scores a 5. India produces extraordinary talent — the IITs, the IIMs, the research institutions that feed Silicon Valley and global academia. But primary and secondary education in much of rural India remains inadequate, with teacher absenteeism, crumbling infrastructure, and curricula that have not kept pace with the demands of a modern economy. The nation that exports engineers to Google cannot reliably teach its own village children to read.

Gender equality earns a 4 — one of the lowest scores in the series for a democracy. India’s gender gap in economic participation is among the widest of any major nation. Violence against women is pervasive and inadequately addressed — the statistics on sexual assault, domestic violence, and dowry-related deaths are staggering and widely documented. Female labour force participation has actually declined in recent decades, a trajectory that defies the normal pattern of economic development and reflects deep structural and cultural barriers.

India holds the largest elections on Earth — an extraordinary civic achievement. Whether the democracy they produce is strengthening or weakening is no longer an ambiguous question. Source: Reuters

Democratic governance earns a 5, reflecting genuine democratic infrastructure alongside deepening concerns about backsliding under the current government. India holds the largest elections on Earth — an extraordinary logistical and civic achievement. But press freedom has declined sharply, with India falling to 159th on the Reporters Without Borders Press Freedom Index. Religious minorities, particularly Muslims, face increasing marginalization through legislative action, judicial inaction, and the mainstreaming of Hindu nationalist rhetoric. The independence of institutions — the Election Commission, the judiciary, the civil service — has eroded under sustained political pressure. India’s democracy is real. Whether it is strengthening or weakening is no longer an ambiguous question.

Environmental Stewardship: 19 out of 50

Delhi's air quality routinely reaches levels the WHO classifies as hazardous. Hundreds of thousands of premature deaths annually are attributed to air pollution. Source: Reuters / AFP

Water and air quality earn a 2 — one of the lowest environmental scores in the entire series and the defining environmental failure of India’s scorecard. Indian cities dominate global lists of the most polluted urban environments. Delhi’s air quality index routinely reaches levels that the WHO classifies as hazardous — levels at which simply breathing is a health risk. Hundreds of thousands of premature deaths annually are attributed to air pollution. The Ganges, sacred to hundreds of millions, remains severely polluted despite decades of cleanup pledges and billions of rupees spent on remediation programmes that have produced minimal results.

The Ganges at Varanasi — sacred to hundreds of millions, catastrophically polluted despite decades of cleanup pledges and billions spent on remediation. Source: Brett Cole Photography

Emissions per capita earn a 6 — one of India’s better scores, and an important equity argument that the Contribution Index must acknowledge. Despite being the world’s third-largest total emitter, India’s per capita emissions — roughly 2 metric tons per person — remain far below Western nations. The average Indian produces one-seventh the carbon of the average American. India did not create the climate crisis, and its citizens consume a fraction of what their Western counterparts consume. The per capita score reflects this reality.

The Bhadla Solar Park in Rajasthan — one of the world's largest solar installations — sits alongside continued expansion of coal-fired power. India is building both simultaneously. Source: Reuters

But the trajectory is concerning. India’s total emissions are rising, driven by industrial growth, urbanization, and continued reliance on coal. Renewable energy earns a 5, reflecting a genuine duality: India has some of the world’s largest solar installations — the Bhadla Solar Park in Rajasthan is one of the largest on Earth — alongside continued expansion of coal-fired power. India is building both solar farms and coal plants simultaneously, and the coal plants will lock in emissions for decades.

Biodiversity and habitat protection earn a 3. India’s ecological diversity is extraordinary — from the Western Ghats to the Sundarbans to the Himalayan foothills — but enforcement of environmental protections has been inconsistent, and development pressure on critical habitats is intense and accelerating. Deforestation earns a 3, reflecting the steady encroachment of agriculture, mining, and infrastructure projects on forest land.

A Bengal tiger in India's shrinking forests. Roughly 2,000–2,500 remain in the wild — a testament to both India's extraordinary biodiversity and the accelerating development pressure on the habitats that sustain it. Photo: Tom Brakefield — Stockbyte/Thinkstock. Source: Encyclopædia Britannica.

Systemic Behaviour: 22 out of 50

India’s systemic behaviour is middling across all parameters — not catastrophic in any single dimension but not constructive in any either. It is the score of a nation that participates in the international system without investing in it.

Arms exports earn a 5 — India is not a major arms exporter but is among the world’s largest arms importers, spending tens of billions on weapons systems from Russia, France, and the United States. This spending reflects a security environment shaped by adversarial relationships with Pakistan and China, but it also reflects a choice to prioritize military hardware over human development.

Corruption earns a 4. India’s corruption challenges are well documented — from petty bribery in local government to large-scale procurement scandals at the national level. The anti-corruption movement that swept the country in the early 2010s produced some institutional reforms, but structural corruption remains deeply embedded in governance at every level.

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earn a 4. India hosts significant refugee populations — Tibetans, Sri Lankan Tamils, Rohingya — but its approach to refugee management is ad hoc, with no national asylum law and inconsistent treatment that varies by the political convenience of the moment.

The Neighbour Test: 14 out of 50

India’s Neighbour Test scores are uniformly low — 3s and a 2. This is the score of a regional hegemon that treats its neighbourhood as a sphere of influence rather than a community of partners.

Multilateral orientation earns a 3. India participates in international institutions — the UN, BRICS, the G20 which it hosted in 2023 — but selectively, optimizing for national prestige and strategic advantage rather than collective benefit. Its refusal to condemn Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, driven by dependency on Russian arms imports and a non-aligned tradition that has calcified into strategic ambiguity, cost it credibility with Western democracies without earning it meaningful goodwill elsewhere. India wants a permanent seat on the UN Security Council but behaves, on the world stage, like a nation that views multilateral institutions as platforms for status rather than vehicles for collective action.

Treatment of allies earns a 3. India’s relationships with Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Nepal, and Bangladesh are shaped by a hegemonic posture that, while understandable given genuine security concerns, limits India’s contribution to regional stability and cooperation. Smaller neighbours experience Indian engagement as pressure rather than partnership — a dynamic visible in India’s management of water resources shared with Bangladesh, its interventions in Sri Lankan politics, and its economic leverage over Nepal.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 3. A nation that proclaims itself the world’s largest democracy while its press freedom ranking falls to 159th, that champions non-alignment while conducting nuclear tests, that promotes pluralism while its governing party pursues Hindu nationalist policies that marginalize 200 million Muslim citizens — the gap between stated values and observed behaviour is wide and widening.

Long-term stewardship earns a 2 — the lowest score on India’s Neighbour Test. A nation of 1.4 billion people whose air pollution kills hundreds of thousands annually, whose rivers remain catastrophically polluted despite decades of cleanup pledges, and whose emissions trajectory threatens global climate targets is not practising stewardship. It is borrowing against the future of its own children and every other nation’s children, and the bill is already coming due in respiratory disease, water contamination, and agricultural disruption.

The distance between Indian individual generosity and Indian national policy is the widest of any nation in this series. Azim Premji has donated over $21 billion to education and rural development — one of the largest philanthropic commitments in human history. The Tata Trusts, with assets exceeding $100 billion, have funded healthcare, education, and rural development across India for over a century. Nandan Nilekani’s investments in digital public infrastructure — particularly Aadhaar, the biometric identity system that has brought hundreds of millions into the formal economy — have transformed governance at a scale that few philanthropic efforts anywhere have matched. These contributions are extraordinary — and they are an indictment. When individuals must give away billions to compensate for what the world’s largest democracy will not fund publicly, the philanthropy is not a rebuttal of the nation’s underperformance. It is its most damning evidence.

The Editorial Conclusion

India scores 79 out of 200. The fifth-ranked underperformer.

The sleeping giant. India possesses the human capital, the democratic tradition, the civilizational depth, and the sheer scale to be the most consequential positive force on Earth. 1.4 billion talented, resilient, increasingly educated people could, under different policy direction, transform the global trajectory on poverty, climate, and governance simultaneously. No nation on this list has more potential positive impact. The gap between India at 79 and India at 150 is not a matter of resources. It is a matter of political choices — choices about where to invest, whom to protect, how to govern, and whether to treat the rest of the world as a community or a chessboard.

The potential is not hypothetical. It is visible in every Indian scientist working abroad, every tech entrepreneur building from Bangalore, every philanthropist writing cheques that their government will not, every doctor staffing a rural clinic that their health ministry has failed to supply. The talent is there. The will — at the individual level — is there. What is missing is a national politics that matches the ambition of its own citizens.

India is not failing for lack of capacity. It is failing — for now — for lack of the political choices that would unleash that capacity for the common good. The giant is not sleeping because it is tired. It is sleeping because its government has not yet decided to wake it up.
