The Iran Crisis

“The Colonel”

Part LXII: Days Thirty-Eight Through Forty-Two: The Rescue, the Escalation, and the Easter Ultimatum

April 6, 2026

An image shared by Iranian state television purporting to show U.S. aircraft destroyed during the mission to find a stranded airman in Iran.Credit...Social Media/UGC, via Reuters

Monday April 6, 2026

He hiked up a 7,000-foot ridgeline. Injured. Alone. Armed with a pistol. Wedged into a mountain crevice in the Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad Province of southwest Iran while two forces — one trying to save him, one trying to capture him — raced through the darkness below.

He rationed his beacon, because the Iranians could track its signal as well as the Americans. He waited. He knew both sides were looking for him.

For thirty-eight hours, the colonel was the war in miniature: the gap between what American military power can do and what it cannot, between the technological dominance that can put SEAL Team 6 into the mountains of Iran and the geopolitical reality that still cannot open a 21-mile waterway. The United States can find one man in a mountain crevice in enemy territory. It cannot make Iran open the Strait of Hormuz.

The colonel came home on Saturday night. The strait is still closed. And on Easter Sunday morning, the president of the United States posted this to Truth Social:

This was the Iran war on Easter Sunday, April 5, 2026. Day thirty-eight.

I. The Colonel

The F-15E Strike Eagle from the 494th Fighter Squadron at RAF Lakenheath was hit by Iranian fire on Friday morning, Good Friday, April 3. Both crew members ejected. The pilot was rescued within six hours. The weapons systems officer — an Air Force colonel — could not be found.

Surveillance planes and drones combed the area. Nothing. The military described him as “status unknown.” US Central Command was preparing a statement saying the plane had gone down and the pilot had been rescued.

Then, fourteen hours after the jet was hit, a beacon signal. Intermittent, as trained. The colonel was rationing it. Central Command immediately scrapped the prepared statement. Defense Secretary Hegseth called Trump: as long as there was a chance, they needed to keep the pilot’s rescue secret.

At Langley, the CIA developed a deception plan. They spread word through Iranian networks that the airman had already been found and was moving out of the country in a ground convoy. Iranian search parties shifted toward the roads. The colonel remained in his crevice on the ridgeline.

The CIA’s deception appeared to cause confusion. But the Iranians intensified their broader search, calling on citizens via state television to capture “the enemy’s pilot or pilots” for a reward. Iranian search parties assembled at the base of the mountain. One was there when the colonel was still above them.

On Saturday morning, while Trump was posting “Time is running out — 48 hours before all Hell will reign down on them,” US military officials were in the final stages of preparing the rescue. One hundred Special Operations forces, led by elements of SEAL Team 6. Delta Force and Army Rangers on standby. Dozens of warplanes, helicopters, aerial tankers, surveillance aircraft. The CIA used a piece of technology unique to the agency to confirm the colonel’s identity and location.

They waited until dark.

As the commandos landed, US warplanes dropped bombs. Bright orange blasts lit up the silhouettes of the surrounding mountains. From his hiding place, the colonel directed his rescuers to the areas where he could see Iranians advancing. The commandos fired to keep them back. They did not engage in a direct firefight — the territory was strongly opposed to the Iranian regime, and it was unclear how close the Iranian forces actually got.

The colonel was rushed to a helicopter. Flown to a remote airstrip inside Iran that Special Operations had previously prepared for exactly this contingency.

Then the final twist. The nose gear of at least one, possibly both, C-130 transport planes got stuck in the sandy dirt. Hours passed. Efforts to free the wheels failed. Three replacement aircraft were called in. Pentagon and Central Command officials waited. The mission that had seemed nearly complete was suddenly uncertain again.

The replacement planes arrived. The colonel and the commandos boarded. American warplanes bombed the two disabled C-130s rather than leave them for Iran.

As the sun was rising, three planes launched in succession from the remote Iranian airstrip.

When word reached the White House that the aircraft had cleared Iranian airspace, Trump posted at just past midnight: “WE GOT HIM!”

A senior US military official described the mission as one of the most challenging and complex in the history of US Special Operations. The colonel was flown to Kuwait for medical treatment. He sustained injuries but will be fine. All commandos returned safely.

There were tears across America Saturday morning, including in Toronto. They were appropriate. Whatever the politics of this war — and this series has documented them without flinching — an Air Force colonel came home from the mountains of Iran. The men who went in to get him did something extraordinary. The record holds space for that.

II. The Emboldened

The rescue that produced tears produced something else by Easter morning.

David Sanger, writing in the Times, identified the pivot with precision. Trump might have taken the harrowing episode as a warning about the risks of expanding the war — especially as he weighs possible ground operations inside Iranian territory. Had Iranian forces found the colonel first, Trump could have found himself in exactly the hostage situation that he repeatedly notes ended Jimmy Carter’s presidency.

He did not take it as a warning. He took it as permission.

“The fact that we were able to pull off both of these operations, without a SINGLE American killed, or even wounded, just proves once again that we have achieved overwhelming Air Dominance and Superiority over the Iranian skies,” Trump wrote Saturday night.

By Easter Sunday morning, he had a new ultimatum: power plants and bridges on Tuesday, 8:00 PM Eastern. “Open the Fuckin’ Strait, you crazy bastards, or you’ll be living in Hell.” He added “Praise be to Allah.” He signed it “President DONALD J. TRUMP.”

Tom Nichols had described the Wednesday prime-time address as “a man who looked and sounded tired.” The Easter Sunday post was something different — not low energy but unmoored. Senator Schumer: “ranting like an unhinged madman.” Marjorie Taylor Greene: “insane.” Representative Don Bacon, a Nebraska Republican who has backed the war: “Americans don’t want their President to be profane and vulgar. Part of leadership is self-control.”

Erika Solomon, writing in the Times, identified the structural problem: both sides are now emboldened. Iranian state media published photographs of the charred American aircraft and declared the downing of three American planes in three days “a triumph of divine grace.” Ghalibaf on social media: “If the United States gets three more victories like this, it will be utterly ruined.”

Trump on the rescue: proof of air dominance. Iran on the shoot-down: proof the skies are not dominated.

Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group: “From this point on, this war will become even more dangerous than it was before. This is exactly the kind of escalation trap that results in mission creep — if you constantly think that with more targeting and more pressure you could eventually force the Iranians to capitulate.”

They will not capitulate. This assessment has been consistent since Day One. It remains consistent on Day Thirty-Eight.

III. The Ultimatum Timeline

Anushka Patil, writing in the Times, has compiled the definitive timeline of Trump’s deadlines over the Strait of Hormuz. It deserves to be read as a single document:

March 21: 48 hours or power plants. Iran: the strait is open to everyone except enemies.

March 23: “Productive conversations.” Pentagon postponed strikes five days. Iran publicly denied any talks were underway.

March 26: Wall Street tanked. Deadline extended 10 days to April 6 at 8 PM Eastern. Trump said he was “pausing the period of Energy Plant destruction” at Iran’s request.

March 30: “Great progress.” New threats: all power plants, all oil wells, Kharg Island, possibly all desalination plants.

April 1: Trump said Iran asked for a cease-fire. Iran: “false and baseless.” Cease-fire only when strait “open, free and clear.” “Back to the Stone Ages!!!”

April 4: “Time is running out — 48 hours before all Hell will reign down on them.”

April 6: The original extended deadline. No deal. No power plant strikes.

April 8 (Tuesday), 8:00 PM Eastern: The new deadline. Power Plant Day and Bridge Day.

Carlos Lozada documented the “ahead of schedule” tic in last week’s Times column. The deadline has a companion tic: it cannot hold a date. Each extension is presented as evidence of productive diplomacy. Iran publicly denies the diplomacy each time. The deadline moves. The pattern repeats.

The strait remains closed.

IV. The Intelligence That Was Ignored

Brendan Smialowski / AFP / Getty

Shane Harris, writing in The Atlantic, has produced the definitive intelligence accountability piece of the war. The argument is precise and will endure.

This is not an intelligence failure in the Iraq sense. The intelligence community did not get Iran wrong. It got Iran right — completely, consistently, on every major question. The failure is different in kind and more damning in implication.

The Defense Intelligence Agency concluded that Iran’s ability to build an ICBM capable of reaching the United States would take until 2035, and only if Iran was determined to pursue it, which analysts concluded it was not. Trump said Iran “would soon have had missiles that could reach the American homeland, Europe, and virtually any other place on Earth.” Director of National Intelligence Gabbard’s congressional testimony confirmed the 2035 timeline. Trump’s March 2 Medal of Honor ceremony speech said otherwise.

The intelligence community publicly reported in 2025 that Iran was “capable of inflicting substantial damage to an attacker, executing regional strikes, and disrupting shipping, particularly energy supplies, through the Strait of Hormuz.” Trump on March 16, after Iran began striking Gulf neighbors: “They weren’t supposed to go after all these other countries. Nobody expected that. We were shocked.” Hegseth at a March 10 press conference: “I can’t say that we anticipated necessarily that’s exactly how they would react, but we knew it was a possibility.”

Officials from two Arab countries warned Trump and his top aides before the war that Iran could launch counterattacks on them, driving up prices and triggering a global economic crisis. Senior European intelligence concluded the same and shared it with Washington. The Americans were aware of those conclusions. Qatar’s officials, meeting with Harris in early February as US warships were moving into position, pointed out the obvious: a war could make it impossible for Qatar to produce and ship liquefied natural gas. That is exactly what happened.

Joe Kent, Trump’s own nominee to run the National Counterterrorism Center, resigned. His letter: “Iran posed no imminent threat to our nation.”

Senator Ossoff asked Gabbard directly: did the intelligence community assess the threat as imminent? Gabbard said only the president can determine what is imminent. Harris notes: “In fact, it is precisely the job of the intelligence community to make that determination.”

Harris’s formulation is the sharpest single sentence written about this war: “Two decades ago, a president embraced information that turned out to be wrong, and disaster followed. Today, a president disregards assessments that proved to be right, and the predictable comes to pass. There’s a failure of intelligence there too — just not the kind we’re used to seeing.”

V. The War Crimes Accounting

President Trump wrote on social media that “all Hell will reign down” on Iran unless it opens the Strait of Hormuz.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

Edward Wong, writing in the Times, has produced the legal accountability record this war requires and has not previously received in consolidated form.

No other recent American president has talked so openly about committing potential war crimes. Wartime presidents and their aides have usually insisted they were trying to follow international law — even when they violated it. This administration has taken a different approach.

Trump in a January NYT Oval Office interview: “I don’t need international law.” Asked what limits exist on his global powers: “Yeah, there is one thing. My own morality.”

Hegseth: “no quarter, no mercy for our enemies.” A “no quarter” order — to kill all enemy soldiers, including the wounded and those who surrender — is a war crime under international law and the US military code. Hegseth has also fired uniformed lawyers and dismantled offices established to prevent civilian targeting, replacing their function with a single directive: increase “lethality.”

Active-duty Marines are calling the Pentagon the “Department of War Crimes” rather than the “Department of War,” per Representative Seth Moulton, who served in Iraq as a Marine.

One hundred legal experts signed an open letter to Just Security finding that the conduct of the war raises “serious concerns about violations of international humanitarian law, including potential war crimes.” Yale law professor Oona Hathaway, who co-wrote the letter and has worked as special counsel at the Pentagon: “It’s something so clearly unlawful and deeply misguided. It’s hard to fathom how much the rules have been completely thrown out.”

The specific acts already committed or threatened: Strikes that killed nearly 200 schoolchildren in one missile strike. The B1 bridge strike that killed eight Nowruz picnickers. The Mahshahr petrochemical complex — 170 wounded, two years to rebuild, 300,000 residents’ primary employer effectively destroyed. The Bushehr nuclear power plant perimeter struck, one killed, IAEA notified. Forty-six Iranian Red Crescent ambulances damaged, four aid workers killed. An Iranian frigate torpedoed near Sri Lanka — the US asked Sri Lanka to rescue survivors but did not do so itself, a possible Geneva Convention violation. Trump on the frigate: “They said, ‘It’s more fun to sink them.’”

Now: the announced intention to strike every power plant in Iran “probably simultaneously.” Deliberate attacks on civilian infrastructure supplying power to 93 million people. Under any definition of international humanitarian law, this constitutes a war crime.

Karoline Leavitt: “This administration and the United States armed forces will always act within the confines of the law.” The law the president has said he does not need.

VI. “Ahead of Schedule” — The Energy Edition, Week Two

Idrees Kahloon, writing in The Atlantic, has produced the clearest account of why Trump believes he can walk away from the strait: America is largely insulated from the pain its actions are causing. The dynamic is like a psychology experiment played out on a global scale. America can administer shocks to other countries without feeling much pain itself.

The Philippines has declared a state of emergency and may ground civilian aircraft. Japan is cutting ferry services and shutting bathhouses. South Korea is restricting jet fuel exports. Violence is breaking out at gas stations in Pakistan and Bangladesh. European LNG prices are 60 percent higher than before the war. Britain and Italy, where electricity comes disproportionately from gas-fired power plants, face the hardest shortfall. The last tankers from Qatar before Iran bombed Ras Laffan have now arrived. Supplies will diminish quickly. Iran’s attacks destroyed 17 percent of Qatar’s LNG-exporting capacity — years to repair.

Meanwhile Russia could recoup an additional $40 billion in oil exports this year, plowed directly into its war effort against Ukraine. Iran’s oil production may be as high as before the war — earning, according to The Economist, almost twice as much per day due to higher prices. Iran now charges $2 million tolls to the few ships it allows through.

Michael Froman of the Council on Foreign Relations: “Things that Iran and Russia had sought to achieve through negotiations with the United States, they’ve managed to achieve without having to negotiate.”

Macron in Seoul, asked about Trump’s management of the crisis: “When we’re serious, we don’t say the opposite of what we said the day before every day, and maybe one shouldn’t speak every day.”

OPEC Plus warned Sunday of a slow recovery after the war: “Restoring damaged energy assets to full capacity is both costly and takes a long time.” Brent crude: $111 Sunday, up 50 percent since February 28.

Robert Pape, professor of political science at the University of Chicago, published his strategic verdict Monday morning in the Times: the war is not weakening Iran. It is making Iran a fourth center of global power. Not through military capability or economic scale — through control of the world’s most important energy chokepoint. The conventional wisdom assumed a three-pole world: US, China, Russia. That assumption no longer holds.

Iran does not need to physically close the strait to control it. It needs only to hit a cargo ship every few days. Insurance markets do the rest. Modern economies require oil delivered on time, at scale, with predictable risk. When that reliability breaks down, the market seizes. Macron said as much on Thursday: it is “unrealistic” to open the strait by force. “This can only be done in concert with Iran.” He was admitting that oil flows cannot be guaranteed without Iran’s agreement.

Pape’s darker scenario: Iran controlling 20 percent of the world’s oil, Russia 11 percent, China absorbing much of the surplus. A cartel denying the West 30 percent of global supply — not through formal alliance but through converging incentives. The United States faces a binary choice: commit to a long-term military presence to reassert control, or accept a new global energy arrangement. “The Iran war is not a military conflict from which the United States can simply back out, with things reverting to how they were before.” Iran would demand a heavy price in any accommodation. But that price, Pape writes, will be less costly than the alternative future.

VII. The Second Strait — and the Ground Option

An advisor to Iran’s new supreme leader, Ali Akbar Velayati, threatened on Sunday to close the Bab al-Mandab — the narrow shipping route at the southern end of the Red Sea. The Houthis are already there. They have been firing at Israel since Saturday. If the Bab al-Mandab also closes, the shipping routes avoiding Hormuz via the Cape of Good Hope face a second blockade. There is no third route.

Trump is simultaneously weighing a ground operation. Five thousand Marines, paratroopers, and special forces have arrived in the region. The Times published a detailed map analysis of what opening the strait by force would actually require. Iran controls a string of militarized islands — Qeshm, Larak, Hormuz, Abu Musa, Greater and Lesser Tunb — that sit across the shipping lanes. To open the strait, military experts say the United States would need to take all of them. Qeshm is heavily militarized. Larak has ship-monitoring capabilities and controls the route Iran uses for the few permitted ships. Even taking every island would not guarantee shipping returns — Iran can still fire missiles and drones from the mainland, and insurers and shipping companies must also be convinced it is safe.

To reach Kharg Island — where 90 percent of Iranian oil is exported — amphibious forces would have to travel 500 miles into the Persian Gulf. Mark Cancian of CSIS, a retired Marine colonel: “That would be very risky.” Trump to Fox News Sunday: “If they don’t make a deal and fast, I’m considering blowing everything up and taking over the oil.” He has not explained how US forces would hold the island, or why Iran would not simply destroy its own oil installations rather than surrender them.

The riskiest option remains the seizure of 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade uranium from a deep underground storage site in Isfahan — near where the colonel was rescued. Military officials say the risk of casualties is high. Trump did not take this risk in his first term. Sanger notes something has changed: five years in office, a team that does not push back, and the confidence built from Midnight Hammer and the Caracas bedroom. Steve Witkoff, Trump’s special envoy, said Trump was “curious” at the start of the war why Iran hadn’t capitulated. Thirty-five days in, he is still asking.

VIII. The Interceptors Are Running Low

A Ukrainian air-defense system intercepting a drone in Kyiv in 2023. The war in Ukraine helped highlight the crucial role interceptors play in protecting cities.Credit...Evgeniy Maloletka/Associated Press

Nicholas Kulish, writing in the Times, has documented a vulnerability that has received insufficient attention amid the daily strike tallies.

The UAE has intercepted 23 cruise missiles, 498 ballistic missiles, and 2,141 drones since February 28. JINSA estimates it has expended more than three-quarters of its PAC-3 interceptor stockpiles. The defensive math is brutal: defense doctrine calls for two interceptors per incoming missile. The interceptors are expensive, made to order, require advanced electronics and significant testing. The drones they are shooting down cost tens of thousands of dollars. The interceptors cost millions.

Tom Karako of the Center for Strategic and International Studies: “We started this conflict with a big hole. The hole got a lot bigger over the last month.”

If stockpiles deplete to critical levels, cities like Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, Tel Aviv, and American bases across the Gulf lose their protection. Iran knows this. The drone campaign is not failing — it is winning by arithmetic, even as individual drones are intercepted. The accumulation is the strategy.

A cease-fire would not solve the global interceptor shortage. Production cannot be quickly ramped up. The war has created a vulnerability in the defensive architecture that will outlast the war itself.

IX. What Remains

The week began with a prime-time address claiming the war was “nearing completion.” It ended with:

A colonel rescued from a mountain crevice in southwest Iran by Navy SEAL Team 6 in the most complex special operations mission in American history. Two C-130s blown up in the Iranian desert. Three replacement planes carrying the commandos and the colonel into the rising sun.

An Easter Sunday Truth Social post: power plants and bridges on Tuesday. “Praise be to Allah.”

A war crimes accounting from one hundred legal experts and the historical record of a president who said he does not need international law.

An intelligence accounting demonstrating the community was right about everything and was disregarded.

A petrochemical complex in Mahshahr — the economic heart of Khuzestan Province — shut down entirely, 300,000 residents’ primary employer, two years to rebuild.

Two political prisoners executed Easter Sunday for involvement in the January protests. At least thirteen political prisoners executed since the war began.

Iran’s advisor to the new supreme leader threatening the Bab al-Mandab.

Interceptor stockpiles depleting across the Gulf.

Narges Mohammadi — Nobel Peace Prize laureate, heart attack on March 24, denied hospital access — still in her prison cell.

And the April 6 deadline, the one that was supposed to be the last extension, having passed without incident, superseded by Tuesday’s.

The colonel came home. Everything else remains.

X. Monday Morning, Day Thirty-Eight

The April 6 deadline — the one that was supposed to be the final extension — passed without incident. No power plant strikes. The new deadline is Tuesday, April 8, 8:00 PM Eastern. Power Plant Day and Bridge Day.

Overnight into Monday, Israel killed Major General Seyed Majid Khademi, head of intelligence for the IRGC, in a strike on Tehran. Israel’s defense minister Katz: “We are continuing to hunt them down, one by one.” Israel also struck Sharif University of Technology — one of Iran’s most elite institutions, a recruiting ground for top American universities. Gas was cut to the surrounding neighborhood. Streets in the Azadi district were blocked. The wave of strikes on Iranian universities has now reached the institution that most represents what Iran’s educated class was building before the war began.

Iran’s armed forces responded before dawn: “If attacks on civilian targets are repeated, the subsequent phases of our offensive and retaliatory operations will be carried out much more crushingly and extensively.” Iran’s deputy foreign minister Gharibabadi wrote on social media: “The President of the United States has publicly threatened to commit war crimes. It is recommended that, before the name of the US President is recorded in history as a major war criminal, he cease these threats — whose consequences will not be limited to Iran alone.”

Iran’s deputy health minister reported that more than 360 medical, healthcare, educational, and research centers have been targeted in US-Israeli strikes. Twenty-four medics killed. One hundred sixteen injured. Forty-four ambulances out of service.

Gas prices Monday: national average $4.12 a gallon, up 38 percent since February 28. Diesel: $5.62, up 49 percent. Brent crude: $109, up 60 percent. Japan’s prime minister announced she would seek talks with Iran, perhaps as soon as Wednesday. A Kyodo News poll found 90 percent of Japanese say they are worried about the war’s impact on daily life. Japan imports 95 percent of its oil from the Middle East.

The power plants are still standing. The bridges are still standing. The strait is still closed. Tuesday’s deadline is now hours away.

And Robert Pape, professor of political science at the University of Chicago, published his verdict in Monday’s Times: the war is not weakening Iran. It is creating a fourth center of global power. “A new order emerges — not through a formal alliance, but through converging incentives that reinforce one another over time.” Iran does not need to close the strait. It needs only to hit a cargo ship every few days. Insurance markets do the rest. The United States faces a binary choice: commit to a long-term effort to reassert control, or accept a new global energy arrangement in which US control is no longer assured. “The Iran war is not a military conflict from which the United States can simply back out, with things reverting to how they were before.”

The colonel came home from the mountains of Iran on Saturday night. Everything else remains.
