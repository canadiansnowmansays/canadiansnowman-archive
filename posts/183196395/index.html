The Ban That Wasn’t

Part 18 of The Epstein Files: How Trump’s “Heroic” Story Collapses Under Scrutiny

January 1, 2026

Mar-a-Lago club

January 1, 2026

On New Year’s Day 2026, Donald Trump woke up to something he rarely faces: his own media allies exposing one of his most cherished lies.

The Wall Street Journal and the New York Post—both owned by Rupert Murdoch, both historically sympathetic to Trump—published reporting that systematically dismantles the cornerstone of Trump’s defense against questions about his relationship with Jeffrey Epstein: the claim that he heroically banned the sex trafficker from Mar-a-Lago out of moral outrage.

The truth, as documented by Trump’s former media protectors, is far uglier. And it’s hidden in three words Trump has spent years hoping no one would notice.

The Critical Distinction

The Wall Street Journal’s headline reads: “The Incident That Prompted Trump to Ban Epstein From the Mar-a-Lago Spa.”

Read that again. Not Mar-a-Lago. The spa.

For years, Trump has portrayed himself as the one man who saw through Jeffrey Epstein and took action. “I was the only one, or one of the only ones, who banned him,” Trump told reporters. “I had him banned from clubs and various other things, and Mar-a-Lago in particular.”

The carefully crafted narrative suggests moral clarity: Trump recognized Epstein was dangerous and threw him out of his club. End of story. Case closed. Move along.

But the Journal’s reporting, based on interviews with former Mar-a-Lago employees and Epstein staff, reveals something Trump’s vague timeline has obscured: Epstein wasn’t banned from Mar-a-Lago itself. He was banned from using the spa.

That’s not a small distinction. That’s the entire deception.

According to the Journal, what actually happened was this: For years, the Mar-a-Lago spa sent its employees—primarily young women—to Epstein’s nearby Palm Beach mansion for “massages, manicures, and other services.” The spa wasn’t just a facility Epstein visited. It was a source. A pipeline.

Employees warned each other about Epstein. They knew his reputation. They knew he was sexually aggressive, that he exposed himself, that these “appointments” felt less like spa services and more like entries in what would later be documented as a trafficking operation.

The Wall Street Journal uses the phrase “sexually suggestive behavior.” In plain language: sexual harassment and sexual assault.

This went on for years. While Trump and Epstein were close friends, attending parties together, flying on each other’s planes, trading stories about women. While Trump was praising Epstein to New York Magazine in 2002 as a “terrific guy” who “likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side.”

Then came what the Journal calls “the incident.”

An 18-year-old manicurist was sent to Epstein’s house. She came back and reported to her managers that Epstein had pressured her for sex. Her father found out. According to the reporting, he was furious and “raised hell.”

Suddenly, there was talk of Epstein being banned.

But notice what the reporting reveals: banned from the spa. Not from the club. Not from Mar-a-Lago entirely. From the facility that had been supplying him with young women.

The ban wasn’t a moral stand. It was damage control.

“I’m Not Banned”

Jeffrey Epstein and Donald Trump at Mar-a-Lago in 1997

The 2007 New York Post article—also under Murdoch’s ownership—adds another devastating detail.

The Post reported that Mar-a-Lago had confirmed Epstein was banned because he “used the spa to procure girls, including an 18-year-old masseuse whose father went absolutely apeshit when he found out what Epstein tried to do.”

That sounds definitive. Banned. Out. Done.

Except Epstein himself told the Post: “I’m not banned.”

According to Epstein, he had been “recently invited to an event at Mar-a-Lago.” He disputed the entire premise of the ban.

And here’s what should alarm everyone: Trump never contradicted this.

No denial. No correction. No “that’s a lie, he’s not welcome here.” Just silence.

So we have two Murdoch publications—publications that have historically carried water for Trump—telling us:

  1. The spa sent girls to Epstein’s house for years

  2. Employees warned each other about him

  3. An 18-year-old reported being pressured for sex

  4. Mar-a-Lago says he was banned

  5. Epstein says he wasn’t banned and was recently invited

  6. Trump says nothing

If the ban were real and principled, why wouldn’t Trump correct Epstein’s claim? Why allow a convicted sex offender to publicly state he’s still welcome at your club without immediately refuting it?

The simplest explanation is the most damning: because Epstein was telling the truth.

The Timeline Tells the Truth

Membership records provide the final piece of evidence that destroys Trump’s heroic narrative.

According to reporting, Epstein’s membership account at Mar-a-Lago wasn’t closed until October 2007.

October 2007. The same year Epstein pleaded guilty in Florida to soliciting prostitution from a minor and soliciting prostitution. The same year he received his infamous sweetheart deal from then-U.S. Attorney Alex Acosta—the same Acosta Trump would later appoint as Labor Secretary.

If Trump had banned Epstein years earlier out of moral outrage over the spa incident—which employees and reporting suggest occurred around 2003—why was Epstein’s membership account still active in 2007?

The answer is obvious: The “ban” came when Epstein became radioactive, not when Trump discovered his conduct.

This is the pattern we’ve seen throughout Trump’s relationship with Epstein. As long as Epstein was useful, wealthy, connected, and discrete, Trump maintained the friendship. Trump flew on Epstein’s plane. Trump attended Epstein’s parties. Trump invited Epstein to Mar-a-Lago events. Trump praised Epstein publicly.

The relationship only ended when Epstein’s legal troubles made the association embarrassing. That’s not moral courage. That’s self-preservation.

What Trump Himself Has Admitted

The most damaging evidence comes from Trump’s own mouth.

In 2019, aboard Air Force One, Trump was asked about Epstein’s recruitment at Mar-a-Lago. His response confirms everything the Wall Street Journal and New York Post are now reporting:

Trump said people from the spa “were taken to work for Epstein.” He said other employees complained. He said he told Epstein not to take “our people.” He said when Epstein did it again, he told him to get “out of here.”

When asked if that included Virginia Giuffre—the trafficking survivor who would later accuse Prince Andrew—Trump said he “thinks” she worked at the spa. He “thinks” she was one of the people Epstein recruited.

Then Trump added, as if it somehow exonerated him: “She had no complaints about us.”

Read that again. A woman who was trafficked from his club has “no complaints about us.” As if the issue is Mar-a-Lago’s Yelp rating rather than the fact that the club became a recruiting ground for a serial predator.

Trump is admitting on Air Force One that:

  1. Epstein recruited from his spa

  2. Multiple employees were involved

  3. Other staff complained

  4. He had to tell Epstein to stop

  5. Epstein did it again

  6. Virginia Giuffre was likely one of the victims

This isn’t a denial. It’s a confession wrapped in justification.

Why This Matters Now

The significance of this reporting isn’t just historical. It’s actively relevant to the ongoing crisis over the Epstein files.

As documented in Parts 14 through 17 of this series, the Department of Justice has violated federal law by failing to release the full scope of Epstein-related documents by the December 19, 2025 deadline mandated by Congress. The document count has exploded from 100,000 pages released on December 19 to a claimed 5.2 million pages as of December 30—a number that conveniently provides justification for continued delays.

Among those unreleased documents are likely extensive records about Mar-a-Lago, about the spa, about Virginia Giuffre’s employment, about Epstein’s membership, about when that membership actually ended, and about what Trump knew and when he knew it.

Trump has ordered the DOJ to investigate Bill Clinton’s “involvement and relationship” with Epstein while simultaneously claiming the Epstein files release is “a Democrat inspired hoax” that’s wasting the department’s time.

The pattern is clear: weaponize the files against political opponents while suppressing information about himself.

But now even Rupert Murdoch’s media empire—which has functioned as Trump’s praetorian guard for years—is poking holes in the narrative. The Wall Street Journal and New York Post aren’t left-wing outlets. They can’t be dismissed as “fake news” or partisan hit jobs.

When your own allies start documenting your lies, the lies become harder to sustain.

The Accommodation That Preceded the “Ban”

What the Murdoch reporting makes undeniable is this: Before there was any ban, there were years of accommodation.

Years of Mar-a-Lago’s spa sending young women to Epstein’s mansion while employees whispered warnings to each other. Years of Trump and Epstein’s friendship continuing while this pipeline operated. Years of Trump praising Epstein publicly even after Epstein was recruiting from his club.

The accommodation only ended when it threatened to become public scandal. When an 18-year-old’s father “raised hell.” When the quiet arrangement that had worked for years suddenly risked exposure.

That’s not heroism. That’s the same accommodation psychology that has characterized Trump’s entire relationship with Epstein and that now characterizes the DOJ’s handling of the Epstein files.

Accommodate until forced to act. Act only enough to contain immediate damage. Claim credit for the minimal action while obscuring the years of complicity that preceded it.

The “ban” Trump brags about follows the exact same pattern: Do nothing for years, respond only when forced, minimize the response, exaggerate the action, and hope nobody looks at the timeline.

What Comes Next

Congress reconvenes January 5. Senator Chuck Schumer’s resolution directing legal action against DOJ for violating the transparency law will force a vote. Representatives Thomas Massie and Ro Khanna’s inherent contempt charges against Attorney General Pam Bondi will follow.

Meanwhile, the 5.2 million pages of Epstein files remain largely unreleased. Among them, almost certainly, are documents that would provide a fuller picture of Mar-a-Lago’s role in Epstein’s operation. Documents that would show whether the “ban” was real or performative. Documents that would reveal what Trump knew, when he knew it, and what he did about it.

The Wall Street Journal and New York Post reporting, based on employee interviews and historical records, has already demolished Trump’s core narrative. The ban was from the spa, not the club. It came only after years of the spa supplying Epstein with young women. Epstein himself said he wasn’t banned. Trump never contradicted him. The membership wasn’t closed until Epstein’s guilty plea made the association toxic.

Part 19 of this series will examine what Mar-a-Lago actually was during those years: not just a backdrop to this story, but part of its machinery. How the recruitment system worked. What employees witnessed. What Trump admitted on Air Force One. And why the documents still being withheld matter so much.

For now, the takeaway is simpler: The heroic ban story is a lie. Trump’s own former media allies just proved it. And the lies are getting harder to hide.

The test continues.
