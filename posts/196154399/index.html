The Magician’s Gambit

Part II: The Child Prodigy Who Wants to Be Boring

May 2, 2026

By Stephen Gordon | CanadianSnowmanSays

In the race to succeed Jerry Nadler in New York’s Twelfth District — the smallest, richest, most educated congressional district in the country — the candidates have made their identities clear.

Jack Schlossberg is the grandson of a president. George Conway is the ex-husband of a White House counsellor and the co-founder of the Lincoln Project. Alex Bores is the first Democrat elected to the New York State Assembly with a computer science degree.

And Micah Lasher is the one who fired a journalist. At age twelve.

The Research Assistant Problem

The journalist’s name is Joel Stein. He is now a writer at the Atlantic. In 1994, fresh out of college and fact-checking at Reader’s Digest Books, Stein answered an ad for a freelance research assistant paying $20 an hour — $2 more than he was making. The employer was a child magician who had already accomplished Stein’s two great dreams: getting a book deal and appearing on David Letterman.

Stein’s assignment was to dig up interesting tidbits from the history of magic. He could not distinguish between a tidbit and a basic piece of magic history. Lasher’s father had to call and inform him that his services were no longer required.

“Joel’s research was his first real trip into the world of magic,” Lasher wrote in the acknowledgments of The Magic of Micah Lasher: More Than Fifty Tricks That Will Amaze and Delight Everyone—Including You, published in 1996, “and he did a really great job.”

This is the elegant graciousness, Stein notes, that one learns from writing stacks of bar mitzvah thank-you notes. It is also, whether Lasher knew it at twelve or knows it now at forty-four, the foundational political skill: leaving people feeling better about an outcome than the outcome warrants.

Micah Lasher has been doing politics his entire life. He just hasn’t always called it that.

The Close-Up Man

He was twelve. He already had a book deal. Joel Stein was twenty-two and fact-checking at Reader's Digest. Some things are decided early.

At the height of his magic career, Lasher was famous for close-up magic — the kind performed inches from a single person, cards and coins and sleight of hand so precise it reads less like performance and more like a violation of the physical world. He was not, and never wanted to be, a David Copperfield. No stage. No audience of thousands. No grand illusion requiring a budget and a production team.

This is not an incidental detail. It is the analytical key to understanding what kind of politician Micah Lasher is — or is trying to be.

Stage magic is about the spectacle. It works at distance. The mechanism can be crude if the scale is large enough. Close-up magic is the opposite: it works because of precision, because of the complete management of attention, because the hand you’re watching is never the hand doing the work. It requires you to be, in some fundamental sense, smarter than the person in front of you — and to deploy that intelligence in a way they experience as delight rather than manipulation.

His opponents in the Twelfth District are, almost uniformly, stage magicians.

Schlossberg has 1.7 million social media followers. He walks and talks simultaneously. He surfs. He mocks his cousin. He is, as the Atlantic describes him, someone who promotes himself as a politician who “knows how to function in this low-attention economy.” Conway’s entire campaign is a grand illusion: the Republican-turned-Democrat, the converted sinner, the man whose personal drama was so large and so public that it became its own kind of performance even when he was simply showing up at a press conference.

Lasher’s card tricks work inches from your face. His campaign is built for the same proximity.

The Philosophy of Impossible Things

The man who fired Joel Stein at twelve. Now running to succeed Jerry Nadler at forty-four.

Here is what Lasher says magic taught him about policy, in his own words:

“Great magicians start with a goal and work backwards. There is a way that I could walk into this diner, talk to you for 30 minutes, and make myself disappear. It’s simply a function of figuring out the mechanics. In policymaking, I found that one of the greatest impediments to changemaking is that there’s a ton of self-limitation, risk aversion, and lack of imagination. Spending years as a kid looking at things that were impossible with the presumption that there was a way to do it was a pretty good discipline for policymaking.”

This is not a campaign slogan. It is a genuine philosophy, and it is more interesting than anything the other candidates in this race have said about why they want to go to Congress.

Schlossberg talks about fighting Trump. Conway talks about fighting Trump. Lasher talks about what happens after you win the fight — about the mechanics of change, the architecture of policy, the assumption that the impossible outcome is achievable if you work backwards from it with sufficient precision.

From a Canadian perspective, this is the thing that gets lost in every American political campaign I have watched from across the border: the confusion of the fight with the goal. Winning the argument is not the same as passing the law. Defeating the villain is not the same as building the institution. American political culture, particularly in the Trump era, has become so focused on the performance of resistance that the question of what comes after is treated almost as an afterthought.

Lasher’s entire career — Nadler, Bloomberg, Hochul, the state assembly — has been spent in the machinery of what comes after. He is, at forty-four, constitutionally incapable of being the most exciting person in the room. He knows this. He is, in his own telling, better at close-up magic than at putting on a show.

The Twelfth District is about to decide whether that matters.

The Boring Candidate Problem

Joel Stein’s Atlantic piece is, among other things, a gentle argument for Lasher dressed as a profile of a former child magician. Stein’s affection is genuine — the man once fired him at age twelve and he has clearly never recovered from the admiration.

But the piece also identifies the central problem with Lasher’s candidacy: in a race that features a Kennedy grandson, a Lincoln Project co-founder, and a former Palantir data scientist, Lasher has somehow become both the most connected and the least exciting option at the table.

Consider the endorsement stack. Jerry Nadler — the retiring incumbent whose seat this is — has called Lasher the candidate who “stands above the rest.” Governor Kathy Hochul, for whom Lasher served as director of policy, has declared him exactly who New York needs in Washington. Former Mayor Michael Bloomberg is prepared to spend roughly $5 million to make it happen.

This is not a grassroots insurgency. It is the full weight of the New York Democratic establishment placing a bet.

And yet: as of April 2026, Bores, Conway, Lasher, and Schlossberg led in polling, fundraising, and local media attention. The polling tells a specific story — Schlossberg leads at 25%, Conway sits second at 16%, with Lasher and Bores tied at 11%.

The insider candidate, carrying the retiring incumbent’s blessing, a former mayor’s $5 million, and the governor’s imprimatur, is tied for last.

Here is the magic trick hiding in plain sight: Lasher is simultaneously the most establishment candidate in the race and the one running on a message of institutional competence over performative resistance. In a district that just watched democracy wobble, he is asking voters to choose the mechanic over the fighter. That is a harder sell than it sounds, and he knows it — which is why, if you watch him carefully, he never quite admits that is what he is asking.

The hand you’re watching is never the hand doing the work.

This is not because Lasher is unqualified. He is, by any objective measure, the most conventionally qualified candidate in the race. He has worked in policy at every level of New York government. He knows where the machinery is and how to operate it. He fired Joel Stein at twelve, which suggests an early and sophisticated understanding of the principle that competence and likeability are not the same thing.

The problem is that the Twelfth District is voting in June 2026. Donald Trump is president. Democracy is, depending on your level of alarm, either under assault or undergoing a stress test. In that environment, the question voters in a deeply liberal Manhattan district are asking themselves is not “who knows the machinery?” It is “who will fight?”

Lasher’s answer — I will fight, and then I will do the work of governing, which is harder and more important than the fight — is the most honest answer in the race. It may also be the most politically inconvenient.

The Stuyvesant Moment

There is one episode in Lasher’s biography that deserves more attention than it has received.

At fifteen, while a student at Stuyvesant High School, Lasher became editor of the school newspaper and got into a fight with the administration over censorship. The fight was covered in the New York Times.

A fifteen-year-old who goes to war with a school administration over press freedom, generates enough public attention to land in the Times, and then goes on to spend the next three decades in Democratic politics — that is not a boring candidate. That is a candidate who has been careful, for thirty years, to reserve his fights for things that matter.

Close-up magic. Inches from your face. The hand you’re watching is never the hand doing the work.

Whether the Twelfth District notices before June 23rd is the only question that matters.

The View From Here

I watch this race from Toronto, where I was thirteen years old when a president was shot in Dallas and the word Camelot acquired a weight it has never entirely shed. I started this series because Jack Schlossberg is running for Congress and the dynasty question seemed worth examining.

But Micah Lasher is the candidate who makes me think about something different: what democracy actually requires of the people it sends to Washington, and whether the skills most valued in the current political moment — the ability to perform resistance at scale, to generate content, to function in a low-attention economy — are the skills that build institutions or merely the skills that win primaries.

He stopped doing magic because it was crowding out other things. He wanted to be his own person.

On June 23rd, New York’s Twelfth District will decide whether his own person is what they’re looking for.
