Trump vs. The Fed

Why the President is Waging War on America's Central Bank

Trump is Following Erdoğan's Playbook to Control America's Central Bank

August 27, 2025

Lisa Cook, governor of the US Federal Reserve, and Donald Trump. Composite: Bloomberg via Getty Images, AP

TL;DR: Trump just fired a Federal Reserve governor for the first time in U.S. history, escalating his unprecedented attack on the Fed's independence. This isn't just about interest rates—it's about fundamentally reshaping how America's economy is governed. Here's why Trump is doing it, what it means, and why you should care.

The Nuclear Option: What Just Happened

On August 26, 2025, Donald Trump did something no president has ever attempted: he fired a sitting Federal Reserve governor. Lisa Cook, the first Black woman to serve on the Fed's Board of Governors, got the axe over allegations she lied on mortgage applications. Cook isn't going quietly—she's lawyering up with high-profile attorney Abbe Lowell and taking this fight to the Supreme Court.

The targeting is telling: Trump chose to go after Cook—a Black Democratic woman—rather than the two Fed governors he actually appointed (Christopher Waller and Michelle Bowman, both of whom recently dissented for lower rates). This isn't coincidence. Trump's rhetoric about "DEI" appointments and "woke" policies, combined with his well-documented views of women as subordinates, makes Cook the perfect target for his base while serving his Fed takeover goals.

This isn't some bureaucratic reshuffling. This is Trump dropping a bomb on one of America's most sacred economic institutions while simultaneously targeting someone who represents everything his movement opposes: a highly credentialed Black woman in a position of power, appointed by a Democratic president.

The plot thickens: The allegations against Cook came from Bill Pulte, Trump's Federal Housing Finance Agency director who has become an unlikely attack dog against the Fed. Pulte has 3 million Twitter followers and has waged a relentless social media campaign against Fed Chair Powell, even spreading false rumors on official letterhead that Powell's resignation was imminent. He's also sent similar mortgage fraud referrals against Trump political enemies like California Senator Adam Schiff and New York AG Letitia James.

The backstory makes this even wilder. In July, Trump made an unprecedented visit to the Fed's headquarters—the first president to show up there since 2006. He got into a public spat with Fed Chair Jerome Powell over renovation costs, waving documents around like he was on a reality TV show. It was peak Trump: turning a routine inspection into political theater while testing the boundaries of presidential power.

If Trump pulls this off, his appointees will control the Fed's board for the first time. That's not just winning—that's capturing the institution that controls America's money supply.

Why Trump Hates the Fed (It's Not What You Think)

The Growth Obsession

Trump's war on the Fed isn't really about economics—it's about control. Trump thinks like a businessman: cheap money equals good times. He wants the Fed to slash interest rates by 3 percentage points, which would juice the economy and make his debt-fueled agenda much cheaper to finance.

But here's the thing economists know and Trump either doesn't get or doesn't care about: artificially low rates when you're running massive tariffs is like flooring the gas pedal while pouring gasoline on a fire. It's a recipe for inflation that would make the 1970s look tame.

Trump has called Powell "Too Late" and suggested the Fed chairman's "termination cannot come fast enough." This isn't policy critique—this is personal warfare against an institution designed to say "no" to presidents who want easy money.

The Deep State Angle

Trump's Fed attacks fit perfectly into his "drain the swamp" narrative. Here's this building full of PhD economists with their fancy models, making decisions that affect every American without ever facing a single voter. For Trump's base, the Fed represents everything they hate about the establishment: unelected elites making decisions behind closed doors.

Project 2025, the conservative blueprint for Trump's presidency, wants to abolish the Fed entirely and return to the "free banking" system of the 1800s. That's not hyperbole—they literally want to go back to the era of bank runs and financial panics. The architects of this plan see the Fed as a "meddling bureaucracy" that causes more problems than it fixes.

Executive Power on Steroids

This goes way deeper than monetary policy. Trump's legal team is challenging the entire concept of independent agencies. The Justice Department now says these agencies are unconstitutional—that everything in the executive branch should answer directly to the president.

Think about what that means: no more independent Fed, no more autonomous SEC, no more separate Justice Department investigations. Everything runs through the Oval Office. It's the "unitary executive" theory taken to its logical extreme.

The Tariff Trap: How Trump Created His Own Problem

Here's where Trump's economics get really messy. His signature tariff policy directly conflicts with his monetary policy wishes, creating a contradiction that would be hilarious if it weren't so dangerous.

Trump wants massive across-the-board tariffs AND rock-bottom interest rates. Any economist will tell you that's like wanting to drive 100 mph with the brakes on. Tariffs raise prices—that's literally what they do. When prices rise, responsible central banks raise interest rates to cool things down.

The Fed has already said it's holding rates steady partly because nobody knows "what kind of tariffs are going to be imposed, how and when they'll be imposed, and what impact that will have on prices." Trump's own unpredictability is forcing the Fed to stay cautious.

European central bankers are basically saying "we don't have this problem because we're not creating policy uncertainty like the Americans." Ouch.

So Trump faces an impossible choice: abandon tariffs to get lower rates, or keep tariffs and fight the Fed. He's chosen option three: try to capture the Fed so it can't oppose his policies. It's audacious, reckless, and historically unprecedented.

What History Tells Us (Spoiler: This Ends Badly)

The 1970s Nightmare

Richard Nixon with Arthur Burns in 1968. In the run-up to the 1972 election, President Nixon, who appointed Mr. Burns as Fed chair, urged him to cut rates.Credit...Associated Press

America has tried presidential control of monetary policy before. It was called the 1970s, and it sucked.

Fed Chair Arthur Burns caved to pressure from Richard Nixon to keep rates low heading into the 1972 election. Nixon got his landslide, and America got a decade of brutal inflation. It took Paul Volcker jacking interest rates to nearly 20% in the early 1980s—triggering a massive recession—to fix the mess.

The lesson? Short-term political gains from easy money create long-term economic disasters that require painful solutions.

The Turkey Model

Want to see Trump's endgame? Look at Turkey. President Erdoğan fired central bank governors who wouldn't cut rates, replaced finance officials who disagreed, and even fired the statistician who reported high inflation numbers.

Sound familiar? Trump has already fired multiple inspectors general for investigating his administration. He's targeted statisticians who report unfavorable economic data. And now he's firing Fed governors who won't give him the rates he wants. The pattern is identical to Erdoğan's playbook.

Result in Turkey? Inflation spiraled out of control, forcing Turkey to eventually raise rates to 45%—higher than where they started. The country's currency became worthless, and its economy imploded. But Erdoğan consolidated power by eliminating anyone who could contradict his economic fantasies with inconvenient facts.

Trump isn't trying to fix the economy—he's trying to control the narrative about the economy by eliminating independent voices who might contradict him. Fire the Fed governor, fire the statistician, fire the inspector general. Keep firing until only loyalists remain to tell you what you want to hear.

Néstor Kirchner and Cristina Fernández

Argentina followed a similar path with similar results. Presidents like Néstor Kirchner and his successor (and wife) Cristina Fernández de Kirchner systematically undermined their central bank's independence, firing governors who wouldn't print money to fund government spending and replacing economists with political loyalists.

Sound familiar again? The Kirchners manipulated inflation statistics through their national statistics agency INDEC, firing economists who reported accurate data and replacing them with officials who published fake numbers showing lower inflation. They prosecuted private economists who dared publish real inflation figures.

The result? Argentina has suffered multiple currency crises, with the peso losing over 99% of its value against the dollar since 2001. Inflation has routinely hit 50-100% annually. The country has defaulted on its debt multiple times and remains largely shut out of international capital markets.

But here's the key: just like Erdoğan, the Kirchners maintained political power for years by blaming "speculators" and "foreign enemies" for the economic disasters their own policies created. When you control the central bank and the statistics agency, you can always find someone else to blame when your magical thinking meets economic reality.

Countries that compromise central bank independence get currency crashes, capital flight, and runaway inflation. But their leaders get to blame someone else when it all falls apart because they've eliminated the independent experts who could have warned them.

The Legal Battle That Could Change Everything

Supreme Court Showdown

Cook's lawsuit will likely end up before the Supreme Court, creating the first real test of presidential power over the Fed. The legal question seems narrow—can Trump fire a Fed governor "for cause" based on mortgage application issues?—but the implications are massive.

The Court has already signaled it wants to protect Fed independence, calling it "a uniquely structured, quasi-private entity" with special historical protections. But this same Court has been chipping away at other independent agencies, so who knows?

If Trump wins, future presidents will know they can remove Fed governors for minor infractions. That creates a chilling effect where Fed officials start worrying more about keeping the president happy than making good economic decisions.

If Cook wins, it strengthens the institutional barriers protecting Fed independence. But Trump has other tools—he can still pressure officials through public attacks, control over nominations, and regulatory oversight.

The Stakes Are Enormous

This isn't just about one Fed governor. It's about whether America can maintain institutions that protect long-term economic stability from short-term political pressures.

The dollar's status as the global reserve currency depends partly on international confidence in U.S. institutions. When Trump said Powell's termination "cannot come fast enough," the dollar immediately dropped. Markets are watching, and they don't like what they're seeing.

What the Markets Are Really Thinking

The Immediate Market Aftermath

Markets had a fascinating reaction that tells the real story. While stocks initially dipped but then recovered—the S&P 500 even gained 0.2% by day's end—the deeper indicators showed serious concern. The dollar weakened significantly, falling 0.3% against major currencies immediately after Trump's announcement. Treasury yields spiked, particularly on longer-dated bonds, suggesting investors are worried about long-term inflation risks.

Even more telling: gold prices rose 0.45%, and futures markets moved decisively. Dow futures fell 100 points, S&P 500 futures dropped 0.3%, and Nasdaq futures slipped 0.5%. These aren't the moves of a market that thinks this is no big deal.

The dollar fell and longer-dated Treasury yields rose as President Donald Trump's push to remove Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook fueled concern about central bank independence and inflation risks, while gold—traditionally regarded as a safe store of value in times of rising inflation—rose in reaction to the White House move.

Here's the counterintuitive part: undermining Fed independence could lead to higher, not lower, interest rates. If the independence of the Federal Reserve to fight inflation is seen as compromised, it actually will lead not to lower interest rates, but higher interest rates on things like mortgages and small business loans, because people will be anticipating that inflation will go up.

Trump thinks capturing the Fed will give him easy money. But if markets decide the Fed can't be trusted to fight inflation, money becomes expensive fast. "The more the market thinks the White House is running Fed policy, the higher longer-term rates like mortgage rates will go," warned former Fed official Narayana Kocherlakota.

Media Consensus: This Is a Big Deal

The media coverage has been remarkably unified in treating this as a constitutional crisis. MSNBC's Rachel Maddow blog called Trump's offensive against Cook a "five-alarm fire" that "ignores the rule of law and puts global economic stability at risk." Lawrence O'Donnell's show examined how Trump is targeting "the first-ever Black woman on the Federal Reserve board," with legal experts like Andrew Weissmann analyzing the constitutional implications.

The consensus across major outlets is clear: this isn't normal presidential pressure on the Fed. This is an unprecedented attack on institutional independence that could reshape American governance. Even typically measured financial outlets are using language like "dramatic escalation" and "uncharted waters."

China and Russia have been pushing for alternatives to dollar-dominated trade for years. Trump's Fed attacks hand them the perfect argument: "Look, you can't trust American institutions anymore." An index tracking the U.S. dollar against a basket of foreign currencies slid on Tuesday as investors looked to international alternatives.

European Central Bank officials noted they don't face the same level of "homegrown policy uncertainty" as the Fed, precisely because they're not dealing with the kind of unpredictable policy making that creates this mess.

Trump's Real Motivations (The Stuff He Won't Say Out Loud)

The Intersectional Target

Let's be honest about what's really happening here. Trump didn't randomly pick Lisa Cook out of seven Fed governors. He targeted the first Black woman ever to serve on the Fed Board—a Biden appointee who represents everything his movement defines itself against.

Cook is brilliant: she's a Marshall Scholar with degrees from Oxford and Spelman, taught at Harvard and Michigan State, and her economic research on how racial violence suppressed Black innovation has been groundbreaking. But in Trump's worldview, she's a "DEI hire"—his dismissive term for any accomplished person of color in positions of authority.

Trump's pattern is clear: attack successful women of color in powerful positions. He's gone after Kamala Harris, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, and now Cook using the same playbook—question their qualifications, suggest they don't belong, and use dog whistles that resonate with his base while maintaining plausible deniability.

The mortgage fraud allegations are just the excuse. If Trump really cared about mortgage compliance, why not target someone who actually cost taxpayers money? Because this was never about mortgages—it's about sending a message to his supporters about who belongs in positions of power in America.

The Debt Time Bomb

Trump inherits a national debt approaching $36 trillion. Interest payments alone cost over $1 trillion annually. Lower Fed rates would save hundreds of billions in debt service, creating room for tax cuts and spending increases without politically painful choices.

This is about fiscal math, not economic theory. Trump needs cheap money to make his numbers work, and the Fed's independence stands in the way.

Personal Revenge

Donald Trump and Jerome Powell tour the Federal Reserve’s under-construction headquarters.Chip Somodevilla/Getty Images

Trump nominated Powell in 2017, expecting loyalty. Instead, Powell continued raising rates, treating his job as serving the economy rather than the president who appointed him.

Trump clearly views this as personal betrayal. His attacks on Powell go beyond policy disagreements to personal insults—calling him "low IQ" and a "numbskull." This isn't just about interest rates; it's about revenge against someone Trump feels crossed him.

Legacy and Institutional Transformation

Trump may be thinking bigger than just monetary policy. Capturing the Fed would prove that supposedly independent institutions can be brought to heel. It would fundamentally alter the balance between elected officials and technocratic expertise in American government.

If Trump succeeds, future presidents will know that institutional independence is a myth. Every "independent" agency becomes just another arm of presidential power.

The Scenarios: How This Plays Out

Trump Wins Everything

If Cook loses her court case and Trump successfully captures Fed control, we're in uncharted territory. Markets might initially celebrate lower rates, but longer-term consequences could be severe: higher inflation expectations, reduced dollar credibility, and potential financial instability.

The next economic crisis would be worse because markets wouldn't trust the Fed to respond independently of political considerations.

Fed Independence Survives

If Cook wins and institutional barriers hold, Trump would be contained but not defeated. He could still pressure Fed officials through public attacks and use his appointment power when terms expire.

Powell's term ends in May 2026. Even if he stays on the Board until 2028, Trump gets to pick the next Fed chair. That person will know what happens to Fed officials who cross this president.

Congressional Intervention

Congress could step in either direction. If Fed independence creates enough economic chaos, lawmakers might act to strengthen legal protections. But if Trump convinces enough people that the Fed is the problem, Congress might give presidents more formal monetary policy authority.

Market-Driven Resolution

Financial market instability could force a resolution. If Fed independence threats trigger serious economic turbulence, political pressure for Trump to back down could become overwhelming.

But markets can stay calm longer than institutions can survive attacks, so this isn't guaranteed.

Why You Should Care (Even If You Don't Care About Economics)

It's About Power, Not Money

This fight isn't really about interest rates or inflation—it's about whether American democracy can maintain checks and balances against presidential power. But it's also about who gets to hold that power.

Trump chose to fire the first Black woman ever to serve on the Fed Board rather than the two governors he actually appointed. That's not economics—that's sending a message. When Trump talks about "DEI" and "woke" policies, he's telling his base that people like Lisa Cook don't belong in positions of authority.

The targeting matters because it reveals Trump's broader strategy: attack institutional independence while simultaneously appealing to his base's resentments about changing demographics in positions of power. Cook represents both the Fed's independence AND the diversification of American leadership that Trump's movement wants to reverse.

Your Wallet Is at Stake

If Trump succeeds in capturing the Fed, your mortgage rates, credit card rates, and investment returns could all be driven by political calculations rather than economic fundamentals.

Countries with politicized central banks tend to have more volatile economies, higher long-term inflation, and less stable currencies. Ask anyone from Turkey or Argentina how that works out for regular people.

America's Global Standing

The dollar's special status gives Americans enormous economic privileges—cheaper imports, easier international travel, and global financial influence. That status depends on international confidence in American institutions.

Undermining Fed independence signals to the world that U.S. institutions can be captured by political pressure. That makes alternatives to the dollar more attractive and could eventually cost Americans trillions in lost economic advantages.

The Bottom Line

Trump's war on the Fed represents the most serious challenge to central bank independence in modern American history. It's driven by a mix of economic self-interest, personal grievance, and a broader vision of unconstrained executive power.

As former San Francisco Fed advisor Tim Mahedy put it: "Trump is breaking the cardinal rule of central banking: Criticize, but don't politicize. He, and all of us, will pay a steep price if he's successful in his pressure campaign—a cost that we would bear for generations."

The immediate trigger—Lisa Cook's firing—may seem like a sideshow, but it's actually the opening shot in a battle that could reshape American economic governance. If Trump wins, the post-1951 era of Fed independence ends, and we enter uncharted territory where monetary policy serves presidential politics rather than economic stability.

History suggests that political interference in monetary policy leads to economic disasters that hurt ordinary Americans while benefiting politicians in the short run. Countries that have compromised central bank independence—from Turkey to Argentina—have experienced currency volatility, capital flight, and higher inflation. As University of Michigan economist Justin Wolfers told CNN: "History teaches us what can happen when a populist strongman decides to take over a central bank."

Trump's gamble is that he can capture the benefits before the costs become apparent. Whether he's right may determine not just his political legacy, but the future structure of American economic governance itself. The Lisa Cook case is just the beginning—the real battle is for the soul of American economic institutions.

And unlike most political fights in Washington, this one has clear winners and losers. Either institutions maintain their independence, or they don't. There's no middle ground when you're firing Fed governors.

The stakes couldn't be higher, and the outcome is far from certain. Welcome to the most important economic battle you've probably never heard of.
