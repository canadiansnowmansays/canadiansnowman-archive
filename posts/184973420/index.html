Data, Lies, and Deadly Negligence

Ten days ago, I demanded DHS Secretary Kristi Noem’s resignation for lying about Renee Good’s death. Now we have proof the lies go far deeper.

January 18, 2026

Memorial for Renee Nicole Good, we need so much more.

Ten days ago, I published two essays documenting how DHS Secretary Kristi Noem lied about the circumstances of Renee Nicole Good’s death in Minneapolis. Video evidence proved Noem’s claims—that Good “attacked” agents and “attempted to run them over”—were demonstrably false. I demanded Noem’s resignation. She refused, doubled down on her lies, and promised the operations would continue “unabated.”

I warned then about normalization—about accepting state violence backed by systematic dishonesty. I argued that Renee Good’s death wasn’t an isolated tragedy but a predictable outcome of impunity.

This week, two bombshell investigations prove I was right—and reveal the situation is far worse than even I documented.

The New York Times obtained and analyzed the largest-ever trove of ICE enforcement data, exposing systematic lies about who the Trump administration is actually targeting. And Washington Post investigative reporter Nick Miroff revealed the deadly machinery behind these operations: a massively expanded, undertrained, “frazzled” ICE workforce deliberately created under conditions virtually guaranteed to produce lethal encounters.

Together, these investigations don’t just expose lies. They explain how Renee Good died—and why it will happen again.

The Big Lie: What the Data Actually Shows

For a year, the Trump administration has repeated the same justification for its massive deportation campaign: they’re targeting “criminal illegal aliens,” focusing on “the worst of the worst,” removing dangerous criminals from American communities.

DHS Secretary Noem says it constantly. President Trump tweets it. Border Patrol Chief Gregory Bovino declares it in press conferences. It’s the administration’s central talking point, repeated so often it’s become accepted as fact.

The New York Times data analysis proves it’s a lie.

The largest increase in arrests and deportations isn’t criminals. It’s people with no criminal record at all.

The numbers are devastating:

  • Interior deportations have sextupled (increased 6x) for people with zero criminal record

  • Deportations of people with violent criminal convictions only doubled

  • Trump has already deported 230,000 people arrested inside the country in one year

  • Biden deported just 50,000 total over his entire four-year term

Let that sink in: The administration claims to focus on violent criminals, but they’ve increased deportations of people with no criminal record at six times the rate they’ve increased deportations of people with violent convictions.

This isn’t border security. This is mass deportation of anyone ICE can grab.

The shift is even more dramatic when you look at the monthly rates. Under Biden in 2024, ICE deported about 280 people per month who had no criminal record. Under Trump, that rate jumped to 2,100 per month—nearly an eightfold increase.

Meanwhile, deportations of people with violent criminal convictions went from 790 per month to 1,100 per month—barely a 40% increase. People with other criminal convictions went from 2,300 to 3,900 per month—about a 70% increase.

The math is inescapable: If the administration were truly focused on violent criminals, those deportation rates would be skyrocketing. Instead, the explosive growth is in deporting people who’ve never been convicted of any crime.

The New York Times, analyzing ICE data through October, confirms this pattern in arrests—not just deportations. Of all ICE arrests during Trump’s term:

  • 37% had criminal convictions

  • 30% had been charged but not convicted

  • 33% had no criminal record at all

And here’s the damning detail: “That share [no criminal record] more than doubled from a lower number as ICE’s efforts intensified.”

Read that again: As enforcement ramped up, the percentage of people arrested with zero criminal record more than doubled. The administration isn’t shifting toward criminals—they’re deliberately shifting away from them, grabbing whoever they encounter regardless of criminal history.

This means ICE is now arresting nearly as many people with no criminal record (33%) as people with actual convictions (37%). The remaining 30% were merely charged—innocent until proven guilty under our legal system.

By any reasonable definition, the administration is targeting non-criminals at rapidly increasing rates while claiming to focus on “the worst of the worst.” The data proves it’s a systematic lie.

The administration also revoked temporary legal status for millions who entered legally under Biden-era policies, then counted them as deportations when they couldn’t leave fast enough. These aren’t border-crossers or criminals—they’re people who came legally, played by the rules, and are now being hunted because the rules changed retroactively

Where This Actually Happens

The administration’s propaganda shows dramatic raids on gang hideouts, arrests of violent fugitives, operations targeting criminal networks. The reality is starkly different.

ICE arrests now routinely take place at:

  • Home Depot parking lots

  • Car washes

  • Hallways outside immigration courtrooms

  • Residential streets with “plenty of bystanders around”

These are public spaces where families shop, work, and live their daily lives. These aren’t tactical operations against dangerous criminals. These are fishing expeditions in American communities, grabbing whoever ICE encounters.

The choice of public spaces isn’t accidental. Federal immigration officers can arrest people without warrants if they have probable cause the person is deportable and a flight risk. They cannot enter homes without warrants—but they can operate freely in parking lots, stores, and streets. This legal framework explains the Target raids, the Home Depot sweeps, the residential street confrontations.

The Times podcast transcript is damning: “Today’s ICE arrests are taking place in places like Home Depot parking lots, car washes, the hallways of immigration courtrooms and on residential streets, where there are plenty of bystanders around.”

This is why federal agents raided a Target store in Minneapolis—Gregory Bovino and his masked officers marching through the aisles in full tactical gear, dragging out employees while families with children watched in horror. One employee, a U.S. citizen, shouted “I’m a U.S. citizen!” as agents shoved him into an SUV.

This is normalization in action: paramilitary operations in retail stores, courthouses, and residential neighborhoods, targeting not criminals but anyone without perfect documentation.

The Deportation Destinations Tell Their Own Story

Where people are being sent reveals another dimension of the operation’s cruelty. The New York Times reports that roughly 5% of deportees are sent to countries other than their country of citizenship. The U.S. has made agreements with Mexico and Honduras to accept third-country nationals.

Even more disturbing: Some deportees are being sent to African nations that the United States paid to receive migrants—Rwanda, South Sudan, Eswatini, and Ghana. These are deportations-for-hire to countries with no connection to the person being deported.

People are also being deported to repressive regimes including Russia, Iran, and Afghanistan—countries where deportees may face persecution, imprisonment, or death simply for having sought refuge in America.

The Scale of the Operation

The numbers document an enforcement campaign unprecedented in American history:

  • 230,000 people deported from the interior in one year (vs. 50,000 under entire Biden administration)

  • 270,000 deported at the border

  • Operations affect every state and city in the country

  • At-large arrests quadrupled—now the majority in 32 states

“At-large arrests” means ICE agents on streets, in businesses, at homes—not coordinated operations with local law enforcement at jails and prisons. It means masked agents in unmarked vehicles grabbing neighbors, interrogating U.S. citizens, demanding papers.

And the deportations themselves reveal the lie about “criminal aliens”:

  • People deported to countries with no ties to them

  • Deportations to repressive regimes including Russia, Iran, and Afghanistan

  • Massive increases in deportations to Venezuela, India, China, Vietnam

If this were truly about removing dangerous criminals, why are the largest increases in people with no criminal record? Why are arrests happening in parking lots instead of through cooperation with local law enforcement at jails? Why are U.S. citizens being detained, interrogated, and in some cases deported?

The data demolishes the administration’s justifications. This isn’t law enforcement. This is state-sponsored terror designed to make immigrant communities—and anyone who looks like they might be immigrants—afraid to exist in public spaces.

The Deadly Machinery: Why ICE Kills

The data explains who the administration is targeting and where operations happen. But it doesn’t explain why these operations keep turning deadly. For that, we need to understand what’s happened to ICE itself.

Nick Miroff’s investigation for the Washington Post reveals a workforce transformation so reckless it virtually guarantees violent encounters.

The Hiring Binge: Doubling ICE in Months

The Trump administration, flush with billions from the “One Big Beautiful Bill,” has expanded ICE at a pace never before attempted in federal law enforcement:

  • 12,000 new officers and attorneys hired in less than a year

  • ICE workforce more than doubled

  • New agents sent through “fast-track course that has cut training time in half”

For context: The entire U.S. Border Patrol has roughly 20,000 agents. ICE just added more than half that number in ten months.

This isn’t careful expansion. It’s a hiring binge prioritizing numbers over competence, speed over safety, political loyalty over professional standards.

Training Slashed in Half

As ICE doubled its workforce, the administration simultaneously gutted training:

  • Training time cut in half through expedited courses

  • Only about 50% of officers are up-to-date on mandatory use-of-force training

  • No additional psychological screening despite high-stress deployments

  • Compliance with use-of-force requirements “has lagged” under pressure to meet deportation quotas

ICE officers are required to undergo yearly use-of-force training. Half aren’t current on that training. They’re being sent into volatile street confrontations with protesters, into communities where residents are organizing resistance, into situations where split-second decisions mean life or death—without proper training.

Veteran ICE officials warned Miroff “that the administration has lowered ICE’s standards and is on the verge of sending rookie officers into the streets, where they will face angry protesters and volatile crowds, without necessary training and preparation.”

The administration ignored those warnings. Renee Good paid the price.

Jonathan Ross: Case Study in Systematic Failure

ICE Officer Jonathan Ross, the man who killed Renee Good, isn’t a rogue agent or a bad apple. He’s a case study in how the system creates deadly outcomes.

Ross’s background reads like an ideal ICE officer:

  • Iraq War veteran (served with Indiana National Guard on combat patrol)

  • 18 years with Homeland Security (Border Patrol since 2007, ICE since 2015)

  • Member of ICE’s elite Special Response Team—the agency’s highly trained tactical unit

  • Works in ICE’s fugitive-operations divisions (vehicle stops are routine)

  • College graduate with nearly two decades of federal law enforcement experience

By all official metrics, Ross was exactly what ICE claims to want: experienced, trained, combat-tested, trusted with the agency’s most dangerous assignments.

But seven months before Minneapolis, Ross was nearly killed when a suspect dragged him at least 100 yards through Chicago streets during an arrest attempt.

The incident happened during a vehicle stop of Roberto Carlos Muñoz-Guatemala, a Mexican man previously convicted of sexually abusing a minor but not deported. According to court records, Ross used a tool to smash the driver’s window and reached inside—a tactic two ICE officials told Miroff is “generally discouraged because of the risk it poses” to officers.

Ross attempted to subdue Muñoz-Guatemala with his Taser, but the man still managed to hit the gas. The car dragged Ross through the street, weaving back and forth as the driver tried to shake him loose. Ross suffered gashes on his right arm and left hand requiring dozens of stitches. Last month, a jury convicted Muñoz-Guatemala of assaulting a federal officer with a deadly weapon.

Ross nearly died that day. He experienced exactly the kind of vehicle-related trauma that should trigger comprehensive psychological evaluation before any return to duty involving vehicle encounters.

What happened next reveals the systematic negligence:

  • Ross received medical clearance only—no psychological evaluation required by ICE policy

  • He self-certified his readiness to return to duty

  • ICE policy doesn’t mandate psychological screening after traumatic incidents

  • Officers can return to duty once wounds heal and they say they’re ready

  • Seven months later, ICE deployed Ross to Minneapolis for exactly the kind of vehicle encounter that nearly killed him

Multiple current and former ICE officials confirmed to Miroff that Ross wouldn’t have needed psychological evaluation. Medical clearance and self-certification were sufficient.

Think about that: An officer who nearly died being dragged by a vehicle, who watched his life flash before his eyes as a suspect tried to shake him loose at high speed, who spent months recovering from his injuries—that officer can return to high-stress tactical operations without any assessment of trauma, PTSD, or psychological fitness. He just needs his wounds to heal and has to say he’s ready.

Then ICE deploys him to Minneapolis—the city still traumatized by George Floyd’s murder, where thousands of protesters flood the streets daily, where tensions between federal agents and residents are explosive, where every ICE operation draws angry crowds with whistles and cameras. They put this traumatized officer, who nearly died in a vehicle-related incident seven months earlier, in exactly the kind of volatile vehicle situation most likely to trigger a trauma response.

And they give him a weapon.

J.D. Vance essentially admitted this when he defended Ross by noting the previous dragging incident: “So you think maybe he’s a little bit sensitive about somebody ramming him with an automobile?”

Yes. That’s exactly the problem. Ross was “sensitive”—traumatized, hypervigilant, potentially suffering PTSD—and ICE sent him into exactly the situation most likely to trigger a lethal overreaction, with zero psychological screening and no additional support.

That’s not protecting officers. That’s using traumatized agents as disposable weapons.

Two current ICE officials and one former official told Miroff that Ross has a reputation among colleagues as an “aggressive, gung-ho officer”—one described him as “enthusiastic.” Combined with unaddressed trauma from the dragging incident, that aggression became lethal.

“Frazzled Agents, Quick Triggers”

Miroff’s sources inside ICE describe an agency under enormous pressure:

  • Officers pushed to meet aggressive deportation quotas

  • Deployed to hostile cities where local officials oppose their presence

  • Facing organized community resistance—car-powered warning systems, whistles, horns, cellphone cameras

  • Minimal training in de-escalation or crowd control

  • 12,000 brand-new agents with inadequate preparation

One current ICE official told Miroff the Minneapolis shooting was “murder.” Others defended Ross but acknowledged the agency is creating conditions for tragedy.

Miroff summarizes the situation perfectly: “Frazzled agents, quick triggers, and a motor-powered protest movement are creating a uniquely dangerous moment on U.S. streets.”

These aren’t independent variables. The administration deliberately created this situation:

  • Doubled the workforce without proper training

  • Slashed training time to rush agents into the field

  • Eliminated psychological screening after traumatic incidents

  • Demanded aggressive quota-meeting in hostile environments

  • Deployed agents to cities whose leaders explicitly don’t want them there

This is systematic negligence. The administration created the conditions for lethal force, deployed traumatized officers without support, and is now defending the predictable results as heroic law enforcement.

The Connection: Why Renee Good Died

We can now trace a direct line from administration policy to Renee Good’s death on that Minneapolis street.

The Lie About Mission: The administration claims to target violent criminals. The data proves they’re actually targeting anyone they encounter—sixfold increase in people with no criminal records. This puts agents in public spaces confronting ordinary people, not in coordinated operations against known dangerous criminals.

The Lie About Training: The administration claims its agents are highly trained professionals. The reality: 12,000 new hires in months, training cut in half, only 50% current on use-of-force requirements. They’re sending undertrained, overwhelmed agents into volatile situations.

The Lie About Screening: Ross returned to duty after nearly dying, traumatized from being dragged by a vehicle, with no psychological evaluation. ICE sent him to Minneapolis—George Floyd’s city, still traumatized by police violence—and put him in exactly the kind of vehicle encounter most likely to trigger his trauma.

The Lie About Self-Defense: Video evidence proves Ross positioned himself in front of Good’s vehicle, fired as she tried to leave (not ram him), and then left the scene. The “feared being struck” justification is standardized cover story ICE has used in at least 12 shootings since September—all involving vehicles.

Renee Good didn’t die because of a tragic mistake or a split-second misjudgment. She died because:

  1. The administration massively expanded ICE without proper training

  2. They deployed agents to public spaces to grab anyone, not criminals

  3. They returned traumatized officers to duty without psychological screening

  4. They created high-pressure, quota-driven environment virtually guaranteed to produce overreaction

  5. When the predictable happened, they lied about it and called an American mother a “domestic terrorist”

This is systematic. This is deliberate. This is murder by policy.

This Will Happen Again

Elizabeth Bruenig’s essay in The Atlantic carries the most chilling headline: “This Will Happen Again.”

She’s right. And we can be specific about why.

Every element that led to Renee Good’s death remains in place—and in many cases, is accelerating:

The hiring binge continues: ICE continues adding thousands more agents with inadequate training. The administration received billions in new funding and plans to expand operations to multiple cities simultaneously. Where they previously focused on one city at a time (with Gregory Bovino directing operations on the ground), they now plan concurrent operations nationwide.

Training standards remain gutted: Training time is still cut in half. Only about 50% of officers remain current on use-of-force requirements. No additional psychological screening has been mandated. The pressure to meet quotas intensifies as the administration pushes toward its goal of 1 million deportations per year.

Traumatized officers stay in the field: Ross returned to duty without psychological evaluation. ICE policy still doesn’t require screening after traumatic incidents. How many other officers are working with unaddressed trauma, deployed into exactly the situations most likely to trigger lethal overreactions?

Operations target hostile cities: Agents continue deploying to cities where local officials explicitly oppose their presence—Minneapolis, Portland, Chicago, New York. These deployments maximize confrontation between armed federal agents and organized community resistance. It’s a recipe for violence.

No accountability for shootings: The FBI investigates ICE shootings—meaning the federal government investigates itself. Minnesota state investigators were locked out of the Ross investigation, denied access to evidence, and forced to withdraw. This pattern will repeat: federal agents investigating federal agents, with predictable results.

The administration defends every shooting: Noem called Good a “domestic terrorist.” Trump said “She ran him over” despite video proving otherwise. JD Vance called reporters “agents of propaganda” for accurately describing video evidence. When officials defend every shooting regardless of evidence, they guarantee more shootings.

In fact, it’s already happening again. The same day Noem defended Ross in New York, Border Patrol agents shot two people in Portland—a husband and wife from Venezuela. Federal officials immediately claimed they were Tren de Aragua gang members who “attempted to use a vehicle as a weapon against law enforcement.” Portland Police couldn’t confirm whether it was even immigration-related, because federal agents left the scene and wouldn’t provide information.

The driver was shot and drove more than two miles before being found by police. If the agent was truly in imminent danger of being run over, how did the vehicle drive away for over two miles? The injured man had to call 911 himself because federal agents had already left the scene.

Same pattern. Same lies. Same lack of accountability.

And we now know Portland wasn’t the 2nd or 3rd ICE shooting. It was the 11th and 12th since September. All involved vehicles. All featured the identical justification: agents “feared being struck.”

This is a standardized cover story, not a description of events. When the same excuse appears in twelve consecutive shootings, it’s not because all twelve involved genuine threats—it’s because agents know this excuse will be defended by the administration regardless of evidence.

Minneapolis matters not because it was unusual, but because it was usual—and this time there was video from multiple angles to prove the official narrative was a lie.

How many of the other eleven shootings are also lies? We don’t know, because in those cases there was no video, no independent investigation, no accountability. How many other Renee Goods has ICE killed, with their deaths dismissed as “justified” because no video existed to prove otherwise?

We do know this: An administration that lies about one shooting will lie about all of them. An agency that covers up one killing will cover up the next. A system that demands no accountability after Renee Good’s death will produce more Renee Goods.

The conditions are set. The machinery is in place. The normalization is accelerating.

Bruenig is right. This will happen again. And again. And again. Until either the system is dismantled or Americans decide they’re willing to accept regular killings of citizens by federal agents, backed by systematic lies and zero accountability.

Conclusion: Systematic, Predictable, Deadly

This isn’t about one bad agent or one tragic mistake. This is about an administration that:

  • Lies systematically about who they’re targeting (data proves it)

  • Creates deadly conditions deliberately (undertrained agents, traumatized officers, quota pressure)

  • Refuses accountability when the predictable happens

  • Accelerates the violence by expanding the same broken system

Secretary Noem must resign. But resignation isn’t enough.

ICE must be investigated by independent authorities, not the FBI investigating itself. Officers involved in shootings must be publicly identified. Training standards must be restored and enforced. Psychological screening must be mandatory after traumatic incidents. Deployment to hostile cities must end.

Or we dismantle ICE entirely. Because an agency that operates this way—lying about its mission, deploying traumatized and undertrained agents, killing American citizens and then calling them terrorists—has forfeited its right to exist.

I warned ten days ago about normalization. I demanded we refuse to accept lies about state violence. I argued that tolerating Noem’s demonstrable dishonesty would lead to more deaths.

The data proves I was right about the lies. The training crisis investigation proves I was right about the systematic negligence. Renee Good’s death proves I was right about the deadly consequences.

The question now is whether Americans will continue accepting this. Will we normalize paramilitary operations in Target stores? Will we accept that 12,000 undertrained agents can operate without accountability? Will we believe the administration’s lies even when data and video prove otherwise?

Secretary Noem thinks Americans have been so thoroughly conditioned to accept state violence that we’ll accept it even when they kill mothers in the street and then lie about it.

She’s betting on our exhaustion, our resignation, our willingness to look away.

The normalization I warned about? It’s here. Backed by fake statistics about “criminal aliens” and enabled by a deliberately broken training system that produces traumatized agents with quick triggers.

The machinery is running. The lies are flowing. The bodies are piling up.

The normalization stops here. Or it doesn’t stop at all.
