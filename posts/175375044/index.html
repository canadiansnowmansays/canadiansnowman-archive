BREAKING UPDATE (October 10, 2025): Soon after this article published, OMB Director Russ Vought has announced that mass federal worker firings “have begun.” This is the direct execution of the September 24 RIF memo documented in Part 4, where Vought directed agencies to use the shutdown for permanent layoffs. The AFGE lawsuit filed September 30 to stop these terminations as illegal is still pending court review. What we documented as threat is now implementation. The machinery isn’t coming—it’s running.

Six days ago, in Part 4, we identified Russ Vought as the architect building fascist infrastructure in real time. We documented his “post-constitutional” vision, his transformation of OMB into a weapon, and his systematic targeting of Democratic jurisdictions with $28 billion in funding freezes. We showed how Project 2025—which Trump spent the entire 2024 campaign denying—was now being openly embraced and implemented through the government shutdown.

Trump didn’t just embrace it—he celebrated it with an AI-generated video depicting Vought as the Grim Reaper, scythe in hand, walking through Washington to dismantle “Democrat Agencies.” He called the shutdown an “unprecedented opportunity.”

Part 4 ended with a question: Would Americans care enough to stop the machinery before it’s too late?

Six days later, we have answers—and they’re not what authoritarians expected.

The government shutdown hit day 10 on October 10, 2025. Vought’s bureaucratic scythe continues to swing: 750,000 federal workers furloughed, the IRS gutted by 46%, $28 billion frozen exclusively for Democratic cities. Trump posted yesterday: “We’re only cutting Democrat programs.“

But something unexpected happened: Americans started swinging back.

Today—October 9, 2025—a federal judge in Illinois blocked the Trump administration from deploying troops into Chicago and issued extraordinary protections for journalists. Yesterday, federal courts extended restraining orders on Vought’s funding freezes. Labor unions filed multiple lawsuits. Senate Democrats held the line through seven rounds of failed votes.

Most importantly, the judiciary—America’s last institutional firewall—just proved it still has teeth.

But there’s a darker development we need to understand first. While Vought methodically dismantles institutions with his bureaucratic scythe, Stephen Miller has escalated to a qualitatively new level: building a legal framework to categorize political opponents as terrorists requiring state dismantling.

This is Part 5: When the Reaper’s scythe starts swinging, and when Americans start fighting back.

Miller’s Dangerous Escalation: Building the Framework for State Repression

We’ve tracked Miller’s language for months. In August, he called Democrats “a domestic extremist organization” devoted to defending “hardened criminals, gang-bangers, and illegal, alien killers and terrorists.”

But October brought something fundamentally different. Miller didn’t just attack Democrats—he systematically built a legal and conceptual framework for using state power against them.

October 4: The “Terrorism” Framework

Miller posted on X: “There is a large and growing movement of leftwing terrorism in this country. It is well organized and funded. And it is shielded by far-left Democrat judges, prosecutors and attorneys general. The only remedy is to use legitimate state power to dismantle terrorism and terror networks.“

This wasn’t political hyperbole. The key phrase—”the only remedy is to use legitimate state power”—moved from diagnosis to prescription. Miller was explicitly calling for government action against political opponents, framing them as terrorists requiring dismantling.

October 5: Attacking Judicial Independence

When U.S. District Judge Karin Immergut—a Trump appointee—blocked deployment of 200 National Guard troops to Portland, Miller didn’t just criticize the ruling. He rejected the concept of judicial review itself:

“Legal insurrection. The President is the commander-in-chief of the Armed Forces, not an Oregon judge. This is an organized terrorist attack on the federal government and its officers, and the deployment of troops is an absolute necessity to defend our personnel, our laws, our government, public order and the Republic itself.“

Judge Immergut’s ruling stated “This is a nation of Constitutional law, not martial law” and found Trump’s claims about Portland violence “untethered to facts.” Miller’s response: The courts have no authority to constrain presidential military deployment. A federal judge doing her constitutional duty was committing “insurrection against the government.”

October 6: The “Plenary Authority” Moment

During a CNN interview, Miller made an extraordinary claim while defending Trump’s attempt to deploy troops despite the court ruling. He stated: “Under Title 10 of the U.S. Code, the President has plenary authority, has—”

And then he froze. For approximately 15 seconds, Miller sat silent on camera.

After the commercial break, Miller never used the phrase again. “Plenary authority” is a legal term meaning unlimited power that cannot be reviewed or constrained by courts. Miller was arguing the president has essentially unchecked power in certain domains. He realized mid-sentence he’d said the quiet part out loud.

But Miller’s slips didn’t stop there. In the same interview, he made another revealing mistake. When discussing National Guard deployments, he said: “Illinois governor says we’re provoking actions that are unlawful. Why would the mere presence—just think about this for a second. If I put federal law enforcement and National Guard into a nice sleepy Southern town, is anyone gonna riot?”

Not “the president puts.” Not “we put.” I put.

An unelected deputy chief of staff openly claiming personal authority to deploy military forces to American cities. The slip reveals what Trump’s presidency has become: a figurehead for decisions made by unelected ideologues like Miller and Vought.

When asked if calling protesters “terrorists” was hyperbolic, Miller doubled down: “No, if anything, I’m understating the severity of the situation.” He accused protesters and Democratic officials of “trying to overthrow the core law enforcement function of the federal government.”

At an October 6 White House press briefing, a reporter asked: “You called a legal ruling, legal insurrection. Are you recommending the president take action against judges with rulings you disagree with?”

Miller refused to back down: “It’s simply a factually accurate statement that when a judge assumes for him or herself the powers that have been delegated by the Constitution to the president, that that is a form of illegal insurrection.”

October 8: The Latest Escalation

Miller’s most recent statement removes any remaining ambiguity: “Democrat leaders are siding with the domestic terrorists. Democrats are on the side every night of the gangbangers, the predators, and now even the domestic terrorists.“

This is no longer about immigration or crime policy. Miller has systematically expanded who qualifies as a terrorist to include: protesters at ICE facilities, Democratic governors, Democratic mayors, federal judges who rule against the administration, and anyone who opposes Trump’s enforcement priorities.

Why This Matters: The Four-Step Pattern

Miller’s October rhetoric establishes a clear pattern—one we’ve seen in authoritarian regimes throughout history:

Step 1: Categorize opponents as existential threats (terrorists, insurrectionists)
Step 2: Claim unlimited executive authority (plenary power, rejection of judicial review)
Step 3: Call for state action (use “legitimate state power to dismantle terrorism networks”)
Step 4: Reject institutional constraints (courts engaging in “insurrection” have no authority)

This is how authoritarian regimes justify extraordinary measures. You don’t just disagree with opponents—you categorize them as threats to the state itself. You don’t just criticize court rulings—you reject judicial authority entirely. You don’t just want to win politically—you want to “dismantle” enemies using state power.

Connect this to Vought’s machinery: Miller provides the justification, Vought provides the implementation.

Miller says Democrats are terrorists who must be dismantled. Vought freezes $28 billion in funding exclusively for Democratic jurisdictions.

Miller says judges engage in “insurrection.” Vought looks for workarounds to court orders blocking his funding freezes.

Miller says the president has plenary authority. Vought operates under a “post-constitutional” framework that treats legal constraints as obstacles to overcome.

This is characteristic #2 from our fascism framework (violent suppression of opposition), characteristic #3 (scapegoating of “others”), and characteristic #8 (rejection of democratic norms) operating in concert.

Breaking Today: Federal Courts Strike Back

Here’s what authoritarians didn’t expect: The judiciary is fighting back. Hard.

Chicago: Judge April Perry Blocks Troops and Protects Journalists

Today—October 9, 2025—U.S. District Judge April Perry issued a sweeping order blocking Trump administration authorities from deploying forces into Chicago and protecting journalists from federal interference.

The order temporarily enjoins federal defendants, their officers, agents, and assigns from:

“Dispersing, arresting, threatening to arrest, threatening or using physical force against any person whom they know or reasonably should know is a Journalist, unless Defendants have probable cause to believe that the individual has committed a crime.”

Judge Perry’s order also protects journalists’ ability to report: Federal agents may order journalists to change location only if there’s an objectively reasonable time to comply and an objectively reasonable opportunity to report and observe.

Additionally, the order blocks “Issuing a crowd dispersal order requiring any person to leave a public place that they lawfully have a right to be, unless dispersal is justified by exigent circumstances” as defined by Department of Homeland Security Use of Force Policy.

This is extraordinary. A federal judge didn’t just block military deployment—she issued First Amendment protections against federal force, recognizing that the Trump administration’s pattern of targeting journalists requires judicial intervention.

The ruling came in response to Trump’s attempt to deploy National Guard troops into Chicago—part of the same pattern as Portland. Illinois Gov. JB Pritzker (whom Miller has specifically named as supporting “terrorism”) challenged the deployment. Judge Perry found Trump’s justification for military intervention lacking in credibility.

The partisan attack on Judge Perry began immediately. Right-wing accounts posted: “The federal judge who just blocked the National Guard from entering Chicago is April Perry — nominated by Joe Biden in 2024. She also served as Chief of Ethics for Kim Foxx — the George Soros-funded Chicago State’s Attorney. Chicago’s crisis is no accident. It’s by design.”

This is the pattern: When courts rule against Trump, attack the judges. Question their legitimacy. Connect them to conspiracy theories. Soften the ground for ignoring judicial orders entirely.

But Judge Perry’s ruling stands. The troops are blocked. Journalists are protected. The law applies.

Portland: Judge Immergut’s Constitutional Stand

Judge Karin Immergut—remember, a Trump appointee—blocked deployment of 200 National Guard troops to Portland, ruling: “This is a nation of Constitutional law, not martial law.”

Her opinion found Trump’s claims about Portland violence “untethered to facts.” This wasn’t a close call—it was a judicial rebuke of manufactured crisis.

Miller’s response was to call the ruling “legal insurrection” and claim judges have no authority over presidential military deployment. But the ruling stands. The troops remain blocked.

The Pattern: Courts as Firewall

These aren’t isolated rulings. They’re part of a broader pattern of judicial resistance:

July 2025: U.S. District Judge Emmet Sullivan issued a 60-page opinion ruling that Vought violated federal law by taking down the “Public Apportionments Database.” Sullivan wrote: “There is nothing unconstitutional about Congress requiring the Executive Branch to inform the public of how it is apportioning the public’s money. Defendants are therefore required to stop violating the law!“

The U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit denied the administration’s appeal. Circuit Judge Karen LeCraft Henderson wrote: “No Congress should be made to wait while the executive intrudes on its plenary power over appropriations and disclosure thereof.“

This week: A federal judge extended a temporary restraining order (TRO) blocking OMB’s directive to freeze federal assistance, citing unconstitutional withholding.

September 30: Labor unions filed suit challenging Vought’s memo directing agencies to use the shutdown for mass Reduction in Force layoffs. The lawsuit argues nothing in federal law authorizes RIFs during a funding lapse.

October 5: Unions filed a separate lawsuit challenging the Department of Education’s manipulation of employees’ out-of-office emails to include partisan shutdown blame—arguing First Amendment violation and potential Hatch Act violations.

The Government Accountability Office has documented that the Trump administration illegally impounded funds seven times in nine months, with GAO conducting nearly 50 ongoing investigations.

Every illegal action is being challenged. Every overreach is being documented. Every court ruling is a barrier that must be overcome.

This is how democracies survive authoritarian stress tests. Not through violence or revolution, but through institutions doing their constitutional duty. Judges appointed by presidents of both parties saying: “No. The law applies. Your authority has limits.

The Machinery Still Running: Day 10 of the Shutdown

While courts push back and Miller escalates rhetoric, Vought’s bureaucratic warfare continues:

750,000 federal employees furloughed without pay. Another 700,000 “essential” workers laboring without paychecks.

The IRS furloughed 46% of its workforce—34,400 employees—shuttering most operations.

The U.S. Patent and Trademark Office issued the first actual RIF notices, firing 140 employees and closing the entire Rocky Mountain Regional Outreach Office in Denver.

The backpay controversy: OMB argues the Government Employee Fair Treatment Act of 2019 only applied to that specific shutdown. The IRS backtracked on assuring employees of backpay. Trump stated that back pay “depends on who we’re talking about” and “There are some people that really don’t deserve to be taken care of.”

Economic impact: 400 million dollars per day in eventual backpay. 15 billion dollars per week in total economic impact.

Trump posted yesterday: “We’re only cutting Democrat programs. We’ll be cutting some very popular Democrat programs that aren’t popular with Republicans. Frankly, because that’s the way it works. They wanted to do this, so we’ll get a little taste of their own medicine.”

This is systematic political warfare. Punishment of enemies. Rewarding of allies. Using the federal budget as a weapon.

Senate Democrats held the line through seven rounds of failed votes, refusing to give Trump a clean continuing resolution without protections. Only three Democrats broke ranks: Sens. Fetterman (PA), Cortez Masto (NV), and Independent King (ME).

House Speaker Mike Johnson extended the House recess through October 13, refusing to bring members back to negotiate. He rejected calls for a standalone military pay bill despite over 140 co-sponsors. The next military paycheck date: October 15, affecting 1.3 million service members.

The machinery is grinding. People are suffering. Communities are being targeted.

But resistance is building.

What This Means: The Battle for Democratic Institutions

We are at an inflection point. The question isn’t whether authoritarianism can happen in America—we’re watching it be constructed in real time. The question is whether democratic institutions are strong enough, and Americans vigilant enough, to dismantle the machinery before it becomes permanent.

Judge Perry’s ruling today proves something crucial: Institutions still work when people demand they work.

Governor Pritzker challenged the troop deployment. Legal teams filed suit. Judge Perry enforced the Constitution. The troops are blocked.

This is the pattern of successful democratic resistance:

  1. Officials use their authority to challenge overreach

  2. Legal teams document violations and file suit

  3. Courts enforce constitutional constraints

  4. Public pressure sustains the resistance

None of this happens automatically. Pritzker needed to challenge. Lawyers needed to file. Judges needed to rule. And they all needed to know Americans were watching and supporting them.

Miller’s response to Judge Perry will be predictable: Attack her legitimacy. Call her a Biden appointee serving “Soros-funded” prosecutors. Frame her constitutional duty as partisan obstruction.

The right-wing ecosystem is already doing it. That’s Step 1 of delegitimizing judicial authority: Make every judge who rules against you seem illegitimate.

Step 2 is claiming “plenary authority” that can’t be reviewed.

Step 3 is ignoring court orders entirely.

We’re somewhere between Steps 1 and 2. The question is whether we let them reach Step 3.
