The Case for Data Integrity

When politicians fire the people who count jobs, we all lose

September 5, 2025

Last month, I wrote about Trump's firing of Bureau of Labor Statistics Commissioner Erika McEntarfer—a classic case of shooting the messenger when economic data becomes inconvenient. After July's disappointing 73,000 jobs report and massive downward revisions, Trump deployed his standard TACO blame-shifting: rather than address policy failures, attack the institutions measuring them.

Now we have August's jobs data: just 22,000 jobs added, the weakest monthly gain since April. The timing is almost too perfect—weak enough to justify Fed rate cuts, but not catastrophic enough to signal recession. Which raises an uncomfortable question: when you fire statisticians for accurate reporting, how do we know what's real anymore?

The Pattern Becomes Clearer

Remember the context from my earlier analysis: McEntarfer was confirmed 86-8 by the Senate with overwhelming bipartisan support. Even JD Vance voted for her confirmation. But when the July data showed economic weakness, the Republican capitulation was immediate. Senator Markwayne Mullin, who had voted for McEntarfer, suddenly declared on Fox News that she was generating "fake reports."

This wasn't about data quality—it was about data convenience.

Now, one month later, we're seeing the downstream effects of politicizing statistical agencies. The August report shows continued weakness, but in a way that perfectly serves Trump's political narrative. Coincidence? Maybe. But when you've already fired one commissioner for inconvenient truths, the credibility of the entire system comes into question.

Market Reaction: The Numbers Don't Lie

As I write this Friday morning, markets are already reacting to the August jobs data in ways that validate concerns about credibility. Stocks rose to record highs despite the weak 22,000 jobs number, with the S&P 500 climbing 0.2% and traders betting the data gives the Fed cover for rate cuts.

The two-year Treasury yield dropped sharply to 3.47%, its lowest level since 2022, as traders ramped up bets on Fed rate cuts with a 12% chance now priced in for a jumbo half-point cut in September.

Here's what's telling: investors are treating weak employment data as good news because it justifies easier monetary policy. The Fed has been holding rates steady, waiting for clear signs that the labor market is cooling before cutting. Today's weak jobs data gives them exactly that justification.

Traders immediately ramped up their Fed rate cut bets. There's now an 87.8% probability of a quarter-point cut at the September 16-17 Fed meeting, with a 12.2% chance of a jumbo half-point cut. The odds of additional cuts through December also spiked significantly.

This is exactly the perverse incentive that comes from politicizing statistical agencies. When markets start celebrating bad economic news because it gives the Fed cover to cut rates—rather than worrying about underlying economic weakness—the entire information ecosystem becomes corrupted.

Think about what's happening here: investors are essentially betting that either (1) the jobs data is weak enough to force Fed action but not weak enough to signal recession, or (2) the data might be politically influenced anyway, so focus on the policy implications rather than economic fundamentals.

Friday's jobs report painted a miserable picture of the labor market in practically every metric, with unemployment rising to 4.3%, the highest since 2021. In a healthy system, markets would be concerned about what this means for economic growth, corporate earnings, and employment prospects. Instead, they're celebrating because weak data equals easier money from the Fed. That's not how healthy markets should respond to employment data.

Meet the Replacement: From Professional Statistician to Political Operative

The contrast between who was fired and who was nominated tells you everything about where this is heading.

Erika McEntarfer came to the job with impeccable credentials: over 20 years in federal statistical work, positions at Treasury, Census Bureau, and the White House Council of Economic Advisers. She was confirmed by the Senate 86-8 with overwhelming bipartisan support—even JD Vance and Marco Rubio voted for her. She was a career professional doing professional work.

E.J. Antoni, Trump's nominee to replace her, represents the opposite approach. Antoni is chief economist at the Heritage Foundation and a contributor to Project 2025. More telling: he appeared on Steve Bannon's podcast the day McEntarfer was fired, calling her "incompetent" and essentially auditioning for her job on air.

Antoni's track record speaks volumes about his approach to data integrity:

  • He's joked that "the 'L' is silent" in BLS (implying it's "BS")

  • Called for "DOGE to take a chainsaw to the BLS"

  • Described BLS health insurance figures as "phoney baloney"

  • Wants to eliminate monthly jobs reports in favor of quarterly ones

  • Has called BLS methodology changes "Orwellian tricks"

Bannon later bragged about pushing Antoni's nomination, calling him "the guy that almost single-handedly took it down by going through their numbers." When your biggest endorsement comes from someone who wants to "take down" the agency you're supposed to lead, that's not about improving data quality—that's about political control.

Harvard economist Jason Furman, who served under Obama, said bluntly: "I don't think I have ever publicly criticized any Presidential nominee before" but Antoni is an "extreme partisan and does not have any relevant expertise."

Even Trump's own former BLS commissioner, William Beach, warned that appointing a political operative would be devastating: "Suppose that they get a new commissioner and they do a bad number. Well, everybody's going to think, 'well, it's not as bad as it probably really is,' because they're going to suspect political influence. So, this is damaging."

That's exactly the point. When you replace career professionals with political loyalists, every number becomes suspect—even when it's accurate.

When I covered McEntarfer's firing, I focused on the immediate TACO psychology—Trump's pattern of accepting favorable results while attacking unfavorable ones as rigged. But the August data reveals something more troubling: once you break the independence of statistical agencies, every subsequent report becomes suspect.

Your portfolio is already paying the price: International investors are taking notice. When governments start firing statisticians, smart money starts hedging bets. That means higher borrowing costs, reduced foreign investment, and slower economic growth.

The Fed is flying blind: Jerome Powell needs accurate employment data to set interest rates. When that data becomes politicized, monetary policy becomes guesswork. Bad Fed decisions destroyed wealth in 2008—they can do it again.

We're heading toward banana republic territory: In my previous analysis, I noted how countries like Russia and China lost statistical credibility. Once that happens, international organizations create their own estimates because official data becomes worthless.

The Technical Reality Still Matters

As I explained before, the commissioner doesn't actually manipulate data collection. William Beach, Trump's own former BLS commissioner, made this clear: the commissioner has no access to data collection and doesn't see final numbers until Wednesday before Friday publication.

But here's what's changed: when you fire a commissioner for accurate reporting, you send a message to everyone in the system. Career statisticians start wondering if their jobs depend on producing politically convenient numbers. That's how institutional corruption begins—not through direct orders, but through fear and intimidation.

The August numbers—22,000 jobs—might be completely accurate. But after firing McEntarfer, how can we be sure? When you shoot the messenger, every subsequent message becomes suspect.

Why Your Government's Chief Bean Counter Can't Actually Cook the Books

I get it. When you see a bureaucrat get fired and then suddenly the next report looks more favorable, your BS detector starts beeping. But here's the thing about how jobs data actually gets made—it's virtually impossible for one person to manipulate.

William Beach should know. Trump appointed him to run the Bureau of Labor Statistics during his first term. After McEntarfer got fired, Beach went on CNN and explained something crucial: "The commissioner doesn't do anything to collect the numbers. The commissioner doesn't see the numbers until Wednesday before they're published."

Let me break down how this actually works:

The data comes from everywhere: Thousands of businesses across the country report their hiring numbers. These aren't going through some political commissar—they're coming directly from HR departments at companies like yours.

Computers do most of the work: The raw data gets fed through algorithms that have been fine-tuned over decades. Career statisticians—not political appointees—run these systems.

Nobody sees the final number until Wednesday: Even the person supposedly "manipulating" the data doesn't get to see what it says until three days before publication.

The process is designed to be manipulation-proof: Because guess what? This exact scenario—politicians getting mad at bad numbers—was always going to happen eventually.

Here's Why You Should Actually Care About This

"OK," you might be thinking, "but this is just Washington drama. How does this affect me?"

Fair question. Let me put it in terms that hit your wallet.

Your 401k depends on accurate data: When portfolio managers can't trust government numbers, they demand higher returns to compensate for the extra risk. That means your retirement account grows slower. Companies pay more to borrow money. The whole system gets more expensive.

The Fed needs real numbers to set interest rates: Jerome Powell isn't just guessing when he decides whether to cut rates. He's looking at employment data, inflation numbers, and economic growth figures. If those numbers are cooked, the Fed makes bad decisions. Remember 2008? Bad Fed decisions can destroy your home value and your job.

International investors are watching: The dollar is the world's reserve currency partly because everyone trusts American economic data. When that trust erodes, foreign money goes elsewhere. Less foreign investment means fewer American jobs and lower economic growth.

Think I'm being dramatic? Let's look at what happens in countries where politicians control economic statistics.

Russia: Official Russian economic data is so unreliable that international organizations create their own estimates. Result? Russian companies pay massive premiums to borrow money, and foreign investment has collapsed.

China: Everyone knows Chinese growth numbers are manufactured to hit government targets. International businesses have learned to ignore official data and create their own measures. China's statistical credibility is so shot that even favorable numbers get ignored.

Venezuela: The government simply stopped publishing inflation data when it got too embarrassing. Businesses had to rely on black market exchange rates and social media reports to make decisions. The economy imploded.

We're not there yet. But firing statisticians for doing their jobs is exactly how countries start down that path.

International Embarrassment, Real Consequences

The National Association for Business Economics called McEntarfer's firing "an unprecedented attack on the U.S. statistical system." International statistical organizations are watching how America handles this crisis of credibility.

Here's what other countries are thinking: if the United States fires statisticians for inconvenient data, why should anyone trust American economic reports? That loss of trust has real consequences:

  • Higher borrowing costs as investors demand risk premiums

  • Reduced foreign investment as international money seeks more reliable markets

  • Weakened dollar as reserve currency status depends partly on statistical credibility

  • Policy mistakes as bad data leads to bad decisions

The Systematic Incompetence Continues

As I noted in my previous coverage, the Trump administration couldn't even provide coherent rationale for McEntarfer's firing. When your own officials can't justify dismissing a Senate-confirmed commissioner with bipartisan support, it exposes the systematic pattern: attack institutions rather than address policy failures.

This systematic incompetence is now compounding. Each month's jobs data will be viewed through the lens of political manipulation. Even accurate numbers lose credibility when you've already established that statisticians get fired for inconvenient truths.

While Trump systematically attacks economic measurement institutions, he ignores actual threats to institutional integrity—like the IRS never auditing Jeffrey Epstein's massive tax schemes despite obvious red flags, as Senator Ron Wyden revealed.

The Deeper Problem: Short-Term Politics vs. Long-Term Reality

Here's what really bugs me about this whole situation. Politicians—from both parties—love to take credit when economic numbers look good and blame the messenger when they look bad. But here's the thing: good data helps you make good decisions, even when (especially when) the news is bad.

Let's say you're running a business. Would you rather have an accountant who tells you the truth about your finances, or one who makes the numbers look good to keep you happy? Obviously, you want the truth. Bad financial news means you can fix problems before they destroy your company. Fake good news means you keep making bad decisions until reality catches up with you.

The same logic applies to the whole economy. When jobs data shows weakness, that's valuable information. It tells the Fed they might need to cut interest rates. It tells businesses they should be careful about expansion. It tells workers they might want to update their skills.

When you fire the person delivering bad news, you don't fix the underlying problem—you just stop getting warned about it.

And here's the kicker: even from a purely political perspective, this strategy is dumb. Let's say Trump actually succeeds in installing a yes-man who makes the numbers look better. What happens when the next recession hits? When unemployment spikes to 8% and your statistician has been reporting 4%? You lose all credibility, instantly.

The smart political play—if you care about governing effectively rather than just winning news cycles—is to protect statistical integrity so people trust the data when it's favorable to you.

What Should Actually Happen

Look, I'm not naive. Politicians are always going to spin economic data to make themselves look good. That's politics. But there's a huge difference between spinning numbers and corrupting the process that produces them.

If Trump (or any president) wants better economic numbers, here's how to get them: implement better economic policies. Create jobs. Boost growth. Reduce unemployment. When the economy actually improves, the statistics will reflect that improvement.

But in the meantime, we need to protect the integrity of the measurement system. Here's what that looks like:

Congress needs to act: Statistical agency heads should have fixed terms that span multiple administrations, just like Fed governors. You can't fire them for producing accurate but unfavorable data.

Business leaders need to speak up: Every CEO who depends on reliable economic data for planning should be screaming about this. When government statistics lose credibility, private sector decisions get worse and more expensive.

The media needs to explain this stuff: Most people don't understand how statistical agencies work. News outlets should be explaining why data integrity matters instead of just treating every revision as a political scandal.

Voters need to care: This shouldn't be a partisan issue. Republicans should want accurate data when they're in power, and Democrats should want accurate data when they're in power. Bad data helps nobody make good decisions.

What Happens Next

The damage to statistical credibility doesn't reverse quickly. Even if Trump appointed the most qualified, independent-minded commissioner tomorrow, international observers would remain skeptical. Trust takes decades to build and moments to destroy.

Meanwhile, every monthly jobs report becomes a political Rorschach test. Strong numbers? Trump takes credit. Weak numbers? Deep state manipulation. The actual economic reality gets lost in political theater.

But here's the deeper problem: when politicians successfully attack statistical independence, they create incentives for future manipulation. Why report accurate but politically damaging data when you know the messenger gets shot?

The Real Test

August's 22,000 jobs might represent genuine economic weakness requiring policy response. Or it might reflect political pressure on a statistician system already traumatized by leadership changes. We can't know for certain—and that uncertainty is exactly the problem.

When you can't trust economic data, everyone makes worse decisions. Businesses postpone investments. The Fed misreads economic signals. International partners hedge their bets. The entire information infrastructure that supports economic decision-making starts to crumble.

That's the real cost of shooting the messenger: it's not just about one fired commissioner or one month's data. It's about undermining the foundation of evidence-based governance in a complex economy.

The Bottom Line

We're at a crossroads. Either we protect the institutions that provide accurate economic information, or we go down the path of countries where government statistics are propaganda tools rather than measurement instruments.

This isn't really about Trump, and it's not really about jobs data. It's about whether we want to live in a reality-based society where accurate information drives decision-making, or whether we want to prioritize short-term political convenience over long-term institutional integrity.

The costs of choosing convenience are enormous: worse economic policy, reduced investment, loss of international credibility, and ultimately lower growth and fewer opportunities for regular Americans.

The benefits of choosing integrity are equally large: better decision-making, maintained credibility, continued economic leadership, and a foundation for sustainable prosperity.

Here's the thing that really gets me: this should be the easiest choice in the world. Accurate information helps everyone make better decisions. When politicians start firing the people who provide accurate information, they're not just attacking their opponents—they're attacking the foundation of rational decision-making itself.

And that hurts all of us.

As I concluded in my previous analysis, this systematic pattern of attacking institutions rather than addressing policy failures represents a fundamental threat to democratic governance. The August jobs data—whatever its accuracy—demonstrates how quickly statistical credibility can erode once political considerations override professional independence.

The choice remains clear: restore institutional independence and rebuild statistical credibility, or continue down the path toward politicized data that serves political narratives rather than economic reality.

Your 401k, your job prospects, and your economic future depend on getting this right.
