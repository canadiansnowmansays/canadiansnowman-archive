Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 1: Before Columbus — The Taíno and the World That Was Lost

February 23, 2026

This panoramic view of eastern Cuba’s tropical karsts and palms feels timeless as if you were stepping into the land the Taino called Cubanacan

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Before the blockades, before the Revolution, before the sugar plantations and the slave ships and the American naval bases and the Soviet missiles, there was an island of extraordinary beauty and a people who had lived on it for centuries without anyone trying to take it from them.

The Taíno called their island Cubanacan — “center place” — and by the time Christopher Columbus arrived on October 27, 1492, approximately 100,000 to 500,000 of them lived there, depending on which historical estimate you accept. The range reflects not just scholarly uncertainty but something more troubling: we know so little about the Taíno because so few survived to tell their own story. Within a century of Columbus’s landfall, they were gone. Not diminished. Not marginalized. Gone.

This is where Cuba’s story begins — not with revolution or embargo or Cold War posturing, but with one of the most complete destructions of a human civilization in recorded history. Understanding what was lost in those first decades matters, because it established the template for everything that followed: Cuba as a place whose fate is decided by outsiders, and whose people pay the price.

The World Columbus Found

An artistic/diorama-style depiction of Taíno life: figures in ceremony or dance around a bohío, ocean in background

The Taíno were not a primitive people struggling at the edge of survival. They were a sophisticated agricultural society that had developed over centuries from Arawak-speaking ancestors who migrated from the South American mainland. They cultivated cassava, sweet potato, maize, and tobacco. They fished with nets and carved magnificent canoes large enough to carry a hundred people across open water. They played a rubber ball game — one of the earliest known in the Americas — in ceremonial courts. They had a complex spiritual life centered on zemís, carved figures that embodied ancestral spirits and natural forces. They lived in villages of several hundred people, organized under a system of hereditary chiefs called caciques, and their society had developed enough surplus and stability that they had time for art, ceremony, and the kind of elaborate social ritual that marks a culture confident in its own continuity.

When Columbus arrived, the Taíno greeted him with gifts and curiosity. Columbus, in his journal, noted their generosity and gentleness. “They are so ingenuous and free with all they have,” he wrote, “that no one would believe it who has not seen it.” He also noted, with the matter-of-fact pragmatism of a man on a commercial expedition, that they would make excellent servants.

That observation contained, in embryonic form, the entire catastrophe that followed.

Columbus claimed the island for the Spanish Crown and returned the following year with seventeen ships, twelve hundred colonists, and livestock. The colonization of Cuba proceeded in earnest after 1511, when Diego Velázquez de Cuéllar led a military conquest of the island that took approximately three years and established the first permanent Spanish settlements. The Taíno resisted. Their resistance was brave, determined, and utterly futile.

The Mechanics of Erasure

The destruction of the Taíno proceeded through several overlapping mechanisms, each devastating on its own, catastrophic in combination.

The first was disease. The indigenous peoples of the Americas had no immunity to the pathogens that European colonizers carried — smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza — and the epidemics that swept through Taíno communities were apocalyptic in scale. Population estimates for Cuba before contact range widely, but the trajectory after contact is consistent: collapse. By 1550, the native population had fallen by an estimated 90 percent or more. Communities that had sustained themselves for generations simply ceased to exist, their survivors too few and too sick to maintain the agricultural and social systems that had supported them.

The second mechanism was the encomienda system — the formal structure of Spanish colonial labor extraction. Under encomienda, groups of indigenous people were “commended” to individual Spanish colonists, who were theoretically responsible for their Christian education and welfare in exchange for their labor. In practice, it was slavery with paperwork. Taíno men were sent to pan for gold in Cuba’s rivers and streams, laboring under conditions of extreme deprivation for the benefit of colonists who had no interest in their survival beyond their immediate productive capacity. The gold deposits were modest and largely exhausted within a generation, but the labor demands did not stop — they shifted to agriculture, construction, and the emerging sugar economy.

The third mechanism was what we might today call cultural destruction — the systematic dismantling of Taíno social structures, spiritual practices, and ways of life. The Spanish Crown required the conversion of indigenous people to Christianity, which meant the suppression of zemí worship, the disruption of traditional ceremonies, and the replacement of indigenous social organization with Spanish colonial hierarchy. The cacique system was co-opted where useful and destroyed where resistant.

The fourth mechanism was simple violence. Bartolomé de las Casas, the Spanish priest and historian who became one of the earliest European critics of colonial brutality, documented in harrowing detail the massacres, mutilations, and systematic terror that Spanish forces used to suppress Taíno resistance and control the surviving population. His account, A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies, written in 1542, reads as a catalogue of atrocities so extreme that later European readers refused to believe it. Modern scholarship has largely confirmed his essential testimony.

Hatuey and the First Resistance

A close-up of the Hatuey monument in Baracoa—poignant, weathered, looking skyward—evokes endurance and the long memory of resistance

One figure from this period deserves particular attention, because he has become something of a founding symbol of Cuban resistance — a thread that runs forward from 1512 all the way to the present.

Hatuey was a Taíno cacique who had originally lived in Hispaniola (modern Haiti and the Dominican Republic) and had witnessed firsthand the Spanish destruction of indigenous society there. He fled to Cuba with a group of survivors, warned the local Taíno of what was coming, and organized an armed resistance against Velázquez’s forces. He was eventually captured in 1512, tied to a stake, and sentenced to be burned alive.

The story most often told about Hatuey comes from Bartolomé de las Casas, who claims to have witnessed his execution. A Franciscan friar approached Hatuey before the fire was lit and offered him the opportunity to convert to Christianity so that he could go to heaven. Hatuey asked whether Christians went to heaven. The friar said yes. Hatuey declined baptism. He had no desire, he said, to spend eternity in the company of such people.

Whether the exchange happened exactly as recorded is uncertain. But the story has endured for five centuries because it captures something true about the nature of the encounter between the Taíno and the Spanish — the moral clarity of a man who had watched an entire civilization be destroyed and declined to accept the spiritual legitimacy of its destroyers. Hatuey is today considered the first hero of Cuban resistance, and his story appears in school curricula across the island. The most popular Cuban beer brand is named after him.

The pattern he represents — dignity in the face of overwhelming power, defiance as a form of cultural survival, the refusal to grant legitimacy to those who would extinguish you — recurs throughout Cuban history with a consistency that is either inspiring or heartbreaking depending on your perspective. Often both simultaneously.

What Was Lost

The near-total destruction of the Taíno within a century of contact represents one of the most complete cultural erasures in human history. Unlike the indigenous peoples of North America or the Andes, who survived in numbers sufficient to maintain living cultures and languages into the present, the Taíno of Cuba and the Caribbean were reduced to biological and cultural remnants so small that for centuries they were treated as simply extinct.

Recent genetic research has complicated that picture. Studies of Cuban and Caribbean populations have found significant Taíno genetic heritage in people who identify as Afro-Cuban, mixed-race, or even European-descended Cuban — evidence that biological extinction was not as complete as colonial records suggested. Small communities that identified as Taíno descended persisted in isolated areas, intermarrying with African slaves and Spanish settlers, preserving fragments of language, agricultural knowledge, and spiritual practice in forms that were not recognized as indigenous survival precisely because they did not look like what Europeans expected indigenous survival to look like.

But the civilization was gone. The language — Taíno left its mark on Spanish and English in words like canoe, hammock, barbecue, hurricane, and tobacco, all of which entered European languages through Taíno — is no longer spoken as a living tongue. The ball courts are archaeological sites. The zemís are in museum collections in Madrid and New York. The cassava cultivation the Taíno developed remains a staple of Cuban agriculture, as does tobacco, but those are remnants — fragments of a world that was comprehensively dismantled within a single human lifetime.

The population that replaced the Taíno — Spanish colonists, and the hundreds of thousands of enslaved Africans who would be brought to the island over the following three centuries — built their lives on ground cleared of its original inhabitants with a thoroughness that would have been unthinkable to the people who lived there before Columbus arrived.

The Pattern Established

Bartolomé de las Casas portrait (with indigenous figure) was a rare insider voice of conscience

What the Spanish did to the Taíno established a pattern that Cuba would experience repeatedly across the next five centuries, in varying forms and with varying degrees of violence. An outside power arrives with its own interests, its own ideology, and its own conviction that what it is doing is justified by history, commerce, religion, or geopolitics. The people already living on the island — or brought to the island against their will — are incorporated into the system as labor, or displaced, or destroyed, depending on their utility and their capacity for resistance. Their welfare is not the primary concern of the power making the decisions.

Spain did not come to Cuba to serve the Taíno. It came to extract gold, and when the gold ran out, to grow sugar. The United States did not come to Cuba in 1898 to serve the Cuban people. It came to establish strategic dominance over the Caribbean and commercial advantage for American businesses. The Soviet Union did not come to Cuba in the 1960s to serve the Cuban people. It came to establish a strategic outpost ninety miles from the American coast. And when Donald Trump orchestrates a blockade that will, by the estimates of American analysts tracking Cuba’s oil reserves, leave Cuban hospitals without the fuel to run their equipment by the middle of March 2026, the Cuban people’s welfare is not the organizing consideration.

This is the pattern. It has been running for five hundred and thirty years.

The Taíno did not ask to be at the center of it. Neither has anyone who has lived on that island since.
