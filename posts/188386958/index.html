When Democracy Fights Back

The Two-Front War

Part 15: How Redistricting and Voter Suppression Became a Coordinated Strategy

February 23, 2026

North Carolina’s enacted 2025 congressional map (used for 2026 elections), showing aggressive mid-decade redistricting that shifts boundaries in the east to create an additional Republican-leaning seat—part of the ‘Front One: The Maps’ architecture to rig outcomes before votes are cast (Source North Carolina General Assembly Redistricting page)

For the past fourteen parts of this series, I’ve been tracking one front of a war over American democracy: who draws the maps. The gerrymandered districts, the court battles, the referendums, the scorecards.

But maps are only half the equation.

While state legislatures have been redrawing congressional districts in Texas, Florida, Virginia, and a dozen other states, Congress has been working on the other half: restricting who gets to vote on those maps. And the federal government has been working on a third track that nobody voted for at all — seizing ballots, suing states for voter data, and laying the groundwork to challenge election results before a single vote is cast in November.

These aren’t separate stories. They’re the same story.

Gerrymander the maps. Restrict the voters. Intimidate the administrators. Three fronts, one objective: make the electorate as small, as controllable, and as favourable as possible before Election Day arrives.

Front One: The Maps

You know this part. We’ve covered it in detail.

Texas added five Republican seats through mid-decade redistricting. California retaliated with five Democratic seats. Florida is preparing to add two to four more Republican seats in a special session in April. Virginia’s Supreme Court imposed new maps that could flip three to four seats to Democrats — if voters approve a referendum on April 21. The Callais case at the Supreme Court could gut Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act entirely, opening the door to racial gerrymandering across the Deep South.

The net math, as we documented in Part 13, ranges from D+2 to R+3 — almost exactly where the New York Times’ own tracker landed when they published it on February 17.

That’s the architecture. Now here’s who gets to walk through the door.

Front Two: The Laws

The House passes the SAVE America Act (February 11, 2026) on a near-party-line 218-213 vote—requiring strict proof-of-citizenship for voter registration. This ‘Front Two: The Laws’ measure could disenfranchise millions despite rare noncitizen voting, serving as a ready-made alibi for future election challenges.

On February 11, the House passed the SAVE America Act on a 218-213 vote. Every Republican voted yes. Every Democrat but one — Henry Cuellar of Texas — voted no.

The bill would require documentary proof of citizenship to register to vote. Not a driver’s licence — that doesn’t prove citizenship. A passport, a birth certificate, a naturalization certificate. In person.

Let that sink in. Thirty-six percent of Americans don’t have a valid passport. Eleven percent of registered voters don’t have ready access to their birth certificate. Nine percent of eligible voters lack any documentary proof of citizenship at all. That’s roughly 21 million Americans who could lose the ability to register.

And the problem the bill purports to solve? It barely exists. Utah reviewed its entire voter registration list — more than 2 million names — and found exactly one confirmed noncitizen registration and zero instances of noncitizen voting. Georgia audits before the 2024 election found 20 noncitizen registrations out of 8.2 million voters. Nine of those actually cast a ballot. The Bipartisan Policy Center documented just 77 confirmed cases of noncitizen voting in 24 years nationwide.

The SAVE America Act would also effectively kill mail and online voter registration, since new registrants would need to appear in person with their documents. When Kansas tried a similar proof-of-citizenship requirement in 2013, 31,000 people — 12 percent of all registration applicants — were blocked. The state could identify only 39 noncitizens who had registered in the previous 14 years. A federal court struck the law down in 2018.

Required documentary proof under the SAVE America Act: passport, birth certificate, or naturalization papers—not a standard driver’s license. An estimated 21 million eligible Americans lack easy access to these, illustrating the barriers imposed by ‘Front Two: The Laws’ to shrink and control the electorate.

But the SAVE Act is actually the moderate bill. The real ambition is the Make Elections Great Again Act — yes, the MEGA Act — introduced by House Administration Committee Chairman Bryan Steil on January 30. It includes everything in the SAVE Act plus:

Ban universal vote-by-mail. The eight states and D.C. that automatically send ballots to every registered voter would be prohibited from doing so. Voters would have to affirmatively request a mail ballot.

Ban counting ballots received after Election Day. Fourteen states and D.C. currently accept mail ballots postmarked by Election Day but arriving after. That practice — which disproportionately affects military voters, rural voters, and voters in states with slow postal service — would end.

Ban ranked-choice voting in federal elections. Maine and Alaska, which use the system, would be forced to abandon it.

Ban “ballot harvesting.” Except for immediate family members or caregivers, nobody could collect and deliver another person’s mail ballot. The bill would make it a felony to deliver more than four ballots.

Mandate monthly voter roll purges. States would be required to compare their voter lists against the Department of Homeland Security’s citizenship database on a monthly basis — the same database that multiple courts have found to be riddled with errors.

The irony is exquisite. Republicans who argued in 2021 that Democrats’ For the People Act was an unconstitutional federal takeover of state elections are now proposing a federal takeover of state elections. Senator Lisa Murkowski, Republican of Alaska, noticed: “When Democrats attempted to advance sweeping election reform legislation in 2021, Republicans were unanimous in opposition because it would have federalized elections, something we have long opposed.”

Neither bill is likely to become law. The Senate filibuster requires 60 votes, and Republicans hold only 53. Thune has said there aren’t “anywhere close to the votes” to eliminate the filibuster for this legislation. But passage isn’t the only purpose these bills serve.

If the SAVE America Act fails in the Senate — as expected — Trump and his allies will have a readymade narrative for November: the election wasn’t secure because Democrats blocked the bill that would have secured it. The legislation that can’t pass becomes the excuse for the fraud claims that will follow. The bill is the alibi.

Front Three: The Raids

FBI agents execute a January 28, 2026 raid on Fulton County’s Elections Hub, seizing over 700 boxes of 2020 election materials—establishing federal intimidation tactics in ‘Front Three: The Raids’ and setting precedents for challenging results or accessing data mid-cycle.

And then there’s what’s happening outside the legislative process entirely.

On January 28, FBI agents arrived at the Fulton County, Georgia, Elections Hub with a search warrant and seized more than 700 boxes of ballots, tabulator tapes, digital ballot images, and voter rolls from the 2020 election. Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard showed up in person — a domestic law enforcement action attended by the nation’s top intelligence official, who has no domestic law enforcement authority.

Trump ordered Gabbard to facilitate a phone call between himself and the FBI agents executing the warrant — the President of the United States directly inserting himself into the seizure of election materials from a county he has accused, without evidence, of costing him the 2020 election.

The warrant was based on an affidavit that, as NPR documented in a detailed analysis, omitted key findings from previous state investigations — findings that concluded the county’s election results were accurate. Georgia had already counted, recounted, hand-recounted, and audited the Fulton County results. Every court challenge failed. The affidavit originated with Kurt Olsen, who advised Trump during his attempts to overturn the 2020 results and now serves as Trump’s “director of election security and integrity.”

The Fulton County seizure is not an isolated incident. The Justice Department has sued at least 23 states and the District of Columbia to obtain detailed voter registration data. Federal courts in several states have rejected these attempts. The NAACP has asked a judge to prohibit the government from using seized data for anything other than the stated criminal investigation — including voter roll purges, election administration, or immigration enforcement.

The Brennan Center’s Wendy Weiser put it plainly: the seizure could be a practice run for federal officials trying to access voting machines or ballots during the 2026 midterms themselves. If the FBI claims authority to take election materials under the guise of fraud investigations now, it could attempt the same while votes are being counted in November.

Trump himself has been explicit. He called on Republicans to “nationalize” elections and “take over the voting in at least 15 places.” He told the New York Times he regretted not ordering the National Guard to seize voting machines in 2020.

This is not subtlety. This is not inference. This is stated intent.

The Vise

Here’s how the three fronts work together.

Redistricting rigs the architecture — it determines how many votes each party needs to win each seat. The SAVE Act and MEGA Act rig the electorate — they determine who is allowed to cast those votes. And the Fulton County playbook rigs the aftermath — it establishes a mechanism for challenging results that don’t go the right way.

Think of it as a vise with three jaws:

First, draw maps that require fewer of your voters and more of theirs to produce the same result. That’s the gerrymander.

Second, make it harder for their voters to register, to vote by mail, to have their ballots counted. That’s the voter suppression.

Third, if all of that still isn’t enough, establish the precedent that federal agents can seize ballots, demand voter data, and challenge results after the fact. That’s the intimidation.

No single jaw is sufficient on its own. Gerrymandering doesn’t work if turnout swamps the margins. Voter suppression doesn’t work if the maps are fair. Intimidation doesn’t work if institutions hold firm. But together, they create a system in which the margin for democratic accountability keeps shrinking.

Each front reinforces the others. The SAVE Act’s failure becomes the justification for questioning results. The FBI raids intimidate election officials into compliance. The gerrymandered maps ensure that even a successful resistance on one front isn’t enough to overcome the structural disadvantage on the others.

What Canada Doesn’t Have

I keep coming back to this because it’s where I live and it’s what I know.

Canada doesn’t have voter ID legislation designed to reduce turnout — we have universal voter registration through our tax system. You file your taxes, you’re registered. Canada doesn’t have proof-of-citizenship requirements at the polls — we have multiple ways to verify identity, including having a registered voter vouch for you. Canada doesn’t have a federal government seizing ballots from provincial election offices — the very concept is unthinkable.

We have our problems. Our politics can be ugly and our leaders can be disappointing. But we settled the basic question of electoral architecture sixty years ago, and we settled the basic question of voter access decades before that. Nobody in Canada runs for office on a platform of making it harder for citizens to vote. The idea would be disqualifying.

In America, it’s a legislative priority.

What to Watch

The SAVE America Act now sits in the Senate, where it is expected to fail. But watch for two things: whether Trump escalates pressure to eliminate the filibuster, and whether any version of the MEGA Act’s provisions get attached to must-pass legislation like a government funding bill.

Fulton County’s lawsuit seeking the return of its ballots is pending. The NAACP’s motion to restrict use of the seized voter data is before a judge. Gabbard is scheduled to testify before the Senate Intelligence Committee on March 18.

And in the background, the DOJ’s lawsuits against 23 states for voter data continue to work through the courts. Each one is a test case. Each one establishes precedent. Each one moves the line a little further.

November is nine months away. The maps are being drawn. The rules are being rewritten. And the enforcement mechanisms are being tested.

Three fronts. One objective. And the clock is running.
