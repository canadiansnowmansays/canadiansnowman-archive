When Democracy Fights Back

The Legal Trap

Part 5: When a Partisan Lawsuit Accidentally Threatens All Gerrymandering

November 14, 2025

If Trump’s DOJ wins against California, they might create precedent that destroys gerrymandering nationwide—including in Texas

In Part 4, we documented the stunning hypocrisy of the Trump Justice Department: giving legal cover to Texas’s partisan gerrymander while immediately suing California for doing the exact same thing.

But there’s a delicious irony buried in this partisan lawsuit: if DOJ wins, they might accidentally create legal precedent that ends up invalidating every Republican gerrymander in the country.

Sometimes the most partisan actors create the tools for their own destruction.

Why California Will Likely Win

Legal scholars say the case against California faces long odds. Plaintiffs would have to prove that the primary intent of map drawers—and the California voters who approved Prop 50—was to racially gerrymander without proper evidence of racially polarized voting. CalMatters

“It would be a very hard case to win,” said Emily Rong Zhang, an assistant professor of law at UC Berkeley School of Law. CalMatters

Here’s why California has a strong defense:

Defense #1: The Maps Are Nearly Identical on Racial Representation

Analysis by the Public Policy Institute of California shows the Prop 50 plan matches the independent Citizens Redistricting Commission plan almost exactly on minority representation. The CRC plan includes 13 districts where voters of color constitute at least 55% of the citizen voting age population. The proposed plan has 14. PPIC

Both plans also include 6 Asian American, 2 Black, and 7 Latino “influence districts” where minority voters comprise at least 30% of the population—enough to influence electoral outcomes. PPIC

If California was engaging in racial gerrymandering, you’d expect to see dramatic changes in minority representation. Instead, the racial makeup is virtually unchanged.

The map changed partisan outcomes (that’s the point), but it didn’t significantly alter racial representation. And that distinction is legally critical.

Defense #2: Partisan Gerrymandering Is Explicitly Legal

Here’s the kicker that makes DOJ’s case so difficult: In Rucho v. Common Cause (2019), the Supreme Court ruled that partisan gerrymandering claims are non-justiciable—meaning federal courts can’t touch them.

This creates an important legal distinction: racial gerrymandering claims can still be brought, whereas the Supreme Court has clearly said that partisan gerrymandering claims are non-justiciable. KCRA

The DOJ is claiming that the focus of legislators who proposed redrawing the map was to make more safe Hispanic voting districts rather than simply more safe Democratic-leaning districts. KCRA

But California’s defense is straightforward: We were engaged in partisan gerrymandering (which is legal), not racial gerrymandering. The fact that some of those districts happen to have significant Hispanic populations doesn’t make it racial—it makes it geographic and political.

Republicans love to point out that Hispanic voters don’t vote monolithically. If that’s true (and it is), then drawing districts to advantage Democrats isn’t the same as drawing districts to advantage Hispanic voters. It’s political, not racial.

Defense #3: Democratic Legitimacy

Nearly 65% of California voters approved Proposition 50. CNNNBC News This wasn’t a backroom legislative deal—it was a statewide referendum with extensive public debate.

Surveys showed 85% of Democrats, 60% of independents, and even 22% of Republicans supported the measure. NPR Support crossed demographic lines: 70% of voters under 30, 67% aged 30-44, and 56% aged 65 and over voted yes. UCR News

Compare this to Texas, where Abbott was initially resistant to redistricting until Trump personally called him, and the majority of Texas’s GOP congressional delegation was wary of the plan. Chiggerfest No Texas voter referendum. No public approval. Just legislative fiat at Trump’s command.

If courts are going to invalidate democratic referendums with 65% approval, they better have extraordinarily compelling evidence of unconstitutional conduct. DOJ’s evidence? Some legislators mentioned responding to Texas’s efforts to “suppress minority voters.”

That’s not proof of racial intent—that’s proof of partisan response to partisan action.

Defense #4: The Sunset Provision Shows Good Faith

California’s map includes a critical feature that Texas lacks: Prop 50 allows the governor to circumvent the state’s nonpartisan redistricting commission for three elections but returns redistricting authority to the commission after the 2030 Census. The Washington Post

This temporary measure shows California isn’t trying to permanently entrench partisan advantage—it’s responding to a specific crisis (Texas redistricting) with a time-limited countermeasure.

Courts typically look favorably on sunset provisions as evidence of good-faith emergency action rather than permanent power grabs.

The Three Possible Outcomes

This lawsuit will likely produce one of three results, each with dramatically different implications for American democracy:

Outcome A: Selective Enforcement Wins (Probability: Low)

The court strikes down California’s map but allows Texas, Missouri, and North Carolina maps to stand.

This would be the nightmare scenario—explicit confirmation that DOJ enforces voting rights laws only against Democrats.

Why it’s unlikely: Even conservative judges might balk at such obvious double standards. Federal judges care about institutional legitimacy, and a ruling this nakedly partisan would damage the judiciary’s credibility.

Consequence if it happens: Complete death of neutral law enforcement. Every DOJ action becomes presumptively partisan. Blue states stop cooperating with federal civil rights enforcement.

Outcome B: The Accidental Reform (Probability: Moderate)

The court rules that California’s map violates the 14th/15th Amendments AND establishes precedent that applies equally to all mid-decade redistricting.

This would accidentally create the national redistricting standards that Congress has failed to pass.

Why it’s possible: Courts hate being used for partisan purposes. Conservative judges might establish bright-line rules that say: “If California’s map is illegal, so is Texas’s. Apply these standards everywhere.”

The precedent would require examining:

Whether race was a predominant factor in drawing districts

Whether communities of color were packed or cracked for partisan gain

Whether map drawers explicitly cited race in public statements

Now apply those standards to Texas:

DOJ itself claimed four Texas districts were “coalition districts” that used race

Those districts were held by Black and Latino Democrats and were explicitly dismantled

Civil rights advocates argue the new Texas map illegally disadvantages minority communities

Same for Missouri. And North Carolina. And Louisiana.

Consequence: Republicans hand opponents a legal weapon to challenge every GOP gerrymander. Trump DOJ accidentally creates the framework to undo Trump-backed redistricting.

This would be a spectacular own goal.

Outcome C: California Wins, Cycle Accelerates (Probability: High)

The court upholds California’s map, finding it’s partisan (legal) not racial (illegal) gerrymandering.

Why it’s most likely: This requires the smallest legal leap. Courts can point to PPIC data showing racial composition barely changed, note the 65% voter approval, and rule that partisan motivation doesn’t equal racial gerrymandering just because some Democratic districts have Hispanic voters.

Consequence: Exactly what I warned about in Part 3—the doom loop accelerates. More states redraw mid-decade. 2026 becomes the first truly pre-determined midterm election. Both parties fight with gerrymanders, voters become more cynical, and democracy erodes.

The Louisiana v. Callais Wildcard

But there’s a fourth possibility that could make everything moot.

The Supreme Court is currently weighing Louisiana v. Callais, which could strike down the part of the Voting Rights Act that requires creating districts in which racial and ethnic minorities have a chance to elect their preferred candidate. CalMatters

If the Court guts this provision of the VRA, it could render both the Prop 50 maps and those drawn by the independent redistricting commission unconstitutional. CalMatters

Here’s the cruel irony: If SCOTUS strikes down Section 2 of the VRA, it would actually help California’s defense.

DOJ’s entire case rests on claiming California violated the VRA by improperly considering race. But if the VRA no longer requires considering race, then California can’t have violated it.

Meanwhile, Texas’s maps—which Texas admits were drawn “regardless of racial makeup”—would still stand because partisan gerrymandering is explicitly legal per Rucho.

In other words: A conservative Supreme Court trying to gut voting rights protections might accidentally make California’s maps more legally defensible than Texas’s.

The universe has a dark sense of humor.

The Precedent Problem Republicans Aren’t Thinking About

If DOJ wins this case and establishes that California engaged in racial gerrymandering, they’ve just created a roadmap for challenging every Republican redistricting effort:

Step 1: Show that race was discussed in legislative debate (California legislators mentioned responding to efforts to “suppress minority voters”)

Step 2: Show that minority-held districts were targeted (true in both California and Texas)

Step 3: Argue that partisan gain = proxy for racial motivation when minorities vote heavily Democratic

If that’s the legal standard, Texas is toast. So is North Carolina. So is Louisiana.

Justin Levitt, a constitutional law expert who served in DOJ under President Barack Obama, said the agency misinterpreted the relevant case law in the Texas letter. The Texas Tribune But now they’ve opened a door they might not be able to close.

The beauty of legal precedent is that it cuts both ways. You can’t establish rules that apply only to your enemies. And while this DOJ might try selective enforcement, courts eventually have to apply consistent legal principles.

When Democracy Requires Broken Tools

As Adelaide Stone Van Gessel, a California voter, told NPR: “I don’t agree with partisan gerrymandering in practice. But we can’t play the game like decent people anymore. Those rules are out the window. So, we have to do what we have to do.” NPR

That sentiment—”we have to do what we have to do”—captures the tragedy of this moment.

In Part 3, I wrote about the doom loop: both parties, acting rationally within a broken system, collectively destroying democracy. This DOJ lawsuit is that doom loop in action.

California shouldn’t have passed Prop 50 in an ideal world. But we don’t live in an ideal world—we live in a world where:

Texas redrew maps at Trump’s command

DOJ gave legal cover then refused to enforce

Missouri and North Carolina followed suit

Trump’s political team openly coordinates redistricting strategy

The Justice Department prosecutes Democratic states while protecting Republican ones

The only thing worse than gerrymandering is selective enforcement of anti-gerrymandering laws. At least when both sides gerrymander, there’s a perverse equilibrium. But when the Justice Department becomes a partisan weapon that prosecutes Democratic gerrymanders while enabling Republican ones, you don’t have a democracy anymore.

You have a rigged game where one team gets to break the rules with impunity while the other gets penalized for trying to compete.

Conclusion: The Trap Within the Trap

This lawsuit will likely fail on the merits. California’s legal defense is strong, the voter mandate is clear, and the evidence of racial gerrymandering is thin.

But even if California wins, we all lose.

Because the existence of this lawsuit—the brazen double standard, the conflict of interest, the selective enforcement—reveals that the Justice Department has been fully weaponized as a partisan tool.

And that, ultimately, is more dangerous than any gerrymander.

Gerrymandering rigs elections. But a partisan Justice Department rigs the entire legal system.

When Americans can’t trust that laws will be enforced fairly regardless of party, when DOJ gives cover to allies and prosecutes opponents, when the top civil rights official’s private law firm sues states that her government department then joins—the rule of law is dead.

The only silver lining? Republicans might be too clever for their own good.

If this case goes badly for DOJ, they’ll have established precedent that can be used to challenge every Trump-backed gerrymander in the country. They’ll have opened a legal door they can’t close.

Sometimes the most partisan actors create the tools for their own destruction.

Let’s hope the courts force them to live with the precedent they’re trying to create.
