What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 5: The Tax Dodge — How Trump Turned Nearly a Billion in Losses Into Decades Without Paying Income Tax (1995-2018)

December 23, 2025

“That Makes Me Smart”

At the first presidential debate in September 2016, Hillary Clinton confronted Donald Trump about his refusal to release his tax returns. “Maybe he doesn’t want the American people to know that he’s paid nothing in federal taxes,” Clinton suggested.

Trump’s response became infamous: “That makes me smart.”

Three weeks later, The New York Times published leaked pages from Trump’s 1995 state tax returns showing a staggering $916 million loss — a tax deduction so substantial it could have allowed Trump to legally avoid paying any federal income taxes for up to 18 years. The documents confirmed what Clinton had suspected: Trump had structured his financial affairs to pay little or nothing in federal income taxes, even while earning millions of dollars annually from The Apprentice, his casino operations, and real estate deals.

Trump’s tax avoidance wasn’t technically illegal — it was far more insidious. He used legal loopholes designed to benefit wealthy real estate developers to transform epic business failures into personal tax windfalls. A comprehensive 2018 New York Times investigation revealed Trump had engaged in tax dodges for decades, helping his parents dodge taxes and receiving at least $413 million from his father’s real estate empire. While contractors went unpaid, investors lost everything, and workers faced layoffs, Trump extracted millions in personal wealth and avoided contributing to the society that enabled his enrichment.

This is the story of how America’s tax code allows the wealthy to privatize gains while socializing losses — and how Trump perfected the art of turning financial catastrophe into personal profit, all while ordinary Americans paid their full share.

The $916 Million Loss: “Financial Wreckage” Becomes Tax Gold

In October 2016, The New York Times received an envelope postmarked from New York City with a Trump Tower return address. Inside were the first pages of Trump’s 1995 New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut state income tax returns.

The documents revealed that Trump had declared a net operating loss of $915,729,293 — nearly a billion dollars.

The sources of this staggering loss were Trump’s business catastrophes of the early 1990s:

  • Three bankrupt Atlantic City casinos (Trump Taj Mahal, Trump Plaza, Trump’s Castle)

  • The failed Trump Shuttle airline

  • The Plaza Hotel in Manhattan, purchased for $393 million and hemorrhaging money

  • Trump’s 282-foot yacht, which he was forced to sell

These weren’t abstract business losses. They represented real devastation: contractors unpaid, workers laid off, small businesses bankrupted, investors wiped out. As we documented in Part 2, the human cost was immense.

But for Trump personally, these losses became extraordinarily valuable.

Under IRS rules in effect in 1995, net operating losses (NOLs) could be carried forward for 15 years and backward for 3 years. This meant Trump’s $916 million loss could potentially eliminate his taxable income from 1992 through 2010 — nearly two decades — if his combined income during that period was less than $916 million.

Tax experts hired by The Times calculated that a $916 million loss “would have been large enough to wipe out more than $50 million a year in taxable income over 18 years.”

The implications were stunning. Trump could earn:

  • $50,000 to $100,000 per episode of The Apprentice

  • $45 million in salary and bonuses as chairman and CEO of Trump Hotels and Casino Resorts (1995-2009)

  • Millions more from real estate deals, licensing fees, and other ventures

And pay zero federal income taxes on all of it.

“Do you realize you can create $916 million in income without paying a nickel in taxes?” asked Joel Rosenfeld, assistant professor at NYU’s Schack Institute of Real Estate, describing what he would tell a client with Trump’s tax situation.

Or as another expert put it: “He has a vast benefit from his destruction.”

How It Worked: The Mechanics of Tax Avoidance

Understanding Trump’s tax avoidance requires understanding how America’s tax code treats real estate developers with extraordinary generosity.

Real Estate Depreciation Commercial real estate can be depreciated for tax purposes over 39 years. This means owners can deduct the theoretical “wear and tear” on buildings from their taxable income, even if the buildings are actually appreciating in value. Trump’s 1995 returns showed $15.8 million in losses from real estate depreciation alone.

Net Operating Losses When a business has more deductible expenses than income, it generates a net operating loss. For businesses organized as partnerships or LLCs — Trump’s preferred structure — the owner can deduct these losses against other income. If the losses are large enough, they can eliminate tax liability entirely for multiple years.

The “Partnership Interest-for-Debt” Loophole This is where Trump’s tax engineering became especially aggressive. When his casino companies restructured debt in bankruptcy, Trump’s advisors argued he could exclude the forgiven debt from his income by treating the old bonds as partnership debt.

Normally, when debt is forgiven, the IRS treats that as income — because you received money (when you borrowed) that you no longer have to pay back. But Trump’s lawyers found a loophole. If a partnership exchanges its equity for outstanding debt, it might not have to recognize income from the debt forgiveness.

Here’s the critical detail: Trump’s own lawyers explicitly refused to give a tax opinion that this position was “more-likely-than-not to prevail” if the IRS challenged it. They estimated Trump’s prospects of success at 50/50 at best.

Tax experts who analyzed the situation for The Tax Policy Center concluded: “If the IRS disputed Trump’s exclusion of income from just these three bond restructurings [Taj Mahal, Plaza, Castle], it could have eliminated about half of his $916 million of NOLs. And Trump might have begun to pay taxes sooner.”

In other words, Trump took an aggressive, legally questionable position that inflated his losses — and apparently the IRS never challenged it, or if they did, the challenge didn’t succeed.

The Double Benefit: Losing Other People’s Money

The most galling aspect of Trump’s tax avoidance is that he created massive tax deductions from losses that were largely borne by others.

How the Scheme Worked:

  1. Borrow Massively: Trump borrowed billions to build and acquire casinos, hotels, and other properties. He personally guaranteed hundreds of millions but put little of his own money at risk.

  2. Extract Personal Wealth: Even as the businesses failed, Trump paid himself millions in salary, bonuses, and management fees. He shifted personal debts onto the casino companies’ balance sheets.

  3. Let the Businesses Fail: When the over-leveraged companies couldn’t service their debt, Trump let them file for bankruptcy protection.

  4. Dump Losses on Others: Bondholders got pennies on the dollar. Contractors got 30 cents on the dollar or nothing. Workers lost their jobs. Shareholders in Trump’s public company saw their stock fall from $35.50 to 17 cents.

  5. Claim Tax Deductions: Trump then claimed the business losses as personal tax deductions, even though he had extracted personal wealth from the failing businesses and the actual losses fell on creditors, contractors, and investors.

As investigative journalist David Cay Johnston explained: “Trump dumped the real costs of all this on investors who saw gold in his brand name, but who lost everything even as he was paid tens of millions of tax-free dollars.”

During the 13 years Trump served as chairman of Trump Hotels and Casino Resorts, the publicly traded company lost $1.1 billion. Investors were wiped out. Trump was paid $82 million — likely all of it received income-tax-free thanks to his NOLs.

Johnston’s conclusion: “So while Trump made money at every turn, the banks that lent him money, the workers and small businesses who delivered for Trump, and the investors in his casino company all got stiffed. And while they paid taxes on whatever income they did manage to collect, Trump enjoyed at least $916 million of tax-free income.”

The Known Years: A Pattern of Zero Tax Liability

While Trump has never released his full tax returns, fragmentary records reveal a consistent pattern:

1978-1979: Trump incurred no federal income tax liability, according to New Jersey gambling regulators who reviewed his returns when Trump sought a casino license.

1984: Trump’s return from this year, disclosed in two New York State legal disputes in the early 1990s, showed he paid no federal income taxes.

1993: According to a 1995 Division of Gaming Control report, Trump’s net operating loss in 1993 would “be more than sufficient to satisfy all anticipated gains” — meaning no taxes owed.

1995: The $916 million loss year, allowing for up to 18 years of zero federal income tax liability.

1996-2010: Potentially no federal income taxes paid if the 1995 loss exceeded accumulated income during this period.

Tax experts estimate that Trump could have legally avoided all federal income taxes from the mid-1990s through at least 2010, covering his entire run on The Apprentice (2004-2015) and much of his time as a public company CEO.

“Smart” or Socially Destructive?

Trump and his surrogates defended the tax avoidance as savvy business. After The Times published the 1995 returns, Trump’s campaign released a statement: “Mr. Trump is a highly-skilled businessman who has a fiduciary responsibility to his business, his family and his employees to pay no more tax than legally required.”

Rudy Giuliani, Trump’s surrogate, went further on ABC’s This Week: “He’s a genius at how to take advantage of legal remedies that can help your company survive and grow. I want a man who’s a genius at figuring out how to take this country, that’s moving in the wrong direction.”

New Jersey Governor Chris Christie called Trump’s tax avoidance proof that “he’s an absolute genius at understanding the tax code.”

This framing presents tax avoidance as laudable — proof of intelligence and business acumen. But it ignores fundamental questions about the social contract:

Who Pays for Government Services? While Trump paid little or no federal income taxes for years, he benefited from:

  • FBI protection

  • Secret Service when he became a candidate

  • Federal courts to enforce his contracts and bankruptcy protections

  • Infrastructure that enabled his businesses

  • A military that protected his international operations

  • Regulatory frameworks that allowed him to operate casinos and real estate developments

All of these government services were funded by ordinary Americans who paid their full tax share.

Is It “Smart” to Shift Costs to Others? Trump’s tax strategy depended on dumping business losses onto creditors, contractors, and investors while extracting personal wealth. Forest Jenkins got 30 cents on the dollar for his work. Edward Friel’s company went bankrupt. Thousands of workers lost their jobs. Bondholders lost billions.

Those people all paid their income taxes in full, even as Trump — who enriched himself from their work and investment — paid nothing.

Does This Represent Sound Policy? America’s tax code treats real estate developers with extraordinary generosity, allowing depreciation deductions for assets that may be appreciating, permitting passive loss treatment, and enabling creative debt restructuring. These provisions were designed to encourage real estate development.

But Trump’s example reveals the policy failure. He built casinos that failed, left economic devastation in Atlantic City, extracted personal wealth, and then used the failures to avoid taxes for decades. This is exactly opposite of what the tax incentives were intended to achieve.

The IRS That Didn’t Bark

Perhaps the most important accountability failure in Trump’s tax history is what didn’t happen: aggressive IRS enforcement.

A $916 million loss is extraordinary. As NYU professor Joel Rosenfeld told The Times: “The IRS, when they see a negative $916 million, that has to pop out.”

Yet there’s no evidence the IRS successfully challenged Trump’s aggressive tax positions. Tax experts who reviewed Trump’s approach to excluding forgiven debt from income concluded his own lawyers wouldn’t certify the position as more-likely-than-not to succeed — suggesting it was aggressive at best, potentially abusive.

If the IRS had challenged and prevailed on just the debt forgiveness exclusions, it could have eliminated roughly half of Trump’s $916 million in NOLs, requiring him to pay hundreds of millions in taxes over subsequent years.

Why didn’t this happen? Possible explanations:

  1. Resource Constraints: The IRS has been systematically underfunded for decades, reducing its capacity to audit wealthy individuals with sophisticated tax strategies.

  2. Complexity: Trump’s tax structures involved partnerships, real estate depreciation, debt restructurings, and complex legal arguments that require specialized expertise to challenge.

  3. Legal Uncertainty: Some of Trump’s positions, while aggressive, may have been defensible under existing tax code provisions.

  4. Settlement: The IRS may have challenged Trump’s positions and reached a confidential settlement that wasn’t made public.

  5. Success: Trump’s positions may have been challenged and survived IRS scrutiny.

Without Trump’s full tax returns and IRS audit history, we can’t know which explanation applies. But we know the outcome: Trump apparently succeeded in avoiding federal income taxes for years using strategies his own lawyers thought had at best a 50-50 chance of surviving IRS challenge.

The Broader Pattern: Tax Code as Wealth Preservation

Trump’s tax history illuminates how America’s tax system perpetuates and amplifies wealth inequality.

The Working Class Pay: Ordinary Americans have taxes automatically withheld from their paychecks. Their income is reported to the IRS by employers. They have few deductions available. They pay their full share.

The Wealthy Optimize: High-income individuals can:

  • Structure income as capital gains (taxed at lower rates)

  • Use partnerships and LLCs to claim business losses against personal income

  • Employ real estate depreciation to shield income

  • Carry losses forward to offset future years’ income

  • Hire sophisticated tax attorneys to find every available loophole

The result is a two-tiered system where wage earners pay full freight while sophisticated investors legally avoid much of their tax liability.

Trump didn’t create this system, but he exploited it masterfully. His tax history demonstrates how someone can:

  • Lose nearly a billion dollars

  • Dump those losses onto creditors and investors

  • Extract tens of millions in personal wealth

  • Pay zero federal income taxes for decades

  • And claim this makes him “smart”

What Should Have Happened

Trump’s tax history should have resulted in:

Aggressive IRS Enforcement: The IRS should have challenged Trump’s aggressive tax positions, particularly the debt forgiveness exclusions. If his own lawyers wouldn’t certify them as more-likely-than-not to succeed, they deserved intense scrutiny.

Public Disclosure: Presidential candidates should be required to release full tax returns. Voters deserved to know Trump’s complete tax history before electing him.

Tax Code Reform: Trump’s ability to avoid taxes while enriching himself from business failures demonstrates need for fundamental tax reform, including:

  • Limits on loss carryforwards

  • Restrictions on using business losses to offset unrelated income

  • Recapture rules requiring repayment of tax benefits when businesses fail

  • Tighter rules on debt forgiveness exclusions

Criminal Investigation: If Trump’s tax positions crossed the line from aggressive planning to fraud, criminal investigations were warranted. The fact that his own lawyers wouldn’t certify some positions suggests they may have been abusive.

None of this happened. Trump’s tax returns remained secret until after his election. IRS enforcement appears to have been minimal or ineffective. Tax code reform that might prevent future Trumps hasn’t materialized. No criminal tax investigation has been publicly disclosed.

The Cost to Society

The societal cost of Trump’s tax avoidance extends beyond the uncollected taxes.

Lost Revenue: If Trump paid even modest federal income taxes during 1995-2010, it would have contributed tens of millions to national priorities: infrastructure, education, defense, social programs.

Moral Hazard: Trump’s success in avoiding taxes while enriching himself from business failures creates incentives for others to pursue similar strategies.

Eroded Trust: When wealthy individuals visibly avoid taxes while ordinary Americans pay their full share, it undermines trust in the fairness of the system.

Policy Distortion: Tax benefits supposedly designed to encourage productive real estate development instead subsidize speculative overleveraging and personal wealth extraction.

Opportunity Cost: The money Trump didn’t pay in taxes was money that couldn’t fund veteran services, infrastructure repairs, or deficit reduction.

Hillary Clinton’s campaign calculated the implications during the 2016 campaign: “This bombshell report reveals the colossal nature of Donald Trump’s past business failures and just how long he may have avoided paying any federal income taxes whatsoever. In one year, Donald Trump lost nearly a billion dollars... He apparently got to avoid paying taxes for nearly two decades -- while tens of millions of working families paid theirs.”

“If We All Did as Trump Does”

Investigative journalist David Cay Johnston captured the fundamental problem with Trump’s tax avoidance: “If we all did as Trump does, we would, to quote a line Trump employs often, not have our country anymore.”

This is the ultimate accountability failure. Trump benefited enormously from American society’s investments:

  • Courts that enforced his contracts

  • Bankruptcy protection that allowed him to restructure debts

  • Infrastructure that connected his properties

  • Security that protected his assets

  • A regulatory system that allowed him to operate casinos

  • A legal framework that enabled his real estate empire

Yet he contributed little or nothing in federal income taxes to maintain these systems. He extracted wealth while socializing costs. He claimed this made him “smart.”

But if everyone with Trump’s resources adopted Trump’s strategies, there would be no revenue to fund courts, infrastructure, security, or any government services. The system only works because most people—including wealthy people—pay their share.

Trump’s tax history represents a massive free-rider problem. He benefited from a functioning society while avoiding his fair share of the costs. And rather than face accountability for this, he became president.

Conclusion: The Pattern Complete

Across five installments, we’ve documented Trump’s lifetime pattern of evading accountability:

Part 1: Fraudulent bone spurs draft dodge and housing discrimination Part 2: Casino bankruptcies that devastated contractors while enriching Trump Part 3: Trump University fraud settled for $25 million without admission of guilt Part 4: Trump Foundation used as personal slush fund, forced to dissolve Part 5: Nearly a billion in losses converted to decades of tax avoidance

The pattern is unmistakable:

  1. Engage in illegal, unethical, or socially destructive behavior

  2. Use wealth to hire sophisticated lawyers

  3. Deny everything and attack investigators

  4. Delay accountability through litigation

  5. Settle with money while admitting no wrongdoing

  6. Face no criminal charges

  7. Claim vindication

  8. Repeat

This pattern culminated in Trump’s presidency, where he faced impeachment twice, incited an insurrection, attempted to overturn an election, and was criminally indicted on multiple charges — only to have all criminal cases collapse after his re-election in 2024.

The Georgia election interference case, dismissed in November 2025, represents the end point of a lifetime spent evading accountability. Pete Skandalakis, the prosecutor who moved to dismiss the case, wrote that pursuing Trump after he leaves office in 2029 “would be nothing short of a remarkable feat.”

Translation: Trump has run out the clock again.

American institutions have consistently failed to hold Trump accountable. Civil settlements provide symbolic victories without real deterrence. Criminal prosecutions face political complications and resource constraints. The wealthy enjoy protections unavailable to ordinary citizens.

Until these structural asymmetries are addressed, Trump won’t be the last wealthy American to evade meaningful accountability for a lifetime of misconduct. He’s simply the one who perfected the art.

Part 6 will turn to Trump’s presidency and the Mueller Report’s documentation of ten instances of obstruction of justice. When Special Counsel Robert Mueller found substantial evidence that Trump obstructed justice but was prohibited from indicting a sitting president, he left the judgment to Congress. Congress failed to act. What followed was a pattern that would repeat: overwhelming evidence, no accountability, and Trump’s continued evasion of consequences.
