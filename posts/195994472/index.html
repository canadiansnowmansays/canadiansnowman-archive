The $1,000 Pill and the Childhood Taunt

How America Discovered Fat Was a Disease (After All)

Fatty, fatty, two by four, couldn't get through the bathroom door

April 30, 2026

There was a taunt you heard on every schoolyard in North America, a singsongy cruelty as reliable as recess itself: Fatty, fatty, two by four, couldn’t get through the bathroom door. It was delivered with the casual brutality children reserve for anything that deviates from the norm — and it accomplished something specific. It told the heavy kid that their body was a moral failure. Not a medical condition. Not a genetic variable. Not a complex metabolic reality. A failure of character. A personal referendum they were losing.

That taunt didn’t stay on the schoolyard. It grew up, put on a suit, and ran for office.

Consider Chris Christie — Governor of New Jersey, two-time presidential candidate, and for the better part of a decade the most publicly humiliated large man in American political life. His weight was not a private matter. It was a national conversation topic, a late-night punchline, and a weapon deployed most enthusiastically by Donald Trump — who has spent his adult life in a body his own physicians have placed in the clinically overweight-to-obese range — calling Christie “Krispy Kreme” from rally podiums with the same casual cruelty as the kids on the playground, except with a microphone and a television audience of millions. Christie quietly underwent Lap-Band surgery in 2013, kept it secret for months, and spent the next decade having his body serve as a public referendum conducted by people who had never met him. In 2024, he appeared visibly transformed — slimmer, more energetic — and declined to explain why, which is entirely his right and nobody’s business, and would be treated as such if we had ever collectively decided that obesity was a medical condition rather than a moral one.

The closing irony is almost too neat for fiction. The man who weaponized the fat joke maintained, throughout his political career, a diet that nutritionists would classify as a clinical experiment in cardiovascular risk — Big Macs and Quarter Pounders, Filet-O-Fish by the bag, and by multiple former aide accounts, twelve Diet Cokes a day, as though the word "Diet" on the can were doing meaningful work. His own White House physician declared his health "excellent" in 2018, a finding the medical community received with the polite skepticism it reserves for press releases issued under political duress. This same man is now the President who struck the deals with Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk that extend GLP-1 obesity coverage to millions of Americans for the first time in Medicare's history. Donald Trump — Big Mac enthusiast, Krispy Kreme name-caller, accidental champion of obesity medicine — is a punchline that history wrote for itself. You genuinely could not write it.

The taunt also went to work, more quietly, in insurance underwriting departments, medical school curricula, congressional testimony, and the editorial pages of a thousand magazines that ran before-and-after photos as if human transformation were a spectator sport. The cultural consensus was straightforward and merciless: fat people were fat because they lacked discipline. Eat less, move more, try harder. Fail publicly if you must, but understand the failure is yours alone.

That consensus is now collapsing under the weight — again, pun intended — of some of the most striking clinical evidence in modern pharmacology. A class of drugs originally designed to treat diabetes has detonated the foundational assumption that obesity is a moral condition. It is not. It is a disease. A chronic, biologically complex, stubbornly heritable disease. And we now have medicines that treat it with an effectiveness that would have seemed implausible a decade ago.

The question is not whether the drugs work. They do. This morning, Eli Lilly reported first-quarter 2026 earnings that answered the Wall Street question definitively. The medical question — who actually gets access to these drugs — remains stubbornly open.

The Long, Cruel History of Getting This Wrong

Before the science, a brief tour of the damage done by the prevailing mythology.

The medical establishment’s formal flirtation with obesity as a treatable condition goes back generations — and its track record is not distinguished. Amphetamines were prescribed for weight loss through the 1950s and 60s. Fen-phen, a combination drug that showed real efficacy, was pulled from the market in 1997 after it was linked to heart valve damage. Rimonabant, a cannabinoid receptor blocker that suppressed appetite, was rejected by the FDA in 2007 over psychiatric side effects. Every promising pharmacological intervention seemed to come with a sting in the tail, reinforcing the cultural message: there is no shortcut. Suffer through it. You probably deserve to.

Public health campaigns throughout this era focused relentlessly on behaviour modification — diet, exercise, portion control — while largely ignoring the biological architecture underlying obesity. Research was accumulating, quietly and without celebrity, showing that body weight is regulated by a ferociously complex system of hormones, genetic predispositions, gut microbiome characteristics, and neurological feedback loops. That when you lose weight through caloric restriction, your body actively fights back — reducing metabolic rate, increasing hunger hormones, driving you relentlessly toward the weight it has decided you should be. That for many people, the biological deck is simply stacked against sustained conventional weight loss in a way no amount of willpower can fully overcome.

None of this appeared on the cover of Time. What appeared on the cover of Time was a photograph of a headless obese torso, a visual shorthand for the epidemic — and for the attendant judgment.

Then a funny thing happened. Science, unglamorous and methodical as it always is, intervened.

The Accidental Revolution

Nobody set out to solve obesity. They were trying to solve diabetes.

Glucagon-like peptide-1 — GLP-1 — is a hormone your gut releases after eating. It signals your pancreas to produce insulin, tells your brain you’re full, slows gastric emptying, and generally coordinates the metabolic response to a meal. Researchers had understood the basic mechanism since the 1980s. What took decades was synthesizing stable pharmaceutical analogs — compounds that mimic GLP-1’s effects long enough to matter clinically.

Semaglutide, developed by Novo Nordisk, became Ozempic in 2017 — a weekly injectable approved for Type 2 diabetes management. Doctors and patients began noticing something that wasn’t quite the headline finding of the trial: people were losing significant amounts of weight. Not the 5–8% that previous pharmacological interventions had achieved with fanfare and later recalled with embarrassment. Real weight. Clinical weight. Life-changing weight.

The clinical programme for obesity followed. Wegovy — same molecule, higher dose, new indication — was approved in 2021. The results were striking enough that the business world took notice well before the medical mainstream caught up. Eli Lilly, meanwhile, had been developing tirzepatide — a dual agonist that activates both GLP-1 and GIP receptors simultaneously, a more aggressive mechanism that produced even more aggressive results. Mounjaro arrived for diabetes. Zepbound arrived for obesity. In a head-to-head clinical trial, Zepbound demonstrated 47% greater relative weight loss than Wegovy. The science was not subtle.

The implications kept expanding. Researchers found these drugs reduced cardiovascular risk, protected against fatty liver disease, improved sleep apnea, and showed early promise in areas from kidney disease to neurodegenerative conditions. A Phase 2 clinical trial published in early 2025 suggested semaglutide may reduce alcohol and tobacco cravings. We appear to have stumbled onto a class of drugs that doesn’t just treat obesity — it rewires the brain’s relationship to appetite, reward, and compulsion at a fundamental level. The full implications of that finding remain to be understood. But the direction of travel is extraordinary.

By mid-decade, Eli Lilly had crossed a threshold that would have seemed fantastical in any earlier era of the pharmaceutical industry: it became the first drug company to surpass a $1 trillion market capitalization, carried there almost entirely by the GLP-1 wave. Mounjaro and Zepbound, in their combined run, had already surpassed Merck’s Keytruda as the world’s best-selling medicine.

This morning confirmed the trajectory was not slowing. Lilly’s first-quarter 2026 results landed with the subtlety of a dropped anvil. Worldwide Mounjaro revenue hit $8.66 billion for the quarter — up 125% year-over-year, blowing past analyst expectations of $7.26 billion. Zepbound posted $4.16 billion in U.S. revenue, up 80% from the same period last year. Total company revenue came in at $19.80 billion against an expected $17.62 billion. Net income reached $7.40 billion — nearly triple the $2.76 billion reported a year earlier. The company promptly hiked its full-year revenue guidance to between $82 billion and $85 billion. Lilly’s stock rose more than 6% before the market opened.

A disease that had been dismissed as a lifestyle choice was now generating more quarterly revenue than most countries’ annual healthcare budgets. The schoolyard fat-shamers, had they tracked the stock price, might have rethought their career in cruelty.

The Pill Changes Everything. Almost.

The injectable format of these drugs was always going to limit the market. Weekly self-administered injections are manageable — millions of diabetics do it — but they create genuine barriers. Needle aversion is real. Cold-chain logistics restrict availability in lower-income countries and lower-income communities. The psychological burden of a chronic injection adds friction to an already difficult disease management journey.

Then came the pills.

The FDA approved the Wegovy pill on December 22, 2025 — the first oral GLP-1 to reach the market. Novo Nordisk’s oral semaglutide showed comparable effectiveness to the injections in weight loss trials. Eli Lilly, never content to watch from the sidelines, moved aggressively with its own candidate: orforglipron, a small molecule — meaning it can be manufactured through conventional chemical synthesis rather than the complex biological processes required for peptide drugs — that was approved by the FDA on April 1, 2026, under the brand name Foundayo.

The timing and the details matter. Novo Nordisk’s pill requires the user to take it on an empty stomach, followed by a 30-minute wait before eating or drinking anything other than water. For a person with a structured morning routine and a reliable schedule, manageable. For a shift worker, a parent of young children, or someone with an unpredictable day, that requirement becomes a daily negotiation with a drug that demands more accommodation than many patients can reasonably provide.

Foundayo has no such restrictions. Once daily, any time, with or without food. Lilly’s CEO David Ricks described it as a way to “reach the planet” — and that ambition is not entirely marketing language. Removing cold-chain requirements opens up global distribution in a way that injectable peptides cannot match. The manufacturing pathway for small molecules is also more scalable, which matters enormously when you’re contemplating treating not a niche condition but one that affects hundreds of millions of people worldwide.

The clinical results for Foundayo are serious. In the Phase 3 ATTAIN-1 trial, participants taking the highest dose lost an average of 27.3 pounds — roughly 12.4% of body weight — at 72 weeks. Nearly 60% lost at least 10% of their body weight; almost 40% lost 15% or more. For a person carrying a medically dangerous extra hundred pounds, losing 40 changes the clinical picture entirely — potentially the difference between a joint replacement and functional mobility, between controlled blood pressure and cascading cardiovascular risk.

Foundayo’s early real-world numbers, reported by CEO David Ricks in an interview with CNBC this morning, tell a story beyond the clinical trials. More than 20,000 people started taking the pill in its first few weeks on the market, with over 1,000 new patients beginning the drug every single day. The figure that should give every health policy analyst pause: more than 80% of those patients are new to GLP-1 medications entirely. They were not switching from Zepbound or Wegovy. They were people who had not, until a pill became available, found their way into this treatment universe at all.

This is the market-expansion thesis made real. The pill is not cannibalizing the injections. It is reaching a population the injections never touched — needle-averse patients, patients without reliable cold-storage or injection supplies, patients for whom the weekly shot was simply one barrier too many. Ricks estimated this morning that global GLP-1 use will grow from approximately 20 million patients at the end of 2025 to 30 million by year-end 2026. Ten million new people gaining access to a demonstrably effective treatment, in a single year. The science has cleared every hurdle put in front of it.

Lilly’s injectable Zepbound, for those who can manage the injection, remains the more powerful option — consistent trial evidence of 20%-plus body weight reduction, and in a head-to-head trial, 47% greater relative weight loss than Wegovy. The portfolio now spans pill to shot, from meaningful to transformative. The science arrived. The manufacturing is scaling. The drugs are real.

Everything else is politics and money.

The Catch (There Is Always a Catch)

Here is where the Canadian outsider must stop and say what the pharmaceutical press releases never quite lead with.

These drugs work. They exist. GLP-1 penetration remains at roughly 2% of the obese population worldwide. Two percent. In the United States, where four in ten adults are clinically obese, where obesity drives diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer risk, joint degeneration, sleep disorders, and — as the PNAS journal recently documented — more than 16,000 preventable deaths per year among just two coverage groups, two percent of the people who could benefit from proven treatment are receiving it.

The barrier is not scientific. It is financial. It is political. It is, in a word that keeps appearing in the medical literature whether the authors intend it or not, structural.

Without insurance, the monthly cost of these drugs can exceed $1,000. For the branded oral pills, the starting dose Wegovy pill runs $300 a month; the higher doses of Foundayo reach $400. These are, to borrow the unusually candid language of CVS Caremark’s own spokesman, “egregiously high list prices” — and he was critiquing the very manufacturers whose products his company distributes.

Insurance coverage, meanwhile, has been moving in the wrong direction. From 2025 to 2026, 12 million Americans lost coverage for Zepbound, and another 12 million lost coverage for Wegovy. Employers loved the concept of covering these drugs until they received the invoices. About 55% of commercial employers currently cover GLP-1s for obesity, but 15% of those have already dropped coverage because the costs proved unsustainable. The drugs work, the data is unambiguous, the downstream savings on diabetes, cardiovascular events, and joint replacement are calculable — and still the coverage is evaporating, because the system is optimized for short-term quarterly costs rather than long-term population health.

The class dimension is not incidental. It is central. Obesity prevalence is higher among economically disadvantaged populations. The drugs that treat it most effectively are priced for the economically comfortable. A schoolteacher in Massachusetts named Meghan Lena, whose story NPR recently told, was devastated when CVS Caremark dropped Zepbound from her coverage — and then received a letter informing her that her insurance premium was going up 20% to cover the cost of the GLP-1 drugs that were no longer available to her. She was being charged for a benefit she was denied. “That was the first moment,” she said, “that I was like, holy cow, these insurance companies are now making medical decisions for me.”

Researchers at King’s College London, studying the parallel situation in the United Kingdom, described the emerging landscape as a “two-tier obesity treatment system” where access depends on wealth rather than medical need. They noted something that deserves to be said plainly: the very diagnostic criteria used to gatekeep access to these drugs are systematically under-applied to women, people from minority ethnic communities, low-income patients, and those with severe mental illness. The people most likely to have obesity. The people least likely to receive treatment for it.

The taunting, it turns out, did not stop at the schoolyard. It just put on a different costume.

The Fine Print No One Reads

There is one more inconvenient truth buried in the clinical literature, and it changes the entire economic and policy calculus.

Data published in the British Medical Journal in January 2026 established what the longer trials had been hinting at: patients who stop taking GLP-1 weight loss injections regain weight four times faster than people who stop conventional diet and exercise programs. Four times. The biology, once engaged, does not let go gracefully.

This is not a side effect. This is a feature of the underlying disease. Obesity is a chronic condition, and its pharmacological treatment is, necessarily, a chronic commitment. GLP-1 drugs are not a course of antibiotics you complete and move on from. They are more like antihypertensives — medications that manage a condition as long as you take them and lose efficacy when you stop. That is not a criticism of the drugs. It is a description of the disease.

But it transforms every conversation about access, coverage, and cost. Providing these drugs to millions of people is not a one-time intervention. It is a permanent line item. The insurance industry, which has already shown its willingness to drop coverage when it becomes inconvenient, is being asked to make a multi-decade commitment to a patient population it has historically preferred not to treat. The math of that commitment — weighed against avoided hospitalizations, reduced diabetes incidence, lower cardiovascular event rates — almost certainly favours coverage. But almost certainly is not the same as definitely, and it requires a time horizon that quarterly earnings reports do not encourage.

The Policy Scramble

Governments are not entirely blind to this. The Trump administration — in one of its more coherent policy intervals — struck deals with both Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk in late 2025 to reduce GLP-1 prices for federal beneficiaries. Under the negotiated agreement, Medicare and state Medicaid program prices for Ozempic, Wegovy, Zepbound, and Mounjaro will drop to $245 per month — and Medicare will cover Wegovy and Zepbound for patients with obesity and related comorbidities for the first time in its history. The drugs companies, in exchange, received tariff exemptions on imported pharmaceuticals — a bargain that says as much about the administration’s negotiating style as it does about the underlying public health imperative.

It is real progress. It is also late. The PNAS study estimated that expanding access to these drugs among elderly Medicare patients alone would prevent 7,480 deaths annually. Expanding access among Medicaid beneficiaries would prevent another 8,970 deaths per year. More than 16,000 lives a year — the rough equivalent of four September 11ths — turned on the question of whether the United States was willing to cover a medication for the disease its own public health establishment classifies as a chronic epidemic.

The global picture has its own complexities. Patents for major GLP-1 drugs are set to expire in large markets including China, India, Brazil, Canada, and Turkey — countries that collectively hold 40% of the global population and roughly a third of all adults classified as obese. Generic and biosimilar versions of these drugs could, in principle, make the treatment accessible at a fraction of the current cost. That transition is not guaranteed, not uniform, and not imminent — but it represents perhaps the most significant public health inflection point on the horizon.

Meanwhile, the market itself is already pricing the acceleration. The global GLP-1 weight loss drug market, valued at $13.84 billion in 2024, is projected to reach $48.84 billion by 2030 — and this morning’s earnings suggest that projection may be conservative. Eli Lilly has a more potent injectable in late-stage trials — retatrutide — that in early data showed weight loss exceeding anything currently approved. Novo Nordisk is advancing amycretin, a next-generation compound, through Phase 3 development. The pipeline is not slowing. If anything, the first generation of GLP-1 obesity drugs may turn out to be what the first statins were — important, transformative, and eventually superseded by something better.

The investors know this. The pharmaceutical companies know this. The patients sitting on waiting lists or scanning their coverage letters are learning it the hard way.

What We Actually Owe Each Other

The children who chanted that schoolyard taunt were not monsters. They were repeating what the culture had taught them: that fat was funny, that fat was a choice, that fat was fair game for public ridicule because the fat person had failed at something all of us were expected to do. That belief was not incidental. It shaped how insurance companies wrote exclusions, how physicians allocated their time and compassion, how employers made hiring decisions, how media covered an epidemic it preferred to mock rather than understand.

The GLP-1 revolution does not erase that history. But it does, quietly and rather dramatically, refute its foundational premise. The drugs work precisely because obesity is not a character flaw correctable by motivation. It is a biologically complex, chronically dysregulated condition that responds to medication the way hypertension responds to an ACE inhibitor — not because the patient suddenly found willpower, but because the underlying mechanism was addressed.

That should settle the moral question, even if it doesn’t settle the political one.

What remains is a choice that is not really about pharmacology. It is about whether a society that spent decades dismissing obesity as self-inflicted is willing to treat it as the disease its own scientists have established it to be. Whether a health system built to manage acute conditions can reorganize itself around a chronic one. Whether access to breakthrough medicine will be distributed according to medical need or according to the ability to pay $400 a month indefinitely.

From where I sit, watching from the country to the north that invented public healthcare and then spent fifty years half-dismantling it, the American answer to that question is not obvious. It rarely is. The gap between what the science establishes and what the policy delivers has always been vast, and in the United States, it is bridged by money in ways that would make a Canadian wince.

But the drugs are real. The deaths are preventable. The two-tier system — where Ozempic is a celebrity lifestyle accessory for some and an unattainable prescription for others — is not a medical outcome. It is a political choice.

The fat kid on the schoolyard grew up to be a patient. They are still waiting for the culture to catch up to the science.
