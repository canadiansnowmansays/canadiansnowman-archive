The Myth of the Tariff Golden Age

A Historical and Economic Analysis

Episode 6: Constitutional Guardrails - How Checks and Balances Can Save Us

July 3, 2025

Welcome to Episode 6 of "Trump Tariff and Trade Thursdays." Over the past five weeks, we've deconstructed the golden age mythology, examined historical reality, and analyzed modern evidence. This week, we explore the constitutional safeguards that can prevent economically destructive trade policies—regardless of political rhetoric or golden promises.

The beauty of America's constitutional system is that it doesn't rely on any single person's wisdom, restraint, or economic literacy. Even when presidents promise to recreate mythical golden ages, institutional checks can prevent real-world disasters.

The Founders' Economic Wisdom

The Constitution's framers understood the dangers of concentrated economic power and designed a system to prevent policy disasters. Their experience under the Articles of Confederation, when individual states imposed destructive trade barriers against each other, informed their approach to federal trade authority.

Article I, Section 8 grants Congress explicit power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations." This wasn't an oversight or technicality—it was a deliberate choice to place trade policy under legislative rather than executive control. The founders wanted trade decisions made by representatives accountable to diverse constituencies, not by a single executive who might favor particular regions or industries.

Alexander Hamilton, despite supporting some protection for infant industries, warned against excessive tariffs in Federalist 35: "It is therefore evident that the same arguments which prove the necessity of a government as energetic as the one proposed to us, prove the necessity of a government as energetic as the one proposed to us." He understood that trade policy required careful balance, not executive whim.

Congressional Authority: The Primary Check

Congress retains ultimate authority over trade policy, despite decades of executive expansion:

Constitutional Foundation: All tariff legislation must originate in the House of Representatives under the Constitution's revenue clause. This gives representatives from export-dependent districts direct influence over trade policy.

Delegation vs. Abdication: While Congress has delegated some trade authority to the executive branch through various acts, it retains the power to reclaim this authority at any time. The Trade Expansion Act, Trade Act of 1974, and other legislation include termination clauses and congressional review provisions.

Political Constraints: Representatives from agricultural states, port cities, and export-dependent manufacturing districts have strong electoral incentives to oppose sweeping tariff increases. This creates natural political coalitions against protectionist overreach.

Recent Examples: During the 2018-2020 trade wars, congressional opposition limited some proposed tariff increases and created pressure for negotiated settlements rather than escalating protection.

Judicial Oversight: Courts as Economic Referees

Federal courts provide crucial oversight of executive trade actions, reviewing both procedural compliance and constitutional authority:

Statutory Interpretation: Courts examine whether executive trade actions comply with congressional delegations of authority. Recent decisions have scrutinized the use of national security justifications for tariffs on allies, questioning whether such measures exceed statutory authority.

Due Process Requirements: Trade actions affecting specific companies or industries must follow administrative law procedures, including public comment periods and economic impact assessments. Courts can overturn actions that fail to meet these requirements.

Constitutional Limits: The Supreme Court has never recognized unlimited executive authority over trade policy. Even during wartime, courts have maintained that trade restrictions must serve legitimate governmental purposes and comply with constitutional constraints.

International Law Constraints: U.S. participation in international trade agreements creates legal obligations that courts can enforce. While presidents can withdraw from agreements, they cannot ignore existing commitments without congressional approval.

Recent Judicial Interventions

Courts have already demonstrated willingness to constrain executive trade actions:

Steel and Aluminum Tariffs: Multiple federal courts have heard challenges to the national security justifications for steel and aluminum tariffs on allies like Canada and the European Union. While some cases remain pending, courts have shown skepticism about broad national security claims.

Section 232 Reviews: The Court of International Trade has required detailed economic justifications for tariffs imposed under national security authorities, rejecting blanket claims that don't address specific security concerns.

Administrative Procedure Challenges: Courts have overturned trade actions that failed to follow proper administrative procedures or provide adequate economic analysis of their impacts.

International Constraints: Treaties and Agreements

America's participation in international trade agreements creates additional constraints on unilateral protectionism:

World Trade Organization: WTO rules prohibit discriminatory tariffs and require justification for trade restrictions. While the U.S. can withdraw from the WTO, such action would isolate America from global trade rules and invite retaliation.

USMCA/NAFTA: Regional trade agreements create legal obligations that limit tariff options with major trading partners. Violating these agreements could trigger dispute resolution mechanisms and authorized retaliation.

Bilateral Investment Treaties: These agreements provide legal protections for American companies operating abroad, but also constrain domestic trade policies that might affect foreign investors in the United States.

Federalism: State-Level Constraints

State governments provide additional checks on destructive federal trade policies:

Export Promotion: State governments actively promote exports from their jurisdictions, creating political pressure against policies that harm export industries. Governors from agricultural and manufacturing states often lobby against tariff increases.

Legal Standing: States can challenge federal trade actions that harm their economic interests, providing additional avenues for judicial review of executive trade policies.

Political Mobilization: State-level politicians can organize opposition to destructive trade policies, creating grassroots pressure that complements congressional and judicial constraints.

Private Sector Checks: Economic Realities

Market forces provide practical constraints on tariff policies:

Supply Chain Integration: Modern manufacturing's reliance on international supply chains means that tariffs often harm domestic industries, creating business opposition to protection.

Consumer Resistance: Higher prices due to tariffs create consumer backlash that translates into political pressure against protectionist policies.

Investment Uncertainty: Unpredictable trade policies discourage business investment, creating economic costs that generate opposition from business communities.

Financial Market Reactions: Stock markets and currency values reflect investor skepticism about protectionist policies, creating additional economic and political costs.

The Limits of Executive Power

Recent experience demonstrates that even presidents with strong protectionist instincts face significant constraints:

Implementation Challenges: Complex modern economies make it difficult to implement sweeping tariff changes without creating unintended consequences that generate political opposition.

Bureaucratic Resistance: Career officials in trade agencies often resist economically destructive policies, slowing implementation and creating procedural obstacles.

International Pressure: Diplomatic costs of unilateral protectionism create pressure from defense and foreign policy agencies that compete with trade policy objectives.

Hope for Economic Sanity

The constitutional system works slowly but effectively. While it cannot prevent all bad policies, it creates multiple opportunities to correct course before economic disasters occur:

Gradual Implementation: Constitutional constraints often force gradual rather than immediate policy changes, allowing time for economic evidence to influence political calculations.

Political Learning: Electoral consequences of failed policies create incentives for politicians to modify approaches that harm their constituencies.

Institutional Memory: Congressional staff, judicial precedents, and bureaucratic expertise preserve lessons from past policy failures, making it harder to repeat historical mistakes.
