When Democracy Fights Back

The Database

Part 9: How the Trump Administration Quietly Demanded the Personal Data of Every Voter in America

February 9, 2026

There’s a scene in every heist movie where the audience realizes the robbery didn’t start when the vault door opened. It started months earlier — with the blueprints, the reconnaissance, the inside man. The dramatic moment is just the payoff.

On January 28, 2026, America watched the dramatic moment: FBI agents loading 700 boxes of ballots onto trucks in Fulton County, Georgia, while the Director of National Intelligence lurked in the parking lot like a character from a bad spy novel. That story dominated headlines for a week.

But the real heist started eight months earlier. And almost nobody was paying attention.

The Letters

Assistant Attorney General Harmeet K. Dhillon, who signed the letters sent to secretaries of state demanding complete voter registration data.

In May 2025, letters began arriving at the offices of secretaries of state across America. They came from the Department of Justice’s Civil Rights Division, signed by Assistant Attorney General Harmeet K. Dhillon, and they contained a polite but unmistakable demand: hand over your complete, unredacted voter registration lists.

Not the publicly available versions that campaigns and researchers routinely access. The complete files. Every registered voter’s full name, residential address, date of birth, driver’s license number, and the last four digits of their Social Security number.

At least 47 states and the District of Columbia received these letters. Let that number sink in. The DOJ didn’t target a handful of suspicious jurisdictions. They went after virtually the entire country.

The legal theory was creative, I’ll give them that. Dhillon cited the Civil Rights Act of 1960, the National Voter Registration Act, and the Help America Vote Act — laws designed to protect voting rights — as justification for what amounted to the largest federal demand for personal voter data in American history.

The stated purpose? “Voter roll maintenance enforcement and compliance.” Translation: we want to check if your voter lists are clean.

The unstated purpose? That became clear soon enough

An example of a Department of Justice letter demanding voter registration information from a state, similar to those sent in May 2025.

The Refusal

Most states said no.

The reasons were straightforward. State privacy laws in many jurisdictions explicitly prohibit sharing driver’s license numbers and Social Security information with outside parties — including the federal government. Wisconsin’s election commission pointed to a state law that “explicitly bans election officials from disclosing information like driver’s license numbers to most people who aren’t election officials.” They voted 5-1 against compliance. The lone dissenter, a Republican, warned a lawsuit was coming. He was right.

Massachusetts Secretary of State Bill Galvin didn’t mince words: “I have absolutely no intention of handing over the names, addresses, dates of birth, political party affiliation, phone numbers, driver’s license numbers, and social security numbers of our voters to an administration that has demonstrated a pattern of using citizens’ private information to go on outrageous fishing expeditions in an attempt to settle petty grievances.”

Here’s the thing about Galvin’s objection: it wasn’t partisan posturing. Republican secretaries of state were pushing back too, though many did so more quietly. Kentucky’s Republican Secretary of State Michael Adams pointed to the Constitution itself, quoting Reagan:

“The federal government did not create the states; the states created the federal government.”

As a Canadian watching this, I found that particularly delicious. The party of Reagan was now demanding exactly the kind of federal overreach Reagan built his career opposing.

Massachusetts Secretary of State Bill Galvin, who strongly objected to the DOJ’s demand, citing privacy concerns and refusing to hand over sensitive voter data.

The Lawsuits

When states refused, the DOJ sued. And sued. And sued again.

The litigation came in waves. A first round in September 2025. Then six more states in December. Then four more, plus the District of Columbia. By early 2026, the DOJ had filed federal lawsuits against 24 states and DC for refusing to comply. The Brennan Center for Justice has been tracking every letter, every response, every lawsuit — and the map they’ve built tells the story at a glance.

Nearly every state that’s been sued is one that Trump lost in 2020. That’s not a coincidence. It’s a tell.

Meanwhile, the states that complied voluntarily — Arkansas, Indiana, Kansas, Wyoming — are all Republican-led. Another 11 states, all with Republican leadership, signaled willingness to cooperate through a confidential memorandum of understanding the DOJ was circulating. Alabama, Mississippi, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, South Carolina, South Dakota, Texas, Tennessee, Utah, and Virginia.

The math is simple: red states hand over their voters’ data. Blue states get sued. And the DOJ builds a national voter database one way or another.

Brennan Center map showing DOJ demands and lawsuits over voter data. Key: Dark shading indicates states sued for non-compliance; lighter categories show partial responses or full cooperation.

The Memorandum

That confidential memorandum of understanding deserves its own section, because it reveals what the DOJ actually plans to do with all this data.

At a December 4, 2025 hearing in the California lawsuit, Eric Neff — the acting chief of the DOJ’s Voting Section — made a courtroom disclosure that Stateline was the first to report. Neff identified the 11 states willing to comply and, more importantly, outlined the DOJ’s plan: run the voter data through federal databases — including a revamped system at the Department of Homeland Security — to flag voters the feds deem ineligible, then push states to remove those voters from the rolls.

Read that again. The DOJ’s plan isn’t to help states maintain their own rolls. It’s to build a parallel system that overrides them.

Eileen O’Connor, a former DOJ trial attorney who served under Obama, Trump’s first term, and Biden, called it what it is: “I think with each passing lawsuit, they are clearly trying to create a national database of every voter in the country.”

This isn’t how voter roll maintenance works. States already have the expertise and tools to keep their lists accurate. They cross-reference death records, change-of-address data, and information shared through interstate compacts. The federal government has neither the infrastructure nor the legal authority to do this work — and the track record of federal “matching” programs is notoriously error-prone. Anyone who’s ever been flagged for a “name match” at an airport knows how reliable these systems are.

But accuracy isn’t the point. Control is.

The Judge Who Saw Through It

On January 15, 2026, a federal judge in California dismissed the DOJ’s lawsuit seeking the state’s voter data. The ruling was devastating — not because of the legal technicalities, but because of what the judge said about the DOJ’s credibility.

“While the DOJ has told this Court that its purpose for demanding the sensitive voter information of Californians is ‘voter roll maintenance enforcement and compliance,’” the judge wrote, “representations made by the DOJ elsewhere paint a starkly different picture that this Court cannot ignore.”

The judge went further, describing “an alarming picture regarding the centralization of Americans’ information within the Executive Branch — without approval from Congress or Americans themselves.”

An Oregon court reached the same conclusion and dismissed the DOJ’s case there too.

Two federal judges, looking at the same evidence, reached the same verdict: the DOJ’s stated purpose didn’t match its actual behavior. When your own judges — operating under rules your party spent decades shaping — tell you your case doesn’t hold up, that’s not a legal setback. That’s a credibility collapse.

But here’s what those rulings didn’t do: they didn’t stop the campaign. The DOJ kept filing new lawsuits. As of this writing, 24 states plus DC remain under legal siege, and the DOJ has made clear it intends to keep going.

The Minnesota Connection

U.S. Attorney General Pam Bondi, whose January 2026 letter to Minnesota Gov. Tim Walz demanded voter rolls alongside an end to sanctuary policies—described by the state’s Secretary of State as a ‘ransom note’.

Which brings us back to Minnesota and Pam Bondi’s sledgehammer.

The DOJ had already sued Minnesota for its voter rolls in 2025. The state had already refused. The legal process was grinding along. Then, in late January 2026, two American citizens were killed by federal immigration agents in Minneapolis — Renee Good, a 37-year-old mother of three, and Alex Pretti, a 37-year-old ICU nurse. Neither had any connection to the immigration enforcement operation that took their lives.

What did the Attorney General of the United States do in response?

She wrote Governor Tim Walz a letter. Not a letter of condolence. Not an offer to investigate. A letter demanding three things: end sanctuary policies, hand over welfare records, and — oh yes — turn over the voter rolls the DOJ had already been suing to obtain.

“I am confident that these simple steps will help bring back law and order to Minnesota,” Bondi wrote.

Senator Chris Murphy of Connecticut decoded it immediately: “This has never been about safety or immigration. It’s a pretext for Trump to take over elections in swing states.”

Election law expert Rick Hasen of UCLA was blunter: “This is outrageous and reprehensible. It shows ICE is not in Minneapolis for law enforcement or immigration purposes. They are there to stir up s*** in blue states and try to extort them.”

Minnesota’s Secretary of State Steve Simon delivered the definitive response: “The law does not give the federal government the authority to obtain this private data. It is deeply disturbing that the U.S. Attorney General would make this unlawful request a part of an apparent ransom to pay for our state’s peace and security.”

A ransom note. That’s what the chief election official of an American state called a letter from the Attorney General of the United States.

And he wasn’t wrong.

What the Database Is For

Let me lay this out plainly, because the implications deserve to be stated without euphemism.

The Trump administration is building a national voter database containing the personal information of every registered voter in America. It is doing this without congressional authorization, over the objections of the majority of states, in defiance of at least two federal court rulings, and using immigration enforcement as leverage when the legal route fails.

This database will be used to flag voters the federal government considers ineligible — based on matching algorithms run through DHS systems originally designed for immigration enforcement. States will be pressured to purge those voters from their rolls. Given the well-documented inaccuracy of federal matching programs, the inevitable result will be that legitimate American citizens lose their right to vote.

All of this is happening eight months before midterm elections in which Republicans face the prospect of losing the House, and potentially the Senate.

David Becker, executive director of the Center for Election Innovation & Research, put it this way: “What the DOJ is trying to do is something that should frighten everybody across the political spectrum.”

He’s right. And the database is just the foundation. What got built on top of it — in Fulton County, in Puerto Rico, and in the Oval Office — is the subject of Part 10.
