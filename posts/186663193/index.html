Well it’s Groundhog Day….again

Same old... same old... How Trump turned every day into an endless loop

February 2, 2026

By Stephen Gordon aka CanadianSnowmanSays

Phil Connors had it easy. At least his loop eventually ended.

On February 2nd, 2026, Punxsutawney Phil’s handlers proclaimed: “There is a shadow here on my ground. Six more weeks of winter abound.” Up in Wiarton, Ontario, Phil’s cousin Willie made his own prediction—six more weeks of winter for Canada too. At least groundhogs are reliable. They see their shadow, they predict winter, winter happens, and everyone moves on with their lives.

But America doesn’t need a groundhog to predict repetitive cycles anymore. They have Donald Trump, and his shadow predicts something far more exhausting than winter—six more weeks of invented crises, real chaos, chickening out, and declared victories. Except instead of six weeks, it’s been years. And instead of winter ending, the loop just... keeps... going.

Phil Connors had it easy. Bill Murray’s weatherman in Groundhog Day only had to relive the same day until he learned to be a better person. America? You are stuck in your own infinite loop, except your protagonist isn’t learning anything, and the day keeps getting worse.

Donald Trump has turned the presidency into Groundhog Day, except instead of Sonny and Cher's "I Got You Babe" waking us up each morning, it's a Truth Social notification about whatever imaginary crisis he's invented today. At least Bill Murray got to learn French poetry and ice sculpting. Americans just get tariff threats and constitutional crises. The issue changes, but the pattern remains maddeningly, exhaustingly, predictably the same.

There is a shadow here on my ground. Six more weeks of winter abound." Phil predicted winter. Willie predicted winter. But the shadow at 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue? Six more weeks of the same damn loop.

Let me walk you through the four-step cycle that’s become as reliable as sunrise—and just as inevitable.

Step 1: Invent an Imaginary Crisis

Trump’s special talent isn’t dealmaking. It’s crisis manufacturing. After decades, even centuries, of America functioning perfectly well without his intervention, suddenly you’re on the brink of catastrophe.

Trade policies that worked for generations? “We just won’t have a country very long,” he warns ominously.

Immigrants who’ve been contributing to their communities for decades, paying taxes, raising families, building businesses? Suddenly they’re an existential threat. “We won’t have a country,” he declares.

The Greenland situation really captures the absurdity. After 250 years of managing just fine without owning Greenland—through two world wars, the Cold War, and the entire invention of the internet—Trump discovers that suddenly, inexplicably, urgently, he can't survive another day without annexing Danish territory. It's like waking up one morning and deciding you absolutely must own your neighbor's driveway or civilization will collapse. Do you feel it? That creeping sense of déjà vu?

As Phil Connors said “Do you ever have déjà vu, Mrs. Lancaster?”

“I don’t think so, but I could check with the kitchen.”

Yeah, you should all be checking with the kitchen at this point.

Step 2: Create a Real Crisis to Solve the Imaginary One

Here’s where Trump’s pattern gets dangerous. To address problems that don’t exist, he creates actual chaos that definitely does.

Worried about trade? Threaten tariffs of "200 or 300 or 500 percent" that would genuinely wreck the global economy. Because nothing says "art of the deal" like threatening to blow up the entire store because you don't like the prices. That'll show 'em.

Concerned about urban crime? Send federal forces into Los Angeles. “If we didn’t send them into Los Angeles, the rest of Los Angeles would have been burned down to the grid.” Spoiler alert: Los Angeles was not, in fact, on the verge of complete incineration. But federal troops in American cities sure created real problems.

Portland? “Portland is burn.” (Grammar his, not mine.)

Minneapolis? “You wouldn’t have a Minneapolis. It wouldn’t be—I don’t think it would be existed.”

Let that sink in. The city wouldn’t “be existed.” That’s presidential-level English right there.

And Greenland? "We need Greenland from the standpoint of national security," he announced, as Denmark frantically checked their NATO membership card and wondered if the mutual defense pact worked both ways, or if there was fine print about "not including threats from your own allies who've decided they really, really want your frozen real estate."

The results are predictable and harmful: lasting economic damage from tariff uncertainty, mayhem in American streets (that’s claimed actual American lives), and NATO allies literally preparing military defense scenarios against the United States. Not Russia. Not China. Us as in US.

Step 3: TACO (Trump Always Chickens Out)

This is where the pattern becomes most predictable. After creating genuine chaos, Trump blinks.

Every. Single. Time.

“We have yet another 90-day extension in the trade war truce between the US and China.”

“He is now reversing his stance, holding off taxing imports from Mexico and Canada.”

“President Trump says he is pulling National Guard troops from Chicago, Los Angeles, and Portland.”

“The White House shifting strategy in Minnesota.”

“Donald Trump saying that the United States would not use military force now to acquire Greenland.”

See the pattern? Threaten, chaos, retreat. Threaten, chaos, retreat. It's like watching someone repeatedly touch a hot stove and act surprised each time it burns—except this person keeps inviting everyone else to touch it too, then declaring victory when everyone's hands are bandaged.

Step 4: Declare Victory

Here’s the genius move, the pièce de résistance of the Groundhog Day loop: after backing down from every confrontation, Trump declares total victory for solving the crisis he created—even though Americans are objectively worse off.

Trade victories? American farmers are staring at $44 billion in losses. Canada and other major trading partners are actively making deals to rely more on China than the United States. America isn't winning the trade war; Trump is driving allies into America's adversary's arms and somehow calling it strategy. It's like pushing your friends toward your rival at a party, then high-fiving yourself for "making connections happen."

The Greenland “win”? Trump proudly announced: “And have all military access.”

Congratulations, Mr. President. You've won something the US already had since the 1951 Defense of Greenland Agreement under President Truman. It's like claiming victory for discovering that water is wet, or taking credit for the sun rising this morning. "Folks, I negotiated with the sun. Tremendous deal. It's going to rise every single day now. You're welcome."

And those federal forces in American cities? “Every city that we’ve gone to has been like liberal—crime has gone down 50, 60, 78 percent.”

Except crime was already going down in each city before his forces arrived. And in Minneapolis, two out of three homicides in 2026 were committed by federal agents. They didn’t make the cities safer; they made them deadlier.

These aren’t victories. They’re self-inflicted wounds that Trump has somehow convinced his supporters to call winning.

Punxsutawney Phil: “I have seen my shadow. Six more weeks of winter!” Trump’s America: “I have seen this pattern. Six more weeks of the same damn loop.”

Step 5: Repeat with a New Issue

And here’s where it gets truly maddening. Just like Happy Death Day, Palm Springs, Russian Doll, and other films that put their own spin on the infinite time loop concept, Trump keeps finding new crises to run through the exact same pattern.

The issue changes. The chaos is fresh. But the loop remains identical.

Invent crisis. Create real chaos. Chicken out. Declare victory. Repeat.

America is living in Groundhog Day, except Phil Connors eventually learned to play piano, ice sculpt, and become a better person. Trump just keeps touching the same hot stove, burning himself and everyone around him, and calling it warmth. At least Phil was funny. This is just exhausting.

The difference between Groundhog Day the movie and Trump’s America? Bill Murray’s character eventually broke the loop by growing as a person. Trump breaks nothing except wind. He just keeps spinning the wheel, and Americans keep waking up to the same nightmare with a different name.

At least in the movie, Phil Connors was the only one who remembered the loop. In Trump’s America, you all remember. You’re all trapped in it together. And unlike Phil, who had to become selfless to escape, Trump’s loop feeds on selfishness, sustained by people who somehow see the pattern and call it leadership.

So here we are again. Another day, another crisis, another loop.

I don’t know about you, but I’m ready for February 3rd.

From my vantage point north of the 49th parallel, I'm ready for February 3rd. The question is: is America?
