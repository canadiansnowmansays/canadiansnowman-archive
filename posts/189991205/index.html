The Contribution Index

Brazil

Part IV: The Burning Guardian

March 20, 2026

NASA satellite view showing thick smoke from fires raging along Brazil’s deforestation frontier in the Amazon — highlighting the “burning guardian” crisis and global climate implications. Source: NASA Earth Observatory

Part IV — Underperformer #9

Brazil is the custodian of the Amazon — the largest tropical rainforest on Earth, home to roughly 10 percent of all species, and a carbon sink so vast that its health directly affects the global climate. This is not a metaphor or a rhetorical flourish. The Amazon’s survival is, in measurable scientific terms, one of the most important environmental questions facing humanity.

Brazil did not choose this responsibility. Geography assigned it. But the choices Brazil makes about the Amazon affect every human being on the planet, and those choices place it squarely on the underperformer list.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 28 out of 50

Brazil's Sistema Único de Saúde — the SUS — provides universal healthcare in theory, and the theory is a genuine achievement. For a nation of 215 million people spread across a continent-sized territory, building a publicly funded health system that reaches the Amazon basin and the favelas of São Paulo and the sertão of the northeast is not a trivial accomplishment. In practice, the system is chronically underfunded, operating at roughly 60 percent of the per capita spending of comparable middle-income nations. Life expectancy sits at approximately 76 years — the gap between its wealthy and its poor populations is one of the widest in the hemisphere, with violence, inequality, and uneven healthcare delivery all contributing. The score of 5 reflects a genuine structural commitment that has never received the resources it would need to deliver on its promise.

A Brazilian family holding their Bolsa Família card — the program lifted millions out of poverty (a 6 in human development), though progress has stalled amid broader challenges. Source: Brazil Reports

Education earns a 5. Brazil’s PISA scores rank it in the bottom third of participating nations — an underperformance significant enough that it cannot be explained by resource constraints alone. The structural problem is well documented: Brazilian schools are chronically under-resourced, teacher training is inadequate, and the dropout rate at secondary level — particularly among boys from low-income households — is severe. Brazil also maintains one of the world’s most significant private university sectors, producing engineers and lawyers and economists at global competitive levels. The two systems coexist: a private education track that functions and a public education track that does not, separated by economic class, and the divide between them reproduces inequality across generations.

Democratic governance earns a 6. Brazil’s democracy is real — it has peaceful transfers of power, genuine elections, a free press, and an independent judiciary that has demonstrated, repeatedly, a willingness to hold power to account. The Lula prosecutions, the Bolsonaro accountability proceedings, and the conviction of those responsible for the January 8, 2023 insurrection attempt — in which supporters of the defeated Bolsonaro stormed the Planalto, the Supreme Court, and the National Congress — all reflect a democratic system capable of self-correction. The institutions held. The 6 rather than a 7 reflects how close they came to not holding, and what the durability of those institutions looks like when they are tested by a president who spent four years delegitimising them.

Poverty reduction earns a 6, reflecting one of the most remarkable stories in development economics. The Bolsa Família programme, introduced under Lula’s first administration and expanded significantly under subsequent governments, conditioned cash transfers on school attendance and healthcare visits — lifting an estimated 20 to 30 million Brazilians from extreme poverty between 2003 and 2014. It became a global model for conditional cash transfer programmes. The economic crisis of 2014 to 2016, the pandemic, and the deliberate dismantling of social programmes under Bolsonaro reversed significant gains. Lula’s return to power in 2023 restored and expanded the programme under the name Bolsa Família Renovado. The direction of travel is again positive but fragile.

Gender equality and minority rights earn a 6. Brazil is 56 percent Afro-Brazilian by self-identification — the largest African-descended population outside Africa. It is also one of the most unequal societies on Earth along racial lines: Afro-Brazilian Brazilians earn roughly 40 percent less than white Brazilians, are vastly overrepresented in prison populations, and face police violence at rates that amount to structural crisis. Brazil’s femicide rate is among the highest in the world. LGBTQ+ rights are formally protected by the Supreme Court, which has been more progressive on these questions than the elected legislature — a judicial activism that has drawn intense hostility from evangelical political blocs that now hold significant power in the National Congress. The score reflects a country whose formal legal commitments to equality exceed what its institutions actually deliver.

Environmental Stewardship: 25 out of 50

Herds of cattle on recently cleared rainforest land in Brazil — cattle ranching remains the primary driver of deforestation, contributing to the essay’s critique of environmental stewardship failures. Source: The Washington Post

Deforestation earns a 3 — one of the lowest environmental scores in this series, and the number that anchors Brazil on the underperformer list regardless of everything else.

An area roughly equivalent to the size of France has been cleared from the Amazon since 1970. The pace has varied — it slowed dramatically under Lula’s first government as enforcement was tightened and satellite monitoring improved, and it accelerated dramatically under Bolsonaro as environmental agencies were defunded, fines were cancelled, and the implicit signal sent to the agricultural frontier was that the rules no longer applied. The Lula restoration has again turned the curve — deforestation in the Amazon fell 50 percent in 2023 compared to the peak Bolsonaro years. The long-term trend, however, is what the scorecard measures. And the long-term trend is a forest that is roughly 20 percent gone, with scientists warning that 25 to 40 percent deforestation will trigger a dieback feedback loop that converts large portions of the remaining forest to savanna regardless of what Brazil then decides to do. The Amazon approaching its own tipping point is the environmental emergency of our era.

The drivers of deforestation are not mysterious: cattle ranching is responsible for roughly 80 percent of Amazon clearing, and Brazilian beef and soy are globally traded commodities whose markets are in Europe, China, and North America. The bancada ruralista — the agricultural lobby in the Brazilian Congress — is one of the most powerful political forces in the country, consistently blocking environmental enforcement and weakening protected area designations. The forest is being cleared because there is money in clearing it, and the political system has not yet generated enough countervailing force to stop the clearing at a meaningful scale.

Renewable energy earns a 7, reflecting Brazil’s extraordinary natural endowment. Brazil generates roughly 85 percent of its electricity from renewable sources — primarily hydroelectric, with a rapidly growing wind and solar sector that has attracted substantial private investment. This is a genuine achievement, and it means Brazil’s electricity grid is one of the cleanest in the world. The caveat is the Amazon: Brazil’s hydroelectric dams have displaced Indigenous communities, flooded critical ecosystems, and — because decomposing organic matter beneath reservoir surfaces releases methane — are not as carbon-neutral as their classification suggests. The Belo Monte dam, the world’s fourth-largest, displaced tens of thousands of people and dramatically altered the Xingu River ecosystem. Clean electricity, complicated cost.

Biodiversity protection earns a 4. Brazil contains more documented species than any nation on Earth — approximately 10 percent of all life on the planet by some estimates. The legal framework for protection is extensive: the Atlantic Forest, the Cerrado savanna, and the Amazon all have protected status provisions. The enforcement is intermittent, the penalties historically weak, and the political will to prioritise biodiversity over agricultural expansion has been inconsistent. The biodiversity is extraordinary. The protection of it is inadequate.

Systemic Behaviour: 25 out of 50

Tax haven and corruption facilitation earns a 3 — the number that reflects Brazil’s defining systemic failure. The Lava Jato investigation, which began in 2014 as a money laundering probe in the city of Curitiba, expanded to reveal a corruption ecosystem so pervasive that it reached the presidency, virtually every major political party, the national oil company Petrobras, and the largest construction firms in Latin America. Billions of dollars in public procurement contracts were systematically looted through kickback schemes. The investigation prosecuted and imprisoned a former president, put the current president on trial, and implicated hundreds of politicians across the spectrum. That Brazil’s institutions were strong enough to conduct this investigation is a genuine credit to the rule of law. That the corruption it revealed had operated for decades without interruption is the structural failure.

Arms exports earn a 7. Brazil is not a significant arms exporter by global standards, and it has generally avoided the kind of arms transfer relationships to conflict zones that drag other nations’ scores down. The Embraer military aircraft division exports to several countries, but Brazil’s arms posture is defensive and domestically oriented rather than globally projective.

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earns a 5. Brazil has accepted significant numbers of Venezuelan refugees — over 500,000 are registered, making it one of the largest Venezuelan refugee-receiving nations in South America. The Operação Acolhida programme, which organises reception, documentation, and resettlement, has been praised by the UNHCR as a model for regional response. The 5 reflects genuine contribution at regional scale offset by the capacity constraints of a nation dealing with its own significant poverty.

Adherence to international law earns a 5. Brazil participates in international institutions and has generally respected multilateral legal frameworks. The Bolsonaro years damaged Brazil’s international legal standing — withdrawal from agreements, delegitimisation of international human rights bodies, the president’s publicly stated contempt for the Inter-American Court of Human Rights — and the Lula administration’s return to international engagement has been genuine but is still rebuilding credibility lost during that period.

Exploitation versus investment in developing nations earns a 5. Brazil’s economic relationships with its South American neighbours are a mix of legitimate commerce, infrastructure investment through development banks, and the occasional assertion of continental influence that smaller neighbours find uncomfortable. Brazil is not an extractive power in the way that some larger economies operate in the developing world. It is a regional giant that has been inconsistent in its regional stewardship.

Individual Brazilians have tried to compensate for what their government has not delivered. Jorge Paulo Lemann’s foundation has invested hundreds of millions in education reform, backing teacher training programmes and curriculum development at a scale the federal government has not matched. The Instituto Ayrton Senna, founded in memory of the three-time world champion, has reached tens of millions of students through partnerships with state education systems. The Lemann Foundation’s Todos Pela Educação coalition has produced policy research that has shaped Brazilian education reform debates across administrations. But philanthropic giving in Brazil remains far below its potential relative to national wealth, and the structural corruption that pervades public institutions complicates accountability at every level.

The Amazon does not need a foundation. It needs a government willing to protect it.

The Neighbour Test: 19 out of 50

Brazil sees itself as a regional power and a global voice of the developing world, and acts accordingly — but its regionalism is more often about status than service.

Multilateral orientation earns a 4. Brazil participates in BRICS, Mercosur, UNASUR, and the UN, and Lula has positioned himself as a broker between the Global South and Western institutions. The positioning is genuine in its aspiration but inconsistent in its execution — Brazil’s multilateral engagement tends to follow its economic interests and its leadership ambitions, and when those diverge from collective benefit, it is usually the collective benefit that yields.

Response to global crises earns a 4. Brazil’s response to the Venezuelan refugee crisis — structured, humane, and at significant scale — is the strongest entry on this card. Beyond that, Brazil’s capacity to project crisis response is limited by its domestic priorities and its own economic instability.

Treatment of allies earns a 4. Brazil’s relationships with its South American neighbours are shaped by a combination of benign neglect, the gravitational pull of its economic weight, and periodic assertions of continental leadership that smaller states find more imposing than collegial. It does not destabilise its neighbours. It does not invest in them at a level commensurate with its capacity.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 4. The gap between what Brazil says at COP summits and what Brazil does in the Amazon is one of the widest consistency gaps in this series. Brazil has produced some of the world’s most eloquent environmental diplomacy. Its foreign ministers have spoken movingly about the Amazon at international forums. The same governments have presided over the legal frameworks that allow it to burn.

Long-term stewardship earns a 3 — because a nation that holds the lungs of the planet in its hands and allows them to be burned for cattle ranches is making the most consequential long-term stewardship failure on Earth. Brazil’s Amazon policy is not merely a domestic environmental issue. It is a global stewardship catastrophe — one that affects atmospheric carbon levels, rainfall patterns, global temperature, and the survival of tens of thousands of species that exist nowhere else. Future generations of every nation on Earth will live with the consequences of what Brazil decides to do with the Amazon in the next decade. The 3 reflects that weight.

Brazilian activists and Indigenous groups rally against anti-environment policies and Amazon destruction — civil society efforts highlight the gap between rhetoric and domestic actions. Source: The Guardian

The Editorial Conclusion

Brazil scores 97 out of 200 — the ninth-ranked underperformer.

The Amazon makes Brazil’s underperformance a global emergency rather than merely a national failing. Every nation on this list answers primarily for what it does within its own borders. Brazil answers for something larger: the custodianship of a planetary system. The burning guardian. The nation that holds the lungs of the world in its hands — and cannot stop setting them on fire.

The tragedy is that Brazil knows this. Its scientists understand the tipping points. Its diplomats articulate the stakes with eloquence. Its civil society — the Indigenous leaders, the rubber tappers, the environmental prosecutors, the activists who have died protecting the forest — has fought with extraordinary courage against the agricultural frontier that advances without them. The knowledge is there. The courage is there. What is missing is the sustained political will to match the responsibility that geography assigned.

The countdown continues. It gets harder from here.
