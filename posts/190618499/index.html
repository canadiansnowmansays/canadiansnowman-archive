Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 10: Abandonment: The Aid Cuts That Kill (2021-2026)

April 22, 2026

Opening: Back to Where We Started

An elderly woman sits in a blue plastic chair in the village of Badgor, in Afghanistan’s Kunar Province. In her arms, wrapped in a vibrant blanket, is a tiny child. Around them, other children hover. They’re waiting to be seen by a doctor - one of the last mobile medical teams still operating after the Trump administration slashed U.S. aid.

This is the photograph from the New York Times article that started this entire series.

We’ve now traced 2,500 years of Afghan history to understand how we arrived at this moment. From the Persian Empire to Alexander the Great, from the Mongol apocalypse to British disasters, from the Soviet catastrophe to American occupation - 2,500 years of invasion, resistance, and suffering.

And now, in 2026, Afghan children are dying not from bombs or bullets, but from preventable malnutrition and lack of basic medical care.

Because America - after twenty years of occupation, after creating economic dependency, after promising to support Afghan women and children - cut the aid that millions depended on to survive.

This is the story of the unique cruelty of abandonment, and how the 2,500-year pattern continues in a new form.

The Dependency Creation (2001-2021)

During twenty years of occupation, the U.S. and international community didn’t just fight the Taliban. They built an entire economic and social structure dependent on foreign support.

The Health System

By 2020, Afghanistan’s health infrastructure was almost entirely aid-dependent:

  • Clinics in remote areas like Nalej - funded by international NGOs

  • Mobile medical teams reaching isolated villages - funded by USAID

  • Nutrition programs preventing child starvation - funded by World Food Program

  • Training for doctors and nurses - supported by foreign aid

  • Medical supplies - imported with foreign funding

The Economic Structure

By 2021, foreign aid represented approximately 40% of Afghanistan’s GDP.

This meant:

  • Government salaries paid by foreign money

  • Infrastructure maintained by foreign funding

  • Schools operated with international support

  • Agriculture dependent on foreign assistance programs

The Social Programs

During occupation, programs supported:

  • Girls’ education

  • Women’s rights organizations

  • Civil society groups

  • Orphanages

  • Refugee assistance

All dependent on foreign funding.

The Trap

Afghanistan had become entirely dependent on a level of aid it couldn’t sustain internally. The economy couldn’t function without it. Millions would die without it.

And everyone knew it.

The Taliban Return (August 2021)

When the Taliban swept back into power in August 2021, international aid stopped almost immediately.

The Immediate Crisis

  • Banks froze (international banking cut off)

  • Government employees unpaid

  • Health workers fled or stopped working

  • Supply chains collapsed

  • Food prices skyrocketed

  • Currency collapsed

The humanitarian crisis began immediately, even before aid cuts.

The Dilemma

International donors faced a choice:

  • Continue aid and risk supporting Taliban regime

  • Cut aid and cause humanitarian catastrophe

Most chose to dramatically reduce aid while maintaining some humanitarian assistance.

The U.S., under Biden, continued some humanitarian support - about $1 billion annually through 2023. Not enough to sustain the economy, but enough to prevent mass starvation.

It kept millions alive.

The Trump Administration Cuts (2024)

Community health council members sit in front of the clinic of Larga, in Muqur district of Ghazni province, Afghanistan, which closed due to US funding cuts at the end of January 2025. Elise Blanchard/Getty Images

In 2024, the Trump administration made a different choice.

The USAID Dismantling

The administration dismantled USAID (U.S. Agency for International Development) and slashed humanitarian aid across the board, including to Afghanistan.

U.S. humanitarian assistance to Afghanistan dropped from approximately $1 billion annually to nearly zero.

The Stated Rationale

The administration’s position: No American taxpayer money should support a Taliban regime, even indirectly.

The Actual Effect

The aid didn’t go to the Taliban government. It went to:

  • NGOs running health clinics

  • World Food Program preventing child starvation

  • International organizations providing medical care

  • Programs keeping children alive

When the funding stopped, the programs stopped.

And children began dying.

The clinic of Larga, Afghanistan, sits empty in August 2025 since its closure due to US funding cuts, which has left locals in the remote village cut off from health services Elise Blanchard/Getty Images

The New York Times Investigation (February 2026)

The February 4, 2026, New York Times article documented the catastrophe:

450 Health Clinics Closed

Including the clinic in Nalej where:

  • Malika Ghullami had safely given birth to two children in past years

  • Midwives and nutritionists had served 850 families

  • Women could receive prenatal care

When funding stopped, staff left. The clinic closed. Women now had to travel over an hour on “spine-jarring dirt tracks” to reach the nearest functioning clinic.

Malika Ghullami’s Twins

Pregnant with twins, Malika went into labor one winter morning. Without the nearby clinic:

  • She had to be driven over an hour to another facility

  • One twin was stillborn

  • The other survived only a few hours

She blamed her inability to get regular checkups at the now-closed clinic. Other mothers in the area recounted similar losses.

Three-Month-Old Zakia

Sharifa Khawari’s baby, Zakia, has been vomiting since birth. For weeks, Sharifa hoped her husband would earn enough at the coal mine to afford a taxi to the nearest clinic.

His pay barely covers food. Zakia continues to suffer without medical care.

The Statistics

  • 4 million children at risk of dying from malnutrition

  • The most in 25 years

  • 17 million Afghans (40% of population) face acute hunger

  • 7 provinces face critical food insecurity (final stage before famine)

  • 2.8 million refugees expelled from Iran and Pakistan, returned to a country struggling to absorb them

Nine-Month-Old Muhammad Ali

In a Kabul nutrition ward, Muhammad Ali is too weak to ingest milk. His mother, Karima Malikzada, says her husband’s meager income as a housekeeper means they often eat only once a day.

These aren’t war casualties. These are children dying from preventable conditions because programs that kept them alive no longer exist.

Malnourished children in Kabul

The Unique Cruelty

Every previous empire that invaded Afghanistan left destruction:

  • The Mongols massacred and burned

  • The British executed and razed villages

  • The Soviets bombed and mined

But America’s abandonment is uniquely cruel in a different way.

Phase 1: The Invasion (2001) Arguably justified - al-Qaeda attacked from Afghan sanctuary

Phase 2: The Occupation (2002-2021) A choice - America decided to try nation-building despite 2,500 years of evidence it was impossible

Phase 3: The Withdrawal (2021) Inevitable - the occupation was never sustainable

Phase 4: The Aid Cuts (2024-2026) Purely cruel - America created dependency, then withdrew support

The Difference

Previous empires destroyed through violence during occupation.

America is destroying through abandonment after occupation.

The children dying today are dying from policy choices, not military operations.

The Moral Reckoning

Let’s be clear about responsibility:

The mothers losing babies - they’re not responsible. They didn’t invite invasion. They didn’t create dependency. They didn’t choose this.

The children dying - they’re not responsible. They were born into a system created by foreign powers. They have no agency. They’re simply casualties.

The Taliban - they share responsibility. Their extreme interpretation of Islam, their harboring of terrorists, their oppression of women - these created conditions for crisis.

But America bears responsibility too:

  1. Invaded Afghanistan

  2. Stayed for 20 years

  3. Created economic structures dependent on American funding

  4. Promised to support Afghan women and children

  5. Withdrew militarily (inevitable)

  6. Cut humanitarian aid (choice)

Those dying children are dying from American policy.

Not from Taliban policy alone. From the combination of American abandonment and Taliban oppression.

The Preventable Tragedy

Here’s what makes this particularly galling:

$1 billion annually to prevent mass child starvation is:

  • A rounding error in the U.S. federal budget

  • Less than the Pentagon spends on a single weapon system

  • Less than one month of the Afghan war cost

  • 0.017% of the U.S. federal budget

America could afford to continue humanitarian aid without supporting the Taliban government.

NGOs, the World Food Program, and international organizations could deliver aid without going through Taliban structures.

It’s not a question of capability. It’s a question of will.

And the Trump administration chose to cut it.

The Historical Pattern Continues

For 2,500 years, the pattern has been:

  1. Empire invades with confidence

  2. Empire achieves initial military success

  3. Empire discovers occupation is unsustainable

  4. Empire eventually withdraws

  5. Empire leaves Afghanistan worse than they found it

America has completed steps 1-5.

But America added a new step: 6. Empire cuts the aid that people were dependent on 7. Children die from preventable causes

This is the first time in 2,500 years that an empire has abandoned Afghanistan through aid cuts rather than just military withdrawal.

Previous empires left destruction behind. America left dependency behind, then cut the support.

The Three Generations

Let’s think about what this means for Afghan families:

Grandmother’s generation (born ~1945):

  • Grew up during the golden age

  • Might have attended school, maybe even university

  • Saw Afghanistan modernize and develop

  • Experienced hope

Mother’s generation (born ~1975):

  • Born during the golden age’s end

  • Grew up during Soviet invasion and civil war

  • Experienced Taliban rule

  • Lived through American occupation

  • Saw brief return of hope, then its destruction

Child’s generation (born ~2020):

  • Born into American withdrawal and Taliban return

  • Has known nothing but crisis

  • Now faces starvation because aid was cut

  • May not survive to have children of their own

Three generations. Each experiencing a completely different Afghanistan. Each suffering from decisions made by distant empires.

The Count: 2,500 Years Complete

Let’s count the complete timeline we’ve traced:

Total time period: ~2,575 years (550 BCE to 2026 CE)

Periods of genuine peace and self-determination:

  • Parts of Persian period: ~50 years

  • Parts of stable dynasty periods: ~100 years

  • The golden age: 59 years (1919-1978)

  • Total: ~200-250 years maximum

That’s less than 10% of recorded history.

For 90%+ of its recorded history, Afghanistan has been:

  • Invaded

  • Occupied

  • Fought over

  • Recovering from invasion

  • Experiencing civil war

  • Or now, abandoned to starvation

The Question: Will It Ever End?

At the start of this series, I asked: How long has Afghanistan been suffering?

The answer: 2,500 years.

The deeper question: Will it ever end?

The honest answer: Probably not.

The mighty Hindu Kush

Because Afghanistan’s curse is geographic and structural:

  • It will always sit at crossroads of civilizations

  • It will always have mountain valleys resisting central control

  • It will always have tribal structures prioritizing local over national loyalty

  • Empires will always be tempted to try controlling it

Unless...

Unless empires learn the lesson that 2,500 years has been teaching:

Leave Afghanistan alone.

Don’t invade it. Don’t occupy it. Don’t try to remake it. Don’t create dependency and then abandon it.

If you care about Afghans:

  • Provide humanitarian aid with no strings attached

  • Support Afghan-led development

  • Respect Afghan sovereignty

  • But don’t invade, don’t occupy, don’t impose

The Woman in Badgor: 2,500 Years of Endurance

Let me end where we began.

An elderly woman sits in a blue plastic chair, holding a small child. They’re waiting for medical care that might not come.

That woman has lived through:

  • Maybe the tail end of the golden age

  • Definitely the Soviet invasion

  • The civil war

  • Taliban rule (first time)

  • American occupation

  • Taliban return (second time)

  • And now, aid cuts

She’s seen peace and prosperity. She’s seen apocalyptic destruction. She’s seen hope and betrayal.

And now she sits, waiting, in a country that has been suffering for 2,500 years.

Her story is Afghanistan’s story.

Her endurance is Afghanistan’s endurance.

Her suffering is Afghanistan’s suffering.

And her question - unspoken but implicit in that photograph - is the question that 2,500 years has been asking:

When will the empires finally leave us in peace?

The Final Lesson

If there’s one lesson from 2,500 years of Afghan history, it’s this:

Afghans outlast empires.

The Persians are gone. Alexander’s empire fragmented. The Mongols left. The British withdrew. The Soviets collapsed. The Americans left.

But Afghans remain.

In their mountains. In their valleys. Enduring.

They survived Alexander. They survived the Mongols. They survived the British. They survived the Soviets. They survived the Americans.

And they’ll survive this too - if the world allows them to.

The question is: How many children have to die before empires learn to leave Afghanistan alone?

Postscript: What Can Be Done?

This series has been about history, not policy. But history suggests some clear lessons:

1. Restore Humanitarian Aid The U.S. and international community should restore funding for:

  • Health clinics in remote areas

  • Nutrition programs preventing child starvation

  • Mobile medical teams

  • Food assistance

This can be done through NGOs and international organizations without supporting the Taliban government.

2. Pressure the Taliban The Taliban must allow humanitarian access and stop restricting women from working in healthcare and education.

3. Support Afghan-Led Solutions Rather than imposing external models, support what Afghans themselves develop.

4. Learn from History Never again should any empire think it can invade, occupy, and remake Afghanistan. It cannot be done.

5. Accept Reality Afghanistan will remain Afghanistan - tribal, traditional, resistant to central control, fiercely independent.

Accept this. Work with it. Don’t try to change it through force.

Closing: The Photograph

That woman in Badgor. That child in her arms. That closed clinic in Nalej. That three-month-old vomiting with no care. That nine-month-old too weak to eat.

They are the latest casualties in a 2,500-year pattern.

The empires change. The weapons modernize. The justifications evolve.

But the pattern remains:

  • Empires invade

  • Afghans resist

  • Empires leave

  • Afghans suffer

2,500 years of this cycle.

Perhaps, finally, it’s time to break it.

Not by invading again. Not by occupying again. Not by trying to remake Afghanistan again.

But by simply leaving Afghanistan alone - and providing humanitarian support to help Afghans rebuild on their own terms.

Those children don’t deserve to die because empires never learn.

Those mothers don’t deserve to lose babies because of distant policy decisions.

That woman in Badgor deserves to be able to get medical care for her child.

After 2,500 years of suffering, Afghanistan deserves peace.

Real peace. Afghan peace. Peace that comes from empires finally learning to stay out.
