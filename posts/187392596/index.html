“Nobody Understands a Word”

Part 34: When Racism Becomes Presidential Policy (2025-2026)

April 2, 2026

Performer on stage during the Super Bowl LX halftime show (February 9, 2026), which prompted President Trump’s social media critique that “nobody understands a word this guy is saying” and called the performance an “affront to the Greatness of America.” Source: USA Today / Super Bowl 2026 halftime performance photograph.

Part 33 documented Trump’s 50-year pattern of racism from 1973 through 2024—from housing discrimination to the Central Park Five, from birtherism to “shithole countries,” from casino discrimination to falsely claiming Haitian immigrants ate pets.

The pattern was clear: racist behavior, denial when confronted, attacks on critics, refusal to apologize, zero accountability.

Some might have hoped that Trump’s election to a second term—coming after criminal convictions, civil fraud judgments, and comprehensive documentation of his lifetime of misconduct—might temper his behavior. Perhaps the weight of the presidency, the scrutiny of history, or simple human decency might cause him to change.

They were wrong.

During Trump’s second presidency, the racist pattern didn’t slow down. It accelerated. And in February 2026, during a single devastating week, Trump would demonstrate that 50 years of consequence-free racism had taught him he could say and do anything—depicting former presidents as apes, attacking Black cultural expression as un-American, and creating an America where millions of citizens wonder if they truly belong.

This is the story of how Trump’s racism became presidential policy.

Somalis as “Garbage” (2025)

Members of the Somali-American community in the Twin Cities — home to approximately 87,000 Somali immigrants and their descendants, the largest such population in the United States. In early 2025, President Trump referred to Somali immigrants as "garbage" during a cabinet meeting. Source: AP Photo

During a cabinet meeting in early 2025, President Trump described Somali immigrants as “garbage.” The comment was captured on video: “I don’t want them in our country. Their country is no good for a reason. Their country stinks. We’re going to go the wrong way if we keep taking in garbage into our country.”

The statement was not made in private. It was not taken out of context. It was not a casual remark that slipped out. Trump openly declared, in an official government setting, that people from Somalia—a nation of over 18 million people, many of whom have fled war and persecution—were “garbage” unfit to enter the United States.

Minnesota is home to the largest Somali-American population in the United States, with approximately 87,000 Somali immigrants and their descendants living primarily in the Twin Cities area. Somali immigrants have built businesses, raised families, sent their children to college, and contributed to their communities. Many came to the United States as refugees, escaping violence and civil war, seeking safety and opportunity.

Trump’s comment reduced them all to refuse. Not people fleeing war. Not refugees seeking safety. Not immigrants pursuing the American dream. Garbage.

The video circulated widely. Some expressed outrage. Somali-American leaders condemned the comments. News organizations reported the story. And then nothing happened. Trump did not apologize. He did not clarify. He did not walk back the comment. The administration did not discipline anyone. There were no consequences.

Sombreros on Democrats (October 2025)

In October 2025, during the government shutdown, Trump posted an AI-generated image of House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries, the first Black person to lead a party in the House of Representatives, with a superimposed sombrero and mustache. The image was accompanied by claims that Democratic leaders wanted to provide free healthcare to undocumented immigrants.

The racist nature of the image was obvious: a Black American leader depicted with stereotypically Mexican imagery to suggest he prioritized immigrants over Americans. The image played on multiple racist tropes simultaneously—questioning Jeffries’s loyalty to American citizens, suggesting Democrats were aligned with immigrants against “real” Americans, using crude racial caricature as political messaging.

Jeffries called the image “racist and bigoted.” Democratic leaders condemned it. And Vice President JD Vance said he thought it was “funny” and that the administration was “having a good time.”

The pattern was familiar by now: deploy a racist image, defend it as humor, ignore criticism, move on. The video was played on a loop on a video monitor inside the White House briefing room during the shutdown, a visual reminder that the president’s racist mockery of the Democratic leader was official administration messaging.

There were no consequences.

The Ape Video (February 6, 2026)

Late on the night of Thursday, February 5, Trump’s Truth Social account posted a 62-second video. The first portion promoted conspiracy theories about the 2020 election, featuring narration from Phil Waldron, a retired Army colonel who was subpoenaed by the January 6 committee for efforts to overturn the 2020 election. The video originally aired during a 2021 event hosted by Mike Lindell, the MyPillow CEO and prominent election conspiracy theorist.

Then, in the final seconds, spliced onto the end, was a clip showing Barack and Michelle Obama depicted as apes. Their faces were edited onto primate bodies as “The Lion Sleeps Tonight” played in the background.

The depiction of Black people as apes is not simply offensive. It is not just another insult. It is a racist trope with a specific and brutal history.

For centuries, comparing Black people to apes was used to justify slavery. The claim that Black people were less evolved, more primitive, closer to animals than to humans, was deployed to argue they were not fully human and therefore could be enslaved. After slavery ended, the same imagery was used to justify segregation, to rationalize lynching, to deny civil rights. When civil rights activists marched for equality, opponents depicted them as apes to suggest they were not deserving of full citizenship.

Depicting the first Black president and first lady as apes invokes that entire history. It is not merely insulting. It is dehumanizing. It places them outside the category of full humanity. It suggests they were never truly qualified to hold the positions they held because they are fundamentally other, fundamentally less than human.

The video remained on Trump’s account for twelve hours. It was posted late Thursday night and remained visible throughout Friday morning as millions of Americans began their day, checked social media, and saw the President of the United States sharing an image depicting the Obamas as apes.

During those twelve hours, the White House cycled through three different explanations.

Explanation 1: It’s Just a Meme

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt initially defended the video depicting the Obamas as apes, dismissing objections as "fake outrage." Hours later, the story changed: it was a staffer error. Then Trump said he made no mistake. Three explanations. No apology. Source: AP Photo / Getty Images

At first, White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt defended the post. She did not distance the president from it. She did not express regret. She attacked critics for objecting to it.

“This is from an internet meme video depicting President Trump as the King of the Jungle and Democrats as characters from the Lion King,” Leavitt said in a statement. “Please stop the fake outrage and report on something today that actually matters to the American people.”

The defense was that the racism didn’t matter. The broader video—which apparently depicted various Democrats as animals and Trump as a lion they all bowed to—was just a joke. The fact that it specifically depicted the Obamas as apes, invoking centuries of racist imagery, was irrelevant. Critics were manufacturing “fake outrage” over harmless internet humor.

The message was clear: the administration saw nothing wrong with the post and had no intention of removing it.

Explanation 2: A Staffer Did It

Around noon on Friday, the story changed. The video was suddenly deleted. And the White House offered a new explanation: an unnamed staffer had posted it “erroneously.”

A spokesperson said: “It was a meme that was posted by a staffer on the president’s true social account. It was from a Lion King video depicting, as you pointed out, different Democrats as different animals. I think Kamala Harris was depicted as a turtle in this video meme. The president did take it down. He spoke with lawmakers today out of respect for them, including Sen. Tim Scott. The post was removed.”

The shift was remarkable. Hours earlier, the post was defensible—just a meme, stop the fake outrage. Now it had been posted “erroneously” by a staffer and removed “out of respect” for lawmakers who objected.

But this explanation raised obvious questions. If a staffer posted it erroneously, why did the White House initially defend it? If Trump removed it out of respect for lawmakers, why didn’t he apologize? If the post was an error, who made the error and why weren’t they identified?

Explanation 3: I Didn’t See It, But I Didn’t Make a Mistake

Later Friday, Trump spoke to reporters on Air Force One while traveling to Palm Beach for the weekend. His explanation contradicted the White House’s previous statements and internal logic.

Trump said he had watched the beginning of the video, which was about voter fraud, and thought it was “really, really strong.” He said he passed it along to “people” to post to his account, but that he “didn’t see the whole thing,” including the portion depicting the Obamas as apes.

“I look at thousands of things,” Trump said. “And I looked at the beginning of it. It was fine... I gave it to the people, generally they’d look at the whole thing but I guess somebody didn’t.”

So: Trump saw part of the video, liked it, and gave it to unnamed people to post. Those people failed to review the entire video before posting it to his account. The racist content at the end was therefore not his fault.

But when reporters asked if he would apologize, Trump refused.

“No, I didn’t make a mistake,” Trump said.

This was incoherent. If he didn’t see the racist portion of the video, how could he know whether posting it was a mistake? If his staff posted content to his account without properly reviewing it, wasn’t that a mistake? If the content was offensive enough to delete after twelve hours, wasn’t posting it in the first place an error?

Trump’s position appeared to be: I didn’t see the racist part, my staff should have caught it but didn’t, we deleted it after public outcry, but I made no mistake and will not apologize.

When asked directly whether he condemned the racist portion of the video, Trump said, “Of course I do.”

But “of course I do” is not an apology. Condemning racism in the abstract while refusing to apologize for sharing racist content is not accountability. It is evasion.

The Republican Response

The response from Republicans was unusual, if brief.

Senator Tim Scott of South Carolina, the only Black Republican in the Senate and chair of the National Republican Senatorial Committee, wrote on X that he was “praying” the video was fake “because it’s the most racist thing I’ve seen out of this White House.” He called on Trump to remove it.

For Scott to publicly call something from the Trump White House “the most racist thing” he’d seen was extraordinary. Scott had remained silent during years of Trump’s racist rhetoric. He had not condemned the birther conspiracy. He had not objected when Trump called African nations “shitholes.” He had not spoken out when Trump called Somali immigrants “garbage.”

But depicting the Obamas as apes crossed a line, even for Scott.

Other Republicans joined him. Representative Mike Lawler of New York said the post was “wrong and incredibly offensive” and should be deleted with an apology. Representative Michael Turner of Ohio called the images “offensive, heart breaking, and unacceptable.” Senator Roger Wicker of Mississippi called the video “totally unacceptable.” Senator Pete Ricketts of Nebraska wrote that “a reasonable person sees the racist context.” Senator Katie Britt of Alabama said the content “should have never been posted to begin with, and is not who we are as a nation.”

For a moment, it appeared there might be accountability. Republicans were speaking out. The post was deleted. Surely an apology would follow.

But Trump refused. “I didn’t make a mistake,” he said.

Trump spoke with Tim Scott on the phone. Trump described the conversation warmly: “He was great. Great guy.” The confrontation was over. Scott did not push further. The other Republicans who had condemned the video moved on to other topics.

The brief window of Republican accountability closed. The pattern held.

Three Days Later: The Super Bowl Attack (February 9, 2026)

On February 9, 2026—just three days after deleting the video depicting the Obamas as apes, and days after speaking with Senator Tim Scott and claiming to condemn racism—Trump demonstrated that the brief Republican criticism had changed nothing.

Following the Super Bowl LX halftime show, Trump posted to social media:

The racist dog whistles were unmistakable:

“Nobody understands a word this guy is saying” - dismissing Black speech and music as incomprehensible, echoing decades of racist attacks on hip-hop, rap, and African American Vernacular English.

“Doesn’t represent our standards” - positioning himself as defender of white American culture against Black artistic expression. Whose standards? “Our” standards—meaning white America’s.

“Disgusting, especially for young children” - framing Black cultural performance as morally corrupting, the same argument segregationists made about integration and the same language used to attack rock and roll, jazz, and every form of Black music that entered mainstream culture.

“Affront to the Greatness of America” - casting Black artists as fundamentally un-American, echoing his birtherism attacks on Obama and his “shithole countries” comments about predominantly Black nations.

“This ‘Show’ is just a ‘slap in the face’ to our Country” - Black cultural expression is an attack on America itself.

Trump then pivoted to praising his own presidency—”setting new standards and records every single day”—positioning himself as the embodiment of “real America” in contrast to the performer he’d just denigrated.

The timing was revealing. Just days after Republican senators had called the Obama-as-apes video “the most racist thing” they’d seen from the White House, just days after Trump claimed “of course” he condemned racism, he was back to attacking Black cultural expression as un-American and disgusting.

Senator Tim Scott, who had been “praying” the ape video was fake and who Trump had praised as a “great guy” after their phone call, said nothing. The Republicans who had briefly objected to the ape video remained silent. The pattern held.

This wasn’t a one-off outburst or justified cultural criticism. It was the same racist playbook Trump has deployed for 53 years: attack Black people and Black culture as threatening, foreign, disgusting, and un-American, while positioning himself as defender of white American values and arbiter of what constitutes “real” American culture.

The ape video brought brief Republican pushback. Three days later, Trump was attacking Black cultural expression again. The criticism had meant nothing. The accountability had vanished. The racism continued.

The Consequences: American Citizens Wondering If They Belong

On February 9, 2026—the same day Trump posted his attack on the Super Bowl halftime performance—The Atlantic published an article that revealed what Trump’s 53-year pattern of racism has accomplished.

American citizens are afraid to speak Spanish in public.

Veronica was born in California to a family with roots in Mexico. She flies the American flag on national holidays and proudly sings “The Star-Spangled Banner” at sporting events. At her son’s baseball game, she was chatting with another mom in Spanish when she suddenly stopped mid-conversation and looked around nervously at the other parents in the bleachers.

“Is this going to get me in trouble?” she thought.

Two weeks later, after watching immigration agents arrest and kill U.S. citizens in Minneapolis, Veronica sat down with her 14-year-old son to explain what he should do if law enforcement asked whether he was in the country legally. She told him that agents would be more likely to focus on her because of her darker complexion.

“I’m a U.S. citizen,” she reminded him. “You’re going to see me again.”

Think about that. An American citizen had to prepare her teenage son for the possibility that his mother—a U.S. citizen—might be detained by immigration agents because of the color of her skin and the language she speaks.

“Am I Proud to Be an American?”

Robert is a 17-year-old high school student in Arizona. He plays soccer. He gets good grades. He’s learning Spanish. His great-great-great-grandfather immigrated to the U.S. from Mexico in the late 1890s. Robert was born in Arizona.

But Robert now carries a photo of his passport on his phone in case authorities demand proof that he was born here. His parents warned him to stay under the speed limit to avoid drawing attention from police. He has drifted away from friends who support Trump’s immigration policies.

He hears the demeaning words some classmates use when talking about politics. He feels hostility from others because of the color of his skin.

“All of this stuff brought me to the question of: Am I proud to be an American?” Robert said. “Do I want to be called an American? Is that a source of pride or is that a source of shame?”

A 17-year-old American citizen, whose family has been in this country for 126 years, is asking whether being American is a source of shame.

The Military Veteran’s Daughter

A man who served in the U.S. military told The Atlantic about a recent conversation with his 26-year-old daughter. She was born in Germany while he was stationed overseas. She has dual citizenship. His family has been in the United States for more than 100 years.

His daughter called and asked: “Can I be deported?”

He didn’t know what to tell her.

“They’re doing lots of crazy stuff,” he told his daughter. “I don’t know what could happen.”

She asked for help getting a U.S. passport. He agreed. Before hanging up the phone, he told his daughter that he should be her first call if she ever ran into trouble with immigration authorities.

A veteran’s daughter—born to an American military family that has been in the U.S. for over a century—is afraid she might be deported.

The Child Who Wanted an “English-Sounding Name”

One mother told The Atlantic that her son came home from school upset. He has a Spanish first name. He asked his mother: “Why wasn’t I given an English-sounding name, like my brother?”

An American child asked his mother why she gave him a name that marks him as different, as other, as not quite American enough.

The Business Owner Preparing for Checkpoints

A prominent Phoenix restaurateur, whose family’s history in America spans generations, now lives with constant anxiety. When his family travels through areas that might have immigration checkpoints, or when they get close to the U.S.-Mexico border, his behavior changes.

“I’m the darkest one in my family,” he said. “I always turn down the music, I’m always very serious when we cross borders.”

His wife and children have made fun of him for being paranoid.

“You guys don’t understand,” he told them.

He understands what they don’t: that in Trump’s America, having darker skin makes you suspect, even if you’re an American citizen, even if your family has been here for generations, even if you’re crossing borders within your own country.

The Students Afraid to Go to School

The Atlantic reported that students are particularly affected. Some have asked whether college scholarships could be rescinded because of their ethnicity. Some now prefer remote learning because of the possibility of immigration raids at their schools.

School administrators are preparing for immigration-enforcement operations at pickup and drop-off.

Think about that: American schools are preparing for the possibility that immigration agents will raid school grounds and detain American students.

The Fear of Speaking Spanish

A 27-year-old who works in financial services changed the route he drives to work to avoid ICE vehicles.

A small-business owner worries that her citizenship might be stripped because her mother came to the U.S. unlawfully.

People are sharing videos of Americans being stopped by authorities. They’re saving lawyers’ phone numbers in their phones. They carry photos of their birth certificates and passports.

About two-thirds of U.S.-born Latinos told the Pew Research Center that they feel their situation has worsened over the past year. Nearly half said they feel less safe in their area because of mass-deportation operations.

American citizens feel less safe in their own country because of the color of their skin and the language they speak.

Trump’s Message: Who Is “Really” American?

Trump’s 53-year pattern has sent a consistent message about who counts as “really” American:

Real Americans have certain skin colors.

Real Americans have “English-sounding names.”

Real Americans speak English, not Spanish.

Real Americans listen to certain kinds of music and find other kinds “disgusting.”

Real Americans come from Norway, not Haiti or Somalia or “shithole countries.”

Everyone else—no matter how many generations their family has been here, no matter their citizenship, no matter their military service, no matter their birth certificate—is suspect, foreign, not quite American enough, potentially deportable.

On February 9, 2026, Trump attacked a Super Bowl halftime performer for cultural expression he claimed represented values that were “disgusting” and “an affront to the Greatness of America.” He complained that “nobody understands a word this guy is saying.” He positioned himself as defender of “our standards” against a performer who didn’t “represent our standards of Success, Creativity, or Excellence.”

The message was clear: this performer’s artistic expression—rooted in Black American culture—was un-American, incomprehensible, morally corrupting to children, and offensive to “our Country.”

That same day, The Atlantic documented American citizens afraid to speak Spanish at baseball games, American teenagers asking if being American is “a source of shame,” American children upset about having Spanish names, and American veterans’ daughters asking if they could be deported.

Trump’s message about who is “really” American has consequences. Those consequences include American citizens wondering if they belong in their own country.

The Through-Line from 1973 to 2026

In 1973, Trump’s company marked rental applications from Black people with a “C” for “colored” and denied them housing.

In 2026, American citizens carry photos of their birth certificates because the color of their skin makes them suspect.

In 1989, Trump spent $85,000 calling for the execution of five innocent Black and Latino teenagers.

In 2026, American teenagers ask their parents why they weren’t given “English-sounding names.”

In 2011, Trump spent five years claiming America’s first Black president wasn’t really American.

In 2026, American citizens whose families have been here for over a century ask whether they’re “really” American.

In 2018, Trump called predominantly Black nations “shitholes” and asked for more immigrants from Norway.

In 2026, a military veteran doesn’t know how to answer when his daughter asks if she could be deported.

In 2024, Trump falsely claimed Haitian immigrants in Springfield were eating pets.

In 2026, Veronica stops speaking Spanish mid-conversation at her son’s baseball game and wonders if it will “get her in trouble.”

In February 2026, Trump posted a video depicting Barack and Michelle Obama as apes, then three days later attacked a Super Bowl performer for cultural expression “nobody understands.”

That same day, American citizens wondered if speaking Spanish would get them detained, if having a Spanish name made them un-American, if the color of their skin meant they could be deported despite being born here.

The pattern across 53 years is unmistakable. The message has been consistent. And the consequences are devastating.

Conclusion: From Racist Behavior to Presidential Policy

Trump’s racism isn’t just offensive rhetoric. It has become presidential policy.

When the President of the United States calls Somali immigrants “garbage,” immigration enforcement treats them as such.

When the President depicts the opposition party leader with racist stereotypes and his Vice President calls it “funny,” racism becomes official government messaging.

When the President depicts Black former presidents as apes and Republican senators offer only brief objections before moving on, the party signals that racism is acceptable.

When the President attacks Black cultural expression as un-American and “disgusting,” millions of Americans absorb the message about who belongs and who doesn’t.

The real-world consequences are measurable: American citizens afraid to speak Spanish, teenagers questioning whether being American is shameful, veterans’ daughters asking if they can be deported, children upset about their names.

From 1973 to 2026, Donald Trump has engaged in systematic racist behavior spanning 53 years. The evidence is overwhelming. The pattern is unmistakable. The consequences are devastating—American citizens now wonder if they belong in their own country.

And yet Trump has faced zero accountability.
