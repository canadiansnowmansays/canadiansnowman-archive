Khamenei’s Iran

Part 7 Addendum: Strategic Idiocy - Eliot A. Cohen on Fifty Years of Iranian Strategic Folly — and Why a Military Strategist Reached the Same Conclusion This Series Did

March 26, 2026

AFP / Getty

Part 7 of this series — “The Ring of Fire Burns Out” — was written in January 2026, before the war. It documented the collapse of Iran’s regional proxy network and concluded that four decades of foreign investment had produced strategic catastrophe: Syria lost, Hezbollah crippled, Hamas devastated, hundreds of billions spent, thousands of Iranians killed in foreign wars — all while ordinary Iranians went hungry.

On March 23, 2026, as the war entered its fourth week, Eliot A. Cohen published “The Strategic Follies of the Islamic Republic” in The Atlantic. Cohen is not a polemicist. He is one of America’s most serious military strategists — a professor at Johns Hopkins School of Advanced International Studies, a former counsellor of the State Department, and the author of foundational texts on military strategy and civil-military relations. He reaches conclusions through a different methodological door than this series. He arrives at the same place.

His central argument deserves to be read in full. Its essence: the Islamic Republic has a 47-year record not of strategic brilliance but of consistent folly. The Western tendency to attribute extraordinary cunning and farsightedness to Iranian leadership — “strategic patience,” “thinking half a dozen moves ahead,” “mastering the art of winning by losing” — gives the regime far too much credit.

Cohen traces the record methodically. The eight-year war with Iraq, which Iran didn’t start but prosecuted catastrophically — human-wave attacks by teenagers against artillery and poison gas. The 1980s decisions to attack American shipping, producing the tanker war of 1987-88 in which the US Navy sank Iranian ships and destroyed Iranian bases, escorting Kuwaiti tankers through the Strait successfully. Decades of terrorism and assassination attempts that gained Iran nothing but international condemnation. The construction of the proxy network that this series documented — tens of billions spent building Hezbollah, Hamas, Syrian infrastructure, Iraqi militias — and then watching it collapse in fourteen months between October 2023 and December 2024.

“The past half century reveals a record not of strategic brilliance, but of consistent folly,” Cohen writes, “as the regime has waged wars badly — failing to achieve its objectives, creating new enemies, and inflicting more damage on itself than on others.”

That sentence is the external validation of this series’ analytical framework.

This series began from a different premise — accountability journalism, measuring a ruler against his own stated standards, documenting the gap between revolutionary promise and documented reality. Cohen begins from military strategy, examining operational decisions and their outcomes. Neither approach was designed to corroborate the other. The convergence is therefore meaningful.

What Cohen adds that Part 7 could not fully document at the time of writing is the strategic logic of the current war’s outcome. Iran’s decision to build its political identity around “death to America” and “death to Israel” was always, Cohen argues, a 47-year war of choice. Every escalation that led to February 28 was the culmination of folly, not brilliance. The regime that is now being bombed built the conditions for its own bombing through decades of decisions that prioritized ideological posturing over national interest.

Cohen’s conclusion carries particular weight given his professional standing: “Meanwhile, what of Iran itself? The capital city running so short on water that there has been serious discussion of having to move it; a drug-addiction problem that is among the worst in the world; hundreds of billions spent on a badly damaged nuclear program, or lost in foreign investment or to sanctions; and a population so seething with anger at its rulers that its risings against them every few years can be controlled only by massacring thousands of unarmed civilians.”

That is not a journalist’s indictment. That is a military strategist’s assessment.

Part 7 documented the ring burning out. Cohen explains why it was always going to burn — because it was built by leaders who were, in his unsparing final word, “idiots.”

The ring is ash. The war is in its fourth week. And the most serious strategic analysts in the world are now writing what this series wrote in January, before the bombs fell.

The analysis required no revision. History — and Eliot A. Cohen — provided the footnote.

— Stephen, March 2026
