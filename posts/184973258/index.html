The Insurrection Act

When Killing Citizens Isn’t Enough

Trump threatens military occupation of Minnesota after ICE kills American mother. 1,500 soldiers are preparing to deploy.

January 18, 2026

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth outside the Pentagon on Thursday. (Kevin Wolf/AP)

Ten days ago, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot and killed Renee Nicole Good in Minneapolis. DHS Secretary Kristi Noem lied about the circumstances, calling Good a “domestic terrorist” despite video evidence proving otherwise. President Trump watched the video and lied about what it showed. The administration defended Ross, attacked critics, and promised operations would continue “unabated.”

Now, the inevitable escalation: Trump is threatening to invoke the Insurrection Act and deploy active-duty military forces to Minnesota. The Pentagon has ordered approximately 1,500 soldiers from the Army’s 11th Airborne Division in Alaska to prepare for possible deployment.

This would mark the first invocation of the Insurrection Act since President George H.W. Bush deployed troops during the 1992 Los Angeles riots—riots that killed dozens and caused widespread destruction.

The pattern is now complete and horrifyingly clear:

Kill an American citizen. Lie about it. Defend the killer. Investigate the critics. Threaten military occupation.

This is how democracies die.

The Insurrection Act: What It Is and Why It Matters

The Insurrection Act, a federal law dating to 1807, permits the president to take control of a state’s National Guard or deploy active-duty troops domestically in response to a “rebellion.” It is considered a last resort, invoked only when law enforcement cannot maintain order during civil unrest.

The law has been used sparingly in American history—most recently in 1992 when Los Angeles burned after the Rodney King verdict, leaving 63 dead and causing over $1 billion in damage. Before that, President Lyndon Johnson invoked it during the 1967 Detroit riots that killed 43 people and destroyed 2,000 buildings.

These were genuine catastrophes—cities on fire, dozens dead, law enforcement overwhelmed.

Minnesota is experiencing protests. Not riots. Not rebellion. Not chaos that local authorities cannot control. Protests—Americans exercising their First Amendment rights after watching federal agents kill a citizen on video and lie about it.

The distinction matters. Enormously.

What’s Actually Happening in Minnesota

Let’s be precise about the situation Trump characterizes as requiring military intervention:

The precipitating incident: ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot Renee Nicole Good on January 7. Good was a 37-year-old U.S. citizen, mother of a 6-year-old, attempting to leave a situation where masked federal agents surrounded her vehicle and gave conflicting orders. Video from multiple angles proves she was not attempting to ram Ross, despite administration claims. Ross fired three shots, left the scene, and federal agents blocked medical care for 15 minutes.

The federal response: Rather than accountability, the administration:

  • Called Good a “domestic terrorist”

  • Defended Ross with demonstrable lies

  • Sent 100 more federal agents to Minneapolis

  • Launched criminal investigation of Governor Tim Walz and Mayor Jacob Frey

  • Shut out state investigators from the shooting investigation

  • Continued aggressive enforcement operations

  • Threatened the Insurrection Act

The community response: Thousands have protested peacefully. Some protesters use whistles and horns to alert communities to ICE presence—neighborhood watch systems protecting neighbors from federal agents. There have been confrontations, shouting, and tension. There has been no widespread violence, no destruction of property, no deaths except those caused by federal agents.

Local law enforcement response: Minneapolis police and Hennepin County sheriffs have maintained order. Governor Walz mobilized the Minnesota National Guard on Saturday—but has not deployed them. They are on standby. State authorities are managing the situation.

This is not Los Angeles 1992. This is not Detroit 1967. This is not even close.

This is a president threatening military occupation because a state’s leaders criticized his administration for killing one of their citizens.

Trump’s Threat: The Timeline

Trump threatened to invoke the rarely used act if officials failed to quell protests against ICE agents.

Thursday, January 15: Trump posts on social media that unless Minnesota officials stop protesters from “attacking” ICE agents, he will “institute the INSURRECTION ACT” and “quickly put an end to the travesty that is taking place in that once great State.”

Note the framing: Protesters are “attacking” agents. In reality, protesters blow whistles, shout, record with cellphones, and trail federal vehicles. Some have thrown objects. No ICE agent has been seriously injured. Meanwhile, ICE agents have shot and killed one person (Renee Good) and wounded another (Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis, a Venezuelan migrant).

Friday, January 16: Trump seems to walk back the threat, saying there’s no reason to invoke the law “right now.” He adds: “If I needed it, I’d use it.” The Justice Department announces criminal investigation of Walz and Frey for allegedly impeding federal law enforcement—a transparently retaliatory move to silence critics.

Saturday, January 17: The Pentagon orders 1,500 soldiers from the 11th Airborne Division to prepare for deployment. The unit specializes in cold-weather operations. Defense officials characterize this as “prudent planning,” but acknowledge the troops are on prepare-to-deploy orders in case violence escalates.

Governor Walz mobilizes the Minnesota National Guard to support local authorities, but does not deploy them. Walz and Frey plead for protesters to remain peaceful.

Also Saturday: Conservative influencer Jake Lang (a pardoned January 6 rioter) organizes a counter-protest in Minneapolis, vowing to burn a Quran and march through a Somali neighborhood. Counter-protesters massively outnumber Lang’s group. Lang is chased into a hotel lobby with water balloons thrown at him in 10-degree weather. No one is seriously hurt. A federal judge restricts ICE agents from retaliating against people “engaging in peaceful and unobstructive protest activity.”

This is the “rebellion” Trump claims requires military intervention.

The Legal and Constitutional Crisis

Invoking the Insurrection Act would create multiple constitutional crises simultaneously:

1. The Posse Comitatus Problem

The Posse Comitatus Act, passed in 1878, prohibits using federal troops for domestic law enforcement. The Insurrection Act creates an exception—but only when certain conditions are met, including that local authorities cannot maintain order.

Trump already tried deploying National Guard troops to Los Angeles, Chicago, and Portland last year. In December, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that those deployments violated Posse Comitatus because the administration “failed to identify a legal way in which the military could execute the laws in Illinois.”

Minnesota authorities are maintaining order. Governor Walz has mobilized but not deployed the National Guard. Local police are managing protests. There is no legal justification for federal military deployment under Posse Comitatus standards.

The administration knows this. They’re threatening deployment anyway.

2. The First Amendment Problem

A federal judge just ordered ICE agents not to retaliate against peaceful protesters. Trump is now threatening to deploy military forces specifically because people are protesting—exercising their First Amendment rights after watching federal agents kill a citizen.

This is using military force to suppress constitutionally protected speech. It’s using the apparatus of national defense against Americans exercising their rights. It’s exactly what the Posse Comitatus Act was designed to prevent.

3. The Federalism Problem

The Insurrection Act allows the president to override state sovereignty and take control of state National Guard forces. This is an extraordinary power, justified only by genuine emergency that states cannot handle.

Minnesota can handle this. Governor Walz has mobilized the Guard. Local authorities are maintaining order. State and local officials are managing a tense but controlled situation.

Trump wants to override state authority not because Minnesota can’t maintain order, but because Minnesota’s leaders criticized his administration for killing Renee Good and lying about it.

This isn’t federalism. It’s punishment. It’s authoritarian retaliation dressed in legal language.

4. The Due Process Problem

Beyond the immediate military threat, the Trump administration is systematically dismantling legal protections for immigrants. According to the American Immigration Council, the administration has vastly increased detention times for people awaiting immigration hearings. Many detainees are now forgoing their legal cases entirely rather than spend months in jail.

The administration wants to go further: eliminate immigration hearings altogether. A federal judge ruled this practice illegal, but the administration is appealing. This means people are being deported without ever seeing a judge, without legal representation, without the opportunity to present their cases.

This is due process collapsing under authoritarian pressure. People choosing deportation over indefinite detention. Hearings eliminated. Constitutional protections erased. And when combined with military deployment threats against states that object, it creates a complete system of unchecked power: federal agents kill citizens, lie about it, deport people without hearings, and threaten military force against anyone who protests.

5. The Investigation Weaponization

The Justice Department is investigating Minnesota Gov. Tim Walz and Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey over possible obstruction

The Justice Department’s criminal investigation of Walz and Frey is transparently retaliatory. They’re being investigated for “impeding federal law enforcement” by... criticizing federal agents who killed a citizen.

Mayor Frey called the self-defense claim “bullshit” after watching video evidence. Governor Walz called the operation a “campaign of organized brutality” and urged residents to record ICE officers. Both are protected by the First Amendment. Both are appropriate responses from elected officials when federal agents kill their constituents.

Now they face criminal investigation for exercising their rights and doing their jobs.

This is what authoritarian regimes do: Kill, lie, investigate critics, threaten military force.

The Lies Continue: Sunday Morning on National Television

On Sunday morning, Secretary Noem demonstrated why accountability is impossible under this administration. Appearing on CBS’s ‘Face the Nation,’ Noem flatly denied that DHS had used pepper spray or chemical agents against protesters—tactics explicitly limited by Judge Kate M. Menendez’s Friday injunction.

‘That federal order was a little ridiculous,’ Noem said, ‘because that federal judge came down and told us we couldn’t do what we already aren’t doing.’

When CBS confronted her with video evidence of federal agents deploying chemical agents, Noem immediately backtracked and blamed protesters. But Judge Menendez had already rejected this excuse in her Friday ruling, finding evidence of chemical agent use ‘uncontroverted’ on at least four occasions. The judge explicitly found that agents used chemicals to punish ‘protected First Amendment rights to assemble and observe,’ not in response to violence.

Noem also claimed that ‘every single individual’ arrested by immigration agents had broken the law, and that 70% had committed or been charged with violent crimes specifically. The New York Times data analysis proves this false: 33% of those arrested have no criminal record at all. Even DHS’s own spokesperson refused to defend Noem’s statement, retreating to the department’s actual position that 70% had been ‘convicted or at least charged with a crime of some kind’—not violent crimes, and not ‘every single individual.’

Meanwhile, Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche defended DOJ’s refusal to investigate Ross’s killing of Renee Good, saying ‘We don’t just go out and investigate every time an officer is forced to defend himself.’ But DOJ is investigating Good’s widow, and federal prosecutors in Minnesota are resigning in protest.

This is the pattern: Lie about the killing. Lie about chemical weapons. Lie about arrest statistics. When caught, blame victims. Investigate critics. Refuse accountability. This is systematic dishonesty from the very top.

The Pattern: From Killing to Occupation

Let’s trace the escalation clearly:

January 7: ICE kills Renee Good. Video shows she was trying to leave, not ram agents. Officer who shot her left the scene. Federal agents blocked medical care.

January 7-8: Administration immediately calls Good a “domestic terrorist” and defends Ross. Noem, Trump, and Vance lie about video evidence. They attack local officials who contradict the lies.

January 8: Two more ICE shootings in Portland. Same justification: “feared being struck by vehicle.” Federal agents leave scene. Information blackout. This brings total to 12 ICE shootings since September, all with identical justification.

January 9-14: Protests intensify. Thousands take to Minneapolis streets. ICE continues operations. 100 more agents deployed. Federal government blocks state investigators from accessing evidence. Administration doubles down on defending Ross.

January 15: Trump threatens Insurrection Act. Characterizes protesters as attacking agents. Demands Minnesota officials stop protests or face military deployment.

January 16: Justice Department launches criminal investigation of Walz and Frey. Trump walks back Insurrection Act threat slightly but keeps it on table. Both moves designed to intimidate and silence critics.

January 17: Pentagon orders 1,500 troops to prepare for deployment. Walz mobilizes but doesn’t deploy National Guard. Federal judge orders ICE not to retaliate against peaceful protesters.

January 18: Here we are. 1,500 soldiers on prepare-to-deploy orders. Potential first Insurrection Act invocation in 34 years. Military occupation threatened for a state whose “crime” was objecting to federal agents killing their citizens.

This is the escalation I warned about ten days ago. This is normalization accelerating beyond anything I predicted. This is how democratic systems collapse—not all at once, but through precisely this kind of step-by-step escalation where each outrage becomes the justification for the next.

The 11th Airborne Division: Who Trump Wants to Deploy

The soldiers preparing for deployment aren’t riot police or National Guard. They’re the Army’s 11th Airborne Division, based in Alaska, specializing in cold-weather operations.

These are combat troops. Infantry battalions. Soldiers trained for warfare, not domestic law enforcement. Deploying them to Minneapolis would put armed military forces trained to fight enemies on American streets to confront American citizens exercising constitutional rights.

The optics would be devastating: Soldiers who deploy to defend America deployed against Americans in an American city.

The practical risks would be immense: Combat troops aren’t trained for crowd control or de-escalation. They’re trained to neutralize threats. Putting them in volatile protest situations creates massive potential for tragedy.

We already know what happens when undertrained agents encounter protesters: ICE doubled its workforce, cut training in half, and produced 12 shootings in four months, including Renee Good’s death.

Now Trump wants to deploy soldiers with even less domestic law enforcement training into an even more volatile situation.

What could possibly go wrong?

What Walz and Minnesota Face

Governor Walz is in an impossible position:

If he backs down: He legitimizes Trump’s threats, abandons his constituents, betrays his oath to defend Minnesota’s sovereignty, and sets precedent that any state criticizing federal misconduct will face military threats.

If he stands firm: Trump may invoke the Insurrection Act, deploy federal troops over state objections, take control of Minnesota National Guard, and create a constitutional crisis that could reach the Supreme Court—which recently ruled against Trump on similar deployments but might rule differently with Insurrection Act invocation.

If violence occurs: Trump will claim vindication and justify deployment, regardless of who initiates violence or whether federal agents provoke it.

Walz has threaded the needle so far: mobilizing the Guard without deploying them, pleading for peaceful protest, maintaining order through existing authorities, and making clear he can handle the situation without federal military intervention.

But he’s operating against an administration that wants escalation, not de-escalation. An administration that sees military deployment as victory, not failure. An administration that would benefit politically from chaos in a Democratic state.

Attorney General Keith Ellison is seeking a restraining order, calling the operation a “federal invasion.” Minnesota is suing the Trump administration, alleging the surge is politically motivated and unconstitutional.

These are the actions of state authorities doing their jobs—defending their sovereignty and protecting their citizens from federal overreach. Trump characterizes this as “rebellion” requiring military response.

The Broader Context: National Guard Deployments

Trump has already deployed National Guard troops to multiple cities:

Los Angeles (June): Trump seized control of California National Guard over Governor Newsom’s objections. About 4,000 Guard members deployed with 700 active-duty Marines after protests against ICE turned violent “in a few instances.” The deployment was legally contested and eventually ended after the Supreme Court’s December ruling.

Washington, D.C.: More than 2,600 National Guard deployed, with mission extended through end of 2026. This deployment remains legally contested but Trump has more authority in D.C. as federal jurisdiction.

The pattern: Deploy military forces to cities with Democratic leadership after ICE operations produce protests. Frame protests as violence requiring military response. Keep forces deployed long-term regardless of conditions.

Minnesota would fit this pattern perfectly. And unlike previous deployments, this one would invoke the Insurrection Act—a more powerful legal tool with fewer restrictions.

The Stakes: Democracy vs. Authoritarianism

What’s at stake here transcends Minnesota:

If Trump deploys troops successfully: Any state that criticizes federal misconduct faces potential military occupation. Any governor who objects to federal agents killing citizens faces criminal investigation and military override. Any mayor who calls federal lies “bullshit” faces federal prosecution.

If the deployment is contested: Constitutional crisis goes to Supreme Court, which must decide whether president can deploy military against peaceful protesters in states that don’t want them. The Court already ruled against Trump once on similar issues—but Insurrection Act invocation is different legal territory.

If violence erupts: Whether from protesters, federal agents, provocateurs, or military forces, Trump uses it to justify further escalation. Portland’s “Battle of Federal Buildings” model but with Army infantry instead of Border Patrol.

If deployment succeeds without violence: Trump establishes precedent that criticism of federal killings justifies military deployment. Other states face similar threats. Chilling effect on dissent becomes overwhelming.

This isn’t about immigration enforcement anymore. This is about whether America remains a constitutional republic with federalism, civil liberties, and limits on executive power—or becomes an authoritarian system where the president deploys military forces against states whose leaders criticize him.

This Will Not End Well

There is no good outcome here:

Scenario 1 - Trump backs down: Unlikely. He’s already threatened the Insurrection Act publicly. Backing down looks weak. His base expects action. And the administration benefits politically from confrontation with Democratic states.

Scenario 2 - Walz capitulates: Silence criticism, stop supporting protesters, accept federal agents killing citizens without objection. This betrays Minnesota, establishes terrible precedent, and guarantees more killings with less accountability. Also unlikely—Walz has gone too far to retreat now.

Scenario 3 - Legal resolution: Courts block deployment, Trump backs down citing judicial ruling. Possible, given Supreme Court’s December ruling—but Insurrection Act gives president more power than National Guard deployments. Could take weeks to resolve while troops sit in Minnesota or Alaska on standby.

Scenario 4 - Deployment proceeds: Trump invokes Insurrection Act, deploys 1,500 troops to Minneapolis, takes control of Minnesota National Guard. Constitutional crisis. Potential violence. National outrage. Comparison to authoritarian regimes becomes impossible to avoid.

Scenario 5 - Violence erupts before deployment: Protesters, counter-protesters, federal agents, or provocateurs cause serious injuries or deaths. Trump claims vindication, deploys troops, says he tried to prevent it. This is probably the administration’s preferred outcome—violence justifies deployment and makes Trump look decisive.

I warned about normalization. This is beyond normalization. This is the playbook of authoritarian regimes: Kill, lie, defend killers, investigate critics, threaten military force, deploy troops.

Each step has precedent somewhere in American history. The combination—the entire sequence in response to criticism of a federal killing—has no precedent in modern American democracy.

Conclusion: The Test of American Democracy

Renee Nicole Good was killed by ICE Officer Jonathan Ross on January 7, 2026. Her death was predictable—the result of systematic negligence, undertrained agents, and an administration that prioritizes quotas over safety.

The administration’s response was also predictable: lies, defense of the killer, attacks on critics, continuation of the policies that caused the death.

But threatening to deploy active-duty military forces to Minnesota because state leaders objected to the killing? That crosses a line that even this administration hadn’t crossed before.

This is the test. This is the moment when Americans—and their elected representatives, and the courts, and the military itself—decide whether we’re still a democracy with constraints on executive power, or whether we’ve become something else.

Governor Walz mobilized the National Guard but hasn’t deployed them, demonstrating he can maintain order without federal intervention. Minneapolis police are managing protests. A federal judge ordered ICE not to retaliate against peaceful protesters. The system is working at the state and local level.

Trump wants to override all of it because Minnesota’s leaders criticized him for killing Renee Good and lying about it.

1,500 soldiers are preparing to deploy. The 11th Airborne Division is on standby. The Insurrection Act is being invoked to punish a state for exercising First Amendment rights.

I’ve written three essays demanding accountability for Renee Good’s death. I’ve documented the lies, the systematic negligence, the pattern of violence. I’ve warned about normalization.

But I never imagined we’d reach the point where an American president would threaten military occupation of an American state because that state’s leaders objected to federal agents killing one of their citizens.

The normalization I warned about? We’re past that now. We’re in new territory—authoritarian territory, where criticism of federal killings triggers military threats.

This is how democracies die: Not in one dramatic coup, but through exactly this kind of escalation where each abuse becomes the justification for the next, where killing citizens leads to defending killers leads to investigating critics leads to threatening military force.

The question is whether Americans will accept it.

1,500 soldiers are waiting for the answer.
