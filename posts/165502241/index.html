The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 5: Nuclear Renaissance - Small Modular Reactors Lead the Way

July 9, 2025

Nuclear Power's Biggest Reinvention Since Atoms for Peace

Nuclear power is experiencing its most dramatic transformation since the 1950s. Forget everything you think you know about massive construction projects, cost overruns, and decades-long timelines. The global Small Modular Reactor (SMR) market represents one of the most promising segments within the nuclear energy industry, characterized by innovative reactor designs with electrical outputs typically below 300 MWe.

This isn't your grandfather's nuclear power. SMRs promise to deliver clean, reliable electricity faster, cheaper, and safer than traditional nuclear plants. For the AI energy crisis, they offer something no other clean technology can match: massive amounts of carbon-free electricity available 24/7, regardless of weather.

The Scale of the Opportunity

Market growth projections vary significantly based on deployment scenarios, with conservative estimates valuing the global market at approximately $10-15 billion by 2030, while optimistic scenarios suggest potential market values exceeding $150-200 billion by 2035.

But market size barely captures the potential impact. Nuclear plants last far longer than any other source of energy except possibly hydro. We are seeing plants lasting beyond 60 years now, and it seems likely that, for some at least, the operating lifetime is 80 years or more.

This longevity matters enormously for AI infrastructure investments. Data centers are billion-dollar, decades-long commitments. Having power sources that can reliably operate for 60-80 years provides unprecedented energy security.

The Leading Players

NuScale Power: The clear frontrunner. The current VOYGR-6 design, a scalable 462 MWe SMR, was submitted for NRC review on January 1, 2023, and was approved on May 29, 2025.

NuScale Power is in advanced discussions with several potential customers for its small modular reactor technology and could deliver an operating power plant in 2030 "if we get closure on a deal here soon," CEO John Hopkins said.

The Technology: NuScale's SMR designs employ 9 feet (2.7 m) diameter by 65 feet (20 m) high reactor vessels that use conventional cooling methods and run on low enriched uranium fuel assemblies based on existing light water reactor designs.

Global Expansion: On 29 August 2024, Nuclear Power Ghana and Regnum Technology Group signed an agreement to build a 12-module nuclear power plant in Ghana during the US-Africa Nuclear Energy Summit in Nairobi, Kenya.

The Factory Revolution

The game-changer is manufacturing. SMRs should avoid some of this construction risk, as they are partly or wholly built in a factory and then simply delivered and installed on-site. This process should be much shorter overall than large nuclear construction.

Manufacturing partner Doosan has 12 NuScale modules in production now and could deliver 20 per year as orders materialize. This industrialization of nuclear power production could dramatically reduce costs and construction times.

The Economics: A proposed NuScale 920 MW plant would require about 35 acres compared to the massive footprints of traditional nuclear plants. This compact design opens up many more potential sites, especially near data centers.

Perfect for AI Data Centers

SMRs and AI data centers are a perfect match:

Right-Sized Power: Modern hyperscale data centers need 100-500 MW of power. SMRs can be sized exactly to match, with Individual NuScale Power Modules are intended to be kept in an underground pool and each are expected to produce about 77 megawatts of electricity.

Baseload Reliability: Unlike renewables, SMRs provide constant power 24/7/365. This is exactly what AI data centers need—no weather dependence, no storage requirements, just steady, predictable electricity.

Co-location Potential: Like Oklo, which inked a nonbinding agreement with data center developer Switch for up to 12 GW of capacity over 20 years, NuScale has openly courted IT and AI customers. The compact design allows SMRs to be located directly adjacent to data centers.

Scalability: Need more power? Add more modules. SMRs can scale from single units to large multi-module plants, growing with data center expansion.

The Uranium Supply Chain Challenge

But SMRs face a critical supply chain bottleneck that could derail their promise: uranium fuel supply. Current global uranium production is approximately 50,000-55,000 tonnes per year, while annual demand is around 65,000-70,000 tonnes. The deficit is currently met by drawing down existing stockpiles and recycled weapons-grade uranium, but those sources are finite.

The Resource vs. Production Reality:

The United States has substantial uranium reserves - approximately 207,400 tonnes of recoverable uranium, ranking it among the top 10 globally. Canada has even more, with around 588,500 tonnes, making it the world's second-largest uranium reserve holder after Australia. So the raw material exists in North America.

The problem isn't geological - it's economic and regulatory. U.S. uranium production has collapsed from over 16,000 tonnes annually in the 1980s to just 173 tonnes in 2023. Most domestic uranium mines shut down when cheap imports from Kazakhstan, Russia, and other countries flooded the market. Environmental permitting for new mines can take 7-10 years, even when the uranium is readily accessible.

Canada remains a major producer (about 7,000 tonnes annually), but most of that goes to existing international contracts. If hundreds of SMRs deploy rapidly, North America would need to restart mothballed mines and open new ones - a process that takes years and billions in investment.

The HALEU Problem:

Most advanced SMRs, including many of the leading designs, require High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU) - uranium enriched to 5-20% U-235, compared to the 3-5% used in traditional reactors. Having uranium ore is only the first step; you also need enrichment facilities. Currently, Russia is virtually the only commercial supplier of HALEU globally, creating a massive geopolitical vulnerability.

TerraPower, Bill Gates' nuclear company, has already experienced this firsthand. TerraPower delayed deployment because it could not get sufficient access to HALEU fuel, though it has since made new agreements with South African sources. This illustrates how the uranium supply chain involves multiple bottlenecks: mining, conversion, enrichment, and fuel fabrication.

The North American Response

Geopolitical tensions are driving action. The war in Ukraine and broader tensions with Russia have made uranium supply security a urgent national security issue for both the U.S. and Canada.

Canada's Strategic Opportunity:

Canada is positioning itself as the democratic world's uranium superpower. Saskatchewan, which already produces about 13% of global uranium, has significant expansion potential. Cameco Corporation, one of the world's largest uranium producers, is ramping up production at its Cigar Lake and McArthur River mines. The Canadian government has recognized uranium as a critical mineral essential for national security.

More importantly, Canada is exploring domestic enrichment capacity. Currently, Canadian uranium ore gets shipped overseas for enrichment - often to Russia or China - then comes back as reactor fuel. There's growing political and industry support for building enrichment facilities in Canada, which would create a complete North American uranium fuel cycle independent of adversaries.

U.S. Strategic Initiatives:

The United States is taking this seriously too. The ADVANCE Act, signed in 2024, streamlines nuclear licensing and specifically addresses fuel supply security. The Department of Energy is investing billions in domestic uranium production and HALEU enrichment through companies like Centrus Energy and American Centrifuge Operating.

The Biden administration banned Russian uranium imports starting in 2024, with full phase-out by 2028. This creates immediate pressure to develop North American alternatives. Some estimates suggest the U.S. needs to increase domestic uranium production by 500-1000% to meet long-term SMR deployment goals.

The Timeline Challenge:

The question is speed. Building new uranium mines takes 7-10 years due to environmental permitting. Constructing enrichment facilities takes 5-7 years and billions in investment. Meanwhile, the first commercial SMRs are supposed to be online by 2030.

This creates a classic chicken-and-egg problem: SMR companies need guaranteed fuel supplies to attract investors, but mining companies need guaranteed demand to justify new mines and enrichment plants. The solution may require government backing similar to what built the original nuclear industry in the 1950s-60s.

The Strategic Stakes:

If North America can build a complete, independent uranium fuel cycle by 2035, it would give SMRs a massive strategic advantage over any energy technology dependent on Chinese or Russian supply chains. Solar panels rely on Chinese manufacturing, but SMRs could be completely "Made in North America" from fuel to reactor.

This isn't just about energy security - it's about maintaining technological leadership in the AI era. The countries that control critical energy supply chains will have enormous leverage over AI development and deployment.

The Global Race Is On

United States: The US government is keen to attract SMR project developments and private investors. The US Department of Energy has spent over US$1.2bn on SMR and has announced additional funding for the next decade that could amount to US$5.5bn.

Europe: The European Commission created the European Industrial Alliance in February 2024 to facilitate the development of SMRs in Europe by the early 2030s.

Asia: China, South Korea, and Japan are all developing their own SMR programs, recognizing the strategic importance of advanced nuclear technology.

Real Projects, Real Timelines

The competition features both established nuclear industry players and innovative startups. Traditional nuclear vendors like GE Hitachi, Westinghouse, and Rosatom have developed SMR designs leveraging their existing technological expertise, while newcomers such as NuScale Power, TerraPower, and X-energy have attracted significant investment with novel approaches.

TerraPower: Founded by Bill Gates, TerraPower is building its first Natrium reactor in Wyoming. Strategic partnerships with KBR and HD Hyundai in 2025 enhance global deployment, while HALEU fuel deals with Centrus and ASP Isotopes address past delays.

X-Energy: Founded in 2009 at Rockville, Maryland, X-Energy develops the Xe-100, a high-temperature gas-cooled reactor (HTGR) delivering 80 MWe per unit (200 MWth), scalable to 320 MWe in a four-pack or 960 MWe with 12 units.

The Reality Check

Not everything is smooth sailing. NuScale's proposed flagship SMR project in Utah has closed because it became uneconomic, as the projected cost of construction increased by 75 percent (to $9.3 billion) between 2021 and 2024.

This failure highlights key challenges:

Cost Escalation: Construction costs have soared due to inflation in materials and labor. Factory production doesn't guarantee immunity from broader economic pressures.

Supply Chain Constraints: DOE is well aware that constricted supply of uranium could squeeze out SMRs. TerraPower, for example, has already delayed deployment because it could not get sufficient access to HALEU fuel.

Market Risk: SMRs must compete with rapidly falling costs of renewables plus storage. Any delays in deployment risk losing economic competitiveness.

The Investment Surge

Despite challenges, private investment is flooding in. In October 2024, Amazon's Climate Pledge Fund led a $500 million Series C-1 round with investors including Ares Management and NGP for X-Energy.

This corporate interest from tech giants signals that the AI industry recognizes SMRs as critical infrastructure for their energy-intensive future.

Regulatory Progress

The UK aims to supply up to 25% of the UK's electricity from nuclear power by 2050. In July 2023, the country launched the Great British Nuclear plan to support both SMR technology and large nuclear power plants.

In the US, regulatory frameworks are evolving, with the US Nuclear Regulatory Commission beginning to develop specific guidelines for fusion facilities distinct from fission regulations. This regulatory clarity is essential for investment and deployment.

SMRs vs. Other Clean Technologies

How do SMRs compare to other solutions for the AI energy crisis?

Vs. Solar/Wind: SMRs provide baseload power without intermittency issues or massive storage requirements. However, they're more expensive upfront and take longer to deploy.

Vs. Batteries: For long-duration storage (days to weeks), SMRs are far more economical than batteries. But batteries can respond to demand changes much faster.

Vs. Fusion: SMRs use proven technology available today, while fusion remains 5-10 years away. But fusion promises even cleaner, safer operation with abundant fuel.

The Timeline for AI

For the immediate AI energy crisis (2025-2030), SMRs offer the most realistic large-scale clean baseload solution. Contributing to this escalation are energy security imperatives, supportive regulatory environments, rising electricity demand—particularly from the rapid proliferation of datacenters and artificial intelligence (AI) infrastructure.

While the first commercial SMRs won't be operational until the late 2020s, they could provide the backbone for massive AI infrastructure expansion in the 2030s. The question is whether the industry can overcome current challenges and deliver on its promises—and whether North America can build the uranium supply chain to fuel them.
