The Venezuela Invasion Series

The Occupation Begins

"We Are Going to Run the Country" — With No Plan, No Presence, No Exit

January 3, 2026

January 3, 2026

Just hours after announcing the capture of Nicolás Maduro, President Trump made a statement that stunned even his own advisers: “We are going to run the country.” No timeline. No plan. No clarity about who actually governs Venezuela. Just an open-ended American occupation of a South American nation—announced from a private club in Florida.

At 11:53 AM EST on Saturday, January 3, 2026, President Trump stood before reporters at Mar-a-Lago and declared that the United States would occupy Venezuela indefinitely.

“We are going to run the country until such time that we can do a safe, proper and judicious transition,” he said, offering no details about what that transition would look like, who would lead it, or how long American control would last.

When pressed on the timeline, Trump was vague: “I’d like to do it quickly, but it takes a period of time.”

When asked who specifically would govern Venezuela, Trump pointed to the officials standing behind him—Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, and Gen. Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff. “For a period of time, the people that are standing right behind me, we’re going to be running it,” he said.

When asked if that meant U.S. troops would occupy Venezuela, Trump said: “We’re not afraid of boots on the ground if we have to have them.”

The only problem: The Pentagon had already announced that U.S. forces had withdrawn from Venezuela after the raid. There were no visible signs of an American military presence in Caracas by midday Saturday. The United States closed its embassy in Venezuela in 2019, leaving no diplomatic infrastructure to administer the country.

So who, exactly, is running Venezuela?

The answer, as of Saturday evening: Nobody knows.

“THERE IS ONLY ONE PRESIDENT”

Vice President Delcy Rodríguez of Venezuela, who was sworn in as the country’s interim leader on Saturday, during an interview in Caracas, Venezuela, in September.Credit...Adriana Loureiro Fernandez for The New York Times

At 10:00 AM, Trump told reporters that Venezuela’s vice president, Delcy Rodríguez, had been sworn in as interim president and would work with the United States.

“Marco is working on that directly,” Trump said, referring to Secretary of State Rubio. “He just had a conversation with her, and she’s essentially willing to do what we think is necessary to make Venezuela great again.”

Two hours later, Rodríguez delivered a televised address to Venezuela that made clear she viewed the United States as an illegal invader that must be rejected.

“There is only one president in this country, and his name is Nicolás Maduro Moros,” she said to thundering applause.

Rodríguez appeared flanked by Venezuela’s defense minister, attorney general, and the heads of the country’s legislature and judiciary—a unified front that directly contradicted Trump’s claim that the United States would run Venezuela. The defense minister and attorney general also publicly criticized Trump and the U.S. military action.

“We are determined to be free,” Rodríguez said. “What is being done to Venezuela is a barbarity.”

Her speech demolished Trump’s narrative within hours of his press conference.

“We had already warned that an aggression was underway under false excuses and false pretenses, and that the masks had fallen off, revealing only one objective: regime change in Venezuela,” she said. “This regime change would also allow for the seizure of our energy, mineral and natural resources. This is the true objective, and the world and the international community must know it.”

Significantly, Venezuelan state television labeled Rodríguez as “Vice President” throughout her address—not “President” or “Interim President.” When she finished speaking, the state broadcaster said that Rodríguez, as vice president, had just made clear that Maduro remained Venezuela’s president.

Earlier in the day, Rodríguez had demanded that the Trump administration provide “immediate proof of life” for Maduro and his wife, Cilia Flores. Trump responded by posting a photo on Truth Social showing a blindfolded, handcuffed man in a gray sweatshirt holding a bottle of water—an image he said was Maduro aboard the USS Iwo Jima.

The trophy photo was striking as the first public image of a captured head of state made public by a sitting U.S. president. When the United States killed Osama bin Laden and Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, no photos of their bodies were released. Trump posted Maduro’s image before his press conference, turning the capture into spectacle.

THE OPPOSITION LEADER TRUMP WON’T BACK

While Trump claimed he would work with Maduro’s vice president—who then publicly rejected him—he simultaneously dismissed Venezuela’s actual opposition leader, the person most Venezuelans voted for.

María Corina Machado emerges as top potential successor after Máduro’s fall

María Corina Machado, Venezuela’s leading opposition figure and 2024 Nobel Peace Prize winner, released a statement Saturday morning celebrating Maduro’s capture and asserting she was ready to lead.

“Today we are prepared to assert our mandate and seize power,” she wrote.

Roughly two hours later, Trump was asked about Machado during his press conference. He said they had not spoken and described her as “a very nice woman” who “doesn’t have the support” in Venezuela to lead.

He never mentioned Edmundo González, the opposition candidate who actually won the 2024 election according to independent monitors and whom the United States has recognized as Venezuela’s legitimate president.

The rejection was stunning given that Secretary of State Rubio had previously called Machado “the personification of resilience, tenacity and patriotism.” In October, she dedicated her Nobel Peace Prize to Trump, calling him her “decisive support.”

A spokeswoman for Machado and González declined to comment on Trump’s remarks.

So Trump won’t work with:

  • Maduro (captured and being transported to New York)

  • Rodríguez (publicly defying U.S. occupation)

  • Machado (dismissed as lacking support)

  • González (not even mentioned)

The question remains: Who governs Venezuela?

Trump’s answer: “We are going to run the country.”

But how, exactly, when there’s no U.S. military presence, no embassy, and every Venezuelan leader either captured, defiant, or rejected?

THE PANAMA PRECEDENT

The last time the United States deposed a Latin American leader was December 1989, when President George H.W. Bush sent U.S. troops into Panama to capture Gen. Manuel Noriega, who had been the country’s military dictator for six years.

The parallels to Venezuela are striking—and the differences revealing.

The Similarities

Both were indicted on federal drug-trafficking charges:

  • Noriega: Accused of taking millions in bribes from traffickers, turning Panama into cocaine smuggling capital

  • Maduro: Accused of overseeing “Cartel de los Soles,” narco-terrorism conspiracy

Both faced months of military buildup:

  • Panama 1989: President Bush sent nearly 2,000 soldiers and Marines to reinforce 10,300 already there

  • Venezuela 2025-26: Trump deployed 15,000 troops to Caribbean, USS Gerald R. Ford carrier strike group, largest regional deployment since Cuban Missile Crisis

Both operations involved overwhelming force:

  • Panama: 27,000 troops participated

  • Venezuela: 150 aircraft launching across hemisphere, Delta Force raid, cyberattack on power grid

Both sought to “restore democracy”:

  • Panama: Install opposition government

  • Venezuela: Unclear (Trump rejected opposition leader, can’t work with vice president)

The Differences

Noriega fled. Maduro was captured quickly.

When U.S. troops invaded Panama, Noriega fled with his mistress in an unmarked Hyundai, briefly emerged at a Dairy Queen, then took refuge at the Vatican Embassy. U.S. forces surrounded the building and blasted heavy metal from Black Sabbath and Guns N’ Roses until he surrendered days later.

Maduro tried to reach a steel-reinforced safe room in his compound but Delta Force operators breached the door “in a matter of seconds,” according to Trump. The entire operation—from airstrikes dismantling air defenses to Maduro’s capture—took 2 hours and 20 minutes.

Gen. Dan Caine said the military had practiced on a replica of Maduro’s safe house for months. “We find Maduro and understand how he moved, where he lived, where he traveled, what he ate, what he wore, what were his pets,” Caine said. A CIA source within the Venezuelan government had monitored Maduro’s location in recent days.

Trump said he watched the raid in real time from Mar-a-Lago: “I watched it literally like I was watching a television show. It was an amazing thing.”

Congressional authorization: Then vs. Now

Panama 1989: Congress debated and authorized the intervention. While controversial, it followed democratic process.

Venezuela 2026: No Congressional authorization. No declaration of war. No Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF).

When asked about Congressional approval, Trump said: “Congress has a tendency to leak,” before adding that members were called “immediately after” the operation.

Rubio said it was not the kind of mission where you could give Congress notice in advance because it was “largely a law-enforcement operation.”

But Trump quickly stepped in: “They knew we were coming,” he said of Venezuelan forces, suggesting operational security wasn’t actually the issue.

Casualties: Confirmed vs. Unknown

Panama 1989: 23 U.S. troops killed, 314 Panamanian soldiers dead, 202 civilians killed.

Venezuela 2026: Trump said no American troops were killed. He suggested some were injured when a helicopter was hit but remained flyable. Two U.S. officials said about half a dozen soldiers were injured overall.

Venezuelan officials reported multiple deaths of “officials, soldiers and civilians.” At least one older woman, Rosa González, was killed when an airstrike hit a civilian apartment building near the Caracas airport. A U.S. official would not comment on Venezuelan casualties.

At least 115 people had been killed in 35 boat strikes since September as part of Trump’s Venezuela campaign before Saturday’s invasion.

Exit strategy: Clear vs. Nonexistent

Panama 1989: U.S. forces withdrew. Noriega was convicted of drug trafficking and served 17 years in prison. Panama returned to democratic rule.

Venezuela 2026: Trump says the United States will “run the country” with no timeline. “I’d like to do it quickly, but it takes a period of time.”

When asked how long, Trump immediately turned to rebuilding oil assets: “We’re going to have our very large United States oil companies, the biggest anywhere in the world, go in, spend billions of dollars, fix the badly broken infrastructure, the oil infrastructure, and start making money for the country.”

The focus was not on democratic transition. It was on oil extraction.

THE GAZA ECHO: WHEN REGIME CHANGE MEETS GOVERNANCE VACUUM

The Venezuela occupation is beginning to resemble another recent case where military force removed leadership without a plan for what comes next: Gaza.

The pattern:

Israel/Gaza:

  1. Military operation to remove Hamas leadership

  2. Claim of “temporary” control

  3. No clear plan for governance

  4. International condemnation

  5. Humanitarian crisis

  6. Prolonged occupation with no exit strategy

U.S./Venezuela (emerging):

  1. Military operation to remove Maduro ✓

  2. Claim of “temporary” control ✓ (”until transition”)

  3. No clear plan for governance ✓ (Trump contradicts himself, Rodríguez defies him, opposition rejected)

  4. International condemnation ✓ (see below)

  5. Humanitarian crisis emerging ✓ (civilians killed, power cut, airstrikes on apartment buildings)

  6. Prolonged occupation? ✓ (Trump: “takes a period of time”)

The “Who Governs?” Crisis:

In Gaza, Israel removed Hamas but had no answer for who would govern. International organizations? Palestinian Authority? Israel itself? The question remains unresolved, contributing to ongoing instability.

In Venezuela, Trump removed Maduro but has no answer for who governs:

  • Maduro: Captured, en route to New York trial

  • Rodríguez: Says she’s vice president, Maduro still president, rejects U.S. control

  • Machado: Trump says she lacks support

  • González: Not mentioned

  • U.S. officials: “We are going to run the country” but no presence on ground

The power vacuum is complete. And power vacuums invite chaos.

THE OIL OBJECTIVE: NO LONGER EVEN DISGUISED

Throughout his press conference and interviews, Trump made clear that Venezuela’s oil—not counter-narcotics, not restoring democracy—was the central objective.

Trump’s statements:

“We’re going to have our very large United States oil companies, the biggest anywhere in the world, go in, spend billions of dollars, fix the badly broken infrastructure, the oil infrastructure, and start making money for the country.”

“It’s going to make a lot of money.”

About past Venezuelan governments: “They stole our oil.”

“We’ll be selling large amounts of oil to other countries.”

When asked how long the U.S. would occupy Venezuela, Trump immediately turned to rebuilding oil assets rather than democratic transition.

When asked how this aligns with his “America First” policy of avoiding foreign entanglements, Trump said: “I think it is,” citing the need for oil and energy.

The numbers:

Venezuela claims 303 billion barrels in proven oil reserves—the largest of any country on Earth. But it produces only about 1 million barrels per day, around 1 percent of global production, due to decades of underinvestment and mismanagement.

Energy Aspects, a research firm, estimated that adding another 500,000 barrels per day would cost $10 billion and take about two years. Major increases would require “tens of billions of dollars over multiple years.”

Trump appears to be counting on U.S. oil companies to make that investment—and to extract Venezuelan oil for American benefit.

Chevron’s immediate response:

Chevron, the main Western company still operating in Venezuela (producing about a quarter of Venezuela’s oil), released a statement Saturday saying it was “trying to ensure the safety of its employees and its operations.”

“With more than a century in Venezuela, we support a peaceful, lawful transition that promotes stability and economic recovery,” said Kevin Slagle, a Chevron spokesman. “We’re prepared to work constructively with the U.S. government during this period, leveraging our experience and presence to strengthen U.S. energy security.”

The company had clearly prepared for this moment. The statement was released within hours of Trump’s announcement.

Stephen Miller’s framework vindicated:

As I documented in Part 6 (The Whisperers), Stephen Miller provided the ideological justification for seizing Venezuelan oil: “American sweat, ingenuity and toil created the oil industry in Venezuela. Its tyrannical expropriation was the largest recorded theft of American wealth and property.”

Trump repeated this framework almost verbatim: “They stole our oil.”

The logic: American companies built Venezuela’s oil infrastructure in the early 20th century. Venezuela nationalized it in the 1970s. Therefore, it’s American property that was stolen. Therefore, the United States has the right to “reclaim” it.

This is not counter-narcotics. This is not restoring democracy. This is resource extraction justified through imperial logic.

CONGRESSIONAL RAGE: “BOY WERE WE WRONG”

The reaction from Congress was swift and furious—including from Trump’s own party.

Democrats:

Rep. Adam Smith (D-WA), top Democrat on House Armed Services Committee: “Trump clearly has no idea what comes next. How are we going to run Venezuela when we have no presence in the country. Where does this go?”

Sen. Jack Reed (D-RI), top Democrat on Senate Armed Services Committee: “This is ludicrous. No serious plan has been presented for how such an extraordinary undertaking would work or what it will cost the American people. History offers no shortage of warnings about the costs—human, strategic, and moral—of assuming we can govern another nation by force.”

Sen. Jeanne Shaheen (D-NH), top Democrat on Senate Foreign Relations Committee: The capture was “entirely inconsistent” with what top officials told lawmakers in closed-door briefings in recent weeks. “Because the president and his cabinet repeatedly denied any intention of conducting regime change in Venezuela when briefing Congress, we are left with no understanding of how the Administration is preparing to mitigate risks to the U.S. and we have no information regarding a long-term strategy.”

Rep. Jim Himes (D-CT), top Democrat on House Intelligence Committee: The administration “must immediately brief Congress on its plan to ensure stability in the region and its legal justification for this decision.”

Republicans (mostly supportive):

Sen. Mitch McConnell (R-KY): Said the president “has broad constitutional authority and long historical precedent for the limited use of military force.” Called Maduro a “thug” and said he expected Congress to be briefed soon. “A free, democratic, and stable Venezuela, led by Venezuelans, is in America’s national security interests.”

Sen. Roger Wicker (R-MS), chairman of Senate Armed Services Committee: “This arrest was the culmination of a monthslong effort by the Trump administration to degrade the narco-terrorist organizations that Maduro oversaw.” Called for briefing from senior military and law enforcement leaders.

But then there’s Marjorie Taylor Greene:

Just days before she is expected to resign from Congress, Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene (R-GA)—once one of Trump’s staunchest allies—broke with him publicly:

“Americans’ disgust with our own government’s never ending military aggression and support of foreign wars is justified because we are forced to pay for it, and both parties, Republicans and Democrats, always keep the Washington military machine funded and going. This is what many in MAGA thought they voted to end. Boy were we wrong.”

The statement is remarkable because Greene represents the MAGA base that propelled Trump to office on promises of ending “forever wars” and “regime change” operations.

Trump campaigned on putting “America First”—which he defined as avoiding foreign military entanglements, bringing troops home, and not trying to run other countries.

Now Trump is announcing an open-ended occupation of Venezuela with no exit strategy, explicitly to extract oil.

Even Greene recognizes the betrayal.

THE INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE: AUTHORITARIANS VS. DEMOCRACIES

The global reaction to Trump’s Venezuela invasion revealed a stark divide—not along ideological lines as expected, but along authoritarian vs. democratic governance lines.

Condemned by:

Brazil (President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva): “The bombings on Venezuelan territory and the capture of its president cross an unacceptable line. These acts represent a grave affront to Venezuela’s sovereignty and yet another extremely dangerous precedent for the entire international community.” Recalled “the worst moments of interference in the politics” of the region.

Colombia (President Gustavo Petro): “Alert the world: Venezuela has been attacked!” Deployed forces to Colombia’s border with Venezuela anticipating a massive refugee crisis. “I reject the aggression against the sovereignty of Venezuela and of Latin America.”

Mexico: “Condemned energetically” Washington’s unilateral military action, warned of regional instability, urged dialogue. Foreign ministry: “Mexico makes an urgent call to respect international law, as well as the principles and purposes of the U.N. Charter, and to cease any act of aggression against the Venezuelan government and people.”

Gerardo Fernández Noroña, influential leader of Mexico’s governing Morena party: “President Maduro has not been captured; he was deprived of his liberty through a military intervention by the United States government. He is a prisoner of war. They seek through this to subdue the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela in order to seize its natural resources.”

Cuba (President Miguel Díaz-Canel): Condemned “a criminal attack” and demanded “urgent reaction” from the world.

Russia: “An act of armed aggression against Venezuela. This is deeply concerning and condemnable. The pretexts used to justify such actions are unfounded.” Called for urgent UN Security Council meeting.

(Note: Russia’s condemnation is hilariously hypocritical given its invasion of Ukraine, but we’ll address that in Part 8.)

Iran: “A flagrant violation of the national sovereignty and territorial integrity of Venezuela... a clear violation of the basic principles of the United Nations Charter.”

Chile (President Gabriel Boric): “We express our concern and condemnation of the military actions by the United States taking place in Venezuela, and we call for seeking a peaceful solution to the serious crisis affecting the country.”

Spain: “We urge everyone to de-escalate the situation and act responsibly. International law and the principles of the U.N. Charter must be respected.” Offered to mediate to broker a negotiated and peaceful solution.

Switzerland: Called for “de-escalation, restraint, and respect for international law, including the prohibition of the use of force and the principle of respect for territorial integrity.”

European Union (Kaja Kallas, foreign policy chief): “Under all circumstances, the principles of international law and the UN Charter must be respected. We call for restraint.”

Supported by:

Argentina (President Javier Milei): “Liberty advances.”

Rep. Carlos Gimenez (R-FL): “Thank you President Trump for your leadership & resolve and to our brave service members who have conducted a stellar military operation.”

Ukraine (Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha): Ukraine defends “the right of nations to live freely, free of dictatorship, oppression and human rights violations.” Accused Maduro’s government of violating “all such principles in every respect.”

(Note: Ukraine’s support is strategically dangerous for them—see Part 8.)

Cautious/Hedging:

Germany (Chancellor Friedrich Merz): “Maduro has led his country into ruin” and the U.S. intervention is “complex and requires careful consideration.” “The objective now is an orderly transition to an elected government.”

France (President Emmanuel Macron): The Venezuelan people could “only rejoice” at the end of Maduro’s dictatorship. Did not address U.S. approach. (Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot earlier wrote the military operation “violates the principle of non-resort to force that underpins international law.”)

United Kingdom (Prime Minister Keir Starmer): “I will want to talk to the president” to “establish the facts.” Later wrote the U.K. sheds “no tears” about the end of Maduro’s regime and “will discuss the evolving situation with US counterparts.”

The pattern:

  • Authoritarian governments (Russia, Iran, Cuba) condemned

  • Democratic left-leaning governments (Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Chile) condemned forcefully

  • Right-wing populists (Milei, Gimenez) celebrated

  • European democracies hedged, urged restraint, called for “respect for international law”

  • Ukraine supported (dangerously undermining their own legal position)

The divide isn’t left vs. right. It’s authoritarians who invade vs. democracies that respect sovereignty—except the United States just became the invader.

THE PRECEDENT: OCCUPATION WITHOUT EXIT

If Trump’s Venezuela occupation succeeds—or even if it simply continues without consequence—the precedent established is devastating:

The President can:

  1. Designate a foreign leader as criminal/terrorist unilaterally

  2. Issue arrest warrant through DOJ

  3. Build military force without Congressional approval

  4. Invade sovereign nation to “execute warrant”

  5. Frame invasion as “law enforcement” to bypass War Powers

  6. Announce U.S. will “run the country” indefinitely

  7. Extract natural resources as explicit objective

  8. Face no Congressional constraint or judicial review

This applies to ANY country:

  • Iran (oil reserves, existing “terrorist” designations)

  • Cuba (strategic location, “communist dictatorship”)

  • Nicaragua (canal route, leftist government)

  • Bolivia (lithium for batteries, resource nationalism)

If Congress does nothing—and so far, Congress has done nothing—then Trump establishes that presidents can invade countries, capture leaders, and announce open-ended occupations without any legislative check.

This is the imperial presidency the Founders feared and the Constitution was designed to prevent.

Trump just demonstrated it works.

Trump has no answers to these questions.

He announced an occupation with no plan, no timeline, and no clarity about who governs or how this ends.

CONCLUSION: THE OCCUPATION NO ONE PLANNED

On December 10, I published “The Threat Matrix,” predicting Trump would use the Iraq WMD playbook to justify Venezuela regime change.

On December 16, Trump confessed the objective: “Return to the United States of America all of the Oil, Land, and other Assets.”

On January 3, Trump invaded Venezuela, captured Maduro, and announced: “We are going to run the country.”

Every element I documented has now manifested. But the story doesn’t end with Maduro’s capture—it begins there.

Trump says the United States will occupy Venezuela indefinitely. But:

  • There’s no U.S. military presence in the country

  • The Venezuelan government rejects U.S. control

  • The opposition leader Trump won’t support

  • No plan exists for governance

  • No timeline for withdrawal

  • No Congressional authorization

  • No international legitimacy

The only clear objective: oil extraction.

The Panama precedent offered a model—remove dictator, restore democracy, withdraw. Trump has abandoned that model for something more like Gaza: remove leadership, announce control, create power vacuum, trigger humanitarian crisis, face no consequences, extract resources, extend occupation indefinitely.

The occupation is beginning. But nobody knows who’s actually in charge, how this ends, or what happens when Venezuelans resist American control of their country.

Trump watched the raid “like a television show.” Now he’s trying to run a country like a reality TV production—with no script, no plan, and no understanding of what comes next.

The cameras are still rolling. The occupation has begun. And Trump just made every other question secondary to this one:

What happens when an American president invades a sovereign nation, announces he’ll “run the country,” has no plan to do so, and faces no consequences?

We’re about to find out.
