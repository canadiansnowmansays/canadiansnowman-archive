The Penalty Thrown Out

Part 23: Electoral Victory Eliminates Consequences (2024-2025)

February 24, 2026

A New York State appeals court to hear oral arguments in Donald J. Trump’s civil fraud case in the fall, and a decision could come before the end of the year. NYT

Trump was ordered to pay $454 million. With interest accruing daily, the amount quickly climbed above $500 million. The judgment became official, and the clock started ticking.

Under New York law, Trump couldn’t appeal without posting a bond or paying the full judgment. Paperwork was filed on February 23, 2024, giving Trump 30 days to pay or secure a bond.

The question became: Could Trump, who claimed to be worth billions, actually come up with half a billion dollars? And would AG Letitia James actually seize his properties if he couldn’t?

The $100 Million Offer

Trump’s lawyers initially offered to post just a $100 million bond, arguing that posting the full $454 million would cause “irreparable harm.”

They claimed the ban on borrowing from New York banks (one of Judge Engoron’s penalties) made it impossible to secure such a large bond. They argued Trump’s vast real estate holdings and the independent monitor already overseeing the company were sufficient security.

Judge Anil Singh of the appeals court rejected that argument. Trump must post a bond covering the full amount.

However, Singh did pause the three-year ban on Trump seeking loans from New York banks—specifically to help Trump secure the necessary bond.

The irony was stark: The court had to temporarily lift one of Trump’s penalties so he could comply with another penalty.

Can Trump Actually Afford It?

Trump claimed he was worth several billion dollars and testified during trial that he had about $400 million in cash. But he also said posting the full bond would be impossible without selling properties at “fire sale” prices.

AG James said publicly she would seek to seize Trump’s assets if he couldn’t pay. The drama built: Would Trump Tower be seized? 40 Wall Street? His golf courses?

For a moment, it seemed like we might finally see Trump’s assets auctioned off to satisfy a judgment. Real accountability, real consequences, real financial pain.

Of course not. Trump eventually secured the bond and filed his appeal. No properties were seized. No fire sales occurred.

In all, Trump faced at least $543.4 million in personal legal liabilities from this ruling and two other civil court judgments (the E. Jean Carroll verdicts totaling $88.3 million).

The Formal Appeal

Trump formally appealed the judgment in July 2024, filing a 95-page brief with New York’s Appellate Division asking them to overturn Judge Engoron’s ruling entirely.

The appeal repeated arguments Trump’s lawyers made throughout:

  • The AG’s case focused on transactions outside the statute of limitations

  • The relevant law was misapplied

  • The deals were profitable with no victims

  • The penalty violated the Eighth Amendment’s prohibition on excessive fines

“It violates centuries of New York case law,” Trump’s lawyers wrote, arguing the Attorney General shouldn’t be able to sue over alleged violations that are “purely private in nature.”

Defense lawyers argued: “There were no victims and no losses.”

After the judgment against Mr. Trump was handed down, Attorney General Letitia James said that “there cannot be different rules for different people in this country, and former presidents are no exception.”Credit...Jefferson Siegel for The New York Times

James’s office responded by noting Trump’s lawyers had already been sanctioned and fined for making these same arguments during trial.

“Once again, the defendants are raising arguments that they were already sanctioned and fined for,” a spokesperson said. “We won this case based on the facts and the law, and we are confident we will prevail on appeal.”

The Appellate Division Hears Arguments

The Appellate Division heard oral arguments in September 2024. Observers noted the judges appeared receptive to possibly reducing or reversing the judgment.

Then in November 2024, Trump won the presidential election. Everything changed.

Trump would soon be president again, with all the protections and powers that office provides. The case took on new urgency—or perhaps lost urgency, depending on your perspective.

The Appeals Court Decision: August 2025

On August 21, 2025, the Appellate Division’s First Department issued its ruling.

The court’s 323-page opinion was complex, with all five judges agreeing the penalty should be thrown out, but for different reasons:

  • Two judges said Trump was properly held liable for fraud, but the $454 million fine was excessive and violated the Eighth Amendment’s prohibition on excessive fines.

  • Two judges said the trial court was wrong to find Trump committed fraud at all and the case should be retried—but they joined the decision “with great reluctance” to allow the case to proceed to the state’s highest court.

  • One judge said Attorney General James shouldn’t have brought the case in the first place.

But crucially: The court upheld Engoron’s finding that Trump committed business fraud by overstating asset values. The fraud finding stands.

The court also upheld the non-monetary sanctions, calling them “well crafted to curb defendants’ business culture.” Trump and his sons still face business restrictions and bans.

What the court threw out was the half-billion-dollar financial penalty, ruling it was unconstitutionally excessive.

Trump’s “Total Victory”

US President Donald Trump expressed his delight on social media at having the huge fine quashed. Photo: AAP

Trump declared “TOTAL VICTORY” on social media: “I greatly respect the fact that the Court had the Courage to throw out this unlawful and disgraceful Decision that was hurting Business all throughout New York State. The amount, including Interest and Penalties, was over $550 Million Dollars. It was a Political Witch Hunt.”

Eric Trump posted: “Total victory in the sham NY Attorney General case!!! After 5 years of hell, justice prevailed!”

But was it really a “total victory”?

The appeals court confirmed Trump committed fraud—systematic, decade-long fraud. The business restrictions remain. Trump’s reputation as a fraudster is now established in court findings at two levels of New York’s judiciary.

What Trump won was escape from the $454 million penalty—not because he didn’t commit fraud (the court confirmed he did), but because the penalty was deemed excessive under the Constitution.

AG James Fights Back

Attorney General James wasn’t done. On September 4, 2025, her office filed notice of appeal to the New York Court of Appeals, the state’s highest court.

She’s seeking to reinstate both the fraud liability finding and the monetary penalty.

That appeal is pending. It will take months or years to resolve. Meanwhile, Trump operates his businesses and serves as president.

What Accountability Remains?

Here’s where we stand as of late 2025:

The fraud is established: Two levels of New York courts found Trump committed systematic business fraud for more than a decade.

The monetary penalty is gone: The $454 million judgment was thrown out as constitutionally excessive.

The business restrictions remain: Trump and his sons still face bans on serving as officers in New York companies, though Trump is now president.

The independent monitor remains: Barbara S. Jones continues to oversee the Trump Organization.

The case continues: AG James has appealed to seek reinstatement of the penalty.

Trump is president: With all the powers, protections, and leverage that office provides.

So what accountability has Trump actually faced for decades of fraud?

  • He posted a bond while appealing (which he got back when the penalty was thrown out)

  • He has some business restrictions, but he’s president now anyway

  • He has an independent monitor, but the organization continues operating

  • His reputation as a fraudster is confirmed, but his supporters don’t care

  • He might eventually face a reinstated penalty if James wins her appeal—years from now

In practical terms: Trump has faced virtually no consequences for systematic fraud spanning decades that enriched him and his family by hundreds of millions of dollars.

The Constitutional Escape Hatch

The Eighth Amendment’s prohibition on “excessive fines” became Trump’s escape hatch. The appeals court ruled that $454 million was too much, even for fraud this massive and persistent.

But consider what this means: If the penalty for massive, decade-long fraud is deemed “excessive,” what penalty would be appropriate? $100 million? $50 million? A slap on the wrist?

Trump committed fraud that earned him $354 million according to Judge Engoron. If a penalty greater than $454 million is “excessive,” then Trump keeps much of his ill-gotten gains.

This creates perverse incentives: Commit fraud at a massive enough scale, and any punishment proportional to the crime will be deemed excessive and thrown out.

Still Operating, Still President

The most striking fact: Despite being found liable for systematic fraud by two levels of courts, Trump’s businesses continue operating. He continues making deals. And he’s president of the United States.

No assets were seized. No businesses were shuttered. The Trump Organization continues under independent monitoring, but it’s still Trump’s company, still making money, still operating as if the fraud findings don’t matter.

And Trump himself? He’s in the White House, wielding presidential power, conducting foreign policy, making decisions that affect millions—all while being an adjudicated fraudster.

What This Case Reveals

The Trump Organization fraud case reveals several troubling truths:

Fraud pays: Trump enriched himself by hundreds of millions through systematic fraud. He’s been caught, found liable, and... he keeps most of it.

Appeals work: With enough money for lawyers and enough willingness to delay, even clear-cut liability can be overturned or reduced on technical grounds.

Presidential power shields: Trump’s election victory changed everything about this case. The urgency evaporated. The leverage shifted.

The system breaks down at scale: When fraud is big enough, when the defendant is powerful enough, when the delays are long enough, accountability becomes nearly impossible.

The Incomplete Victory

AG James did her job brilliantly. She investigated thoroughly, brought a meticulous case, won at trial, secured a massive judgment.

Judge Engoron did his job. He examined evidence carefully, issued detailed findings, imposed significant penalties.

But the system ultimately failed to hold Trump fully accountable. The penalties were thrown out. Trump continued operating. He won the presidency. The case drags on.

What lesson does this teach? That if you commit fraud at massive enough scale, for long enough, while rich and powerful enough, you can escape real accountability. You might face some restrictions. You might be declared a fraudster. But you won’t lose your wealth, and you can still become president.

The Two Realities

There are now two established realities about Trump and fraud:

Legal reality: Trump committed systematic business fraud for more than a decade. This is established fact in multiple court rulings.

Political reality: Trump’s supporters don’t care. He was elected president anyway. The fraud findings don’t hurt him politically.

This disconnect—between what courts find and what voters accept—is perhaps more troubling than the fraud itself. It suggests that for politically powerful figures, legal liability has become disconnected from real-world consequences.

The Message Sent

The Trump Organization fraud case sends a clear message to wealthy, powerful people considering fraud:

Go ahead. Lie to banks. Inflate asset values. Commit systematic fraud for decades. If caught:

  1. Hire good lawyers

  2. Delay as long as possible

  3. Appeal everything

  4. Run for office

  5. Win, and the penalties might vanish

Even if the fraud finding stands, even if courts confirm you’re a fraudster, you can still operate your businesses, maintain your wealth, and hold the highest office in the land.

That’s not accountability. That’s a roadmap for escaping it.

What Remains

While Trump escaped the $454 million penalty, some things remain:

The historical record: Trump is an adjudicated fraudster. Courts at two levels confirmed it. That can’t be erased.

The business restrictions: At least for now, Trump and his sons face limitations on their business activities in New York.

The ongoing appeal: James is fighting to reinstate the penalty. If she wins at the state’s highest court, Trump might eventually face financial consequences—though by then, he’ll have been president for years.

The monitor: Barbara S. Jones continues watching the Trump Organization, though how effective that oversight is remains unclear.

But Trump himself? He’s president, his businesses operate, his wealth remains intact, and the fraud that should have ended his career is just another thing he got away with.

From the $454 million fraud judgment to the 34 felony convictions, the pattern remains unchanged: Trump faces legal liability, declares victory, delays until circumstances shift in his favor, and pays nothing. The man who never pays has turned accountability evasion into an art form—and American institutions have yet to find an answer.

Conclusion: The Criminal Conviction and Fraud Judgment Sections

This concludes the criminal conviction and fraud judgment sections of the Trump Accountability Project. We’ve documented Trump’s unprecedented criminal conviction on 34 felony counts—and his escape from any punishment through an unconditional discharge. We’ve examined the massive Trump Organization fraud findings—and the throwing out of the $454 million penalty on constitutional grounds.

The pattern across these sections is consistent:

  1. Engage in illegal, unethical, or socially destructive behavior

  2. Use wealth to hire sophisticated lawyers

  3. Deny everything and attack investigators

  4. Delay accountability through litigation

  5. Leverage political position to avoid consequences

  6. Face no meaningful punishment

  7. Claim vindication

  8. Continue as if nothing happened

From falsifying business records to systematic fraud spanning decades, Trump has mastered the art of legal liability without real accountability.

American institutions have consistently failed to hold Trump accountable. Criminal convictions provide symbolic victories without deterrence. Civil fraud findings establish wrongdoing without financial consequences. The wealthy enjoy protections unavailable to ordinary citizens.

Trump escaped the $454 million fraud penalty through appellate maneuvering and constitutional technicalities. The fraud findings stand, but the financial consequences vanished. This pattern of evading accountability extends beyond criminal and civil cases. During his first term as president, Trump systematically violated the Constitution's explicit prohibitions on presidential self-dealing—and once again faced zero consequences.
