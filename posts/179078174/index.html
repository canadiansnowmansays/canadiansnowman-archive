The Intelligence Operation

Part 2: The Protection Network Fractures

November 18, 2025

Former Trump insider Lev Parnas reveals the intelligence operation behind the Epstein files (Coffee & Tea podcast, Nov 15, 2025)

PART II: THE PROTECTION NETWORK FRACTURES

IV. THE WEAK ZEBRAS

Few figures better illustrate the protection network’s collapse than Dan Bongino. The former Secret Service agent turned conservative media personality built an entire career—and made millions of dollars—promising to expose government corruption and “unleash the Epstein files.” His audience tuned in daily for years to hear him promise that when he finally got access to power, he would tear down the corrupt establishment and reveal the truth about Epstein’s operation.

Then Trump named him Deputy Director of the FBI.

And the moment the Epstein files actually started dropping, Bongino went silent on the one issue he’d built his career promising to expose. While he’s continued feuding with Trump critics like Representative Thomas Massie and making public appearances on other topics, his signature promise to “unleash the Epstein files” has vanished from his commentary. The man who spent years telling his audience he’d expose the truth the moment he got access to power now has that access—and won’t discuss what he’s learned.

Parnas described the situation on the podcast: “This is a man that literally, every day, 24 hours a day, made a career and a life talking about the corruption and unleashing the Epstein files. And with all these drops and everything... you don’t even hear his name. You don’t see him at the White House. You see Kash Patel... You haven’t seen [Bongino] at the White House. You haven’t seen him stand behind Trump in the Oval.”

Host Dean Blundell described Bongino as “that weak kind of zebra on the edge of the herd”—vulnerable because “he spent years and made millions saying to everybody that he knows the Epstein files are this bad, he’s going to get to the bottom of it. He gets labeled and named the deputy director of the FBI, and as soon as we find out that Donald Trump isn’t just in it, he IS the Epstein files... Dan Bongino goes radio silent.”

The silence is strategic. Bongino understands, according to Blundell’s analysis, that “this train is starting to come to a slow stop.” He sees that some people will get off that train by denouncing Trump and preserving their reputation. Those who don’t face two outcomes: criminal liability for covering up evidence, and permanent destruction of their credibility.

Parnas has a plan: “When I go to DC, I’m going to confront Bongino. We’re going to basically do some interesting stuff to make sure... if he’s not ready to come out, we’re gonna put him on the spot. Just like Laura Loomer does to everybody else, we’ll give him a little taste of their own medicine.”

The strategy is to force the “weak zebras” to make a choice publicly: continue protecting Trump and face legal exposure, or break ranks and preserve what’s left of their credibility.

The Trump-Greene Rupture

Perhaps the most visible crack in Trump’s operation emerged this week with his complete break from Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene. For years, Greene served as one of Trump’s most loyal attack dogs—defending him on every issue, attacking his critics, and fundraising off her proximity to him. Trump called her a “future star” and boosted her repeatedly.

Then Greene criticized efforts to block the Epstein files and pushed back on some Trump foreign policy positions.

Trump’s response was immediate and vicious. He withdrew his endorsement, called her a “ranting lunatic,” and tested new insults including “Wacky Marjorie.” When she responded by blaming her stance on the Epstein files for the rupture, Trump escalated further by calling her a “RINO”—the most cutting insult in Trump’s vocabulary, reserved for Republicans he considers traitors.

According to Parnas’s sources, “Trump is doubling down on a campaign to make an example of her—calling members of Congress, threatening primaries, and warning other Republicans: ‘You see what happens if you step out of line.’ He thinks that by humiliating her, he can scare everyone else back into submission.”

But the strategy is backfiring. Instead of isolating Greene and intimidating other Republicans into silence, Trump’s attack is exposing his desperation. Republicans who can “read polling and see what’s coming in 2026 and 2028” are starting to question whether tying themselves to Trump’s cover-up of a sex trafficking and intelligence operation is good for their long-term political survival.

Parnas’s sources indicate that “the vote to release more Epstein-related material could see 50 to 100 Republicans quietly breaking and voting yes.” The reasons vary: “Some out of self-preservation, some out of genuine disgust, and some because they see the writing on the wall and don’t plan on going to prison for Donald Trump.”

That last category is particularly significant. These aren’t Republicans having a moral awakening about sex trafficking. They’re Republicans who understand they could face criminal liability for obstructing justice and covering up evidence of foreign intelligence compromise of American leadership. They’re making the same calculation Bongino is making: get out now, or go down with Trump.

Steve Bannon’s Admission

Nothing illustrates the growing panic better than Steve Bannon’s recent comments to conservative audiences. Bannon, who served as Trump’s chief strategist and remains influential in MAGA media circles, has been telling rooms full of conservatives: “If we lose the midterms and we lose 2028, some in this room are going to prison, myself included.”

As Parnas observed, “That’s not the language of a confident, unstoppable movement. That’s the language of people who know exactly what they did—and know that if the political winds change and the cover is stripped away, they’ll be answering questions under oath, not on a podcast.”

Bannon isn’t predicting Democratic persecution. He’s acknowledging that what he and others did—the obstruction, the cover-up, the protection of a foreign intelligence operation on American soil—constitutes actual crimes that carry actual prison sentences once political protection disappears.

This is the sound of the machine beginning to understand it might not hold.

The Tucker-Fuentes-Heritage Foundation Meltdown

The fracturing isn’t limited to the Epstein files. The same week that Trump broke with Greene and Bongino went silent, Tucker Carlson decided to platform Nick Fuentes—a white nationalist and Holocaust denier who openly praises Hitler and spreads antisemitic conspiracy theories.

The conservative movement immediately began eating itself. The Heritage Foundation’s president backed Tucker’s decision, then had to apologize after facing internal revolt. Board members of Heritage’s antisemitism task force resigned in protest. Ben Shapiro and other mainstream conservatives publicly torched Tucker. Republican senators felt compelled to denounce the interview.

As Parnas assessed it: “This is not a clean, unified movement. It’s a brawl. And all of it traces back to the same sickness: Trump normalized extremists like Fuentes, and now the old guard has no idea how to push them back into the shadows without losing the base Trump trained.”

The connection to the Epstein story is direct: Trump built his movement by unleashing forces the Republican establishment thought they could control. Now, as the Epstein files threaten to expose Trump as a Russian intelligence asset rather than a nationalist strongman, the extremist base he cultivated is turning on anyone who shows the slightest independence.

Parnas’s conclusion: “That’s the foundation rotting.”

The Suspicious Silence

Beyond Bongino and Greene, other Trump allies have gone notably quiet as the Epstein files dropped:

  • Stephen Miller: Trump’s senior advisor and architect of his immigration policies has been “suspiciously silent,” as Parnas noted. “He’s hiding on the Air Force base. He’s scared.”

  • Kash Patel: Despite his role as FBI Director, Patel has been invisible on the Epstein issue—even as he appeared in the Situation Room with Trump regarding other matters.

  • Pam Bondi: After her July announcement closing the investigation, then her May 2025 briefing to Trump, then her November reopening of the investigation as a pretextual cover, Bondi has become the designated scapegoat—set up to take the fall if the scheme collapses.

The pattern is clear: those closest to Trump during his first term, who understand what the intelligence files actually contain, are either going silent or being set up as fall guys. The “weak zebras” are trying to calculate their exit. The true believers are being sacrificed. And the machine that protected everyone for so long is losing its ability to enforce silence.

V. THE WARNING TO ENABLERS

On the November 15 podcast, Lev Parnas delivered a direct message to Trump administration officials and MAGA media figures still participating in the cover-up. The warning was stark and personal:

“Get a lawyer. Preserve all records.”

The reason, as Parnas explained, is that those who continue covering up will face two consequences: complete loss of credibility and serious criminal liability. Parnas warned that those who protect “a massive child pedophile ring that put America’s national security and economy” at risk while helping “hand that country over to Vladimir Putin and Moscow” will be seen as traitors to their country.

This isn’t rhetorical flourish. Parnas is describing actual legal exposure for obstruction of justice, conspiracy to conceal evidence, and potentially more serious charges related to covering up foreign intelligence operations against the United States.

Parnas characterized the choice facing Trump enablers bluntly: “You either bought it, or you thought you could see what you could do in a very short period of time to see if you could outrun the long arm of the law and Justice and democracy.”

The message names specific individuals as vulnerable:

  • Pam Bondi

  • Kash Patel

  • Dan Bongino

  • Stephen Miller

These are the people who “can see” what’s coming. They’re the “weak members of the herd” who understand they face either criminal exposure or permanent reputational destruction—or both.

But Parnas’s warning extends beyond named individuals. It applies to everyone in Congress, media, and the administration who has participated in blocking transparency about the Epstein files. Those who acknowledge their mistake and break with Trump—admitting “the president’s a pedophile,” he’s “running a pedophile protection racket out of the White House,” and “I made a mistake”—might salvage their reputations.

Those who don’t make that choice face two consequences:

  1. Complete loss of credibility—being “labeled a nuclear hypocrite”

  2. Criminal liability—”answering questions under oath, not on a podcast”

The warning is clear: The train is slowing down. The station where people can get off by denouncing Trump and preserving their legal standing is approaching. Those who don’t get off will ride it all the way to prison.

Parnas’s message isn’t just prediction—it’s an ultimatum. The machine that protected Epstein’s network and the foreign intelligence operations connected to it is breaking down. Those who helped Trump obstruct justice and cover up evidence have a narrow window to cooperate before they become defendants rather than witnesses.

The question for each person involved is simple: Will you be remembered as someone who finally told the truth, or as someone who went to prison protecting a pedophile and a foreign intelligence asset?

Parnas’s warning to individual enablers—get a lawyer, preserve records, choose sides—addresses those already inside Trump’s operation. But the larger strategic question remains: How does democracy counter a movement built on personality cult dynamics, especially now that we understand the Epstein files document not just sex crimes but intelligence compromise?

The answer isn’t converting Trump’s base to progressive politics. It’s something more achievable, and perhaps more powerful: making them stay home. Understanding why requires rethinking what victory looks like.
