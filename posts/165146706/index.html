The American Contradiction

How Anti-"WOKE" Politics Betrays the Nation's Foundational Promise

Week 2: Religious Minorities - The Foundation Cases

June 11, 2025

Trump looks resolute, signing executive orders that contradict the Constitution on his desk. One document protects inclusion, the other bans it. Guess which one is actually American? 📜

The Original American Experiment

The American experiment began with religious groups fleeing persecution, yet each successive wave of religious minorities faced systematic discrimination from those who had arrived earlier. From a Canadian perspective, where religious accommodation is constitutionally protected and generally less contentious, the American pattern of religious exclusion appears particularly contradictory to stated American values.

Understanding religious inclusion in America requires recognizing that the very concept of religious freedom was revolutionary in the 18th century. Most European nations maintained established churches and restricted or prohibited other religious practices. The American decision to prohibit religious tests for office and guarantee free exercise represented a radical departure from global norms.

However, this constitutional commitment to religious freedom has been repeatedly tested by the arrival of new religious groups, and the pattern of initial exclusion followed by eventual inclusion has remained remarkably consistent.

The Quakers: Peaceful Radicals

The Quakers, now viewed as quintessentially American in their peaceful principles and commitment to social justice, were banned from multiple colonies, imprisoned, and in some cases executed for their beliefs. Their practice of allowing women to speak in religious meetings was considered dangerous to social order. Their refusal to take oaths or bear arms was seen as a threat to colonial defense.

Pennsylvania, founded by William Penn as a haven for religious tolerance, was viewed with suspicion by neighboring colonies as a dangerous experiment that would weaken colonial unity and attract undesirable elements. The "Holy Experiment" of religious tolerance was criticized as naïve and potentially treasonous.

Yet Pennsylvania became one of the most prosperous and stable colonies, attracting diverse settlers who contributed to economic growth and cultural development. The Quaker commitment to fair dealing with Native Americans initially produced better relations than in other colonies. Their early opposition to slavery provided moral leadership that eventually influenced national policy.

The Quaker experience established a pattern that would repeat throughout American history: initial fear and exclusion of religious minorities, followed by recognition that diversity of belief strengthens rather than weakens community bonds.

Catholics: The Persistent "Other"

Catholic Americans faced systematic discrimination that lasted well into the 20th century, despite representing one of the largest religious groups in the nation. The persistence of anti-Catholic sentiment reveals how deeply religious prejudice can become embedded in American culture.

Colonial and Early National Period

Catholic settlers faced legal restrictions in most colonies. Maryland, founded as a Catholic haven, was the exception rather than the rule. Catholics were often prohibited from holding office, practicing law, or voting. The Quebec Act of 1774, which guaranteed religious freedom to Catholics in former French territories, was cited as one of the "Intolerable Acts" that justified American independence.

The Irish Catholic Wave

The massive Irish immigration of the 1840s-1850s intensified anti-Catholic sentiment. "No Irish Need Apply" signs were common in employment advertisements. The Know-Nothing Party gained significant political power by opposing Catholic immigration and political participation.

Irish Catholics were characterized as racially inferior, culturally backward, and religiously dangerous. They were accused of owing allegiance to a foreign power (the Pope) and therefore incapable of true American citizenship. Their poverty was attributed to moral failings rather than historical circumstances.

Building American Infrastructure

Despite systematic discrimination, Irish Catholics built much of America's 19th-century infrastructure. They constructed railroads, canals, and urban buildings while facing employment discrimination and social exclusion. Their labor was essential to American economic development, yet their contributions were rarely acknowledged.

The Catholic school system developed partly as a response to Protestant dominance in public education. Rather than undermining American values, Catholic schools often produced citizens with strong commitments to community service and social justice.

Political Integration

Catholic political participation gradually increased despite persistent prejudice. Catholic Americans served with distinction in the Civil War, demonstrating their patriotic commitment. However, anti-Catholic sentiment remained strong enough to affect the 1928 presidential election, when Al Smith's Catholicism was a significant campaign issue.

John F. Kennedy's 1960 presidential campaign finally broke the Catholic barrier, but only after he explicitly addressed concerns about Catholic loyalty to America. His famous speech to Protestant ministers in Houston demonstrated how deeply anti-Catholic sentiment had penetrated American political culture.

Mormons: The American-Born "Foreign" Religion

The Mormon experience provides a unique case study because this was a religion founded in America by Americans, yet it faced perhaps the most systematic persecution of any religious group in American history.

Origins and Early Persecution

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, founded by Joseph Smith in 1830, immediately faced violent opposition. Smith was killed by a mob in Illinois in 1844. Mormons were driven from Missouri by state-sanctioned violence. The state of Illinois was unable to protect Mormon communities from systematic harassment.

The Great Migration

Brigham Young's decision to lead Mormons to the Utah Territory represented one of the largest religious migrations in American history. The journey itself demonstrated remarkable organizational capability and community commitment. The establishment of Salt Lake City and surrounding communities showed impressive economic and social development.

Federal Conflict

The Utah War of 1857-1858 saw federal troops marching against American citizens because of their religious beliefs. The conflict arose partly from genuine concerns about Mormon political independence, but also from religious prejudice and misinformation about Mormon practices.

The long delay in Utah statehood (not achieved until 1896) resulted primarily from religious discrimination. Utah had the population and economic development to qualify for statehood much earlier, but Mormon religious practices, particularly polygamy, were considered incompatible with American values.

Economic and Social Contributions

Despite systematic persecution, Mormon communities developed innovative approaches to agriculture, irrigation, and community organization. The Mormon emphasis on education, economic cooperation, and social welfare created stable, prosperous communities in challenging environments.

Mormon pioneers developed much of the American West, establishing trade routes, agricultural techniques, and urban centers that benefited the entire region. Their commitment to economic self-sufficiency and community support created models that influenced broader American development.

Contemporary Integration

Today, Mormon Americans are fully integrated into American society, with members serving at the highest levels of government, business, and civic life. The 2012 presidential candidacy of Mitt Romney, while unsuccessful, demonstrated how completely Mormon Americans have been accepted into mainstream American politics.

The transformation from persecuted minority to accepted mainstream represents one of the most complete examples of American religious integration. The same practices once considered dangerous to American values are now viewed as examples of American virtues: strong families, community commitment, educational achievement, and economic success.

Jewish Americans: From Quota to Quota

Jewish Americans experienced multiple waves of discrimination and acceptance, with their treatment often serving as a barometer of American attitudes toward religious and ethnic minorities.

Early Jewish Communities

Colonial Jewish communities were small but generally faced fewer restrictions than in European countries. However, many colonies maintained religious tests for office holding that excluded Jews along with Catholics and other non-Protestants.

19th Century Integration

German Jewish immigrants of the mid-19th century integrated relatively successfully into American economic and social life. Many became successful merchants and professionals, contributing to American economic development while maintaining religious traditions.

Eastern European Immigration

The massive Eastern European Jewish immigration of 1880-1920 faced much greater resistance. These immigrants were often poorer, more religiously observant, and culturally distinct from earlier German Jewish immigrants. They faced employment discrimination, social exclusion, and accusations of being unassimilable.

University Quotas

Elite American universities maintained explicit or implicit quotas limiting Jewish enrollment from the 1920s through the 1960s. Harvard, Yale, and other prestigious institutions restricted Jewish admission to maintain "geographic diversity" and prevent "too much" Jewish representation.

These quotas were justified using arguments about maintaining institutional culture and preventing the dominance of any single group. The language used was remarkably similar to contemporary arguments about diversity programs, but applied in reverse—to maintain exclusion rather than promote inclusion.

Professional Restrictions

Many professions, law firms, and social clubs excluded Jewish Americans through the mid-20th century. Medical schools maintained quotas. Hotels and resorts advertised "restricted clientele" to exclude Jewish guests.

World War II and Aftermath

Jewish American military service during World War II, combined with growing awareness of the Holocaust, significantly changed American attitudes toward Jewish inclusion. The contradiction between fighting fascism abroad while maintaining discrimination at home became increasingly difficult to defend.

The post-war period saw rapid elimination of most formal restrictions on Jewish Americans. University quotas were abandoned, professional barriers fell, and social acceptance increased dramatically.

Contemporary Contributions

Jewish Americans have contributed disproportionately to American achievements in science, arts, business, and public service. Nobel Prize winners, Supreme Court justices, business leaders, and cultural innovators of Jewish background have enriched American society far beyond their percentage of the population.

The transformation from quota restrictions to full acceptance demonstrates how quickly American attitudes can change when exclusion is recognized as counterproductive to American interests.

Muslims: The Contemporary Test

Muslim Americans currently face the same pattern of suspicion and exclusion that previous religious minorities experienced, despite Islam being one of the world's major religions with a long history in America.

Historical Presence

Muslims have been present in America since colonial times, initially through enslaved Africans who maintained Islamic practices despite systematic attempts at forced conversion. Free Muslim immigrants arrived throughout the 19th century from various countries.

Post-9/11 Backlash

The September 11, 2001 attacks created a wave of anti-Muslim sentiment reminiscent of earlier periods of religious persecution. Muslim Americans faced employment discrimination, surveillance, and social exclusion despite the fact that the vast majority condemned the attacks and cooperated with security investigations.

Military Service

Muslim Americans serve in the U.S. military at rates proportional to their population, often facing the same challenges of religious accommodation that previous minorities experienced. Military service has historically been a path to acceptance for religious minorities, and Muslim military service follows this pattern.

Political Participation

Muslim Americans have increasingly participated in American politics, with several serving in Congress and other elected offices. However, they continue to face campaign attacks based on religious identity, similar to those faced by Catholic and Jewish candidates in earlier eras.

Economic Contributions

Muslim Americans have high rates of educational achievement and entrepreneurship, contributing significantly to American economic development. Their professional success follows the pattern of previous religious minorities who overcame initial discrimination through individual achievement and community development.

The Pattern and Its Implications

The religious minority experience in America reveals a consistent pattern:

  1. Initial Exclusion: New religious groups face systematic discrimination justified by claims about American values and social stability.

  2. Economic Contribution: Despite discrimination, religious minorities contribute significantly to American economic and social development.

  3. Military Service: Military service provides a path to acceptance and demonstrates patriotic commitment.

  4. Gradual Integration: Over time, religious minorities become accepted and integrated into mainstream American society.

  5. Historical Amnesia: Once integrated, the history of discrimination is often forgotten or minimized.

This pattern reveals the fundamental contradiction in contemporary anti-"woke" politics. The same political movement that claims to defend religious freedom is often most hostile to religious minorities who are currently experiencing the discrimination that previous minorities overcame.

From a Canadian perspective, where the Charter of Rights and Freedoms explicitly protects religious freedom and multiculturalism, the American pattern of religious exclusion followed by integration appears unnecessarily painful and economically wasteful. The energy spent on excluding religious minorities could be better directed toward including them as full participants in American society.

The religious minority experience also demonstrates that diversity of belief strengthens rather than weakens democratic institutions. American religious freedom has produced not chaos and conflict, but innovation and resilience. The competition of ideas in a free marketplace of religion has generated social movements for justice, educational institutions, and community organizations that have enriched American society.

Conclusion

The religious minority experience reveals America's constitutional framework at work: initial prejudice giving way to recognition that diversity of belief strengthens democratic institutions. Each group faced identical arguments about incompatible values and divided loyalties, yet each group's inclusion enhanced rather than threatened American democracy. From Quaker pacifism to Mormon communitarianism to Jewish intellectualism to Muslim entrepreneurship, religious diversity has consistently enriched American society. The pattern is clear, yet contemporary anti-"woke" politics repeats the same exclusionary arguments against religious minorities that have been proven wrong throughout American history.
