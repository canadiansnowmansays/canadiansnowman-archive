The Iran Crisis

The Crown Prince’s Gambit

Reza Pahlavi’s Bet on Trump

January 13, 2026

Part 4 of The Iran Crisis Series

Sunday, January 11, 2026. Reza Pahlavi, the 65-year-old son of Iran’s last shah, appeared on Fox News’ “Sunday Morning Futures” to make a direct plea to President Donald Trump.

“They know that you’re not going to throw them under the bus, as has happened before,” Pahlavi told Trump, addressing him directly through the camera. “This is why they are empowered—by the hope that you have their back.”

Host Maria Bartiromo pressed the question that had been building all week: Did Pahlavi want U.S. forces to “take out” Iran’s Supreme Leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei?

Pahlavi didn’t hesitate: “The people of Iran have responded and reacted positively to a promise of intervention.”

Then came the line that would define his role in Iran’s crisis: “We need to cut the snake’s head off for good so it can no longer be a threat to Iranian interests, to American interests, to regional interests. And the only solution is to make sure this regime goes down for good and the Iranian people can liberate themselves.”

It was an extraordinary moment: An exiled crown prince, whose father had been deposed in the 1979 Islamic Revolution, was publicly asking the American president to use military force to overthrow Iran’s government—and claiming he spoke for the Iranian people.

The question hanging over everything: Was Pahlavi a genuine democratic leader channeling popular will? Or an opportunist betting that Trump’s Venezuela operation created a pathway back to power?

Who Is Reza Pahlavi?

Born in Tehran in 1960, Reza Pahlavi was once the crown prince of Iran. His father, Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, ruled Iran from 1941 until the 1979 Islamic Revolution forced the family into exile. Reza was 17 when revolution erupted. He’s now 65, having spent 46 years—most of his life—living in exile, primarily in the United States.

For decades, Pahlavi was dismissed as a relic, a pretender to a throne that no longer existed. Many Iranians despised the Pahlavi dynasty for its repression, corruption, and subservience to Western powers. The Shah’s secret police (SAVAK) had tortured and killed thousands. The monarchy’s opulence while ordinary Iranians struggled economically helped fuel the 1979 revolution.

The idea of restoring the monarchy seemed absurd to most observers of Iranian politics. Every major protest movement since 1979—the 2009 Green Movement, the 2022 “Women, Life, Freedom” uprising—had been notable for avoiding monarchist symbolism. Iranians wanted freedom from theocracy, but they didn’t want a return to monarchy.

That began changing in recent years.

The Unexpected Return of Monarchist Sentiment

Something shifted in Iran between 2022 and 2025. Videos from protests increasingly showed demonstrators carrying the pre-1979 Iranian flag—the monarchy’s flag, with its lion and sun emblem. Chants of “Long live the Shah” began appearing. Graffiti praising the Pahlavi dynasty spread through cities.

The New York Times noted: “Mr. Pahlavi, 65, has long been a divisive figure among Iran’s opposition, many of whom disdained the idea of restoring a monarchy that Iranians ousted because of its repressive rule. But he appears to have gained a base of support inside Iran in recent years, according to some analysts.”

But analysts caution that monarchist chants may reflect anti-regime sentiment more than genuine pro-Pahlavi conviction. When protesters shout “Long live the Shah,” they might mean:

  • We want ANY alternative to the Islamic Republic

  • We want the economic competence of pre-1979 Iran

  • We want Persian nationalism over Islamic ideology

  • We’re deliberately choosing the symbol most offensive to the current regime

As one Iranian political analyst (speaking anonymously) told Western researchers: “The monarchy represents everything the Islamic Republic hates. Chanting for the Shah is the ultimate rejection. But if you asked these same protesters, ‘Do you actually want Reza Pahlavi as your king?’—many might say no. They want what the monarchy symbolizes: stability, prosperity, nationalism. Not necessarily monarchy itself.”

This ambiguity works in Pahlavi’s favor politically—he can claim broad support while the actual level of personal loyalty remains untested. But it also means his support might evaporate if tested. Crowds chanting his name in defiance of the regime doesn’t necessarily translate to votes in a free election.

Several factors explain the monarchist revival, however genuinely committed:

1. Distance sanitizes history. Forty-six years is long enough for nostalgia to replace memory. Young Iranians—those under 30, who represent a majority of Iran’s population—have no personal experience of the Shah’s repression. They only know the Islamic Republic’s repression. The pre-1979 Iran of their imagination looks better than the Iran they’re living in.

2. Economic competence matters. Whatever the Shah’s political failures, Iran’s economy functioned during his rule. There was inflation, inequality, and corruption—but nothing like the grinding economic collapse of 2018-2025. When you can’t afford bread, authoritarian competence starts looking attractive compared to authoritarian dysfunction.

3. Nationalist appeal. The Pahlavi era represented Persian nationalism and pride. Iran had a strong military, cultural influence, and international respect. The Islamic Republic, by contrast, has isolated Iran internationally and subordinated Persian identity to Islamic ideology. “No to Gaza; no to Lebanon; my life only for Iran”—the protest chant captured this sentiment.

4. Lack of alternatives. Iran’s reformist politicians were crushed or co-opted. The 2009 Green Movement leaders were imprisoned or fled. The 2022 protest leaders were jailed or killed. In the absence of organized democratic opposition, Pahlavi emerged by default as the most visible opposition figure.

5. International legitimacy. Pahlavi speaks fluent English, has connections in Washington, appears on Western media, and presents as a modern democrat rather than monarchist restorationist. For protesters hoping for international support, he’s a figure Western powers might work with.

Whether this monarchist revival represents genuine popular sentiment or desperate grasping for any alternative remains debated. But by January 2026, Pahlavi had become symbolically important in ways he hadn’t been for decades.

Thursday, January 8: The Call to Action

As Iran’s protests intensified, Pahlavi made a crucial decision: He would directly call for mass demonstrations, coordinating with other opposition groups.

Thursday, January 8, Pahlavi joined members of several activist groups in issuing calls for nationwide protests at 8 PM. The coordination was unprecedented—Kurdish groups, women’s rights activists, labor organizations, and the exiled crown prince all aligned around a single moment.

The Times reported this “helped accelerate the movement. Huge crowds took to the streets of large metropolises and marginalized rural provinces alike.”

This was Pahlavi’s gambit. By putting his name and legitimacy behind specific protest dates and times, he was betting:

  • He could actually mobilize crowds (proving influence)

  • The regime wouldn’t be able to crush the protests (proving vulnerability)

  • International attention would protect protesters (proving foreign support)

  • Trump might actually intervene (proving the Venezuela lesson applied to Iran)

If these bets paid off, Pahlavi would be positioned as the face of Iran’s democratic transition. If they failed, he’d be exposed as irrelevant, and protesters who’d taken his advice would pay with their lives.

Thursday night’s protests were massive. Tehran, Mashhad, Bushehr, Shiraz, Isfahan, Karaj—demonstrations erupted across all 31 provinces. Crowds chanted “This is the final battle. Pahlavi will return.”

His first bet had paid off: He could mobilize people.

Then the regime cut the internet and began the massacre.

Friday-Saturday: The Massacre Unfolds

As documented in Part 3 of this series, Iran’s government responded to Thursday’s protests with overwhelming violence. By Sunday, human rights groups estimated hundreds killed, possibly thousands. Bodies stacked at forensics centers. Hospitals overwhelmed. More than 10,000 arrested.

Pahlavi faced a crucial test: How would he respond when the regime chose mass violence over accommodation?

In a video message Saturday, January 10, he doubled down. He asked Iranians to continue taking to streets over the following two days and called for workers in key sectors—especially oil and gas—to begin a nationwide strike.

This was a high-risk move. People were dying. The internet was down. Security forces were using military weapons against civilians. And Pahlavi was asking protesters to stay in the streets and escalate by striking in critical industries.

The message: Don’t give up. Keep pushing. Help is coming.

But was it?

Sunday Morning: The Direct Appeal to Trump

Sunday morning, Pahlavi appeared on Fox News for the interview that would define his role in the crisis.

Maria Bartiromo introduced him by noting: “Several Iranian rights groups on Sunday said the death toll, after two weeks of protests, had reached the hundreds.”

Then she gave Pahlavi the platform to make his case directly to Trump.

The appeal was explicit about Venezuela:

“Since the U.S. operation to capture Venezuela’s president, Nicolás Maduro,” The Times noted in its coverage, “Mr. Trump has indicated several times that he was considering striking Iran’s leadership.”

Pahlavi understood perfectly that Trump’s Venezuela precedent had emboldened Iranian protesters. His entire pitch rested on that precedent continuing.

The ask was direct:

“They know that you’re not going to throw them under the bus, as has happened before. This is why they are empowered—by the hope that you have their back.”

Translation: Remember Obama in 2009? He promised support to Iran’s Green Movement but didn’t deliver. Protesters were massacred. Don’t repeat that mistake.

“President Trump, as the leader of the free world, has closely observed your indescribable bravery and has declared that he is ready to help you,” Pahlavi said in the interview, addressing Iranian protesters.

The call for regime change was unambiguous:

Asked if he wanted U.S. to “take out Khamenei,” Pahlavi responded: “The people of Iran have responded and reacted positively to a promise of intervention.”

Then: “We need to cut the snake’s head off for good so it can no longer be a threat to Iranian interests, to American interests, to regional interests. And the only solution is to make sure this regime goes down for good and the Iranian people can liberate themselves.”

This was not a call for sanctions. Not a call for diplomatic pressure. This was a direct request for U.S. military intervention to decapitate Iran’s government.

Trump’s Cool Response

But Trump’s response was notably lukewarm.

Asked Thursday—before Pahlavi’s Sunday interview—whether he would meet with Pahlavi, Trump said: “He seems like a nice person, but I’m not sure that it would be appropriate at this point to do that as president.”

Think about what Trump didn’t say:

  • He didn’t call Pahlavi “a great leader”

  • He didn’t say “I’ll definitely meet with him”

  • He didn’t endorse Pahlavi as Iran’s future leader

  • He didn’t commit to supporting a Pahlavi restoration

Instead: “Seems like a nice person” but “not sure” about meeting. That’s diplomatic coldness dressed as courtesy.

The contrast with Trump’s treatment of other foreign leaders is stark. When Trump likes someone, he says so loudly and repeatedly. He praised Argentina’s Milei extensively. He called Saudi Arabia’s MBS “a friend.” He literally invited foreign leaders to Mar-a-Lago within days of taking office.

But Pahlavi? “Nice person.” “Not sure.”

Why the distance?

Trump’s Calculation: Why He’s Wary of Pahlavi

Trump has several good reasons to keep Pahlavi at arm’s length, despite Pahlavi’s public appeals:

1. Legitimacy questions. Trump doesn’t want to be seen as installing a monarch. Even Trump—who loves strongmen and has authoritarian instincts—understands that “helping democracy” plays better politically than “restoring monarchy.” If he backs Pahlavi explicitly, it becomes “Trump wants to make this guy king.”

2. Unknown popular support. Yes, crowds chant “Long live the Shah.” But how much of Iran’s population actually wants monarchy restored? Trump learned in Venezuela that capturing a president doesn’t automatically mean controlling a country. Backing Pahlavi could trap him supporting an unpopular figure.

3. The Iraq lesson. The U.S. invasion of Iraq in 2003 installed leaders (like Ahmed Chalabi) who had American support but limited Iraqi legitimacy. The result was disaster. Trump has claimed he always opposed the Iraq War. He won’t want to repeat its mistakes.

4. International complications. Explicitly backing monarchist restoration would alienate potential allies. European partners who’ve been horrified by Trump’s Venezuela operation would find Iranian monarchy restoration even worse. Even conservative Arab monarchies might worry about precedent—if America can restore one monarchy, can it depose another?

5. Strategic flexibility. By not committing to Pahlavi, Trump keeps options open. Maybe he strikes a deal with reformist elements in Iran’s government. Maybe a different opposition leader emerges. Maybe he doesn’t intervene at all. Committing to Pahlavi closes doors.

6. Pahlavi’s political baggage. The Shah’s legacy is deeply controversial, even among Iranians who hate the Islamic Republic. SAVAK’s torture, the corruption, the Western subservience—these aren’t ancient history. Trump doesn’t want to own that baggage.

So Trump threads a needle: Threaten intervention (keeping protesters hopeful), but don’t endorse Pahlavi (keeping options open).

The Monarchist Tension: Democracy or Autocracy?

This brings us to the fundamental tension in Pahlavi’s position.

In his Washington Post op-ed published January 6, Pahlavi positioned himself as “not as ruler-in-waiting, but as steward of national transition.” He promised: “Continuity of institutions where possible.” He proposed a referendum to determine Iran’s future government form—implying Iranians could choose whether to restore monarchy or establish a republic.

This is the democratic Pahlavi: A transitional figure who’ll help Iran become a democracy, then step aside if that’s what people want.

But many of Pahlavi’s supporters want something different: absolute monarchy restored, period. They don’t want democracy—they want a strong leader who can restore order, crush the clerics, and return Iran to pre-1979 glory.

The Atlantic’s analysis noted this ambiguity: “Pahlavi says goal is ‘help Iran transition to democracy’ but many supporters want ‘absolute autocracy’ restored. This ambiguity both helps (broad appeal) and hurts (elite hesitation).”

This is Pahlavi’s impossible position: He needs to be democratic enough to win Western support and appeal to progressive Iranians. But he needs to be monarchist enough to mobilize his base and differentiate himself from other opposition figures.

The tension can’t be fully resolved. Either he’s genuinely committed to democracy (in which case he might lose in a free election), or he’s planning to consolidate power (in which case he’s lying about democratic commitment).

Trump, watching from Washington, has to consider: If we help Pahlavi take power, what will Iran actually get? Another democratic ally? Or another authoritarian state, just with different leadership?

The Venezuela Precedent: What Iranians See vs. What Trump Sees

Pahlavi’s entire strategy rests on the Venezuela precedent emboldening both protesters and Trump. But there’s an uncomfortable reality: Venezuela might argue against intervention, not for it.

What Iranian protesters see in Venezuela:

  • Trump will actually use force to remove authoritarian leaders

  • Military intervention is possible and effective

  • International law won’t stop him

  • This could work for Iran too

What Trump sees in Venezuela:

  • Easy military victory but complicated political aftermath

  • Maduro captured, but who runs Venezuela now? His former VP

  • Oil control not working as planned (companies saying “uninvestable”)

  • Senate pushback (52-47 vote against him)

  • International condemnation

  • No clear end state

If Trump is already having second thoughts about Venezuela—and there’s evidence he is—why would he want a much more complicated version in Iran?

Venezuela: 28 million people, oil-dependent economy, relatively weak military, no nuclear program, no major regional alliances Iran: 80 million people, sophisticated military, potential nuclear weapons program, strategic location, remaining ties to Russia/China

If Venezuela is proving harder than expected, Iran would be exponentially more difficult.

So Pahlavi’s appeal rests on a precedent that might not actually support his case. The more Venezuela goes badly for Trump, the less likely he is to try it again in Iran—regardless of how many Iranians chant monarchist slogans.

Friday, January 9: Khamenei’s Response

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei addressed the nation Friday morning, and he mentioned Pahlavi’s influence explicitly:

“Last night in Tehran and in some other places, a group of vandals came and damaged buildings belonging to their own country just to please the president of the United States.”

Khamenei recognized that Trump’s promises and Venezuela’s precedent were emboldening protesters. But he also sent a message: You’re not fighting for Iran. You’re fighting for America. You’re traitors, not patriots.

The nationalist card is powerful in Iran. Even Iranians who hate the Islamic Republic often resent American interference. Khamenei was gambling that by framing protesters as American puppets, he could peel away nationalist support.

And Pahlavi’s open embrace of Trump—his direct appeal for U.S. military intervention—played into Khamenei’s hands. Every time Pahlavi asks America to “cut the snake’s head off,” Khamenei can point and say: See? They’re working for foreign powers.

This is the trap of exile opposition: To mobilize international support, you must appeal to foreign powers. But appealing to foreign powers undermines domestic legitimacy.

The Critical Question: What Happens If Trump Says No?

Everything Pahlavi has done this week—the Thursday call for protests, the Saturday call for strikes, the Sunday appeal to Trump—rests on one assumption: Trump might actually intervene.

But what if he doesn’t?

What if Trump decides:

  • Iran is too hard

  • The political costs are too high

  • The military risks are too great

  • Venezuela is enough for now

  • Pahlavi doesn’t have sufficient legitimacy

  • Other priorities matter more

Then Pahlavi will have encouraged hundreds or thousands of Iranians to risk their lives for nothing. They went to the streets believing “Trump has their back.” They stayed in streets while security forces massacred them because they thought help was coming.

If help doesn’t come—if Trump is bluffing, or if his threats don’t materialize into action—then Pahlavi’s gambit fails completely.

He’ll be exposed as someone who mobilized people for slaughter without being able to deliver the protection he promised. The monarchist revival will be revealed as built on false hope. And the regime will claim vindication: See what happens when you trust foreign-backed exiles?

This is the enormous risk Pahlavi is taking. He’s betting everything on Trump. And Trump has given him very little reason to believe that bet will pay off.

What Trump Actually Said Sunday

After Pahlavi’s Fox News interview Sunday morning, Trump spoke to reporters on Air Force One Sunday evening. Asked about Iran, he said:

“We’re looking at it very seriously, the military’s looking at it. And we’re looking at some very strong options.”

He said he’d been briefed on military strike options. He said he was getting “hourly reports” on the crackdown. He said Iran’s leaders had crossed a red line: “It looks like it. There seems to be some people killed who weren’t supposed to be killed.”

But he also said: Iran’s leaders “called him Saturday” and wanted to negotiate. “A meeting is being set up, but we may have to act because of what’s happening.”

Notice what this implies: Trump is considering both military strikes AND negotiations. He’s keeping all options on the table. He’s not committed to regime change. He’s not backing Pahlavi. He’s applying pressure while leaving himself maximum flexibility.

This is not the response Pahlavi was hoping for. Pahlavi wants unambiguous commitment to regime change. Trump is offering ambiguous threats that could lead to anything—or nothing.

Breaking: Trump's Promise of Help (January 13, 2026)

Two major developments validate Pahlavi's entire strategy—and dramatically raise the stakes.

First, The New York Times reported at 12:01 PM ET that Iranian government officials acknowledged the massacre's true scale: A senior health ministry official told The Times that approximately 3,000 people have been killed. Another official confirmed seeing internal reports of "at least 3,000 dead," with the toll expected to climb. U.S. intelligence conservatively estimates more than 600 protesters killed, noting this crackdown is "far more violent than those in 2022 or other recent uprisings."

If confirmed, this would be among the worst death tolls in recent Iranian history.

Second, President Trump posted on Truth Social what Pahlavi had been desperately hoping to hear:

"Iranian Patriots, KEEP PROTESTING — TAKE OVER YOUR INSTITUTIONS!!! Save the names of the killers and abusers. They will pay a big price. I have cancelled all meetings with Iranian Officials until the senseless killing of protesters STOPS. HELP IS ON ITS WAY. MIGA!!!"

Three critical developments:

1. "HELP IS ON ITS WAY" - This is the explicit commitment Pahlavi solicited in his Fox News interview. Trump isn't hedging. He's promising intervention. With 3,000 dead, the urgency is undeniable.

2. Cancelled meetings - Trump abandoned the diplomatic track entirely. No more Oman channel, no more negotiations, no more ambiguity. He chose Pahlavi's path: confrontation over accommodation.

3. "Save the names" - Trump is explicitly telling protesters to document regime crimes for future prosecution. This suggests he envisions post-regime accountability - exactly what Pahlavi has been advocating.

Pahlavi's bet suddenly looks less impossible. Trump didn't just fail to distance himself from the crown prince - he adopted Pahlavi's entire framing. "Take over your institutions" echoes Pahlavi's call for protesters to seize control. "Help is on its way" validates the hope Pahlavi offered.

But the critical question remains: What does "help" mean? Military strikes? Arms shipments? Intelligence support? Financial backing? Trump's statement is deliberately ambiguous - creating hope without committing to specifics.

And with 3,000 already dead, the margin for error has vanished. If Trump's "help" arrives but proves insufficient? If strikes hit regime targets but don't topple the government? If protesters rise up expecting American intervention that never fully materializes?

Then Pahlavi's gambit transforms from brilliant foresight to catastrophic miscalculation - and thousands more Iranians pay the price for believing Trump's promise.

The next 48-72 hours will determine which outcome we're witnessing. But with a death toll potentially reaching 3,000, the stakes have never been higher.

Conclusion: The Exile’s Impossible Bet

Reza Pahlavi has made an extraordinary gamble. He’s positioned himself as the face of Iran’s opposition, coordinated nationwide protests, called for strikes in critical industries, and publicly asked the American president to use military force to overthrow Iran’s government.

If it works—if Trump intervenes, if the regime falls, if Iranians rally around him—he’ll be vindicated. He’ll go from exiled pretender to national leader, possibly restoring his family’s legacy.

But if it fails—if Trump doesn’t intervene, if the regime survives, if Iranians blame him for encouraging a massacre—he’ll be finished as a political figure. Worse, he’ll be responsible for having given false hope to people who died because of it.

The cruel irony is that Pahlavi has almost no control over the outcome. His fate rests entirely on Trump’s decision. And Trump has every reason to be cautious—the Venezuela precedent Pahlavi is counting on might actually argue against repeating the experiment.

So we’re left with questions:

Does Pahlavi genuinely speak for Iranians, or is he an opportunist exploiting their desperation?

Would he actually establish democracy, or would he consolidate a new autocracy?

Will Trump back him, ignore him, or use him as leverage for negotiation?

Most importantly: How many Iranians will die because they believed Pahlavi’s promise that Trump “has their back”?

The answers will determine whether the crown prince’s gambit was brilliant foresight or reckless delusion.

And hundreds—possibly thousands—of Iranians have already paid the price while the world waits to find out.
