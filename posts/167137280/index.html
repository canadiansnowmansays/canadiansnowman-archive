What Last Week's Extreme Weather Tells Us About Our Changing Climate

When it's too hot for a husky at 5:30 AM, you know something's seriously wrong

June 30, 2025

Stephen Lawton
Jun 30, 2025

The Northeast just lived through its most intense heat wave in years. Boston hit 102°F—the hottest June day since 1925. New York reached 100°F for the first time since 2012. But this wasn't just another hot week. It was a glimpse into our climate future.

Are you feeling hot under the collar when thinking about climate change? You're not alone.

Last week's brutal heat wave across the US and Canada wasn't just uncomfortable—it was historic. As someone living in Toronto, I experienced this firsthand. Here I was, looking at my four-year-old husky who usually gets a daily 20-kilometer run, knowing I couldn't safely take her out even at 5:30 AM because the overnight low had only dropped to 27°C (about 81°F)—far too dangerous for any dog, even one with incredible stamina bred for cold weather.

My complaints feel minor when I think about those whose homes have been ravaged by wildfires around the world, but rising temperatures affect us all in our own ways. For years, I've been able to beat the heat by leaving at dawn when temperatures were usually no warmer than 22°C. But last week changed that routine entirely. When even the literal "dog days of summer" become too dangerous for an actual dog—a husky bred for cold weather, no less—you know we've crossed into new territory.

As I watched the temperature records tumble one by one, I couldn't help but think about how perfectly this extreme weather illustrates the climate story that's been unfolding for decades. Let me walk you through what just happened, what the science tells us about where we're heading, and why some people still resist accepting this reality.

A man pours water over his head to cope with record-breaking heat at Times Square, June 24, 2025—the day New York hit 100°F for the first time since 2012. (Photo by Liao Pan/China News Service/VCG via Getty Images)

When the Mercury Soared: Last Week's Record-Breaking Heat

Picture this: It's Tuesday, June 24th, and you're stepping outside in Boston. The thermometer reads 102°F—hotter than any June day since 1925, when your great-grandparents might have been young adults. In New York, Kennedy Airport hit 100°F, a temperature the city hadn't seen since 2012.

This wasn't just a regional phenomenon. The heat dome stretched across North America, affecting over 190 million Americans. Toronto recorded its hottest day in nine years. Humidity made it feel even worse—with dew points in the 70s, many places felt like 110°F or higher.

But here's what made this heat wave particularly telling: it wasn't just breaking records, it was shattering them. Tuesday's heat came on top of 39 new or tied heat records Monday. That's not normal weather variation—that's a climate signal.

Power Transmission lines during high temperatures in Columbia, South Carolina, US on June 24th (Sam Wolfe/Bloomberg vis Getty Images)

When Infrastructure Buckles

The impacts went far beyond personal discomfort. Amtrak had to slow down trains because tracks get dangerous when they exceed 128°F. Con Edison begged New Yorkers to dial back their air conditioning as the electrical grid strained under demand. Across the continent, from New York to South Carolina, power grids faced unprecedented stress as millions cranked up air conditioning to survive the brutal heat.

In Virginia, the heat was so extreme that a drawbridge literally got stuck—expanding metal prevented it from closing properly, requiring the fire department to hose it down with water just to make it functional again. When bridges start malfunctioning because of temperature, you know you're dealing with something beyond normal summer weather.

This image shows the Chesapeake Fire Department in Virginia pouring water on a bridge that became stuck in extreme heat on June 24, 2025. (Chesapeake Fire Department/Facebook / FOX Weather)

These aren't just inconveniences—they're previews of our climate future, where extreme heat becomes a regular test of our infrastructure.

This Wasn't Just About North America

While we were sweltering in Toronto and New York, Europe was experiencing its own intense heat patterns. Satellite imagery shows massive heat domes stretching across both continents—those ominous red and orange swirls that meteorologists track like approaching storms. When you see these visualizations, it becomes clear that extreme heat isn't a local weather quirk; it's a planetary phenomenon.

The same atmospheric patterns that brought record-breaking temperatures to Boston and Toronto were part of a larger system affecting millions across multiple continents. This is what climate scientists mean when they talk about global warming—it's not just that individual places get hotter, but that the entire atmosphere's behavior is shifting.

The Bigger Picture: 50+ Years of Undeniable Warming

Last week's heat wave isn't an isolated incident—it's part of a clear, accelerating pattern that's been building for decades.

The numbers don't lie: 2024 was the warmest year since global records began in 1850. Earth is now about 2.65°F (1.47°C) warmer than it was in the late 1800s. The 10 warmest years on record? They're all recent.

The Gold Standard of Climate Evidence: Mauna Loa's 67-Year Record

To understand just how unprecedented our current situation is, we need to look at one of the most important scientific datasets in climate research: the atmospheric CO2 measurements from Hawaii's Mauna Loa Observatory. Since 1958, this remote facility perched high on Hawaii's largest mountain has been measuring the concentration of carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere, creating what scientists call the Keeling Curve—named after researcher Charles David Keeling who began the measurements.

The data tells an unambiguous story. When measurements began in 1958, atmospheric CO2 stood at 313 parts per million (ppm). By 2024, that number had risen to 424.6 ppm. In 2025, monthly averages at Mauna Loa exceeded 430 ppm for the first time in human history—and likely the first time in over 3 million years.

This isn't just numbers on a chart. The Mauna Loa Observatory has captured what scientists consider the most conclusive evidence of human-caused climate change. The location is ideal for these measurements: situated in the middle of the Pacific Ocean, far from major industrial sources, it provides a clean baseline reading of the planet's atmospheric composition. The steady, relentless upward climb of the Keeling Curve mirrors the steady increase in global temperatures we've experienced over the same period.

The Keeling Curve: Atmospheric CO2 measurements at Mauna Loa Observatory since 1958, showing the relentless rise from 313 ppm to over 430 ppm in 2025. (Scripps Institution of Oceanography/NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory). For readers wanting to track this data in real time, Ralph Keeling and his team maintain the @Keeling_curve X account, which provides daily updates on atmospheric CO2 levels from Scripps Institution of Oceanography. The account makes the abstract concept of rising emissions tangible - you can literally watch the numbers climb day by day.

The Threat to Critical Climate Monitoring

Here's what makes this even more concerning: this irreplaceable scientific record is now under threat. The Trump administration's proposed budget seeks to shut down the Mauna Loa Observatory as part of broader cuts to NOAA's climate research programs. The proposal would eliminate NOAA's Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research entirely, including facilities that comprise the US government's greenhouse gas monitoring network.

This represents more than just budget cuts—it's an attempt to eliminate the very tools scientists use to track climate change. When policymakers target the measurement systems that provide objective data about our changing climate, it becomes harder to understand the problem, let alone solve it.

But here's what's really striking about the current warming: the pace of change is accelerating. While global temperatures have risen at about 0.17°F per decade since 1901, the rate of warming has been much faster since the 1970s—roughly 0.15 to 0.20°C per decade. That's climate change in real time.

It's Not Just Getting Hotter Everywhere

Climate change isn't painting the world with one broad brush. The North, West, and Alaska have warmed the most, while some parts of the Southeast have seen less change. The Arctic is warming particularly fast—as ice and snow disappear, the region reflects less sunlight, creating a feedback loop that amplifies warming.

Recent research reveals something concerning: there's been a clear step-up in warming since around 1990. We're not just on a steady warming trend; we're on an accelerating one.

This Is Unprecedented in Human History

Here's the context that really matters: we have temperature records going back much further than modern instruments. Ice cores give us data from 800,000 years ago. Geological evidence extends back millions of years.

With all that data, scientists can say with confidence: the current warming is faster than anything in at least 2,000 years. We're in uncharted territory.

What Science Says We Need to Do (And It's Not Hopeless)

First, let's be clear about something: there is no scientific debate about what's causing this warming. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change—thousands of the world's top climate scientists—states it unequivocally: "Human activities, principally through emissions of greenhouse gases, have unequivocally caused global warming."

But here's the thing: knowing the problem also means knowing the solutions.

The Challenge Is Massive but Achievable

To keep warming to relatively safe levels (1.5°C above pre-industrial), we need to cut global emissions by almost half by 2030. That sounds impossible until you realize what we're already seeing:

  • Solar and wind costs have plummeted

  • Electric vehicle adoption is accelerating

  • Countries representing most of the global economy have net-zero commitments

  • Green hydrogen is becoming economically viable

It's Not Just About Technology

The IPCC emphasizes something called "climate resilient development"—solutions that reduce emissions while making life better. Think: clean energy that improves health (especially for women and children), public transit that reduces air pollution and creates jobs, or nature-based solutions that protect biodiversity while storing carbon.

Some of the most cost-effective solutions involve protecting and restoring forests, improving agriculture, and other land-use changes. The IPCC estimates these would cost about $400 billion globally—which sounds like a lot until you realize it's less than what governments currently spend subsidizing these same sectors.

The Math Is Clear, But the Politics Is Hard

Why Climate Disagreement Persists: Understanding the Divide

Despite overwhelming scientific consensus, climate change remains a polarizing issue in some regions, particularly the United States. Understanding why disagreement persists is crucial for developing effective communication strategies.

The Scale of Denial

Here's something that might surprise you: nearly 15% of Americans still deny that climate change is real, according to recent research using AI to analyze millions of social media posts. Given the overwhelming scientific evidence, why does this persist?

The Problem of False Equivalence

But here's the deeper question: why do we treat a 15% minority opinion as deserving equal consideration with scientific consensus? We don't give flat-earthers equal time in geography classes. We don't treat vaccine denial as a legitimate "other side" in medical journals. Yet somehow, climate denial gets treated as a valid counterpoint to decades of research by thousands of scientists.

This false equivalence isn't accidental—it's been deliberately manufactured. When infrastructure literally buckles under extreme heat and we're still debating whether climate change is real, we've allowed political ideology to override objective reality.

The Big Tobacco Playbook

The strategy is surprisingly simple and was perfected by the tobacco industry: create doubt. A tobacco company memo from the 1960s laid it out clearly: "Doubt is our product since it is the best means of competing with the 'body of fact' that exists in the mind of the general public."

When tobacco's health effects became undeniable, the industry transitioned this same playbook to climate change. Organizations with names like the "Advancement of Sound Science Center" emerged, funded first by tobacco companies, then by oil companies including Exxon.

Here's the kicker: Exxon's internal documents show their own scientists accurately predicted global warming as early as the late 1970s, even as the company publicly funded climate denial.

It's Not Really About the Science

Psychology research reveals that climate denial often isn't really about the science—it's about protecting worldviews and identity.

The strongest predictor of climate denial is what researchers call "exclusionary and anti-egalitarian preferences"—essentially, opposition to multiculturalism and feminism. Political affiliation is huge too, with Republican voters showing the strongest correlation with climate denial.

But here's what's really fascinating: these same communities often show skepticism toward other scientific recommendations, like COVID-19 vaccines. It's not climate science specifically they distrust—it's institutional expertise in general.

When Leaders Know Better But Choose Denial

This brings us to a particularly troubling aspect of climate denial: it's often not about genuine confusion over the science. Take the Trump administration's approach. Trump's budget proposal to shut down Mauna Loa wasn't made because they genuinely believed the science was wrong—it was made despite knowing the science was right.

The pattern is telling: when you're committed to expanding fossil fuel extraction and rolling back environmental regulations, climate science becomes an inconvenient truth. It's politically easier to attack the messengers than to deal with the message. Trump removed climate scientists from advisory panels, buried climate reports, and banned the term "climate change" from government communications—not because the science was flawed, but because it threatened the agenda.

This isn't ignorance—it's strategic denial. When economic interests and ideological commitments conflict with scientific reality, some leaders choose to wage war on reality itself. The proposed elimination of climate monitoring at Mauna Loa represents the logical endpoint of this strategy: if you can't change the data, eliminate the ability to collect it.

The Echo Chamber Effect

Social media analysis shows that climate believers and deniers exist in almost completely separate online worlds. They share different news sources, follow different influencers, and rarely interact with each other. Former President Trump, for example, was identified as one of the most influential climate denial voices on Twitter.

This creates a situation where people can live in completely different information universes, making productive dialogue extremely difficult.

Why We Can't Keep Treating Denial as Legitimate

Here's the uncomfortable truth: treating climate denial as a legitimate "other side" has real-world consequences. While we debate whether bridges will expand in extreme heat, bridges are literally getting stuck and requiring fire departments to cool them down. While we argue about whether infrastructure can handle rising temperatures, power grids are failing and trains are slowing down.

We've created a situation where a small minority's ideological opposition to climate science gets equal weight with the overwhelming consensus of actual climate scientists. This isn't democratic discourse—it's a recipe for paralysis while the climate crisis accelerates around us.

The question isn't why 15% of people deny climate change—the question is why we've allowed 15% to hold 85% hostage to their denial.

Moving Forward: Every Degree Matters

Why Optimism Is Realistic

Despite the political challenges, there are reasons to be hopeful. Low-carbon technologies are advancing rapidly. Countries are making more ambitious commitments. And extreme weather events like last week's heat wave are making climate change undeniably real for more people.

Some researchers argue we should stop focusing so much on climate denial and instead focus on solutions. After all, 85% of Americans do accept that climate change is real—that's a strong majority to build on.

The Window Is Closing, But It's Still Open

Every fraction of a degree of warming makes a significant difference in how powerful the effects will be. That means every action we take to limit further warming makes a big difference, especially for vulnerable communities.

The science is clear: we need bolder global climate commitments, and we need them fast. We need to transition to clean energy and reach net-zero emissions as soon as possible. And we'll need to remove some carbon that's already in the atmosphere.

But here's the encouraging part: many climate solutions benefit society in other ways. Better air quality, more jobs, energy security, improved health outcomes—climate action isn't just about preventing future disasters, it's about building a better world today.

When Reality Meets Ideology

As I write this, the temperature has finally dropped in the Northeast—a dramatic swing of 30-40 degrees in some places from the peak of the heat wave. The record-breaking heat wave is over, at least for now. But the larger pattern continues.

Last week's extreme weather was both a warning and an opportunity. A warning because it shows us what's coming if we don't act decisively. An opportunity because it makes climate change tangible and immediate for millions of people.

We can keep debating whether climate change is real while heat records tumble around us, or we can channel our energy into implementing solutions that we know work. The tools exist. The financing exists. The knowledge exists.

What we need now is the collective will to use them.

The choice is ours, but we need to make it soon. The climate isn't waiting for us to figure out our politics.
