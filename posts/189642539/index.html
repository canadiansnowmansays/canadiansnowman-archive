The Iran Crisis

The War Widens

Part XXVIII: Day Three — First Blood, Broken Ceasefire, and the Fracture Within

March 2, 2026

Fatemeh Bahrami / Anadolu / Getty
By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 2, 2026

Three soldiers died at Camp Arifjan in Kuwait on Sunday night.

The Department of Defense has not yet released their names. What is known: they were killed by an Iranian strike on a US military base in a country that did not ask to be part of this war. They are the first Americans to die in combat in the Trump-Netanyahu operation against Iran. They will not be the last. “Sadly, there will likely be more before it ends,” Trump said in a video message Sunday afternoon, before the names were known. “That’s the way it is. Likely be more.”

That sentence — the flattest acknowledgment of acceptable loss in recent American presidential memory — was delivered by a man who ran twice on the promise of stopping wars. It landed in the same news cycle as three other developments that, taken together, mark Day Three as the moment the Iran Crisis became something categorically different from what it was on Day One.

Hezbollah re-entered the war. Israel shattered the Lebanon ceasefire. The domestic MAGA coalition fractured publicly. And in a six-minute phone interview with the New York Times, Trump described a war with no clear endgame, compared it favorably to Venezuela, and said the fighting could last “four to five weeks.”

The war that started as a defined operation has become an open-ended one. The region is widening. The politics are shifting. The bodies are accumulating.

The Lebanon Front

At approximately 3 AM Monday morning in Beirut, explosions struck the Dahiya — the southern outskirts of the Lebanese capital, a densely populated commercial and residential area that serves as Hezbollah’s stronghold. Israeli strikes. No evacuation warnings. Residents fleeing in the dark, cars jammed on roads out of the neighborhood.

Sally Hijazi, 33, described running to her four-year-old daughter’s room, picking her up, rushing out with her husband. “We didn’t know where to go. My parent’s house is also in Dahiya, so going there wasn’t an option. I’m so afraid. I don’t know what to do.”

This is what the ceasefire shattering looks like at street level: a mother holding a child in the dark, nowhere to go.

Hezbollah took responsibility for the initial rocket fire into northern Israel that triggered the Israeli response. Its statement was explicit: it was acting to avenge the death of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. After a year of restraint under ceasefire terms — a year in which Naim Qassem’s statements had been carefully calibrated to mourn without committing, to condemn without mobilizing — Hezbollah has moved. Whether this is a tactical strike designed to satisfy domestic and organizational pressure, or the beginning of sustained reengagement, is the question the next 24 hours will answer.

At least 31 people were killed in Lebanon in Israeli airstrikes by early Monday, the Lebanese Health Ministry reported. The Israeli military chief of staff, General Eyal Zamir, said Israelis should “prepare for many prolonged days of combat ahead.” The Israeli military urged residents in approximately 50 villages in eastern and southern Lebanon to immediately evacuate.

Lebanon’s prime minister Nawaf Salam called the rocket launches “irresponsible” and said Lebanon would “not allow the country to be dragged into new adventures.” President Joseph Aoun condemned the launches as undermining Lebanon’s efforts to stay out of the conflict. The Lebanese government is publicly repudiating Hezbollah’s action — a political reality that would have been unimaginable a year ago and that illustrates exactly how much the political landscape in Lebanon has shifted since the 2024 ceasefire.

This is the paradox of the Lebanon front’s re-opening: Hezbollah acted. Lebanon’s government condemned the action. Israel struck back at Hezbollah, not Lebanon. And 31 Lebanese civilians are dead in the Dahiya.

UPDATE, March 2: Mohammad Ra’ad — head of Hezbollah’s parliamentary faction, the Loyalty to the Resistance Bloc, and the organization’s senior political figure in Lebanon’s legislature since 2000 — was reportedly killed in Israeli strikes on Beirut Monday morning. Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz simultaneously declared Hezbollah secretary-general Naim Qassem “now a marked target for assassination,” saying “anyone who follows Khamenei’s path will soon find himself in the depths of hell.” Qassem is alive. He is hunted. The IDF confirmed striking “several senior Hezbollah terrorists in Beirut” without naming targets. Independent confirmation from Lebanese authorities and international agencies was still pending as of the latest reporting.

The pattern from the Iran operation is now repeating in Lebanon: Hezbollah re-entered the war, and within hours Israel began eliminating its leadership layer — political first, secretary-general designated next. Ra’ad’s reported death answers the question posed at the end of this section. This is not a gesture. It is a war.

Part XXVII of this series argued that the axis of resistance had “failed when the war came” — that Hezbollah, under ceasefire terms and disarmament pressure, was “not in a position to get involved.” That assessment was correct as of Sunday evening. Monday morning revised it. Not a retraction — Hezbollah’s action is limited, not the full mobilization that would have been unimaginable under the pre-2024 force structure. But the ceasefire is gone. The Lebanon front is open. The calculation that the crown jewel had been permanently neutralized requires a qualifier: degraded significantly, neutralized for now, status now uncertain.

The Kuwait Casualties and the Gulf War

Three US service members killed at Camp Arifjan. Five more seriously wounded. Multiple aircraft crashed at Al Jahra — crews survived, Kuwaiti Defense Ministry confirmed. The cause of the aircraft losses has not been officially confirmed; Iran has been targeting US bases throughout the region.

The New York Times’ satellite imagery and video analysis documented six US military facilities struck in the first two days: the Fifth Fleet naval headquarters in Manama, Bahrain (two satellite communications terminals destroyed, buildings damaged or destroyed); Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait (multiple building roofs collapsed); the military base at Erbil International Airport in Iraq (four structures destroyed, fires continuing into Monday morning); Jebel Ali Port in Dubai (smoke from US Navy recreational area); Camp Buehring in Kuwait (drone detonated inside perimeter).

Iran has fired a staggering volume of munitions at the Gulf region over two days: 390 ballistic missiles and 830 drone attacks, according to government reports compiled by the Times. The vast majority were intercepted. The UAE reported 541 drones fired, 506 intercepted; 165 ballistic missiles, 152 destroyed. Kuwait reported 97 ballistic missiles and 283 drones intercepted over two days. The interception rates are extraordinary — a testament to the air defense architecture the US has built in the region over decades. But interception rates of 90%+ still mean hundreds of impacts when the volume is this large.

Iran is losing this exchange operationally. Its missile launchers are being destroyed faster than it can fire them. Its naval assets are being sunk. Its IRGC headquarters is rubble. But the political cost of the exchange — three American dead, bases struck, aircraft crashed, Gulf civilians killed in drone debris — is accumulating regardless of the military outcome.

A Chinese national was killed in Tehran crossfire Monday morning, Chinese Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Mao Ning confirmed. Beijing has now absorbed a casualty. How that calculation enters the geopolitical picture — alongside the coming Monday opening of Chinese markets, the yuan, the Shanghai Composite — will be one of the first clear signals of whether the back-channel that Trump announced and Larijani denied has any structural support from the one power that could make it real.

“Four to Five Weeks” — The Timeline That Wasn’t

In a roughly six-minute phone call with the New York Times on Sunday afternoon — conducted from Mar-a-Lago, shortly after Trump received news of the first American casualties — Trump described the war he started.

“Well, we intended four to five weeks.” He added: “It won’t be difficult. We have tremendous amounts of ammunition. You know, we have ammunition stored all over the world in different countries.”

Four to five weeks is not a plan. It is a number attached to confidence without a mechanism. Trump offered no description of what military operations over those four to five weeks would accomplish, no definition of what conditions would determine their end, and no account of how the stated military objective — degrading Iran’s weapons infrastructure — connects to the stated political objective, which Trump described in mutually contradictory ways within the same six-minute call.

At first: the Venezuelan model. “What we did in Venezuela, I think, is the perfect, the perfect scenario.” Remove the top leader, keep the existing government structure in place, make the remaining leadership “more cooperative and friendly.” A pragmatic transaction with a surviving institution.

Then, minutes later: the Iranian people overthrowing their own government. “That’s going to be up to them about whether or not they do.” A popular uprising, not a negotiated transition.

Then: “I have three very good choices” for who should lead Iran — unnamed, unrevealed, held in reserve until “the job is done.”

Three visions of endgame in six minutes. The first is transactional pragmatism — deal with whoever survives and can be worked with. The second is the promise Trump made to Iranians celebrating in the streets — that America would be there for them. The third implies a specific leadership outcome Trump has already identified.

These are not three versions of the same plan. They are three different wars with three different purposes. The administration has not chosen between them. Trump told the Times that his advisers had explained why the Venezuelan model couldn’t be replicated in Iran — the vast differences in culture, history, military structure, clerical entanglement — and that he “remains enamored” of it anyway.

That is the sentence that defines the strategic situation on Day Three. The president knows the analog doesn’t apply. He is using it anyway.

The Pentagon, notably absent from Trump’s framing: US officials have expressed concern that the conflict is depleting munitions reserves critical to scenarios like a Taiwan conflict or Russian incursion into Europe. Trump made no mention of this in his Times interview. He mentioned “ammunition stored all over the world in different countries” as evidence the campaign could be sustained. The reserves question and Trump’s confidence are pointing in different directions.

The School in Minab

More than 175 people, mostly children, were reportedly killed when an elementary school in Minab was hit by an apparent airstrike on Feb. 28 during a wave of attacks across Iran by the U.S. and Israel.Credit...Mehr News Agency

The death toll at Shajarah Tayyebeh girls’ elementary school in the southern town of Minab has risen to at least 175 — most of them children. Iranian state and state-affiliated media reported the search for survivors ended Sunday. The school sits adjacent to an IRGC naval base. Satellite imagery shows that in 2013 the school building was part of the base; by 2016 it had been walled off and was no longer connected. Whether the strike was targeting the adjacent base, and by what margin the school was missed or not distinguished, has not been officially answered. The Israeli military did not respond to requests for comment. CENTCOM said it was “looking into” reports of civilian harm.

UNESCO condemned the strike. Malala Yousafzai said she was “heartbroken and appalled.” The Center for Civilians in Conflict called for “immediate de-escalation, maximum restraint.”

175 children, most of them girls, in a school with book bags covered in blood and ashes. Rescue workers retrieving a severed arm from rubble. A mural visible on what was once a classroom wall.

This is the event that, in the historical accounting of this war, will be named alongside the nine Israelis killed in Beit Shemesh, the three Americans at Camp Arifjan, and the 22 killed in Pakistan. The fact that the base next door was a legitimate military target does not resolve the question. The law of armed conflict requires parties to avoid or minimize harm to civilians — even when military objectives are valid. Whether that standard was met here is a question that does not have a quick answer, and that will not go away.

The MAGA Fracture

Trump ran twice on a promise to stop wars. He ran specifically against the Middle East interventionism that defined the Bush-era Republican party. That promise was the ideological foundation of MAGA’s foreign policy identity.

On Saturday, he started a war in the Middle East.

The fracture has been building since the first strikes. Tucker Carlson, the most-watched right-wing media figure in America, met with Trump three times in the Oval Office over the past month — each meeting roughly 90 minutes — to urge him against striking Iran. Carlson’s argument, according to someone familiar with the conversations, was direct: “You need to stand up to Israel, or else you’re going to be destroyed and the country is going to be destroyed.” His framing of Israel as a country of 9 million people with no resources, whose interests Trump was prioritizing over America’s, is the MAGA-nativist critique of the operation in its sharpest form. Carlson went on ABC News on Sunday and called the decision to strike “absolutely disgusting and evil.”

Erik Prince, Blackwater founder and Trump ally: “I don’t see how this is in keeping with the president’s MAGA commitment.”

Marjorie Taylor Greene, who recently broke with Trump: “Always America last.”

Curt Mills of The American Conservative: “An elite-driven war, driven, frankly, by the ‘deep state.’”

These are not fringe critics. They are figures Trump has publicly embraced, platformed, and in some cases protected. When Tucker Carlson calls something “absolutely disgusting and evil,” the segment of the MAGA base that watches Tucker Carlson hears that.

A Reuters/Ipsos poll released Sunday — conducted before the first American casualties were announced — found only 25% of Americans approve of the strikes. Among Republicans, 55% approve. That means 45% of Trump’s own party is skeptical or opposed. And 42% of GOP voters said they would be less likely to support the campaign if it leads to US troops being killed or injured. The poll was taken before three of them were.

Representative Ro Khanna and Representative Thomas Massie — the same pair who forced partial release of the Epstein files — are teaming up to force a War Powers Resolution vote in the House. The War Powers clock started Saturday. Trump has 60 days before the statute requires congressional authorization or withdrawal. Whether he respects that deadline, litigates it, or ignores it will be one of the constitutional tests this conflict produces.

A senior White House aide told reporters they believe the MAGA anger will blow over — “especially if the conflict lasts only a short time.” A former Trump aide offered the darker read: the president’s support could erode in the long term if additional troops are killed. “He ran because a big part of the MAGA base did not want another war in the Middle East,” Khanna said on Meet the Press. “This is a betrayal of a decent chunk of the MAGA base.”

The White House official’s response, given anonymously: Trump’s “first instinct is always diplomacy” but Iran “failed to make a deal.” “As a result, President Trump is taking decisive action to eliminate major national-security threats to the American people.”

The tension between those two sentences — diplomacy as first instinct, but also decisive military action as the response to failed diplomacy — will be the political story of the weeks ahead.

“Our Resources Are Done”

Against all this — the widening fronts, the first American deaths, the MAGA fracture, the Larijani public denial of negotiations — there is the private Iranian calculation that Arash Azizi documented in The Atlantic on Sunday.

A source close to Qalibaf told Azizi, speaking on background given wartime sensitivity: “The Islamic Republic has no way but to end the conflict with the U.S. and focus on economic development. Our resources are done. That’s the only way forward.”

That source envisaged Iran establishing diplomatic ties with the United States. Taking a position on Israel similar to Saudi Arabia — conditional recognition tied to Palestinian diplomacy, not Khamenei’s quixotic destruction campaign. Relaxing socially repressive domestic laws. A slight political opening.

Azizi’s analysis adds something that reframes the entire succession picture: the National Security Council effectively sidelined Khamenei after June’s 12-day war. It has been running Iran since summer 2025. The supreme leader killed Saturday was, in the most functionally relevant sense, already a figurehead. The council — dominated by Larijani and Qalibaf — survived the assassination intact. Trump did not decapitate Iran’s government. He removed its ceremonial head while the governing nervous system continues to function.

Ali Larijani, Iran’s Supreme National Security Council Secretary and informal crisis coordinator, in a recent portrait—his March 1, 2026 social media denial of negotiations with the US contradicted Trump’s claim of back-channel openness, highlighting the regime’s public defiance amid private exhaustion (source: ABC News/AAWSAT/Reuters file photo).

This creates the paradox that defines the diplomatic situation on Day Three: Larijani publicly posts that Iran will not negotiate. Qalibaf’s circle privately says “our resources are done.” Both statements are simultaneously true. The public denial is performance — mandatory face-saving for the IRGC and the domestic audience. The private calculation is the reality that the Larijani-Qalibaf faction will eventually act on, if the war gives them the space to act without appearing to surrender under fire.

The Azizi report also identifies the three-person interim committee’s composition — Pezeshkian (reformist), Mohseni-Ejei (ultra-hardline judiciary), Arafi (continuity cleric) — as less decisive than it appears. The clerics will not match the security council’s military power. The Assembly of Experts will choose the next supreme leader. But if Qalibaf and Larijani are calling the operational shots, the ideological temperature of whoever sits in Khamenei’s chair matters less than the pragmatic calculation of the men who have been running the country for eight months already.

One additional dimension Azizi documents: oppositionists inside Iran announced the foundation of the Strategic Council of Republicans Inside Iran on Sunday — 70 political figures, names sent to US and European governments but not publicly declared, emphasizing their republican (not monarchist) identity and their presence inside the country (not diaspora). This is the third option between regime survival in modified form and chaos. Whether the covert dimension of the operation — which Eliot Cohen in The Atlantic describes as including “outreach to leaders of a coup or popular movements” and potential weapons and communications smuggling — is engaging with this council is unknown. If it is, it is not being discussed publicly. If it isn’t, the democratic opposition inside Iran is a signal sent into the same darkness as Reza Pahlavi’s.

The Energy Picture, Monday Morning

Brent crude briefly crossed $82 a barrel as Asian markets opened before retreating to around $79 — still up roughly 8-10% from Friday’s close. European natural gas futures surged more than 20% on Monday. Goldman Sachs analysts said those prices could roughly double if Hormuz shipments were halted for one month — a shock they compared to Russia’s Ukraine invasion.

A small fire broke out at a Saudi oil refinery on Monday after two intercepted drones caused debris to fall. The refinery has a capacity of over half a million barrels per day. The fire appeared far smaller in scale than the 2019 attack that briefly knocked out half of Saudi production capacity — but the fact that debris reached a refinery of that size is the warning shot. Amy Myers Jaffe’s conditional still holds: “The biggest question is what, if any, oil installations get damaged. If the answer to that is none, my opinion is the price of oil will come back down.” The answer is now “some debris, minor fire, contained.” The next answer may be different.

European travel and leisure stocks dropped sharply Monday — IAG down more than 5%, TUI and Carnival down more than 7%. Oil companies rose: BP and Shell up 5% in London, Japan Petroleum Exploration up nearly 12% in Tokyo. The Brent-WTI spread widening — global benchmark premium over US domestic price — continues to price the pain differentially: Europe, China, Japan, South Korea bearing more of the oil shock than energy-independent US.

The 10-year US Treasury yield climbed to 3.98%. Gold surged to around $5,400 per ounce, roughly 14% higher than a month ago. The Monday morning gold movement — from Sunday night’s “modest” $5,267 to $5,400 — is no longer modest. The market is moving from pricing disruption toward pricing extended uncertainty.

Asian stocks retreated: Tokyo’s Topix down more than 1%, Hong Kong’s Hang Seng down more than 2%, Taiwan’s Taiex down roughly 2%. S&P 500 futures pointed to more than 1% decline for the Monday US session.

Barclays’ Ajay Rajadhyaksha’s Sunday note — “we would recommend not buying any immediate dip; the risk-reward doesn’t seem compelling” — looks more prescient with each new development.

What Day Three Has Settled

Day Three has settled three things that were uncertain on Day Two.

It confirmed that Hezbollah’s ceasefire was conditional, not permanent. The death of Khamenei — its patron, its ideological anchor, its legitimating relationship — was sufficient to override the disarmament and ceasefire calculus. The Lebanon front is open. The crown jewel is damaged but not gone.

It confirmed that the MAGA coalition is fracturing in real time. Tucker Carlson, Erik Prince, Marjorie Taylor Greene, The American Conservative, and 45% of Republican voters are not background noise. They are the leading edge of a political dynamic that becomes more acute with every American casualty. The White House’s bet that the anger blows over “if the conflict is short” is a bet that four to five weeks is actually four to five weeks.

It confirmed that Iran’s retaliatory capability, while degrading, can reach Americans. Three dead at Camp Arifjan. Aircraft down at Al Jahra. The abstraction of “military bases in the region” is now three flag-draped caskets. The Pentagon’s casualty projections, which Trump said “could be quite a bit higher” than three, are no longer projections. They are a running count.

Day Three has not settled whether Larijani’s public denial or Qalibaf’s private “our resources are done” will determine Iran’s actual decision. It has not settled whether Vahidi’s IRGC continues to fire or whether the political leadership asserts enough control to stop it. It has not settled whether the Lebanon front is a gesture or a reopening. It has not settled whether the 175 children in Minab will produce an international accountability reckoning or be absorbed into the fog of a war moving too fast for institutions to process.

What has changed between Day One and Day Three: the war that was described as a defined operation with a defined objective is now a war with a timeline of “four to five weeks,” a stated endgame that contradicts itself, a second active front, the first American combat deaths, and a domestic coalition that is cracking along fault lines the president was warned about repeatedly before he ordered the first strike.

“No one is going to pretend that this was the plan in 2016,” a former Trump aide told The Atlantic.

It wasn’t. And the Iranian people in Minab, the residents of Dahiya, the families at Camp Arifjan, the tanker operators in the Strait of Hormuz, and the 45% of Republican voters who didn’t sign up for this are paying the price of the distance between the plan and the present.
