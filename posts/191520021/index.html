The Iran Crisis

The Faucet and the Hose

Part LI: Day Twenty-two

March 20, 2026

This powerful photo shows an Iranian man defiantly setting fire to a large poster of the Supreme Leader (with “بی شرف” — “shameless” or “without honor” — written on it) during nighttime protests. The flames light up his face against a dark urban backdrop, evoking resistance, censorship, and the “internal war”

I. The Information Siege

Ninety-nine percent of Iran’s 92 million people have been cut off from the outside world for nineteen days. The internet blackout imposed by the Iranian government from the first hours of the war has now tightened further. Domestic websites and banking apps that functioned for the first two weeks began experiencing disruptions four days ago. The government-approved “super app” went temporarily inaccessible. A cybersecurity expert described the cascading effect: stricter filtering and centralized traffic control “often lead to cascading failures that affect even domestic services.”

The black market for VPN access has exploded — configurations selling for what the average Iranian household spends on food for several days, traceable by the government, blockable within hours. Starlink dishes, which must be installed on rooftops, are being hunted. Residents have been arrested in Fars and Lorestan provinces on charges of selling “unfiltered internet” and sending images to international news organizations. Iran’s police chief confirmed 500 people detained — half of them for transmitting information and images to “enemy countries.”

“Iranians are facing two wars,” said Fatemeh Shams, an Iranian poet in exile in Philadelphia. “One external and one internal war with the regime through the information siege.”

Iran’s foreign minister defended the blackout in an interview with CBS News — conducted via Zoom, a platform inaccessible to most Iranians. “I’m the voice of Iranians,” he said, “and I have to defend their right.” The interviewer noted the disparity. He did not address it.

The regime that cannot feed its people is also ensuring they cannot tell the world about it. The regime that cannot heat their homes is also ensuring they cannot organize against it. The information siege and the military siege are not parallel operations. They are the same operation.

II. The Pattern

More than 7,800 US targets struck. More than 7,600 Israeli. Pete Hegseth calling it the fastest destruction of a modern military in history. And yet: Iran is not crippled. The regime has not fallen. The missiles keep coming.

Neil MacFarquhar, writing in the Times, has named the historical pattern this war has always fit. “Air power is the US drug of choice,” said Caitlin Talmadge of MIT. “We love to believe that it can achieve big political effects and also big military effects, yet the historical record doesn’t support that.” Iran dispersed its forces. Khamenei, before he was killed, ordered succession plans four layers deep for every central post. The goal, in the words of analyst Afshon Ostovar, is “to expand the pain and inconvenience of this war for as many countries in the world as possible.” The regime’s calculus is brutal and coherent: if it survives, it can claim victory regardless of the destruction. Sharon said driving out the PLO from Lebanon in 1982 had “broken the whole infrastructure of violence.” Hezbollah emerged within months. Netanyahu told Congress in 2002 that removing Saddam would produce “enormous positive reverberations.” Almost 4,500 American soldiers died. The Islamic State rose from the rubble.

Franklin Foer, in The Atlantic, adds the democratic betrayal that completes this picture. In January, as protesters filled Iran’s streets, Trump told them: “Help is on the way.” He has since suggested he would deal with a faction of the existing regime, dismissed the protesters’ chances — “I think that’s a big hurdle to climb” — and simultaneously gutted Voice of America’s Farsi-language programming, refused to authorize Radio Free Europe transmitters capable of broadcasting into Iran at the height of the January protests, and dismantled the National Endowment for Democracy. Kari Lake, who oversees Voice of America, reduced Farsi-language broadcasting from round-the-clock to sometimes as little as 30 minutes a day. The administration that launched a war in the name of the Iranian people has systematically destroyed every tool the United States once used to actually help them.

If regime change is the only lasting solution — and that is the administration’s stated premise — then everything the administration has done outside the bombing campaign has made regime change less likely.

III. The Recession Clock

The Atlantic’s Rogé Karma frames the economic dimension with a precision this series has not yet had space for. The IEA has called this the single largest disruption to the global oil supply in history. An economy already showing the worst growth numbers since COVID — 2025 job creation revised from 584,000 down to 116,000; GDP growth collapsed from 4.4 percent to 0.7 percent in a single quarter; consumer spending already pulling back — is now absorbing that historic shock. Karma’s conclusion: if oil reaches $150 or $200 a barrel, “I would be extremely surprised if we did not get a recession.”

The war’s largest beneficiary, he argues, is Vladimir Putin. Russian oil rises in value as Middle East supply is choked. Sanctioned crude becomes the world’s most sought-after alternative. The Kremlin receives a windfall it can direct toward Ukraine. Trump has already lifted some sanctions on Russian oil. Every week the Strait stays closed, Putin grows stronger and Ukraine grows weaker. Alastair Campbell, visiting Ukraine the week before the war began, said it is “very hard to escape the notion that, basically, when it comes to Ukraine, the Americans are on Putin’s side, the way they talk about it.” That observation, from Tony Blair’s closest adviser, is no longer a diplomatic hint. It is a description.

Jerome Powell said Wednesday that the inflationary impact would likely prevent the Fed from lowering interest rates. Bangladesh has rationed fuel and closed universities. Several Philippine local governments have moved to four-day workweeks. Thailand’s state fuel fund is back in deficit. Germany’s expected growth this year would be halved if prices hold. Britain — its government bound by self-imposed fiscal rules to appease bond markets — announced £53 million for heating oil aid while watching its most vulnerable households face a winter that is not yet over.

Trump was elected as the affordability president. The one price his administration could legitimately claim credit for — gasoline, which fell during his first year — is now the fastest-rising consumer cost in the country. It is at $3.88 nationally. It was $2.92 on February 27.

IV. “I Told Him Don’t Do That”

The South Pars coordination story collapsed within 24 hours. Wednesday night Trump posted that the United States “knew nothing” about Israel’s strike. Thursday morning, three Israeli officials briefed on the strike told the Times the United States was informed in advance. By the time Trump spoke at the Oval Office, he had shifted: “I told him don’t do that.” Then: “We’re independent. We get along great. It’s coordinated.”

Three statements. Three contradictions. One morning.

David Sanger’s analysis is precise: Trump has been trying to preserve Iran’s oil and gas infrastructure to contain energy prices. Israel has been deliberately targeting it to collapse the Iranian state — what Israeli officials call “state collapse.” Gabbard confirmed the divergence before the House Intelligence Committee: Israel was focused on killing Iranian leaders; the United States was focused on missiles and naval forces. She said she was “not privy to Israel’s calculus” to attack energy infrastructure. The two allies fighting the same war have different wars in mind.

President Trump has been trying to preserve Iran’s oil and gas infrastructure and keep the country from retaliating at energy facilities throughout the Persian Gulf.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

Netanyahu, at a Thursday press conference, claimed Iran no longer possessed the ability to enrich uranium or manufacture ballistic missiles. He provided no evidence. He said nothing about Iran’s stockpile of highly enriched uranium buried at Isfahan by US strikes last year. He said the war could end “a lot faster than people think,” then added: “There’s still more work to do, and we’re going to do it.” He also confirmed Trump had asked Israel to halt further South Pars strikes and that Israel would comply — after Iran had already retaliated and Ras Laffan was burning. The ceasefire on the gas field arrived after the damage was done.

V. Pearl Harbor

At an otherwise congenial Oval Office meeting with Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, Trump was asked why allies received no advance warning of the assault on Iran. His answer: “We didn’t tell anybody about it because we wanted surprise. Who knows better about surprise than Japan, OK? Why didn’t you tell me about Pearl Harbor, OK?”

Takaichi widened her eyes. Took a visible breath. Kept her arms crossed. Did not respond.

Every American president since the Cold War has treated Pearl Harbor as a subject of reconciliation in the presence of Japanese leaders — a wound acknowledged and transcended, not a punchline. Trump used it as a joke about strategic surprise. The remark was not a slip. It was a declaration that no diplomatic norm, however carefully maintained over 80 years of alliance, is safe in his presence.

Japan has been among the most exposed economies in the war — gasoline prices hit a record high in Tokyo on the day Takaichi flew to Washington. She brought, according to her own statement, “specific proposals to calm the global energy market.” She opened by telling Trump: “I firmly believe it is only you, Donald, who can achieve peace across the world.” She left with a Pearl Harbor joke.

VI. $200 Billion

The Pentagon has asked the White House for $200 billion to fund the Iran war. The figure — nearly a quarter of the entire annual US defense budget — has not yet been formally submitted to Congress, but its existence is now public. Hegseth, asked about it Thursday: “Obviously, it takes money to kill bad guys. As far as the $200 billion, I think that number could move.”

For context: the United States spent $815 billion in direct costs on the Iraq War over 13 years. The entire US contribution to Ukraine’s defense since 2022 totals under $200 billion. The Pentagon is asking for the equivalent of the entire Ukraine commitment — in a single request — for a war now entering its fourth week with no stated timeline for conclusion and no congressional authorization.

Senator Susan Collins called it “considerably higher than I would have guessed.” Senator Lisa Murkowski said the administration could not simply arrive “with an invoice.” Senate Majority Leader Thune and Speaker Johnson neither committed to the request nor rejected it. Democrats responded with outrage, noting that the same Republican majority that cut Medicaid and SNAP because the nation “couldn’t afford them” was now being asked to fund an unauthorized war at this scale.

The constitutional crisis David Frum described in Part L has now acquired a price tag. And separately, the State Department declared an emergency to bypass Congress entirely on a $16 billion arms sale to the UAE, Kuwait, and Jordan — counter-drone technology, medium-range missiles, and air defense systems. The administration that has no congressional authorization for the war is now also bypassing Congress to arm the war’s neighbors.

VII. The Faucet

Ras Laffan’s damage assessment arrived Thursday in numbers that reframe the entire energy dimension of this war. Qatar’s energy minister said Iranian strikes had damaged 17 percent of the country’s LNG export capacity and caused approximately $20 billion in lost annual revenues. Repair timeline: three to five years.

The distinction that matters — the one Atlantic economist Rogé Karma made with the faucet analogy — is now fully operational. Closing the Strait is kinking the hose. Damaging Ras Laffan is attacking the faucet. Hose kinks resolve when the war ends. Faucet damage persists for years regardless.

Qatar had been expected to expand North Field East production starting in November, which would have increased global LNG supply through 2028. That timeline is now in serious doubt. The United States is the world’s largest LNG exporter but has maxed its overseas capacity. There is no obvious replacement.

Brent hit $119 intraday before settling at $108.65 after Trump’s “it will be over soon” comments calmed markets briefly. Treasury Secretary Bessent floated the most paradoxical economic intervention of the war: unsanctioning approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian oil already at sea, in effect using Iranian revenue to tamp down the prices that Iran’s own retaliation created. “We will be using the Iranian barrels against the Iranians,” he said. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve, which the administration failed to refill before the war began, is also under consideration for additional release. The IEA’s member nations have already committed a record 400 million barrel release — and analysts say it will not be sufficient if the war continues.

The WTO on Thursday cut its 2026 trade growth forecast to 1.9 percent — down sharply from 4.6 percent in 2025 — and warned of an additional 0.5 percentage point reduction if energy prices remain elevated. Strait traffic has gone from 138 commercial vessels per day to nearly zero. More than 40,000 flights have been canceled. Twenty thousand seafarers are trapped in the Gulf with, in the words of the International Maritime Organization’s secretary general, “very little assistance.”

VIII. The New Supreme Leader Is Wounded — and Iran Is Run by a Committee

Gabbard told the House Intelligence Committee Thursday that Mojtaba Khamenei — the new supreme leader of Iran, who has not appeared in public since February 28 — was badly injured in an Israeli strike. She acknowledged under questioning that he was more hard-line than his father. She said that given his injuries, the decision-making at the top of the Iranian government was “unclear.”

The New York Times has now clarified what “unclear” means in practice. Three senior Iranian officials confirmed that the country is currently being run by a committee. General Ahmad Vahidi, newly appointed IRGC commander in chief, is running the tactical side of the war. General Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf — a member of Khamenei’s inner circle, former IRGC commander, and current speaker of Parliament — has quietly stepped into Larijani’s strategic role. President Pezeshkian and his vice president are managing the day-to-day functioning of the state. Retired generals and former officials have been called back to service.

The picture that emerges is not collapse. It is adaptation. As Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group put it, the Islamic Republic is running the war as “a networked survival machine, with all hands on deck and authority diffused across overlapping centers of power.” Eliminating the top tier has not produced a vacuum. It has produced a committee.

A photo released by Iranian state media shows President Masoud Pezeshkian, center, with his son Yousef Pezeshkian, in blue jacket, in Tehran last month, before the war began.Credit...West Asia News Agency

The human dimension of this arrived in an unlikely place: the Telegram diary of Yousef Pezeshkian, the president’s 44-year-old son and adviser, who has been posting daily entries since the war began. He has not seen or spoken to his father since February 28. He writes of the fear beneath the public defiance — “I think some political figures are panicking” — and of the central question dividing Iran’s leadership: “How long are we supposed to fight? Forever? Until Israel is destroyed and America retreats? Until Iran is in complete ruins and we surrender? We have to study the different scenarios.”

He worried that Iran’s strikes on Arab neighbors would backfire. He defended his father for apologizing to Gulf states for the strikes — an apology that was reversed within hours under pressure from conservatives and military commanders. He wrote of the killing of Larijani: “I did not want to believe it at all. We should not have allowed the enemy to have another successful assassination.”

And on Day Nineteen, after visiting his grandmother — who was entirely unaware of the war — he wrote: “After 19 days of war, I finally broke down and cried, several times.”

Unless Iran can stop the targeted killings, he wrote, “we will lose the war.”

On Friday morning, Israel killed another one. Ali Mohammad Naini, spokesman for the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps — the man who had led the IRGC’s “cognitive war” against adversaries for years — was killed in an Israeli strike. The elite-thinning continues. The committee absorbs the loss and continues.

IX. Jerusalem and the Holy Sites

Debris from an Iranian missile interception this week landed inside the Al Aqsa Mosque compound — the third holiest site in Islam — and damaged a roof belonging to the Greek Patriarchate, 200 meters from the Church of the Holy Sepulcher. A shard embedded itself in a house in Ras al-Amud. The fragments were three to four yards long.

“The rocket which landed on my home could just as easily have struck Al Aqsa, the Church of the Holy Sepulcher, or the Western Wall,” said one resident. “But unfortunately, there’s nowhere today that’s safe.”

Jerusalem has been largely spared direct targeting throughout this war and previous ones — Iran and its proxies have avoided the city because of the risk of accidentally striking a site sacred to 1.5 billion Muslims. But interception debris does not distinguish. The Old City is less than a square mile. The density of holy sites within that square mile is without parallel anywhere on earth.

An accidental strike on the Church of the Holy Sepulcher, the Western Wall, or Al Aqsa would alter this war in ways no military planner has publicly accounted for. It would not be the first unintended consequence of this conflict. It would be the most irreversible.

X. The Marines and the Islands

President Trump at the White House on Thursday.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

The most significant strategic development of Day Twenty-Two arrived not in a press conference but in a Times confirmation: 2,500 Marines from the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit are moving from the Indo-Pacific to the Middle East, joining approximately 50,000 US troops already in the region. The 31st MEU specializes in ground operations supported by sea and air. Its selection is not random.

The United States is actively weighing two operations that would require ground forces. The first: a takeover of Kharg Island, where Iran loads most of its oil onto tankers — Trump has repeatedly noted he left the oil infrastructure there intact, but on Wednesday threatened to destroy Iran’s oil infrastructure or electrical grid. The second: a potential seizure of the underground nuclear site at Isfahan, where Iran stores approximately 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade highly enriched uranium. Gabbard told the House Intelligence Committee Thursday that the United States has “high confidence” it knows where that stockpile is stored. She declined to say more in open session.

Trump on Thursday: “I’m not putting troops anywhere. If I were, I certainly wouldn’t tell you.” Two days earlier he said he was “not afraid” to put boots on the ground. The $200 billion Pentagon request, at current burn rates, could sustain operations for months. The 31st MEU is days from the Gulf.

Overnight, the UAE announced it had dismantled an Iranian and Hezbollah covert network operating under fictitious commercial cover — money laundering, terrorism financing, threats to national security. The covert war runs parallel to the air war in every Gulf state simultaneously.

The war Trump said would be over soon has a ground component under active planning, a $200 billion price tag, no congressional authorization, no coalition partners, no exit timeline, and a supreme leader whose decision-making capacity is, in the word used by the Director of National Intelligence, “unclear.”

That is the situation at the end of Day Twenty-Two.
