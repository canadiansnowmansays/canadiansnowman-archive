Can Congress Stop Him?

War Powers, Constitutional Crisis, and the Votes That Failed

December 17, 2025

December 17, 2025

CAN CONGRESS STOP HIM?
Short answer: They’re trying. And failing. Repeatedly.

Earlier today, I published Part I of this addendum showing how President Trump openly demanded Venezuela “return” oil that was never ours—essentially admitting the quiet part out loud. Now the question: Can Congress do anything about it?

The answer reveals a deeper crisis about American democracy, war powers, and the willingness of Congress to check presidential overreach when the president’s own party controls the legislature.

THE SCORECARD: THREE STRIKES

Three War Powers Resolutions have been introduced to block Trump’s Venezuela campaign:

October 2025: Resolution to Block Boat Strikes

Sponsors: Sens. Adam Schiff (D-CA), Tim Kaine (D-VA)
Vote: 48-51
Status: FAILED

Republicans voting Yes: Rand Paul (KY), Lisa Murkowski (AK)
Democrats voting No: John Fetterman (PA)

November 2025: Resolution to Block Land Strikes in Venezuela

Sponsors: Sens. Tim Kaine (D-VA), Adam Schiff (D-CA), Rand Paul (R-KY)
Vote: 49-51
Status: FAILED

Republicans voting Yes: Rand Paul (KY), Lisa Murkowski (AK)
Democrats voting No: None (Fetterman switched his vote)

December 2025: Resolution to End All Venezuela Hostilities

Sponsors: Reps. Joaquin Castro (D-TX), Jim McGovern (D-MA), Thomas Massie (R-KY)
Vote scheduled: Friday, December 19th
Expected outcome: Will likely FAIL

The same day the Epstein documents are released.

THE VOTING PATTERN: A CRISIS OF ACCOUNTABILITY

Only two Republicans have consistently voted to restrain Trump’s war-making powers:

  • Sen. Rand Paul (R-KY)

  • Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R-AK)

Only one Republican in the House has joined efforts to block the campaign:

  • Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY)

Every other Republican has voted to give Trump unlimited authority to wage war against Venezuela without Congressional authorization.

The MAGA Contradiction: Trump campaigned on ending endless wars. His supporters voted for “America First” and no more regime change adventures. Yet MAGA Republicans in Congress are voting to give him carte blanche for exactly that.

Only the libertarian-leaning Republicans (Paul, Massie) and one moderate (Murkowski) have remained consistent.

THE CONSTITUTIONAL CRISIS

The War Powers Resolution of 1973 requires:

  • President must consult Congress before introducing armed forces into hostilities

  • Must report to Congress within 48 hours

  • Must cease hostilities within 60 days without Congressional authorization

The 60-day deadline expired in November.

Trump ignored it. Congress did nothing.

THE ADMINISTRATION’S LEGAL THEORY: “ANYTHING GOES”

Senators Tim Kaine and Adam Schiff reviewed the classified legal memo the administration uses to justify the strikes. Their assessment is chilling:

Sen. Schiff: “If that opinion were to be adopted, it would not constrain any use of force anywhere in the world. It’s broad enough to authorize just about anything.”

Sen. Kaine: The administration claims “the president can use force if he believes it serves U.S. interests, citing both the Constitution and international law.” He added: “Those claims are unfounded because only Congress can authorize war.”

The memo essentially gives the president unlimited power to attack anyone, anywhere, if he frames it as serving American interests.

Sen. Mark Warner (D-VA), top Democrat on the Senate Intelligence Committee, noted the legal opinion “does not even mention Venezuela.”

It’s a blank check for war.

ON THE SENATE FLOOR: VOICES IN THE WILDERNESS

Sen. Rand Paul (R-KY):

“For decades, the globalists in Washington have led our country into one disastrous foreign war after another, whether in Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya, Syria, and now Venezuela. The warmongers have recycled these experiments in regime change again and again, and what has it brought? Instability, chaos, suffering, and resentment. It is the height of arrogance to think we can forcibly remove the dictatorship in Venezuela and expect a different result.”

Sen. Tim Kaine (D-VA):

“Anybody in Congress who thinks we ought to invade Venezuela to stop narco trafficking, to regime change, to grab their oil fields — let them introduce an AUMF [Authorization for Use of Military Force] and let’s have a debate and vote in Congress.”

Sen. Adam Schiff (D-CA):

“Congress has not authorized military force against Venezuela. And we must assert our authority to stop the United States from being dragged—intentionally or accidentally—into full-fledged war in South America.”

Sen. Jim Risch (R-ID), Senate Foreign Relations Committee Chair, defending Trump:

“President Trump has taken decisive action to protect thousands of Americans from lethal narcotics. As commander-in-chief, when the president sees a group of terrorists planning to harm America or our allies, he has the right — and not only the right, but the duty — to do something about it.”

Paul, Kaine, and Schiff lost. Risch and the Republican majority won.

WHY CONGRESS IS FAILING

1. Republican Control

Republicans hold both chambers. Party loyalty trumps constitutional responsibility. Voting against Trump on Venezuela would invite primary challenges and accusations of being “soft on narco-terrorists.”

2. The Framing Works

By designating the Venezuelan government as part of a “Foreign Terrorist Organization” (the Cartel de los Soles) and calling the campaign “counterterrorism,” Trump exploits a legal gray area. Republicans argue the War Powers Act doesn’t apply to “counterterrorism operations.”

Sen. Mike Rounds (R-SD): “There is no war in Venezuela. The law that’s involved in this thing would only apply after there have been basically kinetic actions where our people are in harm’s way.”

Translation: As long as it’s only Venezuelans dying, it’s not war.

3. Presidential Veto Power

Even if a resolution passed both chambers, Trump would veto it. Overriding requires a 2/3 majority—67 votes in the Senate, 290 in the House.

With only 2-3 Republicans willing to vote against Trump, override is mathematically impossible.

4. The Body Count Doesn’t Move Them

As of mid-December:

  • 25+ boat strikes

  • 95-98 people killed

  • Including a documented “double-tap” strike that killed survivors clinging to wreckage

The Washington Post reported that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ordered the second strike specifically to kill survivors of the first strike.

Congress demanded unedited Pentagon footage of the strikes.

The Pentagon refused.

A former ICC prosecutor called the strikes “crimes against humanity.”

John Yoo—yes, that John Yoo, author of the Bush administration’s torture memos—told The New Republic that Trump’s legal reasoning doesn’t hold up:

“The only way the strikes have any legal plausibility is if we’re at war with Venezuela and the drug cartels are something like what we saw in Afghanistan after 2001 with the Taliban and Al Qaeda being so intertwined... For some reason the administration doesn’t want to say that’s what they’re doing, and they won’t legally justify it.”

When the architect of the torture memos says your legal reasoning is too thin, that’s saying something.

Still not enough Republicans to pass a War Powers Resolution.

FRIDAY’S VOTE: DECEMBER 19TH

On Friday morning, the House will vote on the Castro-McGovern-Massie resolution directing the President to end hostilities with Venezuela.

Rep. Joaquin Castro (D-TX):

“President Trump has undermined our alliances in the Western Hemisphere, openly interfered in elections, conducted illegal strikes on boats in the Caribbean, and threatened foreign military intervention. He has put our country at the brink of a war with Venezuela without a debate or vote in the Congress. This resolution will ensure that every member of the House is on the record about sending service members to a war that Americans do not want.”

Rep. Jim McGovern (D-MA):

“Donald Trump claimed he would put America first—instead he’s trying to drag us into an illegal war in Venezuela. Whatever this is about, it has nothing to do with stopping drugs. Trump just pardoned the former President of Honduras who was convicted of sending cocaine to the U.S. And last year he pardoned a guy who brought fentanyl in from China via the dark web.”

Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY):

“The Constitution does not permit the executive branch to unilaterally commit an act of war against a sovereign nation that hasn’t attacked the United States. Congress has the sole power to declare war against Venezuela. Congress must decide such matters according to our Constitution.”

Based on the Senate voting pattern, the resolution will fail.

And it’s scheduled for the same day the Epstein documents drop.

THE CONSTITUTIONAL CHECK ISN’T CHECKING

Congress has the sole power to declare war under Article 1 of the Constitution.

But if the president can:

  • Designate Venezuelan government officials as a “terrorist organization”

  • Frame military action as “counterterrorism”

  • Claim he doesn’t need authorization because it’s not technically “war”

  • Kill nearly 100 people without Congressional approval

  • Seize foreign oil and say “we keep it”

  • Deploy the largest armada in South American history

  • Threaten to invade and seize oil fields

  • And veto any attempt to restrain him

Then the constitutional check is meaningless.

Rep. Joaquin Castro: The blockade is “unquestionably an act of war. A war that the Congress never authorized and the American people do not want.”

A 1961 Justice Department memo (written during Cuba tensions) states: “A blockade is a belligerent act which, as a matter of international law, is ordinarily justified only if a state of war, legal or de facto, exists.”

International law recognizes blockades as acts of war. The Trump administration is implementing one without Congressional authorization.

And Congress can’t stop it.

KAINE’S PROMISE: “WE’RE COMING BACK”

Sen. Tim Kaine:

“Even if it fails, we’re coming back. You have to force Congress to look in the mirror and confront the question: Are we going to exercise Article 1 power or abdicate it away?”

So far, Congress is choosing abdication.

Kaine and Schiff plan to keep forcing votes. Make every member go on record. Build political pressure.

But here’s the problem: Public opinion polling shows 70% of Americans oppose military intervention in Venezuela.

And it doesn’t matter.

Republicans won’t vote against their president. The public’s opposition is irrelevant when party loyalty overrides constitutional duty.

WHAT THIS MEANS

Trump now has de facto unlimited war-making authority as long as:

  1. Republicans control Congress

  2. He can frame it as “counterterrorism” or “drug interdiction”

  3. He keeps American casualties low

  4. The media buries it under other stories (like Epstein documents)

The War Powers Act is dead letter. Congressional authorization is optional. The constitutional check on executive war-making has been disabled.

Rep. Jim McGovern: “Congress has a role to play. And we need to step up and not allow an administration—any administration—to drag us into a war.”

But they’re not stepping up. They’re stepping aside.

THE BOTTOM LINE

Can Congress stop Trump?

Constitutionally: Yes. Congress has sole power to declare war.

Practically: No.

Because:

  • Republicans won’t vote against their president

  • Democrats don’t have the numbers

  • Even if it passed, Trump would veto

  • Override requires 2/3 majority—impossible with current numbers

  • The body count doesn’t move enough Republicans

  • War crimes allegations don’t move enough Republicans

  • 70% public opposition doesn’t move enough Republicans

The pattern holds: When presidents want oil, Congress finds excuses to look the other way.

WHAT HAPPENS FRIDAY

On December 19th:

  • Epstein documents will be released (maybe)

  • The House will vote on the War Powers Resolution (definitely)

  • The media will cover both stories

  • The Venezuela story will likely dominate cable news

  • The resolution will likely fail

  • The armada will remain

  • The strikes will continue

  • And Trump will have confirmation that Congress won’t stop him

Sen. Adam Schiff warned: “Sadly, as these strikes get worse, support will only grow for another War Powers Resolution to stop them. Let’s hope by then we are not in a full-fledged war.”

We’re watching the preventable become the inevitable.

THE LARGER PATTERN

This isn’t just about Venezuela. It’s about the death of war powers oversight.

If Trump can:

  • Wage undeclared war for months

  • Kill nearly 100 people without authorization

  • Seize foreign assets

  • Deploy massive military force

  • Threaten invasion

  • And Congress can’t muster 51 votes to stop him

Then every future president inherits the same unchecked power.

The precedent being set is: The president can wage war unilaterally as long as he doesn’t call it war and his party controls Congress.

The Founders gave Congress the power to declare war specifically to prevent this. They feared executive overreach. They wanted deliberation. They demanded authorization.

All of that has been reduced to symbolic votes that fail along party lines.

THE COST OF ABDICATION

Sen. Peter Welch (D-VT):

“There is no more important thing for this Congress to do, than to reassert its responsibility — to accept our responsibility for the powers that were delegated to the United States Congress — particularly about whether we do or don’t go to war.”

Congress is not reasserting its responsibility. It’s surrendering it.

And the cost?

  • Nearly 100 people dead

  • An armada positioned for invasion

  • Oil fields being targeted

  • International law violated

  • Constitutional checks disabled

  • And a president who now knows he can do whatever he wants

On Friday, Congress will vote. Based on the pattern, they’ll fail. And Trump will have his answer:

Congress won’t stop him. The Constitution won’t stop him. Public opinion won’t stop him.

The only thing that will stop him is Venezuela surrendering its oil. Or fighting back. Or enough American casualties to shift the politics.

None of those outcomes are good.

THE FINAL QUESTION

I began Part I by noting that Trump admitted the quiet part out loud—demanding Venezuela’s oil.

The question for Part II was: Can Congress stop him?

The answer is no.

Not because they lack the constitutional power. But because they lack the political will.

70% of Americans oppose this intervention. Nearly 100 people have been killed. War crimes have been alleged. International law has been violated. A blockade—an act of war—has been ordered.

And Congress can’t get 51 votes to say “stop.”

The checks and balances have failed. Not because of some legal technicality. But because one party won’t check a president of their own party, even when he’s openly admitting he wants another country’s oil.

On Friday, they’ll prove it again.
