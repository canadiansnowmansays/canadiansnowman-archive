The Epstein Files

Three Investigations That Explain Everything

Part 28: How One Man Operated With Impunity for Decades

March 12, 2026

Epstein was “a gentleman of the highest integrity,” one of his bankers wrote in a letter vouching for him as he sought a tax break from the U.S. Virgin Islands in 1999. (Dafydd Jones)

While congressional hearings played out and missing files surfaced, three major investigations published between December 2025 and March 2026 finally answered the questions that had haunted the Epstein story for years:

How did a college dropout from Brooklyn become a financier to billionaires? How did he abuse hundreds of girls and women for decades? And why, after all the investigations, all the evidence, all the victims coming forward, were only two people ever prosecuted?

The New York Times published comprehensive investigations that traced Epstein’s entire arc—from his first lie on a job application to his final arrest. What emerged was not the story of a criminal mastermind or a blackmail operation run by intelligence agencies.

It was the story of a relentless scammer who discovered early that if you positioned yourself correctly within hierarchical systems, you could get away with almost anything.

And he was right.

December 16, 2025: Scams, Schemes, Ruthless Cons

How Jeffrey Epstein Got Rich

The Times investigation by David Enrich, Steve Eder, Jessica Silver-Greenberg, and Matthew Goldstein spent months piecing together Epstein’s rise. They interviewed dozens of former colleagues, girlfriends, business partners, and victims. They reviewed thousands of pages of court records, private archives, old recordings.

What they found: “Epstein was less a financial genius than a prodigious manipulator and liar.”

The pattern started at Bear Stearns in 1976.

A Dalton parent asked the 23-year-old math teacher if he’d ever thought about Wall Street. The parent called Ace Greenberg, a top Bear Stearns executive. Greenberg invited Epstein to the offices at 55 Water Street.

Epstein showed up in a turtleneck. He didn’t have the foggiest idea how Wall Street worked. But Greenberg preferred what he called P.S.D.s: those who were “poor, smart and had a deep desire to become rich.”

Epstein fit the bill. He was hired on the spot.

It was an extraordinarily lucky break—the first of many. Dalton administrators, unimpressed with his teaching, had already asked him to leave after the academic year ended.

But the luck ran deeper. Greenberg seated Epstein next to his 20-year-old daughter Lynne at a dinner party. They started dating. Word spread quickly: the new guy was dating the boss’s daughter. Epstein had “protected status” at the firm.

Then Bear Stearns’s human resources department checked his résumé. He’d claimed degrees from two California universities.

Neither school had heard of him.

Michael Tennenbaum, Epstein’s manager at Bear Stearns, regrets that he didn’t end Epstein’s career when he had the chance.Credit...Elizabeth Bick for The New York Times

Michael Tennenbaum, Epstein’s manager, summoned him to his office. “You lied about your education.”

“Yes, I know,” Epstein calmly replied. He’d never graduated from college. Without an impressive degree or two, he said, “I knew nobody would give me a chance.”

Tennenbaum was disarmed. He asked Greenberg what to do. The message was clear: treat Epstein as if he were a normal employee—which made it obvious he wasn’t.

Epstein got his second chance.

The Times reporters identified this as “perhaps the first example of Epstein getting caught cheating—and then avoiding punishment thanks to his uncanny ability to take advantage of those in positions of power. This would become a lifelong pattern.”

At Bear Stearns, Epstein learned the game. He cultivated relationships with senior executives. He flew to a Caribbean conference on the firm’s dime and charged more than $10,000 in jewelry and clothing for his girlfriend to the company. The accounting department caught it. No consequences.

He gave his girlfriend access to “hot deals”—early access to initial public offering shares that could yield fast profits. The firm discovered it. No consequences.

He lent $15,000 to a high school buddy in violation of federal rules governing brokers. The firm found out. No consequences.

Finally, in 1981, Bear Stearns investigated. Two senior executives interrogated him. Epstein denied wrongdoing and was “deeply offended” by the investigation. The firm fined him $2,500 and suspended him for two months.

Rather than accept this, Epstein resigned.

His tenure at Bear Stearns was over. But he’d collected invaluable connections and credentials. And Bear Stearns employees would continue to help him prosper.

The scams escalated.

Through a Bear Stearns contact, Epstein met Michael Stroll, who ran a pinball and video-game company. Stroll gave Epstein $450,000—about 10 percent of his net worth—to invest in a supposed crude-oil deal.

Within two years, most of the money had vanished. Epstein dodged calls. At one point, he sent Stroll a quart of oil to convince him a deal was in the works.

The dispute ended up in civil court. In 1993, a judge ruled that Epstein wasn’t personally liable. Decades later, Stroll told researchers: “I unknowingly seeded his growth.”

By the late 1980s, Epstein had positioned himself within elite circles. He joined the board of the New York Academy of Art—his first prominent board position. After Andy Warhol died, the academy threw a $150-per-person black-tie fundraiser. Photographers captured Tony Bennett and socialites arriving.

The first name listed as co-chairman: Mr. Jeffrey E. Epstein.

In 1987, a fateful plane ride changed everything.

Epstein sat next to Robert Meister, an insurance executive, on a flight to Palm Beach. Meister suggested his friend Les Wexner—the billionaire behind Victoria’s Secret and the Limited—get in touch with Epstein for financial advice.

Harold Levin, Wexner’s financial adviser, met Epstein in his office and immediately got a bad vibe. He called Wexner: “I smell a rat. I don’t trust him.”

Wexner didn’t listen. About a year later, he hired Epstein to be Levin’s boss.

As far as Levin could tell, Epstein won Wexner’s confidence by falsely telling him that Levin had been stealing. Levin quit rather than work for Epstein.

Before long, Wexner had given Epstein power of attorney over his finances. Epstein’s name appeared in government filings as responsible for Wexner’s businesses and charities.

Wexner’s colleagues grew alarmed. One Limited board member hired the private investigations firm Kroll to investigate Epstein’s past. Even Meister—who’d introduced them—urged Wexner to cut ties.

Wexner didn’t listen.

This became the most important contributor to Epstein’s fortune. Epstein’s modus operandi was to instill fear that someone’s finances were a mess, that advisers and family members were inept or exploiting them, and that only he could fix it.

He appeared to deploy that playbook with Wexner. And he rewarded himself lavishly. He seemed to take whatever he thought he deserved from Wexner’s accounts, according to people who later learned what he was doing. The amounts were often measured in tens of millions.

He bought a waterfront mansion in Palm Beach for $2.5 million. Another mansion in New Albany, Ohio, where Wexner was building a development. He leased a grand marble townhouse on the Upper East Side for $15,000 a month.

He cruised around Manhattan in a Rolls-Royce Silver Spirit.

By 1999, Epstein was worth more than $100 million—an amount that would grow substantially. Much of it appeared to have flowed from Wexner, who would later claim Epstein had “misappropriated vast sums of money.”

More importantly, Wexner gave Epstein credibility. In 1989, Ken Lipper—a prominent fund manager—was standing in a ski-lift line in Aspen when Epstein approached him. He said he worked for Wexner, who wanted to invite Lipper to a party.

Lipper attended. He met privately with Epstein, who put millions of Wexner’s dollars into one of Lipper’s funds. Epstein later claimed on a regulatory filing that he was employed by that fund—another lie to boost his credentials.

Michael Tennenbaum, Epstein’s old manager at Bear Stearns, still thinks about it more than four decades later.

“I didn’t realize,” he said, “that I was creating one of the monsters of Wall Street.”

February 17, 2026: The Price of Admission Was Silence

What Did Epstein’s Friends Know?

Claire Merchlinsky/The New York Times; Photographs by Getty

Lisa Miller’s investigation focused on a different question: What did Epstein’s elite friends know between 2009 and 2019—the decade after his conviction and before his final arrest?

Epstein had been convicted in 2008 of sex crimes with minors. His method had been reported in The New York Times: recruit girls as young as 14, persuade them to undress and massage him, force them to have sex, pay them cash.

He was a registered sex offender in New York and Florida. A matter of record.

And yet during that decade, he rebuilt his elite social network. He looked at plans for a new massage room on his private island. He chose marble for his massage room in New York.

At the same time, emails show, he was vetting young women from all over the world for their sexual attractiveness, ranking their attributes, soliciting sex.

“Very beautiful, fresh,” one scout wrote to Epstein in 2011 of a 21-year-old woman. “Nice girl, but almost no English at all,” the scout wrote of another, who was 22.

That Epstein usually traveled with an entourage of “girls”—he also called them “assistants” or “students”—was common knowledge.

Richard Branson called this entourage Epstein’s “harem.” In 2013, Branson wrote: “As long as you bring your harem!”

At least some of Epstein’s friends knew what he meant when he said “massage.”

In 2010, in an email to Boris Nikolic, then the science adviser to the Gates Foundation, Epstein said he was finishing a massage.

“With happy ending I hope,” Nikolic responded, with a winking emoji.

“I’m too impatient, happy beginning,” Epstein replied.

The emails show Epstein organizing massages for friends and connecting friends with women as favors. When Deepak Chopra complained in 2017 of a “crazy” day, Epstein replied: “I’m in Florida, but would like to send two girls.”

Kathryn Ruemmler, former White House counsel under President Obama, implicitly acknowledged she knew the difference between a massage and what Epstein engaged in, referring to it in an email as “your kind of massage.”

She also knew Epstein’s history. He sometimes sought her legal advice, and in 2015, she pointed out to him that a minor “could not legally consent to engaging in prostitution.”

But in 2017, Epstein was accompanying her as she looked at apartments.

On February 3, 2026, she issued a statement through a representative: “I had no knowledge of any ongoing criminal conduct on his part, and I did not know him as the monster he has been revealed to be.”

On February 25, she resigned from Goldman Sachs, where she was the firm’s top lawyer.

The pattern repeated across Epstein’s network. Brilliant, accomplished people—scientists, executives, academics—saw red flags and looked away.

Bill Gates wrote to colleagues in 2011 after visiting Epstein: “His lifestyle is very different and kind of intriguing although it would not work for me.”

Cognitive scientist Joscha Bach acknowledged that Epstein’s “relationship to women in his environment, especially some of his employees, seemed unfriendly at times and disrespectful.”

But Bach said he had “some conversations” with Epstein’s assistants “in which I inquired about their well being.” Nothing they told him “gave reason for concern that anything coercive or illegal could be going on.”

Social psychologist Tessa West described this as “willful inaction.” Even if guests weren’t engaging in harmful behavior, some had to have seen red flags. “They’re doing nothing about it. They’re not saying anything. They’re not discouraging it.”

Epstein’s world functioned as an “in group” with “optimal distinctiveness.” He was the man at the velvet rope, choosing who was “in.” The exclusivity had a multiplying effect. The more top-notch the company, the more people wanted in.

And Epstein had a lot to offer: soft power, opportunity, financial connections, and—crucially for academics—money in a world where academics don’t have any.

The gatherings, the properties, the amenities were designed to seduce and astonish. At Little St. James, the food was “better than any we’ve had at the Ritz,” one visitor wrote.

“The girls” were in attendance at dinner parties and on the plane. Lesley Groff, Epstein’s executive assistant, would book multiple hotel rooms when Epstein traveled.

Self-interest prompted guests to look away. And Epstein’s coded, euphemistic language gave them cover. Unless incontrovertible evidence was “literally in your face,” the provenance of “the girls” could be downgraded to an uneasy feeling or a rumor.

“Any time there is sufficient ambiguity in the behavior of a person, we are motivated to see it in a way that benefits us,” West explained.

Secrets “create a boundary between who’s in and who’s out,” said Columbia University social psychologist Michael Slepian. They enhance insiders’ sense of being chosen. “It actually makes it harder to hide. But it makes it easier to live with.”

Epstein enforced the secrecy. He reprimanded people for leaving sex toys in full view. When someone reached out directly to Mayor Bloomberg’s office to invite him to an Epstein-hosted event, Epstein was furious: “Unless necessary I always prefer that the fewer people that know the better.”

The banality of the emails is striking. Can’t make 1 p.m., how about 1:30? Flying to Paris, to Palm Beach. Here are the flight manifests. Soon-Yi Previn is on her way to Pilates. Sorry to cancel.

Kurt Gray, a moral philosopher at Ohio State, explained how people find themselves complicit: “You want to be part of this group, and be this easy dude on this island. You want to be included. You don’t want to be rejected. And you’re just not thinking about these women or how they got there or their plight or their humanity. It’s this kind of myopia.”

What’s most shocking is that no one said anything.

Between February and March 2026, several more resigned: Ruemmler from Goldman Sachs. Thomas Pritzker from Hyatt Hotels. Borge Brende from the World Economic Forum.

But in 2009, 2010, 2015, 2017—when they were flying to his island, accepting his hospitality, discussing women with him in emails—they stayed silent.

The price of admission to Epstein’s world was silence.

And person after person paid it.

March 1, 2026: Why Investigations Did So Little

Decades of Tips Yielded Charges Against Only Two People

An undated photo of Jeffrey Epstein released by the Justice Department.Credit...U.S. Justice Department, via Reuters

Benjamin Weiser, Matthew Goldstein, and Mike Baker’s investigation answered perhaps the most frustrating question: Why so few prosecutions?

The timeline was damning.

1996: Maria Farmer called the FBI. She told an agent that Epstein had stolen nude photos of her sisters, ages 12 and 16. She said he’d instructed her to take photos of young girls at swimming pools. She said he’d threatened to burn her family’s house down if she told anyone.

For months, she heard nothing. The FBI and police appear to have taken no action.

Years later, FBI agents would knock on her door in North Carolina. They were investigating reports from Florida—abuse that happened in the years after her warning.

2005: Palm Beach police received a complaint from parents of a 14-year-old girl. A sprawling investigation identified as many as 27 girls and young women, ranging in age from 14 to 23.

The state charged Epstein with soliciting prostitution. The FBI began its own investigation.

Dozens of victims told federal investigators about abuse. Prosecutor Ann Marie Villafaña prepared a draft 54-page indictment charging Epstein and three female assistants with sex trafficking of minors.

It never made it to a grand jury.

Under intense pressure from Epstein’s lawyers, Villafaña’s superiors struck a deal over her objections. Epstein would avoid federal prosecution in return for pleading guilty to two state charges.

The non-prosecution agreement also pledged not to pursue charges against any “potential co-conspirators”—including the assistants named in the draft indictment.

The agreement was kept confidential.

“I don’t feel like celebrating 18 months,” Villafaña wrote to a colleague. “He should be spending 18 years in jail.”

Epstein served 13 months.

2010-2015: The Drug Enforcement Administration investigated Epstein for “illegitimate wire transfers which are tied to illicit drug and/or prostitution activities.”

Agents collected information about flights, border crossings, finances, companies. They produced a 69-page memo listing Epstein as one of 17 targets. More than 50 pages are redacted.

The investigation did not lead to charges.

2016: Lawyers representing victims met with Amanda Kramer, a federal prosecutor in Manhattan responsible for human trafficking investigations. They urged her to open a new investigation.

They said Jean-Luc Brunel—a French modeling scout who “helped get girls”—was interested in cooperating.

They said FBI agents from the Florida case were unhappy with the 2008 outcome.

Kramer spoke to a top prosecutor. They agreed she should contact an FBI supervisor and ask if the Florida agents were indeed dissatisfied.

The FBI supervisor never called back. Kramer didn’t follow up.

She took his silence to mean the agents hadn’t actually been unhappy.

No further action was taken.

2019: After The Miami Herald published articles about Epstein’s abuse and his sweetheart deal, prosecutors in New York began investigating.

They focused on conduct from the early 2000s—the same period Florida had investigated. This allowed them to build on work already done.

They adopted a “covert posture,” holding off on additional interviews out of fear the investigation might leak.

In June 2019, they charged Epstein with sex trafficking.

On July 6, he was arrested at Teterboro Airport.

On August 10, he was found hanged in his jail cell.

After Epstein’s death: Prosecutors and FBI interviewed more than three dozen accusers. Women accused more than a half-dozen of Epstein’s associates of either sexual abuse or facilitating abuse.

Some accused prominent men, including the former Prince Andrew, billionaire Leon Black, bank executive James Staley, and President Trump.

Yet prosecutors charged only one other person: Ghislaine Maxwell.

Why?

Prosecutors considered charges against Sarah Kellen, one of Epstein’s longtime assistants. But they regarded her as one of his victims.

After interviewing accusers, prosecutors didn’t find what they regarded as credible evidence of Epstein trafficking underage teenagers to anyone other than himself.

They interviewed one victim, Virginia Giuffre, who said she’d been “lent out” to prominent men. But prosecutors said they couldn’t corroborate her accounts.

“No other victim has described being expressly directed by either Maxwell or Epstein to engage in sexual activity with any other men,” prosecutors wrote in a December 2019 memo.

Federal prosecutors didn’t interview the men named in allegations. They didn’t interview executives at JPMorgan Chase, which was Epstein’s primary bank for 15 years.

They subpoenaed records from Deutsche Bank but by late 2019 seemed to have cooled on bringing charges. In a memo that December, they noted “no immediate indication of financial criminal activity or misconduct.”

They never interviewed Leslie Wexner directly—only his lawyers. The lawyers said Wexner had cut ties around 2007 after discovering Epstein had stolen at least $100 million. No further investigation into Wexner appears in the files.

They didn’t interview Epstein’s accountants. They didn’t interview Leon Black, who paid Epstein $170 million over several years for tax and estate-planning advice.

Senator Ron Wyden of Oregon, the top Democrat on the Senate Finance Committee: “To this day, there has not been a comprehensive, follow-the-money investigation of Epstein’s network performed by any federal law enforcement agency. I find that totally inexcusable.”

In twenty years, exactly two people faced consequences: Jeffrey Epstein (died in custody) and Ghislaine Maxwell (serving 20 years).

Not the co-conspirators. Not the assistants. Not the men accused by victims. Not the financial enablers.

Outside the United States, law enforcement moved swiftly. The UK arrested Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor and Peter Mandelson. Norway charged former Prime Minister Thorbjorn Jagland. France opened investigations. The World Economic Forum accepted its CEO’s resignation.

In the United States: “Full support” for the Commerce Secretary who visited Epstein’s island with his children four years after his conviction.

The Pattern Revealed

Three investigations. Three perspectives. One conclusion.

From 1976 to 2019, Jeffrey Epstein operated with near-total impunity.

He got caught lying on his job application. No consequences.

He abused expense accounts. No consequences.

He violated federal rules. Minimal consequences.

He scammed a businessman for $450,000. Won in court on technicalities.

He built relationships with billionaires by making them fear their advisers were stealing. Then he stole.

In 1996, someone called the FBI warning about photos of underage girls. No action.

In 2008, facing federal sex trafficking charges, he got a sweetheart deal that granted immunity to unnamed co-conspirators.

Between 2009 and 2019, elite friends saw the red flags and stayed silent because the price of admission was silence—and the benefits were worth it.

In 2016, when lawyers urged a new investigation and said FBI agents were unhappy with the 2008 deal, a supervisor’s silence killed it.

In 2019, after Epstein’s death, prosecutors focused narrowly on early 2000s conduct and didn’t pursue financial investigations or interview most of the powerful men named by victims.

The pattern isn’t complicated.

Hierarchical systems protect their own. Elite networks value connections over accountability. Prosecutors make strategic choices that limit exposure for powerful people. Investigations that could follow the money and expose enablers don’t happen.

And everyone who could have stopped it—who saw the red flags, who knew about the conviction, who understood what “massage” meant—stayed silent.

Because the system rewarded silence and punished accountability.

For forty-three years, it worked exactly as designed.

March 5, 2026: The Files Finally Surface

The Justice Department acknowledged on Thursday that it had also identified about a dozen other documents that were “incorrectly coded as duplicative.”Credit...Michael A. McCoy for The New York Times

And then, on March 5, 2026—Day Seven of the Iran war—the Justice Department quietly posted three FBI interview memos to their Epstein files website.

These were the three interviews that MSNBC’s Lisa Rubin and NPR had reported were missing. The ones containing accusations against President Trump. The ones that should have been released months earlier under the Epstein Files Transparency Act.

The interviews were conducted in August and October 2019, shortly after Epstein’s arrest. FBI agents spoke with a woman four times. Only the first interview memo had appeared in the public files. It made no mention of Trump.

Now, after weeks of pressure, investigations by multiple news organizations, and a congressional uproar, the Justice Department released the other three.

Here’s what they contained.

The woman told FBI agents that around 1983, when she was approximately 13 years old, Jeffrey Epstein introduced her to Donald Trump in what she described as “a very tall building with huge rooms” in either New York or New Jersey.

According to the FBI interview summaries, she alleged that Trump “forced her head down to his exposed penis.” She told agents she “bit the s*** out of it.”

In response, she said, Trump struck her and said something to the effect of “get this little b**** the hell out of here.”

These are FBI 302 forms—official interview summaries documenting what a witness told federal agents. They are not proof of what happened. They are documentation that these allegations were made, were considered serious enough for four separate FBI interviews, and were deemed important enough to be included in discovery materials given to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense team.

In the final interview, when FBI agents asked if she “felt comfortable detailing her contacts with Trump,” she reportedly asked “what the point would be of providing the information at this point in her life when there was a strong possibility nothing could be done about it.”

What the point would be.

That’s the question of a woman who has watched powerful men avoid consequences her entire life.

The Justice Department offered no explanation for why these documents had been withheld for months while other files were released. They didn’t address why documents given to Maxwell’s lawyers were deemed too sensitive for the American public.

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt called the allegations “completely baseless accusations, backed by zero credible evidence.” She attacked the accuser’s credibility by pointing to her criminal record. And she argued that “the total baselessness of these accusations is also supported by the obvious fact that Joe Biden’s department of justice knew about them for four years and did nothing with them—because they knew President Trump did absolutely nothing wrong.”

But the woman who made these allegations was interviewed by the FBI in 2019. That’s five years before the 2024 election. The claim that these were politically motivated accusations from “right before the 2020 election”—as the Justice Department had stated in January—was demonstrably false.

And NPR confirmed that even with these three interviews now public, 37 pages of documents related to this woman’s allegations remain missing from the public database. Handwritten notes from the interviews. Law enforcement reports. License records.

Thirty-seven pages still withheld.

The woman was identified in Epstein estate litigation as “Jane Doe 4.” She filed claims against Epstein’s estate for abuse that began when she was 13. Her lawsuit doesn’t name Trump, but it describes being taken to gatherings with “prominent, wealthy men” in New York—matching some of what she told the FBI.

In May 2021, she was deemed “ineligible to receive compensation” by the Epstein Victims’ Compensation Program.

So let’s be clear about what happened here.

A 13-year-old girl was allegedly abused by Jeffrey Epstein and, according to her account, sexually assaulted by Donald Trump. She told the FBI about it in four separate interviews in 2019. Those interviews were documented in official FBI memos. Those memos were given to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense team as part of discovery in a federal criminal trial.

But they were not released to the American public under a law that explicitly required their disclosure.

Until March 5, 2026.

Day Seven of a war.

After journalists calculated the missing pages by comparing document indices and consecutive numbering.

After a member of Congress called it “illegal” and demanded answers.

After three major news organizations documented exactly what was missing and where it should have been.

Only then did three of the four interview memos appear.

And even then, 37 pages remain missing.

This is what elite impunity looks like when the system finally, grudgingly, releases a fraction of what it was legally required to disclose months earlier.

Not because of moral principles. Not because of a commitment to transparency. But because the gaps in the numbering system were mathematically provable and the political cost of continued withholding finally exceeded the political cost of release.

The pattern continues.

For forty-three years, it worked exactly as designed.

And even when it breaks—even when documents surface, even when journalists document the gaps, even when Congress demands answers—it breaks incompletely, reluctantly, and only under sustained pressure.

The question the woman asked the FBI remains unanswered: What’s the point when there’s “a strong possibility nothing could be done about it”?

Three interviews released.

Thirty-seven pages still withheld.

Only two people prosecuted in twenty years.
