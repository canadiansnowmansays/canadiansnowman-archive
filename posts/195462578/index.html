The Rise and Fall of Pam Bondi

The Education of a Loyalist

Part I: How a Tampa prosecutor learned that $25,000 buys silence, and silence buys a career

April 28, 2026

Win Mcnamee / Getty Images

Series Introduction: On April 2, 2026, at 1:07 p.m., Donald Trump fired his attorney general via Truth Social. “We love Pam,” he wrote. Fourteen months. Two binders. Zero Epstein indictments. One subpoena she would never sit for. This series traces the arc of Pamela Jo Bondi — from a Tampa courtroom to the highest law enforcement office in the nation, and back again — through the lens of a single, recurring question: when the interests of justice collided with the interests of the powerful, which side did Pam Bondi choose? The answer, it turns out, was never in doubt. It was established in Florida, years before anyone handed her a binder.

I. The Mayor’s Daughter

Pam Bondi was not born into the kind of family that produces radicals.

Her father, Joseph Bondi, was a professor who served as mayor of Temple Terrace, a quiet suburb northeast of Tampa, from 1974 to 1978. Her mother, Patsy, was an elementary school teacher. The Bondis were civic-minded, respectable, embedded in the fabric of a community where public service meant showing up at zoning hearings and coaching Little League, not storming barricades.

This matters, because the conventional narrative of Pam Bondi — the one repeated at confirmation hearings and in Fox News profiles — positions her as a tough-on-crime prosecutor who rose through sheer grit from Tampa’s courthouses to the pinnacle of American law enforcement. The reality is more interesting and less flattering. She rose through social fluency, media instinct, and an early understanding of which relationships to cultivate and which investigations to quietly let die.

She graduated from King High School in Tampa, earned a criminal justice degree from the University of Florida in 1987, joined the Delta Delta Delta sorority — the connective tissue of a certain kind of Southern Republican womanhood — and got her law degree from Stetson in 1990. She joined the Hillsborough County State Attorney’s Office in 1994 and stayed for eighteen years.

Eighteen years is a long time in a single office. It is long enough to try more than fifty jury trials, to specialize in child abuse cases, to prosecute a former Major League baseball player for probation violations, and to become the office’s de facto spokeswoman — the photogenic prosecutor whom the cameras loved and the local affiliates called first.

It was also long enough to learn the most important lesson of Florida politics: what matters is not what you prosecute. It is what you decline to.

II. The Switch

Pam Bondi registered as a Democrat early in her career. Around 2000, she switched to the Republican Party, aligning herself with the conservative movement at exactly the moment it was becoming the dominant force in Florida politics.

This is not, in itself, unusual. Florida’s political landscape shifted dramatically in the late 1990s, and ambitious prosecutors recalibrated accordingly. What makes the switch worth noting is not its timing but its completeness. Bondi did not drift rightward reluctantly. She rebranded. By 2010, when she launched her campaign for attorney general, she had the endorsement of Sarah Palin, the support of the Tea Party movement, and regular appearances on Fox News and CNN as a legal analyst.

The Palm Beach Post credited her primary victory to her “media-savviness” — a polite way of saying that she understood, earlier than most Florida politicians, that the camera is not a tool for communicating information. It is a tool for communicating loyalty.

She won the 2010 general election comfortably, becoming the first woman to serve as Florida’s attorney general. She would win again in 2014, becoming the first Republican to serve two terms.

The résumé was impeccable. What it concealed was a pattern.

III. The Pattern

The pattern emerged within months of taking office, and it never changed. It had three elements: protect the powerful, punish the vulnerable, and manage the optics.

The Foreclosure Investigators (2011)

In her first year as attorney general, Bondi inherited an active investigation into Lender Processing Services — a financial services company at the centre of the robosigning scandal that had devastated Florida’s housing market. Two attorneys in her Economic Crime Division, June Clarkson and Theresa Edwards, were aggressively pursuing the case. They had given a public presentation outlining their theories about how LPS and its subsidiaries were manufacturing fraudulent foreclosure documents.

Bondi pressured both attorneys to resign.

Her office claimed they were “failing to meet expectations.” What they were actually doing was investigating a company from which Bondi had accepted campaign contributions. LPS had donated to her campaign. A former lawyer from her office was hired by LPS as Senior Vice President of Government Affairs the same week Clarkson and Edwards were shown the door.

Florida, in 2011, was ground zero of the foreclosure crisis. Families across the state were losing their homes to banks wielding documents that turned out to be fraudulent. The attorney general’s office had investigators who were following the trail. And the attorney general fired them.

This was not a scandal that destroyed Bondi’s career. It was barely a speed bump. An investigation was launched into the firings. It went nowhere. The banks got their settlement. The homeowners got the consequences.

The lesson was clear: you can fire the people investigating the powerful, and nothing happens. Remember this lesson. It will recur.

The Execution and the Fundraiser (2013)

In 2013, Bondi persuaded Governor Rick Scott to postpone a scheduled execution because it conflicted with a campaign fundraiser for her re-election.

Read that sentence again. A man on death row — a man whose date with the state’s lethal injection apparatus had been set, whose lawyers had prepared, whose family had been notified — was told to wait because the attorney general had a party to attend.

When the media discovered this, Bondi apologized. Not for the act itself, but for the appearance. The execution was rescheduled. The fundraiser went ahead. The apology was accepted. The cycle moved on.

It was, in retrospect, a small thing. But small things reveal large truths. Bondi did not see the execution and the fundraiser as a moral conflict. She saw them as a scheduling conflict. The difference tells you everything you need to know about how she would later handle matters of considerably greater consequence.

IV. Twenty-Five Thousand Dollars

And then there was Trump.

The chronology is precise, because precision is what people like Bondi rely on you not to exercise.

From 2008 to 2013, the Florida Attorney General’s Office received at least twenty-two complaints about Trump University and its affiliates — complaints from Florida consumers who believed they had been defrauded by a fake university charging up to $35,000 for real estate seminars that delivered nothing of value.

In 2013, New York Attorney General Eric Schneiderman filed a lawsuit against Trump, accusing Trump University of “engaging in persistent fraudulent, illegal and deceptive conduct.” The Orlando Sentinel asked Bondi’s office whether Florida would join the suit. Her spokeswoman told the reporter that the office was “currently reviewing the allegations.”

Four days later, a political action committee called And Justice for All — established by Bondi to support her re-election — received a $25,000 donation from the Donald J. Trump Foundation.

In 2013, the state of Florida dropped an investigation into Trump University after Donald Trump’s foundation made a donation to Attorney General Pam Bondi’s reëlection campaign.PHOTOGRAPH BY MICHELE EVE SANDBERG / CORBIS / GETTY

Florida never pursued any investigation or action against Trump or his university.

Let us be clear about what this means. Twenty-two consumers had filed complaints. New York was suing. Bondi’s own office had publicly confirmed it was “reviewing” the case. Then $25,000 arrived from a charity that was not legally permitted to make political donations — the Trump Foundation would later be ordered to pay a $2,500 fine for the violation, and in 2019, a New York court would order the foundation shut down entirely and Trump to pay $2 million for misusing it.

And the investigation died.

Bondi’s defence was multi-layered and structurally fascinating in its inadequacy. She said she was unaware of the complaints when she solicited the donation. She said she was “never investigating him. Never.” She said she would “never lie” and had been “devastated” by the suggestion of impropriety. Her former campaign consultant explained that she was “actively campaigning for re-election and asking for support from friends and supporters” — as though soliciting $25,000 from a man whose company her office was reviewing was simply the normal course of fundraising.

A bribery complaint was filed. Florida’s Governor Rick Scott — the same governor who had rescheduled an execution for Bondi’s fundraiser — handed the investigation to a southwest Florida prosecutor. The prosecutor concluded there was “insufficient” evidence to proceed. No witnesses were interviewed.

CREW — Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington — filed complaints with the IRS, the Florida Inspector General, and the Florida Ethics Commission. The IRS fined the Trump Foundation. Nobody fined Bondi. Nobody charged Bondi. Nobody investigated Bondi with anything resembling the vigour with which Bondi had declined to investigate Trump.

And here is the detail that transforms this from a Florida corruption story into a national one: in 2016, Trump settled the Trump University fraud case for $25 million. Twenty-five million dollars. The man whose case Bondi’s office was “reviewing” when the $25,000 arrived would eventually pay one thousand times that amount to make the fraud allegations go away.

Bondi’s share of the arrangement was $25,000 and a relationship with the most powerful man in Republican politics.

It was the best investment either of them ever made.

V. The Audition Years

The Trump University episode was not the end of something. It was the beginning.

Once you understand $25,000 as a down payment rather than a donation, the rest of Bondi’s Florida career reads as a prolonged audition for the role she would eventually be awarded.

In 2016, she endorsed Trump for president during the Florida primary, telling reporters she had been “friends with him for many years.” At the Republican National Convention that summer, she led the crowd in “Lock her up” chants directed at Hillary Clinton — the former first lady, senator, and secretary of state whose only crime, in that particular context, was using a private email server. Bondi, who had just declined to investigate a man paying $25 million to settle fraud charges, chanted for the imprisonment of a woman who had been investigated and cleared.

The irony was visible from space. Nobody in the convention hall noticed.

In 2018, while still serving as attorney general, she co-hosted The Five on Fox News for three consecutive days and appeared on Sean Hannity’s show. The Tampa Bay Times called it “unprecedented” for a sitting elected official to host a television program. The Florida Ethics Commission was asked whether this violated the state’s code of ethics. They declined to make a determination.

Bondi was learning — had already learned — that the rules existed for people who lacked the connections to ignore them. She was not such a person.

VI. The Intermission

Pam Bondi / Ballard Partners

In 2019, term-limited out of the attorney general’s office, Bondi joined Ballard Partners — a lobbying firm with deep connections to Trump — and registered as a foreign agent. Her client: the Embassy of the State of Qatar. Her brief: anti-human-trafficking efforts related to the 2022 World Cup.

The future attorney general of the United States, the woman who would be handed the Epstein files, the woman who would be responsible for the federal government’s response to the most consequential sex trafficking case in modern history, spent 2019 lobbying for a foreign government on human trafficking.

She also lobbied for a Kuwaiti company, for the GEO Group (a for-profit prison operator), for Amazon, for Uber, and for the Florida Sheriffs Association.

When Trump was impeached in 2020, she left the Qatari project and joined his legal defence team. When he lost the election, she promoted false claims of voter fraud on Fox News. When the host pressed her for evidence, she had none. When she claimed Trump had won Pennsylvania, he had not.

By 2024, she was running the legal arm of the America First Policy Institute, waiting for the call.

The call came on November 21, 2024 — hours after Matt Gaetz, Trump’s first choice for attorney general, withdrew amid an ethics investigation into allegations of sexual misconduct and drug use.

Pam Bondi was the second choice. She always had been. But second choices, in Trump World, sometimes last longer than first ones.

Sometimes.

VII. What Florida Taught Her

Here is what eighteen years in the Hillsborough County courthouse and eight years as Florida’s attorney general taught Pam Bondi:

That investigations are optional. That the powerful can be accommodated without anyone going to prison. That campaign contributions arrive in precisely the amounts required to make questions disappear. That executions can be rescheduled for fundraisers and the apology will be accepted. That you can fire your own investigators and face no consequences. That you can lobby for foreign governments and then be appointed to enforce American law. That you can chant “Lock her up” about someone else’s emails while your own ethical record is a monument to selective enforcement.

And that $25,000, properly placed, can purchase a relationship that ends with you standing in the Great Hall of the Justice Department as the 87th Attorney General of the United States.

What Florida did not teach her — could not have taught her — was how to survive the one moment when the powerful person she had spent a career protecting decided that she was no longer useful.

That lesson was coming. It arrived on April 2, 2026, at 1:07 p.m., in a Truth Social post that began: “We love Pam.”

Of course they did. They had to.
