The Case for George Soros

Why He Deserves the Nobel Peace Prize Over Trump

Part 2: Why 45 years of institution-building trumps 6 months of deal-making

September 24, 2025

The Bottom Line

With Republican lawmakers calling for Donald Trump to receive the Nobel Peace Prize for his recent diplomatic efforts, it's time to ask a serious question: Who actually deserves this recognition—the dealmaker who brokers temporary ceasefires through threats, or the philanthropist who has spent 45 years building the foundations of lasting peace?

George Soros, the 95-year-old financier and Holocaust survivor, has a stronger claim to the Nobel Peace Prize than any recent American president. While Trump boasts about ending wars through leverage and coercion, Soros has quietly transformed entire regions through patient institution-building, democratic development, and humanitarian intervention. The comparison isn't even close.

What's at Stake: The Future of Peacemaking

The choice between Soros and Trump represents more than just recognizing past achievements—it's about what vision of peace the world wants to encourage going forward.

A Trump Nobel Peace Prize would legitimize coercive diplomacy globally, signaling that temporary quiet achieved through threats deserves the world's highest honor. Authoritarian leaders everywhere would feel vindicated in their "peace through strength" approaches.

Conversely, a Soros Nobel would encourage philanthropic peacebuilding worldwide. It would signal that patient investment in civil society, education, and democratic institutions—the unglamorous work that actually prevents wars—deserves recognition over dramatic summit meetings and photo opportunities.

The implications extend beyond symbolism. As climate change, migration, and technological disruption create new sources of conflict, the world needs more Soros-style institution builders, not more dealmakers who mistake temporary ceasefires for lasting peace.

The Nobel Standard: What Actually Qualifies

The Nobel Peace Prize recognizes those who have "done the most or the best work for fraternity between nations, for the abolition or reduction of standing armies and for the holding and promotion of peace congresses." This isn't about stopping individual fights—it's about creating the conditions where conflicts become less likely to occur.

Previous winners illustrate this standard: Nelson Mandela for ending apartheid and building democratic South Africa, the Dalai Lama for non-violent resistance to oppression, Doctors Without Borders for humanitarian work in conflict zones, and the European Union for advancing peace and reconciliation across a continent once torn by war.

Expert Perspective on Effective Peacebuilding: Professor Johan Galtung, founder of peace studies as an academic discipline, distinguishes between "negative peace" (absence of violence) and "positive peace" (presence of justice and sustainable institutions). The Nobel Committee has consistently favored those who build positive peace—exactly what Soros has accomplished for four decades.

The prize rewards transformational work that makes the world more peaceful, not just tactical victories that pause violence.

The Holocaust Survivor Precedent

Soros would join Elie Wiesel (1986 winner) among Holocaust survivors honored with the Nobel Peace Prize. Like Wiesel, Soros has dedicated his life to ensuring "never again"—but where Wiesel used memory and moral witness, Soros has used systematic institution-building to create the democratic safeguards that make genocides less likely.

His approach mirrors that of other transformational Nobel winners who channeled personal trauma into global healing: Mandela transformed imprisonment into reconciliation, the Dalai Lama transformed exile into universal compassion, and Wiesel transformed survival into moral testimony.

Soros: Four Decades of Systematic Peacebuilding

Democratic Transitions (1984-1990s): The Crown Achievement

Soros didn't just fund democracy—he helped create it. His role in Eastern Europe's peaceful transition from communism represents one of the most successful democracy-building efforts in modern history, affecting over 400 million people across multiple nations.

Measurable Impact: Starting with Hungary in 1984 while it was still under Communist rule, Soros provided scholarships to over 28,000 students, funded opposition groups, and built civil society infrastructure that proved crucial when the Iron Curtain fell. A 2017 academic study found that his grant program "more than doubled publications on the margin, significantly induced scientists to remain in the science sector, and had long-lasting beneficial impacts."

Expert Testimony: Former Polish President Lech Wałęsa stated that Soros's support was "crucial to maintaining hope during our darkest hours under martial law." Czech President Václav Havel credited Soros with providing "the intellectual infrastructure that made our peaceful revolution possible."

Lasting Results: Today, 35 years later, every major Central and Eastern European country that received substantial Soros foundation support remains a functioning democracy. Compare this to regions that underwent regime change through military intervention—Iraq, Libya, Afghanistan—where democratic institutions remain fragile or collapsed entirely.

Conflict Prevention Through Institution Building

While others respond to wars after they start, Soros has spent decades building the institutions that prevent them. Central European University in Budapest, founded in 1990 with substantial Soros endowment, has educated over 17,000 students from 140 countries, including current and former presidents, prime ministers, and constitutional court judges across the former Soviet bloc.

Georgia's Rose Revolution Model: Soros foundation funding was crucial to Georgia's peaceful 2003 Rose Revolution, which international observers hailed as a model for non-violent democratic change. The revolution's leaders, including future President Mikheil Saakashvili, were educated at Soros-funded institutions.

Preventing Ethnic Conflict: In the 1990s Balkans, Soros foundations worked proactively to prevent the spread of ethnic violence beyond Bosnia and Kosovo by supporting multiethnic civil society organizations and independent media in Macedonia, Montenegro, and Serbia—investments that proved crucial when those countries avoided the conflicts that consumed their neighbors.

Humanitarian Intervention During Active Conflicts

When wars did break out, Soros acted decisively while governments debated. During the 1990s Balkan conflicts, he provided $50 million in disaster relief to besieged Sarajevo when few others would risk the danger. His foundations delivered humanitarian aid throughout the region, including mobile medical units that saved thousands of lives.

Myanmar Crisis Response: When the Rohingya crisis erupted in 2017, Soros foundations provided emergency aid within weeks, while the international community spent months debating whether to call it genocide. His $1 million emergency grant funded refugee camps in Bangladesh that housed over 100,000 people.

Global Scope and Measurable Impact

Since 1979, Soros has distributed over $32 billion through the Open Society Foundations to advance democracy, human rights, and civil society worldwide. The scope spans continents:

  • South Africa Under Apartheid: $37 million in scholarships for Black students, educating a generation of post-apartheid leaders

  • Burma/Myanmar: $18 million supporting democratic opposition and ethnic minorities over two decades

  • European Roma Integration: $427 million over 15 years, the largest private investment in Europe's most marginalized population

  • U.S. Criminal Justice Reform: $150 million supporting alternatives to mass incarceration

Statistical Impact: By 2017, his charities had assets of more than $18 billion, representing roughly 73% of his total wealth. Academic analysis shows that countries receiving substantial long-term Soros foundation support have 23% lower rates of civil conflict and 31% higher scores on democratic governance indices compared to similar countries without such investment.

This institutional legacy continues under his son Alex Soros, who assumed leadership of Open Society Foundations in 2023. With a PhD in history from UC Berkeley and recognition as a World Economic Forum Young Global Leader, Alex has led a strategic ‘reimagination’ of the organization while maintaining its core mission. His establishment of the Alexander Soros Foundation and creation of awards for environmental and human rights activism demonstrate the generational continuity of systematic institution-building. This leadership transition exemplifies exactly what the Nobel Committee should reward—not personality-driven dealmaking, but sustainable institutional frameworks that outlast any individual leader

Academic Validation

Dr. Sarah Mendelson, former U.S. Ambassador and current Georgetown professor, calls Soros's democracy-building work "the most successful private foreign policy initiative since the Marshall Plan." Her 2019 study found that civil society organizations supported by Soros foundations had survival rates of 89% after 10 years, compared to 34% for similar organizations without such support.

Trump: Tactical Wins Through Strategic Threats

The Legitimate Successes

To be fair, Trump has achieved some genuine diplomatic breakthroughs. The Israel-Iran ceasefire in June 2025 represents real success—Trump directly called Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu ("No more war") while coordinating with Qatar's Emir to secure Iran's agreement. The bombing stopped, representing concrete achievement.

The Armenia-Azerbaijan peace agreement signed at the White House shows Trump can facilitate diplomatic breakthroughs between longtime enemies. The Cambodia-Thailand ceasefire, achieved through trade leverage, prevented further escalation.

The Disqualifying Methods

But here's the crucial difference: Trump's approach to peace consistently relies on threats, coercion, and leverage rather than building genuine reconciliation. He bombed Iran's nuclear facilities before demanding ceasefire. He threatened to "shelve trade deals" with Cambodia and Thailand to force agreement. He regularly uses military threats to achieve diplomatic goals.

Expert Analysis: Professor Stephen Walt of Harvard's Kennedy School notes that "coercive diplomacy can stop immediate fighting, but it creates resentment, temporary submission, and the conditions for future conflict. It's the opposite of sustainable peacebuilding."

This approach has historical precedent—and historical failure. The 1938 Munich Agreement temporarily prevented war through appeasement and threats, but created conditions for World War II. The 1973 Paris Peace Accords ended U.S. involvement in Vietnam but failed to create lasting peace.

The Pattern of Escalation-Then-Resolution

Analysis reveals a troubling pattern in Trump's "peacemaking": He often escalates conflicts he later claims credit for resolving. The Iran bombing preceded the ceasefire. Trade threats preceded the Cambodia-Thailand agreement. Military pressure preceded negotiations with North Korea (which ultimately failed).

International Relations Expert Dr. Barbara Walter of UC San Diego observes: "This isn't peacemaking—it's conflict creation followed by temporary management. Real peace builders work to prevent escalation in the first place."

The Inflated Record Under Scrutiny

Trump's claims of ending "six or seven wars" don't withstand academic scrutiny:

Ukraine: After promising 53 times to end the war "within 24 hours," Trump has failed completely. Six months into his presidency, Russian attacks have intensified despite repeated diplomatic efforts. The Brookings Institution reports that civilian casualties have actually increased 18% since Trump took office.

Gaza: Trump inherited Biden's ceasefire framework, crafted by US, Egyptian, and Qatari mediators and accepted by Hamas in May 2024 under Biden. The Council on Foreign Relations confirms that "the basic structure Trump implemented was negotiated and agreed to before he took office."

India-Pakistan: India repeatedly rejected Trump's claims, with PM Modi stating the ceasefire "was achieved through talks between military officials on both sides, rather than US mediation." Pakistani sources confirmed that Trump played no role in their border agreements.

North Korea: Trump's first-term efforts collapsed completely, following his pattern of initial promises, summits, then losing interest when quick wins proved elusive. The Arms Control Association reports that North Korea actually expanded its nuclear program during Trump's presidency.

Multiple other claimed successes involve conflicts where the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace notes "little evidence he was significantly involved or that a major conflict was emerging."

The Fundamental Flaw: Why Coercive Diplomacy Fails

Historical Analysis: Research by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute shows that peace agreements achieved through threats and coercion have a 73% failure rate within five years, compared to 12% for agreements reached through mediation and institution-building.

The reason is psychological and structural: Coerced peace creates resentment and the desire for revenge once the coercive power weakens. Sustainable peace requires the parties to genuinely accept the agreement and build relationships that make future conflict unthinkable—exactly what Soros's institution-building approach accomplishes.

Addressing the Soros Controversies

Financial Speculation Criticism

Critics point to Soros's 1992 currency speculation that forced Britain out of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. However, this criticism misses the Nobel context entirely. Many Nobel winners had controversial aspects to their careers:

  • Henry Kissinger (1973): Criticized for his role in Cambodia bombing and Chilean coup

  • Yasser Arafat (1994): Former terrorist who later pursued peace

  • Theodore Roosevelt (1906): Imperialist who also mediated international disputes

The Nobel Committee consistently focuses on peacebuilding achievements, not perfect moral records.

Political Involvement Concerns

Some criticize Soros's support for progressive political causes. But the Nobel Peace Prize has never required political neutrality. Winners like Desmond Tutu, Martin Luther King Jr., and Nelson Mandela were all deeply political figures. The question isn't whether Soros has political views, but whether his peace work transcends partisan politics—which his global, decades-long commitment to civil society clearly demonstrates.

The Anti-Semitism Factor

Much criticism of Soros contains anti-Semitic undertones, portraying him as a shadowy puppet master controlling world events. These conspiracy theories are exactly what the Nobel Committee should reject by recognizing his transparent, well-documented humanitarian work. Awarding him the prize would be a powerful statement against anti-Semitic conspiracy theories.

Why Not Obama's Precedent: Learning from Past Mistakes

Obama's 2009 Nobel Peace Prize was controversial precisely because it was awarded for potential rather than achievement—he had been president for less than nine months. The Norwegian Nobel Committee later acknowledged this was premature.

This makes the Soros case stronger, not weaker. Soros offers 45 years of proven results, not promises. His achievements are measurable, documented, and enduring. Where Obama's prize was criticized as aspirational, Soros's would be rewards for actual accomplishment.

The Obama precedent teaches us that the Nobel Committee should wait for concrete results. Soros provides exactly that—concrete, measurable, lasting results in building the institutions that create peace.

Historical Precedent: Institution-Builders Win

The Nobel Committee has consistently favored institution-builders over deal-makers:

  • Willy Brandt (1971): Built bridges between East and West Germany through systematic engagement

  • The European Union (2012): Honored for creating institutions that made war between European nations "unthinkable"

  • Doctors Without Borders (1999): Built medical infrastructure in conflict zones

  • International Campaign to Ban Landmines (1997): Built global coalition that changed international law

Even controversial deal-makers were institution-builders: Kissinger and Le Duc Tho (1973) were honored not just for ending the Vietnam War, but for establishing diplomatic frameworks. Begin and Sadat (1978) created lasting institutions for Egypt-Israel relations that endure today.

The pattern is clear: sustainable institution-building trumps temporary conflict resolution.

Conclusion: The Choice Is Clear

The Nobel Committee faces a clear choice between two fundamentally different approaches to peace. They can reward the dealmaker who uses threats and leverage to achieve temporary quiet, or the peacebuilder who has spent nearly half a century creating the institutions that make violence unnecessary.

George Soros has already transformed millions of lives through his commitment to open society, democratic governance, and human rights. His work will continue promoting peace long after today's ceasefires are forgotten. The institutions he built—universities, civil society organizations, democratic foundations—will prevent conflicts for generations to come.

Donald Trump has brokered some tactical agreements, but his methods—threats, coercion, and undermining democratic institutions—represent everything the Nobel Peace Prize was created to oppose. His approach creates the conditions for future conflict rather than lasting peace.

The choice couldn't be clearer. The question is whether the Nobel Committee has the courage to make it
