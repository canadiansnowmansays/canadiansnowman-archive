“MY OWN MORALITY”

Trump Says Only He Can Limit His Global Power

January 8, 2026

In Two-Hour NYT Interview, President Declares International Law Applies Only When He Decides It Does

January 8, 2026

THE INTERVIEW: TWO HOURS OF TRUMP UNFILTERED

Wednesday evening, four New York Times reporters entered the Oval Office for what became a nearly two-hour conversation with President Trump, followed by a tour of the White House residence.

The setting: Trump behind the Resolute Desk. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Vice President JD Vance nearby. A file marked “TOP SECRET” visible on the desk. Behind Trump, a photo of Don Jr. crouched under the Resolute Desk—frame engraved “FAVORITE CHILD.”

The interview showcased multiple Trump personas:

  • The world leader (taking hour-long call from Colombia’s Petro mid-interview)

  • The gracious host (summoning valets with Diet Coke)

  • The builder (showing off $400 million ballroom model)

  • The grudge holder (complaining about Nobel Prize, lack of credit)

  • The strongman (declaring his power limited only by his own morality)

But the most revealing moment came when reporters asked about limits on his global power.

“MY OWN MORALITY. MY OWN MIND.”

The question: Are there any limits on your global powers?

Trump’s answer: “Yeah, there is one thing. My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

Not:

  • International law

  • Treaties

  • Congressional authorization

  • NATO obligations

  • UN Charter

  • Geneva Conventions

Just: “My own morality.”

Trump continued: “I don’t need international law. I’m not looking to hurt people.”

The framing is revealing: International law is something he doesn’t “need”—not something that constrains him, but something he can choose to invoke or ignore based on his judgment.

But What IS Trump’s Morality?

Trump says his power is limited only by “my own morality.” But what is that morality? What principles guide it? What constraints does it impose?

The historical record offers little evidence of moral restraint:

Trump has been found liable for sexual abuse. Convicted of falsifying business records. Indicted in four jurisdictions on 91 criminal counts. Impeached twice. His charity foundation was dissolved for self-dealing. His university settled fraud claims for $25 million.

He mocked a disabled reporter. Separated migrant children from parents at the border. Called developing nations “shithole countries.” Attacked Gold Star families. Incited a violent attack on the Capitol that left five dead.

This week alone, his “morality” permitted:

  • Killing 80+ Venezuelan and Cuban defenders

  • Threatening Colombia with invasion

  • Attacking Republican senators who voted against him

  • Claiming personal control of another nation’s oil revenues

  • Announcing indefinite U.S. occupation of Venezuela

When Trump says his power is limited only by his “own morality,” the obvious question is: What morality?

And the obvious answer, based on decades of public behavior: None that has ever constrained him before.

This isn’t a president saying: “My conscience guides me, my principles restrain me, my values determine limits.”

This is a president saying: “Nothing constrains me except my own judgment. And I judge I can do whatever I want.”

“My own morality” isn’t a constraint. It’s the absence of constraint dressed up as principle.

80+ killed in Venezuela. Trump: “We didn’t have a Jimmy Carter disaster.”

Colombia threatened with invasion. Trump: “Sounds good.”

International law? Trump: “I don’t need international law.”

Congressional authorization? Vance: “Fake and unconstitutional.”

The only “morality” evident in Trump’s actions is the morality of power: If I can do it, it’s justified. If it serves my interests, it’s right. If it demonstrates strength, it’s moral.

This is Nietzschean “might makes right” philosophy. Not moral constraint. Moral license.

When Trump says “my own morality” is the only limit on his power, he’s not proposing a high standard. He’s rejecting any standard except his own will.

“IT DEPENDS WHAT YOUR DEFINITION OF INTERNATIONAL LAW IS”

Reporters pressed: Does your administration need to abide by international law?

Trump: “I do.”

But then the qualifier: “It depends what your definition of international law is.”

This is the crucial statement. Trump acknowledges international law exists. But he claims the authority to decide:

  • What counts as international law

  • When it applies

  • How to interpret it

In other words: International law constrains Trump only when Trump decides it does.

The New York Times’ analysis: “At its core is the concept that national strength, rather than laws, treaties and conventions, should be the deciding factor as powers collide.”

Translation: Might makes right. American power is the only law that matters. And Trump decides how to use that power.

THE WORLDVIEW: STRENGTH OVER LAW

Trump tells The Times that U.S. oversight of Venezuela could last years

Trump’s assessment, per the Times, was “the most blunt acknowledgment yet of his worldview.”

The core principle: National strength—not laws, treaties, conventions—should decide outcomes when powers collide.

Applied to Venezuela: U.S. is stronger than Venezuela. Therefore, U.S. can invade, capture leader, seize oil, control government.

Applied to Greenland: U.S. is stronger than Denmark. Therefore, U.S. can demand Denmark sell Greenland, threaten military force if refused.

Applied to Colombia: U.S. is stronger than Colombia. Therefore, U.S. can threaten invasion until leftist president capitulates.

The pattern is consistent: American strength justifies American action. International law is irrelevant when the U.S. decides to act.

GREENLAND VS NATO: “IT MAY BE A CHOICE”

Reporters asked Trump: What’s your higher priority—obtaining Greenland or preserving NATO?

Trump declined to answer directly. But he acknowledged: “It may be a choice.”

Then clarified: NATO is “essentially useless without the United States at its core.”

Translation: If obtaining Greenland means breaking NATO, Trump would consider it. Because NATO without America is worthless. And America—under Trump—decides what matters.

This is not a president who sees NATO as alliance of equals. It’s a president who sees NATO as dependent on American power, valuable only insofar as it serves American interests.

And Greenland? Trump was explicit: “Ownership is very important. Ownership gives you things and elements that you can’t get from just signing a document.”

Not a treaty. Not a lease. Ownership. Like real estate. Because that’s how Trump sees sovereignty: property to be bought, seized, or controlled.

VENEZUELA PRECEDENT: “THIS WAS A REAL THREAT”

Reporters pressed Trump on Venezuela creating precedent for China seizing Taiwan or Russia seizing Ukraine.

Trump dismissed the comparison: “This was a real threat. You didn’t have people pouring into China,” referring to his claim that Maduro “dumped gang members” into the U.S.

The logic:

  • Venezuela threatened U.S. (with migrants, drugs, gangs)

  • Therefore, U.S. invasion was justified

  • China/Russia invasions would be different (not responding to “real threats”)

But who decides what counts as “real threat”? Trump does.

China claims Taiwan is separatist threat. Trump: “That’s up to him, what he’s going to be doing.”

Then Trump added: Xi “may do it after we have a different president, but I don’t think he’s going to do it with me as president.”

The deterrent, in Trump’s view, is his unpredictability. Not international law. Not treaties. Not norms. Just Trump’s willingness to act and Xi’s uncertainty about how Trump might respond.

THE PETRO CALL: COERCIVE DIPLOMACY IN ACTION

President Gustavo Petro of Colombia during an interview with The New York Times in Cartagena on Wednesday.Credit...Nathalia Angarita for The New York Times

Wednesday evening, mid-interview, Trump took a call from Colombia’s President Petro.

The call lasted about an hour—unusually long for Trump and another head of state.

Vice President Vance and Secretary Rubio pulled chairs up to the Resolute Desk. Trump put finger to lips, signaling reporters to be quiet. The call’s contents were off-the-record.

But the Times reporters witnessed coercive diplomacy in action:

Petro, who had told the Times hours earlier “we are in danger” and planned to sleep with Simón Bolívar’s sword, was calling Trump after days of threats.

After the call: Trump dictated a Truth Social post to an aide. “It was a Great Honor to speak with President Petro of Colombia, who called to explain the situation of drugs and other disagreements that we have had. I appreciated his call and tone.”

Notice the framing: Petro “called to explain.” As if Petro sought out Trump to apologize, clarify, accommodate.

Actually: Trump called Petro. But the narrative became: Petro capitulated.

Later, Trump asked the Times reporters: “Do you think Biden could do that?”

The answer he wanted: No. Biden couldn’t intimidate a foreign president into an hour-long phone call mid-interview with the New York Times.

This is Trump’s definition of success: Using threat of force to coerce other nations. Then taking credit when they capitulate.

THURSDAY: THE SENATE REBUKES TRUMP

Sen. Tim Kaine, D-Va., speaks to reporters about a war powers resolution regarding Venezuela on Capitol Hill, Wednesday, Jan. 7, 2026, in Washington. (AP Photo/Mariam Zuhaib)

Thursday afternoon, the Senate voted 52-47 to advance a War Powers resolution for floor debate next week.

The resolution would require Trump to seek congressional authorization for continued military operations in Venezuela.

Five Republicans broke ranks to vote with Democrats:

  • Susan Collins (Maine) - up for re-election 2026

  • Lisa Murkowski (Alaska)

  • Rand Paul (Kentucky)

  • Josh Hawley (Missouri)

  • Todd Young (Indiana)

Senator Rand Paul: One factor that “tipped the vote” was Trump’s comment to the Times that the U.S. might remain involved in Venezuela for years.

Senator Chuck Schumer: “Donald Trump, when he ran, promised no more endless wars. And yet he’s sliding straight into another one in South America.”

The vote was “a rare assertion of congressional authority over the president’s war powers.” It reflected “worries in Mr. Trump’s own party about his bellicose and seemingly open-ended action in Venezuela, undertaken without consultation or authorization by Congress.”

TRUMP’S RESPONSE: “SHOULD NEVER BE ELECTED TO OFFICE AGAIN”

Trump immediately attacked the five Republican senators on social media:

“Susan Collins, Lisa Murkowski, Rand Paul, Josh Hawley, and Todd Young should never be elected to office again.”

He accused them of:

  • Undermining national security

  • Betraying their party

  • Voting to “take away our Powers to fight and defend the United States of America”

Trump added: “They should be ashamed.”

The message to other Republicans: Cross me on war powers, lose my support, face primary challenge.

Collins is the only one up for re-election in 2026. She’s expected to face a competitive race in Maine—a state Democrats have won in the last nine presidential elections.

Trump’s attack on her could backfire (Maine voters might support senator who stands up to president) or help her Republican primary challenger.

But Trump doesn’t care. The message is the point: Defy me, face consequences.

JD VANCE: WAR POWERS ACT IS “FAKE AND UNCONSTITUTIONAL”

Thursday, Vice President JD Vance dismissed the Senate vote:

The War Powers Act is “a fake and unconstitutional law” that “is not going to change anything about how we conduct foreign policy.”

This is the administration’s position: War Powers Act violates Article II of the Constitution by limiting president’s authority as commander in chief.

The constitutional argument:

  • Article I: Congress has power to declare war

  • Article II: President is commander in chief

Presidents have long argued: Commander in chief authority allows military action without congressional declaration of war.

Congress passed War Powers Act (1973) to limit this: President can use force for 60 days without authorization, then must seek congressional approval or withdraw.

Trump’s position (like predecessors): War Powers Act is unconstitutional. President has inherent authority to use military force.

Vance’s statement makes clear: Even if Senate passes resolution, even if House passes it (unlikely), even if Trump doesn’t veto it (he would), administration will ignore it.

Because in their view, it’s “fake and unconstitutional.”

THE SENATE VOTE IS LIKELY SYMBOLIC

The Times noted: “The move on Thursday was largely symbolic, since it is unlikely the House would approve the measure even if the Senate enacted it, and Mr. Trump could always veto the resolution should it end up on his desk.”

So why does it matter?

1. Rare bipartisan rebuke. Five Republicans joined Democrats. Signals concern even within Trump’s party.

2. Puts senators on record. Collins, Murkowski, Paul, Hawley, Young now have to defend their vote (or, in Collins’s case, use it to appeal to Maine independents).

3. Establishes constitutional marker. Future Congress could use this precedent to investigate, hold hearings, cut funding.

4. Responds to Trump’s “much longer” comment. Paul said Trump’s acknowledgment to the Times that U.S. could be in Venezuela for years tipped his vote. Senate is pushing back against open-ended commitment.

But practically? The resolution likely dies. Trump continues Venezuela operations. Congress objects but doesn’t stop him.

Which validates Trump’s worldview: Power matters. Resolutions don’t.

VENEZUELA ACCOMMODATES: POLITICAL PRISONERS RELEASED

Thursday morning, Jorge Rodríguez (head of Venezuela’s National Assembly) announced: “Important number” of political prisoners being released.

Among those freed:

  • Rocío San Miguel - one of Venezuela’s most prominent political prisoners, had investigated authoritarian government. Detained nearly two years. Now at Spanish embassy with daughter.

  • Five Spanish nationals - Spain confirmed, called it “positive step in new phase Venezuela is currently undergoing”

This is accommodation in action.

Rubio’s demands to Venezuela (from Monday briefing):

  • Expel Chinese, Russian, Iranian, Cuban advisers

  • Reopen oil trade

  • Release political prisoners

  • Hand over oil

Venezuela is complying: Rodriguez releasing prisoners. Spain welcoming it. Venezuelan state TV gave short statement, took no questions, moved on to pro-Maduro rally.

The message: Venezuela accommodates Trump’s demands. Because resisting means continued blockade, possible further military action.

Trump’s worldview proved correct (from Venezuela’s perspective): American power matters. Resisting is futile. Accommodate and survive.

NINE TANKERS ESCAPE: BLOCKADE NOT ABSOLUTE

Thursday, New York Times confirmed via satellite imagery: At least nine U.S.-sanctioned tankers have evaded the naval blockade.

The escape routes:

  • Four tankers hundreds of miles into Atlantic Ocean

  • Several heading northeast through Caribbean

  • At least one changed flag to Russia

This complicates the “blockade is absolute” narrative. Some tankers are escaping.

But: The blockade is still devastating. Venezuela’s internal projections show 75% production collapse by year’s end if it holds. Nine tankers escaping doesn’t change that math—it just shows Venezuela is desperately trying everything.

And two tankers were seized Wednesday. So the score is: 9 escaped (so far), 2 captured. U.S. naval dominance is clear even if not 100% effective.

WHAT TRUMP REVEALED: THE PHILOSOPHY BEHIND THE PATTERN

Venezuela. Colombia. Greenland. The pattern has been clear for days.

What Wednesday’s interview revealed: The philosophy underlying the pattern.

Trump doesn’t see international law as constraint. He sees it as tool—something to invoke when useful, ignore when inconvenient.

Trump doesn’t see treaties as binding agreements. He sees them as “documents” inferior to “ownership.”

Trump doesn’t see NATO as alliance of equals. He sees it as dependent on U.S., valuable only insofar as it serves American interests.

Trump doesn’t see congressional authorization as constitutional requirement. He sees it as political obstacle to be overcome or ignored.

The only constraint Trump acknowledges: “My own morality. My own mind.”

And Trump’s morality says:

  • Venezuela required invasion (despite no congressional authorization)

  • Colombia deserves threats (despite being ally)

  • Greenland must become American (despite NATO ally Denmark’s ownership)

  • U.S. control of Venezuela may last years (despite no exit strategy)

  • Political prisoners should be released (happening—accommodation works)

  • International law applies only when Trump decides it does

THE SENATE’S SYMBOLIC REBUKE

Thursday’s 52-47 vote was notable. Five Republicans broke ranks. Senate will debate War Powers resolution next week.

But it’s likely symbolic. House won’t pass it. Trump would veto. Administration dismisses War Powers Act as “fake and unconstitutional.”

So what does the vote achieve?

It establishes: Even some Republicans alarmed by Trump’s “bellicose and seemingly open-ended action in Venezuela, undertaken without consultation or authorization by Congress.”

It responds to: Trump’s admission to the Times that U.S. could be involved for years.

It puts on record: Constitutional concerns about president conducting military operations without authorization.

But it doesn’t stop Trump. Because Trump’s worldview—articulated Wednesday, validated by Vance Thursday—is that presidential power as commander in chief is constrained only by president’s judgment.

“My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

The Senate disagrees. But the Senate can’t stop him either.

CONCLUSION: THE EMPEROR HAS NO CONSTRAINTS (EXCEPT HIMSELF)

Wednesday evening, President Trump told the New York Times his global power is constrained only by his “own morality.”

Thursday, the Senate pushed back: 52-47 vote advancing War Powers resolution requiring congressional authorization for Venezuela operations.

Thursday, the administration responded: JD Vance dismissed War Powers Act as “fake and unconstitutional.”

This is the defining dynamic of Trump’s second term:

Trump acts. Invades Venezuela. Threatens Colombia. Demands Greenland. Seizes tankers. Controls foreign oil revenues. Threatens NATO allies.

Congress objects. Democrats demand hearings. Some Republicans vote for War Powers resolution. Schumer warns of “endless war.”

Trump ignores them. Because in his worldview, American power—and his control of it—is the only constraint that matters.

“I don’t need international law.”

“It depends what your definition of international law is.”

“My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

Venezuela proved Trump’s willingness to act without authorization. Colombia proved the threats work. Greenland shows the pattern continues.

And Wednesday’s interview proved: This isn’t impulsive. It’s philosophical.

Trump genuinely believes American presidents—specifically, him—should be constrained only by their own judgment. Not by Congress. Not by courts. Not by treaties. Not by international law.

Just “my own morality.”

The Senate can pass resolutions. Allies can object. International law scholars can cite violations.

But Trump already answered: “I don’t need international law. I’m not looking to hurt people.”

80+ killed in Venezuela. Colombia intimidated. Greenland threatened. Years of U.S. control planned.

Trump isn’t looking to hurt people. But his morality—the only constraint he acknowledges—says these actions are justified.

And Thursday’s Senate vote, while symbolically important, likely changes nothing.

Because the emperor has spoken: His power is limited only by himself.

And he’s decided there are no limits.
