The World According to Trump

Episode 3: The Individuals Map — Loyalty, Betrayal, and the Binary That Never Resets

February 27, 2026

A series by Stephen Gordon, CanadianSnowmanSays

In the first two essays of this series, we watched Donald Trump sort the world’s 195 nations into two columns — nice and nasty, beautiful and hellhole, friends and enemies — using personal deference as the sole criterion. We then watched him apply the identical binary to American cities and states, with the same result: places that voted for him are thriving, places that didn’t are cesspools, regardless of any objective measure.

Both maps were revealing. But they shared a limitation: countries and cities don’t talk back. They don’t have press conferences. They don’t go on television to explain their reasoning. They can’t be asked, directly, why they did what they did.

People can.

Which is why the third map — Trump’s map of individuals — is the most clarifying of all. Here, the binary isn’t applied to abstractions like “Canada” or “Detroit.” It’s applied to named human beings, many of whom Trump personally selected, praised, promoted, and called the best in the world. And then sorted into the other column.

The mechanics are identical. The results are devastating.

The Vocabulary of Elevation

Before we examine the falls, we need to understand the heights — because Trump’s praise of individuals follows a remarkably consistent script, and that script makes the subsequent reversals all the more diagnostic.

When Trump elevates someone, the language is superlative and personal. They are “the best,” “a great man,” “one of the truly great.” He invests his own credibility in them — “I picked him,” “everybody agreed he was the right choice,” “people have been telling me for years how brilliant he is.” The praise is never institutional. He doesn’t say the Department of Defense chose Mattis, or that the Republican Party put forward Pence, or that the Senate confirmed Sessions. He chose them. Their greatness is a reflection of his judgment.

This matters enormously for what comes next.

The Falls

James Mattis was “the closest thing to General George Patton that we have” when Trump appointed him Secretary of Defense. By the time Mattis resigned in December 2018 — over Trump’s abrupt withdrawal from Syria and his treatment of allies — he had become “the world’s most overrated general,” a man who was “not too good at his job” and had been “essentially fired.” The same human being. The same record. The only variable: Mattis had, in his resignation letter, pointedly said Trump’s worldview did not align with his own.

Jeff Sessions was a loyal Trump supporter before almost anyone else in the Republican establishment. Trump called his early endorsement “a very big deal.” As Attorney General, Sessions was “a great man” — until he recused himself from the Russia investigation, as the law required. From that moment, Sessions became “weak,” “beleaguered,” “very disappointing,” ultimately “the biggest mistake” of Trump’s presidency. Sessions had not changed. His record had not changed. He had simply, on one consequential matter, declined to subordinate the law to Trump’s personal interest.

William Barr ran the Justice Department in a way that drew widespread criticism for its deference to the White House. Then, after the 2020 election, Barr publicly stated that the DOJ had found no evidence of widespread fraud sufficient to change the outcome. He was gone within weeks, transformed from loyal lieutenant to irrelevant figure, later dismissed by Trump as “a disappointment in every sense of the word.”

Mark Milley, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, was described in glowing terms during his appointment. By the end, Trump said Milley had committed “an act so egregious that, in times gone by, the punishment would have been death” — because Milley had, among other things, called his Chinese counterpart to provide reassurance during a period of heightened tension. Trump described this as treason. Military historians described it as standard protocol.

Mike Pence served four years of unflinching loyalty, rarely contradicting Trump publicly, absorbing indignities that would have broken most politicians. On January 6, 2021, Pence declined to unilaterally overturn the electoral count — something he had no constitutional authority to do regardless. The crowd that stormed the Capitol chanted for his execution. Trump, asked whether he was worried about Pence’s safety as the mob surged through the building, reportedly said he deserved it.

The pattern is not complicated. Each of these men was elevated by Trump, praised by Trump, described as exceptional by Trump. Each of them, at a single consequential moment, declined to prioritize Trump’s personal interest above their constitutional or legal obligations. Each of them was subsequently destroyed in Trump’s public language with the same ferocity he applies to declared enemies.

Today, the pattern produced its most dramatic case study yet — and its most clarifying, because it happened in real time, to identifiable individuals, on a matter of historic significance.

The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that Trump’s sweeping tariffs, imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, were unconstitutional. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote the majority opinion, joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson — Democratic appointees — and by two of Trump’s own nominees: Neil Gorsuch and Amy Coney Barrett.

Justices Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh dissented.

Trump’s response, delivered at the White House within hours of the ruling, stripped the binary bare:

He praised Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh for their “strength and wisdom and love of our country.” He called the six in the majority “a frankly disgrace to our nation” — “unpatriotic,” “disloyal to the Constitution,” “swayed by foreign interests.” He suggested, without evidence, that foreign powers had corrupted the court’s majority through “fear or respect or friendships.”

When asked specifically about Gorsuch and Barrett — his own appointees, confirmed through his own political capital, reliable conservative votes across dozens of cases in both terms — Trump said: “I think their decision was terrible. I think it’s an embarrassment to their families.”

An embarrassment to their families.

Gorsuch and Barrett did not change their judicial philosophy today. They did not become liberals. They did not abandon originalism or depart from the conservative legal tradition that earned them their seats. By the standards of any objective legal analysis, they ruled on the merits of whether IEEPA authorizes peacetime tariffs of unlimited scope. They concluded it does not.

Trump does not read opinions. He reads votes.

And so two of the most consequential conservative jurists of their generation became, in the span of a single afternoon, the judicial equivalent of Canada — a place that was once manageable and is now “very nasty to deal with.” Their decades of distinguished service, their intellectual records, their prior rulings in his favor: none of it accumulated into protection. The loyalty account resets with every consequential decision, and today’s balance was zero.

He added, of the State of the Union address scheduled for the following week — an event where tradition places the justices in the front row: “I couldn’t care less if they come.”

The Criterion Has Always Been the Same

What the individuals map makes visible — more clearly than either the national or domestic maps — is that Trump’s binary has no ideological content whatsoever.

This requires emphasis, because it runs counter to the conventional partisan framing. The conventional framing says Trump is a conservative, his enemies are liberals, and his conflicts with individuals like Mattis or Barr or now Gorsuch and Barrett represent ideological friction. This framing is wrong.

Mattis was a decorated general with a distinguished conservative record. Sessions was one of the most reliably conservative members of the Senate. Barr was a committed institutionalist who had served Republican administrations for decades. Gorsuch and Barrett are constitutional originalists appointed precisely because of their conservative jurisprudential philosophy.

None of that mattered. What mattered was whether, at the moment of maximum consequence, they chose Trump’s personal interest over their institutional obligations. When they did, they were “the best.” When they didn’t, they were disgraces.

This is the individuals map in its final form: not a political map, not an ideological map, not even a partisan map. It is a loyalty map, where loyalty is defined not as shared values or long service or consistent support, but as the capacity to subordinate everything — law, conscience, constitutional obligation, professional judgment — to one man’s immediate needs.

And crucially: it can never be permanently passed. Mattis passed it for two years. Sessions passed it for a year and a half. Pence passed it for four. Gorsuch and Barrett passed it for dozens of cases. None of that mattered on the day they didn’t.

What the Three Maps Tell Us

We now have three complete maps.

The international map sorts 195 nations by whether their governments show Trump personal deference. Norway is nice. Canada is nasty. Strongmen who flatter him are friends. Democratic allies who maintain their independence are problems.

The domestic map sorts American cities and states by whether they voted for him. Red states are thriving, whatever their actual economic indicators say. Blue cities are cesspools, whatever their actual quality-of-life data shows.

The individuals map sorts named human beings — generals, attorneys general, vice presidents, Supreme Court justices — by whether they, at the moment of maximum consequence, chose him over their obligations. Prior loyalty is irrelevant. Institutional role is irrelevant. Constitutional duty is irrelevant. Only the last vote counts.

What all three maps share is the absence of any external reference point. A genuine worldview — even a flawed or mistaken one — measures the world against something: values, interests, ideology, history, data. Trump’s maps measure everything against a single fixed point: Trump himself. Countries are good or bad based on their relationship to him. Cities are thriving or dying based on whether they supported him. People are brilliant or disgraces based on whether they served him.

This is what we mean when we say the world according to Trump isn’t a worldview. A worldview looks outward. This looks inward, always, at the same fixed point, and projects what it sees onto everything else.

A Note From Outside the Map

A Canadian observer — whose country has spent the better part of a year being sorted, unsorted, threatened, and re-sorted on Trump’s international map — watches today’s events with recognition more than surprise.

Canada knows what it feels like to do something entirely consistent with its own laws, its own sovereignty, its own obligations — and to be told that this is “nasty,” that it demonstrates a lack of respect, that it has consequences. Canada knows that prior goodwill does not accumulate into protection. Canada knows that the relationship resets not annually or quarterly but with every significant decision, and that the criterion is always the same.

Gorsuch and Barrett now know it too.

The difference is that Canada is a sovereign nation with resources, alliances, and options. Supreme Court justices have lifetime appointments and cannot be removed for their rulings. In both cases, Trump’s fury is real and his capacity to act on it is limited by structures he did not build and cannot easily dismantle.

That is, perhaps, the most important thing the three maps tell us about the next three years: the binary is absolute, but the power is not. The sorting never stops. The question is whether the institutions being sorted hold their shape.
