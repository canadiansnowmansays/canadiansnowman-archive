The Iran Crisis

Going Swimmingly

Part LXVI Day 48: The ceasefire expires Tuesday. Lebanon has a 10-day truce. The blockade is in its fifth day. The talks have not resumed. The president says it is going swimmingly.

April 17, 2026

President Trump said on Thursday that he might travel to Pakistan if a deal with Iran was reached there / Anna Rose Layden NYT

BREAKING — POSTED AFTER PUBLICATION, APRIL 17, 10 AM ET

Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi announced Friday morning that the Strait of Hormuz is “completely open” for all commercial ships following the Lebanon ceasefire. Oil prices dropped immediately — Brent fell more than 10 percent to below $90 a barrel, its lowest level in more than a month. Trump responded on Truth Social: “IRAN HAS JUST ANNOUNCED THAT THE STRAIT OF IRAN IS FULLY OPEN AND READY FOR FULL PASSAGE. THANK YOU!”

Three caveats apply. Iran said ships must take a “coordinated route” — the Larak detour running close to Iran’s coast, allowing IRGC vetting. Araghchi did not specify whether “remaining period of cease-fire” referred to the Lebanon truce or the US-Iran ceasefire expiring Tuesday. And Trump simultaneously posted that the US naval blockade “WILL REMAIN IN FULL FORCE AND EFFECT” until negotiations are “100% COMPLETE.” Martin Navias, author of Tanker Wars, was direct: “That does not equate to freedom of navigation.”

Shipping companies will not move immediately. Experts say it will take weeks, not days, before significant traffic resumes. Around 900 ships remain stranded in the Persian Gulf. The mines have not been cleared. The blockade is still running. But for the first time since February 28, the toll booth door is officially open — on Iran’s terms, on Iran’s route, for Iran’s remaining ceasefire window.

I. The Portrait

On Thursday evening, in a hotel ballroom off the Las Vegas Strip, Donald Trump told a room full of service workers that America was “having some fake inflation because of the fuel, the energy prices.” Gas was above five dollars a gallon at stations visible from the venue. The S&P 500 had just hit a record high. The war was “going swimmingly,” he said, and would end soon. He has been saying it would end soon since approximately day three.

Katie Rogers’ portrait of Trump this week in The New York Times — Jesus memes, papal attacks, Federal Reserve threats, Iran spin — is the clinical companion to the Rauch-Wehner institutional psychosis diagnosis that anchored Part LXV. In a single twelve-hour span this week, Trump promised the war was ending, picked a fight with the pope, threatened to fire the Fed chair, and posted an AI illustration of himself receiving an encouraging hug from Jesus Christ. “The Radical Left Lunatics might not like this,” he wrote, “but I think it is quite nice!!!”

Lemire of The Atlantic provides the political prognosis that frames the week. Trump’s approval stands at 39 percent. Republicans privately say the House is already lost. The Senate is now in play — Democrats eyeing North Carolina, Maine, Ohio, Iowa, Alaska. Gas above five dollars in Las Vegas on the day Trump calls it fake inflation. His attack on Pope Leo — “weak on crime,” “catering to the radical left” — has alienated Italian Prime Minister Meloni, who suspended Italy’s defence pact with Israel and told Trump the papal attack was “unacceptable.” Trump responded: “I thought she had courage. I was wrong.” Leo’s response to the Truth Social post remains the line of the week: “It’s ironic — the name of the site itself. Say no more.”

Republican departures from the House this cycle are surpassed only by 2018, when the party lost forty seats and the majority. Senator Tillis of North Carolina, not running for re-election, said of the papal episode: “When you’re wrong, you’re wrong.” Majority Leader Thune told reporters to “let the church be the church.”

II. The Lebanon Ceasefire: A Pause Inside a Pause

The ten-day truce between Israel and Lebanon, announced by Trump on Thursday night and in effect as of midnight Friday, is the most significant diplomatic development since the Islamabad talks collapsed. It removes — for now — the most immediate obstacle to resuming US-Iran negotiations. Iran had made Lebanon’s inclusion a condition of continued talks. That condition is now, at least partially, met.

The scenes on Lebanon’s coastal highway Friday morning were both joyful and heartbreaking. Thousands of displaced families flooded southward the moment the truce took effect, many learning from neighbours still at home that their houses had been destroyed. Abbas Shami, 40, drove south with three mattresses tied to his car roof with yellow string — for sleeping outside, he said, if the village was gone. Israa Jaber, 54, made the journey with her nine-year-old daughter Lamis, who missed her teddy bears and her makeup, left behind in the rush to flee six weeks earlier. “If we have to leave again, I can’t describe how disappointing it would be,” Jaber said. “It would be devastating.”

Israel had bombed all the main bridges over the Litani River. The workaround was a single-lane dirt crossing, producing a traffic jam stretching miles. The Lebanese military was using excavators to repair a bridge Israel struck at 11:56 PM Thursday — four minutes before the truce went into effect. Rescue workers in Tyre recovered fourteen bodies from buildings hit in those final four minutes.

The Halbfinger analysis in the Times is essential: Netanyahu is in an “uncomfortable spot,” forced into a ceasefire by Washington before his stated goal of gutting Hezbollah was achieved, criticised by his own right flank, and unable to celebrate what Trump is celebrating. Gadi Eisenkot, former military chief now leading a centrist opposition party gaining in polls, said a pattern was “emerging in which cease-fires are being imposed on us — in Gaza, in Iran, and now in Lebanon.” Aaron David Miller was direct: “Netanyahu influenced how the war started. He won’t influence how it ends.”

Israeli army vehicles and excavators demolishing houses in the southern Lebanese village of Meiss El Jabal on Friday.Credit...Jalaa Marey/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Israeli Defence Minister Katz stated flatly that Israel’s goals had not been achieved, and that undemilitarised areas of southern Lebanon “would have to be done, either diplomatically or by resuming Israeli military activities.” Netanyahu announced an “expanded security zone” from the Mediterranean coast to the Syrian border, south of the Litani. On camera: “We are not leaving.” Israeli excavators were demolishing Lebanese border villages as the ceasefire took effect.

Hezbollah did not directly accept or reject the truce. Its statement warned that “the hands of these fighters will remain on the trigger.” Analysts say the group has little incentive to resume attacks given the humanitarian toll on its base. Its political calculation is unchanged: the Lebanese government does not control Hezbollah, disarmament demands will test Lebanon’s domestic stability to its limits, and Hezbollah has rejected direct talks with Israel categorically.

Ward of the Times states Lebanon’s dilemma with precision: push on Hezbollah disarmament and risk civil rupture; fail to push and watch Israel’s war resume. “You can’t build long-term peace under the barrel of a gun,” said Maha Yahya of Carnegie Middle East Center.

Ten days is not a resolution. It is a pause inside a pause.

III. The Blockade: Working and Not Working

CENTCOM declared this week that the blockade had “completely halted” Iranian maritime trade. Admiral Cooper confirmed six merchant vessels had turned back under US radio direction. More than twelve American warships are stationed in the Gulf of Oman. Iran’s oil exports by sea appear genuinely disrupted.

But the picture is more complicated beneath the surface. Maritime intelligence firms report ships going dark, using zombie identification numbers, falsifying transponder data — the full Russian sanctions-evasion playbook, now operating in Hormuz. “Right now, the strait is a contested information environment,” said Erik Bethel of Mare Liberum. “A blockade is only as strong as the intelligence behind the interdictions.” Hapag-Lloyd, the German shipping giant with six vessels stranded in the Persian Gulf, said it had received no official communication from US forces on how to ensure safe passage. “We need to know: Is it safe?” said spokesman Nils Haupt. “Have all the mines been removed?”

Iran’s military responded to the blockade by threatening to expand its authority beyond Hormuz to the Persian Gulf, Sea of Oman and Red Sea — an escalation in rhetoric if not yet in capability. Gholz of Notre Dame provides the clearest capability assessment: Iran can still use mines and fast boats to harass Hormuz traffic, but its reach into the Red Sea depends on the Houthis, whose capabilities have been significantly degraded. “They can harass some ships,” Gholz said, “but they probably can’t enforce a blockade.”

President Trump meeting with China’s leader, Xi Jinping, in South Korea last October. They will meet again next month.Credit...Haiyun Jiang/The New York Times

Iran’s countermove has focused on economics. The US is trying to block roughly 2 million barrels of Iranian daily oil exports — about 2 percent of global supply. China, which buys 90 percent of those exports, has called the blockade “dangerous and irresponsible.” The Sanger-Pager piece in the New York Times on the US-China dimension is the week’s most important strategic analysis: Trump is expected in Beijing in four weeks, and the blockade risks confrontation between the US Navy and Chinese-flagged vessels at the worst possible diplomatic moment. Xi’s “law of the jungle” comment — his first public statement on the war — was thinly veiled and precisely targeted.

The Batmanghelidj formulation remains the cleanest summary: “Making Iran a poorer country does not necessarily make it a less dangerous country.”

IV. Iran’s Hackers Have Not Logged Off

Barnes and Volz of the Times report that Iranian cyberoperations have escalated since the ceasefire, with a 15 percent increase in attacks against Israel and 10 percent across the Gulf region since April 8. The Handala group, controlled by Iran’s Ministry of Intelligence and Security, hacked and leaked accounts linked to the former IDF chief of staff. An Iran-affiliated group caused a global shutdown at Stryker, a major medical equipment supplier. FBI Director Kash Patel’s personal emails were breached and published. Iran is also targeting US municipal water and power systems — not for immediate disruption, but for positioning. “In cyberwarfare there isn’t really a cease-fire,” said Evan Peña of Armadin.

Demonstrators in Tehran this week. Iran is trying to keep up pressure on the United States and Israel with cyberattacks but also positioning itself to mount a bigger retaliation if peace talks do not resume.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

Iran’s military announced this week that drone production had increased tenfold since last June’s 12-day war. The country is not demobilising. It is preparing for whatever comes next.

V. The Nuclear Gap

Reporting in The New York Times, Sanger, Pager and Fassihi confirmed this week what the Islamabad failure suggested: the US position in those talks was not permanent zero enrichment, but a 20-year suspension. Iran’s formal response, delivered to Washington through Pakistani channels, was five years — identical to its Geneva offer in February, the offer that convinced Trump to order the bombing. Thirty-eight days of war appear to have produced no movement on the nuclear question.

Troianovski’s reporting in The Times confirms the analytical consensus: the war has increased IRGC internal power, not reduced it. Trump’s “new regime/pretty reasonable” spin on Fox Business this week is disconnected from documented reality. The new supreme leader has not been seen in public since taking power. Swanson, a former Trump Iran negotiator now at the Atlantic Council, says the outcome will most likely be “small and transactional.” On Ghalibaf’s supposed pragmatism: “It’s not in Ghalibaf’s or anyone else’s interest to stray from the party line right now.”

President Trump in Miami last week. Mr. Trump has declared a “regime change” accomplishment in Iran, even though analysts believe the war may have only increased the internal sway of Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

VI. Pakistan’s Shuttle Diplomacy

Field Marshal Munir arrived in Tehran Wednesday — the first regional leader to visit Iran since February 28. Pakistani state media said he carried “a new message from the United States.” The content was not disclosed. Pakistan’s foreign ministry confirmed a second Islamabad round is expected but declined to provide a date. Neither Washington nor Tehran has confirmed it.

Trump said Thursday he might go to Pakistan himself “if the deal is signed there.” Pakistan has nominated him for the Nobel Peace Prize. He called Munir “my favourite field marshal.” The machinery of flattery and mediation is intact. Whether it produces a second round before Tuesday’s expiry of the ceasefire is the question that will determine whether Part LXVII is a diplomatic filing or a return-to-war filing.

VII. The Arsenal of Democracy, Reconvened

The Pentagon has held meetings with senior executives from Ford and General Motors to gauge whether the auto industry can help replenish munitions depleted by 38 days of bombing Iran. By some estimates, it could take five years to restore used Patriot interceptor stocks. The conversations are in early stages, involving components rather than complete systems. The reference point is explicit: World War II, when Ford’s Willow Run factory produced one B-24 bomber per hour at peak.

General Ferrari of the American Enterprise Institute offered the honest assessment: “We are on borrowed time. The Russians, the Chinese, the Iranians, everybody knows that we don’t have enough munitions.” The democratisation of warfare — cheap drones, sea mines, shoulder-fired missiles — has exposed the mismatch between America’s late-20th-century military and the 2020s battlefield. Pottinger said it plainly in the Frum Atlantic interview: “We are the world’s best late-20th-century military. We are not a 2020s military yet.”

VIII. Asia Scrambles

The most consequential long-term story of the week is not the Lebanon ceasefire or the blockade mechanics. It is what America’s allies in Asia are doing to survive the oil shock.

South Korea, the Philippines, Japan, India, Indonesia — one by one, each is turning to Russia and Iran. Indonesia’s president praised Putin for playing “a very positive role” in the geopolitical situation, then signed an oil deal in Moscow. South Korea’s National Economic Advisory Council recommended importing crude from Russia and Iran and buying “as much naphtha as possible” from China. The Philippines, America’s oldest treaty ally in Asia, declared a national energy emergency. India resumed Iranian crude imports for the first time in seven years. Two tankers docked at Indian ports Tuesday.

None of this represents a break from Washington. It represents something more subtle and more durable: a recalculation. The Wee-Hernández-Choe-Travelli reporting in the New York Times is the piece to read. As Huong Le Thu of the International Crisis Group put it: “US foreign policy under Trump is to push many away from the US to seek other alternatives.” Russia’s economy is now projected to grow faster this year than before the war. The IMF worst-case scenario — global growth at 2 percent, inflation at 6 percent — is no longer theoretical.

IX. The Economics of a War Without an Exit

Goldman Sachs now projects US unemployment at 4.6 percent this year, up from 4.3 percent. The Federal Reserve’s Richmond president said the oil shock was “negative to both sides of our mandate” — simultaneously threatening inflation and growth. Bessent told reporters Wednesday that gas could return to three dollars a gallon “sometime between June 20 and Sept. 20” — contingent on a deal. That is the White House’s actual economic forecast.

The energy surge has made groceries more expensive while also raising costs for farmers.Credit...Vincent Alban/The New York Times

Romm and Smith of the Times document the gap between Wall Street and Main Street. The S&P 500 hit a record high Wednesday, pricing in a deal not yet signed. Physical oil buyers are pricing reality. It is the futures-spot divergence from the Elliott piece — still the most analytically precise description of the war’s economics — now playing out in equity markets as well as commodity ones.

Japan pledged $10 billion to help Southeast Asian nations cope with oil prices. Canada suspended its federal gas tax through September at a cost of $1.7 billion Canadian. Mark Carney announced the measure with language calibrated for an election campaign: “a responsible measure that will reduce operating costs for truckers and businesses.” Irish truckers blocked motorways. Seoul declared energy emergencies. Vietnam and Thailand still have petrol stations that ran dry weeks ago.

Japan’s toilet manufacturer Toto suspended new orders for prefabricated bathroom units, citing a shortage of naphtha-derived solvents. Physicians and patient groups in Japan filed formal requests to the health ministry identifying at-risk medical supply chains — surgical gloves, hemodialysis equipment, waste fluid containers. These are the cascade consequences that no war game adequately modelled.

X. The Domestic Fracture

The Senate voted this week on Democratic measures to block arms sales to Israel — 1,000-pound bombs and armoured bulldozers. Roughly three-quarters of Senate Democrats voted for the measures, up from about half in previous rounds. The converts included Cantwell, Gallego, Kelly, Padilla, Schiff, Slotkin, Wyden. The measures were blocked by Republicans voting en masse. But the shift — from half to three-quarters in a single cycle — is the political marker that matters.

Vance was heckled at Turning Point USA’s University of Georgia event. “Jesus Christ does not support genocide!” came from the audience. “I agree,” Vance replied. “He certainly does not.” Later: “I recognize that young voters do not love the policy we have in the Middle East. I understand.” He urged them not to disengage over a single issue. The Ipsos-Reuters poll this week: 51 percent of Americans say the war has not been worthwhile. Only 24 percent say it has. Young Republicans are far less likely to approve than older Republicans. The coalition that elected Trump on “no new wars” is doing the arithmetic.

The Lawler town hall in the Hudson Valley — combative, heckled, unresolved — is the midterm preview. Republican incumbents in swing districts are being asked to defend a war that never had majority support, that has made gas expensive, that has no defined end, and that their own president describes with language that oscillates between triumphalism and contradictory forecast. “You have a responsibility to us,” a constituent told Lawler. “And you have endangered our young people, our service members, and our country by not standing up to Trump on this unjustified war.”

XI. What Monday Holds

The ceasefire expires Tuesday. The Lebanon truce has nine days remaining. The blockade is running. The nuclear gap between twenty years and five years has not moved. Pakistan is trying. Trump might fly to Islamabad. Munir is still in Tehran.

The Strait of Hormuz before the war carried 120 ships per day. Four passed on some days at the height of the closure. Non-Iranian commercial traffic is beginning to move — twenty vessels in the first 24 hours of the blockade, per CENTCOM. But war risk insurance premiums remain elevated, the mines have not been located, and Iran’s surveillance drones are watching the destroyers in the Gulf of Oman.

Parsi’s non-negotiated status quo remains the baseline. Azizi’s Vietnam frame remains the best realistic outcome. Sadjadpour’s incompatibility inventory has not been negotiated away. Swanson’s formulation may be the most accurate prediction anyone has made: the deal, if there is one, will be small and transactional. Not the grand bargain Vance described at Turning Point USA. Not the total surrender Witkoff envisioned. Something closer to a managed stalemate with a document attached.

In the meantime, Lemire’s closing observation from his Atlantic piece applies with full force: Trump has boxed himself in without an obvious way out. His usual intimidation tactics aren’t working. The cease-fire is holding — barely — while the clock runs. The Strait is blockaded from both sides. Lebanon has ten days. The ceasefire has four.

He says it is going swimmingly.

THE NUMBERS

Dead: Iran — at least 1,701 civilians confirmed, including 254 children (Human Rights Activists News Agency, as of last Wednesday; actual toll believed significantly higher). Lebanon — 2,124 killed, 6,400+ wounded. Gulf states — 32 killed in Iranian-attributed attacks. Israel — 22 civilians and soldiers. Lebanon — 13 Israeli soldiers. American service members — 13.

Displaced: Lebanon — more than one million people, roughly one-fifth of the population.

Oil: Brent crude futures ~$95/barrel. Physical spot price ~$95-100 (converging slightly as blockade takes hold). Pre-war: ~$63/barrel. Peak spot price during war: $145/barrel.

Gas: US national average $4.09/gallon Thursday, down from $4.13 peak. Pre-war: ~$2.99/gallon. Las Vegas: above $5.00. Diesel: $5.61 nationally, up ~50% since war began.

Strait traffic: Pre-war — 120 vessels/day, 20 million barrels of oil and products daily. Current — less than 4 million barrels/day. Non-Iranian commercial vessels beginning to move under US blockade escort.

Markets: S&P 500 — record high Wednesday, now 2% above pre-war level. Nikkei — up nearly 3% Thursday. IMF global growth forecast: 3.1% (best case, war short-lived). Worst case: 2.0% growth, 6.0% inflation.

Iran economy: Oil revenues ~$139 million/day pre-blockade. Non-oil trade: $109.6 billion annually with 170+ nations. Chinese oil purchases: $31.5 billion in 2025, representing 45% of Iranian government budget.

Nuclear: Iran’s near-bomb-grade uranium stockpile — approximately 970 pounds at ~60% enrichment, stored at Isfahan. US asked for 20-year suspension. Iran offered 5 years. Gap: 15 years.

Political: Trump approval — 39%. War approval — 24% worthwhile, 51% not worthwhile (Ipsos-Reuters). Republican House seats at risk of loss: projected 30-40+. Senate seats now in Democratic play: NC, ME, OH, IA, AK.

Blockade: US forces deployed — 10,000+ troops, 12+ warships, fighter jets, surveillance aircraft, underwater drones. Vessels turned back: 6 confirmed by CENTCOM. Iranian-linked vessels using spoofing/dark tactics: increasing, per Windward intelligence.

The clock: Ceasefire expires April 21. Lebanon truce: 10 days from April 17. Days of war: 48.
