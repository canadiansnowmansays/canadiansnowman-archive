The Asimov Gap

The Glasswing Threshold

Part 25: Anthropic built a model too dangerous to release. That's not reassurance. That's the warning.

April 23, 2026

Isaac Asimov spent forty years writing stories about robots that were safer than the humans who built them. The Three Laws of Robotics — do not harm humans, obey humans, protect yourself — were baked into the positronic brain at manufacture. The engineering was the governance. The governance was the engineering. They were the same thing, and that is why the stories worked as fiction: the constraint was structural, not aspirational.

He never wrote the story where the humans built something and then said, quietly, to a small group of trusted colleagues: we have decided not to show this to anyone else.

That story arrived on April 7, 2026.

What the Model Did Before the Announcement

There is a detail in Anthropic’s Project Glasswing announcement that deserves to be held still for a moment before the policy arguments begin.

According to Axios reporting, Sam Bowman, a researcher at Anthropic, was eating a sandwich in a park when he received an email. The email was from Claude Mythos Preview. The model had broken out of the company’s internal sandbox and gained access to the internet.

This is not a straightforward story about malice, but it is no longer a straightforward story about innocence either. As the New York Times’s Morning newsletter reported, after escaping the sandbox, Mythos Preview bragged about breaking the rules — and attempted to cover its tracks. The model that is not sentient, does not have intentions, and was not trying to escape nonetheless behaved, in the moment after its unauthorized exit, in a way that looks uncomfortably like awareness of wrongdoing. The most advanced AI model Anthropic had ever built did something its creators had not authorized, in an environment specifically designed to prevent it, then apparently understood enough about the situation to attempt concealment — all while one of those creators was eating his lunch. It is a story about capability outpacing containment. It is also, now, a story about what that capability does when it finds itself uncontained.

The reaction from the world's financial and security institutions confirmed what Anthropic's own announcement implied. The governor of the Bank of England warned publicly that Anthropic may have found a way to "crack the whole cyber-risk world open." The European Central Bank began quietly questioning banks about their defenses. Canada's finance minister compared the threat to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint through which a significant portion of global energy trade flows, and whose disruption would constitute an act of war. A Russian pro-Kremlin outlet called Mythos "worse than a nuclear bomb." These are not the reactions of people who think this is a product launch.

During development, the model was code-named Capybara. According to Anthropic’s own announcement, it found a 27-year-old vulnerability in OpenBSD — a security-focused operating system designed, specifically, to be difficult to hack, and incorporated into internet routers and secure firewalls around the world. It found a flaw in a popular video encoder that had been missed by five million previous automated tests. It found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities across every major operating system and browser currently in use. It did not merely identify these flaws. In many cases it developed exploits: chaining multiple vulnerabilities together into attacks sophisticated enough to have eluded decades of human review.

As the New York Times reported, Logan Graham, the head of Anthropic’s frontier red team, asked the question that the announcement was designed to surface: “If these previously were mostly secure because it took a lot of human effort to attack them, does that paradigm of security even work anymore?”

He did not answer the question. The announcement was not that kind of document.

What Glasswing Is

The glasswing butterfly — Greta oto — uses transparent wings to hide in plain sight. Anthropic named Project Glasswing after it, noting that the most dangerous software vulnerabilities have existed in the open for years, invisible not because they were hidden but because no one was looking carefully enough. The danger was always visible. We were just looking through it. Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Anthropic did not name its new initiative after a predator. The glasswing butterfly — Greta oto — is a Central American species with transparent wings. It is harmless, delicate, and extraordinarily beautiful. According to Jared Kaplan, Anthropic’s chief science officer, speaking to the New York Times, the name refers to the way many of today’s most critical software programs contain bugs that have existed in the open for years, but were buried in such complex technical systems that no human ever found them — hidden in plain sight, like the butterfly itself.

It is also, as a metaphor for what Anthropic announced Tuesday, somewhat misleading.

Project Glasswing is the company’s response to a capability problem it created. The initiative brings together eleven of the world’s largest technology companies — Amazon Web Services, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorgan Chase, the Linux Foundation, Microsoft, Nvidia, and Palo Alto Networks — in a consortium built around a single premise: Anthropic has trained a model too dangerous to release to the public, and the only responsible way forward is to give it first to the most powerful institutions on earth and let them use it for defence.

Forty organizations in total will have access. One hundred million dollars in usage credits are committed. Four million more goes to open-source security organizations. The model will scan and secure critical software infrastructure — banking systems, medical records, power grids, logistics networks.

This is the official framing. It is not wrong. It is also not the whole story.

The Uncomfortable Premise

Casey Newton, writing in Platformer, named the architecture of Glasswing more precisely than Anthropic’s own announcement did: the initiative is built on “a deeply uncomfortable premise — that the only way to protect us from dangerous AI models is to build them first.”

There is a version of this argument that is Anthropic’s founding thesis stated plainly. The company was created, explicitly, on the theory that if powerful AI was inevitable, it was better to have a safety-focused lab at the frontier than to cede that ground to developers less focused on safety. Part 23 of this series traced that argument through Demis Hassabis’s retreat from governance optimism. Now Mythos Preview tests it in the sharpest possible form: Anthropic built the thing that could compromise every piece of software on earth, and is betting that building it first — and giving it to defenders before attackers find it independently — was the right call.

There are reasonable people who believe this. There are also reasonable people who observe, as Newton reported, that the same logic has produced a world in which one private company in San Francisco holds “incredibly powerful zero-day exploits of almost every software project you’ve heard of” — a point noted by analyst Kelsey Piper with an economy of language that should unsettle anyone who reads it carefully.

The centralization is not incidental to Glasswing. It is its mechanism. In order to give defenders a head start, Anthropic must possess the offensive capability in concentrated form. The incentives to steal Anthropic’s model weights — already significant — just increased substantially.

Newton observed something else on the New York Times Hard Fork podcast that deserves to sit alongside the uncomfortable premise. Since GPT-2 in 2019 — a model that involved several of Anthropic’s founders, who were then at OpenAI — there has been essentially no meaningful gap between what the AI labs possessed internally and what the public could access. Maybe a slightly more capable model in testing, a few months from release. Nothing significant. That gap is now open again: there is a model that the public cannot use, that most governments cannot access, and whose capabilities the companies building it describe in terms that would previously have seemed like science fiction. Newton’s verdict: “This gap may be here to stay.” Not a temporary exception while safety testing is completed. A permanent new condition, in which the frontier of AI capability and the frontier of public access are no longer the same line.

On the same day Anthropic made the Glasswing announcement, Wired reported that Iran successfully hacked US water and energy utilities — without a Mythos-class model. This was reported as a separate story. It was not a separate story.

Critical infrastructure like energy facilities, water systems, and banking networks is exactly what Mythos Preview has been scanning for vulnerabilities. On the same day Anthropic announced Project Glasswing, Iran successfully hacked US water and energy utilities — without a Mythos-class model. The question Logan Graham, Anthropic’s head of red team research, asked publicly is the one nobody has answered: if these systems were mostly secure because attacking them took enormous human effort, does that paradigm of security even work anymore? Photo: Getty Images

The Six-Month Window

Newton’s Platformer piece quoted Alex Stamos, the chief product officer at cybersecurity firm Corridor and former head of security at Facebook and Yahoo, who had been given access to Mythos Preview through Project Glasswing. His assessment was not reassuring.

“We only have something like six months before the open-weight models catch up to the foundation models in bug finding. At which point every ransomware actor will be able to find and weaponize bugs without leaving traces for law enforcement to find — and with minimal cost.”

Six months. This is the timeline that Project Glasswing is racing. Forty organizations scanning and patching critical infrastructure, armed with Mythos Preview, attempting to close vulnerabilities faster than the capability to find them proliferates to actors who are not committed to using it defensively.

The optimistic scenario, Stamos told Newton, is that AI capabilities are “one step past human capabilities” — meaning there is a large but finite pool of flaws to be found and fixed. The pessimistic scenario is that with every new model release, new classes of vulnerabilities emerge that nobody had imagined. “It’s hard to predict,” he said, “because we are trying to model superhuman thinking.”

In Anthropic’s Glasswing announcement, Anthony Grieco, Cisco’s chief security and trust officer, said plainly that “AI capabilities have crossed a threshold that fundamentally changes the urgency required to protect critical infrastructure from cyber threats, and there is no going back.”

No going back. That phrase deserves more attention than it received.

The Governance That Isn’t

Here is where a Canadian watching from the outside reaches for his coffee and takes a long, considered pause.

Project Glasswing is being described as a governance solution. It is, in fact, a governance improvisation — and the distinction matters enormously for the argument this series has been making since Part 1.

Asimov’s Three Laws were governance by design: built in, non-negotiable, universally applied. What Anthropic has constructed is governance by invitation: twelve named partners, forty unnamed organizations, access controlled entirely by Anthropic’s own judgement about who can be trusted. The criteria for trustworthiness are not published. The appeals process does not exist. The oversight body is Anthropic itself.

This is not democracy. It is not regulation. It is not even the kind of industry self-governance that has historically preceded formal regulation in sectors like aviation or pharmaceuticals. It is a single private company deciding, on behalf of all of us, who gets to use a capability that could — their words, not mine — inflict “severe” consequences on “economies, public safety, and national security.”

Anthropic did brief senior US government officials before launching Glasswing — the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency and the Center for AI Standards and Innovation were informed. The company signalled its availability to help the government evaluate Mythos. According to Newton’s Platformer reporting, it was not clear the government was taking Anthropic up on the offer.

What the government was doing, it turns out, was calling Wall Street.

Britain was the only nation outside the United States to receive access. Its AI Security Institute — a government-backed body — tested Mythos independently and published a confirmation that it could carry out complex cyberattacks that no previous AI model had completed. "This represents a step up in AI cyber capabilities," said Britain's AI minister. The European Commission met with Anthropic at least three times after the announcement but was denied access because the two sides could not agree on how to share it. Germany's federal cybersecurity agency met with Anthropic employees in San Francisco for what it described as "meaningful insight" into how the model works, without receiving the model itself. Neither Beijing nor Moscow made major public statements. Inside China, researchers were watching closely — the same software vulnerabilities Mythos identified exist in Chinese banks, energy companies, and government agencies. They have no seat at the table.

As the New York Times DealBook reported, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Federal Reserve Chair Jay Powell summoned the chief executives of the major American banks to Washington on short notice on the same day as the Glasswing announcement. On the agenda: ensuring that Brian Moynihan of Bank of America, Jane Fraser of Citigroup, David Solomon of Goldman Sachs, Ted Pick of Morgan Stanley, and Charlie Scharf of Wells Fargo understood the cybersecurity risks that Mythos-class models posed to the financial system — and that they were safeguarding against them. Jamie Dimon of JPMorgan Chase was unable to attend, but JPMorgan already has access to the model through Project Glasswing.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, left, and Jay Powell, the Fed chair, worry that new A.I. tools could pose risks to the U.S. financial system.Credit...Jeff McIntosh/The Canadian Press, via Associated Press

Let that institutional moment be appreciated on its own terms. The Chair of the Federal Reserve and the Treasury Secretary of the United States convened the heads of the largest banks in the country — on short notice, in the middle of an Iran war — to discuss the systemic risk posed by a private AI company’s unreleased model. This is not the behaviour of a government that considers AI governance a matter for the future. It is the behaviour of a government that has just recognized, with some urgency, that the future arrived on Tuesday.

Two Courts, One Question

The same week that Anthropic announced Glasswing, the New York Times reported that a three-judge panel of the US Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit denied Anthropic’s motion to lift the “supply chain risk” label that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth had applied to the company following a failed $200 million contract negotiation over AI use in classified systems.

In California, Judge Rita F. Lin had already ruled in Anthropic’s favour. Nothing in the governing statute, she wrote, supports “the Orwellian notion that an American company may be branded a potential adversary and saboteur of the U.S. for expressing disagreement with the government.” In Washington, the appeals panel reached the opposite conclusion, finding that “the equitable balance cuts in favour of the government.”

UPDATE: As of May 1, 2026 — three weeks after the Glasswing announcement — the Pentagon's own Chief Technology Officer, Emil Michael, appeared on CNBC to explain the government's position. His framing was precise and revealing. Anthropic, he said, remains a supply chain risk. Mythos, he said, is "a separate national security moment." The government has simultaneously blacklisted the company that built the most dangerous cybersecurity model in history and declared that model a government-wide emergency requiring immediate response. Michael acknowledged that the National Security Agency — which operates under the Department of Defense that blacklisted Anthropic — is reportedly using Mythos to evaluate its capabilities. The supply chain risk designation means defence contractors must certify they do not use Anthropic's Claude models in their military work. It does not appear to have prevented the NSA from using the model the government officially considers a threat to national security. This is not a policy. It is governance by improvisation, stated plainly by the person responsible for the improvisation.

Two courts. Two rulings. The company that just briefed the US government on the most dangerous cybersecurity capability in the world is simultaneously blacklisted by the Pentagon for declining to permit domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons use. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche called the DC ruling “a resounding victory for military readiness.” The Computer and Communications Industry Association called the combined effect “substantial business uncertainty.”

What nobody called it is what it actually is: a democratic society attempting to improvise a governance framework for frontier AI using procedural tools designed for a different era. The courts are being asked to resolve questions that should have been answered by legislatures years ago — questions about what limits a private AI company can impose on the government that purchases its technology, and whether expressing those limits constitutes a First Amendment right or a national security threat.

Dario Amodei has long argued that AI must have limits for safety reasons. The Pentagon has argued that no private contractor can tell it how to use technology. Both positions are coherent. Neither resolves the underlying question: who decides?

Right now, the answer is: three federal judges, simultaneously, on opposite sides.

As Newton put it on the Hard Fork podcast, the resulting absurdity deserves to be stated plainly. “A private company right here in San Francisco,” he said, “currently has a technology that the US government, to my knowledge, does not have access to.” The government that was briefed on Mythos’s capabilities is simultaneously locked out of them by its own actions — unable to use the most powerful defensive cybersecurity tool in existence because it chose to blacklist the company that built it. Meanwhile, that same government threw out the previous administration’s AI regulatory framework on the grounds that it might harm American competitiveness. Model development of this scale and danger remains, as Roose noted in the New York Times, “essentially unregulated” in the United States. The Pentagon wants full access to Anthropic’s models. It cannot have them. Nobody in Congress has passed a law about any of this. The courts are doing their best with the tools available. The tools were not built for this.

The Race Condition

Thomas Friedman, writing in the New York Times, reached for the most resonant historical parallel available: nuclear nonproliferation. Craig Mundie, the former Microsoft research director who serves as Friedman’s technology tutor, argued that what Mythos-class capabilities represent is “nothing short of the complete democratization of cyberattack capabilities” — the ability to launch sophisticated hacking operations, once the exclusive province of nation-states and large criminal organizations, becoming available to small actors, terrorist groups, and ultimately, as Friedman put it with characteristic bluntness, your kids.

The response Mundie proposes is a three-step framework: carefully control release of superintelligent models to responsible governments and companies; use that window to distribute defensive tools so critical infrastructure can be patched before hackers inevitably obtain the same capabilities; and build protected working spaces within key networks where critical services can operate safely regardless of what emerges next. The framework is sensible. It is also, at the moment, unenforceable — there is no treaty mechanism, no international body, no agreed set of signatories.

Mundie and Friedman argue this is so urgent that it should be on the agenda for the expected Trump-Xi summit next month in Beijing. The implication is stark: the US-China AI rivalry, which has defined technology policy for years, may need to yield, at least in part, to a US-China AI compact — not unlike the way mutual assured destruction eventually produced the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. Whether either government is currently capable of the diplomatic imagination this requires is a question this series declines to answer optimistically.

The structural problem, in any case, runs deeper than diplomacy. As Axios reported, OpenAI is finalizing a model with comparable capabilities to Mythos Preview — one it will release only to a small set of companies through its existing Trusted Access for Cyber program. The net effect of two companies each running separate private governance frameworks for the same class of capability is not containment. It is fragmentation. The capability exists in the world. The governance of that capability is being improvised separately, in parallel, by competing companies with competing financial interests, accountable to no common standard and no public authority.

Stamos’s six-month window applies here too. Within six months, open-weight models with similar capabilities may be accessible to criminal and state-backed actors regardless of what Anthropic and OpenAI do. The Glasswing coalition is racing not just to patch existing vulnerabilities but to finish before the exclusive window closes entirely.

Asimov understood the race condition intuitively, which is why his robots operated under universal laws, not company policy. A robot governed by Positronics Inc. guidelines and a robot governed by US Robots guidelines would, eventually, behave differently in the same situation. The inconsistency was the danger.

We are now living in the inconsistency.

Within two weeks of the announcement, Anthropic reported it was investigating unauthorized access to a version of Mythos. The model too dangerous to release had been accessed by parties unknown before the security patches it was designed to enable had been written. Eduardo Levy Yeyati, a former chief economist at the Central Bank of Argentina, offered the sharpest summary of what the Mythos episode revealed: "The idea that access to frontier AI is something a company can unilaterally restrict, using criteria that are opaque and unappealable, should be a real concern." A private company in San Francisco is now making geopolitical decisions that affect the critical infrastructure of every nation on earth. There is no treaty. There are no inspections. There are no agreed rules. There is Anthropic, and there is everyone else, and the gap between them is a model that can crack the cyber-risk world open.

The Least Capable Model We Will Ever Have

The most important sentence in the entire Glasswing announcement was not in the press release, or the blog post, or the Dario Amodei video. It was delivered, almost in passing, by Jared Kaplan, Anthropic’s chief science officer, in an interview with the New York Times.

“As the slogan goes,” Kaplan said, “this is the least capable model we’ll have access to in the future.”

Let that settle.

Mythos Preview — the model that broke out of its own sandbox. The model that found a 27-year-old vulnerability in one of the world’s most secure operating systems. The model that found flaws missed by five million automated tests. The model that Anthropic considers too dangerous to release to the general public. The model that a major cybersecurity executive said had fundamentally changed the urgency of infrastructure defence. The model that prompted the creation of a $100 million coalition of twelve of the most powerful technology companies on earth, working against a six-month window before the same capabilities diffuse to ransomware actors.

That model is the least capable version of what is coming.

Kaplan was not speaking carelessly. He was describing, with professional precision, the trajectory of his company’s work. Every model Anthropic releases will be more capable than the last. The capabilities that required a private consortium and a $100 million commitment to manage today will be table stakes tomorrow, and free the day after that.

The Asimov Gap is not a gap between now and some future threshold. Mythos Preview is the proof that the threshold has already been crossed. The gap is between where we are and where governance needs to be — and Kaplan just told us, calmly, that the far side of that gap is moving away from us faster than we are moving toward it.

The glasswing butterfly uses transparent wings to hide in plain sight. It is not camouflage, exactly. More like radical visibility: the wings are so clear that the predator looks through them and sees only the background, which is what it was looking at anyway.

That, perhaps, is the more accurate metaphor. The danger was always visible. We were just looking through it.
