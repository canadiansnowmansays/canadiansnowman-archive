The Iran Crisis

The Satire That Became a Documentary

The Iran Crisis Series — Sunday Satire Edition

March 1, 2026

By CanadianSnowmanSays | March 1, 2026

All screenshots from “Puppet Regime” — GZERO Media / Eurasia Group, produced by Ian Bremmer. Episode title: “Trump and Khamenei hold last ditch Iran nuclear talks.” Published February 27, 2026 — the day before the bombs fell. Watch the full episode at gzeromedia.com.

This video was made before the bombs fell.

The writers intended it as satire. A puppet Trump, orange and jowly, on the phone in the Oval Office. A puppet Khamenei, white-bearded and bespectacled, flanked by Iranian flags in what appears to be a bunker. A puppet Netanyahu, grey-suited, sitting quietly next to Trump with the confidence of a man who has already decided how this ends.

Watch it now and decide whether it still qualifies as comedy.

One other thing worth knowing before you do: Puppet Regime is not the work of professional comedians. It is produced by GZERO Media, the media arm of Eurasia Group — the geopolitical risk consultancy founded by political scientist Ian Bremmer. Eurasia Group advises governments, corporations, and institutions on geopolitical risk for a living. Its clients do not pay for jokes. When Ian Bremmer’s shop produces a puppet show depicting the Iran nuclear talks as elaborate mutual performance art, that is not a punchline. That is a risk assessment delivered in felt.

Bremmer published this episode on February 27, 2026. The strikes began at 1:33 AM on February 28.

The consultancy got the call right. So did the puppets.

Watch it now and decide whether it still qualifies as comedy.

The “Even Though” Construction

The entire piece runs on a single grammatical device: every statement is followed immediately by its own contradiction.

“I obliterated it last summer, even though I did not.”

“Weapons that don’t exist, even though they do.”

“Which is why we cannot give it up, which is why you must — but only if you will give up the sanctions which are not killing us, even though they are.”

This was written as absurdism. It turns out it was stenography.

Every official statement in the six weeks leading up to Operation Epic Fury contained exactly this structure. Trump said Iran’s nuclear program had been obliterated in June — American intelligence said it had been damaged, not destroyed. Witkoff said Iran was a week away from a bomb — the IAEA said the program had been set back years. The White House said the talks had stalled — Oman said there had been significant progress. Iran said it was willing to suspend enrichment for five years — the US said that wasn’t enough and struck while the mediator was still describing the session as productive.

Every line. Even though.

The puppet show didn’t invent the “even though” construction. It simply noticed that the entire diplomatic crisis was already written in it.

The Nobel Prize Line

Before February 28, the Nobel Prize bit was a moderately funny observation about Trump’s well-documented vanity — his fixation on Obama’s Peace Prize, his repeated suggestions that he deserved one, his nominees’ campaign on his behalf.

“Give me the prize that was given to me even though it was not. Now we’re talking. Now we are really talking.”

After February 28, the joke has acquired a different weight.

Trump has now launched the largest American military campaign since the invasion of Iraq in 2003. Dozens of civilians are confirmed dead, including children at a girls’ school in Minab. The Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain has been struck. Iranian missiles have killed civilians in Syria, the UAE, and Israel. Dubai’s airports are closed. Two-thirds of Iran’s provinces are reporting strikes.

And the puppet is still asking for the prize.

The joke is that this was never not the motivation. The prize was always real. What changed on Saturday morning is that there are now bodies attached to the pursuit of it.

The Israel Puppet Is the Most Honest Character

The Trump puppet is vain and confused. The Khamenei puppet speaks entirely in loops — give up the weapons that don’t exist, that you can’t give up, that we’re already bombing. But the Netanyahu/Israel puppet is the one running the scene.

“Don’t run. You can’t win a prize for ending a war that you’ve been starting — unless—”

He doesn’t finish the sentence. He doesn’t need to. The puppet show understood something that the official coverage spent six weeks tiptoeing around: the Israeli character in this drama is not the comic relief. It is the stage manager.

Jeffrey Sachs said the same thing on Saturday morning, without the puppets: “This has never been about nuclear weapons. This has been about Israel’s regional hegemony in the Middle East. Israel wants to topple the Iranian regime. Period.”

The puppet writers got there first. They just gave the stage manager a striped tie and let him sit quietly next to the orange one while the war was arranged.

The Canada Line

It lasts approximately two seconds. Easy to miss.

“Canada sold off people who are not protesting, even though—”

That line was throwaway comedy when it was written. A quick jab at Canadian complicity, a nod to the trade war and the protests and the general sense that Canada keeps ending up on the wrong side of its own values.

On Saturday morning, Prime Minister Mark Carney and Foreign Minister Anita Anand issued a joint statement endorsing the strikes. “Canada supports the United States acting to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon and to prevent its regime from further threatening international peace and security.”

The puppet show gave Canada two seconds and a trailing “even though.”

It turns out that was exactly the right amount of time, and exactly the right grammatical structure.

The Closing Line

The video ends on a single line, delivered by the Khamenei puppet as the scene dissolves:

“But I don’t know if we are still talking.”

On Friday, Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi — the man who had spent weeks as the actual mediator between the actual United States and the actual Iran — told the world that “significant progress” had been made and that a deal was within reach.

On Saturday, after the bombs started falling, he posted: “Active and serious negotiations have yet again been undermined. I urge the United States not to get sucked in further. This is not your war.”

The puppet show ended on the exact line that ended the diplomacy.

I don’t know if we are still talking.

They were not.

What Satire Does That Journalism Cannot

This series has published seventeen analytical essays documenting the Iran crisis in real time — the buildup, the false premises, the governance gap, the constitutional bypass, the abandoned civilians, the coalition Iran built against itself in a single morning.

Every word of that documentation is necessary. The record matters. The evidence matters. The citations matter.

But satire does something that documentation cannot. It tells you what everyone already knows but has agreed not to say out loud.

Everyone watching the Geneva talks knew, at some level, that they were theatre. The puppet show said so with orange felt and a striped tie. Everyone watching Trump’s Truth Social video at 2:30 AM knew that “bombs will be dropping everywhere — take over your government” was not a coherent plan. The puppet show encoded that contradiction in a grammatical device and ran it for fifty-five seconds.

The writers of Puppet Regime did not have access to intelligence assessments, diplomatic cables, or the Omani foreign minister’s private communications. They had something more useful: they were paying attention.

Satire is what happens when enough people are paying attention and decide to stop pretending.

A Final Note

This piece was written the morning after Operation Epic Fury began.

The video embedded above was made before the bombs fell. Its writers meant it as comedy. History has since reclassified it.

What it is now is harder to name. Not quite satire. Not quite documentary. Something in between — the thing that exists in the space where what everyone knows and what anyone will officially say have drifted so far apart that only puppets can close the gap.

I don’t know if we are still talking.

Neither does anyone else.
