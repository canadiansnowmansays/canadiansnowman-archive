The Great Canadian Trade War

Part IV

Lumber Tariffs and the Homeowner Security Heist

August 31, 2025

By CanadianSnowmanSays | August 31, 2025

President Donald Trump's trade war with Canada has been a masterclass in turning molehills into mountains—43 pounds of fentanyl, a handful of border crossings, and dairy tariffs that barely bite morphed into a $762 billion trade relationship on the chopping block.

Now, he's pulled another rabbit out of his protectionist hat: a Section 232 investigation branding Canadian softwood lumber a "national security threat." As a Canadian, I'm calling this what it is—a legislative shakedown that stretches credulity and screws over homeowners on both sides of the border.

Forget warplanes and warships; the real casualty here is homeowner security—the dream of affording a home without breaking the bank. Trump's lumber tariffs, layered on existing 35% duties, are jacking up housing costs, inflating the cost of living, and punishing Canadian workers, all under a flimsy national security pretext.

Let's break down the math, expose the absurdity, and chart a path to fairness that puts people over political theater.

Background: The 40-Year Lumber War

Before diving into Trump's latest gambit, we need to understand why this keeps happening. The Canada-U.S. softwood lumber dispute is one of the most enduring trade conflicts between both nations, dating back to 1982 when the U.S. lumber industry first complained that low Canadian stumpage rates constituted an unfair advantage.

Here's the fundamental difference: In Canada, most timber is owned by provincial governments, with stumpage fees (the price to harvest timber) set by law and administrative regulations rather than by market competition. In the United States, most timber is privately owned and sold on the open market, with stumpage fees determined competitively.

The U.S. claims that the Canadian arrangement constitutes an unfair subsidy, making it subject to countervailing duties. The Canadian government disputes this assertion, noting that Canadian timber is provided to such a wide range of industries that the lack of specificity makes it ineligible to be considered a subsidy under U.S. law.

The Track Record: Since 1982, there have been four major iterations of this dispute. In April 2006, the United States and Canada reached a tentative settlement—the Softwood Lumber Agreement—which lasted until 2015. Under its terms, the U.S. would lift duties provided lumber prices stayed above a certain range, and Canada agreed to enforce export taxes and quotas. As part of the deal, more than $5 billion in duty deposits were returned.

But here we are again, proving that this isn't about security or fairness—it's about a cyclical shakedown that happens every time the U.S. industry feels competitive pressure.

Lumber as a National Security Threat? Give Me a Break

On March 1, 2025, Trump signed an Executive Order launching a Section 232 investigation under the Trade Expansion Act of 1962, probing whether Canadian softwood lumber imports threaten U.S. national security. The Commerce Department has until November 26, 2025, to deliver findings, potentially piling new tariffs or quotas atop the 35.19% countervailing and anti-dumping duties already hammering Canadian lumber.

The pitch? Lumber is critical for construction and military logistics (think pallets), and Canada's 25–30% share of U.S. supply—$9 billion in 2024—creates "vulnerabilities." Subsidized Canadian stumpage fees, the administration claims, undercut U.S. producers, threatening domestic capacity.

Sounds serious, right? Except it's nonsense.

Unlike steel or copper, lumber isn't fueling F-35s or naval destroyers. Its military role—pallets, maybe some plywood—is a footnote. The U.S. Lumber Coalition cheers this as protecting jobs, but the real threat is economic, not strategic. Canada's not flooding the U.S. with lumber to destabilize it; we're filling a supply gap American mills can't meet, with U.S. capacity stuck at 70% of demand due to workforce shortages and mill closures.

The national security label is pure Trumpian sleight of hand, bending a 1962 law meant for tanks into a cudgel for trade disputes older than most readers.

Homeowner Security: The Real Casualty

The true threat isn't Canadian lumber—it's the gutting of homeowner security, the ability to buy or build a home without drowning in debt.

The Numbers Are Brutal

The National Association of Home Builders (NAHB) pegs existing duties at adding $7,500–$10,000 to new U.S. home costs, with projections of $15,000–$20,000 if Section 232 tariffs hit. The average impact of all tariffs enacted or expected so far, including on lumber, is nearly $11,000 per single-family home.

Nearly 100 million U.S. households can't afford a median-priced home, which now averages $460,000, according to NAHB. Lumber accounts for 15–20% of construction costs, and with 1.4 million U.S. housing starts annually, that's a massive tax on families.

Voices from the Ground

NAHB CEO Jim Tobin captures the industry's frustration: "I share President Trump's desire to create fair and balanced trade across our borders, certainly would bring back as much production as we can. But until we do that, and it will take years and millions of dollars of investment, we need to make sure that we have a reliable, affordable source of lumber."

Gary Franke, who owns a home framing construction business in Southern California, puts it bluntly: "I've been in this business since 1988, and I've never had a problem getting lumber, but if the price of lumber goes up, we have to pass that along to the customer. We can't sustain that, so we'd pass it along."

John Kalabich, owner of Acme Lumber in Chicago, notes the market reality: He was able to keep prices relatively flat over the past 12 months because of the duty on Canadian lumber. He has also heard from contractors that the demand for small repair work and big-ticket construction has gone down.

The Human Cost

Behind these statistics are real families being priced out of homeownership. Home prices are up nearly 50% since before the pandemic, and mortgage interest rates ticked up to 6.86% last week — a far cry from their pandemic low of 2.65% for a 30-year fixed-rate loan.

The biggest gripe among homeowners with at least one regret about their purchase is ongoing maintenance and hidden costs being more expensive than expected, cited by 42 percent of homeowners. Now imagine adding $15,000–$20,000 to that burden before you even get the keys.

In Canada, B.C.'s forestry sector—40% of our softwood exports—faces mill closures and job losses, with small producers crushed by $9 billion in duties held in escrow.

The Ripple Effect

This isn't just about housing. Higher lumber prices ripple through the cost of living—furniture, flooring, even paper products get pricier. A 2–5% wood price spike can bump construction costs by 1%, and with lumber at $500–$600 per thousand board feet, a 50% tariff could push it past $750, fueling inflation (already 2.4% in June 2025).

American families, already squeezed by Trump's broader tariffs ($1,200–$2,800 per household annually), face a double whammy. Canadians got some relief when Ottawa dropped most retaliatory tariffs on September 1, 2025, as part of Carney's de-escalation strategy, though tariffs on U.S. steel, aluminum, and autos remain in place.

This is personal. Homeowner security means stability—knowing you can afford a mortgage, renovate, or rebuild after a disaster without choosing between groceries and shelter. Trump's tariffs, cloaked in "national security," are a direct attack on that dream, hiking costs for Americans while strangling Canadian communities.

It's not Canada vs. the U.S.—it's both of us vs. a policy that punishes workers and families for political points.

Trump's Legislative Con Game

Trump's use of Section 232 is straight out of his playbook—leverage obscure laws to bully partners into submission. The Trade Expansion Act lets the President slap tariffs if imports threaten national security, with courts giving wide deference. The 2019 American Institute for International Steel case upheld steel tariffs despite shaky justification, and lumber's likely to skate through too.

But this isn't about security; it's about rehashing a 40-year softwood dispute over Canada's stumpage fees, which the U.S. calls subsidies but are just different forestry models.

The Hypocrisy

The hypocrisy is galling. Trump's own USMCA—his "best trade deal ever"—shields 94–95% of Canadian exports, including most lumber, from his IEEPA tariffs, which the Federal Circuit just ruled illegal on August 29, 2025. Yet, he's dodging that constraint by invoking Section 232's national security clause, bypassing Congress and USMCA rules.

It's the same trick he pulled with dairy—raging over 270% tariffs he agreed to, which rarely apply. The lumber probe is less about pallets and more about strong-arming Canada into trade concessions, just as he targeted Apple and the EU for defying his whims.

The Math Doesn't Add Up

Let's do the numbers, because as I've said before, the math always wins.

Canada's $9 billion in lumber exports are just 1.2% of the $762 billion U.S.-Canada trade relationship. The U.S. claims Canada's 25–30% market share threatens its sawmills, but domestic capacity can't scale up fast—mills are closing, and workers are scarce.

Current Market Reality

The framing lumber composite price was down 3.7% for the week ending on Aug. 22, 2025, reaching its lowest level of 2025, yet lumber prices are still 5.8% higher than they were one year ago.

The Department of Commerce announced in August 2025 that it is more than doubling its countervailing duties rate on Canadian softwood lumber imports from 6.74% to 14.63%. Coupled with the anti-dumping rate of 20.6%, tariffs on Canadian lumber will now reach 35.2% (up from the current 14.4%).

Current Impact:

  • Existing 35% duties already cost U.S. consumers $1–2 billion annually

  • Section 232 tariffs threaten another $2–3 billion

  • Compare to Trump's fentanyl gripe: 43 pounds from Canada vs. 21,148 from Mexico

  • Or his dairy complaints: $1.14 billion in U.S. exports face zero tariffs

This is a sledgehammer for a mosquito.

Industry Perspective

The U.S. Lumber Coalition disputes lumber's impact on housing costs, claiming lumber accounts for less than 2% of the cost of building the average new home. But the NAHB says softwood accounts for about 6% of the cost of a single-family home, when lumber, panels and millwork are taken into account.

According to the NAHB/Wells Fargo survey, 78% of builders reported difficulty pricing their homes: They don't know how much it will cost to build them, and they don't know how much they can sell for.

Canada's retaliation, now smarter after dropping consumer tariffs, targets U.S. steel, aluminum, and autos—sectors where we have leverage. But with only 5–6% of our exports vulnerable to Trump's tariffs thanks to USMCA, why escalate over lumber?

The real fight is homeowner security, not symbolic wins. Every dollar added to a 2x4 is a dollar less for families, and every mill closure in B.C. is a Canadian job lost for no good reason.

A Canadian Victory: Fairness Over Theater

What's a Canadian victory? It's not just dodging tariffs—it's a trade relationship that respects our interdependence and prioritizes people over ego. Here's what it looks like:

1. A New Lumber Agreement

Like the 2006 deal, a quota-based system could stabilize trade. B.C. Premier David Eby's call for managed trade is a start—set export limits that let Canada supply 25% of U.S. lumber without triggering duties, balancing U.S. producers and consumer costs.

2. Protecting Homeowner Security

Both nations must cap tariff-driven price hikes. A joint USMCA panel could enforce lumber price stability, ensuring homes stay affordable. The NAHB's plea for relief resonates—tariffs are a tax on families, not a security win.

3. Dropping the National Security Charade

Lumber belongs in trade talks, not Section 232. Canada should push for USMCA dispute resolution, leveraging the August 29 ruling against Trump's IEEPA tariffs to challenge his legal overreach.

4. Strategic De-escalation

Carney's August 22 pivot—slashing consumer tariffs while keeping industrial pressure—was brilliant. Canada should double down, offering to pause lumber retaliation if the U.S. scraps Section 232 plans, letting Trump claim a "win" while protecting 94% of our trade.

What This Means for You

If you're an American family:

  • A new home could cost you $15,000–$20,000 more if Section 232 tariffs hit

  • Renovation projects will become more expensive across the board

  • Even everyday items like furniture and paper products will cost more

If you're a Canadian:

  • B.C. forestry workers face continued mill closures and job losses

  • Your tax dollars are subsidizing a dispute resolution process that's been running for 40 years

  • Consumer goods from the U.S. cost more due to retaliatory tariffs

If you're a young couple trying to buy your first home: Hopeful homebuyers need income of nearly $117,000 to afford a typical home in the U.S. Every tariff-driven price increase pushes that dream further away.

Where's This Headed?

The trade war's trajectory hinges on three factors:

Section 232 Outcome (November 26, 2025)

If Commerce recommends tariffs, expect 25–50% rates, pushing lumber prices to $750–$900 per thousand board feet and adding $15,000–$20,000 to U.S. home costs. Canada's retaliation will hit U.S. construction and autos, but USMCA shields most exports.

If no tariffs, it's a rare Trump backdown, signaling Canada's de-escalation worked.

Supreme Court Ruling (Post-October 14, 2025)

The Federal Circuit's IEEPA ruling could unravel Trump's broader tariff regime. If the Supreme Court upholds it, Canada's leverage grows—our counter-tariffs could outlast Trump's illegal ones. Canada should hold firm, ready to pivot if tariffs collapse.

USMCA 2026 Review

The review is a wildcard. Trump could push to gut USMCA exemptions, exposing all Canadian exports. But his own deal's constraints—needing Congressional approval—limit him. Canada must strengthen USMCA compliance, certifying every lumber export possible to stay tariff-free.

Canada's best play is the poker game—quietly protect the 94–95% USMCA shield, keep pressure on steel and autos, and let Trump's ego think he's winning. A victory isn't zero tariffs; it's a deal that keeps lumber flowing, homes affordable, and B.C. mills open.

Trump's pattern shows he'll move goalposts again. Canada must stay nimble, using math over headlines.
