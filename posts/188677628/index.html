Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 10: The Tightening Vise — From Helms-Burton to Today (1996–2025)

March 25, 2026

President Clinton signs the Cuban Liberty and Democratic Solidarity Act, March 1996, surrounded by families of the four men killed when Cuban forces shot down two Brothers to the Rescue planes days earlier. The legislation codified the embargo into law, making it nearly impossible for any future president to lift it unilaterally. Clinton had reservations. He signed it anyway.

There is a particular kind of cruelty in a policy that has demonstrably failed its stated purpose for sixty-five years and yet continues to be applied, tightened, and defended as though the next increment of pressure might finally produce the result that all the previous increments did not. The American embargo against Cuba is that policy. By any rational measure of effectiveness — has it produced democratic government in Cuba? Has it improved the human rights situation? Has it made ordinary Cubans freer? — it has failed completely. And yet it persists, tightens, and is now, under the Trump administration, being escalated to a full naval blockade.

To understand how this happened — how a demonstrably failed policy became entrenched to the point where no American president has been able to significantly reform it, and some have made it worse — you have to understand the political economy of the Cuban embargo, which is less about Cuba than it is about Florida, less about foreign policy than it is about domestic electoral calculations, and less about what is good for the Cuban people than it is about what is good for a specific community of Cuban-Americans whose politics have been shaped by a trauma that is now more than sixty years old.

This part of the series covers the period from the passage of the Helms-Burton Act in 1996 through the Obama opening, the Trump reversal, and the Biden years — a period in which the fundamental dynamic of the US-Cuba relationship remained essentially unchanged despite enormous shifts in the broader political landscape, and in which the costs of that stasis continued to fall most heavily on the people of Cuba.

Helms-Burton and the Codification of the Embargo

The Cuban Liberty and Democratic Solidarity Act of 1996 — known as Helms-Burton after its primary sponsors, Senator Jesse Helms of North Carolina and Representative Dan Burton of Indiana — was the most significant piece of Cuba legislation since the original embargo, and it transformed the embargo from a presidential foreign policy tool into a congressional mandate that no subsequent president could unilaterally reverse.

The key provision was simple and far-reaching: the embargo could only be lifted by an act of Congress, not by executive order. Previous presidents had maintained the embargo by executive action, which meant they could theoretically also ease or end it by executive action. After Helms-Burton, that option was gone. Any president who wanted to normalise relations with Cuba — to lift the embargo, to restore full diplomatic ties, to allow American companies to invest in the island — would need to bring Congress along. Given the political dynamics of Cuban-American voting in Florida, and Florida’s pivotal role in presidential elections, the prospects for congressional action were consistently poor.

Helms-Burton also imposed sanctions on foreign companies that trafficked in property confiscated from American nationals by the Cuban government — a provision that put American law into direct conflict with the laws of every other country in the world, since no other country recognised the American right to impose these sanctions extraterritorially. Canada, the European Union, and other American allies loudly objected. The Clinton administration waived the most controversial provisions repeatedly rather than enforce them and cause diplomatic crises. The Bush administration did the same. The provisions nonetheless remained on the books, a latent weapon that could be activated when the political calculation changed.

It was activated by the Trump administration in 2019, which allowed Americans to sue foreign companies using confiscated Cuban property — the first time the provision had been fully enforced in twenty-three years, and a move that immediately generated diplomatic protests from Canada, Spain, and the European Union, whose companies faced potential legal liability for their perfectly legal Cuban operations.

The Florida Factor

To understand why no American president between 1962 and 2015 significantly eased the Cuba embargo, you have to understand Florida.

Florida is the largest swing state in American presidential elections. Its electoral votes have decided, or nearly decided, multiple presidential elections — most notoriously in 2000, when George W. Bush’s 537-vote margin in Florida determined the outcome of the entire election. Cuban-Americans are a significant voting bloc in South Florida, concentrated in Miami-Dade County, and they vote in high proportions. For decades, the overwhelming majority voted Republican, and they voted primarily on the Cuba issue: the embargo must be maintained, any softening of American policy toward Cuba is a betrayal of the exiles and their suffering.

The political consequence was predictable: any American politician with national ambitions understood that opposing the embargo would cost them Florida, and losing Florida could cost them the presidency. The embargo became, in effect, a domestic political hostage — maintained not because it was working as foreign policy, but because abandoning it was politically dangerous.

This dynamic was reinforced by the structure of the Cuban-American exile community itself. The political leadership of that community was, for decades, dominated by Cubans who had left in the early years of the Revolution — people who had lost properties, businesses, and social positions when Castro nationalised the economy. Their political project was, understandably, restoration: they wanted their Cuba back, or at least they wanted the regime that had taken it from them destroyed. The embargo served that project regardless of its effectiveness as policy. It expressed the community’s refusal to accept or legitimise what had happened to them.

Little Havana, Miami. For six decades, the exile community that built this neighbourhood has been the most powerful domestic force shaping American Cuba policy — more influential, in practice, than any State Department analysis or diplomatic calculation. Elections in Florida are won and lost here.

Younger Cuban-Americans — second and third generation, or recent economic migrants with different political formations — had more varied views. Some favoured engagement. Some had family in Cuba they wanted to visit and send remittances to. The demographics of the community were shifting. But the political leadership and the institutional infrastructure — the powerful lobbying organisations, the media, the political donor networks — remained oriented toward hardline positions for decades.

Marco Rubio is the political heir to this tradition. Born in Florida to parents who emigrated from Cuba in the 1950s — before the Revolution, though family mythology sometimes conflated this with post-Revolutionary exile — he built his political identity in no small part on the Cuba question. As a state legislator, as a senator, as a presidential candidate, and now as Secretary of State, his position on Cuba has been consistently maximalist: maximum pressure, no engagement, regime change as the only acceptable outcome. He is the apotheosis of a political tradition that has shaped American Cuba policy for sixty years.

The Obama Opening

Barack Obama became the first sitting U.S. president in 88 years to visit Cuba. Stephen Crowley/The New York Times

On December 17, 2014, Barack Obama announced the most significant shift in American Cuba policy in more than fifty years: the United States and Cuba would restore full diplomatic relations, the United States would ease some embargo restrictions by executive action, and the two countries would begin a process of normalisation after more than five decades of hostility.

The announcement was simultaneously shocking and overdue. Polls had consistently shown that a majority of Americans — including, by 2014, a majority of Cuban-Americans — supported normalisation. The embargo had failed by any metric. The Soviet Union was gone. The Cold War justification for the policy had collapsed along with it. And the Obama administration had calculated that a different approach might achieve more than sixty years of sanctions had accomplished.

The reaction was predictable. Marco Rubio called it “the latest in a long line of empty promises” and vowed to fight the normalisation at every turn. Other hardline Cuban-American politicians condemned it. But the public reaction in Cuba was extraordinary — Cubans celebrated in the streets of Havana. The prospect of an end to isolation, of American tourists and investment, of a normal place in the world — this meant something deeply personal to people who had spent their entire lives in a country defined by its siege.

The Obama opening produced real changes. An American embassy reopened in Havana for the first time since 1961. American tourists began visiting Cuba in significant numbers — a wave of Americans, passport in hand for the first time in their lifetimes, descended on Havana with a mixture of curiosity, guilt, and the particular excitement of arriving somewhere forbidden. American investment began to flow in tentatively. Delta and JetBlue launched direct flights from American cities to Havana.

But Obama’s ability to act was constrained precisely by Helms-Burton. He could ease some restrictions by executive action. He could not lift the embargo. He could restore diplomatic relations. He could not deliver the full economic normalisation that would have transformed Cuba’s economic situation. The Helms-Burton straitjacket held.

The Trump Reversal

Donald Trump reversed the Obama opening with remarkable completeness during his first term. By 2019, essentially every Obama-era relaxation of Cuba policy had been reversed, and new restrictions had been added on top. The Helms-Burton Title III provision was activated for the first time, exposing foreign companies to American lawsuits. Cuba was put back on the State Sponsors of Terrorism list — a designation that complicated international financial transactions and added another layer of economic pressure. Remittance limits were tightened, limiting the ability of Cuban-Americans to send money to family on the island.

The stated rationale was Cuba’s support for the Maduro government in Venezuela, and specifically the presence of Cuban military and intelligence personnel in Venezuela who were helping sustain Maduro’s grip on power. The rationale was not entirely without basis — Cuba’s relationship with Venezuela was deep and genuinely supportive of a government with a documented record of human rights abuses.

But the actual effect of the Trump policies fell on ordinary Cubans, not on the Cuban military officers serving in Venezuela. Restricting remittances hurt the Cuban families who depended on them, not the security services. Adding Cuba to the terrorism list complicated the financial transactions of Cuban hospitals trying to import medicines, not the intelligence agencies. The tools of American pressure were systematically blunt, and the bluntness was not accidental — it was a feature of a policy designed to maximise civilian suffering in the expectation that civilian suffering would produce political change.

Biden, despite campaign promises to restore the Obama approach, moved slowly and incompletely. Some remittance restrictions were eased. Cuba was taken off the terrorism list briefly, then controversially put back on. The fundamental structure of the embargo remained. Biden was constrained by the same Florida political calculus that had constrained every president before him — and by the Democratic Party’s fragile coalition in Miami-Dade County, where any softening of Cuba policy risked Cuban-American votes.

The Accumulation of Crises

By the time Trump returned to the presidency in January 2025, Cuba was already in desperate condition — the product not just of American policy but of the accumulated crises of the preceding decade.

Hurricane Irma devastated parts of Cuba in 2017. Hurricane Ian in 2022. The COVID pandemic shut down the tourism sector that had become Cuba’s primary source of hard currency. The Venezuelan economic collapse reduced the oil shipments that had replaced Soviet supplies. The Cuban economy contracted sharply. Emigration accelerated — an estimated 500,000 or more Cubans left between 2021 and 2024, an exodus that drained the country of working-age adults, professionals, and young people at a rate that made the demographic trajectory genuinely alarming.

In July 2021, Cuba experienced its largest protests in decades — tens of thousands of Cubans in cities across the island taking to the streets to chant “Libertad” and, more pointedly, “Se acabó” — it’s over. The protests were a genuine expression of popular frustration: with the blackouts, the food shortages, the government’s failure to provide basic services, the endless sacrifices demanded in the name of a revolution whose achievements were increasingly distant. The government responded with mass arrests — more than a thousand people detained, many sentenced to long prison terms in proceedings that international observers described as deeply flawed.

The 2021 protests did not produce regime change. They did produce the largest wave of political repression in decades. And they produced something else: a government that understood it was genuinely at risk, and that became, if anything, more rather than less rigid in the face of that risk.

What Sixty-Five Years Has Wrought

As Cuba enters 2026 with its fuel supply blockaded, its hospitals struggling, its schools dark, its garbage uncollected, it is worth taking stock of what sixty-five years of the American embargo has actually achieved.

Cuba is still governed by the Communist Party. Fidel Castro died in 2016 at ninety years of age, in his own bed, after six decades in power. His brother Raúl governed until 2018 and remains a powerful figure. Miguel Díaz-Canel, who holds the presidency, is a product of the revolutionary system. The one-party state is intact.

The nationwide blackout in Cuba left about 10 million people without electricity in Havana [File: Norlys Perez/Reuters]

Cuba’s human rights record remains deeply problematic. Political dissidents are imprisoned. Independent journalism is suppressed. The July 2021 protesters received sentences of up to twenty-five years. The freedoms that Cubans lack — to speak, to organise, to vote in contested elections, to leave and return freely — are real and significant.

The Cuban healthcare system, despite everything, still produces health outcomes that shame many wealthier nations. Cuba’s infant mortality rate is lower than that of the United States. Its doctors are deployed around the world in humanitarian missions that represent one of the Revolution’s genuine and sustained achievements. Its pharmaceutical and biotechnology sector has developed vaccines and treatments that have benefited people internationally.

What the embargo has not done — cannot do, it seems, regardless of how tightly it is tightened — is produce a free, democratic Cuba. Sixty-five years of evidence suggests it never will. The Cuban government has survived everything: the Bay of Pigs, the Missile Crisis, the Special Period, the Helms-Burton years, the Trump reversal, the 2021 protests. It has survived by being precisely the kind of revolutionary authoritarian system that treats external pressure as justification for internal control, and that can draw on a genuine historical narrative — Cuba’s five-century experience of outside powers imposing their will — to sustain the loyalty of at least a significant portion of its population.

The people who have not survived — who have paid the price for this sixty-five-year experiment in economic strangulation — are the ordinary Cubans who needed medicines that the embargo prevented from arriving, who needed food that the embargo made more expensive, who lived in poverty deepened by restrictions that served American domestic politics rather than Cuban welfare.

They are still there. The lights are going out again. And the question of what the current administration actually intends to accomplish — regime change by starvation, or something else entirely — is the one this series turns to next.
