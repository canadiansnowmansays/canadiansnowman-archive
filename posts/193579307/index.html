Formosa: The Beautiful Problem

THE BEAUTIFUL PROBLEM

A Series on the Island at the Centre of Everything

April 8, 2026

Series Preface

Taiwan is thirty-six thousand square kilometres of mountains, microchips, and unresolved legal status. It governs itself, elects its leaders, issues its own passports, and fields its own military. It is not formally recognised as a sovereign state by most of the world’s governments, including Canada’s. The People’s Republic of China insists it is a province. The United States insists on strategic ambiguity. Donald Trump insists it pay ten percent of GDP for its own defence. Beijing insists the question is non-negotiable and the answer predetermined.

The island has been claimed, colonised, ceded, occupied, and contested by the Dutch, the Spanish, a Ming loyalist pirate, the Qing dynasty, the Japanese Empire, the Republic of China, and the People’s Republic of China — roughly in that order, and with considerable overlap. At no point in four hundred years of recorded contestation has Taiwan enjoyed an uncontested status. At no point has anyone in a position of power asked the people who live there what they would prefer, and been willing to accept the answer.

This series is an attempt to explain how that came to be, why it matters now more than at any point since 1949, and what is actually at stake when the most dangerous unresolved territorial dispute in the world edges toward resolution on someone else’s timeline. It will go back to the seventeenth century, examine every major claim and counter-claim with as much honesty as the subject permits, and arrive at the present moment — a Taiwanese opposition leader in Nanjing, a transactional American president, a Chinese fleet that now outnumbers the American one by hull count, and a May 2026 summit at which Taiwan’s name will not officially be spoken.

It is written from Canada, which has its own history with this part of the world that it has largely chosen to forget. That history will surface periodically throughout the series, because forgetting has consequences, and because a Canadian diplomat who understood what was coming once stood in Tokyo in 1929 and watched the Pacific begin to catch fire. He was right. His government did not listen.

Some things do not change.

Formosa: The Beautiful Problem publishes in ten parts, beginning soon. It is the most consequential story nobody is talking about clearly — and this series intends to change that.
