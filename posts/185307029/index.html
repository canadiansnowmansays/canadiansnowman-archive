The Davos Rupture

Supporting Voices from Davos

Part 5: How NATO’s Rutte, California’s Newsom, and China’s He Lifeng revealed the impossible positions created by Trump’s approach

January 21, 2026

Part 5 of The Davos Rupture Series

CanadianSnowmanSays

If Carney, Macron, and von der Leyen represented the serious world’s diagnosis and response to the rupture in the international order, several other voices at Davos revealed just how impossible Trump has made the positions of those trying to navigate between American demands and their own interests.

Some walked tightropes. Some called out the complicity. Some seized the opportunity to position themselves as the alternative.

Together, they illustrated the fundamental problem: While serious leaders were having a sophisticated conversation about navigating great power rivalry, Trump was creating crises that forced everyone to choose between subordination and resistance.

Mark Rutte: The Impossible Tightrope

Perhaps no one at Davos embodied the impossible position of Trump’s allies more than NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte.

On Tuesday morning, Trump posted screenshots of private messages from Rutte that were effusive in their praise:

“Dear Donald – what you accomplished in Syria today is incredible. I will use my media engagements in Davos to highlight your work there, in Gaza, and in Ukraine. I am committed to finding a way forward on Greenland. Can’t wait to see you. Yours, Mark.”

The message was warm, personal, flattering—exactly the kind of communication Trump responds to. Rutte knows this. Everyone knows this. Trump interprets any criticism as disloyalty, so allies learned long ago to praise in private.

Hours later, Rutte appeared on a panel in Davos and delivered a very different message to the assembled leaders:

“The main issue is not Greenland now, the main issue is Ukraine. I’m a little bit worried that we might drop the ball focusing so much on these other issues. As we speak, Russian missiles and Russian drones are attacking energy infrastructure in Ukraine. We know that it is now minus 20 degrees in Kyiv, we know that Ukraine can only take care of 60 percent of its own electricity.”

Rutte noted that Russians are losing about 1,000 soldiers every day, and their monthly losses now exceed Soviet losses over ten years in Afghanistan. Yet the Kremlin continues its aggression.

“I need the European allies to keep focus on this issue, and what I’m really worrying about is that because of the peace process and because of the 90 billion [euro loan for Ukraine], we lose sight that in the meantime Ukrainians do not have enough interceptors, enough American gear they particularly need... This focus on Ukraine should be our number one priority, and then we can discuss all the issues, including Greenland.”

The Tightrope

This is what diplomacy looks like in the Trump era: private flattery, public warnings, and the desperate attempt to refocus attention on actual security crises while the American president obsesses over territorial acquisition.

The contrast between Rutte’s texts and his panel remarks reveals the impossible calculus facing NATO and European leaders:

They know Trump responds to praise and personal appeals. They know he interprets criticism as disloyalty. They know he can devastate the alliance if provoked. So they flatter in private while trying to conduct serious statecraft in public.

Rutte’s messages to Trump: “What you accomplished in Syria is incredible,” “I will highlight your work,” “I am committed to finding a way forward.”

Rutte’s message to European leaders: Focus on Ukraine. Don’t let Greenland distract from the actual war where people are dying and cities are freezing.

But here’s the fundamental problem: While Rutte is trying to keep NATO allies focused on Ukraine’s actual defense needs, Trump is posting AI-generated images of American flags planted in Greenland and threatening wine tariffs against France.

The serious conversation and the performative spectacle are happening simultaneously. Rutte is trying to navigate both. It’s unclear if that’s even possible.

Gavin Newsom: “Stop Being Complicit”

California Governor Gavin Newsom delivered some of the forum’s most combative rhetoric on Tuesday, directly challenging European leaders for their response to Trump’s Greenland demands.

“It is time to get serious, and stop being complicit,” Newsom told reporters in Davos. “It’s time to stand tall and firm, have a backbone.”

Newsom called Europe’s response to Trump’s tariff threats “pathetic” and “embarrassing,” urging European leaders to unite and stand up to the United States rather than negotiating individually or hoping compliance will buy safety.

The irony was not lost on observers: an American state governor was lecturing European heads of state about standing up to the American president.

Why Newsom’s Frustration Matters

But Newsom’s frustration reflected a reality that Carney had articulated more diplomatically: when countries negotiate bilaterally with a hegemon, they negotiate from weakness. They compete with each other to be the most accommodating. This is the performance of sovereignty while accepting subordination.

Newsom was essentially telling European leaders: Stop performing. Actually resist.

He was calling out the Rutte approach—the private flattery, the public acquiescence, the hope that being nice to Trump will somehow protect national interests. Newsom was saying it doesn’t work. It never works. Compliance doesn’t buy safety. It just encourages more demands.

His message echoed Carney’s framework: You’re keeping the greengrocer’s sign in the window. You’re participating in rituals you know are false. And it’s not protecting you—it’s just making you complicit in your own subordination.

The Subtext

Newsom’s remarks also carried subtext about American politics. As a potential 2028 presidential candidate, Newsom was positioning himself as the anti-Trump—someone who believes in alliances, respects sovereignty, and understands that bullying allies undermines American security.

His presence in Davos, lecturing European leaders about backbone, was partly about 2026 American politics. But it was also genuinely about frustration with European fecklessness.

Both things can be true: Newsom is politically ambitious, and he’s right that European leaders need to stop hoping that compliance will buy them safety from Trump’s coercion.

He Lifeng: China Seizes the Moment

Chinese Vice Premier He Lifeng delivered his own special address on Tuesday, calling for increased cooperation and dialogue in the global economy.

“While economic globalization is not perfect,” He stated, “countries cannot completely reject it and retreat to self-isolation.”

He detailed China’s economic agenda for 2026, emphasizing domestic demand while maintaining production capacity. “China will open its door wider to the world,” he said, particularly in the services sector.

The Vacuum

The timing was notable. As Trump threatens tariffs and territorial annexation, China was positioning itself as the defender of economic integration and cooperative multilateralism—precisely the role the United States traditionally played in the post-war order.

This is the vacuum Carney warned about. When great powers abandon even the pretense of rules, when they use economic integration as a weapon rather than a mutual benefit, other powers step into the space.

China offering to “open its door wider to the world” while America threatens to close its allies’ access to American markets represents a geopolitical reversal that would have been unthinkable a decade ago.

He Lifeng wasn’t just making economic arguments. He was making a geopolitical pitch: The United States under Trump is unreliable, coercive, transactional. China, by contrast, offers stable economic partnership based on mutual benefit.

The Irony

The irony is thick. China—a country that restricts market access, subsidizes state enterprises, and uses economic leverage for political purposes—was positioning itself as the defender of free trade and globalization.

But the irony doesn’t make the pitch less effective. When the United States threatens its own allies with tariffs over Greenland, when it demands territorial acquisition as the price of alliance membership, when it uses trade as a weapon rather than a tool for mutual prosperity, China’s pitch becomes more credible.

Not because China has changed. But because America has.

The Two Conversations

What’s happening in Davos represents two fundamentally different conversations happening simultaneously:

Conversation One (The Serious One)

How do democratic middle powers navigate a world where great powers have abandoned even the pretense of rules-based order?

How do they build coalitions strong enough to resist coercion?

How do they maintain sovereignty when economic integration has become a weapon?

How do they preserve human rights, territorial integrity, and democratic values in an age of naked power politics?

Carney answered with variable geometry and the greengrocer’s sign framework.

Macron answered with protection, simplification, investment, and a refusal to prefer bullies to respect.

Von der Leyen answered with a comprehensive blueprint for European independence.

Rutte tried to answer by keeping focus on Ukraine while flattering Trump.

Newsom answered by demanding European backbone.

Conversation Two (Trump’s)

How can I use tariffs to force Denmark to sell Greenland?

Why won’t Macron join my Board even though I’m threatening his wine industry?

Should I share these private diplomatic texts on social media?

What AI image should I post next?

When do I get my Nobel Peace Prize?

The Problem

Carney, Macron, and von der Leyen are engaging in the first conversation. Trump is stuck in the second—and his boredom with the substance of the first conversation is precisely what makes him so dangerous.

The serious leaders are trying to build frameworks for cooperation in an age of great power rivalry. Trump is creating the great power rivalry they’re trying to navigate.

They’re trying to solve a problem Trump is actively making worse.

What Makes This Moment Different

Previous U.S. presidents have sometimes acted unilaterally. They’ve exempted themselves from rules when convenient. They’ve used economic leverage to advance American interests. Carney acknowledged as much—the fiction of the rules-based order was always “partially false.”

But they maintained the fiction. They performed the rituals. They showed up to the meetings and at least pretended that international law, alliance commitments, and diplomatic norms mattered.

Trump Can’t Even Be Bothered

Trump can’t even be bothered with the performance. He’s bored of it.

He wants the Board, not the tedious work of peace. He wants the Nobel Prize, but not the patience required to earn it. He wants territorial acquisition, but not the diplomacy that might achieve it consensually. He wants European submission, but not the alliance maintenance that builds genuine partnership.

So he posts AI images, shares private texts, threatens wine tariffs, and wonders why serious people are having serious conversations about the end of an era.

The Greengrocer’s Sign

This is why Carney’s Havel framework is so powerful. For decades, countries maintained the greengrocer’s sign—participating in rituals they knew were partially false because the system provided enough public goods to make the fiction worthwhile.

Trump has made maintaining the fiction impossible. He’s not just selective about rules—he denies they exist. He’s not just exempting America from constraints—he’s using alliances as leverage for territorial acquisition.

You can’t maintain the sign in the window when the shopkeeper is demanding territorial submission as the price of not being economically destroyed.

The Cost of Boredom

When Carney warned that “if you are not at the table, you’re on the menu,” he was describing a world where Trump’s boredom with diplomatic process has real consequences.

Middle powers are being forced to choose: submit bilaterally to American coercion, or band together and risk economic retaliation.

Democratic allies who spent seven decades building the transatlantic alliance are now discussing how to sanction the United States.

NATO members who sent troops to support a fellow alliance member are being punished with tariffs for doing so.

The “rules-based international order” that prevented great power conflicts for 70 years is collapsing not because it was perfect, but because the United States—its primary guarantor—has a president who finds maintaining it too boring to bother with.

The Reactions

Rutte tries to flatter Trump while warning about Ukraine.

Newsom calls out European complicity.

China positions itself as the stable alternative.

European leaders build independence frameworks.

Carney articulates the philosophical break.

Macron declares preference for respect over bullies.

Von der Leyen commits €90 billion to Ukraine regardless of U.S. support.

All of this is happening because Trump is bored of the actual work of maintaining alliances, conducting diplomacy, and building consensus.

The Voices Still to Come

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy was scheduled to speak at Ukraine House in Davos. His appearance would be particularly poignant given Rutte’s warnings that the focus on Greenland risks overshadowing Ukraine’s desperate need for continued military support.

The Financial Times reported that the launch of a planned $800 billion economic package for Ukraine—to be agreed by Europe, Kyiv and the US in Davos—has been derailed by the Greenland crisis.

This is what happens when a president is bored of the actual work of peace. Zelenskyy arrives seeking commitments for his country’s survival while Trump holds meetings about his Board of Peace and territorial expansion fantasies.

German Chancellor Friedrich Merz attended and said he would try to meet Trump on Wednesday. What he could possibly say to bridge the gap between Germany’s commitment to rules-based order and Trump’s “complete and total control” demands remained unclear.

The Countermeasures

European leaders were already discussing retaliatory measures:

  • 93 billion euros in counter-tariffs

  • Restrictions on American companies accessing EU markets

  • Potential deployment of the anti-coercion instrument (the “trade bazooka”) for the first time ever—against the United States

Denmark, notably, declined to attend Davos at all—a symbolic withdrawal that spoke volumes about how seriously the crisis had escalated.

Now Comes the Performance

The serious world has spent two days diagnosing the rupture in the international order:

Mark Carney has taken down the greengrocer’s sign and outlined variable geometry as the alternative.

Emmanuel Macron has declared that Europe prefers respect to bullies and outlined three pillars of response.

Ursula von der Leyen has committed €90 billion to Ukraine and built a blueprint for European independence.

Mark Rutte is walking an impossible tightrope between flattering Trump and warning about Ukraine.

Gavin Newsom is calling out European complicity and demanding backbone.

China is positioning itself as the defender of globalization while America threatens it.

They’ve discussed variable geometry coalitions, warned about subordination versus sovereignty, committed hundreds of billions to Ukraine and European defense, and tried to keep focus on actual security needs.

And now, President Trump has arrived in Davos.

The serious conversation has been had. The frameworks have been articulated. The commitments have been made. The warnings have been issued.

Now comes the performance.
