The “Biden Did Nothing” Lie

Part 26: How the White House Turned Institutional Caution Into Fake Exoneration

March 8, 2026

Karoline Leavitt has repeated this line so many times she probably says it in her sleep. Unfortunately, repetition doesn't make it true. (Getty)

By CanadianSnowmanSays

Every time the Epstein files produce something uncomfortable for this White House, Karoline Leavitt reaches for the same talking point. You’ve heard it. I’ve heard it. We’ve all heard it so many times that it has begun to harden into something resembling accepted fact.

Here it is, from her statement released just yesterday:

“The total baselessness of these accusations is also supported by the obvious fact that Joe Biden’s Department of Justice knew about them for four years and did nothing with them — because they knew President Trump did absolutely nothing wrong.”

She has deployed this line, or close variations of it, on at least half a dozen occasions since the Epstein Files Transparency Act forced the DOJ’s hand. It appears in nearly every White House statement responding to newly released documents. No reporter, to my knowledge, has stopped Leavitt mid-sentence and said: That argument is logically fraudulent, and here’s why.

So let me.

The Argument, Unpacked

The Leavitt formulation rests on a simple syllogism:

Premise: Biden’s DOJ had these allegations for four years.

Premise: Biden’s DOJ did not charge Trump.

Conclusion: Therefore Trump is innocent.

The middle step — the why of Biden’s DOJ not acting — is deliberately erased. And that missing middle is where the entire lie lives.

Because there is a vast difference between “we investigated and found him innocent” and “we chose, as a matter of institutional philosophy, not to act.” The Biden DOJ did not exonerate Trump. It did not investigate these specific allegations and clear him. It operated under a set of constraints — some principled, some arguably cowardly — that prevented it from pursuing a sitting or former president with anything resembling urgency.

Understanding those constraints doesn’t exonerate anyone. It explains a system.

The Garland Doctrine: Caution as Religion

Attorney General Merrick Garland (AP)

To understand why Biden’s DOJ “did nothing,” you have to understand Merrick Garland.

Garland was chosen for a specific reason. After four years of Trump-era attorneys general who functioned as the president’s personal defence lawyers — a period NYU law professor Melissa Murray described as having a “public defender” for the president rather than an attorney general — Biden needed someone who would restore the post-Watergate norm that the Justice Department operates independently from the White House.

Garland took that mandate and made it his entire identity.

The result was an attorney general so determined to avoid the appearance of political motivation that he moved at a pace that infuriated his own party. On the January 6th investigation alone, Garland waited nearly two years before appointing Special Counsel Jack Smith — a delay that progressive critics believe ultimately allowed Trump to run out the clock. As investigative journalists Carol Leonnig and Aaron C. Davis documented in their book Injustice, FBI and Justice Department officials deliberately chose to move cautiously over concerns about the implications of investigating a former and possibly future president.

This wasn’t limited to January 6th. Garland imposed what DOJ officials called the “60-day rule” — avoiding public investigative steps near elections — and applied it with extraordinary conservatism. He even halted investigation activity around the 2022 midterms, when Trump wasn’t even on the ballot.

This is the context Leavitt erases every time she opens her mouth.

The Hunter Biden Proof

If you need a single data point to demolish the “Biden weaponized DOJ” narrative, it is this: Garland’s Justice Department prosecuted the sitting president’s own son.

Think about that for a moment. Biden’s attorney general allowed federal charges to proceed against Hunter Biden — the president’s child — to demonstrate that the DOJ operated without political interference. Whatever you think of that case, it is the definitive refutation of the claim that Biden’s DOJ was a partisan instrument. A partisan DOJ does not indict the boss’s son.

Yet Leavitt’s talking point requires you to believe, simultaneously, that Biden’s DOJ was so ruthlessly partisan it “weaponized” justice against Trump, and so honestly nonpartisan that its failure to charge Trump on the Epstein allegations constitutes proof of innocence.

You cannot have it both ways. Pick one.

What the Epstein Allegations Actually Show

Let me be precise about what the newly released documents contain, because precision matters when the White House is trafficking in fog.

The DOJ released three previously withheld FBI interview summaries — known as 302 reports — from 2019. In these interviews, a woman alleged that Jeffrey Epstein introduced her to Trump in the early 1980s, when she was between 13 and 15 years old, and that Trump sexually assaulted her. The FBI conducted four interviews with this woman between July and October 2019. Only one had been previously released.

The DOJ initially claimed these documents were “incorrectly coded as duplicative.” They were not duplicates.

Now — and this is important — these allegations are uncorroborated. The woman was deemed ineligible for the Epstein Victims’ Compensation Program for reasons that remain unclear. She voluntarily dismissed a lawsuit against Epstein’s estate in 2021, reportedly after receiving a financial settlement.

But here is what the documents do not contain: any indication that investigators assessed the allegations and found them not credible. There is no prosecutorial finding clearing Trump of criminal liability. There is no exoneration memo. There is nothing that says “we looked into this and it didn’t hold up.”

There is only silence. And silence is not exoneration.

The Investigations Didn’t Even Happen Under Biden

Here’s a fact that rarely survives Leavitt’s spin cycle: the two federal investigations into Epstein occurred during the George W. Bush and Trump administrations — not Biden’s.

The first investigation, in Florida, ran from 2006 to 2008 under Bush. It ended with the notorious non-prosecution agreement engineered by Alexander Acosta — whom Trump later appointed as his Secretary of Labor.

The second investigation, in the Southern District of New York, resulted in Epstein’s July 2019 arrest — under Trump’s own DOJ. Epstein died in federal custody a month later, also under Trump’s DOJ.

By the time Biden took office in January 2021, Epstein was dead, Maxwell was awaiting trial, and the investigative phase was functionally complete. The 2019 interviews with the accusing woman had already been conducted. Biden’s DOJ inherited a closed case, not an open one.

So when Leavitt says Biden’s DOJ “knew about them for four years and did nothing,” she is describing a department that inherited completed witness interviews from the Trump era and — consistent with Garland’s broader philosophy — did not initiate new action against a former president based on uncorroborated claims.

That’s not exoneration. That’s institutional inertia.

The TACO Framework

For those who follow my work, this is a textbook example of what I call the TACO framework — Threaten, Accommodate, Capitulate, Obfuscate.

The “Biden did nothing” talking point is pure Obfuscate. It takes a complex institutional reality — Garland’s caution, DOJ independence norms, the evidentiary limitations of uncorroborated testimony, the fact that the investigations predated Biden entirely — and reduces it to a single, misleading sentence designed to shut down inquiry.

It works because it sounds logical on the surface. It works because most people don’t know the internal dynamics of Garland’s DOJ. It works because the media reports Leavitt’s statement in full without dissecting it.

And it works because nobody in the mainstream press has, to my knowledge, written the piece you’re reading right now.

The Bitter Irony

There is one final point worth making, and it is perhaps the cruelest irony of all.

Garland’s entire philosophy — the deliberate caution, the obsessive independence, the refusal to appear partisan — was designed to prevent exactly one thing: a cycle of retaliatory prosecutions where each new administration uses the DOJ to punish its predecessors.

That is precisely what happened anyway.

Under Trump’s second term, Attorney General Pam Bondi’s DOJ has pursued investigations of political opponents, while simultaneously being accused of protecting the president by withholding documents from the Epstein files. The House Oversight Committee — with bipartisan support — has voted to subpoena Bondi over the department’s handling of these very files.

Garland bent over backwards to avoid the appearance of politicization. His reward was to have his restraint repackaged as proof of Trump’s innocence, while the norms he tried to protect were demolished by his successor.

What This Means

The “Biden did nothing” lie matters because it is doing real work in the public discourse. Every time it goes unchallenged, the window of acceptable understanding narrows. The complexity gets flattened. The questions stop being asked.

So let me restate the reality as plainly as I can:

Biden’s DOJ did not exonerate Trump. It did not investigate these allegations and find them baseless. It operated under a philosophy of institutional restraint so extreme that it frustrated even Biden himself, who reportedly complained that Garland moved too slowly. The investigations into Epstein predated Biden’s presidency entirely. And the attorney general who presided over this era of caution also prosecuted the president’s own son to prove he wasn’t playing favourites.

None of that means the allegations against Trump are true. That is not my claim, and I will not make it.

But it does mean that Karoline Leavitt’s talking point — repeated now so many times that it has become reflexive — is not an argument. It is a conjuring trick. And it is long past time someone said so.
