The 16-Year Siege

Part 27: How Four Activists Plotted to Kill Climate Regulation (2005–2026)

March 10, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays

The Conspiracy That Wasn’t Hidden — It Was Ignored

Russell Vought, speaking at the White House in 2019, was part of the core group that prepared the repeal strategy. Credit...Evan Vucci/Associated Press

On February 9, 2026, The New York Times published a story that should have stopped the country cold. It reported that four Trump administration veterans — Russell T. Vought, Jeffrey B. Clark, Mandy Gunasekara, and Jonathan Brightbill — had spent years secretly drafting the legal, scientific, and regulatory framework to dismantle the federal government’s ability to fight climate change. Not to reform it. Not to slow it down. To kill it entirely.

They drafted executive orders. They solicited white papers from scientists willing to dispute the physics of a warming planet. They sought $2 million in secret funding to prepare regulatory documents a future administration could use on “Day 1.” And they operated, by their own written admission, in deliberate secrecy “to prevent media and other conflicted sources from shaming participants and undercutting the work before it is done.”

That is not lobbying. That is not policy advocacy. That is a premeditated campaign to eliminate public health protections that the country’s own scientists — and its own Supreme Court — determined were necessary.

And it worked.

But the campaign to destroy climate regulation is only half the story. The other half is what the Trump administration is doing to the alternatives — the renewable energy technologies that could replace fossil fuels, reduce emissions, and create millions of jobs in the process. Because it turns out that when you refuse to acknowledge the problem and sabotage the solution, what you’re left with is not a policy position. It’s an ideology of destruction.

What the Endangerment Finding Actually Is

Before we examine how these four individuals dismantled it, we need to understand what it was — and why its destruction matters more than almost anything else this administration has done.

In 2007, the Supreme Court ruled in Massachusetts v. EPA that the Clean Air Act gave the Environmental Protection Agency the authority — indeed, the obligation — to regulate greenhouse gases if it determined they endangered public health. Two years later, in 2009, the EPA issued what became known as the “endangerment finding.” It concluded, based on the overwhelming weight of peer-reviewed science, that six greenhouse gases — carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, hydrofluorocarbons, perfluorocarbons, and sulfur hexafluoride — were warming the planet and, in doing so, supercharging storms, wildfires, drought, heat waves, and sea level rise.

This was not a political document. It was a scientific determination, grounded in decades of atmospheric research, reviewed by thousands of scientists, and upheld repeatedly in court. It became the legal foundation for every federal regulation limiting greenhouse gas emissions — from tailpipe standards on automobiles to emission caps on power plants to methane rules for the oil and gas industry.

Without the endangerment finding, the federal government has no legal basis to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act. Period.

That is precisely why these four people spent 16 years trying to destroy it.

The Four Horsemen

Jeffrey B. Clark started earliest. In 2005, as a 38-year-old Justice Department lawyer in the George W. Bush administration, Clark argued in federal court that the Clean Air Act did not give the EPA power to regulate greenhouse gases. The Supreme Court rejected that argument in Massachusetts v. EPA. It was, by all accounts, a defeat Clark never accepted. He would later call climate regulation a “Leninistic” plot to seize control of the economy, describe environmentalists as a “crazy climate cult,” and compare them to the authoritarian pigs in Orwell’s Animal Farm.

Clark resurfaced in 2022 at the Center for Renewing America, a conservative organization run by Russell T. Vought from a pigeon-infested rowhouse near the Capitol. Vought, who served as Trump’s budget director in the first term, had been railing against “climate alarmism” and was using the center to draft sweeping plans for a second Trump administration. Under Vought’s supervision, Clark drafted executive orders that a future president could use to scrap Biden’s climate policies. He also developed legal arguments for repealing the endangerment finding itself.

At the time, Clark was under criminal investigation for his role in Trump’s effort to overturn the 2020 election results in Georgia. Trump preemptively pardoned him in November 2024. The Georgia case was dismissed.

Mandy Gunasekara brought a different kind of credential. She had served as EPA chief of staff during Trump’s first term, and before that, she was an aide to Senator James Inhofe of Oklahoma — the most vocal climate change denialist in the United States Senate. Gunasekara is known in Washington for handing Inhofe a snowball on a cold February day in 2015. Inhofe held it up on the Senate floor as evidence that the planet could not be warming dangerously. This is who was drafting the scientific case against the endangerment finding.

Senator James Inhofe (R-OK) holds up a snowball on the Senate floor in 2015 to mock climate change concerns, with Mandy Gunasekara (who provided the snowball) visible nearby. This became a symbol of denialism.

Gunasekara went on to write the EPA chapter of Project 2025, the Heritage Foundation’s blueprint for a second Trump term. She later described herself as “extremely proud” of work that helped produce “Cooling the Climate Hysteria” — a collection of essays by scholars who reject mainstream climate science, adorned with a melting ice cube on its cover.

Jonathan Brightbill was the quietest of the four, but arguably the most technically dangerous. A partner at the law firm Winston & Strawn, Brightbill had served in the Justice Department’s environment division during Trump’s first term, where his primary role was defending the administration’s repeal of Obama-era climate regulations. His real expertise was in the legal architecture of environmental deregulation.

In the summer of 2022, Gunasekara and Brightbill co-authored a funding pitch seeking $2 million for a secret campaign to kill the endangerment finding. The pitch, obtained by Fieldnotes, a watchdog group investigating the oil and gas industry, laid out their plan: draft regulatory documents a future administration could use to abandon the finding, and solicit white papers from scientists who rejected the scientific consensus on climate change.

The Heritage Foundation agreed to fund at least part of this work.

The Secrecy Clause

Let us pause on one sentence from the Gunasekara-Brightbill funding pitch, because it tells you everything about what these people understood about their own enterprise:

The campaign would operate in secret “to prevent media and other conflicted sources from shaming participants and undercutting the work before it is done.”

Read that again. They knew that public scrutiny would undermine what they were doing. They knew that exposure would cause participants to withdraw. They knew, in other words, that what they were planning could not withstand the light of day. So they operated in darkness.

This is consciousness of guilt. Not in the criminal sense — there is no law against lobbying to destroy scientific findings, however reckless — but in the moral sense. These were people who understood that their project was indefensible on its merits, and who therefore chose concealment over persuasion.

When you have to hide your policy campaign from the public, you have already conceded that the public would not support it.

The Other War: Killing the Alternative

If the campaign against the endangerment finding were happening in isolation, it would be reckless enough. But it is not happening in isolation. It is happening alongside a systematic assault on the renewable energy technologies that represent the only viable path away from fossil fuel dependence.

Consider what the rest of the world has figured out while four ideologues in Washington were drafting secret plans to protect the coal industry.

Vast array of solar panels in a large-scale installation, showcasing one of the cheapest new electricity sources globally (as per IEA data), contrasting U.S. policy sabotage with worldwide clean energy progress.

Solar energy is now the cheapest source of new electricity generation in history. Not the cheapest renewable source — the cheapest source, period. The International Energy Agency reported in 2024 that solar photovoltaic electricity is cheaper than new coal, gas, or nuclear plants in virtually every market on Earth. In many regions, building new solar is cheaper than continuing to operate existing fossil fuel plants.

Wind energy has undergone a similar revolution. Global wind capacity has grown from 17 gigawatts in 2000 to over 1,000 gigawatts by 2024. Offshore wind farms now power millions of homes across Europe and Asia. Onshore wind is among the cheapest electricity sources in the American Midwest and Great Plains — the same regions that produce much of the country’s oil and gas.

Battery storage technology, the historical weak point of renewable energy, has seen costs fall by more than 90% since 2010. Lithium-ion battery packs that cost over $1,100 per kilowatt-hour in 2010 now cost under $140. Grid-scale storage projects are being deployed across the world, solving the intermittency problem that critics once cited as proof that renewables could never replace fossil fuels.

Electric vehicles have gone from novelty to inevitability. Every major automaker on the planet — including the American Big Three — has committed billions to EV production. China, which understood this transition faster than anyone, now dominates global EV manufacturing and battery production. Europe has set a 2035 deadline for ending the sale of new internal combustion vehicles.

And what is the United States doing under Donald Trump? Revoking the scientific finding that justifies regulating the pollution these technologies are designed to eliminate. Rolling back EV mandates. Freezing offshore wind leases. Slashing funding for clean energy research. Pulling out of the Paris Agreement for the second time.

Every other major economy on the planet is racing toward a clean energy future — not primarily out of environmental idealism, but because that is where the economics now point. The United States, under the guidance of four ideologues and a president who calls climate change a “hoax,” is racing in the opposite direction.

It is the equivalent of watching the world transition from horses to automobiles and responding by investing in a bigger stable.

I have written extensively about this transition in my series The Great Energy Challenge: Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies, which examines each of these renewable technologies in detail — what’s working, what’s not, and what combinations of clean power sources might fuel both the AI revolution and a sustainable future. The technologies exist. The economics work. The engineering is proven. What is missing, in the United States, is the political will to deploy them — because that will has been systematically dismantled by the same people who are now dismantling the endangerment finding.

Windmills Kill Birds (And Other Fantasies)

Of all the arguments marshalled against renewable energy, none captures the intellectual bankruptcy of the opposition quite like the war on wind turbines.

Donald Trump has waged a personal vendetta against wind energy for over a decade, beginning with his campaign to block an offshore wind farm near his golf course in Aberdeen, Scotland. He lost that fight — Scotland’s courts were unimpressed — but the grudge metastasized into national policy. As president, he has frozen offshore wind leases, revoked permits for approved projects, and repeated a rotating cast of claims about wind turbines that range from the merely wrong to the genuinely bizarre.

Wind turbines kill birds, he says. They cause cancer, he has claimed. They make property values plummet. They are ugly — an aesthetic judgment that carries particular weight from a man who gold-plates his bathroom fixtures.

Let us address the bird argument, since it is the one most frequently deployed with a straight face. Wind turbines do kill birds — an estimated 140,000 to 500,000 per year in the United States, according to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. That is a real number and it deserves attention.

Now here are some other numbers. Domestic and feral cats kill an estimated 1.3 to 4 billion birds per year in the United States. Building collisions kill 600 million. Power lines kill 25 million. Cars kill 200 million. If you are genuinely concerned about avian mortality and your policy priority is banning wind turbines rather than, say, keeping cats indoors, you are not engaged in wildlife conservation. You are engaged in fossil fuel apologetics dressed in feathers.

And here is the number that puts the entire argument to bed: climate change itself is the single greatest long-term threat to bird populations globally. Rising temperatures are disrupting migration patterns, destroying habitats, and causing population collapses across hundreds of species. The Audubon Society — an organization whose entire mission is protecting birds — has stated unequivocally that climate change threatens two-thirds of North American bird species with extinction. Audubon supports responsibly sited wind energy as part of the solution.

So the argument, stripped to its essence, is this: we must reject the technology that kills thousands of birds per year in order to protect an industry whose emissions are driving hundreds of species toward extinction. This is not conservation. This is a golf course owner who doesn’t like the view.

There is an old image of the ostrich burying its head in the sand to avoid danger. Ornithologists will tell you ostriches don’t actually do this — it’s a myth. But it’s a useful metaphor for a government that refuses to look at the evidence in front of it — that burns gas, oil, coal, and propane while cheaper, cleaner alternatives exist and scale globally, that watches China install more solar capacity each year than the United States has installed in total, and responds by revoking the science and doubling down on fossil fuels. Although in this case, the head may be buried somewhere darker than sand, and one suspects the breathing is no easier for it.

What Changed — and What Didn’t

Here is what makes this story different from the routine swing of policy between administrations.

When a new president takes office, regulations get revised. Priorities shift. Enforcement changes. This is normal. Obama tightened fuel economy standards; Trump loosened them. Biden tightened them again. This is the ordinary, if frustrating, rhythm of American governance.

The endangerment finding is not a regulation. It is a scientific determination — a conclusion, based on evidence, that greenhouse gases endanger public health. To revoke it, the administration must demonstrate that the underlying science has changed. It hasn’t. It has gotten stronger. Every major scientific body on the planet — NASA, NOAA, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, the national academies of science of every industrialized nation — affirms that human-caused greenhouse gas emissions are warming the planet and intensifying extreme weather.

What has changed is not the science. What has changed is the composition of the Supreme Court, the gutting of Chevron deference in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024), and the installation of ideologues in key regulatory positions.

Jeffrey Clark is now the acting head of the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs — the government’s top regulatory official. Russell Vought is once again the White House budget director and Clark’s boss. From these positions, both men are focused on dismantling green initiatives. Clark has personally pushed EPA lawyers to strengthen their legal arguments for repealing the endangerment finding.

The people who wrote the plan are now executing the plan.

They Knew the Science Wouldn’t Hold Up in Court

And the way they executed it revealed something they would rather you not notice.

When the EPA released the full text of its final rule on February 14, 2026, legal experts spotted a telling revision. The draft rule, released back in July, had leaned heavily on claims disputing the science of climate change, drawing in part from a widely criticized Department of Energy report drafted by researchers who reject the scientific consensus. That report was itself challenged in court.

In the final version, the science-denial arguments were gone. Gutted. Replaced entirely by legal reasoning. As Jody Freeman, director of Harvard Law School’s Environmental and Energy Law Program, observed, the revision was designed to make the proposal look less irrational in front of judges.

Think about what that means. They are no longer even pretending to claim the science is wrong. They abandoned the scientific argument because they knew it would make them look, in Freeman’s word, “crazy.” Instead, they are arguing that even if greenhouse gases do endanger public health — which they manifestly do, and which the administration no longer disputes — the Clean Air Act doesn’t give the EPA the authority to do anything about it.

Their arguments are three-fold: that the Clean Air Act applies only to localized pollution, not gases that spread globally; that American vehicle emissions are too small a fraction of global warming to warrant regulation; and that shifting the auto industry toward electric vehicles is so consequential that only Congress, not a regulatory agency, could authorize it.

Richard Lazarus, the Harvard law professor who wrote the definitive account of the original Massachusetts v. EPA case, called this reasoning “absurd.” The EPA was essentially arguing that because the problem is so massive and so global, no single sector of the American economy contributes enough to justify regulation — therefore the government has no authority to do anything at all.

Lee Zeldin, the EPA administrator, framed it differently at the White House announcement: “We looked at what the highest court in the land had said, and we used a very simple metric: If Congress didn’t authorize it, EPA shouldn’t be doing it.”

To be fair, the administration’s supporters frame the repeal differently. They cite projected savings of over $1.3 trillion in regulatory costs. They argue that vehicle emission standards amounted to a de facto electric vehicle mandate that stripped consumers of choice. They contend that after the Supreme Court’s 2024 Loper Bright decision dismantled Chevron deference, agencies like the EPA no longer have the legal latitude to make sweeping regulatory determinations without explicit congressional authorization. These are not frivolous arguments — they reflect real tensions in administrative law, and reasonable people can disagree about where to draw the line between agency expertise and legislative authority.

But none of them address the science. Not one of these arguments disputes that greenhouse gases are warming the planet, that the warming endangers public health, or that the consequences will intensify for decades. The administration’s own final rule no longer even attempts to make that case. What these arguments amount to, stripped of their legal dress, is this: yes, the house is on fire, but the fire department wasn’t properly authorized by Congress, so we’re sending them home. The flames, presumably, will sort themselves out.

He did not mention that the highest court in the land, in Massachusetts v. EPA, had specifically ordered the agency to determine whether greenhouse gases endangered public health and, if so, to regulate them. The agency did both. The courts upheld both. And now the agency’s own administrator was pretending the Supreme Court had said the opposite of what it actually said.

This is gaslighting with a government seal on it.

“Total Victory”

Myron Ebell, who helped the first Trump administration set up its EPA operations and has spent nearly three decades attacking climate science, used a revealing phrase in the Times article: “We are pretty close to total victory.”

Consider what “total victory” means in this context. It means the permanent elimination of the federal government’s ability to regulate the gases that are warming the planet. It means that no future president — not the next one, not the one after that — can simply reinstate regulations, because the legal foundation for those regulations will have been removed. Rebuilding the endangerment finding from scratch would take years and would face immediate legal challenge from the same network of activists and lawyers who spent 16 years tearing it down.

But “total victory” also means something broader. It means locking the United States into fossil fuel dependence at precisely the moment when the rest of the world is moving away from it. It means ceding the clean energy economy — the largest economic transformation since the industrial revolution — to China, Europe, and any other nation smart enough to follow the physics and the economics rather than the ideology.

This is not a policy disagreement. This is demolition — of climate protections and of the economic future that would replace them. And Ebell named the demolition crew: “No amount of outside public support would have done anything if there hadn’t been those four people: Russ and Jeff and John and Mandy.”

Four people. Sixteen years. The destruction of the legal framework protecting 330 million Americans — and, given the scale of U.S. emissions, billions more worldwide — from the consequences of unchecked climate change. And the simultaneous sabotage of the technologies that could have made the whole debate moot.

Where Was the Cavalry?

The most damning element of this story is not the conspiracy. It is the absence of resistance.

Conservative groups and businesses fought the endangerment finding when it was first issued in 2009. But as they lost legal challenges and public concern about climate change grew, the corporate world retreated. By 2017, hundreds of U.S. companies — including oil companies and major manufacturers — had publicly accepted the reality of climate change. Even Trump’s own first-term advisers rejected the most extreme demands to challenge the science. Days before Trump left office in January 2021, his EPA denied a petition from Ebell’s group to reconsider the endangerment finding.

As Michael McKenna, who worked on energy issues in Trump’s first White House, put it: “There just wasn’t an appetite among any of the institutional crowd.”

But Vought, Clark, Gunasekara, and Brightbill didn’t need the institutional crowd. They needed money, secrecy, and a sympathetic president. The Heritage Foundation provided the first. Their operational discipline provided the second. And Donald Trump — who has called climate change a "hoax" and a "con job," though his readers will know by now who is the actual “con” — provided the third.

Neil Chatterjee, a Republican who led the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission during Trump’s first term, identified what happened with unusual clarity: “It’s not the corporate interests. It’s the pure ideological activists who believe that climate change is a hoax, who believe that this was about transferring wealth and driving socialism and destroying renewable energy and promoting left-wing ideology.”

“This is their moment,” Chatterjee said.

Note Chatterjee’s inclusion of “destroying renewable energy” in that list. This was never just about deregulation. It was about preventing the alternative. Because the moment clean energy becomes dominant, the fossil fuel economy — and the ideological infrastructure built around defending it — becomes irrelevant. The war on wind, solar, and EVs is not a side project. It is the other front of the same war.

The Accountability Void

This is Part 27 of The Trump Accountability Project, and by now the pattern is achingly familiar. Four individuals, operating with institutional backing and ideological conviction, have positioned themselves to inflict generational damage on public health, the global environment, and America’s economic competitiveness. They did so in secret, with documented intent, and are now executing their plan from positions of government power.

And there is no mechanism to stop them.

The courts may eventually rule that the repeal of the endangerment finding is arbitrary and capricious — a legal standard that requires the agency to provide a reasoned explanation based on evidence. But this Supreme Court has already signaled its sympathy for limiting agency regulatory authority. The Loper Bright decision stripped away Chevron deference, meaning courts no longer give agencies the benefit of the doubt when interpreting their own statutory authority. The legal terrain has shifted beneath the feet of anyone who would challenge this repeal.

Congress could act, but a Republican majority has no interest in preserving climate regulation. The corporate world could mobilize, but it abandoned this fight years ago. The scientific community can object — and has, loudly — but as Gunasekara demonstrated when she handed a snowball to Senator Inhofe, the people driving this agenda are not interested in evidence. They are interested in the view from the golf course.

Steven J. Milloy, a former Trump transition adviser who runs a website promoting the theory that climate change is not real, said the quiet part out loud: “I hope we don’t blow it. I don’t know when or if this opportunity will come around again.”

He is right to worry. The opportunity exists only because of a specific alignment of ideological actors, judicial composition, and presidential sympathy. But the damage, if done, will long outlast all of them.

The Bottom Line

I write this from Canada, where we share an atmosphere, an ocean, a continental weather system, and increasingly, a wildfire season with the United States. Carbon dioxide does not respect borders. Methane does not carry a passport. What the Trump administration does to American climate regulation does not stay in America. And from where I sit, watching the smoke from American and Canadian wildfires merge into a single, indistinguishable haze, Myron Ebell’s “total victory” looks more like total abdication — not just of environmental responsibility, but of economic rationality, scientific literacy, and basic intergenerational decency.

Donald Trump will never be held accountable for the damage he is enabling to the global environment. Not by the courts, which are stacked in his favor. Not by Congress, which is complicit. Not by the corporate world, which has retreated. Not by the electorate, which chose him knowing his position.

The accountability will come from physics. It will come in the form of storms that hit harder, fires that burn longer, droughts that last years, and seas that swallow coastlines — including, with particular irony, the coastline of Palm Beach, Florida, where Mar-a-Lago sits approximately five feet above sea level.

And it will come from economics. While the United States dismantles its climate framework and wages war on windmills — because they kill birds, because they cause cancer, because they spoil the view from the 14th tee — China will build the solar panels, manufacture the batteries, and dominate the industries of the 21st century. The clean energy economy is coming whether Donald Trump likes it or not. As I documented in The Great Energy Challenge, the technologies to power the future without burning the planet already exist, and they are already cheaper. The only question is whether the United States will lead that future, participate in it, or watch it from the sidelines while four ideologues and a president who thinks wind turbines cause cancer congratulate themselves on their total victory.

The cost will be paid by Trump’s children, his grandchildren, and their children after them. It will be paid by the children of the four people who spent 16 years making this possible. It will be paid by every person alive today under the age of 30, and every person not yet born.

That is what “total victory” looks like. Not a victory over regulations, or over Democrats, or even over the scientific community. A victory over the future — won by people who will not be around to face the consequences, fought in defense of an industry the rest of the world is leaving behind, and celebrated by a man who thinks the greatest threat posed by a wind turbine is what it does to his view.

Four people. Sixteen years. One president who called it a hoax.

And a planet that doesn’t care what any of them believe.
