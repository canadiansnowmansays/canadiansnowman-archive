Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 15: The Hemisphere Looks Away — Latin America, Canada, and the Abandonment of Cuba

April 13, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Tourist stands on the beach next to empty sun loungers as the U.S. blocks shipments of oil from reaching the island nation, in Varadero, Cuba, February 14, 2026. REUTERS/Norlys Perez/File Photo

In the winter of 2026, a behavioral therapist from Ontario named Debbie Sutherland was on vacation in Cayo Las Brujas when the lights went out. Not in the way that lights sometimes go out in a tropical resort — a brief flicker, a generator humming to life, normalcy restored. The lights went out and stayed out. The stores in the nearby mall closed. A section of her hotel went completely dark, reserved for stranded employees who had no way to get home. Only the tourists kept their electricity.

A hotel porter took her aside one evening and told her to pack. She was leaving at six in the morning. Airlines were sending empty jets to take tourists home.

On the road to the airport, she passed abandoned trucks, cars, and motorcycles, apparently out of gas, left where they had stopped. “I’ve never been kicked out of a country before,” said Drew Garneau, a car dealership worker from Nova Scotia who was evacuated from a different Cuban resort around the same time. He had just put down the microphone after performing a Bon Jovi hit at karaoke night when a porter told him the same thing Sutherland had been told.

These are not the dramatic images that appear in foreign policy analyses of the Cuba crisis. They are the human texture of what happens when a superpower applies maximum pressure to a small island and the rest of the world decides, one by one, that the cost of helping is too high. The Canadians went home. The Europeans canceled their bookings. The Russian ships that had promised oil were nowhere to be seen. Brazil and Colombia and Mexico — three left-governed oil-exporting countries that had every ideological reason to help and considerable practical capacity to do so — sent food packages and expressed regret.

Life support machines have become particularly vulnerable parts of the medical system, as blackouts can knock them offline for several minutes until backup generators kick in.

Meanwhile, inside Cuba’s hospitals, the consequences of the hemisphere’s inaction were becoming irreversible. Six Cuban doctors told the New York Times that rapidly deteriorating conditions were causing deaths that would otherwise have been preventable. The chief anesthesiologist at Havana’s largest pediatric hospital said she could see it “in shift handovers, in colleagues’ comments and in children I’ve operated on.” A doctor at the country’s leading maternity hospital reported that three newborns died in February — the most she could recall in a single month — attributing the increase in premature births partly to a shortage of antibiotics. Ninety-six thousand patients were awaiting surgery. Thirty thousand children had delayed vaccines. Twenty thousand patients on dialysis or radiation therapy were receiving inconsistent care. One doctor was in the middle of operating on a two-month-old boy when the lights went out; nurses in the neonatal unit had already made contingency plans for a fully powerless hospital — swaddle the newborns in blankets, put them back in dead incubators, and hope they stayed warm enough to survive. A public health expert at Johns Hopkins, comparing what he was seeing to crises in Afghanistan, Ukraine, and Gaza, said simply: “This is not subtle, this is extreme.”

Cuba entered its most dangerous crisis in sixty-seven years essentially alone. And the story of how it came to be alone tells us something important about the current state of the world — something that goes beyond Cuba, beyond the hemisphere, and beyond the particular cruelties of the Trump administration’s foreign policy.

Mexico: The Cradle That Stood Down

For decades, Mexico’s relationship with Cuba was one of the most consistent acts of principled defiance in Latin American diplomatic history. When the United States pressured every country in the hemisphere to break with Havana after the Revolution, Mexico refused. When the Organization of American States voted to expel Cuba in 1962, Mexico was the only member state that voted against. When successive American administrations demanded allied compliance with the embargo, Mexico maintained trade, maintained diplomacy, and maintained the informal compact in which Castro agreed not to export revolution to Mexican soil in exchange for Mexico’s protection on the world stage.

That protection extended, in more recent years, to oil. As Venezuela’s shipments to Cuba declined because of Venezuela’s own economic disintegration, Mexico stepped into the gap. Under the leftist administrations of Andrés Manuel López Obrador and his successor Claudia Sheinbaum, Mexico became Cuba’s primary oil supplier — a quiet act of solidarity that continued even as Cuba’s economic situation deteriorated and American pressure on the arrangement intensified.

Then, in late January 2026, Trump issued his executive order threatening tariffs on any country that provided oil to Cuba. Mexico halted its shipments within days.

Sheinbaum’s position was genuinely impossible. Mexico is one of the most economically dependent countries on the United States in the world — trade with its northern neighbor constitutes roughly 80 percent of Mexican exports, and the two countries were simultaneously negotiating the renewal of a major trade agreement that gave Washington enormous leverage over Mexican decision-making. The cost of defying Trump over Cuba was not abstract. It was measurable in peso terms, in jobs, in the political viability of a government that had its own domestic priorities and its own survival to consider.

And yet Sheinbaum said it anyway: “You cannot suffocate a people like this.”

The statement was not accompanied by oil shipments. It was the moral observation of a leader who understood what was happening and could not stop it — who could articulate the wrong being done without possessing the power to prevent it. As political statements go, it was honest in a way that most of what was being said about Cuba at that moment was not. It was also entirely without practical consequence for the Cubans going fifteen hours a day without electricity.

Mexico sent food packages. Cuba needed fuel.

What Mexico’s government could not do officially, a grassroots international convoy attempted to do anyway. The Nuestra América Convoy — an international coalition of groups and individuals from Latin America and Europe, inspired by the flotillas that had attempted to reach Gaza — organised a humanitarian aid mission to Cuba in late March. One former fishing boat arrived in Havana on March 25 carrying 14 tonnes of food and medicine, 73 solar panels, and about a dozen bicycles. Two smaller sailboats, the Friendship and the Tiger Moth, went missing shortly after departing Isla Mujeres on March 21, prompting a Mexican navy search and rescue operation. Nine crew members were aboard — from Poland, France, Cuba, and the United States. Díaz-Canel posted his concern on social media. The US Coast Guard said it had been notified but had not been asked to help. The boats were found 80 nautical miles northwest of Havana, delayed by bad weather, and arrived safely on March 28 escorted by the Mexican navy. The convoy coordinator said on docking: “Solidarity with the Cuban people doesn’t stop at borders. It crosses oceans.”

It is worth pausing on that sentence. While the governments of Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico calculated the cost of helping and decided it was too high, nine civilians from four countries — on two small sailboats in Caribbean weather — made a different calculation and acted on it. They could not deliver oil. They could not restore the grid. They brought solar panels and medicine and the symbolic weight of people who chose to show up. In a crisis defined by abandonment, that is not nothing. It is also, measured against what is needed, not nearly enough.

Brazil, Colombia, and the Left That Couldn’t

Brazil and Colombia presented a different version of the same dilemma, and their failure to act is worth examining with some care because it reveals how completely the architecture of international solidarity that once protected Cuba has been dismantled.

Both countries were governed in early 2026 by leftists who had spent their careers invoking Cuba as a symbol of resistance to American imperialism. Brazil’s Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva had been a defender of Cuban sovereignty throughout his long political career. Colombia’s Gustavo Petro, a former guerrilla fighter, had been among the most vocal Latin American critics of American Cuba policy. Both had, as part of their foreign policy programs, pushed to remove Cuba from the United States’ list of state sponsors of terrorism — a designation that cuts Cuba off from international financial systems and makes normal commerce nearly impossible.

In the winter of 2026, both were limiting their help to basic food staples.

The explanation, in both cases, was the same: secondary sanctions risk. Brazil’s national oil company, Petrobras, is publicly traded with extensive ties to American banking and financial markets. A decision to ship oil to Cuba would expose Petrobras to Treasury Department sanctions that could cascade through its entire international financial relationship. The cost was not hypothetical — it was the kind of specific, quantifiable exposure that a company’s legal department could calculate precisely, and the calculation came out the same way every time.

“Any gesture of independence now carries the threat of immediate, devastating retaliation,” said a Mexican political scientist, articulating the constraint that Brazil and Colombia faced as clearly as any official statement. “One simply cannot predict the fallout of President Trump’s ire.”

There was also a domestic political dimension that complicated the picture in Brazil specifically. Lula was facing a re-election contest against Flávio Bolsonaro, the son of the former right-wing president, in what had become a highly competitive race. Cuba’s increasingly draconian crackdowns on dissent — civilian spy networks, new censorship laws criminalizing online criticism of the government — had eroded the support that Cuba traditionally commanded in the Brazilian left. “Cuba’s treatment of the domestic opposition has made it very difficult even for hard-core cadres of the Workers Party to come out in support of the regime,” one political scientist observed.

The Cuban government’s authoritarianism had cost it the political protection that its ideological allies might otherwise have been willing to pay a price to provide. This is one of the crueler ironies of the situation: the repression that the Cuban government uses to maintain its internal control has steadily eroded the external solidarity that was one of its most important forms of protection. The people who suffered under that repression were now also suffering from its diplomatic consequences, though they were not the ones who had chosen it.

The Exodus That Changed Everything

A man at the airport in Havana preparing to leave Cuba and emigrate to the United States. Since 2020, roughly 2.75 million have left the island, according to some estimates / Carlos Batista/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Underlying the geopolitical shifts was a demographic reality that had quietly transformed the Cuba question over the preceding five years.

Since 2020, an estimated 2.75 million Cubans had left the island — the largest demographic exodus in Cuba’s modern history, representing roughly a quarter of the country’s population. The scale dwarfed the Mariel boatlift of 1980. It exceeded the rafters crisis of the mid-1990s. It was not a single dramatic event but a sustained hemorrhage of a population that had concluded, in enormous numbers, that life elsewhere was preferable to life in Cuba under the conditions the Cuban state was able to provide.

These departing Cubans were not, for the most part, ideological dissidents or members of the pre-revolutionary upper class. They were young professionals, skilled workers, people in the prime of their productive lives who saw no viable future in an economy that could not provide reliable electricity, adequate food, or a currency worth holding. Their departure was itself a form of political statement — the most comprehensive verdict on the Cuban government’s performance that any democratic election could have produced — but it was a statement made by absence rather than presence, and its political meaning was ambiguous in ways that complicated any simple reading.

The diaspora was also being transformed by the exodus in ways that the established Cuban-American community in Miami had not fully absorbed. The new arrivals were poorer, younger, less ideologically defined by the anti-Castro politics that had shaped Miami’s Cuban political culture for six decades. Many had left not because they hated the Revolution but because the Revolution had failed to provide a viable life. Their political preferences, to the extent they could be assessed, were less uniformly hardline than those of the exile establishment — more interested in a Cuba that worked than in a Cuba that was ideologically pure.

The exodus was simultaneously reshaping receiving countries. Brazil had seen such a surge in Cuban immigration that Cubans became the top asylum-seeking nationality in 2025, surpassing Venezuelans for the first time. Mexico, Nicaragua, Ecuador, and countries across the region were absorbing Cuban migrants in numbers that were creating their own political pressures. “None of those folks are cheering their government,” one historian observed. “They have all been vectors about what is actually happening in Cuba.”

The 2.75 million who had left were also 2.75 million people who were no longer in Cuba to bear the cost of the blockade — which meant that the population remaining on the island was, on average, older, less mobile, and more dependent on the state’s capacity to provide basic services. The very people least able to absorb the shock of the blockade were the ones left to absorb it.

Canada: The Silence That Speaks

The Canadian dimension of this story is the one that this series, written from Toronto, has the most particular obligation to address honestly.

Canada’s relationship with Cuba has been documented in Part 8 of this series — the decades of diplomatic independence, the Trudeau friendship with Castro, the 1.3 million Canadians a year who chose Varadero over Miami, the quiet pride in a foreign policy tradition that treated Cuba as a country rather than a problem. That tradition was real, and it was genuinely different from what any American administration would have chosen.

By early 2026, Air Canada had suspended its flights to Cuba, citing fuel supply challenges and instability in the power grid. “We do not want to put the health, safety, and well-being of our passengers at risk,” an Air Canada spokesman said. The statement was accurate and responsible as a matter of airline operations. It was also the end of something — the practical expression of a relationship that had been built over forty years of Canadians arriving in Havana with winter pallor and leaving with sunburns and rum, of hotel workers telling Ontario tourists “we would die without Canada,” of a bilateral connection that had survived every American administration’s pressure to sever it.

The Canadian government’s response to the blockade had been, at the diplomatic level, notably muted. Canada was locked in its own trade dispute with Washington — the tariff war that had reshaped Canadian economic and political life throughout 2025 and 2026. A government already fighting for Canadian economic survival on one front had limited appetite for opening a second confrontation with Washington over Cuba. The calculation was understandable. It was also a departure from the tradition of Lester Pearson and Pierre Trudeau — the tradition of saying things that powerful friends preferred not to hear, at the cost of something, because saying them was right.

Sheinbaum said it. Canada did not. The distance between those two positions is not merely a matter of diplomatic style. It is a measure of how much the Trump-era restructuring of North American relationships has constrained Canadian foreign policy independence in ways that extend well beyond trade.

There is a larger question here that Canadians will need to reckon with, not just about Cuba but about what kind of country Canada chooses to be when the cost of principle is real and the pressure from Washington is sustained. The Cuba case is, in this sense, a test case for Canadian foreign policy identity — a preview of the choices that will present themselves repeatedly as American power under the current administration is applied to targets that Canada has historically been willing to defend.

The Cuban people, for their part, have not forgotten. “The Cuban people love Canadians,” Debbie Sutherland was told. “They would die without Canada.” That was the relationship at its warmest. Whether it survives the winter of 2026 — whether the tourists come back, whether the Air Canada flights resume, whether Canada finds its voice — is a question that has not yet been answered.

The Loneliness of the Island

What the material in this part of the series adds up to is a portrait of a kind of international abandonment that has no precedent in Cuba’s modern history.

During the Cold War, Cuba had the Soviet Union. During the Special Period, Cuba had Venezuela, and eventually the pink tide of Latin American left governments that embraced it. During the Obama years, Cuba had a diplomatic opening and the cautious hope of normalization. In each of these periods, the isolation that American policy sought to impose was mitigated by relationships that gave Cuba room to maneuver — economic lifelines, diplomatic cover, the sense that the world beyond Washington was not entirely hostile.

In 2026, those relationships had been systematically dismantled. Venezuela was under American control. Mexico had stood down. Brazil and Colombia had calculated that the cost of helping was too high. Russia dispatched a sanctioned tanker — the Anatoly Kolodkin, carrying 730,000 barrels — toward Cuba in a gesture of solidarity that carried pronounced Cold War echoes and risked a confrontation with the U.S. Navy; after appearing to divert, it delivered its cargo to Matanzas in late March after Trump waved it through saying “They have to survive” — the blockade’s first acknowledged exception, immediately qualified by the White House as “not a policy change.” Ecuador had expelled Cuba’s diplomats. Jamaica had quietly distanced itself. The countries of the Caribbean, which had maintained medical agreements with Cuba that provided hard currency for its government, were ending those arrangements under American pressure.

The Cuban poet José Martí, writing in 1895 of his fear that Cuba would be used for others’ purposes, called the United States “the monster” he had lived inside and learned to understand. What he could not have anticipated was the monster’s capacity, more than a century later, to organize an entire hemisphere’s acquiescence to its will — not through military force but through the credible threat of economic retaliation, applied with a precision and a ruthlessness that left no meaningful space for the kind of principled solidarity that had previously served as Cuba’s buffer against the worst of American pressure.

The island that refused to kneel was still refusing. But it was refusing alone, in the dark, with its hospitals running on improvised power and its people paying thirty-five dollars a gallon for black market gasoline and its government sitting across a table from a Cardinal, trying to find the terms on which it could survive. And on the other side of that table sat an administration whose officials described Venezuela as a “dry run” for Cuba, whose planning documents included wealthy Republican donors with Cuban ancestry as candidates for transition leadership roles in Havana, and whose approach one official summarized in terms that required no interpretation: “We don’t believe in diplomacy.”

What those terms will be — what Cuba becomes on the other side of this crisis — remains, as this series goes to press, genuinely uncertain. What has become clear is where the deadlock sits. Cuba drew a firm red line: pretty much anything was open to discussion except the shape of their government or who their political leaders would be. Washington’s response, through Rubio, was equally direct: the leadership has to change. Two irresistible forces, neither willing to concede the point that matters most to the other. Prediction markets in early April placed the probability of a formal US-Cuba economic deal by June 30 at just 25 percent. The frame in which any outcome will be shaped remains what it has been throughout: not liberation, not democratic transition, but Chapter 11 — a country reorganization, with the focus entirely on business, the creditors having significant say in who runs the restructured entity, and the people who live there present in the arrangement as labor and consumers rather than as its authors.

Meanwhile the gestures continued. Russia announced plans to send a second oil tanker. Two progressive US Congress members visited Havana in early April to meet Díaz-Canel, denouncing what they called an “economic bombing” of the island. Cuba freed three American citizens held for common crimes. The White House confirmed on April 8 that discussions were continuing “at the highest levels of our government.” Each side was signaling enough to keep talking, not enough to resolve anything.

The women’s rally was held on the anniversary of former First Lady Vilma Espin’s birthday [Ramon Espinosa/AP Photo]

The people of Cuba are still there. They have been there through everything this series has described. They will be there when this chapter ends and the next one begins, as they have always been — bearing the cost, keeping the culture alive, refusing, in the ways available to ordinary human beings, to be entirely defined by what is being done to them.

That refusal is not nothing. In five hundred and thirty years of Cuban history, it may be the most consistent thing of all.
