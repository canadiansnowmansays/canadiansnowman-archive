The Auditors Who Couldn’t Find a Mirror

The Department of Government Efficiency discovers that waste, fraud, and abuse were inside the house all along

April 12, 2026

By Stephen Gordon, CanadianSnowmanSays

A note to readers: Today you are getting two Sunday Satire essays for the price of one — a 2-for-1 special that, in the spirit of the subject matter, we will not be auditing too closely. This is Part One. It concerns the men who were sent to find waste, fraud, and abuse in the federal government, and what they found — and didn’t find — when they looked. Part Two, “What We Owe the Conqueror,” follows shortly. It concerns a triumphal arch. You will understand why the two belong together as companion essays on the same day.


There is a particular kind of comedy that only governments can produce — the kind where the arsonist is appointed fire chief, the fox is given a clipboard and a lanyard and told to audit the henhouse, and the man who golfs one out of every four days on the taxpayer’s dime announces that he’s going to root out “waste, fraud, and abuse.”

Ladies and gentlemen, I give you the Department of Government Efficiency.

For those of you who haven’t been following along — and I confess there are days when even I consider taking up something less absurd, like competitive yodeling — here is the basic plot. In late 2024, Donald Trump announced that Elon Musk would lead a bold new initiative to slash $2 trillion from the federal budget. Two trillion. That’s trillion with a T, the kind of number you only encounter in astronomy textbooks and American deficit projections.

Musk accepted the assignment with all the humility we’ve come to expect from a man who once launched a car into space because he could. “Balanced budget is going to happen,” he declared on his own social media platform, which also happened to be one of the companies whose competitors he was now in a position to defund.

How did this grand experiment in fiscal discipline turn out?

The federal government ran a $1.8 trillion deficit in fiscal 2025. Spending increased by $301 billion over the prior year. The national debt grew by $2.25 trillion in Trump’s first year back. Budget analysts across the spectrum — from the Cato Institute’s Dominik Lett to Brookings’s Jessica Riedl — peg DOGE’s actual savings at somewhere between $7 billion and $40 billion annually, depending on how generously you count. Even at the high end, that’s roughly two days of federal spending. Congress rejected nearly all of the proposed cuts. Musk himself, in a December interview, described his tenure with admirable understatement as “a little bit successful.”

Meanwhile, DOGE’s year of carnage — the fired workers, the gutted agencies, the cancelled grants — left wreckage that no spreadsheet can capture. Professor Brooke Nichols’s mortality tracker, before it was retired in February 2026, estimated that DOGE-driven cuts to foreign aid programs had contributed to 781,343 deaths, most of them children. The IRS itself projected more than $500 billion in long-term revenue losses from DOGE-driven workforce cuts. CNN reported that the cuts hampered emergency preparedness, degraded cyber defenses, disrupted broadcasting into Iran, and slowed the government’s ability to help Americans stranded abroad.

Across Washington, federal employees who feared their jobs would be next reportedly brushed up on the basics of playing Dodge Ball — or, as it’s now known in the capital, DOGE Ball.

All of this, to buy America roughly forty-eight hours of operating costs. At most.

But here’s the part that elevates this from tragedy to farce: while DOGE was busy cancelling newspaper subscriptions at NASA and hunting down diversity training PowerPoints, the actual waste, fraud, and abuse was happening right under its nose — perpetrated by the very people who sent DOGE on its mission in the first place.

Exhibit A: The Golf

Let us begin with the Commander-in-Chief himself.

Donald Trump — the man who in 2016 promised voters “I’m going to be working for you, I’m not going to have time to go play golf” — spent roughly a quarter of his days in 2025 at golf clubs. Eighty-eight visits, according to the Trump Golf Tracker. Every trip to Mar-a-Lago required Air Force One, C-17 cargo planes to airlift the presidential motorcade, Coast Guard vessels with mounted weapons to patrol the waterfront, bomb-sniffing dogs, and a Secret Service contingent that could staff a small-town police department. HuffPost and the tracking site estimate the annual taxpayer tab at somewhere between $71 million and $110 million, with a projected term total approaching $300 million.

Not once did the president play at the military golf course at Joint Base Andrews — a facility that exists precisely so that presidents can play without creating a logistical carnival. He did, however, fly over it in a helicopter at a cost of $115,000, presumably to confirm that it wasn’t up to his standards.

Then in July, he took a $10 million taxpayer-funded trip to Scotland to cut the ribbon on his new golf course in Aberdeenshire — the profits from which flow directly into his own pocket.

Where was DOGE on this? Musk, the man who tweeted that government spending needed to decrease by $3 billion per day, apparently saw nothing worth auditing in the $300,000-per-round presidential golf habit. Perhaps he was too busy celebrating $660 million in lease terminations on the DOGE website. Efficiency, Trump-style: fore.

Exhibit B: The Flying Palace(s)

Then there is the matter of aircraft.

Kristi Noem — the now-former Homeland Security Secretary whose previous experience governing South Dakota had apparently convinced her that she needed to travel like a Bond villain — leased a Boeing 737 for DHS at a purchase price of $70 million. This airplane, which was ostensibly intended for deportation flights, featured a queen-sized bed, multiple showers, a full kitchen, four large flat-screen televisions, and a bar. For context: the actual conditions at Trump-era immigration detention facilities have been described by the Atlantic’s Caitlin Dickerson as reeking of bleach, with airless cafeterias and rancid smells. The migrants get the rancid cafeteria. The Secretary got the queen bed and the wet bar.

DHS also purchased two Gulfstream jets for a reported $200 million. And ICE’s former deputy chief, Madison Sheahan — whose previous career was at Louisiana’s Department of Wildlife and Fisheries, earning the nickname “fish cop” from ICE colleagues — ordered 2,500 vehicles custom-wrapped with ICE branding at a cost of millions. This, for an agency that has never had marked vehicles, operates covertly, and depends on the element of surprise. “We’re just hiding them in a parking garage somewhere because we don’t want to drive them,” one officer told the Examiner. The branded fleet is useless. The agency is now scrambling to get the remaining deliveries unwrapped.

Even Trump — a man whose own apartment looks like Versailles had an affair with a gold mine — reportedly found Noem’s spending a bit much, and the plane was a factor in her abrupt reassignment to a newly concocted envoy role.

And then — the pièce de résistance — there is the Qatar Jet.

In 2025, the Qatari government offered the United States a Boeing 747 as a “gift.” Trump accepted. The “free” plane, however, will require hundreds of millions to retrofit for presidential use. The Air Force Secretary told Congress the cost would be “less than $400 million,” while Democratic lawmakers and aviation experts have placed the figure closer to $1 billion. The New York Times and congressional budget analysts traced a $934 million transfer from the over-budget Sentinel nuclear missile modernization program — believed to be earmarked for the jet’s transformation.

Let me say that again, slowly: the administration reportedly redirected nearly a billion dollars from the program that maintains America’s nuclear deterrent to redecorate an airplane.

And under the terms of the agreement? When Trump leaves office, ownership of this taxpayer-upgraded aircraft transfers to the Trump Presidential Library Foundation. A foreign government’s $400 million “gift” gets retrofitted at public expense, used briefly as Air Force One, and then becomes Donald Trump’s personal plane in retirement. Senator Tammy Duckworth called it what it was. Senator Ted Cruz, no Democrat, called it a national security risk.

DOGE had no comment. Perhaps Musk was busy terminating a $15,000 contract for workplace accessibility software somewhere.

Exhibit C: Surf and Turf at the Pentagon

Pete Hegseth, the Defense Secretary, arrived at the Pentagon with a simple message: no more wasting taxpayer money. “We need to know when we spend dollars,” he told Fox Business in February 2025. “We need to know where they’re going and why.”

By September, his department answered the question with a $93.4 billion blowout — the largest single-month spending total by any federal agency since at least 2008, according to Open the Books — dumping $50.1 billion in the fiscal year’s frantic finale like a gambler chasing losses at a closing casino. This, from the man who weeks earlier stood at Quantico and called it “completely unacceptable” to see “fat generals and admirals in the halls of the Pentagon.”

A note of fairness: end-of-year spending rushes are a bipartisan disease. The federal “use-it-or-lose-it” budget cycle has encouraged September splurges for decades, under presidents of both parties. But DOGE was supposed to be the cure. And this is what the cure looked like:

$15.1 million on rib-eye steak. $6.9 million on lobster tail. $2 million on Alaskan king crab. $124,000 on ice cream machines. $26,000 on sushi preparation tables. A $98,329 Steinway & Sons grand piano for the Air Force chief of staff’s home. $225.6 million on furniture, including $12,540 for fruit basket stands and over $60,000 for Herman Miller recliners. $26,000 on a violin. $21,750 on a custom handmade Japanese flute. $5.3 million on Apple devices. $3.5 billion on cable television. And $3,160 on stickers featuring Dora the Explorer, Frozen, and Paw Patrol.

Maybe the fat generals got fat on fifteen million dollars’ worth of rib-eye.

This bacchanal took place just weeks before the administration launched a war against Iran that cost an estimated $11.3 billion in its first week — and counting. When asked how Congress would pay for the war, House Appropriations Chairman Tom Cole shrugged: “War is never paid for when you fight it.” So you’ll pay more at the gas pump, absorb the inflation, and watch the deficit climb. But asking Hegseth to cut back on his lobster budget? That’s apparently unreasonable.

Exhibit D: The Fox Auditing Himself

Which brings us back to the auditor-in-chief.

Elon Musk, the man entrusted with finding government waste, runs companies that have received at least $38 billion in government contracts, loans, subsidies, and tax credits over the past two decades. SpaceX alone holds over $15 billion in contracts with NASA and the Defense Department. Last year alone, $6.3 billion in federal and local funding was committed to Musk’s companies — the largest single-year amount on record.

DOGE staffers were given access to the very agencies that regulate Musk’s businesses: the FAA (which oversees SpaceX launches), NHTSA (which investigates Tesla crashes), the NLRB (which had pending cases against multiple Musk companies), and NOAA (whose weather satellites Musk has reportedly eyed for privatization). Before Trump took office, Musk’s companies faced at least $2.37 billion in potential liability from pending agency enforcement actions. Many of those actions have since stalled.

Meanwhile, SpaceX replaced United Launch Alliance as the top Space Force contractor, winning a contract worth just under $6 billion. Starlink was installed across the White House. The FCC reopened consideration of roughly $900 million in broadband subsidies that SpaceX had previously been denied. And the Pentagon began exploring a SpaceX-proposed “Golden Dome” missile defense system — potentially worth $25 billion — structured as a subscription service.

Who was responsible for identifying potential conflicts of interest in all of this? According to the White House press secretary: Elon Musk himself. The fox doesn’t just audit the henhouse — he decides when the audit applies.

A Note of Fairness

A serious person could argue — and some have — that DOGE’s real value wasn’t in the dollar figures but in exposing structural dysfunction: the zombie contracts, the duplicative programs, the bureaucratic inertia that no president of either party has managed to dislodge. They could point out that Congress, not DOGE, controls the purse strings, and that lawmakers from both parties killed most of the proposed cuts. They could note that presidential golf costs and lobster purchases are rounding errors in a $7 trillion budget, and that the Qatar jet replaces a Boeing program that was already $3 billion over budget and three years behind schedule. These are not frivolous points.

But they miss the central absurdity. The argument was never that DOGE would solve the deficit by cancelling newspaper subscriptions. The argument — the one Trump and Musk made loudly, repeatedly, and in all capital letters on social media — was that the federal government was riddled with waste, fraud, and abuse, and that they were the men to root it out.

They were right about the diagnosis. They just never thought to examine the patient in the mirror.

The Ledger

On one side of the ledger: cancelled newspaper subscriptions, fired food safety inspectors, gutted foreign aid that by one careful academic estimate contributed to the deaths of three-quarters of a million people, and an IRS so depleted it may never recover the revenue it was built to collect.

On the other side: $110 million in presidential golf at the president’s own properties, a $70 million deportation plane with a wet bar, $270 million in vanity aircraft for DHS, 2,500 branded ICE vehicles that agents refuse to drive, a $400 million “gift” jet requiring up to $1 billion in taxpayer-funded retrofits that the president keeps when he leaves office, $93 billion in Pentagon spending in a single month including $15 million in rib-eye and a Steinway grand piano, and an efficiency czar whose own companies hold $38 billion in government contracts — auditing himself.

DOGE was looking for waste, fraud, and abuse.

It was right there all along.
