The Kash Patel Files

Over My Dead Body

Part II: The Warnings That Went Unheeded

April 21, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

How did the most pre-warned disaster in modern American governance come to pass?

The question is not rhetorical. Kash Patel is not a figure who crept into power unnoticed. He was not slipped through the system by bureaucratic oversight. He was not a stealth nominee whose true nature emerged only after confirmation. Patel is, instead, the rare catastrophe that arrived with a full dossier of warnings — from the Attorney General, from the CIA Director, from the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, from the Vice President of the United States — and was installed anyway.

The warnings were not subtle. They were not whispered through back channels. They were screamed.

And every person who screamed them was subsequently removed from Donald Trump’s orbit, clearing the path for the man they had tried to stop.

“Over my dead body.”

Attorney General William Barr testifies: “We don’t conduct criminal investigations to collect information and put it out to the public.“ | Andrew Harnik/AP Photo

That was Attorney General Bill Barr’s response when Trump entertained naming Patel deputy director of the FBI during the first term. Barr — no liberal, no Never Trumper, a man who had gone to considerable lengths to protect Trump from the consequences of the Mueller investigation — confronted the White House chief of staff directly to block the appointment. The language was not diplomatic. It was existential.

Barr’s objection was not ideological. It was competence-based. As Elaina Plott Calabro reported in her definitive August 2024 profile of Patel in The Atlantic — drawing on nearly 40 associates — what alarmed officials across the first Trump administration was not that Patel had a particular plan they feared. It was that he appeared to have no plan at all. His priorities were entirely subject to a mercurial president’s wishes. As one official put it to Calabro: the danger was not a specific agenda but an infinite willingness to do whatever was asked, without question, without hesitation, without regard for consequence.

“What wouldn’t a person like that do, if asked?”

Barr was not alone.

When Trump planned to name Patel deputy director of the CIA in the final weeks of the first administration, CIA Director Gina Haspel — a career intelligence officer who had spent decades in clandestine operations — threatened to resign. She was not bluffing. Trump relented only after an intervention by Vice President Mike Pence and others.

And when Patel was installed as chief of staff to the acting secretary of defence after the 2020 election — the period when Trump was actively seeking to overturn the results — General Mark Milley, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, took the extraordinary step of warning Patel directly not to break the law.

“Life looks really shitty from behind bars,” Milley reportedly told him.

Three separate officials, at the pinnacle of American national security — the Attorney General, the CIA Director, and the nation’s top military officer — independently concluded that Patel was too dangerous to hold a senior position. Each took personal and professional risks to block his advancement. And each was subsequently marginalised or removed from Trump’s world, their warnings filed under the same category as every other inconvenient truth that interfered with the president’s preferences.

The West Africa Incident

If the warnings were theoretical — what might Patel do if given real authority? — the answer came on October 30, 2020, four days before the presidential election, in an episode that should have ended Patel’s career in government permanently.

SEAL Team 6 was awaiting the Pentagon’s green light for a hostage rescue operation in West Africa. Philip Walton, a 27-year-old American, had been kidnapped from his farm near Niger’s border with Nigeria. As multiple agencies coordinated final details, the State Department was working to secure airspace permission from Nigerian officials — a critical diplomatic prerequisite to flying military aircraft into foreign territory.

Around noon, Patel called the Pentagon with an update: Secretary of State Mike Pompeo, he said, had gotten the approval. The mission was a go.

The SEALs were close to landing in Nigeria when Defence Secretary Mark Esper discovered that the State Department had not, in fact, secured the overflight clearance. The aircraft were diverted, flying in circles for an hour as officials scrambled to alert the Nigerian government to their position. The operation window was narrowing. Either they abort the mission and risk their hostage being killed, or they proceed into foreign airspace and risk their soldiers being shot down.

The clearance came through at the last moment. Walton was rescued. No one was killed.

But the celebratory mood turned to fury as officials tried to understand what had happened. According to Mark Esper in A Sacred Oath: Memoirs of a Secretary of Defense During Extraordinary Times (2022), Pompeo claimed he had never even spoken with Patel about the mission, much less told him he had received the airspace rights. Esper’s team concluded that Patel had simply made the approval story up.

Anthony Tata, the Pentagon official who had originally received Patel’s green light, confronted him in a rage.

“You could’ve gotten these guys killed!” Tata shouted. “What the fuck were you thinking?”

Patel’s response, according to two people familiar with the exchange: “If nobody got hurt, who the fuck cares?”

Patel denies saying this. He has offered a different account of the incident. But three former senior administration officials independently cited this near-catastrophe as one of their foremost recollections of his tenure. They remain unsettled, in part because they still have no idea what motivated him. Was it cynicism — the election was four days away, and a successful hostage rescue would be a final victory for Trump? Was it incompetence — a man with no military or operational experience simply not grasping the consequences? Was it the same pathological impulsiveness that would later produce premature tweets about the Charlie Kirk and Brown University suspects?

The question was never answered, because the question was never asked. No investigation was opened. No accountability was imposed. Walton was safe, the SEALs were safe, and the incident was filed away — another warning that went unheeded.

The Merch Empire

There is a particular quality to Patel’s years between the two Trump administrations that tells you everything you need to know about the man who would become FBI director. It is not the podcast appearances, which are standard fare for Trump-orbit figures. It is not the consulting work, though the $300,000 from Trump’s Save America PAC and $130,000 from Trump Media deserve notice. It is the merchandise.

The “official K$H wine” at $233.99 for six bottles — Chardonnay, Cabernet Sauvignon, and Pinot Noir, bearing the slogan “Fight With Kash” in camouflage print, sold by a man whose security detail would later, according to The Atlantic, consider requesting breaching equipment because he was unreachable behind locked doors after drinking to excess. The Fight With Kash Punisher Intarsia Reversible Scarf at $25, which he wore to the Conservative Political Action Conference. The TAKE A LAP RHINO tank tops at $35. The Kash Krew Golf Polos at $50. The children’s book — The Plot Against the King — in which a wizard named Kash saves King Donald from the sinister machinations of “Hillary Queenton.” The playing cards featuring the story’s characters, marketed as “the collector’s item of the century.” The challenge coin with a Punisher skull, spider-eye sockets, and random national security terms that former colleagues said he wasn’t even involved in. And — most remarkably — pills that he said “reverse” the effects of COVID vaccines. “Mrna detox, reverse the vaxx n get healthy.”

This is the man who was about to be entrusted with the integrity of the FBI’s forensic laboratories, its counterintelligence operations, and its role in the public health infrastructure of the United States. A man selling branded wine, anti-vax snake oil, and Punisher scarves on Truth Social between posts about how he would “come after” journalists and use the government to target Trump’s enemies. Readers who wish to explore the full range of K$H merchandise can visit fightwithkash.com, though Calabro noted in her Atlantic profile that most of the merch was removed from the site just as her article went to press.

As Calabro reported, Patel seemed caught between two impulses that coexisted without ever resolving: a brewing resentment of elites and an abiding desire to be seen as one. He complained constantly about money — that he couldn’t make any, that nobody would hire him, that anyone with his experience should be on the board of Raytheon or Boeing. And yet the money he was making came from the least elite sources imaginable: supplement scams, novelty scarves, and a children’s book whose literary ambitions peaked at rhyming “Kash” with “stash.”

This is not a man who was preparing for the directorship of the FBI. This is a man who was preparing for a career as a third-tier influencer. The directorship was a detour that happened to come with a government jet.

The Confirmation

FBI Confirmation Hearing, January 30 2025

David Frum, writing in The Atlantic the day after Trump announced his intention to fire Christopher Wray and nominate Patel in December 2024, framed the stakes with precision: this was not merely a bad nomination. It was a constitutional crisis. For more than four decades, the FBI director had been a position above politics. Directors served across administrations. The unwritten rule — no dismissal except for compelling cause — had bulwarked American law and freedom for half a century.

Trump was junking it altogether. And the nomination of Patel, Frum wrote, “slathers frosting and sprinkles on the outrage.”

The confirmation hearing, in January 2025, produced the assurances that confirmation hearings are designed to produce. Under questioning from sceptical Democrats, Patel vowed that “there will be no retributive actions” and that he was not aware of any plans to punish FBI staff who had been part of investigations into Trump. He told senators that “all FBI employees will be protected against political retribution” and that “no one will be terminated for case assignments.”

Several Republicans wavered. The White House mounted a pressure campaign. Patel was confirmed 51 to 49.

The next day — literally the next day — Trump administration officials, including Emil Bove, Trump’s former defence attorney now serving as acting deputy attorney general, forced out the bureau’s top six executives, along with the heads of the FBI field offices in Washington and Miami.

“Those firings sent shock waves,” said John Sullivan, a former section chief in the intelligence division.

“It was hundreds of years of experience in one brutal swoop,” a senior executive told The New York Times.

Every promise Patel made under oath was broken within 24 hours of his confirmation. Not within weeks. Not within months. Within hours.

The Guardrails

The pattern is worth naming explicitly, because it is the mechanism by which the catastrophe was engineered.

Every person who tried to prevent Patel from reaching a position of authority was removed from the picture — not by Patel, but by the broader project of which Patel was a component. Barr was gone. Haspel was gone. Milley was gone. Pence was gone. Esper was gone. The institutional Republicans who might have blocked a 51-49 confirmation were either out of the Senate or had learned the lesson of their predecessors: opposing Trump’s nominees was a career-ending move.

By the time Patel stood before the Senate Judiciary Committee in January 2025, there was no one left to say “over my dead body.” The people who would have said it were politically dead already.

And so the 51-49 vote was not a vetting. It was a capitulation — the final one in a long sequence of capitulations that began when the Republican Party decided that loyalty to one man was more important than the institutional integrity of the nation’s premier law enforcement agency.

The man they installed had been warned about by the Attorney General, the CIA Director, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, and the Vice President. He had nearly gotten SEAL Team 6 killed and responded with “who the fuck cares.” He had sold anti-vax supplements and Punisher scarves. He had written a children’s book casting himself as a wizard saving a king. He had promised to “come after” journalists. He had been denied the deputy directorship of the FBI once already, by an attorney general who risked his own position to block it.

And they confirmed him anyway.

What happened next — the purges, the perks, and what The Atlantic and The New York Times would later document as the drinking, the breaching equipment, the hollow bureau, the war with Iran — was not a surprise. It was a delivery. Every official who voted to confirm Patel, every senator who bent to the pressure campaign, every Republican who told themselves that perhaps the wizard would grow into the role — they got exactly what they ordered.

The bill is still arriving.

The bill arrived on Day 1 — with the firing of the bureau’s top six executives, the demand for a list of every agent who had worked January 6, and a man named Emil Bove whom the workforce would come to describe as halfway between a mob enforcer and the grim reaper.
