The Six They Tried to Indict for Speech

Part 17: When the Grand Jury Said “Hell No” to Criminalizing Political Speech

February 13, 2026

Clockwise from the top: Rep. Jason Crow, Rep. Chris Deluzio, Rep. Maggie Goodlander, Sen. Elissa Slotkin, Rep. Chrissy Houlahan and Sen. Mark Kelly. (Demetrius Freeman/The WashingtonPost; Getty Images; AP; Stephen Voss/For The Washington Post)

By CanadianSnowmanSays
February 13, 2026

BREAKING: February 11, 2026

The Justice Department presented criminal charges against six Democratic lawmakers to a federal grand jury.

The crime: Recording a 90-second video reminding military service members of their legal duty to refuse illegal orders.

Trump’s demand: “SEDITIOUS BEHAVIOR, punishable by DEATH!”

The DOJ charge: “Interfere with, impair, or influence the loyalty, morale, or discipline of the military or naval forces of the United States.”

The grand jury vote: ZERO.

Not a single grand juror voted to indict Senator Mark Kelly. Not a single grand juror voted to indict Senator Elissa Slotkin. Not a single grand juror voted to indict Representative Jason Crow. Not a single grand juror voted to indict Representative Maggie Goodlander. Not a single grand juror voted to indict Representative Chrissy Houlahan. Not a single grand juror voted to indict Representative Chris Deluzio.

Six lawmakers. Zero indictments. Complete rejection.

This Is The First Time DOJ Tried to Criminalize Political Speech

We’re seventeen parts into this series. We’ve seen a lot.

We’ve seen prosecutors charge critics with financial fraud (Letitia James). We’ve seen them charge former officials with espionage (John Bolton). We’ve seen them charge journalists for covering protests (Don Lemon, Georgia Fort).

But this is the first time the Department of Justice has directly sought to classify critical speech from prominent Trump detractors as a crime.

Not financial transactions they claim are fraud. Not classified documents they claim weren’t properly handled. Not presence at a protest they claim is participation.

Speech. Political speech. Reminding service members to follow the law.

And the grand jury—ordinary citizens asked to determine if there’s probable cause—said no.

Not just “no” to one charge. Not just “no” to one person.

Zero votes to indict any of the six lawmakers on any charge.

This is what it looks like when citizens refuse to let their government prosecute speech.

The “Crime”: A 90-Second Video About Following the Law

Let’s be very clear about what these six lawmakers actually did.

November 18, 2025:

Senator Elissa Slotkin (D-Michigan), a former CIA analyst, organized a video message with five other Democratic lawmakers, all of whom served in the military or intelligence agencies:

  • Senator Mark Kelly (D-Arizona) - Retired Navy captain, astronaut, 39 combat missions

  • Representative Jason Crow (D-Colorado) - Former Army Ranger

  • Representative Maggie Goodlander (D-New Hampshire) - Navy veteran

  • Representative Chrissy Houlahan (D-Pennsylvania) - Former Air Force officer

  • Representative Chris Deluzio (D-Pennsylvania) - Former Navy officer

The video was 90 seconds long.

In it, they reminded service members of their duty under the Uniform Code of Military Justice to resist unlawful directives.

The exact words:

“This administration is pitting our uniformed military and intelligence community professionals against American citizens. Our laws are clear. You can refuse illegal orders.”

That’s it.

They didn’t name specific policies. They didn’t call for mutiny. They didn’t encourage insubordination.

They reminded service members of established military law: the duty to refuse illegal orders.

This isn’t controversial legal theory. This is settled military law, established after Nuremberg, reinforced in military training, required by the UCMJ.

Every service member is taught this. “I was just following orders” is not a defense for illegal acts.

Trump’s Response: “Punishable by DEATH!”

Trump’s reaction was immediate and extreme.

November 21, 2025 (three days after the video):

Trump posted on Truth Social:

“SEDITIOUS BEHAVIOR, punishable by DEATH!”

And:

“IT WAS SEDITION AT THE HIGHEST LEVEL, AND SEDITION IS A MAJOR CRIME.”

Not “this is misleading” or “this is incorrect.”

DEATH.

Six lawmakers—including two sitting U.S. Senators—reminded troops to follow the law, and the President of the United States demanded they be executed.

Think about that.

Mark Kelly flew 39 combat missions. He’s an astronaut. He’s a retired Navy captain. He’s a United States Senator.

And Trump called for his execution for reminding troops they must refuse illegal orders.

The Prosecution: Political Appointees Doing Trump’s Bidding

Fast forward to February 2026.

Earlier this month: Four of the lawmakers disclosed that FBI agents had approached them seeking voluntary interviews. All declined.

“It was clearly, when our lawyers sat down with them, just about checking a box and doing what the president wanted them to do,” Slotkin said. “Their heart wasn’t even in it.”

February 11, 2026 (Tuesday):

Prosecutors under the supervision of Jeanine Pirro (D.C. U.S. Attorney, former Fox News host, staunch Trump ally) presented charges to a federal grand jury.

The prosecutors leading the presentation (both political appointees):

  1. Steven Vandervelden - Former colleague of Pirro’s from the Westchester County DA’s office

  2. Carlton Davis - Former staffer for House Oversight Committee Chairman James Comer

Both Trump loyalists. Both political appointees. Both presenting charges against six lawmakers who reminded troops to follow the law.

The charge they sought: A felony making it illegal to “interfere with, impair, or influence the loyalty, morale, or discipline of the military or naval forces of the United States.”

Translation: Reminding troops they can refuse illegal orders = interfering with military discipline.

The Grand Jury: Zero Votes

Grand juries typically operate behind closed doors. We rarely know vote counts.

But in this case, sources familiar with the proceedings revealed the stunning result:

When it came time to vote, none of the grand jurors agreed there was sufficient probable cause to charge any of the lawmakers with a crime.

Not one vote for Kelly. Not one vote for Slotkin. Not one vote for Crow. Not one vote for Goodlander. Not one vote for Houlahan. Not one vote for Deluzio.

Complete and total rejection.

The grand jury looked at what DOJ presented and said: This isn’t a crime. This is speech. This is protected. This is absurd.

“If Things Had Gone a Different Way, We’d Be Preparing for Arrest”

Kelly and Slotkin held a news conference on February 12, the day after the grand jury vote.

They learned about the attempted indictment through media reports.

Slotkin’s words:

“If things had gone a different way, we’d be preparing for arrest.”

Let that sink in.

Two United States Senators—elected by millions of Americans to represent them in Congress—came within a grand jury vote of being arrested for reminding troops to follow the law.

Kelly’s assessment:

“This is not a good news story. This is a story about how Donald Trump and his cronies are trying to break our system to silence anyone who lawfully speaks out against them.”

Slotkin’s question—the one that matters most:

“If the president can do this to us — sitting senators — who else can he do it to?”

If DOJ can present charges against U.S. Senators for political speech, who is safe?

Former officials? Already prosecuted (Comey, Bolton). State officials? Already prosecuted (Letitia James). Sitting Senators? Just tried to prosecute six of them. Journalists? Already arrested (Lemon, Fort).

Who’s left?

The Letitia James Pattern: When Grand Juries Refuse

This is the second time we’ve seen grand juries completely reject DOJ’s politically motivated prosecutions.

Letitia James (New York Attorney General):

First attempt: Norfolk grand jury - REJECTED (insufficient evidence)

Second attempt: Alexandria grand jury - REJECTED (refused to indict)

Third attempt: Second Alexandria grand jury - REJECTED (again refused)

DOJ took Letitia James to three different grand juries in two different cities. Citizens refused every single time.

The Six Lawmakers:

First attempt: D.C. grand jury - REJECTED (zero votes to indict)

We don’t know if DOJ will try again with a different grand jury. With James, they tried three times. Will they grand jury shop for these six lawmakers too?

But here’s what the pattern shows:

Citizens are refusing to criminalize political opposition.

Grand jurors—ordinary Americans summoned for jury duty—are looking at these cases and saying: No. This isn’t what the justice system is for. We won’t be part of this.

They’re refusing to indict state officials for paying off mortgages early. They’re refusing to indict senators for reminding troops to follow the law.

Grand juries are the last line of defense—and they’re holding.

The Mark Kelly Evolution: From Part 13 to Part 17

Remember Part 13 of this series? That was about Mark Kelly.

Let’s trace what’s happened to Kelly for making that 90-second video:

January 5, 2026 - Pentagon Action (Part 13):

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth issued:

  • Formal Letter of Censure (permanent military record)

  • Retirement rank reduction proceedings (Captain → Commander)

  • ~$12,000/year pension cut (~$240,000+ lifetime)

Charges under UCMJ Articles 133 & 134.

Kelly’s “crime”: The same video. Reminding troops to refuse illegal orders.

February 11, 2026 - DOJ Criminal Charges (Part 17):

DOJ tried to criminally indict Kelly (and five others) for the exact same video.

Pentagon: Administrative punishment DOJ: Criminal charges

Both for the same speech.

February 12, 2026 - Judge Blocks Pentagon:

A federal judge halted the Defense Department’s efforts to censure Kelly, ruling:

“The effort to do so trampled on Senator Kelly’s First Amendment freedoms and threatened the constitutional liberties of millions of military retirees.”

So where does this leave us?

Pentagon tried to punish Kelly administratively → Judge blocked it (First Amendment)

DOJ tried to indict Kelly criminally → Grand jury rejected it (zero votes)

Both the courts AND the citizens are refusing to let the government punish political speech.

Why This Is Different: Prosecuting Speech Directly

Seventeen parts into this series, this is the most nakedly authoritarian prosecution yet.

Previous cases had pretexts:

James Comey: Allegedly lied to Congress (pretext for writing critical book)

Letitia James: Allegedly committed mortgage fraud (pretext for prosecuting Trump Org)

John Bolton: Allegedly violated classified document rules (pretext for writing tell-all)

Jerome Powell: Allegedly lied about Fed building renovation (pretext for not cutting rates fast enough)

Ilhan Omar: Allegedly committed financial fraud from marriage (pretext for being Muslim, Somali, critic)

Don Lemon/Georgia Fort: Allegedly participated in protest, not covered it (pretext for documenting ICE killings)

Every previous case had a thin legal veneer. DOJ could claim it wasn’t about criticism—it was about the underlying crime.

The Six Lawmakers: NO PRETEXT.

They didn’t handle classified documents. They didn’t file incorrect financial forms. They didn’t participate in a protest.

They spoke. That’s it. They recorded a video expressing political views.

And DOJ tried to make that a crime.

As Brendan Nyhan, professor of government at Dartmouth College, told the Washington Post:

“Prosecuting people for speech criticizing the president is in some ways even more dangerous, especially given these are legislators acting in their public role and especially given that they were calling for the military and national security state to follow the law.”

This is what authoritarianism looks like: Direct criminalization of political speech.

The Republican Response: “They Probably Should Be Indicted”

Most Republicans have been silent.

But some have actively supported the prosecution attempt.

House Speaker Mike Johnson (R-Louisiana):

“Any time you’re obstructing law enforcement and getting in the way of these sensitive operations, it’s a very serious thing, and it probably is a crime. They probably should be indicted.”

Think about what Johnson is saying:

Reminding troops to refuse illegal orders = “obstructing law enforcement” A 90-second video = “getting in the way of sensitive operations” Political speech by lawmakers = “probably a crime”

One Republican has publicly condemned it:

Senator Thom Tillis (R-North Carolina):

“Political lawfare is not normal, not acceptable, and needs to stop.”

One. Out of 53 Republican Senators.

Senate Democrats held a special caucus meeting on February 13 to discuss the situation.

Senator Brian Schatz (D-Hawaii):

“The fact that they failed to incarcerate a United States senator should not obviate our outrage. They tried to incarcerate two of us. I am not entirely sure the United States Senate can survive this if we do not have Republicans standing up.”

He’s right. The fact that the grand jury rejected the charges doesn’t make the attempt okay.

DOJ tried to criminally indict six sitting members of Congress for political speech.

They failed because grand jurors refused.

But they tried.

And they’ll try again.

The Intimidation Is the Point

Slotkin said it plainly:

“The intimidation was the point — to get other people beyond us to think twice about speaking out.”

Even though the prosecution failed, the intimidation worked.

Six lawmakers now know:

  • FBI agents came to their doors

  • DOJ presented charges to a grand jury

  • They came within a grand jury vote of arrest

  • Their lawyers had to prepare for potential indictment

  • They spent time and money defending themselves

For speech.

Every other member of Congress watched this happen. Every current or former military officer serving in government watched. Every critic of the administration watched.

And they learned: Speak out, and they’ll try to indict you. Maybe it won’t succeed. But they’ll try.

That’s the chilling effect in action.

The process is the punishment. Even failed prosecutions accomplish their goal: silence through fear.

“Who Else Can He Do It to?”

Slotkin’s question haunts this entire series:

“If the president can do this to us — sitting senators — who else can he do it to?”

Let’s answer it.

Who can be prosecuted?

The answer to Slotkin’s question: Everyone. The president can try to prosecute anyone.

The real question: Will courts and grand juries keep saying no?

So far, they have. Mostly.

  • Comey: Dismissed by judge

  • James: Rejected by three grand juries

  • Kelly censure: Blocked by judge

  • Six lawmakers: Rejected by grand jury

  • Lemon/Fort: Courts rejected charges, DOJ arrested anyway (still in court)

  • Bolton: Proceeding to trial

  • Powell: Investigation active

  • Omar: Investigation active

The system is holding. Barely.

Judges are dismissing cases. Grand juries are refusing to indict. Courts are blocking administrative punishments.

But DOJ keeps trying. And occasionally succeeding.

Bolton’s case proceeds. Lemon and Fort were arrested despite court rejections. Powell and Omar face active investigations.

The question is how long the system can hold against a president determined to prosecute critics.

With Letitia James, DOJ went to three grand juries. What if they try that here?

    • Virginia? Maryland? Where can they get friendlier grand jury?

  3. Change the charges

    • If “interfering with military discipline” doesn’t work, try something else

    • Sedition? Conspiracy? Obstruction?

  4. Expand the targets

    • Try to indict them one at a time instead of all six

    • Focus on the ones with less protection

  5. Increase the pressure

    • More FBI interviews

    • Subpoenas for records

    • Freeze assets

    • Make the process more painful

We know they’ll try again because they always do.

When courts dismissed Comey, they indicted James. When grand juries rejected James three times, they arrested Lemon and Fort despite courts. When judges blocked Kelly’s censure, they tried criminal charges.

The pattern is clear: When one method fails, try another. When courts block you, ignore them.

Conclusion: The Grand Jury That Saved Six Lawmakers

Seventeen parts into this series, we’ve seen the enemies list claim:

Former officials. State officials. Sitting senators. Federal Reserve chairs. Immigrant members of Congress. Journalists.

Now six more lawmakers at once—the biggest mass prosecution attempt yet.

For writing books critical of Trump, the administration indicted former officials.

For prosecuting Trump’s businesses, they went after state prosecutors.

For covering protests, they arrested journalists.

For reminding troops to follow the law, they tried to indict six members of Congress.

And for the first time, they weren’t even hiding behind a pretext. They directly tried to criminalize political speech.

Trump demanded they be executed.

DOJ tried to indict them.

Political appointees presented the case.

The House Speaker said they “probably should be indicted.”

And a grand jury—ordinary citizens doing their civic duty—said no.

Zero votes. Complete rejection. Six lawmakers saved.

But Slotkin is right: “If things had gone a different way, we’d be preparing for arrest.”

The fact that it failed doesn’t make it okay. The fact that a grand jury refused doesn’t mean the next one will.

The attempt itself is authoritarian. The attempt itself is the threat. The attempt itself should terrify everyone who values constitutional democracy.

Because as Slotkin asked: “If the president can do this to us — sitting senators — who else can he do it to?”

The answer is everyone. And the only thing stopping him is courts and grand juries saying no.

They said no this time.

Will they say no next time?

They failed because grand jurors refused. The question is whether courts and citizens can keep saying no—or whether eventually, one grand jury will say yes.
