Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 7: Africa, Exodus, and the Shadow of Collapse (1975–1991)

March 16, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Powerful group photo of Cuban soldiers in Angola, posing with rifles and artillery in bush setting—multiracial unit, determined expressions.

In November 1975, Cuba did something that no one had expected of a small island nation of eight million people: it sent an army to Africa.

The Angolan civil war had been running since before Portuguese decolonization formally ended in that country, and by the autumn of 1975, the South African Defence Force had crossed into Angola in support of UNITA and the FNLA — the factions backed by South Africa, Zaire, and implicitly the United States — against the MPLA government that Cuba and the Soviet Union supported. South African forces were moving on Luanda. The MPLA was in danger of losing a capital it had just won.

Fidel Castro made the decision to intervene essentially unilaterally. He informed the Soviets before acting but did not wait for their approval. Within weeks, Cuban troops — eventually numbering more than 36,000 at the peak of the intervention — had halted the South African advance, stabilized the MPLA government, and demonstrated something that permanently altered the world’s understanding of what Cuba was: a country capable of genuine, autonomous military action in pursuit of its own foreign policy vision, not simply a Soviet proxy doing Moscow’s bidding.

The Angola intervention, which would last in various forms until 1991, was the most significant act of Cuban foreign policy in the Revolution’s history. It also, for the generation of Cubans who served there, represented the most direct personal encounter with the consequences of the revolutionary state’s decisions about their lives.

What Angola Meant

To understand why Cuba went to Angola, it is necessary to set aside the Cold War frame that American commentators typically applied to the intervention. Washington saw Cuba’s Angola policy as evidence of Soviet expansionism through Cuban proxies. That reading was wrong in important ways.

Cuban troops marching or grouped with MPLA fighters—camouflage, weapons, tropical backdrop—symbolizes the autonomous military projection.

The Cuban commitment to Africa was rooted in something the Soviet Union did not share: a genuine identification between the Cuban Revolution and African liberation. Cuba had a large Afro-Cuban population whose ancestors had been enslaved and brought to the island from West and Central Africa. The ideological framework of the Revolution had always included an anticolonial dimension — a solidarity with peoples struggling against European and American dominance — that resonated with the situation of Angola, Mozambique, and other African countries in the process of decolonization. When Castro looked at South Africa deploying its military against an African government, he saw colonialism in its rawest current form. He reacted to it as a genuine political conviction, not as a strategic calculation.

Henry Kissinger, who was Secretary of State at the time, was privately furious. He recognized that the Cuban action was not simply a Soviet operation, and that the willingness to confront South Africa directly — something the United States had shown no appetite for — gave Cuba a legitimacy in Africa and the Non-Aligned Movement that its socialist alignment with Moscow would otherwise have compromised. Cuba was demonstrating that it could be both a Soviet ally and an independent actor with its own values and its own interests. That combination was genuinely difficult for American foreign policy to handle.

The practical cost to Cuba was enormous. Over the sixteen years of the Angolan intervention, an estimated 2,000 to 5,000 Cuban soldiers died — combat deaths, disease, accidents. Tens of thousands more served and returned home with the complicated residue of combat experience. Cuba also deployed forces to Ethiopia, supported the Sandinistas in Nicaragua, maintained military advisers in Yemen, Mozambique, and elsewhere. For a country of Cuba’s size and resources, this level of military engagement was extraordinary. It was paid for partly by Soviet subsidies, but also by the sacrifice of Cubans who had no personal connection to the conflicts in which they were being asked to fight.

Mariel: The Revolution Hemorrhages

Multiple small boats overloaded with Mariel émigrés heading to Florida—waves, flags, families aboard—visually dramatic for the hemorrhage moment.

In April 1980, a group of Cubans drove a bus through the gates of the Peruvian embassy in Havana, seeking asylum. One Cuban guard was killed in the incident. The Cuban government, infuriated, withdrew its guards from the embassy — and within seventy-two hours, ten thousand Cubans had crowded into the embassy grounds seeking to leave the country.

The scene was a significant embarrassment. Castro’s response was characteristically audacious: he announced that anyone who wished to leave Cuba was free to do so via the port of Mariel, and he invited Cuban exiles in Miami to come and pick them up. What followed over the next five months was one of the largest mass migration events in Caribbean history. Approximately 125,000 Cubans departed through Mariel, an exodus driven by the accumulated frustrations of twenty years of revolutionary society — the rationing, the political controls, the lack of economic opportunity, the simple desire for a different life.

The Carter administration was caught badly off guard. American law and American political culture made it difficult to turn away Cuban refugees, but the scale and the chaotic nature of the boatlift overwhelmed the government’s capacity to manage the arrivals. Castro, who had expelled the troublesome and the dissatisfied in a single stroke, had simultaneously created a crisis for a political rival and rid himself of citizens whose restlessness could have become a domestic political problem. Among the Mariel arrivals were several thousand prisoners and psychiatric patients whom Castro had released specifically to be included in the exodus — a deliberate contamination of the refugee flow that poisoned American public opinion toward the Mariel Cubans for years.

The Miami exile community, which had been waiting since 1959 for regime collapse, instead absorbed 125,000 new arrivals who were poorer, darker-skinned, and less ideologically hardened against Castro than the earlier waves of emigration. The Mariel boatlift reshaped Miami’s Cuban community and complicated the politics of the exile movement, introducing class and racial tensions that the earlier emigration had papered over with the common bond of anti-Castro politics.

For those who remained in Cuba, the boatlift was a complicated moment. Many Cubans who stayed supported the Revolution genuinely, or had built lives too embedded to abandon, or had family members who had already left and couldn’t bear the thought of separation. Others stayed because they had nowhere to go, or because the uncertainty of exile seemed worse than the familiar difficulties of staying. The departure of so many people left gaps in Cuban society — professional, personal, communal — that would take years to fill.

The Reagan Years: Pressure and Stalemate

Ronald Reagan’s election in November 1980 brought a significant escalation in American rhetorical hostility toward Cuba, combined with relatively limited new practical action. Reagan described Cuba as a Soviet proxy actively destabilizing Central America — which was partly true, in the sense that Cuba was supplying and training the Sandinista government in Nicaragua and the FMLN guerrillas in El Salvador — and made rolling back Cuban influence a stated priority of his administration.

The practical tools available were limited. The embargo was already in place. Military action against Cuba directly would have been politically costly and potentially catastrophic. Covert operations — continuing the CIA tradition of support for anti-Castro groups and attempts at economic sabotage — continued but produced no significant results. Radio Martí, established in 1985 to broadcast uncensored news and American propaganda into Cuba, represented the administration’s most visible new Cuba policy initiative, and its practical impact on Cuban society was marginal.

What the Reagan years did accomplish was a tightening of the embargo’s enforcement and an escalation of the pressure on third-country companies doing business with Cuba. The campaign to make Cuba pay a price for its Angola intervention and its Central American activities was sustained and serious, even if it fell well short of regime change.

Castro’s response to Reagan was to dig in. The Rectification Campaign, launched in 1986, was Castro’s answer to Mikhail Gorbachev’s simultaneous glasnost and perestroika reforms in the Soviet Union — and it went in precisely the opposite direction. Where Gorbachev was introducing market mechanisms and political openness in an attempt to reform a sclerotic system, Castro was tightening central control, rolling back the limited market freedoms that had been cautiously introduced in the early 1980s, and reasserting ideological discipline. Castro watched what Gorbachev was doing in the Soviet Union and saw not a model but a warning: economic liberalization, in his reading, was the first step toward political liberalization, and political liberalization was the first step toward the loss of the Revolution.

He was not wrong about the sequence. He was wrong about whether preventing it was possible.

Gorbachev and the Writing on the Wall

Castro and Gorbachev, Havana, April 1989. The Soviet leader had come to deliver a message: the subsidies were ending, the Cold War alliance was unwinding, Cuba was on its own. Castro saluted. He did not accept it. Two years later, the Soviet Union was gone.

When Mikhail Gorbachev visited Havana in April 1989, the visit had the surface form of a socialist solidarity meeting and the underlying reality of a breakup. Gorbachev was preparing to normalize Soviet relations with the United States, to withdraw from strategic commitments the Soviet Union could no longer afford, and to wind down the subsidies that had kept Cuba economically viable for three decades. He needed Castro to understand that the arrangement was ending, and to begin preparing for its end.

Castro understood. He refused to accept it.

The two men’s disagreement was both personal and structural. Castro believed Gorbachev was making a catastrophic mistake — that the reforms were undermining the Soviet state rather than renewing it, that political liberalization would destroy what economic liberalization was supposed to save. Gorbachev thought Castro was an ideological fossil, admirable in his courage but incapable of understanding that the world had changed. They parted with warm words and deep mutual incomprehension.

The events of the next two years confirmed Castro’s worst fears and Gorbachev’s worst failures. The Berlin Wall fell in November 1989. The Soviet satellites in Eastern Europe shed their communist governments in rapid succession through the winter of 1989–1990. The Soviet Union itself dissolved in December 1991, its constituent republics declaring independence while the Communist Party that had governed it for seventy years was swept aside.

For Cuba, the consequences were immediate and devastating. Soviet oil deliveries collapsed. The guaranteed sugar price disappeared. Soviet industrial goods, spare parts, and food imports stopped arriving. The subsidies that had been transferring billions of dollars annually to the Cuban economy simply ceased. Cuba’s economy, built on the assumption of permanent Soviet patronage, was suddenly confronted with a world in which that patronage no longer existed.

The Special Period in Time of Peace — the Cuban government’s euphemism for the catastrophe that followed — had begun before anyone had named it. How Cubans survived it, and what survival cost them, is the subject of the following part of this series.

The Balance Sheet of Thirty Years

The period from 1962 to 1991 is the part of Cuban history most often compressed or skipped in popular accounts of the Revolution — the long middle, between the dramatic early years and the crisis of the 1990s, where the Revolution settled into the routines of governance and the world moved around it.

It deserves more than compression. These were the years in which the Revolution proved it could endure — not just in the face of American hostility, but in the face of the complications that governing actually entails. The health and education achievements that gave Cuba a human development profile unlike any other country at its income level were consolidated in these years. The military and diplomatic capacity that allowed Cuba to project power in Africa and influence in Central America was built in these years.

They were also the years in which the Revolution’s authoritarianism calcified from emergency measure to permanent condition. The persecution of homosexuals, the suppression of religious practice, the imprisonment of political dissidents, the control of intellectual life — none of these were relaxed as the external threat diminished. They became structural features of Cuban society, the price extracted from Cuban citizens for the government’s survival.

The Soviet subsidies that made these thirty years economically viable also made them, in an important sense, deceptive. Cuba looked more viable than it was. The underlying productive capacity of the economy — its ability to generate the income it needed to sustain itself without external subsidy — was never developed, because there was never the economic pressure to develop it. When the subsidy ended, the deception was exposed.

The Cuban people who had lived those thirty years, who had sent their sons to Angola and watched their friends leave at Mariel and stood in the queues and navigated the ration books and raised families in one-party state while keeping alive the cultural richness and human warmth that the state could regulate but not extinguish — those people did not deserve what was coming.

They rarely do. That is perhaps the most consistent thing about Cuban history.
