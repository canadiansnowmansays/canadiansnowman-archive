The Deadline Bondi Ignored

What Happened December 3-5, and What It Reveals

December 6, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
December 6, 2025

Five members of Congress—Republicans and Democrats, Senators and Representatives—gave Attorney General Pam Bondi a deadline. Friday, December 5, 2025. No later.

They wanted a briefing on the “new information” she claimed justified reopening Epstein investigations into Democrats. They wanted to understand what “procedural hurdles” might prevent DOJ from meeting the statutory December 19 deadline to release all Epstein files. They wanted to know whether the Trump administration would actually deliver transparency or another carefully managed performance.

The deadline came and went.

Bondi didn’t brief Congress. She didn’t acknowledge their request. She didn’t explain the “new information.” She didn’t update anyone on progress toward the December 19 release.

What she did instead—in the days leading up to that deadline—tells you everything you need to know about this administration’s priorities.

On December 4, one day before Congress’s deadline, Pam Bondi issued a memo directing all federal law enforcement agencies to investigate antifa groups for “tax crimes.” She instructed the FBI to compile lists of domestic terror groups. She ordered prosecutors to prioritize cases against left-wing activists. She devoted her time, her authority, and her department’s resources to weaponizing the Justice Department against political opponents.

While Congress waited for answers about a president’s ties to a sex trafficker, Bondi spent the day before their deadline hunting for tax violations among anti-fascist activists. Then she let the deadline pass without response. She had all of December 5 to provide the briefing Congress requested. She chose silence instead.

The pattern we’ve documented across eleven episodes continues. The accommodation psychology adapts. The cover-up evolves. But the fundamental calculation remains unchanged: use every tool available to protect Trump, punish his enemies, and run out the clock on accountability.

Thirteen days remain until the statutory deadline. And based on what happened December 3-5, there’s zero indication DOJ is preparing to deliver what Congress demanded and the law requires.

I. THE DEADLINE CONGRESS SET

On December 3, a bipartisan group of lawmakers sent Bondi a letter that should have been unnecessary. The Epstein Files Transparency Act—passed 427-1 in the House, unanimously in the Senate, signed by Trump on November 19—already established clear requirements. But Bondi’s behavior since signing warranted intervention.

Senators Jeff Merkley (D-OR), Lisa Murkowski (R-AK), and Ben Ray Luján (D-NM) joined Representatives Thomas Massie (R-KY) and Ro Khanna (D-CA)—the bill’s lead sponsors—in making a simple request:

“In the interest of transparency and clarity on the steps required to faithfully implement the Epstein Files Transparency Act, we request a briefing either in a classified or unclassified setting, to discuss the full contents of this new information in your possession at your convenience, but not later than Friday, December 5th, 2025.”

The letter was specific about what prompted the request. On November 14—five days before Trump signed the transparency bill—DOJ announced Jay Clayton would lead “new investigations” into Jeffrey Epstein’s ties to Bill Clinton, Larry Summers, and Reid Hoffman. At a November 19 press conference, Bondi justified these investigations by citing “information [that] has come forward, new information, additional information.”

But DOJ’s own memo from July 7 had stated unequivocally: “This systematic review revealed no incriminating ‘client list.’ There was also no credible evidence found that Epstein blackmailed prominent individuals as part of his actions.” The review found “no evidence that could predicate an investigation against uncharged third parties.”

So what changed between July and November? What “new information” emerged in precisely the window when Congress was forcing Trump’s hand on transparency?

The lawmakers wanted to know. They also wanted assurance that DOJ was actually preparing to meet its statutory obligation:

“In light of the short 30 day deadline to release the Epstein Files, we are particularly focused on understanding the contents of any new evidence, information or procedural hurdles that could interfere with the Department’s ability meet this statutory deadline.”

The letter included another critical element—victim protection. The lawmakers emphasized that “protecting survivors’ privacy is central to the law’s intent” and urged DOJ to coordinate directly with victims’ attorneys to establish secure processes for identifying information requiring redaction.

This wasn’t a partisan attack. This was the bill’s authors—from both parties—trying to ensure the law they wrote would be implemented as intended rather than weaponized as Trump and Bondi had weaponized everything else.

Senator Merkley told reporters after the law passed that he expected exactly this kind of obstruction: “They will likely say that releasing information will compromise ongoing investigations — maybe investigations that they will start in order to make that argument.”

That prediction proved accurate within days. The November 14 investigations provided exactly the excuse Merkley anticipated.

December 5 came and went. No briefing. No response. No acknowledgment.

The Justice Department confirmed receipt of the letter to media outlets but declined to comment further. The silence speaks volumes.

II. WHAT BONDI DID INSTEAD

On December 4—one day before Congress’s deadline—Pam Bondi sent a memo to all federal law enforcement agencies and prosecutors that revealed her actual priorities.

The document, obtained by Reuters and Bloomberg and reported by The Guardian, had nothing to do with Epstein. Its subject: implementing Trump’s executive order declaring antifa a “domestic terrorism organization.”

Bondi’s language in the memo reveals the administration’s actual priorities:

“These domestic terrorists use violence or the threat of violence to advance political and social agendas, including opposition to law and immigration enforcement; extreme views in favor of mass migration and open borders; adherence to radical gender ideology, anti-Americanism, anti-capitalism, or anti-Christianity; support for the overthrow of the United States Government; hostility towards traditional views on family, religion, and morality; and an elevation of violence to achieve policy outcomes, such as political assassinations.”

Let’s be clear about what this describes: political opponents. People who oppose Trump’s immigration policies. People who support gender equality. People who criticize capitalism or Christianity. This isn’t a description of terrorism—it’s a description of mainstream progressive politics reframed as domestic terror.

The memo instructed all federal law enforcement agencies to “review their files and holdings for Antifa and Antifa-related intelligence and information and coordinate delivery of such material to the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) for review” within 14 days.

But the most revealing section focused on taxes:

“Federal law enforcement and federal prosecutors should consider any applicable tax crimes in cases in which extremist groups are suspected of defrauding the Internal Revenue Service. As it receives referrals for violations of tax obligations, the [justice department] should investigate and, where appropriate, prosecute those responsible.”

Tax crimes. Not violence. Not actual terrorism. Tax violations by non-profit organizations that fund progressive causes.

This follows DOJ’s efforts earlier this year to investigate Open Society Foundations—George Soros’s philanthropic network—after the killing of Charlie Kirk. The pattern is clear: find technical violations to justify stripping tax-exempt status from organizations that fund opposition to Trump.

While Bondi hunted for tax irregularities among anti-fascist activists, she ignored Congress’s deadline for transparency about a president’s relationship with a convicted sex trafficker.

The contrast isn’t subtle. It’s deliberate.

III. THE JUDICIAL INTERVENTION

The law passed by Congress gave the Justice Department 30 days to release the files related to the Epstein investigation in its possession.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

While Bondi spent December 4 focused on antifa rather than Epstein, a federal judge in Florida actually moved the transparency process forward.

Judge Rodney Smith of the Federal District Court for the Southern District of Florida issued a brief order on December 5 approving the release of grand jury documents from the nearly 20-year-old Florida investigation of Jeffrey Epstein. The investigative material dates to 2005, when police first opened an inquiry into allegations Epstein was abusing teenage girls. That investigation ended in 2008 with Epstein’s sweetheart plea deal—state charges of soliciting prostitution, 13 months in a work-release program.

Judge Smith wrote that the Epstein Files Transparency Act “allowed the court to unseal the documents,” noting the legislation required release of “the unclassified records, documents, communications and investigative materials that relate to Jeffrey Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell.”

The order came after Congress passed the law overriding protective orders that had previously barred release of grand jury materials. Earlier this year—before the legislation—judges in Florida and New York had declined similar DOJ requests to unseal files, citing Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 6(e) protecting grand jury secrecy.

But now Congress has spoken. The law specifically directs DOJ to seek judicial approval for grand jury release. Judge Smith granted it.

The irony is stark: a judge appointed to enforce the law is doing more to implement transparency than the Attorney General charged with executing it.

Judge Smith’s order applies to the Florida case materials. Similar motions will need to be filed and approved for the New York grand jury materials—those relating to the 2019 federal indictment that ended with Epstein’s death in jail.

But even this judicial progress comes with limitations. As former federal prosecutor Glenn Kirschner explained in November, grand jury transcripts represent only “a small fraction” of the total investigative file. They contain witness testimony, but not the underlying evidence—not the photographs, not the communications, not the intelligence assessments, not the FBI’s investigative memos documenting what agents learned and from whom.

Representative Dan Goldman made the same point when Bondi announced she would seek grand jury materials: “Nice try. A distraction from the broader unreleased record, which likely includes agent interviews, seized devices, photographs, and communications.”

The grand jury release is better than nothing. But it’s also the easiest piece for DOJ to provide—creating the appearance of transparency while the most significant materials remain locked in Winchester.

IV. THE PHOTO RELEASE AND WHAT IT REVEALED

Little St. James, the private island owned by Jeffrey Epstein. Credit...Gabriella N. Baez for The New York Times

Two days before Bondi missed Congress’s deadline, House Democrats provided a window into what the Epstein files actually contain—and why DOJ is so invested in controlling their release.

On December 3, the House Oversight Committee’s Democratic minority released more than 150 photos and videos from Jeffrey Epstein’s Little St. James Island estate. The images came from the U.S. Virgin Islands Department of Justice in response to the committee’s request.

Representative Robert Garcia (D-CA), the ranking member, framed the release simply: “These new images are a disturbing look into the world of Jeffrey Epstein and his island. We are releasing these photos and videos to ensure public transparency in our investigation and to help piece together the full picture of Epstein’s horrific crimes.”

The images are indeed disturbing. Multiple bedrooms and bathrooms, partially furnished. A room containing what appears to be a dentist’s chair with plastic masks of men’s faces hanging on the walls. A telephone with a speed-dial index showing first names—Darren, Rich, Mike, Patrick, Larry—with women’s names redacted “out of an abundance of caution.”

One photo shows a blackboard in what appears to be a study. Scribbled on it: “Power.” “Deception.” “Plots.” “Political.” “Truth.” “Music.” “Intellectual.” Other words are redacted.

The videos show walk-throughs of bedrooms and bathrooms, the coastline and helipad, the pool area. The opulent grounds resemble a luxury resort—palm trees, winding paths overlooking the ocean. The setting where systematic abuse occurred over decades.

The photos were taken the year after Epstein’s death and don’t reveal new details about his specific activities—no smoking gun evidence in visible documents or damning materials left lying around. But they document the physical infrastructure that enabled his operation: the isolation, the luxury, the private spaces where abuse occurred away from scrutiny.

Republicans on the Oversight Committee immediately criticized Democrats for “chasing headlines” by releasing materials as “never-before-seen” when similar images had been published previously. The GOP spokesperson noted the committee had received approximately 5,000 documents from JPMorgan Chase, Deutsche Bank, and the Virgin Islands, and would “make them public soon, just as the Committee has already done with the more than 65,000 pages produced during this investigation.”

“It is odd that Democrats are once again releasing selective information, as they have done before,” the statement said.

The partisan sniping obscures what the photo release actually demonstrates: the Epstein files contain extensive documentary evidence of the infrastructure that enabled his crimes. Financial records from major banks. Travel logs. Property records. Communications. Intelligence assessments. The photos of the island compound are just the visible surface.

Democrats on the committee also announced receipt of records from JPMorgan Chase and Deutsche Bank but have not yet released them publicly. Representative Garcia stated the committee would “review and release” these records “in the coming days.”

In an appearance on MSNBC’s “Chris Jansing Reports” on December 3, Garcia said the banking records represent “the biggest thing that we’re going to learn... in the next few days.” He explained they received “about 5,000 pages of photos, videos... and then a substantial amount of bank records” that are “very important to understand the money that was transferred from these incredibly wealthy and powerful men to Jeffrey Epstein, how Jeffrey Epstein obtained his wealth, where was the money coming from.”

These banking records could provide a window into Epstein’s business dealings and connections—JPMorgan was Epstein’s primary bank until 2013, when Deutsche Bank took over until 2018. Both banks reached legal settlements in 2023 over their involvement with Epstein, paying $290 million and $75 million respectively to his accusers.

The financial paper trail is exactly what investigators following money laundering and foreign intelligence operations would find most valuable. And it’s exactly what DOJ has the strongest incentive to keep hidden.

V. MAXWELL’S COMPLICATION

Ghislaine Maxwell is serving a 20-year prison sentence for sex trafficking.Credit...Joe Schildhorn/Patrick McMullan, via Getty Images

Also on December 3, another development complicated DOJ’s careful choreography around grand jury releases.

Ghislaine Maxwell’s lawyer, David Oscar Markus, filed a letter in Manhattan federal court indicating Maxwell would soon file a habeas petition challenging her conviction and seeking release from prison.

Maxwell is serving a 20-year sentence for sex trafficking after her December 2021 conviction. She was moved in July from a Florida federal prison to a minimum-security camp in Texas—about a week after being interviewed for two days by Todd Blanche, the Justice Department’s second-in-command and one of Trump’s former lawyers.

Blanche’s interview of Maxwell came amid the firestorm over Trump’s handling of the Epstein files. According to reports, Maxwell told Blanche she had no knowledge of wrongdoing by Trump.

Now Maxwell is seeking release. Her lawyer’s letter to Judge Paul A. Engelmayer stated that releasing grand jury materials from her case “would create undue prejudice so severe that it would foreclose the possibility of a fair retrial should Ms. Maxwell’s habeas petition succeed.”

Maxwell takes no position on DOJ’s request to release grand jury materials, but argues that doing so while her appeal is pending would contaminate any potential retrial. It’s a clever legal move—not opposing transparency, but highlighting how it could affect her case.

The practical effect, however, is to give DOJ another reason for delay. Bondi can point to Maxwell’s concerns about prejudicing a retrial as justification for caution in releasing materials. Never mind that Maxwell has already exhausted her direct appeals—the habeas petition is a long shot at best.

But in the accommodation psychology playbook, you don’t need legitimate reasons for delay. You just need reasons that sound legitimate enough to justify another week, another month, another review process.

VI. THIRTEEN DAYS AND COUNTING

December 19 is thirteen days away.

The Epstein Files Transparency Act requires DOJ to release “all unclassified records, documents, communications, and investigative materials” by that date. Not some materials. Not a curated selection. All of them.

The law allows specific exemptions:

  • Materials that would jeopardize an ongoing federal investigation

  • Information that could identify victims

  • Classified materials requiring declassification review

  • Grand jury materials (requiring judicial approval, now granted)

  • Explicit depictions of child abuse

The law explicitly prohibits withholding information “solely to prevent embarrassment, reputational harm, or political sensitivity, including to any government official, public figure, or foreign dignitary.”

But as we documented in Part 11, the prohibition against embarrassment-based withholding is toothless when every other exemption provides cover. DOJ won’t claim it’s withholding documents to prevent Trump’s embarrassment. It will claim ongoing investigation exemption. National security. Victim protection. Source and method protection.

The investigation launched November 14—investigating Clinton, Summers, and Hoffman based on Bondi’s mysterious “new information”—provides blanket cover. Any document DOJ claims is “related to” the probe can be withheld. Since the investigation nominally covers Epstein’s ties to public figures, virtually everything is related.

With thirteen days remaining, there’s no public indication DOJ is preparing a comprehensive release. No announcement of a portal or database. No coordination with victims’ attorneys about redaction processes. No response to Congress’s request for a briefing on implementation. The Justice Department has not responded to any media inquiries about how it plans to comply with the law.

Just silence. And memos about antifa tax crimes.

VII. WHAT THE PATTERN REVEALS

Place December 3-5, 2025 in the timeline we’ve documented:

February 21, 2025: Bondi tells Fox News the Epstein client list is “sitting on my desk right now to review.”

May 2025: Bondi briefs Trump that his name appears in the Epstein files.

July 7, 2025: DOJ releases memo stating there is no “client list,” no evidence of blackmail, no basis for charges against “uncharged third parties.” Bondi announces no further files will be released.

September 2025: Representative Thomas Massie files discharge petition to force vote on Epstein Files Transparency Act. Trump and White House officials pressure Republicans not to sign, calling it “a very hostile act to the administration.”

November 14, 2025: DOJ announces new investigations into Clinton, Summers, and Hoffman—conveniently providing “ongoing investigation” exemption just before transparency bill passes.

November 18, 2025: House passes Epstein Files Transparency Act 427-1. Senate passes unanimously. Trump signs November 19.

December 3, 2025: Bipartisan lawmakers request briefing from Bondi by December 5 on implementation progress and the “new information” justifying investigations.

December 4, 2025: Bondi issues memo directing investigation of antifa groups for tax crimes—one day before Congress’s deadline.

December 5, 2025: Bondi’s deadline to brief Congress passes without response or acknowledgment.

Every stage follows the same pattern. Information manipulation. Strategic investigations as shields. Using every tool of executive power to protect Trump while weaponizing DOJ against his opponents. And when forced into transparency, finding new ways to delay, dilute, and distract.

The antifa memo isn’t random. It’s strategic. It signals to Trump’s base that DOJ is focused on the “real” threats—left-wing activists, progressive philanthropists, people who oppose his agenda. It generates headlines that crowd out coverage of Bondi’s non-response on Epstein. It reminds Republicans in Congress that crossing the administration carries consequences.

And it burns time. Thirteen days becomes twelve becomes eleven. With each day that passes, DOJ gets closer to December 19 with less time to be held accountable for what it doesn’t deliver.

CONCLUSION: THE CLOCK RUNS DOWN

Across twelve episodes, we’ve documented every stage of Trump’s accommodation psychology as it applies to the Epstein files. We’ve seen information manipulation, strategic investigations as shields, weaponization of Justice Department independence, threats to Congress, and strategic retreat when forced.

Now we’re seeing the endgame: running out the clock.

December 3-5 revealed the strategy in miniature. Congress demands accountability. Bondi spends December 4 weaponizing DOJ against political opponents. December 5 passes without acknowledgment of Congress’s request. Meanwhile, Judge Smith does more to advance transparency from the bench than the Attorney General does from her office.

The House Democrats’ photo release shows what the files actually contain—the infrastructure of systematic abuse, the connections to powerful figures, the financial paper trails through major banks. Maxwell’s habeas petition provides another excuse for delay. And all of it happens while Bondi investigates antifa for tax violations.

Thirteen days remain until December 19.

In Part 13, we’ll examine what to expect when that deadline arrives, what the accommodation psychology predicts DOJ will actually deliver, and what happens if the release is as sanitized as everything we’ve documented suggests it will be.

But the essential pattern is already clear. When given a choice between briefing Congress on Epstein transparency and hunting for tax violations among Trump’s political opponents, Pam Bondi chose the latter.

Then she let the deadline pass in silence.

The accommodation psychology doesn’t end until the public decides it ends. Congress set a test. Bondi failed it. December 19 will be another test.

The choice remains yours.
