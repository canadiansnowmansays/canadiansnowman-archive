Two Kings

Or, The Empire Strikes Back (Politely)

May 3, 2026

Stephen’s Sunday Satire

On Tuesday, the White House posted a photograph of President Trump and King Charles III standing together on the South Lawn. The caption read: “TWO KINGS.” It included a crown emoji.

This was posted by the official account of a republic founded, two hundred and fifty years ago this summer, on the principle that there should be no kings.

It was posted on the same day that Charles stood before a joint meeting of Congress and reminded the room, in the gentlest possible English, that “executive power is subject to checks and balances.” Democrats gave him a standing ovation. Republicans also stood, but with the slightly uncertain air of people who suspect they have just applauded something they were supposed to be against.

And it was posted two days after the president told Norah O’Donnell, on national television, “I’m not a king. If I was a king, I wouldn’t be dealing with you.”

So we find ourselves in a situation in which: the president says he is not a king; the White House says he is a king; an actual king flew three thousand miles to tell Congress — while being very charming about it — that nobody should be a king; and millions of Americans have been marching in the streets holding signs that say “No Kings.”

Stephen Leacock could not have written this. He would have tried, and his editor would have sent it back with a note that said, “Too much.”

The visit, we were told, was to celebrate the 250th anniversary of American independence from Britain. That is to say: a king was invited to a birthday party celebrating the day his ancestors were fired.

“Happy anniversary,” one imagines the monarch thinking, as Air Force One — no, RAF Voyager Vespina — touched down at Joint Base Andrews. “So glad the revolution worked out.”

The first event was afternoon tea at the White House, which is the most British thing one can do short of apologizing for the weather. Charles and Camilla were received at the South Portico by the president and first lady. Behind them, visible to the cameras, hung a painting of Trump with his fist raised after the 2024 assassination attempt. One assumes the King noticed. One assumes the King said nothing. This is what the British mean when they speak of diplomacy.

The couples then toured the South Lawn, where they visited a beehive shaped like the White House. The White House posted this on social media with the caption “BEE BEST,” a reference to the first lady’s initiative. I mention this not because it is satirically important but because I want you to know that it happened.

There followed a garden party at the British ambassador’s residence. Six hundred guests. Fortnum & Mason tea. Hambledon sparkling wine. The ambassador, Sir Christian Turner, presided with the serene confidence of a man whose leaked private remarks — criticizing America’s handling of the Epstein files and calling the “special relationship” nostalgic and backward-looking — had been published by the Financial Times that very morning.

One imagines the canapés were excellent.

The speech to Congress was the centrepiece, and by all accounts Charles performed it with the skill of a man who has spent seventy-seven years waiting for the job and intends to be good at it.

He quoted Oscar Wilde: “We have really everything in common with America nowadays except, of course, language.” Bipartisan laughter. He praised the Founders for “balancing contending forces and drawing strength in diversity.” He reminded the chamber that Magna Carta and English common law were the intellectual foundations of the American experiment — a point that is historically unassailable and, under the circumstances, wonderfully passive-aggressive.

He praised NATO. This is the alliance the president has spent a decade threatening to abandon. He defended the Royal Navy. This is the service the president recently insulted. He spoke about protecting nature. This from the man the president once dismissed as “the Prince of Whales” in a tweet.

And then came the line: “Executive power is subject to checks and balances.”

The room rose. Both sides. Though — and reporters were careful to note this — the applause from the Democratic side was, shall we say, more resounding.

The president later called the speech “great” and said he was “very jealous.” It is unclear which part he was jealous of.

But the true masterpiece was the state dinner.

The king, raising his glass, addressed the small matter of the East Wing, which the president has ordered demolished to make way for a ballroom — a project currently contested in the courts and which, since Saturday’s shooting, the administration has argued is essential for presidential safety.

“On this occasion,” Charles said, “I cannot help noticing the readjustments to the East Wing, Mr. President, following your visit to Windsor Castle last year.” He paused. “And I’m sorry to say that we British, of course, made our own small attempt at real estate redevelopment of the White House in 1814.”

The room laughed. The president laughed. Whether everyone understood what had just happened is another question. The King of England had just compared the president’s demolition of part of his own house to an act of war committed by the British Empire during the burning of Washington. And he had done it in a toast. With champagne. In a room full of Supreme Court justices, three of whom had just ruled against their host.

This, for the uninitiated, is how the British conduct foreign policy.

The gift exchange deserves its own paragraph, because the irony was so precisely calibrated that one suspects the Palace employs satirists.

Charles gave Trump a framed facsimile of the original 1879 design plans for the Resolute Desk — the desk in the Oval Office, built from the timbers of a British ship, gifted by Queen Victoria. The subtext: We gave you the desk. We gave you the office. Enjoy it while it lasts.

Trump gave Charles a facsimile of an 1785 letter from John Adams to John Jay, describing Adams’ meeting with King George III — the meeting in which the first American ambassador told the British monarch, with exquisite awkwardness, that the colonies had left and were not coming back.

One could not invent better satirical props. The king gave the president a reminder that the furniture is borrowed. The president gave the king a letter about the breakup.

And then there were the flags.

Local government workers in Washington, D.C., are seen replacing an Australian flag on display with the Union Jack, at a location along 17th Street near the White House on Friday. The D.C. government mistakenly placed both flags around the White House in preparation for a pending visit from King Charles. (Andrew Leyden/Getty Images)

Fifteen Australian flags were hung on lampposts across Washington, D.C. in preparation for the British monarch’s arrival. Not British flags. Australian flags. They were spotted and photographed before anyone in an official capacity appears to have noticed, and were quietly being replaced as reporters began asking questions. In fairness to whoever orders the bunting for the District of Columbia, the Australian flag does contain the Union Jack. But it also contains the Southern Cross and represents a country that is roughly ten thousand miles from the United Kingdom and has, notably, not been Britain since 1901.

The King, arriving by motorcade through streets lined with the flag of a country he also technically heads but which was definitely not the point, is reported to have said nothing. Diplomacy.

The Canadian observer — which is to say, this one — watches all of this from a particular angle.

We are, after all, the other former colony. The one that didn’t revolt. The one that kept the monarch on the money and the parliamentary system intact and said, “No thank you, we’ll do it differently,” in 1867, and has been quietly doing it differently ever since. We are the country Charles mentioned at the state dinner, in the context of the FIFA World Cup. Canada and the United States will co-host it this summer. “In one sense, as heads of state, we are joint hosts,” the King said, before noting that he had more Commonwealth countries competing than Trump had American ones, and adding, with a smile: “I like my odds.”

Trump nodded.

From up here — from the country that Leacock wrote about, the country of Mariposa and muddy roads and church socials that end in committee — the view is surreal. A king flew to a republic to celebrate the republic’s independence from his own family. The republic posted “TWO KINGS” while its citizens marched under banners that said “NO KINGS.” The president, who says he is not a king, demolished part of his own palace to build a ballroom, and used a shooting at someone else’s dinner as the justification. And the actual king — the one with the crown and the lineage and the thousand-year-old institution — stood in the well of Congress and gently suggested, to sustained applause, that power should have limits.

Two kings, the White House said. But only one of them seemed to know what the word means.

A final thought, from the Canadian side of the border, for any Americans still reading.

The hive is not the only model. Bees are remarkable creatures — industrious, cooperative, essential to the ecosystem — but they do not choose their queen. She is born to it. The colony serves her, or it dies. There is no mechanism for dissent. There is no midterm election in a beehive.

You, on the other hand, have one.

The Founders who declared independence 250 years ago — the ones Charles praised so eloquently in the chamber where their successors now sit — designed a system with a feature the hive does not have: a peaceful, regular, non-negotiable mechanism for telling the people in charge that their time is up.

November 2026 is six months away. The House. The Senate. The governors. The state legislatures. Every seat that matters.

Bee brave. You can last.
