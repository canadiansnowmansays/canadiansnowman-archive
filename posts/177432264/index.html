When Accommodation Psychology Destroys What It Should Save

Episode 13: The Anti-TACO Catastrophe

October 31, 2025

October 31, 2025

Introduction: The Cruelest Irony of Accommodation Psychology

October 2025 proved accommodation psychology’s most devastating paradox: Trump accommodated on everything except the one issue where Americans desperately needed him to accommodate. While he retreated on Ukraine military aid, delayed pharmaceutical tariffs, and blocked Epstein transparency—the government shutdown that began October 1st reached 31 days by month’s end, becoming the second-longest in U.S. history.

For the first time in the TACO series, we witnessed the anti-TACO: accommodation would have served Trump’s interests, protected working families, and demonstrated actual governing capability—yet accommodation psychology made him psychologically incapable of retreat. Serial accommodators lose the ability to recognize when accommodation is the rational choice.

The shutdown crisis reached its climax October 31st when two federal judges ordered the Trump administration to use contingency funds for SNAP benefits—forcing the accommodation Trump refused to make voluntarily. Judge John McConnell declared: “SNAP benefits have never, until now, been terminated.” Courts had to compel what strategic thinking should have provided: accommodation to feed 42 million Americans.

Key October Milestones:

  • October 1: Government shutdown begins

  • October 17: Trump-Putin call leads to Tomahawk denial

  • October 20: Federal courts run out of funding

  • October 27-28: Grijalva still unseated 35+ days after election

  • October 30: Trump accommodates Xi extensively on tariffs

  • October 31: Federal judges force SNAP accommodation Trump wouldn’t provide

  • November 1: SNAP benefits expire without full funding

The Government Shutdown Anti-TACO: Courts Force What Trump Couldn’t Give

Republicans control White House, House (219-213), and Senate (53-47), yet need 60 Senate votes to pass spending. House passed clean CR September 19th; Senate Democrats blocked it demanding ACA subsidy extension for 24 million Americans. Republicans falsely claimed this meant “healthcare for undocumented immigrants”—though ACA enrollment requires citizenship documentation.

September’s analysis predicted Trump should accommodate: fund through December 31st, claim “responsible governance,” force healthcare fight elsewhere. Instead, cascading crises emerged: SNAP benefits facing cutoff, federal courts unfunded, 830,000 working without pay, 1 million furloughed, military pay uncertain.

The October 31st Judicial Intervention

In emergency rulings, Judge John McConnell (Rhode Island) and Judge Indira Talwani (Massachusetts) ordered the administration to use SNAP contingency funds. This marked the first time in program history benefits faced complete suspension.

McConnell ruled: “There is no doubt that the $6 billion contingency funds are appropriated funds that are without a doubt necessary to carry out the program’s operation. The shutdown doesn’t do away with SNAP, it just does away with the funding of it.”

Talwani wrote: “Plaintiffs are likely to succeed on their claim that Defendants’ suspension of SNAP benefits is unlawful.” She gave the administration until Monday to report whether they’d provide partial benefits using the $5.3 billion contingency fund or full benefits using additional funds.

The administration had reversed its pre-shutdown plan to use contingency funds, claiming they lacked legal authority—forcing 25 states and D.C. to sue. National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett criticized the rulings, claiming using emergency funds meant “we don’t have our emergency funds in case we have a hurricane”—prioritizing hypothetical disasters over actual hunger.

The Accommodation Psychology Trap

Trump accommodated pharmaceutical tariffs (delayed), Tomahawk missiles (denied), even Canada faced only 10% symbolic retaliation—yet couldn’t accommodate on funding that would have:

  • Paid military and federal workers

  • Prevented SNAP crisis for 42 million Americans

  • Kept federal courts operating

  • Demonstrated governing capacity

The cruelest irony: when federal judges forced the accommodation Trump wouldn’t make voluntarily, even that proved insufficient—$5.3 billion can’t cover the $8-9 billion monthly SNAP cost. Voluntary accommodation would have prevented the crisis; forced accommodation can only partially mitigate it.

For once, TACO would have been the right choice. And accommodation psychology made it impossible.

Three Accommodations Trump Made Instead

Ukraine/Putin: The Tomahawk Theatre

October 6: Trump told reporters he was “close to deciding” on Tomahawks for Ukraine with 1,000+ mile range. October 16: Putin called Trump for two hours before Zelensky meeting, warning Tomahawks would harm relations.

Trump immediately accommodated. He told reporters: “I did actually say to him, ‘Would you mind if I gave a couple thousands of Tomahawks to your opposition?’... He didn’t like the idea.” Within days, Trump explained Tomahawks were “too complex” requiring “minimum six months” training: “The only way a Tomahawk is going to be shot is if we shot it, and we’re not going to do that.”

Zelensky left empty-handed October 17th. Washington Post compared Trump to Charlie Brown repeatedly falling for Lucy’s football trick. Russian state media openly ridiculed Trump’s weakness: “Putin uses flattery effectively as part of his tool kit to manipulate the president.”

From threatening “couple thousand Tomahawks” to explaining training complexity in 6 days—classic accommodation through technical justifications.

China Tariff TACO: Kristof’s “Knife Fight” Prophecy Fulfilled

Nicholas Kristof warned Trump “started a trade war and soon found that he was carrying a tariff to a knife fight.” China’s leverage: controlling 90% of rare earth supply essential for military equipment. A single submarine requires four tons.

The October 30th Trump-Xi meeting at Gimhae Air Base validated every prediction. Terry Lynch, CEO of Power Metallic Mines, told Kristof the West needs a “Manhattan Project-scale effort” to develop alternatives, requiring 5-7 years. “In that interim time, we’re going to have to make a deal with China.”

Trump’s Concessions:

  • Cut fentanyl tariffs from 20% to 10% (CNN: “extraordinary concession”)

  • Cancelled threatened 100% additional tariffs

  • Extended tariff truce through November 2026

  • Taiwan “never came up”

  • Agreed to TikTok framework despite security concerns

  • Will visit China April 2026

Xi’s Strategic “Concessions”:

  • One-year rare earth suspension—not the April restrictions still disrupting factories

  • Vague soybean promises

  • Fentanyl cooperation pledges (never previously honored)

Trump rated the meeting “12 out of 10” while accommodating on every substantive issue. As Kristof predicted: “a brilliant move by Xi, allowing Beijing to retain its leverage without causing such disruption that America would make all-out efforts to break China’s near-monopoly.”

Hours before, Trump announced resuming nuclear testing to appear tough—but only delivery systems, not warheads. Deflection theater while preparing accommodation.

Center for American Progress: “The Trump administration’s approach to China is in a strategic free fall.” The one-year suspension ensures China can reimpose restrictions before alternatives exist—permanent leverage through temporary suspension.

The Grijalva/Epstein Procedural Block

Adelita Grijalva won Arizona’s 7th District September 24th by 70%. As of October 28, she remains unseated—35+ days later, leaving 800,000 constituents without representation.

Grijalva pledged to sign the Massie-Khanna discharge petition as her first act, providing the 218th signature to force release of all Epstein files. Speaker Johnson blamed the shutdown, though the House hasn’t held regular session since September 19th.

Two Florida Republicans were sworn in within 24 hours of their victories. Johnson’s precedent excuse fails—previous delays didn’t involve blocking transparency votes affecting a sitting president.

White House mounting “pressure campaign” on Republican women to withdraw signatures before Grijalva’s sworn in. Arizona Attorney General threatened legal action October 22nd.

Until sworn in, Grijalva cannot collect salary or provide constituent services. As Rep. Ansari noted after meeting Epstein survivors: “Mike Johnson looked them in the eye, promised accountability... Now he is using his power to protect the very men who enabled that abuse.”

Other October Accommodations

Pharmaceutical: September 26 announcement of 100% tariffs effective October 1st became “still being prepared.” Pfizer CEO received “three-year grace period.”

Judicial: October 14 Federal Circuit Court deadline on illegal tariffs—Trump appealed rather than comply.

Canada: Only 10% symbolic tariff for Reagan ad “retaliation” while accommodating substance elsewhere.

The October Convergence: When Every Domain Accommodates Simultaneously

October proved accommodation operates simultaneously across every policy domain—except the one that mattered most:

What Trump Accommodated:

  • Ukraine military aid (Tomahawks denied)

  • China’s demands (tariff cuts, Taiwan silence)

  • Pharmaceutical deadlines (perpetually delayed)

  • Epstein transparency (Grijalva blocked)

  • Judicial rulings (appealed not complied)

What Trump Couldn’t Accommodate—Until Courts Forced It:

  • Government funding (shutdown reaches 31 days)

  • Federal worker pay (1.4 million affected)

  • SNAP benefits (judicial intervention required)

  • Military paychecks (uncertainty continues)

  • ACA subsidies (24 million Americans affected)

When accommodation psychology captures decision-making, strategic accommodation becomes psychologically impossible. Trump accommodated threats to powerful interests but couldn’t accommodate threats to working families—until federal judges forced partial accommodation that voluntary action would have prevented entirely.

Conclusion: When Courts Must Force the Accommodation America Needs

October 2025 validated accommodation psychology’s cruelest theorem: serial accommodators lose the capacity to accommodate strategically. Trump accommodated Putin’s demands, China’s leverage, pharmaceutical companies, and personal exposure risks—yet couldn’t accommodate on government funding that would have helped millions.

The October 31st judicial intervention proved the point definitively. When Judge McConnell declared “SNAP benefits have never, until now, been terminated” and Judge Talwani ruled the suspension “unlawful,” they forced the accommodation Trump psychologically couldn’t make voluntarily. But forced accommodation comes too late and too little—$5.3 billion can’t cover $8-9 billion in monthly costs.

For the first time, Americans wanted accommodation. Federal workers needed paychecks. SNAP recipients required food assistance. This was the ONE TACO Americans requested—and accommodation psychology made it impossible until courts intervened.

The Inverted Priority Structure:

  • Putin’s demands: Accommodated immediately

  • China’s leverage: Accommodated extensively

  • Pharmaceutical profits: Accommodated indefinitely

  • Personal Epstein exposure: Accommodated through procedure

  • 42 million Americans’ food assistance: Required judicial force

October proved accommodation psychology doesn’t serve American interests—it serves power and self-preservation. When federal judges must compel the government to feed its citizens while the president voluntarily accommodates foreign adversaries and corporate interests, democracy faces existential crisis.

Americans wanted Trump to TACO. Mathematical logic demanded it. Basic humanity required it. And accommodation psychology made it impossible—proving serial accommodation destroys not just strategic flexibility but basic governing capacity.

The fact that courts had to force even partial SNAP accommodation while Trump voluntarily accommodated everything else reveals accommodation psychology’s ultimate danger: it inverts democratic priorities until protecting the powerful matters more than feeding the hungry.

Democracy cannot survive when accommodation serves only those who don’t need it.
