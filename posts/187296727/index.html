Part 39: The One Crime He Can’t Escape (2017-2029)

April 21, 2026

President Donald Trump made a 2024 White House run official on November 15, 2022, in the grand ballroom at Mar-a-Lago. Kimberly Leonard/Insider

Throughout this 47-part series, we’ve documented Trump’s lifetime pattern of evading accountability. From Vietnam to Atlantic City, from Trump University to tax fraud, from the Mueller Report to COVID-19, from impeachments to criminal indictments—Trump has escaped consequences for conduct that would have destroyed anyone else’s career and freedom.

But there’s one crime Trump cannot escape through the usual mechanisms. One ongoing pattern of misconduct where the statute of limitations hasn’t started running. One vulnerability that Trump’s 2024 election victory cannot eliminate.

The Twenty-Second Amendment to the Constitution prohibits any person from being elected president more than twice. Trump served 2017-2021. If he serves 2025-2029, he cannot run again. Ever. Which means that after January 20, 2029, Trump will be a private citizen with no election to shield him, no presidential immunity to protect him, and an extensive record of financial crimes committed during both presidencies.

This essay examines Trump’s ongoing presidential profiteering from 2017 through his current term ending in 2029—documenting how Trump is creating evidence of crimes in real-time, crimes that will remain prosecutable after he leaves office for the final time, and crimes he cannot pardon himself for or dismiss through presidential power.

The Constitutional Vulnerability: The 22nd Amendment

No person shall be elected to the office of the President more than twice." The 22nd Amendment — ratified in 1951 — is the one constitutional constraint Trump cannot circumvent, pardon, dismiss, or outrun.

Every previous Trump accountability failure involved the same escape mechanisms:

  • Statutes of limitations ran out before prosecution (Trump University, Foundation, tax fraud)

  • He won election making prosecution politically impossible (Mueller Report obstruction, emoluments violations)

  • Political parties protected him through acquittals or dismissals (both impeachments, criminal cases)

  • Judges he appointed dismissed cases (classified documents, January 6)

But the 22nd Amendment creates a hard stop Trump cannot circumvent. After 2029, he cannot run for president again. No election can save him. No political power can protect him. He becomes an ordinary citizen subject to prosecution like anyone else.

This makes his ongoing presidential profiteering during both terms uniquely vulnerable to eventual prosecution. Unlike his past crimes, these are being committed now, documented in real-time, with evidence that will remain fresh and actionable after he leaves office.

First Presidency Profiteering (2017-2021): $160-200 Million

During Trump’s first presidency, he systematically violated the Constitution’s Emoluments Clauses by profiting from his office (see Part 23). The evidence included:

Foreign Government Spending at Trump Properties:

  • Saudi government officials spending hundreds of thousands at Trump International Hotel

  • Chinese government trademarks granted to Ivanka Trump’s companies

  • Foreign diplomats booking rooms at Trump hotels to curry favor

  • Turkey’s government lobbying Trump while spending at Trump properties

Federal Government Spending at Trump Properties:

  • Secret Service paying Trump’s company for hotel rooms at Mar-a-Lago

  • Federal agencies booking conferences at Trump hotels

  • Military and State Department personnel staying at Trump properties

  • Total federal spending estimated at $1.4 million+

Political Spending at Trump Properties:

  • Republican campaigns and PACs booking Trump hotels and golf courses

  • Foreign governments renting Trump properties for events

  • Political fundraisers at Mar-a-Lago generating revenue for Trump

Estimates of Trump’s first-term presidential profiteering range from $160-200 million. This was documented extensively in lawsuits, congressional investigations, and media reports. Trump kept ownership of his businesses, refused to divest, and personally profited from the presidency.

Why First-Term Crimes Weren’t Prosecuted

Trump left office in January 2021. Why wasn’t he prosecuted for first-term emoluments violations?

Several factors prevented prosecution:

Civil vs. Criminal: Most emoluments clause enforcement is civil, not criminal. Courts dismissed emoluments lawsuits on standing grounds, preventing any adjudication.

Novel Legal Territory: No president had ever been prosecuted for emoluments violations. Prosecutors were reluctant to bring unprecedented charges.

Biden’s Calculus: President Biden’s Justice Department chose not to pursue Trump prosecution for emoluments, likely fearing it would appear partisan and set dangerous precedents.

Statutes of Limitations: For some potential charges (tax fraud, false statements), statutes of limitations began running when crimes occurred, creating time pressure prosecutors weren’t prepared to meet.

But the key factor was prosecutorial discretion. No one forced prosecutors to bring charges, and they chose not to.

The Evidence Being Created

Unlike his first presidency, Trump’s second term profiteering is occurring in 2025-2029. Evidence is being created now:

Documentary Evidence:

  • Bank records documenting payments to Trump properties

  • Guest lists showing who paid for access

  • Communications between business associates and foreign officials

  • Tax returns (if seized via warrant) showing income sources

  • Financial disclosures (or lack thereof) required by law

  • Social media posts promoting commercial ventures

  • Court filings from ongoing lawsuits and legal schemes

  • Cryptocurrency transaction records and token sales

  • Federal agency spending records at Trump properties

  • Testimony under oath from officials involved in schemes

This evidence will be available to prosecutors after January 20, 2029. The crimes are being committed now. The statutes of limitations won’t start running until Trump leaves office. And Trump cannot pardon himself for state crimes or for crimes committed after he leaves federal office.

Why 2029 Is Different

After January 20, 2029, Trump’s situation fundamentally changes:

No Election to Win: The 22nd Amendment makes him ineligible. He cannot use election victory to shield himself from prosecution.

No Presidential Immunity: As a private citizen, he has no immunity from prosecution. No OLC memo prohibits charging him.

No Pardon Power: He cannot pardon himself for crimes committed while president. He cannot pardon state crimes. Any federal pardon must come from his successor.

Fresh Evidence: Crimes from 2025-2029 will have recent, clear evidence. Witnesses will remember. Documents will be available. Statutes of limitations won’t have run.

Political Pressure: If Trump’s second presidency ends in chaos or disaster, political pressure for prosecution will be enormous. Prosecutors won’t be able to hide behind Biden’s “move forward” rationale.

State Prosecutors: New York, DC, and other jurisdictions can bring state charges Trump cannot pardon. State prosecutors may be less concerned about political implications.

No Political Future: Trump's age (he'll be 83 in 2029) and ineligibility mean he poses no future political threat that might deter prosecution. His most effective shield has always been the threat of electoral victory. After 2029, that threat disappears.

The Prosecutorial Decision

After 2029, prosecutors will face a decision: pursue Trump for presidential profiteering or let him escape accountability one final time.

The case for prosecution is strong:

  • Clear evidence of crimes

  • Constitutional violations

  • Massive financial corruption

  • Deterrence value for future presidents

  • Justice for constitutional violations

The case against prosecution is political:

  • Appears partisan

  • Could trigger violence from Trump supporters

  • Sets precedent of prosecuting former presidents

  • Potential jury nullification

  • Divisive for country

But unlike previous Trump crimes, these charges wouldn’t rely on novel legal theories or unprecedented applications of law. Tax fraud is tax fraud. False statements are false statements. Wire fraud is wire fraud. Emoluments violations are explicit in the Constitution. These are ordinary crimes committed on an extraordinary scale using presidential office.

The Stakes

If Trump escapes accountability for presidential profiteering after 2029, the precedent is devastating:

  • Future presidents will know they can personally profit from office without consequence

  • The Emoluments Clauses become dead letters

  • Presidential corruption becomes normalized

  • Oligarchic capture of presidency accelerates

  • American democracy fundamentally changes

But if prosecutors act—if they charge Trump with tax fraud, wire fraud, false statements, bribery, or emoluments violations arising from his presidential profiteering—they establish that:

  • Presidents cannot profit from office

  • Constitutional constraints have teeth

  • Wealth and power don’t guarantee immunity

  • Eventually, accountability arrives

The Unique Opportunity

This is the crime Trump cannot escape through his usual methods:

❌ Can’t run out the statute of limitations - it doesn’t start until he leaves office in 2029

❌ Can’t win an election to avoid prosecution - the 22nd Amendment prohibits running again

❌ Can’t use presidential immunity - he’ll be a private citizen

❌ Can’t pardon himself - he’ll be out of office when charged

❌ Can’t get charges dismissed - no presidential power to wield

❌ Can’t claim crimes are too old - evidence from 2025-2029 will be recent and fresh

For the first time in his life, Trump will face accountability he cannot evade. The question is whether prosecutors will have the courage to pursue it.

Trump’s presidential profiteering from 2017-2029 represents the one crime he cannot escape through his usual mechanisms. The evidence is being created in real-time. The statutes of limitations won’t start until he leaves office. The 22nd Amendment ensures he cannot win another election to shield himself.

After January 20, 2029, Trump will be a private citizen facing potential prosecution for eight years of systematic presidential profiteering—from emoluments violations to cryptocurrency schemes to the audacious lawsuit documented in Part 40.

Whether prosecutors will have the courage to act remains to be seen.
