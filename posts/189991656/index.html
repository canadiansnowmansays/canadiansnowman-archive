The Contribution Index

Australia

Part X: The Lucky Country That Chose Coal

April 10, 2026

The Sydney Opera House at sunset, Sydney Harbour. Australia is a country that has never had trouble finding the sun. The question — for its climate policy, its coal exports, and its Pacific neighbours watching the ocean rise — is what it chooses to do with it. Photo: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA)

Underperformer #6

Australia is, by almost every domestic measure, one of the best places on Earth to live. Its citizens are healthy, wealthy, well-educated, and free. Its natural environment is spectacular — from the Great Barrier Reef to the Daintree rainforest to the vast red expanse of the outback. Its democratic institutions are stable. Its multicultural society is, by global standards, remarkably successful. Its quality of life is enviable by any measure.

And yet it lands on the underperformer list. Because what Australia does at home is very different from what Australia does to the planet. And what Australia does to the planet — exporting more coal than any other developed nation while sitting under the most intense solar irradiance of any wealthy country — is a choice so willfully self-defeating that it defines Australia’s scorecard more than any other single factor.

The lucky country. Lucky in geography, in resources, in climate, in institutions. Unlucky in the political courage required to stop digging up the one commodity that is destroying the global climate.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 42 out of 50

Australia’s domestic scorecard is strong — nearly identical to Canada’s, and for similar reasons. Universal healthcare through Medicare provides a genuine safety net, though with the same pressures visible across wealthy nations: wait times, workforce shortages, and mental health services that lag behind physical health coverage. Life expectancy exceeds 83 years. The system works.

Education scores a 9. Australian universities — particularly the Group of Eight research-intensive institutions — are globally competitive, attracting hundreds of thousands of international students annually. Primary and secondary education is well-funded and produces strong outcomes across most of the population. The PISA scores are solid. The research output is significant.

Democratic governance earns an 8. Australia’s compulsory voting system — unique among the nations in this series — produces voter turnout above 90 percent and governments with genuine democratic mandates. The preferential voting system reduces the distortions of first-past-the-post, and the Senate’s proportional representation provides a check on the House of Representatives. Press freedom is strong, the judiciary is independent, and peaceful transfers of power are the norm. The 8 rather than a 9 reflects concerns about media concentration — Australia has one of the most concentrated media markets in the developed world, with Rupert Murdoch’s News Corp dominating print and digital media in ways that shape political discourse — and about the increasing use of national security legislation to restrict journalism on matters of public interest.

Gender equality and minority rights earn an 8. Australia has made significant progress on gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights (marriage equality was approved by public vote in 2017), and multicultural inclusion. The score is held back by the same failure that holds Canada’s score back: the treatment of Indigenous Australians.

The gap between Australia’s treatment of its general population and its treatment of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples is the nation’s deepest structural failure. The Closing the Gap targets — established to address disparities in health, education, employment, and justice outcomes — have been largely unmet. Indigenous life expectancy lags non-Indigenous by roughly 8 years. Indigenous Australians are imprisoned at rates 12 times higher than non-Indigenous Australians. The 2023 Voice referendum — a proposal to establish an Indigenous advisory body in the Constitution — was defeated, a result that many Indigenous leaders described as a rejection of recognition itself. The score of 8 on minority rights reflects a nation whose formal legal commitments to equality are strong for most of its population and catastrophically inadequate for its First Peoples.

Environmental Stewardship: 26 out of 50

The Great Barrier Reef has experienced five mass bleaching events since 1998, three of them since 2020. It is a World Heritage Site being unmade in real time. Source: Great Barrier Reef Foundation / Reuters

This is where Australia’s underperformer status is sealed, and where the gap between what Australia is and what it could be is widest and most frustrating.

Emissions per capita earn a 3. Australia is one of the highest per capita emitters in the developed world — roughly 17 metric tons of CO2 equivalent per person. The country is the world’s largest exporter of coal and one of the largest exporters of liquefied natural gas. In total, the fossil fuels Australia exports produce roughly twice the emissions of Australia’s entire domestic economy when burned overseas. Australia is not merely failing to reduce its own carbon footprint. It is exporting carbon at industrial scale — profiting from the commodity that is destroying the global climate, and doing so from a continent that is itself among the most vulnerable to climate change impacts, from bushfires to coral bleaching to extreme heat events.

Renewable energy earns a 6 — and this score contains both the frustration and the glimmer. Australia has the highest solar irradiance of any developed country. Its rooftop solar adoption is among the highest in the world — roughly one in three Australian homes has solar panels. Wind energy is expanding rapidly. The potential is extraordinary: Australia could, with existing technology, become a renewable energy superpower — exporting clean energy to Southeast Asia through submarine cables, producing green hydrogen at scale, and demonstrating that a resource-rich economy can transition from fossil fuels to clean energy without economic collapse.

The execution lags the potential because of the political power of the fossil fuel lobby, which has shaped Australian energy policy for decades. The mining industry’s influence on both major parties — through donations, lobbying, and the revolving door between mining companies and government — has produced a bipartisan consensus that Australia’s economic future depends on continued fossil fuel extraction. The Adani Carmichael mine in Queensland — one of the largest coal mines in the world, approved despite overwhelming scientific evidence of its climate impact — is the most visible symbol of this political capture. Australia does not lack renewable energy potential. It lacks the political will to prioritise it over coal revenue.

Biodiversity protection earns a 5. Australia is one of the world’s biodiversity hotspots — home to unique species found nowhere else on Earth, from koalas to platypuses to thousands of endemic plant species. It is also, among developed nations, one of the most significant deforestation offenders. Land clearing, particularly in Queensland and New South Wales, continues at rates that would be scandalous in Europe. The Great Barrier Reef — one of the planet’s most important marine ecosystems, visible from space, and a UNESCO World Heritage site — has experienced repeated mass bleaching events driven by ocean warming. The bleaching is not a natural cycle. It is a direct consequence of climate change, driven by emissions from nations like Australia. The nation whose reef is dying is also the nation whose coal exports accelerate the dying.

Andrew “Twiggy” Forrest: iron ore billionaire, green hydrogen evangelist, ocean philanthropist. His personal trajectory — from extractive fortune to environmental penance — is the Australian national story compressed into one very wealthy man. The cheques are real. So was the iron ore. Source: Fortescue Media Centre

The contradiction between Australia’s fossil fuel economy and its environmental potential is embodied in a single person: Andrew “Twiggy” Forrest, who made his fortune in iron ore mining and now pledges billions toward renewable energy through Fortescue and funds ocean conservation and anti-slavery work through the Minderoo Foundation. Forrest’s trajectory — from extractive wealth to environmental philanthropy — is Australia’s national story in miniature: a country that knows what the right thing is, profits enormously from doing the wrong thing, and then writes charitable cheques to manage the cognitive dissonance. Philanthropy as penance is a recurring theme on the underperformer list. In Australia’s case, the penance comes from the same industry that created the sin.

Deforestation earns a 5. Water and air quality earn a 7 — generally good by global standards, though bushfire smoke events have produced hazardous air quality episodes of increasing frequency, and agricultural runoff continues to affect the Great Barrier Reef’s water quality.

Systemic Behaviour: 25 out of 50

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earns a 3 — the lowest score on Australia’s systemic behaviour card and one of the most damning numbers in the series for any democracy.

Australia’s offshore detention policy — warehousing asylum seekers who arrive by boat in facilities on Nauru and formerly on Manus Island in Papua New Guinea — has been condemned by the United Nations, the Australian Human Rights Commission, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Médecins Sans Frontières, and the UNHCR. The conditions documented in these facilities — indefinite detention, inadequate medical care, sexual abuse, self-harm, and the psychological deterioration that accompanies years of confinement without resolution — have been described by medical professionals as constituting cruel, inhumane, and degrading treatment.

The policy is not a failure of administration. It is a deliberate strategy: the Australian government has stated explicitly that the purpose of offshore detention is deterrence — to make the conditions so punishing that people fleeing persecution will choose not to come to Australia. A wealthy democracy that deliberately brutalizes people seeking asylum, as a matter of stated policy, in order to deter others from exercising their legal right to seek protection, cannot score higher than a 3. Some would argue the score is generous.

Arms exports earn a 5. Australia is not a major arms exporter by global standards, but it is a significant arms importer — its AUKUS partnership with the United States and the United Kingdom, centred on nuclear-powered submarines, represents one of the largest defence procurement programmes in the world. Australia’s military engagement — participation in US-led coalitions in Iraq and Afghanistan, increasing military cooperation with Japan and India — positions it as a security actor in the Indo-Pacific rather than a neutral contributor.

Tax haven and corruption facilitation earn a 6. Australia’s governance is generally transparent, though political donation laws have significant loopholes, and the influence of the mining lobby on policy — documented extensively by journalists and researchers — represents a form of institutional corruption that, while legal, undermines the democratic process.

Adherence to international law earns a 6. Australia generally respects multilateral frameworks, though its treatment of asylum seekers has drawn formal criticism from UN human rights bodies, and its withdrawal from the International Court of Justice case brought by Timor-Leste over maritime boundary disputes raised questions about its commitment to international adjudication when the outcomes are commercially inconvenient.

The Neighbour Test: 24 out of 50

Australia’s Neighbour Test tells the story of a nation that means well, engages intermittently, and has one catastrophic blind spot.

Multilateral orientation earns a 5. Australia participates in international institutions and has increased engagement with Pacific Island nations in recent years, partly in response to Chinese diplomatic expansion in the region. The “Pacific Step-Up” strategy represents genuine increased investment — but it was motivated as much by strategic competition with China as by genuine concern for Pacific Island wellbeing. When your Pacific engagement accelerates because Beijing is making inroads rather than because Tuvalu is sinking, the multilateral orientation is strategic rather than principled.

Response to global crises earns a 5. Australia’s disaster response capacity is significant and has been deployed regionally — particularly in response to cyclones and earthquakes in the Pacific. But its response to the global climate crisis — the crisis that most directly threatens its Pacific Island neighbours — has been to continue exporting the fossil fuels that drive it. Crisis response loses credibility when you are contributing to the crisis you claim to be responding to.

Treatment of allies earns a 5. Australia’s relationships with Pacific Island nations have improved from their historical low — the “Pacific family” rhetoric has been backed by increased aid and diplomatic engagement. But the relationship has been paternalistic rather than partnership-based, and Pacific Island leaders have been vocal in their frustration that Australia’s climate policy undermines every other form of support it provides. Former Tuvalu Prime Minister Enele Sopoaga’s statement that Australia’s coal exports represented an existential threat to his nation was not diplomatic hyperbole. It was a factual assessment of the causal chain from Australian mines to Pacific Island submersion.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 4 — the lowest score on Australia’s Neighbour Test. A nation that presents itself as a Pacific partner while being the region’s largest contributor to climate change — the existential threat facing its Pacific Island neighbours — is not merely inconsistent. It is committing slow-motion harm against the nations it claims to support. When Tuvalu and Kiribati face submersion from rising seas, and Australia is the developed world’s largest coal exporter, the consistency gap is measured in meters of ocean. You cannot be a good neighbour to Pacific Islands while exporting the fuel that will drown them.

Long-term stewardship earns a 5. Australia invests in its own future — infrastructure, education, the sovereign wealth fund — but its contribution to global stewardship is fatally undermined by its fossil fuel exports. A nation that builds seawalls at home while exporting the coal that melts the ice that raises the seas that require the seawalls is not practising stewardship. It is practising denial with infrastructure characteristics.

The Editorial Conclusion

Australia scores 117 out of 200 — sixth on the underperformer list but, notably, the highest-scoring underperformer. Its human development is genuinely excellent. Its systemic behaviour is middling rather than catastrophic. Its Neighbour Test, while weak, reflects intermittent engagement rather than active hostility. Australia is on the underperformer list not because it is a bad country — it is, for most of its citizens, an extraordinarily good one — but because it is a country that has made one catastrophic choice and has not yet summoned the political will to reverse it.

The lucky country that chose coal. Australia had every advantage — wealth, sunshine, democratic institutions, educated citizenry, world-class research institutions, and more solar irradiance than any developed nation on Earth — to lead the world in clean energy. Instead, it exports coal. The Adani mine expands. The Great Barrier Reef bleaches. The Pacific Islands sink. And Australia writes another cheque for disaster relief while loading another ship with the commodity that caused the disaster.

The distance between what Australia is and what it could be is smaller than for most underperformers on this list, which makes the failure feel less like tragedy and more like stubbornness. The solutions are not mysterious. The technology exists. The sun is shining — 300 days a year in most of the country. The economics of renewable energy have shifted decisively in its favour. The Great Barrier Reef is begging for a reprieve.

The country simply needs to stop digging.
