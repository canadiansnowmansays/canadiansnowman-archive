In a Senate With So Many Black Hats, Sheldon Whitehouse Wears White

In a Senate full of enablers and cowards, one man keeps doing the work for the people

March 22, 2026

Francis Chung/POLITICO

Robert Mueller died on Friday, March 20, 2026. He was 81, and he had spent the last years of his life battling Parkinson’s disease, largely withdrawn from public life, his extraordinary service to his country long behind him.

Within hours, the President of the United States posted on Truth Social: “Robert Mueller just died. Good, I’m glad he’s dead. He can no longer hurt innocent people!”

Read that again. The sitting president of the United States — publicly, on his own platform, under his own name — celebrated the death of a decorated Marine, a Bronze Star recipient, a man who served his country across twelve years as FBI Director under presidents of both parties, confirmed unanimously by the United States Senate. A man whose reputation for integrity was so unimpeachable that even Newt Gingrich called his appointment as special counsel “superb.”

Donald Trump was glad he was dead.

In a normal political era, that statement alone would end a presidency. In this one, it was a Saturday. Which tells you everything you need to know about where we are.

Mueller’s death, and Trump’s response to it, is the right place to begin a portrait of Senator Sheldon Whitehouse of Rhode Island — because Whitehouse is, in many respects, Mueller’s institutional heir. Not in the sense of investigating Russian interference specifically, though he does that too. But in the deeper sense: he is a former prosecutor who does the actual legal homework, follows the documented evidence, names the mechanisms, and refuses to be distracted by the daily chaos that is the primary weapon of this administration.

In a Senate full of men and women who have chosen accommodation, performance, or silence, Whitehouse wears the white hat. And he actually earns it.

The Accountant

What distinguishes Whitehouse from most of his Democratic colleagues is not passion — the Senate has plenty of passionate Democrats — but precision. He thinks like a prosecutor because he was one, serving as both US Attorney for Rhode Island and the state’s Attorney General before entering the Senate. When he asks a question, it is rarely rhetorical. It is designed to establish a fact, expose a contradiction, or box a witness into a corner from which there is no honest exit.

Watch him with Attorney General Pam Bondi at a recent Judiciary Committee hearing. Whitehouse asks, simply, what became of the $50,000 in cash that the FBI delivered to border czar Tom Homan in a paper bag. Bondi deflects repeatedly, citing a review that found no wrongdoing. Whitehouse does not raise his voice. He simply notes that her answer addresses a different question than the one he asked. Did Homan keep the money? Did he declare it on his tax returns? Did the FBI get it back?

She cannot answer. She will not answer. And in not answering, she answers.

This is the Whitehouse method. He does not need theatrics. The facts do the work when you have done the preparation.

The Irony That Nobody Named

Sen. Sheldon Whitehouse, D-R.I., and former Florida attorney general Pam Bondi at her confirmation hearing. (Getty Images)

The most devastating moment in recent Senate history that received almost no coverage came in the aftermath of the Bondi hearing. Whitehouse noted — with the controlled fury of a man who has learned that fury must be controlled — the extraordinary irony of the timeline.

The day before the hearing, the MAGA Department of Justice arraigned former FBI Director James Comey for allegedly making untruthful statements to Congress.

The day of the hearing, Attorney General Pam Bondi sat in that same chamber and made statements that were, in Whitehouse’s precise framing, not matters of nuance or interpretation. They were checkable. She alleged that Whitehouse and ranking member Dick Durbin had received political contributions from someone she described as an Epstein close confidant. The Federal Election Commission maintains a public website. An intern at the Department of Justice could have verified in minutes that no such contributions existed.

She said it anyway.

Whitehouse put it plainly: “There is unimpeachable record showing that what she said sitting right there is not true.”

One man is arraigned for false statements. Another makes false statements in the same building the following day, to the same committee, and faces no consequence. Whitehouse named this contrast with the precision of a closing argument. Whether the committee — or the country — is paying attention is another matter.

The Perjury Architecture

But Bondi’s false allegations were not even the most structurally damaging thing she did that day. What she left behind was something worse: a coordinated cover-up that had collapsed under its own contradictions, and an FBI Director exposed on multiple demonstrably false statements with no one left to protect him.

The sequence deserves careful attention because it illustrates exactly how this administration handles inconvenient truth — not with a single lie, but with a layered architecture of claims that are individually deniable and collectively indefensible.

Sen. Whitehouse criticizes Patel nomination for FBI director / ABC News

FBI Director Kash Patel testified before the Judiciary Committee that he could not discuss his grand jury testimony in the Mar-a-Lago case — testimony he had given after initially pleading the Fifth and subsequently receiving immunity. His reason: the Department of Justice had sealed the testimony, and a court order prevented him from discussing it. He identified the specific judge: the Chief Judge of the DC Federal District Court.

That was a verifiable claim. And it was verified — just not in the direction Patel intended.

The Chief Judge of the DC Federal District Court subsequently stated publicly and unambiguously that nothing had prevented Director Patel from discussing his grand jury testimony. No court order existed. The seal Patel had invoked did not exist in the form he described.

Caught, Patel offered a second explanation: that he had worked with the Department of Justice to have his transcript publicly released, and therefore there was nothing left to discuss.

Also verifiable. Also false. The transcript had not been publicly released. It has not been publicly released. Whitehouse asked Bondi directly to clarify either claim — to confirm either that the Department of Justice had sealed the testimony, or that any transcript had been publicly released. She did neither. She left Patel fully exposed on both false statements, unwilling or unable to provide cover.

What we are left with is an FBI Director who lied to a Senate committee about the reasons he could not discuss his own immunized grand jury testimony, and an Attorney General who lied to the same committee in a separate matter on the same day, and an administration that arraigns other people for lying to that committee.

Whitehouse did not need to editorialize. He laid the timeline out in sequence and let the contradiction speak. That is what a prosecutor does when the evidence is sufficient.

The Courthouse and the Judge

Beyond the grand theatre of Senate hearings, Whitehouse has been equally methodical about documenting the street-level behaviour of the administration’s enforcement apparatus.

He has described, multiple times and in multiple forums, an incident at the Rhode Island Superior Court that deserves to be better known. ICE agents entered the courthouse in plainclothes and masks, violating an existing agreement with the court not to conduct operations there. They photographed individuals in violation of a court rule. They pursued a target they had confused with a high school intern. They followed a state court judge to his car — parked in a judges-only space — and threatened, in language Whitehouse quoted with characteristic precision, to smash his windows and drag him from the vehicle.

They held the high school intern for half an hour before determining, sheepishly, that a teenager was not the thirty-year-old man they were seeking.

No consequences followed.

Whitehouse raised this directly with then-Secretary Kristi Noem at a Senate hearing. He asked her to instruct her officers to deconflict with local law enforcement — a basic, elementary practice that every law enforcement agency in the country follows as a matter of officer safety and operational competence. Noem’s response revealed that she did not appear to understand the difference between deconfliction and cooperation.

She has since been fired. The behaviour she presided over has not changed.

Whitehouse has also documented the broader legal reality in his state: in Rhode Island, and throughout the First Circuit, it is binding federal law that state and local officials who hold someone under an ICE detainer without a proper warrant can be held civilly liable. This is not a policy preference. It is a court ruling, upheld on appeal. The administration’s demands that local police cooperate with warrantless ICE detentions are, in Whitehouse’s circuit, demands that local officials expose themselves to legal liability. He states this not as a political argument but as a legal fact, which is considerably harder to dismiss.

The Pattern and the Plan

What Whitehouse understands — and what makes him genuinely dangerous to the people he is investigating — is that he does not treat these incidents as isolated outrages. He connects them into architecture.

The Save America Act — which he has argued should be called the Steal the Next Election Act — is not, in his telling, a voter integrity measure. It is a structural intervention designed to guarantee Republican control of Congress for the next fifty years. He does not need to make this argument himself. He quotes the president making it: “Republicans have to win this one... we’ll never lose a race for 50 years.” Trump, as Whitehouse notes with the dry appreciation of a prosecutor whose witness has just confessed on the stand, says the quiet part at maximum volume.

The Fulton County warrant is not a criminal investigation. It is a document seizure using a fake criminal predicate, executed so sloppily that any eventual defendant would have a field day — which is itself evidence that no defendant was ever anticipated. The Missouri US Attorney who obtained the warrant met multiple times with election deniers before filing. The Atlanta FBI field office chief was removed days before the search. The DNI and a senior FBI official attended the search, making themselves witnesses in a case they apparently never expected to go to trial. The warrant affidavit contained what Whitehouse describes as an “escape hatch” — language suggesting there might not even be a case — of a kind he had never seen in any warrant affidavit in his prosecutorial career.

Incompetence? Possibly. Or, as Whitehouse frames it, deliberate rule-breaking by people who knew there would never be a defendant to call them out. All they wanted was the documents.

The Corruption of Your Electric Bill

Perhaps nowhere is Whitehouse’s prosecutorial architecture more fully developed than in his documentation of the administration’s energy policy — a subject that might seem technical until you understand that it is, in his telling, simply corruption with a utility bill attached.

The logic is straightforward, and Whitehouse has stated it with the repetition of a man who knows juries need to hear things more than once. The electrical grid draws first from the cheapest available power sources and works up the generation stack as demand requires. Clean energy — wind, solar — is now cheaper than fossil fuel generation. When a large clean energy project comes online, it displaces a fossil fuel plant at the top of that stack. The fossil fuel plant does not run. Its owners do not get paid. Its fuel dealers do not get paid.

Those owners and dealers gave Donald Trump hundreds of millions of dollars.

So the Trump administration has, with remarkable consistency, done everything in its power to keep cheap clean energy off the grid. The offshore wind projects off Rhode Island — Revolution Wind and Vineyard Wind — were hit with stop-work orders that courts threw out as illegal. Revolution Wind is contracted to deliver electricity at nine cents per kilowatt hour into a grid that averages eighteen cents. Vineyard Wind delivers at just over seven cents. The math is not complicated. Every time those projects are delayed, consumers pay the difference, and that difference flows to Trump’s fossil fuel donors.

Whitehouse has entered the contracted prices into the Senate record. He has cited the court rulings. He has documented that the Department of Energy declared an emergency and ordered old fossil plants to run, adding between three and six billion dollars in higher costs to consumers between now and 2028 — one plant alone costing consumers an extra $600,000 per day. Power bills are up thirteen percent since January 2025, almost three times faster than inflation.

The administration claims clean energy is too expensive. Whitehouse notes, with the precision that is his signature, that they know this claim is false — because when they were taken to court over the offshore wind stop-work orders, they filed affidavits asserting hundreds of millions of dollars in consumer savings. They did not dare deny the economics in court. They simply continued to deny them everywhere else.

Behind the immediate corruption is a longer cascade that Whitehouse documented during his time as chairman of the Budget Committee. Rising fossil fuel consumption means rising climate risk. Rising climate risk means rising homeowners insurance premiums — already at $14,000 annually for a standard policy in Florida, projected to double, triple, or quadruple. Insurance companies are issuing non-renewal notices to customers they have covered for decades. Properties that cannot be insured cannot be mortgaged. Properties that cannot be mortgaged cannot be sold at any price. Home values collapse. The mortgage market follows. The economy follows.

The warnings, Whitehouse notes, come not from environmental groups but from the former head of the Bank of England, the International Financial Stability Board, and the former chief economist of Freddie Mac. The administration’s fossil fuel donors are aware of this cascade. They simply prefer that it happen after they have extracted maximum value from the current arrangement.

The master must be served, as Whitehouse puts it. You pay. They profit. This is not an accident. It is the plan.

Mueller and What Comes After

TIME Magazine cover, July 3, 2017. "The Lie Detector" by David Von Drehle. Photograph by Marco Grob for TIME

Robert Mueller did his job. He documented substantial contacts between the Trump campaign and Russia. He laid out the obstruction in 448 pages. He declined to exonerate. And then Bill Barr summarized four pages of that report into a political weapon, and the moment passed, and the work was buried.

Mueller was constrained — by department policy, by institutional deference, by a belief that the report would speak for itself. It did not speak loudly enough. By the time the full document was released, the narrative had hardened. Trump declared no collusion in all caps, called it a hoax — his reliable tell, as Whitehouse has noted, for something that is demonstrably true — and the press, having already digested Barr’s four-page version, largely moved on.

What Mueller could not do from inside the Justice Department, Whitehouse does from the Senate floor. He is not constrained by department policy or prosecutorial norms or the fear of seeming partisan. He is a senator. He has a floor. He has a microphone. He has the documented record. And he has the prosecutorial instinct to know that you do not wait for the smoking gun — you build the case from what is in front of you, systematically, piece by piece, until the picture is undeniable.

Trump celebrated Mueller’s death because Mueller had tried to hold him accountable and, in Trump’s assessment, had been neutralised. What Trump does not appear to have calculated is that accountability does not die with any one person. It transfers.

In a Senate chamber where accommodation has become the norm, where the working assumption of most members is that survival requires silence, Sheldon Whitehouse keeps standing up and doing the work. He connects the dots. He names the mechanisms. He follows the money. He states the contradictions on the record and lets them sit there, unresolved, for history to examine.

He wears a white hat. In this Senate, that is a genuinely radical act.
