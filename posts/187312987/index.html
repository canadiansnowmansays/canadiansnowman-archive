34 Felony Convictions

Part 20: First President Ever Convicted of Crimes (2016-2025)

February 12, 2026

May 30, 2024. A Manhattan jury is about to convict Donald Trump 34 times. He will become the first president in American history to be a convicted felon. He faces up to four years in prison on each count. He will receive nothing—no jail time, no probation, no fines. Six months later, he will be president again. This is what accountability looks like in America

On May 30, 2024, after deliberating for fewer than 10 hours over two days, a New York jury delivered an unprecedented verdict: former President Donald Trump was guilty. Not on one count. Not on a few counts. On all 34 felony counts of falsifying business records.

The courtroom was silent as the jury foreman repeated “guilty” 34 times. Trump’s demeanor didn’t change during the reading, though his son Eric looked angry. When it was over, Trump had become the first former, current, or future president in American history to be convicted of criminal charges.

Each count carried a potential sentence of up to four years in prison. Trump faced a theoretical maximum of 136 years behind bars, though concurrent sentencing would be likely.

Outside the courtroom, Trump immediately called it “a rigged, disgraceful trial” and declared, “I’m a very innocent man.” He vowed to appeal.

President Joe Biden responded: “The American principle that no one is above the law was reaffirmed. Donald Trump was given every opportunity to defend himself... It’s reckless, it’s dangerous, it’s irresponsible for anyone to say this was rigged just because they don’t like the verdict.”

The conviction should have been a watershed moment. Instead, as we’ll see in Part 20, it became another demonstration that for Donald Trump, accountability is always negotiable.

The Crime: A Scheme to Steal an Election

The 34 felony counts all stemmed from the same criminal scheme: falsifying business records to conceal a $130,000 hush money payment to adult film actress Stormy Daniels in the final days of the 2016 presidential campaign.

The story begins in October 2016. The Access Hollywood tape—with Trump bragging about grabbing women’s genitals—had just leaked and threatened to sink his presidential campaign. Trump’s longtime attorney and self-described “fixer” Michael Cohen approached Stormy Daniels, who was preparing to go public with her account of a sexual encounter with Trump in 2006, months after Melania Trump gave birth to their son Barron.

Cohen set up a shell company, Essential Consultants LLC, and paid Daniels $130,000 on October 27, 2016—just 11 days before the election. Daniels signed a nondisclosure agreement. The story stayed buried. Trump won the presidency.

After the election, Trump reimbursed Cohen. The payments totaled $420,000 (including costs related to the transaction). But here’s where the crime occurred: those payments were falsified in Trump Organization business records.

They were labeled as “legal expenses” and recorded as payments for a “retainer agreement” with Cohen. But prosecutors proved there was no retainer agreement, Cohen did little if any actual legal work for Trump during that period, and the payments were actually reimbursements for the hush money.

Each of the 34 felony counts corresponded to a specific business document, dated between February 14 and December 5, 2017:

  • 11 invoices from Cohen

  • 12 entries in Trump Organization ledgers

  • 11 checks or check stubs (some signed by Trump himself while he was president)

Under New York law, falsifying business records is a misdemeanor unless it’s done “with intent to commit another crime or to aid or conceal the commission thereof.” Manhattan District Attorney Alvin Bragg argued the falsified records concealed multiple other crimes:

  • Violating federal campaign finance law (the payment exceeded contribution limits)

  • Tax fraud (the reimbursement was falsely characterized as income)

  • Election fraud (conspiring to suppress information from voters)

This wasn’t about sex or an affair between consenting adults. This was about election interference. By concealing the Daniels story just days before the 2016 election, Trump denied voters information that could have changed the outcome. Then he covered up the illegal payment with 34 counts of business record fraud.

Michael Cohen paid Stormy Daniels $130,000 in hush money on Trump's orders, then went to prison for it. Stormy Daniels took the stand and testified under oath about what happened. Donald Trump was convicted on all 34 felony counts for the scheme. Cohen did three years. Daniels faced death threats. Trump faced nothing

The Indictment

The Manhattan grand jury voted to indict Trump on March 30, 2023, making him the first former U.S. president to face criminal charges. The indictment was unsealed on April 4.

On April 4, 2023, Trump traveled from Florida to New York and surrendered to the Manhattan DA’s office. He was fingerprinted, processed, and arraigned. He pleaded not guilty to all 34 counts.

Trump immediately returned to Mar-a-Lago and gave a speech to supporters, calling it a “political witch hunt” and saying “We are a nation in decline, and now these radical left lunatics want to interfere in elections by using law enforcement.”

DA Bragg said Trump had engaged in a “scheme” to boost his election chances through hush money payments, then “repeatedly and fraudulently falsified New York business records” to conceal that criminal conduct.

The Trial: Six Weeks of Evidence

The trial began on April 15, 2024, and lasted six weeks. Judge Juan Merchan presided. Trump attended almost every day, often appearing to fall asleep during testimony, sometimes violating the gag order with public attacks on witnesses and court staff.

On April 30, Trump became the first U.S. president to be held in criminal contempt of court, fined $9,000 for 9 violations of the gag order. Judge Merchan warned Trump directly: “The last thing I want to do is to put you in jail. You are the former president of the United States and possibly the next president as well. But at the end of the day I have a job to do. If you continue to violate my orders, I will have no choice but to impose an incarceratory punishment.”

The prosecution called 20 witnesses:

Stormy Daniels: She testified about her alleged sexual encounter with Trump in 2006, offering graphic details. Trump’s lawyers tried to use her testimony to argue she was lying, but her credibility held up under cross-examination.

David Pecker: The former National Enquirer publisher described a “catch and kill” operation he orchestrated with Trump and Cohen during the 2016 campaign. Pecker’s company bought the rights to damaging stories about Trump—including from Playboy model Karen McDougal, who alleged an affair—and buried them to help Trump’s campaign.

Hope Hicks: Trump’s former White House communications director testified that Trump was concerned about how the Daniels story would affect his wife Melania and his campaign. She recalled Trump asking his team to hide the Wall Street Journal article about the payment from Melania and wanting to ensure newspapers weren’t delivered to their residence. Prosecutors later described her testimony as “devastating” for Trump.

Michael Cohen: The most pivotal witness. Trump’s former fixer testified for days, giving jurors an insider’s view of the scheme. Cohen said:

  • Trump directed him to pay Daniels

  • Trump approved the reimbursement plan

  • Trump knew the payments were being falsified as legal expenses

  • The purpose was to influence the 2016 election

Defense attorneys spent days trying to destroy Cohen’s credibility, noting his past lies and his own conviction for crimes including lying to Congress. But enough of his testimony was corroborated by documents and other witnesses that the jury believed him.

The evidence was overwhelming:

  • Bank records showing Cohen’s payment to Daniels

  • Business records showing the falsified “legal expenses”

  • Checks signed by Trump himself

  • Testimony from multiple witnesses corroborating the scheme

  • Audio recordings Cohen had secretly made

  • Cohen’s own guilty plea to federal charges arising from the same conduct

The Defense

Trump’s defense was straightforward: he was innocent, Cohen was an unreliable liar acting on his own, and the retainer agreement was legitimate.

The defense called only two witnesses and rested quickly. Trump himself never testified, despite saying repeatedly he would.

Throughout the trial, Trump’s lawyers made unsuccessful attempts to:

  • Delay or dismiss the case

  • Have Judge Merchan recuse himself

  • Remove the case to federal court

  • Exclude certain evidence

All were denied.

The Deliberations

After closing arguments on May 28-29, 2024, the jury began deliberations. They had to decide whether prosecutors proved beyond a reasonable doubt that:

  • Trump falsified the business records

  • He did so with intent to commit or conceal another crime

  • He did this personally or through others acting at his direction

After fewer than 10 hours of deliberation over two days, they reached their verdict.

The Verdict: Guilty on All Counts

On May 30, 2024, at about 5:05 p.m., the jury announced its decision. The foreman said “guilty” 34 times—once for each count.

Trump, 77 years old, stood expressionless as the verdicts were read. He was now:

  • The first former president convicted of any crime

  • The first sitting or former president to be a convicted felon

  • The presumptive Republican presidential nominee running as a felon

  • Facing up to four years in prison on each count

Judge Merchan scheduled sentencing for July 11, just four days before the Republican National Convention.

The Immediate Aftermath

Trump’s campaign immediately began fundraising off the conviction, casting him as a victim of political persecution. Many Republicans rallied to his defense.

E. Jean Carroll, who had won her own verdicts against Trump, posted simply: “Justice!!”

Trump told reporters: “This was a rigged, disgraceful trial. The real verdict is going to be Nov. 5th by the people.” He was already reframing his criminal conviction as just another political battle to win at the ballot box.

Legal experts weighed in on what came next. Stanford criminal law professor Robert Weisberg noted that while each count carried up to four years, concurrent sentencing meant they’d be bunched together. The 34 counts represented “34 acts of doing pretty much the same thing in 34 different financial entries.”

Whether Trump would face prison time, Weisberg said, was entirely up to Judge Merchan. “It can be just fines or probation or prison for up to 4 years.”

But there was a bigger question looming: Would Trump actually be sentenced at all? And if so, would he face any real consequences?

Political Reality

The conviction had virtually no impact on Trump’s political standing. Within 24 hours of the verdict, his campaign raised over $50 million—turning a criminal conviction into a fundraising bonanza. Republican voters viewed it as proof of “deep state persecution,” not accountability. Trump’s polling numbers remained steady or even improved.

The message was clear: for Trump’s base, even 34 felony convictions were just further evidence of a rigged system, not disqualifying behavior.

The Legal Significance

Despite Trump’s claims of a “rigged” trial, legal observers noted it was conducted fairly:

Weisberg said: “Despite all the noise, there wasn’t anything demonstrably unfair. The jury was selected in a pretty conventional way, and the judge’s rulings were well within the conventional bounds of judicial decision making.”

The jury was unanimous. The evidence was overwhelming. Trump had every opportunity to defend himself and present his case. He chose not to testify.

This was a legitimate conviction in a legitimate trial resulting in a legitimate guilty verdict on 34 felony counts.

What the Conviction Means

Trump’s conviction should have ended his political career. A candidate convicted of 34 felonies for conspiring to defraud voters should be disqualified from holding office—if not legally, then certainly morally and politically.

But this is Trump we’re talking about. The conviction became just another controversy to spin, another grievance to exploit, another way to claim victimhood and persecution.

Trump continued campaigning. His supporters rallied. The Republican Party stood by him. And he went on to win the 2024 election despite being a convicted felon.

The conviction was real. The evidence was real. The guilty verdict was real. But in Trump’s America, even 34 felony convictions don’t necessarily lead to accountability.

Part 20 examines the sentencing—where Judge Merchan gave Trump an “unconditional discharge” with no jail, no probation, no fines, and no restrictions. The judge explicitly stated this special treatment was only because Trump had won the election and was about to become president. Even 34 felony convictions resulted in zero punishment.
