America's 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 2: “Trump’s ‘Concepts of a Plan’ — Nine Years and Counting

December 8, 2025

On September 10, 2024, during his presidential debate with Kamala Harris, Donald Trump was asked a straightforward question: Nine years after first promising to repeal and replace Obamacare, did he have a healthcare plan?

Trump’s answer: “I have concepts of a plan” ABC News.

Concepts. Of. A. Plan.

After nine years. After controlling the White House, Senate, and House of Representatives in 2017-2018. After countless promises that a “beautiful” healthcare plan was just weeks away. After declaring that replacing Obamacare would be “so easy.”

Concepts.

This isn’t just a gaffe. It’s the perfect encapsulation of Republican healthcare policy for the past decade: big promises, zero delivery, and patients left holding the bill.

The Promise Machine (2015-2016)

Trump made healthcare reform a centerpiece of his 2016 campaign. In November 2016, he promised: “When we win on November 8th and elect a Republican Congress, we will be able to immediately repeal and replace Obamacare. Have to do it. I will ask Congress to convene a special session so we can repeal and replace” Ballotpedia.

The word “immediately” appeared constantly. Trump’s campaign website stated clearly: “On day one of the Trump Administration, we will ask Congress to immediately deliver a full repeal of Obamacare” ABC News.

He promised it would be simple. In March 2016, Trump released a seven-point plan claiming he would repeal Obamacare, reduce barriers to interstate sale of health insurance, institute full tax deduction for insurance premium payments, make Health Savings Accounts inheritable, require price transparency, block-grant Medicaid to states, and allow more overseas drug providers Ballotpedia.

Notice what’s missing? Any mechanism to control the costs that had already exploded 2100% since 1960. Any guarantee of universal coverage. Any plan for the millions with pre-existing conditions who would lose protection.

But Trump assured voters it would all work out beautifully. At rally after rally, he promised Americans new healthcare that would cost less but be far better. During the 2016 campaign, Trump pledged to repeal Obamacare and replace it with something “terrific” CNN.

Reality Hits: “Nobody Knew Healthcare Could Be So Complicated” (2017)

Once in office, things got complicated fast.

By March 2017, just 64 days into his presidency, Trump was already backtracking: “And I never said — I guess I’m here, what, 64 days? I never said repeal and replace Obamacare. You’ve all heard my speeches. I never said repeal it and replace it within 64 days” KFF Health News.

Except he had. Repeatedly. In writing. On video. “Immediately” was his word.

Then came the admission that revealed everything. In late February 2017, Trump said: “Nobody knew that health care could be so complicated” PubMed Central.

Actually, everybody knew. Healthcare policy experts knew. Doctors knew. Insurers knew. Members of Congress who had spent years on healthcare reform knew. The only person who apparently didn’t know was the man who had been promising for two years that fixing it would be “so easy.”

Meanwhile, House Republicans introduced the American Health Care Act (AHCA). Some conservative Republicans derided it as “Obamacare Lite” and refused to vote on it KFF Health News. The bill failed even to get a vote.

A Congressional Budget Office report estimated the bill would cut $880 billion from Medicaid over a decade and would eventually cost 53 million Americans their health insurance, including 14 million in the first year. One national poll found that only 17% of Americans supported Trump’s bill, with 56% in opposition PubMed Central.

Trump’s response? He tweeted on March 9: “Despite what you hear in the press, healthcare is coming along great. We are talking to many groups and it will end in a beautiful picture!” KFF Health News

It would not be a beautiful picture.

The McCain Moment: July 28, 2017

After the House finally passed a version of the AHCA, the Senate began work on its own repeal bill. Republicans tried multiple approaches: the Better Care Reconciliation Act, then a straight repeal, and finally a “skinny repeal” that would eliminate just the individual and employer mandates.

The vote came in the early hours of July 28, 2017. Senator John McCain, who had just returned to Washington after being diagnosed with brain cancer, walked onto the Senate floor NPR. Republicans needed 50 votes (with Vice President Pence breaking the tie). They had 49 guaranteed yes votes. McCain’s vote would be decisive.

McCain approached the Senate clerk at 1:29 a.m. and gave a thumbs down — the third “no” vote. Several people gasped. Others applauded The Washington Post.

The seven-year Republican promise to repeal Obamacare was dead.

Why did McCain vote no? After Republicans spent months bickering amongst themselves, McCain was disappointed in the option presented to senators hours before their vote: hobble the ACA and trust that a handful of lawmakers would be able to craft an alternative behind closed doors, despite failing to accomplish that very thing after years of trying. What bothered McCain more was his party’s strategy to pass their so-called skinny repeal measure, skipping committee consideration and delivering it straight to the floor NBC News.

McCain believed in process. In hearings. In bipartisan negotiation. His Republican colleagues wanted to ram through a bill that would strip insurance from millions, without public debate, in the middle of the night.

When asked why he voted no, McCain answered simply: “Because it was the right vote” CNN.

The Broken Promises Continue (2017-2020)

The McCain vote didn’t end Trump’s promises. It just made them vaguer.

In March 2019, Trump tweeted: “The Republican Party will become ‘The Party of Healthcare!’” Two days later, he told reporters “we’re working on a plan now,” but backtracked on the timeline, saying “There’s no very great rush” KFF Health News.

By April 2019, Trump tweeted: “I was never planning a vote prior to the 2020 Election on the wonderful HealthCare package that some very talented people are now developing for me & the Republican Party” KFF Health News.

Notice the evolution: From “immediately” in 2016, to “within 100 days” in early 2017, to “no rush” in 2019, to “not before the 2020 election.”

The plan was always coming. It was always going to be “beautiful” and “tremendous.” It just never actually arrived.

Meanwhile, Trump’s administration took steps to undermine the ACA without repealing it. In 2017, the Trump administration shortened the enrollment period and shut down the federal healthcare exchange for 12 hours nearly every Sunday. In 2018, the Trump administration temporarily suspended risk adjustment payments to insurers — money that’s used to fund insurers with sicker, higher-cost patients CBS News.

In December 2020, during the coronavirus pandemic, the Trump administration asked the Supreme Court to overturn Obamacare. The filing came on the same day the government reported that close to half a million people who lost their health insurance amid the economic shutdown signed up for coverage through HealthCare.gov CNN.

In the middle of a pandemic, Trump was trying to eliminate health insurance for millions.

The 2024 Return: Still No Plan

Fast forward to the 2024 campaign. In November 2023, Trump posted on Truth Social that he was “seriously looking at alternatives” to the ACA Al Jazeera.

Then came the September 2024 debate. Moderator Linsey Davis asked Trump directly: Nine years after he first started running for president on a promise to repeal and replace Obamacare, did he have a plan?

“Obamacare was lousy health care. Always was,” Trump said. “It’s not very good today and, what I said, that if we come up with something, we are working on things, we’re going to do it and we’re going to replace it” ABC News.

When pressed for a yes-or-no answer on whether he had a plan, Trump gave his now-infamous response: “I have concepts of a plan” ABC News.

He added that Americans would hear more “in the not-too-distant future” AJMC.

The “not-too-distant future” has been coming for nine years.

The Second Term: Same Empty Promises (2025)

Now in his second term, Trump has offered no new healthcare plan. Trump and Republicans now own the issue of healthcare, with millions of citizens afflicted by rising premiums and high deductibles amid a wider cost-of-living crisis. Trump lacks a comprehensive plan to bring relief to citizens who lack healthcare or can’t afford their plans CNN.

Instead, what we have are senators like Josh Hawley proposing tax relief measures that, while helpful, don’t address the root cost problem. We have debates over ACA subsidies expiring. We have continued Republican opposition to any government role in price controls, even as costs continue their inexorable rise.

The Pattern Is Clear

Let’s document Trump’s timeline of broken promises:

2015: “Someone has to repeal and replace Obamacare”

2016: “Immediately repeal and replace” / “It’s going to be so easy” / “On day one”

February 2017: “Nobody knew healthcare could be so complicated”

March 2017: “I never said repeal it within 64 days”

July 2017: McCain kills repeal with thumbs-down vote

March 2019: “The Republican Party will become ‘The Party of Healthcare!’” / “We’re working on a plan now”

April 2019: “I was never planning a vote prior to the 2020 Election”

September 2024: “I have concepts of a plan”

2025: Still no plan

Why This Matters

This isn’t just political theater. Real people suffer while Republicans play games with their healthcare.

The Congressional Budget Office estimated Trump’s 2017 proposal would have cost 53 million Americans their health insurance, including 14 million in the first year PubMed Central. These weren’t abstract numbers—these were Americans with cancer, diabetes, heart disease, who would have lost coverage.

McCain understood this. In his 2018 memoirs, McCain wrote: “I was thanked for my vote by Democratic friends more profusely than I should have been for helping save Obamacare. That had not been my goal” PBS. His goal was proper process, real debate, actual solutions—not midnight votes on bills no one had read.

Trump’s approach was the opposite: big promises, no details, and when pressed for specifics, attack the questioner. One political aide told CNN that Trump either “doesn’t know, doesn’t care, or both” PubMed Central about the complexities of healthcare.

The Cost of Empty Promises

While Trump spent nine years promising a plan that never came, what happened to American healthcare?

Healthcare spending continued its rise. Costs that had already increased 2100% since 1960 kept climbing. Healthcare spending reached $4.9 trillion in 2023, or $14,570 per person, accounting for 17.6% of GDP CMS.

Families continued to face medical bankruptcy. Workers continued to ration medications. Rural hospitals continued to close.

And Republicans continued to promise that a solution was just around the corner.

The 2025 Reality

Trump and Republicans argue that their “One Big Beautiful Bill” had already made significant steps to make healthcare more affordable by loosening the power of insurance companies and restoring choice and control CNN. But where are the results? Where is the promised reduction in costs? Where is the “beautiful” healthcare that Trump promised would be “so easy”?

Senator Hawley’s tax relief proposal is more than Trump ever delivered—at least it’s a concrete bill, not just concepts. But as we established in Part 1, tax relief doesn’t solve the fundamental problem of why costs exploded in the first place.

The Accountability Moment

Nine years. Three presidential campaigns. Four years in office with full Republican control of government. Countless promises that a plan was just weeks away.

And America has: Concepts.

This is the Republican healthcare record. Not what they promise. Not what they claim. What they deliver.

When McCain cast his thumbs-down vote in 2017, he gave Republicans a gift: a chance to admit they didn’t have a better plan, and to work across the aisle to improve the system that existed. The members of his party could have realized that the ACA, while imperfect, was still better than anything they could devise, and worked with members on both sides of the aisle to improve the law and make it work better for Americans of all stripes MinnPost.

They didn’t take that gift. Instead, they spent seven more years promising plans that never came, undermining the ACA where they could, and offering nothing substantive to address the crisis of costs.

Trump’s “concepts of a plan” perfectly encapsulates Republican healthcare policy: lots of concepts, zero plans, and American families paying the price.

What Comes Next

In the next installment, we’ll move from political failure to economic reality. Where exactly does that $14,885 per capita go? Why does America spend double what other countries do?

Part 3 will break down the specific cost drivers with hard data:

Administrative Waste: Americans spend $925 per capita on healthcare administration versus $245 in comparable countries—a $680 per capita difference. That’s not just insurance company overhead; it’s the complexity tax of having dozens of payers, each with different billing codes, prior authorization requirements, and reimbursement rates.

Drug Prices: Pharmaceutical spending in the U.S. is $1,443 per capita compared to $466-$939 in other countries. Why? Because America is the only developed nation that doesn’t negotiate drug prices nationally.

Hospital Charges: Unlike every other developed country, American hospitals set their own prices. The same MRI that costs $280 in France costs $1,080 in the U.S. The same hip replacement that costs $7,731 in Spain costs $29,067 in America.

Physician Compensation: American generalist physicians earn $218,173 on average, compared to $86,607-$154,126 in comparable countries. Specialists earn even more. But here’s the surprise: physician salaries aren’t the main driver of excess costs.

The Middleman Tax: Insurance companies, pharmacy benefit managers, and billing specialists add layers of cost that don’t exist in streamlined single-payer or regulated multi-payer systems.

We’ll examine each component with international comparisons, showing exactly where American healthcare diverges from every other developed nation—and why.

Because while Trump offers concepts and Republicans offer tax breaks, the data tells a very specific story about why a routine hospital visit can bankrupt an American family while barely denting a Canadian’s wallet.
