The Great Canadian Trade War

Part II - How a Free Trade Deal Saved Canada from Economic Disaster

A follow-up analysis: When 5% of trade drives 100% of the headlines

August 1, 2025

Stephen Lawton
August 2025

The Plot Twist Nobody Saw Coming

Several months ago, I started writing about Trump's escalating trade war with Canada, analyzing how 43 pounds of fentanyl (0.2% of US seizures) somehow justified tariffs on a $762 billion trade relationship. The numbers didn't add up then, and they're even more bizarre now.

But here's the twist that changes everything: 94-95% of Canadian exports can avoid Trump's tariffs entirely.

Thanks to key provisions in the USMCA trade agreement—the very deal Trump negotiated during his first presidency—the vast majority of Canadian goods qualify for continued tariff-free treatment, even under his threatened 35% rate. This revelation transforms the entire analysis from "economic catastrophe" to "very expensive political theater affecting a tiny slice of trade."

Let me explain what this actually means, because most people haven't heard this crucial detail that completely changes the trade war narrative.

The USMCA Lifeline: When Trump's Own Deal Saves Canada

Let's start with the stunning reality that most news coverage has missed: after all the escalation, threats, and "trade war" rhetoric, only about 5-6% of Canadian exports to the US actually face meaningful tariffs.

How the Real Numbers Work

This isn't just trade jargon—it's the key to understanding why this entire "trade war" is more theater than substance. But the math is more complex than initially reported, so let me break it down precisely:

Trump is using two completely different tariff systems that hit different Canadian exports:

System 1: General "Trade War" Tariffs (25%/35% under IEEPA) When Trump negotiated the USMCA during his first presidency, the deal included "rules of origin" requirements. These rules allow goods to trade tariff-free as long as 60-75% of their content comes from North America.

RBC economists found that only 2.3% of Canadian exports genuinely cannot qualify for USMCA exemption from these general tariffs. Here's how they calculated this: RBC originally estimated that 4.4% of Canadian exports weren't covered by CUSMA based on the 2020 USMCA tariff schedule. However, after accounting for updated U.S. tariff classifications and renumbered product codes from 2022, they revised this down to 2.3%. For example, about 2.1% of the original "non-compliant" share consisted of aerospace products that were simply reclassified under different harmonized system codes. So 97.7% of Canadian trade can avoid Trump's headline "35% tariffs" by proving North American content.

System 2: Sector-Specific Tariffs (applied regardless of USMCA status) These hit specific industries with no USMCA exemption possible:

  • Steel and aluminum: $24 billion in Canadian exports face 50% tariffs (about 3% of total exports)

  • Copper: 50% tariffs starting August 1

  • Auto parts with non-US content: 25% tariffs

The Combined Reality

Total Canadian exports genuinely vulnerable to US tariffs:

  • 2.3% from general tariffs (can't get USMCA exemption)

  • ~3% from steel/aluminum (no exemption available)

  • Additional from copper and auto-related measures

Conservative estimate: 5-6% of total Canadian exports actually face tariffs

What This Means in Plain English

When Trump threatens massive tariffs on Canada, what's actually happening is:

  • US tariffs effectively hit: 5-6% of Canadian exports ($20-25 billion)

  • Canadian tariffs hit: ~$38 billion in US goods with no exemptions

  • Canada is retaliating roughly 2:1 harder than they're being hit

It's like Canada brought a sledgehammer to what turned out to be a much smaller fight than the headlines suggest.

  • Steel and aluminum: Hit with separate 50% tariffs under "national security" provisions

  • Softwood lumber: Subject to longstanding anti-dumping duties plus potential new tariffs

  • Products with high non-North American content: Goods that are essentially made elsewhere but shipped through Canada

  • Uncertified goods: Products that could qualify for USMCA treatment but lack proper documentation

The result? Canada's largest export categories—energy, minerals, agriculture, and most manufactured goods—remain largely protected. Even 90% of auto exports already qualified for USMCA treatment before the trade war began.

The Retaliation Math Problem: When 5% Meets $38 Billion

This is where things get mathematically complex, and where I need more than my slide rule to figure out the implications.

Canada imposed retaliatory tariffs on approximately $38 billion worth of US goods—everything from bourbon to machinery to agricultural products. These tariffs hit 100% of the targeted US exports to Canada, with no exemptions or workarounds.

Meanwhile, Trump's tariffs effectively target 5-6% of Canadian exports to the US.

The asymmetry is significant:

  • US tariffs: Various rates on 5-6% of Canadian exports

  • Canadian tariffs: 25% on targeted US goods ≈ roughly 2:1 retaliation ratio

Canada brought a sledgehammer to what turned out to be a bigger fight than initially calculated, but still much smaller than the rhetoric suggests. Their retaliation hits real US industries with real costs, while the majority of Canadian exporters can still navigate around most US tariffs through USMCA compliance and sector-specific strategies.

The Bourbon vs. Lumber Asymmetry

Consider the specific targeting: Canada slapped 25% tariffs on Kentucky bourbon, a product with no Canadian alternative and strong political symbolism (Mitch McConnell's home state). Every bottle of bourbon now costs 25% more in Canada, with no workaround.

Meanwhile, Canadian lumber—one of the few products actually hit by US tariffs—can often qualify for USMCA treatment if properly documented, or faces only sector-specific duties that existed long before this trade war.

The political calculation was clear: cause pain in Republican strongholds to pressure Trump to back down. This strategy forced US automakers to secure tariff exemptions, but the broader retaliation risked escalating tensions beyond these tactical gains.

The Strategic Miscalculation: Why Canada's Retaliation Backfired

My original analysis suggested Canada's strategic retaliation was designed to create political pressure within the US to end the trade war. Instead, it provided Trump with exactly the justification he needed to escalate.

Trump's letter announcing 35% tariffs explicitly cited Canada's retaliation: "Instead of working with the United States, Canada retaliated with its own Tariffs." Canada walked directly into the escalation trap.

But here's the deeper strategic error: Canada retaliated as if 100% of their exports were at risk, when only 5-6% actually were.

Had Canadian negotiators understood the USMCA exemption's scope, they might have pursued a completely different strategy:

  1. Aggressive USMCA compliance: Rush to certify as many exports as possible under existing rules

  2. Targeted, proportional retaliation: Match the actual economic impact (5-6%) rather than the rhetorical threat

  3. Public education: Highlight the disconnect between Trump's rhetoric and the USMCA reality

Instead, Canada treated a 5-6% problem like an existential threat, escalating in ways that actually made their situation worse.

The Palestine Pivot: When Trade Wars Become Foreign Policy Cudgels

Just as I predicted Trump would move the goalposts, he's done exactly that—but in ways even more revealing than expected.

In July 2025, Trump declared that Canada's recognition of Palestinian statehood would "make it very hard for us to make a Trade Deal with them." Palestine recognition joined fentanyl, digital taxes, and border security as pretexts for Trump's trade grievances.

The sequence is telling:

  • March: Tariffs justified by fentanyl (0.2% problem)

  • June: Trade talks terminated over digital services tax (different issue entirely)

  • July: Palestine recognition becomes a trade deal obstacle (completely unrelated issue)

This validates my original thesis about personality-driven policy. Trump treats trade agreements like personal loyalty tests. Disagree with him on Palestine? That affects lumber tariffs. Impose a tech tax? That affects steel duties.

The USMCA exemption may protect most Canadian exports, but it can't protect Canada from Trump's tendency to conflate unrelated issues into single grievances.

The USMCA Shield Effect: Accidental Genius or Dumb Luck?

Here's the fascinating question: Did Trump's negotiators in 2018 intentionally build Canada a shield against future trade wars, or is this an accidental consequence of maintaining free trade principles?

The USMCA's rules of origin requirements are complex but achievable for most Canadian exporters. They were designed to ensure "North American content" in goods receiving preferential treatment—essentially preventing China from using Canada or Mexico as backdoors to the US market.

But they also created an escape hatch from Trump's own tariff policies. As long as Canadian exporters can document sufficient North American content, they bypass the entire "trade war" regardless of political tensions.

This suggests either:

  1. Strategic foresight: Someone anticipated future trade disputes and built in protections

  2. Institutional persistence: Career trade officials maintained free trade principles despite political pressure

  3. Accidental irony: Trump's "America First" deal accidentally preserved the integrated North American economy he now claims to oppose

Given Trump's demonstrated trade policy inconsistencies, option 3 seems most likely.

The Real Math: What Canada Should Do Now

If only 5-6% of Canadian exports are actually vulnerable, Canada's retaliation strategy needs a complete rethink. Here's the math that matters:

Current Situation:

  • Canadian retaliatory tariffs: ~$38 billion in US goods affected

  • US tariffs actually impacting Canada: ~5-6% of $429 billion (2025 goods trade projection, adjusted for tariff impacts) = ~$20-25 billion

  • Retaliation ratio: roughly 2:1 in Canada's favor

Strategic Options:

Option 1: Proportional Retaliation

  • Reduce Canadian tariffs to match actual US impact (~$20-25 billion in targeted goods)

  • Focus on sectors that can't easily substitute Canadian suppliers

  • Maintain credible deterrent without providing escalation justification

Option 2: USMCA Maximization

  • Aggressive compliance campaign to get more exports under USMCA protection

  • Trade legal costs of certification against tariff costs

  • Reduce Trump's leverage as much as possible (though achieving 100% compliance faces practical challenges as some exporters may lack proper documentation)

Option 3: Strategic Asymmetry

  • Keep current retaliation but demand reciprocal USMCA exemptions for US goods

  • Force Trump to choose between his tariff revenue and his trade deal

  • Create internal US pressure from businesses losing Canadian market access

The slide rule says Option 2 is cheapest, Option 1 is most sustainable, and Option 3 is most likely to trigger further escalation.

The China Precedent: A Warning for Canada

Here's the sobering reality Canada must consider: Trump's escalation pattern with China provides a roadmap for what happens when countries maintain aggressive counter-tariffs.

With China, Trump started with targeted tariffs, China retaliated, and Trump responded by systematically expanding tariffs until they covered virtually all Chinese imports. The final result? A 60% tariff on nearly everything from China, regardless of the original justification.

Canada's 2:1 retaliation ratio puts them at serious risk of following the same trajectory. If Trump perceives Canadian counter-tariffs as excessive (which a 2:1 ratio certainly appears), he has multiple escalation tools:

  • Remove the USMCA exemption entirely (turning 5-6% vulnerability into 100%)

  • Invoke "national security" provisions to bypass trade agreement constraints

  • Expand sector-specific tariffs beyond steel, aluminum, and lumber

  • Use administrative harassment (endless inspections, regulatory delays, compliance audits)

The China precedent suggests Trump views disproportionate retaliation not as a negotiating tactic, but as a challenge to his authority that must be crushed entirely. Canada's strategic miscalculation isn't just about current math—it's about providing Trump with justification for escalating until Canada's protective USMCA shield gets stripped away completely.

The Constitutional Constraint: Courts vs. Tariffs

While Canada worries about trade mathematics, Trump faces a far more serious challenge: federal courts are questioning whether he has legal authority to impose most of his tariffs at all.

The case is V.O.S. Selections v. Trump, and oral arguments were heard today (July 31) at the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. The case centers on whether Trump can use the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to justify his sweeping tariffs, with the Constitution vesting Congress with the power to impose tariffs.

The Legal Problem

Trump's use of IEEPA to impose tariffs is "the first time since it became law in 1977 that it has been invoked by a president to impose tariffs on imports from other countries". The law "does not mention the word tariffs" and judges noted that "when we look at the statute we see foreign exchange, payments, currency" but "tariffs seems to have no friends in that statute".

In May 2025, the U.S. Court of International Trade invalidated much of Trump's tariff program as exceeding presidential authority under IEEPA, ruling that the law does not "confer such unbounded authority". Though the decision was quickly appealed and tariffs remain in effect, the legal foundation of Trump's entire tariff regime is under attack.

The Constitutional Stakes

Legal scholars argue that Trump's interpretation would give presidents "unbounded discretion in wielding a power that is neither granted to him by the Constitution nor delegable by Congress". Consumer advocacy groups filed briefs arguing that "accepting the government's theory would raise serious constitutional questions under the non-delegation doctrine and undermine Congress's exclusive power to levy taxes".

The implications for Canada are enormous: if courts rule that Trump lacks authority to impose most tariffs, the entire trade war could potentially be declared illegal. Canada's retaliation strategy would suddenly look prescient rather than mathematically flawed—they'd be the only country with legally imposed counter-tariffs while Trump's tariffs get struck down pending appeals.

The Timeline Matters

Multiple federal lawsuits are pending, with analysts predicting Trump will "probably continue to lose in the lower courts" and potentially at the Supreme Court, rendering his recent trade deals "illegal". The current case "could dictate how other cases fare" and Trump himself called it "a life-or-death moment for his trade agenda".

This creates a fascinating strategic calculation for Canada: maintain counter-tariffs that may soon be the only legal tariffs in the relationship, or de-escalate against tariffs that courts may potentially eliminate anyway?

The Broader Lesson: When Free Trade Deals Constrain Populists

The USMCA exemption reveals something profound about institutional constraints on presidential power. Trump can impose tariffs unilaterally under emergency authorities, but he can't easily override trade agreements without Congressional approval.

This creates a fascinating dynamic: populist trade policies constrained by the populist's own previous trade policies.

Trump negotiated USMCA specifically to replace NAFTA with something "better for America." But the rules of origin requirements he insisted upon now protect most Canadian exporters from his own tariff threats. His 2018 deal constrains his 2025 options.

This suggests that institutional trade frameworks—even ones negotiated by tariff enthusiasts—tend toward economic rationality over time. The complexity of modern supply chains makes comprehensive trade wars nearly impossible without destroying domestic industries.

The Personal Element Persists

My original analysis emphasized Trump's personal grudges driving trade policy. Recent events have only strengthened this thesis.

The sequence from Mark Carney's appointment as PM through their Oval Office meetings to the Palestine recognition issue shows how personal relationships continue to drive policy outcomes. Trump's threat that Palestine recognition would "make it very hard" to reach a trade deal reveals the extent to which he views economic agreements as personal loyalty tests.

This personality-driven approach explains why the USMCA exemption persists despite escalating rhetoric. Trump's advisers likely understand that actually disrupting 94-95% of Canadian trade would cause immediate economic chaos in both countries. But maintaining the exemption while escalating threats allows maximum political theater with minimum economic disruption.

It's performance art disguised as trade policy.

Conclusion: The Quiet De-escalation Strategy

Canada faces a choice that will define its relationship with the US for years to come. The mathematical reality suggests a strategy that few politicians will want to admit publicly: quietly reduce the retaliatory tariffs without fanfare.

Here's why this makes strategic sense:

The Math Supports De-escalation

  • Canada is hitting back 2 times harder than they're actually being hit

  • Only 5-6% of Canadian exports are genuinely at risk

  • Current retaliation provides Trump with perfect justification for future escalation

  • Legal challenges may eliminate Trump's tariff authority entirely

The Poker Player's Approach

Here's the ace up Canada's sleeve: approach the Americans quietly and suggest reducing retaliatory tariffs, then simply ask, "What will you do for us?"

The beauty of this approach is that Canada can frame it as a "concession" while keeping behind a poker face the fact that they're simply leveling a playing field that was mathematically absurd to begin with. Trump gets to feel like he extracted something from Canada, while Canada gets to negotiate from what's actually a position of strength.

Any experienced negotiator knows: when you're holding a winning hand (94-95% tariff exemption), you don't need to show all your cards. Let the other side think they're getting a great deal when you're actually just correcting an imbalance that favored you 2:1.

The Trump Psychology Factor

Trump will likely interpret any reduction in Canadian tariffs as "making Canada kneel" and declare victory. Perfect. Let him. The alternative—continued escalation following the China model—risks losing the USMCA exemption that protects 94-95% of Canadian trade.

Sometimes the smartest strategy is letting your opponent think they won while you quietly protect what actually matters.

The Institutional Advantage

Canada should focus on what's working: the USMCA exemption that Trump can't easily override without Congressional approval. Aggressive retaliation risks providing him with "national security" justifications to bypass the trade agreement entirely.

A Quiet Victory

The greatest irony is that Canada may have already won this trade war—not through dramatic retaliation, but through boring institutional constraints that protect nearly all their exports while Trump rages about problems that barely affect the actual trade relationship.

The real trade war isn't between Canada and the US. It's between Trump's political instincts and his own previous policy commitments. As long as the USMCA holds, the institutions are winning.

But only if Canada doesn't give Trump an excuse to tear them down entirely. The smartest move may be to fold a strong hand quietly and let Trump think he bluffed his way to victory—while protecting the 94-95% that actually matters.
