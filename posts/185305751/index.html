We Prefer Respect to Bullies

Emmanuel Macron’s Warning to America

Part 3: How France’s president diagnosed the “brutalization of the world” and outlined Europe’s three-pillar response to U.S. coercion

January 21, 2026

Part 3 of The Davos Rupture Series

CanadianSnowmanSays

French President Emmanuel Macron delivered his Davos address wearing aviator sunglasses to protect an eye affected by a burst blood vessel. The look was part defiance, part symbolism—France staring down threats with steely determination.

He opened with dark humor that drew laughter from the audience:

“It’s a time of peace, stability and predictability.”

The irony was thick. The laughter subsided as he shifted to diagnosis:

“It’s clear that we are reaching a time of instability, of imbalances, both from the security and defense point of view and the economic point of view.”

The Brutalization of the World

Macron described what he called a fundamental shift in the global order:

“A shift towards autocracy, against democracy. More violence, more than 60 wars in 2024 – an absolute record, even if I understood a few of them were fixed.”

That last line—”even if I understood a few of them were fixed”—was a pointed reference to Trump’s claim to have “stopped 8 Wars PLUS” and his assertion in the Norway text that some conflicts had been “settled.”

Macron continued:

“Conflict has become normalized, hybrid, expanding into new domains: space, digital information, cyber, trade and so on. It’s as well a shift towards a world without rules. Where international law is trampled underfoot and where the only law it seems to matter is that of the strongest. And imperial ambitions are resurfacing.”

Competition as Subordination

Without naming the United States, Macron delivered one of the sharpest indictments of Trump’s approach heard at Davos:

“Competition from the United States of America through trade agreements that undermine our export interests, demand maximum concessions and openly aim to weaken and subordinate Europe, combined with an endless accumulation of new tariffs that are fundamentally unacceptable – even more so when they are used as leverage against territorial sovereignty.”

The word “subordinate” appeared repeatedly in the speeches of both Macron and Carney—not as diplomatic euphemism but as direct description of what Trump’s tariff threats and Greenland demands represent.

Macron also addressed Chinese competition, noting “massive excess capacities and distortive practices threaten to overwhelm entire industrial and commercial sectors.”

But his central message was clear: “The answer, in order to fix this issue, is more cooperation.”

Two Approaches to Reject

Macron identified two responses Europe must avoid:

First: “To passively accept the law of the strongest, leading to vassalization and bloc politics. I think accepting the sort of new colonial approach doesn’t make sense.”

He warned that leaders who are “too complacent with such an approach will take a huge responsibility.”

Second: “To adopt a purely moral posture, limiting ourselves to commentary. That would condemn us to marginalization and powerlessness.”

Instead, Macron called for “an effective multilateralism because it serves our interests and those of all who refuse to submit to the rule of force.”

France and the G7

As France assumes the G7 presidency, Macron outlined an ambitious agenda to address global economic imbalances:

“This year, France holds the G7 presidency with a clear ambition – to restore the G7 as a forum for frank dialogue among major economies, and for collective and cooperative solutions. Trade wars, protectionist escalation, races towards overproduction will only produce losers.”

He identified the core imbalances as: American overconsumption, Chinese underconsumption and overinvestment, and European underinvestment and lack of competitiveness.

The G7’s objective, he said, would be to “demonstrate that the world’s major powers are still capable of reaching a shared diagnosis of the global economy and committing to concrete actions.”

European Response: Protection, Simplification, Investment

Macron outlined three pillars for Europe’s economic strategy:

Protection – Not Protectionism, But Realistic Defense

“Today’s Europeans are too naive. This is a unique market, open to everybody without checking level playing field.”

He noted that China and the US both protect their markets, but “Europeans are the only ones not to protect their own companies and their own markets.”

On the anti-coercion mechanism (the “trade bazooka”):

“The anti-coercion mechanism is a powerful instrument and we should not hesitate to deploy it in today’s tough environment.”

This mechanism, unofficially known as Europe’s “trade bazooka,” allows the EU to sanction individuals or institutions putting undue pressure on European nations. Macron was saying Europe should use it—for the first time ever—against the United States if necessary.

Macron called for advancing the principle of European preference: “There is a North American preference in your market. There is no European preference today. We are progressively creating it.”

Europe must strengthen its trade defense instruments, including mirror measures to enforce regulatory standards, and improve the quality of foreign direct investment targeting projects with export potential.

On China specifically: “China is welcome, but what we need is more Chinese foreign direct investments in Europe, in some key sectors, to contribute to our growth, to transfer some technologies and not just to export towards Europe.”

Simplification – Reducing Regulatory Burden

Macron emphasized the need to reduce regulations that desynchronize Europe from the rest of the world. He pointed to recent simplification efforts on automotive regulations and called for similar work on chemicals, digital, AI, and banking.

The core principle: accelerate the deepening of the single market so that Europe’s 450 million inhabitants become the true domestic market for all EU companies.

This also means ensuring technological neutrality and non-discrimination within the European Union—ending the practice of discriminating between different sources of energy, which Macron said is “counterproductive for the Europeans themselves.”

Investment and Innovation – Closing the Gap

Macron addressed the fundamental reason for the GDP per capita gap between the US and Europe:

“If there is a GDP per capita so different between the US and Europe, 65% to 70% of the explanation is due to the difference in terms of innovation. And the US was much more innovative because of public and private investment.”

Europe needs to invest much more money in critical sectors: AI, quantum computing, cleantech, defense and security. The size of the common budget must increase.

But public investment alone isn’t enough. Europe also needs more private investment.

“We do have the savings as Europeans, much more than the US, by the way. But this saving is overinvested in bonds and sometimes in equities – but outside Europe.”

The solution: securitization programs and capital markets union to invest European savings in European innovation and equity.

On Greenland

Macron addressed the crisis directly and unapologetically:

“We have decided to join a mutual exercise in Greenland without threatening anyone, but just supporting an ally and another European country, Denmark.”

This was the deployment that Trump characterized as a “provocation” and used to justify 10% tariffs on eight European nations.

Macron’s message was clear: supporting a NATO ally is not a provocation. It’s what allies do.

France’s Strengths

Macron emphasized France’s competitive advantages:

  • Low-carbon electricity supply (exported 93 TWh last year, based on nuclear power)

  • World-class innovation and research capabilities

  • Vivid and active ecosystems in AI, quantum computing, energy transition

  • High-quality infrastructure and large market with strong purchasing power

  • Rule of law and predictability

On that last point, Macron made a subtle but devastating dig:

“We have a place where rule of law and predictability is still the rule of the game. And my guess is that it is largely underpriced by the market... having a place like Europe, which sometimes is too slow, for sure, and needs to be reformed for sure, but which is predictable, loyal and when, well, you know that the rule of the game is just a rule of law, it’s a good place.”

This was Macron saying: unlike America under Trump, Europe still plays by rules. That stability has value. The market hasn’t fully priced it in yet, but it will.

The Closing Lines

Macron concluded with the values statement that became one of the defining moments of Davos 2026:

“We do believe that we need more growth, we need more stability in this world. But we do prefer respect to bullies. We do prefer science to obscurantism, and we do prefer rule of law to brutality.”

These weren’t empty platitudes. Each phrase was a direct rebuke:

“We prefer respect to bullies” – Trump’s approach to allies is bullying, and Europe won’t accept it.

“We prefer science to obscurantism” – A rejection of anti-intellectualism and fact-free governance.

“We prefer rule of law to brutality” – The core distinction between democratic governance and authoritarian coercion.

The Humiliation

The timing of these remarks was particularly pointed. Hours earlier, Trump had posted screenshots of private text messages from Macron on Truth Social. The messages showed the French president trying to bridge the diplomatic gap:

“My friend, we are completely aligned on Syria. We can do great things in Iran. I do not understand what you are doing in Greenland.”

Macron had offered to arrange a G7 meeting in Paris, to have dinner with Trump, to find a path forward. He was offering cooperation, dialogue, a way to work together on shared interests while addressing disagreements.

Trump’s response was to publicly humiliate him by sharing their private diplomatic communications and then threaten France with 200% tariffs on wine and Champagne because Macron declined to join the “Board of Peace.”

This is what being “bored of peace” looks like in practice. Macron was trying to do the actual work of diplomacy—finding common ground, offering dialogue, building consensus. Trump found that boring, so he turned it into a public spectacle and an economic threat.

The Macron Doctrine

Macron’s speech articulated a clear vision:

  1. Name the reality: Call it what it is—a shift toward autocracy, brutalization, a world without rules

  2. Reject false choices: Neither passive acceptance nor moral posturing will work

  3. Build European strength: Through protection, simplification, and investment

  4. Maintain multilateralism: Effective cooperation serves European interests

  5. Stand on values: Prefer respect to bullies, science to obscurantism, law to brutality

Unlike Carney’s philosophical framework or von der Leyen’s institutional blueprint, Macron offered something more visceral—a moral case for European resistance rooted in fundamental values about how nations should treat each other.

He was saying: We know you’re stronger. We know you can threaten us economically. We know you can post our private messages. We know you can mock us and humiliate us.

But we still prefer respect to bullies. And we’re building the strength to make that preference matter.

What Comes Next

As France assumes the G7 presidency, Macron will attempt to restore it “as a forum for frank dialogue among major economies.” Whether that’s possible with Trump in the White House remains to be seen.

The planned G7 meeting Macron proposed—with Ukraine, Denmark, Syria, and Russia on the sidelines—never happened. Trump leaked the private message instead.

But Macron’s three pillars—protection, simplification, investment—are already being implemented. The anti-coercion mechanism is being prepared. The European preference framework is being developed. The capital markets union is moving forward.

Europe is no longer waiting for American permission to defend its interests. It’s no longer hoping that compliance will buy safety. It’s no longer performing sovereignty while accepting subordination.

Macron made that clear: Europe prefers respect to bullies. And it’s building the economic and political strength to make that preference more than rhetoric.
