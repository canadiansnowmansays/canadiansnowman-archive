Part 1

The Question America Won’t Answer: Why Should the Guilty Be Pardoned?

November 30, 2025

A Canadian Investigative Journalist Examines Presidential Clemency from Bush to Trump

On November 27, 2025—the day after Thanksgiving—David Gentile walked out of federal prison after serving thirteen days of a seven-year sentence. He had been convicted by a jury of defrauding 10,000 investors of $1.6 billion through his private equity firm, GPB Capital. More than 1,000 victims submitted statements to the court describing their losses.

“I lost my whole life savings,” one wrote. “I am living from check to check.”

President Trump commuted Gentile’s sentence. The conviction stands, but the accountability ends. Thirteen days for a $1.6 billion fraud. Thirteen days for destroying 10,000 people’s financial security.

As Americans gathered for Thanksgiving weekend 2025, David Gentile had more reason to give thanks than most—he had just been freed from a seven-year sentence after serving less than two weeks. His victims, still trying to recover their life savings, had considerably less to celebrate. This Thanksgiving timing wasn’t coincidental—Trump’s “pardon czar” Alice Marie Johnson announced the commutation on Thanksgiving Day itself, expressing gratitude that Gentile was “heading home to his young children.”

For those pardoned or granted commutations under this administration, Thanksgiving 2025 marked something beyond the traditional American holiday of gratitude. It marked their liberation from accountability, their escape from consequences, their proof that connections matter more than conduct.

Breaking: December 2, 2025 — Two days after this essay was published, President Trump issued “a Full and Complete Pardon” to Juan Orlando Hernández, the former president of Honduras convicted of cocaine trafficking and taking bribes from drug cartels. The timing is remarkable: Trump is simultaneously escalating military operations in the Caribbean claiming to battle drug cartels while pardoning a man prosecutors described as using “the full power and strength of the state” to protect cartel drugs. As The New York Times noted, this “displayed a remarkable dissonance in the president’s strategy.” We’ll examine this case in detail in Part 6, which documents how Trump’s second-term clemency has extended beyond domestic allies to international figures with strategic value.

This raises a question that should make every American—regardless of political affiliation—pause and demand an answer:

Why should the guilty be pardoned?

The Constitutional Framework

The U.S. Constitution grants the President sweeping clemency power in Article II, Section 2: the authority to “grant Reprieves and Pardons for Offenses against the United States, except in Cases of Impeachment.” This power is “one of the most unlimited powers bestowed on the president by the Constitution.”

The Founders granted this authority expecting it would be used judiciously—to correct miscarriages of justice, to extend mercy in exceptional circumstances, to heal national wounds. Alexander Hamilton, writing in Federalist 74, argued for “an easy access to exceptions in favor of unfortunate guilt.”

Note Hamilton’s language: “unfortunate guilt.” The pardon power was meant for the unfortunate—those caught in circumstances deserving mercy—not the well-connected.

Presidential clemency generally takes two forms:

Pardons completely forgive an offense, erasing the conviction as if it never happened. They restore civil rights and remove the legal stigma of a criminal record.

Commutations reduce or eliminate sentences without erasing the conviction. The person remains a convicted felon but serves less time or pays reduced fines.

The Framers placed almost no limits on this power. The President cannot pardon in cases of impeachment, and clemency only applies to federal crimes. Beyond that, the discretion is nearly absolute.

This raises an uncomfortable truth: The pardon power can be the most democratic exercise of mercy or the most tyrannical exercise of favoritism. Everything depends on how it’s used and whom it serves.

Legitimate vs. Illegitimate Purposes

Traditionally, presidential pardons have served several legitimate purposes:

Legitimate Uses:

  1. Correcting Injustice - When evidence proves innocence or prosecutorial misconduct

  2. Proportionality - When sentences are grossly excessive relative to the crime

  3. Rehabilitation - When substantial time served, genuine remorse shown, no further threat posed

  4. National Reconciliation - After wars or civil strife, broad pardons can heal divisions

  5. Humanitarian Concerns - When health issues or circumstances create special hardship

Illegitimate Uses: 6. Political Favor - Rewarding allies, protecting associates, signaling loyalty 7. Obstruction - Pardoning co-conspirators or witnesses to prevent testimony 8. Financial Gain - Creating systems where pardons can be purchased through donations or lobbying 9. Personal Interest - Pardoning family members or those whose prosecution threatens the President

The question “why should the guilty be pardoned?” can be answered legitimately when clemency serves the first five purposes. It becomes indefensible when it serves the last four.

The Analytical Framework

To fairly assess the clemency records of Presidents Bush, Obama, Biden, and Trump, we need consistent criteria. I propose examining each through seven lenses:

1. Connection to the President

  • No connection (stranger granted clemency)

  • Professional connection (administration employee)

  • Political ally (campaign associates, donors)

  • Personal friend or associate

  • Family member

  • Co-conspirator in President’s own alleged wrongdoing

The stronger the connection, the more scrutiny warranted.

2. Nature of the Crime

  • Victimless crimes (marijuana possession)

  • Non-violent offenses (tax evasion, fraud)

  • Crimes against government (lying to Congress, obstruction)

  • Violent crimes (assault, murder)

  • National security crimes (espionage, sedition)

  • Crimes that served the President’s interests

3. Victim Impact

  • No identifiable victims

  • Financial victims (fraud, theft)

  • Physical victims (assault, injury)

  • National security harm

  • Institutional damage (obstruction, witness tampering)

What happens to victims? Is restitution preserved or erased?

4. Stage of Justice

  • After full sentence served

  • After substantial time served

  • Early in sentence

  • Before reporting to prison

  • Before trial

  • Before charges filed (preemptive)

Earlier intervention suggests greater favor.

5. Process Followed

  • Standard DOJ Office of Pardon Attorney review

  • Direct petition outside normal channels

  • Presidential initiative without petition

  • Lobbyist-driven

  • Cash-for-clemency schemes

6. Timing

  • Throughout presidency (consistent policy)

  • End of term (traditional practice)

  • First day of new term (political statement)

  • In response to specific events

7. Public Justification

  • Detailed explanation of mercy grounds

  • Claim of injustice or excessive sentence

  • Humanitarian concerns

  • Criminal justice reform policy

  • Political statement

  • No explanation given

  • False explanations

What to Expect from This Series

Over the next six essays, I will systematically examine the clemency records of four presidents: George W. Bush, Barack Obama, Joe Biden, and Donald Trump. I will apply these seven criteria consistently, letting the evidence speak for itself.

This will not be a partisan exercise. You will see me:

  • Credit Bush’s restraint in limiting pardons for political allies

  • Acknowledge Obama’s focus on criminal justice reform

  • Condemn Biden’s indefensible family pardons

  • Document Trump’s systematic transformation of clemency into political currency

Some will accuse me of “both-sidesism”—creating false equivalence. Others will accuse me of Trump Derangement Syndrome.

The evidence will show that:

  1. Bush used clemency sparingly and showed meaningful restraint

  2. Obama used clemency extensively but primarily for policy purposes

  3. Biden used clemency extensively for policy but egregiously for family

  4. Trump used clemency systematically as a tool of political protection and financial corruption

These are not equivalent. A comparative analysis that treats them as equivalent would be dishonest. But an analysis that refuses to acknowledge wrongdoing across the political spectrum lacks credibility.

The Gentile Case Preview

Let’s return to David Gentile. Apply our framework:

Connection: Unknown whether Gentile has direct ties to Trump, but the pattern suggests financial connections or lobbying intervention.

Nature of Crime: Securities and wire fraud—misrepresenting fund performance to defraud 10,000 investors of $1.6 billion.

Victim Impact: Devastating. One thousand people submitted statements describing losses—”hardworking, everyday people,” including teachers, nurses, veterans, farmers. “I lost my whole life savings. I am living from check to check.”

Stage of Justice: Barely begun. Convicted August 2024, sentenced May 2025 to seven years, reported to prison November 14, released November 27. Served 1.8% of his sentence.

Process: Alice Marie Johnson, Trump’s “pardon czar,” announced the commutation via social media on Thanksgiving.

Timing: November 2025—less than a year into Trump’s second term.

Justification: Johnson said she was “deeply grateful to see David Gentile heading home to his young children.” A White House official argued prosecutors had falsely characterized the business as a Ponzi scheme.

But prosecutors asked the judge to order Gentile to forfeit more than $15.5 million, and a court-appointed receiver had access to more than $700 million “likely to be distributed to investors.” What happens to this restitution now?

Civil lawyer Adam Gana, representing investors: “The stories we’ve heard are just heartbreaking, and it’s just unbelievable that somebody like that would receive a commutation. This is not a case that should be political. This guy belongs in prison.”

The prosecutor’s words at sentencing now echo with bitter irony: “A warning to would-be fraudsters that seeking to get rich by taking advantage of investors gets you only a one-way ticket to jail.”

Unless you get a commutation. Then it’s a thirteen-day inconvenience.

The Two-Tier System

The Gentile case exemplifies what happens when clemency becomes systematized favoritism. It creates:

Tier One: If you have connections, can afford lobbyists, are politically loyal, serve the President’s interests—you escape accountability regardless of harm caused or victims devastated.

Tier Two: If you’re an ordinary American with no connections, no access, no lobbying budget—you serve every day, pay every dollar, and live with consequences.

This isn’t equality before the law. It’s aristocracy.

The Gentile case represents a pattern, not an anomaly. It’s part of a systematic approach that has:

  • Freed 1,500+ people convicted of violent crimes during the January 6 Capitol attack

  • Pardoned the President’s campaign chairman, political advisor, and national security advisor

  • Erased $1.4 billion in restitution and fines owed to victims

  • Created a profitable lobbying industry where criminals pay hundreds of thousands for pardons

  • Protected 77 co-conspirators in the fake electors plot before charges were filed

  • Been explicitly justified as “No MAGA left behind”

  • Directed clemency overwhelmingly to white recipients (85%) while federal prison population is 75% non-white

  • Granted clemency to only 9 drug offenders out of 60,000+ imprisoned for such crimes

But we’ll also document:

  • Biden’s indefensible pardon of son Hunter despite repeated promises not to

  • Biden’s unprecedented preemptive pardons for five family members

  • Obama’s controversial commutation of Chelsea Manning’s espionage sentence

  • Bush’s commutation of Scooter Libby’s sentence

The question “why should the guilty be pardoned?” has different answers from each president. Some are defensible. Others are troubling. And some are devastating to the principle of equal justice.

A Note on Method

I approach this analysis as a Canadian investigative journalist with no partisan stake in American politics. I don’t vote in U.S. elections. My interest is in accountability, evidence, and the rule of law.

Sometimes the clearest perspective comes from outside the partisan trenches. Sometimes the most honest accounting comes from those with no team to defend.

I’ve spent the past year documenting accountability evasion in my 20-part “Trump Accountability Project” series. This clemency analysis extends that investigation but applies the same scrutiny across the political spectrum.

If evidence shows Obama abused clemency, I’ll document it. If Bush’s record proves exemplary, I’ll acknowledge it. If Biden’s family pardons are indefensible, I’ll condemn them. And if Trump’s pattern is uniquely corrupt, I’ll prove it.

The only question: What does the evidence show?

Why This Matters Now

David Gentile is free after thirteen days. His 10,000 victims are still trying to recover their life savings. The prosecutor who promised a “one-way ticket to jail” has been proven wrong.

As I write in late 2025, President Trump has granted clemency to approximately 1,700 people in his second term. More pardons are coming. More victims will be abandoned. More accountability will evaporate.

The question “why should the guilty be pardoned?” isn’t academic. It’s urgent.

Every time we normalize pardons based on connections rather than mercy, we take another step toward a society where justice depends on who you know.

Every time we allow presidents to pardon co-conspirators, we make it easier for the next president to obstruct justice with impunity.

Every time we permit pardons-for-profit, we transform the Constitution’s grant of mercy into a commodity.

Every time we abandon victims by erasing restitution, we declare their suffering matters less than the comfort of well-connected criminals.

The Framers gave presidents clemency power trusting they would use it wisely, sparingly, and for the public good. That trust is being systematically betrayed.

It’s time to examine exactly how, by whom, and what it means for American democracy.
