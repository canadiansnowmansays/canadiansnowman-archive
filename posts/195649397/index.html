The President’s Saturday

Or, A Full Day's Work in the Republic

May 3, 2026

It is a peculiar feature of the American presidency that no matter how carefully one plans one’s Saturday, it never quite ends the way one imagined. This is especially true when one begins it in Florida among cryptocurrency enthusiasts and ends it in Washington being pushed under a table by the Secret Service.

But I am getting ahead of myself.

The day began, as so many presidential days now begin, at Mar-a-Lago. The occasion was what organizers described, without apparent embarrassment, as “the most exclusive crypto and business conference in the world.” Two hundred and ninety-seven guests had been invited. Not on the basis of their expertise in distributed ledger technology or their contributions to monetary theory, but on the basis of having purchased the largest quantities of a digital token called $TRUMP — a coin with no utility, no underlying asset, and no function whatsoever beyond the timeless one of separating hopeful people from their money.

The token, which had once traded at $73, was now worth approximately $2.59. This represented a decline of ninety-six per cent. The guests had, in other words, assembled to celebrate an investment that had performed roughly as well as a sandwich left in the sun. Nobody mentioned this. In the world of cryptocurrency, a ninety-six per cent loss is what is known as “community building.”

The top twenty-nine holders — an inner circle of the especially committed, or perhaps simply the especially slow to sell — were ushered into a VIP reception featuring a champagne toast with the president. What they discussed over their champagne is not recorded. One imagines the conversation had something of the forced gaiety of a lifeboat drill.

Among the day’s speakers were Mike Tyson, the former heavyweight champion of the world, and Tony Robbins, the motivational coach. This is a combination that makes a certain kind of sense if you have been in crypto long enough. First you need someone to tell you that you can achieve anything. Then you need someone to hit you very hard in the face. The sequence may vary.

Guests received Trump-branded merchandise: a commemorative poster, two trading cards, something called a “Fight Fight Fight Red Beauty” watch, and a bottle of cologne. The cologne was not described in detail, but one assumes it smelled of ambition and regret, with base notes of margin call.

Contest rankings, it should be noted, were determined not solely by coin holdings but also by purchases of Trump-branded sneakers, watches, and fragrances. This is an innovation in presidential access. Previous administrations required donors to attend tedious fundraising dinners and write cheques. The current model simply requires you to buy enough cologne. It is, in its way, more honest.

“Crypto is a big industry,” the president told the room. “It’s actually become somewhat mainstream.”

This was received with applause. It is always received with applause. In crypto, everything is always about to become mainstream, in the same way that the bus is always just around the corner.

The fine print on the event’s website noted that “President Trump may not be able to attend” and that the entire affair could be “cancelled for any reason.” In the event of cancellation, attendees would receive a “limited edition TRUMP NFT in lieu thereof.” One struggles to imagine a bleaker sentence in the English language. You spend half a million dollars on a depreciating token, fly to Florida for a champagne toast with the leader of the free world, and the consolation prize, if he doesn’t show up, is a picture of him on a blockchain.

But the president did show up. He gave his keynote. He toasted his investors. And then, because the day was young and the republic is vast, he got on Air Force One and flew to Washington.

The White House Correspondents’ Association Dinner is held annually at the Washington Hilton, a hotel of that particular mid-century enormousness that suggests it was designed to host either a press gala or a regional carpet convention, and is equally suited to both. The dinner is a formal affair in which journalists and politicians pretend, for one evening, that they enjoy each other’s company. The president had boycotted it throughout his entire first term, which was regarded at the time as a breach of tradition and is now, in retrospect, a fairly reasonable instinct.

This year he came. He sat at the head table. The first lady was beside him. The vice-president was in the room. Cabinet secretaries were scattered among the tables like raisins in a pudding. The president of the Correspondents’ Association was mid-programme when a man named Cole Tomas Allen, thirty-one, of Torrance, California — a part-time teacher, former engineering student, and game designer — rushed a security checkpoint carrying a shotgun, a handgun, and several knives.

Shots were fired. The Secret Service moved the president and the first lady to the floor. The room was evacuated. An agent was wounded. The suspect was tackled and taken into custody. He had, it emerged, emailed his family a document shortly before the event, listing his intended targets as administration officials, “prioritized from highest-ranking to lowest.”

It was, by any measure, a terrifying event. And yet what strikes the outside observer — the Canadian observer, specifically, sitting at a safe distance with his dog and his tea — is not the horror of it, which is self-evident, but the schedule that preceded it.

Let us review the day in its entirety.

At lunchtime, the president stood before nearly three hundred people who had purchased access to him through a financial instrument that has lost ninety-six per cent of its value, and he told them crypto was becoming mainstream. He gave them cologne. He clinked glasses with the twenty-nine largest holders, at least one of whom — the contest’s top finisher for the second consecutive year — is currently suing the president’s own crypto platform for fraud. Nobody found this unusual.

By evening, he was in a ballroom in Washington, attending a dinner with the press corps, an institution he has spent the better part of a decade describing as the enemy of the people. He was flanked by cabinet members and members of Congress. A man with a shotgun attempted to enter the room.

And by the following morning, the Department of Justice had filed a letter arguing that the shooting proved the necessity of the president’s proposed new White House ballroom — a construction project currently tied up in court, which a federal judge had previously described as legally dubious. The shooting at someone else’s hotel, in other words, became the argument for building your own.

Representative Lauren Boebert announced she was drafting legislation to authorize the ballroom. Senator Sheehy said he would seek unanimous consent. The wheels of government, so often stuck, turned with a speed that would have been inspiring had the cause been literally anything else.

The Mariposa Belle goes down in Lake Wissanotti. Everyone got off safely — it sank in only six feet of water. In the republic, the lake is deeper, the passengers paid more, and nobody is entirely sure who is steering.

Stephen Leacock once wrote that the essence of humour is the contrast between the thing as it is and the thing as it ought to be. He was thinking, at the time, of small-town Canadian life — of Mariposa, of church socials that went sideways and steamboat excursions that ended in the lake.

He could not have imagined a Saturday like this one. But I think he would have recognized it. The structure is the same. The earnest people who show up believing in something. The grand event that dissolves into chaos. The authorities who immediately find a way to turn the chaos to their advantage. The ordinary citizens left holding, if not a sinking steamboat, then at least a rapidly depreciating token and a bottle of presidential cologne.

The only real difference is one of scale. In Mariposa, the catastrophe was local and the foolishness was gentle. In the republic, the foolishness is national, the catastrophe is armed, and the cologne retails for $99.

But the bus, they assure us, is just around the corner.
