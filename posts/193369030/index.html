Bobby, This Is How You Do It

One Kennedy Honored the FDA's Finest Hour. Another Is Dismantling It

April 6, 2026

Frances Oldham Kelsey, MD, PhD, receives the President's Award for Distinguished Federal Civil Service from President John F. Kennedy in 1962. Photo credit: Special Collections Research Center/The University of Chicago Library

This is a photograph worth studying.

It is August 7, 1962. President John F. Kennedy stands in the Rose Garden pinning the President’s Award for Distinguished Federal Civilian Service onto the lapel of Dr. Frances Oldham Kelsey. She is only the second woman in the award’s history to receive it. She is smiling with the quiet satisfaction of someone who knows exactly what she earned — not applause, not a promotion, not the goodwill of an industry. She earned the knowledge that somewhere in America, thousands of children were running, playing, living full lives, never knowing how close they had come to a very different fate.

What she had done to earn that knowledge was simple, in the way that all genuinely hard things are simple to describe.

She had said no.

Dr. Kelsey arrived at the Food and Drug Administration in September 1960, forty-six years old, a pharmacologist with a medical degree, and one of the few women in federal drug regulation. Her supervisors handed her what seemed like a routine assignment: review the approval application for a new sedative called Kevadon. The active ingredient was thalidomide. It was already a sensation across Europe and Canada, enthusiastically prescribed for anxiety, insomnia, and especially morning sickness in pregnant women. In some countries it was sold over the counter. The company projecting massive American sales — Richardson-Merrell — expected approval within weeks, perhaps days.

Dr. Kelsey reviewed the application. Something was wrong. The safety studies were superficial. The metabolic data was vague. Most troubling, there was virtually no information about the effects on pregnant women, despite the fact that morning sickness was one of the drug’s primary selling points.

She sent the application back. Insufficient data. Request denied.

What followed was a pressure campaign that would have broken most people. Company executives visited her office personally, using their size and presence to intimidate. They called her a petty bureaucrat nitpicking meaningless technicalities. They called her inexperienced, unreasonable, a woman on a power trip drunk on newfound authority. The industry press questioned her competence. Her supervisors were lobbied to overrule her. She was new to the job. She was a woman in an era when women in science faced constant dismissal. There was no support network, no precedent for what she was doing, and the full weight of the pharmaceutical industry telling her she was wrong.

She asked for more data. When the company submitted inadequate responses, she rejected them. And again. She found new reasons to question the research. She stalled for eleven agonizing months.

Then came November 1961, and the reports from Germany: babies born with phocomelia, their limbs absent or grotesquely shortened, tiny hands emerging directly from shoulders, legs stunted or missing, internal organs damaged. More than 10,000 children affected across Europe and Australia. Half would not survive infancy. The cause: thalidomide, taken during pregnancy.

In the United States, the catastrophe that devastated an entire European generation barely registered. A handful of cases emerged from physician samples the company had distributed during the approval process. The epidemic never came.

Because one woman at a desk had refused to sign a piece of paper without evidence.

President Kennedy pinned that medal on her lapel and understood precisely what he was honoring. Not dramatics. Not headlines. Not the courage that charges a hill. The courage that sits at a desk under withering pressure and keeps asking the same quiet, implacable question: where is the data?

John Kennedy’s nephew is now the United States Secretary of Health and Human Services.

On September 22, 2025, Robert F. Kennedy Jr. stood in the Roosevelt Room of the White House — the same building where his uncle had awarded that medal sixty-three years earlier — and announced to the American public that a cheap, decades-old chemotherapy support drug called leucovorin held promise for “hundreds of thousands of kids with autism.” He was accompanied by FDA Commissioner Marty Makary, who had published an op-ed in Politico promising the agency would “approve prescription leucovorin as a treatment for children with cerebral folate deficiency and autistic symptoms,” and who told reporters the drug might help between twenty and fifty percent of all children with autism.

The evidence underpinning this announcement was a small number of studies, the most significant of which — the largest randomized trial — would be retracted by the European Journal of Pediatrics in February 2026 after data re-analysis failed to support its own findings. The drugmaker GSK, which manufactures leucovorin, announced publicly that it had “no commercial interest, no scientific research” underway on the autism claim. It was, said the CEO, “just the administration at this stage.” Veterans inside the FDA described the agency’s new culture to reporters in terms Frances Kelsey would not have recognized: go find data to support this, go find data to support that.

The result of the White House announcement was swift and entirely predictable. Leucovorin prescriptions for children aged five to seventeen surged seventy-one percent above normal in the three months that followed. Families of autistic children — desperate, hopeful, willing to try anything their government told them might help — flooded pharmacies. The drug ran short. Oncology patients who needed leucovorin for its established purpose of managing chemotherapy side effects found it harder to obtain. Doctors reported children being subjected to unnecessary spinal taps. Parents paid out of pocket for a drug their government had essentially endorsed from the most powerful lectern on earth.

On March 10, 2026, the FDA quietly walked it back. Senior agency officials told reporters they could not find sufficient evidence to support leucovorin as an autism treatment. “Right now, we don’t have sufficient data,” an official said.

Insufficient data.

The same three words Frances Kelsey used in 1960. The difference — the only difference, but it is the difference between a medal and a disgrace — is that she said them before the prescriptions were written. Before the families spent their savings. Before the drug ran short for the patients who actually needed it.

“RFK Jr.’s premature and ill-informed announcement led many families to pay out of pocket for this drug when they could have been using that money for better purposes,” said Dr. David Mandell, professor of psychiatry at the University of Pennsylvania. “Now these families are experiencing the whiplash that happens when politics and untested theories get ahead of science.”

Pandora’s box, as one researcher noted dryly, is already open.

There is a temptation to treat the leucovorin episode as an isolated mistake — an excess of enthusiasm, a communication error, a good intention poorly executed. That temptation should be resisted. The leucovorin fiasco is not an aberration in the RFK Jr. tenure at HHS. It is the method. Announce first. Consult the evidence later. When the evidence fails to cooperate, retreat quietly and hope the seventy-one percent prescription surge fades from memory before the next announcement.

Frances Kelsey understood something that Jack Kennedy’s nephew has not learned: that the White House lectern is not a megaphone for wishful thinking. It is a precision instrument. When the most powerful government on earth tells desperate parents that a drug will help their children, those parents act. They act before the data is reviewed. They act before the trials are completed. They act because they trust the institution behind the lectern — or they did, until the institution began abusing that trust.

The FDA’s entire authority rests on one thing: the public’s belief that it is telling the truth, and that the truth is based on evidence. Richardson-Merrell understood this in 1960, which is why they worked so hard to bully Frances Kelsey into signing their approval. They knew that the FDA’s imprimatur was worth more than any advertising campaign. They wanted to borrow the agency’s credibility for their drug.

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. has done something Richardson-Merrell never managed. He has placed himself inside the agency, and he is spending its credibility himself — on drugs without adequate trials, on claims without adequate data, on promises made from the Roosevelt Room that the scientists down the hall cannot keep.

Frances Kelsey worked at the FDA until she was ninety years old. She spent her career protecting people who would never know her name, from a harm they would never know was coming. She did it without press conferences, without social media, without an op-ed in Politico announcing a breakthrough she couldn’t yet prove.

She did it by sitting at a desk, looking at incomplete data, and having the integrity to say: not yet.

Bobby, your uncle gave that medal to Dr. Kelsey for a reason. He understood that the quiet heroism of saying no — of standing firm against pressure, against ridicule, against the powerful voices demanding compliance — is the highest form of public service the FDA can perform.

You still have time to learn what she knew.

The children of parents who can’t afford leucovorin’s off-label prescriptions are counting on it.
