The Iran Crisis

The Bill Comes Due

Part LXX: Day 61. May 1. The War Powers Resolution deadline

May 1, 2026

Day 61. May 1. The War Powers Resolution deadline — the one everyone expected Trump to ignore — has arrived. Oil briefly touched $126 a barrel Thursday before pulling back. Gas is $4.30 nationally, up 27 cents in a single week. The Pentagon testified to $25 billion in war costs. Hegseth called congressional Democrats the biggest adversary America faces. Iran and the United States are still in limbo. In a cemetery in southern Lebanon, gravediggers are preparing 20 graves — nine for fighters already dead, eleven for those expected to die in the battles ahead.

I. The Hearing

On Wednesday morning, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth arrived at the House Armed Services Committee to ask Congress for $1.45 trillion — the largest defense budget in modern American history. Protesters in the hallway shouted “war criminal” and “arrest Hegseth.” Inside, before a single question had been asked, he declared: “The biggest challenge, the biggest adversary we face at this point are the reckless, feckless and defeatist words of congressional Democrats and some Republicans.”

It was, as the Cooper/Schmitt analysis — “Trump’s Plans to Boost Weapons Production Might Not Deliver for Years” — noted, not an auspicious opening for someone who needed 218 House votes to get anything funded. Representative Scott of Georgia, a Republican, said it plainly midway through: “It takes 218 votes to get something across the floor. We’re going to lose some Republican votes, and we’re going to have to have some Dem votes to do the things we have to do to fund the Department of Defense. I just would encourage everybody to keep that in mind.”

The hearing produced three facts of permanent record. Pentagon comptroller Jay Hurst confirmed the first official cost estimate: $25 billion so far. General Caine acknowledged a 14th US service member killed — Maj. Sorffly Davius of the Army National Guard, who died March 6 after a medical emergency in Kuwait, never previously disclosed. And Hegseth, asked whether he advised Trump to launch the war, declined to answer.

The Sanger-Katz/Parlapiano/Badger Upshot analysis — “What $25 Billion Spent on the War in Iran Really Means” — provides the essential context. $25 billion equals the annual NASA budget. It equals pre-war annual USAID spending. It is $190 per US household. It exceeds the Pentagon’s annual munitions budget — spent in eight weeks. The $200 billion supplemental request floated in mid-March has quietly disappeared. Nobody mentioned it at Wednesday’s hearing. Costs of War project director Stephanie Savell: “The costs of war were much larger than the Pentagon’s costs.” The post-9/11 wars will ultimately cost $8 trillion — nearly four times Congressional supplemental appropriations.

The Ryan/Salama/Scherer/Youssef Atlantic analysis — “The Pentagon May Not Be Telling Trump the Full Picture About the War” — sits alongside Wednesday’s hearing as its essential companion. Vance has repeatedly questioned the Defense Department’s depiction of the war in closed-door meetings. His advisers believe Hegseth’s portrayal “has been so positive as to be misleading.” The actual intelligence picture: Iran retains two-thirds of its air force, the bulk of its missile-launching capability, and most of its small fast boats. Hegseth boasted “complete control” of Iranian skies in March. Then Iran downed an American fighter jet in April. The Pentagon’s 8am briefings are timed to when Trump watches Fox News. Former official: “Pete’s TV experience has made him really skilled at knowing how to talk to Trump. I think that’s dangerous.”

II. The Production Gap

The Cooper/Schmitt analysis documents the specific failure that Wednesday’s hearing dramatized. Lockheed Martin has announced plans to increase PAC-3 Patriot production from 600 to 2,000 per year and quadruple THAAD interceptor production — but will not begin until the Pentagon secures funding it does not have. The contentiousness of the Hegseth hearing “did not bode well for the Pentagon request,” congressional staffers acknowledged.

American Enterprise Institute’s Todd Harrison: “If we’re running low after a few weeks of fighting Iran, we’re nowhere near where we need to be for Russia and China. We’ve never built up our stockpile of munitions to what they need to be for war plans.” CSIS’s Jerry McGinn: “We’ve done a lot of war games on the Taiwan Strait, and in all of those games, we run out of key munitions in a couple of weeks.”

The Pentagon is already diverting munitions deliveries meant for allies. Estonia’s six contracted HIMARS units suspended. Other European and Asian countries told the same. The NYT Editorial Board — “The U.S. Military Was Losing Its Edge. After Iran, Everyone Knows It.” — states the strategic consequence plainly: the war has provided “a road map for any country that wants to resist the United States in the future, including Russia and North Korea. For China, the country with the greatest potential to challenge American military might, the war validates its focus on new forms of warfare such as drones and cyber and space power.”

Four priorities the editorial board identifies: counter-drone technology; mass-produced cheap weapons; larger and more flexible industrial capacity; collaboration with democratic allies. Trump then spurned those allies and begged them for help reopening Hormuz. The sequence speaks for itself.

III. The $120 Barrel

Thursday: Brent crude for June delivery briefly exceeded $126 a barrel — the highest since 2022. Then whipsawed back below $115 in volatile trading. Settled around $104-107. Gas national average $4.30 — up 27 cents in a single week, up 44% since the war began. Diesel $5.50, up 46%.

The Schmidt analysis — “Stocks End April on a High, Even as Oil Prices Touch New Peak” — is the market’s verdict on the Pager/Barnes reporting filed earlier this week: Trump is not satisfied with Iran’s proposal to reopen Hormuz without addressing the nuclear program. Rapidan Energy’s Bob McNally: Trump “signaling no end in sight” to restrictions. “The market’s optimism in a near-term opening is dwindling, and you’re starting to see in the data that this is really starting to come home.”

Fed held rates Wednesday. Powell: “We’re very well aware that people are experiencing higher gas prices all over the country now. And that hurts.” Oxford Economics: “Inflation will get worse before it improves.” The World Bank projects energy prices up 24% this year. Chief economist Indermit Gill: “The war is hitting the global economy in cumulative waves: first through higher energy prices, then higher food prices and finally, higher inflation, which will push up interest rates and make debt even more expensive.”

The Nelson analysis — “Rising Fuel Prices Could Force Excruciating Choices on Economic Policies” — documents the central bank stagflation trap. Bank of England held at 3.75% but warned of “material risk” of higher inflation spreading. If Hormuz stays closed and oil hits $140, Britain faces recession risk and 5% inflation by year end. German consumer sentiment at lowest level in three years. Eurozone grew just 0.1% in Q1. Every major central bank simultaneously unable to raise rates to fight inflation without crushing a slowing economy, unable to cut to stimulate without feeding inflation.

An Exxon Mobil refinery in Texas. The United States has not been hit as hard by energy price increases as countries in Asia and Europe.Credit...Meridith Kohut for The New York Times

The oil companies are doing fine. The Cohen piece — “Blockbuster Oil Company Profits Revive Calls for Windfall Tax” — documents BP doubling its first-quarter profits with “exceptional” oil trading contributions. TotalEnergies: $5.4 billion net profit, doubled share buybacks. Five European finance ministers jointly asked the EU Commission to tax excessive profits. Trump, when oil prices started climbing: “The United States is the largest Oil Producer in the World, by far. So when oil prices go up, we make a lot of money.”

The Elliott piece — “Why U.S. Oil Companies Are Not Plugging the World’s Energy Gap” — explains why that statement is worse than it sounds. There are fewer drilling rigs operating in the US now than when the war started. Domestic production might fall in 2026. Exxon and Chevron are not changing their drilling plans. Diamondback Energy CEO: replacing lost Hormuz volume would be “like putting a garden hose into an Olympic-size swimming pool that’s been emptied.” Wall Street wants discipline, not production growth — if Hormuz reopens and oil falls, the companies that drilled more will have wasted the investment. The energy gap will not be filled by American oil companies. It will be filled by a deal, or it will remain unfilled.

IV. The Diplomatic Circuit

Araghchi’s movements since Saturday tell the story: Islamabad with written Iranian response, Oman, back to Islamabad for a second round with Pakistani mediators, then St. Petersburg to meet Putin. A circuit. No American in any of those rooms after Trump cancelled the Witkoff/Kushner flight.

The Nechepurenko analysis — “Top Iranian Official Meets With Putin to Discuss the Mideast War” — confirmed what the circuit map suggests. Russia has provided Iran with satellite imagery of US warship locations, diplomatic cover at the UN (vetoed the Hormuz reopening resolution), and Caspian trade links bypassing the maritime blockade. But Russia will not deliver advanced weapons, and Moscow-linked facilities in Iran including the Bushehr nuclear plant have been attacked at least four times. Russia condemned the attacks and did nothing. Putin’s interest in Araghchi’s visit is almost entirely instrumental: validate Russia’s mediator status, avoid becoming entangled, keep benefiting from the oil price surge. Russia is earning an estimated $200 million per day windfall from Hormuz-elevated energy prices — while simultaneously funding Iran’s adversaries’ analysis of US warship positions.

Abbas Araghchi, Iran’s foreign minister, with President Vladimir V. Putin of Russia in St. Petersburg on Monday. Mr. Araghchi’s visit to Russia follows a weekend of talks in Pakistan and Oman.Credit...Pool photo by Dmitri Lovetsky

The Solomon analysis — “Iran and U.S. Sink Into Awkward Limbo of ‘No War, No Peace’” — is the strategic frame. Iran’s prominent conservative newspaper Khorasan: “a strategic limbo” more dangerous than short-term war. Iran’s own economic newspaper Donya-e-Eghtesad: best-case deal produces 49% inflation. No-war-no-peace: 70%. Return to war: hyperinflation above 120%. Iran’s economists are telling the IRGC exactly what is at stake. The IRGC is choosing the status quo anyway.

Batmanghelidj of the Bourse & Bazaar Foundation: Iran believes it can outlast Trump “on the horizon of several weeks” because Hormuz disruptions are more costly for Trump than for Iran. But: “The no-deal, no-war mode, from the Iranian standpoint, leaves them vulnerable.” The question is which vulnerability each side can tolerate longer. The answer determines whether Part LXXI is a diplomatic filing or a return-to-war filing.

V. The Uranium Problem Trump Created

The Broad/Sanger analysis — “Trump Seeks to Abolish Iran’s Atomic Stockpile, a Problem He Helped Create” — and the Migliozzi/Broad graphic — “How Iran Accumulated 11 Tons of Enriched Uranium” — sit together as the essential historical accounting.

In 2018, when Trump pulled out of the JCPOA while Iran was complying, Iran had zero bomb-capable uranium. Under the Obama deal, Iran had shipped 12.5 tons of enriched uranium to Russia — leaving too little for a single bomb. Trump withdrew. Iran enriched. Today: 11 tons at various enrichment levels. Enough, if further enriched, for 50 to 100 nuclear weapons.

Iran’s latest proposal — delivered via Pakistan and reviewed in the White House Situation Room Monday — would reopen the strait, end the US blockade, and set aside the nuclear program entirely. Trump rejected it. A US official told the Times that accepting the proposal “could appear to deny Mr. Trump a victory.” The Rubio assessment was that the proposal was “better” but that serious sticking points remained. His formulation on Iran charging tolls for Hormuz transit: “This is not the Suez Canal, this is not the Panama Canal, these are international waters.”

Former CIA Director Burns wrote Friday that a good deal requires “tight nuclear inspections, an extended moratorium on the enrichment of uranium and the export or dilution of Tehran’s existing stockpile of enriched uranium in exchange for tangible sanctions relief.” Burns: “Unless the lines are clearly drawn and strictly monitored, the Iranians will paint outside them.” That is exactly what happened in 2018.

VI. The Lebanon That Is Not a Ceasefire

The Goldbaum/Guttenfelder piece — “As Israel Entrenches, Frustration With Hezbollah Turns to Support” — is the essential ground document of Week Ten. Over the first ten days of the truce, Goldbaum traveled across southern Lebanon. In Majdal Zoun, gravediggers were preparing 20 graves — nine for fighters already dead, eleven for those expected to die next time. In Tyre, Suheila, 54, found her son Hussein’s temporary grave in the cemetery, collapsed to her knees, and slapped the hardened earth with both hands: “What is this victory? What is this victory, Hussein?”

Farida Ali Awila at a gas station, watching a Hezbollah member distribute Mojtaba Khamenei fliers for car windshields: “We lost many men, they’re dying for what? For who? Iran? Iran is doing deals behind our back and our men are dying for them?”

The analytical finding Goldbaum documents is important: Shiite disenchantment with Hezbollah leadership is real. Naim Qassem does not inspire the fervent loyalty of the slain Hassan Nasrallah. But with Israeli forces occupying southern Lebanon, demolishing border villages, and Lebanese government forces absent, Hezbollah fighters are seen as the only available protection. Frustration does not become abandonment when the alternative is no defense at all.

The Kershner/Odenheimer/Boxerman/Rasgon analysis — “Hezbollah’s Latest Challenge to Israeli Forces: A Stealthier Drone” — adds the military dimension. Hezbollah is now deploying fiber-optic cable drones — immune to electronic jamming, nearly invisible cable guidance, fly low and fast. Long in use in Ukraine. Range of 15-20 kilometers, meaning Hezbollah can launch from beyond Israel’s six-mile occupation zone. One Israeli soldier killed Thursday by drone near Shomera. At least two other soldiers and a civilian contractor killed by explosive drones this week. Former Israeli NSA Hulata: “If Israel does not find a good solution, it may soon be contending with fiber-optic drones aimed at civilian homes across the border in Israel.”

The War Powers deadline is May 1. The three-week Lebanon truce extension runs to mid-May. The Lebanon truce is what International Crisis Group’s David Wood called “not so much a cease-fire as a limited de-escalation.” The Iran ceasefire is what Khorasan newspaper called a strategic limbo. Two ceasefires in name. One war in fact.

VII. The War’s Invisible Victims

The Eligon/Matonga/Bigg analysis — “Stalled Taxis, Postponed Weddings: War in Iran Hits African Families” — is the accounting the war crimes legal scholars, munitions economists, and oil trading desks are not doing. In Malawi, the government has completely run out of fuel reserves and is negotiating emergency IMF funding. Francis Kazembe, 28, taxi driver in Lilongwe, postponed his wedding because the money has disappeared: “In the past four days, I have twice slept at the filling station.” Women in Zambia pounding corn by hand with mortar and pestle because diesel shortages closed the hammer mills. Zimbabwe nurse Mitchel Londiwe, earning $540 a month, considering sending her children to live with their rural grandmother to save $160 a month on school transport. “Soon, we will look malnourished. I’m afraid.” Black-market diesel in Ethiopia up tenfold. Ethiopia’s demands for its own port intensifying — raising fears of renewed Eritrea conflict. More than 200 million Africans rely on financial support from Gulf workers whose employment has been disrupted.

The Torbati portrait — “Iranians Live With Pain and Powerlessness, Beneath a Smooth Veneer” — is the companion piece from inside Iran. Hip young people at Tehran cafes. Rock concert sold out. “That is all a veneer.” Sara, 40s: “Everyone’s morale is terrible.” Shahrzad, 57: bombs fell 20-30 times daily during the war. “We got used to it.” Her generation survived the revolution and Iran-Iraq war. “Gen Z, nobody can handle them. We are more peace-seeking. The young people are more radical.” Melika, 28: restaurants were packed during the war because saving makes no sense when the future is inaccessible. “Now those things are out of reach for a large portion of society. So they say, ‘Why should we be hard on ourselves? Let’s at least have a nice meal.’”

The day trader at the Kapikoy border crossing, using Turkey’s internet to work: “I think it’s all a game. We’re just being played with.”

VIII. The Hybrid War in Europe

The Specia/Ryckewaert analysis — “Attacks on Jewish Targets in Europe Suggest Hybrid Warfare” — documents the war’s least-visible front. A shadowy group calling itself Harakat Ashab al-Yamin al-Islamiyya has claimed responsibility for 12+ attacks across Britain, Netherlands, Belgium, France and Germany since March — arson at synagogues, Jewish schools, and charity ambulances; vehicles set alight; and Wednesday’s stabbing of two Jewish men aged 34 and 76 in Golders Green, London. 28 people arrested in Britain. Metropolitan Police commissioner Rowley: “Some individuals are being encouraged, persuaded, or paid to commit acts of violence on behalf of foreign organizations and hostile states.” Counterterrorism officials investigating links to Iran.

An arson attack in the Golders Green neighborhood of London in March destroyed four ambulances run by a Jewish organization.Credit...Henry Nicholls/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

International Center for Counter-Terrorism analyst Shtuni: tactics “point strongly in the direction of Iran.” The recruits — teenagers and young adults paid via Snapchat and Telegram — are “cannon fodder.” Their lawyer: “He had no idea the arson would be filmed. He took part to make quick cash.” Shtuni on the pattern: “Its amateurish appearance is a feature, not a flaw — it reflects a deliberate evolution in hybrid warfare that prioritizes deniability, scalability, psychological impact and persistence over dramatic attacks.” The British government announced an additional £25 million ($33.7 million) for Jewish community security Thursday.

Australia: The Royal Commission on Antisemitism and Social Cohesion released its interim Bondi Beach attack report Thursday. A Jewish security organization warned police six days before December’s Hanukkah event that a terrorist attack was “likely” and the community was recording “unprecedented volumes of antisemitic incidents.” Three officers attended the event “at various times.” 15 killed. 40 wounded. The deadliest mass shooting in Australia in 30 years.

IX. The Cheetahs

A female Asiatic cheetah named Dalbar in the Pardisan Park in Tehran in 2017. The species is under constant threat of hunters, speeding cars and wild dogs.Credit...Atta Kenare/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

In the eastern desert of Iran, 21 adult Asiatic cheetahs and 6 cubs have been recorded in 2026 — up from just 17 known wild cats last year. The Mahoozi/Solomon piece — “In Iran, Cheetah Sightings Offer Rare Spots of Hope Amid War” — is the piece to close with. The Asiatic cheetah exists only in Iran now. Ancient Persian kings used them to hunt gazelles. Iran’s national soccer jersey bears their spots. During the 2022 Women Life Freedom protests, a beloved cub named Pirouz became part of the protest anthem. The IRGC’s intelligence arm arrested nine conservationists in 2018 on espionage charges. One died in detention. Just last week, wildlife veterinarian Iman Memarian was arrested without disclosed charges.

Big-cat conservationist Tanya Rosen: “In times of crisis like this, nature and the survival of the Asiatic cheetah against all odds stand as a rare, powerful and deeply needed source of hope for the country.”

The internet has been dark for 61 days. Cooking oil is being smuggled across the Turkish border for a $2 profit. The IRGC generals are running the country by courier. The war cost $25 billion and no one will say how much more it will cost. May 1. The War Powers clock has run out.

Somewhere in Iran’s eastern desert, a cheetah is running.

THE NUMBERS

Dead: Iran — more than 3,000 including 1,700 civilians. Lebanon — roughly 2,500, still rising. Gravediggers in Majdal Zoun preparing 20 graves — 9 already filled, 11 waiting. Gulf states — 32. Israel — 23. Lebanon — at least 17 Israeli soldiers killed in Lebanon conflict, 2 Israeli civilians. American service members — 14 (14th confirmed at hearing: Maj. Sorffly Davius, Army National Guard, March 6). UNIFIL — 6 peacekeepers killed. Bondi Beach, Australia — 15 killed December 2025.

War cost: $25 billion official Pentagon estimate (first ever). ~$1 billion/day. First two days: $5.6 billion in munitions. $200 billion supplemental request — quietly disappeared. Hegseth requested $1.45 trillion defense budget. Lockheed Martin production expansion agreements: exist on paper, not funded, not yet producing.

Oil: Brent briefly above $126/barrel Thursday — four-year high. Settled ~$104-107. Up 40%+ since war began. Gas national average $4.30 — up 27 cents in one week, up 44% since war began. Diesel $5.50, up 46%. Fed held rates. Powell: “People are going to start to feel that.”

Hormuz: Araghchi delivered written Iranian proposal via Pakistan Sunday. Iran proposed: reopen strait, end blockade, set aside nuclear program entirely. Trump rejected it Monday. US position: 20-year nuclear suspension plus enriched uranium transfer. Gap: structural, not bridgeable in weeks. Iranian parliament introducing legislation to charge transit tolls. UAE left OPEC. Mubaraz tanker — first loaded LNG to cross Hormuz since Feb 28, transponder off for a month.

Munitions: Estonia HIMARS delivery suspended. Other European and Asian allies told same. Fewer US drilling rigs operating than when war started. Exxon and Chevron not changing production plans. BP profit doubled. Oil companies exercising capital discipline, not global responsibility.

Lebanon: Israel deploying fiber-optic jamming-immune drones — at least 1 soldier killed Thursday. Hezbollah gravediggers preparing 20 graves in Majdal Zoun. Three-week extension fraying. Hezbollah: will not disarm. Israel: continuing strikes and demolitions. 17 Israeli soldiers killed in Lebanon. Lebanon death toll ~2,500.

Hybrid war Europe: 12+ attacks in UK, Netherlands, Belgium, France, Germany claimed by Iran-linked group. 28 arrested in UK. London stabbing Wednesday — 2 Jewish men hospitalized. £25 million additional UK security funding. Australia Bondi Beach inquiry: police warned six days before. 15 killed.

War Powers: May 1. Expired today. Trump expected to ignore. Fifth war powers resolution blocked last week. No AUMF yet introduced. Murphy: harder for Republicans “to look the other way.”

Iran economy: Internet blackout Day 61, 90 million affected. IMF projects 70% inflation. Cooking oil smuggled across Turkish border for $2 profit. $300 billion in war damages (Iranian government estimate). Donya-e-Eghtesad: no-war-no-peace produces 70% inflation; return to war produces hyperinflation above 120%.

Asiatic cheetahs: 21 adults, 6 cubs recorded in 2026. Up from 17 known wild cats. Found only in Iran’s eastern desert. Conservationist Memarian arrested last week without disclosed charges. The cats persist.

Days of war: 61.
