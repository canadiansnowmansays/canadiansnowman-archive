Hatch Act Violations

Part 25: When the Law Doesn't Matter (2017-2020)

March 3, 2026

The Hatch Act, enacted in 1939, prohibits federal employees from engaging in political activities while on duty, in federal facilities, or using federal resources. The law exists to prevent abuse of government power for partisan political benefit and to protect the civil service from being transformed into a political machine.

For most administrations, Hatch Act compliance is routine. Federal employees understand the rules. When violations occur, they’re usually inadvertent and quickly corrected. The Office of Special Counsel (OSC)—an independent federal agency that enforces the Hatch Act—issues warnings, and employees comply.

Then came the Trump administration.

Trump and his team didn’t just violate the Hatch Act—they systematically shredded it. When the Office of Special Counsel found violations and recommended consequences, Trump simply refused to act. His message was clear: the law doesn’t apply to us. And because the Hatch Act relies on presidential enforcement for political appointees, Trump’s refusal meant no accountability.

This essay documents the Trump administration’s systematic Hatch Act violations, culminating in the unprecedented use of the White House itself as a backdrop for the 2020 Republican National Convention, transforming the people’s house into a campaign prop.

Kellyanne Conway: Serial Violator Without Consequences

Kellyanne Conway, the Trump administration’s most prolific Hatch Act violator, repeatedly used her official position for partisan attacks—facing no consequences despite OSC recommendations. Source: ABC News

Kellyanne Conway served as Counselor to the President from January 2017 to August 2020. In that time, she became the Trump administration’s most prolific and shameless Hatch Act violator.

The Pattern

Conway repeatedly used her official position to advocate for and against political candidates. She attacked Democratic candidates during television interviews while speaking in her official capacity. She promoted Trump’s re-election while speaking from the White House grounds. She made partisan political statements in her official role, violating the Hatch Act over and over.

The Office of Special Counsel investigated and issued findings:

June 2019: OSC issued a report finding Conway had violated the Hatch Act on numerous occasions by “disparaging Democratic presidential candidates while speaking in her official capacity during television interviews and on social media.”

OSC’s conclusion was damning: “Ms. Conway’s violations, if left unpunished, would send a message to all federal employees that they need not abide by the Hatch Act’s restrictions. Her actions thus erode the principal foundation of our democratic system—the rule of law.”

The Unprecedented Recommendation

OSC took an extraordinary step: they recommended that President Trump remove Conway from federal service. This recommendation was unprecedented—OSC had never before recommended removal of a White House official for Hatch Act violations.

Trump’s response? He refused. Conway remained in her position. Trump’s White House Counsel sent a letter to OSC essentially telling them Conway would continue violating the Hatch Act and there was nothing they could do about it.

The Aftermath

Conway mocked the Hatch Act violations as ‘blah, blah, blah’—embodying the administration’s attitude that laws don’t apply to them. Source: The Inquirer

Conway didn’t just continue in her role—she mocked the findings. When asked about OSC’s recommendation, she responded: “Blah, blah, blah... Let me know when the jail sentence starts.”

Her mockery captured the Trump administration’s attitude toward legal constraints: we can violate the law, and you can’t stop us.

Conway continued making partisan political statements in her official capacity until she left the White House in August 2020. She faced no consequences. The Hatch Act, for her, was a suggestion she felt free to ignore.

The 2020 Republican National Convention: Using the White House as a Campaign Prop

The most egregious Hatch Act violations occurred during the August 2020 Republican National Convention. Previous presidents had carefully separated official duties from campaign activities, understanding that using federal property and resources for campaign purposes violated both law and democratic norms.

Trump eliminated that separation entirely. He turned the White House—the people’s house, maintained by taxpayer dollars—into his campaign headquarters for four days.

The South Lawn Acceptance Speech

Setup for Trump’s 2020 RNC acceptance speech on the White House South Lawn, turning federal property into a campaign rally, a blatant Hatch Act violation. The Washington Post

On August 27, 2020, Trump delivered his nomination acceptance speech from the White House South Lawn. More than 1,500 people gathered on federal property for a partisan political rally. The White House grounds were decorated with campaign signage. The speech was pure campaign rhetoric—attacking Biden, promoting Trump’s re-election, making political promises.

This was unprecedented. No previous president had delivered a nomination acceptance speech from the White House. The location choice wasn’t practical necessity—it was a deliberate statement that Trump could use federal property for personal political benefit and no one would stop him.

Federal employees staffed the event. The Secret Service provided security for a campaign function. White House resources—paid for by American taxpayers—supported a partisan political rally. All clear Hatch Act violations.

The Naturalization Ceremony as Campaign Prop

Trump presiding over a naturalization ceremony at the White House, recorded and aired as RNC content—debasement of a federal process into a campaign prop, violating the Hatch Act. Source: The New York Times

During the RNC, Trump participated in a naturalization ceremony at the White House where five immigrants became American citizens. The ceremony was recorded and aired as part of the convention programming.

Using a federal ceremony as campaign content violated the Hatch Act and debased the naturalization process. These five people’s citizenship ceremony was turned into a Trump campaign advertisement. The footage implied that Trump personally had something to do with their citizenship, when in fact the ceremony would have occurred regardless of who was president.

Citizenship and Immigration Services employees were involved in arranging the ceremony. Federal officials participated. And the entire event was staged for campaign purposes—a textbook Hatch Act violation.

Mike Pompeo’s Jerusalem Speech

Secretary of State Mike Pompeo delivering his RNC speech from Jerusalem on official travel—ignoring ethics warnings and using federal resources for partisan politics. Source: Al Jazeera

Secretary of State Mike Pompeo delivered a recorded speech to the RNC from Jerusalem, where he was on official State Department travel. Pompeo spoke in his official capacity as Secretary of State, from a location he reached using federal resources, while on official government business.

Career State Department ethics officials had explicitly warned Pompeo that this would violate the Hatch Act and potentially violate federal regulations prohibiting use of federal resources for political activities. These warnings came from professional civil servants with decades of experience in government ethics. Pompeo ignored them and did it anyway.

The speech was broadcast during prime time at the RNC. Pompeo used his official position and federal resources to promote Trump’s re-election. Career State Department officials were appalled—previous secretaries had scrupulously avoided even the appearance of using their official positions for partisan politics.

Cabinet Officials Campaigning

Multiple Trump cabinet members actively campaigned for Trump’s re-election, often while on official travel or using official positions:

  • Sonny Perdue (Agriculture Secretary): Made partisan political statements at events in official capacity

  • Dan Brouillette (Energy Secretary): Made appearances at campaign events

  • Ben Carson (HUD Secretary): Participated in campaign activities

  • Chad Wolf (Acting DHS Secretary): Participated in RNC programming at the White House

Each instance involved federal officials using their positions or federal resources for campaign purposes—violations of the Hatch Act that in previous administrations would have triggered resignation or removal.

Why the Violations Mattered

The Hatch Act isn’t bureaucratic nitpicking. It serves crucial purposes:

Preventing Coercion: Federal employees shouldn’t fear that their jobs depend on political loyalty. The Hatch Act protects career civil servants from being pressured to support candidates.

Preserving Federal Resources: Taxpayers fund federal salaries, offices, and travel. Using those resources for campaign activities is theft—taking public resources for private political benefit.

Maintaining Trust: Citizens need to trust that federal employees serve the public interest, not partisan political interests. Systematic Hatch Act violations erode that trust.

Protecting Democratic Norms: Democracies require separation between governing and campaigning. When those lines blur, governments become political machines rather than public servants.

Trump’s systematic Hatch Act violations attacked all these principles. His administration demonstrated that laws protecting democratic norms mean nothing if the president refuses to enforce them.

The Enforcement Gap

The Hatch Act’s fatal flaw was revealed by Trump’s violations: the law relies on presidential enforcement for political appointees. The Office of Special Counsel can investigate and make recommendations, but only the president can remove political appointees.

When Trump refused to enforce the law—when he explicitly allowed Conway to continue violating it and when he used the White House itself for campaign purposes—the Hatch Act became unenforceable. OSC could document violations, but they couldn’t force consequences.

This created a precedent: future presidents now know they can violate the Hatch Act with impunity as long as they simply refuse to enforce it against their own appointees.

The Broader Pattern

Trump’s Hatch Act violations fit his larger pattern of treating laws as suggestions that don’t apply to him. Throughout his career and presidency, Trump’s consistent approach was:

  1. Violate the rule

  2. Deny wrongdoing

  3. Attack those who point out the violation

  4. Refuse to face consequences

  5. Dare anyone to stop you

This approach worked for Hatch Act violations because the enforcement mechanism relied on presidential integrity—something Trump utterly lacked.

The Hatch Act violations revealed Trump's contempt for laws separating governing from campaigning. He used federal resources for political purposes with impunity because the enforcement mechanism relied on presidential integrity he didn't possess.
