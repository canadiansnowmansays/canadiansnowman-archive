The Baseline Problem

The Diagnosis Medical Experts Won’t Say on Cable News

Part 9: When Frontotemporal Dementia Explains Everything

March 20, 2026

By mid-February, Trump was admitting he "fell asleep" during Iran war planning briefings. By March 16, he forgot he was president. Medical experts have stopped hedging: this isn't Alzheimer's. It's frontotemporal dementia—and it's accelerating. (AP Photo/Matthias Schrader)

March 20, 2026

In Part 7 of this series, I documented Donald Trump admitting to the Wall Street Journal that he wants “nice, thin blood” flowing through his heart. In Part 8, I showed how that cognitive dysfunction became American foreign policy—the “Dear Jonas” letter demanding Greenland and citing his Nobel Prize snub as justification for threatening a NATO ally.

Three weeks later, at the World Economic Forum in Davos, the international community watched the acceleration in real-time. And medical experts stopped using cautious language.

This is the story of what they said when the cameras were off. And why the diagnosis they’re documenting is far more terrifying than anyone on cable news is willing to explain.

The Davos Meltdown: When the World Was Watching

January 21-22, 2026. The World Economic Forum in Davos, Switzerland. The international stage where American presidents traditionally demonstrate strategic thinking and diplomatic acumen.

Donald Trump demonstrated something else entirely.

Multiple times during his appearances, Trump confused Iceland with Greenland Wikipedia—not just once, not in a single slip of the tongue, but repeatedly. He shared private text messages from world leaders publicly. He delivered rambling, epic speeches that left the assembled dignitaries visibly confused. He undertook a manic social media posting spree The Daily Beast.

And photographers captured something else: the worst bruising on his hand yet The Daily Beast, visible to cameras from around the world.

This wasn’t happening in a campaign rally in front of supportive crowds. This was happening in front of international leaders, diplomats, and journalists—people trained to observe, assess, and report. People who don’t sanewash.

The European press didn’t pretend this was normal. Neither did medical experts watching from the United States.

The Expert Alarm: When Clinical Language Replaced Caution

Dr Vin Gupta speaks at The Critical Path Forward for Affordable, Accessible Health as part of SXSW 2024 in Austin. Hubert Vestil/SXSW Conference & Festivals via

Dr. Vin Gupta is not a partisan commentator. He’s an NBC News medical analyst, former Chief Medical Officer at Amazon, and a pulmonary critical care physician. After Davos, Gupta said on camera: “It seems like it’s getting worse.” The Daily Beast

Not “there are concerns.” Not “some people worry.” “It’s getting worse.” The Daily Beast

He went further. The Nobel Prize letter to Norwegian Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre, Gupta said, “crossed a line of proper adult behavior” and should have triggered “a more thorough public assessment of his neurological fitness.” The Daily Beast

Then came the clinical assessment. Trump’s behavior, Gupta noted, is “consistent with” early Alzheimer’s disease or frontotemporal dementia The Daily Beast. He listed the symptoms: “The acting out, behavior changes, lashing out, acting unreasonable.” The Daily Beast

And then the conclusion: “One could say this is his core fundamental personality based on what we’ve seen over, frankly, his entire adult life, but it’s getting worse.” The Daily Beast

There it is again. The baseline problem, articulated by a medical professional: yes, he’s always been this way. But the deterioration from that baseline is now clinically observable.

Gupta wasn’t alone. But while he and other mainstream experts were carefully hedging with phrases like “consistent with,” a psychologist and cognitive neuroscientist was saying something far more specific—and far more terrifying.

The Diagnosis: Not Alzheimer’s. Frontotemporal Dementia.

Dr. Frank George explains what cable news won't: Trump's cognitive decline isn't Alzheimer's disease. It's frontotemporal dementia—and the disease is removing the inhibitions from his malignant narcissism. "As the dementia progresses, that disinhibition just gets worse and worse. His decisions will become more erratic, more vengeful. It's just getting expressed with no guard rails.”

Dr. Frank George has been studying Trump’s behavior for a decade. His Substack is called The Gaslight Report. And in early 2026, he made a clinical distinction that explains everything we’re watching.

This isn’t Alzheimer’s disease. It’s frontotemporal dementia. And the difference matters. Profoundly.

“God, I wish he had Alzheimer’s,” Dr. George said in an interview with David Pakman. “The world would be a better place if he had Alzheimer’s and was diminishing and going away like in Ronald Reagan’s last couple of years.”

Why would that be better?

“It would be better because he would be diminished and so his underlying personality disorder—the malignant narcissism—would be getting diminished and he’d be coming less in terms of what he was able to do, you know, just everything about his personality.”

But frontotemporal dementia does the opposite.

“With frontotemporal dementia, the person augments because it affects a different part of the brain.”

Let that sink in. Not diminishes. Augments.

The Brain Science: Why This Is Worse

Dr. George explained the critical difference:

“Alzheimer’s affects the hippocampus, mostly the memory areas of the brain. That’s why people forget. Frontotemporal dementia affects the frontal cortex. The areas of the brain, you know, from the frontal cortex are involved with judgment, inhibition of what one might want to do, the confabulations that we see. A lot of these things are regulated by the frontal cortex.”

He offered an analogy that makes the mechanism visceral:

“It’s like drinking. It’s almost like having too much to drink. You know, people say, ‘Oh, I have a couple drinks. I get uninhibited. I’m more social, sort of thing.’ Well, you’re becoming disinhibited. And you get to the point where, you know, the classic ‘hold my beer’ and then they do something really stupid. That type of disinhibition is exactly what we see with frontotemporal dementia.”

“But in Donald Trump’s case, what’s being disinhibited is his underlying malignant narcissism. And that’s the horrific danger of the whole situation.”

Read that again: the disease is removing the guardrails from his personality disorder.

Not making him forget things. Not making him gentler or more confused. Making him more of what he already was. More vengeful. More erratic. More dangerous.

And as the dementia progresses, Dr. George notes, “that disinhibition just gets worse and worse. His decisions will become more erratic, more vengeful. It’s just getting expressed with no guard rails.”

The Physical Symptoms Everyone Can See

Dr. George identified specific physical symptoms consistent with frontotemporal dementia that anyone can observe:

The forward lean: “The forward lean is another classic symptom. And like you said, it’s you think it’s physically impossible. It’s just part of the posture and that posture is consistent as a symptom.”

Trump’s distinctive posture—the wide stance with the torso pitched forward in a way that seems to defy physics—isn’t affectation or vanity. It’s a clinical symptom of frontotemporal dementia.

The gait: “The walking, the gait, the sort of swinging, the wide stance is also symptomatic.”

The shuffling: Multiple observers have noted Trump’s shuffling walk and occasional dragging of his right leg. These aren’t age-related wobbles. They’re consistent with frontotemporal dementia’s effect on motor control.

“That’s why all these symptoms—the physical symptoms and those behavioral symptoms—are consistent with FTD, frontotemporal dementia,” Dr. George concluded.

Anyone who’s watched Trump walk across the White House lawn or up the stairs to Air Force One has seen these symptoms. We just didn’t know what we were looking at.

The Speech Degradation

Then there’s the language collapse that anyone who’s followed Trump for years can hear.

“His entire speech is a symptom,” Dr. George said. “You know, he’s speaking at a fourth grade level typically. You know, where 10 or 15 years ago he was fairly eloquent. You might not like what he was saying, but he said it eloquently. That is just gone.”

“The words he uses now are—he repeats very simple words like ‘big,’ ‘great,’ ‘best,’ you know, over and over and over. They become a great part of his vocabulary. That’s symptomatic, actually. People don’t realize that.”

The paraphasias—the word substitutions and confusions like “obamna” for Obama—aren’t just senior moments. “That’s a symptom of frontotemporal dementia. It is not Alzheimer’s.”

Dr. George emphasized that people miss this because they’re looking at individual moments: “People will look at, well, a couple days ago he glitched in the middle of a speech. Yes. And that’s very serious. But his entire speech is a symptom.”

“When you combine the disinhibition on top of the underlying malignant narcissism, that’s where we start to see the escalation of the vengeance, the retribution, the just the random decisions that go back and forth like ‘I’m implementing 100% tariff.’ A week later he’s getting rid of the tariff. ‘We’re invading Greenland’ and he’s not invading Greenland. You know, just that type of thing that, you know, 10 years ago he just wouldn’t do those sorts of things.”

“That erratic behavior and everything that goes with that, the disinhibition is an augmenting of his underlying personality disorder. And that’s the horrific part of it because as the frontal temporal dementia progresses, that disinhibition just gets worse and worse.”

The Confabulation Problem: He Believes His Own Lies

Here’s what makes frontotemporal dementia even more dangerous in a president: the confabulations aren’t lies in the traditional sense. They’re beliefs.

“He does that pretty consistently,” Dr. George said about Trump’s confusion of people like Nikki Haley and Nancy Pelosi. “And part of what you can detect there is that he believes it. So he will repeat that error.”

Dr. George was asked directly: Is Trump a liar, or is he so confused that he comes to believe the things he says?

“I think he absolutely believes most of them.”

He explained: “Some of it is just, you know, the usual BS, you know, lying. But the things that he repeats over and over, you know, ‘I ended eight wars,’ you know, all that kind of stuff. He believes that. You can ask him that on a lie detector test and he’d pass it. I am absolutely convinced.”

Dr. George described it this way: “He’s truly living in his own reality. If he took a lie detector test and you asked him these questions, he would pass it.”

This isn’t a con artist selling lies. This is a brain manufacturing alternative realities and then believing them with complete conviction.

And you cannot reason with someone whose brain is generating false realities.

“You can’t really confront it because he believes it,” Dr. George noted. If you told Trump “Oh, come on, you didn’t end eight wars,” you’d just be arguing with him. “You wouldn’t get him to recognize that, ‘Oh, yeah, you’re right.’”

The confabulations have become Trump’s reality. And no amount of fact-checking or confrontation will change that.

February: When He Admitted Falling Asleep During War Planning

At a rally in Kentucky in mid-February, Trump revealed something that should have dominated headlines for weeks.

While discussing the Iran military operation, Trump told the crowd about choosing the operation’s name. Military leaders presented him with options. “They gave me like 20 names,” he said. “Then I’m like falling asleep. I didn’t like any of them. Then I see Epic Fury. I said, ‘There you go.’”

The President of the United States admitted he was falling asleep during war planning briefings.

Not at a state dinner. Not during a ceremonial event. During the briefing where military leaders were presenting operational plans for strikes that would kill Iranian military personnel and potentially escalate into regional war.

He was “falling asleep.”

The crowd laughed. The media moved on. No one asked: if the Commander in Chief is falling asleep while generals present war plans, what does that tell us about his cognitive state during actual crisis decision-making?

At the same rally, Trump struggled to pronounce “Kentucky,” repeatedly stumbling over the word before finally giving up and making a joke about it. Someone in the crowd fainted—Dr. Oz was summoned as the medical professional in attendance—and Trump requested they play “Ave Maria” but his staff wasn’t prepared.

These weren’t gaffes. They were symptoms.

And by the time anyone paying attention connected the dots, we were already in March.

The Intimacy Slip: “Jeffrey’s Island”

On March 17, 2026, Trump was asked about Trevor Noah’s Grammy comments about Epstein. Trump responded: “He said that I spent time on Jeffrey’s—Jeffrey Epstein’s Island. I didn’t.”

Not “Epstein’s island.” Not “Jeffrey Epstein’s island.” “Jeffrey’s... Jeffrey Epstein’s Island.”

The correction came after the intimacy slipped out.

“Jeffrey’s island” is how you refer to a place belonging to someone you know personally. Someone you have a relationship with. Someone whose first name is sufficient identification.

It’s how you’d say “I went to Michael’s house” when talking about your friend. Not “Michael Smith’s house” when talking about an acquaintance you barely know.

Trump has spent years insisting he barely knew Epstein. That they weren’t close. That he had no relationship beyond casual social proximity at events.

But his linguistic instinct—the thing that emerges when you’re not fully controlling what you say—reveals familiarity. Intimacy. The kind of casual reference you make to someone who’s been part of your life.

It’s a small detail. But frontotemporal dementia’s disinhibition doesn’t just affect major decisions. It affects filters. The things you normally don’t say—the things you’ve trained yourself not to say for years—become the things that slip out.

The guardrails are gone.

Why This Diagnosis Changes Everything

If Trump had Alzheimer’s, here’s what we’d be watching:

  • Progressive memory loss

  • Gradual difficulty with complex tasks

  • Eventually, inability to recognize people

  • Softening of personality as cognitive function declines

  • A clear trajectory toward incapacity that would make the 25th Amendment inevitable

But frontotemporal dementia means:

  • His narcissism is being amplified, not diminished

  • His impulse control is disappearing, not just weakening

  • His reality testing is failing, not just declining

  • His vengefulness is escalating, not moderating

  • His confabulations are becoming his reality

  • His physical coordination is deteriorating visibly

And there’s no floor. Because the disease removes inhibition progressively.

What he might have thought but not said a year ago, he says now.

What he might have said but not done six months ago, he does now.

What he might have done but walked back three months ago, he doubles down on now.

The trajectory isn’t toward incapacity that everyone can recognize. It’s toward uninhibited malignant narcissism with nuclear codes.

The Medical Professor’s Assessment: “Week Over Week”

On February 6, 2026, a medical professor gave an interview that crystallized what everyone watching had been seeing. The doctor described “deterioration almost week over week” in both Trump’s physical presentation and his speech patterns.

The conclusion: “The rate of decline is accelerating.”

Not just declining. Not just showing symptoms. The rate itself is accelerating.

This is what neurologists mean when they talk about progressive cognitive disorders. The decline doesn’t proceed in a straight line. It curves. It accelerates. What takes six months to manifest in the early stages might take six weeks in the middle stages. Then six days.

A Slate article examining the timeline noted that different forms of cognitive impairment progress at different rates, but pointed out a key fact: if Trump had shown the beginnings of neurodegenerative-caused dementia in 2017—when experts first noted his language struggles—”it would be impossible to hide by now.” Slate

The expert quoted there, Gill Livingston, suggested that if this were full dementia, Trump would now be finding it difficult to write even an incoherent letter about the Nobel Peace Prize or even to remember what the Nobel Peace Prize is Slate.

But that doesn’t rule out what we’re actually observing: cognitive impairment that’s getting progressively worse.

As researcher Carolyn Aldwin put it: “He clearly has difficulties.” Slate

And those difficulties are accelerating.

What We’re Watching

In Part 1 of this series, I opened with Lawrence O’Donnell’s question: “If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?”

Now we have the answer: we’ll know when psychologists and cognitive neuroscientists start documenting that it’s not Alzheimer’s—it’s frontotemporal dementia. When they explain that the disease doesn’t diminish the person, it augments them. When they detail the physical symptoms, the speech degradation, the confabulation mechanism.

When they explain that what’s being disinhibited is malignant narcissism.

And when the rate of decline starts accelerating week over week.

The baseline problem made early detection almost impossible. Trump’s unconventional baseline meant that behaviors that would have been alarming in anyone else were dismissed as “just Trump being Trump.”

But Dr. Frank George has given us the clinical framework to understand what we’re watching. This isn’t normal aging. It isn’t stress. It isn’t even Alzheimer’s.

It’s frontotemporal dementia removing the inhibitions from a malignant narcissist who controls the world’s largest military.

And it’s accelerating.

Seventy-four days after admitting he wants “nice, thin blood.”
