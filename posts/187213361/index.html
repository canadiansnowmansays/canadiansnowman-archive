The Asimov Gap

The Children Question

Part 12: Raising the AGI Generation

February 13, 2026

When Computers Save Us From Caring

A friend recently attended a funeral. Midway through the eulogy, he became convinced it had been written by AI.

The signs were there: a proliferation of abstract nouns, a surfeit of assertions that the deceased was “not just X—he was Y” coupled with a conspicuous lack of concrete anecdotes, and more appearances of the word “collaborate” than you would expect from a rec-league hockey teammate. The eulogy was both too good—grammatically flawless—and not good enough—utterly impersonal.

My friend had no definitive proof. But as Dan Brooks observed in The Atlantic, “when you know, you know.” My friend’s sense was that he had just heard a computer save a man from thinking about his dead friend.

This is what we’re teaching our children.

Not explicitly. Not intentionally. But through a thousand small acts of optimization, efficiency, and outsourced effort, we’re demonstrating that caring is optional when AI is available.

The AGI generation: For children born after 2020, AI isn’t futuristic—it’s just there. Like electricity. Like running water. The question isn’t whether they’ll use it, but what they’ll become while using it.

The children question isn’t whether the next generation will grow up with AGI—they will. It’s what kind of humans they’ll become when machines can fake the very things that make us human: thinking, caring, connecting.

And whether we’ll teach them the difference.

The Proof-of-Work Problem

Traditional education operates on a simple principle: you learn by doing the work.

When a college student writes a term paper on the role of class in Wuthering Heights, the point isn’t to inform the professor—the professor already knows. The point is that putting what you’ve learned into words clarifies your understanding to both your instructor and yourself. Writing is how you discover what you actually think.

AI destroys this learning mechanism.

A Microsoft survey found that 52% of people using AI at work are reluctant to admit using it for “important tasks,” presumably because it might make them look replaceable. Students face the same calculus with even higher stakes: they know they should be ashamed of using ChatGPT to write their essays, but they can’t resist the efficiency.

The result is an asymmetric burden that Dan Brooks identifies as fundamental to bad etiquette: AI-generated content takes seconds to produce but minutes to read. It asks other people to work harder so you can work less.

A tutor Brooks knows sends weekly progress updates to parents. One parent replied with a 3,000-word email featuring section headings, bolded student names, and all the hallmarks of ChatGPT. It almost certainly took seconds to generate but minutes to read. The message it sent was clear: “I want the relational benefits of caring about my child’s education, but I don’t care enough to actually write this myself.”

Now imagine millions of students learning this is normal. They’re not learning the material—they’re learning to outsource thinking. They’re getting good grades without developing the cognitive capabilities those assignments were designed to create.

The social contract of education breaks down when nobody believes anyone did the work.

Worse: students know they’re not learning. Parents know they’re not engaging. Teachers know they’re receiving AI slop. Everyone knows, and everyone participates anyway, because efficiency has become more valuable than education.

We’re raising a generation that knows outsourcing thought is wrong but can’t resist the optimization.

The Developmental Cost

The stakes are higher than just missing educational content. We’re talking about brain development itself.

Neuroscience is clear: the human brain develops through effortful practice during formative years, roughly ages 5 to 25. Neural pathways form through repeated struggle with difficult tasks. The myelin sheaths that make thinking faster and more efficient develop through use. Complex reasoning, creative problem-solving, critical analysis—these capabilities aren’t innate. They’re built through thousands of hours of cognitive effort during the years when brains are most plastic.

It’s “use it or lose it” at a fundamental level.

Children learning language illustrate this perfectly. A five-year-old picks up a new language effortlessly, developing native-level fluency in months. An adult struggles for years and rarely achieves the same fluency. The difference isn’t effort—it’s developmental timing. The neural architecture for language acquisition is most malleable in early childhood.

The same principle applies to critical thinking.

When a 15-year-old struggles to understand a difficult concept in physics or literature or history, their brain is building the neural pathways for complex reasoning. The struggle isn’t a bug—it’s the feature. The confusion, the frustration, the gradual clarity as understanding emerges: these experiences physically reshape the developing brain.

If AI does the thinking during these formative years, those pathways don’t develop.

We’re not just creating a generation that can’t think without AI. We’re potentially creating a generation neurologically incapable of certain types of complex thought. The brain architecture that should have formed through effortful practice during adolescence simply won’t be there.

This isn’t reversible. You can’t go back at 25 and develop the neural infrastructure that should have formed at 15. Adult brains can learn, but they can’t rebuild fundamental cognitive architecture.

The student who uses ChatGPT for every essay from ages 12 to 18 isn’t just missing six years of writing practice. They’re missing the developmental window when their brain should have been building the capacity for organizing complex thoughts, constructing logical arguments, and expressing nuanced ideas.

That window doesn’t reopen.

What schools were designed for versus what students need now. The gap between preparing for stable careers and developing uniquely human capabilities grows wider every year. AI can follow instructions and perform routine tasks better than any human. What it can’t do is make moral judgments, build genuine relationships, or act with courage.

The Care Deficit: When Relationships Become Transactions

The problem extends far beyond homework.

Apple’s iOS 26 introduced an AI feature that summarizes text messages in notifications and lists. Before turning it off, Brooks watched it reduce a group chat—friends joking about a spooky attic door, building connection through shared humor—into this summary: “a conversation about a wooden room.”

The AI removed everything entertaining about the chat to reduce it to bare information exchange. Everything human was stripped away in the name of efficiency.

This represents a fundamental misunderstanding of what communication is for. When your brother texts “How’s it going?”, he’s not seeking information so much as connection. That connection is thwarted if you ask ChatGPT to draft a 50-word reply about how his baby is cute and you love him.

As Brooks puts it: “To prevent hard-core get-it-done types from inflicting slop on the rest of us, we need to agree that my sending you material written by ChatGPT is insulting, the same way you would be insulted if I were to play a recording of myself saying ‘Oh, that’s interesting’ every time you spoke.”

Writing is an act of taking care.

College students write term papers not just to demonstrate knowledge, but because the struggle clarifies their thinking. People write eulogies not to inform attendees about the deceased, but because composing those words forces you to think deeply about the relationship—and demonstrates ongoing commitment even after you can derive no benefit from it. Our goalie is dead, but we care enough to keep thinking about him even after he will stop no earthly puck.

AI removes the caring from acts of care.

And children are watching. They see parents use AI to respond to teacher emails, to reply to friends’ messages, to write thank-you notes. They learn that relationships can be “optimized.” That caring is optional. That efficiency trumps authenticity.

The Social Pressure Cooker

The pressure operates at every level, and it’s crushing.

The student who refuses to use AI for homework gets worse grades than classmates who do. Their parents see the falling GPA, compare it to siblings or neighbors, and pressure them to “use all available resources.” What they mean is: use AI like everyone else. The student who wants to learn authentically is now penalized for their integrity.

In friend groups, the dynamics become toxic. Everyone assumes everyone else might be using AI for messages. Did she actually write that thoughtful birthday text? Or is she just better at prompting ChatGPT? The uncertainty corrodes trust. You can’t tell genuine caring from optimized efficiency.

Nobody wants to be the first to admit they’re using AI—it feels like admitting you’re a fraud. But nobody wants to be the fool who isn’t using it while everyone else is. So everyone uses it, everyone suspects everyone else is using it, and nobody trusts that anyone actually means what they write.

The messages still get sent. The homework still gets done. The relationships maintain their outward forms. But the substance hollows out.

A friend told me recently: “I spent 20 minutes writing a message to my friend about her breakup. Then I wondered if she’d use AI to read it. Then I wondered if she’d think I used AI to write it. Then I felt stupid for spending 20 minutes on something she might not even believe I wrote. Next time I’ll just use ChatGPT.”

This is the race to the bottom. Each person’s decision to use AI makes everyone else’s authentic effort feel foolish. The individually rational choice—save time, use AI, stay competitive—produces a collectively destructive outcome where nobody believes anyone actually cares.

And children are learning this is normal. They’re developing relationships in an environment where trust is impossible because authenticity is unverifiable. Where caring must be performed but cannot be proven. Where the efficient fake is indistinguishable from the effortful real.

What happens to humans who never learn to trust that others care about them?

Judgment in ambiguity. Human connection. Moral courage. AI can simulate empathy, follow rules, and process information brilliantly. But it can’t make moral judgments, form genuine bonds, or act with courage. These remain uniquely human—and matter more as AI gets better at everything else.

What Children Lose: The Invisible Erosion

The erosion happens generationally, and it’s irreversible.

Parents who remember the pre-AI world know what’s being lost: the struggle that develops critical thinking, the inefficient caring that builds empathy, the proof-of-work that makes relationships authentic. We can feel the difference between a human-written message and AI-generated text, even when we can’t articulate exactly what’s wrong.

But children growing up with AI won’t have that reference point. They won’t know what they never had.

They can’t miss what they never experienced.

Like social media natives who never knew life without constant connectivity, AI natives won’t remember when thinking required struggle, when caring required inefficiency, when relationships required work. The difference is deeper: social media changed how we connect; AI changes our capacity for thought and care itself.

What, specifically, do children lose?

Critical thinking developed through struggle. When you can ask ChatGPT for the answer, why wrestle with a difficult concept yourself? But it’s the wrestling that builds the neural pathways. The confusion, the frustration, the gradual clarity—these aren’t inefficiencies to be optimized away. They’re how human minds develop.

Empathy developed through inefficient caring. Caring requires effort. Writing a thoughtful message to a friend takes time. Remembering someone’s birthday and choosing a meaningful gift requires attention. These inefficiencies are the substance of relationships. Outsource them to AI, and you’ve outsourced the caring itself.

Authenticity when everyone assumes AI. If every message might be AI-generated, no message carries the weight of genuine human effort. The same way inflation devalues currency, AI-everything devalues human expression. Children growing up in this environment will struggle to trust that anyone actually cares about them personally.

The capacity to care inefficiently—which is the only way to actually care. A computer takes no care. It processes, outputs, optimizes. It cannot care, because caring inherently involves inefficiency—spending more time than necessary, thinking more deeply than required, connecting more genuinely than optimal.

Remove the inefficiency, and you remove the caring. AI makes it possible to perform all the outward motions of caring—responding promptly, saying the right things, maintaining contact—while removing the actual human effort that makes caring meaningful.

The Classroom Arms Race

Ask teachers what’s actually happening in classrooms, and you’ll hear variations on the same story.

“I’m not teaching anymore. I’m refereeing an arms race between AI generation and AI detection.”

Students who struggled all semester suddenly submit perfect essays. The teacher knows it’s AI-written—the vocabulary is too sophisticated, the structure too polished, the voice nothing like the student’s previous work. But they can’t prove it. AI detection tools flag the essay as “possibly AI-generated” (95% confidence), but students have learned to run AI text through “humanizer” tools that introduce just enough variation to fool the detectors.

The teacher faces an impossible choice: Fail the student based on suspicion? That invites appeals, angry parents, administrative pressure. Or accept that grading is now meaningless? That teaching has become performance art while AI does the actual learning work?

Most teachers choose the latter. They adjust. Lower their expectations. Accept that they’re no longer evaluating student learning—they’re evaluating student ability to prompt AI effectively.

Meanwhile, students openly discuss which AI tools beat which detection systems. Which teachers actually check. Which assignments are safe to fully outsource. The smart students maintain a portfolio of “authentic” writing—usually from early in the semester, before they discovered AI—to establish a baseline voice. Then they prompt AI to mimic that voice for later assignments.

There’s a class divide emerging too. Wealthy students have access to better AI tools, private tutors who teach “AI literacy” (meaning: how to use AI without getting caught), and parents who can hire experts to contest any accusations. Poor students use free ChatGPT and get caught more easily.

The schools respond by banning AI. But the bans are unenforceable. Students use AI at home. Parents help them use AI. The technology is too accessible, too useful, too normalized to ban effectively.

Some teachers try to adapt. They assign in-class essays, oral presentations, projects that require showing work. But there aren’t enough class hours to do everything in person. And ChatGPT can now help students prepare oral presentations, generating outlines and talking points indistinguishable from authentic preparation.

The truth is simpler and darker than schools want to admit: If students want to use AI, and parents don’t object, and the work product is indistinguishable from authentic student work, then traditional academic evaluation is dead.

We can pretend students are learning. We can maintain the forms of education—assignments submitted, grades recorded, diplomas awarded. But everyone involved knows the substance has leaked away.

The students know they’re not learning. The teachers know they’re not teaching. The parents know their children are outsourcing development. Everyone knows, and everyone participates anyway, because nobody knows how to stop the arms race once it’s started.

The Counter-Argument: Haven’t We Heard This Before?

“Kids have always found shortcuts. CliffsNotes, Wikipedia, asking the smart kid for answers. This is no different.”

“Technology always disrupts education. Calculators, spell-check, Google. We adapted before.”

“AI can enhance learning, not replace it. It’s a tool like any other.”

All true to a point. But the differences matter.

CliffsNotes let you skip reading the book; AI lets you skip thinking about it. Calculators replaced arithmetic; AI replaces reasoning. Google provided information; AI provides thought itself.

More critically: previous tools were visible. You knew when you used CliffsNotes instead of reading. You knew when you copied someone’s homework. The shame was possible because the shortcut was obvious.

AI is invisible. You might not know when your friend’s heartfelt message was written by ChatGPT. Your teacher can’t definitively prove your essay is AI-generated. The deception becomes undetectable, which means shame becomes impossible, which means the behavior normalizes.

The final difference: previous technologies augmented human capability. AI replaces human effort entirely. A calculator makes math faster; you still had to understand the problem. AI writes the entire essay; you understand nothing except how to prompt the machine.

This isn’t adaptation. It’s substitution.

What We Owe Our Children

We can’t stop children from accessing AI. That’s impossible and probably counterproductive—they need to understand these tools. We can’t prevent AI from getting more capable. The trajectory is clear.

But we can decide what we teach them about being human.

The question isn’t whether to use AI. It’s what values we model while using it.

We can teach them that efficiency isn’t virtue. That some things should take time. That thinking slowly is valuable. That struggling with difficult concepts builds capacity. That “I could have used AI but I didn’t” is something to be proud of, not ashamed of.

We can teach them that caring requires inefficiency. That relationships aren’t transactions to be optimized. That writing a thoughtful message takes effort precisely because it demonstrates you care. That your time and attention are the most valuable things you can give someone.

We can teach them to value proof-of-work. That doing something yourself matters. That the struggle is the point. That shortcuts might save time but cost something more important—the development of your own capabilities, the authenticity of your relationships, the meaning in your work.

We can build social norms where using AI to avoid caring is shameful. Not using AI for research, or drafting, or brainstorming—those are legitimate uses. But using AI to fake caring about people, to fake doing your own thinking, to fake engaging with education? That should carry social cost.

We can create educational systems that reward thinking, not output. Oral exams. In-class essays. Projects that require showing your work. Assignments where the product matters less than the process. Systems where AI-generated work is obviously insufficient because it lacks the human struggle that’s being evaluated.

Most importantly: we can model the difference. Children don’t primarily learn from what we say. They learn from what we do. If they see us using AI to avoid writing thank-you notes, responding to friends, engaging with their teachers—they learn that caring is optional.

If they see us choosing to write things ourselves, to think through problems without AI shortcuts, to value human connection over optimized efficiency—they learn that humanity has worth beyond productivity.

The 20-Year Timeline

Let me show you what this looks like in practice. Follow one child through the next two decades.

Age 10 (2026): Emma uses ChatGPT for her math homework “just to check answers.” She tells herself she’s learning—she reads the AI’s solutions before submitting them. Her grades improve from B’s to A’s. Her parents see the report card and praise her for “working harder.” They don’t ask whether she’s actually doing the work.

Age 12 (2028): Emma now uses AI for most assignments. She’s learned which teachers check carefully (Mr. Rodriguez, who makes students explain their thinking) and which don’t (Ms. Chen, who just grades final products). She knows how to prompt AI to sound more like her—casual language, occasional errors, her particular quirks. Her friend group communicates partly through AI-assisted messages. Long thoughtful texts are AI-drafted, then tweaked slightly. Nobody is sure anymore who actually wrote what.

Age 15 (2031): Emma hasn’t written an original essay in three years. When Mr. Rodriguez assigns an in-class writing exercise, she stares at the blank page in panic. She literally can’t remember how to develop an argument from scratch. The neural pathways that should have formed through hundreds of hours of writing practice never developed. They’re just gone.

She can’t struggle through difficult concepts anymore—she’s always asked AI for explanations. When she encounters something confusing, her first instinct isn’t to think harder, it’s to ask ChatGPT. The capacity for sustained cognitive struggle, for holding confusion in her mind until clarity emerges, never developed. She missed the window.

Her relationships feel transactional. She assumes everyone uses AI for everything. Trust is theoretical—something she’s heard about but never experienced. When her best friend sends a heartfelt message about Emma’s grandmother’s death, Emma can’t tell if it’s genuine or AI-generated. The possibility that it might be fake makes the comfort feel hollow.

Age 18 (2034): College applications. Emma’s AI writes her essays, generates her resume, crafts her letters of recommendation (her teachers don’t have time to write 30 personalized letters, so they tell students to draft their own for teacher signature—and everyone knows “draft your own” means “have AI draft it”).

She gets into a good school based on an AI-generated profile. But she can’t actually do college-level work without AI. Doesn’t matter—her classmates are in the same situation. Professors have learned to teach to students who outsource thinking. Assignments are restructured around what AI can’t do (in-class presentations, group projects with peer accountability). But there’s only so much you can fit into contact hours.

Age 22 (2038): Emma enters the workforce. Everyone uses AI for everything. She writes emails with AI, conducts research with AI, generates reports with AI. Her manager uses AI to review her AI-generated work. Meetings are scheduled by AI assistants negotiating with other AI assistants. Nobody actually talks to each other except in required face-to-face meetings where everyone reads from AI-generated talking points.

Her relationships are optimized. Her dating app profile is AI-perfected. Her messages are AI-crafted to maximize engagement. Her social media presence is AI-curated. Communication is efficient. Nobody believes anyone actually means anything. Trust is zero.

The forms of human connection remain—dates happen, friendships exist, coworkers collaborate. The substance is gone. Nobody has the capacity for authentic caring because nobody developed it during the years when that capacity should have formed.

Age 30 (2046): Emma has children. She uses AI to write messages to their teachers, respond to their developmental milestones, communicate with her spouse about family decisions. She reads bedtime stories generated by AI, customized for each child’s interests. She never learned to care inefficiently—the neural architecture for that kind of effort-intensive, reward-delayed connection never developed.

She knows something is missing. She remembers, dimly, being 10 years old and writing things herself. But she can’t articulate what was lost. The capacity to recognize authentic human effort as valuable—distinct from and superior to AI-optimized efficiency—that capacity requires having experienced it yourself. Emma never did.

She raises her children the same way she was raised. They use AI from age 6. The cycle continues, deeper. Each generation more removed from the experience of effortful thinking, inefficient caring, authentic struggle.

This isn’t dystopian speculation. This is straight-line extrapolation from current trends if nothing changes.

Every element exists today: students using AI for homework, detection systems being beaten, teachers unable to prove cheating, parents encouraging efficiency, relationships conducted through AI-mediated communication.

The only speculative part is the timeline. How fast does this happen? Does Emma’s story unfold over 20 years or 10 years or 5?

The trajectory is set. The only question is speed.

We know social media correlates with teen depression. We know screen addiction affects brain development. We know physical activity and sleep suffer when screens dominate. But we don’t know the long-term effects of growing up with AI companions, or the optimal balance between AI assistance and independent thinking, or whether digital natives develop different cognitive patterns. Parents are making decisions with incomplete information. Their children are the experiment.

The Asimov Warning We Ignored

In Asimov’s robot stories, robots do physical labor while humans maintain purpose through intellectual and emotional work. The stories assume robots would free humans for higher pursuits: art, philosophy, relationships, creative problem-solving.

Asimov never imagined we’d outsource those pursuits themselves.

He never pictured robots writing our poetry, conducting our friendships, doing our thinking. In his future, robots made life easier so humans could focus on being human. In ours, AI makes being human easier—which risks making us less human.

The Three Laws of Robotics were designed to prevent robots from harming humans. But Asimov recognized that even well-intentioned assistance could diminish human agency. In “The Evitable Conflict,” machines running the economy optimally have been subtly manipulating humans to accept machine control. The manipulation is beneficial—preventing war, ensuring prosperity—but it destroys human autonomy.

We’re doing the same thing, but we’re doing it to ourselves. And we’re doing it to our children.

The Children Question

We can’t protect children from AGI. It’s coming, it’s already partially here, and by the time today’s children are adults, AI capabilities will be orders of magnitude beyond what we have now.

But we can decide what kind of humans they become in that world.

Do we raise children who believe:

  • Efficiency is virtue, caring is optional, thinking is outsourceable?

  • Relationships are transactions to be optimized?

  • Authenticity matters less than optimization?

  • Human effort has no special value?

Or do we raise children who understand:

  • Connection requires inefficiency?

  • Relationships require care?

  • Humanity requires effort?

  • Some things shouldn’t be delegated to machines?

The children question isn’t about AI’s capabilities. Those will advance regardless of what we choose.

It’s about what we teach the next generation about being human in an age of machines that can fake humanity convincingly.

It’s about whether we’ll teach them the difference between a computer saving us time and a computer saving us from caring.

It’s about whether we’ll model the values that make caring, thinking, and connecting worthwhile—or whether we’ll demonstrate that these can all be optimized away.

As Dan Brooks writes in The Atlantic: “A computer takes no care. We should not let it take the experience of caring away from us.”

That’s the lesson children need. Not that AI is dangerous. Not that technology is evil. But that being human—really human—requires inefficiency, struggle, and care that cannot be outsourced.

The children question is whether we’ll teach them that before they forget it’s even possible.
