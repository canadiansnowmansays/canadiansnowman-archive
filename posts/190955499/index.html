Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 16: The Last Word — What Cuba Became

April 15, 2026

Havana during one of the nationwide blackouts that plunged the entire island into darkness in March 2026. For millions of Cubans, this meant fifteen hours a day without power in tropical heat — struggling to cook, fetch water, or simply endure. The cost, as always, fell on ordinary people while decisions were made in rooms they were not invited into.

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

This series began in February 2026 and is concluding in April of the same year. That is an unusual thing to say about a work of historical writing — that it was written while its final chapter was still being lived. Most histories have the advantage of an outcome. They know how the story ends before they begin the telling. This one does not, and that fact is worth acknowledging directly rather than disguising behind false resolution.

What has happened to Cuba between the first essay and this one is documented in the preceding fifteen parts. A blockade, effective for the first time since the Cuban Missile Crisis. A humanitarian crisis of a severity that Cubans had not experienced since the Special Period of the 1990s. A national power grid that failed entirely, plunging the whole island into darkness at once. Secret negotiations between a Cuban intelligence official known as The Crab and an American Secretary of State who had spent his career calling for the Cuban government’s destruction. The Vatican in the middle, providing the institutional trust that sixty-seven years of hostility had made impossible to maintain bilaterally. A demand from Washington that Cuba’s president must go — not as an ultimatum, but as the stated price of any meaningful progress. A Russian state tanker bearing oil steaming toward Cuba under American naval surveillance, its outcome unknown as this was written. A hemisphere that looked at what was happening to ten million people on an island ninety miles from Florida and, one country at a time, decided it could not afford to help. And underneath all of it, a vision articulated by one administration official with the clarity of a confession: not liberation, not democracy, but Chapter 11 — a country reorganization, the whole focus on business.

What happens next — whether the negotiations produce a deal, whether the deal holds, whether Cuba enters a managed transition or a chaotic collapse or some third thing that none of the analysts predicting imminent regime change in 1993 and 2000 and 2006 and 2021 managed to foresee — is not known as these words are written. It will be known by the time they are read. The reader, at this point, knows more than the writer did.

That is appropriate. It is also, in a strange way, the point.

What This Series Was About

This series was not, at its core, about the blockade. It was not about Trump or Rubio or the endgame negotiations or the question of whether Cuba’s Communist Party can survive another crisis. Those things are in the series because they are the context of the moment that triggered the writing. But they are not the subject.

The subject was stated in the Introduction and has not changed: what it has cost ordinary Cubans to live on this island across five and a half centuries.

That question turns out to be answerable, at least in outline. The cost has been enormous, sustained, and borne almost entirely by people who had no meaningful say in the decisions that imposed it. The Taíno paid with their existence. Enslaved Africans paid with their freedom, their families, and their lives across three centuries of plantation labor. The mambises paid with thirty years of war against a colonial power that had already defeated every previous challenge. The Cuban people of the early twentieth century paid with the betrayal of the independence they had nearly won, handed back to Washington in a different colonial arrangement. The generations who lived under Batista paid with the corruption and brutality that the Revolution was a genuine response to, whatever one thinks of what the Revolution became. Those who suffered under the Revolution’s authoritarianism — in the UMAP camps, in the prisons, in the managed silences of a society where saying the wrong thing had consequences — paid with the freedom that the Revolution promised and did not deliver. The balseros of the 1990s paid with their lives crossing the Florida Straits on inner tubes. The 2.75 million who have left since 2020 paid with their country. And the people who remain in Cuba in 2026 are paying with darkness — literal, physical darkness, fifteen hours a day, in the tropical heat, while the men who made the decisions that created this darkness sit in air-conditioned rooms on two sides of a negotiating table and calculate their interests.

Knowing this does not resolve it. Understanding the pattern does not break it. But this series has argued, from its first pages, that the minimum requirement for honest engagement with Cuba’s situation is to see the human beings inside it clearly — not as geopolitical abstractions, not as pieces in a strategic argument, not as the instrument of someone else’s political narrative, but as people with names and lives and the full complement of human dignity that every political system professes to honor and most of them, at one point or another, fail to protect.

Three Cubans

Streets of Havana amid fuel shortages and blackouts, 2026. For people like Daimarilis waiting for buses that never came or Luis David unable to run his tools, every day revolved around ‘blackouts all the time… everything is difficult.

On March 13, 2026, the day Díaz-Canel sat in front of the cameras and acknowledged for the first time that Cuba was talking to the United States, journalists in Havana went into the streets to ask ordinary Cubans what they thought.

Miriam Martínez is sixty-four years old. She works in a cigar factory. She told reporters that she goes thirteen to fifteen hours at a stretch without power, which makes cooking nearly impossible and leaves people without water. She said she supports the Cuban government’s socialist system. She also said: “The country doesn’t have the solution to resolve our problem.”

That sentence — holding both things simultaneously, the loyalty and the honest reckoning — is one of the most Cuban sentences this series has encountered. It does not resolve into a simple political position. It refuses the binary that American Cuba discourse has always demanded: you are either for the Revolution or against it, either a patriot or a dissident, either a subject of a brutal dictatorship or a defender of a sovereign state resisting imperial aggression. Miriam Martínez, sixty-four, cigar factory worker, is none of those things exclusively. She is a person trying to cook dinner without electricity and waiting for someone to fix a problem that is too large for her and too large, she knows, for her government.

Daimarilis Guillot Reyes is twenty-seven. She is a computer science engineer. On the morning of March 13, she spent several hours waiting for a bus that never came. “In the end, it’s the people who suffer,” she said. “Right now, it’s blackouts all the time. Blackouts, blackouts. I think that negotiation would be a feasible way to find a solution in the future.”

She did not say which negotiation, or on what terms, or what she thought a solution should look like. She said blackouts all the time, and she said it twice, and the repetition tells you something about what her days are like — the specific exhaustion of a problem that does not go away, that is there when you wake up in the morning and there when you go to bed at night, that structures your entire life around its absence.

Luis David Marrero de Alma is twenty years old. He is a mechanic. He cannot use his welding machine or his polisher because there is no power to run them. He is buying gasoline on the black market at the equivalent of thirty-five dollars a gallon. “I wish there would be a change,” he said, “because things can’t go on like this. And everything is difficult, everything. Well, it’s not just the oil, everything. The blackouts, everything.”

Streets of Havana under strain during the deepening 2026 crisis — garbage piling up, fewer cars, heavier days. This is what decades of compounded failures looked like on the ground: not abstractions, but people trying to get through another day without electricity or reliable water.

Everything, everything, everything. The young man’s repetition has a different register than the engineer’s — less analytical, more raw, the language of someone who has not yet found the words for a situation that exceeds the available vocabulary. He is twenty years old. He was born three years after the Special Period ended. He has lived his entire adult life in a Cuba that has been in various stages of crisis, managed by a government that alternates between defiance and negotiation, under the pressure of an embargo that has been in place for more than three times as long as he has been alive.

He wishes there would be a change. He does not specify what kind.

These three Cubans — a sixty-four-year-old factory worker, a twenty-seven-year-old engineer, a twenty-year-old mechanic — are the people this series was written about and written for. Not for the analysts or the policymakers or the exile community or the administration officials or the Cardinal or the Crab. For people who spend their mornings waiting for buses that don’t come and their evenings calculating whether the power will be on long enough to cook.

What the Series Found

Sixteen parts and somewhere north of forty thousand words later, this series has arrived at a set of conclusions that are not comfortable but are, as best as the writer can determine, honest.

The United States embargo has been one of the longest and most comprehensively documented failures of coercive foreign policy in modern history. Sixty-five years of economic strangulation have not produced democratic transition, have not improved Cuban political freedoms, have not served the interests of ordinary Cubans, and have — as this series has argued repeatedly — provided the Cuban government with a genuine and credible external enemy whose existence justifies internal repression and excuses domestic economic failure. The blockade of 2026 is the embargo’s most extreme expression, and there is no credible evidence-based theory by which starving a population of fuel produces the conditions for democratic governance. The evidence runs entirely the other way.

The Cuban government has also failed its people, in ways that cannot be attributed to American pressure and that an honest accounting of this history requires stating plainly. The political repression is real and severe. The economic mismanagement has compounded external damage with self-inflicted damage in ways that cannot be disentangled. The government that emerged from the Revolution’s genuine achievements — in literacy, in health care, in the elimination of the most extreme rural poverty — calcified, over decades, into something that prioritizes control over competence and loyalty over expertise. The people who paid the price for that calcification were the same people who paid the price for the embargo. They were always the same people.

And the international community — which has voted at the United Nations to condemn the embargo for more than thirty consecutive years, typically by margins of 180 to two or three — has demonstrated with unusual consistency that moral clarity and practical passivity can coexist indefinitely. The world has known what American Cuba policy is and has done nothing about it for six decades. The blockade of 2026 produced a Mexican president’s statement and an emergency press conference from the Vatican and a hemisphere-wide calculation that the cost of helping was too high. It produced this series, written from Toronto, which is not nothing, but is also not fuel.

The Open Question

The negotiations between Washington and Havana are continuing as this series concludes — no deal has been struck, no breakdown has been declared, and both sides appear to be in the ambiguous middle ground in which gestures are made and positions are maintained and the essential situation remains unresolved.

Here is what is known as of publication. Washington has demanded Díaz-Canel’s removal — not regime change, but a cosmetic reset, with the Castro family network intact, a new face in the presidential office, and the underlying power structure of the Communist Party and GAESA undisturbed. Trump called it “the honor of taking Cuba” and said “I think I can do anything I want with it.” Administration officials described Venezuela as a “dry run” for Havana and said “regime change is lined up” awaiting the president’s signal. Wealthy Republican donors with Cuban ancestry are being discussed as candidates for leadership roles in the post-deal government. The animating vision, in the words of one official, is not democracy or freedom but Chapter 11: a country reorganization, the whole focus on business, billions of dollars to be made.

Cuba has drawn its own line. Pretty much anything is open to discussion, Havana has signalled, except the shape of their government or who their political leaders will be. Rubio has publicly made clear that leadership change is non-negotiable from Washington’s side. That is where the deadlock sits.

What Cuba has offered: the release of 51 prisoners in March, followed by 2,010 more on April 3 — the largest such release in years, framed around Holy Week, universally understood as a gesture toward Washington. An announcement that Cuban exiles will be allowed to invest in the island’s economy. The arrival of an FBI team to investigate the fatal speedboat shooting. Russia’s Anatoly Kolodkin delivered 730,000 barrels of crude to Matanzas in late March after Trump waved it through saying “They have to survive” — the blockade’s first acknowledged exception, immediately called “not a policy change” by the White House. Protests rose from 30 in January to 130 in the first half of March. In Morón, demonstrators attacked the Communist Party headquarters itself, shouting “Freedom!” The White House confirmed on April 8 that discussions are continuing “at the highest levels of our government.” Prediction markets place the probability of a formal deal by June 30 at 25 percent.

That is the state of play. What it becomes — whether the negotiating deadlock breaks toward a deal or toward something more forceful — is the question this series cannot answer, because it has not yet been answered. What it can say is this: if a deal is eventually struck, the question to ask is not whether Washington won or Havana survived, but whether the outcome serves the people who have borne the cost of every previous arrangement made about Cuba without their consent. That question has a consistent answer across five hundred and thirty years of Cuban history. Whether this time produces a different one is what remains to be seen.

The Last Word

José Martí memorial in Havana. His unfinished letter warned that outside powers would decide Cuba’s fate. More than a century later, the island and its people are still here — bearing the cost while others negotiate.

José Martí did not live to see what happened to Cuba after 1898. He landed on the island in April 1895, rode into his first battle six weeks later, and was killed before he could discover whether the liberation he had spent his life working toward would be genuine or betrayed. History has since answered that question definitively.

But Martí wrote something the night before he landed — a letter to his friend Manuel Mercado that was never completed, interrupted by the war he was riding toward — that has been read as his political testament. In it, he described his fear that the United States would use Cuba’s independence struggle for its own purposes, that “the annexationist elements” would find a way to prevent true Cuban self-determination. He had spent enough time inside the monster to understand its appetites.

What he expressed, in that unfinished letter, was not despair. It was urgency — the urgency of a man who knew exactly what was at stake and was acting on it anyway, with full knowledge of the odds. He had been working toward Cuban independence for twenty years. He would have twenty-five more days.

Five hundred and thirty years after Columbus, thirty years after Martí’s death, sixty-seven years after the Revolution, thirty-three years after the intelligence community predicted the government’s imminent collapse, the island is still there. Its people are still there. They are going without electricity and paying thirty-five dollars for black market gasoline and waiting for buses that don’t come, and they are also making music, keeping culture alive, maintaining the human warmth that every visitor to Cuba — including the Canadians who filled the resorts for forty years — has commented on with something approaching disbelief, as if such warmth were incompatible with such hardship, when in fact it has always coexisted with it, stubbornly, as the most enduring form of refusal available to people who have been given very little say in anything else.

Cuban balseros on a makeshift raft. Thousands risked everything crossing the Florida Straits. In 2026, millions more had already left since 2020, paying with their country while those who remained paid with darkness

This series cannot tell you what Cuba will become. No one can, including the people currently negotiating its fate in rooms the Cuban people are not invited into.

What it can tell you — what sixteen parts and five hundred and thirty years of history demonstrate with a consistency that should settle the question — is what Cuba has already survived, and at what cost, and at whose expense, and in whose name.

The political scientist María de los Ángeles Torres has warned that a deal focused on economic opening alone would “crush the aspirations of Cubans both on the island and in exile who have fought to establish democratic rights in their homeland.” She is almost certainly right. And she is almost certainly going to be ignored — because the deal being constructed, if it comes together, is not being constructed with those aspirations in mind. The analyst Quico Toro has looked at both sides of the negotiating table and offered the clearest summary of what he sees: “The revolution has nothing left.” Neither, for that matter, does the ideology that once gave American Cuba policy its claim to moral purpose. What remains is the haggling. Two systems that operate by the same extractive logic, working out the split.

The rest is still being written. It will be written, as it always has been, by forces that consider the Cuban people’s welfare somewhere between secondary and irrelevant, and it will be borne, as it always has been, by the Cuban people themselves.

They have borne everything else. That is not an argument for making them bear this. It is an argument for finally — after five centuries, after everything — asking whether we could choose differently.

We could. We have not. Whether we will is the question that outlasts this series, this crisis, and this moment.

It is also, ultimately, the only question that matters.

This is the conclusion of Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel.

If this series has moved you, the most useful thing you can do is share it — and remember that behind every geopolitical abstraction are people like Miriam, Daimarilis, and Luis David, trying to get through the day.
