The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 10: The Succession Crisis

April 9, 2026

Mojtaba Khamenei at Tehran's annual Quds Day rally, May 31, 2019 — one of his last documented public appearances before the February 2026 strikes that killed his father and propelled him to power. Within two weeks of becoming Supreme Leader, he had still not appeared on camera. Photo: Rouzbeh Fouladi / Middle East Images / AFP via Getty Images.

Editor’s Note

When I wrote this essay in January 2026, the succession crisis was theoretical. Khamenei was 86 and ailing, and the question of who would inherit his theocracy was the kind of slow-burn geopolitical puzzle that analysts had debated for a decade. I laid out the candidates, the factions, the structural impossibilities, and concluded that there was no legitimate heir by the revolution’s own criteria.

I did not expect the answer to arrive within six weeks.

On February 28, 2026, Khamenei was killed in joint US-Israeli strikes. What followed was not an orderly succession. The IRGC attempted to appoint a new Supreme Leader on the day of the assassination itself, bypassing the Assembly of Experts entirely. A formal election process ran from March 3 to 8 — conducted largely online, after multiple attempts to meet in person failed for security reasons. Mojtaba Khamenei, the Supreme Leader’s own son, was formally announced as his successor on March 9.

Dynastic succession. The scenario this essay warned about, delivered under the worst possible conditions, by a body subjected to what Iran International described as “repeated contacts and psychological and political pressure” from IRGC commanders — and whose legitimacy was already in question. The whirlwind has been inherited. Whether Mojtaba can survive it is the question this series cannot yet answer.

— Stephen, April 2026

Who Inherits the Whirlwind?

AUTHOR’S NOTE - ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK: Parts 10-13 shift from documentation of events to analytical framework. These essays examine what Khamenei’s 37-year rule means for Iran’s future based on:

Historical patterns of authoritarian succession (Barbara Geddes research, comparative politics literature on personalist dictatorships)

Iran’s specific institutional weaknesses (documented in Parts 1-9)

Current trajectory (economic collapse, legitimacy crisis, generational rejection)

Regional precedents (Syria’s civil war, Arab Spring outcomes, Soviet collapse, Yugoslavia’s fracturing)

These are analytical projections based on documented evidence, not predictions. The succession crisis, generational rupture, and legitimacy collapse are documented realities; how they resolve remains uncertain.

Analysis draws on: Iran experts including Karim Sadjadpour (International Crisis Group), Ray Takeyh (Council on Foreign Relations), Abbas Milani (Stanford), Suzanne Maloney (Brookings), Mehdi Khalaji (Washington Institute), and Vali Nasr (Johns Hopkins), while applying political science frameworks to Iran’s specific context.

The Islamic Republic of Iran has a succession plan. It’s written into the constitution, managed by a designated body, and theoretically provides orderly transition of power upon the Supreme Leader’s death.

The problem: Ayatollah Ali Khamenei had spent 37 years systematically undermining every institution, destroying every potential successor, and concentrating power so absolutely that no one could legitimately inherit it.

He built a system where succession is both constitutionally mandated and practically impossible.

This isn’t speculation. It’s the predictable result of decades of purges, paranoia, and the elevation of personal power over institutional stability.

When Khamenei died — and at 86 years old with declining health, that moment was approaching — Iran faced not transition but rupture. Not succession but scramble. Not continuity but chaos.

He promised to guard the revolution. Instead, he created the conditions for its collapse.

I. THE AGING AUTOCRAT

Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was born April 19, 1939. As of January 2026, he was 86 years old.

His right arm had been paralyzed since a 1981 assassination attempt — a bomb hidden in a tape recorder during a speech. The blast killed his assistant and permanently disabled Khamenei’s arm. For 44 years, he delivered speeches and conducted religious ceremonies with one functional arm.

Age was catching up in other ways:

Public appearances: Increasingly rare. When he did appear, speeches were often pre-recorded, heavily edited, and broadcast later. Live appearances had become so infrequent that opposition media speculated about body doubles.

Communication patterns: During the June 2025 war, he went silent for twelve days — unprecedented for a leader who normally issued guidance on everything from nuclear policy to proper hijab standards.

Health reports: Iranian intelligence sources and foreign intelligence assessments agreed: his health was declining faster than publicly acknowledged. Exact details remained classified, but the pattern was clear.

Mental state: Multiple reports during June 2025 suggested mental breakdown or incapacitation during the war. Whether temporary stress response or deeper deterioration remained unclear, but the very fact such reports emerged — and were taken seriously — demonstrated vulnerability.

The Islamic Republic’s founder, Ayatollah Khomeini, died at age 86 in June 1989. Khamenei had now reached that age. The symmetry wasn’t lost on observers.

II. THE 1989 SUCCESSION VS. THE 2026 SUCCESSION

A Tehran highway billboard, March 2026, depicting the Islamic Republic's three Supreme Leaders in succession: Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini (left), the revolution's founder; Ayatollah Ali Khamenei (centre), who ruled for 37 years before his assassination on February 28; and Mojtaba Khamenei (right), his son and appointed successor.

When Ayatollah Khomeini died on June 3, 1989, Iran managed a relatively smooth transition. Within hours, the Assembly of Experts convened. Within a day, they selected Ali Khamenei as successor. The Islamic Republic continued without major disruption.

That success created the illusion that the system’s succession mechanisms work. But 1989 and 2026 couldn’t be more different.

1989: Conditions for Smooth Transition

The kingmaker existed: Ali Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani — pragmatic, politically skilled, deeply connected to all factions — orchestrated the process. He had credibility with hardliners and reformists. He could broker deals, manage egos, and enforce consensus.

Constitutional flexibility: The constitution required the Supreme Leader to be a marja (grand ayatollah qualified to issue religious rulings). Khamenei wasn’t. Rafsanjani convinced the Assembly to amend the constitution, removing that requirement. Problem solved.

Elite consensus: Revolutionary elites — military commanders, clerical leaders, political figures — shared recent revolutionary experience. They’d fought together, imprisoned together, built the system together. Disagreements existed, but common revolutionary identity enabled compromise.

System legitimacy: The revolution was only ten years old. Popular memory of the Shah remained fresh. The Islamic Republic still commanded genuine support from significant portions of society.

No major crisis: The Iran-Iraq War had ended in 1988. The economy was stabilizing. No existential threats loomed. The succession occurred during relative calm.

Clear authority: Khomeini’s authority as revolution founder was unquestioned. His word was final. When he endorsed the succession process (before his death), it carried weight.

2026: Conditions for Chaotic Transition

No kingmaker: Rafsanjani died in January 2017. No figure commands comparable respect across factions. President Pezeshkian lacks authority. Former President Rouhani is sidelined. IRGC commanders have power but not legitimacy. Senior clerics have legitimacy but not power.

Constitutional rigidity: After Khamenei’s 37 years of consolidating personal authority, bending rules to accommodate a lesser figure will be harder. Who has the credibility to orchestrate such changes?

Elite fractures: Revolutionary generation is dying or retired. Current elites have no shared struggle, only shared corruption. IRGC enriched itself. Clerics lost credibility. Politicians serve at Khamenei’s pleasure. When he’s gone, what unites them?

System illegitimacy: January 2026 protests span all 31 provinces. Slogans explicitly call for overthrowing the system. Even traditional supporters — bazaar merchants, residents of religious cities — are rebelling. The revolution’s 47-year-old legitimacy has evaporated.

Multiple simultaneous crises:

Economic collapse (50%+ poverty, currency worthless)

Nationwide protests (thousands killed by security forces)

Post-war humiliation (June 2025 military failure)

Regional empire lost (Syria, Yemen, Lebanon proxies collapsed)

International isolation (sanctions, threats of intervention)

No clear authority: Khamenei concentrated all authority in himself. No institution — not the Assembly of Experts, not the presidency, not even IRGC — has independent legitimacy to fill the void.

Political scientist Barbara Geddes’ research on authoritarian transitions found that since World War II, less than 25% lead to democracy. Most produce another form of authoritarianism — often weaker, more unstable, more violent.

Iran in 2026 fits every indicator for chaotic transition.

III. THE FIVE SCENARIOS

Iran expert Karim Sadjadpour outlined five potential post-Khamenei scenarios. Let’s examine each:

Scenario 1: Iran as Russia

Description: Nationalist strongman emerges, replacing Islamism with grievance-driven nationalism. Likely an IRGC general. Continues authoritarian control but drops religious pretense.

Precedent: Vladimir Putin’s Russia — post-Soviet collapse, nationalist restoration, grievance politics, military-intelligence dominance.

Plausibility: Moderate to high.

Key factors in favor:

IRGC controls security apparatus, economy (bonyads), and has guns

Nationalism could unite fractured society better than discredited Islamism

Iranian nationalism has deep historical roots (Persian empire, Cyrus the Great)

External threats (US, Israel) could justify continued militarization

Key obstacles:

Who is strong enough to unify military and clerical factions?

IRGC commanders are rivals, not unified bloc

Iranian nationalism includes secular traditions incompatible with clerical involvement

Clerics won’t willingly surrender institutional power

Likelihood assessment: 35%

Scenario 2: Iran as China

Description: Economic reform without political liberalization. Technocratic leadership focused on growth, stability, and international integration while maintaining authoritarian control.

Precedent: Deng Xiaoping’s China — CCP retained political monopoly while unleashing economic dynamism.

Plausibility: Low to moderate.

Key factors in favor:

Iranian population is educated, entrepreneurial, and hungry for economic opportunity

Ending sanctions could unlock massive growth

Regional integration (especially with China, India) offers economic lifeline

Elite enrichment continues under this model

Key obstacles:

CCP had institutional coherence Iran lacks

Deng had revolutionary credentials and military backing

Iranian economy is structurally corrupt (IRGC monopolies, clerical foundations)

Sanctions relief requires nuclear concessions Iranian hardliners won’t make

Population demands political change, not just economic growth

Likelihood assessment: 15%

Scenario 3: Iran as Syria

Description: Civil war. Elite fractures into competing factions. Regional and ethnic divisions explode. Outside powers (US, Israel, Turkey, Saudi Arabia) back different factions.

Precedent: Syria 2011-present — peaceful protests met with violence, opposition militarizes, country fragments, foreign intervention.

Plausibility: Moderate.

Key factors in favor:

Ethnic divisions ready to exploit weakness (Kurds, Baloch, Azeris, Arabs)

IRGC factionalism could produce competing military units

Regional powers have interest in shaping outcome

History of violent suppression breeds violent resistance

Proliferation of weapons from conflicts in region

Key obstacles:

Iran’s state capacity exceeds Syria’s (despite weakening)

No neighboring safe havens for opposition (Turkey for Syrian rebels)

IRGC more institutionalized than Assad’s military

International exhaustion with Middle East interventions

Memory of Syrian catastrophe could motivate compromise

Likelihood assessment: 25%

Scenario 4: Iran as Ukraine

Description: Popular uprising succeeds, transitioning to democracy. New leadership purges old regime, reorients toward West, seeks integration with international community.

Precedent: Ukraine’s Maidan Revolution (2013-14) — protests overthrow authoritarian president, pro-Western government takes power.

Plausibility: Low.

Key factors in favor:

Massive popular opposition demonstrated (January 2026 protests)

Educated, urban middle class wants democracy

Women’s movement shows organized resistance capacity

Diaspora could provide support, expertise, legitimacy

International community would welcome democratic Iran

Key obstacles:

Ukraine’s military didn’t massacre protesters; IRGC does

Ukraine had institutional pluralism; Iran has IRGC monopoly

Ukrainian opposition had political organization; Iranian opposition is leaderless

Geopolitical context different — West won’t intervene militarily for Iranian democracy

IRGC will kill indefinitely to prevent this outcome

Likelihood assessment: 10%

Scenario 5: Status Quo (Khamenei 2.0)

Description: System survives largely intact. Another elderly cleric selected, continues Khamenei’s policies, maintains repression, manages decline.

Precedent: North Korea — hereditary succession, continued isolation, system persists through sheer brutality.

Plausibility: Low to moderate.

Key factors in favor:

Repression has worked before (1999, 2009, 2019, 2022)

Elite self-interest in system survival

No external force capable of forcing change

IRGC willing to kill indefinitely

Key obstacles:

Previous repressions occurred during relative strength; now system is weak

Khamenei had 1979 revolutionary credentials; no successor does

Economic crisis worse than any previous uprising

Regional empire collapsed (removes safety valve of exporting revolution)

Legitimacy exhausted even among traditional supporters

Likelihood assessment: 15%

The verdict: No scenario is likely (none exceeds 35%). That itself tells the story: succession will probably combine elements of multiple scenarios — nationalist strongman attempting economic reform while battling insurgencies, or brief democratic opening followed by military coup, or chaotic fracturing followed by external intervention.

Uncertainty is the only certainty.

IV. THE CANDIDATES (CONSTITUTIONAL REALITY)

The Iranian constitution specifies how succession works:

The Assembly of Experts — 88 clerics elected to 8-year terms — selects the next Supreme Leader. They’re supposed to choose based on qualification (religious knowledge, administrative competence, justice), not political loyalty.

Reality is different.

The Assembly of Experts: Captured Institution

Guardian Council vets candidates: Twelve-member Guardian Council approves who can run for Assembly. Khamenei appoints six members directly; the other six are jurists nominated by judiciary chief (whom Khamenei appoints). Result: only hardliners approved.

Current composition: The 2024-elected Assembly is entirely hardliners. Reformists were barred from running. Moderate conservatives were disqualified. Only regime loyalists allowed.

Track record: In 37 years, the Assembly has never challenged Khamenei. Not once. They exist to rubber-stamp, not deliberate.

Real power: Intelligence suggests IRGC commanders might simply make the decision regardless of Assembly’s recommendation. Why would military defer to clerics when they have the guns?

The Succession Process (Pre-June 2025)

Shortly before the June 2025 war, Khamenei reportedly requested the Assembly begin preparing for succession. According to leaks, a four-member secret council was examining candidates.

Then war happened. The process went dark.

After US airstrikes, The New York Times reported Khamenei had privately selected “three senior clerics” in case of assassination. Names weren’t disclosed. Whether this constituted official designation or contingency planning remained unclear.

President Pezeshkian’s Warning (November 2025)

In November 2025, President Masoud Pezeshkian made a remarkable statement:

“Harm to the Supreme Leader could cause internal factions to turn on each other, leading to regime collapse.”

This from the president — someone presumably interested in projecting stability. His admission: the succession mechanism might not work. Elite unity is performance, not reality.

The Leading Candidates

Mojtaba Khamenei (Khamenei’s second son):

Born 1969. Conservative cleric. Close to IRGC. Rumors of his selection had circulated for years.

Problems:

Dynastic succession contradicts revolutionary principles

No significant religious credentials

Viewed as opportunist, not scholar

Would confirm everything opponents say about regime corruption

Even hardliners might balk at monarchy

Probability: 25% (nepotism often wins, but too blatant even for this regime)

[UPDATE: Mojtaba was formally announced Supreme Leader on March 9, 2026, after a selection process that confirmed the essay’s core prediction. The IRGC attempted to install him by fiat on the day of the assassination, then applied sustained pressure on Assembly members throughout the formal voting process. The outcome vindicated the 25% probability estimate — and everything written above about why it should not have happened.]

Ebrahim Raisi (deceased):

President from 2021 until his death in helicopter crash May 2024. Hardliner, former judiciary chief, close to Khamenei, implicated in 1988 prison massacres.

Khamenei’s likely choice — made impossible by his death.

Ali Akbar Velayati:

Born 1945. Foreign minister 1981-1997. Senior foreign policy advisor to Khamenei. Moderate hardliner with international experience.

Problems:

Age (81 years old)

Lacks IRGC backing

Too moderate for hardliners, too hardline for reformists

No power base beyond advisory role

Probability: 15%

Gholam-Hossein Mohseni-Ejei:

Current judiciary chief. Hardliner. Close to security establishment.

Problems:

Lacks charisma

No revolutionary credentials

Judiciary deeply unpopular (executions, torture)

Age (67, but in Iranian context that’s not young enough for long-term stability)

Probability: 20%

An IRGC General:

Various names circulate: commanders of Ground Forces, Aerospace Force, Quds Force.

Problems:

Military rule contradicts Islamic Republic’s religious legitimacy

Clerics won’t accept military Supreme Leader easily

Which general? They’re rivals.

Naked military dictatorship removes last pretense of Islamic governance

Probability: 25% (but this might mean military collective rule, not single Supreme Leader)

The Constitutional Reality:

The Assembly of Experts is supposed to evaluate candidates based on:

Religious knowledge (ijtihad — ability to derive Islamic law)

Justice and piety

Political and social insight

Courage and administrative abilities

By those standards:

Mojtaba: Fails #1 (insufficient scholarship), questionable #2 (corrupt)

Velayati: Passes #1, #2, #4, but lacks #3 (power base)

Mohseni-Ejei: Passes #1, #4, questionable #2 (judiciary brutality), lacks #3 (unpopular)

IRGC general: Fails #1 (not a scholar), fails #2 (military enrichment), questionable #3-4 (competent militarily, politically unknown)

No candidate meets the stated criteria. That’s not accident — it’s the system Khamenei built.

V. THE ETHNIC WILDCARD

Iran is 60% Persian. The other 40% includes:

Azeris (16-24%): Largest minority. Northwestern Iran. Some separatist sentiment, though historically integrated.

Kurds (10%): Northwestern Iran. History of armed resistance. Well-organized political movements. Regional autonomy in Iraq provides inspiration.

Lurs (6%): Western Iran. Shia, relatively integrated, but economically marginalized.

Arabs (2-3%): Southwestern Khuzestan province. Sunni majority (in Shia-majority country). Long-standing grievances. Oil-rich region.

Baloch (2%): Southeastern Iran. Sunni. Active insurgency. Impoverished despite bordering Pakistan and Afghanistan (smuggling routes).

Turkmen (2%): Northern border regions. Minimal separatism historically, but potential for exploitation.

During stable authoritarian rule, ethnic divisions stay manageable. During succession crises, they explode.

The Yugoslav Precedent

When Josip Broz Tito died in 1980, Yugoslavia’s ethnic tensions were contained. Within a decade, the country fractured along ethnic lines — Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia, Macedonia, Kosovo — producing the bloodiest European conflict since WWII.

Iran isn’t Yugoslavia. But the parallels are uncomfortable:

Strong man dies, no legitimate successor — check.

Multiple ethnic groups with distinct identities — check.

Economic crisis exacerbating tensions — check.

Regional powers ready to exploit divisions — check (Turkey interested in Azeris, Saudi Arabia in Arabs, etc.).

History of centralized repression masking ethnic grievances — check.

The Immediate Risk

If Khamenei’s death produces chaotic succession with competing factions fighting in Tehran, ethnic regions might see opportunity:

Kurds: Declare autonomy in western provinces, link with Iraqi Kurdistan.

Baloch: Intensify insurgency, declare Sunni autonomous region.

Azeris: Potentially seek union with Azerbaijan (unlikely, but pan-Turkic sentiment exists).

Arabs: Demand autonomy in oil-rich Khuzestan (which Saudi Arabia and UAE would probably support).

This wouldn’t necessarily mean full secession — but even autonomous regions would shatter Iran’s territorial integrity and create permanent instability.

The IRGC knows this. That’s why any succession will likely prioritize territorial control over democratic legitimacy.

VI. THE EXTERNAL FACTOR

US/Israel Interests

United States under Trump: Wants “maximum pressure” to force Iran to renegotiate nuclear deal, end regional proxy support, and cease ballistic missile program. Regime change is discussed but not official policy. Military strikes threatened but not (yet) executed beyond June 2025.

Question: Would the US intervene in chaotic succession? Probably not directly (troop deployment), but special operations, support for opposition, targeted strikes against nuclear facilities — all possible.

Israel: Wants Iran denuclearized. Doesn’t care about government type, just nuclear program elimination. Has demonstrated willingness to strike Iran directly (June 2025). Would likely exploit chaotic succession to destroy remaining nuclear infrastructure.

Regional powers (Saudi Arabia, UAE): Prefer stability over democracy. Would support nationalist strongman who abandons Shia proxy networks. Might intervene in Khuzestan (Arab regions) if Iran fragments.

Russia’s Role

Historical relationship: Russia and Iran cooperated in Syria (now collapsed), on Caspian Sea issues, and in regional balancing against US.

Current reality: Russia is focused on Ukraine, internationally isolated, and can’t project power into Middle East effectively.

The Moscow option: Reports exist of contingency plan for Khamenei to flee to Moscow (like Assad did December 2024). But would Russia accept him?

Strategic value vs. liability: Accepting Khamenei grants leverage over Iran’s succession. But it also associates Russia with a regime that just massacred thousands of protesters, inviting further sanctions and international condemnation.

Verdict: Russia might provide temporary asylum but wouldn’t guarantee Khamenei’s preferred successor.

The Strait of Hormuz — 33 kilometres wide at its narrowest — through which approximately one-fifth of the world's oil and gas passes daily. Mojtaba Khamenei's first written statement declared it "a lever of pressure" to be kept closed; its reopening became the central demand of the April 8 ceasefire. The strait is the Islamic Republic's most powerful remaining strategic card.

China’s Role

Economic interest: China buys approximately 80% of Iran’s oil exports (under sanctions, at discounted prices). Belt and Road Initiative includes Iran as transit route.

Strategic interest: Iran is useful counterweight to US influence in Gulf.

Limitations: China doesn’t intervene militarily outside its immediate periphery. Won’t send troops. Won’t prop up failing regime with military force.

Likely behavior: Work with whoever controls Tehran, regardless of ideology, as long as oil flows and stability returns.

Translation: China is customer, not ally. They’ll buy oil from nationalist strongman or democratic government or military junta — they don’t care. They just want stability.

VII. CLOSING: THE CRISIS KHAMENEI CREATED

Anti-government protests sweep Tehran in January 2026, weeks before the February 28 US-Israeli strikes that killed Ali Khamenei. The uprising — spanning all 31 provinces — had already exposed the regime's eroded legitimacy before the war began. The students who later nicknamed his successor "Mous-Ali" (Rat Ali) and hung toy rats on university campuses were drawing from this same reservoir of contempt. Photo: AP.

For 37 years, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei accumulated absolute power. He:

Purged rivals: Reformist presidents (Khatami), pragmatic competitors (Rafsanjani), potential alternatives (Mousavi, Karroubi) imprisoned or sidelined.

Weakened institutions: Majles (parliament) reduced to rubber stamp, presidency to administrator, judiciary to executioner.

Elevated IRGC: Revolutionary Guards became parallel state — controlling economy, security, foreign policy — answerable only to Supreme Leader.

Concentrated religious authority: Despite limited religious credentials, claimed final interpretation of Islamic law, sidelined genuine religious scholars.

Eliminated succession mechanisms: No grooming of successor, no institutional preparation, no elite consensus-building.

The result: a system where power flows entirely through one man, who was 86 years old, in declining health, presiding over collapsing economy, facing nationwide protests, and admitting to killing thousands.

What he created:

No legitimate successor by his own stated criteria

Multiple power centers (IRGC factions, clerical networks, political figures) positioned to fight over control

Ethnic fractures ready to exploit weakness

Economy in free fall (50%+ poverty, worthless currency)

Population mobilized in opposition (all 31 provinces protesting)

Regional allies gone (Syria, Lebanon, Yemen proxies collapsed or crippled)

International isolation complete (sanctions, threats, no defender)

This is not an accident. This is not bad luck. This is the logical conclusion of 37 years of personalist dictatorship masquerading as Islamic government.

Khamenei promised to guard the revolution. Instead, he hoarded power so absolutely that when he died, the revolution died with him — not because of external enemies, but because he systematically destroyed every institution that might have allowed it to survive his death.

The bunker beneath his compound in Tehran awaited.

So did chaos.

The succession crisis wasn’t coming. It was here. It was just waiting for one 86-year-old man’s heart to stop beating.

And when it did, the whirlwind he created would consume everything.

EPILOGUE: THE WHIRLWIND IS INHERITED

March 2026: The Succession That Wasn’t Supposed to Happen This Way

The preceding analysis was written in January 2026, projecting forward to the succession crisis that would eventually arrive. It arrived on February 28, under circumstances no analyst had fully modelled: not natural death, not a managed transition, but assassination — and a rushed, fractured appointment process that unfolded over the following ten days while bombs were still falling.

Mojtaba Khamenei: The Appointed Heir

The Assembly of Experts — the 88-member clerical body constitutionally charged with selecting Iran’s Supreme Leader — convened in extraordinary circumstances. The IRGC attempted to install Mojtaba by fiat on the day of the assassination, bypassing the Assembly entirely. When that move failed to achieve the required consensus, IRGC commanders began applying what Iran International described as “repeated contacts and psychological and political pressure” on Assembly members. Multiple attempts to hold in-person sessions failed for security reasons; a first electoral session was held online on March 3. The Assembly’s office in Qom was bombed during one of these sessions.

Mojtaba Khamenei was formally announced as Supreme Leader on March 9, 2026.

He had spent decades deliberately avoiding public life, accumulating influence through back channels, building networks inside the IRGC and clerical establishment. His father had reportedly told senior clerics — and the 2024 Assembly of Experts directly — that his son should be excluded from consideration. The Assembly proceeded anyway. What that tells you about the condition of Iran’s institutions requires no further elaboration.

Trump expressed his “disappointment” at the selection, telling Fox News he did not believe the new Supreme Leader “can live in peace.” Oil prices spiked above $120 per barrel on the appointment news and the Strait closure — the market’s own assessment of what dynastic succession under active bombardment meant for regional stability.

The Invisible Supreme Leader

Since his appointment, Mojtaba Khamenei had not appeared publicly. His first communication — delivered nearly two weeks after the war began — was not a speech, not a video, not even a recorded statement in his own voice. It was read aloud by a state television anchor while a still photograph was displayed on screen. He called for continued military resistance, threatened the Strait of Hormuz as a lever of pressure, and demanded US bases across the region be closed or face attack. The words were defiant. The delivery was spectral.

When Nowruz arrived and Iranian President Pezeshkian released a video message for the new year despite credible assassination threats against him, Mojtaba published only a written statement via Telegram. A U.S. official told Axios it was “a big red flag.” Defense Secretary Hegseth had publicly claimed Mojtaba was “wounded and likely disfigured” in the strike that killed his father. Iranian state media had earlier referred to him using the term janbaz — the word reserved for the Islamic Republic’s disabled war veterans.

Three weeks into his Supreme Leadership, no video or audio recording of the new leader had been released. The CIA, Mossad, and intelligence agencies across the region were trying to determine whether photographs published on his Telegram channel were recent. A senior Arab official told Axios with no apparent understatement: “The IRGC are taking over Iran and they are crazy. They are highly ideological and are ready to die and meet Khamenei Senior.”

The students protesting in Tehran were calling him “Mous-Ali” — Rat Ali. They were hanging toy rats from tree branches on university campuses.

The Structural Crisis Comes True

This essay predicted that any successor would inherit a system structurally incapable of managing a peaceful transition: no legitimacy grooming, no elite consensus, multiple competing IRGC factions, ethnic fractures, economic collapse, and a population already in open revolt.

The prediction was correct. Mojtaba inherited all of it, plus a country under active military bombardment, with a leadership decapitated at every level simultaneously. The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace noted that the regime’s functional capacity had been severely weakened, even if the institutional structure had not yet collapsed — and that the “death throes could last a relatively long time.”

Twenty to thirty percent of the population — security forces, officials, clerics, economic beneficiaries — remain loyal, analysts estimate. The other seventy percent, who have watched their country massacred, bombed, and handed to the son of the man who ordered the killing, are a different matter.

What the Succession Tells Us

That the Assembly chose dynastic succession — the one outcome Khamenei himself allegedly opposed, the one outcome that most visibly contradicts the Islamic Republic’s own founding principles — tells you everything about the system’s condition. It had no other choice. There was no legitimate heir by its own criteria. So it abandoned the criteria. And then the IRGC, having effectively chosen the outcome, spent six days pressuring clerics to ratify what the guns had already decided.

The whirlwind that this essay described — the accumulated consequences of 37 years of personalist dictatorship, institutional decay, and revolutionary betrayal — belongs to a man who cannot yet appear in public, who is nicknamed Rat Ali by the students he will need to govern, and who inherited his position the same way Iran’s last dynasty handed down power: from father to son.

The Islamic Revolution overthrew a Shah. It has, in its final act, appointed one.

April 2026: The Invisible Hand

There is a final detail that belongs in the record — one that crystallizes everything this series has argued about power, legitimacy, and the strange half-life of broken systems.

On April 7 and 8, 2026, Mojtaba Khamenei was actively directing Iran’s ceasefire negotiations with the United States. Facing an active assassination threat from Israel, he was communicating with his negotiators via runners passing handwritten notes. No phone calls. No video links. No public presence. A man who may be disfigured, whose physical condition remains unconfirmed by any independent source, who has not appeared on camera in the six weeks since his father was killed — this man was making the decisions that halted forty days of war.

Iranians gather at Enqelab (Revolution) Square in Tehran on April 8, 2026, following the announcement of a US-Iran ceasefire brokered by Pakistan. / AFP

On April 8, a two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan was announced. The Strait of Hormuz, which Mojtaba had publicly vowed to keep closed as a lever of pressure, would reopen to shipping. Iran submitted a ten-point proposal that Trump called a “workable basis on which to negotiate.” US-Iran talks were scheduled to begin in Islamabad on April 10.

The ceasefire is fragile. The negotiations may collapse. A senior Israeli official has made clear that regime change remains one of Israel’s war aims. The IRGC commanders who are effectively running the country are, by the account of regional intelligence officials, ideological enough to welcome martyrdom.

But here is what we know: the system that Khamenei built — hollowed out, shot through, presided over by a ghost — made a consequential strategic decision at the edge of annihilation. Whether that represents the last coherent act of a dying order or the first evidence of a system adapting under impossible pressure is the question this series cannot yet answer.

What it cannot be called is business as usual.

The whirlwind is inherited. The runner carrying notes through the rubble is the Supreme Leader of the Islamic Republic. And the world is waiting to see, in Islamabad, whether he has anything left to negotiate with.
