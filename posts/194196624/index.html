Super PACman

The Labor of It All

Part II: The Union Organizer Who Accidentally Invented the Weapon That Would Be Turned Against Him

May 1, 2026

Sidney Hillman, c. 1940s. Library of Congress.

Sidney Hillman did not set out to build the architecture of modern dark money. He set out to re-elect Franklin Roosevelt.

It is worth holding that contrast in mind as the story unfolds, because the distance between those two intentions — one specific and democratic, one systemic and corrosive — is the distance American campaign finance has traveled in eighty years. Hillman was a pragmatist solving an immediate problem. What he built was a template. And templates, once created, do not ask permission before being used by people their creators would have despised.

This is the part of the story that the billionaires prefer not to tell. The political action committee — the foundational vehicle of American campaign finance, the mechanism that eventually became the super PAC, the dark money conduit, the undisclosed donor pipeline — was invented by organized labor. It was invented specifically because corporations had been restricted from political spending, and the unions wanted a workaround. The irony is not subtle. It is, in fact, one of the most complete ironies in the history of American political economy, and it has been hiding in plain sight since 1943.

To understand why Hillman did what he did, you need to understand what had been done to him first.

The story begins not in 1943 but in 1907, in the final years of the Gilded Age, when the scandals of corporate political spending had become impossible for Congress to ignore. The robber barons — the Rockefellers, the Carnegies, the railroad magnates and Standard Oil men — had been financing political campaigns with industrial-scale corporate money for decades. The 1896 presidential election, in which Republican operative Mark Hanna raised an estimated $3.5 million from corporations to elect William McKinley, had demonstrated with uncomfortable clarity what corporate money at scale could accomplish. McKinley won. The corporations got their tariffs.

McKinley arrives at the White House wearing a hat labeled "Monopoly." The Tillman Act was still eleven years away. J.S. Pughe, Puck, April 29, 1896. Library of Congress.

Theodore Roosevelt, whose relationship with big business was complicated in ways that his progressive reputation sometimes obscures, pushed through the Tillman Act in 1907. Named for Senator Benjamin Tillman of South Carolina — a man whose racism was so spectacular that even by the standards of 1907 it required acknowledgment, and who supported the bill largely because he wanted to embarrass the Republicans who had been feeding at the corporate trough — the Act prohibited corporations from making direct financial contributions to federal candidates.

It was the first federal campaign finance law. It had no enforcement mechanism. It was violated routinely and with minimal consequence for the next several decades. But it established the principle — corporations could not give directly to candidates — that would, through a series of extensions and restatements, eventually produce the conditions that Sidney Hillman was working around in 1943.

The Smith-Connally Act of 1943 extended the Tillman prohibition to labor unions. The stated rationale was wartime unity — with American workers and American corporations both nominally pulling toward the same Allied victory, the government did not want union money distorting the political landscape. The practical effect was that the Congress of Industrial Organizations, one of the two major labor federations in the country and one of the most politically active organizations in American life, had just been told it could not give money directly to the candidates it supported.

Philip Murray, the CIO’s president, called Sidney Hillman.

Hillman was the head of the Amalgamated Clothing Workers of America and one of the most sophisticated political operators in the labor movement. He had been close to Roosevelt since the New Deal. He understood, with a clarity that many of his contemporaries lacked, that labor’s gains in the 1930s — the Wagner Act, the Fair Labor Standards Act, the entire architecture of the New Deal labor settlement — were political achievements as much as economic ones, and that they would be unmade by politicians if labor could not influence who those politicians were.

The Smith-Connally Act said unions could not give money directly to candidates. It said nothing about a separate organization that collected voluntary contributions from union members and spent them on political activity. Such an organization would not be a union. It would be a political committee. And a political committee, technically, was not covered by the prohibition.

The CIO-PAC was established in July 1943. Hillman ran it. Its first major project was the 1944 presidential election, in which it mobilized union members, registered voters, and spent money on political advertising in support of Franklin Roosevelt’s fourth-term campaign.

Roosevelt won. The CIO-PAC worked.

The political action committee had been born.

The Roosevelt campaign of 1944 produced one of the more revealing moments in the early history of the PAC, and it is worth pausing on it because it illuminates something essential about how money and politics interact when powerful people feel threatened.

FDR 1944 (CIO-PAC section) Franklin Roosevelt on the 1944 campaign trail. The CIO-PAC spent $1.5 million to keep him there. He carried thirty-six states. FDR Presidential Library

Republican opponents of Roosevelt, searching for lines of attack, fastened on Hillman’s role in the campaign. The charge — repeated in newspapers, in speeches, in the kind of whisper campaign that was the social media of its day — was that Roosevelt had surrendered control of his own candidacy to a foreign-born union boss with socialist sympathies. “Clear it with Sidney” became a Republican slogan, implying that no decision in the Roosevelt campaign happened without Hillman’s approval.

Hillman had been born in Lithuania. He had immigrated to the United States in 1907 — the same year the Tillman Act passed, as it happens. He was Jewish. The attacks on him were not exclusively about campaign finance. They were about who was allowed to participate in American democracy, and the answer the attacks implied was: not people like Sidney Hillman.

He participated anyway. The CIO-PAC spent approximately $1.5 million on the 1944 election. Roosevelt carried thirty-six states.

Sidney Hillman portrait (c. 1940s, Library of Congress)

The mechanism Hillman built spread quickly, because mechanisms that work tend to spread. The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 made the Smith-Connally prohibition on union political contributions permanent rather than wartime-temporary, which might have been expected to constrain the PAC model. Instead it accelerated it. If unions were permanently prohibited from direct contributions, they permanently needed the workaround. PACs were no longer a wartime expedient. They were infrastructure.

Corporations, watching, drew the obvious conclusion. The Tillman Act prohibited direct corporate contributions. It said nothing about corporate-sponsored PACs that collected voluntary contributions from executives and shareholders and spent them on political activity. By the 1960s and 1970s, corporate PACs were multiplying. By 1974 — the year Congress passed its landmark campaign finance reforms — there were 89 corporate PACs registered with what would become the Federal Election Commission. By 1980 there were 1,206.

Labor had built the tool. Business had copied it, scaled it, and deployed it against the regulatory and labor protections that labor had used the tool to win in the first place.

If Sidney Hillman had lived to see this — he died in 1946, two years after the Roosevelt campaign he had helped to win — it is not clear whether he would have felt responsible, or merely unlucky. He had solved the problem in front of him. He had not designed the system that would emerge from his solution fifty years later. This is the nature of unintended consequences: they do not announce themselves at the moment of creation. They arrive later, wearing someone else’s clothes, speaking someone else’s language, serving purposes that the creator would not have recognized and would not have endorsed.

The Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971, and its more comprehensive 1974 amendments, attempted to bring order to the PAC proliferation. The law created formal rules for how PACs could be organized, how they had to report their activities, and how much they could contribute to candidates. A PAC could give $5,000 to a candidate per election. It had to disclose its donors. It had to maintain a separate bank account for political funds. The FEC would watch.

These were real constraints. They were also, as we will see in the essays that follow, constraints that applied to a particular kind of PAC — the connected PAC, affiliated with a corporation or union or trade association. The rules said less about what would eventually become the more dangerous vehicle: the independent committee, the issue advocacy group, the social welfare organization that was technically not a political committee and therefore technically not bound by the same rules.

The lawyers were already working on it.

Corporate PACs registered with the FEC: 89 in 1974. 1,206 in 1980. Labor built the tool. Business scaled it.

There is a through-line from Sidney Hillman’s workaround in 1943 to the $783 million that twenty-one donor families spent on the 2022 midterms, and the line is not as crooked as you might expect. It runs through the logic of the workaround itself: if the law prohibits direct contributions, create an entity that is technically not the contributor. If that entity is then regulated, create another entity that is technically not the regulated one. The form of compliance is maintained. The substance of the restriction evaporates.

This is not a cynical observation about labor — Hillman was operating in good faith within a system that had specifically targeted union political participation, and the CIO-PAC was a defensive response to a political attack on labor’s power. The irony is not that labor was corrupt. The irony is that the workaround itself was the problem, regardless of who invented it or why. Once the principle was established — that you could circumvent a direct prohibition by routing the money through a separate entity — the principle was available to anyone with a lawyer and a reason.

The corporations had both.

What changed over the subsequent decades was not the principle but the scale, the sophistication, and the degree to which the separate entity became less a workaround and more the primary vehicle. Hillman’s CIO-PAC was a tool in service of a broader political project — the re-election of a president whose policies labor supported. The super PAC, the dark money nonprofit, the undisclosed donor network that spent millions in Virginia in 2026 — these are not tools in service of a broader project. In many cases, they are the project. The money is not supporting a political vision. The money is purchasing a political outcome, through mechanisms so layered and so opaque that tracing the dollar from its source to its destination requires investigative resources that most voters and most journalists do not have.

Sidney Hillman wanted to re-elect Franklin Roosevelt. He built a committee, collected voluntary contributions from union members, and spent the money on an election.

The people who eventually inherited his mechanism wanted something rather different. And they had rather more money to spend.
