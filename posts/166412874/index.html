The Baseline Problem

PART 1: How Do We Know When Trump Gets Dementia?

June 20, 2025

‘This is beyond psychoanalysis - this requires a neurologist’

In June 2025, MSNBC's Lawrence O'Donnell posed a question that exposes a fundamental crisis in American democracy: "If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?" This four-part investigative series examines what neuropsychologists call the "baseline problem" — when someone's cognitive functioning starts from such an unconventional level that further decline becomes nearly impossible to detect.

Drawing on clinical research, expert analysis, and documented evidence, this series explores how Trump's cognitive deterioration has been hiding in plain sight, why media coverage has failed to address it adequately, and what this means for democratic governance. From constitutional confusion to historical ignorance, from media double standards to national security implications, "The Baseline Problem" provides the comprehensive analysis that mainstream media has been reluctant to tackle.

This isn't partisan politics — it's about the fundamental question of cognitive fitness for the most powerful office in the world, and why our current systems for assessment are failing when we need them most.

Introducing the Baseline Problem

On June 19, 2025, MSNBC's Lawrence O'Donnell opened "The Last Word" with a question that cuts to the heart of American democracy: "If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?"

It wasn't a partisan attack or cable news hyperbole. It was a clinical observation that exposes a fundamental problem in how we assess cognitive fitness for political leadership. O'Donnell's insight reveals what neuropsychologists call the "baseline problem" — a situation where someone's starting point is so far from normal that further decline becomes nearly impossible to detect using traditional markers.

"Donald Trump's mental decline, which is more and more obvious," O'Donnell explained, "started from such a low level of mental processing power that it's hard to track his decline. It's from low to lower."

This observation came after Trump made what O'Donnell called the most "indicative of dementia" statement ever uttered by a sitting president.

The Fed Chair Moment: A Case Study in Constitutional Confusion

On June 18, 2025, when asked about Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell's decision not to cut interest rates, Trump said something that would have dominated headlines for weeks if uttered by any other president:

"Maybe I should go to the Fed. Am I allowed to appoint myself at the Fed? I'd do a much better job than these people."

Let that sink in. The President of the United States, in the Oval Office, asked aloud whether he could appoint himself to lead an independent government agency.

O'Donnell was precise in his assessment: "No other president was ever capable of saying anything as stupid or as indicative of dementia as that." But here's the crucial part — this statement reveals multiple layers of cognitive dysfunction:

Constitutional Confusion: Trump doesn't understand that the Fed Chair requires Senate confirmation. He cannot simply "appoint himself" to any position requiring legislative approval.

Separation of Powers Ignorance: The Federal Reserve's independence from direct presidential control is fundamental to its function. Trump's suggestion reveals he doesn't grasp this basic governmental structure.

Executive Limitations: Trump appears unaware that he doesn't even have the legal power to fire the current Fed Chair, let alone replace him unilaterally.

Grandiose Delusion: His belief that he could outperform trained economists and financial experts represents a disconnect from reality that would be concerning in any 79-year-old, let alone one making decisions about war and peace.

The Baseline Problem Defined

But here's where O'Donnell's insight becomes critical. In any other context, these statements would trigger immediate medical evaluation. A CEO, judge, or military officer displaying such fundamental confusion about their role and limitations would face fitness-for-duty assessments.

With Trump, however, we face what neuropsychologists call a premorbid functioning assessment challenge. Traditional cognitive decline detection relies on comparing current performance to previous baselines. But what happens when the baseline itself was already deeply problematic?

O'Donnell noted that Trump "never said that in his first term as president. Am I allowed to appoint myself? He was crazy, but he wasn't that crazy. He was stupid, but he wasn't that stupid."

This suggests measurable decline from an already concerning starting point — exactly the scenario that makes traditional assessment methods unreliable.

Clinical Context: Why This Matters

Neuropsychological evaluation fundamentally depends on establishing premorbid cognitive levels — essentially, how someone functioned before any suspected decline began. Clinicians use various methods to estimate this baseline:

  • Demographic data (education, occupation, socioeconomic status)

  • Historical performance on standardized tests

  • Vocabulary and reading ability (skills that typically resist decline)

  • Informant reports from people who knew the person before symptoms appeared

The problem with Trump is that multiple sources suggest his baseline functioning was already atypical. His communication style has long included:

  • Stream-of-consciousness delivery

  • Frequent tangents and non-sequiturs

  • Hyperbolic language disconnected from factual reality

  • Mixing fictional scenarios with policy discussions

This creates what we might call "diagnostic masking" — where pre-existing patterns obscure the detection of pathological changes.

The Media Response Problem

O'Donnell highlighted a crucial secondary issue: media normalization. He warned that we must "not grow weary of making the point of how singularly stupid and possibly clinically demented Donald Trump's statements are, then we will become part of the normalization process of those statements."

The consequence? "14-year-olds in this country will think it's perfectly normal for a president to say those things."

O'Donnell specifically called out the press corps' double standard: "We must not grow weary of reminding ourselves what the White House press corps would have done if Joe Biden ever said anything like that. And how big the headlines would be in the next day's print editions of The New York Times and The Washington Post, and every newspaper in the country."

This isn't hypothetical. We've seen how minor verbal stumbles by other politicians receive extensive coverage and analysis. Biden's occasional name confusion or verbal hesitations generated immediate calls for cognitive testing, medical transparency, and fitness evaluations.

Trump's suggestion that he could self-appoint as Fed Chair? It barely registered as news.

Historical Precedent and Professional Prediction

O'Donnell revealed that he first asked his central question "on Twitter a week before Donald Trump's first inauguration as president" — establishing that concerns about cognitive fitness preceded any presidency-related stress or decline.

Even more significantly, O'Donnell noted how "a group of mental health professionals got together to write a book about what they perceived as Donald Trump's mental health deficiencies under the title 'The Dangerous Case of Donald Trump.'"

The book, published early in Trump's first presidency, was controversial for violating the "Goldwater Rule" — the professional standard discouraging psychiatrists from diagnosing public figures without direct examination. But O'Donnell pointed out that these professionals made a specific prediction about Trump's trajectory.

"The psychiatrists promised us in the first year of the Trump presidency that there was only one direction that his mental health could go," O'Donnell concluded, "and every day Donald Trump proves them right."

Why This Isn't Partisan Politics

Before dismissing this analysis as partisan attack, consider the stakes. O'Donnell painted a sobering picture of the decision-making context:

"On a day when the president of the United States is reportedly in a meeting in the Situation Room where he is trying to decide how much to support or fully join a war that he opposed a week ago, it must be reported as part of that very same story, that the person who is supposed to be in charge in that Situation Room meeting... doesn't know if he can appoint himself chair of the Federal Reserve."

This isn't about political preferences or policy disagreements. It's about cognitive capacity for complex decision-making during international crises. When someone doesn't understand basic constitutional principles, how can they navigate the nuanced decisions required for national security?

The Assessment Challenge

The Trump case represents a unique challenge in cognitive assessment methodology. Traditional decline markers become unreliable when:

  1. The baseline was already atypical — making change detection difficult

  2. Media has normalized dysfunction — reducing societal recognition of problems

  3. Political considerations override medical ones — preventing objective evaluation

  4. No mechanism exists for mandatory assessment — leaving evaluation to informal observation

This creates a perfect storm where serious cognitive decline can occur in plain sight while remaining largely unaddressed by institutions designed to protect democratic governance.

What Comes Next

Lawrence O'Donnell's question — "If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?" — exposes a fundamental gap in how democracies handle cognitive fitness for leadership. But his analysis goes deeper, revealing specific evidence that decline may already be occurring.

The Fed Chair comment isn't an isolated incident. It's part of a broader pattern of cognitive changes that have been documented by experts, measured by linguistic analysis, and observed by those who've interacted with Trump over time.

In the next part of this series, we'll examine the clinical evidence for this decline — from temporal confusion spanning multiple years to historical ignorance that would concern elementary school teachers. We'll look at expert assessments, statistical analysis of speech patterns, and documented changes that suggest the baseline problem may be masking significant cognitive deterioration.

The question isn't whether Trump's cognitive capacity is declining — multiple lines of evidence suggest it is. The question is whether our democratic institutions and media systems are equipped to recognize and address this decline when it occurs in someone whose baseline functioning was already so far from normal.

As O'Donnell warned, if we become weary of pointing out cognitive dysfunction, we become part of normalizing it. And in a democracy, normalized dysfunction in leadership becomes everyone's problem.

In Part 2, we'll examine the documented evidence of cognitive decline that experts say has been hiding in plain sight. From Joan Rivers "voting" in 2016 to confusing the Declaration of Independence with the Civil War, the clinical markers are more extensive than most people realize.
