Two Weeks That Changed Everything

Part 24: When the Accommodation Finally Started to Break

February 26, 2026

February 26, 2026

Between February 11 and February 25, 2026, three things happened that hadn’t happened in the six years since Jeffrey Epstein’s death:

The full scope of his elite banking connections became undeniable.

Missing documents about accusations against President Trump were discovered and documented.

And powerful men started resigning.

Not all of them. Not the ones who mattered most. But for the first time since Epstein died in federal custody in 2019, the accommodation that had protected his network began to crack.

February 18: What Wexner Revealed Under Oath

Photo illustration: Lindsey Bailey/Axios. Photos: Bloomberg, Astrid Stawiarz, Rick Friedman via Getty Images

One week after Attorney General Pam Bondi refused to turn around and look at Epstein survivors during her congressional testimony, the House Oversight Committee deposed Les Wexner, the 88-year-old founder of Victoria’s Secret and Epstein’s largest known financial benefactor.

The deposition lasted nearly five hours. Wexner testified from his mansion in New Albany, Ohio. And in those five hours, he revealed something that had been hiding in plain sight throughout the entire Epstein scandal: the banking connections that made everything else possible.

Asked about the credentials that led him to give Epstein power of attorney over his finances—essentially control of his entire fortune—Wexner answered plainly:

“His experience at an industrial level, like working at a big company like Bear Stearns, and his personal work for the Rothschild family in France.”

He continued: “Specifically, I spoke to Élie de Rothschild. I mentioned that earlier. So he represented their whole family.”

Under oath. Before the United States Congress.

This wasn’t speculation. This wasn’t conspiracy theory. This was the man who gave Jeffrey Epstein his wealth and his plane and his Manhattan townhouse explaining, on the record, that Epstein’s credentials came from working for one of the world’s most prominent banking families.

At one point during the deposition, Wexner’s attorney leaned in and was caught on a hot mic whispering: “I’ll fucking kill you if you answer another question with more than five words, okay?”

Both chuckled. But the damage was done. The connection was now part of the congressional record.

And it explained what journalists had been documenting for weeks in the Epstein files: extensive business relationships between Epstein and Rothschild banking entities that continued for years after his 2008 conviction for soliciting prostitution from a minor.

The Banking Connections No One Was Talking About

On October 5, 2015—seven years after Epstein pleaded guilty to sex crimes—his company Southern Trust Company Inc. signed a contract with Edmond de Rothschild Holding S.A.

The contract was for $25 million.

The stated purpose: “risk analysis” and “application and use of certain algorithms.”

Twenty-five million dollars for a convicted sex offender to provide algorithm services to one of Europe’s wealthiest banking families.

The contract specified that payment was linked to “outstanding matters between Edmond de Rothschild Holding S.A. and the United States.” In December 2015, Edmond de Rothschild bank entered into a non-prosecution agreement with the Department of Justice under the “Swiss Bank Program,” paying $45 million and admitting they had helped U.S. clients conceal billions in undeclared accounts.

Epstein’s $25 million payment came within days of that DOJ settlement.

Ariane de Rothschild, chair of Edmond de Rothschild bank and holding

Reuters confirmed in early February 2026 that Ariane de Rothschild, CEO of the Edmond de Rothschild Group since 2023, had corresponded with Epstein dozens of times and agreed to multiple meetings between 2013 and 2019—long after his conviction.

The bank initially denied any relationship with Epstein. When documents proved otherwise, they admitted de Rothschild “met with Epstein as part of her normal duties at the bank.”

Her name appears more than 4,400 times in the Justice Department’s Epstein files.

The emails show a personal relationship. In 2015, de Rothschild and Epstein exchanged messages about candles she had helped create for him. “Your favorite mathematical formula is written on it and the smell is made for you only,” she wrote. “It fits with your island.”

In 2016, when de Rothschild was sick with the flu, Epstein invited her to stay at his apartment: “...it would be private. no one the wiser.”

In March 2019—just four months before Epstein’s arrest on federal sex trafficking charges—he emailed her from Paris: “I’m in town for a few days.”

“its been much much too long,” she replied, before they arranged to meet.

None of this is speculation. These are emails released by the U.S. Department of Justice. Business contracts documented in official records. Testimony given under oath before Congress.

And yet, when Wexner revealed the Rothschild connection on February 18, the story barely registered in mainstream American media.

The Connector

There’s another Rothschild connection that has received even less attention: Lynn Forester de Rothschild, who married into the banking family in 2000.

According to Alan Dershowitz—Epstein’s former attorney and the man who negotiated his 2008 plea deal—Lynn Forester de Rothschild introduced him to Epstein at her Martha’s Vineyard estate in the summer of 1996. Dershowitz wrote in his 2019 book that she described Epstein as “an interesting autodidact” he would “enjoy getting to know.”

That introduction led to Dershowitz becoming Epstein’s lawyer and securing a plea deal that gave him 13 months in county jail with work release for crimes that typically carry decades in federal prison.

In July 2025, when Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche interviewed Ghislaine Maxwell over two days, she testified that Lynn Forester de Rothschild introduced Jeffrey Epstein to Prince Andrew. The introduction reportedly happened at Rothschild’s holiday home.

WikiLeaks emails show Lynn Forester de Rothschild maintained a close relationship with Hillary Clinton. In September 2010, when Clinton was Secretary of State, she emailed Rothschild: “I was trying to reach you to tell you and Teddy that I asked Tony Blair to go to Israel as part of our full court press on keeping the Middle East negotiations going.”

Blair had planned to attend a conference in Aspen with the Rothschilds but agreed to Clinton’s request instead.

Clinton’s email concluded: “Let me know what penance I owe you.”

The Secretary of State of the United States asking “penance” of a private citizen for pulling the former Prime Minister of the United Kingdom away from her weekend event.

In January 2015—months before Hillary Clinton announced her presidential candidacy—Lynn Forester de Rothschild emailed Clinton aide Cheryl Mills: “We need to craft the economic message for Hillary.”

None of this establishes criminal activity. But it establishes something arguably more important: the network through which Jeffrey Epstein operated.

He wasn’t a lone financier who somehow gained access to presidents and princes and billionaires. He had connections to one of the world’s most prominent banking families. Those connections opened doors. Those connections provided introductions. Those connections gave him the credentials that men like Les Wexner trusted.

And those connections continued after his conviction.

February 24: The Missing Trump Files

Six days after Wexner’s testimony, MSNBC senior legal reporter Lisa Rubin broke a story that should have been impossible under the Epstein Files Transparency Act.

The Justice Department had withheld specific FBI interview memoranda containing accusations against President Trump.

Rubin’s reporting was based on documents the DOJ itself had released: an index of witness statements produced to Ghislaine Maxwell’s defense team. That index lists every interview, every police report, every document associated with each witness—identified by unique numerical codes.

For one woman who was interviewed by the FBI four times in 2019, Rubin and other journalists found only one of the four interview memos in the public files.

The three missing interviews were the ones where, according to an FBI presentation compiled in 2025, she accused Donald Trump of sexually assaulting her when she was between 13 and 15 years old. Her accusations reportedly included Trump forcing her to perform oral sex and physically hitting her.

The single interview memo that does appear in the public files contains no accusations against Trump. Instead, it shows a woman so afraid of implicating him that she was reluctant to show FBI agents a photograph of Epstein and Trump together—a publicly available photo that had appeared in magazines—”lest she incriminate someone she feared could retaliate against her.”

NPR, examining the consecutive page numbering system used in the Epstein files, calculated that approximately 50 pages are missing that correspond to the three withheld interview memos.

Of the woman’s 15 total documents reflecting her statements to law enforcement, journalists have found only 7. Eight are missing from the public release.

The Epstein Files Transparency Act explicitly prohibits withholding documents based on “embarrassment, reputational harm, or political sensitivity.”

If documents were withheld to protect the President’s reputation, that would constitute a violation of federal law.

The Justice Department has not explained why the documents are missing. The White House responded not by addressing the missing files, but by calling Trump “the most transparent president in history.”

February 25: The Resignations Begin

The next day, two prominent men resigned.

Lawrence Summers discussed relationships with Jeffrey Epstein in a correspondence that lasted for years, documents show.Credit...David Degner for The New York Times

Lawrence Summers announced he would resign from Harvard University at the end of the academic year. The announcement came months after documents showed Summers maintained a close personal relationship with Epstein for years after Epstein’s conviction.

The emails, released in November 2025, showed Summers and Epstein bantering about Summers’s romantic interest in a woman who was not his wife. Epstein described himself as Summers’s “wingman.”

In the exchanges, Summers used racist language to describe the woman, writing to Epstein: “I’d be happy to have a rational affair w yellow peril.”

Summers, a former U.S. Treasury Secretary and one of America’s most prominent economists, had initially tried to continue teaching after the emails were released. But as pressure mounted—including from Senator Elizabeth Warren—he stepped back in November. On February 25, Harvard confirmed he would not return and was resigning permanently.

Bob Kerrey speaking in Omaha in 2012. Credit...Nati Harnik/Associated Press

The same day, Bob Kerrey resigned from the board of Monolith, a Nebraska clean energy start-up where he had served as chairman.

Kerrey, 82, is a former U.S. Senator and former Governor of Nebraska who ran for president in 1992. Documents released by the Justice Department showed he met with Epstein multiple times in 2013 and 2014—years after Epstein’s conviction.

In February 2013, Epstein invited Kerrey to his house while Bill Gates would be there. Kerrey said he’d be out of town but had “enjoyed meeting and talking” with Epstein at an earlier lunch. “Invite me again!” he wrote.

Later emails show invitations involving Ehud Barak, Woody Allen, and Peter Thiel.

Kerrey confirmed his resignation in a text message, saying he was concerned the revelations would “make it difficult for them to succeed.” He added: “If I knew then what I know now, I would not have said yes to a meeting.”

The Nebraska Republican Party had called on political candidates to return donations from Kerrey. At least one Democratic candidate donated Kerrey’s contribution to a Nebraska nonprofit working to end sexual and domestic violence.

The Pattern of Consequences

These resignations bring the total to at least eight prominent figures who have faced career consequences since the Epstein files were released:

Resigned:

  • Lawrence Summers (Harvard professor, former Treasury Secretary)

  • Bob Kerrey (Nebraska company board chairman, former Senator)

  • Kathryn Ruemmler (Goldman Sachs general counsel)

  • Brad Karp (Paul Weiss law firm chairman)

  • Peter Attia (dropped by CBS News)

  • Peter Mandelson (UK: resigned Labour Party, House of Lords, ambassador position)

  • George Mitchell (removed from Queen’s University Belfast)

Stripped of titles:

  • Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor (UK: royal titles, forced from residence)

Staying in position with institutional support:

  • Casey Wasserman (LA 2028 Olympics chairman)

  • Howard Lutnick (Commerce Secretary—White House “fully supports”)

  • Steve Tisch (NY Giants owner—NFL investigating)

Notice the pattern. The most severe consequences have fallen in the United Kingdom. American resignations have come from academic institutions and law firms—not from positions of government power.

The Commerce Secretary who arranged a family vacation to Epstein’s island in 2012—with his four children and nannies, four years after Epstein’s conviction—remains in his position with the full support of the White House.

The Olympics chairman whose flirtatious emails with Ghislaine Maxwell appear in the files remains in his position despite calls for resignation.

And Attorney General Pam Bondi, who refused to turn around and look at survivors and whose department withheld documents potentially in violation of federal law, not only remains in her position but faced no consequences beyond a contentious hearing.

What Changed

So what changed in these two weeks?

The accommodation that protected Epstein’s network for two decades didn’t break because the Justice Department suddenly decided to pursue accountability. It broke because:

  1. Congressional testimony put facts on the record. When Les Wexner testified under oath that Epstein worked for the Rothschild family, that became an undeniable part of the official record. When he revealed a $25 million contract, that became verifiable fact.

  2. Journalists documented what was missing. Lisa Rubin’s reporting on the missing Trump files didn’t rely on anonymous sources or speculation. It relied on comparing the DOJ’s own index against the documents they released. The gaps are mathematically provable.

  3. The scope became undeniable. Ariane de Rothschild’s 4,400 mentions in the files. Fifty pages of missing FBI interviews. Eight prominent men resigning or losing positions. The pattern is too large to dismiss as isolated incidents.

  4. Institutions started calculating. Harvard decided Summers wasn’t worth the controversy. Monolith decided Kerrey wasn’t worth the scrutiny. Goldman Sachs decided Ruemmler had to go. These weren’t moral decisions—they were institutional risk assessments.

But here’s what didn’t change:

No one has been prosecuted. The 2.5 million withheld pages remain withheld. The co-conspirators named in the 2007 draft indictment remain redacted. The Justice Department’s review is “over.”

And President Trump—whose administration withheld documents about accusations against him—declared the DOJ should “just say we have other things to do.”

The Banking Story No One Is Telling

The most significant revelation in these two weeks might be the one receiving the least attention: the documented business relationships between Jeffrey Epstein and Rothschild banking entities.

This isn’t about conspiracy theories. This is about contracts, emails, testimony, and banking settlements that are all part of the public record.

It’s about a convicted sex offender maintaining a $25 million consulting contract with a major European bank seven years after his conviction.

It’s about a banking CEO meeting with that same convicted sex offender dozens of times and corresponding with him hundreds of times after his conviction became public knowledge.

It’s about the man who gave Epstein his wealth explaining under oath that Epstein’s credentials came from working for one of the world’s most prominent banking families.

And it’s about the near-total silence from mainstream American media on these documented facts.

Congress Demands Answers

Representative Robert Garcia speaking from Congress today

As the scope of the cover-up and the banking connections became undeniable, two members of Congress broke the silence that had protected Epstein’s network.

Representative Robert Garcia of California—who had pressed Attorney General Bondi during her February 11 testimony—went public with what the House Oversight Committee had discovered about the missing files.

“I’ve made serious accusations and allegations—not just against Jeffrey Epstein, but also against President Trump—about abuse that she suffered when she was a minor,” Garcia said, referring to the 13-year-old girl whose FBI interviews are missing from the public files.

“We have seen that the DOJ files and the archive manifests clearly show that the interviews and information around this survivor have been removed and are missing from the DOJ files.”

Then he asked the questions that Attorney General Bondi had refused to answer:

“Where are these files? Who removed them? Those questions have to be answered.”

Garcia confirmed what Lisa Rubin had reported: “What we have is a survivor that we have verified, that others have verified. And those files are missing. And it’s not just those—it’s an additional 50% of files that are missing.”

He called the situation what it was: “It is unconscionable. It is illegal.”

Garcia demanded that the interviews and information about the survivor “be released immediately and stopped being hidden from public view.”

The Justice Department has not responded.

Senator Elizabeth Warren addressed the other story the mainstream media was ignoring: the banks.

“There’s another cover-up in the Epstein files that you might not know about,” Warren said. “There’s one group mentioned a whole lot in the files that just stayed under the radar: big bank executives.”

“The executives who helped finance Jeffrey Epstein’s crimes for years and continued to closely associate with him long after he was convicted for sex crimes.”

Warren called on Trump’s bank regulators to launch an investigation. They have the authority, she noted, to fine executives or even ban them from the industry entirely.

“What have they done? Absolutely nothing.”

“If Trump’s regulators refuse to act, Congress needs to do something,” Warren continued. “Congress should be holding hearings to investigate the big banks that helped Epstein in his crimes. Americans deserve accountability.”

Two members of Congress. One demanding the missing files on Trump’s accuser be released. One demanding banks face consequences for financing Epstein’s operation.

Both speaking to a wall of institutional silence.

February 26: The Precedent and the Demand

The wall started to crack.

One day after Senator Warren and Representative Garcia spoke publicly about the cover-up, Garcia made it formal.

The House Oversight Committee conducted a deposition with former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton in Chappaqua, New York. Former President Bill Clinton’s deposition was scheduled for the following day, February 27.

During a break in Hillary Clinton’s deposition, Garcia held a press conference and made the announcement:

“This committee has now set a new precedent about talking to presidents and former presidents. We’re demanding immediately that we ask President Trump to testify in front of our committee and be deposed in front of Oversight Republicans and Democrats.”

The precedent was clear: if both Clintons could be deposed about their Epstein connections, the sitting President whose FBI files were missing from the public release should testify too.

“We want to understand right now, where the missing FBI files are,” Garcia said. “These are files that accuse the president of the United States of serious, serious accusations around sexual abuse, and the fact that they’re not in the files, and have been apparently either removed or discarded, is incredibly concerning.”

For the first time, the Justice Department publicly acknowledged the problem.

“Several individuals and news outlets have recently flagged files related to documents produced to Ghislaine Maxwell in discovery of her criminal case that they claim appear to be missing,” the DOJ said in a statement.

“As with all documents that have been flagged by the public, the Department is currently reviewing files within that category of the production. Should any document be found to have been improperly tagged in the review process and is responsive to the Act, the Department will of course publish it, consistent with the law.”

Notice the language. Not “missing.” Not “withheld.” “Improperly tagged in the review process.”

The files containing a woman’s accusations that President Trump sexually assaulted her when she was 13 to 15 years old weren’t missing. They were just “improperly tagged.”

Even Representative James Comer, the Republican chairman of the Oversight Committee, said the panel was “looking into the NPR report and looking to get an answer from the Department of Justice about what happened.”

Hillary Clinton, in her opening statement, said she “did not recall ever meeting Epstein” and had “no knowledge of crimes committed by him and Maxwell.”

Garcia said Clinton was “completely cooperating with the deposition and the committee” and “answering questions in full faith and in good faith.”

The contrast was stark. The Clintons—who appear in the files but have never been accused of crimes related to Epstein—submitted to depositions and cooperated fully.

President Trump—whose administration released the files with 50+ pages of FBI interviews about him missing, whose Justice Department “improperly tagged” documents containing accusations against him—has not agreed to testify.

Garcia’s demand was now on the record. The precedent was set. The question was whether Trump would follow the same standard he had demanded of the Clintons.

The International Fallout

While the United States debated whether powerful men would face consequences, other countries were already delivering them.

Earlier in the week, the United Kingdom had taken unprecedented action. Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor—formerly known as Prince Andrew—and Peter Mandelson, Britain’s former ambassador to Washington, were both arrested over their ties to Jeffrey Epstein.

Not resigned. Not stripped of titles. Arrested.

The same day Garcia demanded Trump’s testimony, February 26, another major figure fell: Borge Brende resigned as CEO and president of the World Economic Forum.

Brende, a former foreign minister of Norway, had led the organization behind the annual Davos summit for more than eight years. An independent investigation by the forum found that Brende had maintained contact with Epstein into mid-2019—just months before Epstein’s death in federal custody.

The smoking gun was a text exchange from March 2019. Epstein sent Brende a letter written by his lawyers that had been published in The New York Times. The letter referred to Epstein’s conviction and claimed that “the number of young women involved in the investigation has been vastly exaggerated.”

Brende’s response: a thumbs-up emoji.

The World Economic Forum said the investigation found no “additional concerns beyond what has been previously disclosed,” but Brende said he wanted the organization to continue “without distractions.”

Norway—a wealthy, oil-producing nation—was being roiled by the Epstein disclosures perhaps more than any other country.

Thorbjorn Jagland, a former prime minister, was charged with “gross corruption” in connection with his Epstein ties.

Crown Princess Mette-Marit—the future queen—said she regretted “having had any contact with Epstein” after emails showed a “long, close relationship” between them.

Norwegian diplomat Mona Juul, who had been Norway’s ambassador to Jordan and Iraq, quit after reports that she and her husband Terje Rod-Larsen had been left $10 million in Epstein’s will. Police are investigating the couple.

The pattern was clear: countries with functioning institutions were holding powerful people accountable. The UK made arrests. Norway charged former prime ministers and investigated royalty. The World Economic Forum accepted its CEO’s resignation.

In the United States, the Commerce Secretary who took his family to Epstein’s island four years after his conviction remained in office with the White House’s “full support.”

The Olympics chairman whose emails with Maxwell were in the files remained in his position despite calls to resign.

And the President whose FBI interviews containing accusations from a 13-year-old girl were missing from the public files had not agreed to testify—even after both Clintons submitted to depositions.

What It All Means

When the Epstein Files Transparency Act passed with overwhelming bipartisan support, the assumption was that sunlight would bring accountability. The files would reveal the network, and the network would face consequences.

Two weeks in February 2026 showed both the power and the limits of that assumption—and then showed something more: the story was accelerating.

The network was revealed. Contracts were documented. Testimony was given under oath. Missing documents were identified. Congress demanded answers. The Justice Department admitted it was “reviewing” files that had been “improperly tagged.”

Some people resigned. Most didn’t.

Some institutions calculated the risk wasn’t worth it. Others decided it was.

The Clintons—who appear in the files but face no criminal accusations—submitted to depositions and cooperated fully.

President Trump—whose FBI files containing accusations from a 13-year-old are missing from the public release—was formally asked to testify. The precedent had been set. The demand was on the record.

The United Kingdom arrested a prince and a former ambassador. Norway charged a former prime minister and investigated its future queen. The World Economic Forum accepted its CEO’s resignation.

And the United States? The Justice Department was “reviewing” whether documents had been “improperly tagged” while refusing to explain how 50 pages of FBI interviews ended up missing from files that were supposed to be completely transparent.

The Commerce Secretary who visited Epstein’s island with his children remained in office.

The Olympics chairman stayed in his position.

And the President had not agreed to testify.

This is what elite impunity looks like when the accommodation starts to break. Not collapse. Not full accountability. But no longer complete silence either.

The question isn’t whether Jeffrey Epstein operated within an elite network that protected him. The documents make that undeniable.

The question isn’t whether files were withheld. Congress has confirmed they were.

The question now is whether a sitting president will be held to the same standard as his predecessors—whether Trump will testify under oath about his Epstein connections the way the Clintons just did.

And whether the Justice Department will explain how FBI interviews containing accusations against that president ended up “improperly tagged” instead of released to the public as the law required.

Two weeks in February gave us part of the answer. But the story isn’t over.

The survivors are still waiting. Congress is still demanding answers. And the files that were supposed to bring transparency have revealed something darker: a system where the closer you are to power, the less likely you are to face consequences.

It’s not the answer the survivors deserved. But it’s the one that finally has powerful people starting to sweat.
