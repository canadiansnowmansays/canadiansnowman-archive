The Pinocchio Presidency

The Numbers Game

Part 3: When You Fire the Scorekeeper, the Score Gets Very Interesting

February 11, 2026

When you fire the scorekeeper, the score suddenly improves by 100%, and the market's standing ovation lasts exactly two minutes...

Previously on The Pinocchio Presidency: Our hero’s nose had grown from investment fantasies (Part 1) and metastasized through 18 distinct falsehoods on national television (Part 2). The nose now measures roughly 94 feet and requires its own ZIP code. Today, it discovers the stock market.

Prologue: Follow the Nose (It Leads to the Money)

I’ve been staring at economic data for most of my adult life. Ran a business for many years, watched interest rates like a hawk watches field mice as I managed my portfolio, and learned one immutable truth: when someone fires the referee and then starts winning, you don’t just check the replay. You check who bet on the game.

On Wednesday morning, February 11, 2026, the Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that U.S. employers added 130,000 jobs in January—nearly double what economists had forecast. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.3 percent. The S&P 500 jolted half a percent higher at the open. Morgan Stanley’s chief economic strategist declared it “vindication” for the Fed’s holding pattern. The White House Council of Economic Advisers posted in ALL CAPS that the number came in “FAR above expectations.”

And somewhere between Palm Beach and a portfolio of overvalued real estate, a man who had spent the previous week lowering everyone’s expectations watched the stock market rally on numbers produced by an agency whose last director he’d fired for producing bad numbers.

The nose quivered. Not with growth this time—with satisfaction.

Because here’s the thing about the Pinocchio Presidency that Parts 1 and 2 didn’t fully capture: the nose doesn’t just lie for ego. It lies for profit. And the profit motive changes everything about how you read today’s jobs report.

Chapter One: The Billionaire’s Report Card

Let’s talk about who actually benefits when the market rallies on a strong jobs number. Because the celebration on Wednesday morning wasn’t just about American workers. It was about American wealth—and the increasingly narrow slice of Americans who hold it.

The S&P 500 popped 0.5 percent at the open. Energy stocks led. Then industrials. Then technology. In the time it takes to read this paragraph, approximately $200 billion in paper wealth was created.

Now ask yourself: who owns that wealth?

Not the health care workers who accounted for 82,000 of the 130,000 new jobs. Not the construction workers who make up the other bright spot. Not the federal employees who lost another 35,000 positions to the administration’s chainsaw governance.

No. That $200 billion flowed overwhelmingly to the people who already own the market. The top one percent of American households—whose net worth, as the Federal Reserve documented just this quarter, has climbed to a record 32 percent of the national total. The billionaire cabinet members. The tech oligarchs. The real estate mogul in the Oval Office whose financial empire floats on market sentiment the way a yacht floats on water it never touches.

The nose didn’t grow an inch. Why would it? The nose has learned that good numbers are good business.

Chapter Two: The Art of the Expectations Deal

Watch the setup. It’s beautiful, in the way a three-card monte game is beautiful if you appreciate the craft.

Monday, February 9: Kevin Hassett, director of the National Economic Council, goes on CNBC. His message: don’t panic if the jobs number is low. AI is changing the labor market. Deportations are reducing the workforce. “One shouldn’t panic if you see a sequence of numbers that are lower than you’re used to.”

Tuesday, February 10: Peter Navarro, trade adviser, appears on Fox Business. He’s “not expecting a weak number” but also tells investors to “reset expectations.” Translation: brace yourselves, folks.

Wednesday, February 11, 8:30 AM: The number drops. 130,000 jobs. Nearly double the forecast.

The White House pounces. “FAR above expectations!” the Council of Economic Advisers posts. They specifically highlight private-sector job growth and the decrease in government jobs—as though firing federal workers is an achievement rather than a policy choice.

Now, I’ve run a business. I know what it looks like when someone sandbagging their quarterly projections so they can “beat expectations” and watch the stock price pop. Every CEO in America has done this dance at earnings time. You whisper to analysts that things look rough. You let the street set a low bar. Then you step over it and call yourself an Olympic high jumper.

The nose, which had been resting comfortably at 94 feet, extended approximately six inches. Not for the numbers themselves—those might be perfectly accurate—but for the theatrical engineering of a market rally that enriched the performer and his entire supporting cast.

Market goes up. Portfolio goes up. Net worth goes up. Real estate valuations go up. Mar-a-Lago membership—currently $1 million initiation—looks like an even better deal. The billionaires who funded the campaign watch their returns compound. The oligarchs who got Cabinet seats see their holdings swell.

And the home health aide in Akron who just got one of those 82,000 new health care jobs? She’s making $16 an hour and wondering why groceries still cost so much.

The K-shaped economy, they’re calling it. I’d call it the Pinocchio economy: the nose points up for the people at the top, and blocks the view for everyone else.

Chapter Two-and-a-Half: The Two-Minute Standing Ovation

Grab your popcorn, because this is the part the White House won’t be posting about.

At 8:30 AM Eastern, the Bureau of Labor Statistics released its number. 130,000 jobs. Nearly double the forecast. Futures on all three major indices jolted upward. The algorithms did what algorithms do: they read the headline, bought the dip, and moved on.

The celebration lasted approximately two minutes.

By 10:20 AM, here’s what “FAR above expectations!” actually looked like on a screen:

The S&P 500 spiked to 6,993.48 — up 0.80% — then reversed and fell to 6,928.11. Down 0.20%. A full one-percent swing from peak to trough, the entire gain evaporated and then some.

The Dow Jones surged to 50,499.04 — up 0.65% — before cratering to 50,041.60. Down 0.29%. Nearly a full percentage point of whiplash.

The Nasdaq — the tech-heavy index most sensitive to interest rate expectations — spiked to 23,320.62, up a triumphant 1.02%, before plunging to 22,971.87. Down 0.57%. A swing of more than 1.5% from top to bottom. The cliff formation on the chart looks less like a market and more like a ski jump designed by someone who forgot to build the landing.

The S&P 500’s response to “FAR above expectations!” — a two-minute rally followed by a full reversal into the red.

The Dow told the same story. Up 0.65%, then down 0.29%. The billionaire index couldn’t even hold its own celebration.

The Nasdaq was the most honest of the three. Up 1.02%, then down 0.57%. Tech investors did the math: “better than expected” means no rate cuts until July. Your mortgage isn’t getting cheaper. Your growth stocks just got repriced.

Three indices. Three identical cliff dives. One message: the algorithms bought the headline, and then the humans actually read the report.

What did they find? A million jobs wiped from the historical record. Rate-cut expectations pushed from June to July. An economy so narrow that one sector — health care — accounted for more than sixty percent of all gains. Treasury yields climbing, which means borrowing costs just went up, not down.

The White House posted “FAR above expectations!” at approximately the same moment the market began its descent. The nose grew six inches on the headline. The market shaved it back in ninety minutes.

This is what happens when you spend three days sandbagging expectations and then the smart money reads the fine print. The nose celebrates. The market corrects. The health care worker in Akron doesn’t own enough stock to notice either way.

Chapter Three: The Firing That Changed the Scoreboard

Let’s rewind to August 2025, because you can’t understand today’s celebration without understanding last summer’s execution.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics released its July jobs report showing 73,000 jobs added, accompanied by downward revisions of 258,000 jobs for May and June combined. The numbers were ugly. The numbers were also, by every professional standard that has governed American economic statistics since 1884, accurate.

President Trump’s response was not to question his economic policies. It was to fire BLS Commissioner Erika McEntarfer.

McEntarfer had been confirmed by the Senate 86-8—the kind of bipartisan margin that used to signal “this person is so qualified that even Washington agrees.” She had twenty years in federal statistical work. Her crime, in the president’s words, was “faking the Jobs Numbers before the Election to try and boost Kamala’s chances of Victory” and producing “VERY inaccurate numbers.”

Even William Beach—Trump’s own BLS Commissioner from his first term—called the firing “totally groundless” and warned it “sets a dangerous precedent.”

The nose grew twelve feet that week. But here’s what nobody talked about: the market had dropped on those bad July numbers. Trump’s net worth took a hit. His billionaire friends’ portfolios bled red. The bad numbers were bad for business—the president’s business, specifically.

Fire the scorekeeper. Get a new scorekeeper. Watch the scores improve. Watch the market rally. Watch the portfolio recover.

It’s not complicated. It’s just breathtakingly brazen.

Chapter Four: The Numbers Factory (And Why the Boss Can’t Touch the Machines—Probably)

Now, in fairness to the approximately 2,400 career statisticians at the Bureau of Labor Statistics who didn’t sleep well last night, let me explain why today’s specific numbers are almost certainly clean.

The BLS doesn’t operate like a White House communications shop. It operates like a factory—a massive, distributed, mechanized factory where thousands of data points flow in from 119,000 businesses and 60,000 households, get processed through algorithms refined over decades, and produce an output that no single human controls.

Beach explained this on CNN with devastating clarity: “The commissioner of labor statistics has no access to the collection of the data, has no access to the summation of the data. The commissioner doesn’t see the number for the first time until Wednesday morning, usually around 11:00, in a meeting.”

The person Trump fired—and the person Trump will replace her with—doesn’t collect the data. Doesn’t process it. Doesn’t tabulate it. Doesn’t even see it until three days before publication.

The nose, confronted with this engineering reality, had to pivot to a new strategy. You can’t cook the books if the kitchen is locked. But you can fire the health inspector and hope the next one gets the message.

Chapter Five: The Chill That Pays Dividends

You don’t have to put your thumb on the scale to corrupt a statistical agency. You just have to make everyone who works there afraid of what happens when the scale reads wrong. And “wrong,” in this administration, means “bad for the boss’s bottom line.”

Former BLS Commissioner Erica Groshen identified the precise mechanism after the McEntarfer firing. She warned that anxious employees might “discover some glitch” and hesitate to report it. Or face pressure to adjust methodologies. Or simply second-guess the professional judgment calls that statisticians make every month—seasonal adjustments, survey weighting, benchmark revisions—knowing that the last person who presided over unfavorable output got publicly humiliated and fired.

In September 2025, BLS staff took the extraordinary step of issuing a public statement: “Our job is to deliver economic data guided by law and statistical practice—not partisan whim.”

When career civil servants feel compelled to publicly declare they’re not cooking the books, the soufflé has already fallen. And the person who knocked over the soufflé is now celebrating the replacement dessert.

Chapter Six: The Beautiful Contradiction (Or: How to Celebrate and Deny Simultaneously)

Here’s where the satire practically writes itself, because the February 11 report is a masterpiece of cognitive dissonance.

What the White House is celebrating: 130,000 jobs! Beat expectations! Private sector growth! Unemployment down!

What the White House is pretending doesn’t exist: The annual benchmark revision—a standard methodological recalibration against hard tax records—wiped out over one million jobs from earlier estimates. The economy added just 181,000 jobs in all of 2025, down from a previous estimate of 584,000. In percentage terms, this was the largest revision since 2009.

Fed Governor Christopher Waller, who dissented from the January rate hold, described 2025 employment growth in words that should be tattooed on the White House briefing room wall: “Zero. Zip. Nada.”

The administration fired the BLS commissioner for reporting bad numbers, spent an entire year claiming the economy was booming, and now the revised data shows the economy created fewer jobs in twelve months than a single good month would normally produce.

The nose grew fourteen feet on that revision alone, but it was behind a curtain, so nobody in the briefing room noticed.

As Tony Romm reported from the NYT: “The last time that B.L.S. posted a major revision, Mr. Trump fired the leader of the agency.” One wonders whether Brett Matsumoto—the career economist Trump has now nominated to lead BLS—read that sentence before accepting the job. Perhaps someone should slip it under his door with a note: “Congratulations on your appointment. Previous occupant was terminated for producing accurate data. Good luck.”

Chapter Seven: The K-Shaped Nose

Let me tell you what 130,000 jobs actually looks like on the ground, because the market rally obscures a reality that should make the champagne go flat.

Health care added 82,000 positions—more than sixty percent of all gains from a single sector. These are the home health aides, the nursing assistants, the medical billers. Essential workers earning essential-worker wages in a country where the CEO of Mondelez just told investors that consumers are “fed up with the price increases” and “don’t feel good about their personal economic outlook.”

Construction added 33,000. Everything else? Flat or bleeding. Transportation and warehousing lost 11,000. Information lost 12,000. Financial activities lost 22,000. Manufacturing—the sector that tariffs were supposed to resurrect from the dead like some kind of industrial Lazarus—added a grand total of 5,000 jobs. Five thousand. In a country of 160 million workers. You could fit them all in a high school gymnasium.

Meanwhile, the executives are saying the quiet part loud. Delta is selling more premium seats than main cabin. Chipotle discovered that 60 percent of its customers earn over $100,000 and has decided to focus on them. McDonald’s is reviving “Extra Value Meals” because lower-income traffic is collapsing. PayPal withdrew its long-term forecasts, citing a “K-shaped economy.”

The net worth of the top one percent: a record 32 percent of the national total. Households earning under $75,000: spending less on discretionary items than they did in 2019—before the pandemic, before the inflation surge, before the tariff wars.

The nose, you see, is K-shaped too. It points up for the penthouse and blocks the sunlight for the ground floor. The market rally that followed today’s report will add billions to the portfolios of the people who already have billions. The health care worker in Akron will get her $16 an hour and pray her landlord doesn’t raise the rent again.

This is who benefits from good numbers. This is why the expectations were lowered all week. This is why the scorekeeper got fired when the score was bad. Follow the nose. It leads directly to the money.

Chapter Eight: The Trust Deficit

Here’s where I stop laughing and start worrying, because the consequences of this game extend far beyond one man’s portfolio.

When portfolio managers can’t trust government statistics, they demand higher risk premiums. Your retirement account grows slower. Companies pay more to borrow. The whole system gets more expensive for everyone—except the people rich enough to hedge against uncertainty, who actually profit from it.

When the Federal Reserve can’t trust the employment data, it makes worse decisions about interest rates. Today’s report pushed rate-cut expectations from June to July. That means your mortgage stays expensive longer. Your car loan costs more. Your credit card rate stays elevated. Because the Fed has to wonder—just a little, in the back of its institutional mind—whether the data it’s using to set rates for 330 million Americans has been even slightly compromised by the political environment surrounding its production.

When international investors doubt the integrity of American economic reporting, capital flows elsewhere. Look at Russia, where official data is so unreliable that international organizations create their own estimates. Look at China, where everyone knows growth numbers are manufactured to hit targets. Those aren’t cautionary tales from distant lands—they’re previews of what happens when political leaders decide that economic data should serve the leader rather than the led.

The irony is exquisite: Trump’s assault on BLS credibility threatens the very market rally that enriches him. Destroy trust in the numbers, and you destroy the foundation on which the market’s confidence rests. It’s like a man sawing off the branch he’s sitting on while celebrating how high up he is.

The nose has reached a philosophical crisis. It needs the numbers to be credible enough to move markets, but malleable enough to never deliver bad news. It needs the scorekeepers to be independent enough to be believed, but frightened enough to never embarrass the boss. It needs the data to be real and fake simultaneously—Schrödinger’s jobs report, alive and dead until the president opens the box and decides which version to tweet about.

Chapter Nine: A Note from North of the Border

I write these words from Toronto, where we have Statistics Canada—an agency that, whatever its imperfections, has never had its director fired for reporting inconvenient numbers. That’s not because Canadian politicians are more virtuous. It’s because the independence of statistical agencies is treated as a load-bearing wall, not a decorative partition you can knock out for a better view of your portfolio.

Canada’s economy is inextricably linked to America’s. When U.S. economic data loses credibility, Canadian businesses and policymakers lose a critical navigation tool. The Bank of Canada watches the Fed, which watches the BLS, which is now watching over its shoulder for the next presidential Truth Social post.

Up here, we’re watching the nose grow with a mixture of horror and recognition. We’ve seen what happens when democratic institutions erode—we had our own trucker convoy moment. But we haven’t yet reached the point where the prime minister fires the head of Statistics Canada for reporting GDP numbers he doesn’t like.

Yet.

Epilogue: The Nose Rests (The Market Doesn’t)

As I write this, the S&P 500 is down 0.18%. The Dow is down 0.52%. The Nasdaq is down 0.43%. All three celebrated the “FAR above expectations” jobs report for approximately two minutes before reversing into the red — three identical cliff dives that would make an Olympic judge wince.

Somewhere in Palm Beach, someone is watching the same charts I am, wondering why the standing ovation was so short. The answer is simple: the market can read. And what it read — a million jobs revised away, rate cuts pushed to July, an economy running on one sector and fumes — was not worth celebrating, no matter how loudly the White House posted about it.

The billionaires will be fine. They’re always fine. The portfolios will recover, the Mar-a-Lago membership will hold its value, and the K-shaped economy will continue pointing up for the people at the top. But for ninety minutes on a Wednesday morning, the market told the truth that the nose couldn’t: good headlines don’t pay the rent, beat expectations don’t lower your mortgage rate, and firing the scorekeeper doesn’t change the score.

It just changes who believes it.

The nose, for now, rests at approximately 94 feet, six inches. Today’s numbers were probably real. Today’s celebration was definitely performative. And the institution that produced both—the Bureau of Labor Statistics, 141 years of methodological integrity now running on institutional muscle memory and the stubborn professionalism of 2,400 career civil servants—is one bad report away from its next crisis.

The question isn’t whether today’s data can be trusted. It probably can.

The question is whether tomorrow’s can. And whether the people who spent this morning posting “FAR above expectations!” even care about the answer, so long as the nose keeps pointing toward the money.

And on Friday, the same agency delivers the Consumer Price Index—the inflation number that determines whether the Fed cuts rates, whether your mortgage gets cheaper, and whether the K-shaped economy tilts further toward the penthouse. Same institution. Same 2,400 career statisticians. Same political pressure. Same question: can we trust the thermometer when the person who last held it was fired for reading the temperature honestly?

The nose twitches. Friday is only two days away.
