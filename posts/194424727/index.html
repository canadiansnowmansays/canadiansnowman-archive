Holy Holy (Terms and Conditions Apply)

A brief field report on the new state religion, the angels overhead, and the invitation that got lost in the mail

April 19, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays · Sunday, April 2026 · Sunday Satire · 1 of 3

“Holy Hell — this is the White House. 🤦‍♂️🤦‍♂️🤦‍♂️” — @mjfree, X, April 2026

A three-for-one Sunday. We begin in the White House chapel, which is not supposed to exist. The Miracle of the Golden Arches follows. The gymnasium closes it out. Pace yourself.

The angels arrived at the White House sometime last week, accompanied by a live band. Your correspondent has the TikTok footage to prove it. There was a cello. There was a fiddle. There were raised hands, closed eyes, and the repeated assurance — sung with considerable conviction — that God is holy, holy, and in case you missed it, holy again. The Executive Office of the President has apparently expanded its portfolio.

The event, captured across multiple frames of what can only be described as charismatic Christian worship, took place in what appears to be one of the White House’s more formal reception rooms — the kind with arched windows, decorative cornices, and an American flag positioned helpfully to the left of the worship band, lest anyone confuse the proceedings with a non-governmental revival meeting. Badges were visible on several attendees. The podium remained. The Constitution, one assumes, was somewhere in the building.

“Holy, holy” — repeated with the confidence of people who have checked and confirmed that the Establishment Clause does not apply to them personally.

The singing was sincere. That much is beyond dispute. Arms extended upward in the universal gesture of charismatic praise — a posture that communicates simultaneously “I am receiving divine blessing” and “I am also blocking the view of the person behind me.” Eyes were raised ceilingward. Mouths were open. The band played on. This is what faith looks like when it has cleared security.

And then there was the orb.

In this frame — and I invite the reader to examine it carefully — a luminous sphere hovers directly above the head of a gentleman in a dark suit bearing an EEOB credential badge. It is white, perfectly round, and radiates with a quiet certainty that suggests it has not come this far to be explained away by physics. A skeptic would note that video lights and camera lens flares are common at indoor events. A theologian would note that the Executive Office of the President has been visited by a celestial body. I will leave it to the reader to decide which interpretation best serves the national interest.

It is worth noting that former President Trump was once photographed in Riyadh touching a glowing orb of a rather different character. That orb was Saudi. This orb is American. Progress, one supposes, takes many forms.

The theological infrastructure for these proceedings was laid in February, when President Trump established the White House Faith Office — a federal initiative led by Senior Advisor Paula White-Cain and dedicated, in the administration’s own framing, to addressing anti-Christian bias in the United States. The office replaced the previous Office of Faith-Based and Community Initiatives, which had operated under several administrations and had the considerable disadvantage, from the current perspective, of not specifying which faith.

The constitutional question this raises was addressed with admirable directness by Greg Laurie, senior pastor of Harvest Christian Fellowship, who visited the White House this week for one of its worship events and was briefly — briefly — denied entry at the door. He later described this moment of temporary exclusion as a striking metaphor for “the eternal stakes of faith.” The pearly gates, apparently, also have a guest list. He made it inside. He shared a verse from the book of Esther. He reminded the assembled staff that God had placed them there for such a time as this.

On the Establishment Clause, Laurie has been equally forthcoming. In a February blog post, he argued that the phrase “separation of church and state” does not appear in the Constitution — which is technically accurate — and that Thomas Jefferson’s original intent was to keep the state’s influence out of the church, rather than the other way around. This is a reading of Jefferson that Jefferson, who was a Deist and allergic to organised religion in most of its institutional forms, might find surprising. But Jefferson is not available for comment, and Pastor Laurie is, and the White House Faith Office is open for business, so here we are.

The Establishment Clause, briefly consulted, was found to run in only one direction. Convenient, that.

Your correspondent, writing from Canada — a country that has its own complicated relationship with church and state, but has at least had the decency to be embarrassed about it — wishes to raise a small procedural question. The First Amendment to the United States Constitution contains, as its very first words, a prohibition on the establishment of religion by Congress. It was placed first not by accident. The Founders, several of whom had crossed an ocean specifically to escape state religion, felt it worth mentioning before anything else — before speech, before the press, before assembly, before petitioning for redress. Before everything.

One wonders if the invitation to worship at the White House was extended to the full spectrum of American faith traditions. The United States is home to approximately 3.45 million Muslims, 7.5 million Jews, 2.5 million Buddhists, 2.5 million Hindus, and an assorted plurality of Sikhs, Bahá’ís, Jains, and Americans who find the whole enterprise of organised religion somewhat exhausting but are trying to keep an open mind. Perhaps their invitations were sent by standard post and have not yet arrived. It is, after all, a busy time for the postal service.

Allah’s invitation appears to have been lost in the mail. This is surprising only if you have not been paying attention.

The timing, it must be said, was impeccable. While the congregation raised its hands in praise and the angels circled the EEOB orb-bearer, the United States was in the midst of an active military engagement with Iran and continued operations affecting Lebanon — countries that are, in the main, Muslim. The airstrikes, one assumes, were not suspended for the worship set. The targeting coordinates were not paused while the fiddle played. The God being praised as holy, holy, holy did not, to the best of available reporting, respond with instructions to stand down.

There is a long tradition in Western military history of soldiers on both sides of a conflict invoking the same deity’s blessing before battle. History has not resolved this theological puzzle to anyone’s satisfaction. The White House worship session does not resolve it either, but it does add a fiddle, which is something.

I do not question the sincerity of the worshippers. The faith on display appeared genuine, the emotion unperformed, the raised hands unironic. People in the grip of religious experience are not, in that moment, thinking about constitutional law, geopolitical optics, or what a 74-year-old Canadian satirist will make of their TikTok footage. They are thinking about God. This is, in its way, admirable.

What is less admirable is the institutional backdrop — the podium, the flag, the security badges, the reception room of the people’s house — which transforms a private act of faith into a public declaration of whose faith is welcome here. The message delivered, whether intended or not, is clear: this God, this tradition, this expression of worship is at home in the White House. The others may apply for a visitor’s pass.

The First Amendment asked for so little. It asked only that the government not pick a side. It asked only that the house belonging to all Americans not become the house of some Americans’ God. It was not, historically speaking, an unreasonable request. It is being declined, cheerfully, with a live band and a celestial orb, on TikTok, for all the world to see.

The angels, if they were present, have presumably returned to wherever angels go when the set list ends. The orb has dissipated, or relocated, or been explained as video equipment by someone with insufficient imagination. The band has packed up the cello. The worshippers have returned to their desks in the Executive Office of the President, where the daily work of governance continues — tariffs, deportations, the occasional Middle Eastern air campaign.

God, if consulted, has not yet issued a public statement. His press office, like so many others, appears to be understaffed.

As always, your Canadian correspondent remains available to observe the proceedings from a respectful distance, a Timmies in hand, and a complete bewilderment at the speed with which a constitutional republic can redecorate.

The Miracle of the Golden Arches follows. Do not skip it on account of the angels.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.
