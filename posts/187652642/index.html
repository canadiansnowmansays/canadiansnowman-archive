Canada Already Ran Your Experiment

The Trudeau Precedent Demolishes the Case Against Jack Schlossberg

February 11, 2026

I had just turned thirteen years old when John F. Kennedy was assassinated. I mourned him here in Toronto, as millions did across Canada and around the world. I won’t apologize for that, and I won’t let Jonathan Chait, writing in The Atlantic, retroactively diagnose my grief — and the inspiration that preceded it — as some kind of pathology he calls being “Camelot-besotted.

Jack Schlossberg and his grandfather, President John F. Kennedy. Sixty-three years apart, the same question: can the name carry the mission, or does it have to be earned? Democracy says both

Chait’s piece, “Please, Not Another Kennedy,” argues that Jack Schlossberg — JFK’s 33-year-old grandson, now running for Congress in New York’s Twelfth District with Nancy Pelosi’s endorsement — is a dynastic lightweight trading on a famous name. His résumé is thin. His Vogue columns were unremarkable. His primary qualification, Chait insists, is being a Kennedy, and being a Kennedy is actually a negative signal because look at Ted, and Michael, and Bobby Jr.

It’s a clever argument. It’s also one that Canada already tested in real time — and the results should give Chait pause.

The Drama Teacher They Laughed At

In 2012, when Justin Trudeau began seriously pursuing the leadership of the Liberal Party of Canada, the reaction from our commentariat was virtually indistinguishable from Chait’s dismissal of Schlossberg. Every element was there: the famous father, the thin résumé, the celebrity profile that seemed to precede any substantive accomplishment.

Pierre Elliott Trudeau had been Canada’s transformative prime minister — our closest equivalent to the Kennedy mystique, a leader who remade national identity and inspired a generation. When his eldest son entered politics, the sneering was relentless. Justin was a drama teacher. A snowboard instructor. A pretty face. The Conservative Party of Canada distilled the entire critique into a four-word attack ad that became iconic: “Nice hair, though.”

The parallels to Chait’s treatment of Schlossberg are almost eerie. Where Chait mocks Schlossberg’s Vogue columns as “indistinguishable from something that could have been produced by an adequately trained high-school student,” Canadian pundits mocked Trudeau’s classroom career as proof he lacked the gravitas for governance. Where Chait suggests Schlossberg has “failed upward,” Trudeau’s critics argued he had never failed at anything because he’d never been entrusted with anything worth failing at. Where Chait cherry-picks Kennedy family scandals to argue the name itself is tainted, Trudeau’s opponents dredged up Pierre’s polarizing legacy — the National Energy Program, the War Measures Act, Western alienation — to argue the Trudeau brand was toxic outside Montreal and Toronto.

Justin Trudeau won three consecutive federal elections. He governed a G7 nation for nearly a decade. He navigated Donald Trump’s first term, signed a renegotiated continental trade agreement, legalized cannabis, implemented a national carbon price, and managed the early pandemic response. You can argue about how well he did any of these things — many Canadians ultimately concluded not well enough — but the thesis that a dynasty son with a thin résumé is categorically unfit for leadership was demolished by the evidence.

Prime Minister Justin Trudeau and his father, Prime Minister Pierre Elliott Trudeau. Canada already ran this experiment. The drama teacher they laughed at governed a G7 nation for nearly a decade — and was ultimately judged on his own record, not his father's.

The Fallacy of Genealogical Determinism

The most intellectually dishonest move in Chait’s essay is his statistical guilt-by-association argument. He catalogues Kennedy family tragedies and scandals — Ted Kennedy’s Chappaquiddick disaster, Michael Kennedy’s affair with the family babysitter, Michael Skakel’s murder conviction — and then writes: “if you had to pick as your member of Congress a Kennedy male or a random person living in the district, you’re probably safer with the latter.”

Think about what’s actually being argued here. A 33-year-old man who has been involved in precisely zero scandals should be judged unfit for office because of the behaviour of his great-uncles and distant cousins. This isn’t political analysis. It’s genealogical determinism — the idea that family bloodline predicts individual character. It is, frankly, the kind of reasoning that most progressives would reject instantly if it were applied to any other family or any other context.

By this logic, Justin Trudeau should have been disqualified because his father imposed martial law during the October Crisis. By this logic, every Adams after John Quincy was suspect. By this logic, we should audit the family trees of every congressional candidate and calculate a scandal probability index.

And if we’re applying genealogical determinism consistently, we might note that the current President of the United States inherited his career from a father whose business practices included racial discrimination in housing, fraud allegations, and tax schemes that the New York Times documented in exhaustive detail. Fred Trump’s estate was the launchpad for everything Donald Trump became. If bloodline is destiny, Chait has picked a curious moment in American history to enforce that standard — and a curious direction in which to aim it.

Chait is too smart for this argument. He’s making it anyway because it serves a conclusion he reached before he started writing.

What Chait Gets Wrong About RFK Jr.

The centrepiece of Chait’s case is Robert F. Kennedy Jr., whose catastrophic tenure at Health and Human Services has, Chait argues, proved that “being a member of the Kennedy family does not qualify you for public service.” From this he extrapolates that Democrats should abandon their “Kennedy trance” entirely.

But RFK Jr. actually proves the opposite of Chait’s thesis. Bobby Jr. didn’t rise to political prominence because he was a Kennedy. He rose because he built a massive following as an anti-vaccine activist, conspiracy theorist, and populist provocateur. His Kennedy name gave him initial media access, certainly, but his appeal to the MAGA base was rooted in ideology, not dynasty. Trump didn’t appoint him to HHS because his last name was Kennedy. Trump appointed him because he was useful — a Democratic defector who could lend bipartisan cover to an administration intent on dismantling public health infrastructure.

If anything, the RFK Jr. debacle is an argument about the dangers of ideological extremism, not dynastic politics. Plenty of non-Kennedys in the Trump administration have been equally catastrophic in their roles. The family name is incidental to the damage.

And while we’re on the subject of dynasty in government: Chait’s essay appeared during an administration that has installed the president’s son-in-law as a Middle East envoy, elevated his eldest son as a political kingmaker, and turned the family name into a global business brand that trades directly on the power of the presidency. If Democrats maintaining a soft spot for the Kennedys constitutes a “royal family,” what do we call a sitting president whose children and in-laws operate simultaneously as government officials, campaign surrogates, and commercial beneficiaries of the office? Chait’s dynastic alarm, it seems, rings selectively.

What I Remember — And What It Meant

Chait uses the word “Camelot-besotted” the way one might describe a medical condition — something Democrats need to be cured of. I’d like to offer a different perspective from someone who actually lived through the original Camelot, even from across the border.

When Kennedy spoke about going to the moon, about bearing any burden in the defence of liberty, about asking what you could do for your country — these were not slogans for a personality cult. They were invitations to civic participation. They inspired a generation of young people in Canada, in Europe, across the developing world, to believe that democratic governance could be ambitious, that public service was honourable, that nations could aspire to their better angels.

Was there mythology involved? Of course. Was the Kennedy presidency more limited in its actual legislative accomplishments than its rhetoric suggested? Absolutely. But Chait’s reduction of that inspiration to naïve celebrity worship misunderstands something fundamental about how democratic movements sustain themselves. People need to believe that leadership matters, that character and vision are real, that politics can be more than a transactional exercise in interest-group management. The Kennedy legacy, at its best, carried that belief forward.

Consider the contrast with what Americans are being offered now. “Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country” versus an administration that treats government as a vehicle for personal enrichment and loyalty extraction. If Democrats are guilty of romanticizing the Kennedy era, perhaps it’s because the alternative on offer in 2026 is so devoid of idealism that even a sixty-year-old memory looks nourishing by comparison.

The question is not whether Jack Schlossberg deserves a congressional seat by birthright. Obviously he doesn’t. The question is whether he should be disqualified by birthright. And the answer, just as obviously, is no.

The Test That Actually Matters

Here is what the Trudeau precedent ultimately teaches us, and it’s a lesson that cuts both ways.

Justin Trudeau was not destroyed by his father’s name. He was ultimately undone by his own record — by scandals that were his alone (SNC-Lavalin, blackface photos), by policy fatigue, by the natural erosion that comes after nearly a decade in power. Canadians eventually judged him not as Pierre’s son but as his own man, and found him wanting. That is exactly how democracy is supposed to work.

If Schlossberg wins a congressional seat and proves to be a mediocrity, voters will have the opportunity to replace him. If he surprises everyone — as Trudeau did for several years — then the democratic experiment will have produced an unexpected result, which is rather the point. What serves no one is a preemptive disqualification based on who his grandfather was and what his distant relatives did at ski resorts.

Chait concludes by suggesting that Democrats who oppose kings and oligarchs shouldn’t maintain “their own royal family.” It’s a nice line. But Schlossberg isn’t asking to be coronated. He’s asking to run in a Democratic primary, which is the precise mechanism a democracy uses to sort the qualified from the unqualified.

Let him run. Let him be judged on his own merits and his own failures. That’s not Camelot worship. That’s democracy.

I was thirteen when the world lost President Kennedy. Sixty-three years later, I’m not embarrassed by the tears I shed or the inspiration I felt. What I’ve learned in the decades since — watching Pierre Trudeau’s son rise and fall on his own terms in my own country — is that democratic voters are perfectly capable of separating the myth from the man. They don’t need Jonathan Chait to do it for them.
