The Asimov Gap

The Surveillance Question

Part 8: Privacy or Panopticon

January 30, 2026

Larry Ellison CTO and Founder, Oracle

The Vision They’re Building

September 2024, Oracle founder Larry Ellison addressed investors about AI and surveillance:

“Citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.”

Not a warning. Not expressing concern about dystopian possibility. Describing his goal.

Ellison is the second-richest person on Earth. He’s building AI systems. And his vision of the future is one where everyone is constantly monitored, recorded, analyzed.

This isn’t hypothetical. This is the plan.

Sanders asks: “Are we reaching the stage where every phone call that we make, every email and text that we send, every bit of research we do on the internet will be available to the owners of AI? How do we sustain a democracy under those conditions?”

The answer to that last question: We can’t.

Democracy requires privacy. Freedom requires the ability to think, speak, associate without constant monitoring. AI makes ubiquitous surveillance technically possible and economically profitable.

Whether it happens depends on democratic choice, not technological inevitability.

We’re choosing now: Privacy or panopticon. Democracy or surveillance state.

And we’re choosing mostly by not choosing—defaulting to oligarchic surveillance capitalism.

What AI-Powered Surveillance Actually Means

Current surveillance is limited by human capacity:

You can install cameras, but humans must watch footage. You can intercept communications, but humans must analyze them. You can track behavior, but humans must find patterns.

Human limitations create practical privacy: Too much data to analyze. Too expensive to monitor everyone. Too many false positives.

AI removes those limitations.

What AI enables:

1. Facial Recognition Everywhere

  • Cameras throughout public spaces

  • AI identifies everyone instantly

  • Tracks movements, associations, patterns

  • Creates database of where you go, who you see, what you do

  • Continuous tracking without human effort

2. Communication Analysis

  • AI reads all emails, texts, social media posts

  • Understands content, sentiment, intent

  • Flags “concerning” patterns

  • No human review needed until AI identifies “threat”

  • Total communications surveillance at scale

3. Behavioral Prediction

  • AI analyzes patterns

  • Predicts future behavior

  • Identifies people likely to commit crimes (pre-crime)

  • Flags “suspicious” activity based on deviations

  • Minority Report becoming reality

4. Social Graph Mapping

  • AI maps all relationships

  • Identifies who knows whom

  • Tracks information flow through networks

  • Predicts group behavior

  • Every connection monitored and analyzed

5. Aggregation Across Databases

  • AI combines data from multiple sources

  • Financial records, health data, location history, purchases, communications, social media

  • Creates comprehensive profile

  • Total information awareness

Technical feasibility: Exists now. Not future speculation.

Implementation: China already doing this (social credit system). Western democracies have pieces in place. Full deployment is choice, not technical barrier.

China’s Social Credit System: The Proof of Concept

China demonstrates what AI surveillance enables:

How it works:

  • Cameras with facial recognition track citizens

  • AI monitors online behavior, purchases, social connections

  • Algorithms assign “citizenship score” based on behavior

  • Score determines access to services, opportunities

What’s monitored:

  • Traffic violations (jaywalking, running red lights)

  • Financial behavior (paying bills, debt)

  • Social media posts (criticizing government lowers score)

  • Purchases (alcohol, video games tracked)

  • Associations (your score affected by friends’ scores)

Consequences of low scores:

  • Can’t buy plane/train tickets

  • Children banned from good schools

  • Can’t get good jobs

  • Public shaming (faces displayed on billboards)

  • Social and economic exclusion

How AI makes it possible:

  • Facial recognition at scale (tracks hundreds of millions)

  • Natural language processing (analyzes all communications)

  • Pattern recognition (flags “concerning” behavior)

  • Automated decision-making (no human review for most actions)

Without AI: Impossible to implement. Too much data, too complex.

With AI: Fully operational. Chinese government monitoring 1.4 billion people.

This isn’t speculation. It exists. It works. It’s totalitarian control enabled by AI.

The Western Path: Surveillance Capitalism

China uses AI surveillance for political control. Western democracies are building similar infrastructure for profit.

How surveillance capitalism works:

1. Data Collection

  • Every website visit tracked

  • Every purchase recorded

  • Every location logged

  • Every communication monitored

  • Every social connection mapped

Tech companies collect all of this, legally, through “terms of service” no one reads.

2. AI Analysis

  • Build psychological profiles

  • Predict behavior, preferences, vulnerabilities

  • Identify optimal manipulation strategies

  • Understand you better than you understand yourself

3. Behavioral Modification

  • Show content predicted to keep you engaged

  • Recommend products you’re likely to buy

  • Present information confirming your biases

  • Manipulate behavior for profit

4. Commercial and Political Use

  • Advertisers buy access to manipulate you

  • Political campaigns buy access to persuade you

  • Highest bidder gets to influence you

Pattern: Same infrastructure as China, but monetized instead of used for direct state control.

Yet.

The infrastructure exists. Changing who controls it is easy. Changing purpose from profit to political control is trivial.

Once surveillance infrastructure exists, it’s available for any purpose—including authoritarian.

Why Surveillance Kills Democracy

Democracy requires:

1. Free thought

Can’t have if you know you’re being monitored. Self-censorship inevitable.

Research shows: People modify behavior when watched. Even when told surveillance is for benign purposes.

If AI monitors everything you read, write, say—you self-censor. Democracy requires unmonitored space for thought development.

2. Free speech

Can’t have if speech has consequences beyond natural social accountability.

If AI flags “concerning” speech for investigation, people stop speaking freely.

If employers check social media before hiring and AI surfaces anything “controversial,” people self-censor professional opinions.

Democracy requires ability to express unpopular views without surveillance-enabled retaliation.

3. Free association

Can’t have if association is tracked and judged.

If AI maps your social network and you’re penalized for knowing “wrong” people (as in China’s social credit system), you avoid certain associations.

Democracy requires ability to organize, protest, dissent without surveillance monitoring who participates.

4. Privacy of political activity

Votes are secret for reason: Prevents coercion and retaliation.

But if AI knows everything else about you—your communications, associations, patterns—your vote is predictable even if ballot is secret.

And AI can identify activists, organizers, dissenters even before they act. Pre-emptive suppression becomes possible.

Historical example: FBI’s COINTELPRO used surveillance to suppress civil rights and anti-war movements. Identified leaders, disrupted organizing, intimidated participants.

AI makes this vastly more powerful and automated.

5. Protection from abuse of power

Surveillance gives those in power comprehensive information about potential challengers, critics, dissenters.

This creates asymmetric power: Watchers know everything about watched. Watched can’t organize effectively because monitored constantly.

Democracy requires checked power. Surveillance enables unchecked power.

Pattern: Surveillance and democracy are fundamentally incompatible at scale.

Limited surveillance (warrants, specific targets, oversight) is compatible.

Ubiquitous surveillance (everyone, all the time, AI-analyzed) kills democracy.

The Ratchet Effect: Surveillance Expands, Never Contracts

Historical pattern:

1. Emergency powers granted temporarily

Government claims need for surveillance due to crisis (terrorism, crime, pandemic).

2. Powers normalized

Emergency becomes permanent. Infrastructure remains.

3. Scope expands

Originally for terrorism. Then for serious crime. Then for minor violations. Then for political dissent.

4. Never dismantled

Once surveillance infrastructure exists, it’s never voluntarily given up.

Examples:

Patriot Act (2001): Passed after 9/11 as “temporary emergency measure.” Still in effect 20+ years later. Scope expanded far beyond terrorism.

NSA surveillance (revealed 2013): Built to fight terrorism. Used to monitor everyone’s communications, including Americans and allies.

COVID-19 contact tracing: Built for pandemic. Infrastructure remains, available for other purposes.

Pattern: Surveillance powers grow, never shrink.

This is ratchet effect. Each crisis adds surveillance. None removed after crisis ends.

AI accelerates this: Each new AI capability adds surveillance power. Once added, permanent.

Current trajectory: Continuous expansion toward total surveillance. No natural limiting factor. Only democratic choice can stop it.

Privacy-Preserving AI Is Technically Possible

Important point: AI doesn’t inherently require surveillance.

Two paradigms:

Surveillance AI (current default):

  • Data sent to company servers

  • Processed in cloud

  • Company keeps data

  • Analyzed for company benefit

  • You surrender privacy to use service

Privacy-Preserving AI (technically possible):

  • Data processed on your device

  • Never leaves your control

  • Encrypted if must be transmitted

  • Company doesn’t access data

  • You keep privacy while using service

Technologies enabling privacy:

1. On-Device Processing

Modern phones/computers powerful enough to run AI models locally. ChatGPT-level AI can run on your device.

Benefit: Your data never leaves your device. Company can’t access it.

2. Differential Privacy

Mathematical techniques that allow learning from data without identifying individuals.

Benefit: AI can improve from aggregate patterns without surveilling individuals.

3. Federated Learning

Train AI across many devices without centralizing data. Model improves, but data stays distributed.

Benefit: AI learns from everyone’s data without any one entity controlling all data.

4. Homomorphic Encryption

Perform computations on encrypted data. Get results without ever decrypting.

Benefit: AI can analyze your data without anyone (including AI provider) seeing unencrypted data.

5. Zero-Knowledge Proofs

Prove something is true without revealing underlying data.

Benefit: Verify eligibility, credentials, attributes without exposing sensitive information.

All of these are real, working technologies. Some already deployed.

Apple’s approach: On-device AI processing, differential privacy for improvements, minimal cloud reliance.

Shows privacy-preserving AI is commercially viable.

Question isn’t technical feasibility. It’s whether we require it through democratic policy.

Why Oligarchs Prefer Surveillance AI

If privacy-preserving AI is possible, why isn’t it standard?

Because surveillance is profitable.

Surveillance capitalism business model:

  • Provide “free” service

  • Collect comprehensive data

  • Analyze with AI

  • Sell access to manipulate users

  • Profit scales with data collection

More surveillance = more data = better manipulation = higher profit.

This model depends on centralizing data: Company must have access to monetize it.

Privacy-preserving AI breaks this model: If data stays on device, company can’t monetize it (at least not as easily).

Oligarchic incentive: Maximize data collection. Resist privacy protection.

They claim:

  • “AI needs data to work” (false—on-device AI works)

  • “Privacy limits innovation” (false—privacy-preserving innovation is possible)

  • “Users prefer convenience over privacy” (partly true because users don’t understand surveillance extent)

Reality: Surveillance is profitable. Privacy isn’t. Oligarchs optimize for profit.

Democratic alternative: Require privacy by default. Make surveillance opt-in, not opt-out.

The GDPR Model: Democratic Privacy Protection

Europe again shows what’s possible:

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2018):

Key provisions:

1. Consent must be informed and specific

Can’t bury surveillance in 50-page terms of service. Must clearly explain what data is collected and why.

2. Purpose limitation

Data collected for one purpose can’t be used for another without new consent.

3. Data minimization

Companies can only collect data actually needed, not everything possible.

4. Right to access

You can demand to see all data company has about you.

5. Right to deletion

You can demand company delete your data (with some exceptions).

6. Right to portability

You can take your data and move to different service.

7. Accountability

Companies must demonstrate compliance. Serious penalties for violations (up to 4% of global revenue).

Result:

  • Users have much more control

  • Companies more careful about data collection

  • Surveillance reduced (though not eliminated)

  • Democracy asserted control over surveillance capitalism

Did this kill innovation? No. European tech sector still thriving. Companies adapted.

Did this protect privacy? Yes. Significantly better privacy than US, where surveillance largely unregulated.

Pattern: Democratic oversight of AI surveillance works. We just need political will to implement it.

The Prisoner’s Dilemma of Privacy

Why don’t individuals just protect their own privacy?

Because it’s collective action problem:

Individual level:

You can refuse to use services that surveil. But:

  • Most services require some data sharing

  • Network effects mean opting out excludes you socially/professionally

  • Individual privacy protection doesn’t stop others from being surveilled

  • Hard to maintain privacy alone

Like: Living in car-centric society without car. Possible, but severely limiting.

Collective action required: Everyone benefits from privacy protection, but no individual can achieve it alone.

Solution: Democratic policy that protects everyone.

Also addresses power asymmetry: Oligarchs want surveillance. Individuals want privacy. Individuals lack power to resist oligarchs’ surveillance unless government enforces privacy protection.

This is classic case requiring democratic governance. Market won’t solve it because surveillance is profitable.

Surveillance Resistance Tools Exist (But Aren’t Enough)

Individual actions help:

1. Use encryption

Signal for messaging, VPNs for internet, encrypted email.

Benefit: Protects specific communications. Limit: Doesn’t stop metadata surveillance (who you talk to, when, from where).

2. Use privacy-respecting services

DuckDuckGo instead of Google, Firefox instead of Chrome, privacy-focused email.

Benefit: Reduces data collection. Limit: Network effects limit usefulness. Most people still on surveillance platforms.

3. Minimize data sharing

Don’t use social media, don’t carry smartphone, pay cash.

Benefit: Significantly reduces surveillance. Limit: Socially and professionally isolating. Not viable for most people.

4. Use ad blockers, tracker blockers

Block surveillance infrastructure on websites.

Benefit: Reduces tracking. Limit: Constant cat-and-mouse game. Surveillance evolves to evade blockers.

Pattern: Individual tools help but aren’t sufficient. Surveillance infrastructure too comprehensive, too profitable, too powerful.

Real solution requires democratic policy constraining surveillance at source.

What Democratic AI Surveillance Governance Looks Like

Based on GDPR and privacy research:

1. Privacy by Default

AI systems must be privacy-preserving unless user explicitly consents to surveillance.

Current: Surveillance by default, privacy only if you know how to opt out.

Alternative: Privacy by default, surveillance only with informed opt-in.

2. On-Device Processing Requirement

AI services must process data on user device whenever technically feasible. Centralization only when necessary, with justification.

3. Purpose Limitation

Data collected for AI training can’t be repurposed for surveillance without new consent.

4. Meaningful Consent

“I agree to terms of service” doesn’t count. Must specifically consent to surveillance with clear explanation of what’s collected and why.

5. Right to Explanations

When AI makes decision affecting you, you have right to explanation of how decision was made and what data was used.

6. Ban on Certain Uses

Some surveillance uses incompatible with democracy:

  • Real-time biometric surveillance in public (except narrow law enforcement with warrants)

  • Social credit systems

  • Emotion recognition without consent

  • Predictive policing based on demographic profiling

7. Strong Enforcement

Meaningful penalties for violations. Not just fines but criminal liability for executives enabling surveillance violations.

8. Democratic Oversight

Independent agency overseeing AI surveillance. Not captured by industry. Accountable to public.

None of this prevents beneficial AI. All of it prevents surveillance state.

The Counterargument: “Surveillance Keeps Us Safe”

Inevitable response: “We need surveillance to prevent terrorism, crime, abuse.”

Response:

1. Diminishing returns

Pre-9/11 surveillance was limited. Post-9/11 surveillance is comprehensive. Are we dramatically safer?

Evidence mixed: Some plots detected. Many false positives. Some attacks not prevented despite surveillance.

Comprehensive surveillance doesn’t guarantee safety. Creates illusion of security while eroding freedom.

2. Targeted surveillance is different

Narrow surveillance of specific suspects, with warrants, with oversight = compatible with democracy.

Ubiquitous surveillance of everyone, all the time, without suspicion = incompatible with democracy.

We can have first without second. This is historical balance. Now being abandoned.

3. Safety vs. freedom is false choice

Benjamin Franklin: “Those who would give up essential Liberty, to purchase a little temporary Safety, deserve neither Liberty nor Safety.”

Democracy requires accepting some risk. Perfect safety impossible. Surveillance state is not acceptable price for marginal safety improvement.

4. Surveillance power will be abused

History shows: Surveillance powers are always eventually abused for political purposes.

FBI used surveillance against civil rights movement, anti-war activists, political enemies.

NSA surveillance exceeded legal authority repeatedly.

No reason to think AI surveillance will be different. Power gets abused. Comprehensive surveillance = comprehensive power = comprehensive abuse potential.

Better to prevent comprehensive surveillance than trust it won’t be abused.

Orwell’s Warning, Updated

George Orwell’s 1984 (1949) warned about surveillance state:

Telescreens: Two-way televisions monitoring everyone constantly.

Thought Police: Detecting dissent before action.

Newspeak: Limiting expression of dissenting ideas.

Ministry of Truth: Rewriting history and facts.

Orwell was prescient about surveillance, but got technology wrong:

Not government-installed telescreens. Surveillance is:

  • Phones we carry voluntarily

  • Devices we install in homes (Alexa, Google Home)

  • Services we use “freely” (but with surveillance in exchange)

Not Thought Police. But:

  • AI analyzing all communications

  • Flagging “concerning” patterns

  • Pre-emptive intervention possible

Not Newspeak. But:

  • Algorithmic content curation creating filter bubbles

  • Each person seeing different “reality”

  • Consensus reality fragmenting

Not Ministry of Truth. But:

  • AI-generated deepfakes

  • Algorithmic promotion of engagement over accuracy

  • Truth becoming indistinguishable from fabrication

Orwell’s vision is arriving. Not through totalitarian government takeover. Through surveillance capitalism accepted incrementally.

The boot stamping on human face forever might be wearing a corporate logo.

The Prisoner: Surveillance as Control

British TV series The Prisoner (1967) explored surveillance society:

Setup: Man resigns from intelligence agency. Kidnapped to “The Village”—surveillance panopticon where:

  • Cameras everywhere

  • All communication monitored

  • Behavior analyzed and controlled

  • Escape impossible

  • Freedom illusion (residents pretend to be free while constantly watched)

Recurring line: “I am not a number—I am a free man!”

Village response: “You are Number Six.”

Theme: Surveillance reduces humans to data points. Analysis replaces humanity. Control masquerades as freedom.

2025 relevance: We’re building The Village globally, voluntarily, for convenience and profit.

AI is surveillance infrastructure that makes The Village technically feasible.

Question The Prisoner asked: Can you be free if constantly monitored?

Answer the show gave: No. Surveillance is incompatible with freedom.

Democracy better heed that answer.

Closing: The Choice We’re Making

Surveillance infrastructure is being built right now.

AI makes comprehensive surveillance technically possible and economically profitable.

China demonstrates what’s possible: Total information awareness, behavioral control, authoritarian governance.

West is building similar infrastructure, currently used for profit. Could be repurposed for political control trivially.

We’re choosing: Privacy or panopticon.

Default choice (doing nothing) = panopticon.

Active choice (democratic policy) = privacy.

Sanders’ question: “How do we sustain a democracy under those conditions?”

Answer: We can’t. Democracy and ubiquitous AI surveillance are incompatible.

Asimov’s warning: Powerful technology requires democratic governance.

Privacy-preserving AI is technically possible. GDPR proves democratic privacy protection works.

What’s needed: Political will to choose privacy over profit.

Choose now. Once surveillance infrastructure is fully built, dismantling it will be nearly impossible.

Orwell and The Prisoner warned us. We’re not in dystopia yet.

But we’re building the infrastructure. Stone by stone. Camera by camera. Algorithm by algorithm.

Still time to choose differently. Not much time. But enough.

Democracy or panopticon. Privacy or profit. Freedom or surveillance.

Choose.
