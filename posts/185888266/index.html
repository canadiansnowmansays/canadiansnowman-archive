When the Partnership Cracks

Rodríguez Pushes Back, Oil Money Flows, and Prisoners Disappear

January 27, 2026

Venezuela’s acting president Delcy Rodríguez said Sunday she has had “enough” of Washington’s orders, as she works to unite the country after the US capture of its former leader Nicolás Maduro.

January 27, 2026

On January 14, President Donald Trump announced he had spoken by phone with Venezuela’s acting president Delcy Rodríguez. His description was effusive: “We’re getting along very well with Venezuela. We just had a great conversation today, and she’s a terrific person.”

Eleven days later, Rodríguez stood before oil workers in Puerto La Cruz and delivered a very different message: “Enough already of Washington’s orders over politicians in Venezuela.”

The partnership lasted exactly 22 days before the cracks appeared.

Week Three of Trump’s Venezuela operation reveals what happens when the initial cooperation phase ends and reality intrudes. Trump needs Rodríguez to maintain order and deliver oil. Rodríguez needs Trump to not launch a second wave of attacks. But Rodríguez also needs to keep domestic hardliners—especially Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello with his $25 million bounty and control of security forces—from viewing her as a U.S. puppet.

The balancing act is failing. While $300 million in oil money flows into Venezuelan accounts and hydrocarbon laws change to favor American investment, Rodríguez is publicly pushing back against U.S. control. While the government claims 626 prisoners released, independent verification shows only 250—and another 50 have been transferred to unknown locations. While Trump celebrates cooperation, the numbers show the gap between rhetoric and reality widening every day.

This is Week Three: when partnerships crack under pressure no one can sustain.

“ENOUGH ALREADY” - THE PUSHBACK

The warning signs appeared early.

On January 15, Rodríguez delivered her state of the union address to Venezuela’s National Assembly. While announcing prisoner releases and discussing cooperation with Washington, she made her position clear: “If I have to go to Washington, I’ll go standing up, not crawling.”

She claimed she was “in charge” of Venezuela, not the United States. She spent significant time crediting Nicolás Maduro for starting the prisoner release process—signaling she was not breaking continuity with the captured president even while cooperating with the country that captured him.

But these were still carefully calibrated statements—defiant enough for domestic consumption, cooperative enough to avoid antagonizing Trump.

Ten days later, the calibration broke.

On Sunday, January 25, Rodríguez traveled to Puerto La Cruz in Anzoátegui state to speak at a state-owned PDVSA oil refinery. Surrounded by oil workers, she delivered her most defiant statement since Maduro’s capture:

“Enough already of Washington’s orders over politicians in Venezuela. Let Venezuelan politics resolve our differences and our internal conflicts. This Republic has paid a very high price for having to confront the consequences of fascism and extremism in our country.”

She called Maduro a “war prisoner” and said he sends his regards to the Venezuelan people from detention, including “words of encouragement.”

Then came a direct threat to Venezuelans in exile who had celebrated Maduro’s capture: Rodríguez said a “red line” was crossed by the events of January 3 and warned that the government could revoke the nationality of those who “celebrated” the U.S. operation.

Yet even in this defiance, Rodríguez maintained she was willing to engage the United States “face to face” through diplomatic channels, but with “dignity and honor.”

“That is why it is important that we open up spaces for democratic divergence, but that it be politics with a capital P and a V for Venezuela,” she said. “Enough of foreign powers.”

The contradiction is stunning: denouncing U.S. orders while implementing U.S. demands. Calling Maduro a war prisoner while cooperating with his captors. Threatening to revoke exiles’ nationality while maintaining diplomatic talks with Washington.

But the contradiction reveals Rodríguez’s impossible position.

She’s caught between two audiences with incompatible demands. Trump requires cooperation—prisoner releases, oil sales, hydrocarbon law reforms. Cabello and the domestic hardliners require resistance—public defiance, continued repression, loyalty to the Maduro legacy.

Every concession to Washington looks like capitulation in Caracas. Every pushback against Washington risks another military strike.

For 22 days, Rodríguez managed the balancing act through careful language and private cooperation paired with public ambiguity. The January 25 speech shows that equilibrium failing.

Brian Fonseca, a professor at Florida International University who studies Venezuela, told CNN there are “essentially three centers of power” in Venezuela now: the Maduro/Rodríguez civilian faction, Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino López controlling the military, and Diosdado Cabello controlling police, intelligence, and pro-government militias.

Rodríguez must keep all three aligned while satisfying Trump. It’s an impossible task.

Al Jazeera reported on January 24 that the Venezuelan government, “usually outspoken in denouncing US imperialism,” is “clearly biting its tongue to avoid provoking Trump and Rubio.”

But governments can only bite their tongues for so long. Especially governments where power flows from guns, not votes.

The January 25 speech reveals the strain. Rodríguez needs to prove to Cabello and the security apparatus that she’s not Washington’s puppet. She needs to show domestic audiences that Venezuela retains sovereignty despite military occupation and economic control.

So she gives speeches denouncing U.S. orders. Then she implements those orders anyway.

Trump appears unconcerned by the contradiction. He needs Rodríguez to maintain order and deliver oil. As long as she does both, she can say whatever she wants to domestic audiences.

But the contradiction cannot hold indefinitely. Either Rodríguez will have to choose between satisfying Washington and maintaining domestic legitimacy, or the domestic power structure—Cabello’s guns, Padrino López’s military—will choose for her.

Week Three revealed the first major crack in the partnership. Whether it widens into a break depends on how much longer both sides can sustain profitable fiction.

THE OIL DEAL BECOMES REAL

While Rodríguez delivered defiant speeches, the oil deal Trump negotiated was becoming concrete reality.

On January 20, Rodríguez confirmed Venezuela had received the first payment from U.S.-controlled oil sales: $300 million.

She announced the money would go to “the exchange market in Venezuela, the national banks and the central bank.” This was the first tangible proof that Trump’s oil deal—announced with typical hyperbole about “50 million barrels” and “billions of dollars”—was actually real.

Three days later, on January 23, the first cargo ship carrying naphtha arrived at Venezuelan ports. The shipment—460,000 barrels delivered by Vitol—is critical for processing Venezuela’s extra-heavy crude oil. Without naphtha, Venezuelan refineries cannot blend the heavy crude into exportable product.

The last naphtha delivery had arrived in December via Chevron Corporation under a U.S. agreement before Trump’s naval blockade cut off all other suppliers. The January 23 shipment signaled that Washington was willing to actually help restart Venezuelan oil production, not just extract what already existed in storage.

The same day, Venezuela’s National Assembly advanced the most significant overhaul of the country’s oil industry in decades.

The proposed reform to Venezuela’s Hydrocarbons Law passed its first reading on January 23. The law breaks with Hugo Chávez’s 2006 nationalization model, which reserved exclusive crude marketing rights for state-owned oil company PDVSA and required PDVSA to hold majority stakes in all joint ventures.

Rodríguez told the National Assembly on January 15 that the reform would “allow investment flows to be incorporated into new fields, fields where no investment has ever been made and into fields where there is no infrastructure.”

The reform represents a fundamental shift in Venezuelan oil policy—opening the door to foreign majority ownership and operational control that Chávez explicitly prohibited when he nationalized the industry.

But critics note the law is carefully worded to avoid openly breaking with Chávez’s legacy.

Economist Jose Guerra, former director of research at Venezuela’s Central Bank, told Al Jazeera the proposed law is “a law of ambiguity, designed to avoid openly breaking with Chavez’s oil legacy. It is not emphatic about private participation.”

The ambiguity is deliberate. Rodríguez cannot afford to be seen as dismantling Chavismo while Chavista hardliners still control the security apparatus. So the law uses vague language about “investment flows” and “new fields” rather than explicitly stating foreign companies can hold majority stakes.

But in practice, that ship already sailed.

In April 2024, when Rodríguez was serving as Maduro’s oil minister, she introduced Production Participation Contracts (CPPs) that gave private companies effective majority control of oil operations. The CPP framework operates under the opacity of Venezuela’s Anti-Blockade Law, which allows confidentiality and bypasses the existing Hydrocarbons Law requirements.

Rodríguez claimed in January that CPPs increased Venezuelan oil production from 900,000 barrels per day to 1.2 million barrels per day in 2024, and that investments under this model reached nearly $900 million in 2025.

The new hydrocarbon law essentially formalizes what was already happening through CPPs—but does so in carefully ambiguous language that avoids explicitly repudiating Chávez.

So the oil deal is advancing on three fronts: money flowing ($300 million received), infrastructure arriving (naphtha shipments resumed), and laws changing (hydrocarbon reform passing).

But there’s a gap between Trump’s promises and corporate reality.

On January 9, Trump hosted more than a dozen oil company executives at the White House to discuss investments in Venezuela. He promised oil companies would spend “at least $100 billion” to rebuild Venezuela’s energy sector with U.S. “protection.”

Exxon CEO Darren Woods told Trump directly that Venezuela is “uninvestable” in its current state.

ExxonMobil and ConocoPhillips are still owed billions of dollars in outstanding claims from arbitration cases after Venezuela seized their assets in 2007. Without resolution of those debts and dramatic improvements in rule of law, the major oil companies aren’t coming back.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent acknowledged the problem on January 16: “The big oil companies who move slowly, who have corporate boards, are not interested. I can tell you that the independent oil companies and individuals, wildcatters—our phones are ringing off the hook.”

Translation: Major corporations see too much risk. Small operators willing to gamble are interested.

That’s a long way from the $100 billion transformation Trump promised.

Energy Secretary Chris Wright said on January 16 that the U.S. had completed its first Venezuelan oil sale valued at $500 million, and was getting “about a 30% higher realized price” than Venezuela had been getting three weeks earlier.

But higher prices don’t solve the fundamental problem: Venezuela’s oil infrastructure is collapsing after years of underinvestment. Production is around 800,000 barrels per day, down from 3.5 million barrels per day in the 1990s. Rystad Energy estimates it will cost more than $180 billion through 2040 for Venezuelan production to reach 3 million barrels per day.

That kind of investment requires rule of law, enforceable contracts, and political stability—none of which exist in a country run by the triumvirate of Trump’s leverage, Rodríguez’s accommodation, and Cabello’s guns.

So Week Three reveals the oil deal’s reality: Money is flowing. Laws are changing. Infrastructure is arriving. But the transformation Trump promised remains distant, contingent on reforms that would require dismantling the very power structure keeping Rodríguez in office.

THE MISSING PRISONERS - A DARKER STORY

The numbers game over political prisoner releases—which my previous essay documented through January 14—has continued into Week Three. And the gap is widening.

On January 14, the Venezuelan government claimed more than 400 prisoners released. Foro Penal, Venezuela’s leading human rights organization, could verify only 68.

By January 17, Foro Penal had confirmed 139 releases.

By January 23, Foro Penal confirmed 154 releases. The same day, Delcy Rodríguez announced 626 prisoners had been released—without providing a specific date or publishing the list. She called on the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights, Volker Türk, to verify the numbers.

By January 26—today—Foro Penal has confirmed 250 political prisoners released since January 8.

The government claims 626. Independent verification shows 250. The gap has grown from 330 unaccounted prisoners to 376.

But beneath the numbers game lies a darker story that emerged on January 18.

The Washington Post reported that at least 50 political prisoners have been transferred to undisclosed locations within Venezuela’s prison system. Families are pushing for answers but getting none.

These aren’t prisoners slowly being processed for release. These are people who have disappeared into the system while the government announces cooperation.

The Post told the story of Jesús Ramos, a political prisoner who dropped to one knee before sunrise and proposed to Victoria with a ring made of yarn.

“It was an easy yes,” she told the Post.

Then Ramos was transferred. No one knows where. His family doesn’t know if he’s been released, moved to another facility, or something worse.

María Constanza Cipriani, wife of imprisoned opposition adviser Perkins Rocha, told reporters: “My heart is in my mouth,” not knowing whether her husband would be freed or had been among those transferred to unknown locations.

For two weeks, Venezuelan families have been camping outside El Helicoide—Caracas’s notorious political prison—waiting for releases that may never come.

The pattern is clear: The government announces impressive numbers to satisfy Trump. Independent verification reveals a fraction of that total. Meanwhile, some prisoners aren’t being released at all—they’re being moved to locations where families can’t track them and human rights organizations can’t verify their status.

It’s a strategy designed to create confusion while maintaining leverage. Release enough high-profile prisoners who can leave the country (like Rocío San Miguel, relocated to Spain) to generate positive headlines. Keep hundreds more behind bars as political hostages. Transfer the most problematic cases to unknown locations where they disappear from tracking systems entirely.

And announce big numbers that can’t be verified.

The result: Trump gets to claim cooperation. Rodríguez gets to maintain the repression machinery intact. And 50 families don’t know where their loved ones are.

Week Three shows the gap between claimed cooperation and actual results growing wider, not narrower. The government is releasing prisoners—250 verified releases is significant. But the gap between promises (626) and reality (250) has grown by 46 prisoners since my last essay.

More troubling: The 50 disappeared prisoners suggest the government isn’t just slow-walking releases. It’s actively hiding people while claiming cooperation.

As Alfredo Romero, director of Foro Penal, warned in my previous essay: “If there is not a dismantling of the repression machinery, then new political prisoners will be in jail, and the revolving door of repression continues.”

Week Three proves he was right. The machinery remains intact. The revolving door keeps spinning. And 50 prisoners have disappeared into it completely.

CUBA’S HUMANITARIAN CATASTROPHE DEEPENS

People buy food on a street in Havana. (Photo by YAMIL LAGE / AFP)

My previous essay covered the return of 32 flag-draped boxes containing the remains of Cuban security personnel killed protecting Maduro. Week Three brought the human details of what Trump’s oil cutoff means for Cuba’s nine million people.

The New York Times published a detailed report on January 17 documenting Cuba’s deepening energy crisis.

The numbers are stark: Cuba needs 100,000 barrels of oil per day to keep the lights on and its economy functioning. Venezuela had been supplying roughly 35,000 barrels per day—about a third of Cuba’s needs. Mexico had been sending about 22,000 barrels per day, but that dropped to about 7,000 by the end of 2025.

Trump cut off Venezuelan supplies completely. Cuba’s domestic production covers only about 40 percent of needs.

The result is humanitarian catastrophe.

A government-run television and radio broadcaster in central Cuba announced Tuesday it had been off the air for several days because it had run out of diesel to power its station. Without power, many people also don’t have running water.

The Times documented the human impact through Carlos Manuel Vargas, a 78-year-old retired teacher who now works as a security guard. His spouse has cancer, and he needs gasoline to drive to the hospital.

Cuba has implemented an app-based queuing system for gas stations. Vargas’s turn comes up about every two months. Since Maduro’s ouster on January 3, his place in line hasn’t moved. He’s been number 10,231 for nearly two weeks.

“If I have to sell my cellphone or my TV, I will,” Vargas told the Times.

His only other option is buying gasoline at dollar-based gas stations, where lines are shorter but prices are far higher than most Cubans can afford.

Economist Juan A.B. Belt, who studies Cuba’s oil industry, told the Times that Cuba has experienced at least two dozen “widespread, long-duration blackouts” in the past two years alone—even when Venezuelan oil was still flowing.

“The blackouts are amazing,” he said. “These are wide-area and long-duration events. They had so many—some lasting several days and quite a few covering the entire country.”

Without running water and power, the crisis extends beyond inconvenience to basic survival.

Jorge Piñón, a former oil executive and researcher at the University of Texas, assessed the impact: “If Cuba loses that, the impact is basically going to be catastrophic. The chain of events is that the Cuban economy literally collapses, there is no food in the markets, the trains are not moving, the buses are not moving.”

Yet the Trump administration is walking a careful line. According to an administration official who spoke to the Times, the U.S. will not pressure Mexico to reduce or cut off its remaining shipments to Cuba to avoid worsening conditions there.

The administration “does not believe the Cuban government has much oil in storage” and “is concerned that the government will use whatever oil it does have on hand to keep its intelligence and security apparatus going,” the official said.

So Trump’s strategy is calibrated: Cut off Venezuela’s supplies to cripple Cuba’s economy and force concessions, but don’t completely cut off all oil to avoid triggering total collapse and potential migration crisis.

Meanwhile, the State Department announced it was sending $3 million in humanitarian aid through the Catholic Church to areas in Cuba affected by Hurricane Melissa in the fall.

The contradiction is stark: causing a humanitarian crisis through oil cutoffs while sending humanitarian aid to address weather-related damage.

Jeremy P. Lewin, an acting under secretary of state, defended the policy by claiming Venezuelan oil shipments “never benefited Cuban people”—an argument belied by those experiencing blackouts across Cuba.

Trump urged Cuba to “make a deal” or suffer the consequences. “Don’t play games with this president,” Lewin warned.

Cuba’s response has been defiant. President Miguel Díaz-Canel: “Ready to defend the homeland to the last drop of blood.”

But 32 flag-draped boxes returned to Havana on January 15 tell a different story about what happens when defiance meets American military power.

And 78-year-old Carlos Vargas, stuck at number 10,231 in the gas queue with a spouse who has cancer, reveals the human cost of Trump’s regional strategy.

CONCLUSION: THE CRACKS WIDEN

Three weeks after invasion, a clear pattern has emerged.

Week One delivered military triumph: Maduro captured, Delta Force successful, Trump victorious.

Week Two revealed costs and consequences: political prisoners unreleased, Maduro defiant in court, Machado sidelined, Cuba’s 32 bodies.

Week Three shows the partnership cracking under contradictions no one can sustain.

Delcy Rodríguez cannot satisfy both Washington and Caracas simultaneously. Trump demands cooperation—prisoner releases, oil sales, legal reforms. Cabello and domestic hardliners demand resistance—public defiance, continued repression, loyalty to Maduro’s legacy.

The evidence of strain is everywhere:

Oil cooperation is real: $300 million received, naphtha deliveries resumed, hydrocarbon law advancing. But major companies still say “uninvestible.” Money flows while transformation remains distant.

Prisoner releases are real: 250 verified releases since January 8 is significant progress. But the gap between government claims (626) and verified reality (250) has widened by 46 prisoners. Worse: 50 prisoners transferred to unknown locations—disappeared entirely while government announces cooperation.

Public pushback is real: After 22 days, Rodríguez delivered her most defiant statement since Maduro’s capture. “Enough already of Washington’s orders.” Called Maduro a “war prisoner.” Threatened exiles. Then continued implementing U.S. demands anyway.

Regional costs are real: Cuba’s humanitarian catastrophe deepening. TV stations dark. Cancer patients can’t reach hospitals. Thousands experiencing blackouts. Trump threatening more action while sending humanitarian aid.

The fundamental problem cannot be resolved through clever balancing acts or careful language.

You can invade a country. You can capture its president. You can seize oil tankers and install a compliant government. But you cannot force that government to satisfy both its occupier and its domestic power base when those demands are incompatible.

Rodríguez needs Trump to not invade again. She needs Cabello to not stage a coup. She needs to release prisoners for Washington and keep prisoners for Caracas. She needs to reform oil laws for American investors and maintain Chavista legitimacy for domestic audiences.

It worked for 22 days because both sides desperately needed cooperation.

Week Three reveals what happens when the initial crisis passes and domestic politics intrude.

Trump got his spectacle, his captured dictator, his Senate capitulation, his oil deal. Rodríguez got survival, $300 million, and temporary reprieve from American military strikes.

But the January 25 speech—”Enough already of Washington’s orders”—combined with widening prisoner number gaps and disappeared detainees shows the partnership straining under contradictions that cannot be reconciled indefinitely.

The triumvirate of Trump’s leverage, Rodríguez’s accommodation, and Cabello’s guns still holds Venezuela together.

Week Four will reveal whether the cracks widen into breaks—or whether both sides find new ways to maintain the profitable fiction that partnership and occupation are the same thing.
