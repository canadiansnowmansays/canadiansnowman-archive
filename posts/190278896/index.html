The Iran Crisis

“Someone the Enemy Opposes”

Part XXXVIII: Day Nine: Sunday, March 8, 2026

March 8, 2026

I. The Succession — and the Threat

Early Sunday morning, Iranian state television announced that the Assembly of Experts — the body of 88 clerics constitutionally empowered to select Iran’s supreme leader — had reached a majority consensus on a successor to Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, assassinated nine days ago in the opening strike of the war.

The name was not disclosed. The announcement said only that “the matter will reach a conclusion soon.” The omission was not procedural. It was protective. As this series has documented since Day Seven, naming Mojtaba Khamenei — the assassinated leader’s 56-year-old son and the presumed front-runner — makes him an immediate assassination target before he can take office. Israel has made this explicit.

On Sunday morning, Israeli military spokesman Avichay Adraee posted a direct warning on social media: “Israel will continue to pursue the successor and everyone who tries to appoint him. We warn everyone who is planning to participate in the meeting to appoint a successor: We will not hesitate to target you as well.”

Read that again carefully. Israel is threatening to assassinate not just the successor, but every cleric who participates in the selection process. The Assembly of Experts is a constitutional body. Its members are clerics, not soldiers. Israel is threatening to kill people for participating in what is, under Iranian law, a governance proceeding.

Trump had previously declared that it would be “unacceptable” for the clerics to choose Mojtaba. His public intervention produced the opposite of its intended effect. An Assembly member, Ayatollah Mohsen Heidari Alekasir, addressed it directly on Sunday: “Someone who is opposed by the enemy is more likely to be beneficial for Iran and Islam.” The “Great Satan,” he said, had done the Assembly “a kind of service” by publicly objecting to certain candidates.

This is the trap built into attempting to manage a succession from 35,000 feet. Trump’s declaration that Mojtaba would be unacceptable has made Mojtaba more appealing to the clerics deciding. Israel’s threat to kill everyone who attends the selection meeting may accelerate rather than delay it — because delay means indefinite vulnerability, while completion means a new supreme leader who then becomes Israel’s problem. The Assembly is now racing the Israeli Air Force to finish a constitutional process under bombardment.

II. The School — and What Was Said at Dover

When Trump stood at Dover Air Force Base on Saturday afternoon, beside the flag-draped transfer cases of Maj. Jeffrey R. O’Brien, Capt. Cody A. Khork, Chief Warrant Officer 3 Robert M. Marzan, Sgt. 1st Class Nicole M. Amor, Sgt. 1st Class Noah L. Tietjens, and Sgt. Declan J. Coady, a reporter asked him about the elementary school.

On February 28 — Day One of the war — a strike killed at least 175 people at a girls’ elementary school in southern Iran, most of them children. A NYT visual investigation indicates the school was most likely hit by an American airstrike.

At Dover, at the ceremony for the six Americans his war had killed, Trump said: “In my opinion, based on what I’ve seen, that was done by Iran. They have no accuracy whatsoever.”

Hegseth, asked the same question: “We’re investigating.”

The accountability timeline deserves to be stated in full. Day One: the strike. Days One through Four: official silence. Wednesday of that week: Trump administration officials told reporters they were “still investigating” whether it was a US airstrike. Saturday, at Dover: “In my opinion, that was done by Iran.”

The NYT visual investigation says almost certainly American.

There is a specific moral weight to the location of this lie. Trump did not say it at a rally or in a Truth Social post. He said it at Dover Air Force Base, in the same hour he received the remains of Americans killed in the same war. He used the occasion to deflect accountability for 175 dead children, most of them girls, onto the country whose people his aircraft had killed them.

Pete Hegseth, on Thursday, had complained that coverage of American military deaths was motivated by a desire “to make the president look bad.” The president’s own answer at Dover — attributing a confirmed US airstrike to Iranian inaccuracy, in front of the families of his dead — requires no commentary. It is its own document.

III. Water

The war crossed a threshold over the weekend that has received less attention than the oil fires: both sides are now attacking desalination infrastructure.

The United States struck a desalination plant on Qeshm Island in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran’s Foreign Minister Araghchi confirmed the attack, saying it had cut water supplies to 30 villages. “The U.S. set this precedent, not Iran,” he said, calling it “a dangerous move with grave consequences.”

A day later, an Iranian drone damaged a desalination plant in Bahrain. Bahrain’s interior ministry said the facility had been hit, accusing Iran of “indiscriminately” attacking civilian targets. The water authority said supplies were not interrupted.

In the Gulf, desalination is not infrastructure in the way that airports and oil depots are infrastructure. It is the sole source of drinking water for tens of millions of people. A 2008 diplomatic cable from the US Embassy in Riyadh — later released by WikiLeaks — warned that a single desalination plant provided Riyadh with more than 90 percent of its drinking water and that the city “would have to evacuate within a week” if the plant or its pipelines were seriously damaged. Since then, the Gulf’s cities have grown enormously and the foreign worker populations that sustain them have multiplied.

Abdullah Baabood, an Omani academic at Waseda University: “In the Gulf, desalination facilities are not merely infrastructure. They are essential lifelines that supply drinking water to millions. Striking them risks turning a military confrontation into a direct threat to civilian survival.”

What was a military confrontation is already a direct threat to civilian survival in multiple dimensions simultaneously — 175 children in a school, 65 schools damaged, 14 medical centres struck, an airport set ablaze, oil depots burning, and now the water supply. The war is nine days old.

IV. Orange Skies

Saturday night into Sunday morning, the skies over Tehran turned orange. Israel struck multiple oil storage facilities in the capital and the neighbouring city of Karaj — the first significant strikes on Iran’s energy infrastructure since the war began. Until this weekend, the US-Israeli campaign had focused on leadership, security services, missile capacity, and nuclear infrastructure. The oil depots represent a new phase.

Tehran’s main oil refinery is adjacent to one of the targeted storage facilities in the southern Shahr Rey district. The Shahran depot in northwestern Tehran — which Israel had also struck last June — was hit again. Footage from a highway leading to the upscale suburb of Lavasan showed an enormous fire on the horizon. By morning, dense, oily clouds had settled over a city of ten million people.

A 33-year-old Tehran resident named Aryan described it: “With the fire it felt like night became day, and then with all the smoke the day turned back into night again.”

Shirin, a 36-year-old dentist who had decided to remain in the city with her family, said: “We are very, very scared. I want the war to end, immediately.”

Amir, a businessman, had already left. He took his wife and two children to the countryside, and heard the explosion from one of the oil depots while sitting in the mountains outside the city.

Iran’s oil ministry said fire crews were working to contain the fires and asserted there would be no fuel shortages — the ministry had anticipated the strikes and taken precautions. The IRGC launched retaliatory strikes on Israel’s Haifa refinery in response.

The most telling comment came from Iran’s own parliamentary speaker, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf — a hard-liner, not a reformist. His statement on Sunday: “If the war continues like this, there will be neither a way to sell oil nor the ability to produce it.” He said the attacks were “burning not only America’s interests, but also the interests of the region and the world.”

A hardliner telling his own people that continuing the war threatens to eliminate Iran’s ability to produce and sell its primary economic asset is not a declaration of imminent surrender. But it is the first public acknowledgment from inside the hard-line camp that the cost calculus of resistance is becoming unsustainable.

US gas prices rose another four cents on Sunday. The national average is now up 16 percent since the war began — the highest level since August 2024.

V. Lebanon: 394

The Lebanese Ministry of Health confirmed on Sunday that the death toll from the escalation of Israeli strikes had reached 394. The breakdown matters: 83 children, 42 women, 9 medical professionals. Four hospitals have been damaged; several others have been taken out of service due to the threat of further strikes. Health Minister Rakan Nassereddine: “These numbers show that they are targeting civilians, homes and the health sector.”

More than 300,000 people remain displaced inside Lebanon. Israel has warned all residents south of the Litani River to move north, the line long understood as the key front in any renewed conflict with Hezbollah. An Israeli commando raid into the Bekaa Valley on Friday night — ostensibly to search for information on navigator Ron Arad, missing since 1986 — killed 41 people, including three Lebanese soldiers.

Tami Arad, the missing airman’s wife, commented on the operation that was conducted in her husband’s name: “For 40 years, we have lived with the fact that Ron is missing. We want to know what happened to him, but not at any cost. The sanctity of life comes above all certainty and closure for us.”

The woman for whom the raid was ostensibly conducted asked that it not be conducted this way. The 41 people killed in Nabi Sheet did not survive to hear her ask.

VI. A Correction on Larijani

Part XXXVII analyzed Ali Larijani through the lens offered by Kareem Sadjadpour — as a potential Deng Xiaoping figure, a pragmatist who might conclude that saving the revolution required compromising with it, prioritizing economic national interest over 47 years of ideological posture. That framing remains theoretically available as an eventual off-ramp.

But Larijani’s actual Saturday night address was not a pragmatist’s speech. It was a war leader’s speech.

“Americans must know that we will not let them go.” “Our people are with us, our leadership is united, there is no division in fighting Israel and America.” “America is stuck in the swamp of its own miscalculations.” He vowed to avenge Khamenei’s killing. He said the United States “must pay the price.” He urged Trump to “accept he made a mistake and was fooled by Israel.”

The speech was designed to accomplish two things: unify domestic opinion after the visible fracture of Pezeshkian’s apology-and-retraction sequence, and signal to Iran’s adversaries that the civilian president does not represent the country’s actual strategic posture. On both counts it succeeded. Larijani is Khamenei’s designated caretaker on national security and foreign policy. When he speaks, it is the IRGC’s Iran that is speaking.

This does not foreclose the Deng path. Deng himself was a committed revolutionary before he was a pragmatist. The shift from ideological rigidity to strategic flexibility can happen under extreme pressure — indeed, it typically only happens under extreme pressure. The question is whether Iran is experiencing sufficient pressure to produce that shift, or sufficient pressure to harden against it. Larijani’s speech on Saturday night suggests the answer, for now, is the latter.

VII. The War Trump Is Declaring Won

On Saturday, aboard Air Force One, Trump told reporters he had reversed himself on Kurdish forces: “We don’t want to make the war any more complex.” Two days earlier he had been “all in” on a Kurdish offensive. The list of strategic positions Trump has taken and then abandoned in nine days is now long enough to constitute a pattern: four to five weeks maximum → CENTCOM planning 100 days; regime change → keep the regime but change the people; “I got people I’m looking at” → “turns out we killed them”; all in on the Kurds → too complex; “we don’t need boots on the ground” → “at some point maybe we will.”

On the same Air Force One flight, asked whether he would consider sending ground troops into Iran to secure nuclear facilities: “At some point maybe we will. It would be a great thing, but right now we’re just decimating them.”

He also attacked British Prime Minister Starmer for reportedly considering deploying two aircraft carriers to the Middle East — not in support, but as an independent show of presence. Trump’s Truth Social post: “That’s OK Prime Minister Starmer, we don’t need them any longer — But we will remember. We don’t need people that join Wars after we’ve already won!”

Already won. This was posted on a day when Iran struck Haifa, Dubai’s marina, Baghdad’s US Embassy, Kuwait’s airport fuel tanks, Bahrain’s desalination plant, multiple Gulf states’ air defenses, and the UAE absorbed 17 ballistic missiles and 117 drone attacks. The Iranian parliamentary speaker warned that continued war threatens to eliminate Iran’s ability to sell oil. The Assembly of Experts announced a supreme leader selection is imminent. Israel threatened to assassinate everyone who attends the meeting.

The war Trump has declared won is the same war that, as of Sunday morning, has no ceasefire, no back-channel, no diplomatic off-ramp, no congressional authorization, no supplemental funding request, and no end date — except the one that CENTCOM is planning for, which is September.

Day Nine opens with Tehran skies orange from burning oil depots. US gas up 16 percent. Lebanon dead: 394, including 83 children. Iran’s Assembly of Experts moving toward a succession announcement — name withheld because Israel has threatened to assassinate the selection. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed. A desalination plant has been struck on each side. The NIC report said the war was unlikely to produce regime change. Iran is selecting a new supreme leader under bombardment. The war continues.
