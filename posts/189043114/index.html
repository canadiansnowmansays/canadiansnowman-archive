Trump’s Section 122 Gambit

Fresh Off a Supreme Court Loss, the Administration Deploys a Statute Its Own Lawyers Called Inapplicable

February 24, 2026

The administration's response to losing at the Supreme Court, age 4.

CanadianSnowmanSays Feb 24, 2026

Bottom Line Up Front: Four days after the Supreme Court struck down his IEEPA tariffs as exceeding presidential authority, Trump has replaced them with a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — a statute designed for a monetary crisis that no longer exists, invoked by no president in the 52 years since its passage, and characterized by Trump’s own Justice Department lawyers last year as having no “obvious application” to trade deficits. Trump announced via Truth Social on Saturday that he would raise the rate to 15% — the statutory maximum — but as of publication a White House spokesman confirmed that increase was still being worked on with no implementation timeline. Legal challenges are already being prepared by the same groups that beat him at the Supreme Court. The constitutional quicksand didn’t disappear on February 20. It just moved.

The Replacement Nobody Tested

When the Supreme Court issued its 6-3 ruling last Friday, the administration had its replacement ready within hours: Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, a provision so obscure that no president had ever used it in five decades of availability.

By Tuesday, Trump had signed an executive order imposing a 10% global tariff under Section 122. On Saturday morning, he posted on Truth Social that he would raise it to 15% — the statutory maximum the law allows — effective immediately. However, a White House spokesman subsequently confirmed the increase was still being worked on with no timeline for implementation. The 10% rate is what is currently in effect. The administration declared the provision “legally tested.” Trade expert Scott Lincicome of the Cato Institute immediately responded that Trump “was a little out over his skis” with that characterization. No president has invoked it. No court has reviewed it. It has never been tested at all.

Section 122 allows the president to impose tariffs for up to 150 days in response to “large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits” or situations presenting “fundamental international payments problems.” After 150 days, congressional approval is required to extend them.

The administration’s argument is that “balance of payments” encompasses the U.S. trade deficit — the gap between imports and exports that Trump has spent two years trying to reduce. That argument has a problem. Several problems, actually. But the most damaging one comes from an unusual source: the Trump administration itself.

The Self-Contradiction at the Heart of the Gambit

In legal briefs submitted to the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit last year — while arguing that IEEPA gave Trump unlimited tariff authority — the administration’s own Justice Department lawyers wrote that Section 122 did not “have any obvious application here, where the concerns the President identified in declaring an emergency arise from trade deficits, which are conceptually distinct from balance-of-payments deficits.”

Read that again. Trump’s own lawyers told a federal appeals court that Section 122 was conceptually distinct from what they needed and had no obvious application to trade deficits.

That was last year. This year, Section 122 is the centerpiece of the replacement tariff strategy.

This is not a subtle shift in legal interpretation. This is the administration arguing position A to win one court case, losing that case at the Supreme Court, and immediately arguing the opposite of position A to justify the next policy. The lawyers who wrote that Section 122 didn’t apply are presumably the same lawyers now arguing that it does.

The Gold Window Problem

Beyond the self-contradiction lies a deeper structural issue: Section 122 was written to solve a problem that doesn’t exist anymore.

The provision was passed in 1974, three years after President Nixon closed the “gold window” — ending the Bretton Woods system under which the dollar was pegged to gold and other currencies were pegged to the dollar. Before 1971, the United States had begun importing more than it exported, increasing the global supply of dollars. Foreign governments had the legal right to exchange those dollars for gold, creating the risk of a genuine balance-of-payments crisis where America couldn’t honor its obligations.

Nixon ended that system in 1971. Currencies were allowed to float freely against each other. The specific crisis Section 122 was designed to address — a country running out of gold reserves because it can’t balance its international payments under a fixed exchange rate — became structurally impossible.

Gary Hufbauer, a nonresident senior fellow at the Peterson Institute for International Economics who served as a Treasury consultant in the 1970s, put it plainly: “By the time Section 122 was passed in 1974, Congress was giving the president tools to deal with an exchange rate system that no longer existed. In a system of floating exchange rates, there’s no such thing as a balance-of-payments deficit.”

Harvard economist and former IMF Deputy Managing Director Gita Gopinath was equally direct about the factual foundation of Trump’s Section 122 proclamation. In it, Trump claimed the United States faced “fundamental international payments problems” and “an imminent and significant depreciation of its currency in foreign exchange markets.” Gopinath said a country has a balance-of-payments problem “when it is at risk of losing access to financial markets” — when borrowing costs spike because foreign investors lose faith in the country’s ability to repay. “The U.S. does not have that problem,” she said.

Trump has taken a statute designed for a 1971-era monetary emergency, applied it to a 2026 trade deficit in a floating exchange rate world, and called it legally tested. His own lawyers told a court last year it didn’t apply. Economists say the underlying factual premise doesn’t exist. And no court has ever reviewed whether it can be used this way.

The Challengers Are Already Preparing

The same legal groups that brought the case that ultimately reached the Supreme Court are watching closely.

Oliver Dunford, senior lawyer at the Pacific Legal Foundation — which represented businesses including clothing company Princess Awesome in the original IEEPA challenge — was unambiguous: “We’re certainly thinking about it. The problem the president has identified under Section 122 doesn’t exist.”

The Liberty Justice Center, which helped bring the original case, said it was “closely monitoring any new statutes being invoked” and was “not ruling anything out.” The New Civil Liberties Alliance said it was “analyzing” the new tariffs.

This is the same ecosystem of challengers, same legal infrastructure, same institutional motivation — now pointed at a different statute. The IEEPA case took roughly nine months from filing to Supreme Court argument. Section 122 has a 150-day clock before it requires congressional extension. The timelines are not comfortable for the administration.

The 150-Day Cliff

Section 122’s built-in expiration is itself a significant constraint that IEEPA never imposed. The statute explicitly limits tariffs to 150 days without congressional approval. That clock started Tuesday.

Congressional extension faces severe political headwinds. The Senate has twice voted in bipartisan fashion to rescind Trump’s tariffs — on Canada and on Brazil. Only six House Republicans voted last week to rescind Canada tariffs, but those six were mostly retiring members willing to absorb Trump’s retribution threats. With midterm elections approaching and affordability polling as a top voter concern, the political calculus of voting to extend tariffs — effectively voting to keep prices higher — becomes increasingly uncomfortable for vulnerable Republicans.

If Congress won’t extend Section 122 tariffs, and Section 301 investigations take 7-12 months to produce new tariff authority, there is a window — potentially opening around July — where Trump’s replacement tariff structure faces simultaneous legal and legislative pressure. The administration is betting it can fill that window with Section 232 expansions and the results of accelerated Section 301 investigations. Whether those investigations can be completed quickly enough, and survive their own legal scrutiny, remains entirely unresolved.

The Whack-a-Mole Dynamic

What has emerged since Friday is a pattern that deserves a name. Courts strike down one legal theory. The administration immediately deploys another untested authority. Challengers prepare new lawsuits. The new authority has its own vulnerabilities — including, in this case, the extraordinary vulnerability of being explicitly disavowed by the administration’s own lawyers months earlier.

The Supreme Court’s IEEPA ruling was framed as a reckoning. It was also, in a structural sense, the starting gun for the next race.

The administration’s approach has been consistent throughout: find the widest possible interpretation of any available authority, deploy it immediately at maximum rate, and let courts sort it out over years while the policy takes effect. With IEEPA, that strategy extracted over $200 billion in tariffs before the Supreme Court intervened — money the administration is now refusing to refund. With Section 122, the 150-day window may prove too short for the same strategy to work, but the approach is identical.

Meanwhile, the trading partners who made concessions based on IEEPA tariffs are watching a flat 10% replace their negotiated terms — regardless of what they gave up to get them. Indonesia signed a trade deal with the United States on Thursday, agreeing to zero tariffs on American goods in exchange for a 19% rate on its exports. One day later that framework was invalidated. Two days later, Indonesia faces the same 10% as every other country that made no concessions at all. Every government that negotiated over the past year now faces the same question: if making concessions and making none produce identical outcomes, why make them?

Conclusion: The Quicksand Shifted, It Didn’t Disappear

Five days after the Supreme Court delivered what was widely described as a historic constitutional reckoning, Trump’s trade war continues — at 10%, under a statute its own lawyers called inapplicable, designed for an exchange rate crisis that was already obsolete by the time the ink dried, subject to a 150-day clock, and already in the legal crosshairs of the same challengers who beat him on IEEPA.

The boy who cried emergency has discovered a new word: balance-of-payments. Whether it holds up better than “emergency” is the question courts will now spend the next several months answering.

The constitutional whack-a-mole has begun. The mallet is in the same small hands as before. The mole keeps finding new holes.
