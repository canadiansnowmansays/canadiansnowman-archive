The Charlie Kirk Debates

#12: Using Faith to Justify Exclusion - Part II: The Challenge

December 10, 2025

Charlie Kirk challenged others to “prove me wrong” while presenting a version of Christianity that Black pastors, mainstream theologians, and Jesus’s own words all prove fundamentally wrong. His selective use of Scripture to justify exclusion contradicts the faith he claimed to represent.

I. Jesus’s Actual Teaching on Love and Inclusion

The Greatest Commandments

When asked which commandment was greatest, Jesus didn’t cite Leviticus on stoning. He said:

“’Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.’ This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: ‘Love your neighbor as yourself.’ All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments.” (Matthew 22:37-40)

Jesus made love—not punishment, not exclusion, not dominance—the interpretive key for all Scripture. Kirk inverted this, making punishment his interpretive key and love a footnote.

Jesus and the Stoning

Kirk called Leviticus’s stoning laws “God’s perfect law.” Jesus directly contradicted this interpretation.

When religious leaders brought a woman caught in adultery (punishable by stoning under Mosaic law) to Jesus, He didn’t affirm the stoning. He said: “Let him who is without sin among you be the first to throw a stone at her” (John 8:7).

The crowd dispersed. Jesus told the woman: “Neither do I condemn you; go, and from now on sin no more” (John 8:11).

Jesus fulfilled the law not through enforcing its punishments but through offering grace and calling people to transformation. Kirk’s “God’s perfect law” response to Ms. Rachel contradicted Jesus’s own handling of the same legal framework.

Jesus’s Treatment of Outcasts

Kirk demonized and excluded. Jesus welcomed and included:

  • Tax collectors and sinners: Jesus ate with them despite religious leaders’ condemnation (Luke 5:30-32)

  • Samaritans: Jesus made a Samaritan the hero of His most famous parable and ministered to a Samaritan woman (Luke 10:25-37, John 4:1-42)

  • Gentiles: Jesus praised a Roman centurion’s faith and healed a Canaanite woman’s daughter (Matthew 8:10, Matthew 15:21-28)

  • Women: Jesus taught women as disciples and appeared first to women after resurrection (Luke 10:38-42, John 20:11-18)

Jesus’s pattern was inclusion, not exclusion—welcome, not walls—love, not law.

II. Paul’s Teaching on Unity in Christ

Kirk’s racism and sexism directly contradict Paul’s teaching:

“There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” (Galatians 3:28)

When Kirk claimed Black women lack “brain processing power,” he violated this fundamental Christian teaching. When he promoted Great Replacement conspiracy theories, he contradicted Paul’s vision of unity transcending ethnic boundaries.

Paul’s letters emphasize:

  • “Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you” (Romans 15:7)

  • “Do nothing out of selfish ambition or vain conceit. Rather, in humility value others above yourselves” (Philippians 2:3)

  • “Clothe yourselves with compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness and patience” (Colossians 3:12)

Kirk’s performative hyper-masculinity, dominance theology, and combative rhetoric contradicted every one of these commands.

III. Old Testament Commands Kirk Ignored

Kirk cited Leviticus selectively. He ignored these commands from the same book:

On Strangers:

“When a stranger sojourns with you in your land, you shall not do him wrong. You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt: I am the Lord your God.” (Leviticus 19:33-34)

Kirk promoted “America is full” and religious screening of immigrants—directly violating this command.

On Justice:

“You shall do no injustice in court. You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great, but in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor.” (Leviticus 19:15)

Kirk presumed Black pilots unqualified based on race—the definition of partiality.

On Love:

“You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I am the Lord.” (Leviticus 19:18)

This is the verse Ms. Rachel quoted. It appears in the same chapter Kirk claimed to uphold. Kirk ignored it.

IV. How Kirk Failed Every Christian Standard

The Love Test

Jesus’s standard: “By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another” (John 13:35)

Kirk’s record:

  • Called George Floyd a “scumbag”

  • Described Black people as “prowling” to “target white people”

  • Called empathy “made-up” and “damaging”

  • Promoted burning Pride flags

  • Demonized immigrants as replacements for white people

  • Advocated “Nuremberg trials” for doctors

Kirk failed Christianity’s most basic identification test—love.

The Humility Test

Jesus’s standard: “Blessed are the meek” (Matthew 5:5) / “The greatest among you will be your servant” (Matthew 23:11)

Kirk’s record:

  • Performative hyper-masculinity

  • “I worship a God that defeats evil” and “wins in the end” (framing himself on God’s winning side)

  • Dominance theology and conquest rhetoric

  • Dismissing accomplished Black women’s intelligence

  • Claiming to represent God’s will in politics

Kirk chose dominance over servanthood, conquest over meekness, power over humility.

The Judgment Test

Jesus’s standard: “Do not judge, or you too will be judged” (Matthew 7:1)

Kirk’s record: Kirk built his career on judgment—judging Black women’s intelligence, Black pilots’ qualifications, LGBTQ+ people’s worth, immigrants’ motives, Democrats’ spiritual state, Muslims’ compatibility with civilization.

Jesus warned: “Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother’s eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?” (Matthew 7:3)

Kirk saw specks everywhere while ignoring the planks in his own theology.

The Compassion Test

Jesus’s example: Jesus had compassion on crowds (Matthew 9:36), wept over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41), felt compassion for the sick and hungry (Matthew 14:14)

Kirk’s record: Kirk explicitly rejected empathy as “made-up” and “damaging”—directly contradicting Jesus’s entire ministry model.

Jesus’s ministry was defined by compassion. Kirk’s was defined by combat.

V. What Mainstream Christianity Says

The Black Church Response

Black pastors uniformly rejected Kirk’s Christianity as authentic faith:

Rev. Jacqui Lewis: “It’s white nationalism wrapped in talk of Jesus. It’s not Christian. It’s just not.”

Rev. Howard-John Wesley: “How you die does not redeem how you lived.”

Rev. F. Bruce Williams: Rejected Kirk’s martyrdom status before his memorial service even occurred.

Rev. Joel Bowman: Described the memorial as “part memorial service, but another part of it was more like a political rally”—a conflation of Christianity and partisan politics.

These weren’t theological liberals or progressives. These were pastors in historically Black Christian denominations calling out Kirk’s distortion of their shared faith.

The Weaponization Critique

Baptist News Global’s analysis captured the core problem:

“For too long, Christians have been told being ‘pro-life’ or ‘pro-family’ requires supporting policies that strip others of their God-given freedom to choose. That false choice has fueled movements like Kirk’s and convinced believers the way to win souls is through legislation and outrage. But Jesus never forced faith on anyone.”

Kirk turned Christianity from invitation to imposition, from love to law, from grace to government.

The Selective Christianity Problem

Scholar Jay Michaelson identified Kirk’s pattern:

“Christian Nationalism is selective Christianity, affirming some traditional Christian teachings (on masculinity, sexuality, and so on) while rejecting others, such as the importance of empathy, humble speech, love for all people, and concern for the ‘least of these.’ In contemporary Christian Nationalism, these virtues became vices.”

Kirk kept Christianity’s cultural power while abandoning its transformative content.

VI. The Historical Pattern: Faith Weaponized for Power

Kirk’s use of Christianity to justify exclusion follows a long, dark historical pattern:

Slavery: Christians cited Scripture to justify enslaving Black people, claiming they were inferior by divine design.

Colonialism: Christians used faith to justify conquering and subjugating indigenous peoples worldwide.

Segregation: Christians cited Scripture to oppose civil rights, claiming racial mixing violated God’s order.

Apartheid: South African Christians used theology to justify racial separation and white supremacy.

In every case, selective Scripture citation justified power arrangements while ignoring Christianity’s core teachings about human dignity, justice, and love.

Kirk continued this pattern—using Christianity to justify exclusion of immigrants, denigration of Black people, dominance over women, and rejection of LGBTQ+ people—while ignoring the faith’s central commands.

VII. What Kirk’s “Christianity” Actually Served

Kirk didn’t use Christianity to transform politics. He used politics to transform Christianity.

His version of faith served:

  • White demographic dominance (Great Replacement conspiracy, “Christian population” requirement)

  • Male authority (women’s submission, masculinity emphasis)

  • Heteronormativity (LGBTQ+ as “error,” stoning as “perfect law”)

  • National exclusion (closed borders, religious screening)

  • Political power (Seven Mountains, Christian nationalism)

This isn’t Christianity shaping politics to reflect Jesus’s values. It’s politics reshaping Christianity to sanctify partisan preferences.

As Rev. Lewis observed, this is “white nationalism wrapped in talk of Jesus.”

VIII. The Ultimate Contradiction

Kirk claimed to follow Jesus while systematically rejecting Jesus’s example:

  • Jesus welcomed outcasts → Kirk excluded immigrants and LGBTQ+ people

  • Jesus practiced humility → Kirk promoted dominance

  • Jesus showed compassion → Kirk rejected empathy

  • Jesus forgave sinners → Kirk demanded punishment

  • Jesus served the vulnerable → Kirk questioned their competence

  • Jesus said “My kingdom is not of this world” → Kirk promoted theocratic dominion

Kirk used Christianity’s language while inverting its content.

Conclusion: Political Christianity Is Not Authentic Christianity

Charlie Kirk challenged others to “prove me wrong” while presenting a Christianity that:

  1. Cherry-picked Scripture to emphasize punishment while ignoring love

  2. Contradicted Jesus on inclusion, humility, compassion, and judgment

  3. Violated biblical commands on welcoming strangers and loving neighbors

  4. Failed basic Christian tests of love, humility, and compassion

  5. Continued historical patterns of weaponizing faith to justify exclusion

  6. Served political power rather than transformation

  7. Was rejected by mainstream clergy as inauthentic

Black pastors, mainstream theologians, and Jesus’s own words all prove Kirk’s version of Christianity fundamentally wrong.

Kirk didn’t apply Christianity to politics—he applied politics to Christianity, reshaping a faith centered on sacrificial love into an ideology promoting dominance, exclusion, and power.

When Ms. Rachel quoted “love thy neighbor,” Kirk responded with stoning laws. This perfectly captured his theological inversion—replacing love with law, grace with punishment, inclusion with exclusion.

Jesus stopped a stoning. Kirk called stoning “God’s perfect law.”

Jesus welcomed strangers. Kirk promoted “America is full.”

Jesus said “love your enemies.” Kirk called them demons.

Jesus practiced humility. Kirk promoted dominance.

Jesus showed compassion. Kirk rejected empathy.

The evidence is overwhelming: Kirk’s Christianity served political ideology, not Jesus Christ. He weaponized faith to justify the very exclusions Christianity was meant to abolish.

As Rev. Jacqui Lewis said: “It’s white nationalism wrapped in talk of Jesus. And it’s not Christian. It’s just not.”
