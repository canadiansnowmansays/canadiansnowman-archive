The Trump Grift

How the Family Never Stops Selling Their Name (and America’s Soul)

Part 9: When the House Stops Pretending There’s a Game

November 17, 2025

Saturday’s Announcement, Tuesday’s Dinner

November 16, 2025: The New York Times reports the Trump Organization is finalizing a deal to join Saudi Arabia’s $63 billion Diriyah development project. Jerry Inzerillo, the project’s CEO and longtime Trump friend, says it’s “just a matter of time.”

November 18, 2025: Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman—the man who ordered Jamal Khashoggi’s murder—arrives at the White House for his first visit to the United States in seven years. Trump plans to receive him “with all the trappings of a state visit.”

Forty-eight hours between the deal announcement and the state dinner.

If you think that’s coincidence, you haven’t been paying attention to how the Trump family does business.

This isn’t an isolated deal. It’s the latest iteration of a three-generation template Fred Trump built in Brooklyn: extract maximum wealth while transferring all costs and risks to others. What we’re witnessing in November 2025 is the pattern’s final evolution—the systematic monetization of the American presidency itself, combined with something even more revealing: a pathological need to plaster the Trump name on everything from private towers to federal buildings.

The house has stopped pretending there’s a game. And the dealer wants his name on the table.

Breaking: Saudi Arabia’s $63 Billion — “Appeal to Him as a Developer”

The Setup

President Trump and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman review architectural models of the $63 billion Diriyah development during Trump’s May 2025 state visit to Riyadh. ‘You normally wouldn’t do that in a state dinner,’ said the project’s CEO. ‘But that’s because you’re looking at him as a developer.

During Trump’s May 2025 state visit to Riyadh, Saudi officials gave the president a tour of the Diriyah development—a $63 billion project transforming a historic Saudi town into a luxury destination with hotels, retail, and office space.

Jerry Inzerillo, Diriyah’s chief executive and longtime Trump friend, explained the strategy: “It turned out to be a good stroke of luck and maybe a little bit clever of us to say, ‘OK, let’s appeal to him as a developer’—and he loved it.”

During the tour, Trump and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman reviewed architectural plans and models. “You normally wouldn’t do that in a state dinner. It’s not really the normal protocol,” Inzerillo said. “But that’s because you’re looking at the president of the United States as the president of the United States. You’re not looking at him as a developer.“

Except Trump is looking at himself as a developer. That’s not a bug in Saudi strategy. It’s the entire pitch.

The Timing That Reveals the Transaction

  • Saturday, November 16: New York Times reports Inzerillo saying the deal is “just a matter of time” and “nothing announced yet, but soon to be”

  • Tuesday, November 18: MBS arrives for his first visit to the United States in seven years

The crown prince “hopes to sign a mutual defense agreement with Washington and potentially advance a deal to transfer American nuclear technology to Saudi Arabia,” the Times reports.

As the Times notes explicitly: “That sets up a scenario in which Mr. Trump discusses matters of national security with a foreign leader who is also a key figure in a potential business deal with the president’s family.”

Not “potential conflict of interest.” Not “appearance of impropriety.” Direct transactional relationship between presidential decisions and family profit.

The Existing Revenue Stream

This isn’t speculative. Dar Global—”the Trump Organization’s most important foreign business partner and a key conduit to Arab governments and Gulf companies”—already has the Trump family on retainer.

Dar Global paid the Trump Organization $21.9 million in licensing fees in 2024 alone. Some of that money goes directly to the president.

Ziad El Chaar, Dar Global’s CEO, said last month: “You will see us announcing more collaborations with the giga-projects.” (Saudi officials call Diriyah and similar developments “giga-projects”—all owned by the Saudi sovereign wealth fund.)

What MBS Gets Tuesday Night

When the man who ordered Jamal Khashoggi’s murder sits down to a White House state dinner—two days after his government’s CEO announced the Diriyah deal is “just a matter of time”—what’s on the negotiating table?

  • F-35 fighter jets (eliminating Israel’s exclusive regional military edge)

  • $142 billion in additional defense sales

  • Mutual defense agreement

  • American nuclear technology transfer

  • Rehabilitation despite journalist’s murder

  • Legitimacy as regional power broker

And the Trump Organization gets:

  • $63 billion Diriyah licensing deal

  • Continued revenue from existing $21.9 million/year Dar Global relationship

  • Additional “giga-project” deals El Chaar promised are coming

The president of the United States is conducting foreign policy as real estate development. Not metaphorically. Literally. With architectural models reviewed during state dinners while his family business negotiates $63 billion in licensing deals.

The White House Ballroom: Selling the People’s House Itself

The East Wing (outlined in dashes) - including Eleanor Roosevelt’s former office - demolished to make room for the $300 million Trump Ballroom where corporate donors can have their names ‘etched forever’ in the People’s House.

While the Saudi deal dominates headlines, something equally brazen is happening domestically: the literal sale of the White House.

The Project

In July 2025, Trump announced a 90,000-square-foot ballroom expansion, demolishing Eleanor Roosevelt’s East Wing office to make room. Construction began in September and continued through the October 2025 government shutdown while federal workers went unpaid.

Donors receive pledge agreements for “The Donald J. Trump Ballroom at the White House” with discussions about having their corporate names “etched inside the White House forever.” Not metaphorically. Literally inscribed in the People’s House.

The Buyers and What They’re Buying

Alphabet: $22 million (7% of the project) as part of a lawsuit settlement after YouTube banned Trump following January 6.

Palantir: Awarded over $800 million in government contracts in fiscal year 2025—more than any prior year. Trump made them the key player consolidating Americans’ federal data between agencies.

Nvidia: Seeking export licenses for AI chip sales to China and UAE; donated while these regulatory negotiations were ongoing.

Lockheed Martin: Donated over $10 million. Typically awarded tens of billions annually in federal contracts.

Reynolds American: Lobbying Trump to kill Biden’s proposed menthol cigarette ban.

The Return on Investment

According to The Washington Post, the 37 publicly identified ballroom donors hold $279 billion in federal contracts.

The White House released only a partial donor list—still concealing Carrier Group, Nvidia, BlackRock, and Jeff Yass, all with major business before the administration.

Companies with billions in regulatory needs are buying permanent inscription in America’s most sacred building while Trump demolishes Eleanor Roosevelt’s office to make room for corporate logos.

Same template as the Saudi deal. Different venue. Identical mechanism.

Truth Predict: Insider Trading as a Presidential Service

And then there’s the most audacious scheme yet: a betting platform where Americans wager on decisions Trump controls.

October 28, 2025: The Launch

Trump Media announced partnership with Crypto.com to launch prediction markets on Truth Social. Users can bet on:

  • Political elections

  • Interest rate changes

  • Commodity prices (gold, crude oil)

  • Federal policy decisions

  • Tariff announcements

  • Sports

The Conflict Built Into the Product

Trump controls or heavily influences:

  • Interest rates (through Fed appointments and public pressure)

  • Commodity markets (through tariffs, Strategic Petroleum Reserve)

  • Federal policy decisions (he’s the president)

  • Tariff announcements (his signature policy tool)

Imagine the scenario: Trump announces he’s “considering” new tariffs on a Tuesday. Truth Social users bet on whether he’ll actually impose them by Friday. Trump sees the betting patterns—sees which way the “smart money” is flowing—and announces his decision. His company takes a percentage of every bet placed.

This isn’t a casino. It’s monetizing the information asymmetry of executive power itself.

The Insider Network

  • Crypto.com donated $1 million to Trump’s inauguration

  • Crypto.com CEO visited Mar-a-Lago in December 2024

  • Don Jr. is advisor to competing platforms Kalshi and Polymarket

  • Don Jr. sits on Trump Media’s board

The president’s son advises companies competing with his own family’s betting platform, while all of them profit from wagers on the president’s decisions.

The Name Obsession: From Buildings to Federal Infrastructure

But there’s a deeper pathology at work here—one that connects the Saudi deals, the ballroom, and a new push to rebrand America’s most iconic institutions.

Trump’s Lifetime Branding Compulsion

Before he ever ran for president, Trump had already spent decades emblazoning his name on everything within reach:

  • Buildings: Trump Tower, Trump Plaza, Trump International—dozens across Manhattan

  • Failed products: Trump Steaks, Trump Vodka, Trump University, Trump Ice

  • Transportation: His 727 jet fleet, his yacht, his helicopters—all branded TRUMP

  • Golf courses: 17 courses worldwide, each prominently displaying his name

As one observer noted: “Trump emblazons buildings, aeroplanes and golf courses with his own name; regularly speaks about how he has ‘good genes’; and talks of how uniquely talented and clever he is.”

Rolling Stone observed: “Not content to inflict the city with a succession of eyesores bearing his name in outsize letters, he had to buy up more Atlantic City casinos than anyone else.”

This pattern suggests behavior beyond business strategy—a compulsive need for immortality through naming, where seeing his name everywhere serves as proof of his importance and lasting impact.

Now: Rebranding Federal Property

With presidential power, Trump’s name obsession has expanded from private real estate to America’s public institutions:

The Kennedy Center:

  • July 2025: Rep. Bob Onder (R-MO) introduced the “Make Entertainment Great Again Act” to rename the Kennedy Center the “Donald J. Trump Center for the Performing Arts”

  • House Republicans voted to rename the Opera House the “First Lady Melania Trump Opera House”

  • August 2025: Trump already calling it the “TRUMP/KENNEDY CENTER” on Truth Social

  • Legal obstacle: A 1983 law prohibits additional memorials or plaques in the Kennedy Center

  • JFK’s grandson Jack Schlossberg responded: “Trump is obsessed with being bigger than JFK, with minimizing the many heroes of our past, as if that elevates him. It doesn’t.”

Penn Station:

  • April 2025: Trump administration seized control of the $7 billion Penn Station renovation from New York’s MTA

  • Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy joked that “Trump Station has a nice ring to it”

  • Gov. Kathy Hochul said she’d consider renaming it only if Trump funded the entire $7 billion project: “It would not be my favorite thing to call it, but it’ll save us $1.3 billion”—a funding ploy, not genuine openness

The Psychological Pattern

What we’re witnessing isn’t just corruption. It’s a narcissistic pathology manifesting through presidential power:

  • Saudi deal: His name on a $63 billion development while conducting U.S. foreign policy

  • Ballroom: His name etched in the White House forever

  • Kennedy Center: Replacing JFK’s memorial with his own

  • Penn Station: A transit hub used by millions branded as “Trump Station”

  • Federal infrastructure: Signs reading “PRESIDENT DONALD J. TRUMP — REBUILDING AMERICA’S INFRASTRUCTURE” on projects he opposed

The compulsion to see his name everywhere—from private towers to federal buildings—isn’t separate from the grift. It’s the psychological driver behind it.

The Pattern From Previous Episodes

And this isn’t new. As I’ve documented in earlier parts of this series, the same pattern has been playing out throughout 2025:

From your Gulf deals ($5.5B Qatar golf resort, UAE towers, Oman hotels—all generating licensing fees while Trump sets Middle East policy)

From Tim Cook’s playbook ($1M inaugural donation → $1.1B in Apple tariff exemptions)

From Vietnam ($1.5B resort approval while facing 46% tariff threats)

From World Liberty Financial ($2B UAE government investment in Trump family crypto while setting crypto policy)

From the Executive Branch Club ($500K memberships promising White House access)

One business model. Expanding revenue streams. All happening simultaneously.

The Mechanism

This is one template with multiple applications:

  • Create leverage (state dinners, tariff threats, ballroom access, federal funding, insider information)

  • Extract payment (licensing deals, donor fees, inaugural donations, betting platform fees, crypto investments)

  • Transfer costs (to taxpayers, foreign policy, democratic norms, American credibility, market integrity)

  • Claim credit (Middle East peace, fiscal responsibility, infrastructure investment, “democratizing” betting)

Fred’s template. Scaled from $4 million FHA fraud to $63 billion Saudi licensing deals.

And here’s why it works: the marks profit from being marks.

MBS gets F-35s and nuclear technology. Ballroom donors get $279B in federal contracts. Crypto.com gets presidential endorsement. Penn Station gets federal funding. Kennedy Center gets renovation money.

Everyone wins—except the American people who lose government integrity, foreign policy independence, and the People’s House itself.

A Note to Those Who’ve Stopped Seeing It

If your reaction is “more partisan vitriol” or “Trump Derangement Syndrome,” I want you to consider something: you might be normalizing corruption through repetition.

This isn’t a red versus blue argument. This isn’t liberal hysteria about a Republican president.

This is documented transactions:

  • $63 billion Diriyah deal announced 48 hours before MBS state dinner (New York Times)

  • $279 billion in federal contracts held by ballroom donors (Washington Post)

  • $21.9 million paid by Dar Global to Trump Organization in 2024 alone (Trump’s financial disclosure)

  • Kennedy Center renaming bills introduced (Congressional record)

  • “Trump Station” floated by Transportation Secretary (multiple outlets)

These aren’t allegations. These are facts reported by mainstream outlets with dates, dollar amounts, and named sources.

If Barack Obama’s family business received $21.9 million annually from the Chinese government while he set China policy—would you call criticism “partisan vitriol”?

If a President Hillary Clinton demolished part of the White House to build a ballroom funded by defense contractors holding $279 billion in federal contracts—would you defend it as “smart business”?

If Hunter Biden launched a betting platform where Americans wagered on Biden’s tariff decisions—would you call that “democratizing information”?

If Obama proposed renaming the Kennedy Center after himself—would you cite his “cultural contributions”?

Trump would call this “smart business” and “creating jobs”—but using presidential power to create business opportunities for your family isn’t entrepreneurship. It’s corruption.

And the compulsion to plaster your name on federal buildings while profiting from foreign governments isn’t confidence. It’s pathological narcissism enabled by presidential power.

Set aside red, blue, and purple thinking. Look at the pattern:

Multiple schemes. Multiple revenue streams. All documented. All happening simultaneously. All involving the president’s family business profiting from his government decisions. Plus a psychological compulsion to see his name replace American heroes on federal property.

This isn’t partisan. It’s arithmetic and psychology.

The house is dealing with the cards face-up. The only question is whether you’re willing to see them.
