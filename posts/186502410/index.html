A Pattern of Sexual Assault

Part 17: 26 Women, Zero Prosecutions (1970s-2024)

February 3, 2026

September 16, 2005: Actress Arianne Zucker greets Donald Trump and Billy Bush on the set of Days of Our Lives. Minutes before this photo, Trump had bragged on a hot mic about grabbing women "by the pussy." Zucker had no idea what Trump had just said about women like her.

In 2005, while preparing to film a segment for Access Hollywood, Donald Trump didn’t know his microphone was on. What he said would eventually shake American politics:

“I just start kissing them. It’s like a magnet. Just kiss. I don’t even wait. And when you’re a star, they let you do it. You can do anything... Grab them by the pussy. You can do anything.”

This wasn’t hypothetical. This wasn’t locker room banter. This was a confession of sexual assault—a description of behavior that constitutes a crime in every state in America. When the Washington Post published the tape on October 7, 2016, just weeks before the presidential election, many thought Trump’s campaign was finished.

They were wrong. Trump issued a rare public apology, attempted to dismiss it as “locker room talk,” and went on to win the presidency. The tape that should have ended his political career instead became the first test of a new reality: in Trump’s America, accountability for sexual misconduct was optional—at least for him.

The Access Hollywood Tape

The recording was made on September 16, 2005, as Trump and Access Hollywood host Billy Bush rode a bus to the set of Days of Our Lives, where Trump was making a cameo appearance. The tape sat forgotten on a shelf at NBC for more than a decade.

In October 2016, as Trump’s offensive comments about women were making waves, a producer remembered the 2005 taping session and dug up the footage. What they found was explosive.

In the recording, Trump describes trying to seduce a married woman: “I moved on her... I did try and fuck her. She was married.” He then boasts about his ability to assault women: “I just start kissing them. It’s like a magnet. Just kiss. I don’t even wait. And when you’re a star, they let you do it. You can do anything. Grab them by the pussy. You can do anything.”

Billy Bush can be heard laughing along. Bush would later be fired from NBC over the incident. Trump would become president.

The Washington Post obtained and published the video on October 7, 2016. The reaction was immediate and intense. Many prominent Republicans initially distanced themselves from Trump, with some calling for him to withdraw from the race. The public outcry was fierce—the comments were seen as a stark display of misogyny and an endorsement of sexual assault.

Trump issued a statement: “I said it, I was wrong, and I apologize.” He also tried to downplay it as “locker room talk,” a characterization widely criticized for trivializing sexual assault.

Despite the controversy, Trump remained in the race and won the election weeks later. The Access Hollywood tape, which should have been disqualifying, instead became just another scandal that Trump survived.

Years later, Trump cast doubt on the tape’s authenticity despite having apologized for it. Billy Bush confirmed in a 2017 New York Times op-ed: “Of course he said it.” The voice was undoubtedly Trump’s.

The tape has experienced renewed attention in recent years, particularly among Gen Z voters on platforms like TikTok, with many young people hearing the audio for the first time.

Courts have repeatedly found the tape relevant to litigation. Judges allowed transcripts or the tape’s contents into evidence in civil suits, including E. Jean Carroll’s case against Trump.

The Pattern: 26+ Women Come Forward

By the time the Access Hollywood tape emerged, Donald Trump had already been the subject of sexual misconduct allegations for decades. The tape’s release prompted more women to come forward publicly.

Today, depending on how the allegations are counted, between 26 and 28 women have publicly accused Trump of sexual assault, harassment, or inappropriate conduct. Some estimates, including allegations published in the 2019 book “All the President’s Women,” place the number as high as 69.

The allegations span more than four decades—from the 1970s through the 2010s—and follow disturbing patterns: forced kissing, groping under skirts, grabbing breasts and genitals without consent, walking into dressing rooms while contestants were naked, and rape. The alleged incidents occurred in airplanes, department stores, nightclubs, beauty pageants, hotel rooms, and his own properties.

According to multiple compilations, these women include:

1970s-1980s:

  • Jessica Leeds (late 1970s): alleged Trump groped her on an airplane, reaching under her skirt

  • Ivana Trump (1989): accused him in divorce depositions of a “violent assault” she described as rape following a painful scalp reduction surgery (later recanted under confidentiality agreement)

1990s:

  • Jill Harth (1992-1993): filed lawsuit alleging sexual harassment and attempted rape; alleged he groped her under a table during dinner

  • Kristin Anderson (early 1990s): alleged Trump reached under her skirt and touched her through her underwear at a Manhattan nightclub

  • Stacey Williams (1993): alleged Trump groped her at Trump Tower while Jeffrey Epstein watched and smiled

  • Lisa Boyne (mid-1990s): alleged at a dinner Trump forced women to walk across a table while he looked up their skirts

  • E. Jean Carroll (mid-1990s): alleged Trump raped her in a Bergdorf Goodman dressing room

  • Temple Taggart McDowell (1997): alleged Trump suddenly kissed her without consent on two occasions

  • Amy Dorris (1997): alleged Trump groped her and forced his tongue into her mouth at the U.S. Open

  • Karena Virginia (1998): alleged Trump grabbed her arm and touched her breast at the U.S. Open

2000s:

  • Karen Johnson (early 2000s): alleged Trump pulled her behind a tapestry at Mar-a-Lago to kiss and grope her during a New Year’s Eve party

  • Bridget Sullivan and Tasha Dixon (2000-2001): alleged Trump walked backstage at Miss USA pageants while contestants were naked or changing

  • Mindy McGillivray (2003): alleged Trump groped her at Mar-a-Lago

  • Rachel Crooks (2005): alleged Trump kissed her without consent in an elevator at Trump Tower

  • Natasha Stoynoff (2005): alleged Trump pushed her against a wall at Mar-a-Lago and forcibly kissed her during an interview

  • Jessica Drake (2006): alleged Trump grabbed and kissed her and two other women without permission, later offering her $10,000 to return to his room

  • Summer Zervos (2007): alleged Trump groped her and thrust his genitals at her

Trump’s response to every single allegation has been identical: deny, attack the accuser, claim she’s lying for money or fame, assert he’s never met her (even when photographic evidence exists), and declare she’s “not my type.”

In October 2016, Trump publicly vowed to sue all the women who accused him, as well as The New York Times for publishing their stories. He never did.

Trump’s Defense Strategy

When the accusations mounted in 2016, Trump’s response was consistent and predictable. At a September 2018 press conference, while defending Supreme Court nominee Brett Kavanaugh against sexual assault allegations, Trump took the opportunity to attack his own accusers:

“I was accused by four or five women who got paid to make up stories about me... They made false statements about me, knowing they were false. I never met them. I never met these people. And, what did they do? What did they do? They took money in order to say bad things.”

White House press secretary Sarah Sanders said in December 2017 the accusations were essentially “litigated” during the 2016 campaign, with voters knowing about them but choosing to vote for Trump anyway—as if electoral victory somehow erased the allegations or absolved him of responsibility.

The Public Response

A YouGov poll conducted in October 2016 found that 43 percent of respondents found the allegations credible. Predictably, Republicans were least likely to believe the accusations—only 19 percent thought sexual assault would disqualify Trump from the presidency.

After the 2016 election, and following the Harvey Weinstein allegations and the Me Too movement, attitudes shifted somewhat. By 2017, 86 percent of Clinton voters found the allegations credible, while only 6 percent of Trump voters did.

As New York Times columnist Jessica Bennett wrote, Trump has “changed the very nature of how we perceive truth” when it comes to sexual assault. The sheer volume of allegations has become background noise. Americans seem unable to process what these numbers represent.

Bennett quotes the women describing what Trump did: “squeezed my butt,” “eyed me like a piece of meat,” “stuck his hand up my skirt,” “thrust his genitals,” “forced his tongue in my mouth,” “was rummaging around my vagina.”

These aren’t abstract allegations. These are crimes. These are assaults. These are violations that would send any ordinary person to prison.

Why No Consequences?

The pattern of sexual misconduct allegations against Trump—and his complete lack of consequences for them—represents a profound breakdown of accountability.

Out of 26-28 women who publicly accused Trump:

  • None resulted in criminal charges

  • Only two pursued legal action in civil court: E. Jean Carroll (who won) and Summer Zervos (who withdrew her defamation case in 2021)

  • Trump was never investigated by law enforcement for any of these allegations

  • He faced no professional consequences

  • He was elected president in 2016 and re-elected in 2024

According to RAINN statistics, rape is notoriously underreported. Out of every 1,000 sexual assaults, just 25 perpetrators end up incarcerated. Trump’s case fits this pattern perfectly—except that unlike most accused perpetrators, Trump had the wealth, power, and platform to attack his accusers publicly, threaten lawsuits he never filed, and turn each allegation into a political battle where his supporters rallied to his defense.

Billy Bush lost his job at NBC for laughing along with Trump’s confession of sexual assault. Trump became president.

That’s the accountability gap in a nutshell.

The Normalization of Sexual Misconduct

Perhaps the most disturbing aspect of Trump’s pattern of sexual misconduct isn’t just that it happened, or that he escaped consequences—it’s that it’s been normalized.

The Access Hollywood tape, which should have ended any political career, became just another scandal. The 26+ allegations became a list too long to remember. Each new accusation was met with the same denials, the same attacks, the same shrugging acceptance from Trump’s supporters.

Trump and his allies have repeatedly tried to thwart investigations and lawsuits, but he’s been denied at every turn—except in the court of public opinion, where his supporters simply don’t care.

Trump taught Americans that sexual assault allegations against powerful men are just politics, just smears, just part of the game. He normalized the idea that women who accuse famous men must be lying, must be after money, must have ulterior motives.

This erosion of accountability for sexual misconduct extends beyond Trump himself. It set a template for how powerful men can survive such allegations: deny everything, attack the accusers, claim political persecution, wait it out.

What This Says About America

The fact that Donald Trump—a man credibly accused by 26+ women of sexual assault, a man recorded confessing to his pattern of assault—was elected president twice says something profound about American values and accountability.

It suggests that for a significant portion of the electorate, sexual assault allegations simply don’t matter. Or they matter less than other considerations. Or they’re assumed to be false. Or they’re accepted as just how powerful men behave.

None of these explanations is comforting.
