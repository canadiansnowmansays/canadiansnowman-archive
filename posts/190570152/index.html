The Iran Crisis

“Just Don’t Say the W-Word”

Part XLI: Day Eleven: March 10, 2026

March 11, 2026

Photo: Bilal Hussein / AP

I. The Tweet That Wasn’t

At approximately 1:30 in the afternoon on Tuesday, March 10, United States Energy Secretary Chris Wright posted on X that a Navy warship had “successfully escorted” an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz.

The Strait of Hormuz, which has been closed to commercial shipping since Day Two of the war. The Strait through which tanker traffic fell from 24 vessels per day to effectively zero in the first week of Operation Epic Fury. The Strait that Saudi Aramco’s CEO called the center of “the biggest crisis the region’s oil and gas industry has faced.” That Strait.

Wright’s post said it had been reopened.

Within minutes, a military official confirmed it had not happened. The Pentagon’s own chairman of the Joint Chiefs, General Dan Caine, had said at a briefing that same morning that the Pentagon had not been given the task of escorting merchant ships at all. The post was deleted without explanation.

The Thailand-flagged cargo ship Mayuree Naree engulfed in black smoke in the Strait of Hormuz, March 11, 2026. (Reuters)

One tweet. Posted and gone in under an hour. But in that hour, it demonstrated something more precisely than any analyst could have engineered: the Trump administration does not need the Strait to reopen. It needs the sentence “the Strait has reopened.” It needs the word — the signal, the optic, the claim — without the operational reality the word is supposed to describe.

The Atlantic’s Gal Beckerman wrote Tuesday about the administration’s relationship with a different word. His subject was “war.” The mechanism he identified is the same one on display in Wright’s deleted post.

II. Just Don’t Say the W-Word

What is happening in Iran right now? The question has a simple answer. The administration has been working since February 28 to ensure that answer cannot be given simply.

Speaker Mike Johnson, four days into the bombing: “We’re not at war right now.” This was, rather, a “very specific, clear mission — an operation.” The preferred term is “operation” — which Johnson used even as it encompassed assassinating a supreme leader, sinking an Iranian warship, destroying fuel depots and a desalination plant, and losing the lives of seven Americans along the way.

The constitutional reason is straightforward: only Congress can declare war, and if no one calls it a war, there is no reason to declare one. Senator Josh Hawley offered his own definition for the record: it only counts as war, for purposes of a congressional declaration, once there are American “boots on the ground.” Under this theory, seven dead Americans and 140 wounded do not constitute war because they died from the air.

But the avoidance runs deeper than constitutional mechanics. For most Americans, after Iraq and Afghanistan, war means quagmire. “Operation” is preceded more naturally by the word “successful.” It sounds like surgery — you’re usually unconscious while it’s happening. Even more laparoscopic is “surgical strike” — an outpatient procedure that pinches, but just for a moment.

This is not the first administration to reach for softer language. Korea was a “police action” — it cost more than 36,000 American lives. Vietnam was a “conflict.” Libya, under Obama, was a “kinetic military action.” The genre is long and bipartisan.

The most recent addition to the genre came from a different direction entirely. Vladimir Putin invaded Ukraine on February 24, 2022, and called it a “Special Military Operation” from the first hour. He then made it a criminal offense — punishable by up to 15 years in prison — to call it a war. Russians were arrested for holding blank signs in public squares. The word itself became contraband. Putin’s reasons were different from Trump’s: a formal declaration of war would have triggered Russian legal mobilization requirements and made the scale of the conflict undeniable at home. But the instinct was identical — the word carries consequences the leader has decided not to accept.

What distinguishes the Trump administration is not the evasion but the simultaneous aesthetic embrace of the word it is legally and politically avoiding. Putin wanted neither the word nor the image of war — just the territorial conquest. Trump wants the image, the warrior branding, the Department of War nameplate, the “maximum lethality” posture — just not the constitutional obligations that come with naming what he is doing.

Pete Hegseth wanted the Department of Defense renamed the “Department of War” because it “conveys a stronger message of readiness and resolve.” He loves the word “warrior.” He summarised his governing philosophy in a pair of rhyming couplets: “Maximum lethality, not tepid legality; violent effect, not politically correct.”

He loves war. He just doesn’t want to call this one that.

Mike Johnson captured the cognitive dissonance perfectly last week. He said “We are not at war” — then immediately referred to the “Department of Defense,” caught himself, corrected it to the “Department of War,” then appeared to realise what he had done. It is hard to maintain the position that the agency desperate to be called the Department of War has, as Johnson put it, “no intention of being at war.”

Tuesday, Hegseth was asked whether this was the beginning, the middle, or the end. “It’s not for me to posit whether it’s the beginning, the middle or the end,” he said. “He gets to control the throttle.” He added: “I want the American people to understand this is not endless. It’s not protracted.”

Seven Americans are dead. One hundred and forty are wounded. Eight are severely injured. The administration cannot say what winning looks like, how long it will take, or where in the arc of the conflict we currently stand. But it is not endless. It is not protracted.

It is an operation.

III. What the Fragments Say

Protesters hold up photos of children killed by airstrikes on their school in Minab.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

The Tomahawk fragments on the table in Minab — the Ball Aerospace serial numbers, the Globe Motors actuators, the “Made in USA” stamp documented in Part XL of this series — are now in the public record and cannot be walked back. Senator John Kennedy of Louisiana is the sole Republican senator willing to say plainly what they suggest. “We made a terrible, terrible mistake,” he said Tuesday. “I don’t see any reason right now to hide behind the investigation. I think we ought to just look the American people in the eye and say it looks like we made a mistake, and we’re sorry.”

He stands alone in his party.

The administration, meanwhile, has said it is investigating. It has also said — through the president — “Whatever the report shows, I’m willing to live with that.” One hundred and seventy-five people were reported killed at that school. The unit whose job was to ask whether there was a school within 400 feet of the target had been reduced by 90 percent before the first bomb fell. Those two facts belong in the same sentence. Senator Kennedy put them there. His colleagues did not.

IV. The Unconditional Surrender That Isn’t

On Tuesday, White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt resolved the question of what Trump’s demand for “unconditional surrender” actually means.

“When President Trump says that Iran is in a place of unconditional surrender, he’s not claiming the Iranian regime is going to come out and say that themselves,” she said. She added that Trump would decide when Iran no longer posed “a credible and direct threat.”

This sentence deserves a moment of careful reading.

Unconditional surrender has a specific historical meaning. It means the losing party lays down its arms, acknowledges defeat, and submits to the winner’s terms without conditions. It is by definition something the losing party does — not something the winning party declares on the losing party’s behalf. Leavitt has now redefined it as a unilateral declaration by Trump. Iran need not surrender. Iran need not agree to anything. Trump will decide when Iran has surrendered. There are no observable external markers — no signed document, no ceremony, no acknowledgment by the opposing party.

The war ends when Trump says it does.

This is the logical extension of the W-word strategy. If you don’t call it a war, you don’t have to define winning. If you don’t have to define winning, you don’t have to achieve it. You only have to declare it.

The shifting timeline is now on the public record in full. “Four to five weeks” became “two to eight weeks” became “whatever it takes” became “unconditional surrender” became “short excursion” became “very complete, pretty much” became “more determined than ever” became “soon, very soon” became “it’s not for me to posit whether it’s the beginning, the middle or the end.” These statements were made by the same administration over eleven days. No external event forced the changes. No new information arrived. The throttle moved because the throttle moves.

V. The Country That Helped Kill Americans

Monday was also the day the world learned that oil prices can be moved by a phone interview.

In the morning, Trump called CBS. The war, he said, was “very complete, pretty much” and “very far ahead of schedule.” Oil dropped. Markets rose. By afternoon, Brent crude had pulled back from Sunday night’s $119.50 peak to under $90. Then markets closed. That evening, to Republican lawmakers in Florida: “We have won in many ways, but not enough. We go forward more determined than ever to achieve ultimate victory.” Markets reversed. The war had not changed in the interval. Winning is whatever Trump says it is at the moment he is saying it, subject to revision within the same afternoon.

On Friday, Treasury Secretary Bessent issued a 30-day waiver allowing India to buy Russian oil already at sea without retaliation from Washington. Russia has been sharing satellite imagery of US warship positions with Iran since Day One. US officials have confirmed this. The intelligence has helped Iran target American forces. Seven Americans are dead. One hundred and forty are wounded.

The Russian economy had been under significant pressure. Oil and gas companies contributed 44 percent less to Russia’s federal budget in February than in the same month a year earlier. The sanctions architecture built over three years to pressure Moscow over Ukraine was beginning to bite. Then came the oil shock. And then came the waivers.

Bessent said the loosening was narrow. He then said the United States was “considering lifting more sanctions on Russian oil.” Trump on Monday said he would take sanctions off certain countries “until this straightens out.” Putin, in a meeting with Russian officials Monday, was direct: “It’s important for Russian energy companies to take advantage of the current situation.” He also floated halting all Russian energy supplies to Europe.

Janis Kluge of the German Institute for International and Security Affairs assessed the sanctions being unwound as “some of the most effective that the West had taken since the start of the war to pressure Moscow through energy revenues.” Then: “For now, it all evaporated, and the pressure is gone that was really starting to take a toll on the Russian budget.”

Trump’s Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff was asked directly Tuesday whether Russia was sharing intelligence with Iran. Russian leaders had told Trump they were not, he said. “We can take them at their word. Let’s hope that they’re not sharing.”

Let’s hope.

The country whose intelligence is helping kill Americans is having its sanctions lifted to lower US gas prices. Germany’s Chancellor Merz said Tuesday there was “no reason” to ease EU sanctions on Russia. Hungary’s Orbán asked the EU to suspend Russian energy sanctions the same day. The alliance is fracturing in both directions simultaneously.

VI. What Ukraine Is Owed

Volodymyr Zelensky confirmed Tuesday that Ukraine has sent professional, fully equipped drone-interception teams to Qatar, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia — in addition to teams already in Jordan protecting US military bases. Eleven countries have formally requested Ukrainian support.

When asked what Ukraine would receive in return, Zelensky said: “We are raising the issue of missiles for air-defense systems that we currently lack.”

The loop is now fully visible. Iran armed Russia with Shahed drones. Russia used them against Ukrainian cities, power plants, and hospitals. Ukraine spent two years developing the expertise to defeat them. The United States, now watching those same drones target its own soldiers, asked Ukraine for help. Ukraine sent its people. Ukraine is asking for missiles.

The administration lifting sanctions on Russian oil — the country whose Shahed supply chain enabled Iran’s drone arsenal — while asking Ukraine for drone expertise and declining to provide the missiles Ukraine needs to survive is a strategic posture that resists coherent description. The leverage is being applied carefully from Kyiv. “We are raising the issue.” The missiles have not arrived.

VII. Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington

JD Vance was at Dover Air Force Base Monday night when the seventh American killed in the war came home. Sgt. Benjamin N. Pennington, 26, of Glendale, Kentucky — assigned to the 1st Space Brigade at Fort Carson, Colorado — died Saturday night while military health officials were preparing to transfer him to Germany for more advanced care. He had been seriously injured March 1 in the Iranian strike on Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia.

The Pentagon said Tuesday that 140 US service members have been wounded in eleven days. One hundred and eight have returned to duty. Eight are severely injured and receiving the highest level of medical care.

Seven dead. One hundred and forty wounded. Eight severely.

VIII. The Prisons and the Bombs

There is a population in Iran that the war’s architects did not account for in their target sets: the political prisoners.

Since the nationwide protests in January, Iranian prisons swelled with citizens caught in a deadly crackdown. At least two places holding prisoners in Tehran have suffered moderate damage from nearby blasts. At least one detention center has been struck and heavily damaged, confirmed by videos and satellite imagery analysed by the New York Times.

Narges Mohammadi — the human rights activist and Nobel Peace Prize laureate, held at Evin prison — was moved to a prison in the northern town of Zanjan two weeks before the attacks began. Her husband, Taghi Rahmani, has had no information since. “We’ve entered a dark tunnel,” he said. “There’s no telling when we’ll come out of it.”

Ali Asadollahi, an award-winning Iranian poet, was arrested in a nighttime raid on his home roughly a month ago, no formal charges filed. His family went to the Revolutionary Court in Tehran to find his location. The court had been damaged in airstrikes. They do not know where he is.

Craig and Lindsay Foreman — a British couple detained last year during a motorcycle tour around the world — were held at Evin when a blast blew out the windows of the ward. Lindsay Foreman was on the phone with her son when it happened. She described “mass hysteria as women dived under metal bunk beds for cover, waiting for the next strike.”

Iran’s chief justice warned that “any act aligned with the enemy’s will” would be punished. Text messages sent to citizens urged them to report anyone sharing images “with the enemy.”

Shiva Amelirad — the Kurdish activist in Toronto who earlier in this series could not reach her teachers’ union members because the internet was down — named the predicament directly: “The prisoners are taking hits from the Islamic Republic on one side and from the U.S. and Israel on the other.”

No one has asked the administration how many political prisoners, dissidents, and foreign nationals are incarcerated in the facilities it is striking. No one has answered.

IX. Nothing Will Remain

Javad, a Tehran resident who asked to withhold his full name for fear of retaliation, described the overnight strikes Monday into Tuesday as among the worst since the war began. From 10 p.m. to past midnight, bombing could be heard “north, south, east and west.”

The Golestan Palace was damaged badly from an Israeli attack on a police station in Tehran last week.Credit...Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

“It seems they are striking everywhere: homes, schools, mosques, hospitals,” he said. High-voltage electricity lines hit. Gas and water infrastructure threatened. Cultural heritage sites in Isfahan damaged. The black rain that residents first reported after the oil depot strikes — the migraines, the film on the cars, the air that felt like tear gas — has not lifted.

“If they keep hitting Tehran like this for another ten days,” Javad said, “nothing will remain of Tehran.”

The Iranian sentiment is divided in a way that should unsettle anyone who assumes the bombing is building toward liberation. Some Iranians, Javad said, are “comfortable with the bombings — upset if there is a night without bombing, and fear the war might end while the regime remains.” Others are not. “Who decides who is an agent or a person of the Islamic republic? A traffic officer, an ordinary police officer, a criminal court, a family court — do these belong to the Islamic republic? I don’t think so. This is a very strange phase. It’s a human and moral catastrophe.”

Nearly 700,000 people have been displaced in Lebanon. The UN counted 100,000 new registrations in the past 24 hours. In Beirut’s shelters, people are fasting for Ramadan without food. “Yesterday they brought us chicken and rice without chicken,” said Zainab Ibrahim, displaced from Dahiya. The funerals for Iran’s top five military commanders will be held Wednesday. The war is eleven days old.

X. Day Twelve Begins

Chris Wright’s tweet is gone. The Strait is still closed.

The administration has told us the war is “very complete, pretty much” and that it goes forward “more determined than ever.” It has told us it’s a “short excursion” and that “ultimate victory” has not yet been achieved. It has told us Iran has surrendered unconditionally — and that Iran will surrender when Trump decides it has, which are not the same thing.

It has told us Russia is not sharing intelligence and that it hopes Russia is not sharing intelligence. It has lifted sanctions on the country whose satellite imagery is helping target American soldiers in order to lower gas prices at home. It has asked Ukraine for the expertise to defeat the Iranian drones Russia deployed against Ukrainian cities, and declined to provide the missiles Ukraine needs in return.

It has not called any of it a war.

Senator Kennedy stood alone Tuesday and said what the fragments already said: “We made a terrible, terrible mistake.” His party looked away.

Javad in Tehran gave it one word: “catastrophe.”

Day Twelve begins.

End of Day Eleven. Americans killed: 7. Americans wounded: 140; 8 severely. Iran dead: ~1,300. Lebanon dead: 570+; displaced: nearly 700,000. Russian oil sanctions: partially lifted; further loosening under consideration. Wright’s Strait escort tweet: posted, denied, deleted. Unconditional surrender: now defined as whatever Trump declares it to be. Nobel laureate Mohammadi: location unknown. Poet Asadollahi: location unknown. Funerals for Iran’s top five military commanders: Wednesday. The operation continues.
