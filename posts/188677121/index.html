Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 6: The Soviet Embrace — Cuba in the Cold War (1962–1975)

March 11, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

When the Cuban Missile Crisis ended in October 1962, Fidel Castro learned something important about the nature of the alliance he had entered. The Soviet Union and the United States had negotiated Cuba’s fate — the removal of Soviet missiles in exchange for American pledges and the secret withdrawal of American Jupiter missiles from Turkey — without consulting him. Castro found out about the settlement the same way everyone else did: by listening to the radio. He was furious. He had been willing to die in a nuclear exchange. He had not been asked whether he was willing to survive a diplomatic compromise he had no hand in shaping.

The Missile Crisis is usually told as a story about the two superpowers. For Cuba, it was a story about what it meant to be a small country in a world managed by large ones — a lesson Cuba had been learning since 1898 but had briefly dared to think the Revolution had changed. It hadn’t. The Revolution had simply changed who was making the decisions about Cuba’s fate. It had not changed the fact that those decisions were being made in Washington and Moscow, not in Havana.

Castro’s response to this humiliation was characteristically defiant. If Cuba was to be a Soviet ally — and the economic realities left him little choice — it would be an ally on its own terms. Through the 1960s, Cuba and the Soviet Union would maintain a relationship of mutual dependency laced with genuine ideological and strategic tension: Cuba needed Soviet oil, Soviet money, and Soviet military protection; the Soviets needed Cuba’s Caribbean position and the propaganda value of a successful socialist state ninety miles from the American coast. Neither side was entirely happy with the arrangement. Neither felt it could afford to end it.

The Structure of Dependency

Fidel and Raúl Castro meet Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev, early 1960s. The alliance that would subsidize Cuba for three decades was being built — on Soviet terms, at Soviet convenience, as Cuba would discover during the Missile Crisis when Khrushchev settled the standoff without consulting Havana.

The Soviet-Cuban economic relationship that emerged after the Missile Crisis was, in material terms, extraordinarily generous. Moscow guaranteed to purchase Cuban sugar at prices well above world market levels — a subsidy that, at its peak in the late 1970s and 1980s, was transferring several billion dollars per year to the Cuban economy. In exchange, the Soviet Union supplied Cuba with oil at prices far below what the international market would have charged, a second form of subsidy that financed not just Cuba’s energy needs but, through the resale of surplus Soviet oil on international markets, a significant portion of Cuba’s hard currency earnings.

The effect was to make Cuba economically viable in ways that its own productive capacity could not have supported. Cuba’s sugar industry, the backbone of its economy since the seventeenth century, was inefficient and increasingly uncompetitive on world markets. Without preferential Soviet prices, the Cuban economy would have contracted severely. With them, it survived — but at the cost of a dependency that made Cuba’s fate contingent on decisions made in Moscow, subject to revision whenever Soviet priorities changed.

This dependency shaped everything. It shaped the political relationship: Cuba could not afford to break with the Soviet Union, and the Soviets knew it. It shaped domestic economic policy: there was little incentive to undertake the painful reforms that might have reduced dependency when Soviet subsidies made the status quo tolerable. And it shaped the psychology of the revolution itself — the awareness, never quite spoken directly, that the independence Cuba had fought for was qualified, that the island’s sovereignty was real but not unconditional, underwritten by a patron whose continued support required a degree of alignment Cuba would not always have chosen freely.

Soviet-era Ladas (turquoise and red) driving in 1970s Havana—everyday symbol of economic dependency and cultural shift.

For ordinary Cubans, the Soviet relationship meant Soviet goods: Ladas and Volgas on the streets, Bulgarian canned goods on the shelves, Soviet advisers in the ministries and the military academies. Cuba joined COMECON, the Soviet bloc’s economic integration framework, in 1972. The island that had been an American economic colony had become, structurally if not formally, a Soviet one. The revolution’s nationalist promise — Cuba for Cubans, the island’s wealth for its people — had been partially redeemed in the redistribution of land and the expansion of education and healthcare, but had been substantially compromised in the matter of economic sovereignty.

The Che Question: Cuba Against Moscow

Che Guevara, his daughter Aleida and Fidel Castro, in Havana. Photograph: Imagno/Getty Images

The tension in the Soviet-Cuban relationship was most visible in the question of revolutionary solidarity. Castro and his inner circle — particularly Ernesto Che Guevara, the Argentine physician who had become the Revolution’s most iconic figure — were committed to the idea that the Cuban Revolution should serve as a model and a catalyst for liberation movements across Latin America and the Third World. Moscow, engaged in détente with the United States and wary of adventures that might destabilize the delicate balance of Cold War competition, was considerably less enthusiastic.

Guevara represented the romantic, internationalist wing of the Revolution — the belief that guerrilla warfare could replicate the Cuban experience across the continent, that a small determined force could ignite a continental insurrection that would break American dominance of the hemisphere. He left Cuba in 1965 to pursue this vision, first in the Congo, where the attempt was a failure, and then in Bolivia, where it was a disaster. Guevara was captured by Bolivian forces — aided by the CIA — and executed on October 9, 1967. He was thirty-nine years old.

His death was a moment of genuine grief in Cuba and a political turning point. The failure of the Bolivian campaign discredited the foco theory of guerrilla revolution that Guevara and the theorist Régis Debray had elaborated — the idea that a small armed group could serve as the spark for mass insurrection without extensive prior political organization. Cuba’s foreign policy ambitions would have to find different vehicles. The Soviet Union, relieved at the quieting of Cuban adventurism in its most destabilizing form, began to move the relationship onto more comfortable ground.

The accommodation with Moscow was gradual and never complete. Cuba continued to support liberation movements — in Angola, in Ethiopia, in Central America — in ways that sometimes exceeded Soviet preferences. But the ideological romanticism of the 1960s gave way, in the 1970s, to something more institutional and more cautious: a Cuba that had internalized the disciplines of Soviet-style governance while preserving a distinctly Cuban voice on the world stage.

The Revolution Turns Inward: Repression and the Cost of Control

The consolidation of the Revolution as a one-party state in the 1960s required not just the suppression of political opposition but the enforcement of a social conformity that extended well beyond politics. The costs of that enforcement fell on groups that had little connection to counter-revolutionary activity — and the history of what was done to them is part of the honest accounting this series has promised.

The most systematic persecution was directed at gay men, who were classified as ideologically unreliable on the grounds that homosexuality was incompatible with the Revolution’s vision of the new Cuban man — disciplined, masculine, collectively oriented, untainted by the perceived decadence of bourgeois culture. The UMAP camps — Units to Aid Military Production — operated from 1965 to 1968 and imprisoned not only gay men but Jehovah’s Witnesses, practitioners of Afro-Cuban religions, Catholic priests, and others deemed socially deviant or religiously unreliable. The conditions in the camps were harsh, the work forced, the psychological damage lasting.

The UMAP camps were eventually closed after international protest, including from sympathetic foreign leftists whose support Castro could not afford to alienate. But the persecution of gay Cubans continued through other means — social exclusion, professional discrimination, periodic campaigns of public shaming — for decades afterward. The Revolution that had promised to liberate Cuba from class exploitation had imposed its own forms of exclusion on citizens whose only crime was existing differently.

UMAP camp photo—prisoners grouped under thatched roof

Religious believers faced a different but related set of pressures. Cuba was declared an atheist state, and while outright religious persecution was less severe than in some Soviet bloc countries, practicing Christians, practitioners of Santería and other Afro-Cuban traditions, and religious Jews all faced discrimination in employment and education. The revolutionary state regarded religion as a competing source of social authority and ideological allegiance, and it treated that competition with consistent hostility until the constitutional reforms of the 1990s formally ended official atheism.

Intellectuals and artists occupied a more ambiguous space, defined by Castro’s famous formulation from 1961: “Within the Revolution, everything; outside the Revolution, nothing.” The phrase sounded generous, but its limits were determined by the state, and those limits contracted and expanded unpredictably. The Padilla Affair of 1971 — in which the poet Heberto Padilla was arrested, subjected to coerced “self-criticism” in a spectacle that horrified foreign sympathizers, and then held under restrictions for years — marked the moment when many Western leftist intellectuals who had supported the Revolution began to maintain a more critical distance. Jean-Paul Sartre, Gabriel García Márquez, Mario Vargas Llosa, and others who had been enthusiastic supporters now found themselves making more complicated calculations.

Institutionalization: The Revolution Grows Up

By the early 1970s, the Cuba that had come into being in the heat of revolutionary transformation was settling into something more permanent and more bureaucratic. The Communist Party of Cuba, formally established in 1965, developed the institutional apparatus of a governing party rather than a revolutionary movement. Ministries regularized their procedures. The military, shaped by Soviet training and Soviet doctrine, became a professional force rather than a guerrilla army. COMECON membership tied Cuban economic planning to Soviet templates.

In 1976, Cuba adopted a new constitution — its first since the revolutionary government had set aside the 1940 constitution — that formalized the one-party system, established a National Assembly as the nominal legislative body, and enshrined the leading role of the Communist Party as a constitutional fact. The same year, municipal and provincial assemblies were established for the first time, creating at least the form of representative institutions within the single-party framework. Cubans voted; there was only one party and candidates were selected by Party-controlled processes, but the rituals of mass participation were established and taken seriously by the government as a source of popular legitimacy.

The institutionalization of the Revolution coincided with Cuba’s emergence as a genuine international actor. In 1975, Cuba committed military forces to Angola in support of the MPLA government against South African-backed insurgents — an intervention that would define the next decade of Cuban foreign policy and is the subject of the following part of this series. The decision to intervene in Angola was, in retrospect, the moment when Cuba demonstrated that it was not simply a Soviet satellite executing Moscow’s instructions but a country with its own foreign policy interests and its own capacity to project military power in pursuit of them.

That demonstration would reshape how the world understood the Cuban Revolution — and it would set the stage for the complicated final decade of the Soviet relationship, as Gorbachev’s reforms began to unravel the economic foundations on which Cuba’s survival depended.

What These Years Made

The period from 1962 to 1975 was not simply the years of Soviet dependency. It was the years in which Cuba’s revolutionary identity hardened into something durable: a culture of resistance, a set of institutions capable of governing a complex society, a healthcare and education system that genuinely served its citizens, and a foreign policy voice that commanded respect in parts of the world that had little interest in American Cold War frameworks.

It was also the years in which the Revolution’s authoritarianism became structural rather than transitional. The suppression of dissent, the persecution of homosexuals and believers, the control of intellectual life — these were not emergency measures to be lifted when the emergency passed. They were the permanent operating conditions of Cuban society. The people who suffered under them had not chosen them. And the external pressure that provided justification for them — the American embargo, the CIA operations, the continued existence of a hostile superpower ninety miles away — did not create them. It sustained them.

Both things remain true, together, in the accounting of what these years cost the Cuban people.
