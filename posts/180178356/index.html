The Charlie Kirk Debates

#11: Seven Mountains Dominionism - Part I: The Case

December 2, 2025

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to scriptural light. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to your embrace of Seven Mountains Dominionism. At CPAC 2020, you declared: “Finally we have a president that understands the seven mountains of cultural influence.” In 2021, you told a church congregation that Christians must “occupy until I come”—quoting the Seven Mountains battle cry. You’ve partnered with Lance Wallnau, the movement’s founder whom scholars call “the father of American Dominionism.” Prove me wrong that this isn’t a blueprint for theocratic takeover.

Kirk’s Embrace of Seven Mountains Theology

Charlie Kirk’s transformation from secular conservative to Christian nationalist found its theological anchor in the Seven Mountains Mandate—a dominionist ideology teaching that Christians must take authoritative control over seven key sectors of society: government, education, media, arts and entertainment, family, religion, and business.

What Is Seven Mountains Dominionism?

The Seven Mountains Mandate (7M or 7MM) originated in the 1970s when three evangelical leaders—Loren Cunningham (founder of Youth With A Mission), Bill Bright (Campus Crusade for Christ), and Francis Schaeffer—claimed to receive divine revelation about seven spheres of cultural influence that Christians needed to engage.

Initially, Cunningham framed these spheres as areas for evangelism and discipleship—spreading the Gospel by engaging culture. But in 2000, Lance Wallnau, a charismatic pastor and self-proclaimed prophet, met with Cunningham and transformed the concept. Wallnau changed the metaphor from “spheres” to “mountains”—and the mission from evangelism to dominion.

The 2013 publication of Wallnau’s book “Invading Babylon: The 7 Mountain Mandate” (co-authored with Bill Johnson of Bethel Church) crystallized this shift. The goal was no longer just influence—it was conquest. Christians weren’t meant to be salt and light in culture; they were meant to occupy and control it.

Kirk’s Public Endorsement

Charlie Kirk announced his allegiance to Seven Mountains theology publicly and repeatedly:

At CPAC 2020:

“Finally we have a president that understands the seven mountains of cultural influence.”

Kirk praised Trump for understanding this dominionist framework—the idea that Christians must strategically capture control of society’s power centers.

At a Washington State Church (2021): Shortly after launching TPUSA Faith, Kirk told a congregation:

“The Bible says very clearly, to ‘Occupy until I come.’”

This quote from Luke 19:13—a favorite of Seven Mountains adherents—became Kirk’s call to action. Christians weren’t meant to wait for Christ’s return through evangelism. They were meant to establish dominion over earthly institutions until He arrived.

Kirk’s Partnership with Lance Wallnau

Kirk didn’t just endorse Seven Mountains theology—he partnered with its architect. Lance Wallnau, whom religious scholar Matthew Taylor identified as “the father of American Dominionism,” became Kirk’s close collaborator.

In a February interview on Wallnau’s podcast, Kirk announced “Project 81”—an initiative to rally church leaders in swing states to push evangelical Trump support above 81 percent. Kirk told Wallnau’s audience:

“Hard stop, there is not a single biblical issue guided by God’s law that the Democrat Party embraces.”

During the 2024 campaign, Kirk and Wallnau toured together on the “Courage Tour,” holding swing-state events where religious revival and political rally became indistinguishable. Wallnau called Kirk “the face of Christian Nationalism.”

Rob McCoy: The Seven Mountains Introduction

According to scholar Matthew Boedy’s research, California megachurch pastor Rob McCoy introduced Kirk to Seven Mountains theology in 2019. McCoy, both a pastor and local officeholder, embodied the Seven Mountains vision of Christians simultaneously controlling religious and governmental “mountains.”

In 2021, Kirk and McCoy co-founded Turning Point Faith, explicitly targeting pastors and congregations with Seven Mountains messaging. This branch transformed Turning Point USA from a youth political organization into what Boedy calls “the indispensable organization for the seven mountains movement.”

Turning Point’s Seven Mountains Transformation

After embracing Seven Mountains theology, Turning Point exploded in scope and funding:

  • Budget growth: Turning Point raised roughly a quarter-billion dollars since 2016

  • Expanded infrastructure: Staff in every major city reaching all ages

  • Enhanced media: Vastly expanded content creation capabilities

  • Targeted outreach: Women’s conferences, men’s summits, pastor mobilization

  • Political apparatus: Turning Point Action running multimillion-dollar canvassing campaigns

Kirk remade his national organization to align with Seven Mountains goals—capturing each “mountain” through strategic positioning of Christians in positions of power.

The Seven Mountains Vision

Kirk promoted the specific Seven Mountains theology:

The Seven Mountains:

  1. Government: Christians must control political power

  2. Education: Christians must dominate schools and universities

  3. Media: Christians must control news and information

  4. Arts and Entertainment: Christians must shape culture through film, music, and art

  5. Family: Christians must enforce traditional family structures

  6. Religion: Christians must unify churches under dominionist theology

  7. Business: Christians must lead corporations and economic institutions

The Method: Each mountain currently has demonic forces controlling it (according to Seven Mountains teachers like Johnny Enlow). Christians must engage in “spiritual warfare” to vanquish these demons and install Christian leaders “atop” each mountain.

The Goal: Establish Christian dominion over society so completely that God’s kingdom is manifested on earth, paving the way for Christ’s return. Jesus cannot or will not return until Christians have first taken dominion.

Kirk’s Spiritual Warfare Language

Kirk adopted the movement’s spiritual warfare rhetoric. Speaking at an Arizona megachurch in March 2024, he told followers:

“There are demons that are at work... This is a spiritual war first and foremost.”

He described politics not as policy disagreement but as cosmic battle against demonic powers. The 2024 election wasn’t a choice between candidates—it was spiritual warfare where one side represented God and the other Satan.

The “Occupy” Theology

Kirk’s use of “Occupy until I come” reveals Seven Mountains’ theological core. The passage (Luke 19:13) comes from Jesus’s Parable of the Ten Minas, where a nobleman tells his servants to “do business” or “occupy” until he returns.

Seven Mountains teachers interpret this as a divine command for Christians to take control of earthly institutions during Christ’s absence. They apply Old Testament conquest narratives (like Israel’s battles in Deuteronomy 7) to modern cultural warfare. The seven mountains correlate to the seven nations Israel was commanded to conquer in Canaan.

The New Apostolic Reformation Connection

Seven Mountains Dominionism sits at the heart of the New Apostolic Reformation (NAR)—a charismatic Christian movement emphasizing modern-day apostles and prophets with authority to transform society.

NAR teaches that God has restored the office of apostle in these end times. These apostles, along with prophets, will lead the church to take dominion over nations before Christ’s return. Prayer, prophecy, and worship aren’t merely devotional—they’re strategic tools for advancing Christians’ vision of government and society.

Scholar Matthew Taylor’s book “The Violent Take It By Force” documents how NAR leaders train followers for active confrontation to reclaim the seven mountains through influence, leadership, and “if necessary, the use of force.”

Approximately 3 million Americans attend churches openly part of NAR, but its influence extends much wider through social media, podcasts, livestreams, and revival meetings. A 2024 poll indicated 41 percent of American Christians believe in Seven Mountains theology.

Kirk’s Role in Mainstreaming Dominionism

Before Kirk, Seven Mountains theology remained largely confined to charismatic and Pentecostal circles. Kirk brought it into mainstream conservative politics.

As scholar Matthew Boedy argues, Kirk made Seven Mountains “the central organizing element of the Trump era,” bringing dominionist theology “to the precipice of the destruction of our democracy.”

Kirk’s proximity to power, Turning Point’s wealth, and his ability to influence millions made him the perfect vehicle for Seven Mountains’ political ambitions. He translated theological language into political action, mobilizing evangelicals not just to vote but to see themselves as warriors in a cosmic battle to control earthly institutions.

The Turning Point Statement Denying Seven Mountains

After NBC News reported Kirk’s Seven Mountains connections in June 2024, Turning Point issued a statement attempting to distance him from the theology:

“Charlie probably couldn’t tell you what the seven mountains are. Charlie speaks at churches that are focused on those ideas, and others that wouldn’t even know what you are talking about.”

This denial came despite Kirk’s repeated public endorsements of Seven Mountains theology, his partnership with its founder Lance Wallnau, his use of “Occupy until I come” rhetoric, and his transformation of Turning Point USA to align with Seven Mountains goals.

The statement exemplified a pattern: using Seven Mountains language and partnerships while maintaining plausible deniability about the theology’s radical implications.
