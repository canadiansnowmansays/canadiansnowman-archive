Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Part 8: The Soviet Catastrophe (1978-1989)

April 8, 2026

Soviet armoured columns in Kabul, December 1979. The invasion that Moscow expected to last weeks lasted ten years, killed over a million Afghans, and produced the largest refugee crisis in the world AP Archive / Getty Images Historical

Opening: The Wound That Never Healed

On December 27, 1979, Soviet special forces stormed the Tajbeg Palace in Kabul and killed Afghanistan’s communist leader, Hafizullah Amin. Simultaneously, Soviet airborne troops secured Kabul’s airport. Within days, Soviet ground forces crossed from Central Asia.

By early January 1980, over 30,000 Soviet troops occupied Afghanistan. That number would eventually reach 115,000.

Soviet planners assumed this would be brief - a few months to stabilize the situation, install a reliable government, and withdraw most forces.

They would stay for ten years.

In that decade, the Soviets would:

  • Kill over one million Afghans

  • Create five million refugees

  • Plant millions of landmines

  • Destroy thousands of villages

  • Demolish irrigation systems

  • Devastate what 60 years of peace had built

And they would fail. The pattern that defeated Alexander, the Mongols, and the British would defeat the Soviets too.

That woman in Badgor, that closed clinic in Nalej, that three-month-old Zakia dying from preventable malnutrition - they live in a country still recovering from Soviet devastation. Some of those landmines mentioned in the New York Times article are Soviet. Some of those destroyed irrigation systems were demolished by Soviet forces. The trauma runs deep.

This is the story of how a superpower with modern weapons, socialist ideology, and knowledge of British failures would learn the same lesson - and leave Afghanistan worse than they found it.

The Soviet Invasion: Operation Storm-333 (December 1979)

The Soviet army in Kabul, Afghanistan, on Dec. 31, 1979. Francois Lochon/Gamma-Rapho/Getty Images

The Execution

On December 27, 1979, Soviet Spetsnaz (special forces) and KGB operatives attacked the Tajbeg Palace. Afghan guards resisted. In the firefight, Hafizullah Amin was killed.

Soviet propaganda later claimed Amin invited the intervention. The truth: They assassinated him.

At the same time:

  • Airborne troops secured Kabul airport

  • Key government buildings seized

  • Communications centers captured

  • Afghan military units neutralized or co-opted

Within 72 hours, the Soviets controlled Kabul and installed Babrak Karmal as the new leader.

The Justification

The Soviets claimed they were:

  • Responding to Afghan government’s invitation (false - they’d killed that government)

  • Defending socialism against imperialism

  • Preventing American interference

  • Supporting a fraternal socialist state

The reality: They feared losing a client state on their southern border and facing an Islamic fundamentalist regime that might inspire Soviet Central Asian Muslims.

Initial Optimism

Soviet planners believed:

  • Superior firepower would suppress resistance quickly

  • Modern military doctrine would succeed where British colonialism failed

  • Socialist ideology would appeal to poor Afghans

  • A few months of operations, then withdrawal

They had studied British disasters. They assumed Soviet superiority - technological, ideological, organizational - made them different.

They were catastrophically wrong.

The Mujahideen Resistance

Almost immediately, Afghan resistance coalesced.

The mujahideen (”those who engage in jihad”) had several advantages:

1. Terrain: The same mountains that defeated Alexander and the British 2. Popular support: Most rural Afghans saw Soviets as atheist invaders 3. External support: US, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, China poured weapons and money 4. Motivation: Jihad against godless communists 5. Time: They could wait; Soviets couldn’t

The mujahideen were divided into multiple factions - often fighting each other as much as Soviets. But they united against foreign occupation.

The Factions:

  • Hezb-i-Islami (Gulbuddin Hekmatyar) - radical, Pashtun

  • Jamiat-i-Islami (Ahmad Shah Massoud) - moderate, Tajik

  • Various other groups based on ethnicity, region, and ideology

Despite divisions, they shared one goal: Expel the invaders.

Soviet Tactics: The Brutality Escalates

Unable to control the countryside, the Soviets resorted to increasingly brutal tactics.

Aerial Bombardment:

  • Helicopter gunships attacking villages

  • High-altitude bombing of rural areas

  • Napalm and chemical weapons (including toxins)

  • Carpet bombing of suspected mujahideen areas

“Migratory Genocide”:

  • Systematic village destruction

  • Demolition of irrigation systems (echoes of the Mongols)

  • Slaughter of livestock

  • Mining of agricultural areas

  • Goal: Depopulate rural areas supporting mujahideen

Terror Tactics:

  • Mass executions

  • Torture

  • Rape as weapon of war

  • “Butterfly mines” - anti-personnel mines disguised as toys to maim children

  • Booby-trapped objects left in villages

The Convoy System

Soviet forces moved in heavily-armed convoys through mountain passes. Robert Fisk’s account in ‘The Great War for Civilisation’ captures the experience.

Embedded (accidentally) with a Soviet convoy in the Hindu Kush, Fisk describes young soldiers:

  • Fascinated by Western pop music (”Is it true Paul McCartney was arrested in Tokyo?”)

  • Scanning ridges for gunmen

  • Nervous about ambushes

  • Just wanting to survive and go home

These weren’t the iron warriors of Stalingrad. They were conscripts - many from Soviet Central Asia (Uzbeks, Tajiks, Kazakhs), fellow Muslims forced to fight Afghans.

The Ambush Pattern

Fisk describes his convoy being attacked:

“There was a series of sharp reports as their Kalashnikovs kicked into their shoulders... A silence fell over the landscape. Some figures moved, far away to our left, next to a dead tree... gradually the famous Mongol discipline began to come apart.”

Mujahideen would:

  1. Wait for convoys in mountain passes

  2. Attack from heights with RPGs and heavy weapons

  3. Target lead and rear vehicles to trap convoy

  4. Inflict casualties and withdraw before Soviet air support arrived

  5. Repeat daily

Soviet convoys bled continuously. No route was safe.

The Human Cost

Afghan refugees are shown in a camp on Kohat Road outside of Peshawar, Pakistan, in 1980. Peter Bregg/AP

The Soviet war’s toll was devastating for all sides.

Afghan Casualties (estimates vary widely):

  • 1-2 million killed

  • 5-6 million refugees (fled to Pakistan and Iran)

  • 2 million internally displaced

  • Countless wounded and maimed

In a country of 15-16 million, roughly half the population was killed, displaced, or driven into exile.

Soviet Casualties:

  • 15,000+ killed (official Soviet figures, likely understated)

  • 35,000+ wounded

  • Thousands more from disease, accidents, drug addiction

  • Psychological trauma affecting hundreds of thousands

Infrastructure Destruction:

  • Thousands of villages destroyed

  • Irrigation systems demolished

  • Roads mined and cratered

  • Hospitals and schools bombed

  • 10-15 million landmines planted (various estimates)

The Particular Cruelty of Landmines

Those landmines are still killing Afghans today. The New York Times article mentions international aid “once helped clear landscapes scarred by war and mines.”

Soviet mines included:

  • Anti-personnel mines disguised as everyday objects

  • “Butterfly mines” designed to maim rather than kill

  • Mines scattered by aircraft across vast areas

  • No maps, no records of where they were placed

Forty-plus years later, Afghan children still die stepping on Soviet mines.

The Stinger Missiles: Turning Point (1986)

An Afghan guerrilla handles a U.S.-made Stinger anti-aircraft missile. David Stewart Smith/AP

Until 1986, Soviet helicopters dominated Afghan skies. The Mi-24 “Hind” gunship could devastate mujahideen positions with impunity.

Mujahideen had no effective anti-aircraft weapons. They shot at helicopters with rifles and heavy machine guns - rarely effective.

The Game Changer

In 1986, the CIA provided FIM-92 Stinger missiles:

  • Shoulder-fired, portable

  • Heat-seeking, fire-and-forget

  • Extremely effective against helicopters and low-flying aircraft

  • Easy to use with minimal training

Suddenly, Soviet air power became vulnerable. Helicopters were shot down regularly. Pilots flew higher, reducing effectiveness. Casualties mounted.

The Stingers didn’t win the war, but they changed its calculus: Costs rising, victory remaining elusive.

The Soviet Vietnam

By mid-1980s, parallels to America’s Vietnam were obvious:

  • No clear military victory possible

  • Mounting casualties

  • Growing domestic opposition (carefully suppressed)

  • Economic drain

  • International condemnation

  • Demoralization of troops

Soviet mothers began organizing to demand their sons come home. “Cargo 200” - the code for bodies returned in zinc coffins - became a dreaded phrase.

The war was unwinnable. Everyone knew it except the Kremlin hardliners.

Gorbachev and Withdrawal

When Mikhail Gorbachev came to power in 1985, he inherited an unwinnable war draining Soviet resources.

By 1986, he called it a “bleeding wound.”

In 1988, Gorbachev announced Soviet withdrawal. The process began in May 1988 and completed in February 1989.

The Final Departure

The last Soviet commander to leave, General Boris Gromov, walked across the Friendship Bridge into Soviet Uzbekistan on February 15, 1989.

Gromov’s assessment echoes through history:

“Alexander the Great couldn’t do it, the British couldn’t do it, we couldn’t do it, and the Americans won’t be able to do it.”

What the Soviets Destroyed

The Soviet invasion didn’t just kill people - it destroyed Afghanistan’s future.

The Physical Destruction

Remember the irrigation systems (qanats) that took centuries to build? The ones the Mongols destroyed in the 13th century? Some had been rebuilt during the golden age.

The Soviets destroyed them again. Systematically. As policy.

Villages that had stood for generations - rubble. The New York Times mentions “toppled anti-blast walls” - some are Soviet-era. The landmines killing children - many are Soviet.

The Social Destruction

The 1960s-70s Afghan middle class - doctors, engineers, teachers, civil servants - was decimated:

  • Many fled as refugees

  • Those who stayed were caught between communists and mujahideen

  • The educated urban class that had driven modernization largely disappeared

An entire generation grew up in refugee camps in Pakistan and Iran:

  • Educated in madrassas rather than secular schools

  • Radicalized by jihadist ideology

  • Knowing only war, not the golden age

The Ideological Destruction

The Afghan communists’ attacks on Islam, combined with Soviet atheist invasion, created a powerful jihadist narrative:

“Afghanistan is where Islam defeated godless communism”

This narrative attracted foreign fighters - Arabs, Chechens, Uzbeks - who came to fight Soviets. Among them: Osama bin Laden.

The networks, training camps, and ideology developed fighting Soviets would form the basis of global jihadism and lead directly to 9/11.

The Institutional Destruction

Whatever weak institutions Afghanistan built during the golden age - universities, hospitals, government bureaucracy - were shattered.

The Afghan state effectively ceased to exist outside Kabul. Warlords and mujahideen commanders controlled territory through force.

When Soviets left, they left behind:

  • A country in ruins

  • Competing armed factions with no interest in compromise

  • Massive weapons stockpiles

  • A generation knowing only war

  • No functional government

The Civil War Follows (1989-1996)

When Soviets withdrew in 1989, the communist government of Mohammad Najibullah held on until 1992 - longer than most expected.

When it fell, mujahideen factions turned on each other.

The Devastation of Kabul

Kabul - which survived Soviet occupation relatively intact - was destroyed in factional civil war.

Different mujahideen factions fought for control:

  • Massoud’s Tajik forces

  • Hekmatyar’s Pashtun forces

  • Dostum’s Uzbek militia

  • Various other factions

They shelled each other across Kabul. Rockets and artillery reduced neighborhoods to rubble. Tens of thousands died.

The city that had hosted universities and cinemas became a wasteland.

The Taliban Rise from the Ashes (1994-1996)

Out of the refugee camps in Pakistan emerged the Taliban - young men educated in Pakistani madrassas, orphans who’d grown up knowing only war and radical ideology.

Led by Mullah Omar, they promised:

  • Order instead of chaos

  • Islamic law instead of warlord rule

  • Security instead of violence

In 1994-95, they swept through southern Afghanistan. Many Afghans, exhausted by civil war, initially welcomed them.

By September 1996, they captured Kabul. Their first act: Drag the former communist president Najibullah from UN sanctuary, torture him, castrate him, and hang his body from a traffic post.

They brought order - but at terrible price:

  • Women banned from work and education

  • Extreme interpretation of Islamic law

  • Public executions

  • Music, television banned

But after years of chaos, many Afghans accepted it. Better predictable oppression than unpredictable violence.

And in 1996, Osama bin Laden returned to Afghanistan, finding sanctuary under Taliban protection.

The stage was set for 9/11 and the American invasion.

What the Soviet Period Teaches

1. Studying History Doesn’t Prevent Repeating It

The Soviets had complete access to British records. They knew about three disastrous British wars. They studied British failures in detail.

They invaded anyway, assuming their system, military doctrine, and lack of colonial ambitions made them different.

The pattern held. Mountains remained. Afghan resistance persisted. Soviets left defeated.

2. Brutality Doesn’t Bring Victory

The Soviets were far more brutal than the British:

  • Chemical weapons

  • Systematic village destruction

  • Millions of landmines

  • “Migratory genocide” tactics

It didn’t work. It created more resistance, more refugees, more international support for mujahideen.

3. Occupation Requires Legitimacy

The communist government had no legitimacy outside cities. Soviet support made it even less legitimate.

Without legitimacy, occupation requires permanent military force at unsustainable cost - the same lesson the British learned.

4. Costs Always Exceed Expectations

Soviet planners thought: Brief intervention, stabilize, withdraw.

Reality: Ten years, 15,000+ dead, economic drain contributing to USSR’s collapse, creation of global jihadist networks.

The costs weren’t just financial and military - they were strategic and civilizational.

5. Destroying Is Easier Than Building

The golden age took 60 years to build. The Soviets destroyed it in 10.

Those closed health clinics in 2026? Some are in Soviet-bombed buildings. Those irrigation systems that can’t be repaired? Some were deliberately destroyed by Soviet forces. Those landmines killing children? Many are Soviet.

6. The Afghan Pattern Is Eternal

For 2,500 years, every empire learned the same lessons. The Soviets, despite having that entire history available, learned nothing until they experienced it themselves.

The Bridge to America

When the Taliban provided sanctuary to Osama bin Laden and al-Qaeda, they set in motion events bringing yet another superpower to Afghanistan.

The Soviet invasion had:

  • Destroyed Afghanistan’s modernization

  • Created millions of refugees

  • Radicalized a generation

  • Established jihadist training camps and networks

  • Provided ideological justification for global jihad

  • Demonstrated a superpower could be defeated

From this crucible emerged al-Qaeda. From al-Qaeda’s Afghan sanctuary came 9/11. From 9/11 came American invasion.

That three-month-old Zakia - her grandmother lived through Soviet devastation. Her mother was born during Taliban rule. She herself was born into American occupation.

Three generations. Each knowing completely different Afghanistan. Each suffering from decisions made by distant empires.

In Part 9, we’ll trace how America - with access to both British and Soviet history - would spend 20 years and $2 trillion to repeat every mistake.

And in Part 10, how America would then cut the aid millions depended on, causing those children to starve.

The pattern never changes. Empires never learn. Afghans always pay the price.
