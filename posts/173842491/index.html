Charlie Kirk

America's Response

Beyond Partisan Targeting: A Comprehensive Violence Prevention Approach

September 17, 2025

As I write yet another article trying to promote unity and healing in the United States, I find myself literally in tears as I watch in horror the rhetoric becoming more divisive by the hour. I'm in tears not just for the terrible assassination of Charlie Kirk—a beloved conservative figure whose life was cut short by senseless violence—but also because America seems increasingly lost in hatred for each other. Where are the sane voices of reason?

I wrote the original analysis of Charlie Kirk's assassination last week, in the immediate aftermath of that terrible tragedy. Like many people, I felt compelled to try making sense of what had happened and what it might mean for American democracy.

Then I stepped back. I largely set aside writing more or commenting much online, watching as social media became flooded with exactly the kind of rhetoric I had feared—and frankly, I felt overwhelmed by it all. I had been trying to focus on a path forward that included ways to heal rather than further divide the country.

I expected the rhetoric to get worse in the days following Kirk's death, but I didn't expect it to get this bad, this fast. What has really disappointed me is that I don't see others promoting a constructive path forward—possibly because any reasonable voices are simply getting drowned out by the negative rhetoric that dominates our feeds and airwaves.

I'm particularly disappointed in the Administration's response, especially after witnessing JD Vance and Stephen Miller's escalating inflammatory rhetoric yesterday. Their approach seems designed to pour gasoline on an already dangerous fire rather than provide the leadership America desperately needs.

So I find myself asking: Where are the sane voices? Please don't give up. America needs people willing to choose evidence over ideology, healing over division, and solutions over blame. The stakes are too high to let the loudest and most extreme voices be the only ones heard.

Remember the voices of reason from the past when tragedy struck America. Lincoln after the Civil War: "With malice toward none, with charity for all." Reagan after his assassination attempt: choosing grace over grievance, refusing to blame political opponents for a lone gunman's actions. Bush after 9/11: "Islam is peace"—protecting American Muslims from backlash rather than exploiting tragedy for political targeting. Obama after Charleston: leading the country in "Amazing Grace" rather than vengeance. These leaders understood that tragedy calls us to our better angels, not our worst impulses.

Where are those voices today?

When Prevention Becomes Persecution: The Dangerous Path America is Taking

In the days following Charlie Kirk's assassination, America faced a choice: use this tragedy to examine how political violence develops and spreads, or weaponize it for partisan advantage. The evidence suggests the country is choosing the latter path—one that not only dishonors Kirk's memory but actively undermines efforts to prevent future violence.

A snapshot of my social media feed from just the past 30 minutes reveals the alarming speed with which this tragedy has been transformed into a political weapon rather than a call for serious violence prevention.

The Weaponization in Real Time

The response from Trump administration officials has been swift and concerning. Rather than focusing on the specific factors that led Tyler Robinson to commit this act—his online radicalization, his antifascist ideology, his apparent mental health struggles—the administration is using Kirk's death to justify broad targeting of political opponents.

Attorney General Pamela Bondi announced sweeping plans to prosecute "hate speech" and target "radical left" groups, writing: "For far too long, we've watched the radical left normalize threats, call for assassinations, and cheer on political violence. That era is over." This represents a fundamental misunderstanding of both the First Amendment and effective violence prevention strategies.

Vice President JD Vance claimed that "most of the lunatics in American politics today are proud members of the far-Left" and suggested that "when you see someone celebrating Charlie's murder…call their employer." This approach—targeting employment and livelihoods based on political speech—represents exactly the kind of authoritarian overreach that undermines democratic discourse.

Eric Trump spoke ominously of "they" who "tried to do it to my father in Butler...tried to do it to him on a golf course. Now they do it to Charlie," creating a narrative of coordinated political warfare that bears no resemblance to the evidence about Robinson's isolated, online-radicalized actions.

These responses reveal a dangerous pattern: using Kirk's assassination not to understand and prevent political violence, but to justify targeting political opponents while ignoring violence from ideologically aligned sources.

The Memorial Speech America Needs: Leadership in the Face of Tragedy

As America prepares for Charlie Kirk's memorial service, the nation faces a critical moment: what kind of leadership will emerge from this tragedy? The response we've seen so far—partisan targeting and divisive rhetoric—stands in stark contrast to what genuine leadership would offer.

As Kirk's memorial service approaches, I shudder to think of what will be said and how any eulogy will turn to hate. I pray for sanity and healing, but fear we'll witness yet another moment where grief becomes weaponized for political gain. A memorial service should honor the deceased and comfort the grieving—not become a rally for revenge.

Consider the alternative vision outlined on September 11th for how a president should respond to Kirk's assassination. Instead of Trump's blame-focused approach, imagine a leader who actually honored Kirk's legacy:

"My fellow Americans, today we lost Charlie Kirk—a young man who devoted his life to the principle that democracy works best when people with different views engage in respectful dialogue. Charlie traveled our nation with a simple message: bring me your strongest arguments, your toughest questions, your deepest disagreements. He literally set up a table labeled 'Prove Me Wrong' because he believed that truth emerges through fearless, respectful debate.

Today, someone chose a bullet over a debate. Someone chose violence over the vigorous discourse that Charlie championed. This is not who we are as Americans, and it's not what Charlie would want his death to represent.

As president, I could stand here and assign blame. I could point fingers and deepen divisions. But that would dishonor everything Charlie Kirk actually stood for. Instead, I'm calling on Congress to work with me on the Charlie Kirk Campus Safety and Unity Act—legislation that will protect speakers of all viewpoints while strengthening the democratic discourse Charlie believed in.

Charlie Kirk was taken from us by someone who gave up on democracy. Let's honor his memory by proving that democracy still works—that we can come together, even in grief, to solve problems and protect the values he died defending."

This kind of speech—one that focuses on Kirk's actual values of debate and democratic discourse rather than partisan blame—represents the leadership America desperately needs. It acknowledges the tragedy without weaponizing it, honors the victim without demonizing opponents, and charts a path forward that actually reflects what Kirk claimed to stand for.

The Stephen Miller Contrast: When Rhetoric Becomes Weapon

Instead, America is getting rhetoric like Stephen Miller's recent inflammatory statements that transform Kirk's death into a call for partisan warfare. Miller's response exemplifies everything wrong with the current approach—using tragedy to justify targeting political opponents while ignoring the actual factors that lead to political violence.

Miller's framing treats Kirk's assassination as validation for pre-existing political agendas rather than as a call for serious violence prevention. This approach doesn't just fail to honor Kirk's memory; it actively undermines the democratic discourse that Kirk claimed to champion by treating political opponents as enemies deserving of government targeting rather than fellow Americans deserving of constitutional protections.

The contrast is stark: leadership that honors democratic values versus rhetoric that weaponizes tragedy for partisan gain. The former builds the kind of society where political violence becomes unthinkable; the latter creates conditions where future violence becomes inevitable.

The International Embarrassment: When Solidarity Becomes Spectacle

The response has extended beyond American borders in ways that reveal both promise and concern. As a Canadian, I was struck to see the Canadian House of Commons give Charlie Kirk a standing ovation—with both Conservative and Liberal MPs rising together. This rare moment of cross-party unity reflected something important: Charlie Kirk was many things to millions of people, but first and foremost he believed in honest debate. His signature phrase "Prove Me Wrong" when confronting those who opposed him represented a commitment to democratic discourse that transcended partisan boundaries.

However, while this bipartisan recognition of Kirk's dedication to debate was appropriate, it also risks becoming performative solidarity that elevates a figure simply because of how he died, rather than examining the broader dynamics that make political violence possible.

Canada is not immune to these dynamics. We've seen our own political rhetoric become increasingly polarized, and our own online spaces contribute to radicalization. The standing ovation in Parliament, while well-intentioned, suggests we may be more susceptible to American-style political theater than we'd like to admit. This isn't about moral superiority—those of us living in glass houses shouldn't throw stones—but rather recognition that effective violence prevention requires honest analysis, not performative gestures.

The Statistical Reality: Why Selective Targeting Fails

The Trump administration's focus on "far-left" violence ignores substantial evidence about political violence patterns in America. According to data from the Anti-Defamation League, the FBI, and academic researchers, the statistical reality is far more complex than current political narratives suggest.

Right-wing extremism has been responsible for the majority of domestic terrorism deaths in recent years. The ADL reports that right-wing extremists committed 75% of extremist-related killings in 2022, while left-wing extremists committed 4%. The Center for Strategic and International Studies found that far-right attacks increased significantly from 2019-2020, while far-left attacks remained relatively stable and less lethal.

However—and this is crucial—these statistics don't justify targeting all conservatives any more than Kirk's assassination justifies targeting all progressives. The goal isn't to win a statistical argument about which side is "worse," but to recognize that political violence emerges from extremist ideologies that exist on the fringes of both sides of the political spectrum.

The pattern is clear: isolated individuals, often struggling with mental health issues, become radicalized through online communities that provide them with ideological frameworks for viewing political opponents as existential threats deserving of violence. This dynamic operates regardless of the specific ideology involved.

Why Selective Enforcement Makes Violence Worse

The Trump administration's approach—targeting "far-left" groups while ignoring or minimizing far-right violence—doesn't just fail to prevent violence; it actively makes the problem worse in several ways:

Legitimizes extremist narratives: When government selectively targets one form of extremism while ignoring others, it validates the victimization narratives that fuel radicalization. Far-left extremists will point to this selective enforcement as evidence of fascist oppression, while far-right extremists will see it as validation of their views.

Drives extremism underground: Rather than eliminating dangerous ideologies, selective targeting typically pushes them into less visible spaces where they become harder to monitor and counter. This makes actual threats more difficult to identify and prevent.

Undermines law enforcement credibility: When enforcement appears politically motivated rather than evidence-based, it reduces public trust in institutions and makes community cooperation with law enforcement less likely. Effective counterterrorism requires broad community support, not partisan targeting.

Creates constitutional conflicts: Targeting political groups based on ideology rather than specific criminal behavior raises serious First Amendment concerns and invites legal challenges that can actually strengthen extremist movements by giving them constitutional martyrdom narratives.

Misallocates resources: Focusing enforcement on ideological categories rather than behavioral risk factors means missing actual threats while wasting resources on groups that pose little danger.

What Comprehensive Violence Prevention Actually Looks Like

Effective political violence prevention requires moving beyond partisan targeting toward evidence-based approaches that address the actual factors that drive individuals toward violent action:

Online Radicalization Monitoring

Rather than targeting broad ideological categories, focus on specific online spaces and recruitment tactics that push individuals toward violence regardless of their political orientation. This includes:

  • Monitoring forums that glorify political violence from any direction

  • Identifying recruitment patterns that isolate individuals from mainstream communities

  • Developing counter-messaging strategies that provide alternative narratives to extremist content

  • Working with tech platforms to identify and disrupt radicalization pipelines

Mental Health and Social Support Integration

Many individuals who commit political violence show signs of mental health struggles, social isolation, and personal crises that make them susceptible to extremist recruitment. Comprehensive prevention includes:

  • Improving access to mental health services, particularly for young men who are disproportionately represented among political violence perpetrators

  • Training family members, friends, and community members to recognize signs of radicalization and connect individuals with appropriate support

  • Creating off-ramps for individuals who are beginning to embrace extremist ideologies but haven't yet committed to violence

Dehumanization Reduction Across the Spectrum

Political violence becomes possible when individuals view their targets as less than human. This requires addressing dehumanizing rhetoric regardless of its source:

  • Challenging language that portrays political opponents as existential threats to civilization

  • Promoting media literacy that helps people recognize and resist manipulation designed to increase outrage and fear

  • Supporting political leaders who model constructive disagreement rather than demonization

Cross-Ideological Collaboration

Preventing political violence requires cooperation across political boundaries, not partisan targeting:

  • Law enforcement agencies working with community groups from across the political spectrum to identify potential threats

  • Academic researchers studying radicalization patterns without regard to ideological direction

  • Former extremists from various movements working together to develop deradicalization programs

Constitutional Protections for All

Effective prevention must operate within constitutional boundaries that protect legitimate political expression while addressing actual threats:

  • Clear distinctions between protected political speech and genuine incitement to violence

  • Due process protections that prevent political targeting while enabling appropriate intervention

  • Transparency in enforcement that builds public trust rather than partisan suspicion

The False Choice: Security Through Suppression vs. Democracy Through Vulnerability

The Trump administration's response to Kirk's assassination presents Americans with a false choice: either accept authoritarian targeting of political opponents in the name of security, or remain vulnerable to political violence in the name of freedom.

This false binary ignores the successful approaches used by other democracies that have faced similar challenges. Countries like Germany, which has extensive experience combating both far-left and far-right extremism, demonstrate that effective prevention is possible within democratic frameworks—but only when it's applied consistently across the political spectrum based on evidence rather than partisan advantage.

Germany's approach includes:

  • Constitutional protections for political expression combined with clear prohibitions on incitement to violence

  • Intelligence services that monitor extremist movements regardless of ideology

  • Educational programs that inoculate young people against extremist recruitment

  • Social programs that provide alternatives to extremist communities for isolated individuals

  • Legal frameworks that target specific criminal behaviors rather than broad ideological categories

The German model isn't perfect, and it operates in a different constitutional context than the United States. But it demonstrates that democracies can protect themselves from political violence without abandoning their foundational principles—if they're willing to apply prevention measures consistently rather than selectively.

The Real Stakes: Democracy's Survival Requires Honest Accounting

The response to Charlie Kirk's assassination will determine whether America can develop effective approaches to preventing political violence or will remain trapped in cycles of partisan targeting that make violence more likely, not less.

The current path is unsustainable: Using each incident of political violence to target ideological opponents creates escalating spirals of radicalization and retaliation. Robinson's antifascist ideology was partly motivated by viewing conservatives like Kirk as fascists deserving of violence. The Trump administration's response—targeting "far-left" groups while minimizing far-right threats—validates those exact narratives and makes future violence more likely.

The alternative requires political courage: Comprehensive violence prevention means acknowledging that dangerous extremism exists on multiple fronts, that current political rhetoric from various sources contributes to climates where violence becomes thinkable, and that effective solutions require cooperation rather than conquest.

This doesn't mean false equivalencies or "both sides" deflection. It means honest accounting of where threats actually come from, evidence-based responses that address root causes rather than symptoms, and political leadership that prioritizes public safety over partisan advantage.

Moving Forward: What Kirk's Legacy Could Actually Be

Charlie Kirk deserved better than to become a symbol in America's escalating political warfare. His assassination represents a failure of the systems designed to identify and prevent political violence—not because those systems are inherently inadequate, but because they've been subordinated to partisan interests rather than public safety.

Conclusion: The Choice America Must Make

Last night’s 30 minute window of social media activity demonstrate how quickly America transforms tragedy into political ammunition. Charlie Kirk's assassination has already become a tool for targeting political opponents rather than preventing future violence, exactly as my first addendum warned.

This approach doesn't just dishonor Kirk's memory—it actively makes political violence more likely by validating the extremist narratives that motivate individuals like Tyler Robinson. When government targets "far-left" groups while ignoring far-right violence, it confirms antifascist beliefs about fascist oppression. When political leaders use assassination as justification for suppressing opposition, they provide extremists with evidence for their most paranoid claims.

The alternative requires choosing evidence over ideology, prevention over performance, and democracy over authoritarianism. It means recognizing that political violence emerges from extremist ideologies that exist independently of mainstream political parties, that effective prevention requires addressing threats from all sources, and that preserving democracy sometimes requires protecting the rights of those with whom we disagree most strongly.

Charlie Kirk's death offers America an opportunity to move beyond partisan blame toward comprehensive violence prevention. The choice is whether to seize that opportunity or remain trapped in cycles that ensure more Charlie Kirks, more Tyler Robinsons, and more opportunities for would-be authoritarians to exploit tragedy for political gain.

The stakes could not be higher. Democracy's survival requires honest accounting of political violence, not partisan weaponization of it. The time for that choice is now, before the next tragedy makes it too late.
