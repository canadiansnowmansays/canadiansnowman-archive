The Asimov Gap

The Last Invention

Part 26: If AI can build better AI, the governance window may already be closing

April 27, 2026

In 1965, the British statistician I. J. Good wrote a paper that contained one of the most consequential sentences in the history of technology. He called it, with characteristic understatement, an “ultraintelligent machine”: a machine capable of surpassing all the intellectual activities of any human being, including the design of even better machines. From that premise, he derived a conclusion that has haunted every serious discussion of artificial intelligence since.

Scientist, I.J. Good

“The first ultraintelligent machine,” Good wrote, “is the last invention that man need ever make.”

Good meant this as a promise. History has come to read it as a warning.

Late last month, a large crowd gathered in downtown San Francisco to demand that the AI industry stop developing more powerful bots. They carried signs reading Stop the AI Race and Don’t Build Skynet, marched through the city, and gave speeches outside the offices of Anthropic, OpenAI, and xAI. Their specific demand: halt the development of AI models capable of designing future AI models.

The demand was not as radical as it sounds. Twenty of the twenty-five leading AI researchers at DeepMind, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, UC Berkeley, Princeton, and Stanford have identified the automation of AI research as among the industry’s “most severe and urgent” risks. When the people building the thing agree with the people protesting it, the governance question becomes unavoidable.

That question is the subject of this essay.

Protestors in San Francisco, California, marching from Anthropic to OpenAI to xAI, demanding a collective agreement on the pause. [Image credit: AP Photo/Michaël Trazzi]

The Loop

What Silicon Valley is currently in a frenzy about has a clinical name: recursive self-improvement. The concept is simple enough to state and catastrophic enough in its implications to have occupied philosophers, computer scientists, and governance scholars for sixty years without resolution.

The argument runs as follows. AI models are increasingly capable of writing code. AI research is, in large part, a coding problem. Therefore, AI models are increasingly capable of contributing to the development of future AI models. If that capability crosses a certain threshold — if the models become not merely helpful assistants to human researchers but autonomous agents capable of directing the research process itself — the development of AI will no longer be constrained by the pace of human scientific labour. It will be constrained only by compute and energy. And those constraints are being lifted, aggressively, by the same companies doing the research.

This is what Good meant by “the last invention.” Not that AI would be the final human achievement in some poetic sense, but that once built, it would take over the work of building everything that came after.

The question the San Francisco protesters are asking — and that this series must ask — is not whether this is happening. The question is: what happens to governance when the thing being governed is accelerating its own development?

What Is Already True

Before arriving at the speculative, it is worth establishing the actual.

Dario Amodei has estimated that coding tools speed up Anthropic’s overall workflows by fifteen to twenty percent. Anthropic has stated that as much as ninety percent of the company’s code is already written by Claude. OpenAI recently released a model it described, without apparent irony, as “instrumental in creating itself.” These are not marketing claims from the fringe of the industry. They are matter-of-fact statements from the chief executives of the two most powerful AI companies in the world.

Google DeepMind offers a more concrete example. Last year, the company developed an AI coding agent called AlphaEvolve, which was able to make Google’s global data-centre fleet 0.7 percent more computationally efficient on average, and cut the overall training time of Gemini by one percent. These numbers sound modest until you consider what they represent: an AI model making the infrastructure that trains AI models measurably faster. The loop has begun. It is not closed yet. The question is how long that takes.

The current state, Atlantic reporters Matteo Wong and Lila Shroff note, is not recursive but piecemeal. AI tools can write code, find small optimizations, and make discrete parts of the research process faster. Humans still play an essential role in proposing hypotheses, curating training data, setting experimental parameters, and allocating scarce compute. The missing ingredient — what AI insiders call “research taste,” the mix of creativity and intuition held by top engineers — has not yet been automated. The models can execute. They cannot yet imagine.

The question is whether that distinction survives the next decade.

The Forecasts

The forecasts being offered, by serious people at credible institutions, are worth stating plainly.

Sam Altman has said that by 2028 — two years from now — OpenAI plans to have developed a fully “automated AI researcher.” The company has written, in a recent blog post, that by then “we are pretty confident we will have systems that can make more significant discoveries.” Eli Lifland, a researcher at the AI Futures Project, has forecast that AI research and development could be fully automated by 2032. Neev Parikh, a researcher at METR, a nonprofit studying AI coding capabilities, told Wong and Shroff: “I don’t expect a reason for it to slow down.”

These are not the projections of science fiction writers or AI alarmists. They are the working assumptions of people whose job is to measure what AI can currently do and extrapolate carefully from there.

Nick Bostrom, the Swedish philosopher whose work on superintelligence has influenced every serious governance discussion of the past decade, offered Wong and Shroff a phrase that stopped me cold when I read it. He used to call himself a “fretful optimist,” he said. Now he is a “moderate fatalist.”

Bostrom is not a man given to casual pessimism. That he has moved from fretful optimism to moderate fatalism — and that he is doing so in public, in a mainstream publication, with apparent equanimity — tells you something about where the people who have thought longest about this problem currently reside.

The Governance Problem, Compounded

Part I of this series introduced the Asimov Gap as the space between what AI can do and what democratic institutions can govern. Every subsequent part has documented ways that gap is widening: faster capability development, slower regulatory response, the colonization of governance functions by private actors, the erosion of the epistemic foundations on which democratic decision-making depends.

Recursive self-improvement does not merely widen that gap. It changes its fundamental character.

The Asimov Gap, as originally conceived, was a lag problem. Technology advances; governance catches up, eventually, imperfectly, but it does catch up. Aviation regulation followed the Wright Brothers. Nuclear non-proliferation treaties followed Hiroshima. Internet privacy law followed Cambridge Analytica — slowly, badly, but it followed. The lag was always large. It was, in theory, closeable.

Recursive self-improvement converts a lag problem into a speed problem. If AI models begin meaningfully accelerating the pace of their own development, the rate at which capabilities advance is no longer fixed by the pace of human scientific labour. It becomes a function of the models themselves. And the models are getting better at contributing to that process every few months.

Dean Ball, a former AI adviser to the Trump administration, put it without sentimentality in commentary on the self-improvement story: “This could change the dynamics of AI competition, alter AI geopolitics, and much more.” He noted that American institutions are, in many ways, still adapting to the internet — the IRS still processes tax returns using COBOL, a programming language released in 1960. “Should AI models progress faster,” Ball wrote, “public policy, including regulations on safety and security, has even less hope of keeping up.”

Less hope of keeping up. That is the baseline assessment of a former Trump administration official who favours AI development. It is not a position from the governance-sceptic fringe. It is the centre of the conversation.

What the Builders Know (and Won’t Quite Say)

Jack Clark, Anthropic’s head of policy — who appeared in Part XXII of this series discussing the governance gap from the inside — told Wong and Shroff that one of his biggest priorities this year is to better understand “the extent to which we are automating aspects of AI development.”

Read that sentence carefully. The head of policy at one of the two leading AI safety companies in the world does not currently know, with precision, how much of his company’s AI development is being automated by the company’s own AI. He knows it is substantial — ninety percent of the code is written by Claude — but he does not know what that means for the pace and character of future development. And he is prioritizing finding out.

This is not a criticism of Clark. It is an observation about the epistemic condition of the people closest to the problem. The builders are not concealing what they know. They are, in some cases, genuinely uncertain. The system is moving faster than the understanding of the system.

This is the deepest version of the Asimov Gap. It is not merely that regulators don’t understand what the labs are building. It is that the labs themselves are not fully certain what they are building, or how quickly its capabilities will compound, or at what point the human researchers guiding the process will find themselves running to keep pace with a process that is, in some meaningful sense, running itself.

Pushmeet Kohli, DeepMind’s vice president of science, offered the most precise articulation of where the threshold currently stands. A model can optimize things, he said, but it does not have anything to optimize for. “That’s where the human comes in.” The models lack, for now, an intrinsic sense of direction. They can run fast. They cannot yet decide where to run.

The governance question is: what institutions need to exist, and what powers need to vest in them, before the models can decide where to run? And the uncomfortable answer this series has been building toward is: those institutions do not currently exist, and the window in which they could be built is the window that recursive self-improvement is now beginning to close.

The Last Invention, Revisited

Good’s 1965 sentence has always carried a double meaning that its author may not have fully intended. “The last invention that man need ever make” can be read as liberation — we build the thing that builds everything else, and then we are free. It can also be read as termination — we build the thing that builds everything else, and then we are no longer necessary to the process.

The San Francisco protesters read it the second way. So, apparently, does Nick Bostrom, the man who spent decades arguing that humanity could navigate toward the first interpretation.

This series has consistently declined to predict catastrophe. The Asimov Gap is a governance failure, not an inevitable doom. The gap can, in principle, be closed — with sufficient political will, institutional creativity, and international cooperation. Parts III and IV of this series documented regulatory successes. Part VII imagined what an AI-enabled abundance might look like if the governance held.

But the recursive self-improvement story introduces a timeline pressure that the earlier parts of this argument did not fully account for. If AI development meaningfully accelerates its own pace within the next several years — and the people closest to it believe it will — the window for building adequate governance frameworks is not measured in decades. It may be measured in the remainder of this one.

Good thought the last invention would free us from the labour of invention. He did not quite think through what it would mean for the labour of governance — which is slower than invention, more contentious, more dependent on trust, and more vulnerable to being outpaced.

The San Francisco protesters carried signs reading Don’t Build Skynet. The image is melodramatic, and the science fiction reference is inexact. But the instinct behind the sign is not wrong.

The instinct is: there is something here that might outrun our ability to oversee it. And once it has outrun that ability, the question of what to do becomes very much harder to answer.

Moderate fatalism is not a governance framework. It is a forecast about what happens when you don’t have one.
