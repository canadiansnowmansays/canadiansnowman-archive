THE EXCEPTION CLAUSE

Before The Towers Fell

Part VI: How Anti-Muslim Suspicion Was Built Into America Before September 11

April 22, 2026

The story Americans tell themselves about anti-Muslim prejudice begins on September 12, 2001. It begins with shock, with grief, with fear — and with the ugly but understandable transformation of those emotions into suspicion of the Muslim community that the attackers claimed to represent. In this telling, the prejudice was a response to a specific event, proportional in its origins if not in its expression, and therefore in some sense historically legible even when its manifestations were not.

The story is wrong. Not about what happened after September 11 — the persecution was real, and Parts VII and VIII of this series will document it. But about when it began.

Anti-Muslim suspicion in the United States is not a post-9/11 phenomenon. It has a history stretching across two centuries — through the Barbary Wars of the early 19th century, through the immigration restriction debates of the 1920s, through the Iranian hostage crisis of 1979, through the think tanks and policy networks and political rhetoric of the 1990s that had constructed a framework for treating Islam as an inherently political and inherently threatening ideology years before the first plane hit the first tower. September 11 did not create that framework. It activated it — gave it a catastrophic event around which to organize, and removed the social inhibition that had previously kept its more extreme expressions out of mainstream political discourse.

Understanding what September 11 activated requires understanding what was already there.

The Barbary Precedent

The United States’ first sustained engagement with Muslim-majority states was the Barbary Wars of 1801-1805 and 1815 — conflicts with the North African states of Tripoli, Algiers, Tunis, and Morocco over the piracy of American merchant vessels and the payment of tribute. The wars were, in their military and diplomatic dimensions, relatively modest affairs. In their rhetorical and cultural dimensions, they were formative.

The Barbary Wars were America’s first experience of defining itself against a Muslim adversary, and they produced a set of images and narratives about Islam that proved remarkably durable. The Barbary powers were described not merely as piratical states but as civilizationally alien — as representatives of a religion and a culture that was fundamentally incompatible with the commercial and republican values of the new American nation. The First Marine Corps Hymn’s reference to “the shores of Tripoli” embedded the Barbary conflict in American military culture in a way that kept the association between Islam and American military adversarialism alive long after the specific conflicts had been resolved.

The theological framing was present from the beginning. John Adams, negotiating the Treaty of Tripoli in 1796, had insisted on including Article 11, which stated explicitly that “the Government of the United States of America is not, in any sense, founded on the Christian religion” and had “no character of enmity against the laws, religion, or tranquility of Mussulmen.” The article was a diplomatic gesture, intended to establish that American opposition to the Barbary powers was political rather than theological. Its necessity — the fact that Adams felt compelled to make the disavowal explicitly — is itself evidence of how thoroughly the theological framing had already taken hold.

The legacy of the Barbary Wars was not a formal policy of anti-Muslim discrimination. It was a cultural reservoir — a set of images, associations, and narratives about Islam and Muslim peoples that was available to be drawn on whenever a subsequent encounter between the United States and a Muslim-majority adversary required a framework for public understanding. It was the first deposit in an account that would be drawn from, repeatedly, over the following two centuries.

Immigration and the Muslim Question

The immigration restriction debates of the 1910s and 1920s that produced the Johnson-Reed Act of 1924 were primarily framed around race — around the pseudo-scientific categorization of European populations into superior and inferior racial groups, with Southern and Eastern Europeans deemed less fit for American citizenship than Northern and Western Europeans. But the debates also contained an explicit religious dimension that targeted Muslims specifically.

The Asiatic Barred Zone, established by the Immigration Act of 1917, excluded from immigration eligibility virtually all inhabitants of a geographically defined zone that encompassed most of the Muslim-majority world: Arabia, Persia, Afghanistan, India, and most of Southeast Asia. The racial and religious categories overlapped so thoroughly in the legislative imagination that they were rarely distinguished. “Asiatic” was a racial category; it was also, in the territories it covered, substantially a proxy for Muslim.

The 1924 act’s national origin quotas extended this exclusion more systematically, assigning near-zero quotas to the countries of the Muslim-majority world. The effect was not targeted at Muslims by name — the legislation maintained the fiction of racial rather than religious classification — but the result was that immigration from Muslim-majority regions was effectively prohibited for the next four decades.

The template’s third element — the theological critique doing racial work, or in this case the racial classification doing theological work — is visible throughout. The legal mechanism was racial. The communities excluded were substantially Muslim. The distinction between the two was academic for the people it affected.

The Nation of Islam and Domestic Suspicion

A sermon of the Nation of Islam. (Reuters)

The emergence of the Nation of Islam in the 1930s introduced a distinctly American form of Muslim identity into public consciousness — and immediately attracted the sustained attention of the FBI.

J. Edgar Hoover began monitoring the Nation of Islam in the early 1940s, initially under the framework of wartime surveillance of organizations deemed potentially disloyal. The Nation of Islam’s theology explicitly rejected white supremacy, identified white Americans as a class of oppressors, and preached a version of Black separatism that FBI analysts characterized as a threat to domestic order. That the organization was a religious community — that its practices centered on prayer, dietary law, community organization, and theological study — did not exempt it from surveillance. It was, from the FBI’s perspective, a political organization that used religious language as cover.

The monitoring of the Nation of Islam became, after the war, a template for the FBI’s approach to Black political organizations generally and to Muslim communities specifically. The logic was consistent: religious institutions that served politically marginalized communities were presumptively political organizations in religious dress, and their religious character did not constrain the government’s surveillance authority. This is the Reynolds belief-practice distinction applied to community surveillance: the government was not, formally, targeting belief. It was targeting the organizational expression of belief — the mosque, the community meeting, the religious school.

Nation of Islam leader Elijah Muhammad, seated in the hat, listens as Malcolm X speaks at a Nation of Islam convention in Chicago in February 1961. It was Malcolm X who bestowed the title "Honorable" to Muhammad.

Malcolm X was under FBI surveillance from the mid-1950s until his assassination in 1965. The surveillance file ran to thousands of pages. It documented his religious practices, his sermons, his correspondence, his personal relationships, his finances, his travel. The FBI was not merely monitoring a political activist. It was monitoring a Muslim minister — treating his mosque as an intelligence target and his congregation as a potential source of threat.

The framework established by the FBI’s monitoring of the Nation of Islam — religious institution as political organization, Muslim community as security threat — would be available, institutionally and legally, when it was needed again after September 11.

The Iranian Hostage Crisis and the Public Imagination

Tehran Embassy Takeover Launched 444-Day Saga with Long-Standing Implications for Iran, United States, and Global Politics

The Iranian revolution of 1979 and the subsequent hostage crisis — in which Iranian students loyal to Ayatollah Khomeini seized the American embassy in Tehran and held fifty-two American diplomats for 444 days — did more to shape American public understanding of Islam than any previous event in the country’s history.

A footnote for those inclined to forget: six of the Americans who escaped the embassy compound were sheltered by Canadian Ambassador Ken Taylor and his staff, hidden in the Canadian embassy for nearly three months, and smuggled out of Iran on Canadian passports in January 1980. The Canadian Caper, as it became known, was one of the most consequential acts of allied solidarity in Cold War diplomatic history. Canada did not ask for a trade concession in return.

The hostage crisis was the first sustained encounter between American public consciousness and a militant Islamist political movement — the first time that a conflict framed explicitly in Islamic theological terms was played out nightly on American television. The images were visceral: crowds chanting “Death to America,” hostages blindfolded, the Ayatollah’s pronouncements on the Great Satan. The theological framing was not American invention — Khomeini was genuinely constructing his political program in Islamic terms, and the revolutionary movement that brought him to power was genuinely religious in its self-understanding.

But the American reception of those images collapsed the distinction between the Iranian revolutionary movement and Islam generally. The hostage crisis entered American public consciousness not as a story about Iranian politics or the specific history of American intervention in Iran — the CIA-backed coup that had installed the Shah in 1953, the Shah’s decades of American-backed repression — but as a story about Islamic fanaticism. “Islam” became, in the American media and political imagination, a category associated with the images of Tehran: the crowd, the chant, the blindfolded Americans.

The polls from the period are revealing. Anti-Muslim sentiment in the United States increased measurably during the hostage crisis and remained elevated for years afterward. The association between Islam and violence, between Muslim identity and anti-American feeling, between the religion and the political movement, was established in the American public mind not by September 11 but by the 444 days of nightly news coverage from 1979 to 1981.

Ronald Reagan’s election in 1980 was partly a referendum on the hostage crisis, and his administration’s rhetorical construction of Islamic terrorism as a primary security threat — articulated most influentially in the 1984 report on terrorism co-authored by Jeane Kirkpatrick and others — established the framework that would guide American counterterrorism policy for the next two decades.

The Infrastructure of Suspicion

Between the Iranian hostage crisis and September 11, a network of think tanks, policy organizations, academic institutions, and political figures built what scholars have called the Islamophobia industry — an infrastructure dedicated to producing, circulating, and legitimating the argument that Islam as a religion was inherently incompatible with Western democratic values and inherently prone to political violence.

The Center for Security Policy, founded by Frank Gaffney in 1988, became one of the most influential producers of anti-Muslim policy arguments in Washington. The Middle East Forum, founded by Daniel Pipes, published research arguing that Muslim immigration to Western countries constituted a civilizational threat. Steven Emerson’s 1994 documentary Jihad in America argued that American Muslim organizations were fronts for terrorist financing, a claim that was substantially false but that received wide distribution and that shaped FBI and congressional attitudes toward Muslim civil society organizations.

The arguments produced by these organizations were not marginal. They circulated in congressional testimony, in op-ed pages, in the briefings that shaped law enforcement policy. They constructed, in the decade before September 11, a framework within which Muslim Americans were presumptively suspect — within which the Islamic community center was potentially a financing front, the mosque potentially a radicalization site, the Muslim civil liberties organization potentially an Islamist fifth column.

This infrastructure was in place before the first plane hit the first tower. September 11 did not create it. It validated it — or appeared to validate it, to the people who had built it and to the much larger audience that encountered it for the first time in the aftermath of the attacks.

What Was Waiting

When Andy Ogles posted on March 9, 2026 that “Muslims don’t belong in American society,” he was not inventing an argument. He was reaching into a reservoir that had been filled across two centuries — by the Barbary War narratives, by the immigration restriction movement, by the FBI’s approach to the Nation of Islam, by the hostage crisis imagery, by the think tank infrastructure of the 1990s, by the post-9/11 persecution that Parts VII and VIII will examine.

The reservoir was built deliberately, by identifiable people and institutions, over a long period. It was not inevitable. It was constructed. And what is constructed can, in principle, be dismantled.

But first it has to be seen clearly — seen for what it is, which is not a response to events but a framework that was waiting for events to give it permission to operate at full capacity.

September 11 gave it that permission.

What happened next is the subject of the next two parts.
