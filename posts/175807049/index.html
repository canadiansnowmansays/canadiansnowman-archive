The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 6 – When Being Anti-Fascist Becomes “Terrorism”

The Patriotic Duty Trump Wants to Criminalize

October 10, 2025

Part of the series: The Uncomfortable Truth, Fascism Exists in America Today

On September 17, I published an analysis of Trump’s designation of Antifa as a “major terrorist organization.” I documented the constitutional problems, the selective enforcement (far-right extremism gets a pass while far-left activism gets designated), and the dangerous precedent of criminalizing political movements through presidential decree.

I warned that this power, once normalized, wouldn’t stay confined to one ideology. That expanding enemy categories would accelerate. That what started as targeting “Antifa” would eventually encompass anyone opposing Trump’s agenda.

Three weeks later, that prediction came true faster than I imagined.

On October 8, 2025, White House Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller escalated: “Democrat leaders are siding with the domestic terrorists. Democrats are on the side every night of the gangbangers, the predators, and now even the domestic terrorists.”

Not Antifa members. Not specific violent actors. Democrats. Half the country, now categorized as terrorist supporters requiring state “dismantling.”

But something else happened this week that crystallizes the absurdity—and the danger—of this framework. On Wednesday evening, October 8, hundreds of Chicagoans marched through downtown to protest Trump’s deployment of National Guard troops and the escalating ICE raids terrorizing immigrant communities.

Should we label that an “Antifa protest”?

According to Miller’s logic, absolutely. They were opposing federal law enforcement. They were resisting troop deployment. They were—quite literally—being anti-fascist by opposing military occupation of an American city.

Which raises the question this essay explores: When did being anti-fascist—the ideology that defeated Hitler and defined American identity for 80 years—become domestic terrorism?

And more importantly: Why should every American reclaim that label as a patriotic duty?

The Pattern: From Antifa to Everyone

Let’s trace the escalation:

September 17, 2025: I publish analysis warning that Trump’s announcement about designating Antifa would expand to criminalize all political opposition.

September 22, 2025: Five days later, Trump signs the executive order formally designating Antifa—a decentralized ideology, not an organization—as a “domestic terrorist organization.” Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller declares: “We will dismantle Antifa.”

My analysis documented how this violated constitutional protections for political association, created selective enforcement (ignoring deadlier far-right extremism), and established precedent for criminalizing political opposition through presidential decree.

October 4, 2025: Miller expands the definition. He posts on X: “There is a large and growing movement of leftwing terrorism in this country. It is well organized and funded. And it is shielded by far-left Democrat judges, prosecutors and attorneys general. The only remedy is to use legitimate state power to dismantle terrorism and terror networks.”

Notice the expansion: Not just Antifa. “Leftwing terrorism.” Protected by Democratic judges and prosecutors. Requiring state power to “dismantle.”

October 5, 2025: When U.S. District Judge Karin Immergut (a Trump appointee) blocks deployment of 200 National Guard troops to Portland, Miller calls it “legal insurrection.” He claims courts have no authority to constrain presidential military deployment. A federal judge doing her constitutional duty is now engaging in “insurrection against the government.”

October 6, 2025: During a CNN interview, Miller claims the president has “plenary authority”—unlimited power that cannot be reviewed by courts. He freezes mid-sentence for approximately 15 seconds, appearing to realize he said the quiet part out loud. After the commercial break, he never uses the phrase again.

October 8, 2025: Miller completes the expansion: “Democrat leaders are siding with the domestic terrorists.” Not fringe activists. Not violent actors. Democratic leaders. Anyone who opposes Trump’s agenda.

October 9, 2025 (yesterday): After Judge April Perry blocks Trump’s Chicago troop deployment and protects journalists from federal force, Miller doubles down in a lengthy post. He accuses Chicago and Illinois leaders of “committing a crime against every other state” by resisting ICE. He calls opposition “domestic terrorism, planned violence and possible assassination.”

The pattern I warned about in September? It took three weeks to fully materialize.

Wednesday Night in Chicago: An “Antifa Protest”?

While Miller posted his latest escalation, hundreds of Chicagoans were marching through downtown. Let me tell you about this “terrorist” gathering:

Jessica Urbina, 26, came to march by herself. She felt it was important to join her community. NPR quoted her explaining why she showed up.

Leonardo Jimenez told ABC7: “It’s just terrifying, and I, myself, could be targeted. I don’t want to have to carry my passport around.”

U.S. Marine Corps veteran Kevin Ryan, 33, carried a sign reading “Veterans Demand No Troops on Our Streets.” He told reporters: “There’s no emergency that would warrant the National Guard or any kind of federal troops. Our military is meant to deter and defeat military threats, not to police our cities. It’s escalatory and dangerous. These people are not trained to handle crowds, to do police activity. They’re trained to conduct war.”

Viviana Barajas, member of the Illinois Coalition for Immigrant and Refugee Rights, told the crowd: “If Trump thinks his prevalent theatrics to undermine our sovereignty will shut out the passion we have for protecting our people, this is Chicago and he is sorely mistaken.”

So let me ask: Are these domestic terrorists?

A 26-year-old who felt civic duty to join her community? A man terrified of carrying his passport in his own city? A Marine veteran who understands that military force has no place policing American streets? Immigrant rights advocates defending their neighbors?

Under Miller’s framework, yes. They opposed federal law enforcement. They resisted military deployment. They organized to “obstruct” ICE operations. They are, quite literally, anti-fascist.

Should we label this an “Antifa protest”? According to the administration’s own definitions, absolutely.

But here’s the thing: Maybe we should. Not as an accusation—as a badge of honor.

John Pavlovitz Gets It: We’re All ANTIFA

This week, pastor and author John Pavlovitz published a piece titled “We’re all ANTIFA, Donald.” It’s a moral reckoning that cuts through the propaganda with clarity:

“We’re all ANTIFA, Donald; at least, all decent, patriotic Americans are... We are fiercely, steadfastly, unapologetically anti-fascist, and you know why, Donald? Because Americans are supposed to be.”

Pavlovitz continues:

“We’re anti-fascist because that’s the whole reason this nation exists to begin with, the oppressive crucible of coercion that our freedoms were first born out of.

We’re anti-fascist because the hundreds of thousands of our forebears who courageously fought and died on foreign soil were anti-fascist.

We’re anti-fascist because up until recently, every single president we’ve ever had marshaled our nation’s military and economic power to defeat fascism, not to traffic in it.

We’re anti-fascist because fascism is a mortal threat to free speech, to freedom of expression, to body autonomy, to cultural diversity, to life, liberty, and to the pursuit of happiness.”

And then Pavlovitz lands the punch:

“Simply by virtue of the fact that we are anti-fascist Americans, we are dangerous to you, which actually says more about you and your sycophantic coconspirators than it does about us.”

This is the reframe America needs. When Trump calls anti-fascists terrorists, he’s not revealing a threat to America—he’s revealing what threatens him.

Why Miller Calls Opposition “Terrorism”: Because It IS Fascism

In Parts 1-4 of this series, I measured Trump and MAGA against the ten defining characteristics of historical fascism as established by scholars like Umberto Eco and Robert Paxton.

The verdict: 9 out of 10 characteristics align.

Let me remind you what those are:

  1. Ultranationalism - “America First” ideology, nativism

  2. Violent suppression of opposition - using state power to terrorize opponents

  3. Scapegoating of “others” - immigrants, Democrats, judges, journalists

  4. Fusion of state and corporate power - Project 2025’s corporate loyalty tests

  5. Control of information - attacks on media, “fake news” campaigns

  6. Rewriting education and history - banning books, eliminating DEI

  7. Cult of personality - Trump as strongman savior

  8. Rejection of democratic norms - ignoring courts, Congress, rule of law

  9. Glorification of violence and militarism - military occupation of cities

  10. Nostalgia for mythical golden age - “Make America Great Again”

Now consider Miller’s escalation through this framework:

Characteristic 2 (Violent suppression): Miller calls for using “legitimate state power to dismantle terrorism networks”—code for using government machinery to crush political opposition.

Characteristic 3 (Scapegoating): Systematically expanding enemy categories from Antifa to “leftwing terrorism” to Democratic judges to all Democratic leaders to anyone opposing Trump.

Characteristic 8 (Rejecting norms): Calling court rulings “legal insurrection.” Claiming “plenary authority” unconstrained by judicial review, then freezing mid-sentence when realizing he said it out loud. Deploying military to American cities over local objections.

Miller isn’t calling opposition “terrorism” because it’s violent. He’s calling it terrorism because opposing fascism threatens fascist consolidation of power.

When you’re implementing fascism, anyone who is anti-fascist becomes a threat by definition. The label “domestic terrorist” is just the mechanism for criminalizing that opposition.

The Historical Irony: When America Forgot What Americans Are

Pavlovitz’s most devastating point: “We’re anti-fascist because the hundreds of thousands of our forebears who courageously fought and died on foreign soil were anti-fascist.”

Let that sink in.

American soldiers who stormed Normandy were anti-fascists. They were literally fighting fascism. The Greatest Generation defined American identity as opposition to fascist authoritarianism.

My grandfather’s generation knew what Americans were supposed to be: anti-fascist.

They didn’t need to join an organization called “Antifa.” They didn’t need membership cards. They were Americans, and Americans opposed fascism. That’s what made us Americans.

Now, 80 years later, an American president has designated being anti-fascist as domestic terrorism.

The irony would be darkly comedic if it weren’t so dangerous. Trump calls himself a patriot while criminalizing the ideology that American veterans died defending.

This isn’t just hypocrisy. It’s historical amnesia verging on national betrayal.

What Anti-Fascism Actually Means (And Why It’s Not Violence)

In my September Antifa analysis, I emphasized that “Antifa” isn’t an organization—it’s a decentralized ideology. There’s no headquarters, no membership, no leadership structure. This makes the terrorist designation legally absurd and constitutionally problematic.

But it also means something more important: Being anti-fascist is a set of principles, not a tactical endorsement of violence.

What does anti-fascism actually mean?

Based on the ten characteristics I documented in Parts 1-2, being anti-fascist means:

Opposing ultranationalism → Supporting pluralism, diversity, and equal protection regardless of origin or ethnicity

Opposing violent suppression → Defending the right to dissent, protest, and organize politically

Opposing scapegoating → Rejecting the targeting of minorities, immigrants, or political opponents as enemies

Opposing state-corporate fusion → Maintaining separation between government power and private loyalty

Opposing information control → Defending press freedom and truth over propaganda

Opposing educational manipulation → Protecting academic freedom and factual history

Opposing cult of personality → Defending institutional authority over strongman rule

Opposing rejection of norms → Upholding constitutional constraints, judicial review, and rule of law

Opposing militarism → Rejecting military occupation of civilian spaces

Opposing nostalgic nationalism → Embracing progress over mythical golden ages

Look at that list. Is any of that un-American?

Or is that... exactly what the Constitution was designed to protect?

When Jessica Urbina marched in Chicago, she was being anti-fascist. She was opposing military occupation of her city.

When Judge April Perry blocked troops and protected journalists, she was being anti-fascist. She was upholding constitutional constraints on executive power.

When Marine veteran Kevin Ryan carried his sign, he was being anti-fascist. He was defending the principle that military force has no place policing American civilians.

This is what being anti-fascist looks like. And it’s what being American is supposed to look like.

Miller’s Real Fear: That Americans Will Remember

Miller’s escalating rhetoric reveals a fundamental anxiety: What if Americans remember what they’re supposed to be?

What if the hundreds marching in Chicago are joined by thousands? What if Judge Perry’s ruling inspires other judges to enforce constitutional constraints? What if Marine veterans like Kevin Ryan remind Americans that military occupation of cities is un-American?

What if Americans reclaim “anti-fascist” as a core identity rather than accepting it as a slur?

That’s Miller’s nightmare. Because if Americans embrace being anti-fascist—if they understand it means defending constitutional democracy—then the entire framework collapses.

You can’t criminalize half the country as terrorists. You can’t imprison every judge who rules against you. You can’t jail every Democratic leader. You can’t deploy troops to every city that resists.

But you can try to make Americans forget that being anti-fascist is patriotic. You can try to make them afraid of the label. You can try to turn American identity against itself.

That’s the real goal of the “domestic terrorism” designation. Not stopping violence (far-right extremism remains deadlier and largely ignored). Making Americans afraid to oppose authoritarianism.

The Chicago Test: When Protests Become “Terrorism”

Let’s return to Wednesday’s Chicago march with Miller’s framework in mind.

Hundreds of people marched through downtown Chicago. They carried signs. They chanted. They made speeches. Organizations like the Illinois Coalition for Immigrant and Refugee Rights organized the action. People like Jessica Urbina showed up alone out of civic duty.

There was no violence. No property destruction. No attacks on federal agents.

It was, by any objective measure, peaceful protest protected by the First Amendment.

But under Miller’s expanded definition, this is “domestic terrorism.”

Why? Because they opposed ICE operations. Because they resisted military deployment. Because they defended immigrant communities. Because they were being anti-fascist.

So here’s my tongue-in-cheek proposal: Let’s label every protest like this an “Antifa protest.”

Not as an accusation. As a reclamation.

Yes, the Chicago march was an anti-fascist protest. It opposed military occupation of American cities.

Yes, Judge Perry’s ruling was anti-fascist. It defended constitutional constraints on executive power.

Yes, the Marine veteran carrying a sign was being anti-fascist. He opposed militarization of civilian law enforcement.

And every single one of those actions is deeply, fundamentally, patriotically American.

If Miller wants to call that terrorism, fine. But let’s be clear about what that reveals: He’s calling American values terrorism because those values threaten authoritarian power.

The Stakes: Democracy or Authoritarianism

We’re at an inflection point. The machinery I’ve documented in Parts 1-5 isn’t theoretical—it’s running. Russ Vought’s “post-constitutional” OMB freezing 28 billion dollars exclusively for Democratic cities. Miller expanding “terrorism” definitions to encompass all political opposition. Trump openly admitting “We’re only cutting Democrat programs.”

But resistance is building.

Judge Perry blocked troops. Judge Immergut blocked Portland deployment. Union lawsuits are filed. Senate Democrats held the line through seven votes. Hundreds marched in Chicago. Marine veterans are speaking out.

The question isn’t whether authoritarian machinery exists. It’s whether Americans will resist it.

And that requires reclaiming what it means to be American.

My September Antifa essay provided the constitutional and legal framework: Selective enforcement is dangerous. Criminalizing political movements violates the First Amendment. Presidential decrees can’t override constitutional protections.

Pavlovitz provided the moral framework: Being anti-fascist isn’t just legally protected—it’s patriotically required. It’s what the Greatest Generation fought for. It’s what American identity is built on.

Parts 1-4 provided the evidence: Trump and MAGA align with 9 out of 10 characteristics of historical fascism. This isn’t hyperbole—it’s documented reality.

Now comes the synthesis: When an administration implementing fascism calls opposition to fascism “terrorism,” Americans must choose: Surrender that identity, or reclaim it?

What Being Anti-Fascist Requires of You

Pavlovitz ends his piece with this: “The majority of this nation is ANTIFAscist, Donald. That’s what Americans are supposed to be.”

But being anti-fascist isn’t passive. It requires action.

Based on everything I’ve documented in Parts 1-6, here’s what reclaiming anti-fascist American identity looks like:

Reinforce the courts - Donate to organizations like AFGE, ACLU, and Democracy Forward filing lawsuits against illegal executive actions. Contact your state attorney general to file Equal Protection challenges against partisan funding freezes. Support legislation protecting judicial independence.

Reclaim Congress - Demand OMB oversight hearings on Vought’s illegal impoundments (GAO documented seven violations in nine months). Pressure representatives to strengthen the Impoundment Control Act with real enforcement mechanisms. Support standalone military pay legislation.

Build electoral armor - Register one voter per week between now and November 2026 (72 voters per person equals millions registered). Join organizing groups like Indivisible, Swing Left, or Run for Something. Make Vought’s partisan targeting a campaign issue.

Create civic foundations - Support furloughed federal workers through verified fundraiser campaigns or local fundraisers. Form local watchdog groups documenting how funding diversions impact your community. Build whistleblower networks. Create mutual aid networks.

Embrace the label - Say it clearly: “I am anti-fascist. That’s what Americans are supposed to be.”

Don’t run from it. Don’t equivocate. Don’t let Miller’s propaganda make you afraid of the label.

Being anti-fascist means defending the Constitution. It means upholding the rule of law. It means protecting minorities from scapegoating. It means resisting military occupation of cities. It means voting out authoritarians.

That’s not terrorism. That’s patriotism.

The Choice America Faces

In my September essay, I asked: “Do we want counter-extremism efforts that protect democracy, or do we want them to become tools for political control?”

Three weeks later, we have the answer. Counter-extremism has become political control. The “domestic terrorism” designation isn’t about stopping violence—it’s about criminalizing opposition.

But there’s a second question: Will Americans accept that criminalization, or will they reclaim what American identity actually means?

Miller is counting on fear. He’s counting on Americans being too afraid to embrace “anti-fascist” as an identity. He’s counting on propaganda making people believe that opposing authoritarianism is terrorism.

He’s betting that Americans forgot what the Greatest Generation fought for.

Pavlovitz reminds us: They fought fascism. They were anti-fascists. And that’s what made them heroes.

The hundreds who marched in Chicago Wednesday night are their heirs. Not as members of some imaginary “Antifa organization,” but as Americans doing what Americans are supposed to do: resisting tyranny.

Jessica Urbina showing up alone out of civic duty. Leonardo Jimenez terrified to walk his own streets. Kevin Ryan, Marine veteran, demanding no troops on our streets. Viviana Barajas defending her community’s sovereignty.

Those are anti-fascists. And those are patriots.

Miller wants to criminalize that. Trump wants to jail leaders who defend it. The administration wants to make you afraid to be what Americans are supposed to be.

The question is: Will you let them?

Or will you stand with Judge Perry, who blocked troops and defended the Constitution? With the unions filing lawsuits to protect workers? With the senators holding the line? With the hundreds marching in Chicago?

Will you reclaim what it means to be American?

Because here’s the truth Miller doesn’t want you to remember:

We’re all ANTIFA, Donald. At least, all decent, patriotic Americans are.

And no terrorist designation, no presidential decree, no threatening rhetoric can change what Americans are supposed to be: defenders of democracy, opponents of tyranny, upholders of constitutional freedom.

Anti-fascist. American. Same thing.

What Comes Next: From Why to How

You understand the moral case now. Being anti-fascist isn’t terrorism—it’s patriotism. It’s what the Greatest Generation fought for. It’s what Americans are supposed to be.

Miller wants you afraid of that label. Trump wants to criminalize that identity. The administration wants Americans to forget what they’re supposed to be.

But understanding WHY you must resist is only half the battle. The other half is knowing HOW.

Jessica Urbina showed up in Chicago because she felt civic duty. But what specific actions maximize impact? Judge Perry blocked troops—but how do citizens reinforce judicial independence? Marine veteran Kevin Ryan spoke truth—but what infrastructure amplifies those voices?

Parts 7-8 provide your complete action blueprint:

Part 7 shows you how to reinforce institutions—courts and Congress—that create the legal and constitutional firewall against authoritarian overreach.

You know WHY you fight: because being anti-fascist is American identity.

Now it’s time to learn HOW.
