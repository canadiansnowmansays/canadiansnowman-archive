SERIES LAUNCH

Over the next eight weeks, this series will explore the most consequential question of our time: Who controls AI controls everything else.

Five billionaires are racing to build artificial general intelligence—potentially more intelligent than humans—with minimal democratic oversight. This series examines what’s at stake across jobs, surveillance, warfare, elections, and children’s development.

Drawing on Isaac Asimov’s fifty-year exploration of human-robot society, Bernie Sanders’ political warnings, European regulatory success, and historical victories against concentrated power, it shows why the same technology could create universal abundance or unprecedented oligarchy.

We’re choosing now. The window is closing. This series shows why democratic governance matters—and how to achieve it.

New essays publish every Tuesday and Friday.

BREAKING CONTEXT (December 30, 2025)

The conversation about AI’s societal impact is accelerating dramatically. In just the last three days, The New York Times published three major essays:

  • Michelle Goldberg (Dec 29) asked which party will lead the anti-AI movement, noting 71% of Americans believe AI threatens humanity

  • Sal Khan (Dec 27) proposed companies dedicate 1% of profits to worker retraining as automation accelerates

  • Brian Groh (Dec 28) described losing his copywriting career to AI, becoming a tree cutter, then getting injured—embodying the exact displacement-to-desperation pipeline Sanders warned about

And two months earlier (Oct 29), philosophy professor Anastasia Berg warned that students using AI are becoming “subcognitive”—losing basic thinking capacities.

The alarm bells are ringing. This series explores why—and what we can do about it.

On November 18, 2025, Bernie Sanders did something unusual for an 84-year-old senator: He convened a conversation on artificial intelligence at Georgetown University.

Not a hearing. Not a committee meeting. A public conversation—bringing together AI researchers and students to discuss what Sanders called “one of the most consequential issues facing our country and the world.”

The featured guest was Geoffrey Hinton, the computer scientist who won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics for inventing the neural networks that power every major AI system today. Hinton had left Google in 2023 specifically so he could speak freely about AI risks without corporate constraints.

What Hinton told Sanders—and what Sanders then told America—should fundamentally change how we think about AI, democracy, and the future.

The core message: We’re racing toward artificial general intelligence (AGI) without adequate democratic oversight, and a handful of billionaires are making civilizational decisions that should require public deliberation.

This isn’t about whether AI is good or bad. It’s about who decides how AI shapes society: everyone through democratic institutions, or five billionaires because they got there first?

What Sanders Heard

Hinton’s warning was stark: AI systems will likely become “smarter than human beings” potentially within 5-20 years, though estimates vary widely.

This matters because we’ve never created something more intelligent than ourselves. When we do, the consequences—good or bad—will be unprecedented. And right now, that technology is being developed by:

  • Sam Altman (OpenAI)

  • Dario Amodei (Anthropic)

  • Demis Hassabis (Google DeepMind)

  • Mark Zuckerberg (Meta)

  • Elon Musk (xAI)

Five people. Unelected. Unaccountable to the public. Racing as fast as they can.

Sanders connected the dots: “Are we comfortable with a handful of billionaires shaping the future of humanity without any democratic input whatsoever?”

His answer, delivered across multiple speeches: No. We should not be comfortable with that.

The Asimov Gap

Isaac Asimov spent fifty years writing about this exact problem.

Through hundreds of stories and novels—Foundation, I Robot, The Caves of Steel—Asimov explored how humanity could create intelligent machines without losing control of its destiny.

His Three Laws of Robotics are famous:

  1. A robot may not injure a human being

  2. A robot must obey human orders (unless they conflict with the First Law)

  3. A robot must protect its own existence (unless this conflicts with the First or Second Law)

But Asimov’s genius wasn’t the laws themselves—it was showing through story after story that simple rules don’t work when technology becomes powerful enough to reshape society.

His stories demonstrated that governing transformative technology requires:

  • Democratic institutions strong enough to assert control

  • Thoughtful deliberation about consequences

  • Public accountability for those building the technology

  • Continuous vigilance against concentrated power

Asimov died in 1992. He never saw the internet, social media, or AI. But he understood the pattern: When powerful technology develops without democratic governance, oligarchy follows.

This series calls itself “The Asimov Gap”—the space between fiction’s thoughtful exploration of these issues and our society’s hasty, oligarch-driven rush into AI without adequate safeguards.

Sanders didn’t explicitly invoke Asimov, but his warnings mirror exactly what Asimov explored: the danger of concentrated power over transformative technology.

Why This Matters Now

Three reasons this can’t wait:

1. Speed: AGI potentially arrives within 5-20 years, though estimates vary. Once it exists, power dynamics might lock in. The window for democratic oversight is closing.

2. Scale: AI will affect everything—jobs, surveillance, warfare, elections, how we raise children, what it means to be human. No aspect of society untouched.

3. Concentration: Unlike previous technological revolutions (spread across many companies, countries, decades), AI development is concentrated in a few companies racing each other. Winner-take-all dynamics.

Sanders isn’t calling for banning AI. He’s demanding democratic governance before oligarchs consolidate irreversible control.

That’s the urgency.

Why Now: The Conversation Is Accelerating

The New York Times essays published in the last 72 hours confirm what Sanders and Hinton warned about:

Michelle Goldberg notes widespread public unease—71% of Americans believe AI threatens humanity. Yet politicians from both parties remain divided, with many chasing tech investments while ignoring public concerns.

Brian Groh’s story is exactly what Sanders predicted: College-educated worker displaced by AI, forced into physical labor, injured, facing the opioid pipeline that destroyed working-class communities. “What if,” he wrote, “despite my college degree, I wasn’t more capable than my neighbors but merely capable in a different way?”

Sal Khan proposes companies spend 1% of profits on retraining—recognizing that mass displacement without support will destroy both workers and the consumer base capitalism needs.

Anastasia Berg warns that students using AI are becoming “subcognitive”—losing basic thinking capacities. The next generation may lack cognitive tools to participate in democracy.

The warnings are everywhere. But warnings alone won’t create solutions.

This series provides what’s missing: A comprehensive framework connecting scattered concerns (jobs, surveillance, democracy, children) into one argument—AI governance is the choice between democracy and oligarchy.

Asimov explored this through fifty years of fiction. Sanders is fighting it through politics. Europe is proving it’s achievable through policy.

This series shows how we close the gap between warning and action.

The Class Divide in AI Anxiety

At the Georgetown conversation, Sanders polled the room of university students. When he asked if they saw AI as positive for their futures, many hands went up. When he asked if they were concerned about negative impacts, almost every hand in the room went up.

He then shared a contrasting poll he’d conducted weeks earlier in Davenport, Iowa—a working-class audience of about 2,500 people. When asked if AI would be positive for their futures, two hands went up.

Two. Out of 2,500.

This reveals something crucial: The people most vulnerable to AI displacement understand the threat most clearly. College students see both promise and peril. Working-class Americans—who’ve already watched automation hollow out their communities—know what’s coming.

They’ve seen this movie before. Factory jobs automated away. No transition support. Communities devastated. And now the same forces are “climbing the economic ladder,” as copywriter Brian Groh wrote after losing his career to AI.

This isn’t abstract policy debate. For millions of Americans, it’s existential threat.

This series explores why working-class intuition is correct—and why the solution requires democratic governance, not market forces.

The Stakes

If we get this right:

  • AI cures disease, solves climate change, ends material scarcity

  • Productivity gains are shared, work becomes optional, humanity flourishes

  • Democracy strengthens, freedom expands, human potential unleashes

  • Asimov’s optimistic vision becomes real

If we get this wrong:

  • Jobs disappear without safety net, inequality explodes

  • Surveillance becomes ubiquitous, privacy disappears

  • Elections get manipulated beyond recognition

  • Autonomous weapons proliferate, warfare becomes algorithmic

  • Democracy becomes hollow shell while oligarchs rule

  • Asimov’s warnings become reality

Same technology. Completely different outcomes. Difference is governance.

Why Sanders, Why Now

Sanders is 84. This is likely his final major legislative push. He could be focused on anything—healthcare, climate, inequality.

He’s focused on AI because he sees the pattern:

Concentrated power always claims:

  • “We’ll regulate ourselves”

  • “Trust us, we’re different”

  • “Democratic oversight will destroy innovation”

  • “You’ll understand when it’s too late to change course”

Every time, they’re wrong. Every time, democratic oversight makes society better.

Robber barons said regulation would destroy industry—created broad prosperity instead. Tobacco companies said regulation would destroy economy—saved millions of lives instead.

Tech oligarchs are using the same playbook. Sanders recognizes it.

He’s sounding the alarm while democratic intervention is still possible. After AGI, might be too late.

The Choice Before Us

Technology doesn’t determine outcomes. Governance does.

The same AI could create abundance for all or concentrate wealth for few. Could strengthen democracy or enable authoritarianism. Could free humanity or enslave it.

Not because of what the technology can do. Because of who controls it and what rules govern its use.

Asimov spent 50 years exploring that choice through fiction. Sanders is forcing that choice into politics.

This series explores that choice across every dimension where it matters.

The next seventeen essays will show you:

  • What we’re choosing between (oligarchy vs. democracy)

  • How the choice is being made (mostly by not choosing)

  • What democratic AI governance looks like (practical, achievable)

  • Why the window is closing (AGI timeline)

  • What you can do about it (more than you think)

Welcome to the Asimov Gap—the space between the future we could build and the future we’re defaulting toward.

Let’s close that gap while we still can.
