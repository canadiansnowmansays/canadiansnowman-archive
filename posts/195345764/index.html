Talarico Treads The Tough Texas Trail

The seminarian, the false prophet, and the fight over who gets to be a good Christian in America

April 24, 2026

James Talarico. The Republicans aren't afraid of his platform. They're afraid of his pulpit.

By Stephen Gordon — CanadianSnowmanSays

April 24, 2026

A pastor named Brooks Potteiger said James Talarico should be “crucified with Christ.” A podcast host named Joshua Haymes told his listeners he prays that “God kills” the Democratic Senate candidate. Both men later clarified that they weren’t calling for actual violence — just metaphorical death, the spiritual kind. You know. The kind where God handles the details.

Talarico’s response? “You may pray for my death, Pastor, but I still love you. I love you more than you could ever hate me.”

That exchange, reported today in Elizabeth Bruenig’s profile for the Atlantic, tells you everything you need to know about the race that’s coming. Not the polls. Not the money. Not the maps. The theology. Because what’s happening in Texas right now isn’t just a Senate campaign. It’s a referendum on who owns Christianity in American politics — and whether that monopoly can be broken.

The Bruenig Thesis

Bruenig’s piece, “James Talarico’s Tough Sell,” frames the central tension cleanly. Talarico is a 36-year-old Presbyterian seminarian running on a progressive platform — higher wages, Medicare for All, immigration reform, firearm regulation — wrapped in explicitly Christian language. He argues that the teachings of Jesus endorse the policies the left embraces. He calls Christian nationalism “the worship of power instead of Christ” and “a betrayal of Jesus of Nazareth.”

This is not new theology. It’s the tradition of Martin Luther King Jr., of William Sloane Coffin, of Raphael Warnock. What’s new is where he’s doing it. Texas. Deep-red, evangelical, mega-church Texas. The state where Ken Paxton backed a law mandating the Ten Commandments in every public school classroom — and sued the districts that refused to comply.

Bruenig identifies the paradox at the heart of Talarico’s candidacy: the very thing that makes him dangerous to Republicans — a Democrat who speaks fluent evangelical — is the same thing that makes moderates and nonreligious voters uneasy. He’s caught between two audiences who both suspect he’s performing for the other one.

The Faithful and the Skeptical

The Atlantic piece is built on more than a dozen voter interviews, and they paint a complicated picture.

On one side: voters like Nikkie S., a Black woman who appreciates the way Talarico offers Texans “an opportunity to prayerfully reconsider their assumptions about how Christianity speaks to politics.” On the other: Greg Camacho, a Catholic high school teacher in San Antonio who agrees with Talarico on immigration and energy policy but says he’s “allergic” to any candidate who uses faith as part of his brand. Camacho called Talarico’s argument about Mary’s consent and abortion rights “silly and unfortunate and almost offensive.” He doesn’t plan to vote in November.

That’s the gap Talarico has to close. Camacho isn’t a Republican. He’s a persuadable Democrat who finds the faith messaging off-putting. Multiply him by a few hundred thousand, and Talarico’s coalition has a hole in it.

Then there’s the other flank. David Stroot, a nonreligious landman in Fort Worth, told Bruenig he’s exhausted by the God talk: “I just feel like every time he’s interviewed, it’s just instantly about faith.” But Stroot plans to vote for Talarico anyway — because the alternative is worse.

That “because the alternative is worse” dynamic may be Talarico’s most reliable path. He doesn’t need every secular Democrat to love the sermons. He needs them to prefer sermons about loving your neighbour to sermons about millstones and divine violence.

The Paxton Contrast

And this is where Bruenig’s piece delivers its sharpest blade — not through argument, but through juxtaposition.

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton at CPAC, 2022. Since this photo was taken, he has been impeached, federally investigated, divorced amid allegations of infidelity, and is now running for the U.S. Senate on a platform of biblical morality. He calls his opponent a "false prophet." (Brian Snyder/Reuters)

Ken Paxton has called Talarico a “false prophet” and an “anti-Christian.” On a podcast earlier this month, Paxton complained that everything Talarico says “is as far from the gospel of Jesus Christ as could possibly be imagined.” He then alluded — darkly, unmistakably — to the passage in Matthew where Jesus says it would be better to tie a millstone around your neck and throw yourself into the sea than to mislead God’s children.

Let that sit for a moment. An impeached attorney general, who faces federal investigation, whose ex-wife — a sitting state senator — accused him of infidelity, who backed conspiracy theories about the 2020 election, who appealed the Dallas County voting extension on his own ballot — that man is calling a Presbyterian seminarian a false prophet.

Bruenig doesn’t editorialize the contrast. She doesn’t have to. She simply places Paxton’s record next to his rhetoric and lets the reader do the math. Bribery allegations. Fraud charges. A law career defined by punitive enforcement of religious orthodoxy through state power. And across the stage, a man whose response to a death prayer was “I still love you.”

Bruenig also drops in the Pete Hegseth comparison — the Defence Secretary who called on God to deliver “overwhelming violence” to America’s enemies “in the name of Jesus Christ.” That’s the brand of Christianity Talarico is running against. Not faith itself, but faith weaponized. Faith as a cudgel rather than a compass.

The Two-Front Campaign

What Bruenig sees — and what the Republican attacks confirm — is that Talarico is running two campaigns simultaneously.

The first is a Senate race. Standard Democratic platform, strong fundraising, record primary turnout — 2.3 million voters, nearly double the preceding midterm primaries. He won by seven points without a runoff. He carried white voters, Hispanic voters, young voters, and independents. That’s a formidable coalition.

The second is a theological argument. And this one is harder to win, because it requires persuading two very different audiences of two very different things: conservative Christians that his faith is real, and secular progressives that his faith won’t compromise their rights.

His answer to the second group is smart. Talarico has described the separation of church and state as “sacred” — and mainly “for the benefit of the Church.” That’s not a constitutional argument. That’s a theological argument for secularism. He’s telling nonreligious voters: I want to keep government out of religion as much as you do, and I want it for religious reasons. It’s an elegant move.

His answer to the first group is riskier. His theology professor at Austin Seminary, Cynthia Rigby, told Bruenig that “the jury’s out on what’s real Christianity and what’s fake Christianity.” That’s intellectually honest. It’s also not a bumper sticker. In a state where 700-seat megachurches preach doctrinal certainty every Sunday, “the jury’s out” is a hard sell.

What the Right Is Afraid Of

Here’s what I find most revealing about the Bruenig piece: the intensity of the Republican response.

Allie Beth Stuckey, a Texas-based evangelical influencer, wrote in the Daily Wire that Talarico is “a leftist atheist’s idea of a good Christian” — “a progressive culture warrior in lockstep with the secular world” who is “uninterested in foundational Christian principles like sin, repentance, or salvation.” Turning Point USA accused him of speaking “the language of an evangelical while completely undermining the central truth claims of the Scripture.”

That’s not dismissal. That’s fear. You don’t mobilize the Daily Wire, Turning Point, and a podcast death prayer against a candidate you think is going to lose. You do it against a candidate who might break something you’ve spent forty years building.

Since Reagan, the Republican coalition has rested on a bargain: evangelical Christians provide the votes, and the party provides the judges, the school prayer bills, the abortion restrictions, the cultural war infrastructure. That bargain held through Bush, through the Tea Party, through Trump. It held because there was no credible alternative on the other side — no Democrat who could walk into a Baptist church in Fort Worth and sound like he belonged there.

Talarico threatens that monopoly. Not because he’ll convert millions of evangelicals overnight — he won’t. But because he gives permission. He gives Christian voters who are uncomfortable with Trump, uncomfortable with Paxton, uncomfortable with millstone rhetoric and Ten Commandments mandates, a place to go that doesn’t require them to leave their faith at the door.

As Bruenig puts it: Talarico is “campaigning not only for a Senate seat in a red state, but to redefine who gets to be a good Christian in America.”

That’s why they’re praying for his death.

The Canadian View

I’m watching this from Toronto with a peculiar mix of fascination and horror.

In Canada, we settled the faith-in-politics question decades ago — mostly by deciding not to talk about it. Our politicians go to church or they don’t. Nobody runs campaign ads about it. The idea of a Senate candidate being called a “false prophet” by his opponent, or having a pastor pray for his death on a podcast, would be so far outside the norms of Canadian political discourse that it would be genuinely unintelligible.

But I also know that what happens in Texas doesn’t stay in Texas. If Talarico wins in November — if a progressive Christian Democrat takes a Senate seat in the reddest of red states — it won’t just change the math in Washington. It will change the vocabulary. It will prove that the evangelical-Republican alliance is not a law of nature. It will show that “Christian voter” and “Republican voter” are not synonyms.

And if he loses? The Republican playbook holds. The megachurches keep their monopoly. The millstone rhetoric goes unchallenged. And the next Democrat who tries to talk about Jesus in Texas will be told, as Democrats always are, that it can’t be done here.

Bruenig’s closing line is the right one: “A Democratic loss in Texas would be unremarkable. An upset could be a significant salvo in the battle for the soul of the country.”

She’s not wrong. But I’d add one thing: the battle for the soul of the country is also, unavoidably, a battle for the soul of the church. Talarico knows that. Paxton knows that. And somewhere in Texas, a pastor is praying about it — though I suspect his prayers and Talarico’s sound very different.
