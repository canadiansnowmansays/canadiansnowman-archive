The Venezuela Invasion Series

When Reality Hits the Imperial Project

Military Triumph Meets Political, Economic, and Diplomatic Chaos

January 11, 2026

January 11, 2026

Week One of Trump’s Venezuela adventure was military triumph: Delta Force captured Maduro, U.S. jets bombed Caracas, American forces killed 80+ people, Trump declared “we’re in charge.” The invasion succeeded brilliantly. Week Two is when reality hit—and it hit from every direction simultaneously. Oil executives said “uninvestable.” The Senate rebelled 52-47. Latin America scrambled for survival strategies. Iran’s death toll exploded into the hundreds. Mexico panicked. Cuba got threatened. Greenland faced “the hard way.” And through it all, one pattern became undeniable: you can invade a country with military force, but you cannot control the political, economic, and diplomatic consequences that follow. This is the story of Week Two, when Trump’s imperial project collided with the limits of American power.

THE OIL EXECUTIVES SAY “UNINVESTABLE”

Before the political rebellion came the economic verdict.

Friday, January 10: Trump convened America’s most powerful oil executives at the White House, promising they would “rapidly rebuild Venezuela’s dilapidated oil industry” and invest “at least $100 billion.”

Exxon Mobil CEO Darren Woods delivered the response that should end Trump’s imperial fantasy:

“We’ve had our assets seized there twice, and so you can imagine to re-enter a third time would require some pretty significant changes. Today it’s uninvestable.“

Two words that exposed the fundamental flaw: you can invade a country with Delta Force, but you cannot make unprofitable investments profitable with military threats.

Even Harold Hamm—Oklahoma oil tycoon and one of Trump’s closest industry allies—stopped short of committing. “It excites me as an explorationist,” Hamm said carefully. “But Venezuela has got its challenges.”

Two sources close to the companies: Not aware of any actual $100 billion commitment Trump announced.

The economic reality: Venezuelan oil costs ~$80/barrel to extract (heavy crude requiring extensive refining). Market price: ~$60/barrel. But beyond immediate price math, the fundamental problems are legal protections, investment security, political stability, and companies’ bitter experience with asset seizures. Restoration timeline: 10-15 years. Required investment: $100-200 billion. Companies have been burned twice before.

Energy Secretary Chris Wright’s solution: U.S. Export-Import Bank might provide “credit support”—meaning taxpayers would insure private companies’ risky investments.

Trump’s claim: “Our giant oil companies will be spending at least $100 billion of their money, not the government’s money.”

Reality: Companies won’t invest their own capital without government guarantees—socializing risk, privatizing profit.

The military invasion succeeded brilliantly. The economic extraction is failing before it begins. [I’ll examine this economic failure in greater depth in a subsequent essay, including why flooding markets with Venezuelan oil would actually hurt American oil producers—the opposite of Trump’s stated goal.]

THE SENATE REBELS: 52-47 AND THE WAR POWERS RECKONING

Just days after Trump celebrated his military triumph in Venezuela, he suffered a rare defeat at home—and it came from his own party.

Thursday, January 9: The Senate voted 52-47 to open debate on limiting Trump’s military actions in Venezuela. Five Republicans joined Democrats in what CNN called “the clearest sign that the president might not enjoy as free a hand as he has become accustomed to.”

The five Republican defectors:

  • Susan Collins (Maine)

  • Josh Hawley (Missouri)

  • Lisa Murkowski (Alaska)

  • Rand Paul (Kentucky)

  • Todd Young (Indiana)

Senator Josh Hawley’s reasoning: “Would Congress need to weigh in if the administration decided they needed to commit troops to the future for hostilities? Based on what I know and my reading of the Constitution, I just kind of think we would have to vote on that.”

The measure (Tim Kaine resolution): Would bar further use of U.S. military for “hostilities within or against Venezuela”—invoking the 1983 War Powers statute that requires joint resolution with presidential signature.

Why it’s happening: Midterm elections loom in November 2026. Republicans facing competitive races are distancing themselves from Trump’s imperial overreach.

Trump’s response: Lashed out on social media, urged electoral defeat of all five defectors—including Susan Collins, whose re-election is critical to Republicans maintaining their Senate majority.

Collins’ response: Suggested Trump would rather see a Democrat win.

Trump’s supporters see it differently: Many in his base view the Venezuela operation as bold “America First” action, praising it as effective “law enforcement” against narco-terrorism without the nation-building mistakes of Iraq and Afghanistan. The partisan divide reflects fundamentally different views of presidential power and American intervention.

The constitutional lie exposed: Trump and Vance claimed “all presidents have considered War Powers Resolution unconstitutional.” The New York Times’ Charlie Savage fact-checked this as misleading and false.

The 1993 Justice Department memo: “Congress’ power to declare war necessarily includes the power ultimately to determine whether the nation is at war or in peace. A joint resolution ordering that troops be withdrawn from certain hostilities is in essence a declaration that the nation will not be at war.”

Translation: The specific War Powers mechanism Kaine is using is widely accepted as constitutional. Trump and Vance are lying about legal consensus.

Next step: Decisive vote expected this week. If it passes, Trump would need to withdraw forces or veto the resolution (which requires two-thirds override).

The broader pattern: After a year of largely compliant Republican Congress with no appetite to challenge Trump, lawmakers concerned about political futures are finally asserting themselves.

House actions this week also showed cracks:

  • 17 Republicans sided with Democrats on ACA subsidy extension

  • Dozens joined Democrats in unsuccessful veto override attempts

  • Spending bills rejected Trump’s steep cuts

  • Senate agreed to Jan 6 police memorial plaque (despite White House claiming police caused violence)

Carl Hulse (NYT): “Public sentiment in terms of how Trump is behaving as president and what he’s doing as president keeps sinking and sinking and sinking. So I think on this issue and other issues, you’re going to find our Republican colleagues saying, ‘You know, maybe following Trump is like Thelma and Louise — right over the cliff.’”

LATIN AMERICA SCRAMBLES: “ALL TRYING TO AVOID CONFLICT WITH TRUMP”

By dawn on January 3, WhatsApp had already lit up across Latin America with frantic messages from heads of state, grappling with how to respond to the region’s biggest political event in decades.

The United States had just swept into Venezuela and captured its president.

The responses exposed fault lines cracking Latin America apart—and revealed a common goal across ideological divides: self-preservation.

Jorge G. Castañeda (former Mexican foreign minister): “All of the countries are trying to avoid a conflict with Trump.”

Trump made his intentions explicit: “American dominance in the Western Hemisphere will never be questioned again.”

MEXICO: BOXED IN ON ALL SIDES

The panic: A senior Mexican government official told The New York Times: “When we saw what they did in Venezuela, it made us think, ‘Oh boy, this is more serious than we thought, and we are on the list of who could be next, and worse, we have been warned.’“

President Claudia Sheinbaum faces impossible constraints:

  • Must reject U.S. intervention (sovereignty)

  • Cannot provoke Trump (largest trading partner)

  • Must appease leftist party (holds majority, never condemned Maduro)

  • Trump Thursday: “We are going to start now hitting land, with regard to the cartels” in Mexico

The White House message: After Sheinbaum posted UN charter passage about sovereignty, the White House responded with Trump clip: “She’s a good woman, but the cartels are running Mexico.”

Mexican officials: “Had to rethink how to communicate our position and proceed with absolute caution.”

The Maduro indictment problem: Mentions Mexico 25 times, charging Maduro with partnering with Sinaloa Cartel and facilitating drug money through Mexico. Sheinbaum is “acutely sensitive” to being linked to Maduro.

Sheinbaum’s team tracking anxiety: Created chart showing Mexico mentions vs. other countries in U.S. media. Mentions rose after Venezuela attack, dropped when Greenland coverage surged. Official: “Expressed relief that Mexico was, for now, out of the spotlight.”

Cabinet divisions: Sharp disagreements over how carefully to calibrate Venezuela response. Some worry repeated condemnations will backfire on tariff negotiations and USMCA free trade review this summer.

Mexico’s strategy now: Hit cartels even harder, go after Mexican officials with organized crime ties. “Actions speak louder than words.”

Jesús Silva-Herzog (analyst): “The president is boxed in on all sides.”

The broader accommodation: Over past year, Mexico imposed 50% tariffs on Chinese goods, stepped up border enforcement, taken on cartels—all to satisfy Trump. He still threatens invasion.

BRAZIL: LEVERAGE BUT CAUTION

President Lula has more room to push back:

  • Trade with China roughly doubles U.S. trade

  • Just secured EU free-trade deal (more leverage)

  • Poll numbers rose during Trump feud

  • Favored to win fourth term this year

Lula’s response: One of clearest rebukes, calling Venezuela invasion “yet another extremely dangerous precedent.”

But tactful: Mostly avoided naming Trump directly. Pushing back on Washington plays well domestically, but punishing fight to defend unpopular autocrat Maduro risks presidential campaign.

Celso Amorim (Lula’s chief foreign policy adviser): “Of course we want to have good relations with the United States. It’s a very important country in every sense. But there’s no way to avoid condemning this action.”

Behind scenes: Lula and Colombia’s Petro discussed building diplomatic coalition against Trump interventions, hoping to win support from Mexico, Chile, France, Spain. But idea preliminary, no traction yet.

COLOMBIA: DEFIANCE THEN ACCOMMODATION

Initial response: President Gustavo Petro posted incessantly, sparred with Elon Musk, shared casualty photos, said “Latin America must emancipate itself again.”

Trump escalated immediately: Called Petro “sick man,” accused him of “trafficking cocaine,” said “he is not going to be doing it very long.” Asked if considering military action against Colombia: “It sounds good to me.”

The crisis: Petro called Colombians to streets, said he’d take up arms himself, government expanded his security.

The capitulation: Nearly hour-long call Wednesday (partly brokered by Rand Paul). Petro pledged to fight cartels, increased coca eradication. They “agreed to soon meet.”

Political reality: Petro’s handpicked successor on ballot in May. Colombians want to avoid tariffs or attack. Accommodation was necessary for political survival.

ARGENTINA: REWARDED FOR LOYALTY

President Javier Milei: Trump’s “most enthusiastic ally” in region.

The payoff: Trump threw Milei $20 billion lifeline during economic crisis, helped him win Congressional midterms.

The strings: Argentina agreed to changes on intellectual property, opening critical minerals.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent (Friday): Milei “continues to deliver with full force.”

The pattern: Right-wing allies praised and rewarded. Left-wing critics threatened with invasion. Everyone calculating survival.

The regional shift: Chile, Bolivia, Honduras swung right in recent months. Brazil, Colombia, Peru could follow this year—giving Trump like-minded leaders throughout Americas.

GREENLAND: “THE HARD WAY” AND EUROPE’S PANIC

While Venezuela occupied headlines, Trump doubled down on territorial expansion elsewhere.

Friday, January 10: “I would like to make a deal the easy way, but if we don’t do it the easy way, we’re going to do it the hard way.”

Earlier, Stephen Miller (Monday): Asked to rule out military force to seize Greenland—refused. “The real question is, by what right does Denmark assert control over Greenland? Obviously, Greenland should be part of the United States.”

Miller didn’t just refuse to rule out force—he mocked the idea Europe could stand up to American troops.

EUROPE’S RESPONSE: NEAR PANIC

More than dozen diplomatic/military officials interviewed by NYT: Miller’s comments “blew apart” Denmark PM Mette Frederiksen’s yearlong effort to quietly dissuade Trump. They “stunned a visibly frazzled” Frederiksen and created “sense of near panic” among European counterparts.

Frederiksen’s warning: U.S. attempt to take Greenland by force would “mean the end of the NATO alliance.”

Danish Defense Ministry: If Greenland invaded, Danish soldiers “allowed to shoot without waiting for instructions from superiors.”

The NATO problem: Forcibly taking Greenland would violate Article 5 (attack on one = attack on all). Denmark is founding NATO member.

Trump’s dismissal: “When we own it, we defend it. You don’t defend leases the same way. You have to own it.”

Trump’s justification (repeated false claim): “If we don’t do it, Russia or China will take over Greenland”—falsely suggesting Greenland “surrounded by Chinese and Russian warships.”

Reality: Russia/China active in Arctic, but not surrounding Greenland. U.S. already has military base on Greenland and “full military access” through defense agreement with Denmark.

ORLA JOELSEN’S LETTER: THE HUMAN VOICE

While Miller questioned Denmark’s right to Greenland, a Greenlandic citizen named Orla Joelsen wrote directly to Congress:

“The people of Greenland are not for sale. The citizens of Greenland are not slaves. Slavery was abolished in 1865, and the idea that a people or their land can be acquired has no place in the modern world.”

Joelsen’s appeal:

  • Children to elderly “feeling unsafe and deeply unsettled”

  • “There are no Russian or Chinese naval forces operating in Greenlandic waters. Claims suggesting otherwise are false and misleading.”

  • U.S. already has “full military access to Greenland” through defense agreement

  • “You took an oath to the Constitution, not to President Trump.”

  • “The world is watching. The people of Greenland are watching. Action is required now.”

The parallel to Venezuela: Both populations saying “we don’t want this.” Trump saying “I don’t care what you want.” Justifications (narco-terrorism, Chinese/Russian threat) disputed by locals.

EUROPEAN OPTIONS UNDER DISCUSSION

Option 1: Legal status change without ownership change

  • Greenlanders vote independence, associate with U.S. for security/economic benefits

  • “U.S. could act in Greenland as if it owned it, but without doing so”

  • Would require “significant economic offer” (Denmark provides ~$1B/year now)

Trump’s response: “Ownership is very important... psychologically needed for success. I think that ownership gives you a thing that you can’t do, whether you’re talking about a lease or a treaty.”

Option 2: NATO military buildup in Greenland

  • Maritime exercise with warships/icebreakers

  • Counter Chinese/Russian Arctic activity

  • Symbol of allied commitment

Option 3: Rare minerals deal

  • Provide U.S. with Greenland minerals for high-tech/military manufacturing

  • Without formal sovereignty transfer

The consensus: Europe must avoid damaging confrontation through negotiation. Concern expressed privately: “Greenland is not about security or economic resources, but about Mr. Trump’s desire to ‘make America greater again’ and go down in history as having expanded American territory for the first time in decades.”

European meetings cascade: Tuesday Paris, Wednesday Paris, Thursday Brussels, Friday Brussels—constant coordination trying to find response that doesn’t escalate but doesn’t appear weak.

Republican pushback in Congress:

  • Senator Roger Wicker (Armed Services Chair): Greenlanders don’t want to be bought, U.S. should respect that

  • Senator Lisa Murkowski: Trump rhetoric “profoundly troubling,” option should be “taken off the table”

IRAN: THE PARADOX IN ACTION—DEATH TOLL EXPLODES

An image taken from a social media post verified by The New York Times showing crowds of people around body bags on the outskirts of Tehran.

As Week Two progressed, the most dramatic—and deadly—manifestation of Trump’s Venezuela precedent played out in Iran.

THE DEATH TOLL SURGES: ESTIMATES AMID INTERNET BLACKOUT

Sunday, January 11 (6:53 PM ET)—latest estimates (note: verification extremely difficult due to three-day internet blackout):

Iran Human Rights (Oslo): 192 killed

HRANA (Washington): Nearly 500 protesters + almost 50 security personnel killed

Center for Human Rights in Iran (New York): “Hundreds and possibly thousands of deaths”

Hadi Ghaemi (CHRI director): “The violence has certainly skyrocketed in the past several days as the internet shutdown has been implemented. There are hundreds and possibly thousands of deaths. It will only become clear when the internet shutdown ends.“

Important caveat: All death toll estimates are aggregated from limited sources during severe communications blackout. Actual casualties remain unverified and could be substantially different once information access restores.

Verified videos Sunday: Dozens of black body bags lined up outside Forensic Diagnostic Centre in Kahrizak (Tehran outskirts). Crowds unzipping bags to identify loved ones, people weeping, offering comfort.

The scale: HRANA tracking 570+ protests in 185 cities across all 31 provinces. More than 10,000 arrested total.

Hospitals overwhelmed: Running out of blood, emergency rooms overwhelmed with gunshot wounds and pellet-gun injuries to eyes. Security forces occupying hospitals in Kermanshah and Karaj. Injured protesters removed from hospitals to detention facilities. Protesters fearful of seeking treatment.

Government crackdown intensifies: Armed men firing weapons on empty streets to intimidate. Revolutionary Guards + paramilitary “roaming streets in large numbers” with machine guns. Power cuts, highway lights turned off Saturday night.

Prosecutor General Mohammad Movahedi Azad (Saturday): Prosecute “without leniency, mercy or appeasement.” Charge: “Enemy of God” (carries death penalty).

Supreme Leader Khamenei (Friday): “Will not back down.”

Government announced: Three-day national mourning for security forces killed. Called for “National Resistance March” on Monday.

THE VENEZUELA CONNECTION: EMBOLDENING BOTH SIDES

Iranian protesters explicitly citing Venezuela as proof Trump will support them:

Chants from protests: “This is the final battle. Pahlavi will return.”

Reza Pahlavi (exiled crown prince) on Fox News Sunday morning—direct appeal to Trump:

“They know that you’re not going to throw them under the bus, as has happened before. This is why they are empowered—by the hope that you have their back.”

Asked if he wants U.S. to “take out Khamenei”: “The people of Iran have responded and reacted positively to a promise of intervention.”

“We need to cut the snake’s head off for good so it can no longer be a threat to Iranian interests, to American interests, to regional interests. And the only solution is to make sure this regime goes down for good and the Iranian people can liberate themselves.”

Venezuelan precedent made explicit: “Since the U.S. operation to capture Venezuela’s president, Nicolás Maduro, Mr. Trump has indicated several times that he was considering striking Iran’s leadership.”

But Khamenei also using Venezuela to justify crackdown:

President Pezeshkian (initially sympathetic, now hardened): “The enemy has brought trained terrorists into the country”—referring to U.S.

Pezeshkian’s Venezuela reference (Sunday): “Someone who shamelessly steals a country’s presidency and then says he was after that country’s oil is not someone who cares about you.”

Parliament Speaker Mohammad Ghalibaf (Sunday): “If the United States takes military action, both the occupied territories and U.S. military and shipping lanes will be our legitimate targets.” Both U.S. and Israeli military bases could be targets.

The paradox playing out in real-time:

Emboldening democracy: Iranian protesters believe Trump will support regime change (citing Venezuela)

Emboldening authoritarianism: Khamenei using Venezuela to claim foreign interference justifies crackdown

Same invasion inspiring both protesters and autocratic violence.

TRUMP BRIEFED ON IRAN STRIKE OPTIONS

Saturday: Multiple U.S. officials confirmed Trump has been briefed on “new options for military strikes in Iran”—both lethal force and nonlethal (cyberattacks to restore internet).

Trump Saturday: “Iran is looking at FREEDOM, perhaps like never before. The USA stands ready to help!!!”

Earlier Trump: “Locked and loaded and ready to go” if Iran kills protesters.

The Atlantic analysis (January 10): “Is the Iranian Regime About to Collapse?”—examining five conditions for successful revolution. Conclusion: For first time since 1979, Iran meets nearly all five boxes.

The missing piece: IRGC commanders still unified. “If they did” defect, “the regime would not survive.”

THE BROADER PATTERN

As I documented in my January 5 essay 'Venezuela: The Paradox, Emboldening Both Democracy and Authoritarianism,' this pattern was predictable.

The paradox:

  • Venezuelan invasion gives protesters hope (Trump supports regime change)

  • Venezuelan invasion gives autocrats excuse (foreign interference justifies crackdowns)

  • Same precedent working both directions simultaneously

Iran validates the pattern. Protesters chanting for Pahlavi, believing Trump has their back. Khamenei ordering massacres, claiming U.S. interference justifies it.

Both citing Venezuela.

CUBA: “MAKE A DEAL, BEFORE IT’S TOO LATE”

Sunday morning, Trump escalated from Venezuela to Cuba:

Truth Social post: “No more oil or money” to Cuba from Venezuela. U.S. military “will be involved in enforcing distance.” “Make a deal, before it is too late.”

Trump shared post: Suggesting Marco Rubio should be Cuba’s president. Trump: “Sounds good to me.”

Cuban President Miguel Díaz-Canel response: “Cuba is a free, independent and sovereign nation. No one tells us what to do.” “Ready to defend the homeland to the last drop of blood.”

The pressure on Rodríguez: Must cut U.S. into oil production, must cut off Cuba. If she doesn’t comply, Trump could seize tankers heading to Cuba—U.S. already engaged in “partial blockade of Venezuela’s oil industry.”

The economic impact on Cuba:

  • Needs ~100,000 barrels/day

  • Domestic production covers less than half

  • Venezuela supplying 30,000-35,000 barrels/day

  • Loss would be “catastrophic”

Unclear what “deal” means: Cuba has little to offer U.S. in return.

The pattern expanding: Venezuela → Greenland → Iran → Cuba. Trump threatening multiple countries simultaneously, all citing Venezuelan precedent or using Venezuelan oil as leverage.

MACHADO’S NOBEL DESPERATION: NORWAY SAYS NO

The week also brought surreal developments around María Corina Machado, Venezuela’s opposition leader who won the 2024 election but was barred from ballot.

Monday, Fox News: Machado offered to give her Nobel Peace Prize to Trump. “We believe the Venezuelan people—because this is a prize of the Venezuelan people—certainly want to give it to him and share it with him.”

Trump Thursday: “It would be a great honor” to accept, adding it was “a major embarrassment to Norway” that he didn’t win the prize.

Norwegian Nobel Institute (Friday): “Once a Nobel Prize is announced, it cannot be revoked, shared or transferred to others. The decision is final and stands for all time.”

The accommodation trap springs: Machado won election, had “greatest popular legitimacy to lead nation.” Trump rejected her—doesn’t have “necessary support or respect” to govern. Backed Delcy Rodríguez (Maduro’s VP) instead.

Now Machado desperately trying to win Trump’s favor by offering most prestigious prize to the dictator who invaded her country and backs her opponent.

Trump meeting Machado next week in Washington—after rejecting her as leader.

RODRÍGUEZ USES U.S. MILITARY AGAINST INTERNAL RIVALS

Perhaps the most revealing development of Week Two: the occupation became operational.

Friday, January 10: A tanker called the Olina left Venezuelan port without authorization, carrying 500,000 barrels of oil belonging to Alex Saab—billionaire Colombian businessman, former Maduro ally, indicted by U.S. in 2019 for money laundering.

The internal power struggle:

  • Rodríguez + brother Jorge = control economic policy and legislature

  • Saab = owed business to Maduro, wife Cilia Flores, her relatives (most under U.S. sanctions)

  • “Tense personal relationship” between Rodríguez and Saab

  • Different factions of Venezuela’s fractured ruling coalition

What Rodríguez did: Enlisted U.S. military support to force tanker back.

U.S. Coast Guard and Navy boarded Olina in Caribbean, forced return to Venezuelan port.

Trump confirmed: Tanker “departed Venezuela without our approval,” returning “in coordination” with Rodríguez.

The stunning accommodation:

Just one week earlier, U.S. military attacked Caracas, killing at least 100 Venezuelans.

In September, Rodríguez told NYT: “The Pentagon always had a strategic objective of obtaining Venezuelan reserves. There’s no doubt that one of the strategic objectives is what is called regime change.”

Now: U.S. soldiers helping Rodríguez assert control of Venezuela’s oil against internal rivals.

Rodríguez speaks “all the time” with Marco Rubio.

The occupation isn’t rhetorical—it’s operational. U.S. forces are helping Venezuelan interim leader consolidate power against Venezuelan factions.

This is what “we’re in charge” actually looks like.

DIPLOMATIC CONTRADICTIONS: RESTORING TIES WHILE DENOUNCING “KIDNAPPING”

Friday announcements created head-spinning contradictions:

Venezuela: “Exploring restoring diplomatic ties,” sending delegation to Washington

U.S.: Diplomats visited Caracas to assess “potential phased resumption of operations” at embassy (closed since 2019)

Rodríguez’s statement: BEGAN by denouncing “kidnapping” of Maduro and “flagrant violation of international legal order,” THEN announced “diplomatic exploratory process” to address “consequences arising from the aggression.”

Trump: Says getting along “extremely well” with government that’s essentially same as week ago (minus Maduro).

The legal problem (Elliott Abrams, former Trump envoy):

“If Delcy Rodríguez, who was appointed vice president by Maduro, is the legitimate interim president, doesn’t that mean you think Nicolás Maduro was a legitimate president? And if you do, doesn’t that affect his trial? Does he have head-of-state immunity?“

Maduro’s lawyers already fighting: Bruce Fein claims backing from “Flores’s family” (Maduro’s wife), says “Maduro’s insiders—including brother-in-law—suspected betrayal.” Barry Pollack (represented Maduro at arraignment) disputes this. Internal factions even fighting over legal representation.

Embassy reopening complications: Mold in Caracas’s muggy climate, diplomats’ safety concerns, legal questions about recognizing Rodríguez while prosecuting Maduro.

PUTIN’S STRATEGIC SILENCE: GLOBAL INFLUENCE COLLAPSING

Week since Maduro captured. Putin has said nothing.

Why: “He has one goal, which is to come out on top in Ukraine, and everything else is subordinated to that goal,” said Hanna Notte (Eurasia program director).

Russian restraint examples:

  • Wednesday: U.S. seized Russian-flagged tanker—only 3-paragraph Transport Ministry statement

  • Maduro capture: Complete silence

  • Nation that “periodically threatened nuclear war” now shows “exceptional restraint”

The broader unraveling of Russian global power:

  • Syria: Assad regime collapsed after decade of Russian intervention

  • Iran: Mass protests imperil Kremlin-friendly government

  • Armenia/Azerbaijan: Signed peace at White House (not Moscow mediation)

  • Central Asia/Caucasus/Moldova: Eroding Russian influence

  • Venezuela: Top Latin American partner captured, Russia silent

Friday: Russia launched nuclear-capable Oreshnik missile at western Ukraine (near EU border)—because “he couldn’t do anything in Latin America, couldn’t do anything against Trump, so he struck Ukraine,” said Ukrainian analyst.

Putin’s calculation: Ukraine negotiations trump everything. Can’t risk antagonizing Trump over “peripheral issues.” Willing to sacrifice Venezuela and global influence for Ukraine victory.

Putin’s long game: If Greenland invasion happens, NATO destroyed = Putin’s dream. Worth staying silent on Venezuela to potentially gain that.

CONCLUSION: THE LIMITS OF MILITARY POWER

Week One was military triumph. Week Two exposed every limit of what military force can actually achieve.

Military success:

  • Captured Maduro ✓

  • Bombed Caracas ✓

  • Killed 80+ ✓

  • Installed compliant leader ✓

  • Declared “we’re in charge” ✓

Everything else:

  • Oil companies say “uninvestable” ✗ (detailed economic analysis in forthcoming essay)

  • Senate rebels 52-47 ✗

  • Latin America calculates survival, not compliance ✗

  • Iran death toll explodes, both sides cite Venezuela ✗

  • Greenland threatens NATO collapse ✗

  • Cuba defiant despite threats ✗

  • Mexico panicked but boxed in ✗

  • Russia silent but Ukraine still at war ✗

You can invade a country with Delta Force. You can capture its president with Special Operations. You can bomb its military with F-35s.

But you cannot:

  • Make unprofitable oil extraction profitable (more on this economics in next essay)

  • Force Congress to surrender war powers

  • Prevent allies from calculating self-preservation

  • Control how precedents embolden both protesters and autocrats

  • Threaten NATO allies without consequences

  • Occupy indefinitely without costs

The gap between military capability and political/economic/diplomatic reality is unbridgeable.

Trump invaded Venezuela expecting oil would flow, Congress would comply, allies would cheer, enemies would cower.

Reality delivered: “Uninvestable.” Senate rebellion. Panicked allies. Exploding death tolls. Diplomatic chaos. Operational occupation with no exit strategy.

Week One: Military triumph

Week Two: Every other kind of failure

The invasion was easy. Everything after is hard.

And no amount of military force changes the math, the politics, the economics, or the global response.

That’s the difference between conquering territory and governing consequences.

Trump learned how to do the first. Week Two is teaching him the cost of the second.
