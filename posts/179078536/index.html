PART III: DEFLATING THE CULT

Understanding the Epstein files as documentation of foreign intelligence compromise rather than mere sex scandal requires rethinking opposition strategy. The goal isn’t converting MAGA into Democrats. It’s deflating the cult.

VI. THE STRATEGY FORWARD

As Parnas explained in his November 16 Substack article: “Trump didn’t just win by converting Republicans. He woke up a slice of the electorate that never voted—the far-right, the disaffected, the conspiracy crowd—and pointed them at our democracy.”

The mistake Democratic strategists make is assuming those voters can be persuaded through policy arguments or moral appeals. They can’t. But they can be disillusioned.

“Our job now is to: Expose Trump as a fraud to his own base, so those people see he’s not a strongman, he’s a puppet—a balloon dictator inflated by Russian kompromat, billionaire donors, and right-wing media. Show them that the ‘warrior’ they worship is terrified of the Epstein–Russia truth, terrified of losing leverage, terrified of going to prison. Make them stay home.”

The key metric isn’t Democratic votes gained. It’s Trump votes lost: “A no-show from a former Trump voter is a win for democracy. A no vote for Trump is a vote for us.”

This strategy explains why focusing on the Epstein files—particularly the intelligence aspect—is so powerful. It’s not a partisan issue. As Parnas emphasized on the podcast: “If you’re a Democrat or Republican, you don’t want to protect pedophiles. You don’t want to allow criminals... This is not about right or left. This is about being able to speak about policy again, being able to agree to disagree.”

The two issues that can break Trump’s hold on his base are:

  1. The Economy: Everyone cares about gas prices, food costs, inflation—regardless of party

  2. The Epstein Files: Nobody—Republican or Democrat—wants their president running “a pedophile protection racket out of the White House”

These issues work because they transcend partisan identity. A Trump voter who believed he would “drain the swamp” confronted with evidence that Trump spent 40 years compromised by Russian intelligence doesn’t need to become a Democrat. They just need to stay home.

That’s the achievable goal. That’s what Parnas described as making them say: “You know what, I’m out. I’m done. I’m not voting for this guy.”

What Democracy Needs

Parnas emphasized on the podcast what he’s been preaching “for months and months”: “You see how good it feels to be on the offense. This is part of exactly what we need to be. We need Donald Trump, we need MAGA to be on the defense.”

The two issues putting Trump “on his ass” right now are “the Epstein files, the economy. Those are the main two things... and we don’t need a million different things. We need to keep pushing it because those are things that are not a partisan issue.”

This strategic focus matters because, as Parnas noted, these issues work across the political spectrum: “Both parties—it’s not about if you’re a Democrat or Republican, you want gas, you want your prices of beef and eggs and everything to be lower. If you’re a Republican or a Democrat, you don’t want to protect pedophiles.”

The goal Parnas articulated is “getting our country back. This is about being able to speak about policy again, being able to agree to disagree. Being able to try to say, okay, well, I’m more Progressive, I’m more conservative, and still go have coffee afterwards or go watch a baseball game.”

He described the current moment as about “getting back to the Thanksgiving Day table” where families destroyed by political division can reconcile. The polarization, Parnas argued, “is the total opposite of what America is all about. This is a total opposite of what makes America great, and this is why America was a beacon and looked up to by every country in the world.”

The villain in this story isn’t just Trump. “It wasn’t just Donald Trump,” Parnas explained. “It was because of people like Vladimir Putin who have been working this for a long time.” The Russian operation to compromise American leadership, the weaponization of cultural division, the elevation of extremists—these represent a systematic effort to destroy American democracy from within.

Trump, according to Parnas, “unleashed” an “internal far-right fraction inside of the United States that didn’t come out and vote. There was hidden... that was part of our plan back in 15, 16, to hit that part to be able to unleash him.”

The metaphor Parnas used is chilling: “It’s like watching those movies when you unleash the criminals out of the cells, and then they take over the prison. Well, this is what’s basically happening. He basically unleashed them, and then also what he was able to do is put them in certain positions of power, and now some of these people are not only unleashed, but they are in control and in power.”

What Can Stop Him

Parnas is clear about what can stop Trump and the forces he’s unleashed: “There is one thing Trump and every wannabe strongman fears: a united, loud, informed public that refuses to be bullied.”

The requirements are:

  • Keep exposing the truth behind Epstein, Russia, and Trump’s fear

  • Keep pushing Republicans in Congress until some grow a backbone

  • Keep pressuring DOJ and demanding transparency

  • Keep showing up—calling, voting, refusing to normalize

But success requires understanding what Trump and his inner circle have planned if legal and political pressure becomes too intense. Parnas warned about the dark scenarios they’ve gamed out:

  • Misusing the Insurrection Act to turn federal power against American citizens

  • Reckless foreign adventures (Trump “runs on peace” but renamed the Defense Department the “Department of War” and has “10,000 troops in the Mediterranean with an aircraft carrier”)

  • Using government shutdowns, mass deportations, and administrative chaos as tools of political control rather than policy

These aren’t hypotheticals. According to Parnas’s sources, Trump’s team has actively planned these scenarios as contingencies.

The counter is sustained pressure from an informed public that understands what’s actually at stake. Not partisan warfare. Democratic survival.

The Pathological Liar Problem

Parnas and Blundell discussed a fundamental challenge in covering Trump: his pathological dishonesty. As Blundell noted, “You can say whatever you want, what you see him do is completely different because he’s a pathological liar.”

Parnas agreed, explaining why people sometimes struggle to understand Trump’s actions: “There’s only a small group of people that really understand, know what’s really going on.” He compared it to Ukraine, where “nobody except for me, Rudy, John Solomon, Victoria Toensing, Joe diGenova, Devin Nunes, Kash Patel—nobody else really knew anything about what was going on in Ukraine.”

“Everybody else, like Pompeo, John Bolton, all of the leadership, all of the diplomats to Congress—everybody else thought that there was one policy.” That’s the shadow diplomacy pattern: public statements for general consumption, actual policy known only to a small group implementing the real agenda.

This is why, Parnas argued, “you have to cover everything he says with the fact that he’s a pathological liar. While he says one thing to you, he’s completely doing something else behind the scenes.”

The Epstein files represent the same pattern. Trump’s public statements about “never knowing Epstein well” or “banning him from Mar-a-Lago” serve as cover while the documentary evidence reveals decades of close coordination—including, potentially, intelligence operations run through Epstein’s network with Trump as a compromised asset.

VII. REFRAMING THE SERIES

Across eight episodes, we documented Trump’s accommodation psychology as it escalated through increasingly desperate stages. We tracked his progression from information manipulation to strategic silence, from complexity overload to institutional force, from weaponizing government to abusing legal process. We showed how he orchestrated a pretextual DOJ investigation to block a congressional discharge petition. We revealed bipartisan elite protection and institutional resistance to transparency.

At each stage, the explanation seemed clear: Trump was protecting himself and powerful allies from exposure for knowing about—and perhaps participating in—Jeffrey Epstein’s sex trafficking operation. The accommodation appeared to be about shame, embarrassment, and potential criminal liability for sex crimes.

But we were analyzing the surface while missing the depth.

Trump’s accommodation didn’t cross every constitutional boundary because of embarrassing photos. It went nuclear because the files document something far more dangerous to him: evidence of a 40-year Russian intelligence operation that compromised him, with Jeffrey Epstein serving as one conduit between Trump and the Kremlin.

The June 24, 2018 email where Epstein referenced Vitaly Churkin as his past handler and positioned himself to provide Putin insight on Trump through Lavrov. The evidence of Epstein advising multiple countries’ finance ministers and meeting directly with Putin. The sealed DOJ files containing intelligence assessments connecting Epstein’s network to foreign actors. Ghislaine Maxwell’s knowledge of which intelligence services were watching, recording, and collecting. The bipartisan elite silence that transcends normal partisan incentives.

The sex trafficking was real, devastating, and criminal—Virginia Giuffre and other victims deserve justice, not minimization. But for Trump specifically, the accommodation psychology that drove him to weaponize the Justice Department and risk constitutional crisis wasn’t primarily about shame over those crimes. It was about what those crimes enabled: a systematic intelligence operation that used sexual compromise to control American political leadership.

It was about kompromat. About documented intelligence compromise. About proof that the “America First” president spent decades bowing to Vladimir Putin not out of admiration or ideology, but because Putin held his leash.

That’s why both Trump’s DOJ and Biden’s DOJ refused to prosecute beyond Maxwell. That’s why judges sealed documents citing “national security.” That’s why elite media coverage remained muted. That’s why congressional leadership on both sides avoided prioritizing transparency.

The discharge petition doesn’t just threaten to expose sex crimes at Mar-a-Lago. It threatens to expose documented intelligence compromise of American political leadership across party lines—by Russia, possibly by Israel, potentially by other foreign powers that ran collection operations through Epstein’s network.

That’s why Trump weaponized the Justice Department. That’s why he ordered a pretextual investigation designed to block Congress indefinitely. That’s why the accommodation crossed every boundary. That’s why constitutional mechanisms face institutional resistance even from those who should champion transparency.

And that’s why—as Lev Parnas warned those still protecting this operation—continuing the cover-up doesn’t just make someone a hypocrite. It makes them a traitor to their country.

The files document who was controlling whom, and how.

Trump will do anything—anything—to prevent that documentation from becoming public.

Now we understand why.

WHAT COMES NEXT

Trump’s Sunday night capitulation looked like surrender. The headlines called it a “rare retreat,” a “reversal,” evidence that “he lost control of his party.”

But those who have studied Trump’s accommodation psychology across eight episodes recognize a different pattern: the strategic retreat to a prepared defensive position.

Consider the timing. On Friday—two days before his public capitulation—Trump ordered Attorney General Pam Bondi to launch a new investigation into Epstein’s ties to Democrats. Bondi immediately assigned Manhattan U.S. Attorney Jay Clayton to lead the probe. By Sunday night, when Trump posted his “I DON’T CARE” reversal, that investigation was already in place.

Representative Massie saw it immediately. On ABC’s “This Week” Sunday morning, hours before Trump’s capitulation, Massie warned: “If they have ongoing investigations in certain areas, those documents can’t be released. So this might be a big smoke screen, these investigations, to open a bunch of them... as a last-ditch effort to prevent the release of the Epstein files.”

Former federal prosecutors agree. Barbara McQuade, former U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Michigan, told TIME: “My cynical view of this announcement is that it is a strategic effort to block the release of further documents in the Epstein case. If there is a pending investigation, the DOJ can assert executive privilege and try to prevent any more documents from being released.”

The Epstein Files Transparency Act—which the House votes on today, November 18—contains the loopholes that make this strategy viable. The bill allows DOJ to withhold materials that would “jeopardize an ongoing federal investigation.” It gives the department 30 days to release files—30 days to redact, to claim investigative privilege, to decide what Congress actually receives.

Trump’s “capitulation” wasn’t defeat. It was Phase 2.

In Part 10, we’ll examine the strategic retreat in detail: how the ongoing investigation creates legal cover to withhold the most damaging files, what the bill’s loopholes actually allow, why legal experts believe DOJ will never release the complete intelligence assessments, and what happens when the sanitized version of “transparency” arrives—with Trump’s name scrubbed from the files that matter most.

The vote will pass today. The real fight over what Congress actually receives is just beginning.
