The Baseline Problem

The Moral Cortex

PART 12: When Cognitive Decline Erases the Line Between Cruelty and Strategy

April 11, 2026

There is a question serious people have been avoiding.

Not whether Donald Trump is cruel. That debate ended years ago. Not whether he is erratic. Easter weekend closed that file permanently. The question serious people have been avoiding is this: at what point does cruelty stop being a character flaw and become a symptom?

I believe we have reached that point.

What Happened on Easter Sunday

At 8:03 AM on Easter Sunday, the President of the United States posted this to Truth Social:

“A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again.”

He was referring to Iran. Seventy million people. Ancient Persia. One of the oldest continuous civilizations on Earth.

By Tuesday, he had backed down — agreeing to a ceasefire. By Wednesday, he was excitedly musing about partnering with Iran to collect tolls on ships passing through the Strait of Hormuz.

Genocide to partnership in 72 hours. This is not the arc of a negotiator. This is not even the arc of a bully. This is the arc of a man whose internal compass — moral, strategic, emotional — is no longer functioning as a reliable instrument.

Fareed Zakaria, appearing on Ezra Klein’s podcast this week, called the Easter post “the culmination of something that had been going on for a while” — the abandonment of the entire moral framework America had maintained since 1945. He is right about what it means geopolitically. But there is another layer he did not address.

What does it mean clinically?

The Prefrontal Cortex Does Not Separate Morality From Cognition

This is the part the political press refuses to engage with, because it sounds like armchair psychiatry. It is not. It is neuroscience.

The prefrontal cortex — the region most vulnerable to age-related neurodegeneration — is responsible for two functions that are, in healthy adults, deeply intertwined: impulse control and moral reasoning.

These are not metaphors for good behaviour. They are cognitive operations. The capacity to think: if I say this, what happens to other people — that is a prefrontal function. The capacity to hold a course of action in mind long enough to evaluate its consequences — that is a prefrontal function. The capacity to feel the weight of another human being’s suffering before acting — that is a prefrontal function.

When that region degrades, what you lose is not just strategic coherence. You lose the neural architecture that makes moral calculation possible.

A healthy 79-year-old can retain this capacity. Many do. But a 79-year-old with the documented pattern of behaviours we have catalogued in this series — the accelerating oscillation, the grandiose threats followed by abrupt reversals, the inability to hold a consistent position across hours let alone days — that 79-year-old is showing us something.

The Pattern Has Accelerated

When this series began, we were documenting lapses. Verbal tangents. Confusions of person and place. The subtle erosion of the baseline.

We are no longer in that territory.

In November, Trump threatened to execute members of Congress within 24 hours of being forced to sign legislation he opposed. In January, he explained his own blood physiology in terms his physician called nonsensical. In April, he threatened to erase a civilization of seventy million people — then reversed within three days and proposed a business partnership with the country he had just threatened to annihilate.

Ezra Klein, in his monologue this week, said what many have been thinking: this behaviour should trigger a wellness check. He noted that Trump is becoming more dangerous and erratic as he ages, and as his presidency fails. Klein is not a clinician. But he named the trajectory correctly.

What we are watching is not political volatility. It is not strategic misdirection. Those explanations require a level of sustained intentionality that the pattern no longer supports.

Moral Collapse Is Not Separate From Cognitive Collapse

Here is what Zakaria said that deserves to be held up to the light.

He described the Easter post as the moment America “simply abandoned the entire moral weight that the United States had brought to its world role ever since World War 2.” He noted that Trump seemed to forget — almost immediately — that Iranians are human beings. That they have parents worried about their children. That they have families.

But there is something Zakaria didn’t have in front of him. Something Trump said himself, in the Oval Office, to a New York Times interviewing team on January 8th of this year.

Asked what constrained his power, Trump answered without hesitation.

“Yeah, there is one thing. My own morality. My own mind. It’s the only thing that can stop me.”

Sit with that for a moment. Not the Constitution. Not international law. Not Congress, the courts, or the will of the American people. His own morality. His own mind.

Now ask the question this series was built to ask: what happens when the mind making that claim is no longer functioning reliably? What happens when the morality Trump identifies as the sole constraint on his power is itself a product of a degrading prefrontal cortex — the very region responsible for moral reasoning, impulse control, and the capacity to feel the weight of consequences?

The answer came on Easter Sunday.

He posted a genocide threat at 8 AM. He reversed within 72 hours with no coherent explanation. He pivoted to a business proposal with the same country. He expressed no awareness that anything unusual had occurred.

In a person of intact cognition, you can argue about the morality of threatening a civilization. You can say it was calculated, cynical, deliberately provocative. Those are moral arguments about choices.

But what Trump demonstrated was not a choice in any meaningful sense. The inability to sustain moral consistency — to hold the human weight of a decision across even a few days — is not cruelty alone. Cruelty, at minimum, requires knowing what you are doing. What we are observing is something that has moved beyond cruelty into a different register entirely.

The only thing that can stop him is his own morality.

His own morality threatened to erase seventy million people on Easter Sunday morning.

It is the moral equivalent of a man who cannot remember, three days later, that he threatened to burn the house down.

The Voices You Would Not Expect

The MAGA coalition that built him has begun to name what it sees / Candace Owens, X, April 9, 2026; Donald Trump, Truth Social, April 9, 2026)

Tucker Carlson called the Easter post “a moral crime against the people of the country.” Meghan Kelly said it was “completely irresponsible and disgusting.” Marjorie Taylor Greene called for the 25th Amendment. Alex Jones agreed with her. Candace Owens called Trump a “genocidal lunatic.”

These are not voices from the left. These are the people who helped put him in office.

And when they said so publicly, Trump did what he always does when cornered.

The Projection Reflex

In a 482-word Truth Social tirade posted Thursday, Trump called Carlson, Kelly, Owens and Jones “stupid people,” “troublemakers,” and “nut jobs” who “will say anything necessary for some ‘free’ and cheap publicity.” CNN

The word choice is worth pausing on. “They have one thing in common, Low IQs,” Trump wrote. Yahoo! A man who threatened to erase a civilization on Easter Sunday, reversed course within 72 hours, and then proposed a business partnership with the country he had just threatened to annihilate — that man is calling Tucker Carlson low IQ.

He mocked Carlson’s career, took aim at Kelly’s history with him, insulted Owens while referencing her feud involving France’s first lady, and blasted Jones over his Sandy Hook lies and legal ruin. The Hollywood Reporter It was less a presidential statement than a family brawl — with Iran policy, podcasts, and wounded ego thrown into the same blender.

The responses were instructive. Jones said on his show: “Trump’s mad that he’s wrong. He’s mad that he got set up by Israel. Once a man, twice a child. This is dementia.” NBC News Owens was more surgical. She responded in a single sentence: “It may be time to put Grandpa up in a home.” Latin Times Greene, not directly targeted but incensed, wrote: “We NEVER changed — Trump did.” The Hollywood Reporter

This is the projection reflex in clinical form. Trump accused four people of having low IQs for opposing a war he himself appeared to abandon within days. He accused them of seeking cheap publicity — in a 482-word personal screed that attacked a woman’s appearance, mocked a man’s bankruptcy, and relitigated a debate question from a decade ago.

Trump claimed his critics had “been thrown off Television, lost their Shows, and aren’t even invited on TV because nobody cares about them.” Zero Hedge These are projections of his own deepest fears — irrelevance, rejection, loss of platform — attributed wholesale to the people who just called him a monster.

The clinical literature on frontotemporal deterioration describes exactly this pattern: the inability to distinguish between external criticism and internal anxiety, the compulsive need to invert accusation, the loss of proportionality in response. You do not write 482 words attacking four podcasters when you are confident in your position. You write 482 words like that when something in the accusation has landed — and you lack the cognitive architecture to process it any other way.

Candace Owens called it in one sentence. Alex Jones called it on his show. Marjorie Taylor Greene called it on X.

The only thing that can stop him, Trump told the New York Times, is his own morality.

His own morality wrote all 482 of those words.

The Question This Series Has Always Been Building Toward

Reputations, Zakaria observed in closing, take a lifetime to build and are very easy to destroy.

It is true for human beings. It is true for nations. And it is true for institutions — the presidency, the cabinet, the congressional leadership that has watched this unfold and chosen silence.

The founders, as Klein noted, feared exactly this: we have entrusted tremendous power to a self-dealing narcissist and demagogue who is becoming more dangerous and erratic as he ages.

The Baseline Problem series was never really about diagnosing a president. It was always about this: the gap between what we are observing and what our institutions are willing to name.

That gap has never been wider.

And the president has never been more dangerous.
