The Trump Accountability Project

Trump Organization Fraud

Part 22: $454 Million Judgment (2018-2024)

February 19, 2026

Judge Arthur F. Engoron presides during Donald Trump’s civil fraud trial at New York State Supreme Court, November 2023. Getty Images / Michael M. Santiago.

For decades, Donald Trump built his reputation on a simple premise: he was a brilliant businessman, a self-made billionaire, a master dealmaker. He wrote books about it. He starred in a reality TV show about it. He ran for president on it.

It was all based on fraud.

On February 16, 2024, after an 11-week trial and three years of investigation, New York State Supreme Court Judge Arthur Engoron issued a devastating 92-page ruling: Donald Trump, his adult sons, and the Trump Organization had engaged in persistent and repeated fraud spanning more than a decade.

They had systematically inflated the value of Trump’s assets by hundreds of millions—sometimes billions—of dollars to deceive banks, insurers, and business partners. The purpose was simple: to secure better loan terms, lower insurance premiums, and favorable business deals that Trump couldn’t have obtained with honest financial statements.

Judge Engoron didn’t mince words. The frauds, he wrote, “leap off the page and shock the conscience.” The defendants’ “complete lack of contrition and remorse borders on pathological.”

The penalty: more than $450 million in total, including $363.8 million in disgorgement of ill-gotten gains and pre-judgment interest. With interest continuing to accrue, the judgment quickly grew to over $500 million.

New York Attorney General Letitia James declared: “For years, Donald Trump engaged in massive fraud to falsely inflate his net worth and unjustly enrich himself, his family, and his organization. While he may have authored the ‘Art of the Deal,’ our case revealed that his business was based on the art of the steal.

New York Attorney General Letitia James announces the $454 million civil fraud judgment against Donald Trump and the Trump Organization, February 16, 2024. Getty Images / Michael M. Santiago.

The Fraud: A Systematic Deception

The scheme was both audacious and simple. For years, Trump submitted annual “Statements of Financial Condition” to banks, insurers, and business partners. These documents purported to list Trump’s assets and their values, forming the basis for his claimed net worth.

The problem: the values were wildly inflated, sometimes absurdly so.

Judge Engoron found that Trump’s statements, signed between 2014 and 2021, overvalued his assets by between $812 million and $2.2 billion. But the fraud went back much further. Between 2011 and 2021, Trump increased his net worth on paper by 17-39% ($812 million to $2.2 billion) through systematic overvaluation.

The Specific Frauds

Judge Engoron cited numerous examples:

Trump Tower Penthouse: Trump claimed his penthouse was 30,000 square feet when it was actually about 10,000 square feet—tripling its size and thus its value. This wasn’t a minor error or good-faith estimate; it was a fabrication that persisted for years.

Mar-a-Lago: Trump valued his Palm Beach estate as high as $739 million as a private residence. But Mar-a-Lago operates as a social club, not a residence, under deed restrictions Trump himself agreed to. Judge Engoron noted the property should have been valued at about $75 million based on its actual use—off by nearly 900%.

Aberdeen Golf Course: Trump valued his Scottish golf course based on potential future development of residential properties—but the land had no development rights and wasn’t zoned for housing. He was claiming value for something that legally could not exist.

Rent-Stabilized Apartments: Trump valued rent-stabilized apartments as if they could be rented at market rate, when they were legally restricted to far lower rents.

The pattern was consistent: take an asset, imagine the most optimistic possible value (or make one up), ignore legal restrictions and market reality, present it to banks and insurers as fact.

The Investigation: Three Years of Resistance

Attorney General Letitia James began investigating the Trump Organization in early 2019, with public litigation beginning in August 2020 to enforce subpoenas.

Trump fought every step. In February 2022, Judge Engoron ruled in favor of James’s subpoenas. Trump still didn’t comply.

In April 2022, Trump was found in contempt of court for refusing to turn over documents and was fined $110,000—a trivial sum for a purported billionaire, but a rare instance of Trump actually being punished for defying a court order.

In September 2022, James filed her lawsuit against Trump, Donald Jr., Ivanka, Eric, former CFO Allen Weisselberg, former controller Jeffrey McConney, and ten related companies.

Eric Trump testifies during the Trump Organization civil fraud trial, New York State Supreme Court, November 2023. Reuters / Jane Rosenberg (courtroom photo, November 2023).

The case sought $250 million in disgorgement and a ban on doing business in New York.

Trump’s defense: the financial statements included disclaimers telling banks to do their own due diligence, so any sophisticated party should have known not to rely on Trump’s numbers.

The Summary Judgment: Fraud Established Before Trial

In September 2023, before the trial even began, Judge Engoron issued a partial summary judgment: Trump and the defendants had committed fraud for years.

The disclaimers, Engoron ruled, didn’t absolve Trump of responsibility for lies in his financial statements. The trial would determine only damages and additional penalties.

Trump’s lawyers filed motion after motion trying to stop the trial. Engoron sanctioned five Trump lawyers $7,500 each in September 2023 for “repeating these frivolous arguments” already rejected by courts multiple times.

In November 2022, Engoron appointed retired judge Barbara S. Jones as an independent monitor to oversee the Trump Organization and prevent further fraud.

The Trial: Eleven Weeks of Evidence

The trial ran from October 2, 2023, to January 11, 2024—eleven weeks of testimony laying bare the Trump Organization’s fraudulent practices.

Judge Engoron presided over a bench trial—he, not a jury, would decide. Trump’s lawyers later claimed he hadn’t been allowed a jury trial, but court records show they consented to trial without jury, and Engoron noted a jury wouldn’t have been an option anyway since the state sought only disgorgement, not criminal penalties.

Trump attended many days, often making comments to reporters attacking the judge, the attorney general, and the case. Engoron imposed a limited gag order after Trump attacked the judge’s law clerk on social media.

The evidence was overwhelming:

  • Financial statements showing inflated values

  • Testimony from accountants and experts about standard practices

  • Internal Trump Organization emails discussing valuations

  • Testimony from Trump himself

President Donald Trump testifies under oath during the Trump Organization civil fraud trial, New York State Supreme Court, November 6, 2023. Getty Images / Michael M. Santiago.

During Trump’s testimony, Engoron noted Trump “rarely responded to the questions asked, and he frequently interjected long, irrelevant speeches on issues far beyond the scope of the trial”.

Trump’s defense: the banks and insurers were sophisticated parties who made money on the deals, so there were no victims. His lawyers repeated: “There were no victims and no losses.”

But as Engoron would make clear, fraud is fraud regardless of whether the victim realizes they’ve been defrauded. Banks gave Trump better loan terms based on false statements. Insurance companies gave him lower premiums. That’s loss and victim enough.

The Verdict: $454 Million and Massive Restrictions

On February 16, 2024, Judge Engoron issued his final ruling:

Monetary Penalties:

  • Trump and defendants: more than $450 million total

  • $363.8 million in disgorgement and pre-judgment interest

  • With interest accruing, grew to over $500 million

Business Restrictions:

  • Trump, Weisselberg, McConney: banned from serving as officers/directors of any New York company for three years

  • Weisselberg and McConney: additionally banned from financial management roles in New York companies for life

  • Donald Jr. and Eric: banned from serving as officers/directors for two years

  • Trump and companies: banned from applying for loans from New York banks for three years

  • Creation of Independent Director of Compliance at Trump Organization

Engoron concluded the Trumps and their company benefited to the tune of $354 million in “ill-gotten gains” through the scheme.

In his written decision, Engoron said Trump and co-defendants engaged in frauds that “leap off the page and shock the conscience”. Their “complete lack of contrition and remorse borders on pathological. They are accused only of inflating asset values to make more money.”

“When confronted at trial with the statements, defendants’ fact and expert witnesses simply denied reality, and defendants failed to accept responsibility or to impose internal controls to prevent future recurrences,” Engoron wrote.

The Immediate Response

Donald Trump at the New York State Supreme Court for his civil fraud trial, October 2023 AP Photo / Eduardo Munoz Alvarez.

Trump immediately declared it a “crooked New York State judge” and vowed to appeal.

AG James responded: “The scale and scope of Donald Trump’s fraud is staggering—and so, too, is his ego and his belief that the rules don’t apply to him.”

James said publicly she would seek to seize Trump’s assets if he couldn’t pay—Trump Tower, 40 Wall Street, his Westchester golf course all potentially on the auction block.

The Deeper Pattern: Fraud as Business Model

The Trump Organization case reveals fraud wasn’t an occasional lapse—it was Trump’s business model for decades.

Trump systematically lied to banks, insurers, and business partners. He inflated his net worth by hundreds of millions or billions. He claimed his apartment was three times its actual size. He valued properties as if legal restrictions didn’t exist. He presented these lies in official financial statements, signed under oath.

And it worked. Banks gave him better loan terms. Insurers gave him lower premiums. Business partners made deals based on false representations. Trump got richer by lying.

When caught, Trump’s defense was: “Everyone does it.” “The banks should have known.” “There were no victims.” “Political witch hunt.”

But fraud is fraud. If Trump was willing to commit systematic fraud for decades to enrich himself, what else was he willing to do?

Why This Matters

The Trump Organization fraud case matters because it reveals everything about Trump’s business career—the image of success, the claims of wealth, the reputation as a dealmaker—was built on lies.

Trump didn’t become rich through brilliant business acumen. He became rich (or claimed to be) by lying about how rich he was, which enabled deals he couldn’t have made otherwise, which made him actually richer, which he then lied about being even richer—a recursive cycle of fraud compounding fraud.

His presidential campaigns were based on this false image. “I’m really rich,” Trump said announcing his 2016 campaign. “I’m a successful businessman.” Voters believed it. It was fraud.

His presidency was premised on the idea he knew how to make deals, negotiate, run things like a business. All based on a fraudulent business career.

And now he’s president again, despite courts finding he committed systematic fraud for decades.

What Came Next

Judge Engoron issued a landmark ruling finding Trump liable for massive fraud and ordering him to pay over $450 million. Trump and his sons were banned from running New York companies. The case should have ended Trump’s business career.

But this is Trump we’re talking about. In Part 22, we’ll see how Trump fought back—how he struggled to post the bond, how he appealed the verdict, how an appeals court ultimately threw out the monetary penalty while confirming the fraud, and how Trump continues operating his businesses as if nothing happened.

The fraud is established. The judgment was issued. But accountability? That’s a different story.
