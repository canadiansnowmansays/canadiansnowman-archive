The Baseline Problem

PART 2: The Evidence: Documenting a Pattern of Decline

June 23, 2025

Clinical Markers and Professional Assessment

When Joan Rivers died in September 2014, Donald Trump was still hosting "The Apprentice" and hadn't yet announced his presidential campaign. The idea that this comedy legend would somehow vote for Trump in 2016 would have been absurd for multiple reasons — but most obviously because she was dead.

Yet in November 2024, when author Ramin Setoodeh interviewed Trump for his book about "The Apprentice," Trump confidently declared: "I thought she might have been a Republican. I know one thing: she voted for me, according to what she said."

This wasn't a slip of the tongue or verbal stumble. It represents a temporal confusion spanning multiple years — exactly the type of cognitive error that neurologists consider a red flag for dementia.

But here's the crucial point that Lawrence O'Donnell identified: because Trump's baseline functioning was already so problematic, even glaring cognitive errors like this barely register as news. The media and public have become so accustomed to Trump's unconventional statements that genuine clinical markers of decline are dismissed as "Trump being Trump."

The Clinical Evidence Hidden in Plain Sight

Setoodeh, who interviewed Trump six times for his book, was clear about what he observed: Trump had "severe memory issues" and "couldn't remember things." As someone who spent the most time with Trump in detailed interviews, Setoodeh's assessment carries significant weight.

But the Joan Rivers claim isn't isolated. It's part of a documented pattern of cognitive changes that experts say would trigger immediate medical evaluation in any other context.

1. Historical Confusion: The Declaration of Independence Error

On June 19, 2025, during an Oval Office press conference, Trump looked up at a display of the Declaration of Independence and said:

"You look right up there, I don't know, see the Declaration of Independence. And I say, I wonder if you, you know, the Civil War always seemed to me maybe that could have been solved without losing 600,000 plus people."

As O'Donnell noted, this reveals that Trump "did not know that the Declaration of Independence was written and signed 85 years before the Civil War." This isn't a complex historical detail — it's the basic chronology that, as O'Donnell put it, "we knew in the third grade."

Clinical Significance: Confusion about well-established historical facts, especially when looking directly at relevant documents, suggests problems with both memory consolidation and contextual processing.

3. The "Great Day for the World" Foreign Policy Ramble

On June 20 2025, Trump posted a lengthy tweet about foreign policy that demonstrates multiple cognitive decline markers in a single statement. The post begins with announcing a treaty between Congo and Rwanda, then deteriorates into a stream-of-consciousness ramble:

Clinical Analysis: This single post demonstrates multiple concerning patterns:

  • Circumstantiality: Starts with one topic (Congo/Rwanda) and spirals through unrelated conflicts

  • Grandiose delusions: Positioning himself as singular world peace broker

  • Tangential thinking: Jumping from Africa to South Asia to Balkans to Middle East with no logical connection

  • Persecution complex: Obsessive focus on not receiving Nobel Prize recognition

  • Poor reality testing: Claiming resolution of conflicts that remain active or unresolved

  • Stream of consciousness: Run-on sentences without clear organizational structure

Comparison Standard: Imagine if Joe Biden had posted this exact rambling, incoherent foreign policy statement. It would have generated immediate calls for cognitive evaluation and fitness assessment.

4. The "Some Other Things" Language Breakdown

During a July 1, 2019 Fox News interview with Tucker Carlson, when asked about urban problems in American cities compared to clean Japanese cities, Trump provided this response:

"I'm gonna maybe and I'm looking at it very seriously we're doing some other things you probably noticed like some of the great important things that we're doing now but we're looking at it very seriously because you can't do that"

Clinical Analysis: This statement demonstrates what linguists call "circumlocution" and "semantic poverty" - using many words to convey little meaningful information. Key markers include:

  • Incomplete sentence construction: "I'm gonna maybe" without completing the thought

  • Vague referencing: "Some other things you probably noticed" provides no specific information

  • Repetitive phrasing: "Looking at it very seriously" repeated within the same sentence

  • Poor syntactic organization: Ideas strung together without logical connectors

  • Semantic emptiness: Many words used but minimal actual content conveyed

Temporal Context: This 2019 interview provides important baseline comparison - even five years ago, Trump's language processing showed concerning patterns that have since accelerated.

5. The Shark vs. Electrocution Obsession

Throughout 2024 and 2025, Trump has repeatedly told a bizarre story about choosing between electrocution from a sinking electric boat and a nearby shark attack. The scenario is detailed, fictional, and treated as serious policy consideration:

"Let's see your boat goes down, and I'm sitting on top of this big powerful battery, and the boat's going down... So I have a choice of electrocution or a shark... If there was a shark or you get electrocuted, I'll take electrocution every single time. I'm not getting near the shark."

Clinical Analysis: This represents several concerning patterns:

  • Tangential thinking: Starting with electric vehicle policy and veering into fictional scenarios

  • Perseveration: Repetitive return to the same unlikely scenario across multiple events

  • Poor reality testing: Treating imaginary situations as legitimate policy considerations

6. The Hannibal Lecter Fixation

Trump regularly references the fictional serial killer Hannibal Lecter as if he were a real person, calling him "the late, great Hannibal Lecter" and "a wonderful man." He's done this at multiple rallies while discussing immigration policy.

Clinical Concern: Persistent confusion between fictional characters and real people, especially when the fictional character is associated with cannibalism and murder, raises questions about reality testing and appropriateness of associations.

7. Phonemic Paraphasia

Cornell Psychology expert Harry Segal has noted Trump's "phonemic paraphasia — when he begins a word and can't finish it." This is a specific type of speech error where someone starts a word but cannot complete it, often substituting similar-sounding syllables.

Medical Significance: Phonemic paraphasia is considered an early sign of dementia, particularly affecting language processing areas of the brain.

Statistical Documentation of Decline

Beyond individual incidents, researchers have documented measurable changes in Trump's speech patterns that suggest cognitive deterioration:

Rally Analysis (2016 vs. 2024)

  • Rally Length: Increased from 45 minutes (2016) to 82 minutes (2024)

  • All-or-Nothing Thinking: 13% increase in terms like "always" and "never"

  • Negative Language: 32% more negative than positive words (vs. 21% in 2016)

  • Profanity: 69% increase in swear words

  • Tangential Speech: Documented increase in topic-switching without logical connections

Linguistic Complexity Analysis

Neurolinguistic experts who reviewed "decades of Trump's on-air interviews and compared them to Q&A sessions since his inauguration" found "differences are striking and unmistakable." The analysis revealed:

  • Shorter sentences with less complex grammatical structure

  • More repetition of phrases and concepts

  • Increased confusion of words and phrases

  • More tangents with fewer logical connections between topics

Expert Consensus: Multiple language experts "all agreed there had been a deterioration."

Professional Assessments

Cornell University Analysis

Harry Segal, senior lecturer in Psychology and Psychiatry at Weill Cornell Medicine, provided a comprehensive assessment:

"What's alarming is how the rate of Trump's bizarre speech and political decisions have been increasing. He gave an answer about childcare to the Economic Club of New York so incoherent that even his supporters were concerned. Last week he got cognitively lost in a rally and began to talk about the 'eight circles' that Biden filled up with journalists. No one on his staff has been able to explain the reference."

Segal identified multiple clinical markers:

  • Strange gait patterns consistent with neurological changes

  • Phonemic paraphasia in speech

  • Decline in complexity of words and concepts

  • Increased impulsivity — "another sign of incipient dementia"

  • Avoidance behaviors — refusing debates and canceling interviews to avoid spontaneous responses

Republican Observer Analysis

Even supporters have noted changes. Republican advisor Rick Wilson observed:

"He is incoherent. His inability to articulate any thought or position without constant asides, constant lapses, the verbal aphasias he is experiencing at various points. As a very close observer of Trump since 2015, I can tell you the man you're watching today is not the Trump of 2015, nor is he the Trump of 2020, he's not even the Trump of 2024."

Critical Point: Wilson specifically noted that "there is a decline in Trump's ability to communicate, there is a decline in Trump's ability to articulate his thoughts."

Medical Professional Concerns

Former White House physician Jeffrey Kuhlman stated that while Trump "appears in good health" for his age, he nonetheless raised concerns about "cognitive decline in reasoning, memory, and processing speed, consistent with his advanced age."

Other medical professionals have noted that Trump "exhibits cognitive decline in reasoning, memory, and processing speed, consistent with his advanced age."

The "Eight Circles" Mystery

One particularly concerning incident highlighted by Segal was when Trump "got cognitively lost in a rally and began to talk about the 'eight circles' that Biden filled up with journalists. No one on his staff has been able to explain the reference."

This type of unexplainable reference — where someone makes statements that have no apparent basis in reality and cannot be explained even by close associates — is particularly concerning from a cognitive assessment perspective.

Comparison to Normal Aging

It's important to distinguish between normal age-related cognitive changes and pathological decline. Normal aging might involve:

  • Occasional word-finding difficulties

  • Slower processing speed

  • Some decline in working memory

Pathological changes include:

  • Multi-year temporal confusion (Joan Rivers voting)

  • Historical fact confusion (Declaration/Civil War)

  • Persistent fictional scenario obsession (sharks/electrocution)

  • Unexplainable references (eight circles)

  • Phonemic paraphasia

  • Significant personality and behavioral changes

Trump's documented changes go well beyond normal aging patterns.

The Assessment Challenge

Temple University cognition lab director Dr. Jamie Reilly noted the complexity of assessment: "There are people who count syllables, and they count the speech rate, and they count the number of things like how much he curses and things like that, and have noted changes. The question of whether that is a marker of a cognitive — of a disease process is really, really tricky."

However, clinical psychologist Dr. Ben Michaelis observed: "What we're seeing with Trump is, basically, he's moving further and further away from linearity, from linearity to tangentiality, so he's sort of weaving together ideas, and then it's moving further afield towards circumstantiality. And that's really where you sort of start talking about a topic, and then you just really sort of lose the thread entirely."

Why Detection Is Difficult

The baseline problem makes traditional cognitive assessment nearly impossible because:

  1. Pre-existing Communication Style: Trump's long history of unconventional statements makes new abnormalities less noticeable

  2. Media Normalization: Years of unusual behavior have created tolerance for increasingly problematic statements

  3. Political Polarization: Objective medical assessment becomes difficult when viewed through partisan lenses

  4. Lack of Formal Testing: No mechanism exists for mandatory cognitive evaluation of sitting presidents

  5. Gradual Progression: Dementia typically develops slowly, making day-to-day changes less apparent

Clinical Significance

Dr. Zachary Rubin noted that the evidence suggests "concerning patterns that would warrant clinical evaluation in any other context." The combination of:

  • Temporal confusion spanning years

  • Historical fact disorientation

  • Persistent fictional preoccupations

  • Language processing difficulties

  • Unexplainable references

  • Documented statistical decline in communication complexity

Together, these form what neuropsychologists call a "constellation of symptoms" that would typically trigger comprehensive cognitive evaluation.

The Expert Consensus

Multiple professionals from different political backgrounds have reached similar conclusions:

  • Harry Segal (Cornell): "Accelerating cognitive decline"

  • Rick Wilson (Republican strategist): "Decline in ability to communicate and articulate thoughts"

  • Ramin Setoodeh (Journalist): "Severe memory issues"

  • Dr. Ben Michaelis (Clinical Psychologist): Movement toward "circumstantiality" and losing "the thread entirely"

This cross-partisan professional agreement suggests the evidence transcends political interpretation.

What This Means

The documented evidence suggests that Lawrence O'Donnell's question — "If Donald Trump gets dementia, how will we know?" — may have already been answered. The clinical markers are present, the expert assessments are consistent, and the statistical analysis confirms measurable decline.

But this raises an even more troubling question: if the evidence is this clear to medical professionals, linguistic experts, and careful observers, why hasn't it been adequately covered by mainstream media?

In Part 3, we'll examine the media double standard that has allowed these concerning changes to occur largely without the scrutiny that would be applied to any other political figure displaying similar symptoms.
