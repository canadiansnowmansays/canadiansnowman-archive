The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 2: The Enemy From Within and Without

January 22, 2026

The Perpetual Threat Narrative as Control Mechanism (1999-2009)

In the decade after consolidating power, Ayatollah Khamenei perfected a governing doctrine that would define his tenure: Iran exists in permanent siege by enemies seeking its destruction.

This wasn’t entirely paranoia. The United States maintained hostile posture. Israel conducted covert operations. Regional rivals worked against Iranian interests. International sanctions imposed real costs.

But Khamenei transformed legitimate security concerns into an all-consuming ideology where every problem, every failure, every protest became proof of foreign conspiracy. Economic collapse? Enemy sabotage. Currency crash? Enemy manipulation. Demonstrations? Enemy orchestration.

The doctrine served dual purposes: deflect blame for domestic failures and justify unlimited force against dissent.

Two protest movements in this period—1999 and 2009—would test the system and establish a pattern that continues to January 2026: when Khamenei speaks, the killing escalates.

I. JULY 1999: FIRST BLOOD

The spark seemed small: closure of Salam, a reformist newspaper supporting President Mohammad Khatami’s cautious opening. University students, believing in the possibility of change within the system, organized peaceful protests on Tehran University campus.

What happened next would shatter that faith forever.

The Night Raid - July 8-9, 1999

A burnt out room in a University of Tehran dormitory on 10 July 1999 (AFP)

Around midnight, security forces and plainclothes Basij militia stormed Tehran University dormitories. Students sleeping in their rooms woke to batons, clubs, and fists. Eyewitnesses reported security forces setting rooms on fire. One student was killed outright. Hundreds more were beaten, bloodied, dragged into vans.

By morning, images circulated of a protester holding up his friend’s bloodied shirt—a photograph that would define the moment. Over 1,500 students were arrested that night.

President Khatami, the reformist elected by landslide just two years earlier, condemned the violence but did nothing to stop it. He couldn’t. Real power didn’t rest with elected presidents.

The Taboo Breaks

Iranian students demonstrating in Tehran in 1999 before the unrest turned violent

What began as protests over press freedom exploded into larger demonstrations. Within days, more than 10,000 protesters filled Tehran streets, spreading to university cities including Tabriz, Mashhad, and Isfahan.

For the first time since 1979, protesters openly chanted: “Death to Khamenei.”

The barrier had broken. The Supreme Leader—whose position supposedly derived from divine selection and revolutionary legitimacy—was being publicly denounced by the revolution’s children.

The Military Threat

On July 11, 24 IRGC commanders delivered a letter to President Khatami. The message was explicit: “Our patience has run out. We cannot tolerate this situation any longer...If you do not take a revolutionary decision and act strongly against these traitors, we will.”

This was a coup threat in writing. Twenty-four senior Revolutionary Guard commanders telling the elected president they would act “with or without” his approval.

Khatami caved. He condemned the protesters as “rioters” and endorsed security forces’ actions. Demonstrations were crushed within days.

His Standard, His Failure

Two years earlier, in 1997, Khamenei had proclaimed: “Students are the revolution’s children. They deserve dialogue, not violence.”

Test that standard against July 1999: Security forces raided dormitories at night, beat sleeping students, set rooms on fire, arrested 1,500, and killed at least one. Then IRGC commanders threatened the elected president with military action if he didn’t endorse the violence.

The revolution promised political participation and freedom of expression. Its Guardian responded to peaceful protest by ordering midnight raids on sleeping students.

By his own standard—treating students as “revolution’s children” deserving “dialogue”—he failed. By revolutionary principles promising political freedom, he failed. By basic Islamic principles emphasizing mercy and justice, he failed.

But he succeeded in one thing: establishing a pattern. When Khamenei speaks, when the Supreme Leader signals his will, violence escalates and dissent is crushed.

The students learned: reform within the system was impossible. The system was Khamenei.

II. THE 2009 GREEN MOVEMENT: MILLIONS MEET FORCE

A decade later, Iranians tried again—this time in numbers that dwarfed 1999.

June 12, 2009: The Rigged Election

Presidential election results were announced with suspicious speed: incumbent Mahmoud Ahmadinejad won in a landslide. Mir Hossein Mousavi, the reformist challenger who had drawn massive crowds throughout the campaign, allegedly received far fewer votes than expected—even losing his own hometown.

Iranians didn’t believe it. Within hours, protesters filled Tehran streets. Within days, up to 3 million demonstrators marched—the largest protests since the 1979 revolution itself.

Their slogan was simple: “Where is my vote?”

They weren’t calling for revolution. They weren’t demanding regime change. They were asking for their ballots to be counted honestly—for the democratic process promised by the Islamic Republic’s constitution.

June 19: Khamenei Speaks

For seven days, Khamenei remained silent while protests grew. Then came Friday prayers, June 19, 2009.

In a nationally televised sermon, Khamenei declared the election result final and warned explicitly: continued protests would lead to “bloodshed and chaos.” He placed responsibility squarely on opposition leaders, effectively giving security forces permission—or orders—to act.

One official later told Reuters: “Our Imam only answers to God. He cares about people and the Revolution. He was very firm and said those rioters should be crushed.”

“Crushed” was the operative word.

The Crackdown

Amateur video turns Neda Agha-Soltan into icon of Iran unrest

What followed was systematic violence. Basij militiamen on motorcycles attacked protesters with clubs and chains. Security forces used live ammunition. One moment crystallized the brutality: Neda Agha-Soltan, a 26-year-old woman, shot in the chest by a Basij militiaman. Her death was captured on video, watched by millions worldwide.
Between June 2009 and February 2010, more than 30 protesters were killed. Over 4,000 were arrested. Opposition leaders Mousavi and Mehdi Karroubi were placed under house arrest—where they remain 17 years later, never charged, never tried, simply disappeared into indefinite detention.

The IRGC Ascendant

The Green Movement revealed the true power structure. When millions challenged Khamenei’s authority, the IRGC and Basij “saved” the regime through force.

Abbas Milani’s observation became undeniable fact: “Khamenei knows that without the IRGC he’d be out of a job in twenty-four hours.”

The Islamic Republic—supposedly a religious democracy—had become a military dictatorship with clerical cover. The Supreme Leader depended entirely on Revolutionary Guards willing to shoot Iranian citizens to keep him in power.

His Standard, His Failure

In countless speeches, Khamenei proclaimed: “The people’s vote is sacred in Islam. Elections demonstrate the Islamic Republic’s commitment to popular will.”

Test that standard: When 3 million Iranians questioned vote counting, he called them rioters and ordered them crushed. When citizens exercised their constitutional right to peaceful assembly, he threatened them with death. When they persisted, he unleashed armed militias.

The Islamic Republic’s constitution, which Khamenei swore to uphold, guarantees in Article 27: “Public gatherings and marches may be freely held, provided they do not violate the principles of Islam.”

Three million Iranians gathering to ask “Where is my vote?” violated no Islamic principle. They were exercising constitutional rights. The violation came from Khamenei’s security forces who shot them, arrested them, disappeared them.

By his own proclaimed standard—that “people’s vote is sacred”—ordering the massacre of citizens asking for honest vote counts is the ultimate sacrilege.

III. THE FOREIGN ENEMY ALGORITHM

Across both crises, Khamenei deployed the same explanation: foreign enemies orchestrated the unrest.

In 1999: “Foreign powers and domestic agents seek to weaken the Islamic Republic through cultural invasion.”

In 2009: “The Americans, British, and Zionists want to create a ‘color revolution’ to overthrow our system. These protests serve foreign interests, not Iranian ones.”

The logic became formulaic:

  1. Domestic problem emerges (rigged election, economic crisis, policy failure)

  2. Iranians protest

  3. Blame foreign powers for orchestrating protests

  4. Frame protesters as unwitting tools or conscious traitors

  5. Justify unlimited force as defending sovereignty against external attack

This algorithm had practical benefits: it absolved leadership of responsibility, delegitimized protesters’ genuine grievances, and transformed political opposition into national security threat requiring military response.

But The Logic Eats Itself

Apply Khamenei’s framework consistently and it collapses:

If CIA and MI6 can organize 3 million Iranians to protest simultaneously across multiple cities—if foreign intelligence can coordinate actions involving diverse social classes, age groups, and political factions—then by definition, Iran’s security apparatus has failed catastrophically.

If enemies can penetrate Iranian society so deeply they control mass movements, then the Guardian has failed to guard. If foreign powers manipulate Iranian politics at will, Iran has no sovereignty.

There are only two possibilities:

Possibility 1: The protests reflect genuine domestic discontent over real grievances (rigged elections, economic mismanagement, political repression), and Khamenei lies about foreign orchestration to justify violence.

Possibility 2: Foreign powers genuinely control events inside Iran to this degree, proving complete security failure and making a mockery of claims to sovereignty and independence.

Either interpretation indicts the Guardian.

IV. THE REAL PATTERN: KHAMENEI’S INTERVENTION = ESCALATED KILLING

Comparing 1999 and 2009 reveals a consistent pattern that would repeat in 2019, 2022, and 2025-26:

Phase 1 - Initial Protests: Demonstrations begin over specific grievance (newspaper closure, election fraud, fuel prices, hijab enforcement, economic collapse)

Phase 2 - Limited Violence: Security forces use tear gas, arrests, some beatings. Death toll limited—single digits or low double digits.

Phase 3 - Khamenei Speaks: Supreme Leader makes public statement framing protests as foreign-orchestrated attack on system, calling protesters “rioters” or “seditionists,” warning of consequences.

Phase 4 - Massacre: Within hours or days of Khamenei’s statement, violence escalates dramatically. Live ammunition replaces tear gas. Death toll jumps to dozens, hundreds, or thousands. Systematic arrests follow.

The correlation is undeniable. When the Supreme Leader signals his will, the killing accelerates.

The Revolutionary Standard Inverted

Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, despite his authoritarianism, maintained in 1979-80: “If officials do wrong, the people have a duty to point it out and guide them. Islam gives the people the right to remove leaders who betray the revolution.”

Khomeini established that leaders were accountable—to revolutionary principles, to Islamic values, and ultimately to the people.

Khamenei eliminated that accountability. Under his rule, questioning the Supreme Leader became not just illegal but heretical—a crime against Islam itself.

The revolution promised government by the people, for the people, accountable to the people. Khamenei delivered government over the people, against the people, enforced through bullets.

The 2009 Lesson

When the Green Movement finally subsided in early 2010, Iranians learned what 1999 students had learned: the system couldn’t be reformed from within. Khamenei was the system. The IRGC enforced the system. Elections were performative theater—permitted only so long as they produced acceptable results.

Any serious challenge would be met with force. Any demand for accountability would be framed as foreign conspiracy. Any mass mobilization would be shot.

But they also learned something else: the regime feared the people. Why else respond to peaceful protests with such violence? Why else blame every demonstration on elaborate foreign plots?

The killings revealed weakness, not strength. A confident, legitimate government doesn’t shoot millions of citizens asking for honest vote counts. A stable system doesn’t require armed militias attacking unarmed demonstrators.

The question wasn’t whether Iranians wanted change. The question was whether the bullets would run out before the protesters did.

CLOSING: THE ENEMY DOCTRINE’S FATAL FLAW

By 2010, the pattern was set and the doctrine perfected: every crisis blamed on external enemies, every protest framed as foreign conspiracy, every challenge met with escalating force.

But the logic contained its own destruction.

If enemies are everywhere—manipulating currency, organizing protests, controlling Iranian society from within—then sovereignty is fiction. If CIA and Mossad operate freely across Iran coordinating mass movements, then three decades of Khamenei’s “guardianship” produced catastrophic security failure.

If, alternatively, the grievances are genuine and domestic—economic mismanagement, political repression, systematic corruption, rigged elections—then blaming foreigners is a lie to justify killing Iranians whose only crime was demanding the revolution’s promises be kept.

Either version indicts the Guardian by his own standards.

The revolution promised independence from foreign control. If Khamenei’s telling is accurate, Iran under his watch became more penetrated, more controlled, more manipulated by hostile powers than ever before.

The revolution promised Islamic justice, mercy, and accountability. Khamenei delivered bullets, prisons, and permanent unaccountability cloaked in religious rhetoric.

Most devastatingly: the revolution promised to be for the Iranian people, lifting up the oppressed masses (mostazafin). By 2009, the oppressed masses were chanting “Death to the Dictator”—meaning him.

The enemy wasn’t foreign. The enemy was the system itself, and the man who built it.

The bunker was already under construction. It would take two more decades and three more massacres to finish it.
