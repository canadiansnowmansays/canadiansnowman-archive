The Trump Accountability Project

E. Jean Carroll

Part 18: $88.3 Million in Verdicts, $104.7 Million With Interest (1995-2026)

February 5, 2026

President Donald Trump; E. Jean Carroll.Getty Images; AFP

Of all Trump’s accusers—26, 28, perhaps as many as 69 women depending on how you count—only one has had her day in court. E. Jean Carroll, a longtime advice columnist, didn’t just accuse Trump of sexual assault. She sued him. Twice. And she won. Twice.

The verdicts represent the only time in Trump’s life that he’s faced legal accountability for sexual misconduct. A jury found him liable. A judge confirmed he committed rape as commonly understood. Trump was ordered to pay $88.3 million in total damages—an amount that has grown to approximately $104.7 million with interest as of February 2026, accumulating at nearly $22,000 per day.

And yet, even this historic accountability is incomplete. Trump has posted bonds and appealed. He continues to attack Carroll. He’s now president again, with all the protections that office provides. The money remains tied up in appeals that could take years.

Carroll won in court. But did she achieve accountability? That’s a more complicated question.

The Allegation

Carroll alleged in a June 2019 New York magazine article that Trump raped her in a Bergdorf Goodman department store dressing room in the mid-1990s. She described a chance encounter that turned violent: Trump pushed her against a wall, pulled down her tights, and forcibly penetrated her with his fingers and penis.

Carroll had told two friends immediately after the attack but hadn’t reported it to police—a common response to sexual assault, particularly in that era. She came forward publicly in 2019, after years of watching other women accuse Trump and after the #MeToo movement made it somewhat safer for assault survivors to speak out.

Trump’s response was swift and predictable. He called her a “whack job,” claimed he’d never met her (despite a photograph of them together at a party in 1987), said she was “not my type,” and accused her of making up the story to sell her memoir.

The Legal Path: Two Lawsuits, Two Trials

Carroll sued Trump for defamation in 2019 over his denial of her rape allegation. But the statute of limitations had long expired on any battery claim—rape charges couldn’t be brought decades after the fact.

Then New York changed the law. In May 2022, the state passed the Adult Survivors Act, opening a one-year window for victims of sexual assault to file civil suits regardless of when the assault occurred. It was designed for cases exactly like Carroll’s.

Carroll filed a second lawsuit in November 2022, this time including both battery and defamation claims based on Trump’s continued attacks on her, including statements he made in October 2022 on Truth Social.

Trial One: The Sexual Abuse Finding

The second lawsuit (confusingly called “Carroll II” because it was filed second but went to trial first) began in April 2023 in federal court in Manhattan.

The evidence was compelling:

Corroborating testimony: Two friends—Lisa Birnbach and Carol Martin—testified that Carroll had told them about the assault immediately after it happened. Judge Kaplan later wrote that their testimony “corroborated the essence of Ms. Carroll’s account of a violent, traumatic sexual assault.”

The Access Hollywood tape: The recording was admitted as evidence showing Trump’s pattern of behavior and his own description of how he assaults women.

Similar accusers: Jessica Leeds and Natasha Stoynoff testified about Trump assaulting them in remarkably similar ways—sudden aggressive sexual contact, kissing and groping without consent.

Photographic proof: A photograph from 1987 showed Trump and Carroll together, demolishing his claim that he’d never met her.

Trump’s deposition: Excerpts were played for the jury, including Trump mistaking Carroll for his ex-wife Marla Maples in a photograph, then doubling down on his “not my type” defense despite the confusion.

Trump didn’t attend the trial. He didn’t testify. His lawyers’ defense was that Carroll couldn’t prove the assault happened and that her story had changed over time.

On May 9, 2023, after deliberating for less than three hours, a jury of six men and three women delivered its unanimous verdict:

  • Trump was liable for sexually abusing E. Jean Carroll

  • Trump was liable for defaming Carroll by calling her a liar

  • Trump must pay $5 million in damages (nearly $3 million for sexual abuse, about $2 million for defamation)

The jury stopped short of finding Trump liable for “rape” under New York’s narrow legal definition at the time, which required forcible vaginal penetration specifically by a penis. But Carroll testified that Trump penetrated her with both his fingers and his penis—the jury apparently believed the digital penetration but wasn’t convinced beyond a preponderance of the evidence about penile penetration.

That technical distinction wouldn’t last long.

Judge Kaplan’s Clarification: It Was Rape

E. Jean Carroll after Judge Kaplan upheld the $5 million verdict, July 19, 2023. That day, the judge ruled Trump had raped her. Six months later, a jury awarded her $83.3 million more. Total: $88.3 million. Trump still hasn't paid.

After the verdict, Trump’s lawyers tried to use the jury’s “no” finding on rape to argue that the $2 million award for sexual abuse was excessive—after all, they claimed, Trump had only been found liable for groping, not rape.

Judge Lewis Kaplan demolished that argument in a July 2023 ruling:

“The finding that Ms. Carroll failed to prove that she was ‘raped’ within the meaning of the New York Penal Law does not mean that she failed to prove that Mr. Trump ‘raped’ her as many people commonly understand the word ‘rape.’ Indeed... the jury found that Mr. Trump in fact did exactly that.”

Kaplan explained that New York’s definition of “rape” at the time was “far narrower” than the common understanding—it applied only to forcible vaginal penetration by a penis. Forcible penetration with fingers was classified as “sexual abuse,” not “rape.”

But the evidence, Kaplan wrote, “convincingly established, and the jury implicitly found, that Mr. Trump deliberately and forcibly penetrated Ms. Carroll’s vagina with his fingers, causing immediate pain and long lasting emotional and psychological harm.”

In August 2023, dismissing Trump’s defamation counterclaim, Kaplan was even more explicit: The jury’s finding “establishes against him the substantial truth of Ms. Carroll’s ‘rape’ allegations” and confirms that “Mr. Trump did in fact digitally rape Ms. Carroll.”

Congressman Ted Lieu read Judge Kaplan’s findings into the Congressional Record: “The jury’s finding of sexual abuse therefore necessarily implies that it found that Mr. Trump forcibly penetrated her vagina... in other words, that he raped her.”

So let’s be clear: Donald Trump raped E. Jean Carroll. A jury found it. A federal judge confirmed it. That is established legal fact.

Trump’s Response: Attack, Attack, Attack

Most people, when found liable for sexual assault and defamation, might show restraint. Trump doubled down.

The day after the May 2023 verdict, Trump appeared on CNN and continued his attacks: “She’s a whack job... I don’t know who the hell she is... The trial was rigged.”

He continued on Truth Social and at campaign events. As Carroll’s attorney would later tell the court, Trump “continued to defame Ms. Carroll even as this trial was ongoing.” In one Truth Social post, he vowed to deny the allegations “a thousand times.”

Judge Kaplan had already issued a partial summary judgment in September 2023 on Carroll’s first lawsuit (the 2019 defamation claim), finding Trump liable for his statements while president. The jury’s verdict in the sexual abuse case, Kaplan ruled, had “plainly established” that Trump’s 2019 denials were false.

A second trial was scheduled solely to determine how much Trump would pay in damages for those earlier defamatory statements.

Trial Two: $83.3 Million in Damages

The defamation damages trial began in January 2024. Trump actually attended this one—though not for long.

The jury heard evidence about the consequences of Trump’s attacks:

  • A flood of death threats against Carroll

  • Ongoing harassment from Trump’s supporters

  • Damage to her reputation and career

  • The emotional toll of being repeatedly called a liar by a former president

Trump’s presence at trial was disruptive. Judge Kaplan threatened to remove him from the courtroom for loud talking during Carroll’s testimony. Trump eventually walked out during Carroll’s attorney’s closing argument, leaving after about 10 minutes and not returning until after the break.

Carroll’s attorney Shawn Crowley told the jury: “He gets to lie. He gets to threaten. He gets to ignore a jury verdict. He gets to defy the law and the rules of this courtroom... This isn’t a campaign rally. It’s not a press event. It’s a court of law and Miss Carroll’s life. Donald Trump sexually assaulted her. He defamed her. He is not the victim.”

Attorney Roberta Kaplan argued that the punitive damages should be enough to “make him stop” defaming Carroll—pointing out that Trump had vowed to keep attacking her “a thousand times.”

On January 26, 2024, after less than three hours of deliberation, the jury delivered a stunning verdict:

  • $18.3 million in compensatory damages ($11 million for reputation repair, $7.3 million for emotional harm)

  • $65 million in punitive damages

  • Total: $83.3 million

The jury’s message was unmistakable: Trump’s conduct was “remarkably high, perhaps unprecedented” in its reprehensibility.

Trump had already left the courthouse. In a statement, he called it “Absolutely ridiculous!” and vowed to appeal: “Our Legal System is out of control, and being used as a Political Weapon.”

Total damages across both verdicts: $88.3 million.

E. Jean Carroll, center, and her attorneys Shawn Crowley, left, and Roberta Kaplan walk out of the Manhattan federal courthouse Friday January 26, 2024 in New York City. (Brendan McDermid/Reuters)

The Appeals: Delay, Delay, Delay

Trump immediately appealed both verdicts. To do so, he had to post bonds totaling over $90 million—money Carroll can’t access until all appeals are exhausted, potentially including the Supreme Court.

Judge Kaplan affirmed the $83.3 million verdict in February 2024. Trump appealed to the Second Circuit Court of Appeals.

In September 2024, the appeals court heard arguments. Trump’s lawyers argued that presidential immunity should have prevented some evidence from being admitted at trial, that the damages were excessive, and that Carroll’s statements after the first verdict (saying “yes he did” rape her when asked about the jury not finding liability for rape) were defamatory against Trump.

The Second Circuit rejected all arguments, upholding both the liability finding and the $83.3 million judgment. The court noted that while Trump made the statements as president, the underlying conduct—the rape—occurred while he was a private citizen, so immunity didn’t apply.

The $5 Million Verdict Appeal:

In December 2024, the Second Circuit upheld the $5 million sexual abuse verdict. Trump asked for a rehearing before the full court (en banc), which was denied in June 2025.

On November 11, 2025—now as president—Trump asked the Supreme Court to overturn the verdict. His petition argued that Judge Kaplan made “highly prejudicial errors” by allowing the jury to hear testimony from other accusers (Jessica Leeds and Natasha Stoynoff) and the Access Hollywood tape. Trump’s lawyers claimed these violated federal evidence rules and that the case presented “an important question of federal law” creating conflicts among circuit courts.

Trump’s defense to the Supreme Court: “There were no eyewitnesses, no video evidence, and no police report or investigation. Instead, Carroll waited more than 20 years to falsely accuse Donald Trump, who she politically opposes, until after he became the 45th President, when she could maximize political injury to him and profit for herself.”

In other words: Trump’s argument is that because he raped Carroll when no one was watching and she didn’t report it to police in the 1990s, he should face no consequences.

Carroll’s attorneys filed their opposition brief in mid-January 2026, arguing that Trump’s petition fails to show any genuine circuit split and that the Second Circuit already found any alleged evidentiary errors didn’t affect Trump’s “substantial rights”—a “fatal defect” for his petition.

Trump filed his final reply brief on January 29, 2026. The Supreme Court will hold a private conference on February 20, 2026 to decide whether to hear Trump’s appeal. It takes four justices to agree to hear the case. The Court rejects the vast majority of petitions—only about 1-2% are accepted for review. The justices could announce their decision as early as February 23, though they typically consider petitions at multiple conferences before granting them, meaning a decision may not come until March or later.

Carroll’s attorney Roberta Kaplan has expressed confidence the Court will not intervene: “We do not believe that President Trump will be able to present any legal issues in the Carroll cases that merit review by the United States Supreme Court.”

The $83.3 Million Verdict Appeal:

The Second Circuit upheld the $83.3 million defamation verdict in September 2025, calling Trump’s conduct “remarkably high, perhaps unprecedented” in its reprehensibility. That appeal has not yet reached the Supreme Court.

Has Trump Paid?

No. Trump posted bonds to cover both judgments during his appeals, but Carroll won’t see that money until all appeals are exhausted. Given that Trump is now president and can potentially drag out appeals for years, it’s unclear when—or if—Carroll will actually collect.

The Growing Debt:

Under New York law, both judgments accrue interest at 9% annually. As of February 1, 2026:

  • First judgment ($5 million, May 2023): Has grown to approximately $6.23 million with interest

  • Second judgment ($83.3 million, January 2024): Has grown to approximately $98.44 million with interest

  • Combined total owed: ~$104.7 million

  • Daily interest accumulation: ~$21,772

The longer Trump delays payment through appeals, the more he owes. Every day adds nearly $22,000 to his debt to Carroll.

Trump set aside $5.5 million in a court-controlled account for the first verdict, but Carroll has no access to those funds while appeals continue. For the second verdict, Trump posted a $91.6 million bond (110% of the judgment to cover interest and appeal costs).

Meanwhile, Trump continues to operate as if nothing happened. He’s president. He’s wealthy. He faces no restrictions on his behavior.

The ABC News Settlement: Sensitivity About the “R” Word

Despite Judge Kaplan’s explicit finding that Trump “raped” Carroll by the common definition of the word, Trump remains extraordinarily sensitive to being called a rapist in public.

In December 2024, Trump settled a defamation lawsuit against ABC News and anchor George Stephanopoulos for $15 million. The lawsuit stemmed from a March 2024 interview in which Stephanopoulos said Trump had been found “liable for rape” by a jury—a statement that was technically incorrect under New York’s narrow legal definition at the time, though Judge Kaplan had ruled it was rape by common understanding.

The settlement required ABC News to issue a statement of “regret” and pay Trump $15 million toward his presidential library, plus $1 million in legal fees.

The irony was stark: Trump fights being called a rapist even though a federal judge explicitly ruled that he raped Carroll, yet he’s simultaneously appealing the verdict that established that fact.

It’s another example of Trump’s selective relationship with accountability—attack those who accurately describe what courts found, while trying to overturn the court findings themselves.

The Incomplete Accountability

E. Jean Carroll did what few assault survivors can do: she proved her case in court. Twice. She won verdicts totaling $88.3 million—now grown to over $104 million with interest. She forced Trump to be declared liable for sexual abuse (rape by common definition) and defamation by two separate juries and multiple judges.

And yet:

  • Trump hasn’t paid a penny of the judgments

  • Trump continues to attack Carroll and deny the assault

  • Trump was elected president despite the verdicts

  • Trump’s appeals could delay payment for years

  • Trump may ultimately use his presidential power to influence or delay the process further

This is accountability in Trump’s America: technically liable, legally responsible, judicially condemned—but practically consequence-free.

What Carroll Achieved

Despite the incomplete nature of accountability, Carroll’s victory matters:

She established the truth in court: A jury heard all the evidence and found Trump liable. A judge confirmed it was rape. That verdict stands in the historical record.

She exposed the pattern: The trial brought testimony from other Trump accusers and admitted the Access Hollywood tape as evidence, documenting Trump’s long pattern of sexual assault.

She proved it’s possible: For decades, Trump’s accusers were dismissed, attacked, and ignored. Carroll proved that with resources, determination, and a good legal team, even Trump can be held accountable—at least partially.

She sent a message: The $88.3 million in verdicts—now over $104 million with accumulating interest—sends a message about the cost of sexual assault and defamation. Trump can’t escape that number, even if he escapes payment. Every day of delay adds nearly $22,000 more to his debt.

But Carroll also proved how incomplete accountability can be for powerful men. Even with jury verdicts, judicial findings, and over $104 million in judgments (and growing daily), Trump continues on as if none of it matters. Because for him, apparently, it doesn’t.

The Larger Pattern

Carroll’s case exists within the larger pattern of Trump’s 26+ accusers. She’s the only one who got her day in court. The only one who won. The only one who forced Trump to be declared liable.

But she’s also still waiting for actual payment. Still watching Trump attack her. Still seeing him serve as president despite being found liable for rape.

If even Carroll—who did everything right, who had evidence, who won in court, who proved her case—can’t achieve full accountability, what hope is there for the other 25+ women Trump allegedly assaulted?

The answer, unfortunately, is clear: None. Trump proved that if you’re wealthy enough, powerful enough, shameless enough, you can rape women, be found liable in court, and still face no meaningful consequences.

You just have to keep appealing, keep attacking, keep denying—and maybe win an election.
