Broken Institutions (1970s-Present)

Rigged Rules: How America's Political Plumbing Failed

October 17, 2025

From my perch in southwestern Ontario, where our parliamentary system forces coalition-building even amid heated debates (hello, every federal election), America’s institutional breakdowns look like a slow-motion car crash we both risk. The X post’s warning about tyranny becoming law hits harder when you realize the U.S. system—designed by Jefferson and Madison to check power—now amplifies it.

The “No Kings” rallies tomorrow aren’t just venting; they’re symptoms of a machine that’s rusted, warped, and tilted toward extremes. This isn’t ancient history; it’s the wiring from the 1970s onward that turned moderate disagreement into existential gridlock.

The Primary Trap: Rewarding the Fringe

America’s presidential primaries, once smoke-filled-room affairs run by party elders, democratized in the 1970s with McGovern-Fraser reforms. Sounds good, right? More voter power. But in practice, it hands the nomination to the most ideologically pure candidates in low-turnout contests dominated by activists.

In Iowa caucuses or New Hampshire primaries, a fired-up 10% of the party can crown the nominee. Moderates? Sidelined. This birthed Gingrich-style warriors and, later, Trump—candidates who thrill the base but alienate the center. Contrast that with Canada’s leadership races: broader electorates and ranked ballots pull toward consensus, though our own party infighting shows it’s no panacea.

No wonder American nominees arrive in the general election already battle-hardened against their own party, let alone the opposition.

Gerrymandering’s Carve-Up

If primaries select extremists, gerrymandering packs them into safe seats. Post-2010 census, both parties redrew districts like jigsaw puzzles favoring their side—creating 90% “safe” House seats where incumbents face no real threat. The result? Polarized echo chambers in Congress, where reelection means pandering to the base, not solving problems.

From up here, it’s equal parts wild and worrisome: Canada’s independent commissions aim for fairer maps, but even we deal with cries of urban bias in ridings. America’s partisan carve-ups just crank that tension to eleven, turning democracy into a funhouse mirror.

A 2023 study by the Brennan Center found gerrymandering boosted polarization by 20% since the 1990s. It’s not just unfair; it’s anti-democratic, turning elections into coronations.

Money as the Ultimate Kingmaker

Citizens United in 2010 supercharged dark money, letting corporations and billionaires flood campaigns with unlimited “speech.” Super PACs now outspend candidates 10-to-1, drowning out small donors. The 2020 cycle alone saw $14 billion spent—more than Canada’s entire 2021 federal election budget times twenty.

This tilts power to donors, not voters. Tech moguls fund blue causes; oil barons back red ones. The average American? A spectator. In Canada, our spending caps and public funding keep things grounded—elections cost ~$600 million CAD total—but even we face donor influence whispers. America’s cash deluge buys ads that demonize opponents, not debate ideas, making every race a mud-wrestling match.

Congressional Compartmentalization

Once, lawmakers lived in D.C. year-round, sending kids to the same schools, sharing golf games. Now? They fly home weekly, bunk in shared crash pads, and avoid cross-aisle mingling. The 1994 “Gingrich rules” formalized this—no more bipartisan retreats. Add 24/7 media scrutiny, and compromise becomes a scarlet letter.

Polarization metrics from the Lugar Center show cross-party collaboration at 1950s lows. Bills pass on party-line votes 70% of the time, up from 20% in the 1970s. It’s a far cry from our Parliament Hill, where MPs from rival parties still grab beers post-session, though our own caucus silos are no stranger.

Eroding Norms: From Watergate to January 6

Institutions rely on unwritten rules—respecting elections, peaceful transfers, judicial independence. Watergate cracked that facade in the 1970s; Iran-Contra and Clinton’s impeachment widened it. But Trump’s era shattered it: challenging 2020 results, pressuring officials, storming the Capitol.

This norm erosion isn’t partisan—both sides bend rules—but the asymmetry matters. Republicans have weaponized the filibuster to block nominees; Democrats pack courts in response. From a Canadian eye, it’s alarming: Our notwithstanding clause is controversial enough without routine election denialism. Pew polls show 65% of Americans now doubt the system’s fairness, eroding trust that once held the fragile union together.

The Compounding Crisis

These broken parts don’t fail alone—they interact. Primaries elect ideologues who gerrymander safe seats, chase donor dollars, shun bipartisanship, and ignore norms. The result? A Congress passing fewer laws (down 50% since 1970s peaks) while polarization soars.

The Jefferson quote feels prophetic: When institutions enable tyranny—unaccountable power, voter suppression, endless gridlock—rebellion simmers. Tomorrow’s rallies signal that Americans sense the rot.

Fixing it won’t be easy—reforms like ranked-choice voting or independent redistricting face the very gridlock they aim to cure. But ignoring it risks turning “say no to kings” from protest chant to civil strife.

The 2024 midterms saw record spending and the lowest trust in election integrity ever recorded. Now in 2025, with both parties claiming the system is rigged (just for different reasons), the institutional breakdown feels complete.

Thoughts for my Next Essay

Broken institutions create broken people. Or maybe it’s the other way around. When Americans no longer trust their democracy to work, when every election feels like an existential threat, something shifts in the human psyche. The neighbor with the different yard sign stops being Bob from the PTA and becomes The Enemy. The cousin from across the border—the one you used to share summers with—becomes a stranger whose politics make them unreachable. How did we get to the point where politics rewired our brains for warfare?...
