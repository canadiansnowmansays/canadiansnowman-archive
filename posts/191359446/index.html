The Iran Crisis

"The Off-Ramps"

Part XLIX: Days Nineteen and Twenty: The Pragmatist Is Dead, the Dissenter Is Gone, and the War Has Nowhere to Go

March 18, 2026

In a single overnight operation, Israel killed the man most capable of negotiating an end to the war and the commander of the militia most capable of suppressing a popular uprising against the regime.

I. The Killing — Confirmed

By Tuesday afternoon, Iranian state media had confirmed what Israel announced in the early hours: Ali Larijani was dead. State television called him a martyr. Photographs of Larijani appeared alongside images of the slain supreme leader Ayatollah Khamenei. The Tasnim News Agency, affiliated with the Revolutionary Guards, added that Larijani’s son Morteza and the head of his personal security detail had also been killed in the same strike near Tehran.

Israel also confirmed the killing of Gholamreza Soleimani, commander of the Basij — the plainclothes militia of one million that has manned checkpoints across Tehran, enforced the internet blackout, and crushed the January protests that killed thousands of Iranians. The IRGC itself confirmed Soleimani’s death through Fars News. Israel struck Basij checkpoints across Tehran in the aftermath, publishing aerial footage of what appeared to be strikes on a busy road in central Tehran.

The killing came on the eve of Nowruz — the Persian New Year. Iranians traditionally gather in the streets at night to jump over bonfires. The government issued text message warnings and state television broadcasts telling citizens to remain inside, saying Israeli agents were planning to turn the celebration into chaos and unrest. Heavy security presence was reported in cities across Iran. The killing of the de facto ruler of the country, on the first night of the new year, was not accidental timing.

Iran’s First Vice President, Mohammad Reza Aref, had narrowly escaped an assassination attempt the day before when Israel bombed a building in the Bonyad Shahid compound as he was visiting.

A senior Iranian official described the mood among colleagues as “deep shock” and “pervasive anxiety” — officials calling each other, wondering who would be targeted next. An Iranian Revolutionary Guards member, speaking anonymously, said the killings had made him “more resolved to fight.” One conservative analyst pushed back on the idea that any killing could stop Iran: “The great supreme leader of the country was martyred and the country is still standing. This proved that there is no figure in this country whose death will really change the course of the war.”

But the analysts outside the government were telling a different story.

II. Elite Thinning

The New York Times published a news analysis Tuesday morning under the headline: “Larijani’s Killing Could Allow the Military to Tighten Its Grip on Iran.” The argument requires no embellishment.

Larijani was the bridge — between hard-line military elements and moderate political factions, between the IRGC generals who installed Mojtaba and the civilian structures that remained. He was the one figure with the clout, relationships, and pragmatic temperament to negotiate an end to this war. He had lost the succession argument. But he had survived as the de facto ruler, the man still capable of creating elite consensus in the aftermath.

Now he is gone. What remains is the layer beneath him — which is harder.

Hamidreza Azizi, an expert on Iranian security issues at the German Institute for International and Security Affairs: “This process of elite-thinning, every layer that you remove, the next layer is going to be more hard-line.” Larijani’s skills would have been essential for creating consensus among elites in the aftermath of the war. “Now that it seems everything is in the hands of the military elite, it’s very difficult to imagine how and if they can come up with some ideas, or if they can show enough flexibility, to accept the ideas of the other side to end the war.”

Ali Alfoneh, senior fellow at the Arab Gulf States Institute: “Israel is assassinating anyone who can negotiate with the U.S. Their agenda is different than Trump’s. Only hard-line IRGC is left.”

The killing elevates Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf — speaker of Iran’s Parliament, former Revolutionary Guards commander, the link between Mojtaba Khamenei, the state bureaucracy, and the Guards. His assessment of the war, according to analysts: he is going to continue it. “They believe they are going to create another Vietnam War for the United States.”

Netanyahu announced the killings saying: “We are undermining this regime in the hope of giving the Iranian people an opportunity to remove it.” He added: “This will not happen all at once, and it will not happen easily.” Israeli Foreign Minister Saar said the killings moved Israel closer to toppling the government, while acknowledging that “the regime can only be toppled by the Iranian people.” Iran’s state media organised pro-government rallies in Tehran within hours of the announcement — a show-of-strength response the government has used repeatedly since the war began.

Trump, in the Oval Office Tuesday: “The man who was responsible for the killing of 32,000 people” was killed — his characterisation of Larijani. He did not mention that on Monday he had said he had no idea who Iran’s leaders were.

III. The Resignation

National Counterterrorism Center Director Joseph Kent attends a House Homeland Security hearing entitled "Worldwide Threats to the Homeland," on Capitol Hill in Washington, D.C., Dec. 11, 2025. Kent resigned, saying Iran did not pose an immediate threat. (Elizabeth Frantz, Reuters)

Joe Kent, Director of the National Counterterrorism Center, posted his resignation letter to social media on Tuesday morning. It was addressed to President Trump. It bore the official seals of the Director of National Intelligence and the National Counterterrorism Center. It was signed by hand.

The letter’s core argument: Iran posed no imminent threat. The war was started “due to pressure from Israel and its powerful American lobby.” A “misinformation campaign” by high-ranking Israeli officials and American media “wholly undermined your America First platform and sowed pro-war sentiments to encourage a war with Iran. This echo chamber was used to deceive you into believing that Iran posed an imminent threat to the United States, and that should you strike now, there was a clear path to a swift victory. This was a lie and is the same tactic the Israelis used to draw us into the disastrous Iraq war.”

Then, the sentence that no political response can fully absorb: “As a veteran who deployed to combat 11 times and as a Gold Star husband who lost my beloved wife Shannon in a war manufactured by Israel, I cannot support sending the next generation off to fight and die in a war that serves no benefit to the American people nor justifies the cost of American lives.”

Shannon Kent was a Navy cryptologist and JSOC operator, killed by an ISIS suicide bomb in Manbij, Syria, in January 2019. She left behind two sons.

When Trump nominated Kent to lead the National Counterterrorism Center, he wrote on Truth Social: “Joe knows the terrible cost of terrorism, losing his wonderful wife Shannon, a Great American Hero, who was killed in the fight against ISIS. Joe continues to honor her legacy by staying in the fight.”

Trump appointed Kent specifically because of Shannon’s death. He invoked her sacrifice as proof of Kent’s commitment. Kent has now turned her death into the most personal argument possible against the president’s war. The man Trump elevated to honour his wife’s memory is using that memory as a resignation letter.

Trump’s response in the Oval Office Tuesday: “I always thought he was weak on security.”

The reaction across the political spectrum was precisely the coalition fracture the MAGA anti-war wing has been waiting for — and precisely the fracture the pro-war establishment moved immediately to contain.

Tucker Carlson: “Joe is the bravest man I know, and he can’t be dismissed as a nut. He’s leaving a job that gave him access to highest-level relevant intelligence. The neocons will now try to destroy him for that. He understands that and did it anyway.”

Representative Don Bacon (former Air Force Brigadier General, Armed Services Committee): “Good riddance. Anti-Semitism is an evil I detest, and we surely don’t want it in our government.”

Senator Lindsey Graham: Kent was “echoing Democratic talking points.”

Amos Hochstein (Biden administration senior official): “No one should be taking anything he says seriously — even if you happen to agree with some elements.” He called Kent a neo-Nazi and a racist on social media.

Ilan Goldenberg (J Street): Kent’s letter was antisemitic. “A secret conspiracy of the media and Israelis to deceive Trump into going to war with Iran is ugly stuff that plays on the worst antisemitic tropes.”

Senator Mark Warner (top Democrat, Senate Intelligence Committee), who has long accused Kent of politicising intelligence: “On this point, he is right: there was no credible evidence of an imminent threat from Iran that would justify rushing the United States into another war of choice in the Middle East.”

Laura Loomer attacked Kent and his wife Heather as anti-Israel, reiterating criticism she has made repeatedly. Tulsi Gabbard — Kent’s boss, whose own anti-interventionist instincts are well-documented — issued a carefully worded statement that did not mention Kent by name: “After carefully reviewing all the information before him, President Trump concluded that the terrorist Islamist regime in Iran posed an imminent threat and he took action based on that conclusion.” A statement notable entirely for what it did not say.

The important distinctions: Kent has a genuine history of conspiracy theories including Jan. 6 claims. His framing — “a war manufactured by Israel,” “Israel’s powerful American lobby” — crosses into territory that critics correctly identify as deploying antisemitic tropes regardless of the underlying intent. He can be both a legitimate anti-war voice and someone whose framing of that opposition is contaminated. Senator Warner managed to hold both of those things simultaneously. Most commentators on both sides did not.

What cannot be dismissed: Kent had access to the highest-level intelligence on Iranian capabilities. He is a 20-year Green Beret, CIA officer, and 11-deployment combat veteran. He is a Gold Star husband. He was confirmed by the Senate to his position. And he chose to resign rather than continue.

IV. The Press Conference — No Endgame

Irish Taoiseach (Prime Minister) Micheal Martin speaks with US President Donald Trump at the White House, on 17 March, 2026 [Evan Vucci/Reuters]

Trump hosted Irish Taoiseach Micheál Martin at the White House Tuesday as part of the annual St. Patrick’s Day meeting. The juxtaposition was uncomfortable for everyone in the room. Ireland has official neutrality in conflicts. The war is deeply unpopular in Ireland and across Europe. Martin had urged restraint. He was now sitting next to the man conducting the war.

Trump told Martin that he believed the people of Ireland were “very happy” with the US-Israeli war on Iran. The New York Times reported that Martin “maintained what appeared to be a pained smile.” Martin did not agree. He did not publicly contradict. He said “the people in the Middle East can live in peace” and that “a peaceful resolution of conflict” was the goal. He defended Keir Starmer when Trump attacked him. He called Trump “a very earnest person.” The diplomacy of constrained discomfort.

On the war itself, Trump said:

“We don’t need or desire any help” from NATO. The alliance is making “a very foolish mistake.” “I’m disappointed in NATO.” This was a “great test” and NATO failed it.

On Joe Kent: “I always thought he was weak on security.”

On Iran’s nuclear programme: Iran could have had a bomb “within one month” of the June 2026 strikes. The New York Times’ David Sanger noted this was a “misstatement of the intelligence” — it might have taken weeks or a month to enrich to bomb-grade, but producing an actual weapon would have taken months longer.

On what happens next: “Look, if we left right now it would take 10 years for them to rebuild, but we’re not ready to leave yet, but we’ll be leaving in the near future.” No timeline. No conditions. No definition of done.

On Vietnam: “I’m really not afraid of that. I’m really not afraid of anything.”

On ground troops: not afraid to put them in Iran.

On the war’s nature: “just a military operation to me” — and then, moments later, referred to it as a war.

On what has been accomplished: “We’ve wiped out their navy, wiped out their military in every aspect. Everything is gone.” Eighteen days in, Brent crude is at $103, 17 vessels have been attacked in the Hormuz region, and the Strait remains functionally closed to allied shipping.

Trump considered this “a very big day.” He said at the Capitol: “We knocked out things that nobody thought were possible to knock out.” He did not specify what.

V. The Coalition Update

France’s Macron said Tuesday that France had begun discussions with India, European countries, and Arab nations about a coalition to secure the Strait. The conditions he attached made the announcement nearly meaningless: “Under no circumstances can this be a military operation” and “France did not choose this war.” The coalition he is contemplating requires de-escalation with Iran first — which is to say, it requires the war to be over before the coalition operates.

The USS Gerald Ford is headed to Souda Bay, Crete for repairs after a laundry room fire on March 12 that took more than 30 hours to extinguish. The Ford has been supporting the Iran war and is in its tenth month of deployment. Repairs expected to take at least a week.

Trump on Truth Social: “Because of the fact that we have had such Military Success, we no longer ‘need,’ or desire, the NATO Countries’ assistance — WE NEVER DID!” He said this on the same day a carrier group supporting the war left for repairs and the only confirmed country helping the United States is Israel.

Iran-backed militia attacked the US Embassy in Baghdad again Tuesday evening — a second attack on the same day. Iraq’s military spokesman said Iraq would pursue and prosecute the perpetrators.

VI. The Costs

The World Food Programme reported Tuesday that the war threatened to push an additional 45 million people into acute hunger worldwide — potentially bringing the global total to a record. Around 319 million people already suffer from acute hunger. Shipping costs have risen 18 percent. Fertilizer exports from the Gulf, which account for roughly a quarter of world production, are disrupted, threatening next year’s crop yields particularly in sub-Saharan Africa and Asia. The WFP is already cutting rations to famine-affected populations in Sudan; in Afghanistan it cut food aid recipients from 8 million to 1.5 million. “This would take global hunger levels to an all-time record, and it’s a terrible, terrible prospect,” said Carl Skau, the WFP’s deputy executive director. “The consequences are falling on the world’s most vulnerable people who are already living in dire conditions.”

Delta Air Lines CEO Ed Bastian: jet fuel costs have doubled since the start of the quarter. The airline has instituted multiple fare increases to recoup them.

Lebanon: 912 people killed, 2,200+ wounded. Israel issued a large-scale evacuation warning for the city of Tyre and all surrounding towns — the first such warning for Tyre during the war. Residents were told to head roughly 20 miles north immediately. Only the city’s small Christian quarter was exempted. A second Israeli division — the 36th — has joined ground operations in southern Lebanon. Three divisions are now operating there.

Khiam has still not been taken.

VII. Day Twenty — The Intelligence Minister, Tel Aviv, and the Nuclear Plant

Esmaeil Khatib, the Iranian intelligence minister, during a ceremony in Tehran in 2024.Credit Vahid Salemi/Associated Press

Israel did not wait for dawn. Overnight Tuesday into Wednesday, Israeli strikes killed Esmaeil Khatib, Iran’s Minister of Intelligence. Israeli Defense Minister Katz confirmed the killing Wednesday morning: “Israel’s policy is clear and unequivocal: no one in Iran has immunity, and everyone is a target.” Khatib’s ministry oversaw surveillance, espionage, covert operations worldwide, the brutal crackdown on the January protests, and what Israel called “terrorist activities against Israeli and American targets around the world.”

In three consecutive mornings — Sunday, Monday, Tuesday — Israel has killed the Basij commander, the de facto ruler of Iran, and now the intelligence minister. The elite-thinning Azizi described has accelerated beyond what analysts were modelling.

Iran retaliated. The Revolutionary Guards announced a large ballistic missile attack against Tel Aviv — a cluster missile strike that hit multiple sites in and around central Israel. Two people, a man and a woman, were killed in Ramat Gan, just outside Tel Aviv — the first Israeli civilians killed in the war’s third week. At least one other person was injured by shrapnel in Bnei Brak. A rapid-response vehicle belonging to Israel’s Magen David Adom emergency service was struck. Train service in central Israel was suspended.

Then came the most alarming single development of the war’s third week: a projectile struck the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant on the Persian Gulf coast Tuesday evening. The IAEA confirmed Iran notified it of the hit. No damage to the plant, no injuries to staff. Rafael Grossi, the IAEA’s chief, called for “maximum restraint” to prevent risk of a nuclear accident. Bushehr is an operating reactor, constructed with Russian assistance, running on Russian fuel. A struck operating nuclear reactor — even one undamaged — is a category of escalation this war has not previously reached.

Israel meanwhile escalated in Beirut. Central neighbourhoods that had been considered safe came under strike, sometimes without warning. In the Zuqaq al-Blat and Basta areas, a pre-dawn unannounced strike killed at least 10 people and wounded 27. A second building in Zuqaq al-Blat was hit hours later, fire breaking out on upper floors. In the Bachoura neighbourhood, Israel issued an evacuation warning, residents fled in their cars as the morning call to prayer mixed with honking and gunfire — and then the building collapsed in a plume of smoke. It was the first time an entire building in central Beirut had been destroyed since the war began.

“I think this war will be longer than the last one,” said Hassan Jawad, 40, who fled Bachoura on his motorcycle with his wife, daughter, and son. “My father, my grandfather lived through times like this. Nothing ever changes, it’s happening over and over again.”

“Beirut is not safe anymore,” said another resident, Abu Hussein, 67, who left on foot with his 82-year-old aunt, stepping over broken concrete and shards of glass. “Absolutely not.”

Israel also announced it would begin striking bridges across the Litani River from noon Wednesday, cutting off Hezbollah’s ability to move reinforcements and supplies. The evacuation line — already pushed north to the Zahrani — now applies to Tyre and all surrounding towns as well.

Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE all reported intercepting missiles and drones in the early hours of Wednesday. An Iranian projectile landed near the Al Minhad base in the UAE — the Australian military’s Middle East headquarters. No injuries, minor facility damage. Australia has ruled out joining the war but has deployed a reconnaissance aircraft and air-to-air missiles to the UAE.

IX. What the Analysts Are Saying

Four significant analytical pieces published Wednesday morning in The Atlantic, alongside a major Lemire/Youssef reconstruction in the same publication, form the most comprehensive outside assessment of the war’s strategic position yet.

Adam Serwer traces how “America First” became “America Alone.” Trump spent a decade trashing allies as freeloaders. Now he is begging for their help with the Strait and finding no takers. Serwer frames it not as strategic miscalculation but as ideological fantasy — a specific American myth of total self-sufficiency, the stoic yeoman who needs no one, traced from Jefferson through Reagan and now Trump. “My attitude is we don’t need anybody,” Trump announced. “We’re the strongest nation in the world.” Serwer’s response: “This fantasy of complete independence is oblivious to how those alliances long served American interests. Americans are learning the hard way that the economic costs of the autarky pursued by Trump are far worse than those of the ‘globalism’ he opposes.”

Pegah Banihashemi and Paul Poast make the structural case for why Trump may simply not be able to end this war. The Islamic Republic was built in upheaval and has survived every crisis by closing ranks. The IRGC is not just a military — it is an economic network, a political actor, and the central pillar of regime survival, embedded across energy, infrastructure, and construction. External threats, historically, have strengthened rather than weakened it. Iran does not need to win. It needs not to lose. “All it has to do, as the saying goes about insurgencies, is not lose.” Even a unilateral American declaration of victory would not end the war if Iran continues to fight asymmetrically — drones, proxies, cyber, Hormuz. “Wars do not always end when one side says they do.”

Jonathan Lemire and Nancy Youssef provide the most detailed reconstruction of the war’s political trajectory yet. The Venezuela template was the model: a clean raid, a compliant successor, a cut of the oil. There is no Delcy Rodríguez in Tehran. Trump is now “trapped in a needless war of choice that he started and is unable to finish.” American casualties are now 13 dead and more than 200 injured. The war has cost an estimated $11 billion in its first six days. Republicans privately admit the House appears lost for the midterms. Trump has made “remarkably little effort to sell the war, or explain why it had to happen now.” When asked about the risk of terror attacks in retaliation: “Like I said, some people will die. When you go to war, some people will die.”

Gershom Gorenberg, writing from Jerusalem, offers the view from inside Israel — a country that has been in essentially continuous war for two and a half years. Sirens at 3 a.m. Adult children in Tel Aviv woken six times in a night. Schools still largely closed. Nearly 51,000 more Israelis emigrated than returned in 2025, triple the pre-2023 rate, young and educated. A country whose government just approved nearly $2 billion in coalition pork-barrel spending — subsidies for ultra-Orthodox men who do not serve in the military — on the same night it boosted defence spending by 30 percent, forcing cuts to reconstruction of the very northern communities now under Hezbollah fire again. Gorenberg quotes Sun Tzu: “It is best to keep one’s own state intact; to crush the enemy’s state is only second best.” Netanyahu, he writes, is “aiming only for second best.”

Netanyahu’s regime-change strategy — explicitly targeting Iran’s internal security apparatus to create conditions for a popular uprising — is assessed by analysts as unlikely to succeed in the near term. The Basij “is a very large group of people with a lot of machine guns and they will not give up,” said Lt. Col. Shahar Koifman, former head of the Israeli military intelligence directorate’s Iran desk. “The regime will emerge from this event scarred but alive.” Vali Nasr of Johns Hopkins: “There’s enormous hatred of the Islamic Republic. But now there’s also considerable hatred of the United States and Israel and considerable worry about the future of the country itself.”

X. The Two Decisions

David Sanger’s deep reporting on the Isfahan nuclear operation and the Kharg Island question clarifies the two decisions Trump is circling.

Isfahan. The 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade uranium stored deep under a mountain represents one of Iran’s last remaining strategic assets. Seizing or destroying it would be, by Sanger’s account, “one of the boldest and riskiest military operations in modern American history — far more complex and dangerous than the effort to kill Osama bin Laden.” No one knows for certain where all the fuel is. If canisters are pierced, the escaping gas is both toxic and radioactive. If canisters come too close together, the risk of nuclear criticality. Iran almost certainly anticipated this scenario — George Perkovich of Carnegie: “Instead of 20 or so containers there are hundreds or thousands. They are going to do many things to bedevil anyone trying to get it.”

Before the war, Iran’s foreign minister Araghchi had offered to blend the fuel down to reactor grade under IAEA inspection, keeping it in-country. Kushner and Witkoff rejected the concept. They offered to supply Iran with low-enriched uranium, free and forever. Iran rejected that. There was talk of a follow-up meeting. The war began instead. “All wars end sometime,” Sanger writes, “and in any future cease-fire negotiation, there could be another chance. But for now there is no evidence any off-ramp is under active negotiation.”

Kharg Island. The Marines moving from Japan toward the Gulf have revived speculation about a seizure operation. Trump threatened in 1988 to “go in and take it.” His press secretary posted that quote this week. Clayton Seigle of CSIS: Iran has refrained from attacking “the biggest oil and gas targets that would be most crippling for the world economy” — preserving them as escalation options. Taking Kharg Island could trigger precisely those retaliatory strikes. Caitlin Talmadge of MIT: “Seizing Iran’s most important oil hub would fully eliminate any economic incentive Iran has to let any traffic go through the strait. So it could backfire.” And critically: Kharg Island is 400 miles from the Strait of Hormuz. Controlling it does not reopen the Strait.

XI. The Economic Cascade

The Wednesday gas price figures are now official: $3.84 per gallon nationally, up 29 percent since February 28. The New York Times’ data analysis confirms this is the second-largest four-week price increase in at least 30 years — bigger than the start of the Ukraine war in 2022, bigger than the post-recession surge of 2009. Only Hurricane Katrina in 2005 produced a larger four-week spike. In New Mexico and Arizona, prices are up more than $1.17 per gallon. A truck owner driving 1,000 miles per month is paying $47 more. The increases are falling hardest on lower-income households and older, less fuel-efficient vehicles.

The LNG crisis is the underreported dimension of the war’s economic damage. Qatar Energy shut down liquefied natural gas production after Iranian strikes on energy installations. That single shutdown removed roughly a fifth of global LNG supply from the market. Asia — which receives more than 80 percent of Qatar’s LNG exports — is in what one analyst called “full price competition.” Japan, Singapore, Thailand, Taiwan, Pakistan, and Bangladesh all generate a third or more of their electricity from natural gas. Utilities across the region are competing for remaining spot-market cargoes at record prices. Countries that can switch back to coal are doing so. Thailand ordered coal-fired plants to operate at full capacity. Bangladesh’s electricity from coal has risen sharply since the war began. South Korea is boosting nuclear and coal generation. Taiwan is preparing to restart a retired coal plant.

“If you’re an importer, you say: ‘Well, there are two major crises in five years. We can’t keep dealing with this,’” said Henning Gloystein of Eurasia Group.

Ukraine’s Zelensky, touring European capitals, announced that more than 200 Ukrainian military experts are now deployed in the UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait helping defend against Iranian drones. “Our teams are already in the Emirates, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and on the way to Kuwait,” he told Britain’s Parliament. Thirty-four more are “ready to deploy.” Ukraine has years of experience countering the same Shahed drones Iran is launching across the Gulf — and is leveraging that expertise into security partnerships and arms industry contracts.

XII. Japan — Thursday

Japan’s Prime Minister Takaichi arrives at the White House Thursday for what was supposed to be a routine summit — trade, China strategy, the bilateral relationship. The Iran war has consumed it. Trump wants Japan’s minesweepers. Japan’s pacifist constitution, Article 9, makes overseas combat missions legally questionable. Only 9 percent of Japanese support the US-Israeli attack on Iran, according to the Asahi Shimbun. The situation does not yet qualify as a “survival-threatening” threat to Japan that would permit a military response under the 2015 collective self-defence legislation.

Japan imports 95 percent of its oil from the Middle East. It cannot afford the Strait to stay closed. It cannot afford to be seen as abandoning its alliance with the United States. It cannot afford to be seen as joining a war its population opposes by a nine-to-one margin. “If Takaichi goes there and says, ‘This is a huge concern for us,’ and Trump turns around and says, ‘So what are you going to do about it?’ — I don’t know how Japan answers that question,” said Jeffrey Hornung of RAND.
