The Iran Crisis

“We Don’t Need Anybody”

Part XLVIII: Day Eighteen: Trump’s Coalition That Isn’t, Lebanon’s Ground War, and the Strategic Logic of a Selective Strait

March 17, 2026

Doug Mills/The New York Times

I. The Press Conference

At a White House appearance on Monday, President Trump said something that contained, in three sentences, the entire paradox of his approach to this war.

“You mean for 40 years we’re protecting you and you don’t want to get involved in something that’s very minor?” he said, addressing the European allies who have rebuffed his calls to send warships to the Strait of Hormuz.

Then: “We don’t need anybody; we’re the strongest nation in the world.”

Then: “I’m almost doing it in some cases not because we need them but because I want to find out how they react.”

He launched a war without consulting his allies. He turned down Prime Minister Starmer’s offer of two British aircraft carriers one week ago — “We don’t need people that join Wars after we’ve already won!” He is now expressing frustration that allies will not join a war he launched without telling them, toward objectives he has not defined, and is characterising their refusal as a test he designed. He claimed “numerous countries have told me that they’re on the way” to the Strait. Asked to name them, he said he’d “rather not say yet.”

On the question of negotiations with Iran: “We have people wanting to negotiate. We have no idea who they are.” The war that was launched to prevent Iran from having nuclear weapons is now in its eighteenth day and the president does not know with whom he would negotiate to end it.

II. The Coalition Scorecard

The responses from US allies on Monday were unambiguous.

Germany: “This is not our war; we did not start it.” Defense Minister Boris Pistorius at a Berlin news conference. He added: “We want diplomatic solutions and a swift end to the conflict, but sending more warships to the region will likely not help achieve that.” And then, the line that should be cut out and kept: “What does Donald Trump expect from, say, a handful or two of European frigates in the Strait of Hormuz? He needs them to achieve what the mighty US Navy cannot manage on its own there, is that it? That’s the question I’m asking myself.”

France: Foreign ministry statement: “Posture has not changed: defensive it is.” Macron has said he would consider escorts once hostilities stabilize — not during a war.

Britain: Prime Minister Starmer said Britain would not be “drawn into wider war.” He reiterated that the conflict had “never been envisioned as a NATO mission.” He noted, carefully, that he has “a good relationship with the president” and said British officials were working with European partners on what could be done collectively to reopen the Strait — once the war ends.

Australia: No. Transport minister Catherine King: “That’s not something we’ve been asked or that we’re contributing to.”

Japan: No. Pacifist constitution. Prime Minister Takaichi told parliament Japan had no plans to send warships. The issue will come up at her Washington meeting with Trump on Thursday.

Italy: Not involved in Strait operations, though a missile frigate is in the eastern Mediterranean. An Italian aircraft was destroyed Sunday in a drone attack on the Ali Al Salem base in Kuwait. No Italian personnel were injured.

Poland: Ruled out. Foreign minister Sikorski: “We currently have a war on our borders.” He added something pointed: “It’s a bit worrying that President Trump refers to NATO as ‘them’ or ‘Europe’ rather than ‘us’. There are procedures in place. From what I understand, these haven’t yet been initiated within NATO.”

China: Not yet responding directly. Foreign ministry: both sides still discussing the summit. Committed to “de-escalation.” Bessent is in Paris meeting his Chinese counterpart. A US-China statement “reaffirming the stability of the relationship” is expected in coming days.

South Korea: Will “communicate closely with the United States.” No commitments.

The EU’s top diplomat, Kaja Kallas, said Europe was “discussing what we can do” regarding the Strait — the most positive response of the day, though it contained no commitments and no timeline.

Nick Carter, Britain’s former chief of defence staff, said on BBC Radio that NATO was “a defensive alliance” designed around “articles essentially oriented towards the defense. It was not an alliance that was designed for one of the allies to go on a war of choice and then oblige everybody else to follow.” This is not a criticism. It is a description of what NATO is, which is different from what Trump apparently believes it is.

The coalition that does not exist is the most significant military-political fact of Day Eighteen.

III. What CENTCOM Is Saying

Adm. Brad Cooper, the leader of U.S. Central Command, in November.Credit...Avishag Shaar-Yashuv for The New York Times

Admiral Brad Cooper, head of US Central Command, gave a video briefing Monday that contained impressive statistics and no answer to the central question.

In 16 days of combat: more than 6,000 combat missions. More than 100 Iranian naval vessels destroyed. 90+ military targets on Kharg Island hit, including mine bunkers and missile storage. Iran has conducted more than 300 attacks by missiles and drones against more than a dozen countries.

On the interceptor question: Israel’s military spokesman confirmed Monday that 70 percent of Iran’s missile launchers have been rendered inoperable — “destroyed, broken or made inaccessible” — and are “being hunted in real time.” Iran has fired 300+ ballistic missiles at Israel, roughly half with cluster munitions. Hezbollah has fired 1,000+ shorter-range rockets and drones at Israel since March 2.

On when the Strait reopens, and how: Admiral Cooper offered only that the US military would “continue to rapidly deplete Iran’s ability to threaten freedom of navigation in and around the Strait of Hormuz.” No mechanism. No timeline. No answer.

This is the structural problem that Germany’s Pistorius named. The mightiest navy in the history of the world cannot, seventeen days in, explain how it will reopen a waterway that runs along Iran’s coastline to a country that has mined it, placed drones above it, and has nearly unlimited capacity to deploy small fast boats that cost a fraction of the interceptors used against them.

Mark Dubowitz and Richard Goldberg, writing in The Atlantic from a hawkish pro-war perspective, offer the sequencing argument: first degrade Iran’s launcher capacity, then shift to maritime protection. The carrier strike group moving down the Red Sea, they note, suggests naval capacity is being positioned for that next phase. “Once launcher capacity is sufficiently reduced, US forces can shift toward providing tanker protection and maritime escorts.” This is the most coherent strategic logic publicly available for why the Strait is not yet open. It is also, by implication, an acknowledgment that it will not open until the military campaign achieves its primary objectives — which, per the IDF, will take at least three more weeks.

The IEA, responding Monday, said it could release additional reserves “as and if needed” beyond the 400 million barrels already announced. Fatih Birol: the current release “can provide a buffer for now” but “it is not a lasting solution.” After the full release, the IEA will have over 1.4 billion barrels remaining.

IV. Lebanon

Smoke rises over southern Lebanon during Israeli ground operations near Khiam, as the conflict expands beyond border zones—echoing warnings of potential protracted occupation and rising displacement (now over 1 million Lebanese). Source: The New York Times / Al Jazeera

Israel formally announced on Monday a “ground maneuver” in southern Lebanon, expanding its operations beyond the immediate border zone into what the military described as “new areas.” Defense Minister Israel Katz said Israeli forces would not stop until the safety of Israeli border communities was “guaranteed.” He added that Lebanon faced territorial losses unless Hezbollah was disarmed. His warning about an occupation — a scenario Lebanese civilians are openly describing as their deepest fear — echoed Israel’s 1982 invasion, which lasted eighteen years.

The focal point of the ground fighting is Khiam — a hilltop town a few miles north of the border, overlooking the Upper Galilee, controlling the roads connecting southern Lebanon’s interior to its border towns and the Bekaa Valley. Israeli forces entered Khiam early in the conflict at what residents describe as “breakneck pace” and were subsequently repelled by Hezbollah fighters. The town has not been taken.

Iman Ibrahim, 30, who fled her village of Blida when the war escalated: “I feel like this is preparation for an occupation, and I’m afraid history will repeat itself. Everything we used to hear from our grandparents about occupation, we’re living it now.”

Lebanon dead: 886 and rising. Wounded: 2,000+. Displaced: 800,000+. Israel has conducted more than 1,100 strikes on Lebanon since March 2. UN Secretary General Guterres, who was in Beirut on the weekend, called for an immediate end to the fighting. The UN Human Rights Council on Monday heard from experts who said US and Israeli strikes on Iran were “unlawful” and had cost more than 1,000 civilian lives. They noted that more than 7,000 Iranians were credibly reported to have died in the government crackdown on protesters earlier this year and that at least 30 protesters still faced execution.

V. The China Calculation

Michael Schuman, writing in The Atlantic, argues that the war has inadvertently exposed one of Beijing’s central vulnerabilities: it cannot protect its friends when American hard power is applied directly.

When Trump took Maduro, China condemned it and then quickly softened. When Khamenei was killed, Beijing’s foreign minister could only call it “unacceptable.” China denied providing Iran with anti-ship missiles despite evidence it has supplied weapons components to Russia. Beijing is Iran’s largest oil customer and has been granted passage through the Strait — on Iran’s terms, not on terms negotiated with Washington. But it will not arm Iran in battle, will not risk secondary sanctions, and will not send warships to a war it did not start.

The lesson being absorbed across the global south, in Schuman’s reading, is that China offers trade and diplomatic cover but not protection when the United States decides to act. Santiago Villa of the Atlantic Council, studying Latin American responses to the Venezuela operation, concluded that China’s lackluster support for Maduro had “exposed a gap between its rhetorical commitments and its actual capacity, or willingness, to defend political partners when confronted with hard American power.”

And yet the war is also, simultaneously, expanding China’s room for maneuver. The carrier strike group that was in the South China Sea has been redirected to the Gulf. THAAD systems from South Korea have been moved to defend against Iranian drones. Japan and Taiwan face delays in US arms deliveries. Beijing’s freedom of action in the Pacific has grown while Washington’s attention is consumed by oil prices, alliance management, and a Strait it cannot reopen.

Steve Tsang of the SOAS China Institute: “From Beijing’s perspective, Trump is projecting weakness, not strength.” China’s optimal strategy is to “sit pretty rather than engage proactively” — watch as the US burns interceptors, alienates allies, and locks itself into a Middle East it vowed to leave.

Trump, for his part, told reporters Monday: “We’re really helping China here.” He said this about US efforts to open the Strait of Hormuz. He meant it as a reproach. China heard it as a description.

VI. The Case for the Other Side

It would be a failure of analytical honesty not to acknowledge what the hawks are saying — not because they are right, but because their argument is coherent and their data is partially real.

Henry Nicholls / AFP / Getty

Dubowitz and Goldberg, both associated with the Foundation for Defense of Democracies, argue that the outlines of victory are emerging. Their core claims: 70 percent of Iran’s missile launchers are inoperable. Ballistic missile launches are down 90 percent. Drone launches are down 95 percent. More than 40 senior commanders were killed in the opening strike. 135 nations co-sponsored a UN resolution condemning Iran. The IRGC personnel who run the protest-suppression apparatus are now being targeted — and are reportedly reluctant to man their checkpoints. “For the first time,” they write, “repression forces may fear for their own survival just as protesters have for years.”

They argue that Phase 3 — the political dimension — may be enabled by Phase 1’s military degradation. Mojtaba’s ascension, in their reading, accelerates rather than stabilises regime erosion: “A polished cleric in the mold of Hassan Rouhani could again provide the IRGC political cover. Mojtaba offers no such illusion. His elevation signals a harsher, weaker, more corrupt order — and therefore a more fragile one.”

These are not unreasonable arguments. The military degradation is documented. The IRGC’s internal vulnerabilities are real. The argument that a weaker, more extreme regime is a more fragile one has historical support.

What the argument does not address: the 441 kilograms of near-weapons-grade uranium still in the ground at Isfahan. The gap between tactical military success and strategic outcome that Youssef’s four-phases framework has already identified. The fact that Phase 3 — the political change — “belongs to the Iranian people,” which is another way of saying no one in Washington controls it. And the Citrinowicz warning, delivered in Week One and unrefuted since: “A war that was meant to prevent Iran from having a bomb could be the war that actually pushed Iran beyond the Rubicon to reach a bomb.”

The hawks may be right that the war is going well militarily. They have not answered what happens the morning after it ends.

VII. The Afternoon

As Trump’s press conference concluded, the afternoon brought a cascade of developments that deepened almost every line of the essay.

The electricity grid. US-Israeli strikes targeted an electricity distribution center supplying a large section of Tehran’s eastern neighborhoods. Power was knocked out for several hours before being restored. Apartment buildings in residential neighborhoods were also destroyed. Mohammad Hossein, a Tehran resident speaking on Clubhouse: “They seem intent to destroy Iran. We are very worried.” The targeting of civilian electrical infrastructure — distinct from oil depots, military sites, and command nodes — represents a new category of strike.

One million displaced in Lebanon. The Lebanese government confirmed Monday evening that more than one million people had been displaced in the country — roughly one in every six Lebanese. The figure crossed the threshold as Israeli ground forces continued expanding into “new areas” of the south. The G7 nations minus the United States — Canada, France, Germany, Italy, and Britain — released a joint statement saying they were “gravely concerned” by the escalating conflict in Lebanon. They condemned Hezbollah’s attacks and Israel’s attacks on civilians and UN peacekeepers simultaneously, and warned that any Israeli ground offensive “would have devastating humanitarian consequences and could lead to a protracted conflict.”

Jerusalem. Iranian missile fragments fell in the vicinity of the Al-Aqsa Compound, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and the Jewish Quarter of the Old City. A spokesman for the Latin Patriarchate said a fragment landed several hundred feet from the church. At least one person was injured. Missile fragments from the war’s most contested religious sites landing on the most contested religious real estate on earth.

The Abu Dhabi oil field. A drone struck the Shah oil and gas field belonging to the Abu Dhabi National Oil Company. No injuries. One of the UAE’s own oil production facilities, now burning.

Mojtaba’s second statement. Iran’s new supreme leader issued his second communication — he would retain all officials appointed by his father. The man who has not appeared in public since being announced as supreme leader is issuing personnel directives. The IRGC generals who installed him are watching.

The uranium admission. Trump, at his Monday press conference, said something notable: “You can’t let the most violent, vicious country in the last 50 years have a nuclear weapon, because the Middle East will be gone. Israel will go first without question, and they’ll certainly take a shot at us before we get our act together.” He is “considering a risky operation to send forces into Isfahan, a city of 2 million people in the center of the country, to recover 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade uranium,” the New York Times reported. The president who launched a war to prevent Iran from obtaining nuclear weapons is now contemplating a ground operation in the middle of a country he is simultaneously bombing from the air, to retrieve enriched uranium from an underground facility his own bombs have been unable to destroy. Trump separately dismissed the idea of Israel conducting a nuclear strike on Iran: “Israel would never do that.”

On Iran’s leadership: “We don’t know who their leaders are. Look, all of their leaders are dead, as far as we know.” Trump said this eighteen days into a war. “We have people wanting to negotiate. We have no idea who they are.”

The EU final answer on the Strait. Kallas confirmed after the Brussels foreign ministers meeting: the EU will not expand Operation Aspides to the Strait of Hormuz. “For the time being, there was no appetite in changing the mandate.” Germany had expressed skepticism before the meeting and the 27-nation bloc agreed. “This is not Europe’s war, but Europe’s interests are directly at stake.” The answer is no.

Markets. Brent closed at $100.21, down 2.8 percent on the day — markets apparently reassured by... something. WTI closed at $93.50, down 5.3 percent. The S&P 500 rose 1 percent, recouping some of last week’s losses. Still down 2.6 percent since the war began.

Israel. Schools began reopening in parts of Israel deemed relatively safe — southern periphery areas, Negev, some West Bank settlements. About a million Israeli schoolchildren have been learning remotely since February 28. The IDF confirmed it was prepared for “at least three more weeks and even more if we need to.”

VIII. The Killing of the Pragmatist

On Tuesday morning, Israel killed Ali Larijani.

In Fassihi’s succession story, published two days ago and drawn on in Part XLVII of this series, Larijani emerged as the critical counterweight to the IRGC generals who installed Mojtaba Khamenei. He was head of Iran’s Supreme National Security Council, close confidant of the slain supreme leader, pragmatist, negotiator — the man who argued for a moderate successor, who briefly de-escalated Iran’s Gulf strikes on March 7 before being overridden by the generals, who presented the dead father’s written will opposing hereditary succession. He was, in Fassihi’s framing, “Iran’s Delcy” — the figure who could potentially deal with the United States to end hostilities the way Venezuela’s Delcy Rodríguez had after Maduro’s removal.

He had overseeen Iran’s nuclear negotiations with Washington. He was the liaison with Russia, Qatar, and Oman. He had kept Iran’s channels to the outside world open. He was the one figure with the clout to work across Iran’s factional divides, the pragmatic reputation to give any deal credibility, and the institutional relationships to enforce compliance. He was the faction the IRGC generals defeated in the succession fight. He was the faction Israel has now killed.

Israel also announced the killing of Gholamreza Soleimani — commander of the Basij militia, the plainclothes force of approximately one million that crushed the January protests and has been manning checkpoints across Tehran throughout the war. The Basij commander who led the force that killed thousands of Iranian protesters is dead. The man who could have ended the war through negotiation is dead.

Iran had not officially confirmed either killing as of the morning report. Two Iranian officials speaking anonymously said they believed Larijani had been killed. His X account posted an image of a handwritten statement honoring Iranian sailors killed in a US attack — the timing was unclear, and it was not clear who had posted it or when it was written.

Israel’s Foreign Minister Saar said the killings moved Israel “closer to toppling Iran’s government,” while acknowledging that the regime could only be toppled by the Iranian people themselves. He has said this before. The Dubowitz/Goldberg argument in Section VI of this essay — that killing the repression apparatus weakens the regime from within — just received its most significant test. The Basij commander is dead. The checkpoints remain. Tehran’s streets are still empty at night.

What Larijani’s death does to the prospect of a negotiated end to this war is not complicated. Thomas Wright, writing in The Atlantic this morning, argued that the window for Trump to declare victory and walk away was “rapidly closing.” Iran’s willingness to fight until the United States agrees to guarantees against future attack — the restoration of deterrence, not surrender — was already Iran’s stated position. The killing of the one figure who might have bridged that gap does not make a deal more likely.

Wright names three realistic options remaining. First: escalate dramatically — a raid on Isfahan for the uranium, or a seizure of Kharg Island, both of which carry catastrophic risk profiles. Second: prepare for a prolonged war the administration has not built public support for and for which Vance himself said the day before the war began there was “no chance.” Third: a negotiated settlement — which Iran, now better positioned for a protracted conflict than it was on February 27, will demand on terms worse than the administration could have obtained before the first bomb fell.

There is a fourth factor Wright identifies that runs beneath all three options: the personal. Mojtaba Khamenei lost his father, his wife, his son, and his sister in the opening hours of this war. He was injured himself. The United States stripped many officials targeted by Iran of their government security on the first day of Trump’s second term — the officials involved in Soleimani’s killing and other operations now navigate without protection. “For some within the administration,” Wright writes, “the safest strategy may appear to be pressing the conflict until Israel succeeds in killing Mojtaba Khamenei as well.”

The war that was meant to be Venezuela is not Venezuela. The off-ramp just got narrower.

Eliot Cohen in The Atlantic — no dove, a serious strategic thinker who served in the George W. Bush administration — offered on Tuesday morning what may be the most balanced assessment of the entire conflict: both sides are making large errors. The hawks, he argues, are right that the Iran problem is real, that the regime is genuinely dangerous, that all previous approaches have failed, and that ignoring Iran is impossible. The opponents are right that the administration has botched domestic support, Congressional authorisation, and alliance management “with a rare degree of thoroughness.” His conclusion: “responsible advocacy requires a measure of intellectual honesty sorely missing from both sides.”

Day nineteen. The pragmatist is dead. The window is closing. The choices remaining are all worse than the choices that existed on February 27.
