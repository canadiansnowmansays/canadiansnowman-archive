The Helen Keller Administration

Part 2: The Cumulative Cost — How 34 Loyalty Appointments Broke American Governance

February 26, 2026

Helen Adams Keller / By Charles Whitman / Platinum print, 1904 / National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian Institution

This piece uses Helen Keller’s name and portrait as a metaphor to contrast her extraordinary intellectual and moral achievements—despite being born blind and deaf—with an administration that chooses not to see, hear, or speak uncomfortable truths. The comparison honors Keller’s legacy as one of America’s greatest thinkers and advocates; it is not intended to mock or diminish her disabilities in any way. I hold deep admiration for her life and work.

This is Part 2 of our latest installment in the “Blind Leading the Blind” series. Part 1 introduced twelve new additions to the administration’s roster of loyalty-over-competence appointments. This installment examines the cumulative institutional damage across all 34 documented cases, updates the status of our original 22 profiles, and assesses the systemic consequences for American governance entering 2026.

Introduction: The Compound Interest of Incompetence

In finance, compound interest is the most powerful force in wealth creation — small gains accumulating on top of previous gains, growing exponentially over time. The Helen Keller Administration has discovered the inverse principle: compound incompetence. Small failures accumulate on top of previous failures, each one creating conditions that make the next failure more likely, more severe, and harder to reverse.

When we launched this series in May 2025, we documented individual cases of loyalty trumping competence. Ten months later, the story is no longer about individual appointments. It’s about what happens when an entire government operates on the principle that loyalty is the only qualification that matters — and how the resulting failures feed on each other in ways that no single appointment could produce.

A surgeon general who questions vaccines reinforces an HHS Secretary who defunds vaccine research. An EPA administrator who prioritizes deregulation validates an Interior Secretary who opens public lands to extraction. An Attorney General who weaponizes prosecution enables a Deputy Chief of Staff who criminalizes dissent. A Fed Chair nominee who genuflects to presidential pressure emboldens a Treasury Secretary who threatens to investigate anyone who doesn’t.

No single appointment is the catastrophe. The catastrophe is the system.

Section I: The Original 22 — Ten Months Later

Our May 2025 analysis profiled 22 key appointments. Here is where they stand, ten months on. In nearly every case, the concerns raised during vetting have not merely been validated — they’ve been exceeded.

The National Security Catastrophe

Pete Hegseth (Secretary of Defense): Our original analysis documented his April 2025 Signal chat security breach. Since then, Hegseth has presided over military strikes against Iran’s nuclear facilities in June 2025, constitutional crises involving unauthorized deployment of National Guard troops against California protesters, and what former Pentagon officials have described as “total chaos” at the Department of Defense. Reports emerged in the summer of 2025 that the White House had begun looking for his replacement — an extraordinary development for a Cabinet secretary barely six months into his tenure. He remains in the position, apparently because replacing him would require Trump to admit the original appointment was a mistake.

The progression we documented — from Signal chat leak to California constitutional crisis to Iran nuclear strikes — represents the most dangerous escalation of incompetence in the entire administration. The same man who couldn’t manage a group chat was photographed in the Situation Room as the United States launched its first military strikes against Iran.

Tulsi Gabbard (Director of National Intelligence): Confirmed 52-48 after a grueling process in which over 100 former national security officials warned against her nomination. Gabbard, whom Russian state TV once called “our girlfriend,” now oversees the entire U.S. intelligence community. The woman who met with Syrian dictator Assad and defended Russia’s invasion of Ukraine controls the intelligence briefings delivered to the President of the United States.

Career intelligence officers have continued their quiet exodus. The institutional knowledge walking out the door — decades of experience in counterterrorism, counterintelligence, and strategic analysis — cannot be replaced by loyalty. But in the Helen Keller Administration, that’s not a bug. Intelligence professionals who ask inconvenient questions are a liability. Those who provide the answers the president wants to hear are assets.

Kash Patel (FBI Director): Our original analysis documented Patel’s QAnon promotion, his literal enemies list, and his children’s books about “King Donald” and “Hillary Queenton.” Since then, Patel has eliminated the FBI’s internal auditing office that oversaw warrantless surveillance, demanded lists of agents who investigated January 6, and reportedly placed Russia experts on leave.

Patel represents the purest distillation of the Helen Keller Administration’s approach to law enforcement: the FBI exists not to investigate crimes but to investigate the president’s enemies. He has transformed the bureau from an independent law enforcement agency into what career agents describe as a political enforcement arm. The agents who remain do so with the understanding that their career prospects depend not on the quality of their investigations but on the direction of them.

The Public Health Disaster

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. (Secretary of Health and Human Services): Every warning issued during his confirmation — by the 75 Nobel Prize winners, the 17,000 doctors, his own family members — has been vindicated. Kennedy’s rollback of vaccination protocols has coincided with rising preventable disease outbreaks. His termination of hundreds of millions in mRNA vaccine research funding defunded the technology that helped end the COVID pandemic. The American Medical Association’s unprecedented formal censure of a sitting HHS Secretary stands as a permanent marker of institutional judgment.

Kennedy’s most lasting damage may be the brain drain at the CDC, where epidemiologists and public health scientists have departed at rates unseen since the agency’s founding. When the next pandemic arrives — and it will — America’s capacity to detect, track, and respond to it will be measurably diminished because of decisions Kennedy made in 2025.

Casey Means at her February 2025 confirmation hearing: A wellness influencer without an active medical license, poised to lead public health messaging under RFK Jr.—exemplifying the administration’s distrust of established science. Getty Images via NPR (Senate hearing, 2025).

And now Casey Means sits before the Senate, seeking to become the Surgeon General who serves under Kennedy — a wellness influencer who questions vaccines confirming as the nation’s top doctor under an HHS Secretary who spent decades spreading anti-vaccine conspiracy theories. The compound effect: America’s public health messaging will be controlled by two people who fundamentally distrust the medical establishment they lead.

The Justice Department Weaponization

Pam Bondi (Attorney General): Our original analysis warned about Bondi’s potential to politicize the DOJ. What has actually occurred exceeds those warnings. The prosecution of James Comey over a “86 47” seashell — an act of protected First Amendment expression that Bondi pursued as a federal crime — was merely the opening act.

As documented in our December 2025 two-part series, Bondi’s DOJ installed two of Trump’s personal lawyers — Alina Habba and Lindsey Halligan — as U.S. Attorneys despite their lack of prosecutorial experience. Both appointments were ruled unlawful by federal judges. Halligan admitted to submitting grand jury indictments that the grand jury never actually voted on. Habba lost an $83 million case for Trump and didn’t know how to authenticate evidence in court.

Both women were selected precisely because they lacked the professional standards that would have prevented them from doing what career prosecutors refused to do: indict Trump’s perceived enemies regardless of evidence. When career U.S. Attorney Erik Siebert refused to bring charges against Comey because the evidence was insufficient, he was forced out within hours. Halligan was sworn in as his replacement and had indictments — fraudulent ones, as it turned out — within days.

The internal damage is staggering. DOJ morale surveys show the lowest employee satisfaction in department history. Seventy-three percent of career attorneys report concerns about political interference. Forty-five percent are actively seeking transfers or exit. Three Assistant Attorneys General resigned publicly over the Comey investigation. The institution that is supposed to represent blind justice now represents something very different.

The Economic Wrecking Ball

Howard Lutnick (Secretary of Commerce): The SEC investigation into potential market manipulation related to Lutnick’s tariff recommendations, which we flagged in our original analysis, has continued without resolution. His mishandling of USMCA renegotiations contributed to retaliatory measures from Canada and Mexico. U.S. trade competitiveness has continued to decline.

Scott Bessent (Secretary of the Treasury): Bessent’s tenure has been marked by the near-default during debt ceiling negotiations we documented, followed by increasingly unconventional policy proposals. His refusal to rule out criminal investigation of the incoming Fed Chair over monetary policy decisions represents a new frontier in the politicization of economic governance. The Treasury Secretary is supposed to be the adult in the room on fiscal policy. Instead, Bessent functions as the administration’s economic enforcer, using the threat of DOJ prosecution to ensure compliance across government.

Stephen Miran (Federal Reserve Board, now departing): Miran’s “Mar-a-Lago Accord” — which proposed deliberately devaluing the dollar while maintaining its reserve currency status, a logical impossibility that economists compared to “trying to be invisible while wearing a neon sign” — mercifully never gained policy traction. But his departure from the Fed Board creates the vacancy that Kevin Warsh’s nomination is designed to fill, completing the administration’s takeover of the institution that controls American monetary policy.

The Environmental Rollback

Doug Burgum (Secretary of the Interior): Our original analysis documented his mishandling of Bears Ears National Monument policy. Burgum has continued to prioritize energy extraction over conservation, and his department now faces Steve Pearce’s nomination to run the Bureau of Land Management — an appointment that would place an oil industry veteran with a 4% environmental rating in charge of 245 million acres of federal land. Burgum and Pearce together would represent the most extraction-friendly Interior Department in modern American history.

Chris Wright (Secretary of Energy): Wright’s climate denial has translated directly into policy. The 18% decline in renewable energy investment we documented in the first quarter of 2025 has continued, with international clean energy investors increasingly directing capital to countries with more predictable policy environments. America is falling behind in the energy transition not because the technology doesn’t work, but because the Secretary of Energy doesn’t believe in it.

The Inner Circle

Stephen Miller (Deputy Chief of Staff): Our September 2025 deep dive into Miller as “The Political Whisperer” documented his role as the ideological architect of the administration’s most extreme policies. Nothing in the intervening months has diminished his influence. His September speech at Charlie Kirk’s memorial — which critics compared to Goebbels’ 1932 invocation of Nazi martyr Horst Wessel — demonstrated his continued mastery of authoritarian rhetoric. Miller remains the author of executive orders, the driver of immigration enforcement policy, and the figure who, more than any other, shapes the administration’s approach to dissent, protest, and opposition.

Miller is the one member of the Helen Keller Administration who is not blind, deaf, or dumb. He sees exactly what he’s doing. He hears the warnings and is energized by them. And he speaks with calculated precision designed to achieve specific psychological and political effects. He is the competent extremist in a cabinet of incompetent loyalists — which makes him the most dangerous person in the administration.

Russell Vought (OMB Director): Vought’s implementation of Project 2025 continues to reshape government from within. His approach to budgeting — ideological rather than analytical — has created fiscal chaos that compounds with every continuing resolution and shutdown threat. The 38-day government shutdown we documented in November 2025, the longest in American history, was a direct consequence of Vought’s approach to budget negotiations: no compromise, no bipartisanship, only capitulation.

Section II: The Institutional Autopsy

Beyond individual appointments, the Helen Keller Administration has produced measurable institutional damage across every major category of governance.

The Brain Drain: America’s Lost Expertise

The most consequential damage may be the one that’s hardest to photograph or tweet about: the departure of career professionals across the federal government.

At the FBI, senior agents with decades of counterterrorism experience have left rather than serve under Kash Patel’s politicized leadership. At the CDC, epidemiologists who tracked disease outbreaks have departed rather than implement RFK Jr.’s anti-vaccine agenda. At the DOJ, career prosecutors who spent their lives building cases against organized crime, public corruption, and white-collar fraud have resigned rather than participate in political prosecutions. At the Pentagon, generals have requested early retirement rather than report to a Defense Secretary who shares classified information in group chats.

This brain drain operates on a timescale that extends far beyond the current administration. It takes decades to develop an experienced epidemiologist, a seasoned federal prosecutor, or a senior intelligence analyst. The institutional knowledge these professionals carry — about how systems work, where the pressure points are, what has been tried and failed before — cannot be replaced by political appointees who arrive with loyalty but without expertise.

When the next pandemic hits, when the next financial crisis erupts, when the next foreign policy crisis demands experienced hands, the professionals who would have managed those crises will be gone. They’ll be in private practice, at universities, or in state government. They will not be answering the phone at the agencies that need them most.

International Credibility: The Trust Deficit

America’s international standing has deteriorated along multiple dimensions simultaneously.

Allied confidence in U.S. military reliability has declined since Hegseth’s security breaches and the unilateral Iran strikes. NATO coordination has weakened. Intelligence sharing — which depends on trust that shared information will be protected — has been compromised by the Signal chat leak and by Gabbard’s appointment to oversee the intelligence community.

Diplomatic effectiveness has suffered as ambassadorships and State Department positions have been filled with loyalty appointments rather than career diplomats. Jeremy Carl, if confirmed, would represent the United States at the UN while promoting “white culture” rhetoric to an international body of 193 nations.

Economic partners have responded to Lutnick’s trade confrontations and Bessent’s unconventional Treasury management by diversifying away from U.S.-dependent supply chains. The politicization of the Federal Reserve — investigating the current chair, nominating a replacement Trump expects to follow orders — has raised questions among international investors about the safety of U.S. government bonds.

Each of these developments is manageable in isolation. Together, they represent a fundamental shift in how the world views American governance: not as flawed but fundamentally competent, but as unpredictable, unreliable, and increasingly driven by one man’s personal grievances rather than institutional interests.

Democratic Norms: The Erosion Accelerates

The Helen Keller Administration’s assault on democratic norms operates on multiple fronts simultaneously:

Judicial defiance: The administration has repeatedly ignored or circumvented court rulings, from the Halligan appointment (remaining in office despite being ruled unlawful) to the deployment of troops against protesters despite judicial orders blocking such action. Each ignored ruling weakens the principle that government operates under law.

Prosecutorial weaponization: The DOJ’s pursuit of Trump’s perceived enemies — Comey, Letitia James, Jerome Powell — using politically motivated investigations and, in Halligan’s case, fraudulent grand jury proceedings, transforms the justice system from a check on power into an instrument of it.

Congressional capitulation: The Senate continues to confirm manifestly unqualified nominees because Republican senators fear Trump’s retribution more than they value institutional governance. The “loyalty tax” we documented in May 2025 — where opposing Trump risks career destruction — has only strengthened. The Senate’s advice and consent role has become a rubber stamp.

Press intimidation: The designation of domestic protesters as “terrorists,” the prosecution of journalists’ sources, and the rhetorical framing of any criticism as “fake news” or “enemy of the people” creates a climate where accountability journalism becomes increasingly dangerous.

Section III: The Helen Keller Paradox

The paradox at the heart of this analysis is that the Helen Keller Administration is simultaneously incompetent and effective.

It is incompetent at governance. Public health outcomes have worsened. Economic competitiveness has declined. International alliances have frayed. Constitutional norms have eroded. Institutional expertise has fled. By every objective measure of government performance, the administration is failing.

But it is remarkably effective at its actual goals. The Justice Department has been weaponized against Trump’s enemies. Federal agencies have been purged of independent professionals. Environmental regulations have been dismantled. Public health infrastructure has been weakened. The Fed’s independence has been attacked. Public lands have been opened to extraction. Democratic norms have been eroded.

The incompetence is not a failure of the system. It is the system.

When you appoint a Defense Secretary who leaks classified information, you don’t get competent defense management — but you do get a Defense Secretary who will deploy troops against domestic protesters without asking constitutional questions. When you appoint an Attorney General who dropped an investigation after receiving a donation, you don’t get independent justice — but you do get an Attorney General who will prosecute your enemies. When you appoint a Surgeon General who questions vaccines, you don’t get improved public health — but you do get a Surgeon General who won’t challenge the HHS Secretary’s anti-vaccine agenda.

The blindness is selective. The deafness is strategic. The silence is calculated.

Helen Keller, the real one, understood this dynamic perfectly. She wrote in 1913: “Our democracy is but a name. We vote? What does that mean? It means that we choose between two bodies of real, though not avowed, autocrats. We choose between Tweedledum and Tweedledee.”

A century later, the administration that bears her name — unworthily, as a metaphor for their chosen ignorance — has proven her point in ways she could never have imagined. The difference is that Helen Keller, who couldn’t see, hear, or speak, saw American democracy more clearly than the 34 officials who can see, hear, and speak but choose not to.

Section IV: The 34 — A Complete Accounting

The Three Wise Monkeys of the Helen Keller Administration: "I See No Evidence. I Hear No Arguments. I Speak No Facts." The ancient Japanese proverb advised moral virtue — see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil. Trump's administration has inverted it into an operational doctrine: ignore evidence, dismiss criticism, suppress truth. Helen Keller, who could neither see nor hear, spent her life pursuing all three. This administration, which can do both, refuses all three.

Conclusion: What Helen Keller Would Have Said

Helen Keller spent her life fighting against the assumption that disability meant incapacity. She was blind and deaf, and she became one of the most perceptive and eloquent Americans who ever lived. She advocated for workers’ rights, women’s suffrage, disability rights, and democratic socialism. She was investigated by the FBI — J. Edgar Hoover’s FBI, not Kash Patel’s — for her political activism. She was fearless, brilliant, and relentlessly committed to using whatever platform she had to speak truth to power.

The administration I’ve dubbed the Helen Keller Administration shares none of her qualities. Its members can see but choose blindness. They can hear but choose deafness. They can speak but choose silence — or worse, lies.

Helen Keller once wrote: “I do not want the peace which passeth understanding. I want the understanding which bringeth peace.”

The 34 officials documented in this series want neither understanding nor peace. They want power, and they have discovered that incompetence, properly directed, is a tool for acquiring it. Agencies hollowed of expertise become easier to control. Norms eroded by defiance become easier to ignore. Institutions weakened by loyalty purges become easier to weaponize.

We began this series asking whether American institutions were strong enough to withstand a systematic approach to appointing people whose primary qualification is loyalty rather than competence. Ten months later, the answer is becoming clear: the institutions are bending. Some have already broken.

The courts have, in some cases, held the line — ruling the Habba and Halligan appointments unlawful, blocking some troop deployments, maintaining some constitutional boundaries. But courts can only enforce their rulings if the executive branch respects them, and this administration has demonstrated repeatedly that it views judicial rulings as obstacles to be circumvented rather than boundaries to be respected.

Congress has largely failed in its oversight role. The “loyalty tax” ensures that Republican senators confirm unqualified nominees rather than risk Trump’s retribution. The Senate’s advice and consent power — one of the Constitution’s most important checks on executive overreach — has been reduced to a formality.

The press continues to document the failures, but documentation without accountability is just record-keeping. And the career professionals who once served as the immune system of democratic governance — the prosecutors who refused political cases, the scientists who insisted on evidence, the diplomats who maintained international relationships, the military officers who upheld constitutional boundaries — are leaving.

Helen Keller, who understood darkness better than anyone, also understood light. “Keep your face to the sunshine,” she wrote, “and you cannot see a shadow.”

The Helen Keller Administration has turned its face away from sunshine — from expertise, from accountability, from the constitutional principles that illuminate democratic governance. All they can see now are the shadows they’ve created.

And the rest of us are left to navigate in the dark they’ve made.
