The Charlie Kirk Debates

#3: LGBTQ + Biblical Interpretation vs. Extremist Rhetoric - Part I: The Case

October 8, 2025

Charlie Kirk referenced biblical “stoning” in relation to LGBTQ+ individuals, calling it “God’s perfect law.” Let’s examine this position using his own “Prove Me Wrong” methodology.

Kirk’s Position: In His Own Words

In a June 2024 episode of his podcast “The Charlie Kirk Show,” Charlie Kirk made one of his most disturbing statements about LGBTQ+ individuals. Responding to children’s entertainer Ms. Rachel, who had quoted the Bible’s “love thy neighbor” verse to explain her support for Pride Month, Kirk said:

“By the way, Ms. Rachel, you might wanna crack open that Bible of yours. In a lesser referenced part of the same part of scripture is...in Leviticus 18 is that, ‘thou shall lay with another man, shall be stoned to death.’ Just saying. So, Ms. Rachel, you quote Leviticus 19, love your neighbor as yourself. The chapter before affirms God’s perfect law when it comes to sexual matters.”

This wasn’t an off-the-cuff remark or a misstatement taken out of context. Kirk deliberately referenced biblical prescriptions for violence against gay men and explicitly called these laws “God’s perfect law when it comes to sexual matters.” He was making a theological argument that Old Testament penalties for homosexuality reflected divine justice rather than ancient cultural practices. Kirk made these statements as part of his broader campaign against what he called the “alphabet mafia”—his go-to insult for LGBTQ+ Americans. Throughout 2023-2024, Kirk escalated his attacks, blaming gay and transgender people for everything from economic inflation to declining birth rates to what he called “the feminization of American men.”

On “The Charlie Kirk Show,” Kirk repeatedly argued that biblical law should guide society and that Americans had been “deceived” into accepting “sexual perversion” that God explicitly condemned. He positioned himself not as a hateful bigot, but as a faithful Christian with the courage to speak biblical truth.

Kirk’s statements weren’t isolated incidents. They were part of a systematic campaign that included calling LGBTQ+ Americans the “alphabet mafia,” comparing gender-affirming medical care to crimes worthy of “Nuremberg trials,” and claiming that LGBTQ+ visibility in schools and media was a coordinated attack on Christian families.

Kirk’s Supporting Arguments

Based on his documented statements across multiple platforms, Kirk’s biblical violence references rested on several key claims:

1. The Bible Is Clear and Unchanging

Kirk argued that biblical law represents God’s perfect and unchanging moral standards. He claimed that Leviticus 20:13’s death sentence for male homosexual acts reflected divine justice, not ancient cultural practices. To question such laws, according to Kirk, was to question God himself.

He positioned himself as defending true Christianity against liberal theologians who “water down” God’s clear commands to make them more politically acceptable.

2. Natural Law and Social Decay

Kirk claimed that homosexuality violated natural law and that societies accepting such behavior inevitably collapsed. He argued that biblical prohibitions weren’t arbitrary religious rules—they reflected observable patterns about how human societies work.

According to Kirk, LGBTQ+ acceptance led to declining birth rates, weakened families, and cultural confusion about the gender roles God designed for human flourishing.

3. Christian Courage vs. Cultural Cowardice

Kirk framed his extreme rhetoric as Christian courage—the willingness to speak biblical truth despite social pressure. He argued that mainstream Christianity had been corrupted by cultural accommodation and that faithful Christians needed to restore biblical standards regardless of the consequences.

He compared himself to the apostles, who spoke uncomfortable truths to hostile audiences, positioning his campus confrontations as modern Christian witness in a pagan society.

4. Religious Freedom Under Attack

Kirk argued that his biblical interpretation was protected religious speech and that any consequences for his statements violated First Amendment principles. He claimed that social criticism, platform policies, or institutional responses to his rhetoric amounted to government-imposed religious persecution, conflating private criticism with government censorship.

He contended that religious freedom required protecting even unpopular biblical interpretations and that secular authorities had no right to dictate how Christians should understand Scripture.

5. Authentic Christian History

Kirk claimed his position reflected consistent Christian teaching throughout church history. Contemporary acceptance of homosexuality, he argued, was recent liberal innovation rather than authentic Christian doctrine.

According to Kirk, faithful Christians throughout history had maintained biblical sexual ethics despite cultural pressure, and contemporary believers should follow their example of theological consistency.

Kirk presented these arguments not as personal hatred toward LGBTQ+ individuals but as faithful Christian witness that prioritized God’s truth over human approval.

Examining Kirk’s Supporting Evidence

Let’s fairly evaluate Kirk’s theological and constitutional claims, even while ultimately assessing whether they hold up to scrutiny.

Kirk’s Biblical Arguments

Kirk’s approach to reading the Bible does raise real questions that many conservative Christians wrestle with: How seriously should we take difficult biblical passages? What happens when Scripture seems to conflict with modern values?

These aren’t trivial concerns. Many faithful Christians worry about watering down biblical teaching to make it more socially acceptable. Kirk tapped into genuine anxieties about whether churches are compromising core biblical principles for cultural approval.

His emphasis on God’s authority over human opinion reflects beliefs that millions of Christians hold about where moral standards come from.

But Kirk’s Bible reading has major problems. He ignored the vast body of conservative biblical scholarship about how Old Testament law relates to New Testament Christianity. He failed to distinguish between different types of biblical laws that even conservative Bible scholars typically separate when studying Levitical texts.

Most problematically, Kirk offered no explanation for why some Levitical laws (about sexuality) should be applied literally while others (about diet, clothing, agriculture) are universally ignored by contemporary Christians. His selective literalism suggests convenience rather than consistency.

Kirk’s Natural Law Claims

Kirk’s argument about natural law connects to legitimate philosophical traditions about moral reasoning and human nature. Natural law theory has real intellectual history among both religious and secular thinkers.

Kirk correctly noted that traditional Christian teaching has historically viewed heterosexual marriage as the norm for human sexuality and family formation. This position reflects centuries of theological development rather than mere cultural prejudice.

His concerns about social consequences of changing sexual norms tap into broader conservative worries about family stability, child welfare, and cultural values—concerns that deserve consideration rather than dismissal.

But Kirk’s natural law analysis lacks any real evidence. He provided no systematic proof that LGBTQ+ acceptance causes the social problems he blamed on it. Contemporary research consistently shows that LGBTQ+ individuals and families achieve comparable outcomes to their heterosexual counterparts when not subjected to discrimination and rejection.

Kirk also ignored the substantial body of scientific research documenting same-sex behavior across species and the biological foundations of sexual orientation variation. His “natural law” claims contradicted rather than reflected observable natural phenomena.

Kirk’s Christian Courage Claims

Kirk’s positioning of extreme rhetoric as Christian courage reflects real concerns about cultural pressure on religious believers and the challenge of maintaining unpopular convictions in pluralistic societies.

His argument about apostolic witness connects to genuine theological traditions about prophetic courage and willingness to speak difficult truths despite social costs. The New Testament does record examples of early Christians maintaining unpopular positions despite persecution.

Kirk’s concern about cultural accommodation reflects ongoing debates about how Christians should engage with secular culture while maintaining doctrinal integrity.

But Kirk’s “courage” framework misrepresents both biblical witness and Christian tradition. The apostolic model emphasized love, mercy, and redemption rather than condemnation and exclusion. Jesus consistently demonstrated compassion toward sexual outcasts while challenging religious authorities who prioritized judgment over mercy.

Kirk ignored substantial Christian traditions of social justice, inclusion, and care for marginalized communities that represent equally valid interpretations of Christian courage. His selective application of “courage” principles suggested political rather than theological motivation.

Kirk’s Religious Freedom Claims

Kirk’s constitutional argument reflects legitimate concerns about religious liberty in pluralistic societies and the proper balance between anti-discrimination principles and freedom of conscience.

The tension between religious conviction and civil rights enforcement does represent genuine constitutional complexity that courts and scholars continue to navigate. Religious freedom does protect unpopular theological positions from government interference.

Kirk was right that the First Amendment provides robust protection for religious speech, including biblical interpretation that others find offensive or harmful.

But Kirk’s religious freedom analysis confused protected speech with consequence-free speech. The First Amendment prevents government censorship but doesn’t eliminate social accountability or criticism of religious claims. Kirk demanded immunity from disagreement rather than protection from censorship.

Kirk also failed to distinguish between private religious belief and public policy advocacy. While he could hold and express biblical views privately, his platform as a political influencer created different standards for public responsibility and social consequences.

Major Problems in Kirk’s Arguments

Kirk’s biblical violence references contained several fundamental problems that undermine his credibility as a serious theological voice.

The Cherry-Picking Problem

Kirk provided no systematic framework for interpreting biblical law. He applied literal interpretation selectively while ignoring vast portions of Levitical law that contemporary Christians routinely disregard. This approach suggests convenience rather than consistency.

Kirk also failed to engage with New Testament texts that explicitly address the relationship between Old Testament law and Christian theology, particularly Paul’s discussions of law and grace in Romans and Galatians.

The Jesus Problem

Kirk’s approach completely ignored Jesus’s own method of interpreting Old Testament law. The Gospels consistently show Jesus prioritizing mercy over judgment, love over condemnation, and inclusion over exclusion. Kirk’s references to biblical violence directly contradict Jesus’s teaching and example.

Jesus’s response to the woman caught in adultery (John 8) specifically rejected stoning as an appropriate Christian response to sexual sin, instead demonstrating forgiveness and restoration. Kirk’s position requires arguing that Jesus was wrong about how to apply Old Testament law.

The Love Problem

Kirk’s theological framework conflicts with Jesus’s identification of love as the greatest commandment (Matthew 22:37-39). His rhetoric promoted fear, rejection, and dehumanization rather than the love that Jesus commanded toward all people, including enemies.

The apostolic teaching that “perfect love drives out fear” (1 John 4:18) directly contradicts Kirk’s approach of using fear of divine violence to motivate behavior change.

What Biblical Scholarship Actually Shows

To properly evaluate Kirk’s claims, we need to understand what mainstream biblical scholarship—including conservative scholarship—actually teaches about Old Testament law and its contemporary application.

The Three-Category Framework

Conservative biblical scholars have traditionally distinguished between three categories of Old Testament law:

  • Ceremonial Law: Ritual requirements specific to ancient Israel’s worship system, fulfilled in Christ

  • Civil Law: Governmental regulations for ancient Israel as a theocratic state, not directly applicable to contemporary secular governments

  • Moral Law: Universal ethical principles that transcend cultural context

Even conservative interpreters typically place Levitical sexual regulations in the complex intersection of these categories rather than treating them as simple moral absolutes requiring literal contemporary application.

How Christians Have Always Read Scripture

Christian Bible interpretation has consistently emphasized several principles that Kirk ignored:

  • Reading through Christ: Understanding the Old Testament through the lens of Jesus’s teaching and example

  • Progressive Revelation: Recognizing how God’s revelation develops from Old Testament to New Testament

  • Cultural Context: Distinguishing between universal principles and culturally specific applications

  • Scripture Interprets Scripture: Using clearer biblical teaching about love, mercy, and redemption to understand difficult passages

What the New Testament Actually Says

The New Testament provides extensive guidance on how Christians should approach Old Testament law that Kirk completely ignored.

Paul’s Teaching on Law and Grace

The Apostle Paul, writing to communities struggling with exactly these questions, consistently emphasized that Christians are not bound by Old Testament ceremonial and civil law requirements. His letters to Romans, Galatians, and Corinthians specifically address how the new covenant in Christ relates to Old Testament legal prescriptions.

Paul’s conclusion was clear: “Christ is the culmination of the law” (Romans 10:4) and Christians live under grace rather than law. This doesn’t eliminate moral standards but transforms the foundation from external compulsion to internal transformation.

The Jerusalem Council Decision

Acts 15 records the early church’s definitive decision about Old Testament law requirements for Gentile Christians. The apostolic leadership, including Peter and James, explicitly rejected the requirement to follow Old Testament legal prescriptions, establishing the principle that Christian faith transcends rather than requires Old Testament legal compliance.

Jesus’s Interpretive Method

Throughout the Gospels, Jesus consistently interpreted Old Testament law through principles of love, mercy, and human flourishing rather than rigid literalism. His famous declaration that “the Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath” (Mark 2:27) established principles that prioritize human welfare over legal literalism.

Understanding Kirk’s Evolution

Kirk’s extreme rhetoric about LGBTQ+ individuals represents a dramatic escalation from his earlier public statements. In his 2020 book “The MAGA Doctrine,” Kirk focused primarily on economic and constitutional issues while avoiding explicit discussion of sexuality and gender issues.

His evolution toward biblical violence references reflects his broader transformation from secular libertarian to Christian nationalist—a change that fundamentally altered his approach to questions of religious authority, cultural engagement, and political methodology.

This evolution is significant because it shows Kirk’s position wasn’t based on consistent theological development but rather on ideological transformation that prioritized political mobilization over careful biblical scholarship.
