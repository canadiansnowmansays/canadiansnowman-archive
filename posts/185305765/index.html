Building European Independence

Ursula von der Leyen’s Blueprint

Part 4: How the European Commission President turned Trump’s threats into a structural transformation of Europe’s relationship with the world

January 21, 2026

Part 4 of The Davos Rupture Series

CanadianSnowmanSays

European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen delivered perhaps the most forward-looking of the major addresses at Davos 2026, framing the current crisis not as a catastrophe to be endured but as an opportunity to fundamentally restructure Europe’s relationship with the world.

Where Mark Carney provided philosophical framework and Emmanuel Macron offered moral clarity, von der Leyen brought something different: a detailed blueprint for European independence backed by concrete commitments, legislative proposals, and billions of euros in investment.

Her message was clear: Europe is building independence. Not as a reaction to recent events, but as a structural imperative that Trump’s behavior has made urgently necessary.

And unlike Trump’s performative “Board of Peace,” she came with actual plans.

The Nixon Shock Parallel

Von der Leyen opened with historical perspective, noting that 1971—the year of Davos’s founding—was also the year of the “Nixon shock,” when the US decision to delink the dollar from gold effectively collapsed the Bretton Woods system.

“In an instant, the foundations of the Bretton Woods system and the entire global economic order set up after the war effectively collapsed,” she explained. “But it also had two major effects. It inadvertently created the conditions for what would become a truly global outlook and it provided a sharp lesson for Europe to strengthen its economic and political power. It was a warning to reduce our dependencies.”

The parallel was unmistakable: Another American president is upending the international economic order. Europe must respond by reducing dependencies—again.

The Nixon shock forced Europe to rethink its reliance on the dollar. The Trump shock is forcing Europe to rethink its reliance on American security guarantees and market access.

European Independence: From Skepticism to Consensus

Von der Leyen addressed the evolution in thinking about European independence:

“When I used this term, European independence, around a year ago, I was surprised by the skeptical reactions. But less than one year on, there is now a real consensus around this. The sheer speed and almost unthinkable scale of the change have driven this.”

She emphasized that this need for independence is structural, not reactive: “This need is neither new nor a reaction to recent events; it has been a structural imperative for far longer.”

The key point: “The good news is, we acted immediately. Whether on energy and raw materials, defence or digital, we’re moving fast. But the truth is also that we will only be able to capitalize on this opportunity if we recognize that this change is permanent.”

And then, echoing both Carney and Macron:

“Of course, nostalgia is part of our human story, but nostalgia will not bring back the old order.”

This wasn’t wishful thinking or warning. It was statement of fact. The old order—where Europe relied on American security guarantees while focusing on economic integration—is gone. Permanently.

Trade Diversification: The Americas Agreement

Von der Leyen announced a major breakthrough—the EU-Americas trade agreement signed in Asunción, Paraguay, just days before Davos:

“After 25 years of negotiations, the European Union and Latin America have created the largest free trade zone in the world, a market worth over 20% of global GDP; 31 countries, with over 700 million consumers.”

This wasn’t just an economic announcement. It was a geopolitical statement.

The message: “We are choosing fair trade over tariffs, partnership over isolation, and sustainability over exploitation.”

While Trump threatens tariffs against allies and demands territorial acquisition, Europe is building the largest free trade zone in the world—without America.

Von der Leyen detailed an aggressive trade diversification agenda:

  • New agreements completed: Mexico, Indonesia, Switzerland

  • Negotiations advancing: Australia, Philippines, Thailand, Malaysia, UAE

  • And then the centerpiece: India

“There’s still work to do, but we are on the cusp of a historic trade agreement. Indeed, some call it the mother of all deals. One that would create a market of 2 billion people, accounting for almost a quarter of global GDP.”

The India deal alone would create a market larger than the US-EU relationship. Combined with the Americas agreement, Europe is systematically reducing its dependence on the American market.

The conclusion: “Europe will always choose the world, and the world is ready to choose Europe.”

This is the diplomatic version of: “We don’t need you anymore.”

EU Inc: The 28th Regime

Von der Leyen announced a radical reform to remove barriers to business growth within Europe—the “28th Regime” creating “EU Inc.”:

“We will soon put forward our new 28th Regime. The ultimate aim is to create a new, truly European company structure – we call it EU Inc. – with a single and simple set of rules that will apply seamlessly across the EU.”

The specifics matter:

  • Entrepreneurs can register a company in any member state within 48 hours, fully online

  • The same capital regime applies across the entire EU

  • Businesses can operate across member states with a single set of rules

Currently, Europe’s 450 million person market is fragmented by different regulations in each member state. Companies face “a new set of rules every time they expand into a new member state,” which “acts as a handbrake on the growth and profit potential of companies.”

EU Inc changes that fundamentally. European companies could operate across the continent as easily as American companies operate across U.S. states.

The goal: “If we get this right and if we move fast enough, this will not only help EU companies grow, but it will attract investment from across the world.”

The Savings and Investment Union

Von der Leyen outlined plans for deep capital market integration:

“We need a large-scale, deep and liquid capital market that attracts a wide range of investment. This will allow businesses to find the funding they need, including equity at lower costs, here in Europe.”

She identified a fundamental problem: Europeans have more savings than Americans, “but this saving is overinvested in bonds and sometimes in equities – but outside Europe.”

European savings are funding American and Asian growth instead of European innovation.

The solution:

  • Securitization programs to mobilize capital

  • Capital markets union to integrate financial markets across the EU

  • Market integration covering trading, post-trading, asset management

  • Supervisory framework reform to make regulation more efficient

The goal is ensuring “that capital flows where it is needed, to startups, to SMEs, to innovation, to industry.”

This is about creating a true European capital market that rivals New York and can fund European companies at scale.

Energy Independence: The Affordable Energy Action Plan

“Energy is a choke point for both companies and households,” von der Leyen stated, announcing an “affordable energy action plan.”

The diagnosis: European energy prices are too high and too volatile, creating competitive disadvantages for both businesses and households.

The solution: homegrown energies—nuclear and renewables—to bring down prices, cut dependency, and end price volatility.

Europe is “massively investing in our energy security and independence with inter-connectors” to create a true energy union.

“Homegrown, reliable, resilient and cheaper energy will drive our economic growth and secure our independence.”

This addresses one of Europe’s key vulnerabilities: dependence on imported energy. By investing heavily in nuclear and renewables, Europe is building energy independence that doesn’t require cooperation from—or vulnerability to—any external power.

Defence: From Unthinkable to Reality

Von der Leyen highlighted the transformation in European defence—perhaps the most dramatic shift in European policy in decades:

“We have done more on defence in the last year than in decades before. We have started a surge in defence spending up to €800 billion till 2030.”

This represents a fundamental break with 70 years of European security policy. For decades, Europe relied on American security guarantees and underspent on defense. That era is over.

The results are already visible:

  • Tripled the market value of European defence companies since January 2022

  • Created three leading European defence tech unicorns working on AI-powered battlefield intelligence and advanced drones

  • Member states stepping up investment at record levels

“All of this would have been unthinkable even a few years ago,” von der Leyen noted.

She was right. Just five years ago, European leaders would have dismissed massive defense spending increases and indigenous defense tech development as politically impossible and economically unaffordable.

Trump’s unreliability as a security partner made the impossible necessary.

Ukraine: The €90 Billion Commitment

On Ukraine, von der Leyen was unequivocal:

“We all want peace for Ukraine. We recognize President Trump’s role in pushing the peace process forward and we will work very closely with the United States and we all agree that Ukraine must be in a position of strength to go to the negotiation table.”

The diplomatic language acknowledging Trump’s role was necessary. But the substance revealed Europe’s determination to act independently:

A €90 billion loan for Ukraine for 2026 and 2027 to:

  • Bolster its defence on the battlefield

  • Strengthen its defence capabilities for a peace agreement

  • Keep basic services running

And critically: “In parallel, we decided to permanently immobilize the Russian assets, and that we reserve the right to make use of them.”

This is Europe saying: We’re funding Ukraine’s defense whether America does or not. And we’re keeping Russian assets frozen as leverage, regardless of what any peace deal might require.

The message to Russia: “Europe will always stand with Ukraine until there is a just and lasting peace.”

The message to Trump: We appreciate your engagement on Ukraine, but Europe’s support doesn’t depend on yours.

Greenland: The Heart of the Matter

Von der Leyen concluded with Greenland, calling it “an issue which cuts to the heart of” European independence, partnerships, prosperity and security.

On Arctic security, she emphasized Europe’s commitment and capabilities: “Our EU member, Finland, one of the newest NATO members, is sending its first icebreakers to the United States.”

This was a subtle rebuke: Europe has Arctic capabilities right now. We don’t need to acquire Greenland to secure the Arctic. We need to work together.

Then came the key line: “The proposed additional tariffs are a mistake, especially between long-standing allies.”

She invoked the trade deal agreed between the EU and US just last July:

“In politics, as in business, a deal is a deal. And when friends shake hands, it must mean something.”

This was von der Leyen saying: You made a deal with us six months ago. Now you’re threatening to break it over Greenland. This violates the most basic principle of international relations—agreements must mean something.

The warning was clear:

“Plunging us into a downward spiral would only aid the very adversaries we are both so committed to keeping out of the strategic landscape.”

Europe’s response would be “unflinching, united and proportional.”

The Four-Point Greenland Strategy

Von der Leyen outlined Europe’s concrete approach to the Greenland crisis:

First: Full solidarity with Greenland and Denmark – sovereignty is non-negotiable. This isn’t a position Europe will compromise on. Denmark is a member state. Greenland’s right to self-determination is absolute.

Second: Massive European investment in Greenland’s economy and infrastructure. Europe will work with Greenland and Denmark to support the local economy—showing that partnership, not coercion, is how allies support each other.

Third: Cooperation with the US on wider Arctic security, including European defense spending on icebreaker capability and other equipment vital to Arctic security. Europe is willing to invest in shared Arctic security—but through cooperation, not submission.

Fourth: Strengthened partnerships with UK, Canada, Norway, Iceland and others. Europe is building a coalition of Arctic democracies to ensure security through cooperation rather than domination.

And finally: “Europe needs to adjust to a new security architecture and the realities that we are now facing.”

Europe is preparing its own security strategy to be published later in 2026, with an upgraded Arctic strategy at its core.

The fundamental principle: “It is for sovereign people to decide their own future.”

The Blueprint for Independence

Von der Leyen’s speech provided the most comprehensive blueprint for European independence yet articulated:

Trade: Diversify away from U.S. dependence through massive free trade agreements (Americas, India, others)

Business: Create EU Inc. to make Europe a seamless market for entrepreneurs and capital

Capital: Build a true capital markets union to fund European innovation with European savings

Energy: Invest massively in homegrown nuclear and renewables to end energy dependence

Defence: Surge spending to €800B by 2030, build indigenous defense tech capability

Ukraine: Commit €90B regardless of U.S. support, maintain frozen Russian assets

Arctic: Invest in icebreakers and capabilities, build coalition of democratic partners

Each element addresses a specific vulnerability that Trump’s approach has exposed. Each element can be implemented regardless of what America does.

The Conclusion

Von der Leyen’s closing captured the entire moment:

“When I started preparing for this year’s address, security in the High North was not the main theme. But in many ways, it feeds into the wider point I started with today, that Europe must speed up its push for independence from security to economy, from defence to democracy... The point is that the world has changed permanently and we need to change with it too.”

This was diplomatic language for: Trump has forced our hand. We’re building a Europe that doesn’t need American security guarantees or American market access to survive. And we’re doing it because you’ve made it existentially necessary.

The speech had everything:

  • Historical perspective (Nixon shock parallel)

  • Concrete commitments (€90B for Ukraine, €800B for defense)

  • Legislative proposals (EU Inc., capital markets union)

  • Trade announcements (Americas agreement, India deal pending)

  • Strategic vision (four-point Greenland strategy)

  • Moral clarity (sovereignty is non-negotiable)

And unlike Trump’s Board of Peace—a performative structure with no substance—every element of von der Leyen’s blueprint is backed by actual money, actual legislation, actual institutional change.

This is what serious statecraft looks like. This is what happens when leaders stop maintaining the fiction and start building the alternative.

Europe is taking down the greengrocer’s sign. And it’s replacing it with something real.
