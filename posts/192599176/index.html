The Iran Crisis

“The Second Month”

Part LX: Days Thirty-Three Through Thirty-Six: What the War Has Become

March 30, 2026

The war is one month old and has entered its second.

In the first month: 11,000+ US strikes. 150+ Iranian vessels damaged or destroyed. Thirteen American service members dead. Nearly 300 wounded — approximately 225 with traumatic brain injuries. At least 1,551 Iranian civilians dead including 236 children, per HRANA — a toll the UN ambassador stopped updating on March 11. More than 1,200 dead in Lebanon. More than 50 dead in Gulf countries. At least 16 dead in Israel.

Brent crude: $115 Monday morning. Up 56 percent since February 28. Gas nationally: $3.99. Approaching $4 for the first time since August 2022. Diesel: $5.42. Up 44 percent.

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. The MAGA coalition is fracturing. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre was closed on Palm Sunday for the first time in centuries. The USS Gerald Ford — one of two American aircraft carriers in the war — withdrew to Croatia after a fire broke out in the laundry. Army Rangers and Navy SEALs have arrived in the Middle East with no assigned missions. Iran is allowing 20 cargo ships through Hormuz this week and calling it a “sign of respect.” Trump is calling it regime change.

This is not a daily dispatch. This is a monthly reckoning.

I. The Volleyball Team

A camera captured the explosion over the sports hall.Credit...Shiraze News, via Telegram

On the first day of the war, February 28, a weapon bearing the hallmarks of a newly developed US-made ballistic missile was used in an attack that struck a sports hall and adjacent elementary school in the southern Iranian city of Lamerd.

The sports hall, at the time of the strike, was being used by a female volleyball team. Helma Ahmadizadeh was ten years old, a fourth-grader. Elham Zaeri was a fifth-grader. Both were at volleyball practice when the missile struck. At least 21 people were killed. A hundred more were injured.

Christiaan Triebert and John Ismay, writing in the Times, have now documented — through visual analysis and weapons experts — that the weapon was the Precision Strike Missile, or PrSM, a short-range ballistic missile that had completed prototype testing only last year. CENTCOM confirmed its use on March 1. Admiral Brad Cooper announced it had been used in combat “for the first time.” The military has been touting its debut.

The PrSM is designed to detonate just above its target and blast small tungsten pellets outward — designed to kill troops and destroy unarmored vehicles. The sports hall and adjacent elementary school had been separated from the IRGC compound next door by walls for at least 15 years — verifiable on Google Maps, Apple Maps, and Wikimapia, publicly available mapping platforms. The school’s social media account showed children regularly using the premises.

Satellite imagery captured March 9, 2026 shows the sports hall and adjacent elementary school in Lamerd, Iran, separated from the neighboring Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps compound by walls that have been in place for at least 15 years. A US Precision Strike Missile struck the hall on the first day of the war while a female volleyball team was training inside. At least 21 people were killed, including 10-year-old Helma Ahmadizadeh. Satellite image captured by Airbus DS, March 9, 2026. Annotation by Leanne Abraham/The New York Times. Published March 29, 2026.

A US official confirmed to the Times on condition of anonymity that the missile used in the Lamerd strike was the PrSM. Jeffrey Lewis, the nuclear nonproliferation expert at Middlebury College who identified the Iranian missile fragment in the West Bank schoolyard last week: “While we knew PrSM was fired, this is the first look we’ve gotten at the business end of the system.”

CENTCOM’s statement: “We’re aware of the reports and are looking into them. U.S. forces do not indiscriminately target civilians, unlike the Iranian regime.”

Helma Ahmadizadeh was ten years old. This is what the first day of the war looked like in Lamerd.

II. 50,000 Troops and One Carrier Gone

The U.S.S. Tripoli entering the Singapore Strait earlier this month. The 2,500 Marines who have been dispatched to the Middle East are part of the U.S.S. Tripoli amphibious ready group.Credit...Edgar Su/Reuters

The scale of the American military deployment in the Middle East has now reached 50,000 troops — roughly 10,000 above the regional baseline of approximately 40,000.

Helene Cooper, writing in the Times, has provided the definitive accounting. The 2,500 Marines of the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit aboard the USS Tripoli have arrived. The 2,000 paratroopers of the 82nd Airborne’s Immediate Response Force have deployed. And Sunday night, Eric Schmitt and Cooper confirmed that several hundred US Special Operations forces — Army Rangers and Navy SEALs — have also arrived. No assigned missions. Options: Kharg Island, Isfahan uranium raid, Hormuz clearing operations.

That is the escalation side of the ledger.

The other side: the USS Gerald Ford — one of the war’s two aircraft carrier strike groups — withdrew from the region on March 23 and sailed to Crete. By Friday it had arrived in Croatia. It is not clear where it is headed. The reason for its withdrawal, per multiple reporting: “constant mishaps, including a fire that broke out in the laundry.”

One of America’s two nuclear-powered aircraft carriers in an active war zone — a vessel with 4,500 personnel, 75 aircraft, and a displacement of 100,000 tons — has been sidelined in part by a laundry fire.

The force now assembled in the Middle East is substantial and, in context, inadequate for the scenarios being discussed. Israel used more than 300,000 troops for its operations in Gaza. The US-led coalition that invaded Iraq in 2003 was close to 250,000 at the outset. Iran is roughly a third the size of the continental United States with 93 million people. Cooper’s military expert assessment is plain: “Taking, let alone holding, a deeply complex country of its size and complexity and weaponry with 50,000 troops, military experts say, is not doable.”

III. Not Toothless

Trump has described Iran as “all but obliterated.” CENTCOM has said Iranian attack rates are “down 90 percent.” Neither statement means what it sounds like.

Nicholas Kulish, Helene Cooper, Isabel Kershner and Erika Solomon, writing in the Times, have documented the hidden metric: Iran’s hit rate — the proportion of attacks that successfully penetrate defenses — has more than doubled since March 10. Fewer attacks, more damage per attack.

Kelly Grieco of the Stimson Center: “Adversaries adapt. There are signs here that we don’t have a defeated adversary and that we may have one that’s adapting and learning.” The US military may have mistaken reduced activity for reduced capacity — Iran may have been repositioning, not losing capability, while integrating new intelligence and surveillance data into its targeting.

The 90 percent reduction in launch rate is not the same as 90 percent of Iranian missiles being destroyed. Admiral Cooper’s careful wording was precisely not that claim. One US official estimated Iran still possesses thousands of Shaheds and possibly hundreds of ballistic missiles — with the caveat that US intelligence on Iranian capacity is limited.

Iran has also introduced a new weapon: ballistic missiles with cluster-munition warheads that break open above ground and disperse dozens of bomblets across several miles. The optimal defense — Arrow 3 interceptors operating above the atmosphere — is costly and in short supply. Lower-tier interceptions cannot stop the warhead from releasing its payload. A Tel Aviv man was killed Friday by a bomblet from a cluster-munition missile.

The strategic implication, from Farzan Sabet of the Geneva Graduate Institute: “Once that sense of insecurity and instability has been created, you don’t need thousands or even hundreds of launches a day. You might be able to do that with dozens of successful penetrations.”

The Saturday single-day record confirmed this: 701 strikes documented by HRANA — 24 civilians killed, 88 injured — three-quarters hitting Tehran. The Haifa oil refinery was struck Monday morning — shrapnel hit a large fuel container, starting a fire. No casualties. But Haifa. The Tabriz Petrochemical Complex was struck Monday in the early hours, sparking a fire that was contained. The war is expanding geographically while Iran’s launch rate falls and its accuracy rises.

IV. The Toll Booth

Iran is no longer merely blocking the Strait of Hormuz. It is formalizing control over it as sovereign territory — and using access as a diplomatic instrument.

Iran’s Parliament has moved to legislate a toll system for ships passing through the strait, asserting “sovereignty, control and oversight.” The IRGC has been selectively granting passage: Pakistan’s flag vessels, Thai oil tankers, and now, as Trump announced Sunday night aboard Air Force One, 20 more cargo ships starting Monday. Trump calls each announcement a “sign of respect” and evidence that negotiations are progressing.

Edward Fishman, writing in the Times, has identified the precise mechanism: Iran has borrowed America’s own playbook. The US sanctions campaign against Iran worked not by sanctioning every bank but by sanctioning a few strategically — “killing the chicken to scare the monkeys” — to reshape the risk tolerance of the entire global banking system. Iran has done the same with drones and the strait: by striking a small number of ships, it has reshaped the risk calculus of the entire global shipping industry. Traffic is down 90 percent. The few ships transiting are moving under Iranian escort, paying Iranian fees, following Iranian clearance codes.

Fishman’s verdict on Bessent’s “jiu-jitsuing the Iranians” framing: “If either side in this war has pulled off an act of jujitsu, it is Iran. For the first time since 1995, Tehran can sell oil directly to the United States and use the American financial system to collect payment. By closing the Strait of Hormuz for a few weeks, it has secured sanctions relief that in some respects surpasses what it achieved via the 2015 nuclear deal.”

Trump Sunday night: “I think we’ve had regime change. It truly is regime change.” The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps runs Iran. The nuclear program’s enriched uranium remains at Isfahan. The clerics remain in place. The toll booth remains operational. Only the leaders have changed.

David Ignatius on Washington Week: “If he tries to walk away from this having left global commerce in a very precarious situation, the world will just be irate.” Ignatius floated a UN-supervised international Hormuz regime — modelled on the Black Sea corridor that reopened Ukrainian grain exports — as a possible exit architecture. It would require Iran’s buy-in. Iran is currently demanding recognition of its sovereignty over the strait as a precondition for any deal.

V. The Houthis — Two Chokepoints

On Saturday, the Houthis entered the war by launching a ballistic missile at Israel. Then a second wave: cruise missiles and drones. Both intercepted without casualties. Their spokesman: attacks will continue “until the aggression ends against all the fronts of the resistance.”

This is the development the global shipping industry feared most. The Houthis control the Bab-el-Mandeb strait — the southern entrance to the Red Sea, through which ships pass to and from Europe, East Africa, and the Indian Ocean. During the Gaza war, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping forced companies to reroute around Africa’s Cape of Good Hope, adding thousands of miles and weeks to transit times.

Saudi Arabia has already been rerouting oil exports through the Yanbu pipeline on the Red Sea coast — using overland infrastructure to bypass the Hormuz blockade. If the Houthis now threaten Red Sea shipping, that bypass route is also at risk.

Both of the world’s critical energy chokepoints are now under simultaneous threat for the first time in modern history. The Houthis have the “geographical ability to destabilize not just the Middle East but the entire world,” a Chatham House researcher told the Times. They have made no secret of their willingness to use it.

VI. Tehran — The 30-Day Blackout

A Red Crescent Society worker at an auto service center targeted by U.S.-Israeli airstrikes in Tehran on Saturday / Arash Khamooshi for The New York Times

NetBlocks confirmed Sunday: Iran’s internet blackout has continued for 30 consecutive days. Connectivity remains at 1 percent of ordinary levels. 92 million people have been effectively cut off from the outside world for the entirety of the war.

Saturday was among the heaviest single days of strikes since February 28: 701 strikes documented in 24 hours. Three-quarters hit Tehran. A Norwegian Refugee Council official inside Tehran: “On Saturday early morning we had attacks very close by at 1 a.m., then again at 3 a.m. Those I can speak from personal experience. They feel so close by.” She added: “Actually nowhere seems to be safe.”

The Iran University of Science and Industry was struck — BBC Persian showed razed buildings and flames. A Qatari-owned television network’s Tehran headquarters was damaged — 10 people injured. A water reservoir in Khuzestan province was struck. The Tabriz Petrochemical Complex was struck Monday.

Iran’s Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf, marking the 30th day of the war, addressed the Marines directly: Iran’s forces were “waiting for American soldiers to enter on the ground so they can set them on fire.” He described the American 15-point peace plan as an attempt to achieve by diplomacy what the United States “failed to achieve in war.”

From inside Tehran: “I can’t even put into words what it was like last night,” said Nassrin, 62, in a text message. “We got no sleep. It was hours and hours of explosions.”

VII. Saudi Arabia’s Silence

The kingdom will not acknowledge American casualties on its own soil.

The Saudi Ministry of Defense has not acknowledged the Iranian strike on Prince Sultan Air Base that injured approximately two dozen American troops and damaged two KC-135 aerial refueling aircraft. Its official statement mentioned only intercepted drones and fragments falling “in a military area” — with no injuries. Trump at the Saudi sovereign wealth fund conference in Miami: “Saudi Arabia fought, Qatar fought, UAE fought.” Saudi government: no comment.

Vivian Nereim, writing in the Times from Riyadh, has documented the architecture of this silence. People briefed by American officials say Crown Prince MBS has been pressing Trump to continue the war privately, calling it a “historic opportunity.” Saudi officials publicly reject that characterization. The kingdom simultaneously hosts American forces, denies US bases exist, presses for continued war, and publicly demands restraint — collecting no acknowledgment of any contradiction from anyone.

Bader Al-Saif of Kuwait University: “The Gulf states are fighting on various fronts: defending against daily, misguided Iranian attacks and an Israeli-U.S. narrative trying hard to portray the Gulf on their side in the war. Neither Iran, Israel or the United States is listening.”

VIII. The Kurdish Dilemma

AFP / Getty

Robert F. Worth, writing in The Atlantic, spent time inside a PJAK underground base in the snow-capped mountains near the Iraq-Iran border — entering through a cave opening, walking 50 yards down a dripping man-made passageway, and emerging into a brightly lit room where a dozen Peshmerga commanders greeted him.

These are the people the war was supposed to liberate Iran through. They have thousands of sleeper-cell members inside Iran ready to join the fight. Hundreds of Iranian Kurds in exile in Europe have been returning to the mountains over the past two months. There is one problem: PJAK is listed by the United States as a terrorist organization — because of its ties to Abdullah Ocalan, the imprisoned PKK founder whose portrait hangs on the wall of their meeting room. The CIA may already be funding them covertly per CNN reporting, but doing so openly would trigger a Turkish crisis. On March 5, Trump said a Kurdish ground invasion would be “wonderful.” On March 7, he reversed. Turkey had spoken.

The Syria precedent hangs over every conversation. Six weeks before the Iran war began, the Kurdish statelet in northeastern Syria — built in partnership with US forces fighting ISIS — was overrun. The US military stood by and did nothing. “Disposable allies” is the Kurdish reading of American history.

Adib Watandust, 72, who has been in the Kurdish movement since the mid-1970s, said something no American official has said publicly: the regime welcomed this war. “For dictators, war is a gift.” He believes the path to Iran’s transformation runs through a general strike inside the country, not through Peshmerga boots crossing the border.

The 23-year-old woman who fled Tehran two months ago, speaking to Worth at a café in Erbil: “I always dreamed of those groups coming to Kurdistan, but deep down I wish they would not do that. I know the slightest thing the Kurds will do, the Islamic regime will bomb every place” in the Kurdish region where her family still lives.

PJAK co-chair Peyman Viyan told Worth the Kurds would “very likely” take part in a ground invasion “soon” — then said plans were “not ready yet.” She pleaded for a “guarantee of Kurdish rights in the future.” As Worth observed: as if anyone could guarantee anything about the future of Iran.

IX. Trump at War

The president’s allies have always said that his unpredictability is his superpower, and that it keeps his enemies guessing.Credit...Tierney L. Cross/The New York Times

Erica L. Green, writing in the Times, has produced the definitive portrait of Trump as commander-in-chief.

Trump is briefed daily via short montage videos of strike footage — less than a minute — that CENTCOM also posts on X. General Caine — the military’s Trump whisperer — lays out risks and consequences without advocating for a position. This became difficult when Trump publicly claimed Caine believed military action would be “something easily won.” That is not what Caine told him.

Jeffrey Goldberg on Washington Week framed the central problem: “There are at least two Iran wars taking place right now. There’s the war being waged by the U.S. military and the Israelis — and the other war is playing out inside the brain of Donald Trump.”

Peter Baker on Trump’s calculation: gas prices come back down, markets recover before midterms. “At least that’s what he’s telling himself.” Susan Glasser cited the Pakistani foreign minister’s assessment: “Right now, the only strategic goal I can identify for the conflict is reopening the Strait of Hormuz that was closed as a result of the war.” She added the word nobody wants to use: “Quagmire.”

David Ignatius drew the comparison that stops the conversation: “I’m reminded of Vladimir Putin in February 2022, who sailed into Ukraine thinking it’d be over in a week and is still stuck four years later in a war he can’t get out of. And it happens when people are flattered and they don’t listen to the evidence.”

Princeton historian Julian Zelizer to Green: “He’s doing the same techniques he always does — threatening people, insulting people, seizing attention. He’s learning that it doesn’t always work. He’s doing the art of the deal in a way that’s just creating chaos.”

Trump prefers to call the war an “excursion” because he was advised against calling it a war — he never sought congressional authorization. When asked about the US strike on an Iranian elementary school that killed 175 children on Day One: “I don’t know enough about it.” A preliminary US investigation has now determined the US was responsible.

Sunday night, aboard Air Force One: “I think we’ve had regime change. It truly is regime change.” The IRGC runs Iran. The enriched uranium remains at Isfahan. Only the leaders have changed.

X. The Coalition Fractures

Elaine Godfrey, writing in The Atlantic, has documented the fracture through a precise lens: the Flagrant podcast, and Andrew Schulz — comedian, 42, who voted for Trump in 2024 and is now describing the war as an occasion to “hasten the rapture.”

The manosphere-to-Trump pipeline that helped power the 2024 comeback is running in reverse. Joe Rogan: Trump’s moves in Iran are “so insane, based on what he ran on.” This week: MAGA is “a movement of a bunch of fucking dorks.” Shawn Ryan, former Navy SEAL and CIA contractor, Trump endorser, on Joe Kent’s resignation: “Every single one of these things is a complete fucking lie.”

The data behind the anecdotes: while a great majority of self-identified Republicans approve of the war, only about half of Republican-leaning independents say the same. Among voters aged 18-29: less than half. Pollster Dan Cassino: the most important metric is “don’t know” responses — which have “gone through the roof.” These voters are not going to vote Democrat. They are going to stay home. Democrats have already flipped 30 state legislative seats since Trump’s election — average 13-point swing. Mike Cernovich on X: “A generational coalition, squandered.”

The antisemitic drift in anti-war MAGA commentary — Carlson, Owens, Fuentes suggesting “Israel tricked Trump into this” — is the darkest downstream consequence of a war that has no clear objective, no exit plan, and no coalition. If it ends in failure, the faction that predicted failure will be vindicated, along with its most poisonous ideas.

XI. Palm Sunday

Cardinal Pierbattista Pizzaballa leading a prayer service to mark Palm Sunday in Jerusalem, amid restrictions on gathering in large groups because of the war in Iran. Credit: Pool photo by Ammar Awad

Israeli police prevented Cardinal Pierbattista Pizzaballa — the Latin Patriarch of Jerusalem — and the guardian of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre from entering the church on Palm Sunday. “The first time in centuries” that senior church officials have been unable to celebrate Palm Sunday Mass at the site, per the Latin Patriarchate.

Italy’s Meloni called it an “offense.” France’s Macron said it joined “a concerning sequence of violations of the status of holy sites in Jerusalem.” US Ambassador Huckabee — a Baptist minister — called it “difficult to understand or justify.”

All holy sites in Jerusalem’s Old City have been officially closed to the public since the war with Iran began one month ago. Al-Aqsa. The Western Wall. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre. The holiest sites of three faiths — all closed for a month because Iranian missile shrapnel has been falling in the Old City.

Netanyahu reversed course Sunday night after the international outcry — ordering authorities to give the Cardinal “full and immediate access.” Too late for Palm Sunday.

Easter is this week.

XII. The Midtransition War

David Wallace-Wells, writing in the Times, has named what this war actually is: a midtransition war — a conflict shaped by an energy transition that is halfway complete but not finished.

Global investment in fossil fuels dropped by more than a third between 2015 and 2020. Clean energy investment grew nearly twice as high. But the world still draws a majority of its energy from legacy systems. The midtransition has tightened fossil fuel markets, concentrated fossil fuel supply, and made energy infrastructure more attractive as a military target — because striking it causes disproportionate damage in a world that has not yet built its renewable replacement. The head of the International Energy Agency has called this conflict “the greatest global energy security threat in history.”

The counter-example is Spain — which has doubled its renewable capacity in five years and cut fossil fuel influence on its electricity price by 75 percent. Spain’s prime minister has been the war’s most outspoken Western critic. Wallace-Wells: “The Spanish energy transition is far from complete, but the country has already bought back the right to speak its conscience.”

The water dimension closes the frame. A six-year Iranian drought pushed the country toward “water bankruptcy” before the protests, before the war. Desalination plants — the region’s most critical civilian infrastructure — have now become military targets in Bahrain and Kuwait. The Gulf draws 90 percent of its drinking water from desalination. “In the meantime,” Wallace-Wells writes, “vulnerabilities proliferate like heat.”

XIII. What It Costs

Butter chicken disappearing from Indian menus. Australian farmers planting less wheat. South Koreans urged to take shorter showers. Formula 1 canceling Bahrain and Saudi Arabia races. Cancer drugs not reaching patients. Gas lines at a Costco in San Antonio, Texas. Thirty minute waits.

This week the ripple became policy. Victoria — Australia’s second-largest state — is making most public transit free for one month. Tasmania: free buses and ferries for three months. Australia’s federal government is halving the fuel excise for three months. Prime Minister Albanese: “We’re acting now to be over-prepared.” Australia imports 90 percent of its fuel from Asian refineries that source their crude from the Middle East.

Dina Guillen, 46, a home health care worker from the Rio Grande Valley, had planned the 520-mile drive to Dallas for spring break. She went to Corpus Christi instead. Saved $100 in gas. She told the Times: “We’ll wait until the summer and see what happens. It’s scary what’s going on.”

Will Gottsegen, writing in The Atlantic: “Airfares are certainly the canary in the coal mine.” Average domestic airfare: $167 in February. $414 in mid-March. Hong Kong-London: up 560 percent. Bangkok-Frankfurt: up 505 percent. 52,000+ flights canceled since February 28. United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby says his company needs to raise ticket prices another 20 percent to break even.

Goldman Sachs argued Monday that markets may be “mispricing” the potential economic damage of persistently high energy prices — making the case for rate cuts “especially compelling.” The bond market is beginning to price recession, not inflation control.

XIV. The Three Journalists

A funeral ceremony on Sunday in Beirut for Lebanese journalists Ali Choeib, Fatima Ftouni, and Mohammad Ftouni, who were killed in an Israeli airstrike on Saturday. Credit: David Guttenfelder for The New York Times

Three Lebanese journalists were killed Saturday in an Israeli airstrike near the southern Lebanese town of Jezzine: Ali Choeib of Al-Manar, Fatima Ftouni of Al-Mayadeen, and her brother Mohammad Ftouni, a cameraman. A paramedic was killed in a second strike.

Israel said Choeib was a Hezbollah intelligence operative. It posted a composite image as evidence — half press vest, half military uniform. On Sunday the military acknowledged that part of the image had been photoshopped. It called it an “illustration.” It then posted a replacement photograph — grainy, no distinguishing insignia — whose provenance it declined to explain.

Hundreds gathered in driving rain in Beirut on Sunday to bury the three. Wails and ululations. White shrouds adorned with roses. “He was our eyes in the south,” said Caroline Kareem, 32, a nurse at the cemetery.

Human Rights Watch: “Just reporting on the advancement of Israeli troops or engaging in propaganda does not make someone a military target.” Lebanon has filed a complaint with the UN Security Council. The Israeli military declined to comment on the killing of Fatima and Mohammad Ftouni. It has not provided evidence that either was a combatant. They were in the same car.
