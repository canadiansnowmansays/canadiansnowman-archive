Afghanistan: The Eternal Battleground

The Eternal Battleground

Epilogue: The Pattern Continues

April 29, 2026

Pakistan Goes to War With Afghanistan

I began writing this epilogue on February 27, 2026 — the day Pakistan formally declared itself in “open war” with Afghanistan. I am finishing it on April 27th. In the two months between those two dates, 400 people were killed in a single airstrike on a drug rehabilitation centre in Kabul, a ceasefire brokered for Ramadan was violated within hours, China convened peace talks in a city 3,000 miles from the fighting, and Pakistani shells struck a university in Kunar province while students were inside.

The centre was named Omid.

In Dari, that means hope.

The pattern doesn’t just continue. It accelerates.

The Photo That Completes the Circle

Taliban soldiers looking toward Pakistan from the Afghan side of the Torkham border crossing, on Friday.Credit...Wahidullah Kakar/Associated Press

Look at the photograph published in the New York Times on February 26th: Three Taliban soldiers positioned on a rocky mountain slope near the Torkham border crossing. One crouches in the foreground, aiming a rifle with a scope toward Pakistan.

The terrain is instantly recognizable to anyone who has studied Afghan history. These are the same mountains where:

  • Alexander’s Macedonian phalanx bled for three years (329-327 BCE)

  • British soldiers were ambushed during the catastrophic 1842 retreat

  • Soviet convoys were destroyed in narrow defiles

  • American helicopters crashed trying to reach remote valleys

The mountains haven’t changed in 2,500 years.

The rifles are modern. The fighters now serve the Taliban government rather than resisting foreign occupation. But the fundamental reality remains: Afghans defending their territory from mountain positions, using geography as their greatest weapon.

The Latest Chapter in the Oldest Story

What happened:

  • Friday, February 26, 2026: Afghan troops attacked Pakistani border positions

  • Hours later: Pakistan struck Kabul (population 6 million), Kandahar (where the Taliban’s supreme leader lives), and border provinces

  • At least one ammunition depot in Kabul bombed

  • Pakistan’s defence minister: “Our cup of patience has overflowed. Now it is open war between us and you.”

The death toll on day one:

  • Pakistan claimed 133 Afghan fighters killed at the border

  • Afghanistan claimed 55 Pakistani soldiers killed

  • Actual casualties from Kabul and Kandahar airstrikes: unknown

The human cost from the first days:

  • Families in border villages hiding in basements during shelling: “When firing begins from both sides, we rush our children into the basements and wait for hours, uncertain of what will happen next.”

  • A wounded Afghan woman in a Jalalabad hospital, hit by mortar fire

  • A farmer near Torkham: “The border clashes have now become routine, and it has become almost impossible to live here.”

  • Friday prayers held in a mosque with a hole blown in its ceiling

This happened during Ramadan. The holy month that UN officials had hoped would broker peace.

Eight Weeks That Tell the Whole Story

Week One. Within days of the “open war” declaration, the United Nations had documented 56 Afghan civilians killed — 24 of them children. Another 129 were injured, including 41 children and 31 women. Some 66,000 Afghans had been displaced. The bombs did not land on a country in stable condition. They landed on a country where 21.9 million people — nearly half the entire population — already required humanitarian assistance before a single Pakistani strike fell. The World Food Programme had already been warning of “severe food insecurity” across 46 districts.

The UN Human Rights Chief, Volker Türk, described what was happening with bleak precision: the violence was “piling misery on misery.”

Week Two. The strikes moved from the border provinces into the cities. On March 13, Pakistan struck civilian homes in Kabul and Kandahar. Women and children were among the dead. A fuel depot near Kandahar airport was destroyed. A 29-year-old labourer named Abdul Wahid told AFP that he and four family members were wounded when his house was hit at 12:10 in the morning. Pakistan said it had struck “terrorist hideouts.” It always says it struck terrorist hideouts.

Displacement reached 115,000. The World Food Programme suspended emergency food distributions to approximately 160,000 people. Pakistan had already sealed the border in October, cutting off landlocked Afghanistan’s transit trade. The compounding effect — war layered on famine layered on economic strangulation — had become almost impossible to calculate.

Week Three. The diplomats were busy elsewhere. China tried. Russia tried. Iran tried. Qatar and Turkey facilitated calls. The Arab Gulf nations that had previously mediated Afghanistan-Pakistan talks were, as one analyst noted, “bogged down by their own war” — referring to the US-Israel conflict with Iran that ignited the Middle East barely 48 hours after Pakistan declared open war on Afghanistan. Within seventy-two hours in late February, two separate major military conflicts erupted across the same broad region. The diplomats who might have intervened in one were consumed by the other.

The United States declared that Pakistan has “a right to defend itself” — a statement Pakistani officials privately interpreted, according to the New York Times, as a green light to continue. This is the same United States that spent $2 trillion and 20 years in Afghanistan, abandoned it in 2021, then gutted its aid programs, and now blesses its neighbour’s right to bomb it into further ruin. Washington’s relationship with Afghanistan has, throughout its entire arc, been an exercise in consequential indifference.

Then Pakistan bombed a building called Hope. Four hundred dead. The world’s attention briefly flickered. And within forty-eight hours, both sides agreed to stop fighting — for a holiday.

The Eid Ceasefire. On March 18, Pakistan and Afghanistan announced a “temporary pause” in hostilities for Eid al-Fitr, at the request of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey. Pakistan’s information minister said the ceasefire would run from midnight on March 19 to midnight on March 24. Pakistan offered the gesture “in good faith and in keeping with Islamic norms.”

It lasted hours. On March 19 — the first day — Taliban officials reported that Pakistan conducted 72 mortar attacks in Kunar province, firing 25 artillery shells in Nari District alone. Two civilians were killed in Nuristan. More shelling on March 22. Twenty-two religious clerics, eleven from each side, issued a joint statement pleading for the ceasefire to be extended and calling for a “sustainable and dignified” end to the conflict.

It wasn’t extended. On the night of March 24, the moment the pause expired, Pakistan carried out a drone strike on the 209 Al-Fatah Corps in Balkh province — in northern Afghanistan, hundreds of miles from the border. The war was not contracting. It was reaching deeper into the country.

Even Ramadan could not stop it. Even Eid could not hold.

The China Talks. In early April, with the fighting subsided to sporadic cross-border exchanges, China convened what became known as the “Urumqi process” — week-long trilateral talks in the northwestern Chinese city of Urumqi, running from April 1 to April 7. Cross-departmental delegations from Pakistan, Afghanistan, and China — including officials from foreign affairs, defence, and security — met in what China’s Foreign Ministry spokesperson described as “candid and pragmatic” discussions conducted in a “problem-solving, results-oriented approach.”

The three sides issued joint language: Afghanistan and Pakistan were “Muslim brothers and neighbours.” Dialogue was “the viable and effective way to resolve complex international disputes.” The Urumqi process was “substantive.” A follow-up meeting was expected by the end of April.

But the fundamental gap remained. Pakistan demanded “visible and verifiable action” against the TTP on Afghan soil. Pakistan’s foreign ministry spokesperson was blunt: “The burden of a real process lies with Afghanistan.” The Taliban sent a mid-level delegation and expressed willingness to talk. But the TTP continued to operate. Suicide bombings continued inside Pakistan. And Pakistan’s military campaign — Operation Ghazab lil Haq — was never formally suspended.

Week Eight. On April 15, something quietly remarkable happened. Tribal elders from eastern Afghanistan and Pakistan’s Chitral district met — at a Pakistani military facility, on the Pakistani side of the border — and agreed to reopen key routes linking Kamdesh and Barg-i Matal in Nuristan after nearly two months of closure. Both sides committed to a ceasefire across areas including Nari, Kamdesh, Bargi Matal, Arandu, and Chitral. Pakistani forces asked the communities not to allow Pakistani Taliban to shelter in the area.

The Taliban government was not invited. Not consulted. Not represented.

The tribal structure — the same structure that has operated in these valleys since before Alexander arrived — simply bypassed Kabul and solved a local problem through local negotiation. Loyalty flows to tribe and valley, not to the capital. It has for 2,500 years. It still does.

Then today. April 27. Mortars and missiles struck the city of Asadabad, capital of Kunar province. Sayed Jamaluddin Afghani University was hit. Afghanistan’s Ministry of Higher Education reported approximately 30 students and professors wounded and extensive damage to buildings and grounds. Seven dead. Eighty-five wounded. Students, women, children among the casualties.

Pakistan denied it. Its Information Ministry called the reports “a blatant lie” and “fake propaganda.” The same denial pattern as the Omid centre. The same competing narratives. The same civilian bodies.

The trigger, according to Afghan sources: Pakistani military forces shot and killed an Afghan child near the border city of Spin Boldak the night before. Afghan forces responded. Six Pakistani soldiers were reportedly killed in the clash. And then the shells fell on a university.

A building called Hope in March. A university in April. The targets are the infrastructure of recovery — healing and learning. The two things Afghanistan needs most.

Omid

The airstrike hit a drug rehabilitation center in Kabul, Afghan officials said.Credit...Sayed Hassib/Reuters

The building was a former United States military base.

After the Americans left in 2021, the Taliban government converted it into a drug rehabilitation centre. Afghanistan has long been the world’s leading source of illegally produced opium — a distinction with deep roots in the decades of war that shattered the country’s institutions and left addiction as one of its most visible wounds. The government had interned thousands of people suffering from addiction in facilities like this one. The centre was named the Support and Treatment Centre, Omid. A billboard bearing that name stood atop an adjacent building on the night of March 16th.

Omid. Hope.

At approximately 9 p.m. local time, Pakistan struck it. The main building — 180 feet long, used for meals and prayer — was destroyed. Adjacent buildings caught fire. Two hundred patients were staying in the complex. Emergency workers pulled bodies from smoking rubble through the night and into Tuesday morning. A reporter on the ground saw at least 80 bodies in body bags before dawn. The final toll reached at least 400 dead and 250 wounded — the deadliest single strike of the conflict.

What reporters found in the debris was not ammunition. It was identical sandals — the kind distributed to patients. White and blue gowns. Bottles of medicinal syrup. Pills. Bunk beds, 20 to 30 per building. The Norwegian Refugee Council’s country head, who visited the site, said he saw no military facilities in the immediate area.

Pakistan said there had been secondary detonations — proof, it claimed, of an ammunition depot. Its Information Minister stated that “all targeting has been done with precision only at those infrastructures which are being used by Afghan Taliban regime.” Pictures shared by emergency workers throughout the night showed no weapons, no ammunition, no military equipment.

One side is lying. The rubble cannot lie.

On Tuesday morning, hundreds of people pressed against the compound’s entrance. Among them was Basmina Khudadadi, who had brought her brother fresh clothes just a month before his admission. She had not yet told his wife. Muhammad Haidari, 23 years old, stood near the ruins searching for news of two uncles admitted in February. “I don’t know if they are alive or dead,” he said. “Each one has children and family waiting for them to return home.”

These are the names this war produces. Not strategies. Not geopolitical calculations. Not the carefully worded statements of information ministers. Just a 23-year-old man standing in the smell of burned flesh and melted iron, not knowing whether to grieve or hope.

There is something almost unbearably symbolic about the Omid centre’s specific biography. A former American military base. Converted to healing. Bombed by Pakistan — the country that for decades served as the primary external patron of the Taliban. Now that same Taliban government is the victim. The same United States that built the base declared Pakistan’s right to destroy what was built inside it. Every layer of Afghanistan’s modern catastrophe is present in one building. The patients in their identical sandals were not combatants in any of these decisions. They were the residue of all of them.

The debris found in the ruins of the Omid centre: identical sandals distributed to patients, blue hospital gowns, blister packs of pills, medicine bottles. The Border That Bleeds: Kurram District

But this isn’t just about Pakistan versus Afghanistan. The violence along that 1,600-mile mountainous border is more complex, more intractable, than any simple national conflict.

Consider Kurram district — “the Parrot’s Beak” that extends into Afghanistan, bordering the Afghan provinces of Khost, Paktika, and Nangarhar. Parachinar, its main city, is just 62 miles from Kabul.

The New York Times reported in October 2024 on a region of “majestic peaks, ancient maple forests and fertile fields” where Sunni and Shiite Muslims have been fighting intermittently for months — the latest flare-up in a conflict that has simmered for decades.

  • October 12, 2024: At least 16 killed in clashes

  • September 2024: 46 dead in sectarian fighting

  • July 2024: Nearly 50 killed in a weeklong battle

  • 2007–2011: Nearly 2,000 deaths in sustained warfare

A funeral in the Kurram district of Pakistan in October 2024 for Sunni Muslims killed in sectarian fighting.Credit...Dilawer Khan/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

A funeral photograph from October shows rows of coffins draped in fabric, mountains in the background, hundreds of mourners. Sunni Muslims killed in sectarian fighting. The same landscape. The same violence. Different victims.

A university student from Parachinar: “It is like a war between two countries, not a dispute between tribes. Innocent people are suffering, and the government doesn’t care.”

A farmer who has fought in the clashes: “I hate the violence, but in a region where the government has almost no control, it is compulsory to take up arms to protect ourselves and our land.”

The pattern: many people in Kurram learn to use heavy weapons from childhood. Self-defense has become a way of life. The Pakistani government has been “helpless to stop” the violence.

Sound familiar?

How Afghanistan’s Wars Created This

Here’s what makes Kurram’s violence so revealing: it’s not ancient tribal hatred. It’s the direct result of Afghanistan’s wars spilling across that ungovernable border.

Kurram has a long history of sectarian strife between Shia and Sunni groups. The deadliest period of sectarian violence in recent years was reported between 2007 and 2011, during which more than 2,000 people were killed.

The timeline:

Before 1979: Shiites and Sunnis in Kurram “lived largely in harmony for centuries, despite occasional violence.”

1979: Two simultaneous events changed everything — the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan and the Iranian Shiite revolution.

1980s: The influx of Sunni Afghan refugees and the establishment of mujahideen groups (backed by Pakistan and the US to fight Soviets) changed Kurram’s demographic balance. Result: two major episodes of violence, mostly Shiites killed.

Mid-1990s: Taliban rise in Afghanistan. They provided “arms and manpower to their fellow Sunnis in Kurram.” Hundreds killed.

2001: US invasion of Afghanistan. Pakistani Sunnis near Kurram harboured fleeing Taliban and Al Qaeda militants. Shiites of Kurram did not. This earned Shiites the “enmity of those groups.”

2007: Bloody clashes led to the expulsion of Sunnis from Parachinar.

2008: Pakistani Taliban (TTP) arrival “further deepened sectarian violence.” A 45-day gun battle devastated Kurram. Hundreds killed. Villages burned.

2011: Peace agreement — after nearly 2,000 deaths in four years.

2015: Disgruntled TTP members established ISIS-K. Five suicide attacks in Parachinar in 2017.

2021: Taliban takeover of Afghanistan. Tribes in Kurram obtained “advanced American weapons left behind by Afghan troops.”

2024: Fighting continues. “The violence has demonstrated the limited reach of Pakistan’s government along the frontier.”

Do you see the pattern?

Every Afghan war — Soviet, civil, Taliban, American, Taliban again — created waves of violence that crashed across the border into Pakistan. Refugees fled. Militant groups formed. Weapons proliferated. Sectarian tensions that had been manageable for centuries became full-scale warfare.

A political science professor from the University of Peshawar: “These events eroded traditional cultural values, fractured once-unified tribes along sectarian and socioeconomic lines, and heightened tensions in Kurram.”

A community elder in Parachinar: “The violence, particularly the wave that began in 2007, has transformed local land disputes into full-scale sectarian clashes, widening the gap between Shiites and Sunnis. I am skeptical that people from the two sects in Kurram will be able to coexist peacefully anytime soon.”

The geography hasn’t changed. The 1,600-mile border is still mountainous, still porous, still ungovernable. But 45 years of continuous warfare in Afghanistan transformed peaceful coexistence into perpetual violence.

Why This Is Happening: The Same Old Story

Pakistan accuses Afghanistan’s Taliban government of harboring the Pakistani Taliban (Tehreek-e-Taliban Pakistan), who have killed hundreds of Pakistani security personnel and claimed responsibility for a bombing in Islamabad that killed dozens. The Afghan Taliban deny hosting the Pakistani Taliban. But privately, Afghan officials acknowledge the TTP’s presence.

Here is the pattern:

  • Afghanistan’s geography makes it impossible to fully control who operates in the country

  • Mountain valleys shelter groups the government can’t or won’t eliminate

  • Neighbouring countries blame Afghanistan for the instability

  • Cross-border raids and reprisals escalate

  • Civilians suffer

Sound familiar? It should. This is the exact same dynamic that led to British invasions (blaming Afghanistan for tribal raids into India), justified the Soviet invasion (blaming Afghanistan for Islamist influence spreading north), and triggered the American invasion (blaming Afghanistan for harbouring Al Qaeda).

The geography hasn’t changed. The tribal structure hasn’t changed. The pattern hasn’t changed.

The Bitter Irony

Pakistan supported the Afghan Taliban for decades. Taliban leaders lived in Quetta during the US occupation. After the Taliban returned to power in 2021, Pakistan initially welcomed them. There were even talks about Afghanistan joining a China-Pakistan economic corridor.

Now, less than five years later, Pakistan’s military calls them “warlords” and says Afghanistan is “a space where a nonstate militia is sitting.”

This is the pattern. Support today becomes hostility tomorrow. Allies become enemies. Friends become targets. The cycle never ends.

  • The US armed the mujahideen against the Soviets, then invaded Afghanistan two decades later

  • Pakistan supported the Taliban, now bombs them

  • Russia was among the first countries to recognize the Taliban government, now pressures them to control militant groups

And now a new player at the table: India reopened its full embassy in Kabul in October 2025 — its first high-level diplomatic engagement with the Taliban since 2021. Indian Foreign Minister Jaishankar hosted Taliban Foreign Minister Muttaqi in New Delhi, announced India was “fully committed to the sovereignty, territorial integrity and independence of Afghanistan,” and invited Indian companies to explore Afghan mining opportunities.

Pakistan’s defence minister responded by accusing Afghanistan of operating as an Indian “proxy.”

Read that again. The country that created the Taliban, funded the Taliban, housed the Taliban in Quetta for twenty years — is now accusing Afghanistan of being India’s proxy because India reopened an embassy.

The relationships change. The geography doesn’t. The suffering doesn’t.

Even the Taliban Can’t Break the Pattern

Taliban security personnel arrived at the scene of the Omid strike. The government vowed to retaliate against Pakistan.Credit...Wakil Kohsar/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

Here is what makes this moment so revealing: the Taliban are now the government. They control Afghanistan. They’re not fighting foreign occupation — they’re the ones being bombed.

And yet the fundamental problems remain unchanged. The UN Security Council reports that the Taliban government provides weapons — rifles and drones — to the Pakistani Taliban. Al Qaeda continues to operate under Taliban patronage. Multiple militant groups move freely through Afghan territory.

Why can’t the Taliban control their own country? Because of the same factors that have operated for 2,500 years:

  • The mountains make it impossible to police every valley

  • The tribal structure means loyalty flows to tribe and valley, not to Kabul

  • The ethnic divisions mean Pashtun Taliban can’t fully control Tajik or Hazara regions

  • The border is 1,600 miles of mountainous terrain that no government has ever secured

Ahmad Shah Durrani discovered this in the 1760s when his empire collapsed after his death. The Taliban are discovering it now.

And in April 2026, a small event in Nuristan proved the point more eloquently than any analyst could. On April 15, tribal elders from eastern Afghanistan and Pakistan’s Chitral district — neighbours divided by the Durand Line but connected by centuries of shared life — met at a Pakistani military facility and agreed to reopen key routes between Kamdesh and Barg-i Matal that had been closed for two months by the fighting. They committed to a local ceasefire. Pakistani forces asked the communities not to shelter TTP fighters, and the elders agreed.

The Taliban government was not invited. Not consulted. Not represented.

The tribal structure simply did what it has always done: it solved a local problem through local negotiation, because Kabul is far away and the valley is right here. This is how Afghanistan has actually functioned for millennia — not through central authority, but through the ancient, granular diplomacy of elders who share water and pasture and blood. No empire has ever replaced it. No government has ever fully controlled it. The Taliban, who conquered Kabul, cannot govern Nuristan without the permission of the men who live there.

The Human Face: Still the Same

The reports from the border describe villages where families evacuate as a preventive measure. Where those who remain “rush our children into the basements and wait for hours, uncertain of what will happen next.”

A schoolteacher in Kurram district: “Some villagers have taken up positions alongside security forces in the trenches and are participating in the exchanges of fire. This is a critical time. We must stand with our forces, just as communities across the border are backing Taliban fighters.”

Read that again: Pakistani villagers and Afghan villagers, who share ethnic ties and family connections going back centuries, are now shooting at each other from trenches.

This is what the 1,600-mile border through the mountains does. It doesn’t separate nations cleanly. It cuts through tribal territories. It divides families. It creates permanent instability.

The same geography that protected Afghanistan from conquest makes it impossible to govern peacefully.

The Economic Catastrophe

Since October, Pakistan has kept key border crossings closed to civilians and traders, reopening them only intermittently to expel Afghans living in Pakistan. Over a million Afghans were expelled in 2025 alone. The suspension of trade has hurt both economies.

So on top of:

  • American aid cuts that closed 450 health clinics

  • Malnutrition affecting 4 million children

  • Closed schools for girls under Taliban rule

  • Economic collapse from sanctions

Afghanistan now faces:

  • Airstrikes on its two largest cities

  • Border closings that strangle trade

  • Mass expulsions adding to the refugee crisis

  • Open warfare with its most important neighbour

And those children in Badgor, in Nalej, in the Kabul nutrition ward? They're still starving. The closed clinics are still closed. The mobile medical teams are still stretched impossibly thin. And now their capital has been bombed, a ceasefire has come and gone, peace talks have been convened in a Chinese city most Afghans will never see, and the shells are falling again — this time on a university.

The Family in the Dark Room

Saman, Farhad, and their two children — Victoria, 4, and Yusuf, 2 — in hiding in Islamabad. They never go outside. They keep the curtains drawn. Every knock on the door terrifies their daughter. Image: AI illustration based on George Packer's reporting in The Atlantic.

The numbers — 115,000 displaced, 400 dead in a single night, a million expelled — risk becoming abstractions. George Packer’s devastating portrait in The Atlantic today, published this same March 17th, prevents that.

Saman and Farhad are Afghan special forces veterans in their mid-20s, hiding in two rooms of a house in Islamabad with their two small children. They never go outside. They keep the curtains drawn and their voices down. They’ve been in hiding since fleeing Afghanistan in 2022, vetted three times by the US refugee program, medically cleared, approved for resettlement — and then abandoned when the Trump administration closed the door to virtually all Afghan refugees.

Their daughter is four years old. Her name is Victoria, after the American Army captain who helped them escape Kabul. Whenever she hears a knock on the door, she grabs her mother and says: “Mama, don’t answer! It’s the police!”

Their son is two. He was born in Pakistan and has never known anything else.

Pakistan is now deporting Afghan refugees en masse because of the war — the same war this epilogue has been documenting. Banners have gone up across Islamabad offering a $35 reward to citizens who report the presence of Afghans in their neighbourhoods. Mosque loudspeakers threaten imprisonment for Pakistanis who rent to Afghans. The government announced it would expel all 19,973 Afghans with pending US immigration cases. Saman and Farhad are among them.

“What hurts most,” Saman wrote to Packer, “is not only what we lost — our home, our work, our dignity — but the feeling of being unwanted by a world that once promised protection.”

This family served alongside Americans. Their daughter is named after one. The country they served has barred the door. The country they fled to is hunting them through their own neighbourhood. And the war that is now consuming the region has made their situation immeasurably worse.

The woman in Badgor is waiting for medicine that isn’t coming. Saman is waiting in a dark room for an answer that may never come. They are two faces of the same abandonment, separated by 300 miles of the same country.

Read Packer’s full portrait at The Atlantic: “Abandoned by America” (March 24, 2026).

What the Two Photographs Represent

Look at the first photograph again: three Taliban soldiers on a rocky mountainside, rifles ready, watching for the next attack.

Now look at the funeral photograph from Kurram: rows of coffins, mountains in the background, hundreds of mourners. October 2024. Sunni Muslims killed in sectarian fighting — violence that traces directly back to the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979.

Two photographs. The same mountains. One shows the fighters. One shows the cost.

Together, they represent an unbroken line:

  • The Bactrian tribesmen who ambushed Alexander’s soldiers in these same mountains

  • The Afghan warriors who destroyed the British army in the 1842 retreat

  • The mujahideen who broke the Soviet 40th Army

  • The Taliban who outlasted America’s twenty-year occupation

They are positioned on terrain that has been a battlefield for longer than recorded history.

The empires change. The weapons evolve. The uniforms are different. The justifications shift.

But the mountains remain. The resistance endures. The pattern continues.

The 2,500-Year Count

Let me update the historical count from Part 3:

February 26, 2026: Pakistan bombs Kabul and Kandahar and declares "open war" March 16, 2026: Pakistan bombs a building called Hope and kills 400 people seeking treatment March 19, 2026: An Eid ceasefire is violated on its first day April 7, 2026: China convenes peace talks in Urumqi; all sides agree dialogue is "the viable and effective way to resolve complex international disputes" April 15, 2026: Tribal elders in Nuristan reopen mountain routes without consulting Kabul — because the tribal structure has never needed Kabul's permission April 27, 2026: Pakistani shells strike a university in Kunar. Seven dead. Eighty-five wounded. Pakistan denies it happened.

2,476 years from first Persian conquest to today.

In all that time, how many years of genuine peace? Perhaps 250. Maybe 300 if we’re generous.

That is 90% warfare and 10% peace.

And it is still happening.

The Lesson Still Unlearned

Pakistan’s military has studied Afghan history. They know about the British disasters. They know about the Soviet catastrophe. They watched America’s failure in real-time over twenty years.

They bombed Afghanistan anyway.

Why? Because they believe their situation is different. They share a 1,600-mile border. They have legitimate security concerns. The Pakistani Taliban really are using Afghan territory to launch attacks.

All of this is true.

It was also true when:

  • The British said Afghan tribes were raiding into India

  • The Soviets said Islamic extremism was spreading from Afghanistan

  • The Americans said Al Qaeda was operating from Afghan territory

Every invasion has justification. Every bombing campaign has reasons. Every empire believes its circumstances are unique.

The geography doesn’t care about justification. The mountains don’t distinguish between legitimate security concerns and imperial ambition. The pattern operates regardless of whether the grievances are real.

Afghanistan cannot be controlled through military force.

2,500 years of history prove this. Pakistan is learning it now. And Afghan children are paying the price. Again.

The Woman in Badgor, March 17, 2026

Remember how this series started? With a New York Times article on February 4, 2026, about mothers losing babies because Trump cut US aid to Afghanistan.

That woman in Badgor, holding her child while waiting for one of the last mobile medical teams in Kunar Province, is still there.

The aid is still cut. The clinics are still closed. The children are still starving.

And now her country has been at war for three weeks. Not with a distant superpower arriving with trillion-dollar budgets and twenty-year plans. With her neighbour. The country millions of Afghan refugees fled to. The country with which Afghanistan shares culture, language, religion, and centuries of history.

In the eight weeks since Pakistan declared open war: a drug rehabilitation centre has been destroyed, a ceasefire has been declared and broken, peace talks have been convened in a Chinese city and concluded with carefully worded communiqués, tribal elders have quietly reopened mountain routes the governments couldn't, and today — the day I finish this — shells struck a university in Kunar province, the same province where that woman waits for medicine that isn't coming.

The 1,600-mile mountainous border hasn’t changed since Alexander crossed it 2,300 years ago. It still divides families. It still creates tribal territories that span both countries. It still makes cooperation difficult and conflict easy.

And that woman’s child, if he survives the malnutrition, will grow up in a country still at war. Still suffering. Still trapped by geography and history and the refusal of nations to learn from the past.

The Pattern That Never Ends

This is why I wrote this series.

Not to document ancient history for academic interest. Not to catalog long-dead empires for historical curiosity.

But to show that the pattern is alive. Active. Operating right now.

As I finish these words on April 27, 2026:

  • Afghan children are starving because of aid cuts

  • Pakistani bombs have been falling for two months — on cities, on a hospital, on a university

  • A ceasefire was brokered for Ramadan and broken within hours

  • China convened peace talks that produced careful language and no enforceable agreement

  • Tribal elders in Nuristan solved their own problems because no government could

  • Families are hiding in basements near the border

  • A family hides in a dark room in Islamabad, their four-year-old flinching at every knock

  • Taliban soldiers are positioned on mountains that have seen 2,500 years of warfare

  • A building named Hope is rubble

  • A university in Kunar is damaged, its students and professors among the wounded

  • The cycle continues

When will it end?

Honestly? Probably never.

Not as long as Afghanistan’s geography makes it valuable but ungovernable. Not as long as its tribal structure creates resilience but prevents centralization. Not as long as neighbouring powers believe they can solve Afghan problems through military force.

The mountains will remain. The valleys will stay isolated. The tribes will endure. And Afghanistan will continue to be the graveyard where empires’ ambitions go to die.

What We Owe Afghanistan

We can’t change Afghanistan’s geography. We can’t rewrite 2,500 years of history. We can’t eliminate the tribal structure or move the mountains or make the Hindu Kush less formidable.

But we can stop making it worse.

We can:

  • Stop bombing it (looking at you, Pakistan)

  • Stop cutting aid that keeps children alive (looking at you, America)

  • Stop believing that military force will solve problems that 2,500 years of military force couldn’t solve

  • Stop treating Afghanistan as a chess piece in regional power games

  • Stop forgetting that Afghans are people, not abstractions

And we can remember.

Remember that those starving children in Nalej aren’t just statistics. Remember that the woman in Badgor waiting for medical care is the latest in a line stretching back through the Soviet devastation, the British invasions, the Mongol apocalypse, to the first Persian governors who learned that Afghanistan bows to no one. Remember that the 400 dead in the Omid centre were not soldiers. They were patients. They were people who came seeking treatment for an addiction that was itself the product of forty years of someone else’s wars. Remember Victoria, four years old, named after an American soldier, hiding behind curtains in a city that is hunting her family.

Remember that the pattern exists. That it repeats. That it will continue repeating until we learn.

The Eternal Battleground

Twenty-five hundred years.

From Cyrus the Great to Pakistan’s F-16s. From Persian satrapies to Taliban government. From Bactrian cavalry to modern rifles with scopes.

The same mountains. The same valleys. The same pattern.

Afghanistan, the eternal battleground.

The graveyard of empires.

The land where good intentions and military might go to die.

The country where, despite everything, Afghans endure.

They outlasted the Persians. They outlasted Alexander. They outlasted the Mongols. They outlasted the British. They outlasted the Soviets. They outlasted the Americans.

They will outlast Pakistan.

They will outlast us all.

Because the mountains remain. The resistance endures. And the pattern never ends.

Pakistan bombed a building called Hope. Hope is still standing.
