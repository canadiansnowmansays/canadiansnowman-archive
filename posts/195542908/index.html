Canada: How the Dominion Was Forged

How the Dominion Was Forged

Introduction: The Modest Banker

April 26, 2026

Prime Minister Mark Carney responds to a question during an event in Ottawa on Thursday, April 23, 2026. THE CANADIAN PRESS/Adrian Wyld

In January 2026, a publicly modest banker who had never run for office stood before the assembled leaders of the global economic order at Davos and did something that Canadian prime ministers almost never do.

He picked a fight.

Not loudly. Not with theatrics or insults or the chest-thumping performative aggression that has become the lingua franca of twenty-first-century politics. Mark Carney picked his fight the way Canadians build things — carefully, with evidence, in complete sentences, and with the quiet confidence of a man who has run two central banks and does not require a tutorial on how money works from a real estate developer who has been bankrupt six times.

The American president had spent months menacing Canada. Tariffs. Annexation jokes that were not jokes. The 51st state. The suggestion — delivered with the casual cruelty that is Trump’s signature — that America’s oldest ally, its largest trading partner, its most stable and loyal neighbour, was simply a territory waiting to be absorbed. And the Canadian political establishment had, for a time, done what Canadian political establishments have done for two and a half centuries: absorbed the indignity, looked for the diplomatic off-ramp, tried to manage the relationship.

Then Carney arrived.

He had not planned on this. He was a banker, not a politician. He had run the Bank of Canada through the 2008 financial crisis with a steadiness that earned him the job of running the Bank of England — the first non-Briton to hold that post since the bank’s founding in 1694. He was the kind of Canadian who succeeds internationally by being quietly competent, the kind of man who makes the machinery work while louder people take the credit. He was not, by any measure, the obvious candidate to become the face of resistance to the most powerful bully on the planet.

But Canada has a way of producing the right person at the wrong time. Or perhaps the wrong person at the right time. Either way, Carney stepped forward, won the Liberal leadership, won a general election on a platform built almost entirely around the proposition that Canada would not be bullied, and then — just two weeks ago, on April 13, 2026 — swept three federal by-elections to clinch a majority government. The fourteenth prime minister in Canadian history to do so. A man who announced his leadership run barely sixteen months ago, with the Liberal Party trailing badly in the polls, now holds a majority assembled from by-election wins and floor crossings — described by the Toronto Star as an “anti-Trump coalition.” Pierre Poilievre and the Conservatives, who had held a twenty-point lead not so long ago, watched four of their own MPs cross the aisle.

The speech Carney delivered in the wake of that victory was not the sort of thing Canadians are accustomed to hearing from their leaders. It was not hedged. It was not diplomatic. It was not cautious. It was — by the restrained standards of Canadian political rhetoric — incendiary.

He spoke of Canadians choosing Okanagan wine over California. Of families booking vacations to Prince Edward Island instead of flights to Florida. Of small, individual acts of solidarity, repeated millions of times. And the crowd — a Canadian crowd, a people not generally given to eruptions of nationalist fervour — roared.

Lawrence O’Donnell, the American cable news anchor, aired the speech and told his audience something that has almost never been said on American television: learn from this. Learn from Canada. “Small individual acts of solidarity repeated millions of times,” O’Donnell said. “That is what it will take to beat Trumpism.”

He paused. Then he added: “That wasn’t Hungary we were watching just then. That is Canada.”

An American television anchor holding up a Canadian prime minister as a model for American resistance. In the entire history of the relationship between these two countries, that sentence does not get spoken. Americans do not look north for instruction. They look north for cold weather jokes, for polite stereotypes, for a border they mostly forget exists. They do not — ever — look north and say: that is what courage looks like, and we should take notes.

The fact that it happened — the fact that Mark Carney, a man who speaks in paragraphs and carries the emotional intensity of a well-organized spreadsheet, became the international face of resistance to Donald Trump — tells you something important. Not just about Carney. Not just about Trump. About Canada.

Because Canada does not produce this moment by accident. The country that produced Mark Carney is the same country that survived the conquest of 1759 by choosing negotiation over subjugation. The same country that watched the American Revolution and decided, deliberately, not to join it. The same country that built itself through confederation rather than civil war, through railway deals rather than Manifest Destiny, through peacekeeping rather than gunboat diplomacy. The same country that designed the Avro Arrow — the most advanced interceptor on earth — and then destroyed it with acetylene torches because Washington preferred that it didn’t exist.

Carney did not arrive from nowhere. He arrived from a country with a specific history, a specific temperament, and a specific relationship with the colossus next door — a relationship that has been, for two and a half centuries, the defining fact of Canadian existence.

This series tells that story. Not as a patriotic exercise — Canada has enough of those, and most of them are boring. Not as an anti-American polemic — Canada’s relationship with the United States is too complicated, too intimate, and too consequential for sloganeering. But as an honest reckoning with how a country that began as a contested frontier between two European empires became, against considerable odds, one of the most successful democracies on earth — and what happens now that the relationship that shaped it is being tested as never before.

It begins on a beach in Cape Breton, at a fortress that changed hands between empires the way other countries change governments. And it ends — for now — with a modest banker who never expected to be leading a country through its most serious confrontation with the United States since Confederation, standing before a nation that never expected to need rallying against its closest ally.

The Dominion was not born. It was forged — in fire, in compromise, in the permanent negotiation between what Canada wanted to be and what the world would allow it to become.

This is the story of that forging.
