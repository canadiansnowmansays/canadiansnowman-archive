Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 9: The Special Period — The Catastrophe Nobody Talked About (1991–2000)

March 23, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

The flag that kept Cuba alive. Soviet subsidies transferred billions of dollars annually to Havana for three decades. When it came down in December 1991, Cuba lost 35 percent of its GDP in two years.

In 1989, the Berlin Wall fell. By 1991, the Soviet Union was gone. The Cold War, which had shaped global politics for forty-five years and Cuban politics even more completely, was over. The West celebrated. Francis Fukuyama declared the end of history. American triumphalism was at its peak.

In Cuba, the lights went out.

Not metaphorically. Literally. The Soviet subsidies that had sustained the Cuban economy for thirty years — preferential oil prices, guaranteed sugar purchases, development assistance, military hardware — disappeared almost overnight. Cuba had been importing approximately 13 million tonnes of Soviet oil per year. That figure collapsed to around 6 million tonnes by 1992 and continued to fall. The power plants that ran on Soviet oil went dark for hours, then half-days, then longer. By 1993, rolling blackouts of sixteen hours a day were common across the island. In rural areas, the electricity simply stopped.

Cuba called what followed the Período Especial en Tiempo de Paz — the Special Period in Time of Peace. The name was a bureaucratic euphemism for a catastrophe of the first order. What Cubans experienced in the early and mid-1990s was an economic collapse that, in terms of its human impact, rivals the worst peacetime humanitarian crises of the twentieth century. It was the most severe test of Cuban endurance since the independence wars. And it was almost completely invisible to the outside world, which was busy celebrating the Cold War’s end and had no particular interest in attending to the suffering of the people on the losing side’s client list.

This is the part of Cuba’s story that most needs to be told, because it is the least known — and because it is happening again, right now, as Cuban fuel reserves deplete toward zero and the hospitals go dark and the garbage piles up in Havana’s streets. The Cubans facing the current blockade have been here before. They know what this looks like. They know what it costs.

The Soviet Lifeline and Its Sudden End

To understand the Special Period, you have to understand how completely Cuba had come to depend on Soviet support in the three decades since the Revolution.

The relationship was not a comfortable one. Castro had been furious about Khrushchev’s handling of the Missile Crisis, and Soviet-Cuban relations went through periods of genuine tension. Castro’s support for revolutionary movements in Latin America and Africa — sending Cuban troops to Angola, supporting the Sandinistas in Nicaragua — sometimes embarrassed Moscow and complicated Soviet diplomatic calculations. The Soviets, for their part, frequently lectured Castro about economic rationality and the inefficiency of Cuban state enterprises, and Castro frequently ignored them.

But the material dependence was real and deep. The Soviet Union purchased Cuban sugar at prices well above world market rates — effectively subsidising the Cuban economy by billions of dollars per year. It supplied Cuba with oil at preferential prices that were, by the late 1980s, roughly half the world market rate. It provided technical assistance, military equipment, and development financing. Cuba’s food supply, its transportation system, its industrial production — all were calibrated to Soviet inputs that were assumed to be permanent.

They were not permanent. Mikhail Gorbachev’s reforms, the collapse of Eastern European communism in 1989, and the final dissolution of the Soviet Union in December 1991 ended the relationship with a speed that left Cuba with no time to adjust. The subsidised oil stopped. The guaranteed sugar purchases stopped. The development financing stopped. Cuba was left holding an economy built on foundations that had just been removed.

What the Special Period Felt Like

The statistics of the Special Period are staggering. Cuba’s GDP fell by approximately 35 percent between 1989 and 1993 — a deeper economic contraction than the United States experienced during the Great Depression. Food imports fell by 50 percent. Oil imports fell by more than 50 percent. The average Cuban’s caloric intake dropped from approximately 3,000 calories per day to around 1,900 — below the minimum recommended by nutritionists for an active adult. Average Cubans lost an estimated 10 to 20 pounds over the course of the early 1990s.

But statistics do not capture what the Special Period actually felt like to live through. For that, you need the details.

Cuba, 1990s. The Soviet Union collapsed. Cuba got on its bicycle.

Oxen replaced tractors in Cuban fields, because there was no fuel to run farm machinery. Bicycles replaced buses in Cuban cities, because there was no fuel to run public transport — the Cuban government imported more than a million bicycles from China in a single year, and the image of Havana as a city of cyclists, which persists to this day, dates from this period. Horse-drawn carts reappeared on city streets. People who had grown up as urbanites found themselves growing vegetables on rooftops, in vacant lots, in any available space, because the food distribution system had collapsed and the state ration book — the libreta — no longer provided enough to eat.

The healthcare system that was the Revolution’s proudest achievement came under extraordinary stress. Medicines that had been imported from the Soviet bloc were no longer available. Medical equipment broke down and could not be repaired because spare parts were unavailable. Preventable diseases began to reappear. An epidemic of optic neuropathy — a form of nerve damage linked to nutritional deficiency — affected more than 50,000 Cubans, with thousands losing significant vision. It was a disease of starvation, in a country that had prided itself on eliminating the diseases of poverty.

The psychological toll was immense. Cubans who had genuinely believed in the Revolution’s promise — who had accepted its constraints and its authoritarianism because the material improvements in their lives seemed real and continuing — now watched those improvements reverse. The free healthcare was still nominally available, but without medicines it was often symbolic. The free education continued, but graduates faced an economy with no jobs. The libreta ration provided survival-level calories and nothing more. The gap between the ideology the government continued to proclaim and the reality Cubans were living every day became impossible to ignore.

The Government’s Response: Painful Adaptations

The Cuban government’s response to the Special Period was a mixture of ideological stubbornness, pragmatic flexibility, and genuine cruelty.

The ideological stubbornness showed in the government’s initial insistence that the crisis was temporary and that socialist principles could not be compromised. For the first two or three years, the response was essentially to tell Cubans to endure. This made the early crisis worse than it needed to be, because reforms that could have mitigated the damage were delayed out of ideological rigidity.

The pragmatic flexibility eventually showed in measures that represented significant departures from revolutionary orthodoxy. In 1993, Cuba legalised the holding of U.S. dollars — an extraordinary step for a country whose entire ideological framework was built on resistance to American capitalism. Cubans with access to dollar remittances from family in Miami or elsewhere suddenly had significant advantages over those without. A two-tier economy began to emerge: a peso economy for most Cubans, grinding and austere; and a dollar economy for those with foreign connections, which allowed access to goods unavailable in the peso system.

The government also legalised self-employment in a limited range of occupations and authorised private restaurants — paladares — operating out of people’s homes. Farmers’ markets, long prohibited, were allowed to reopen. Foreign investment in tourism was actively courted, and the construction of resort hotels with foreign capital produced a tourist industry oriented entirely toward bringing in foreign currency, with Cuban citizens largely excluded from the hotels and beaches that were generating that currency.

Urban farming / organoponicos — rooftop gardens and vacant lot farms that Cubans built in Havana out of necessity.

This last arrangement — the apartheid tourism system, as some critics called it, in which Cubans could not access the hotels and beaches available to foreigners — was one of the Special Period’s most corrosive legacies. It created a visible, daily reminder that the island’s resources were being mobilised for foreigners’ comfort while Cubans went hungry. The resentment it generated has never fully dissipated.

The American Response: Tighten the Screws

While Cubans were losing weight and their oxen were pulling their plows and their children were going to school on empty stomachs, the American government’s response was to tighten the embargo.

In 1992, the Cuban Democracy Act — the Torricelli Act — prohibited foreign subsidiaries of American companies from trading with Cuba and prohibited ships that had docked in Cuba from entering American ports for 180 days. This was specifically designed to complicate Cuba’s access to third-country trade at its most vulnerable moment.

In 1996, the Helms-Burton Act went further still, imposing sanctions on foreign companies that did business with Cuba using expropriated American property, and effectively removing from the executive branch the ability to lift the embargo unilaterally — from 1996 onward, only Congress could end it. The Helms-Burton Act was passed in the aftermath of Cuba shooting down two small planes operated by Brothers to the Rescue, a Cuban-exile organisation that had been dropping anti-government leaflets over Cuba — a genuine provocation by any standard, but one that the Clinton administration used as justification for a legislative tightening of the embargo that went far beyond anything warranted by the incident.

The pattern was consistent and revealing: whenever Cuba showed weakness — whenever the Special Period created a genuine possibility that the Cuban government might collapse — the American response was not to ease the pressure and encourage a soft landing, but to tighten the vise. The stated goal of the embargo was regime change. When the regime was at its most vulnerable, the tools of its potential destruction were sharpened.

What this meant for ordinary Cubans was that their period of maximum suffering was also their period of maximum external pressure. They were hungry and exhausted and disillusioned, and the outside world was simultaneously ensuring that their situation would get no better through normal economic channels.

And yet the government did not fall. The regime that American policy had been trying to topple for thirty years survived the Special Period, bloodied and reduced but intact. The Cubans who had endured it emerged with something that might be called, with appropriate ambivalence, a hardened pride — the knowledge that they had survived what should have broken them, that their country was still there, that the island had paid an extraordinary price and was still standing.

The Rafter Crisis and the Human Dimension

In the 1990s, Cuban raft crossings to the United States surged as the island plunged into the “Special Period.” Abbas/Magnum Photos

One of the Special Period’s most human moments came in the summer of 1994, when a wave of Cubans desperate enough to take their chances on improvised rafts in the Florida Straits — the balseros — created a crisis that brought the United States and Cuba into direct negotiation.

An estimated 35,000 Cubans took to the sea that summer on inner tubes, makeshift rafts, and anything that might float. Some made it. Many did not — the Straits of Florida are ninety miles of shark-patrolled, current-swept open water, and the death toll among the balseros has never been accurately established, but it was certainly in the hundreds and possibly in the thousands. It was an act of mass desperation by people who had calculated that the risk of drowning was preferable to remaining in the Cuba of 1994.

The balsero crisis produced a negotiated agreement between the Clinton administration and the Cuban government: the United States would accept 20,000 Cuban immigrants per year through legal channels, and Cuba would prevent its citizens from launching illegal sea crossings. The crisis subsided. The people who had already left — more than 30,000 of them picked up by the U.S. Coast Guard and held at Guantánamo Bay — were eventually admitted to the United States.

But the crisis revealed something that should have been obvious: when people are desperate enough, they will risk death to escape. The Special Period had produced that level of desperation. And the embargo — which had been tightened specifically to maximise Cuban suffering — had contributed directly to the conditions that drove those people into the water.

There is no way to establish a precise causal accounting for the balsero deaths. But when policy is designed to make life intolerable for ten million people, and some of those people die trying to escape, the connection is not metaphorical. It is direct.

What the Special Period Tells Us About Now

The Special Period is the essential context for understanding what is happening in Cuba in February 2026.

Cuba survived the Special Period. It took a decade, and it cost an almost incalculable amount of human suffering, and it left permanent scars — on the economy, on the social contract between the government and the people, on the Cuban sense of what security means. But the country came through it. The government remained. The Revolution survived, transformed and diminished, but present.

Gladys Valdés, 76, prepares coffee by candlelight during a blackout in Havana, March 2026. She has lived through this before — the Special Period of the 1990s brought the same darkness, the same improvisation, the same question of how long it would last. (Norlys Perez/Reuters)

The current crisis has similarities and differences. The similarities are obvious: a sudden loss of fuel supply, economic collapse, blackouts, food shortages, hospitals struggling, people suffering. The differences are that Cuba in 2026 is entering this crisis from a position of already-existing weakness — the economy never fully recovered from the Special Period’s structural damage, and the decade of crises since 2010 has depleted the resilience that got Cubans through the 1990s. There is less to fall back on. The social fabric is more frayed. The emigration of the past fifteen years has drained the island of a significant portion of its young working-age population.

When Jorge Piñón of the University of Texas estimates that Cuba’s fuel reserves could be fully depleted by mid-March 2026, he is describing not just a fuel crisis but a civilizational stress test for a society that has already been through several of them and is running short of reserves — material, psychological, demographic — with which to absorb another.

The Cubans who are living through the current blockade survived the Special Period. Their parents and grandparents rebuilt a country from near-nothing in the 1990s. They know what this looks like. They know how to endure.

But endurance has a limit. And the people engineering this crisis know it.
