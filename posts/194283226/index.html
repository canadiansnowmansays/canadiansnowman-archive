The Miracle of the Golden Arches

Healing with One Hand, Picking Pockets with the Other

April 19, 2026

Stephen’s Sunday Satire Published: Sunday, April 19, 2026 - 2 of 3

This is your lucky Sunday, a 3-for-1 special. The first involved angels, a celestial orb, and the Establishment Clause. This one involves McDonald's, a hundred-dollar bill, and the Strait of Hormuz. The gymnasium comes last.

Please don't mistake this for a pattern — my generosity remains entirely contingent on the cascading chaos and carnage that continues to emanate from the capital. When Washington slows down, so do I. Washington has not slowed down.

Last Sunday, the President of the United States posted a photograph of himself in a white robe.

The light fell at the angle painters reserve for the transcendent. The hand was extended. The posture was unmistakable to anyone who has spent time in churches, galleries, or the devotional wing of Western civilization’s accumulated imagery. By Sunday evening, the image had been seen by several million people. By Monday morning, it had been deleted. When asked, the President explained that he had been depicting a doctor. A Red Cross worker. A man of medicine going about his rounds.

The gymnasium chaplain — Pope Leo XIV, a man whose institution has been navigating this particular kind of claim for approximately two thousand years — had already weighed in, with the mildness of someone who holds a very long institutional memory and has seen this before. He suggested, gently, that healing and bombardment are not the same activity. He did not elaborate. He didn’t need to.

The President called him weak on crime.

We will return to the question of healing.

On Monday, one day after the deletion, the White House staged what it described as a celebration of the no-tax-on-tips policy. The venue was the Oval Office. The occasion was lunch. A DoorDash driver named Sharon Simmons, from Arkansas, delivered McDonald’s to the most powerful address on earth. The President met her at the door. He gave her a hundred-dollar bill. Photographers were present. The President noted, to the assembled press, that the scene didn’t look staged.

It looked staged.

Before we proceed, a brief word about the policy being celebrated, because its name is doing considerable work that its mechanics do not fully support. “No tax on tips” is not, strictly speaking, no tax on tips. Sharon Simmons must still declare the hundred dollars as income. She still pays Social Security and Medicare taxes on it. Her state may tax it as well. What the policy actually provides is a federal income tax deduction — up to twenty-five thousand dollars annually in qualified tips, which reduces her taxable income when she files her return. This is a genuine benefit, particularly for workers in higher brackets. It is also worth noting that more than a third of tipped workers don’t earn enough to owe federal income tax at all, in which case the deduction is worth precisely nothing to them. The policy is called no tax on tips because “a federal income tax deduction for qualified voluntary gratuities received in eligible occupations, subject to FICA, state taxes, and income phase-outs” does not fit on a campaign sign. Campaign signs, fortunately, have limited real estate.

I want to be precise about what was being celebrated. The no-tax-on-tips policy is a genuine benefit to workers like Sharon Simmons, who depend on gratuities and for whom the difference between taxed and untaxed income is not theoretical. This is real. The hundred dollars is real. The handshake is real. What is also real — and what Sharon Simmons told CNN, outside the White House, after the cameras had followed her back to the street — is that she is struggling with higher gas prices. Higher gas prices caused by a war. A war started by the man who just handed her a hundred dollars.

This is not a contradiction Sharon invented. She simply described her life.

The President healed her with one hand and picked her pocket with the other. She noticed both. She mentioned both. She thanked him for one.

Allow me to offer some context from the bleachers — I have been in them since February, which I realise is becoming a recurring theme, but the gymnasium remains booked and I came for the curling.

Since the war in Iran began at the end of February, the price of a barrel of oil has increased by forty-four percent. The international benchmark closed last week at just over ninety-five dollars. Analysts have said, with the careful language of people who are paid to say things carefully, that elevated prices will persist through the end of 2026. This is the supply chain through which Sharon Simmons’s gas prices travel on their way from the Strait of Hormuz to the pump in Arkansas.

The Strait of Hormuz, you will recall, is currently being blockaded. By the United States Navy. On the President’s instruction. After the ceasefire talks in Islamabad produced twenty-one hours of deliberation and no agreement and a press conference in which the Vice President explained that the ball was in Iran’s court, which remains, as previously noted in this space, not how dodge ball works.

The blockade is the reason the gas prices are high. The gas prices are the reason Sharon Simmons is struggling. Sharon Simmons is the reason the photo-op was staged. The photo-op is the reason the President handed her a hundred dollars. The hundred dollars is the reason the President could look into the cameras and insist it didn’t look staged.

The President looked into the cameras and insisted it didn’t look staged.

We return, as promised, to the question of healing.

Pope Leo, from his position of long institutional memory, made a distinction that seemed obvious to most observers and apparently scandalous to the President: that you cannot simultaneously claim the posture of a healer and pursue the conduct of a siege. That the white robe and the naval blockade do not belong in the same photograph. That a hundred-dollar bill handed to a woman whose gas prices you raised is not a miracle. It is a transaction. And not a favorable one for Sharon.

The President deleted the photograph. He did not delete the blockade.

The oil is still at ninety-five dollars a barrel. The ceasefire expires Tuesday. The janitor is still working. Sharon Simmons drove back to Arkansas.

I do not know if she kept the hundred dollars.

I know she still needs gas.
