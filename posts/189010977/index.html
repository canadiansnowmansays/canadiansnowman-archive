The Distaste of the Union

A Canadian’s Honest Assessment of America’s Condition

February 24, 2026

February 24, 2026

Mr. Speaker, Mr. Vice President, Members of Congress, and my fellow observers of this ongoing experiment in democratic governance:

I come before you today not to deliver platitudes about American greatness, but to offer what the official State of the Union address will not: an honest accounting of where this nation actually stands. Consider this an unofficial assessment from outside your partisan trenches - where distance provides clarity that proximity obscures.

The state of the Union is... let’s say “challenging.” Though if we’re being honest - and that’s rather the point of this exercise - the state of the Union tastes bitter. Hence: The Distaste of the Union.

Opening Remarks: On Comparative Realities

The President will undoubtedly proclaim tonight that America has never been greater, that the first year of his second term represents historic achievement, and that a golden age has dawned.

I will offer a different perspective: The only golden dawn visible is the golden glow reflecting off the Oval Office fixtures. America has rarely been more divided, constitutional norms have rarely been more strained, and the gap between official rhetoric and documented reality has rarely been wider.

This is not partisan criticism. This is pattern recognition based on evidence.

Let me be clear about my methodology: I will fact-check claims as they’re made. I will cite sources. I will distinguish between what Trump has genuinely accomplished (there are some things), what he’s exaggerated, and what he’s fabricated entirely.

Think of this as the post-game analysis that airs after the performance has ended and the audience has left the stadium. The difference is: I’m delivering it in real-time.

On Border Security: Success Through Questionable Means

The President will almost certainly tout historic low border crossings - under 10,000 per month, numbers not seen in decades.

What I’ll concede: This is factually accurate. Border security objectives were achieved.

What he won’t mention: The methods employed.

  • Deportations to third countries migrants never came from

  • Refugee admissions slashed to the lowest levels in program history, with slots reserved predominantly for white South Africans

  • Use of the Alien Enemies Act of 1798 - a wartime law applied in peacetime - drawing condemnation from constitutional scholars

  • Federal takeover of state National Guard units over governors’ objections

  • Only 500,000 actual deportations versus the promised one million

  • Government data showing most arrested immigrants lacked criminal records

I can acknowledge success while questioning whether the end justifies the means. That’s not partisanship. That’s constitutional analysis.

The distaste? Achieving a policy goal through methods that may not survive judicial review and that strain the very constitutional limits meant to prevent governmental overreach.

On Economic Prosperity: The K-Shaped Mirage

The President will celebrate GDP growth. He will likely cite the 4.3% third-quarter expansion. He may claim that inflation is defeated, that America is affordable again.

What I’ll concede: GDP did grow 4.3% in Q3. That’s real growth.

What he won’t mention: Who benefited.

The K-shaped economy isn’t a conspiracy theory. It’s documented in the data:

  • Wealthy Americans maintained spending, saw portfolio gains

  • Lower-income households cut back

  • Real disposable income remained flat

  • Credit card balances rose $24 billion in Q3 - people are spending on debt, not prosperity

  • Consumer confidence declined five consecutive months

  • Only 36% approve of Trump’s economic handling - his worst mark ever

The distaste? Celebrating GDP numbers that mask the reality that prosperity flows upward while working families still struggle with affordability. Inflation around 3% isn’t “defeated” - it’s persistent. Grocery prices remain stubbornly high. Mortgage rates remain elevated.

When you promise to “make America affordable again” and deliver a bifurcated economy where only the wealthy benefit, you haven’t succeeded. You’ve just changed which Americans are struggling.

On Government Efficiency: Death Disguised as Savings

The President will showcase DOGE’s achievements. He’ll cite billions in “wasteful spending” eliminated. He may present Elon Musk as a heroic reformer cutting bureaucratic fat.

What I’ll concede: 317,000 federal workers did leave employment. Agencies were indeed downsized.

What he won’t mention: The cost in human lives.

This is where the distaste becomes nausea.

The elimination of USAID represents the single most catastrophic policy failure in modern American peacetime history:

  • Between 500,000 and 1,000,000 preventable deaths in 2025 alone (multiple independent analyses)

  • The Lancet documented that USAID programs saved 92 million lives over two decades

  • HIV treatment cut off for 176,000 people

  • An adult dies from HIV every 3 minutes due to PEPFAR cuts

  • A child dies every 31 minutes

  • 14 million additional deaths projected by 2030 if cuts continue

  • 360,000 people dead from lack of food and medication by mid-July (Senator Schatz)

The administration produced zero evidence of fraud or waste justifying this elimination. Elon Musk’s ideological opposition to foreign aid drove policy. DOGE officials manually blocked even life-saving expenditures that Secretary of State Rubio had authorized.

Meanwhile, disease control capacity collapsed. Disaster response degraded. Cancer research suffered. Inspector Generals - Congress’s mechanism for policing waste and fraud - were purged, eliminating actual oversight.

The distaste? Calling mass death “government efficiency.” Labeling humanitarian catastrophe as “savings.” The casualties from USAID elimination alone exceed American combat deaths in Vietnam, Korea, Iraq, and Afghanistan combined.

This isn’t cutting waste. This is ideological destruction with a body count.

On Law and Order: Extrajudicial Disorder

The President will tout his tough stance on drug cartels. He may reference military operations in the Caribbean. He will position himself as defender of American lives against the fentanyl crisis.

What I’ll concede: The fentanyl crisis is real and devastating.

What he won’t mention: That his “solution” violates domestic and international law.

By year’s end, the U.S. military conducted at least 29 strikes on boats in the Caribbean and Pacific, killing 105 people - all without due process, identification, or legal justification.

The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights called these “extrajudicial killings” violating international law. Human Rights Watch reached the same conclusion. So did multiple UN special rapporteurs.

The most damning detail: “double-tap” strikes. After initial attacks left survivors in the water, second strikes deliberately killed them rather than rescue them. CNN reported that Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth allegedly gave verbal orders to SEAL Team Six to “leave no survivors” - though the Pentagon denies this.

Here’s the strategic absurdity: The strikes targeted cocaine smuggling from South America. The overdose crisis stems primarily from fentanyl manufactured in Mexican labs using Chinese chemicals. These operations served no drug interdiction purpose.

The real objective appeared to be laying groundwork for regime change in oil-rich Venezuela.

The hypocrisy: Trump indicted Venezuelan President Maduro for cocaine trafficking, then pardoned former Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernández, who was convicted in federal court for conspiring to export cocaine to the United States.

The distaste? Conducting extrajudicial executions, violating the federal murder statute and international law, all while claiming to fight drugs - when the operations target the wrong substance entirely and may actually serve regime-change objectives disguised as drug policy.

On Justice: Weaponization He Claimed to End

The President will decry the “weaponization” of the Justice Department against him. He will position himself as victim of political persecution.

What I’ll concede: He believes this narrative deeply.

What he won’t mention: That he’s doing exactly what he claims to oppose.

Trump abandoned all pretense of prosecutorial independence:

  • Direct, public orders to investigate and indict political enemies (James Comey, Letitia James)

  • Pushed out U.S. Attorney who found no legitimate basis for indictments

  • Installed Lindsey Halligan, a personal lawyer with zero prosecutorial experience

  • Obtained grand jury indictments days later

  • Judge dismissed cases - Halligan improperly appointed

  • DOJ continues attempting prosecution anyway

  • Miami investigation into “grand conspiracy” by Russia inquiry officials - fishing expedition for revenge

This demolished the post-Watergate norm of Justice Department independence. Trump justified it as “reciprocal retaliation” for Biden’s alleged weaponization - a claim without evidence that nonetheless provides political cover for genuine abuse.

The distaste? Claiming to restore justice while weaponizing it. Positioning yourself as victim while victimizing others. Destroying institutional norms while claiming to defend them.

When the Justice Department becomes an instrument of presidential revenge rather than impartial law enforcement, the rule of law itself is the casualty.

On Civil Rights: Erasing Rather Than Expanding

The President will celebrate ending “woke” ideology. He will frame DEI elimination as restoring merit. He will claim victory over divisive race-centered policies.

What I’ll concede: Many Americans have legitimate concerns about DEI implementation.

What he won’t mention: The systematic erasure of civil rights progress and historical memory.

The comprehensiveness is striking:

  • Federal employees threatened with “adverse consequences” for failing to report colleagues who defied DEI purges

  • Billions cut from schools and colleges with diversity programs

  • Pentagon reviewed military libraries for books addressing racism and sexism

  • Research grants mentioning “equity” or “racial minority” systematically eliminated

  • Confederate statues restored and celebrated - explicitly honoring those who fought to preserve slavery

  • Accused Smithsonian of focusing too much on “how bad slavery was”

  • Transgender Americans excluded from public life through reinterpreted Title IX

  • Government documents restricted to “biological sex” only

These actions effectively rewrote Civil Rights Act of 1964 protections through executive reinterpretation rather than legislative change.

The distaste? There’s a difference between reforming implementation and wholesale erasure. Between addressing concerns and eliminating protections. Between merit-based hiring and systematic exclusion.

When you restore Confederate statues while accusing the Smithsonian of focusing too much on slavery’s horrors, you’re not restoring merit. You’re revising history.

On Trade: Chaos Disguised as Strategy

The President will tout his tariff policy. He will claim to be protecting American workers and industries. He may cite announced investments or plant openings.

What I’ll concede: Some companies announced U.S. investments. Taiwan Semiconductor’s commitment was significant.

What he won’t mention: The economic chaos and comprehensive failure of stated objectives.

“Liberation Day” tariffs in April triggered financial market meltdowns so severe Trump partially reversed course. The effective tariff rate hit 18% - highest since 1934.

The results:

  • Manufacturing lost more than 50,000 workers since Trump took office

  • No factory boom materialized

  • Manufacturing sector continued two-year decline

  • 2025 trade deficit significantly higher than previous year - contradicting stated goals

  • Companies stockpiled before tariffs, then passed costs to consumers

  • California firms: $11.3 billion in tariff costs (Jan-May)

  • Port of Los Angeles: 70% capacity

  • Southern California: 40% decline in trade/logistics job postings

The China trade war required farmer bailouts. The Supreme Court appeared skeptical of emergency power claims.

The distaste? Promising a manufacturing boom, delivering manufacturing job losses. Promising trade balance improvement, delivering increased deficits. Creating economic disruption without achieving stated objectives while claiming victory.

On Foreign Policy: One Success, Multiple Exaggerations

The President will cite diplomatic achievements. He will likely emphasize the Gaza ceasefire. He may claim to have “solved” multiple conflicts.

What I’ll concede: Trump’s team played a significant role in the October 2025 Gaza ceasefire - a genuine diplomatic accomplishment.

What he won’t mention: The fragility of that success and the failures everywhere else.

Gaza reality:

  • Israel killed 406+ Palestinians during the “ceasefire”

  • Full aid delivery continues to be blocked

  • Phase two negotiations stalled

  • Critics call it temporary pacification rather than genuine peace

Ukraine failure:

  • Promise to end war “within 24 hours” proved hollow

  • Russia unwilling to accept terms

  • No resolution achieved

Exaggerated claims:

  • “Solved eight wars in eight months” lacks substance

  • Thailand-Cambodia deal (July) collapsed by December

  • Rwanda-DRC fighting persisted

  • India-Pakistan noted minimal U.S. involvement

Contradictions:

  • Greenlighted Iran nuclear site strikes - contradicted non-interventionist promises

Alliance damage:

  • UK stopped sharing drug-trafficking intelligence (avoiding complicity in killings)

  • European allies horrified at USAID deaths

  • Canada condemned “economic betrayal”

The distaste? Inflating one success into eight. Claiming victories that didn’t occur. Conducting strikes that contradict stated principles. Damaging alliances built over 75 years.

On Constitutional Governance: Limits Tested, Norms Shattered

The President will celebrate executive action. He will claim to have restored American sovereignty and strength.

What I’ll concede: He signed record numbers of executive orders and actions.

What he won’t mention: The constitutional questions raised.

Volume isn’t legitimacy. Activity isn’t achievement. Trump pursued power accumulation across multiple domains simultaneously:

  • Routinely invoked emergency authorities for non-emergencies

  • Fired officials in ways conflicting with statutory limits

  • Centralized control by challenging Congressional checks

  • Claimed authority to nullify statutes (TikTok ban)

  • Delayed or refused appropriated funds

  • Dismantled agencies without Congressional approval

  • Declared armed conflicts without Congressional authorization

  • Federalized National Guard units over governors’ objections

Congressional Republicans’ reluctance to defend institutional prerogatives represents a concerning development transcending partisan advantage. When the legislature declines to protect constitutional authorities, the system of checks and balances weakens regardless of which party temporarily benefits.

The distaste? Governing through emergency powers meant for actual emergencies. Accumulating power by circumventing rather than working with Congress. Setting precedents that future presidents - of any party - can exploit.

Constitutional limits exist to protect democracy itself, not to obstruct preferred policies.

On Legislative Achievement: Historic Weakness

The President will tout his executive accomplishments.

What I’ll concede: Executive actions were indeed numerous.

What he won’t mention: The absence of legislative success.

Five bills signed into law in one year. That’s the weakest first-year legislative output in over a century. The “One Big Beautiful Bill” tax package represents the primary accomplishment.

This legislative vacuum meant Trump governed almost entirely through executive action - making most changes vulnerable to judicial invalidation or future presidential reversal.

Durable change requires Congressional approval. Compromise and negotiation. Building coalitions. Passing laws.

The distaste? Confusing executive action volume with governance success. Ignoring that lasting change requires legislation. Bypassing Congress while claiming to restore democracy.

The Accounting: What He’ll Never Mention

Beyond individual policy failures, the year can be measured in preventable casualties:

The Body Count:

  • 500,000-1,000,000 deaths from USAID elimination

  • 105 extrajudicial killings in boat strikes

  • 406 Palestinians killed during Gaza “ceasefire”

  • Increased mortality from degraded disease control and disaster response

  • Projected 28,785 annual deaths if Medicaid cuts proceed

No modern peacetime administration has presided over this scale of preventable death. These weren’t casualties from pursuing national security objectives. They resulted from ideological preferences implemented without regard for humanitarian consequences.

The Constitutional Crisis: Presidential power expanded dangerously when courts and Congress refused to enforce limits. The “unitary executive theory” provided legal veneer for actions that previous eras might have considered impeachable.

The Alliance Damage: Traditional allies faced a dilemma: continue cooperation and risk complicity in human rights violations, or distance themselves and weaken 75-year-old alliance structures. Many chose distancing.

The Legislative Failure: Five bills in one year. Historic weakness masked by executive hyperactivity.

The Economic Bifurcation: GDP growth masking a K-shaped economy where prosperity flows upward while working families struggle.

The distaste? These outcomes aren’t debatable. They’re documented. Measurable. Verifiable through independent sources.

The Rhetorical Strategy: What to Watch For Tonight

When the President speaks, watch for these patterns:

1. Confusing Activity with Achievement

  • “We’ve done more than any administration” (volume ≠ effectiveness)

  • Record executive actions (quantity ≠ quality)

  • “Unprecedented action” (unprecedented ≠ successful)

2. Claiming Credit for Inherited Trends

  • Economic recovery trajectory began under Biden

  • Oil production already at record highs

  • Some border security improvements started before January 2025

3. Selective Framing

  • GDP numbers without mentioning who benefited

  • Border security without mentioning methods

  • “Efficiency” without mentioning death tolls

  • “Strength” without mentioning alliance damage

4. Exaggeration Patterns

  • One success inflated to many

  • Marginal changes presented as revolutions

  • Correlation claimed as causation

5. Strategic Omissions

  • No mention of USAID deaths

  • No mention of extrajudicial killings

  • No mention of legislative weakness

  • No mention of constitutional violations

  • No mention of DOJ weaponization he’s conducting

A Note on Distaste vs. Disgust

Why “distaste” rather than “disgust”?

Disgust suggests emotional revulsion. Distaste suggests considered rejection based on evaluation.

I’m not emotionally disgusted by this administration - I’m analytically opposed to specific policies based on their documented outcomes. There’s a difference.

Some policies I can acknowledge as successful even while questioning methods (border security). Others represent comprehensive failures (affordability, manufacturing, USAID).

The distaste comes from the gap between rhetoric and reality. The confusion of activity with effectiveness. The celebration of achievements that don’t exist. The concealment of catastrophic failures behind rhetorical flourish.

Good governance requires honest accounting. When rhetoric diverges this far from documented reality, the system breaks down. Not because of partisan disagreement, but because shared facts become impossible.

The Path Forward: Resolutions for Reality

If I were advising this administration - which I’m decidedly not - here’s what I’d recommend:

1. Acknowledge What Worked, Adjust What Didn’t

  • Border security numbers succeeded → maintain them

  • USAID elimination catastrophic → restore life-saving programs immediately

  • Economic bifurcation worsening → policies targeting working families, not just wealthy

  • Extrajudicial killings indefensible → return to lawful interdiction

2. Govern Through Congress

  • Five bills in one year is embarrassing

  • Legislative action creates durable change

  • Executive orders are vulnerable to courts and future presidents

3. Respect Constitutional Limits

  • Emergency powers for actual emergencies

  • Congressional approval for agency elimination

  • Courts’ role in checking executive power

  • These limits protect everyone long-term

4. Value Human Life Above Ideology

  • When policies produce mass casualties, reverse course

  • 500,000-1,000,000 deaths isn’t “efficiency”

  • 105 extrajudicial killings isn’t “strength”

5. Rebuild Rather Than Destroy

  • Alliance relationships matter

  • Institutional norms serve democracy

  • Prosecutorial independence protects everyone

  • Constitutional checks prevent tyranny

Conclusion: The Taste Test

The state of the Union, as I assess it from outside your partisan trenches:

Some things work:

  • Border security numbers (if not methods)

  • Gaza ceasefire (if not durability)

  • Executive action volume (if not effectiveness)

Most things don’t:

  • Economic promises unfulfilled

  • Manufacturing jobs lost, not gained

  • Legislative record historically weak

  • Constitutional norms strained

  • Alliance relationships damaged

  • Government capacity degraded

Some things are catastrophic:

  • USAID elimination: 500,000-1,000,000 dead

  • Extrajudicial killings: 105 dead

  • DOJ weaponization destroying prosecutorial independence

  • Civil rights progress systematically erased

The distaste isn’t partisan. It’s empirical.

When you claim the “most successful first month in history” while producing the weakest legislative output in a century, that’s not success - that’s spin.

When you promise affordability and deliver a K-shaped economy where only the wealthy benefit, that’s not achievement - that’s failure dressed as victory.

When you eliminate “waste” and produce 500,000-1,000,000 deaths, that’s not efficiency - that’s catastrophe.

The distaste is this: America deserves better than rhetoric divorced from reality. Better than activity confused with achievement. Better than promises broken while claiming victory.

Democracy requires honest accounting. Shared facts. Evidence-based assessment. When the gap between official narrative and documented reality becomes this wide, the system itself is endangered.

So tonight, when you hear claims of historic achievement and golden ages, ask: Where’s the evidence? What are the costs? Who actually benefited? What’s being concealed?

The answers are in the data. The receipts exist. The question is whether anyone - president, Congress, media, voters - is willing to look honestly at them.

That’s the real state of the Union: A nation capable of greatness, led by an administration confusing motion with progress, volume with effectiveness, and rhetoric with reality.

The taste? Bitter. But truth often is.

From outside your borders, watching with concern,

A Canadian observer who prefers evidence to narrative

All claims are cited and verifiable. All data is documented. All conclusions are evidence-based.

The distaste isn’t partisan preference. It’s what happens when you actually check the claims against the facts.
