The Power Consolidation

How Trump, Rodríguez, and Cabello Secured Control After the Senate Capitulated

January 18, 2026

January 18, 2026

Week One of Trump’s Venezuela adventure delivered spectacular military success: Delta Force captured Maduro, U.S. jets bombed Caracas, American forces killed 80+ people, Trump declared “we’re in charge.” The invasion succeeded brilliantly.

Week Two revealed what comes after military triumph: the grinding, messy work of actually controlling a country. And in Venezuela’s case, that work is producing an emerging triumvirate—Trump in Washington, Delcy Rodríguez in Caracas, Diosdado Cabello controlling the guns—that bears little resemblance to the democratic transition Trump promised.

This is the story of how power is being consolidated in occupied Venezuela, who’s doing the consolidating, and what it reveals about the gap between invasion and governance.

THE SENATE CAPITULATION: HOW TRUMP BROKE THE RESISTANCE

Senate Majority Leader John Thune, R-S.D., speaks during a news conference at the Capitol, Tuesday, Jan. 13, 2026, in Washington. (AP Photo/Rod Lamkey, Jr.)

On January 8, the Senate delivered a rare bipartisan rebuke: 52 senators voted to advance a war powers resolution limiting Trump’s Venezuela military authority. Five Republicans—Susan Collins (Maine), Josh Hawley (Missouri), Lisa Murkowski (Alaska), Rand Paul (Kentucky), and Todd Young (Indiana)—joined all Democrats in what CNN called “the clearest sign that the president might not enjoy as free a hand as he has become accustomed to.”

Trump’s response was immediate and furious. On Truth Social: “Republicans should be ashamed of the Senators that just voted with Democrats in attempting to take away our Powers to fight and defend the United States of America. Susan Collins, Lisa Murkowski, Rand Paul, Josh Hawley, and Todd Young should never be elected to office again.”

What happened next demonstrated Trump’s ability to bend Congress to his will through sheer pressure.

Over six days, Trump personally called the defectors. Secretary of State Marco Rubio called them. The administration deployed what The Hill described as a “profanity-laced rant” to Collins. Trump publicly attacked Young during a Detroit Economic Club speech: “And then you have a gentleman from Indiana, Todd Young. You say, ‘Why are you voting against?’ They can’t give you an answer.”

The pressure worked.

When the final vote came Wednesday, January 14, two senators had flipped: Hawley and Young.

Todd Young’s explanation revealed what secured his capitulation: “After numerous conversations with senior national security officials, I have received assurances that there are no American troops in Venezuela. I’ve also received a commitment that if President Trump were to determine American forces are needed in major military operations in Venezuela, the Administration will come to Congress in advance to ask for an authorization of force.”

Note the careful phrasing: “major military operations.” Not “any military operations.” Not “extended occupation.” Major operations.

Secretary of State Rubio’s letter to Senate Foreign Relations Committee Chairman Jim Risch—shared with the defecting senators—contained similar hedging: “Should the President determine that he needs to introduce US Armed Forces into hostilities in major military operation in Venezuela, he would seek congressional authorizations in advance (circumstances permitting).”

Circumstances permitting.

The final vote was 50-50, with Vice President JD Vance casting the tie-breaking vote to kill the resolution.

Republican leaders used a procedural maneuver, arguing the resolution was no longer “privileged” because the U.S. is not currently engaged in “hostilities” with Venezuela. Senate Majority Leader John Thune: “We have no troops on the ground in Venezuela. We’re not currently conducting military operations there.”

This is technically true and fundamentally dishonest. The U.S. military is seizing Venezuelan oil tankers weekly, enforcing a blockade, and—as we’ll see—actively helping Venezuela’s interim government consolidate internal control. American military force is determining Venezuela’s fate every single day.

Collins, Murkowski, and Paul held firm despite the pressure. Rand Paul was particularly clear: “This is one of the most important debates of the last seven decades. What I said to the president is, ‘This debate is bigger than me, bigger than him. It’s about the Constitution.’”

But the resolution died anyway.

Senator Tim Kaine, who sponsored the measure, said it was “disappointing that my colleagues let the president sort of beat them into submission.” He promised more war powers resolutions are coming—on Colombia, Mexico, Cuba, Iran, Nigeria, and Greenland.

The pattern is now established: Trump can threaten senators into submission by questioning their patriotism, threatening their re-election, and offering vague assurances that constrain nothing. Constitutional war powers mean whatever Trump says they mean.

The occupation of Venezuela has congressional blessing through capitulation.

THE CABELLO PROBLEM: TRUMP’S MOST DANGEROUS TARGET

Diosdado Cabello, Venezuela’s interior minister, welcoming a flight bringing Venezuelan migrants from Mexico last year.Credit...The New York Times

While Trump was securing Senate compliance, a more fundamental problem was emerging in Caracas: Diosdado Cabello.

The New York Times published a major profile of Cabello on January 15 under the headline “Maduro’s Enforcer Faces an Uneasy Transition, and a Bounty on His Head.” The piece captures why Cabello represents Trump’s most dangerous unresolved problem in Venezuela.

Cabello, 62, serves as Venezuela’s Interior Minister—a position giving him control over police, intelligence services, the national guard, and the pro-government militias known as colectivos. In late 2024, he installed a cousin to run SEBIN, Venezuela’s secret police. Through these positions, analysts say, he’s arguably the second most powerful figure in Venezuela—or possibly the most powerful.

Brian Fonseca, a professor at Florida International University, told CNN: “There’s essentially three centers of power in Venezuela right now. You have the Maduro center of gravity, which [acting president] Delcy Rodríguez is an extension of. You have [Vladimir] Padrino López, who is minister of defense. The third, arguably the most important, is Diosdado Cabello.”

Cabello’s name appears second on the superseding indictment against Maduro—right below the captured president. The U.S. government has placed a $25 million bounty on his head (raised from $10 million in 2020) and charged him with conspiracy to commit narco-terrorism, conspiracy to import cocaine, and firearms violations.

When asked about Cabello during his January 14 phone call with Rodríguez, Trump was dismissive: “I know the number one,” he said, brushing aside questions about Venezuela’s “number two.”

But Cabello isn’t background scenery. And the Times profile reveals why he’s so dangerous.

For over a decade, Cabello has hosted a weekly television show called “Con el Mazo Dando” (”With the Club Striking”)—often running over three hours before a live studio audience. The format is simple: Cabello brandishes a plastic club, cracks jokes, shares what he claims is exclusive intelligence about Venezuela’s enemies, and calls out critics by name.

What happens next is predictable and terrifying.

The Times documented the pattern: After Cabello ripped into opposition politician Juan Pablo Guanipa on his show, government agents arrested Guanipa, charged him with terrorism and treason, and sent him to prison. He remains there.

Amnesty International has denounced the show as a tool of repression. In July 2025, the organization criticized Cabello for announcing a journalist’s arrest on the program.

Multiple former intelligence agents, detainees, and senior Venezuelan officials told U.N. investigators that Cabello gives direct orders to SEBIN, including whom to arrest, release, and torture. The Times quotes a former Venezuelan prosecutor, Zair Mundaray, who investigated Cabello: “You could make an encyclopedia of all the crimes Diosdado Cabello has committed. If there’s something to steal, he steals it.”

Cabello opposed the July 2024 elections that Maduro agreed to hold in exchange for partial U.S. sanctions relief. After Maduro lost decisively but declared himself the winner anyway, he appointed Cabello as Interior Minister in August 2024—widely interpreted as an acknowledgment that the government would rely on brute force to maintain power.

From his new position, Cabello immediately launched “Operation Knock Knock”—deploying security forces to raid homes and arrest government opponents. The government said it arrested more than 2,000 people for protesting election results.

Now, with Maduro captured and Rodríguez as acting president, Cabello continues to wield enormous power through control of Venezuela’s security apparatus. And he’s sending mixed signals about cooperation with the United States.

Publicly, Cabello has supported Rodríguez. But he’s also continued condemning the U.S. raid, calling it in one speech a “barbaric, treacherous attack.”

In a recent broadcast alongside police commandos, Cabello said Venezuela remained calm after the U.S. attack because of the state’s monopoly on weapons. “We are guarantors of the country’s tranquillity,” he declared.

Verónica Zubillaga, a Venezuelan sociologist at the University of Illinois Chicago who studies violence in Venezuela, told the Times this was both reassurance and threat: “It’s a very revealing speech about the role he wants to play, and it’s also a threat about what could happen if people go after him. It’s a warning to be careful, because he can set off waves of extreme violence.”

This week, Cabello launched a public relations counter-offensive against the U.S. narco-terrorism narrative.

At a Tuesday, January 13 press conference, Cabello announced that Venezuelan security forces had seized 7,148 kilograms of drugs in the first days of 2026. The largest operation netted 6,850 kilograms in the Orinoco River basin on January 5. Cabello emphasized this represented “one of the biggest blows to the logistical networks that attempt to use Venezuelan waterways for drug trafficking.”

He also claimed Venezuela seized nearly 70 tons of drugs in 2025—a record.

Then came the defiant declaration: “The Cartel of the Suns has never existed, and it is not an argument for the United States’ attack on Venezuela; the argument is oil.”

The “Cartel of the Suns” is what the U.S. Justice Department calls the alleged drug-trafficking network headed by figures within Venezuela’s government and military—including Cabello himself. By claiming it doesn’t exist while simultaneously announcing massive drug seizures, Cabello is trying to have it both ways: demonstrating anti-drug credentials while denying the criminal network he’s accused of running.

For Trump, Cabello represents an impossible problem. The U.S. cannot credibly claim to be fighting narco-terrorism while working with a government where the Interior Minister—who controls all security forces—is himself indicted for narco-terrorism with a $25 million bounty on his head.

But removing Cabello would destabilize the very government Trump needs to maintain order. Cabello controls the colectivos, the intelligence services, the police, and has deep ties to the military. The Times quotes Risa Grais-Targow, the Latin America director for Eurasia Group: “He’s just as bad a guy as Maduro, if not more.”

Trump’s solution appears to be pretending the problem doesn’t exist. “I know the number one,” he said dismissively. Translation: I’m working with Rodríguez, and I don’t want to talk about the man with the guns standing behind her.

But Cabello isn’t going anywhere. He’s making that clear through his drug seizure announcements, his defiant rhetoric, and his continued control of repression machinery.

The triumvirate is forming: Trump sets policy from Washington, Rodríguez implements it from the presidential palace, and Cabello ensures compliance through control of violence.

RODRÍGUEZ CONSOLIDATES: CABINET MOVES AND TRUMP’S “GREAT CONVERSATION”

While Cabello controls the guns, Delcy Rodríguez is consolidating bureaucratic and diplomatic control.

On Monday, January 13, Rodríguez announced her first cabinet changes—moves that signal both continuity with Maduro and careful positioning for Trump.

The most significant appointment: Captain Juan Escalona as Minister of the Presidency.

Escalona is a well-known figure in Venezuela—not for policy expertise, but for his closeness to Maduro. He was a member of Maduro’s personal security circle, and the captured president used to make public jokes about him during nationally televised events. His promotion to head the Ministry of the Presidency sends an unmistakable signal: loyalty to the Maduro inner circle is being rewarded, not purged.

Rodríguez’s announcement emphasized Escalona’s “loyalty, ability, and commitment” to ensuring continuation of “our Bolivarian Government’s plans.” The message is clear: the core leadership remains unchanged despite Maduro’s absence.

The second appointment involved moving Vice Admiral Aníbal Coronado from the Ministry of the Presidency to head the Ministry of Ecosocialism, replacing Ricardo Molina—apparently to make room for Escalona’s promotion.

Rodríguez also appointed General Gustavo Enrique López as the new commander of the Presidential Honor Guard—the unit responsible for protecting the president’s security. She recognized the outgoing general’s “dedication, loyalty, and unwavering commitment.”

Additionally, she appointed Calixto Ortega Sánchez as vice president for the economy—a sensitive post that will negotiate the distribution of Venezuela’s oil wealth with the Americans.

The pattern is consistent: Rodríguez is surrounding herself with Maduro loyalists in key security and administrative positions. This is not a government preparing for democratic transition. This is a government preparing to survive under new management.

And Trump appears perfectly comfortable with that arrangement.

On Wednesday, January 14, Trump announced he had spoken by phone with Rodríguez—their first confirmed direct communication since Maduro’s capture.

Trump’s description was effusive: “We’re getting along very well with Venezuela. We just had a great conversation today, and she’s a terrific person.”

When asked about Diosdado Cabello—Venezuela’s Interior Minister with a $25 million bounty—Trump brushed aside the question: “I know the number one.”

Rodríguez confirmed the call, describing it as positive. She announced she was sending envoys to Washington to meet with senior U.S. officials—on the same day María Corina Machado met with Trump at the White House.

The diplomatic contradiction is stunning.

Just ten days earlier, Rodríguez had appeared on Venezuelan state television denouncing Maduro’s “kidnapping” and calling it a “flagrant violation of the international legal order.” She declared that Maduro was Venezuela’s “only president” and that “Venezuela shall never be a colony ever again.”

In her January 14 speech focusing on political prisoners, Rodríguez spent significant time crediting Maduro for starting the release process—signaling she was not breaking continuity with the captured president.

Yet simultaneously, she’s having “great conversations” with Trump, sending envoys to Washington, and announcing a “diplomatic exploratory process” to address “consequences arising from the aggression.”

The U.S. and Venezuelan governments have begun discussions to restart diplomatic relations, including potentially reopening the U.S. embassy in Caracas (closed since 2019).

This raises the question former Trump envoy Elliott Abrams articulated: “If Delcy Rodríguez, who was appointed vice president by Maduro, is the legitimate interim president, doesn’t that mean you think Nicolás Maduro was a legitimate president? And if you do, doesn’t that affect his trial? Does he have head-of-state immunity?”

These contradictions don’t bother Trump. He needs Rodríguez to maintain order. Rodríguez needs Trump to not launch a second wave of attacks and to eventually lift sanctions.

The accommodation is working—for now.

Rodríguez announced in her Wednesday speech that Venezuela is entering a “new political moment” that allows for “coexistence,” “living together,” and “recognition of the other.”

Translation: We’ll release some prisoners, we’ll talk nicely about cooperation, we’ll have pleasant phone calls with Trump. But the same people will remain in power, backed by the same security apparatus, enforcing the same authoritarian control.

Trump gets to claim success in Venezuela. Rodríguez gets to survive. Cabello keeps his guns and his $25 million bounty.

THE BLOCKADE TIGHTENS: SIX TANKERS AND COUNTING

The fourth pillar of the consolidation strategy is the oil blockade—and it’s intensifying weekly.

Since your last essay covering the dramatic Olina incident, the U.S. has continued aggressive tanker seizures:

On Thursday, January 15, U.S. forces seized the Veronica in the Caribbean—the sixth sanctioned tanker captured as part of Trump’s effort to control Venezuelan oil.

The ship is currently listed as flying the flag of Guyana and is considered part of the shadow fleet that moves oil in violation of U.S. sanctions. According to its registration data, the ship has also been known as the Gallileo, owned and managed by a company in Russia. A tanker with the same registration number previously sailed under the name Pegas and was sanctioned by the U.S. Treasury Department for moving illicit Russian oil.

U.S. Coast Guard tactical teams conducted the boarding, with Marines and sailors from USS Gerald R. Ford participating in the operation.

Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem posted video footage showing helicopters approaching the ship, armed troops dropping down by rope, with at least nine people visible on deck. She described it as “another ‘ghost fleet’ tanker ship suspected of carrying embargoed oil” that “departed Venezuela attempting to evade US forces.”

“There is no outrunning or escaping American justice,” Noem declared.

The Marinera seizure earlier in January was particularly dramatic. After the tanker fled Venezuela’s blockade, changed its name from Bella 1 to Marinera, and hoisted a Russian flag, Russia sent a submarine and destroyer to protect it.

When U.S. forces arrived to seize it anyway, the Russian ships withdrew.

Trump told Fox News: “They decided not to mess around with us.”

Russia’s Foreign Ministry condemned the seizure as a “gross violation” of international maritime law. On Friday, January 10, the Kremlin thanked Washington for agreeing to release two Russian citizens from the Marinera’s crew—a small diplomatic gesture that prevented further escalation.

The pattern is clear: The U.S. is willing to confront Russia directly over Venezuelan oil, and Putin—focused entirely on Ukraine negotiations—won’t risk antagonizing Trump over a Latin American ally he’s already written off.

After Maduro’s capture, at least 16 tankers fled Venezuelan ports simultaneously, trying to evade the U.S. quarantine by overwhelming enforcement. They transmitted false names, turned off tracking systems, tried every evasion tactic available.

It didn’t work. The interdictions have led to a sharp decline in crude exports. Tankers now remain in Venezuelan waters rather than risk seizure.

Only oil tankers linked to Chevron Corporation can freely travel with Venezuelan crude. Chevron spokesperson Bill Turenne reported operations “continued without disruption.”

The message is unmistakable: Play by U.S. rules, get U.S. protection. Defy U.S. control, get seized.

Morningstar DBRS warned in January that “heightened geopolitical tensions and enforcement actions linked to Venezuela have elevated risks for the global property and casualty insurance sector, particularly in specialty lines such as marine and aviation insurance with exposure to Caribbean shipping lanes.”

The Wall Street Journal described “paralyzed tanker traffic” to Venezuelan ports as “an existential crisis for a regime that runs on oil revenue.”

But the crisis is precisely the point. By seizing tankers, blocking exports, and forcing all legitimate oil traffic through U.S.-approved channels, Trump has created total leverage over Venezuela’s government.

Rodríguez can have phone calls with Trump and send envoys to Washington. But she can’t sell oil without American permission. She can’t operate her government without American acquiescence.

This is what “we’re in charge” actually looks like.

CONCLUSION: THE TRIUMVIRATE COMPLETE

Two weeks after the invasion, the outlines of Venezuela’s new power structure are clear.

Trump controls from Washington—deciding which tankers get seized, which political prisoners must be released, what diplomatic gestures are required.

Rodríguez manages from Caracas—appointing Maduro loyalists to key positions, having “great conversations” with Trump, maintaining bureaucratic machinery while calling American actions both “kidnapping” and grounds for “diplomatic cooperation.”

Cabello holds the guns—controlling SEBIN, police, national guard, and colectivos, announcing drug seizures to counter narco-terrorism charges, warning he can “set off waves of extreme violence” if challenged.

The Senate capitulated after brief resistance. Two Republican senators flipped after receiving vague assurances about “major military operations” requiring congressional approval “circumstances permitting.” The war powers resolution died 50-50 with Vance breaking the tie.

This is not the democratic transition Trump promised. This is not María Corina Machado—the Nobel Peace Prize winner who actually won the 2024 election—taking power.

This is an occupied country where the occupier has decided that the previous authoritarian government’s infrastructure—minus the captured president—is the most efficient way to maintain order and extract resources.

You can invade a country with Delta Force. You can capture its president with Special Operations. You can seize oil tankers with Coast Guard boarding teams.

But you cannot create democracy through military occupation while empowering the same security apparatus that crushed it.

Week Two revealed the triumvirate that will govern Venezuela: Trump’s leverage, Rodríguez’s accommodation, and Cabello’s guns.

Democracy never had a chance.
