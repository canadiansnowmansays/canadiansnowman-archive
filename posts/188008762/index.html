Part 28: What Trump’s Grandchildren Will Pay for His Climate Denial (2009–2060+)

A Letter to the Future

This essay is not really about Donald Trump. It is about the people who will live with what he has done — long after he is gone, long after the four activists who built his climate demolition strategy are gone, long after every senator who enabled it and every voter who cheered it has left the stage.

It is about inheritance. Not the financial kind — though that enters the picture too, as we will see when we get to Mar-a-Lago and the coastline it sits on. It is about the other inheritance: the atmospheric one. The one you cannot put in a trust, cannot renegotiate, cannot declare bankruptcy on, and cannot pardon your way out of.

Carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere today will continue warming the planet for centuries. That is not a political opinion. That is physics. And it means that the consequences of revoking the endangerment finding in 2026 will not be fully felt until people who will not be born for decades are old enough to understand what was done to them and why.

This is the accountability time-lag — and it is, perhaps, the cruelest feature of the entire Trump legacy.

The Physics of Delay

To understand why climate decisions made today matter for generations, you need to understand one fact about carbon dioxide that separates it from almost every other policy problem in American politics.

Most political damage is reversible, or at least containable. Bad tax policy can be corrected in the next budget cycle. Reckless deregulation of banks can be addressed after the crash. Even foreign policy disasters — wars begun on false pretenses, alliances shattered — can be repaired over time, painfully but genuinely.

Carbon dioxide does not work this way.

Once CO₂ is emitted, approximately 40% of it remains in the atmosphere for centuries. Some persists for millennia. The warming it causes is essentially cumulative — each ton adds to the total heat trapped in the system, and that heat does not dissipate on any timescale meaningful to human civilization. The oceans, which absorb much of the excess heat, release it slowly over decades, creating a thermal lag that means the full warming impact of today’s emissions will not be realized for 20 to 40 years.

This means that the greenhouse gases released because of the Trump administration’s deregulation — the emissions from power plants no longer subject to limits, from vehicles no longer required to meet efficiency standards, from oil and gas operations no longer bound by methane rules — will continue warming the planet well into the 2060s, 2070s, and beyond.

Donald Trump’s youngest son, Barron, born in 2006, will be in his 50s and 60s when the full consequences of his father’s climate policies reach their peak warming effect. Trump’s grandchildren — there are currently ten of them — will live their entire adult lives in a climate measurably worsened by decisions made during these years.

This is not metaphor. This is atmospheric chemistry.

What the Science Projects

Let us be specific about what the inheritance looks like, because the power of the climate denial movement has always rested on vagueness — on the assumption that “warming” sounds gentle, that “a few degrees” sounds trivial, that “by 2060” sounds impossibly distant.

It is none of these things.

Heat. The National Climate Assessment, produced by the U.S. government’s own scientists, projects that without significant emissions reductions, average temperatures in the United States will rise by 3 to 7 degrees Fahrenheit by mid-century. That range sounds modest until you understand what it means in practice. Heat waves that currently occur once every 20 years will occur every two to three years. Cities like Phoenix, Houston, and Miami will experience weeks-long stretches where nighttime temperatures do not drop below 90°F — meaning the human body cannot cool itself through normal thermoregulation. Heat-related deaths, already the leading weather-related killer in the United States, will multiply.

Water. Precipitation patterns are shifting in ways that produce both more drought and more flooding — a paradox that makes perfect sense when you understand that a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture and releases it more violently. The American Southwest is in the grip of a megadrought that paleoclimate data suggests is the worst in at least 1,200 years. The Colorado River, which supplies water to 40 million people across seven states, is shrinking. Lake Mead, the largest reservoir in the country, has dropped to levels not seen since it was first filled in the 1930s.

Fire. Wildfire seasons in the western United States have lengthened by nearly three months since the 1970s. The area burned annually has more than doubled. The fires that devastated Los Angeles in early 2025 — fires that burned through Pacific Palisades and Altadena with a ferocity that stunned even veteran firefighters — are not aberrations. They are the new baseline. And that baseline will worsen as temperatures rise, snowpack diminishes, and vegetation dries earlier each year.

Storms. Warmer ocean temperatures fuel more intense hurricanes. The proportion of Category 4 and 5 hurricanes has increased measurably since the 1980s. Storm surge — the wall of seawater pushed ashore by hurricanes — is amplified by sea level rise, meaning that storms that would have been damaging 30 years ago are now catastrophic. Hurricane Ian, which devastated southwest Florida in 2022, caused over $100 billion in damage. It will not be the last storm of that magnitude. It may not even be the worst this decade.

Sea Level. This is where the inheritance becomes most tangible, most ironic, and most personal to the Trump family.

Five Feet Above Sea Level

Mar-a-Lago, Donald Trump’s private club and de facto second White House, sits on a barrier island in Palm Beach, Florida. Its elevation is approximately five feet above sea level. Five feet.

Global sea level has already risen approximately 8 to 9 inches since 1900. The rate of rise is accelerating — it has more than doubled since 1993. Current projections, depending on emission scenarios, estimate an additional 1 to 4 feet of rise by 2100, with some models projecting up to 6.5 feet if ice sheet dynamics prove more sensitive than expected.

Even the conservative end of those projections creates an existential problem for coastal South Florida. The geology of the region — porous limestone bedrock — means that seawalls are largely ineffective. Water does not just come over the barriers; it comes through the ground. During king tides, the highest tides of the year, streets in Miami Beach and Fort Lauderdale already flood with saltwater bubbling up through storm drains, even on sunny days.

Palm Beach County’s own climate vulnerability assessment identifies flooding, saltwater intrusion into freshwater aquifers, and coastal erosion as the primary near-term threats. By mid-century, much of southeast Florida’s infrastructure — roads, sewage systems, water treatment plants — will face chronic inundation events that make the current king tide flooding look quaint.

Simulated flooding of Mar-a-Lago and surrounding Palm Beach area under higher sea level rise scenarios (e.g., 2-6 feet), depicting the estate partially submerged— a stark visual of the future risk to Trump’s own legacy property.

Mar-a-Lago will not be swallowed by the sea next year, or even next decade. But by the time Trump’s grandchildren are middle-aged, the property will face routine flooding events that would be economically devastating today. By the time their children inherit it — if inheritance of a barrier island property below the projected high-tide line remains viable — the question will not be whether the ocean claims it, but how much it costs to delay the inevitable.

The man who revoked the endangerment finding to protect the fossil fuel industry lives at an elevation that the fossil fuel industry’s emissions are slowly erasing.

If irony were currency, the Trump estate would never need another revenue stream.

The Perfect Accountability Dodge

The climate time-lag creates what may be the most effective accountability evasion in the history of American politics.

Consider the structure. A president takes office, revokes climate regulations, and increases fossil fuel production. The additional emissions enter the atmosphere immediately. But the warming those emissions cause does not peak for decades. By the time the hurricanes are stronger, the droughts are longer, the fires are worse, and the seas are higher, the president who enabled it is out of office — probably out of life.

No one is ever held specifically responsible, because the causal chain between any single policy decision and any single climate event is diffuse enough to provide permanent deniability. “You can’t prove this hurricane was caused by my deregulation” is technically true and entirely beside the point. The contribution is statistical, not individual — like a tobacco company arguing that you cannot prove this cigarette caused this cancer. The tobacco companies made that argument for decades. They were eventually held accountable anyway. But it took 40 years and millions of deaths.

Climate accountability will take longer. The damage is slower, the causal chains are longer, and the responsible parties will be harder to corner — because they will be dead.

This is not an accident. The people who designed the 16-year siege against the endangerment finding understood the time-lag perfectly. It is their greatest strategic asset. They can destroy the regulatory framework today, claim victory today, and leave the consequences to people who have no voice in the decision and no mechanism for redress.

Steven Milloy, the former Trump transition adviser, said it plainly: “I hope we don’t blow it. I don’t know when or if this opportunity will come around again.”

The opportunity he is referring to is the chance to lock in irreversible atmospheric damage while the political conditions are favorable. He is racing against the clock — not the climate clock, but the political one. Because once the damage is done, it cannot be undone. Not by the next president. Not by the one after that. Not by anyone alive today.

What Other Countries’ Children Will Inherit

The United States is the largest cumulative emitter of greenhouse gases in history. That is not a close contest. American emissions since the Industrial Revolution exceed those of China, the current largest annual emitter, by a wide margin when measured cumulatively. This matters because cumulative emissions are what determine the total amount of warming — it is the stock, not the flow, that sets the thermostat.

When the United States abandons climate regulation, the impact is not confined to American territory. It is global. And the cruelest aspect of the climate inheritance is that the people who will suffer most are those who contributed least.

Bangladesh, a country of 170 million people, is responsible for approximately 0.4% of historical global emissions. It faces catastrophic flooding from sea level rise that could displace tens of millions. Small island nations in the Pacific — Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands — face the literal erasure of their countries. Sub-Saharan Africa, which has contributed minimally to global emissions, faces the most severe projected impacts on agriculture, water security, and human health.

These are not abstractions. These are countries with children who will grow up in a climate they did nothing to create, shaped by decisions made in a rowhouse near the U.S. Capitol by four people they will never know, funded by a foundation they have never heard of, enabled by a president who called the whole thing a hoax.

The inheritance is not just American. It is planetary. And it is profoundly, irreversibly unjust.

The Renewable Road Not Taken

In Part 27 of this series, I described the systematic assault on renewable energy that accompanies the destruction of climate regulation. Here, it is worth pausing on what the inheritance could have looked like — the future that was available and was deliberately refused.

As I documented in The Great Energy Challenge: Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies, the tools to decarbonize the American economy are not speculative. They exist. They are, in most cases, already cheaper than the fossil fuel alternatives. Solar, wind, geothermal, advanced nuclear, battery storage, green hydrogen — these are not laboratory curiosities. They are deployed technologies, scaling globally at rates that exceed even the most optimistic projections of a decade ago.

The AI revolution — the single largest new source of electricity demand in a generation — could have been the catalyst for the greatest clean energy buildout in history. Data centers require enormous, reliable power. They could be powered by next-generation nuclear reactors, by solar-plus-storage arrays, by offshore wind. The technology exists. The economics work. The demand is insatiable.

Instead, the Trump administration is ensuring that AI’s energy appetite will be fed primarily by fossil fuels — gas plants, expanded drilling, perhaps even coal. The emissions from powering the AI revolution with fossil fuels rather than clean energy will compound the inheritance. Every large language model trained on gas-fired electricity adds to the atmospheric burden that Trump’s grandchildren will carry.

The road not taken is not a fantasy. It is the road that China, Europe, and much of Asia are already traveling. The United States had the technology, the capital, the workforce, and the demand to lead the clean energy transition. What it lacked — what was deliberately, systematically, secretly removed — was the regulatory framework that made the transition necessary and the political will that made it possible.

The inheritance could have been clean air, energy independence, technological leadership, and a stable climate. Instead, it will be stronger storms, longer droughts, rising seas, and the knowledge that the tools to prevent it all were available and were consciously, documentedly, refused.

The Children Who Cannot Vote

Youth climate activists protesting, holding signs and banners for climate justice (including references to cases like Juliana v. United States)—symbolizing the children and future generations who will inherit the consequences without a vote or voice in today’s decisions.

There is a legal and moral concept that runs through environmental law called the public trust doctrine. In its simplest form, it holds that certain natural resources — water, air, navigable waterways — are held in trust by the government for the benefit of present and future generations. The government, as trustee, has an obligation not to destroy or diminish those resources for short-term gain.

In 2015, a group of young Americans filed a lawsuit called Juliana v. United States, arguing that the federal government’s promotion of fossil fuels violated their constitutional rights by contributing to climate change that would harm them throughout their lives. The case was filed by plaintiffs ranging in age from 8 to 19. They argued, essentially, that the government was breaching its trust to future generations.

The case was ultimately dismissed on standing grounds — the courts ruled that climate policy was a matter for the legislative and executive branches, not the judiciary. But the core argument has never been answered on its merits, because its merits are unanswerable: the people who will bear the greatest burden of today’s climate decisions have no representation in the process that makes them.

Trump’s youngest grandchild cannot vote. Will not be able to vote for years. Has no lobbyist, no Heritage Foundation, no secret $2 million war chest. Has no mechanism whatsoever for influencing the decisions being made right now that will shape the climate of her adult life.

Neither do the children of Bangladesh, or Tuvalu, or the Marshall Islands, or the hundreds of millions of young people across the developing world who will inherit an atmosphere they had no hand in degrading.

The 16-year siege described in Part 27 was waged by adults with power, money, and access, against a future populated by people with none of those things. That is not governance. That is not even negligence. It is a deliberate transfer of consequences from those who benefit to those who cannot object.

The Court They’ve Been Waiting For

There is one more dimension to the inheritance that makes the accountability dodge nearly perfect: the Supreme Court itself has been redesigned for this moment.

When the Court decided Massachusetts v. EPA in 2007, it ruled 5-4 that the Clean Air Act obligated the EPA to act on greenhouse gases. Justice John Paul Stevens wrote that climate change was a grave threat and that the EPA had offered nothing but a “laundry list of reasons not to regulate.” It was the most important environmental law decision in American history.

Chief Justice Roberts dissented. So did Justices Scalia, Thomas, and Alito. Roberts argued that addressing climate change was the function of Congress and the executive branch, not the courts — regardless of whether global warming exists, what causes it, or the extent of the problem.

Here is where the inheritance becomes structural. Of the five justices who ruled in favor of Massachusetts — Stevens, Kennedy, Souter, Ginsburg, and Breyer — not one remains on the Court. Every single seat has turned over. Meanwhile, three of the four dissenters — Roberts, Thomas, and Alito — are still there. And the Court has added three Trump appointees: Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett.

Environmental groups and Democratic-led states have already vowed to challenge the repeal. California’s attorney general called the administration’s actions an assault on environmental protections aimed at putting fossil fuel profits ahead of public health. The Sierra Club’s senior lawyer acknowledged the risk of bringing the case before a more conservative Court but argued that standing by while the EPA destroyed its own authority out of fear of a negative ruling was not a viable strategy. He is right about the courage required. But the environmental movement is now in the position of needing to win before a Court that was specifically constituted to rule against them.

The case will likely move first through the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals, which upheld the original endangerment finding in 2012 and would likely do so again. But the administration is not playing for the D.C. Circuit. It is playing for the nine justices above it.

And here is the cruelest twist for the inheritance: a negative Supreme Court ruling would not merely uphold the repeal — it could make the destruction permanent. As the energy consulting firm Clearview Partners noted, if the Court rules in the EPA’s favor, it would lock in the policy against reversal even in a future Democratic administration. The children who inherit the consequences would find not just the regulations gone, but the legal authority to reinstate them gone as well.

The inheritance, in other words, could become permanent — not because the science changed, not because the threat diminished, but because the Court was reshaped while the planet was warming.

What Accountability Looks Like When the Guilty Are Gone

This is Part 28 of The Trump Accountability Project, and we have arrived at the hardest truth the series has to offer.

For every other entry — the fraud, the tax evasion, the obstruction, the impeachments, the criminal cases, the sexual misconduct, the racism — there is at least a theoretical mechanism of accountability. Courts can convict. Juries can award damages. Congress can impeach. Voters can reject. The mechanisms have largely failed, as this series documents, but they exist.

For climate destruction, the mechanism does not exist. There is no court that will hold Donald Trump accountable in 2060 for the emissions he enabled in 2026. There is no jury that will assess damages against Russell Vought and Jeffrey Clark for drafting the executive orders that dismantled climate regulation. There is no election in which voters can punish Mandy Gunasekara and Jonathan Brightbill for their secret $2 million campaign to kill the endangerment finding.

The guilty will be gone. The damage will remain.

And that is precisely the point. The climate time-lag is not a bug in the accountability system. For the people who engineered this outcome, it is the feature. It is what makes the whole enterprise possible — the knowledge that you can inflict permanent, irreversible, planetary harm and never face a single consequence for it.

In 27 parts, this series has documented how Donald Trump has escaped accountability for fraud, tax evasion, obstruction of justice, sexual assault, election interference, racism, and ongoing grift. In every case, at least one mechanism — legal, electoral, institutional, or social — could theoretically have held him accountable, and in every case, that mechanism failed.

Climate is different. Climate is the one domain where accountability is not merely difficult but structurally impossible within the lifetime of the perpetrators. The feedback loop between action and consequence is too long. The causal chain is too diffuse. And the victims — the primary victims, the ones who will bear the heaviest cost — have not been born yet.

There is, however, one irony the architects did not anticipate. By killing the EPA’s authority to regulate greenhouse gases, they may have removed the very shield that was protecting the fossil fuel industry from direct legal liability. For years, the existence of EPA oversight had blocked many climate lawsuits in federal court — disputes over greenhouse gases had to go through the agency, not the judiciary. Now that the EPA has voluntarily destroyed its own regulatory power, the courthouse doors may have swung open. Climate cases against oil companies, previously confined largely to state courts, may now be brought in federal court as well. And the repeal could force states to build their own patchwork of climate regulations — exactly the kind of regulatory chaos that even the American Petroleum Institute warned against. They wanted surgical removal of vehicle emission standards, not the wholesale demolition of federal climate authority. They got the demolition. The litigation and regulatory fragmentation that follows may prove to be the one form of accountability that the four horsemen did not plan for.

The Bottom Line

Donald Trump is 79 years old. Russell Vought is 49. Jeffrey Clark is 58. Mandy Gunasekara is in her early 40s. Jonathan Brightbill is roughly the same age. Myron Ebell is in his 70s. Steven Milloy is in his 60s.

None of them will live to see the full consequences of what they have done.

Trump’s grandchildren will. Your grandchildren will. My family’s children will. The children of Palm Beach will watch the ocean rise toward their doorsteps. The children of Phoenix will endure summers that test the limits of human survival. The children of California will breathe smoke. The children of coastal Bangladesh will migrate. The children of the Marshall Islands will lose their country.

All of them will inherit a climate shaped, in measurable part, by the decisions of four people in a rowhouse near the Capitol, funded by a foundation that preferred secrecy, enabled by a president who called it all a hoax, and defended by a Supreme Court that stripped the government of the tools to stop it.

The inheritance cannot be declined. It cannot be contested. It cannot be renegotiated.

It can only be lived.

Myron Ebell calls this “total victory.” The children who will inherit the consequences had a different word for it, in the Juliana case.

They called it a betrayal.
