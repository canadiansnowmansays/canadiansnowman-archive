The Trump Grift

Part 8: How Eric Trump’s memoir "Under Siege" inadvertently documents the systematic monetization of the American presidency

October 24, 2025

CanadianSnowmanSays
October 24, 2025

The Art of the Deal: Deliver the Goods

“You can’t con people, at least not for long. You can create excitement, you can do wonderful promotion and get all kinds of press, and you can throw in a little hyperbole. But if you don’t deliver the goods, people will eventually catch on.” —Donald J. Trump, The Art of the Deal

Earlier this month, Senator Chris Murphy released a damning 32-second video documenting what he calls “the most corrupt White House in history.” Using Forbes data, Murphy traces Trump’s systematic wealth extraction: from $2.3 billion in 2024 to an estimated $6+ billion spike when World Liberty Financial opened to public trading in September 2025. The timeline is stark:

  • January 2025: World Liberty Financial launch (+$246M)

  • January 2025: $TRUMP coin launch (+$200M)

  • April 2025: Second memecoin round (+$430M)

  • May 2025: UAE government investment through WLFI (+$60M)

  • September 2025: WLFI public trading (+$5B family windfall)

Just days after Murphy’s video dropped, Eric Trump released his memoir Under Siege: My Family’s Fight to Save Our Nation (October 14, 2025). The timing isn’t coincidental. It’s revealing.

Murphy’s video presents the facts. Eric’s book presents the feelings: “under siege,” “unprecedented opposition,” “attacks” and “lawfare,” “America itself was under siege.” What makes Eric’s book invaluable isn’t just what it admits—it’s what it reveals through omission and framing. When someone systematically profits from public office then writes a book about being “attacked,” every grievance becomes evidence. Eric’s memoir is a masterclass in victimhood rhetoric—positioning systematic corruption as patriotic resistance. Every complaint is a confession. Every grievance is evidence.

Because when you’re running the world’s most sophisticated wealth extraction operation, accountability feels like persecution.

As FBI agents searched Mar-a-Lago on August 8, 2022, Eric Trump watched news helicopters circle overhead. “I joked that the footage ran like an infomercial,” he admits in his memoir, describing the “stunning landscaping, close-up aerial views of the facilities and signature tower, with water glistening on either side.” Within 24 hours, the Trump campaign would shatter fundraising records. Even a federal criminal investigation had become a monetization opportunity.

This moment perfectly encapsulates what Eric Trump has given us in Under Siege: a lengthy confession disguised as a defense. The book, marketed as a response to persecution, instead provides the most detailed insider account of how the Trump family systematically converted the presidency into profit while maintaining the fiction of separation from their business empire. Coming on the heels of Murphy’s video, it reads like the defendant’s own testimony—handing us the evidence we need to connect the dots from 2017 to 2025.

The Handoff That Never Happened

The ‘separation’ announcement that Eric Trump keeps framed in his office. In his memoir, he reveals that during the presidency he ‘traveled the country buying golf courses’ and closed ‘some of our biggest deals.’ (Image from ‘Under Siege’ by Eric Trump)

In Eric Trump’s office hangs a framed newspaper headline: “OK, Boys, Biz Is All Yours,” dated January 12, 2017—eight days before his father’s inauguration. Eric describes this as “the moment I felt the shift had actually happened,” when he took “the burden of the business off my father’s shoulders.”

But the next sentence destroys the illusion: “In Donald Trump’s letter to employees, which we wrote together...”

Together. Eight days before taking the oath of office, Donald Trump was co-writing Trump Organization communications with his son. Eric would become “power of attorney for Donald Trump” and “officer of virtually every legal entity in the portfolio, which numbered in the hundreds.” The separation was theater.

What followed, by Eric’s own admission, was not sacrifice but acceleration. “I traveled the country buying some of the best golf courses, in the best zip codes, at huge discounts,” he writes. “Despite the constant barrage, some of our biggest deals closed during that period.” This blueprint for business-as-usual under the Oval Office’s shadow set the stage for the post-presidency windfalls Murphy documents—from memecoins to UAE-backed crypto schemes.

The Template: From Company Driver to Middle East Envoy

Hidden within Eric’s corporate hagiography is something more damning: the blueprint for converting personal loyalty into government power. He celebrates the story of Brian Baudreau, who rose from Trump Organization driver to general manager of their Las Vegas hotel:

“When we needed someone to run the building, Brian’s hand was back up. ‘I know nothing about running a hotel, but I also knew nothing about building a sixty-four-story building, or banks, or architects… but we made it all happen.’”

Eric presents this as inspirational—loyalty and enthusiasm trumping expertise. But this wasn’t just a corporate philosophy. It became the template for government appointments. Dan Scavino went from golf club manager to White House Communications Director. Brad Parscale, a website contractor Eric met on a plane, became campaign manager. And Steve Witkoff, praised by Eric for his “steadfast loyalty” attending Trump’s Manhattan trial, would be appointed Special Envoy to the Middle East, with his son joining the Treasury Department.

The pattern is explicit: demonstrate loyalty to the family business, receive government power. Eric documents it proudly, apparently unaware he’s describing a corruption pipeline—one that funneled foreign investments like the UAE’s WLFI stake straight back to the family.

Every Crisis, A Revenue Stream

Throughout “Under Siege,” Eric reveals how the Trump family transformed each legal challenge into a fundraising bonanza. The Mar-a-Lago raid generated “more money in the past 24 hours than ever before in recent history!” The mug shot brought in $7.1 million in three days. “Two years later we’d break another fundraising record, based on one of the sham trials in New York City.”

Even Eric’s charity work followed this model. Describing his St. Jude fundraising, he admits: “We could utilize those golf facilities free of charge, or at cost, subsidize our expenses and labor, recruit our members, and call in favors from our vendors.” The charity’s impressive 9.2% expense ratio was achieved by subsidizing it with Trump Organization assets—enhancing the family brand while claiming altruism.

This crisis-to-cash playbook didn’t end with the presidency. It evolved into the 2025 crypto surges Murphy highlights, where every “attack” on Trump became another pump for $TRUMP coins and WLFI shares.

The International Shell Game

What Eric refuses to discuss is as revealing as what he admits. Despite repeatedly referencing “amazing projects overseas” and international success, he provides zero specifics about foreign deals during or after the presidency. This glaring omission becomes more significant when contrasted with documented evidence of over $15 billion in foreign government investments in Trump properties post-presidency.

The only international detail that slips through? An admission in the later chapters about France: When Trump threatened 25% tariffs on French wines—while the Trump Organization owned Virginia’s largest vineyard—Eric felt compelled to deny it was “a ploy to boost sales of our wine.” The very fact he raises and dismisses the question reveals his awareness of the conflict. In a book full of “siege” stories, the silence on foreign cash flows screams complicity.

The Cryptocurrency Confession

Buried near the very end of his book, Eric drops a bombshell in a single sentence: “When we lost our banking, we quickly became a leader in cryptocurrency.”

That’s it. No details, no timeline, no explanation of how a real estate company pivoted to become a crypto “leader.” This stunning admission—which aligns with documented evidence of Trump’s $5 billion gain from a single cryptocurrency launch—receives less attention than Eric’s complaints about banking discrimination.

The timing is crucial. The “de-banking” Eric describes occurred in 2021, after January 6th. Major institutions including Chase, Goldman Sachs, and Signature Bank closed hundreds of Trump Organization accounts. Eric presents this as persecution. But transforming from real estate to cryptocurrency “leader” in three years isn’t persecution—it’s pivoting to an unregulated market where presidential connections and publicity matter more than traditional banking relationships.

Thriving Through “Siege”

Perhaps the most damning thread running through Eric’s narrative is how well the Trump Organization performed during this supposed persecution. By February 2024, Eric emailed supporters: “We are the strongest we have ever been financially.” Trump himself posted about having “almost five hundred million dollars in cash.” They had “paid down virtually all of our debt” and completed “greatest real estate transactions this decade.”

This is not the language of sacrifice. It’s the language of a business that found new profit centers—from fundraising off prosecutions to pivoting to cryptocurrency—while maintaining the fiction of victimization.

The Projection Pattern

Throughout “Under Siege,” Eric repeatedly accuses others of exactly what his own narrative proves the Trump family did. He rails against politicians who “enter government broke and leave wealthy beyond imagination,” while documenting his family’s exponential wealth growth. He attacks the Clinton Foundation while his own foundation was shut down under prosecution. He claims Democrats “weaponized” government while celebrating how personal loyalty to Trump translated into government appointments.

Most tellingly, he accuses others of using their positions for profit while simultaneously bragging about the Trump Organization’s success during the presidency, their pivot to cryptocurrency after banking troubles, and their ability to monetize every legal challenge they faced.

The Ultimate Grift

Eric Trump set out to write a defense against charges that his family profited from the presidency. Instead, he produced a confession. In his own words, with his own timeline, using his own examples, he documents:

  • Active business expansion during the presidency

  • The systematic conversion of personal loyalty into government appointments

  • The monetization of every investigation and prosecution

  • The use of charitable work to enhance the family brand

  • The pivot to cryptocurrency when traditional banking abandoned them

  • Thriving finances during supposed persecution

The book itself represents the final evolution of the Trump grift: charging $32 to read an admission of guilt while claiming victimhood. Eric wants credit for the success while denying the corruption that enabled it. He wants sympathy for the investigations while bragging about profiting from them.

But perhaps most revealing is what Eric won’t discuss: the specific foreign deals, the cryptocurrency details, the $15 billion in documented foreign investments. After wading through hundreds of pages, the most damning details merit only single sentences or silence. Even in confession, the full scope of the grift remains hidden.

Donald Trump once wrote in The Art of the Deal: “You can’t con people, at least not for long... if you don’t deliver the goods, people will eventually catch on.” Eric Trump has now proven something more troubling: they can document their corruption in their own words, publish it for profit, and still claim persecution. The grift hasn’t ended. It’s simply evolved to include selling the evidence.

In the end, Eric Trump has given us what no investigation could: a family member’s account of how the presidency became a business accelerator, how loyalty became currency, and how even criminal investigations became profit centers. He meant to write a defense. Instead, he wrote a confession.

The marks just have to pay $32 to read it.
