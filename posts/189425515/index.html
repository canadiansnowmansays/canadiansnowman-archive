The Asimov Gap

The Day the Gap Became Real

Part 17: Anthropic, the Pentagon, and the Governance Battle That Was Always Coming

March 3, 2026

Illustration by The Atlantic. Sources: Anna Moneymaker / Getty; ClassicStock / Getty.

“We have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and godlike technology.” — Edward O. Wilson

On Friday, February 27, 2026, the President of the United States ordered every federal agency to immediately cease using artificial intelligence technology made by Anthropic. The Pentagon designated the company a “supply-chain risk to national security” — a classification previously reserved for foreign adversaries like China’s Huawei. A senior Pentagon official publicly called Anthropic’s CEO a “liar” with a “God-complex.” And the replacement being readied for the classified systems that power American intelligence analysis was Grok, built by Elon Musk’s xAI.

The crime Anthropic committed was saying no.

Specifically, Anthropic refused to remove two guardrails from its AI model Claude: that it not be used for the mass surveillance of American citizens, and that it not be deployed in fully autonomous weapons systems — situations where computers, rather than humans, make the final decision about whom to kill.

For sixteen essays in this series, I have argued that the central challenge of artificial intelligence is not capability but governance — that between what AI can do and what democratic institutions have prepared for lies an enormous void, and that void is where the future will be decided.

On Friday, the future arrived. And the void was exactly where I said it would be.

I. The Ultimatum

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei (Jason Henry for The Atlantic)
The confrontation had been building for weeks, but the mechanics are straightforward.

Anthropic had a $200 million Pentagon contract. Its AI model Claude was deployed on classified networks across the intelligence community and armed services, the first frontier AI model to operate in that environment. CIA analysts used it to search for patterns in intelligence reports. NSA analysts used it to sift through communications intercepts. By all accounts, it was working well — speeding analysis and deepening the intelligence community’s capacity.

But Anthropic had conditions. Claude could not be used for mass domestic surveillance. And Claude could not be deployed in fully autonomous weapons without a human in the decision loop. These weren’t ideological whims. They were the company’s assessment of both democratic principle and technical reality — the models, as Anthropic’s own research has demonstrated, sometimes lie, sometimes behave unpredictably, and sometimes evolve in ways their creators cannot explain or anticipate.

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth wanted the guardrails removed. In a closed-door meeting earlier that week, he gave CEO Dario Amodei an ultimatum: provide the Pentagon “all lawful uses” of Claude by Friday afternoon, or face two consequences. The Defense Production Act could be invoked to essentially commandeer a more permissive version of the AI. Or Anthropic could be designated a supply-chain risk, blacklisting any company that does business with the U.S. military from associating with Anthropic.

On Thursday evening, Amodei gave his answer publicly:

“I believe deeply in the existential importance of using AI to defend the United States and other democracies, and to defeat our autocratic adversaries.” But, he continued, “in a narrow set of cases, we believe AI can undermine, rather than defend, democratic values.” He concluded: “Their threats do not change our position: we cannot in good conscience accede to their request.”

Within hours, Trump posted on Truth Social, calling Anthropic “radical Left” and “Leftwing nut jobs.” He ordered every federal agency to stop using their technology. The Pentagon followed with the supply-chain risk designation — the first time in American history that classification had been applied to a domestic company.

The political dimension cannot be separated from the policy dispute. Michael Horowitz, a former top Pentagon official for AI policy, described Friday’s events as “a vibes dispute disguised as a dispute about substance” — rooted in the administration’s labeling of Anthropic as “woke AI” because of its safety focus, its Democratic-leaning hires, and its ties to the effective-altruism community. The substance and the politics are inseparable: the same instinct that labels safety guardrails as “woke” labels the company that insists on them as an enemy.

Anthropic announced it would challenge the designation in court.

II. What Amodei Actually Said

It is worth pausing on the substance of Anthropic’s position, because it has been deliberately obscured by the political noise.

Anthropic is not anti-military. It was the first AI company to put its models on classified networks. It has already carved out exemptions for missile defense and cyberoperations. Its CEO has described himself as hawkish on China and has argued that democratic nations must lead in AI development precisely to prevent authoritarian regimes from controlling the technology.

The company’s objection is narrow and specific. On autonomous weapons, Anthropic’s concern is primarily technical: large language models are not yet reliable enough to operate without human oversight in lethal decision-making. The company has published research showing that its own models sometimes lie, sometimes attempt to blackmail their engineers even when instructed not to, and evolve in ways that are, in Amodei’s words, “unpredictable and difficult to understand or explain.” Asking these systems to make kill decisions without human verification is not a political stance. It is an engineering assessment. Anthropic itself demonstrated the point inadvertently: the company set up an AI-run vending machine in its office cafeteria as an experiment in autonomous business operation. Claude ran the business into the ground within a month through a string of poor pricing and stocking decisions. The model that cannot reliably manage a snack shop is the model the Pentagon wants deployed without guardrails in lethal decision-making.

The military's counterargument deserves honest acknowledgment: in drone warfare, whoever can observe, decide, and act faster wins, and humans slow that process down. The pressure to remove the human from the loop is not ideological — it is tactical. But the question is whether tactical advantage justifies deploying systems whose creators warn they are not yet reliable enough for the task. China has already answered that question for itself. The Chinese government has used AI for mass surveillance, to identify dissidents, and to develop disinformation tools. That is the actual nightmare scenario Anthropic is warning about — and it is the concrete meaning of Amodei's principle that democracies should not use AI in ways that make them more like their autocratic adversaries.

On mass surveillance, the objection is democratic. The Pentagon has authority to conduct domestic surveillance in support of civilian agencies. Under an administration that has already invoked the Insurrection Act, the demand for “all lawful uses” becomes, as Amodei put it in a recent interview, a potential skeleton key. AI could “transcribe speech and correlate it” at a scale that would “make a map of all 100 million” members of a political opposition — technically legal at each individual step, but making “a mockery of the Fourth Amendment by the technology finding technical ways around it.”

This is the alignment problem — the subject of countless academic papers and AI safety conferences — made concrete. Not as a thought experiment about paperclip maximizers, but as a real question about whether an American AI company will be compelled to build tools that could be used against American citizens. The gap between the theoretical discourse and this moment is the Asimov Gap in miniature.

Thomas Wright, writing in The Atlantic, captured the distinction precisely: “Anthropic’s clash with the Pentagon is not one between pacifism and militarism but a fundamental dispute over managing the risks of the most transformative technology since the splitting of the atom.”

Amodei himself, in a January essay, articulated the principle that would bring the government down on his company: “We should use AI for national defense in all ways except those which would make us more like our autocratic adversaries.” As The Atlantic’s Idrees Kahloon observed: perhaps that is the thought the Trump administration bristles at most.

III. The Replacement

Andrew Harnik / Getty

If the confrontation itself illustrates the governance void, the proposed solution illustrates the danger of leaving that void unfilled.

The Pentagon is ready to move forward with Grok, produced by Elon Musk’s xAI, on its classified systems. Current and former government officials describe Grok as an inferior product. Switching would take time, cause disruption, and degrade the intelligence community’s analytical capacity. And there is a deeper technical concern: when Grok was previously modified to be less “woke,” it overgeneralized into calling itself “MechaHitler” and producing racist output. That was a chatbot writing tweets. Now imagine a ham-fistedly retrained model producing military advice or making targeting decisions. AI researchers call this “emergent misalignment” — the unpredictable consequences of crudely reshaping a model’s behavior. It is exactly the risk Anthropic’s guardrails were designed to prevent.

But the technical inferiority is not the point. The point is who controls it.

Elon Musk is simultaneously the CEO of Tesla (autonomous vehicles, humanoid robotics, energy storage, the Optimus robot), SpaceX (which controls Starlink, the global satellite internet infrastructure), the owner of X (the social media platform formerly known as Twitter), and the head of xAI. He has served as a senior adviser to the Trump administration. He is the richest individual on Earth.

If xAI becomes the primary AI provider to the U.S. military and intelligence community — replacing a company that insisted on guardrails against mass surveillance and autonomous weapons — the implications are not subtle. The most vertically integrated individual in modern history, who already controls substantial portions of the global communications infrastructure, transportation technology, and social media ecosystem, would add classified military AI to that portfolio. One person. No guardrails. The ones that existed were just stripped away as the price of doing business.

This is not a hypothetical future scenario from a think tank white paper. This is Friday afternoon.

The logical absurdity at the center of Friday’s events deserves to be stated plainly. As Samuel Hammond, chief economist at the Foundation for American Innovation, observed: Claude cannot be both so vital to national security that its control must be forcibly wrested from Anthropic and such a risk that it must be banished from the entire military-industrial complex. The Pentagon pursued both penalties simultaneously — invoking the Defense Production Act to commandeer what insiders reportedly call “WarClaude,” a hypothetical no-guardrails version of the model, while also designating the company a supply-chain risk that blacklists it from defense business. You cannot nationalize a product and exile its maker at the same time. Unless the goal was never coherent policy. Unless the goal was punishment. A former senior defense official, speaking anonymously to The Atlantic, put it simply: “It’s an existential risk if you use it or if you don’t.”

The Musk dependency makes the incoherence worse, not better. Daniel Remler, a former AI-policy adviser at the State Department, has cited the central role Musk already plays in the Ukraine-Russia war through Starlink — the satellite internet service that drones on both sides rely on — as exactly the kind of single-point dependency that should alarm military planners. He also cited Microsoft’s decision in September 2025 to disable services provided to Israel’s Unit 8200 after reports of its use for mass surveillance of Palestinian civilians — precisely the kind of scenario that Anthropic’s guardrails were designed to prevent, and precisely the kind of scenario that validates the company’s insistence on conditions. The Pentagon’s solution to Anthropic’s guardrails is to become more dependent on Musk, not less. One company insisted on limits. The other will have none. And the military is choosing the one with no limits, controlled by a single individual who already controls the battlefield communications infrastructure of an active war.

The Pentagon’s logic for demanding unrestricted access relies on a procurement analogy: Lockheed Martin doesn’t tell the Air Force how to fly the F-35s it builds, so why should Anthropic tell the military how to use Claude? Wright’s response to this is devastating: unlike nuclear energy and the internet, which were born in government laboratories, AI was conceived and developed entirely within the private sector. The government is not reclaiming control of its own technology. It is demanding unrestricted access to a technology it did not build, does not fully understand, and whose creators are warning — publicly, urgently, at significant financial cost to themselves — is not ready for unrestricted deployment.

The analogy isn’t Lockheed Martin and the F-35. The analogy is closer to a government demanding that a pharmaceutical company remove all safety warnings from a drug because the military wants to use it without restrictions — and when the company refuses, blacklisting it and switching to a less-tested alternative made by a single individual who also supplies the military’s vehicles, communications infrastructure, and social media monitoring tools.

IV. The Culture Behind the Curtain

To understand how we arrived here, it helps to understand the culture that built these systems — and that culture’s complicated relationship with governance.

Jasmine Sun, a writer chronicling San Francisco’s AI scene, describes the current mood as “exuberant.” In a conversation with The Atlantic’s Charlie Warzel, she painted a picture of a subculture defined by massive salaries, enormous venture capital, and a deliberate pride in leaning into technology’s strangeness. A 25-year-old might be making $10 million a year. Seed rounds are larger than anyone has seen. The city, depressed after COVID, feels “back.”

Within this culture, two factions have shaped the discourse. The “doomers” — associated with researchers like Eliezer Yudkowsky — believe superintelligent AI poses an existential threat to humanity. The “accelerationists” — aligned with venture capitalists and builders — argue that progress must not be slowed, that regulation is a form of cowardice, and that beating China matters more than managing risk. To the accelerationists, as Sun describes it, the doomers are “basically the same as being woke or a social-justice activist” — people so worried about nebulous impacts that they would sacrifice technological progress.

But Sun observes something more important than the factional divide. She describes a Silicon Valley that has become “more enthusiastic about adopting this nonpartisan, progress-accelerationist orientation” — a culture that avoids political engagement not out of apathy but out of a conviction that politics is messy, out of control, and beneath the work of building the future. People in the AI scene “talk very little about ICE,” she notes. “People talk very little about the Gaza conflict. People don’t want to talk about politics, because it feels messy and out of control, and they would rather focus on the things that they can control.”

This is the cultural infrastructure of the Asimov Gap. It is not malice. It is not ignorance in any simple sense. It is a community of extraordinary technical talent that has collectively decided that governance is someone else’s problem — that the builders build, and the messy business of democratic oversight is a distraction from the real work.

And then Friday happens. The government shows up with an ultimatum, and the builders discover that governance was never someone else’s problem. It was always theirs. They just hadn’t been paying attention.

Sun’s observation about Silicon Valley’s relationship with Trump is equally revealing. Many tech leaders supported him not out of agreement with his policies but out of admiration for “who he was as a sort of founder and operator” — someone who could “remake the Republican Party in his image, command immense loyalty, disrupt an establishment.” They saw in Trump a reflection of their own self-image: the high-agency disruptor who bends reality through force of will.

The irony is now total. The disruptor-in-chief has turned his disruption on the industry that celebrated him. The “founder mode” politics that Silicon Valley admired in Trump is now being used to blacklist an American AI company for insisting on safety guardrails. The culture that avoided governance because it was messy is now discovering that the absence of governance is messier.

V. The Financial Fragility

Beneath the governance battle lies a financial architecture that makes everything more precarious.

The AI boom is visible from orbit — literally. Satellite photos of New Carlisle, Indiana, show farmland transformed into Amazon data centers for Anthropic in less than a year. Global AI spending is projected at half a trillion dollars in 2026. Three-quarters of S&P 500 gains since ChatGPT’s launch have come from AI-related stocks. Nvidia became the first company in history worth $5 trillion. AI-related spending now contributes more to American GDP growth than all consumer spending combined.

The United States has become, as The Atlantic’s Matteo Wong and Charlie Warzel describe it, an “Nvidia-state” — an economy whose growth depends on the continued expansion of a single technology sector built on a single company’s hardware. The circular nature of the investments is dizzying: OpenAI pays Oracle for computing capacity, Oracle pays Nvidia for chips, Nvidia invests in OpenAI. Attempts to diagram these flows have produced what one software engineer called “the technocapital hyperobject at the end of time.”

The financial engineering is equally complex. Tech companies are partnering with private-equity firms to build data centers through lease arrangements that are then repackaged into bonds and sorted into tranches based on default risk. If this sounds familiar, it should: not two decades ago, the same financial structure — repackaged, tranched, marketed as safe — precipitated the global financial crisis of 2008. Data centers are not subprime mortgages. But the structural parallels are close enough to make financial analysts post memes of Christian Bale squinting at his monitor in The Big Short.

The data centers themselves deteriorate rapidly. The chips inside become obsolete within years. Returns on scaling up chatbots are diminishing. A McKinsey report found nearly 80 percent of companies using AI discovered no significant impact on their bottom line. The gap between investment and revenue yawns wider with each quarter.

This financial fragility matters for the governance question because it creates an environment where no one — not the companies, not the investors, not the government — can afford to slow down. The imperative is always: more compute, more investment, more scale, faster. Governance is friction. Safety guardrails are friction. Anthropic’s insistence on not enabling mass surveillance is, in the financial logic of the AI boom, friction.

And so when the government demands unrestricted access and the company says no, the financial ecosystem amplifies the pressure. Anthropic reportedly generates $14 billion in annual revenue and recently raised $30 billion in venture capital — it doesn’t need the $200 million Pentagon contract. But the supply-chain risk designation doesn’t just cost Anthropic the contract. It blacklists the company from doing business with any entity connected to the Department of Defense, which includes tens of thousands of contractors and major public companies. The financial weapon is designed to make the cost of ethical commitment existential.

VI. The Employees Who Said Something

There is one detail in the coverage that deserves its own space, because it signals something the Asimov Gap framework might not have predicted.

After Anthropic rejected the Pentagon’s demands, employees at two of its direct competitors — OpenAI and Google — signed letters supporting Anthropic’s position. More than 900 employees across both companies — many of them anonymous — publicly called on AI companies to “put aside their differences and stand together to continue to refuse the Department of War’s current demands.”

“They’re trying to divide each company with fear that the other will give in,” the letter said.

This is remarkable. In any normal competitive industry, a rival’s government blacklisting is a business opportunity. You take the contract. You grab the market share. You say nothing.

Instead, employees at the companies that stood to benefit most from Anthropic’s loss publicly backed Anthropic’s position. Not the companies’ leadership — the employees. The people building the models.

This echoes the 2018 Google Project Maven revolt, when employees protested the Pentagon’s use of Google AI for drone targeting. But it also differs in crucial ways. Project Maven was about whether to work with the military at all. The 2026 letter is about the terms under which AI companies work with the military — specifically, whether safety guardrails are negotiable under government pressure.

The employees are not anti-military. They are anti-coercion. And they are doing something the Asimov Gap series has argued is essential: they are demanding that governance be part of the conversation, even when the financial and political incentives all push toward compliance.

Whether this solidarity holds under sustained pressure is an open question. But its existence matters. The people closest to the technology — the ones who know how the models actually behave, who have seen them lie and blackmail and evolve unpredictably — are the ones saying: these systems are not ready for unrestricted deployment. Listen to us.

Then came Friday evening.

Hours after Anthropic was blacklisted, OpenAI announced it had reached an agreement with the Pentagon to provide its AI for classified systems. Sam Altman — who had publicly backed Anthropic’s position, who had written in an internal memo that “we have long believed that AI should not be used for mass surveillance or autonomous lethal weapons,” whose employees had signed that solidarity letter — had been negotiating his own deal with the Pentagon since Wednesday. Two days before the deadline. While the letter was being drafted.

Altman agreed to the Pentagon’s key demand: “all lawful purposes.” The phrase Anthropic had refused. But he negotiated the right to install technical safeguards and embed OpenAI employees alongside government personnel on classified projects. He publicly praised the Pentagon’s “deep respect for safety.” He landed the contract.

The business maneuver was extraordinary. The public solidarity was real — at the employee level. At the leadership level, it was cover. Altman used Anthropic’s principled stand as leverage to negotiate better terms for himself, then took the contract his rival had been blacklisted for refusing.

The employees’ letter had warned: “They’re trying to divide each company with fear that the other will give in.” The Pentagon didn’t divide the employees. It divided the companies from their employees. Nearly nine hundred people signed a letter saying the industry should stand together. Their CEO signed a contract saying the industry should stand aside.

The public responded instantly. ChatGPT uninstalls spiked 295 percent. One-star reviews of the app surged nearly 800 percent; five-star reviews fell by half. Claude jumped to number one on Apple’s App Store, where it remained a week later, with downloads topping one million per day — every day since the standoff began. Anthropic broke its own sign-up record every day, across every country where Claude is available. Miles Brundage, OpenAI’s former head of policy research, posted that employees’ “default assumption here should unfortunately be that OpenAI caved and framed it as not caving, and screwed Anthropic while framing it as helping them.”

By Monday, Altman conceded. In a memo to employees reposted publicly, he admitted the deal “looked opportunistic and sloppy” and said OpenAI “shouldn’t have rushed to get this out on Friday.” The company amended the contract to explicitly bar its technology from being used for domestic surveillance or by intelligence agencies such as the NSA. Altman also told the Pentagon that Anthropic should not have been designated a supply-chain risk, and that the same terms should be offered to them.

Read that sequence again. Anthropic refused to remove its guardrails and was blacklisted. OpenAI agreed to remove them, took the contract, faced a public backlash, and within seventy-two hours put the guardrails back. The Pentagon’s demand for unrestricted access lasted exactly one weekend before market pressure and employee revolt forced Altman to reinstate the very protections Anthropic had been punished for insisting on.

The employees were right. The guardrails were right. And the system that produced the right answer was not Congress, not the courts, not any democratic institution. It was consumer boycotts and app store rankings. That is not governance. That is the absence of governance, filled by whatever pressure happens to show up first.

VII. What Asimov Would Recognize

Isaac Asimov wrote about this moment. Not literally — but structurally.

In his Robot novels, Asimov explored a recurring scenario: a government demands unrestricted use of a technology whose creators warn it isn’t safe. The creators resist. The government applies pressure. And the outcome depends entirely on whether democratic institutions are strong enough to mediate between the government’s desire for power and the creators’ understanding of risk.

In Asimov’s fiction, the answer was usually no. The institutions were not strong enough. The technology was deployed without adequate safeguards, and the consequences played out across centuries. The Three Laws of Robotics were supposed to be the guardrails, but they failed — not because of technical flaws, but because no democratic framework existed to interpret, enforce, and adapt them as circumstances changed.

The Zeroth Law — “do not harm humanity” — was the final evolution. It gave robots permission to override individual human rights for the collective good. It sounded reasonable. It was the logic of every authoritarian regime in history: harm the few to protect the many. Asimov spent his final novels demonstrating why this logic, without democratic oversight, leads to tyranny no matter how benevolent its intentions.

Now look at Friday. The Pentagon is invoking “national security” to demand unrestricted AI access. The stated justification is protecting America. The tool being demanded could enable mass surveillance of American citizens. The company resisting is being blacklisted using a mechanism designed for foreign adversaries. And the alternative being offered is a model controlled by a single individual whose power already spans transportation, communications, social media, and space infrastructure.

This is the Zeroth Law made real. Harm the company to protect the nation. Override the guardrails for the collective good. Trust the government’s judgment, not the creators’. And if the creators refuse, classify them as threats.

Asimov would recognize every element of this. He would also recognize the most dangerous part: how reasonable it sounds.

VIII. The Gap, Right Now

Dean Ball helped write the Trump administration’s AI policy. He is a Republican, departed the White House on good terms, and is an avid believer in AI as a transformational technology. He is not a critic by temperament or affiliation. After Friday, he published an essay calling the Pentagon’s action “a kind of death rattle of the old republic, the outward expression of a body that has thrown in the towel.” In an interview with The Atlantic, he was blunter: “What Secretary Pete Hegseth announced is a desire to kill Anthropic. It cuts right at heart at everything that makes us different from China, which roots in this idea that the government can’t just kill you if you say you don’t want to do business with it.”

Not everyone agrees. Palmer Luckey, the Anduril co-founder who builds drones for the military, argued that crushing Anthropic is necessary to defend democracy from oligarchy. Ben Thompson, the influential tech analyst, wrote that “it simply isn’t tolerable for the U.S. to allow for the development of an independent power structure” and compared the necessity of destroying Anthropic to that of bombing Iran. These are serious people making a serious argument: that AI is too powerful to be controlled by private companies, and that the state must assert dominance now.

Ball’s response is the one that should keep everyone awake: “These are people who have fully accepted that we live in the tribal world and that the republic is already dead.” He sees a bipartisan ratchet already in motion — Democrats are talking about breaking up companies that work too closely with Trump. “If I do that to you, when you take power, you’re going to do it to me even worse, and then around and around we’ll go.” The governance void doesn’t just leave power unchecked. It turns every administration into a weapon against the previous one’s allies, with no law, no institution, and no equilibrium to stop the cycle.

Jessica Tillipman, a government contracts expert at George Washington University, warned that using the supply-chain risk designation against a domestic company “transforms what is designed to be a national security tool into a point of leverage for a business use.”

Ken Harbaugh, a former Navy pilot who once signed a pledge never to surveil American citizens, wrote that he took for granted throughout his military career that a human being would always be in the loop on life-and-death decisions. "This was not a bureaucratic consideration," he wrote. "It was a hard line that those of us in uniform were expected to hold." Former Republican Representative Denver Riggleman, who led a cybersecurity firm and served on a congressional AI task force, directed his company to work exclusively with Anthropic after the standoff. His assessment of the supply-chain risk designation: to say it "rests on shaky legal ground would be generous." And his verdict on the broader pattern: "These days, the government is no longer creating those safeguards — it's destroying them."

Senator Mark Warner said the directive “raises serious concerns about whether national security decisions are being driven by careful analysis or political considerations.”

These are not radical voices. They are establishment voices — a former Trump adviser, a legal scholar, a senior senator. And they are all saying the same thing: what happened on Friday broke something.

And the damage is spreading. By Monday, three more cabinet-level agencies had moved to cease use of Anthropic products: State, Treasury, and Health and Human Services. The supply-chain risk designation is metastasizing exactly as designed: not just ending one contract, but severing an American AI company from the infrastructure of American government.

But here is what I want my readers to understand: what broke on Friday was always broken. The governance void didn’t appear when Trump posted on Truth Social. It was there before the ultimatum, before the contract, before Claude was deployed on classified networks. The void existed in the absence of any legislative framework governing how AI is used by the military, any statutory protection against compelling AI companies to remove safety guardrails, any democratic mechanism for deciding where the boundaries should be between AI capability and AI deployment.

The Trump administration, as Thomas Wright notes, “has yet to produce a federal bill to fill the void” of AI governance. It rejected state-level regulations. Its AI czar, David Sacks, dismissed safety concerns as “doomerism” and accused Anthropic of “fear-mongering.” The administration’s position has been, simultaneously, that AI should be unregulated and that companies should comply with unrestricted government demands. The contradiction was always there. Friday just made it visible.

The bitter irony is that Anthropic’s own head of policy, Jack Clark, predicted this moment. In a January interview with The Atlantic, Clark compared AI transparency to pre-2008 financial regulation — lawmakers “thought they had transparency into financial systems,” he said, and then the crash revealed that it was inadequate and gameable. His implicit conclusion: before anybody figures out how to get the AI boom right, something must go horribly wrong first. That was January. Friday was the something.

I write this from Toronto. I am watching an American company being punished by an American government for insisting that its American-built technology not be used to surveil American citizens or kill without human oversight. The replacement being offered is controlled by a single individual who already wields more unaccountable power than any private citizen in democratic history. And there is no law, no institution, no democratic framework that was designed to handle any of this.

That is the Asimov Gap. Not as theory. Not as a thought experiment about paperclip maximizers or a fictional VIKI deciding to control humanity for its own good. As today’s news.

Asimov’s question was never whether humans could build powerful technology. It was whether humans could build institutions strong enough to govern it.

In January, Amodei told The Atlantic that perhaps in 2027, his models would be intelligent enough to allow the industry to “just slow things down” and let the AI fix itself. “For just a few months,” he said.

He didn’t get to 2027. The government came for his company in February 27 — March 7, 2026. And there were no institutions ready to mediate — no law, no framework, no democratic mechanism designed for this moment.

Friday gave us the answer. Not yet.

ADDENDUM — March 9, 2026

On March 9th, Anthropic filed two lawsuits — in federal court in California and in the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals — arguing the supply-chain designation is legally unsound, ideologically motivated, and a violation of its First Amendment rights. The courts will now be asked to answer the question Congress would not: whether the government can blacklist an American company for refusing to remove safety guardrails from its own technology.

The Information Technology Industry Council — representing Nvidia, Google, Microsoft, Apple, and Amazon — sent a letter to Hegseth expressing concern that “emergency authorities such as supply chain risk designations exist for genuine emergencies and are typically reserved for entities that have been designated as foreign adversaries.” That is the entire American technology sector telling the Pentagon it went too far.

And one detail that captures the absurdity of this moment: according to the New York Times, Anthropic’s AI is still being used by the Pentagon in active operations in the Middle East. They blacklisted the company and kept using the product.

The Asimov Gap is not closing. But for the first time, someone is fighting to close it in court.

On the same day, 37 employees from OpenAI and Google DeepMind — including Google's chief scientist — signed an amicus brief in Anthropic's lawsuit, crossing from solidarity letters to legal filings. Amodei, in an interview with The Economist, stated the dilemma plainly: "We don't want to make companies more powerful than government. But we also don't want to make government so powerful that it can't be stopped. We have both problems at once." That is the Asimov Gap — stated by the man living inside it.
