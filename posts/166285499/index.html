Trump's Foreign Policy

Episode 4: The Authoritarian Dividend: How Putin, Xi, and Khamenei Are Cashing In

June 20, 2025

Stephen Lawton June 20, 2025

The most chilling aspect of Trump's foreign policy chaos isn't what America is losing—it's what our adversaries are gaining. While Trump sits at the Resolute Desk making impulsive decisions about Ukraine and Iran, Putin, Xi Jinping, and even Iran's leadership are executing patient, coordinated strategies to permanently replace American global leadership. They're not just exploiting American weakness; they're building lasting alternatives to the world America made.

Bottom Line Up Front: While Trump stumbles from Ukraine abandonment to Iran escalation, authoritarian leaders are methodically constructing alternative global governance structures. This isn't opportunistic exploitation—it's strategic coordination to permanently replace American-led international order with authoritarian alternatives.

Putin's Perfect Game: From Pariah to Peacemaker

Vladimir Putin has achieved something that seemed impossible three years ago: rehabilitation from international pariah to essential diplomatic partner. The recent U.S.-Russia talks in Saudi Arabia, which excluded Ukraine despite the war being fought on Ukrainian territory, represent Putin's complete diplomatic victory over American strategic thinking.

Putin's method has been masterful in its simplicity. He launched an unprovoked war against a democratic neighbor, absorbed international sanctions and isolation, then waited for American attention to wander. Trump's abandonment of Ukraine and pivot to Iran threats provided exactly the distraction Putin needed to consolidate territorial gains while being welcomed back to international diplomacy as a necessary partner for "peace."

The strategic implications extend far beyond Ukraine. Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov's warning that direct U.S. military support for Israel "would radically destabilize the entire situation" positions Russia as the voice of restraint while America contemplates military escalation. This represents complete role reversal: the aggressor counseling moderation to the traditional defender of international law.

Putin has simultaneously achieved territorial expansion in Ukraine, diplomatic rehabilitation internationally, and positioning as a regional peacemaker—all while America fragments its attention across multiple crisis theaters without strategic coherence. Most importantly, he's demonstrated that patient authoritarianism can outlast democratic attention spans and strategic consistency.

Xi's Strategic Patience: The Long Game Pays Off

Xi Jinping's approach has been even more sophisticated than Putin's direct aggression. While Putin seized territory, Xi has been building alternative institutions that may permanently replace American global leadership. The Belt and Road Initiative, Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, and Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership represent infrastructure for a world where Chinese leadership substitutes for American reliability.

Trump's Ukraine abandonment and Iran escalation threats provide perfect validation for Chinese arguments about American unreliability. When Xi tells Asian partners they can't depend on distant American guarantees, he can point to concrete examples of American fickleness and poor strategic judgment. The speed of Trump's pivot from pressuring Ukraine to threatening Iran demonstrates exactly the kind of attention deficit that makes American security guarantees questionable.

More strategically, Trump's focus on potential Iran military action diverts American attention from the South China Sea, where Chinese activities continue expanding without meaningful American response. Every day American strategic focus remains fragmented across Ukraine abandonment and Iran escalation is another day for Chinese consolidation of territorial claims and regional influence.

Xi's most significant achievement may be temporal: he's convinced regional partners that Chinese influence is permanent while American attention is temporary. This perception becomes self-fulfilling as partners make long-term commitments based on assumptions about Chinese staying power versus American fickleness.

Iran's Calculated Defiance: Strength Through American Weakness

Iran's response to Trump's threats reveals how even regional powers can exploit American strategic confusion. Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei's rejection of Trump's "unconditional surrender" demand—"Intelligent people who know Iran, the nation and the history of Iran will never speak to this nation in the language of threats"—demonstrates confidence that American threats lack credibility.

This confidence stems directly from Iran's observation of Trump's Ukraine policy. If America will pressure a democratic ally under attack to negotiate with its aggressor, Iranian leaders reasonably conclude that American threats against authoritarian regimes may be equally hollow. Trump's pattern of maximalist rhetoric followed by equivocation creates precisely the credibility gap that emboldens defiant responses.

Iran's strategic calculation appears sophisticated: accept short-term military pressure from Israel while positioning for long-term gains through American strategic exhaustion. Iranian officials understand that American attention spans are limited and that domestic political pressure will eventually force Trump to seek negotiated settlements rather than sustained confrontation.

The regional implications are significant. Arab allies watching American pressure on Ukraine followed by threats against Iran conclude that American policy varies based on Trump's personal preferences rather than consistent strategic principles. This uncertainty drives regional partners toward alternative security arrangements that don't depend on American reliability.

The Shanghai Cooperation Organization: Alternative Architecture

The most concrete manifestation of authoritarian coordination is the expansion and deepening of alternative institutional structures. The Shanghai Cooperation Organization, originally focused on Central Asian security, has evolved into a comprehensive alternative to Western-led institutions. Recent expansion to include Iran as a full member while America fragments its attention demonstrates how quickly alternative structures can gain momentum.

These institutions matter because they provide frameworks for cooperation that explicitly exclude Western participation. When member states have disputes, they're resolved within SCO frameworks rather than through Western-mediated processes. When economic cooperation is needed, they use SCO financial mechanisms rather than Western institutions. When security coordination is required, they rely on SCO military cooperation rather than Western alliance structures.

The institutional architecture being built will outlast current leadership in all participant countries. Future leaders will inherit established frameworks for cooperation that don't require American participation or Western approval. This represents permanent structural change rather than temporary exploitation of American weakness.

Economic Dimensions: The BRICS Alternative

The economic component of authoritarian coordination has accelerated dramatically during Trump's tenure, with BRICS expansion and de-dollarization efforts gaining momentum precisely as American economic leadership becomes erratic. Trump's tariff policies that contributed to U.S. economic contraction provide perfect justification for alternative economic arrangements.

The proposed BRICS currency, while technically challenging, represents more than monetary policy—it's infrastructure for trade and investment that bypasses American financial systems. When combined with expanding bilateral trade agreements denominated in local currencies, these arrangements reduce dependence on American economic stability and policy consistency.

Member countries are essentially creating parallel economic structures that can function independently of American participation. This matters because economic interdependence has historically been a source of American leverage. When major economies develop alternative frameworks for trade, investment, and currency exchange, American economic influence diminishes correspondingly.

Military Cooperation: The Axis of Authoritarian Mutual Defense

Perhaps most concerning is the deepening military cooperation between authoritarian powers. Recent joint military exercises between Russia, China, and Iran aren't just symbolic demonstrations—they're operational coordination that creates muscle memory for future cooperation against Western interests.

These exercises serve multiple strategic purposes. They demonstrate to regional partners that authoritarian powers can provide security cooperation without Western participation. They create operational familiarity that would prove valuable in future conflicts. Most importantly, they signal to Western allies that authoritarian powers can coordinate militarily in ways that multiply their individual capabilities.

The contrast with NATO coordination is stark. While Trump threatens allies with trade wars and questions alliance commitments, authoritarian powers are deepening military integration and expanding joint capabilities. This coordination advantage becomes significant if future crises require sustained military cooperation.

Information Warfare: Coordinated Narrative Control

The information dimension of authoritarian coordination may be most sophisticated. Russian, Chinese, and Iranian information operations increasingly coordinate messages about American unreliability, Western hypocrisy, and authoritarian stability. These aren't separate propaganda efforts—they're coordinated campaigns that reinforce common themes across different audiences.

The effectiveness of this coordination is visible in global polling showing declining confidence in American leadership and increasing acceptance of authoritarian governance models. When multiple authoritarian powers simultaneously argue that American democracy is chaotic and unreliable, the message gains credibility through repetition and apparent independence.

Trump's own behavior provides perfect material for these information campaigns. His abandonment of Ukraine followed by Iran escalation threats demonstrates exactly the kind of strategic incoherence that authoritarian propaganda portrays as characteristic of democratic governance. Every Trump policy reversal becomes evidence for authoritarian arguments about democratic unreliability.

The Institutional Replacement Project

The most ambitious aspect of authoritarian coordination is the systematic construction of alternative governance institutions. This isn't just about replacing individual American policies—it's about replacing the entire framework of international law, economic cooperation, and conflict resolution that America has led since World War II.

Alternative institutions are being built for every aspect of global governance: economic coordination through BRICS expansion, security cooperation through SCO mechanisms, information sharing through authoritarian media partnerships, and conflict resolution through non-Western mediation frameworks. These institutions explicitly reject Western values while providing functional alternatives to Western-led processes.

The goal appears to be creating a parallel international system that can operate independently of Western participation. When this alternative architecture is complete, authoritarian powers won't need to challenge Western institutions—they can simply ignore them and operate through their own frameworks.

Regional Dominance Through American Distraction

While America fragments its attention between Ukraine abandonment and Iran escalation, authoritarian powers are systematically establishing regional dominance in their respective spheres. Putin consolidates control over former Soviet space, Xi expands influence throughout the Pacific, and Iran builds proxy networks across the Middle East.

This regional consolidation matters because it creates facts on the ground that future American policy will struggle to reverse. Every day American attention remains divided between multiple crisis theaters is another day for authoritarian powers to deepen regional influence and establish permanent advantages.

The coordination is remarkable in its division of labor: Russia focuses on European periphery, China concentrates on Pacific expansion, Iran builds Middle East influence. This geographic specialization prevents direct competition between authoritarian powers while maximizing pressure on American alliance structures globally.

The Temporal Advantage: Authoritarian Patience vs. Democratic Attention Deficits

Perhaps the most significant advantage authoritarian coordination enjoys is temporal. Authoritarian leaders can plan in decades while democratic leaders struggle to maintain consistent policies across election cycles. Trump's abandonment of Ukraine after three years of support, followed by immediate escalation against Iran, perfectly demonstrates democratic inconsistency that authoritarian patience can exploit.

Xi Jinping and Putin know they can outlast individual American presidents and their policies. This temporal advantage allows patient strategic planning that exploits predictable American policy reversals. They don't need to defeat American power directly—they just need to outlast American attention spans and strategic consistency.

Looking Forward: The Point of No Return

The most sobering aspect of current authoritarian coordination is its momentum. Alternative institutions gain legitimacy through use, alternative economic arrangements become embedded through habit, alternative security partnerships develop operational capability through practice. At some point, these alternative structures become self-sustaining and competitive with Western arrangements.

UC Berkeley's M. Steven Fish warned that American power and prestige "have all been squandered by Trump" because authoritarian powers "will be able to terrorize" American allies while America focuses on "Greenland and the Panama Canal." This prediction seems increasingly accurate as authoritarian coordination accelerates while American strategic focus fragments.

The question is whether we've already passed the point where alternative institutions become competitive with American-led arrangements. When BRICS economies represent larger GDP than G7 economies, when SCO military cooperation rivals NATO capabilities, when authoritarian information coordination shapes global narratives more effectively than democratic messaging—at what point does American leadership become historical rather than contemporary?

Conclusion: The series has traced how Trump's reactive chaos masquerading as strategy has accelerated American decline while enabling coordinated authoritarian expansion. The next question is whether this trajectory can be reversed or whether we're witnessing the permanent transition from American-led to multipolar global order dominated by authoritarian cooperation.
