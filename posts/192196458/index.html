The Reckoning

MAHA in Freefall: While Kennedy was body-slamming Twinkies, his actual agenda was being dismantled from within

March 30, 2026

Mark Peterson / Redux

Yesterday, in this publication’s Sunday Satire Series, I argued that Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s meme machine — the Twinkie suplex, the action figure, the birthday steak — was a masterwork of distraction, papering over a collapsing policy record with viral content while children caught measles in Texas.

Last week, The Atlantic confirmed it in detail.

What follows is the RFK Files assessment of what these developments mean for Kennedy's tenure, his agenda, and the public health infrastructure he has spent fourteen months dismantling.

The short version: the walls are closing in. The slightly longer version follows.

The man inside the building says the quiet part out loud

Getty Images

On the morning of March 25, 2026, Acting CDC Director Jay Bhattacharya stood in front of the agency’s beleaguered staff at its Atlanta headquarters and said something that no senior health official in this administration has previously managed:

“I think it is vital that every kid in this country get the measles vaccine. Absolutely vital.”

Thirteen months. That is how long it took for someone inside this administration — inside the building Kennedy runs — to say, clearly and without qualification, that children should be vaccinated against a disease that is killing them. The first child died from measles in early 2025. The first public service announcement about the outbreaks from within the administration came thirteen months later.

Bhattacharya did not stop there. He told staff the agency needed to “move on” from the chaos of the past year, encouraged employees to “remove politics” from their work, and said something that reads, in context, as a direct rebuke of his own boss:

“You can’t just snap your fingers and make people healthy again.”

Make America Healthy Again. Kennedy’s slogan. Contradicted, inside Kennedy’s department, by the man currently running Kennedy’s premier agency.

Bhattacharya acknowledged publicly that he has had “scientific disagreements” with Kennedy. “Do we agree on every single item? No,” he told staff. He added, with diplomatic care: “We may have disagreements about scientific matters, but he listens.”

The carefully chosen word there is listens. Not agrees. Not acts. Listens.

Staff gave Bhattacharya nearly thirty seconds of applause when one employee asked how he planned to rebuild trust in CDC leadership. That is not applause for a man. That is applause for the act of acknowledging reality.

The MAHA collapse: a timeline

For readers of the RFK Files, the pattern is familiar. What is new is the speed and simultaneity of the unraveling. Consider what has happened in recent weeks alone.

A federal judge ruled that the CDC’s January shrinking of the childhood vaccine schedule was probably illegal, and that Kennedy likely broke the law when he remade the CDC’s vaccine advisory panel in his own image. All decisions made by that panel, the judge ordered, should be put on hold.

Robert Malone, the committee’s vice chair, a Kennedy ally and prominent MAHA figure, resigned.

The FDA’s vaccine chief, Vinay Prasad — after Kennedy the senior official most antagonistic toward pharmaceutical companies — will leave his position at the end of April.

The confirmation of Casey Means, the wellness influencer Trump nominated as surgeon general and a like-minded Kennedy ally, appears stalled in the Senate. This after Means sat before a Senate committee and declined to recommend the measles vaccine to a hypothetical mother during a measles outbreak — a performance so extraordinary that even Republican senators struggled to defend it.

Trump signed an executive order shielding the makers of the weed killer glyphosate from legal liability — a direct betrayal of Kennedy, who has a history of advocating against glyphosate, and who now faces fury from his own MAHA base.

And then there is the CDC director problem. The agency has now burned through: a Senate-confirmed director Kennedy pushed out for refusing to fire experts without cause and approve recommendations without scientific evidence; a biotech entrepreneur with no medical degree who was quietly removed with no explanation and shuffled sideways to run an agency he is equally unqualified for; and now Bhattacharya, who runs both the CDC and the NIH simultaneously from offices approximately 650 miles apart — and who told staff on March 25 that he expected his permanent replacement to be announced imminently. The administration missed that deadline.

The political management of Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

None of this is accidental. The RFK Files documented it earlier and The Atlantic has now confirmed it publicly: a December poll conducted by longtime Republican strategists Tony Fabrizio and Bob Ward forecasted “electoral downsides” for candidates who supported doing away with vaccine recommendations. The White House subsequently pressured HHS to avoid any more vaccine-policy changes and installed a new chief counselor, Chris Klomp, to rein in the department.

This is the architecture of damage control. Kennedy is not governing HHS. He is being governed — managed by political operatives reading midterm polls, constrained by courts striking down his actions as probably illegal, contradicted inside his own department by acting officials who apparently concluded that children dying of measles was an unacceptable cost of ideological loyalty.

All of this explains why Kennedy has retreated from commenting on vaccine issues in public. Instead, he has spent this year celebrating his inverted food pyramid and making vague threats to companies that sell highly processed snacks. He has also been posting AI-generated videos of himself body-slamming a man in a Twinkie costume.

The meme strategy, in this light, is not just distraction. It is the visible symptom of a man who has been stripped of his primary agenda and handed a consolation prize: be the shirtless wellness guy. Fight the Twinkies. Stay away from the vaccines. The Limp Bizkit soundtrack is the sound of a political operation containing its own Secretary.

What Kennedy said to the father

I want to close this entry with a single reported detail from The Atlantic, because it renders the entire Kennedy tenure in miniature.

During the West Texas measles outbreak that claimed the lives of two young girls, Kennedy publicly endorsed the measles vaccine — reluctantly, under pressure, to the fury of his fellow anti-vaccine activists. That was the public position. The meme-facing version. The performance.

Privately, Kennedy told the father of one of the girls who died: “You don’t know what’s in the vaccine anymore.”

To the father of a child who died of measles. While publicly endorsing the vaccine. While his department posted AI content about Twinkies.

The gap between the meme and the mandate has a name now. It was delivered in private, to a grieving man, by the Secretary of Health. It is the most honest thing Kennedy has said in fourteen months of public office, and it is the most damning.

That is the RFK Files record. That is what the waterproof jeans are covering.
