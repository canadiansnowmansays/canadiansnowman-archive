Now This is Fake News

PART 4: The Body Count: What the Ministry of Truth Hides

October 30, 2025

In the Caribbean and eastern Pacific, boats like this have been struck by U.S. missiles. 57 dead. No drugs recovered. No briefings held. No questions answered.

The Ministry of Truth is operational. The propagandists are credentialed. The independent journalists are working from outside the building.

And while everyone debates press credentials and First Amendment principles, people are dying.

At least 57 people have been killed in U.S. military strikes on boats in the Caribbean and eastern Pacific since September. The most recent attack—on October 28, just yesterday—killed 14 people in a single day. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posts videos on X, declares them “narco-terrorists,” and moves on. No press conferences. No questions answered. No evidence presented.

This is what the press purge enables: military operations with body counts but no accountability, claims without evidence, and a death toll that keeps rising while the “next generation” press corps stays silent.

This is the Ministry of Truth in practice—not just controlling information, but eliminating the mechanism by which power gets questioned. The result isn’t just a press freedom issue. It’s people dead in international waters based on claims the Pentagon won’t verify, killed by a military that faces no tough questions because there’s no one left in the building to ask them

What Happens When Nobody’s Watching: 57 Dead and Counting

Here’s what we know: Defense Secretary Hegseth has announced a series of strikes on boats in the Caribbean and eastern Pacific, claiming they carried drugs and were operated by “narco-terrorists” and “Designated Terrorist Organizations.” The pace has accelerated dramatically—from one strike every few weeks in September to three strikes in a single day on October 27, killing 14 people. Hegseth posts videos on X. That’s the Pentagon’s entire public accountability: social media announcements with dramatic footage.

Here’s what we don’t know: Whether any drugs were actually on these boats. Who the 57 dead people were—names, identities, nationalities. What intelligence justified the strikes. What the rules of engagement are. Why the military chose to bomb vessels rather than board and arrest them, as the Coast Guard has done successfully for decades with actual drug interdiction operations.

Here’s what should alarm everyone:

Venezuelan officials claim their investigations of the first incident—which killed 11 people—found no drugs and no gang members. Just 11 dead civilians. The Venezuelan government asked: “How can there be a drug cartel if there’s no drugs here?”

Secretary of State Marco Rubio admitted in interviews that the U.S. could have intercepted and boarded the first boat but chose to bomb it instead. The administration had the capability to arrest, prosecute, and convict—the traditional law enforcement approach. They chose killing.

Legal experts, including former Justice Department lawyers who worked in the Office of Legal Counsel, have publicly condemned the strikes as unlawful under both domestic and international law. They call them extrajudicial killings—premeditated killing without due process, trial, or legal authority. Law professor Mary Ellen O’Connell said the strikes “violated fundamental principles of international law.” Luke Moffett of Queen’s University Belfast stated that striking ships without grounds of self-defense constitutes extrajudicial killing.

The United Nations issued a statement on October 21: A group of UN human rights experts declared that the use of lethal force in international waters without proper legal basis constitutes “extrajudicial executions,” and that military action against another sovereign state represents “an even graver breach of the UN Charter.”

Amnesty International released a statement today—October 29—declaring these strikes are “murder” and demanding Congress act immediately to stop them. “The Caribbean and eastern Pacific are not warzones where the U.S. military can bomb boats the White House claims carry enemies,” said Daniel Noroña, Amnesty’s Advocacy Director for the Americas. “These air strikes also send a chilling message of tacit approval to other leaders seeking to extrajudicially execute people.”

Waters off Colombia and Venezuela where U.S. military strikes have killed dozens since September. No evidence of drugs recovered. No press conferences held. No questions answered.

The Colombian and Venezuelan governments have formally accused the United States of extrajudicial murder. International allies are distancing themselves. Legal accountability through the International Criminal Court or universal jurisdiction probes is now a real possibility.

Lawmakers from both parties have expressed alarm. Senator Andy Kim, who previously worked at the Pentagon and State Department, said “I’ve never seen anything quite like this before. We have no idea how far this is going.” Democrats insist the strikes violate international law. Even some Republicans have questioned whether Trump complied with the War Powers Resolution, which demands presidential consultation with Congress before introducing forces into hostilities.

Questions are mounting: Did the boats actually carry drugs, or were they fishing vessels on established routes? Legal scholar Greg Grandin questioned whether a boat carrying 11 passengers would be used for drug smuggling, noting that smugglers would devote space to cargo, not people. He called it “bringing the logic of Gaza into the Caribbean, in terms of unaccountability, impunity and an expansive notion of national defense to justify what is, in effect, just extrajudicial killing.”

This is precisely the kind of story that would have the Pentagon press corps working overtime.

Daily briefings demanding evidence. Questions about rules of engagement. Requests for casualty identification. Scrutiny of the legal justification. Follow-up on whether any drugs were actually recovered from any vessel. Pressure on the administration’s claim that each boat destroyed saves 25,000 lives when fentanyl deaths continue climbing domestically.

Questions about why bombing was chosen over boarding when the Coast Guard successfully interdicts drug vessels regularly through arrest and prosecution. Questions about what happens to the intelligence value lost when everyone dies instead of being interrogated. Questions about whether killing suspects without trial is now official U.S. military policy.

Instead, there’s silence from inside the building. The outlets with credentials aren’t asking these questions. Hegseth posts videos on X with dramatic music and declarations of victory. That’s the Pentagon’s official statement. No briefing. No questions. No accountability.

The accountability gap is being filled by independent journalists, legal experts, human rights organizations, and concerned citizens working from outside. In my own recent essay “Another Boat Hit: A Canadian’s Take on the Latest U.S. Drug War Escalation” I examined how these strikes—justified as drug interdiction—lack the transparency and evidence that should accompany lethal military force, and how alternatives like boarding and arrest have proven effective without killing anyone.

But independent analysis isn’t enough. These operations are happening in real-time, escalating rapidly (from one strike every few weeks to three in a single day), and deserve the kind of daily, aggressive questioning that only a functioning press corps can provide.

Without reporters inside the Pentagon who can ask follow-up questions at briefings, demand clarification on the record, and hold officials accountable face-to-face, military operations proceed with whatever narrative the Pentagon chooses to provide via social media.

This is what the press purge enables: military action with minimal oversight, claims without evidence, and a death toll that keeps rising while the “next generation” press corps posts nothing, asks nothing, challenges nothing.

This is the Ministry of Truth in practice: not just controlling information, but eliminating the mechanism by which information gets challenged. The result isn’t just a press freedom issue—it’s 57 dead and counting. It’s extrajudicial killings that legal experts call murder. It’s military operations that may constitute war crimes proceeding without the scrutiny that could stop them.

The Legal Reckoning Looms

While bodies pile up in the Caribbean, the legal challenges to Hegseth’s press policy continue to build.

As of early November, no lawsuits challenging the press restrictions have been filed yet. But the groundwork is being methodically laid by organizations that now have an even more urgent case: a press purge that enables military operations with body counts but no accountability.

The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press has issued multiple letters since September calling the policy unconstitutional. Their October statements described the restrictions as “too restrictive” and expecting them to “pose a significant impediment” to journalism. Now they can point to a concrete example of what happens when the Pentagon operates without adversarial press coverage: 57 people dead, zero evidence presented, minimal scrutiny.

PEN America labeled October 15—the day of the mass walkout—”a dark day for U.S. press freedom.” The Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression (FIRE) called it “an unconstitutional attempt to single out reporters for less favorable treatment.” Both organizations can now demonstrate that press restrictions aren’t abstract—they have immediate, deadly consequences.

The legal blueprint exists: The Associated Press’s February lawsuit over White House access restrictions, where a judge ruled that government cannot punish news organizations for editorial decisions by restricting access. An appeals court issued a stay, but the precedent stands: viewpoint discrimination in press access violates the First Amendment.

The Pentagon situation is even more clear-cut because it’s not about one editorial decision—it’s about establishing a permanent system where only pre-approved information can be reported and only friendly outlets get access. Prior restraint combined with viewpoint discrimination, wrapped in a policy that treats soliciting information as potentially criminal.

And now there’s a body count to point to. Fifty-seven dead. Operations that legal experts call murder. All conducted while the Pentagon’s press corps—the one Hegseth hand-picked—asks no questions.

What legal experts have said on the record: Multiple First Amendment lawyers have described the press policy as “textbook” constitutional violation. Jane Kirtley at the University of Minnesota: “The idea that it is somehow improper for journalists to seek out information from the agency that they’re covering is very problematic.”

Media law experts can now argue that the press restrictions aren’t just unconstitutional—they’re dangerous. When the Pentagon can conduct military operations that kill dozens without facing daily press scrutiny, the result is exactly what we’re seeing: operations of questionable legality proceeding unchecked.

Based on coordination among press freedom organizations and the timeline of similar cases, formal legal challenges could come by late November or December. The Pentagon Press Association is working with RCFP and other organizations to build what could be a coalition case that argues press restrictions enable military operations without accountability.

Hegseth’s team is betting on delay—that courts will move slowly, that the new system will become normalized. But with bodies piling up and international condemnation mounting, that calculation may be changing. Courts move faster when they see immediate, demonstrable harm. Fifty-seven dead is demonstrable harm.

The Broader Pattern Crystallizes

The Pentagon purge isn’t isolated. It’s part of a coordinated assault on accountability across the Trump administration:

Press restrictions everywhere:

  • AP lawsuit over White House access (in court)

  • Trump lawsuits against New York Times and Wall Street Journal (filed)

  • ABC and CBS settling lawsuits over coverage (completed)

  • Special briefings for pro-Trump influencers while traditional outlets are frozen out

  • Cabinet officials limiting traditional press while embracing friendly media

  • Cuts to Voice of America and Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty

Military operations without oversight:

  • 57 killed in Caribbean/Pacific strikes with no evidence presented

  • Operations that UN experts call “extrajudicial executions”

  • Legal experts, including former DOJ lawyers, calling it murder

  • No Congressional authorization

  • Gerald R. Ford Carrier Strike Group deployed to region

  • Trump hinting at ground operations: “The land is next”

Information lockdown at Pentagon:

  • Press corps purged and replaced with propagandists

  • Civilian workers facing expedited firing procedures

  • NDAs and polygraphs for remaining staff

  • Practically zero press briefings for months

  • One-way communication via social media only

Each piece alone could be explained away. Together they reveal the strategy: eliminate adversarial journalism, proceed with controversial operations, present only the official narrative, silence anyone who might contradict it.

The Pentagon just demonstrated what this looks like in practice. Fifty-seven people dead based on unverified claims, with no press corps inside to demand answers, while propagandists with credentials stay silent.

This is how accountability dies: not dramatically, but methodically. One restriction at a time. One purge at a time. One body at a time.

The Pentagon briefing room. Empty of adversarial reporters. Silent while 57 die without explanation.

Conclusion: When Words Lose All Meaning, Bodies Count

We now have Pentagon press credentials held by an outlet that received Russian propaganda payments, a bankruptcy-filing conspiracy site that settled defamation lawsuits, and a streaming service run by an election denier—while the Associated Press, Reuters, and The New York Times cover the military from outside the building.

We have a Defense Secretary who purged the press corps, fired civilian workers, imposed NDAs and polygraphs, and now oversees military operations that have killed 57 people with zero evidence presented publicly.

We have legal experts calling it murder. UN experts calling it extrajudicial execution. Amnesty International demanding Congress stop it. Lawmakers expressing alarm. Allied governments formally accusing the U.S. of extrajudicial killings.

We have no press conferences. No evidence. No accountability. Just videos posted on X and a death toll that climbs.

And we’re told this represents progress. Transparency. “Honest journalism.”

If this is what “real news” looks like—pre-approved, unauthorized-question-free stenography that stays silent while people die—then perhaps we need to revisit what “fake news” meant all along.

The outlets now holding Pentagon credentials aren’t there to report the news. They’re there to ratify it. And in doing so, they’ve become exactly what they claimed to oppose: fake news in its purest form—information shaped by power, for power, distributed to maintain power, while bodies pile up in international waters.

The projection is complete. For years we heard about “fake news” from mainstream media. Now we have the real thing: government-approved outlets producing government-approved coverage, calling it independent journalism while people die and no one with building access asks why.

The journalists who walked out are still working. They’re still reporting. They’re still asking about the 57 dead. The only difference is that now, Pete Hegseth doesn’t have to answer them inside his building.

Which was, perhaps, the point all along. Not to improve journalism, but to eliminate it. Not to reach new audiences, but to control the message. Not to fight fake news, but to create it. And not to enhance accountability, but to kill it—along with 57 people whose names we don’t know, whose families will never see justice, whose deaths proceed without evidence or explanation or anyone with credentials asking why.

Welcome to the new Pentagon press corps. This is fake news. This is the Ministry of Truth.

And this is the body count.
