The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 5: The Revolutionary Guards State

February 2, 2026

How the IRGC Built an Economic Empire While Iranians Starved

$1 billion to Hezbollah while Iranians starve — Mass funeral for Hezbollah members killed in Israeli strikes, November 2025. Despite disrupted Syrian routes and killed IRGC operatives, Iran transferred roughly $1 billion to Hezbollah in 2025 using Turkey/Iraq channels, maritime shipments, cryptocurrency, and gold transfers (US Treasury).

In the first ten months of 2025, Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps transferred over $1 billion to Hezbollah in Lebanon—funding weapons, salaries, and operations for Iran’s primary regional proxy.

During those same ten months, 60% of Iranians experienced food insecurity according to the Ministry of Social Welfare. Seven million Iranians faced acute hunger. Inflation ran at 42-48% annually. The currency collapsed to 1.4 million rials per US dollar. Daily electricity blackouts left cities dark for 3-4 hours.

One billion dollars to foreign fighters. Sixty percent of Iranians malnourished.

This isn’t foreign policy. This is theft dressed in revolutionary rhetoric.

For 37 years, Ayatollah Khamenei has presided over the transformation of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps from revolutionary militia into kleptocratic empire. The IRGC now controls an estimated 20-40% of Iran’s economy—between $100-200 billion in assets—through a web of front companies, no-bid contracts, tax exemptions, and outright monopolies.

The 1979 revolution was fought partly over economic inequality and elite corruption under the Shah. The revolutionaries promised to serve mostazafin—the oppressed masses. They promised economic justice.

By every measurable standard, Khamenei’s Iran features worse economic inequality, worse elite corruption, and more systematic impoverishment than the monarchy the revolution overthrew.

This isn’t external criticism. This is measurement against revolutionary standards—the promises made in 1979, the principles Khamenei claims to uphold.

By those standards, his economic record is systematic betrayal.

I. HOW THE IRGC BECAME AN ECONOMIC EMPIRE

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps was created in 1979 to protect the revolution from internal and external enemies. Originally, it was ideologically driven militia—fighters motivated by revolutionary fervor rather than professional military ambition.

The Iran-Iraq War (1980-88) hardened them. Over eight brutal years, the IRGC fought Iraqi forces, organized mass mobilizations, and developed parallel command structures to Iran’s regular army. They earned battlefield credibility and political power.

After the war ended, the IRGC didn’t demobilize. Instead, Ayatollah Khomeini—and then his successor Khamenei—rewarded them with economic opportunities framed as “reconstruction.”

The Khatam al-Anbiya Construction Headquarters

In 1989, the IRGC established Khatam al-Anbiya—officially an engineering and construction arm to rebuild war-damaged infrastructure.

Initially, this made strategic sense. Iran needed massive reconstruction. The IRGC had engineers, organizational capacity, and workforce. Why not deploy them for national development?

But what began as reconstruction became monopolization.

By the 1990s and 2000s, Khatam al-Anbiya secured no-bid government contracts worth billions. Oil pipelines. Highways. Dams. Airports. Telecommunications networks. The organization faced no competitive bidding, no transparency requirements, no accountability.

According to Reuters investigative reporting, by 2010 Khatam al-Anbiya had become one of Iran’s largest contractors, with annual revenues estimated at $8-10 billion.

The Privatization Scam

In the 2000s, Iran embarked on “privatization” of state-owned enterprises. On paper, this was economic liberalization—selling state assets to private sector.

In reality, it was wealth transfer to the IRGC.

State-owned companies were sold at below-market prices to IRGC-linked entities or front companies. Regular Iranians couldn’t compete with IRGC financial and political power. The result: “privatization” meant transferring state assets from visible government control to IRGC control disguised as private sector.

A 2013 Reuters investigation documented how billions in state assets—telecommunications companies, oil operations, infrastructure—ended up in IRGC hands through this process.

The Symbiotic Relationship

By the time Khamenei consolidated absolute power in the 2000s, the relationship between the Supreme Leader and the IRGC was symbiotic:

  • Khamenei needed the IRGC: They provided security against domestic threats (protesters), enforced his will, and maintained the system against opposition.

  • The IRGC needed Khamenei: He provided political cover, legal immunity, tax exemptions, and ensured no rival power center could challenge their economic dominance.

It wasn’t clear whether Khamenei controlled the IRGC or the IRGC tolerated Khamenei because he enabled their enrichment. Perhaps both were true.

What was clear: by 2009’s Green Movement, the IRGC had become the revolution’s primary economic beneficiary and the regime’s primary enforcer. When millions protested that year, IRGC-backed Basij militia crushed them—protecting both Khamenei’s power and their own economic interests.

II. THE SECTORS THEY CONTROL

Revolutionary Guards: Military force or economic empire? — IRGC soldiers march beneath portraits of Ayatollah Khomeini and Ayatollah Khamenei at an official parade. What began as revolutionary militia in 1979 now controls an estimated 20-40% of Iran's economy ($100-200 billion) through front companies, no-bid contracts, and monopolies—while maintaining tax exemptions and legal immunity.

According to a 2020 U.S. Treasury Department report and investigations by Bloomberg and Reuters, the IRGC and affiliated entities control significant portions of:

Oil and Gas

  • IRGC-linked companies operate oil fields, refining operations, and export networks

  • Particularly dominant in smuggling operations that evade sanctions

  • According to Iran International reporting, IRGC controls shadow tanker fleets and front companies that move sanctioned oil

Telecommunications

  • Control of major telecom companies provides both revenue and surveillance capabilities

  • Allows monitoring of communications, internet control, censorship infrastructure

  • Acquired through “privatization” of state-owned Telecommunications Company of Iran

Ports and Shipping

  • Control of major ports (particularly Bandar Abbas) enables import-export dominance

  • Facilitates both legal trade and sanctions evasion

  • Generates revenue through fees, control of logistics, smuggling

Construction and Infrastructure

  • Khatam al-Anbiya remains the dominant contractor for major projects

  • Builds dams, highways, pipelines, airports, military facilities

  • No-bid contracts worth billions annually

Banking and Finance

  • Control of banks provides access to capital flows, monetary policy influence

  • According to U.S. Treasury reports, IRGC-linked banks facilitate sanctions evasion

  • Enables financing of both domestic operations and foreign proxies

Import-Export

  • Dominance at borders and ports creates import monopolies

  • Control of critical goods (food, medicine, consumer products) during shortages

  • Enormous profit margins during economic crisis

Mining

  • Copper, zinc, other mineral extraction controlled through front companies

  • Export revenues flow to IRGC-linked entities

  • According to Bloomberg, mining operations worth billions annually

The Scale

Estimates vary, but credible assessments suggest IRGC control of:

  • Conservative estimate: 20% of Iranian economy (~$100 billion)

  • Higher estimates: 40% of Iranian economy (~$200 billion)

The variance reflects opacity—the IRGC operates through networks of front companies, shell corporations, and affiliates that obscure true ownership.

What’s undisputed: the IRGC is Iran’s largest economic actor outside formal government, operating with legal immunity, tax exemptions, and no public accountability.

III. SETAD: KHAMENEI’S PERSONAL EMPIRE

Beyond the IRGC, Khamenei controls his own massive economic empire: Setad Ejraiye Farmane Hazrate Emam (Headquarters for Executing the Order of the Imam), commonly known as Setad.

Origins

Setad was created in 1989 to manage properties abandoned after the 1979 revolution or confiscated from regime opponents. Initially framed as caretaker organization for “ownerless” property, it evolved into vast economic conglomerate.

The Scale

A 2013 Reuters investigation—based on Iranian corporate filings, interviewed sources, and financial analysis—estimated Setad’s holdings at approximately $95 billion.

For context: that’s nearly twice the value of the Shah’s Pahlavi Foundation that revolutionaries denounced as corrupt in 1979.

What Setad Controls

According to the Reuters investigation:

  • Real estate holdings worth tens of billions

  • Stakes in major Iranian companies (including telecommunications, automotive, energy)

  • Banks and financial institutions

  • Agricultural operations

  • Portfolio of investments across economy

The Accountability Problem

Setad answers only to Khamenei. It files no public financial reports. It pays no taxes. Iranian parliament has no oversight. Regular Iranians have no visibility into how their Supreme Leader’s organization operates.

The contradiction is stark: Khamenei lectures Iranians about Islamic values, sacrifice, and serving the oppressed—while controlling an opaque $95 billion empire.

IV. THE NUMBERS DON’T LIE

The revolution promised economic justice. Measure the results.

According to analysis by Iranian economic newspaper Donya-ye-Eghtesad and verified by World Bank data, approximately 1% of Iranians now hold 33% of the nation’s wealth—concentrated primarily among IRGC commanders, Khamenei appointees, and those connected to the Supreme Leader’s office.

The Verdict: By every measurable economic indicator, Iranians under Khamenei’s 37-year guardianship face worse poverty, worse inequality, worse corruption, and worse daily suffering than under the Shah the revolution overthrew. The revolution promised economic justice. It delivered economic catastrophe.

V. THE “RESISTANCE ECONOMY” THAT NEVER RESISTED

In 2014, facing intensified sanctions, Khamenei announced a new economic policy: the “Resistance Economy.”

The rhetoric promised:

  • Economic self-sufficiency reducing dependence on West

  • Domestic production replacing imports

  • Equitable distribution of resources

  • Innovation and entrepreneurship

  • Support for Iranian businesses

The reality delivered the opposite.

Self-Sufficiency?

Iran remains heavily dependent on:

  • Chinese imports for consumer goods, machinery, technology

  • Food imports (Iran can’t feed its population domestically)

  • Gasoline imports (despite vast oil reserves, inadequate refining capacity)

  • Medicine imports (domestic pharmaceutical production insufficient)

According to Iran Open Data, approximately 80% of Iranian oil exports now go to China—creating dependency on a single buyer who dictates prices and terms.

Equitable Distribution?

While Khamenei promoted “Resistance Economy,” IRGC entities and Setad continued accumulating wealth. Tax exemptions for connected entities continued. No-bid contracts continued. Monopolies expanded.

According to Iranian economic analysis and World Bank poverty estimates, the percentage of Iranians living below the poverty line increased from approximately 30% in 2018 to over 50% by 2025.

Supporting Iranian Businesses?

IRGC monopolies crushed private sector competition. Small businesses couldn’t compete with entities enjoying:

  • No-bid government contracts

  • Tax exemptions

  • Access to subsidized foreign currency

  • Political protection

  • Legal immunity

The “Resistance Economy” resisted only one thing: equitable economic policy.

VI. WHILE THE ELITE FEAST

A little girl sitting on the sidewalk asking for change, Iran

In December 2025, facing spiraling economic crisis, Khamenei addressed Iranians: “We must all reduce consumption of food, water, energy, and fuel. This is our duty as Iranians.”

The order came from a man whose office (Setad) controlled an estimated $95 billion. From the Guardian of a revolution fought partly over economic inequality. From the leader who presided while IRGC commanders lived in luxury compounds and transferred billions to foreign proxies.

The IRGC Elite Lifestyle

Investigative reporting and leaked documents reveal IRGC commanders’ lifestyles:

  • Luxury compounds in Tehran’s wealthiest neighborhoods (Farmanieh, Elahieh)

  • Vacation properties on Caspian Sea coast

  • Foreign properties through family members in Turkey, UAE, occasionally Europe

  • Access to subsidized foreign currency for personal use

  • Import licenses for luxury vehicles while ordinary Iranians couldn’t afford fuel

  • Private education for children abroad (often in the West they denounce)

  • Medical treatment in European hospitals (again, in the West they claim to oppose)

The Tax Exemption Scandal

Before the 2019 protests, it emerged that Khamenei had issued orders exempting IRGC entities—including massive organizations like Khatam al-Anbiya—from taxation.

The wealthiest entities in Iran paid no taxes. Meanwhile, ordinary Iranians faced:

  • VAT on purchases

  • New “inflation taxes” in 2025

  • Subsidy removals (fuel, bread, basic necessities)

  • Increased fees for government services

The poorest Iranians—those least able to pay—bore the tax burden. The wealthiest entities—those most able to contribute—were exempt.

While Iranians Starved, Money Flowed Abroad

According to U.S. Treasury reports and Iran International investigations, in 2025 Iran transferred:

  • Over $1 billion to Hezbollah (first ten months)

  • Hundreds of millions to Syrian regime (before December collapse)

  • Significant funds to Houthi rebels in Yemen

  • Support for Iraqi Shia militias

Meanwhile, 60% of Iranians experienced food insecurity. Seven million faced acute hunger. Children suffered malnutrition that will affect them for life.

The choice was explicit: feed foreign proxies or feed Iranians. Khamenei chose proxies.

VII. THE REVOLUTIONARY BETRAYAL MADE EXPLICIT

The 1979 revolution was fought partly because the Shah’s family and military-connected elite enriched themselves while ordinary Iranians struggled.

Revolutionary slogans:

  • “Economic justice!”

  • “End corruption!”

  • “Serve the people, not the elite!”

  • “Down with the monarchy’s exploitation!”

Compare 2025:

  • Military-clerical elite enriched through IRGC monopolies and Khamenei-controlled Setad

  • According to Donya-ye-Eghtesad, 1% hold 33% of wealth (worse concentration than Shah’s era)

  • Tax exemptions for connected entities, increased burden on poor

  • Billions to foreign proxies while Iranians starve

  • Systematic corruption exceeding anything under the monarchy

The Logic Trap

Apply Khamenei’s own revolutionary standards:

On Economic Justice (2014 “Resistance Economy” speech): “Economic justice is not just about raising GDP. It’s about equitable distribution, about ensuring every Iranian can live with dignity. The Islamic Republic serves the people, especially the oppressed masses.”

Test against 2025:

  • Equitable distribution? 1% hold 33% of wealth, IRGC controls 20-40% of economy

  • Dignified living? 60% malnourished, 50%+ in poverty, 7 million hungry

  • Serving oppressed masses? The oppressed masses are in streets demanding his overthrow

On the Shah’s Corruption (multiple speeches across decades):

Khamenei frequently invoked the Shah’s economic failures to justify the revolution: elite corruption, inequality, military enrichment, popular suffering while elites feast.

Test: By every metric—inequality, poverty, elite enrichment, corruption, popular suffering—Khamenei’s Iran is measurably worse than the Shah’s Iran.

The fox wasn’t just in the henhouse. The fox owned the henhouse, paid no taxes on the henhouse, transferred henhouse resources to foreign foxes, and told the chickens to eat less while lecturing them about Islamic values.

CLOSING: THE EMPIRE BUILT ON BROKEN PROMISES

In January 2026, as Iranians protest across all 31 provinces, they understand what Khamenei spent 37 years obscuring:

The Islamic Republic’s economic system isn’t aberration or temporary crisis or external imposition. It’s the designed outcome of systematic choices that prioritized IRGC enrichment, foreign adventurism, and elite power over Iranian welfare.

The “Revolutionary Guards State” isn’t metaphor. It’s literal description: a state run by and for Revolutionary Guards, where economic policy serves IRGC interests, where the Supreme Leader’s $95 billion empire operates without accountability, where 1% control 33% of wealth while 60% can’t afford food.

By the revolution’s own standards—serving mostazafin, providing economic justice, ending elite corruption—Khamenei’s economic record is complete failure.

Worse than failure: betrayal. The revolutionaries who denounced the Shah’s corruption built a system featuring worse corruption. The leaders who promised to serve the oppressed built an empire that oppresses them.

When Khamenei ordered Iranians to “reduce consumption” in December 2024 while IRGC transferred billions to Hezbollah, he inadvertently told the truth: after 37 years of his guardianship, ordinary Iranians must consume less so the elite and foreign proxies can consume more.

The revolution promised bread. Khamenei and the IRGC ate it. Then told Iranians to be grateful for crumbs.

And wrapped it all in revolutionary rhetoric about resistance, justice, and serving the oppressed.

The bunker beneath Khamenei’s compound in Tehran protects him from external enemies. But it can’t protect him from the economic catastrophe he created, the hungry Iranians he failed, or the revolutionary promises he systematically betrayed.

The Iranian people understand the con. The numbers don’t lie. The IRGC built their empire on the backs of the revolution’s broken promises.

And Iranians are done paying for it.
