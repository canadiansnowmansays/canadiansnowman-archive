The Iran Crisis

“The Retreat That Isn’t”

Part LIII: Day Twenty-Four: Trump Winds Down His Goals While the War Escalates, Natanz Is Struck, and America Funds Its Adversary to Lower Gas Prices

March 21, 2026

Illustrating the “Sanctions Paradox” where the U.S. temporarily lifted restrictions on Iranian oil already loaded and largely destined for evaders like China’s fleet, with marginal real impact on prices.

I. The Sanctions Paradox

On Friday, the United States Treasury Department temporarily lifted sanctions on Iranian oil currently at sea — authorizing approximately 140 million barrels to be sold to most countries, effective immediately, valid through April 19. The license applies to oil loaded on vessels as of March 20.

Read that sentence again. The United States, which launched a war against Iran twenty-four days ago in the name of maximum pressure, has now temporarily removed maximum pressure on Iranian oil to manage the domestic economic consequences of the war it launched.

Treasury Secretary Bessent’s framing was characteristically direct about the paradox while attempting to dissolve it: “We will be using the Iranian barrels against the Iranians to keep the price down for the next 10 to 14 days as we continue this campaign.” Iran, he added, would see “little economic benefit” because it would have difficulty accessing any revenue generated. Most Iranian oil moves through China’s shadow fleet of tankers that already evade American sanctions — meaning the oil is already largely spoken for, already bought and accounted for, and the practical impact on global prices is likely marginal. Analysts said as much immediately. Daniel Tannebaum, a former Treasury official: “I don’t see a scenario where Iranian crude is going to be imported into the U.S. Firstly, the available crude is a question as most barrels are already spoken for.”

The move followed the earlier easing of sanctions on Russian oil. In the span of one week, the administration that came to office on “maximum pressure” has eased sanctions on both of its principal adversaries to manage the economic consequences of a war it chose to start. Brent crude settled at $112 on Friday — a 54 percent increase since February 28. Gas nationally is approaching $4 a gallon. The S&P 500 is on course for its fourth consecutive week of losses, the first such streak since the Trump tariff introduction approximately one year ago.

The Jones Act waiver saves approximately 5 cents per gallon. The Iranian oil sanctions lift will likely save less. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve, which the administration failed to refill before the war began, is being tapped. The IEA’s record 400 million barrel release has not moved prices meaningfully. Every tool the administration has reached for has been a signal, not a solution. The signal being sent: the administration is more afraid of $4 gas than it is committed to maximum pressure.

II. The Retreat That Isn’t

On Friday evening, Trump posted on social media that he was considering “winding down our great Military efforts” in the Middle East. He said the United States was “getting very close to meeting our objectives.” He did not mention regime change. He did not mention removing Iran’s nuclear fuel stockpile. He softened the nuclear language to “making sure we are in a position to powerfully react” if Iran tries to reconstitute. He handed the Strait of Hormuz problem to the countries that use it — “If asked, we will help these Countries in their Hormuz efforts, but it shouldn’t be necessary once Iran’s threat is eradicated” — even as the United States has spent three weeks insisting the strait is a core American interest.

David Sanger, writing in the New York Times, identified the retreat with precision: the post omitted any reference to regime change through popular uprising, the objective Trump had set on the first night of the war. It omitted the requirement that Iran’s nuclear fuel stockpile must be removed from the country. It essentially turned the Hormuz problem over to others. The stated goals of the war have been quietly narrowed to fit the results the war has actually produced.

And yet. On the same day Trump posted about winding down, Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz said the US-Israeli campaign would “escalate significantly” this week. Netanyahu has said there is “more work to do.” The Pentagon’s $200 billion request is on its way to Congress. 2,500 additional Marines were confirmed Friday for deployment next month — on top of the 2,200 already steaming toward the Gulf. American warplanes and A-10 Warthogs are hunting Iranian fast-attack boats in the strait. Kharg Island and Isfahan remain under active planning consideration.

One concrete development cuts against the isolation narrative: twenty-two countries have now signed a statement signaling willingness to help secure Hormuz shipping. The UAE joined Saturday — the first Arab Persian Gulf nation to do so. The statement says countries are ready to “contribute to appropriate efforts to ensure safe passage” and that preparations are underway. This is the first coalition movement of the war, however tentative. Signaling willingness is a long way from warships in the water — the count of confirmed coalition vessels remains zero — but the number has moved. Iran’s decision to target Diego Garcia the same day Britain reversed its base position may have clarified minds across the Gulf in ways Trump’s demands did not.

The retreat is real in terms of stated objectives. The war is not retreating. These two facts are simultaneously true, which is perhaps the most accurate description of the Trump administration’s strategic position on Day Twenty-Four: the goals have shrunk to fit the results, while the military machine continues to expand. Tom Nichols called it a gambler chasing losses. The gambler is now also telling the casino he’s thinking about leaving — while putting more chips on the table.

III. Natanz

Satellite image showing damage at Natanz nuclear facility — Capturing the latest reported strike on Iran’s key enrichment site, with questions about underground impacts and the next potential target at Isfahan.

On Saturday morning, Iran reported that its nuclear facility near Natanz had been struck. Iranian state media confirmed the strike, said no radioactive leakage had been detected, and said there was no danger to nearby residents. The Israeli military denied responsibility. The US military could not immediately be reached for comment.

Natanz is the heart of Iran’s nuclear program. It has produced most of Iran’s nuclear fuel. US-Israeli strikes targeted Natanz earlier in the war, with satellite images showing destruction of entrances to an underground cavern holding centrifuges for uranium enrichment. The United States and Israel also struck it in June. The IAEA said Saturday it was investigating Iran’s report.

Netanyahu claimed Thursday that Iran no longer possessed the ability to enrich uranium or manufacture ballistic missiles. He provided no evidence. He said nothing about Iran’s stockpile of approximately 970 pounds of highly enriched uranium buried at Isfahan. Gabbard said the United States has “high confidence” on its location but declined to say more in open session. Experts and analysts say it is likely impossible to destroy the Iranian nuclear project entirely from the air — leaving Trump weighing whether to send ground forces for a dangerous mission to seize the uranium from inside Iran.

The Natanz strike — whoever carried it out — represents a potential threshold crossed. Previous strikes targeted entrances and above-ground infrastructure. The question now is whether the underground enrichment chambers themselves have been reached, and whether the highly enriched uranium stockpile at Isfahan is next. That operation, if it comes, would require ground forces. The Marines are approximately one week away.

IV. Diego Garcia

On Friday, Iran targeted the joint US-British military base on Diego Garcia — a coral atoll in the Indian Ocean, approximately 2,500 miles from Iranian territory. Britain’s defense ministry confirmed the attack was unsuccessful, without specifying what weapons Iran used. Experts noted that Iran has previously fired ballistic missiles at much shorter distances; targeting Diego Garcia suggests either new long-range capability or a significant expansion of Iran’s target envelope.

The attack came on the same day Britain reversed its earlier position and said American forces could use Diego Garcia to strike Iranian forces threatening shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. The reversal — Starmer had previously refused to allow British bases to be used for offensive operations — came under sustained pressure from Trump, who had called Starmer “no Winston Churchill” and repeatedly attacked Britain for staying out.

Iran’s foreign minister had warned Britain that providing bases constituted “participation in aggression.” Iran appears to have acted on that warning within hours of Britain’s announcement. The base was targeted before it could be used. The sequence — Britain grants access, Iran strikes the base, on the same day — is not coincidence. It is a direct message to every country considering compliance with American requests for support: participation has a cost.

V. The Trouble With Kharg

Illustration by The Atlantic. Sources: Ian Hitchcock / Getty; Orbital Horizon / Getty; Kaveh Kazemi / Getty.

Brynn Tannehill, writing in The Atlantic, has produced the most detailed analysis yet of the Kharg Island option — and it is not reassuring.

Kharg is eight square miles of rocky island in the northern Persian Gulf, 20 miles off the Iranian coast, home to approximately 20,000 people, mostly oil workers, and the departure point for roughly 90 percent of Iran’s oil exports. Taking it would give the United States a powerful bargaining chip — unable to export most of its oil, Iran would face the same economic pressure it is attempting to inflict on others by closing the strait. That is the theory.

The practice is considerably harder. Taking Kharg quickly is likely achievable — the 2,200 Marines on three warships approaching the Gulf are trained for exactly this kind of amphibious operation, and US strikes have already destroyed more than 90 targets on the island including bunkers for naval mines and missiles. Holding it is a different problem. The operation would likely exceed 15 days — about how long an expeditionary unit can operate without logistical support — and Kharg is 140 miles from American assets in Kuwait but extremely close to Iran’s coast. Iran could crater the runway with short-range ballistic missiles, cutting off air resupply. It could deploy its mosquito fleet of drone boats. It could use loitering munitions — the Iranian version of Russia’s Lancet — to hunt resupply vessels. And it could set the oil facilities on fire, as Saddam did with Kuwait’s oil fields in 1991, sickening US troops on a small island with nowhere to move upwind.

If the United States takes and holds Kharg successfully, Iran may be forced to negotiate on American terms. If it takes Kharg and cannot hold it, the United States will face the worst possible outcome: a visible military failure, mass casualties, and an exit under drone fire. Tannehill’s conclusion is measured but sobering. This administration has never attempted anything like this. A land seizure inside an adversary’s territory, held until the cessation of hostilities, is a different category of military commitment than aerial bombardment. It is a category this war has not yet entered. The Marines are days away from making that decision live.

U.S. Marines conducting amphibious beach landing — Representing the approaching Marine expeditionary force (2,200 en route, plus 2,500 more) and the high-risk potential operation against Kharg Island.

VI. The Lies

The New York Times editorial board published Friday under the headline “Trump Is Hiding the Truth About the War in Iran.” It is worth documenting what the board actually catalogued, because the pattern is now long enough to constitute a record.

Trump claimed the June attacks “obliterated” Iran’s nuclear program. Iran retains approximately 970 pounds of highly enriched uranium — potentially enough for 10 warheads. He said the US military had a “virtually unlimited supply” of high-end munitions. The Pentagon has had to withdraw weapons from South Korea to sustain operations. He said “nobody” believed Iran would retaliate by attacking Arab countries. Intelligence and military advisers had warned of precisely this scenario. He posted that Iran, “working in close coordination with the Fake News Media,” had spread fake videos of a burning American aircraft — while providing no examples of American outlets having done so, and threatening treason charges against them. He said on March 7 that the elementary school strike in Minab — which killed at least 175 people, most of them children — “was done by Iran.” The US military’s own preliminary investigation concluded an American missile struck the school. Trump has not retracted the statement.

Illustration by Rebecca Chew/The New York Times

The board’s historical frame is precise: the pattern echoes Vietnam and Iraq, where small lies grew into larger ones — My Lai, Haditha — and where the refusal to confront reality led to strategic errors that cost thousands of lives and decades of institutional credibility. Lying about war is not simply a moral failure, the board argues. It is a strategic one. It encourages the cabinet and the generals to mislead one another. It creates conditions in which deadly mistakes and war crimes become more common. It makes allies wary. “Starting a war is the most serious action that a political leader can take,” the board writes. “Presidents owe American service members and their families the truth about why they are being asked to fight.”

Jamelle Bouie, Michelle Cottle, and David French, on the Times Opinion podcast, added the dimension this series has been tracking from the beginning: Trump does not understand true believers. He has succeeded throughout his political career by watching people who claimed to hold firm convictions bend. Republicans bent. Allies bent. Adversaries were expected to bend. Iran has not bent. The Islamic Revolutionary Republic was forged in an eight-year war with Iraq that it fought in defiance of the world. “He’s making the very fundamental mistake that so many war leaders make,” French said, “which is totally misunderstanding the mind of the enemy.”

VII. The Midterm Clock

Lisa Lerer, writing in the New York Times, has mapped the political consequences of the war with the precision this series has been unable to provide because it has been focused on the battlefield. The picture is not good for Republicans.

Gas is approaching $4 a gallon nationally. Diesel is at $5.16. The S&P 500 has lost nearly 4 percent since February 28. The generic ballot for Democrats is at plus eight or nine points. Democratic energy around the November midterms is, by multiple measures, at historic highs.

The Republican message is “transitory” — a word the Biden administration used about inflation in 2021, and spent years being mocked for. “What we’re paying at the gas pump is a small price to pay in order to save millions of lives in the future,” said Representative Buddy Carter of Georgia, running for Senate in a state where gas prices have risen nearly 40 percent in a month. Vice President Vance told supporters in Michigan the prices “won’t last forever.”

Democrats, in private strategy documents circulating among House members, have been advised to focus less on the war’s legality and the Israel partnership and more on the three C’s Senator Schumer has been articulating in caucus meetings: chaos, costs, and corruption. The top-testing messages are economic: Trump is raising gas prices while cutting Medicaid and food assistance. The war touches all three C’s simultaneously.

Some MAGA voices are breaking. Representative Lauren Boebert: “I am tired of the industrial-war complex getting all of our hard-earned tax dollars.” A Republican operative in California: “Trump had one job and that was cost of living. He and Republicans have not been able to deliver on that.”

“The enemy always has a vote,” said Representative Crow of Colorado, a former Army Ranger. Iran voted. It voted to close the Strait of Hormuz. It voted to strike Ras Laffan. It voted to fire at Diego Garcia. Every vote Iran cast raised gas prices. Every raised gas price is a Republican problem in November. The midterm clock and the war clock are now the same clock.

VIII. Hamas on the Knife’s Edge

The war has created an impossible position for Hamas — and the way Hamas has navigated it reveals the fracture lines running through the entire axis of resistance.

Hamas is part of Iran’s network of regional militias. Iran is its largest military backer — funding, equipment, training. Hamas officials described Khamenei as a martyr and sent a congratulatory letter to his son Mojtaba on his appointment as supreme leader, encouraging continued support. At the start of the war, Hamas condemned the US-Israeli attack as “a direct assault on the entire region.”

Hamas also has strong ties with Qatar — which has hosted its senior political leadership in Doha for years and channeled hundreds of millions of dollars into Gaza for salaries, infrastructure, and humanitarian relief. Those officials in Doha have been receiving incoming missile alerts as Iran strikes the country that hosts them.

For two weeks, Hamas said nothing about Iran’s attacks on Qatar. On Day Fifteen, it issued its first and only statement on the subject — calling on Iran not to target regional countries. It did not mention Qatar by name.

“Hamas is walking on the knife’s edge,” said Iyad al-Qarra, a Palestinian analyst. “There are two currents in Hamas: one close to Iran, which is stronger, and another close to Arab states, which is weaker.” Both currents are visible in Hamas’s statements — and in its silences.

The knife’s edge is the war itself. Every day it continues, the two currents pull harder in opposite directions. Qatar has expelled Iranian diplomats. The countries Hamas depends on for political survival and the country it depends on for military support are now formally on opposing sides of an active shooting war.

IX. War’s Gravity

The New York Times published a chart this week under the headline “War’s Gravity: Many countries want nothing to do with the war in Iran. They’re pulled in anyway.” The orbital diagram shows approximately 30 countries arranged around the war’s center — some by choice, most by circumstance, a few desperately trying to maintain distance while being pulled inexorably closer.

At least 39 energy sites in nine countries have been damaged since February 28, a Times analysis found. At least 47 attacks confirmed through Friday, with more occurring seemingly every day. Kuwait struck two consecutive days. Ras Laffan damaged with a three-to-five year repair timeline. SAMREF at Yanbu hit. Habshan gas facilities suspended. An Israeli refinery in Haifa. An empty kindergarten in Rishon LeZion hit by an Iranian cluster munition. Debris inside Al Aqsa. A roof of the Greek Patriarchate 200 meters from the Church of the Holy Sepulcher. On Saturday, a drone struck the headquarters of the Iraqi National Intelligence Service in Baghdad’s Al Mansour district — an upscale neighborhood housing embassies and residential buildings. The war has now reached Iraq’s own intelligence infrastructure.

The Lebanon front has acquired its own strategic frame. Israel has been attacking bridges over the Litani River — the 90-mile waterway cutting across southern Lebanon — creating a choke point on Hezbollah’s supply lines while potentially establishing its own occupation boundary. UN Security Council Resolution 1701, which ended the 2006 war and was supposed to keep the area south of the Litani free of Hezbollah fighters, failed. A subsequent 2024 ceasefire also largely failed. Israel now appears to be enforcing its own version of a buffer zone by air and ground rather than waiting for international agreements that have twice collapsed. Many Lebanese fear the Litani becomes a new Israeli occupation line. The pattern of the past two decades suggests that fear is well-founded. Over a million people have been displaced in Lebanon. Many sleep in cars, on roadsides, and in schools. “Where do they want us to go?” said Hasnaa Ali Hashem, 55, sheltering in a Beirut stadium as rain poured through concrete columns and soaked makeshift tents.

The Strait of Hormuz was supposed to be too big to fail. “The classic line of everybody, including myself, up to this point, was always, it’s too big to fail, it will never close,” said Neil Crosby of Sparta energy analysis. “Western or allied naval powers will never allow this to happen.” Over 480 tankers are stranded on one side of the strait. Over 300 idle on the other. The strait that the Carter Doctrine declared a core American interest in 1980 has been closed for twenty-four days by the country the United States chose to attack.

The war has no coalition. No exit strategy. No congressional authorization. No definition of victory that has not already been revised downward. It has a $200 billion price tag, a retreating supreme leader who governs by committee, a junior ally with different objectives, and a domestic political crisis that tightens with every cent gas prices rise.

And it has gravity. Every country in that orbital chart is being pulled in. Most of them want nothing to do with it. The war does not care what they want.
