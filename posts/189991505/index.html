The Contribution Index

UAE

Part VIII: The Mirage

April 3, 2026

Underperformer #7

The United Arab Emirates hosted COP28 — the United Nations Climate Change Conference — in November 2023. The president of the conference was Sultan Al Jaber, who simultaneously served as CEO of the Abu Dhabi National Oil Company, one of the largest petroleum producers on Earth.

That single fact tells you nearly everything you need to know about the UAE’s relationship with global citizenship: it is a performance. Exquisitely staged, impressively funded, architecturally stunning — and fundamentally disconnected from the reality of its own choices.

The UAE is the world’s most sophisticated practitioner of nation-as-brand. Every skyscraper, every airline, every museum franchise, every international sporting event is calibrated to project an image of modernity, tolerance, and forward-thinking ambition. The branding is world-class. The reality behind it is what this essay measures.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 29 out of 50

The UAE has built gleaming cities from sand in a single generation. Abu Dhabi and Dubai have gone from fishing villages and trading posts to global metropolises within living memory. The wealth is real, the ambition is extraordinary, and the material outcomes for Emirati citizens — who constitute roughly 10 percent of the population — are genuinely impressive.

Life expectancy is approximately 78 years. Healthcare for citizens is well-funded and modern. Education investment has expanded rapidly, with significant spending on universities — including satellite campuses of NYU, the Sorbonne, and other Western institutions — that project intellectual ambition.

But democratic governance earns a 2. The UAE is an authoritarian federation of seven hereditary monarchies — emirate rulers whose positions are inherited, not elected. There are no meaningful elections, no independent judiciary, no press freedom, and no political opposition. The Federal National Council, the closest thing to a legislature, is a consultative body with no legislative power — half its members are appointed by the rulers, the other half selected through a tightly managed electoral process in which fewer than 5 percent of citizens are eligible to vote. Dissent is not tolerated. The cases of Ahmed Mansoor, a human rights activist sentenced to ten years for social media posts, and dozens of other political prisoners document what happens to those who challenge the system.

Gender equality earns a 4. The UAE has made significant strides in female education — women now constitute the majority of university graduates — and workforce participation has improved, particularly in government and professional sectors. The UAE was the first Gulf state to appoint female cabinet ministers, and its gender balance council has set targets for women in leadership. But legal frameworks remain discriminatory in significant ways. Personal status law, governed by Sharia, disadvantages women in marriage, divorce, inheritance, and child custody. LGBTQ+ rights are nonexistent — homosexuality is criminalized and punishable by imprisonment.

Migrant workers in the dormitory of a labour camp outside Abu Dhabi [Getty]

And then there is the question that defines the UAE’s human development score more than any other: whose human development are we measuring?

Roughly 90 percent of the UAE’s population consists of foreign workers — construction labourers from South Asia, domestic workers from the Philippines and East Africa, service industry employees from across the developing world. Many labour under the kafala sponsorship system, which ties a worker’s legal residency to their employer. Workers cannot change jobs without their employer’s permission. They cannot leave the country without an exit permit. Their passports are frequently confiscated upon arrival. They live in labour camps, often in conditions that human rights organisations have documented as systematically exploitative — crowded dormitories, wages delayed or unpaid, working conditions in extreme heat that cause injury and death.

The gleaming towers of Dubai were built by workers who often earned less in a month than a tourist spends on a single dinner in the restaurants those towers house. The Louvre Abu Dhabi was constructed by labourers whose working conditions were investigated by Human Rights Watch. The stadiums that host international events are maintained by workers whose wages and living conditions would be illegal in the nations whose teams play in them.

A nation’s human development score should reflect how it treats all the people within its borders, not just the 10 percent who hold citizenship. The UAE’s score of 29 reflects this reality. If it were scored on citizen outcomes alone, it would be significantly higher. That it is not scored that way is a deliberate choice of this framework — and one that the UAE’s own branding would prefer you not think about.

Environmental Stewardship: 20 out of 50

Emissions per capita earn a 2 — the UAE has one of the highest carbon footprints per person on Earth, roughly 20 metric tons per year. This is driven by energy-intensive desalination (the UAE desalinates more seawater than almost any nation), universal air conditioning in extreme heat, a petroleum-dependent transportation system, and an economy built entirely around hydrocarbon extraction and export. The irony of hosting a global climate conference while producing roughly 20 metric tons of CO2 per citizen would be comedic if the consequences were not catastrophic.

Renewable energy earns a 4. The Masdar City project — Abu Dhabi’s showcase sustainable city, launched in 2006 with ambitions to be the world’s first zero-carbon, zero-waste city — has been significantly scaled back from its original vision. The UAE has invested in solar capacity, including the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum Solar Park, one of the largest in the world. But these projects remain a small fraction of the national energy picture. The UAE’s economy is built on fossil fuel extraction — oil and gas revenues constitute roughly 30 percent of GDP and the overwhelming majority of export earnings — and no amount of solar panels changes that fundamental reality until the extraction model itself changes. A nation that installs solar panels on one side of the desert while pumping oil on the other is not transitioning. It is hedging.

Biodiversity earns a 4. The UAE possesses significant marine ecosystems — coral reefs, mangroves, and seagrass beds along its coastline — and has made some investment in marine protected areas. But coastal development has been aggressive, and the ecological impact of projects like the Palm Jumeirah — an artificial island built by dredging millions of tonnes of sand from the sea floor — has been documented as causing significant damage to marine habitats. The NEOM-adjacent developments along the Red Sea coast raise similar concerns.

Water and air quality earn a 5. Desalination provides clean drinking water but at enormous energy cost and with significant brine discharge that damages marine environments. Air quality in urban areas is affected by dust, construction particulates, and industrial emissions.

Masdar City is a CO2-neutral science town.

Systemic Behaviour: 15 out of 50

The UAE’s systemic behaviour scores are remarkably uniform — 3s across every parameter. This uniformity is itself telling: it reflects a nation that does a little of everything wrong without doing any single thing catastrophically, a kind of distributed mediocrity that is harder to critique than a single dramatic failure but no less consequential in aggregate.

Arms exports and conflict contribution earn a 3. The UAE has been a significant participant in the Yemen war — providing weapons, mercenaries, and direct military support to factions in a conflict that has produced one of the world’s worst humanitarian crises. It has supplied arms and military support to factions in Libya, Sudan, and the Horn of Africa. The UAE’s military footprint — including bases in Eritrea and Somaliland — extends well beyond its borders and well beyond any defensive rationale.

Tax haven and corruption facilitation earn a 3. Dubai has become an increasingly important node in global financial opacity. The city attracts wealth that prefers not to be scrutinised — Russian oligarchs, Iranian sanctions evaders, African kleptocrats, and international criminals have all found Dubai’s financial infrastructure accommodating. The Dubai International Financial Centre operates under its own legal framework, separate from UAE federal law, creating a zone of regulatory flexibility that attracts capital precisely because it asks fewer questions than London or New York. The golden visa programme and real estate market have been documented as channels for money laundering at significant scale.

Refugee and humanitarian contribution earns a 3. Despite possessing the financial resources to be among the world’s leading refugee resettlement nations — and despite being located in one of the world’s most refugee-producing regions — the UAE has accepted negligible numbers of refugees. Its position is that it hosts millions of foreign workers, which it characterises as a form of humanitarian contribution. The Contribution Index does not accept this framing: workers under the kafala system are labourers whose legal status is tied to their employers, not refugees offered protection and a path to stability.

The UAE claims significant philanthropic output through state-affiliated entities — the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum Global Initiatives reports annual disbursements exceeding $1 billion across education, healthcare, and humanitarian programmes. The figures are substantial and some of the programmes deliver genuine benefit. But when the ruling family is the philanthropic sector — when the same individual makes the policy decisions, controls the national wealth, directs the charitable spending, and manages the national brand — the distinction between contribution and reputation management dissolves entirely. Emirati philanthropy is soft power with a charitable receipt. It builds hospitals in the developing world while migrant workers who built Dubai’s skyline live in labour camps a twenty-minute drive from the Burj Khalifa. The giving is real. The intent is branding.

The Neighbour Test: 14 out of 50

The UAE’s Neighbour Test scores are uniform 3s with a 2 on consistency. This reflects a nation whose international engagement is overwhelmingly transactional — calibrated for commercial advantage, diplomatic positioning, and brand management rather than collective benefit.

Multilateral orientation earns a 3. The UAE participates in international institutions — the UN, OPEC, the Gulf Cooperation Council — but instrumentalises them for national advantage. Its hosting of COP28 was the most visible example: an international climate conference used as a platform for national branding and fossil fuel industry positioning rather than genuine climate leadership.

Response to global crises earns a 3. The UAE has contributed to some humanitarian efforts and disaster relief operations, but at levels that are modest relative to its extraordinary wealth and invariably aligned with diplomatic objectives rather than humanitarian need.

Treatment of allies earns a 3. The UAE’s relationships with its Gulf neighbours have been defined by the Saudi-led blockade of Qatar (2017-2021) — in which the UAE was a primary instigator — and by a broader pattern of competitive rather than cooperative regional engagement. Its military interventions in Yemen and Libya have been conducted with minimal coordination with international partners.

Consistency of values versus interests earns a 2 — the lowest score on the UAE’s card, and the number that captures the essence of the mirage. A nation that brands itself as modern and tolerant while criminalising homosexuality and exploiting migrant labour. That hosts a climate conference while pumping oil. That builds museums and universities as brand extensions while jailing human rights activists. That presents itself as a beacon of the future while operating a governance system unchanged since the eighteenth century.

The gap between the UAE’s brand and the UAE’s reality is not a failure of public relations. It is not an inconsistency that better messaging could resolve. It is the deliberate, sustained, and extraordinarily expensive construction of an image designed to attract investment, tourism, and international legitimacy while changing as little as possible about the fundamental distribution of power, wealth, and rights within the country.

Long-term stewardship earns a 3. The UAE has made genuine investments in future-oriented infrastructure — renewable energy research, space exploration, artificial intelligence. But these investments are calibrated to position the UAE as a post-oil economy rather than to contribute to global stewardship. The distinction matters: building a sovereign wealth fund for your own future is prudent financial management. Contributing to the global future — through development aid, technology transfer, refugee acceptance, emissions reduction — is stewardship. The UAE does the former. It has not yet demonstrated a sustained commitment to the latter.

The Editorial Conclusion

The UAE scores 78 out of 200. The seventh-ranked underperformer.

The mirage: a nation that has mastered the art of appearing more than it is. The architecture is real — the Burj Khalifa is the tallest building on Earth. The ambition is real — the UAE has sent a probe to Mars. The fossil fuel wealth is spectacularly real — Abu Dhabi’s sovereign wealth fund holds roughly $1 trillion.

But the contribution — to human rights, to environmental stewardship, to the international order, to the basic dignity of the workers who built its skyline — is the mirage. It shimmers beautifully in the desert heat, it draws millions of visitors from around the world, and it vanishes when you get close enough to examine what is actually there.

A nation of extraordinary wealth that criminalises dissent. A host of climate conferences that pumps oil. A builder of museums that exploits labourers. A champion of tolerance that imprisons homosexuals. The UAE has proven, beyond any doubt, that money can buy a brand. The Contribution Index asks whether it has bought anything more.

The scorecard says it has not. Not yet.
