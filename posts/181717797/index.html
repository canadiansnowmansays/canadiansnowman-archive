What Donald Trump Should Have Been Held Accountable For...But Never Was

Part 4: The Trump Foundation — A Personal Slush Fund Masquerading as Charity (1987-2019)

December 17, 2025

“Incredibly Effective Philanthropy”

When New York State Justice Salliann Scarpulla ordered Donald Trump to pay $2 million in damages in November 2019 for illegally using his charitable foundation for personal and political gain, Trump’s response was characteristically defiant. In a statement tweeted late Thursday evening, Trump claimed: “All they found was incredibly effective philanthropy and some small technical violations, such as not keeping board minutes.”

It was a lie. The court had just found a “shocking pattern of illegality” involving unlawful coordination with Trump’s presidential campaign, repeated and willful self-dealing, and systematic breaches of fiduciary duty. Trump had been forced to admit under oath to personally misusing charitable funds. His foundation was shuttered under court supervision. And his three adult children were ordered to undergo mandatory training to ensure “this type of illegal activity never takes place again.”

“Some small technical violations” did not capture the reality of what the Donald J. Trump Foundation had become: a personal slush fund that Trump used to pay his business’s legal settlements, buy portraits of himself, make illegal political contributions, and turn a charity fundraiser into a campaign event.

This is the story of how Trump weaponized charity for personal gain — and once again faced only minimal accountability for systematic lawbreaking.

The Foundation: Created for Good, Corrupted for Greed

The Donald J. Trump Foundation was established in 1987 to receive royalties from Trump’s book Trump: The Art of the Deal and distribute them to charitable causes. Trump’s three adult children — Donald Jr., Ivanka, and Eric — served on the board. For the first two decades, the foundation functioned relatively normally, distributing grants to various charities.

But the foundation’s character changed fundamentally after 2008. Trump stopped contributing his own money. From 2009 to 2015, Trump donated nothing to his own foundation. Instead, it subsisted largely on a $5 million gift from pro-wrestling moguls Vince and Linda McMahon. (Linda McMahon would later be appointed by Trump as head of the Small Business Administration — a detail that takes on new meaning in light of the foundation’s corruption.)

Despite not contributing his own money, Trump continued to use the foundation’s funds as if they were his personal checking account. The foundation became a vehicle for:

  • Settling legal obligations of Trump’s for-profit businesses

  • Purchasing items for Trump’s personal benefit

  • Making illegal political contributions

  • Coordinating with Trump’s presidential campaign

All of these activities violated fundamental charity law. None resulted in criminal prosecution.

Self-Dealing 101: Using Charity Money for Personal Benefit

The most blatant violations involved “self-dealing” — using charitable funds to benefit Trump personally. New York Attorney General Letitia James documented multiple instances:

The Portrait Purchase (2007) Trump used $10,000 from his foundation to purchase a six-foot-tall portrait of himself at a charity auction. The portrait was not donated to a museum or used for the foundation’s charitable purposes. Instead, it was hung on the wall of the Trump National Doral golf resort in Miami — Trump’s for-profit business property.

When The Washington Post inquired about this in 2016, Trump campaign spokesman Boris Epshteyn claimed Doral was doing the foundation a “favor” by storing “the charity’s art collection” on its wall. “Right, of course, he’s doing a good thing for his foundation,” Epshteyn said.

The court did not agree. Using charitable funds to buy a portrait of yourself that ends up decorating your for-profit business is textbook self-dealing. As part of the settlement, Donald Trump Jr. was forced to reimburse the foundation $10,000 for the portrait cost.

The Tim Tebow Helmet (2012) Trump used foundation funds to purchase a helmet signed by former NFL player Tim Tebow. Again, the helmet was not used for charitable purposes but for Trump’s personal benefit. The foundation was later reimbursed $12,000 for this and other sports memorabilia purchased at charity events.

The Champagne and Sports Memorabilia (Various) The settlement required Trump to reimburse the foundation $11,525 for sports paraphernalia and champagne purchased at a charity gala. These were personal purchases made with charitable funds.

Tax law expert Lloyd Hitoshi Mayer of Notre Dame Law School explained the problem: “In each case, the foundation gave money to real charities. How could it be bad for a foundation to give money to charity? But the key is that the money in each instance bought something for Trump in return. In other words, Trump took money from the Trump Foundation, but the end result of the transaction often benefitted him.”

This is precisely what self-dealing rules are designed to prevent. Charity assets must benefit charitable purposes, not the personal interests of the charity’s directors.

Using Charity to Pay Business Debts

Perhaps the most egregious self-dealing involved Trump using his foundation’s money to settle legal obligations of his for-profit businesses. The Washington Post‘s David Fahrenthold uncovered at least $258,000 in such payments:

The Mar-a-Lago Flagpole Settlement (2007) The Town of Palm Beach fined Trump’s Mar-a-Lago club $120,000 for erecting an oversized flagpole in violation of zoning rules. Rather than pay the fine from his business, Trump negotiated a settlement: he would donate $100,000 to a veterans charity, and the town would waive the fine.

The Trump Foundation — not Trump personally or his business — wrote the $100,000 check. In effect, Trump used charitable funds donated by others to settle a legal obligation of his for-profit business.

The Martin Greenberg Golf Hole-in-One Settlement (2010) Martin Greenberg claimed he won a $1 million prize for scoring a hole-in-one at a 2010 charity golf tournament at Trump National Golf Club. The club denied the award, arguing the hole was shorter than the required 150 yards. Greenberg sued.

On the day Trump and other parties told the court they had settled the case, the Trump Foundation made its first and only grant to the Martin B. Greenberg Foundation: $158,000. The Washington Post reported that the grant was directly linked to the legal settlement, using charitable funds to pay Trump’s business obligation.

To raise the money for this settlement payment, the Trump Foundation auctioned a prize of lifetime golf membership at Trump-owned golf courses. The winning bid brought a $157,000 donation to the Trump Foundation, which was then immediately paid out to settle Trump’s legal liability.

The D.C. Preservation League (2013) The Trump Foundation made a $5,000 grant to the D.C. Preservation League. According to The Washington Post, the nonprofit’s support helped the Trump Organization obtain rights to convert Washington D.C.’s historic Old Post Office Pavilion into the Trump International Hotel.

In acknowledgment of the donation, the Trump Foundation received advertising in the event programs — but the ads promoted Trump’s hotels rather than the foundation, potentially violating IRS self-dealing rules.

All of these transactions shared a common pattern: Trump used his charitable foundation to benefit his for-profit business empire, violating the fundamental principle that charity assets must serve charitable purposes.

The Illegal Political Contribution: The Pam Bondi Connection

In 2013, the Trump Foundation made a $25,000 contribution to a political committee supporting Florida Attorney General Pam Bondi’s re-election campaign. This violated federal law, which strictly prohibits 501(c)(3) charitable organizations from making political contributions.

The timing was suspicious. Bondi’s office had received complaints from Florida residents who felt defrauded by Trump University. Her office was considering whether to join New York’s investigation and lawsuit against Trump University.

Shortly after Trump’s foundation donated to Bondi’s political committee, Bondi’s office decided not to pursue the Trump University case.

In March 2016, Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) filed a complaint with the IRS about the potentially illegal donation. In September 2016, it was reported that the donation clearly violated laws against political contributions from nonprofit organizations.

Trump reimbursed the foundation with his own money and paid the IRS a $2,500 excise tax as a penalty — an astonishingly light punishment for what appeared to be an illegal political contribution potentially linked to dropping an investigation of Trump University.

Trump denied the donation was connected to the Trump University lawsuit, claiming it was for Bondi’s “performance as Attorney General.” But the timeline and circumstances raised obvious questions about quid pro quo corruption.

In November 2019, Bondi joined Trump’s White House staff to handle communications about the ongoing impeachment inquiry, prompting objections from ethics watchdogs. The woman who received Trump’s illegal political contribution and then declined to investigate Trump University became Trump’s defender during impeachment.

The Iowa Fundraiser: Turning Charity into Campaign Event (2016)

The centerpiece of the New York Attorney General’s lawsuit was a January 28, 2016, fundraiser Trump held in Des Moines, Iowa. Ostensibly organized by the Trump Foundation to raise money for veterans, the event was actually orchestrated by Trump’s presidential campaign as counter-programming to a Fox News Republican primary debate that Trump was boycotting.

The fundraiser was nationally televised. Trump stood on stage and gave away oversized checks to Iowa charities, with each check emblazoned with his campaign slogan: “Make America Great Again.” The foundation raised $2.8 million that evening.

But as the New York Attorney General’s investigation revealed, Trump’s presidential campaign — not the foundation — controlled the distribution of the funds. Campaign staff directed the timing, amounts, and recipients of the disbursements. The foundation had ceded control of over $2.8 million in tax-exempt charitable contributions to a political campaign.

This was flagrantly illegal. 501(c)(3) charitable organizations are strictly prohibited from participating in political campaigns. The foundation’s board never met to discuss or approve the campaign’s role in directing the fundraiser and fund distribution. Trump’s campaign simply took over a charitable event and used it for political purposes.

Justice Scarpulla’s ruling was unequivocal: “Mr. Trump’s fiduciary duty breaches included allowing his campaign to orchestrate the Fundraiser, allowing his campaign, instead of the Foundation, to direct distribution of the Funds, and using the Fundraiser and distribution of the Funds to further Mr. Trump’s political campaign.”

The judge found that Trump had allowed his political campaign to weaponize his charity for campaign purposes. The fact that the money eventually reached veterans charities didn’t excuse the illegality. As Scarpulla wrote: “Taking into consideration that the Funds did ultimately reach their intended destinations, i.e., charitable organizations supporting veterans, I award damages on the breach of fiduciary duty/waste claim against Mr. Trump in the amount of $2,000,000.”

The Board That Never Met

The Trump Foundation’s board of directors consisted of Trump, Donald Jr., Ivanka, and Eric Trump. According to the settlement documents, the board had not met since 1999 — a period of nearly two decades.

Without board oversight, Trump had complete control over the foundation’s funds. The board failed to provide oversight, failed to adopt legally required policies and procedures, and failed to prevent Trump’s systematic self-dealing and illegal political activity.

As part of the settlement, Trump admitted that these failures “contributed to the Foundation’s participation” in the illegal transactions documented by the Attorney General. His three children were ordered to undergo mandatory training on charitable organization duties — essentially remedial education on how not to break charity law.

The absence of board meetings wasn’t just poor practice; it enabled systematic illegality. Trump had created a charitable entity with no internal controls, no oversight, and no accountability — precisely the conditions that allowed him to treat it as a personal slush fund.

The Investigation: Journalism Meets Law Enforcement

The Trump Foundation’s illegal activities came to light primarily through the investigative journalism of The Washington Post‘s David Fahrenthold. Starting in 2016, Fahrenthold methodically documented the foundation’s spending, uncovering the pattern of self-dealing and questionable expenditures.

Fahrenthold’s reporting prompted law enforcement investigations. The New York State Attorney General’s office discovered systematic violations, including:

  • Failure to register properly in New York State

  • Self-dealing transactions benefiting Trump personally

  • Illegal political contributions

  • Failure to maintain basic corporate formalities

  • Unlawful coordination with Trump’s political campaign

In December 2016, shortly after Trump’s election, his attorneys attempted to dissolve the Trump Foundation voluntarily. But the New York Attorney General’s office blocked the dissolution, insisting it could not be dissolved while under investigation.

In June 2018, then-New York Attorney General Barbara Underwood filed a comprehensive lawsuit against the Trump Foundation and its directors, documenting what she called “a pattern of persistent illegal conduct over more than a decade.”

Trump’s Defense: Attack and Deflect

Faced with overwhelming evidence of illegality, Trump deployed his standard playbook:

Deny Reality: Despite admitting to the illegal conduct in settlement documents, Trump claimed on Twitter that investigators found only “incredibly effective philanthropy and some small technical violations.”

Attack the Prosecutor: Trump called Attorney General Letitia James’s lawsuit “politically motivated” and claimed it was part of a partisan witch hunt by “sleazy New York Democrats.”

Whataboutism: Trump repeatedly demanded investigations of the Clinton Foundation, attempting to deflect from his own foundation’s crimes.

Minimize and Reframe: Trump’s spokesperson characterized the $2 million payment as the foundation being “pleased to donate an additional $2 million” to worthy organizations — reframing court-ordered damages as voluntary charity.

Claim Victory: Despite being forced to dissolve his foundation under court supervision, admit to illegal conduct, and pay $2 million in damages, Trump claimed vindication because the court didn’t impose even harsher punitive damages.

None of these tactics changed the underlying facts: Trump had systematically violated charity law, used charitable funds for personal and political purposes, and only escaped criminal prosecution through a settlement agreement.

The Settlement: Minimal Accountability, Again

The final settlement, announced in November 2019, required:

Financial Payments:

  • $2 million in damages to be distributed among eight charities

  • $1.8 million in remaining foundation assets to be distributed to the same charities

  • Reimbursement of $11,525 for improperly purchased items

  • Total: approximately $3.8 million to be distributed to legitimate charities

Admissions:

  • 19 paragraphs of factual admissions by Trump and the foundation

  • Admission of personal misuse of funds

  • Admission of systematic failure of board oversight

  • Admission of illegal coordination with political campaign

Future Restrictions:

  • Trump barred from serving on any New York charity board without:

    • Majority of independent directors

    • Expert legal counsel in nonprofit law

    • Accounting firm oversight

    • Total ban on any self-dealing

  • Mandatory training for Trump’s children on charitable duties

Dissolution:

  • Foundation dissolved under court supervision

  • Corporate existence terminated in New York State

The Attorney General had originally sought to bar Trump from serving on any charity board in New York for ten years. He avoided that complete ban by agreeing to restrictions on future service.

Notably absent from the settlement: criminal charges.

Why No Criminal Prosecution?

The Trump Foundation case presented clear evidence of criminal violations:

Tax Fraud: Using charitable funds for personal benefit and business obligations violated tax laws governing nonprofits. These violations carry potential criminal penalties.

Election Law Violations: The $25,000 illegal contribution to Pam Bondi’s political committee violated federal election law. The IRS excise tax Trump paid was civil, not criminal, punishment.

Campaign Finance Violations: Using the Iowa fundraiser to benefit Trump’s political campaign violated multiple campaign finance and tax laws.

Wire Fraud: Using the foundation to solicit charitable contributions that were then misused could support wire fraud charges.

Conspiracy: If the Pam Bondi contribution was indeed linked to dropping the Trump University investigation, it could support corruption charges.

Yet no criminal charges were filed. The New York Attorney General referred the matter to the IRS, the Federal Election Commission, and the U.S. Department of Justice’s Public Integrity Section. None resulted in criminal prosecution.

Why not? Possible explanations include:

  1. Civil Settlement Achieved Goals: The foundation was dissolved, funds distributed to proper charities, and Trump forced to admit wrongdoing. Prosecutors may have felt these goals were achieved without criminal charges.

  2. Political Considerations: Criminally charging a sitting president presented complications. Prosecutors may have calculated that a civil settlement was more achievable.

  3. Resource Constraints: Criminal prosecutions require proof beyond reasonable doubt and enormous resources. Civil cases have lower burdens of proof.

  4. Lack of Traditional “Victim”: The money ultimately went to charities (albeit improperly). Prosecutors may have felt traditional fraud victims were absent.

  5. Complexity of Charity Law: Charity law violations are civil matters unless prosecutors can show willfulness and specific criminal intent.

But none of these explanations is fully satisfying. The evidence showed willful, repeated violations over more than a decade. Trump personally benefited financially. The foundation was used for political corruption. Criminal prosecution was warranted and feasible. It didn’t happen.

The Pattern Holds: Settlement Without Real Accountability

Once again, Trump faced consequences just serious enough to appear meaningful while avoiding the accountability that would truly deter future misconduct:

  • Financial: $2 million is real money, but barely meaningful for someone who claims to be a billionaire.

  • Admissions: Trump admitted wrongdoing in legal documents but immediately denied it publicly.

  • Restrictions: Future restrictions on charity service matter only if Trump ever serves on another charity board in New York.

  • Reputation: Trump’s supporters saw the case as political persecution; his reputation among them was unharmed.

Most critically, Trump faced no criminal charges. He didn’t go to trial. He didn’t face a jury. He didn’t risk prison time. He didn’t face disqualification from political office.

The Trump Foundation case demonstrates a fundamental asymmetry in American accountability systems. If a ordinary citizen uses a charity as a personal slush fund, makes illegal political contributions, and coordinates with a political campaign to turn a charitable fundraiser into a campaign event, they face criminal prosecution.

But if you’re wealthy enough to hire sophisticated lawyers and politically powerful enough to make prosecutors hesitate, you can settle with civil penalties while avoiding criminal accountability.

The Lasting Questions

The Trump Foundation case leaves disturbing unanswered questions:

The Bondi Connection: Was the $25,000 illegal contribution to Pam Bondi’s political committee linked to her decision not to investigate Trump University? The timeline is suspicious, but no investigation determined the truth.

The McMahon Contributions: Linda McMahon’s family donated $5 million to Trump’s foundation. She was later appointed to Trump’s cabinet. Was this patronage payback?

The Full Scope: The investigation focused on transactions from 2014 forward due to statute of limitations issues. What illegal activity occurred in the foundation’s first 27 years?

The IRS Response: The Attorney General referred the matter to the IRS for potential additional tax penalties. Did the IRS pursue any action beyond the small excise tax for the Bondi contribution?

The DOJ Decision: Why did the Department of Justice’s Public Integrity Section decline to bring criminal charges despite referrals from the New York Attorney General?

These questions matter because they reveal the limits of accountability for powerful Americans. The system “worked” in the sense that Trump’s foundation was shut down and some restitution was made. But the system failed to answer whether a sitting president committed criminal corruption involving his charity.
