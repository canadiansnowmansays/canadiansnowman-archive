America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 3: “It’s the Prices, Stupid — Why American Healthcare Costs 2100% More”

December 9, 2025

In 1992, James Carville famously distilled Bill Clinton’s presidential campaign to three points, the most memorable being: “It’s the economy, stupid.”

Thirty-three years later, America’s healthcare crisis requires similar clarity: It’s the prices, stupid.

Not access. Not quality. Not innovation. Not lawsuits. Not “lazy” patients or “greedy” doctors or “government bureaucracy.”

It’s the prices.

Americans pay more — vastly, incomprehensibly, bankruptingly more — for the same healthcare services that cost a fraction of the price everywhere else in the developed world. An MRI that costs $1,420 in the United States costs $280 in France. A vial of Humalog insulin that costs $300 in America costs $32 in Canada. A hip replacement that costs $40,364 in the US costs $7,731 in Spain.

This is Part 3 of Code Blue, our 10-part investigation into America’s 80-year failure to solve healthcare. In Part 1, we praised Senator Josh Hawley’s tax relief proposal while noting it doesn’t address the underlying cost disease. In Part 2, we documented nine years of Republican promises without delivery. Now we answer the fundamental question: Where does the money actually go?

The answer will make you angry. It should.

The $1.4 Trillion Question

Start with the big number: Americans spent $4.9 trillion on healthcare in 2023. That’s $14,885 per person — more than double the $7,371 average across comparable OECD countries.

If American healthcare costs matched the OECD average, Americans would save approximately $1.4 trillion annually. That’s $11,200 per family of four. Every year. Not as a one-time rebate, but as permanent savings.

Where does that $1.4 trillion go? Let’s follow the money.

Administrative Waste: The $680 Per Person Tax

The single largest difference between American healthcare and every other system isn’t medical — it’s clerical.

The United States spends $925 per capita on healthcare administration. Canada spends $551. France spends $400. The UK spends $268. The Netherlands spends $245.

That’s a $680 per capita difference between the US and comparable countries — $272 billion annually in pure administrative overhead.

What does that buy? Armies of billing specialists. Insurance claims adjusters. Prior authorization reviewers. Medical coders. Collections agencies. Hospital billing departments with more employees than clinical staff.

A 2020 study in the Annals of Internal Medicine found that American physicians spend 18% of their work time on administrative tasks — paperwork, billing codes, insurance authorization forms. Canadian physicians spend 9%. German physicians spend 7%.

The Cleveland Clinic employs 1,400 billing clerks. The Mayo Clinic has entire buildings devoted to nothing but processing insurance claims. A typical American hospital employs more billing staff than nurses.

Meanwhile, Medicare — America’s single-payer system for seniors — operates with 3% administrative overhead. Private insurance companies average 12%. Some private plans exceed 20%.

Why? Because Medicare doesn’t spend money on:

  • Marketing departments

  • Profit margins for shareholders

  • Executive compensation packages (UnitedHealth’s CEO made $23.5 million in 2022)

  • Claims denial departments designed to maximize rejection rates

  • Networks designed to confuse consumers and limit choice

This isn’t a bug in the American system. It’s a feature. Administrative complexity isn’t waste — it’s revenue protection. Every denied claim is money saved. Every prior authorization delay is a chance the patient will give up or pay out of pocket. Every confusing bill is an opportunity for surprise charges.

Drug Prices: The Insulin Scandal

Few things illustrate American healthcare pricing better than insulin.

Insulin was discovered in 1921 by Canadian researchers Frederick Banting and Charles Best at the University of Toronto. Banting sold the patent to the university for $1, declaring: “Insulin does not belong to me, it belongs to the world.”

In 2023, Americans paid an average of $300 per vial of Humalog insulin. Canadians paid $32. The medication is identical — manufactured by the same company (Eli Lilly) in the same factories.

Why the 840% price difference? Because they can.

The United States is the only developed country that prohibits the government from negotiating drug prices. Medicare was explicitly forbidden from negotiating prices until the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 — and even then, negotiations only cover 10 drugs initially, expanding slowly to 20 by 2029.

Every other developed country negotiates. The result:

Humira (arthritis medication):

  • United States: $77,586 per year

  • United Kingdom: $12,000 per year

  • Switzerland: $19,000 per year

Harvoni (Hepatitis C cure):

  • United States: $94,500 for 12-week treatment

  • Australia: $24,000 for 12-week treatment

  • Egypt: $900 for 12-week treatment

EpiPens (emergency allergy treatment):

  • United States: $600+ for 2-pack

  • Canada: $170 for 2-pack

  • France: $85 for 2-pack

Americans spend $1,443 per capita annually on pharmaceuticals. Canadians spend $939. Germans spend $939. The French spend $619. The British spend $466.

The pharmaceutical industry’s defense? “Research and development costs!” But R&D accounts for only 13-18% of drug company spending — less than marketing (25-30%) and far less than profits (15-20% margins).

If Americans paid Canadian prices for prescription drugs, they would save approximately $370 billion annually.

Hospital Pricing: The Chargemaster Con

In 2013, journalist Steven Brill published a Time magazine investigation that should have sparked revolution: “Bitter Pill: Why Medical Bills Are Killing Us.” His discovery? American hospitals use secret price lists called “chargemasters” that bear no relationship to actual costs, market rates, or rational pricing.

Examples from Brill’s investigation:

  • A chest X-ray that Medicare reimburses at $20.44 was billed to an uninsured patient at $283

  • A CBC blood test that costs $11 at wholesale was billed at $199.50

  • Tylenol pills that cost 1.5 cents each were billed at $1.50 per pill — a 10,000% markup

This isn’t ancient history. A 2020 study found:

  • Average U.S. hospital MRI cost: $1,119

  • Average French hospital MRI cost: $280

  • Average Australian hospital MRI cost: $215

Hip replacement:

  • United States average: $40,364

  • Spain: $7,731

  • France: $11,461

  • Australia: $16,122

Appendectomy:

  • United States average: $33,000

  • United Kingdom: $8,009

  • Netherlands: $6,385

  • Australia: $3,814

Giving birth (uncomplicated vaginal delivery):

  • United States average: $10,808

  • United Kingdom: $2,641

  • Spain: $1,950

  • France: $3,541

The reason? In every other country, prices are regulated, negotiated, or set by government agencies. In America, hospitals can charge whatever they want — particularly to uninsured patients and out-of-network providers.

Even insured Americans aren’t protected. “Surprise billing” — where an in-network hospital uses out-of-network anesthesiologists or radiologists — trapped millions of patients in five- and six-figure bills until Congress finally banned the practice in 2022.

Physician Compensation: The Doctor Paradox

Here’s where the conversation gets uncomfortable: American doctors are paid significantly more than doctors anywhere else in the world.

Primary care physicians (general practitioners):

  • United States: $218,173 average

  • Germany: $154,126

  • Canada: $125,000

  • France: $95,585

  • United Kingdom: $86,607

Specialists:

  • United States: $316,000 average

  • Germany: $210,000

  • Canada: $180,000

  • Australia: $247,000

Before physician readers close this essay in fury, let’s be absolutely clear about what this means — and what it doesn’t.

American doctors aren’t overpaid for the system they work in. They’re compensating for:

  • Medical school debt averaging $200,000-$300,000: German medical school is free. Canadian medical school costs roughly $15,000 per year. French medical school costs even less. American doctors start their careers $200,000-$300,000 in debt before they see their first patient.

  • Malpractice insurance premiums 5-10 times higher: An OB-GYN in Germany pays roughly $15,000 annually for malpractice insurance. An American OB-GYN pays $75,000-$200,000 depending on location. These costs are baked into American physician compensation.

  • Administrative burden requiring extensive support staff: American physicians spend 18% of their work time on paperwork, billing codes, and insurance authorization forms. Canadian physicians spend 9%. German physicians spend 7%. That administrative burden means American doctors either work longer hours or see fewer patients — both reduce effective income.

  • Shorter patient visits: The average American primary care appointment is 12-15 minutes. In Germany, it’s 20-30 minutes. In France, it’s 15-20 minutes. American doctors are compensated for volume because the system demands volume.

Here’s what German doctors get that American doctors don’t:

  • Graduated medical school debt-free

  • Work 45-50 hour weeks instead of 60-70 hours

  • Spend 20-30 minutes per patient instead of 12 minutes

  • Handle minimal insurance paperwork

  • Face negligible malpractice risk

  • Enjoy higher job satisfaction and lower burnout rates

German primary care physicians earn $154,126 annually. But they’re not “underpaid” — they’re appropriately compensated within a system that doesn’t saddle them with debt, paperwork, and malpractice insurance costs.

The uncomfortable systemic truth:

You cannot reduce American healthcare spending from $14,885 per capita to $7,371 per capita without addressing physician compensation. But the solution isn’t “pay doctors less” — it’s “fix the system that requires them to be paid more.”

If you:

  • Made medical school free or low-cost (like Germany)

  • Reduced malpractice insurance to European levels through tort reform

  • Eliminated 90% of insurance authorization paperwork

  • Allowed 20-30 minute patient appointments instead of 12-minute assembly-line medicine

...then American physician compensation could align with international norms without anyone taking a pay cut relative to their actual working conditions.

A surgeon performing a $40,000 hip replacement isn’t pocketing $40,000. Most goes to the hospital, administrators, insurance processing, and various middlemen. The surgeon might net $2,000-$3,000 for a complex procedure requiring years of training.

The real scandal isn’t that American doctors earn $218,000. The scandal is that a system this expensive delivers such mediocre outcomes while burning out the physicians trapped inside it.

American doctors report lower job satisfaction than peers in universal healthcare systems. Not because they’re overpaid, but because they’re drowning in paperwork, fighting insurance companies, rushing through 12-minute appointments, and experiencing moral injury from turning away patients who can’t pay.

Any honest healthcare reform must address physician compensation — not by cutting salaries in isolation, but by fixing the broken system that inflates costs at every level.

That’s the conversation neither party wants to have. Because it requires admitting that “just pay doctors less” is political fantasy, while “restructure medical education, malpractice law, and insurance billing” is hard legislative work.

The Middleman Economy

Between you and your doctor sits an entire economy of middlemen, each extracting revenue:

Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs): Three companies (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control 80% of prescription drug processing. They negotiate “rebates” from drug manufacturers, set formularies, and determine which pharmacies are “in network.” Their profits: $30+ billion annually.

Medical billing companies: Entire firms exist solely to help doctors navigate insurance billing codes. Cost: $20-$40 billion annually.

Prior authorization companies: Insurance companies outsource treatment approval to third-party firms that deny 13-18% of requests, requiring doctors to spend hours fighting for medically necessary care.

Hospital consolidation profits: Since 2010, over 1,400 hospitals have merged, creating regional monopolies. The result? A 2020 RAND study found consolidated health systems charge private insurance 224% of Medicare rates — more than double what Medicare pays for identical services.

Revenue cycle management companies: These firms help hospitals maximize billing to “recapture revenue.” Translation: finding ways to upcode, unbundle, and maximize charges.

None of this exists in other developed countries. Not because they’re anti-business or anti-innovation, but because their systems are designed around care delivery, not revenue extraction.

The Quality Myth

The standard American defense: “We pay more, but we get better care.”

The data says otherwise:

Life expectancy:

  • United States: 78.9 years

  • Canada: 82.7 years

  • Switzerland: 83.4 years

  • Japan: 84.4 years

Infant mortality (per 1,000 live births):

  • United States: 5.4

  • Canada: 4.3

  • Germany: 3.1

  • Japan: 1.8

Maternal mortality (per 100,000 live births):

  • United States: 23.8

  • Canada: 11.2

  • Germany: 4.0

  • United Kingdom: 7.0

Avoidable deaths: The Commonwealth Fund found the US ranked last among 11 high-income countries in “mortality amenable to healthcare” — deaths that could have been prevented with timely, effective medical care.

Americans aren’t getting better outcomes. They’re getting bankrupted for mediocre results.

The Path Forward

The good news: We know exactly how to fix this. Other countries have solved these problems using different approaches — some with single-payer systems, some with regulated private insurance, some with hybrid models.

In Part 4, we’ll examine what works: How Switzerland achieved universal coverage with competing private insurers. How Germany combines public and private insurance with strict price controls. How Singapore uses mandatory health savings accounts. How Canada delivers care through provincial single-payer systems.

The solutions exist. The question isn’t “Can it be done?” The question is: “Why haven’t we done it?”

For 80 years, Americans have been told healthcare reform is impossible, too complex, or un-American. Meanwhile, Americans pay double what every other developed country pays, for worse outcomes, while going bankrupt from medical bills that would cost a fraction of the price across any border.

It’s not impossible. It’s not too complex. And it’s not un-American to demand that a medical system serve patients instead of extracting maximum revenue.

In Part 4, we’ll show you exactly how other countries did it — and why their solutions could work in America.
