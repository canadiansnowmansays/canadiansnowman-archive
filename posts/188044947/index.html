Tesla 2026

Transition and Execution

Part 8: An Open Letter to Elon Musk — Where Is Tesla on Sodium-Ion and Solid-State?

February 16, 2026

Elon Musk on stage at the original Battery Day 2020

Dear Elon,

I’m a Canadian investigative journalist, a Tesla shareholder, and the author of two series you probably haven’t read — America’s Next Industrial Revolution, a ten-episode analysis of the autonomous vehicle industry, and Tesla 2026: Transition and Execution, a seven-part examination of your January 28 earnings call and what it revealed about the company’s pivot from automaker to AI and robotics platform.

In that series, I analyzed the bombshells — the end of the Model S and Model X, the $20 billion-plus commitment to autonomous and robotics infrastructure, the fleet deployment strategy. I presented the bull case and the bear case. I examined the merger speculation. I called 2026 Tesla’s year of execution.

I thought the series was finished. It isn’t.

Because in the weeks since that earnings call, something has happened in battery technology that your transformation thesis cannot afford to ignore. And Tesla’s silence on it is disappointing.

Over the course of these series, I have defended Tesla’s strategic vision more often than I have criticized it. I have argued that your vertical integration model is the most consequential manufacturing innovation since Henry Ford’s assembly line. I have made the case, with data, that your Megapack business may eventually eclipse your automotive division in both revenue and margin. I am writing today not to criticize, but to ask questions. Specific questions. The kind of questions I suspect your engineering team is already asking internally, and that your shareholders deserve to hear answered publicly.

They concern two battery chemistries — sodium-ion and solid-state — that are no longer theoretical. They are in production vehicles. They are in grid-scale storage systems. They are being manufactured at scale by your competitors. And Tesla, as far as anyone outside Giga Texas can tell, has no public position on either one.

I would like to understand why.

The Pattern: What LiDAR Teaches Us

Before I ask the specific questions, I need to acknowledge a pattern. Because you and I both know the industry has heard this kind of concern before.

In 2019, you told investors that LiDAR was “a crutch” and predicted that companies relying on it would find themselves trapped at a local maximum. Your engineers reportedly disagreed. The industry disagreed. Waymo was spending billions on sensor fusion. Your competitors were stacking laser arrays on their rooftops like Christmas ornaments.

You bet on cameras. Vision only. And for several years, it looked like the most expensive wrong answer in automotive history. FSD timelines slipped. Intervention rates were high. Waymo pulled ahead in actual autonomous deployment.

Then something happened. FSD version 12 arrived with an end-to-end neural network that threw away the rule book. Version 13 pushed intervention-free driving past 400 miles. Your Austin robotaxi service launched. The camera-only approach didn’t just survive — it became the architecture that made scalable autonomy possible, precisely because it avoided the sensor dependency that made Waymo’s system difficult to scale beyond geofenced cities.

The LiDAR bet wasn’t obviously right at the time. It may still not be entirely settled. But it taught the industry something important about how you think: you are willing to endure years of criticism for a conviction that the physics and the economics will eventually vindicate.

So when I ask about sodium-ion and solid-state, I am asking with genuine respect for the possibility that you see something the rest of the industry doesn’t. But I am also asking because the LiDAR bet had a cost — years of delayed FSD deployment, billions in development spending, a Waymo head start that Tesla is still working to close. And the cost of being wrong on battery chemistry, in 2026, could be considerably larger.

The Sodium-Ion Question

On February 5, 2026, CATL and Changan unveiled the world’s first mass-production passenger vehicle powered by a sodium-ion battery — the Nevo A06, in Inner Mongolia. I covered the technical details extensively in my Energy Challenge series (Essay #18, “The Battery Revolution the United States Is Choosing to Miss”), so I will not repeat them here. The short version: CATL’s Naxtra chemistry has reached energy density parity with lithium iron phosphate, retains 91 percent capacity at minus 40 degrees Celsius, and is made from salt. MIT named sodium-ion one of its ten Breakthrough Technologies for 2026. Global shipments are projected to exceed 1,000 GWh within four years. CATL has publicly declared a “dual-chemistry era.”

Changan Nevo A06 powering through heavy snow in Inner Mongolia—the visual proof of 91% capacity at –40°C.

The question is not whether sodium-ion works. It does. The question is whether it has a place in Tesla’s ecosystem.

Here is why I think it should.

Megapack. Your energy storage business deployed 46.7 GWh in 2025, up 48 percent year-over-year. Revenue hit $12.8 billion. Gross margins reached nearly 30 percent — double what you earn selling cars. The Megapack is, by any measure, one of the great industrial success stories of the decade. But it runs entirely on lithium-ion cells, sourced primarily from CATL and LG. Sodium-ion’s advantages — lower cost per cycle, longer cycle life (CATL has demonstrated over 6,000 full cycles at 100 MWh scale), zero thermal runaway risk, no lithium or cobalt in the supply chain — are tailor-made for stationary storage. Grid operators don’t care about energy density. They care about cost per cycle, safety, and longevity. Sodium-ion wins on all three.

Large Tesla Megapack installation array

Cold weather. This one is personal to me. I live in Toronto, where the Great Lakes moderate the worst of Canadian winters — we complain about minus 15, not minus 40. But your mother was born in Regina, Saskatchewan, where minus 40 is not a hypothetical. It is January. And every EV owner from Saskatoon to Stockholm to Sapporo knows what extreme cold does to a lithium-ion battery — range collapses, charging slows, the vehicle you paid a premium for becomes a lesser version of itself. Sodium-ion’s cold-weather performance — 91 percent capacity retention at minus 40 — is not an incremental improvement. It is a category change. Tesla sells vehicles in these markets. Tesla operates Supercharger networks in these markets. A sodium-ion Powerwall optimized for northern climates would be genuinely transformative for millions of households. The Haldeman family knew Saskatchewan cold. The technology to solve it now exists. It is made from salt.

Supply chain independence. Your Shanghai Megafactory is producing Megapacks with CATL cells. Your LFP supply chain runs through China. In a world of escalating tariffs, export controls, and supply chain fragility, a battery chemistry built on sodium, aluminum, and iron — materials available domestically in abundance — offers strategic resilience that lithium cannot match.

The scaling argument doesn’t apply here. I suspect your first instinct will be the one you have applied to many emerging technologies: it works in the lab, but can it scale? With sodium-ion, the answer is already yes — and the reason is the one that should matter most to you. Sodium-ion cells can be manufactured on existing lithium-ion production lines with minimal retooling. CATL has stated publicly that their sodium-ion lines are drop-in conversions of LFP equipment. The chemistry is different. The factory is nearly identical. This is not solid-state, where new manufacturing processes must be invented. This is a chemistry swap on equipment Tesla already owns and operates. CATL shipped 9 GWh of sodium-ion cells in 2025. They are projecting over 1,000 GWh within four years. Their sodium-ion grid storage system has achieved costs of four cents per kilowatt-hour. The manufacturing problem you would rightly raise for other chemistries has, in this case, already been solved — by your own primary cell supplier, on production lines that look almost exactly like yours.

On cost: I will be honest about the numbers, because credibility matters more than advocacy. Wood Mackenzie estimates sodium-ion cell costs at approximately $59 per kWh in 2025, compared to $52 for LFP. Sodium-ion is not cheaper yet on a per-kWh basis. It is close, and IRENA projects it will reach $40 per kWh at scale — but it is not there today.

However, the metric that matters for grid storage is not cost per kilowatt-hour. It is cost per kilowatt-hour per cycle. CATL has demonstrated over 6,000 full cycles at the 100 MWh scale for sodium-ion, compared to the industry standard of roughly 4,000 for LFP. When you amortize the purchase price across cycle life, sodium-ion is already competitive — and its trajectory is steeper, because it hasn’t yet benefited from the economies of scale that drove LFP from $169 per kWh pack-level in 2020 to $108 today. The raw material cost floor tells you where this ends: sodium carbonate trades at $200 per ton. Lithium carbonate trades at $15,000. That is not a gap that closes.

Your own supplier is already there. LG Energy Solution, which makes your 2170 cells, has partnered with Sinopec to build a sodium-ion pilot line in Nanjing. Panasonic, your other major supplier, is watching the space closely. The chemistry is not fringe. It is entering your supply chain whether you adopt it or not.

Peak Energy, an American startup backed by a national laboratory, is already deploying grid-scale sodium-ion storage in the United States. Energy Vault has locked in 1.5 GWh of their cells for AI data center applications. The market is moving.

So the questions:

Is there a sodium-ion Megapack in development? If not, why not? If the answer is that lithium-ion cost declines will outrun sodium-ion’s advantages before sodium-ion achieves scale, I want to understand the math. Because at 70 percent lower cathode material costs and a supply chain that doesn’t depend on geopolitically contested minerals, the math I’m seeing favors at least a parallel program.

Has Tesla evaluated sodium-ion for Powerwall, specifically for cold-climate markets? If so, what was the conclusion?

And if CATL is right about the dual-chemistry era — if both chemistries will coexist, serving different applications — is Tesla’s position that they are wrong? Or is it that Tesla simply hasn’t gotten around to it yet? Because there is a significant difference between a strategic decision and an oversight.

The Solid-State Question

The second question is harder, because the timeline is longer and the technology is less mature. But it is no longer theoretical, and the competitive landscape has shifted dramatically in the past twelve months.

Verge TS Pro motorcycle with Donut Lab solid-state pack at CES 2026

In January 2026, Donut Lab — a Finnish spinoff of Verge Motorcycles — deployed all-solid-state batteries in production vehicles. Not prototypes. Production motorcycles, on public roads, available for purchase. The specs: 400 Wh/kg energy density, charges in under ten minutes, 600 km range, 100,000-cycle longevity. These are the numbers the industry said were five years away. They are here now.

In February, FAW Group installed a lithium-rich manganese semi-solid-state battery exceeding 500 Wh/kg in a test vehicle — roughly double the energy density of today’s best lithium-ion. Range: over 1,000 kilometers. GAC completed China’s first automotive-grade all-solid-state production line, with cells exceeding 60 Ah capacity. China will release its first national standard for solid-state EV batteries in July 2026.

Stellantis and Factorial have validated 375 Wh/kg solid-state cells that charge from 15 to 90 percent in 18 minutes. Stanford published a silver coating breakthrough in January that improves crack resistance fivefold. KAIST demonstrated that inexpensive divalent anion doping can significantly improve ion transport in solid electrolytes.

The industry consensus has crystallized: semi-solid-state in production vehicles now, at 300–350 Wh/kg. Small-series fully solid-state by 2027. Mass production by 2030.

Every major competitor has a public solid-state roadmap. Toyota. Mercedes-Benz. BMW. Hyundai. Stellantis. BYD. Samsung SDI. CATL has entered trial production of 500 Wh/kg cells. Panasonic — your own supplier — announced mass solid-state battery production beginning this fiscal year, initially targeting humanoid robots and drones.

Robots. Like Optimus.

Tesla’s public position on solid-state appears to be silence.

Now — I will say what I think you would say, because I want you to know I have considered it carefully.

Solid-state does not scale. Not yet. And the manufacturing challenges are genuinely harder than sodium-ion, harder than the 4680, possibly harder than anything the battery industry has attempted at automotive volume. Ceramic electrolytes are brittle. Sulfide-based materials are moisture-sensitive. Existing gigafactories are not optimized for solid-state processes. UC Riverside researchers warned in 2025 that even minor defects can cause catastrophic failure under fast-charging conditions — and unlike liquid electrolytes, solid interfaces cannot self-heal. Bob Galyen, former chief technology officer of CATL itself, told IEEE Spectrum that solid-state costs are approximately where conventional lithium-ion was a decade ago, and that most solid-state companies fall short on at least three of his five “golden rules” of battery commercialization. Most serious analysts do not expect gigafactory-scale production before 2030. Donut Lab’s motorcycle batteries are impressive, but they are small-series production — hundreds of units, not hundreds of thousands. The validation process alone — factory construction, prototype runs, customer duty cycle testing, defect discovery — takes years.

You know this better than anyone. You lived it with the Model 3. You called it “manufacturing hell.” And the 4680 experience reinforces the point — a battery that was supposed to halve costs by 2024 is still, in early 2026, struggling with yield rates and charging curves. If scaling a new format of an existing chemistry proved this difficult, imagine scaling an entirely new chemistry with manufacturing processes that don’t exist yet at automotive volume.

I take this argument seriously. It is the strongest case for Tesla’s current approach to solid-state, and I will not pretend otherwise.

But I would make two counterpoints.

First, the same scaling objection was made about lithium-ion itself in 2012. It was too expensive, too hard to manufacture at volume, too dependent on immature supply chains. The company that proved the skeptics wrong was Tesla. The Gigafactory was built on the conviction that manufacturing scale would drive costs down faster than critics believed possible. If that conviction applied to lithium-ion then, why does it not apply to solid-state now — especially when Toyota, Samsung, CATL, and your own supplier Panasonic are all investing billions in precisely that scaling challenge? And two recent developments have shifted the timeline. The UK firm Ilika demonstrated in February 2025 that its solid-state cells can be manufactured using standard roll-to-roll equipment already deployed in existing gigafactories — the same equipment your factories use. And GAC’s completed automotive-grade production line, combined with China’s forthcoming national standard for solid-state EV batteries due in July, signal that at least one major industrial power has decided the scaling problem is solvable and is building regulatory infrastructure around that assumption.

Second, and more specifically: Panasonic has announced that its initial solid-state production will target humanoid robots and drones — applications requiring high energy density in compact, lightweight form factors where the premium cost of solid-state is justified. That description fits Optimus exactly. If Tesla’s long-term vision depends on a humanoid robot that must operate untethered for extended periods, the battery chemistry that offers 400–500 Wh/kg in a safe, non-flammable package is not a luxury. It is a requirement. And your own supplier is building it — for someone. The question is whether that someone will be you.

None of which erases the scaling challenge. It is real. But Tesla turned the 4680 from “manufacturing hell” into a real product — the same team could do it again with solid-state, but only if it starts now. Scaling is a reason to begin, not a reason to wait.

Which brings me to the 4680 — because it illustrates the cost of starting late on a manufacturing problem.

The 4680 program has consumed enormous engineering resources and capital since Battery Day in September 2020. The dry electrode process — acquired through Maxwell Technologies for the specific purpose of reducing manufacturing costs — has been a multi-year struggle. Reports of 70–80 percent cathode losses in trial production. Cells collapsing in use. The Cybertruck’s poor charging curve. The structural battery pack abandoned for the Model Y. The L&F supply contract written down by 99 percent.

And yet — in January 2026, Tesla announced it was putting 4680 cells back in certain Model Y variants, citing dry electrode manufacturing breakthroughs. The program isn’t dead. It may finally be turning a corner.

So perhaps the calculation is that the 4680 investment is too deep to abandon and too close to working to diversify. Perhaps Tesla believes that the path forward is not a chemistry change but a manufacturing process improvement — that dry electrode lithium-ion at scale will deliver cost advantages that solid-state cannot match for a decade. Perhaps the NC05, NC20, NC30, and NC50 cell variants planned for this year represent a diversification strategy within lithium-ion that Tesla considers sufficient.

If so, I want to understand the reasoning. Because here is what I see from the outside:

The 4680 was announced in 2020 as the key to a $25,000 Tesla. Six years later, that car doesn’t exist. The Cybertruck, the primary 4680 consumer, is selling at a run rate of roughly 20,000–25,000 units annually against a production capacity of 250,000. The charging curve remains inferior to the 2170 cells from Panasonic. The cost savings that were supposed to halve battery costs have not materialized at anything close to the originally promised scale.

Meanwhile, a startup in Finland has solid-state batteries in production motorcycles. A Chinese state-owned automaker has semi-solid-state cells at 500 Wh/kg in test vehicles. Your own battery supplier is prioritizing solid-state for the exact category of product — humanoid robots — that Tesla considers central to its long-term vision.

If solid-state delivers on even half of what the current data suggests — 400–500 Wh/kg, sub-10-minute charging, no flammability risk, 100,000-cycle life — what happens to the competitive position of a company that bet everything on optimizing the previous generation of chemistry?

The Steelman: Why Tesla Might Be Right

I want to be fair. Because there is a case — a strong case — for Tesla’s current approach, and intellectual honesty requires stating it clearly.

Tesla’s lithium-ion ecosystem is genuinely extraordinary. The vertical integration — from mining rights to cell manufacturing to pack design to vehicle architecture to grid storage software — represents billions of dollars and a decade of engineering. No other company on earth has built anything comparable. Switching chemistries is not flipping a switch. It is retooling factories, requalifying suppliers, redesigning thermal management systems, rewriting battery management software. It is billions more in capital expenditure during a period when Tesla is already planning over $20 billion in capex for this year alone.

Lithium-ion prices are still falling. BloombergNEF reported $108 per kWh at the pack level at the end of 2025, down roughly 40 percent in five years. If that decline continues — and Chinese overproduction suggests it will — the cost advantage of sodium-ion narrows and the timeline for solid-state cost competitiveness lengthens. LFP pack prices for stationary storage fell as low as $70 per kWh in 2025, down 45 percent from 2024. Wood Mackenzie does not expect sodium-ion to reach cost parity with LFP until approximately 2035. That is a long time to wait for a chemistry that is not yet cheaper than the one Tesla already uses at scale.

And on solid-state, the scaling skepticism is not just Tesla’s — it is the industry’s. As I noted above, even the former chief technology officer of the world’s largest battery company has said he hasn’t seen cost numbers “even close to competing with liquid-state, lithium-ion batteries.” When the man who ran CATL’s engineering shares your skepticism, that is not stubbornness. That is engineering judgment.

The Megapack business is working. It doesn’t just work — it is Tesla’s most profitable division, with 30 percent margins and a $4.96 billion deferred revenue backlog. The Megapack 3 delivers 5 MWh per unit, up from 3.9 MWh. The Houston Megafactory will add 50 GWh of annual capacity when it comes online in late 2026. Combined with Lathrop and Shanghai, Tesla will have 133 GWh of annual manufacturing capacity. That is an empire built on lithium-ion, and it is growing.

And there is the possibility — which I cannot rule out — that Tesla’s internal battery research is further along on next-generation chemistries than anyone outside the company realizes. Tesla acquired Maxwell Technologies. Tesla has partnerships with Panasonic and LG. Tesla has the data from billions of miles of real-world battery performance. If anyone has the engineering depth to make a late, decisive move into sodium-ion or solid-state at the right moment, it is probably Tesla.

I acknowledge all of this.

The Policy Headwind

I will keep this brief, because this letter is about technology, not politics. The current policy environment — rescinded emissions regulations, phased-out residential storage credits, tariffs disrupting battery cell supply chains — makes new battery investment harder for every company, Tesla included. But sodium-ion actually helps in this environment. It reduces dependence on imported lithium. It uses domestically abundant materials. It is resistant to the supply chain disruptions that tariffs create. A sodium-ion Megapack would be more American, not less. If there is a battery chemistry that aligns with both Tesla’s commercial interests and the current political reality, it is sodium-ion.

What I Am Asking

I am not asking Tesla to abandon lithium-ion. That would be absurd. I am not suggesting the 4680 program was a mistake — the dry electrode breakthrough, if it holds, could still deliver on the cost promises that justified the investment.

I am asking whether Tesla has room for parallel technologies. Because the company that pioneered the Gigafactory, that proved utility-scale battery storage could work, that built the Megapack into a $12.8 billion business — that company, of all companies, should be leading the dual-chemistry era, not watching it happen from the sideline.

CATL is running lithium-ion and sodium-ion in parallel. BYD is doing the same. LG is building sodium-ion pilot lines while continuing to supply your 2170 cells. The industry is not choosing one chemistry. It is choosing both.

Is Tesla choosing neither? Or is Tesla choosing to wait?

If it is the former, I want to understand the reasoning. If it is the latter, I want to understand the timeline. Because the window in which Tesla can enter the sodium-ion market as a leader rather than a follower is measured in quarters, not decades. And the window for solid-state — where every major competitor has a public roadmap and Tesla has silence — is closing faster than the industry expected even twelve months ago.

I have covered your company’s strategic decisions with what I hope is fairness and analytical rigor. I have defended the camera-only bet when it was unpopular. I have argued that the Megapack business is undervalued. I have made the case that Tesla’s manufacturing model is the template for American industrial competitiveness.

I am asking these questions because I believe the answers matter. Not just for Tesla’s shareholders, but for the trajectory of the energy transition itself. Tesla helped create the battery storage industry. Whether it continues to lead that industry depends on the decisions being made right now, in this quarter, about which chemistries to pursue and which to ignore.

The rest of the world is not waiting. China has a sodium-ion passenger vehicle on the road. Finland has solid-state batteries in production motorcycles. Your own suppliers are building pilot lines for chemistries you haven’t publicly acknowledged.

The camera bet worked because the physics was on your side and the economics eventually followed. The question I am asking — the $64,000 question — is whether the physics and the economics are on your side this time too.

Or whether, this time, the rest of the world is right.

I have a suggestion.

In September 2020, you held Battery Day and unveiled the 4680 — a vision for halving battery costs through dry electrode manufacturing and vertical integration. It was bold. It was specific. It gave investors, engineers, and competitors a clear picture of where Tesla was going and why.

Six years later, the battery landscape has changed more dramatically than anyone predicted that day. Sodium-ion has gone from laboratory curiosity to production vehicles. Solid-state has gone from perpetual promise to production motorcycles and 500 Wh/kg test vehicles. Your Megapack business has grown from a side project into a $12.8 billion division with 30 percent margins. The 4680 itself has had a journey no one anticipated — years of struggle followed by what may finally be a breakthrough.

It is time for Battery Day 2.

Not a vague reassurance that Tesla is “looking at everything.” A specific, Tesla-style presentation that lays out your multi-chemistry roadmap — or explains, with data, why you don’t need one. Show us the cost curves. Show us how the 4680 fits alongside — or instead of — sodium-ion for Megapack and solid-state for Optimus. Tell us what your engineers have concluded and why.

You changed the industry’s understanding of what was possible with Battery Day 2020. The industry needs that clarity again — and so do your shareholders. Your competitors are making their bets in the absence of any signal from the most vertically integrated battery manufacturer on earth. That silence is not strategic ambiguity. It is a vacuum. And vacuums get filled by other people’s narratives.

If anyone has the engineering depth to solve hard battery manufacturing problems, it is Tesla. You spent six years proving that dry electrode manufacturing was incredibly difficult and then did it anyway. So do what you do best. Get on stage. Show us the plan.

You are not afraid of the big stage. This would be a worthy use of it.

Respectfully,

Stephen Gordon Tesla shareholder Publisher, CanadianSnowmanSays Toronto, Canada
