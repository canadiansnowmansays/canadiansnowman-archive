The Evils of Foreign Commentary, Oh My Oh My

Part 1: The Exposure and The Double Standard

November 25, 2025

Part 1: The Exposure and The Double Standard

I. The Accidental Exposure

On November 22, 2025, X’s “About This Account” feature did something unexpected: it told the truth. For users who bothered to click through to the profile details of prominent MAGA accounts, the platform revealed where these supposedly all-American patriots were actually located. The results were startling.

MAGANationX, with its 392,000 followers and relentless stream of Trump content, operated from Eastern Europe. Dark MAGA broadcasted from Thailand. MAGA Scope’s 51,000 followers received their talking points from Nigeria. America First’s 67,000-strong audience got their content from Bangladesh. Most remarkably, IvankaNews—boasting over 1 million followers—pumped out Trump family propaganda from Nigeria.

These weren’t isolated cases. Dozens of accounts that had positioned themselves as voices of American conservatism turned out to be foreign operations. Some of these accounts were regularly amplified by Elon Musk himself, their engagement-optimized content perfectly calibrated to trigger the platform’s recommendation algorithm. The economic incentive was clear: X’s creator payment program meant American engagement dollars flowed to wherever the most inflammatory content originated, regardless of geography or authenticity.

The Centre for Information Resilience documented accounts that had stolen photographs of European models, creating entirely fictional personas of supposed Trump supporters. These weren’t Americans expressing political views. They were foreign nationals operating profitable deception businesses, their true identities and locations hidden behind stolen faces and fabricated biographies.

When the exposure triggered backlash, X briefly removed the location feature, then restored it under pressure. But the damage to the narrative was done. For years, these accounts had operated with impunity, their foreign origins invisible to the tens of millions of Americans who consumed and shared their content. The “About This Account” feature hadn’t created a problem—it had simply made visible what was always there.

II. The Reagan Ad Rage

Two months earlier, Ontario Premier Doug Ford launched a $75 million advertising campaign featuring Ronald Reagan’s 1987 radio address opposing tariffs. The ad was straightforward: it showed Reagan explaining why trade barriers hurt both countries, with text identifying it as a message from the Government of Ontario about the importance of the Canada-US trade relationship.

Every element was transparent. The ad identified its sponsor. It stated its purpose. It used publicly available historical footage of an American president explaining American policy. The Reagan Foundation complained about “selective editing,” but fact-checkers confirmed the ad accurately represented Reagan’s anti-tariff position—he had indeed argued that tariffs hurt consumers and damaged relationships with trading partners.

Donald Trump’s response was immediate and explosive. He declared the ad “FAKE” and “fraudulent” despite its accurate use of Reagan’s words. He terminated ALL trade negotiations with Canada. When that wasn’t enough, he later increased tariffs by 10% as explicit punishment for Ontario’s advertisement.

Think about that progression. A foreign government aired a transparent political advertisement using an American president’s own words to argue against tariffs. In response, the incoming American president:

  • Accused Canada of fraud

  • Ended trade negotiations affecting $2.7 billion in daily bilateral trade

  • Imposed additional tariffs that economists projected would slow Canadian GDP growth by 0.9-1.0%

  • Called it foreign interference in American politics

Premier Ford eventually suspended the ad after speaking with Prime Minister Carney. But the damage to Canadian interests was already done, and the double standard was stark: transparent Canadian government advocacy was treated as an act of war, while covert foreign accounts posing as Americans faced no consequences whatsoever.

The contrast couldn’t be clearer. Ontario said “we’re the Government of Ontario, and here’s why tariffs hurt both our countries.” The foreign MAGA accounts said “I’m America First from Real America, USA” while actually being a profit-motivated operation in Bangladesh. One triggered trade retaliation. The other got amplified by the platform owner.

III. The Crucial Distinction

The Canadian Security Intelligence Service defines foreign interference as “clandestine, deceptive, or coercive activities directed by foreign states or their agents to advance their strategic objectives.” The crucial word is “clandestine.” Not foreign. Clandestine.

The Ontario ad wasn’t clandestine. It was literally impossible to be more transparent. Government of Ontario. Purpose clearly stated. Historical footage clearly sourced. Anyone watching it knew exactly who was speaking and why.

The foreign MAGA accounts were the definition of clandestine. Hidden locations. Stolen identities. Fabricated personas. Concealed profit motives. Audiences had no idea they were consuming content created by foreign nationals with economic incentives to maximize engagement through inflammatory content.

This distinction matters because legitimate political commentary—including foreign political commentary—serves a democratic function. When Bob Rae, Canada’s former UN Ambassador, appeared on Fox News defending Canadian sovereignty, viewers knew they were hearing from a Canadian diplomat. When Charlie Angus, who just retired after 21 years as MP for Timmins-James Bay, called Trump a “malignant narcissistic slug” on MeidasTouch, everyone understood they were hearing a Canadian politician’s perspective.

The problem isn’t that foreigners have opinions about American politics. The problem is when foreigners pretend to be Americans while operating profitable disinformation businesses. The problem is deception, not geography.

Canadian political scientist David Frum provides an interesting test case. Born in Toronto, Frum became a naturalized American citizen in 2007 after serving as President George W. Bush’s speechwriter (he coined the phrase “axis of evil”). As a staff writer for The Atlantic, he’s been one of the most prominent conservative critics of Trump, writing books including “Trumpocracy” and “Trumpocalypse” that warn of authoritarian tendencies.

Frum’s Canadian origins don’t delegitimize his analysis of American politics. His transparency about his background, his American citizenship, his openly stated conservative philosophy, and his documented expertise create legitimacy. Readers know who Frum is, where he comes from, and what analytical framework he brings. That’s the opposite of the foreign MAGA accounts’ deception model.

IV. The Canadian Stake

Americans sometimes express puzzlement at the intensity of Canadian attention to American politics. Why do you care so much? It’s not your country.

The numbers explain it. Canada sends 75% of its exports to the United States. The countries share the world’s longest land border and the largest bilateral trading relationship, worth $2.7 billion in daily trade. More than 400,000 people cross that border every day for work, commerce, and family.

When Trump threatened 25% tariffs, Canadian economists projected GDP growth would slow from 2.0% to 0.9-1.0%. When Trump joked about Canada becoming the 51st state, it wasn’t funny in a country that’s spent 157 years defining itself as not-American. When Trump threatened to terminate USMCA, he was threatening the framework that governs $2 trillion in annual trilateral trade.

Canadians don’t watch American politics as entertainment or out of idle curiosity. They watch it the way you’d watch someone making decisions about your mortgage, your job security, and your health insurance—because the decisions directly affect your life.

But there’s also a cultural dimension that reveals the hypocrisy. Critiquing American politics is something of a Canadian national pastime—though being Canadian, we generally do it politely. We’ve been at it for decades, often through comedy that lays the groundwork for critical analysis.

Lorne Michaels, the creator and executive producer of Saturday Night Live, is from Toronto. For fifty years, SNL has been America’s primary vehicle for political satire, teaching Americans to laugh at their own politicians and institutions. The show’s Canadian creator established the cultural permission structure that makes critical political commentary acceptable, even celebrated. Mike Myers satirized American culture through Wayne’s World and Austin Powers. Dan Aykroyd, Jim Carrey, Seth Rogen, Catherine O’Hara, Eugene Levy—Canadian comics have spent generations making Americans laugh at themselves, often with sharper edge than Americans might deploy against their own institutions.

This isn’t unique to Canada. John Oliver from Britain and Trevor Noah from South Africa have made entire careers as foreigners offering comedic commentary on American politics and culture. The foreign perspective isn’t a bug—it’s often a feature, providing critical distance that can be harder to achieve from inside the cultural bubble.

Here’s the hypocrisy: comedy lays the groundwork, creates the space, legitimizes the critique. For five decades, foreign voices have been teaching Americans to examine their politics critically, to question their leaders, to see patterns and absurdities that insiders might miss. Nobody accuses Lorne Michaels of foreign interference when SNL skewers American politicians—including, one suspects, Trump himself, for whom the show appears to be appointment viewing second only to Jimmy Fallon. Nobody calls John Oliver a foreign agent when he dissects American policy failures.

But remove the laugh track, add citations and evidence, make the same critical observations in systematic analysis rather than satire, and suddenly the foreign perspective becomes “interference.” The critique that’s celebrated when wrapped in jokes becomes threatening when presented with documentation. It’s the same foreign voice, the same critical distance, the same observations—just without the comedy to make it palatable.

This creates a spectrum of Canadian voices analyzing American politics, from retired statesmen to professional journalists to citizen analysts. Bob Rae, as former UN Ambassador and former Ontario Premier, brings diplomatic expertise. Charlie Angus brought 21 years of parliamentary experience and viral media appearances before his recent retirement. CNN’s Daniel Dale, who grew up in suburban Toronto, developed his systematic fact-checking methodology covering the Ford brothers before applying it to Trump. David Frum brings insider Republican expertise and conservative intellectual credibility. Dean Blundell, working closely with Trump insiders like Lev Parnas, brings investigative journalism and access to primary sources.

And then there are citizen analysts like me—publishing under my name, from my location, with transparent methodology and extensively documented analysis across 289 essays and approximately 500,000 words since May 2025.

What unites this spectrum—from diplomat to journalist to citizen analyst—is transparency. Canadians analyzing American politics aren’t hiding who they are, where they’re from, or why they’re paying attention. We’re not stealing identities or concealing profit motives. We’re watching American politics because American politics directly affects Canadian lives, and we’re honest about that.

The question isn’t whether foreigners should comment on American politics. The question is whether they should do so honestly or deceptively. Ontario’s Reagan ad was honest. The foreign MAGA accounts are deceptive. That’s not a close call.
