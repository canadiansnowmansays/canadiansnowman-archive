The Great Canadian Trade War

The Great Border Security Inversion

How America's Gun Violence Crisis Became Canada's Cross-Border Problem While Fentanyl Theater Justified Trade War

September 3, 2025

The next installment in The Great Canadian Trade War series

By CanadianSnowmanSays
September 2025

Welcome back to The Great Canadian Trade War series. Over the past months, we've documented how Trump justified massive tariffs over 43 pounds of fentanyl (representing 0.2% of America's drug problem), how the USMCA actually protects 94-95% of Canadian exports, and how Canada finally corrected its mathematically absurd retaliation strategy.

Today, I want to share data that reveals something even more striking: while Canada was solving America's tiny fentanyl problem, American guns were flooding north in record numbers—with virtually no policy attention.

When I saw this morning's CBSA X post with the above pic showing 19 firearms seized from U.S. residents in just one week in Ontario, I couldn't help but think: this single week's seizure received less attention than 43 pounds of fentanyl that somehow justify a trade war.

It's a perfect example of the old saying: don't throw stones when you live in a glass house.

The Fentanyl Theater: A Problem That Barely Exists

The Numbers That Started a Trade War

In July 2025, I documented how Donald Trump's trade war with Canada was built on mathematical absurdities. The headline justification—Canadian fentanyl flooding America—crumbled under basic arithmetic:

  • 43 pounds: Total fentanyl seized at the Canadian border in 2024

  • 21,889 pounds: Total fentanyl seized at all U.S. borders in 2024

  • 0.2%: Canada's actual contribution to America's fentanyl seizures

  • 96.6%: Mexico's contribution to the same seizures

For every pound of fentanyl from Canada, 500 pounds came from Mexico. Yet Canada faced 35% tariffs while Mexico maintained the standard 25% rate.

The Success Story Nobody Wanted to Hear

More remarkably, Canada's response to these concerns was swift and effective:

  • $1.3 billion invested in border security

  • 10,000 additional border agents hired

  • 97% reduction in fentanyl seizures from December 2024 to January 2025

  • Creation of a "Fentanyl Czar" position

By any reasonable metric, Canada solved the problem it was asked to address. Yet rather than acknowledging success, the Trump administration escalated tariffs and expanded justifications to include dairy policies (that Trump himself had negotiated), digital taxes, and eventually Palestinian recognition.

The Context Manipulation: How 43 Pounds Became a "Crisis"

U.S. Customs and Border Protection data reveals how statistical manipulation can create false narratives:

  • 2021: 22 pounds seized at northern border

  • 2022: 14 pounds seized (36% decrease)

  • 2023: 2 pounds seized (91% decrease from 2021)

  • 2024: 43 pounds seized (2,050% increase from 2023)

Here's the sleight of hand: if you ignore the 2021-2023 decline and focus only on 2023-2024, you get a terrifying "2,050% increase!" But this is what statisticians call "cherry-picking the baseline." Canada went from being essentially a non-factor (2 pounds) to being a very small factor (43 pounds), while still representing only 0.2% of America's total fentanyl problem.

It's like complaining that your neighbor's leaky faucet went from 1 drop per hour to 20 drops per hour while ignoring the burst dam flooding your basement. The percentage increase sounds massive, but the actual scale remains microscopic compared to Mexico's 21,148 pounds.

The Real Crisis: American Guns Flooding North

The Explosion Nobody Discusses

While politicians focused on 43 pounds of fentanyl, a genuine security crisis was unfolding in the opposite direction. Canadian firearms seizures tell a story of escalating threat, though comprehensive year-by-year data reveals the challenges of tracking cross-border weapons flows.

Available Data Points:

  • 2016-2020 average: 436 seizures annually (656 individual firearms)

  • 2021: 407 seizures (1,121 individual firearms) - notable increase in items per seizure

  • 2022: 581 firearms seized from U.S. sources specifically

  • 2024: 850 firearms seized (Jan-Oct), with 750 from U.S. sources

Data Limitations: CBSA reporting methods have evolved over time, with some years measuring "seizure events" while others count individual firearms. Regional data is sometimes available when national totals are not, and different agencies use different reporting periods.

The Clear Trend: Despite reporting inconsistencies, the documented increase from 2022 (581 from U.S.) to 2024 (750 from U.S.) represents a 29% increase in just two years. More importantly, the pattern of U.S. sourcing is consistent and overwhelming.

The Toronto Reality Check

Toronto Police Service data provides the starkest illustration of America's role in Canada's gun violence:

  • 717 crime guns seized in Toronto in 2024

  • 88% traced to U.S. sources

  • 515 handguns seized, with 91% traced to U.S. sources

  • Consistent pattern: 70-88% of Toronto crime guns traced to U.S. since 2018

This isn't statistical noise—it's a documented pattern of American weapons systematically undermining Canadian public safety.

The Broader Drug Flow: A 118% Increase

Another border bust: #CBSA officers at the Blue Water Bridge port of entry prevented $43.7M of cocaine from entering Canada from the U.S.

The weapons trafficking parallels broader contraband flows from America to Canada:

  • 2022: 3.8 million grams of drugs seized coming from U.S.

  • 2024: 8.3 million grams seized (118% increase)

  • Comparison: Canada seizes more drugs from U.S. (8.3 million grams) than U.S. seizes going south (5.26 million grams total drug seizures at northern border)

The directional flow contradicts the entire premise of American criticism. Canada is intercepting more American contraband than America is intercepting Canadian contraband.

The Net Security Threat Assessment

When you combine all cross-border contraband flows, the data reveals who represents the actual security threat to whom:

Net Assessment: Canada intercepts 58% more American drugs (8.3M vs 5.26M grams) than America intercepts Canadian drugs, while America provides 750+ firearms annually that undermine Canadian public safety. The country being criticized for "lax border security" is actually doing the heavy lifting on both sides of the border.

Additional Context:

  • 88% of Toronto crime guns traced to U.S. sources

  • 23,721 Canadian border apprehensions (1.5% of U.S. total)

  • $1.3 billion Canadian investment in border security

  • 97% reduction in Canadian fentanyl seizures achieved

The Mathematical Devastation

Scale of Problem vs. Scale of Response

The numbers reveal policy responses inversely proportional to actual threats:

Trump's Fentanyl "Crisis":

  • 43 pounds seized (0.2% of U.S. total)

  • Policy response: 35% tariffs on $762 billion trade relationship

  • Response multiplier: 10 million times larger than the problem

America's Gun Contribution to Canadian Crime:

  • 750+ firearms annually crossing north

  • 88% of Toronto crime guns from U.S. sources

  • Policy response: Minimal to non-existent

  • Response multiplier: Effectively zero

The Cost-Benefit Analysis

Canada's Investment in Solving America's Concerns:

  • $1.3 billion in border security spending

  • 10,000 additional border agents

  • 97% reduction in fentanyl seizures achieved

  • Result: Escalated criticism and higher tariffs

America's Investment in Solving Canada's Gun Violence:

  • Unknown: No dedicated cross-border gun violence prevention programs identified

  • No reduction: 88% of Toronto crime guns still trace to U.S. sources

  • Result: Continued weapons trafficking with no policy response

The mathematical imbalance is stark: Canada solved a problem representing 0.2% of America's fentanyl seizures while receiving escalated punishment, yet America's contribution to 88% of Toronto's gun crime receives no policy attention.

The Policy Inversion: When Success Breeds Punishment

The Perverse Incentive Structure

Canada's experience reveals a dangerous precedent: solving problems identified by a larger neighbor can result in punishment rather than recognition. The timeline demonstrates this perverse relationship:

December 2024 - January 2025:

  • Canada achieves 97% reduction in fentanyl seizures

  • Trump announces escalation to 35% tariffs

  • Justification expands beyond original concerns

Throughout 2024:

  • Canadian firearms seizures from U.S. increase 29% over 2022 levels

  • American policy attention to weapons trafficking: minimal

  • Trade criticism intensifies despite Canada addressing original concerns

The Glass House Principle

The phrase "don't throw stones when you live in a glass house" captures the fundamental hypocrisy at play. America's glass house is constructed from:

  • 47,000 annual gun deaths (2023 data)

  • $280 billion annual cost of gun violence

  • Leading cause of death for American children and teens

  • Documented weapons trafficking undermining neighbor's safety

Yet the stones being thrown are tariffs justified by a drug problem that:

  • Represents 0.2% of America's fentanyl seizures

  • Has been reduced 97% through Canadian cooperation

  • Never justified the scale of economic response deployed

International Implications

This inversion carries broader implications for international cooperation. If solving problems identified by a larger neighbor results in escalated punishment rather than recognition, smaller countries face impossible choices:

  1. Ignore the problem: Face criticism for inaction

  2. Solve the problem: Face escalated criticism and expanded justifications

  3. Retaliate proportionally: Be accused of aggression despite mathematical justification

Canada chose option 2, achieving remarkable success in addressing American concerns, only to discover that success provided new pretexts for criticism rather than resolution.

The Human Cost: When Numbers Represent Lives

Both Drugs and Guns Kill

The fundamental tragedy underlying this analysis is that both drugs and guns represent genuine threats to human life. Fentanyl has devastated American communities, contributing to over 70,000 overdose deaths annually. Canadian gun violence, while lower than American rates, still represents families destroyed and communities traumatized.

American Fentanyl Crisis:

  • 70,000+ overdose deaths annually involving synthetic opioids

  • Devastating impact on families and communities

  • Legitimate concern deserving serious policy attention

Canadian Gun Violence (exacerbated by U.S. weapons):

  • 289 firearm homicides in Canada (2023)

  • 88% of Toronto crime guns traced to U.S. sources

  • Rising violence linked to American weapons availability

The Policy Malpractice

The tragedy is not that America expressed concern about fentanyl—legitimate problems deserve attention. The tragedy is that using minimal Canadian contributions to justify massive trade disruptions while ignoring America's role in Canadian gun violence represents a form of policy malpractice that makes both countries less safe.

Effective policy would address both concerns proportionally:

  • Continued cooperation on fentanyl interdiction

  • Serious attention to cross-border weapons trafficking

  • Policy responses scaled to actual threat magnitude

  • Recognition of success when problems are addressed

Current policy ignores mathematical reality:

  • 10-million-to-1 response ratios

  • Success punished with escalation

  • Larger problems ignored while smaller ones justify major disruptions

  • Evidence-based cooperation replaced with theater

The Broader Pattern: Selective Enforcement of Border Concerns

The Weaponization of Statistics

The pattern suggests statistics are being weaponized rather than analyzed. Small Canadian contributions to American problems become justifications for major policy responses, while large American contributions to Canadian problems disappear from policy discourse entirely.

This selective use of data serves political rather than analytical purposes:

  • Politically useful statistics get amplified beyond their significance

  • Politically inconvenient statistics get ignored despite their magnitude

  • Policy responses become disconnected from evidence

  • International cooperation suffers as partners lose trust in good-faith analysis

Solutions: Toward Evidence-Based Border Security

Proportional Policy Responses

Effective border security requires responses proportional to actual threats:

For Fentanyl:

  • Maintain current cooperation (97% reduction achieved)

  • Focus resources on Mexico border (96.6% of problem)

  • Recognize Canadian success rather than escalating demands

  • Continue information sharing without trade linkage

For Firearms:

  • Serious bilateral cooperation on weapons trafficking

  • Enhanced information sharing on crime gun traces

  • Joint task forces targeting trafficking networks

  • Policy coordination on legal market regulations

The Cooperation Framework

Both countries benefit from evidence-based cooperation:

Shared Interests:

  • Reducing drug trafficking from all sources

  • Preventing weapons from reaching criminal networks

  • Maintaining economic integration while ensuring security

  • Building public trust in border management

Mutual Benefits:

  • American consumers benefit from Canadian resources at fair prices

  • Canadian communities benefit from reduced American weapons trafficking

  • Both benefit from integrated supply chains and economic cooperation

  • Regional security improves through coordinated law enforcement

Beyond the Glass House

Moving past the glass house dynamic requires acknowledging mutual vulnerabilities:

American Acknowledgment:

  • Constitutional and policy choices create externalities for neighbors

  • Weapons trafficking undermines other countries' public safety

  • Trade pressure on statistical noise damages cooperation on real issues

  • Success in addressing concerns should be recognized, not penalized

Canadian Acknowledgment:

  • Fentanyl concerns are legitimate even when Canadian contribution is small

  • Border security requires constant vigilance and investment

  • Trade relationships can be affected by security concerns

  • Communication about successes and challenges should be proactive
