The Rise and Fall of Matt Gaetz

The Rise and Fall (then Pivot) of Matt Gaetz

Part 3: The Fall and Pivot

November 19, 2025

Matt Gaetz, then a Florida congressman, outside the Capitol last year. A bipartisan House Ethics Committee investigation determined there was substantial evidence that he had sex with the 17-year-old in exchange for money.Credit...Haiyun Jiang for The New York Times

The Ethics Committee

The House Ethics Committee began investigating Gaetz in April 2021, examining allegations of sexual misconduct, illicit drug use, acceptance of improper gifts, and obstruction.

Over the following years, investigators reviewed nearly 14,000 documents and interviewed more than two dozen witnesses. They subpoenaed Venmo for Gaetz’s payment records. They obtained testimony from multiple women who attended parties where Gaetz was present—parties that featured drugs, sex, and cash payments.

The committee documented payments totaling over $90,000 from Gaetz to twelve women between 2017 and 2020 that investigators concluded “were likely in connection with sexual activity and/or drug use.”

One woman, paid more than $5,000 by Gaetz between 2018 and 2019, told investigators that “99 percent of the time that (Representative Gaetz and I) were hanging out, there was sex involved.”

The Venmo transactions included notes like “Gift,” “Refreshments,” “being awesome,” and “being my friend.” Payment amounts ranged from $100 to over $700. Women testified that they frequently had to text Gaetz asking to be paid: “I don’t want to be that girl, but can you please go ahead and pay me what we agreed upon?”

Gaetz also inquired about “party favors” and “vitamins” via text—code words for drugs.

In September 2024, Gaetz informed the Ethics Committee he would no longer cooperate with their investigation. When asked to provide a list of sexual partners, he told reporters the committee was “just nosy.”

The Nomination

On November 13, 2024—nine days after Donald Trump won the presidency—Trump announced he would nominate Matt Gaetz to serve as Attorney General of the United States.

The reaction in Washington was immediate and visceral.

“I was shocked,” said Senator Susan Collins.

“I don’t think this is a serious nomination for attorney general,” said Senator Lisa Murkowski.

Senior Justice Department officials called the pick “insane” and “laughable.” One official said Gaetz was “the least-qualified nominee in the history of the department.”

Gaetz would have been the least qualified Attorney General nominee in modern history. He had never worked as a prosecutor, never served as a judge, never held a senior position at any level of the Justice Department. His legal experience consisted of a brief stint at a small Florida law firm before entering politics—and a suspension from the Florida Bar in 2021 for failing to pay a $265 fee.

But qualifications weren’t the point. Trump wanted someone who would “end Weaponized Government”—by which he meant someone who would use the Justice Department to pursue his enemies and protect his friends. Gaetz had spent eight years proving he would do exactly that.

Hours after the announcement, Gaetz resigned from Congress, effective immediately. The move had a strategic purpose: by leaving the House, he would no longer be subject to the Ethics Committee’s jurisdiction.

Or so he thought.

The Eight Days

Gaetz arrived on Capitol Hill on November 20, 2024, escorted by Vice President-elect JD Vance, to meet with Republican senators whose votes he would need for confirmation.

He didn’t get them.

Republican after Republican told the Trump transition team they would not support Gaetz. Details of the Ethics Committee investigation began leaking to the press. Witnesses had testified that Gaetz paid them for sex. Venmo records documented the transactions. The seventeen-year-old girl had described her encounters with Gaetz in explicit detail.

“There’s no path to 50,” sources close to Trump told ABC News.

On November 21, Trump called Gaetz with the news: he didn’t have the votes.

At 12:41 PM that day, Gaetz posted on social media that he was withdrawing his nomination. “It is clear that my confirmation was unfairly becoming a distraction to the critical work of the Trump/Vance Transition,” he wrote.

Senator Mike Rounds offered the most honest assessment: “I think there was advice offered, rather than consent.”

The nomination had lasted eight days—tied for the third-shortest withdrawn nomination in American history.

The Report

The Ethics Committee vote on whether to release their report on Gaetz split Republicans. Chairman Michael Guest and two other Republicans voted against release. But two Republicans—Dave Joyce and Andrew Garbarino—crossed over to vote with Democrats.

On December 23, 2024, the committee published its findings.

The 37-page report concluded that there was “substantial evidence that Representative Gaetz violated House Rules and other standards of conduct prohibiting prostitution, statutory rape, illicit drug use, impermissible gifts, special favors or privileges, and obstruction of Congress.”

It detailed the payments to women. The drug use—cocaine, ecstasy, marijuana. The Bahamas trip where sex with multiple women was the “payment.” The lies to the State Department to help a sexual partner obtain a passport.

And it documented the testimony of the seventeen-year-old girl, now an adult, who told investigators she was “certain” of her sexual encounters with Gaetz.

Gaetz filed a federal lawsuit that morning to block the report’s release, calling it “untruthful and defamatory.” By evening, with the report already public, he acknowledged the lawsuit was moot.

The Soft Landing

Ten days later, One America News—the far-right network that had spread 2020 election conspiracy theories until Dominion Voting Systems sued them—announced that Matt Gaetz would host a nightly talk show.

“The Matt Gaetz Show” premiered on January 2, 2025. His first episode focused on opposing Mike Johnson’s reelection as Speaker—the same position Gaetz had rendered vacant by ousting McCarthy fifteen months earlier.

He also joined Cameo, selling personalized video messages for hundreds of dollars.

By March 2025, a poll conducted by Tony Fabrizio—Donald Trump’s own pollster—showed Gaetz leading the Republican field for Florida Attorney General in 2026. He had 39 percent support to incumbent James Uthmeier’s 21 percent. Forty percent remained undecided.

“Sometimes the AG itch doesn’t go away with one scratch,” Gaetz told Axios.

He was also considering a run for governor.

The man the House Ethics Committee found substantial evidence had violated Florida’s statutory rape laws was now the frontrunner to become Florida’s chief law enforcement officer.

The Victim

In October 2025, a federal judge in Florida unsealed court documents from a civil case related to the Greenberg investigation. They revealed for the first time that the girl at the center of the scandal had been “a then-homeless 17-year-old high schooler.”

The New York Times contacted her lawyer, Laura B. Wolf, who agreed to confirm basic biographical information. Wolf said her client believed the public should have “a fuller understanding of how she had been victimized.”

“Power imbalances can be age, but they can also be financial,” Wolf said. “My client had little economic security, which allowed for financial leverage over her.”

Fritz Scheller, Joel Greenberg’s lawyer, said the girl was “a victim that embodied unwavering strength and courage.”

In response to the Times’ questions, Gaetz texted: “I never had sex with this person. This person threatened me with a lawsuit if I didn’t pay her $2.3 million. She never sued me because her story is fiction.”

The phrasing was familiar. In January 1998, Bill Clinton stood before the cameras and declared: “I did not have sexual relations with that woman, Miss Lewinsky.”

Clinton was impeached for lying about consensual activity with an adult intern.

Gaetz was nominated to be Attorney General after the Ethics Committee found substantial evidence he had sex with a homeless seventeen-year-old for $400.

The girl never sued. She had already testified under oath to the House Ethics Committee and the Department of Justice. She had provided contemporaneous text messages and corroborating witnesses.

She had been seventeen years old, working at McDonald’s, living in a homeless shelter, trying to save money for braces.

He was a thirty-five-year-old congressman with a family fortune, a famous father, and the protection of the most powerful political movement in America.

The Meaning

Donald Trump nominated a man under investigation for child sex trafficking to serve as the nation’s chief law enforcement officer. When that nomination collapsed in eight days under the weight of evidence documented by a bipartisan congressional committee, Trump moved on. Gaetz landed a television show. Polls suggest he may be the next Attorney General of Florida.

Joel Greenberg is serving eleven years in federal prison.

The girl got her braces.

The House Ethics Committee found substantial evidence that Matt Gaetz violated Florida’s statutory rape laws. No prosecutor has charged him. No court will try him. His political career continues.

This is how accountability works in America in 2025: it doesn’t.

The powerful fail upward. The vulnerable are used and discarded. And the systems designed to deliver justice find reasons—always reasons—why they cannot act.

“The vulnerable circumstances most crime victims face are rarely known to the public,” Laura Wolf told the Times.

Now you know.
