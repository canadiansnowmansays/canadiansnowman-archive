The Charlie Kirk Debates

#18: “Bail Out” Paul Pelosi’s Attacker - Part I: The Case

January 19, 2026

Charlie Kirk’s “Prove Me Wrong” wasn’t rhetoric—it was a radical call to evidence, a dare to dismantle weak claims with stronger ones. That’s the spirit guiding these debates: Direct engagement with his words, as if he’s here to rebut, because silencing the dead dishonors the discourse he ignited. No gloating over gaps; just the grind of applying his standard back at him, from constitutional ink to scriptural light. If the address feels forward, it’s by design—honoring a provocateur by provoking thought. With that, let’s turn the mirror:

Charlie Kirk, let’s apply your “Prove Me Wrong” standard to your call for an “amazing patriot” to bail out the man who fractured Paul Pelosi’s skull with a hammer. Just three days after David DePape broke into the Pelosi home seeking to kidnap Nancy Pelosi, you told your audience on October 31, 2022: “If some amazing patriot out there in San Francisco or the Bay Area wants to really be a midterm hero, someone should go and bail this guy out. Bail him out and then go ask him some questions.” You spent years claiming to defend law and order, yet when a right-wing conspiracy theorist nearly murdered an 82-year-old man, you called for his release. Prove me wrong.

Kirk’s Response to the Paul Pelosi Attack

The Attack: What Actually Happened

On October 28, 2022, at approximately 2:27 AM, David DePape broke into the San Francisco home of House Speaker Nancy Pelosi carrying zip ties, duct tape, rope, and a hammer. Unable to find Nancy Pelosi—who was in Washington, D.C.—DePape confronted her 82-year-old husband, Paul Pelosi. When police arrived, they witnessed DePape striking Paul Pelosi in the head with the hammer. The blow fractured Pelosi’s skull and caused serious injuries requiring emergency surgery.

During his subsequent federal trial, DePape testified about his intentions: he planned to hold Nancy Pelosi hostage, interrogate her on video about what he believed were Democratic conspiracies, and “break her kneecaps” if she didn’t confess to “lying.” Prosecutors documented that DePape maintained a “hit list” that included California Governor Gavin Newsom and actor Tom Hanks. His online footprint revealed extensive engagement with QAnon conspiracy theories, election denial claims, and other far-right extremist content.

Federal prosecutors charged DePape with attempted kidnapping of a federal official and assault on an immediate family member of a federal official. A federal judge later sentenced him to 30 years in prison. In a separate state trial, he received life in prison without the possibility of parole.

Kirk’s Immediate Response: Conspiracy Theories and Calls for Bail

Just three days after the attack, on October 31, 2022, Charlie Kirk dedicated substantial airtime on “The Charlie Kirk Show” to promoting baseless conspiracy theories about the assault while simultaneously calling for DePape’s release on bail.

Kirk’s opening framing rejected any connection between the attack and right-wing rhetoric:

“Why is the Republican Party, why is the conservative movement to blame for gay, schizophrenic nudists that are hemp jewelry-makers breaking into somebody’s home, or maybe not breaking into somebody’s home? Why are we to blame for that exactly?”

Note Kirk’s deliberate dehumanization: rather than calling DePape by name or referring to him as “the attacker” or “the assailant,” Kirk labeled him a “gay schizophrenic nudist”—characterizations designed to distance him from the conservative movement while simultaneously promoting the debunked conspiracy theory that the attack was the result of an “illicit gay sexual tryst gone awry.”

This conspiracy theory—which originated in a fake news article published by the Santa Monica Observer and was briefly amplified by Elon Musk before he deleted his tweet—had already been thoroughly debunked by law enforcement. San Francisco District Attorney Brooke Jenkins explicitly stated: “We have nothing to suggest that these two men knew each other prior to this incident.”

The “Amazing Patriot” Call

Kirk then made his most inflammatory statement, calling for DePape’s release:

“And why is he still in jail? Why has he not been bailed out? By the way, if some amazing patriot out there in San Francisco or the Bay Area wants to really be a midterm hero, someone should go and bail this guy out. I bet his bail’s like 30[,000] or 40,000 bucks. Bail him out, and then go ask him some questions.”

This extraordinary statement came with a thin veneer of deniability:

“I’m not qualifying it. I think it’s awful. It’s not right.”

Yet Kirk’s subsequent comments revealed his actual position. He framed DePape’s detention not as appropriate treatment of someone who committed a violent political assault, but as evidence of a double standard:

“But why is it that in Chicago you’re able to commit murder and be out the next day? Why is it that you’re able to trespass, second-degree murder, arson, threaten a public official, cashless bail. This happens all over San Francisco. But if you go after the Pelosis, oh, you’re [not] let out immediately. Got it.”

Kirk was apparently unaware—or chose not to mention—that DePape was being held without bail because he had been placed on a federal hold for attempted kidnapping and assault of a federal official’s family member, crimes for which bail is typically denied due to danger to the community and flight risk.

The False Flag Conspiracy

Kirk also promoted the baseless claim that DePape’s online presence had been fabricated:

“And, by the way, why is it that the media hasn’t mentioned that they’re all these, allegedly, far-right websites that popped up attributed to him and then they were taken down a few days later?”

This conspiracy theory relied on misunderstanding how the Internet Archive’s Wayback Machine works. While some far-right figures claimed that the recent appearance of DePape’s blog in the Wayback Machine proved it was created after the attack, CNN’s Andrew Kaczynski and other fact-checkers documented that portions of DePape’s blog had been archived months earlier. The “recent” timestamps reflected when the Wayback Machine crawler had accessed the site, not when the site was created.

Kirk’s Pattern on Political Violence

This wasn’t Kirk’s first time downplaying or justifying political violence when it served his political objectives:

January 6 Defense: Kirk played a significant role in promoting the “Stop the Steal” movement and was involved in organizing bus trips to Washington, D.C. for the January 6, 2021 rally. After the Capitol attack, he initially defended the rioters while claiming Turning Point USA had no official role.

“Second Amendment Remedies”: Kirk regularly invoked gun rights in explicitly political terms, suggesting that the Second Amendment existed as a check on government tyranny—a dog whistle that armed resistance to Democratic governance might be justified.

Dehumanization Campaign: Throughout 2022-2024, Kirk systematically dehumanized Democrats and progressive activists, calling them “domestic enemies,” “groomers,” “pedophiles,” and “Marxists”—rhetoric that creates a permission structure for violence by portraying political opponents as existential threats rather than fellow citizens.

The Paul Pelosi incident revealed Kirk’s actual position: when someone motivated by the conspiracy theories Kirk promoted attempted to assassinate a Democratic leader, Kirk’s response was to promote more conspiracy theories and call for the attacker’s release.

The Political Context

Kirk’s statements came just days before the 2022 midterm elections. His framing was strategic: by promoting the “gay sexual encounter” conspiracy, he sought to neutralize what could have been a politically damaging story for Republicans—that a right-wing extremist, motivated by the conspiracy theories Republican figures had promoted, had attempted to assassinate the Democratic Speaker of the House.

Rather than condemn the attack unequivocally or reflect on whether extreme rhetoric might contribute to extreme actions, Kirk chose to:

  1. Deny any connection between conservative rhetoric and the attack

  2. Dehumanize the attacker with homophobic slurs

  3. Promote debunked conspiracy theories

  4. Call for the attacker’s release

  5. Frame his detention as partisan prosecution

  6. Claim media coverage was a “smear” of MAGA

This response pattern would become familiar: when confronted with violence committed by someone who shared his political views, Kirk’s default was to deny, deflect, and accuse his critics of unfairly “blaming” conservatives.

Kirk called for an “amazing patriot” to bail out a man who broke into the Speaker’s home and fractured an 82-year-old’s skull with a hammer, promoted a debunked conspiracy theory about a gay sexual encounter to distract from the political motivations, denied any connection between Republican rhetoric and the attack while the attacker testified he was motivated by right-wing conspiracy theories about “evil in Washington” and stolen elections, and framed the detention of a violent criminal as partisan prosecution. Three years later, Kirk himself was assassinated in an act of political violence—the very kind of violence he had normalized, defended, and denied. The trial testimony, police evidence, DePape’s own words, and the life-without-parole sentence all prove Kirk catastrophically wrong on every count. Let’s examine the evidence.
