The Epstein Files

What the Files Actually Revealed

Part 22: The Difference Between What We Expected and What We Got

February 14, 2026

3.5 million pages released. Heavy redactions everywhere. ‘Masseuses’ visible; the rest blacked out. The Epstein files: expectation vs. reality

February 14, 2026

For months, the phrase “Epstein files” conjured images of a massive revelation: a client list naming dozens of powerful men in a trafficking ring. Proof of systematic blackmail. Evidence that would finally bring accountability to an elite cabal that had protected Jeffrey Epstein for decades.

Then the Justice Department released 3.5 million pages, 2,000 videos, and 180,000 images.

And what did we actually get?

We got confirmation that Epstein cultivated relationships with some of the world’s most powerful men. We got proof that many of them continued those relationships even after his 2008 conviction for soliciting a minor for prostitution. We got emails showing wealthy men treating women as commodities, discussing “working girls” and “civilians,” asking Epstein for introductions to young women for sex.

What we didn’t get was evidence of a trafficking ring serving powerful men.

What we didn’t get was a client list.

What we didn’t get was proof that anyone beyond Jeffrey Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell will ever face criminal charges.

And the gap between those two realities—what we expected versus what the files revealed—tells us everything about how elite impunity actually works in America.

What the FBI Found (and Didn’t Find)

The Associated Press conducted a comprehensive review of internal Justice Department records released in the files. Their February 8 analysis was blunt:

“While investigators collected ample proof that Epstein sexually abused underage girls, they found scant evidence the well-connected financier led a sex trafficking ring serving powerful men.”

Let that sink in. The FBI examined Epstein’s bank records. They searched his homes in New York, Florida, and the Virgin Islands. They spent years interviewing victims. They reviewed thousands of emails.

And they found no evidence of systematic trafficking to other men.

Brad Edwards, an attorney who has represented more than 200 Epstein victims in civil lawsuits, told the BBC and ABC News that “Jeffrey Epstein was the pimp and the john. He was his own No. 1 client.” Edwards said that while “a select few” of Epstein’s friends might have “engaged in sexual acts with a select few of the girls,” for the most part Epstein’s social world and his criminal world “did not overlap.”

Redacted flight records and emails: Black bars hide details, but the absence speaks louder—no systematic ring serving the powerful.

This isn’t what Edwards would say if he was trying to protect powerful men. He earns money by suing perpetrators. He has every incentive to identify as many abusers as possible.

But the evidence isn’t there.

Federal prosecutors wrote in internal memos that videos and photos seized from Epstein’s properties “didn’t depict victims being abused or implicate anyone else in his crimes.” An examination of Epstein’s financial records “found no connection to criminal activity” beyond paying the girls he himself abused.

Most significantly: there was no client list.

When then-FBI Deputy Director Paul Abbate asked in December 2024 whether the “client list” referenced in media coverage actually existed, FBI agents confirmed it did not. On February 19, 2025—two days before Attorney General Pam Bondi claimed on Fox News that the client list was “sitting on my desk right now”—an FBI supervisory special agent wrote: “While media coverage of the Jeffrey Epstein case references a ‘client list,’ investigators did not locate such a list during the course of the investigation.”

So What DID the Files Reveal?

If not a trafficking ring, then what?

The files revealed something perhaps more depressing: an unaccountable elite who gave a sexual predator a free ride because he offered them things they wanted.

As Robert Draper wrote in The New York Times on February 12: “The documents lay bare the once-furtive activities of an unaccountable elite, largely made up of rich and powerful men from business, politics, academia and show business. The pages tell a story of a heinous criminal given a free ride by the ruling class in which he dwelled, all because he had things to offer them: money, connections, sumptuous dinner parties, a private plane, a secluded island and, in some cases, sex.”

Little St. James: Secluded island paradise. Epstein’s offering—luxury, privacy, connections. The files show who accepted the invitation.

Nicole Hemmer, a Vanderbilt history professor, told the Times: “We must ask ourselves how we have produced an elite that is so immature, reckless and arrogant.”

The files document Epstein’s web of connections: Bill Clinton and Kevin Spacey on African tours aboard his private jet. Woody Allen and Noam Chomsky in his orbit. Kenneth Starr and Kathryn Ruemmler seeking his counsel. Larry Summers and various financial titans. Crown Princess Mette-Marit of Norway. And, of course, his well-documented friendship with Donald Trump before they reportedly fell out in the mid-2000s.

The Lolita Express: Epstein beside his black jet. Symbol of elite access and post-conviction free rides. The files confirm who flew.

The files show businessmen like James Staley (former Barclays CEO) writing to Epstein in 2014 about how upper-class Americans had been “bought off” and wouldn’t protest inequality. They show Steve Bannon exchanging hundreds of texts with Epstein in 2019. They show Peter Attia, the wellness influencer, going “into JE withdrawal” when he didn’t see Epstein and confirming in crude terms that “pussy is, indeed, low carb.”

They show men who treated association with a convicted sex offender as a networking opportunity.

The Accountability Scorecard

Here’s what’s remarkable about the fallout from the Epstein files: who has faced consequences and who hasn’t.

In the United Kingdom:

Peter Mandelson resigned from the Labour Party, quit the House of Lords, and now faces a police investigation after the files showed $75,000 in payments from Epstein and evidence he may have shared market-sensitive government information. He was removed as British ambassador to the United States. Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s top aide resigned over recommending Mandelson for the role.

Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor was stripped of his royal titles by King Charles III. He was forced out of his 30-room residence at Royal Lodge. U.K. police are weighing whether to formally investigate him. The Prince and Princess of Wales issued a statement saying they are “deeply concerned” by the revelations.

In the United States:

Kathryn Ruemmler resigned as general counsel of Goldman Sachs after emails showed her extensive friendship with Epstein—addressing him as “sweetie” and “Uncle Jeffrey,” advising him on managing fallout from his conviction, and being mentioned in more than 10,000 documents. She had repeatedly claimed only a professional relationship.

Brad Karp stepped down as chairman of Paul Weiss law firm after emails showed him gushing “You’re amazing” to Epstein and maintaining contact through 2019, after Epstein’s crimes were national news.

Peter Attia was dropped by CBS News after appearing in the files more than 1,700 times with crude emails about women.

But here’s who’s staying in their positions:

Casey Wasserman remains chairman of the Los Angeles 2028 Olympics despite flirtatious emails with Ghislaine Maxwell and calls for his resignation.

Howard Lutnick remains Commerce Secretary with the White House’s “full support” despite emails arranging a family visit to Epstein’s private island in December 2012—with his four children and nannies—four years after Epstein’s conviction. Lutnick had previously claimed he vowed in the mid-2005s to “never be in a room with that disgusting person ever again.”

Steve Tisch remains co-owner of the New York Giants despite appearing at least 440 times in the documents with emails showing Epstein connecting him with women. The NFL announced it would investigate but has taken no action.

Notice the pattern? The most severe consequences have fallen on people in the United Kingdom. Americans in positions of power largely remain there.

The Ones Who Lied About Their Relationships

Perhaps more revealing than the relationships themselves are the lies people told about them.

Elon Musk posted in 2025 that Epstein “tried to get me to go to his island and I REFUSED.” The files show Musk emailing Epstein in 2012: “What day/night will be the wildest party on your island?” (Musk has been among the most vocal advocates for releasing the Epstein files and has maintained he declined invitations to visit.)

Howard Lutnick claimed he vowed never to see Epstein again after learning about his crimes in the mid-2000s. The files show him arranging a family vacation to Epstein’s island in 2012, complete with children and nannies.

Kathryn Ruemmler and Goldman Sachs maintained for years that she had a “strictly professional relationship” with Epstein. The files revealed her calling him “Uncle Jeffrey,” discussing her dating life, and wearing gifts from him to her Goldman job interview.

Peter Mandelson’s relationship with Epstein was described by officials as someone “he barely knew.” The files showed extensive correspondence, payments, and meetings.

The lies aren’t about whether they knew Epstein. Everyone admits that now. The lies are about the nature of those relationships—attempts to minimize, to claim professional distance, to pretend the friendships weren’t as close as the emails reveal them to be.

What Trump Knew and When

The files also shed new light on President Trump’s shifting explanations about Epstein.

In 2002, Trump famously told New York Magazine that Epstein was a “terrific guy” and noted, “It is even said that he likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side.”

The files now reveal that in 2006—before Epstein’s arrest but during the police investigation—Trump told a police chief “that it was known in New York circles that Epstein was disgusting” and suggested police should also focus on Maxwell, according to a memo.

So Trump knew in 2006 that Epstein was “disgusting” but had praised him publicly in 2002 for liking women “on the younger side.” The timeline matters.

The files show more than 5,300 files containing over 38,000 references to Trump. Many are news clippings. But some include handwritten notes from a victim who recalled being brought to Mar-a-Lago to meet Trump, with Epstein saying “This is a good one, huh?” The notes don’t suggest misconduct by Trump.

There are also unverified FBI tips about Trump from a spreadsheet investigators created—tips with “no dates or names” that couldn’t be independently corroborated.

Trump has maintained the files “absolved” him. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche emphasized that many tips about prominent people were “quickly determined to not be credible.”

What’s clear is that Trump and Epstein had a real friendship before they reportedly fell out, and that Trump’s public statements about what he knew and when have shifted significantly over the years.

The Real Scandal: Elite Impunity

Broken scales of justice: One side intact, the other shattered. Two decades, two people accountable. Elite impunity endures.

Here’s what makes the Epstein files genuinely scandalous: not what they prove about a trafficking ring, but what they reveal about a system that protected a predator for decades.

Epstein received a sweetheart plea deal in 2008 that let him plead guilty to soliciting prostitution from a minor rather than face federal sex trafficking charges. He served 13 months. He returned to polite society. Powerful men continued to associate with him, to fly on his plane, to attend his parties, to seek his counsel.

Some surely didn’t know the extent of his crimes. The plea deal was designed to minimize their severity. But plenty of people knew enough—or should have known enough—and chose to maintain relationships anyway because Epstein offered access, connections, money, and influence.

As The Atlantic’s Gilad Edelman wrote: “Smarmy jerks can get obscenely wealthy in this country just by managing other people’s money. Men who sexually abuse women and children can be charming. The most accomplished people in our society can be terrible judges of character. They can, in the pursuit of proximity to wealth and power, ignore or downplay what’s right in front of them.”

In twenty years, exactly two people have been held accountable: Jeffrey Epstein, who died in federal custody, and Ghislaine Maxwell, serving 20 years for sex trafficking.

Not the co-conspirators named in the 2007 draft indictment—their names remain redacted.

Not the “three others who were his personal assistants and were suspected of participating in a conspiracy to recruit underage girls.”

Not the wealthy and powerful men who maintained friendships with him for years after his conviction.

Two people. In twenty years.

What Happens Next

Representatives Thomas Massie and Ro Khanna, who wrote the Epstein Files Transparency Act, say the Justice Department is still withholding documents and improperly redacting co-conspirators’ names while exposing victims.

“They, through incompetence, failed to redact the victims’ names,” Massie said, “while at the same time intentionally withholding documents and redacting documents that contain names of people that should probably be investigated.”

The 2007 draft indictment remains heavily redacted. The 2.5 million pages still withheld remain in DOJ storage. Congress has subpoena power but limited enforcement mechanisms.

And on February 2, 2026, President Trump said the quiet part out loud: “Frankly, the DOJ, I think, should just say we have other things to do.”

That’s where we are. Three million pages released. An unaccountable elite exposed. And the person who ran the Justice Department for most of the investigation now saying it’s time to move on.

The files didn’t reveal a trafficking ring. They revealed something arguably worse: a system where wealth and connections provide immunity, where powerful men can maintain friendships with a convicted sex offender without consequence, where the only people who go to prison are the ones who committed the crimes themselves—not the systems and networks that enabled them.

That’s the real scandal the Epstein files revealed.

Not a client list. Not a blackmail operation. Not a conspiracy.

Just elite impunity, documented in 3.5 million pages, and a justice system that has proven incapable of holding anyone accountable except the two people who are already gone.

Jeffrey Epstein: The convicted predator at the center. The only one fully held accountable. The system that shielded him? Still standing.
