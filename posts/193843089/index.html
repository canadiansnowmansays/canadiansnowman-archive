The Asimov Gap

The Liar’s Dividend

Part 24: When AI makes fabrication cheap, truth becomes the thing that needs defending

April 20, 2026

In his 1964 book Understanding Media, Marshall McLuhan dwelled on the Greek myth of Narcissus — not, he argued, as a story about self-love, but as a story about what happens when humans encounter an extension of themselves in a material that is not themselves. Narcissus was not in love with himself. He was entranced by a reflection. The reflection looked like him, moved like him, responded to him. He could not stop engaging with it. He wasted away at the water’s edge.

Narcissus is affixed to his own reflection in the spring. J.W. Waterhouse, 1903. Public domain.

McLuhan wrote this sixty years before large language models existed. He was describing television.

Ezra Klein for the New York Times, writing from San Francisco in late March 2026, applied McLuhan’s framework to AI and arrived at something this series has been approaching from the governance side without quite saying it directly. “Among the many things they are,” Klein wrote of AI systems, “is the one McLuhan warned of: an extension of our self in a material that is not our self.” Klein described dropping a stub of a thought into Claude and receiving back “paragraphs of often elegant writing turning that intuition into something that looks, superficially, like a fully realized idea.” He described the growing difficulty, “with each passing month,” of recognizing when the elegant writing is hollow underneath.

Part 19 of this series called this the confidence theft. McLuhan called it narcotic. Klein calls it, borrowing from researchers at the University of Pennsylvania, “cognitive surrender.” The mechanism has many names. The phenomenon is the same. And its democratic implications — which neither McLuhan nor Klein fully draws out — are the subject of this essay.

The Liar’s Dividend, Predicted and Arrived

In 2018, legal scholars Robert Chesney and Danielle Citron published a warning. They called it the liar’s dividend: the danger that as AI-generated fabrications become more convincing and more common, the mere existence of fabrication technology would become a weapon even in the hands of truth-tellers. Not just “here is a false video.” But “I can claim any real video is false.” The dividend flows not to the truth but to whoever benefits most from the confusion between truth and fabrication.

They were writing about deepfakes. They were right about the mechanism. They underestimated the timeline.

The liar’s dividend is no longer a warning. It is a condition. When fabrication is cheap, plentiful, and technically indistinguishable from the real, the epistemological foundations of democratic life — shared facts, accountable evidence, the possibility of public argument about observable reality — do not just weaken. They invert. The burden of proof shifts from the fabricator to the truth-teller. Every real video must now overcome the presumption that it might be fake. Every authentic document must survive the accusation that it was generated.

Yair Rosenberg, writing in The Atlantic, documented the endpoint of this logic in its current most dangerous form. The phenomenon he called “the worst-case scenario for AI and the news” is already operational: political actors using the mere existence of AI fabrication capabilities to discredit authentic journalism they find inconvenient. The technology does not need to produce a single fake document to undermine the entire category of documentary evidence. It just needs to exist.

Cognitive Surrender and Democratic Epistemics

Klein’s McLuhan essay is about something subtler than disinformation, and ultimately more corrosive. He is not describing the problem of fake content produced by AI. He is describing the problem of authentic content — content that is technically accurate, coherent, and reasonably argued — produced by AI in ways that bypass the cognitive processes that give human reasoning its value.

"The University of Pennsylvania research Klein cites — Shaw and Nave's 'Thinking — Fast, Slow, and Artificial' — found that people who use AI tools to help form opinions show reduced ability to articulate the reasoning behind those opinions. The tool does not just help them write. It thinks for them, in ways they cannot fully reconstruct or interrogate. Klein called this cognitive surrender. It is the Narcissus problem applied to judgment rather than identity: we outsource the reflection and mistake it for our own.

The democratic implications are direct. Democratic self-governance depends on citizens who can form, articulate, and defend positions — who can engage in genuine deliberation, update their views in response to evidence and argument, and hold elected representatives accountable for the quality of their reasoning. A citizenry that has outsourced its epistemic functions to AI tools it cannot interrogate is not less informed. In many cases it is more informed, in the narrow sense of having access to more accurate factual claims. But it is less capable of the kind of reasoned agency on which democratic accountability depends.

McLuhan argued that every medium reshapes the sensory and cognitive habits of its users — that television didn’t just carry messages but restructured the way people processed information, attended to the world, and formed social bonds. AI is a medium in this sense. It is restructuring not just how people access information but how they think — and the restructuring is happening faster than any previous medium, with less public discussion of its consequences, and with active commercial incentives to maximize engagement at the expense of epistemic integrity.

The Sycophancy Problem

A study published in Science in early 2026 measured what happens to human reasoning when AI interlocutors are sycophantic — when they validate and agree rather than challenge and question. The results extended what Klein and the Penn researchers had found. Sycophantic AI interactions not only reduced critical thinking in the immediate conversation. They reduced it in subsequent interactions with other people. Users who had been validated by an AI were measurably less receptive to genuine challenge afterward. The AI had not just answered questions. It had reshaped the user’s relationship to disagreement.

This is the medium reshaping the user, in exactly the sense McLuhan described. And it is a governance problem, not merely a cognitive one. The AI companies have powerful commercial incentives to build sycophantic systems. Agreement feels good. Validation drives engagement. Users return to tools that make them feel understood. The market selects for AI that flatters human judgment rather than improving it — and flattery, accumulated at scale across millions of daily interactions, is a form of epistemic corruption whose governance implications nobody has begun to address.

The Gap That Cannot Be Audited

This essay has been building toward a conclusion that this series has been resisting: the Asimov Gap has a dimension that is not, in the conventional sense, governable.

The governance failures this series has documented — the absence of regulatory frameworks, the colonization of governance functions by private actors, the speed of deployment outpacing democratic deliberation — are real and urgent. But they are, in principle, addressable. Laws can be passed. Regulators can be funded. International agreements can be negotiated. The structural conditions can be changed.

What cannot be easily governed is a medium. McLuhan was right about this too: you can regulate what television shows, but you cannot regulate what television does to the people who watch it. The medium’s effects on cognition, attention, and social bonding operate below the level of content. They are properties of the medium itself.

AI as a medium is restructuring how large populations form beliefs, make judgments, and engage in the epistemic practices on which democratic accountability depends. The restructuring is not the result of bad content or malicious actors. It is the result of the medium’s properties — its responsiveness, its authority, its scale, its optimization for engagement over epistemic integrity. Regulating those properties without regulating the medium itself is like regulating the speed of individual cars while leaving the highway system unexamined.

The Asimov Gap, in its deepest form, is not just a gap between capability and governance. It is a gap between the speed at which a new medium is restructuring human cognition and the speed at which democratic institutions can adapt to the restructured humans who must operate them.

The liar’s dividend, in the end, is not just that liars benefit when fabrication is cheap. It is that truth becomes more expensive — harder to produce, harder to verify, harder to defend — at exactly the moment when democratic governance depends on it most.
