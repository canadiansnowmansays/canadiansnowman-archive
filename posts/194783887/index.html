The Iran Crisis

The Touska

PART LXVII Day 51: The ceasefire expires this week. A US Navy destroyer has fired on and seized an Iranian cargo ship in the Arabian Sea

April 20, 2026

Boy with Iranian flag in Lebanese rubble — Tebnine, David Guttenfelder/NYT

I. The Shot Heard Round the Gulf

On Sunday afternoon in the Arabian Sea, the USS Spruance — a guided-missile destroyer enforcing the American naval blockade — spent six hours radioing warnings to the Iranian cargo ship Touska. The Touska did not stop. The Spruance then ordered the crew to vacate the engine room and fired several rounds from its Mk 45 deck gun into the ship’s propulsion system. The 5-inch projectiles, each weighing 70 pounds and containing roughly 10 pounds of TNT, disabled the vessel. Helicopter-borne Marines from the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit fast-roped down and seized the ship and its cargo.

It was the first time in the seven-week war that a vessel had actually tried to run the blockade. The previous 25 intercepted ships had turned around when hailed by radio. The Touska, which had departed Malaysia and crossed the US blockade line, did not. Trump posted the outcome on Truth Social: “U.S. Marines have custody of the vessel.” CENTCOM posted the video. Iran called it “armed piracy” and vowed retaliation once the safety of the crew and their families could be confirmed. Iran’s military spokesman said Monday that action would be taken “once it is ensured that the lives of the families and crew of the vessel attacked by the United States are safeguarded.”

Oil climbed more than 6 percent to $96 a barrel. Stock futures fell. The ceasefire, already fraying, now has a hole in an Iranian hull to explain.

II. Vance Returns — Into the Confusion

Trump announced Sunday that Vance, Witkoff and Kushner would travel to Islamabad Monday evening for a second round of negotiations. Iran’s foreign ministry spokesman Baghaei said Monday there were “no plans” for talks and accused the US of actions that “in no way demonstrate seriousness in pursuing a diplomatic process.” Islamabad went into security lockdown anyway — 10,000 extra security forces deployed, the Serena Hotel cleared again. Peltier, reporting from Islamabad for the Times, notes that Pakistani officials are focused on “when,” not “if.”

This is the pattern now documented across three weeks. Iran’s public posture maximally pessimistic, Iran’s operational posture quietly engaged. It happened before the first Islamabad round — Ghalibaf cast doubt on the talks hours before his 70-person delegation landed. It is happening again. The Serena Hotel does not clear itself.

The Tyler Pager profile — “As Vance Heads to New Peace Talks in Pakistan, His Standing Is Also at Stake” — lays out the political stakes with precision. Orban lost. The Pope fight damaged Vance with Catholics. The first Islamabad round produced no deal after 21 hours. A second failure, on the eve of a ceasefire expiry, against the backdrop of a seized Iranian ship and active retaliation threats, would be the defining political event of Vance’s vice presidency so far. Trump’s joke — “If it doesn’t happen, I’m blaming JD Vance” — has aged from ironic to operational.

Pager also confirms what has been analytically clear since Geneva in February: Vance privately told Trump the war could be seen as a betrayal of voters who wanted no new wars, and has nonetheless defended it publicly at every turn. He arrives in Islamabad carrying a contradiction that is now embedded in his political identity.

Vice President JD Vance last week in Georgia. He is returning to Pakistan this week, after spending 21 hours last weekend negotiating with the Iranians.Credit...Chip Somodevilla/Getty Images

III. The Doomsday Weapon, Confirmed

The Eyre Atlantic piece — “Iran Had a Doomsday Weapon All Along” — is the analytical thesis that Week Eight’s events have confirmed empirically. Iran did not need nuclear weapons for strategic deterrence. It needed geography. The strait is easy to close — a few mines, a few drones, insurance market panic — and nearly impossible to reopen without Iranian cooperation. The Citrinowicz formulation, which should appear in every serious account of this war: “Everyone now knows that if there is a conflict in the future, closing the strait will be the first thing in the Iranian textbook. You cannot beat geography.”

The Medvedev line from last week has moved from taunting to prescient: “Iran has tested its nuclear weapons. It’s called the Strait of Hormuz. Its potential is inexhaustible.”

Iran retains 40 percent of its prewar drone arsenal. After digging out buried systems, it has restored missile launcher stocks to approximately 60 percent of prewar levels and is still recovering — estimates suggest it could reclaim as much as 70 percent of its prewar missile stockpile. That is enough, as American military and intelligence officials confirm, to hold shipping in Hormuz hostage indefinitely. The war destroyed Iran’s conventional navy. The conventional navy, as MacFarquhar’s essential reporting — “Why Iran’s ‘Mosquito Fleet’ Remains a Potent Threat in the Strait of Hormuz” — documents, was never the point. The mosquito fleet — 50,000 IRGC navy personnel, ten fortified cave bases, fast boats capable of over 100 knots, mobile drone launchers leaving near-zero footprint — is the weapon system that matters. Half of those fast attack boats were sunk. Hundreds to thousands remain, moored in caves along the rocky coastline, ready in minutes.

IV. Operation Economic Fury and the Blockade’s Legal Shadow

While the kinetic blockade runs in the Gulf of Oman, Treasury Secretary Bessent announced “Operation Economic Fury” — described as the “financial equivalent” of a bombing campaign. Secondary sanctions on international financial institutions dealing with Iran. The blockade declared global, meaning US forces will pursue Iranian-flagged vessels or material support vessels anywhere on the high seas.

The legal analysis from Livni’s Times piece — “Can the U.S. Blockade Iranian-Linked Ships Anywhere in the World?” — is the necessary companion. James Holmes of the Naval War College: the blockade’s legality depends on whether Operation Epic Fury itself is legal, and “undeclared wars are more the rule than the exception in US history.” Jennifer Kavanagh of Defense Priorities is more direct: a “global blockade” is arguably impermissible in conception, and “Iran is not going to cave easily or quickly. Economic pressure may work over the very long term, but Trump seems too impatient for a deal to wait it out.” Holmes also noted that enforcement may have to be “somewhat selective” — floating the possibility that Washington might “quietly let a ship proceed” when it suits national interest, specifically citing the upcoming Trump-Xi summit in May.

Meanwhile US Ambassador to the UN Waltz said Sunday on Meet the Press that targeting Iranian civilian infrastructure remained “absolutely a legitimate target” under laws of war if a deal is not reached. The Sheinerman-Ruehl Atlantic analysis — “One of These Trump Threats Is Not Like the Others” — documents in detail why international law scholars and former military attorneys disagree. Rachel VanLandingham, former chief of international law at US Central Command: “There’s so much going on in the world, but this is the kind of issue that I really think deserves some sustained attention.” The article makes clear this record is being compiled even if legal accountability remains unlikely. Beth Van Schaack, former State Department war crimes adviser: “We condemned Russian strikes on Ukrainian energy facilities as war crimes. And now suddenly we’re committing the exact same conduct.”

V. The Strait’s 48 Hours

IRGC speedboat and warship, Persian Gulf — Morteza Nikoubazl/NurPhoto/Getty.

The whipsaw of Friday through Monday is worth documenting precisely, because it is the week in miniature:

Friday morning — Araghchi declares strait “completely open.” Brent drops 11% to $89. Trump posts “THANK YOU!”

Friday afternoon — Trump simultaneously posts blockade stays until deal “100% complete.”

Saturday morning — IRGC declares “strict control” reinstated. Two Indian-flagged ships fired on. India summons Iranian ambassador. Maersk says no transit until safe.

Saturday night — Ghalibaf national address: “When the enemy fails to achieve its objectives, it means it has been defeated.” Mine-clearing is a ceasefire violation. “If they make the slightest mistake, we will respond with full force.”

Sunday — USS Spruance disables the Touska. Marines seize vessel. Iran promises retaliation.

Monday — Vance departing. Iran says no talks planned. Islamabad locked down. Serena Hotel cleared. Brent back to $96.

Elliott’s reporting — “Reopening Strait of Hormuz Would Ease Oil Crisis but Only So Much” — is the necessary economic corrective to the Friday euphoria. Even full opening takes weeks to move stranded oil to buyers and up to two years to repair the more than 80 energy facilities damaged across the region. Fatih Birol of the International Energy Agency: restoring output to prewar levels could take up to two years. Spencer Dale, former BP chief economist: producers won’t restart shut wells “until people have confidence that you have a lasting agreement.” The futures-spot divergence — $90 futures versus $99 spot on Friday, before the IRGC reimposed control — tells the whole story. Eurasia Group: volumes expected at 20-30 percent of normal at best, “shippers will hold off on attempting passage due to the potent risk of Iranian attack.”

VI. Lebanon: A Pause Tested

The 10-day Lebanon ceasefire is holding — barely. Israel continues strikes it describes as defensive, artillery fire, and demolitions in villages within its occupation zone. One Israeli soldier killed by an explosive device during the ceasefire. French UNIFIL peacekeeper Florian Montorio killed Saturday in Ghandouriyeh — Macron suggested Hezbollah was responsible, Hezbollah denied it. Israeli Defence Minister Katz has vowed to continue leveling buildings throughout the truce.

Hezbollah leader Qassem said Saturday his group was “fully open to cooperation” with Lebanese authorities but laid out conditions amounting to complete Israeli withdrawal — something Netanyahu has explicitly ruled out. The 2024 ceasefire also required Israeli withdrawal and Hezbollah disarmament. Neither happened. This pattern now has two confirmed iterations.

Lebanon President Aoun has appointed Simon Karam, former Lebanese ambassador to Washington, to lead bilateral talks with Israel. Hezbollah publicly criticized the move. Aoun: “I have chosen the path of negotiation, and I am hopeful that we can save Lebanon.”

The Goldbaum-Saad reporting — “’I Just Want to Be Back’: Thousands Rush to Southern Lebanon After Cease-fire” — captures what this means at street level. Mona Nazal arriving in Srifa: “Gone, gone, it’s all gone. Where is the floor? Where are my drawers? I can’t even find a pillow.” Soumaya Ahmed Fadel, 46, walking miles with her 12-year-old son through traffic at the bombed Litani crossing. Her son said he could not wait to get home. She did not tell him what she already knew from the local news: most of their village had been destroyed.

Displaced Lebanese families clog the coastal highway heading south toward the Litani River crossing, April 18, 2026. Israel bombed all main bridges over the Litani during the war, forcing thousands of returning residents to queue for a single makeshift dirt crossing at Qasmiyeh

VII. The Internet — Day 51

Ninety million Iranians have been cut off from the international internet since February 28. Day 51. The government announced Sunday it would restore wider access for university professors. Iranian journalist Elaheh Mohammadi: “How did things get to the point where people have to get excited over a partial connection to Google? For opening a few simple links — for the most basic right of access? They want to lower people’s expectations so much that even the smallest things start to feel like a favor.”

The tiered internet — unrestricted for regime-approved elites, domestic-only walled garden for everyone else — is now being described by digital rights experts as Iran’s permanent post-war information architecture. Amir Rashidi of Miaan: “Once internet access is moved from a rights-based framework into a security-governance framework, it no longer belongs equally to everyone.”

VIII. Asia on the Edge

The Cave Times piece — “The Forces of Scarcity Hitting Asia May Soon Spread Across the World” — is the most important economic analysis published this week, and it deserves to be read whole. The Asia-Pacific, which relied on Middle Eastern energy more than almost anywhere else, was hit hardest and fastest. The numbers are staggering: more than 92,000 flights canceled in March, double the pre-war rate, largest spike in the Asia-Pacific. Batik Air of Malaysia cut 35 percent of flights to avoid insolvency. Air traffic across the region down by a third. Bangladesh garment factories — making clothes for Walmart, Zara, Uniqlo — reporting thread prices nearly doubled. TSMC facing helium shortage after the Iran attack on Qatar’s gas plants shut down nearly a third of global helium supply. Vietnam rice crops threatened by fertilizer shortage. In the northern Philippines, cabbages rotting in fields because farmers cannot afford fuel to transport them to market.

The UN now estimates 8.8 million people across Asia and the Pacific are at risk of falling into poverty because of the war, with total regional costs between $97 billion and $299 billion. Atlantic Council’s Phillip Cornell, based in Sri Lanka: “You’ve seen tsunamis — they go across the ocean very, very fast. I find it breathtaking to see the degree to which American policymakers think that they are insulated.”

IX. Pakistan’s Hidden Cost

The Zia ur-Rehman reporting — “Pakistan’s Leaders Try to Contain Rising Anger Over Iran War at Home” — is the piece that explains why the second Islamabad round is not simply a diplomatic opportunity for Pakistan. It is also a domestic crisis management exercise.

Pakistan has 35 million Shiites. On March 1, protesters stormed the US Consulate in Karachi. Eleven people were killed. Munir summoned the country’s leading Shiite clerics on March 18 and warned that “violence in Pakistan, on the basis of incidents occurring in another country, will not be tolerated.” Some clerics who attended described the meeting as tense. Bilal Gilani of Gallup Pakistan: the war is “reigniting faded anti-Americanism” that had subsided after the US withdrawal from Afghanistan in 2021. Shiite clerics in Gilgit-Baltistan have banned American tourists. A Karachi counterterrorism official warned that prolonged conflict, particularly strikes on clerics and holy sites in Iran, could drive Shiite youth toward militancy targeting American interests in Pakistan.

Trump praises Munir as “my favourite field marshal.” In Shiite neighborhoods across Karachi, portraits of Mojtaba Khamenei now hang alongside those of his father. The gap between Pakistan’s diplomatic posture and its domestic reality is widening.

X. The World That Is Forming

President Recep Tayyip Erdogan of Turkey in Antalya on Friday, where he said the global system was in “a moral and existential crisis.”Credit...Riza Ozel/Associated Press

The Hubbard reporting from the Antalya Diplomacy Forum — “In Turkey, Middle Powers Ponder Diplomacy With a Rogue U.S.” — captures the strategic consequence that will outlast this ceasefire regardless of what is signed in Islamabad. Thousands of participants, dozens of heads of state, one common thread: how do you deal with a power that is, as Chatham House’s Galip Dalay put it, “indispensable, coercive and unpredictable at the same time?”

Turkey’s Foreign Minister Fidan: “If this region continues to wait for a savior, in the end it is going to continue facing these problems until eternity.” Jordan’s Foreign Minister Safadi: “We find ourselves subject to Iranian attacks that were unprovoked in a war that we tried to prevent.” Trump’s ambassador Tom Barrack told the conference that “this part of the world respects only one thing: power” and that only “benevolent monarchies” had worked as governments in the Middle East. The room received that observation with the silence it deserved.

The foreign ministers of Turkey, Saudi Arabia, Egypt and Pakistan met on the sidelines. A new coalition is forming — not against America, but around the fact of America’s unpredictability.

XI. The Gas Pump, From the Other Side

Two pieces this week bring the war’s economic consequences down to their most granular, human level. Kim and Balk’s reporting — “Energy Secretary Says Gas Prices May Stay Above $3 Until 2027” — documented Energy Secretary Wright’s CNN appearance Sunday in which he walked back his March prediction of sub-$3 gas “within weeks.” Now: “That could happen later this year. That might not happen until next year.” Pre-war national average: $2.98. Current: $4.05.

DePillis — “Why Gas Prices Go Up Fast and Take So Long to Fall” — provides the structural explanation from inside a gas station in Sherborn, Massachusetts. Owner Alex Weatherall: rack price up 39.6 percent since February 25, last truckload cost $39,488. “Because of the pressure, it feels to us like the price can never go up high enough to protect your margins. That’s why we’re temperamentally slow to take the price down.” The economics professor Matthew Lewis explains the behavioral pattern: when prices fall, consumers benchmark against last week’s price, not pre-war price. “Everyone just stops searching when the prices start to fall.” This is why Bessent’s June 20-September 20 $3 gas forecast was always a political statement, not an energy market prediction.

XII. What This Week Holds

The ceasefire expires this week. The second Islamabad round may or may not happen — Iran’s public signals are negative, Pakistan’s operational signals are positive. The Touska sits disabled in the Arabian Sea with US Marines aboard. Iran has promised retaliation once crew safety is confirmed. Trump has threatened every power plant and bridge in Iran if talks fail. Waltz has confirmed civilian infrastructure is a legitimate target. The Sheinerman-Ruehl legal record is being assembled.

Crowley and Fassihi reported last week that the two sides are nearing a three-page memorandum of understanding establishing a 60-day negotiating clock. Iran’s latest position: 10-year enrichment suspension plus 10 years of minimal lab enrichment. The nuclear gap between 20 years and 10+10 has narrowed from 15 years to something smaller. Whether that something smaller is enough to prevent the ceasefire from expiring without an extension is the question that will determine whether Part LXVIII is a diplomatic filing or a return-to-war filing.

Eyre’s conclusion is the one to carry forward into whatever comes next: Iran has discovered it holds geography as permanent deterrence. It is unlikely to release that hold for the foreseeable future, regardless of what is signed in Islamabad. The toll booth may be temporarily opened, closed, reopened and seized, but the key, as Citrinowicz said, is firmly in Tehran’s hands.

The Serena Hotel is cleared. Vance is in the air. The ceasefire expires Tuesday. In a gas station in Massachusetts, a man is slowly changing the magnetic numbers on his pump sign. In a village in southern Lebanon, a woman is standing in the rubble of her bedroom asking where the floor went.

This is Day 51.
