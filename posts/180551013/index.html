Part 6

Trump’s Second Term: When the System Collapsed

December 30, 2025

1,700 People Pardoned, $1.4 Billion in Victim Restitution Erased, “No MAGA Left Behind”

If Trump’s first term established disturbing patterns, his second term transformed them into a systematic operation. In less than one year (January-November 2025), Trump granted clemency to approximately 1,700 people—more than Bush (8 years), more than his own first term (4 years), and approaching Obama’s total (8 years).

But the numbers barely capture the story. Trump’s second term revealed:

  • Mass pardons for violent criminals (1,500+ January 6 rioters)

  • A documented lobbying industry where pardons cost hundreds of thousands or millions

  • $1.4 billion in victim restitution erased by presidential fiat

  • A pardon attorney who explicitly stated “No MAGA left behind”

  • Preemptive pardons for 77 co-conspirators before charges filed

This isn’t clemency. It’s the systematic destruction of accountability for anyone with the right connections.

Day One: The January 6 Mass Pardons

On January 20, 2025—Trump’s first day back in office—he issued approximately 1,500 pardons and 14 commutations for people convicted in connection with the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack.

This single act granted more pardons in one day than most presidents grant in entire terms.

The Crimes

The January 6 defendants were convicted of various offenses:

  • Trespassing (entering restricted Capitol grounds)

  • Assault on law enforcement (attacking police officers)

  • Destruction of property (breaking windows, damaging offices)

  • Seditious conspiracy (plotting to overthrow the government)

  • Other violent crimes

Among those pardoned or commuted:

  • Members of the Oath Keepers and Proud Boys convicted of seditious conspiracy

  • Individuals who assaulted Capitol Police officers

  • People convicted of bringing weapons to the Capitol

  • Those who threatened members of Congress

The Justification

Trump’s proclamation declared: “This proclamation ends a grave national injustice that has been perpetrated upon the American people over the last four years and begins a process of national reconciliation.”

He characterized all January 6 prosecutions as politically motivated persecution of his supporters.

The Legal Complications

The scope of Trump’s January 6 pardons became contentious. Some defendants facing separate charges—including gun possession, child pornography, and threatening FBI agents—argued the mass pardons covered these crimes too. In several cases, DOJ attorneys concurred, effectively expanding pardons to cover crimes beyond January 6 conduct.

The Victim Response

Capitol Police officers who were assaulted watched their attackers walk free. Capitol Police sergeant Aquilino Gonell and others who testified about their injuries and trauma saw Trump erase accountability.

One officer who was crushed in a doorway, suffering traumatic brain injury, saw his attacker’s sentence commuted to time served.

Trump’s mass pardons for January 6 participants were widely condemned by police unions and officers involved.

Applying Our Framework

1. Connection: Political—these were Trump supporters who attacked the Capitol to keep Trump in power

2. Nature of Crime: Range from trespassing to violent assault on officers to seditious conspiracy

3. Victim Impact: Capitol Police officers assaulted and injured, members of Congress threatened, democratic process attacked

4. Stage: Various—some before trial, some after conviction, some during appeals

5. Process: Presidential proclamation on first day, no individual review

6. Timing: Immediate upon taking office—political statement

7. Justification: Characterization of all prosecutions as political persecution

The Unprecedented Nature

No president has ever granted mass pardons to people convicted of violent crimes against law enforcement officers. The January 6 pardons represent the largest single-day grant of clemency to violent offenders in American history.

This wasn’t mercy for the unfortunate. This was political protection for political allies who committed violence in service of keeping Trump in power.

The Corruption of Process: “No MAGA Left Behind”

Trump’s approach to clemency in his second term revealed systematic corruption of the process itself.

The Pardon Attorney

Trump fired Liz Oyer, head of the Office of the Pardon Attorney, and installed political loyalist Ed Martin. Martin’s approach was explicit.

After one pardon, Martin posted: “No MAGA left behind.”

This wasn’t a slip or informal comment. It was the stated philosophy: political loyalty to Trump determines clemency.

Liz Oyer’s Testimony

After her firing, Oyer testified to the Senate, accusing the Justice Department of “ongoing corruption” and alleging that “the leadership of the Department of Justice appears to value political loyalty above the fair and responsible administration of justice.”

This is the former head of the Office of the Pardon Attorney—the person responsible for maintaining the integrity of the clemency process—testifying under oath that the Trump administration had corrupted it.

The Lobbying Industry

Trump’s second term saw the emergence of a documented lobbying industry for pardons.

The Joseph Schwartz Case:

Joseph Schwartz operated a nursing home fraud scheme that defrauded Medicare and Medicaid. He was convicted and sentenced.

To obtain a pardon, Schwartz’s associates paid $960,000 to two lobbyists—Brett Tolman (a former U.S. Attorney) and a partner—to petition the Trump administration.

Schwartz received his pardon.

This is the first documented case in modern American history where a specific dollar amount paid to lobbyists can be directly connected to a presidential pardon.

The House Judiciary Committee documented this transaction. It suggests many more such arrangements that remain undocumented.

Historical Context: Money and Clemency Before Trump

The Schwartz case isn’t the first time money influenced presidential pardons. It’s important to acknowledge the precedent.

Bill Clinton’s Marc Rich Pardon (January 2001):

On his final day in office, Clinton pardoned Marc Rich, a commodities trader who had fled the country in 1983 to avoid prosecution for tax evasion, wire fraud, and illegal oil trading with Iran during the hostage crisis. Rich faced potential decades in prison.

The connection to money was documented:

  • Rich’s ex-wife Denise Rich donated $450,000 to Clinton’s presidential library

  • She donated $100,000+ to Hillary Clinton’s Senate campaign

  • She donated to Democratic causes

  • Rich hired high-powered lawyers and lobbyists

The pardon sparked outrage. Federal prosecutors who built the case called it a betrayal. Congressional hearings investigated. Even Clinton later said he regretted it.

But the payment-to-pardon link was indirect. Denise Rich’s donations went to political causes and Clinton’s library—not directly to pardon lobbyists with a specific dollar figure attached to a specific outcome.

The Marc Rich precedent shows:

  1. Money influencing clemency isn’t new

  2. Last-day pardons for wealthy fugitives have happened before

  3. Presidential pardon power can override prosecutorial work

  4. Political donations + lobbying + wealthy defendant = pardon risk

What makes Trump’s second term different:

  1. Direct Documentation
    Schwartz case: $960,000 to specific lobbyists leading directly to pardon granted.

  2. Systematic Scale
    Clinton: One notorious case that damaged his legacy.
    Trump: Documented lobbying industry, likely dozens of similar arrangements.

  3. Explicit Policy
    Clinton: No stated policy of donations-for-pardons.
    Trump: “No MAGA left behind” openly declared by pardon attorney.

  4. Victim Restitution Erasure
    Clinton: Rich fled before trial, no victims waiting for restitution.
    Trump: $1.4 billion in restitution owed to fraud victims eliminated.

  5. Mass Pardons
    Clinton: 140 pardons on last day, most routine.
    Trump: 1,500+ violent criminals pardoned Day One.

The question isn’t whether money influenced clemency before Trump—it clearly did. The question is whether Trump systematized what was previously sporadic corruption, creating an explicit cash-for-clemency infrastructure with stated political loyalty tests.

The evidence suggests yes.

The Scale of Corruption

By November 2025, the pattern was clear:

  • Systematic bypass of DOJ review: Traditional clemency process abandoned

  • Explicit loyalty test: “No MAGA left behind” stated policy

  • Documented payments: $960,000 for Schwartz case, $1.8 million from Trevor Milton (who also hired Attorney General Pam Bondi's brother as pardon attorney), likely many more

  • Victim erasure: At least $1.4 billion in restitution and fines eliminated (including Shirleys' $17.8 million)

  • No individual review: Mass pardons granted without case-by-case analysis

The Office of the Pardon Attorney—established to maintain fairness and integrity—had been transformed into a political operation serving Trump’s interests.

The Racial Disparity

A December 2025 report by Rep. Ayanna Pressley (D-Mass.) analyzing Trump’s 2025 clemency actions revealed stark racial disparities.

Among the 1,700+ people granted clemency in 2025:

- 85% were white

- 8% were Black

- 5% were Latino

Compare this to the federal prison population:

- 25% white

- 34% Black

- 36% Latino

The disparity is systematic, not coincidental. The January 6 defendants—who make up 90% of Trump’s clemency recipients—are overwhelmingly white. The crimes pardoned are overwhelmingly political (attacking the Capitol to keep Trump in power), not the drug offenses that disproportionately imprison Black and Latino Americans.

Only nine clemency actions benefited people convicted of drug offenses—out of more than 60,000 people in federal prison for such crimes.

The pattern is clear: If you’re white, attacked the Capitol for Trump, and are politically aligned, you receive clemency. If you’re Black or Latino, imprisoned for drugs, and politically unconnected, you’re ignored.

One exception proves the rule: Trump pardoned Juan Orlando Hernández—a foreign head of state convicted of 400 tons of cocaine—after six months. But he freed Hernández not for mercy toward drug offenders, but for strategic election leverage in Honduras.

Hernández wasn’t freed because Trump cares about drug sentencing reform. He was freed because he was valuable to Trump’s foreign policy. The 60,000+ Americans—disproportionately Black and Latino—imprisoned for far smaller drug offenses remain in prison.

The clemency gap isn’t just about political connections or cash payments. It’s also about race. Trump’s systematic use of clemency reinforces rather than corrects the racial disparities in the criminal justice system.

As Rep. Pressley wrote to Alice Marie Johnson, Trump’s “pardon czar”: “Pardons and commutations have the power to transform people’s lives. It should not be limited to wealthy people and political allies.”

The irony: Johnson herself was freed by Trump in his first term after serving 21 years for a first-time, nonviolent drug offense. Yet under her watch as “pardon czar” in 2025, only nine drug offenders received clemency while 1,500+ January 6 rioters walked free.

The Pattern in Action: Brief Examples

While Part 7 will examine individual cases in detail, several examples illustrate the systematic pattern:

David Gentile defrauded 10,000 investors of $1.6 billion. He was sentenced to 7 years. Trump commuted his sentence after 13 days in prison. Alice Marie Johnson, Trump’s “pardon czar,” announced it on Thanksgiving Day.

George Santos, the disgraced former congressman, served 3 months of an 87-month sentence for massive fraud and identity theft before Trump commuted his sentence. Trump’s justification: “At least Santos had the Courage, Conviction, and Intelligence to ALWAYS VOTE REPUBLICAN!”

Juan Orlando Hernández, former president of Honduras, was convicted of facilitating 400 tons of cocaine into the United States and taking millions in cartel bribes. He was sentenced to 45 years. Trump pardoned him after less than 6 months, while simultaneously:

  • Bombing alleged drug boats in the Caribbean (killing 80+ people)

  • Threatening Mexico and Canada with massive tariffs over fentanyl flows

  • Calling Venezuela’s Maduro a “cartel boss”

  • Engaging in “formal armed conflict with the cartels”

The Hernández pardon came two days before Honduras’s election, with Trump explicitly tying it to his preferred candidate winning.

More examples from Trump's systematic pattern:

Michelle Fiore, a former Nevada GOP politician, raised funds to create a memorial honoring fallen police officers. She spent the money on cosmetic surgery and personal items instead. Convicted in 2024 of wire fraud, Trump pardoned her before sentencing. The donors who gave to honor fallen officers received no restitution.

The Shirleys were convicted of 12 counts of bank fraud and tax evasion. They owed $17.8 million in restitution and faced 12-year sentences. Trump pardoned them. They paid nothing. They served no time. The fraud victims received nothing.

Trevor Milton, founder of Nikola Corporation, defrauded investors of hundreds of millions by faking promotional videos and making false claims about production capability. He donated $1.8 million to Trump and hired Pam Bondi's brother as his pardon attorney. Trump pardoned him. (Pam Bondi is Trump's current Attorney General.)

Philip Esformes orchestrated the largest healthcare fraud in U.S. history—$1.4 billion in Medicare fraud. Sentenced to 20 years, Trump commuted his sentence in 2020. The twist: Esformes was arrested again on new criminal charges after Trump freed him, proving Trump's clemency recipients can return to crime immediately.

These aren’t isolated incidents. They’re examples of a systematic approach where:

  • Political loyalty determines clemency

  • Cash payments to lobbyists secure pardons

  • Victim restitution is erased without consideration

  • Foreign policy is weaponized through clemency

  • Strategic value to Trump matters more than evidence or justice

The GOP Politicians Pattern

Trump granted clemency to multiple convicted Republican lawmakers:

  • George Santos (fraud, identity theft—commuted after 3 months)

  • Michael Grimm (New York congressman, tax fraud—pardoned)

  • John Rowland (Connecticut congressman and governor, corruption—pardoned)

  • Steve Stockman (Texas congressman—commuted in 2020)

The common thread: Republicans who supported Trump.

The Fake Electors: Preemptive Protection

On November 9, 2025, Trump’s pardon attorney Ed Martin announced Trump had signed a proclamation granting preemptive pardons to 77 people associated with the fake electors plot to overturn the 2020 election results.

This included:

  • Rudy Giuliani (Trump’s personal attorney)

  • Mark Meadows (former White House Chief of Staff)

  • 75 others involved in creating false electoral certificates

These pardons were granted before any federal charges were filed. They’re purely protective—preventing potential future prosecutions.

The Significance

This is Trump pardoning his own co-conspirators in a scheme to overturn an election. These aren’t people convicted of crimes—they’re people who helped Trump try to remain in power illegally.

The pardon is admission that crimes may have been committed, but prevents accountability before charges can even be filed.

The Numbers: Scale of Systematic Corruption

As of November 18, 2025:

  • ~1,700 total clemency actions in second term (10 months)

  • 1,500+ January 6 pardons (violent criminals)

  • 144 individual pardons (exactly matching his first-term total)

  • 77 preemptive fake elector pardons

  • Multiple GOP politicians convicted of fraud

  • Documented $960,000 cash payment for one pardon (likely many more)

  • $1.4 billion in victim restitution erased per House Judiciary Committee

The Victim Restitution Erasure

The House Judiciary Committee documented that Trump’s clemency actions erased approximately $1.4 billion in victim restitution and fines.

This means:

  • Fraud victims won’t recover their losses

  • Taxpayers won’t recover stolen funds

  • Crime pays—if you have Trump connections

Previous presidents preserved victim restitution even when granting clemency. Bush, Obama, and Biden generally maintained that convicted individuals still owed victims compensation, even if prison sentences were reduced.

Trump eliminated this accountability entirely for his favored recipients.

The Grade

Using our analytical framework across all seven criteria, Trump’s second-term clemency earns an F.

Why F, not just D?

  1. Systematic, not isolated: This isn’t occasional abuse—it’s standard operating procedure

  2. Explicit corruption: “No MAGA left behind” openly stated policy

  3. Documented payments: Cash-for-clemency proven, not alleged

  4. Mass violent pardons: 1,500+ violent offenders freed on political grounds

  5. Victim erasure at scale: $1.4 billion—unprecedented in American history

  6. Corrupted process: Pardon attorney fired, loyalist installed, traditional review abandoned

  7. Preemptive self-protection: 77 co-conspirators pardoned before charges

This isn’t poor judgment. It’s the transformation of presidential clemency into a tool of political protection, financial transaction, and systematic victim erasure.
