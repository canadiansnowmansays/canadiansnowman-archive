The Venezuela Invasion Series

The Threat Matrix

A User's Guide to Justifying Regime Change

December 10, 2025

Inspired by Jon Stewart’s recent Daily Show monologue comparing Venezuela and Iraq, Maduro and Hussein. Sometimes a comedian sees patterns that pundits refuse to acknowledge. Thanks to Jon and The Daily Show for the clarity.

Pop quiz, America:

A country with massive oil reserves is run by a brutal dictator with a prominent mustache and a military beret. American politicians are suddenly—urgently, desperately—concerned about a deadly threat emanating from that country. A threat that’s killing Americans. And oh, by the way, the dictator is connected to America’s other enemies. The threat?

A) Weapons of Mass Destruction + Al Qaeda connection
B) Narcotics flowing across your borders + Iran connection
C) Both work, depending on the decade

If you answered C, congratulations. You’ve been paying attention. If you answered A or B specifically, you’re about to get a masterclass in American foreign policy reruns.

THE CASTING CALL: A TALE OF TWO DICTATORS

Before we dive in, let’s address the elephant—or rather, the mustache—in the room.

Look at these two men:

Saddam Hussein: Black beret. Prominent mustache. Military uniform. Authoritarian stare.

Nicolás Maduro: Red beret. Prominent mustache. Military-style presentation. Authoritarian stare.

If you were casting a movie called “Operation Definitely Not About Oil II: The Sequel,” could you find better visual continuity? It’s almost like Hollywood called and said, “We need someone who looks exactly like the last dictator we invaded, but in a different color scheme.”

It’s almost too perfect. The American public, whether consciously or not, looks at Maduro and sees... Saddam with a wardrobe change. Same energy. Same aesthetic. Same convenient oil reserves.

This isn’t conspiracy theory. This is marketing. When you’re selling regime change, aesthetics matter. A dictator who looks like the last dictator we invaded makes the narrative easier to sell. It triggers pattern recognition. “Remember that guy we took down? Well, we’ve got another one. Same mustache. Different beret. Also has oil. What are the odds?”

The human brain loves patterns. And American foreign policy loves exploiting that.

Now let’s look at the actual patterns.

ACT I: THE GREATEST HITS

Let me paint you two pictures. See if you can spot the differences.

Picture One: Iraq, 2003

The Dictator: Saddam Hussein. Brutal? Absolutely. Gassed his own people? Yes. A monster? No question.

The Threat Narrative: Weapons of Mass Destruction. Aluminum tubes that could only be for centrifuges! Mobile biological weapons labs! Yellowcake uranium from Niger! “We don’t want the smoking gun to be a mushroom cloud,” warned Condoleezza Rice.

The Bigger Villain Connection: Saddam → Al Qaeda → 9/11. Never mind that Saddam (secular Ba’athist) and Osama bin Laden (religious extremist) hated each other. Dick Cheney said they were connected. Repeatedly. On every Sunday show. Until 70% of Americans believed Saddam was personally involved in 9/11.

The Urgency: We cannot wait. American lives are at stake. The threat is imminent.

The Evidence: Trust us. We have intelligence. It’s classified, but it’s definitely there.

Oh, and by the way: Iraq happens to have 115 billion barrels of proven oil reserves. But that’s just a coincidence. This is about protecting Americans.

Picture Two: Venezuela, 2025

The Dictator: Nicolás Maduro. Brutal? Absolutely. Destroyed his country’s economy? Yes. A tyrant? No question.

The Threat Narrative: Drugs. Venezuela is a “narco-state” flooding America with poison. Fentanyl is killing our children! The cartels are operating with impunity! Maduro is personally responsible for the overdose crisis!

The Bigger Villain Connection: Maduro → Iran → Hezbollah → Terrorism. Marco Rubio on Fox News warning about “Iran’s influence in Venezuela.” Iranian missiles in our hemisphere! Terrorist cells! A foothold for our enemies 90 miles from Florida! (Wait, that’s Cuba. But you get the idea.)

The Urgency: We cannot wait. American lives are at stake. The threat is imminent.

The Evidence: Trust us. We have intelligence. Look at the statistics. Something must be done.

Oh, and by the way: Venezuela happens to have 303 billion barrels of proven oil reserves—the largest in the world. But that’s just a coincidence. This is about protecting Americans.

ACT II: THE PLAYBOOK

Let me show you how this works, because apparently we’re doing this again.

STEP 1: Start With a Real Problem

This is important. You can’t manufacture everything. You need a kernel of truth.

  • Iraq: International terrorism exists. 9/11 just happened. Americans are terrified.

  • Venezuela: A drug crisis exists. Fentanyl overdoses are real. Americans are dying.

The problem doesn’t have to be caused by your target. It just has to exist.

STEP 2: Connect Your Target to the Problem

Now comes the creative part.

Iraq: Saddam Hussein → Al Qaeda.

Was there any evidence connecting them? No. Did Saddam (a secular dictator) and Osama bin Laden (a religious extremist) hate each other? Yes. Did that matter? No.

Dick Cheney simply asserted the connection. Repeatedly. On every news show. Until enough Americans believed it that they could invade.

Post-invasion finding: No connection. Oops.

Venezuela: Maduro → American overdose crisis.

Is Venezuela involved in drug trafficking? Yes. Is it the primary source of fentanyl killing Americans? No—that’s China manufacturing it and Mexican cartels smuggling it. Does Venezuela even share a border with the United States? No.

Does that matter? You tell me.

STEP 2.5: Connect Your Target to an Even BIGGER Villain

But wait—there’s more! A single dictator with drugs or weapons isn’t quite scary enough. You need to connect them to America’s existing arch-nemeses. The threats Americans already fear.

Iraq: Saddam → Al Qaeda → 9/11

The inconvenient truth: Saddam Hussein (secular Ba’athist who drank whiskey and wore Western suits) and Osama bin Laden (religious extremist who wanted to overthrow secular Arab governments) despised each other. Bin Laden literally called for Saddam’s overthrow. They were enemies.

The marketing pitch: Just ignore that and keep saying they’re connected.

Dick Cheney on Meet the Press. Condoleezza Rice in press briefings. Donald Rumsfeld at Pentagon pressers. Same message, different suits: “Saddam has ties to Al Qaeda.”

The result: By 2003, 70% of Americans believed Saddam was personally involved in 9/11.

Required evidence: None. Just repetition.

Sound familiar? Know anyone else who lies and repeats until his base believes him? Turns out Dick Cheney pioneered a technique that would later become a whole political brand.

Venezuela: Maduro → Iran → Hezbollah → Terrorism

The inconvenient truth: Venezuela and Iran are 6,000 miles apart. Their primary connection is that they’re both oil-producing countries under U.S. sanctions. Venezuela’s drug trafficking (which is real) has nothing to do with Iran.

The marketing pitch: Marco Rubio on Fox News: “Iran’s influence in Venezuela represents a threat to our hemisphere.” Unnamed intelligence officials warning about Hezbollah cells in Latin America. Iranian missiles potentially within range of Florida!

The result: TBD, but the seeds are planted.

Required evidence: “Intelligence suggests...” which is code for “trust us.”

Why This Step Matters:

Because Americans have threat fatigue. We’re told everything is a threat. To break through the noise, you need to connect your target to something Americans already fear deeply.

  • Iraq couldn’t just be about a dictator; it had to be about THE TERRORISTS who attacked us on 9/11

  • Venezuela can’t just be about Maduro; it has to be about IRAN (nuclear weapons! Middle East terrorism! Hezbollah!)

It’s threat multiplication. Take a real dictator (Maduro), add a real problem (drugs), then multiply by existing fear (Iran = terrorism). Suddenly you’re not just dealing with a South American country in chaos—you’re facing an existential threat in your own hemisphere connected to your sworn enemy half a world away.

The Pattern:

  • Find pre-existing American fear (Al Qaeda after 9/11, Iran always)

  • Draw lines connecting it to your target (no matter how tenuous)

  • Repeat until the connection seems obvious

  • Invade

STEP 3: Make It Existential

The threat must be:

  • Imminent enough to demand immediate action

  • Vague enough to avoid verification

  • Scary enough to override rational debate

Iraq: “We cannot wait for the final proof to come in the form of a mushroom cloud.”

Venezuela: “We cannot stand by while another American child dies from an overdose.”

See how that works? Who wants to be the politician arguing for patience when mushroom clouds or dead children are on the table?

STEP 4: Prepare the “Liberation”

  • Iraq: Freedom! Democracy! Liberation! (Oh, and here are the oil field contracts for Halliburton, ExxonMobil, and Shell.)

  • Venezuela: Freedom! Democracy! Humanitarian intervention! (Oh, and here are the provisional agreements for oil extraction once we “stabilize” the country.)

STEP 5: Dismiss the Skeptics

Iraq:

  • UN Weapons Inspectors: “We can’t find anything.”

  • Response: “That just proves how well-hidden they are!”

  • Experts: “This doesn’t add up.”

  • Response: “You’re emboldening Saddam!”

Venezuela:

  • Drug policy experts: “The fentanyl is coming from China and Mexico.”

  • Response: “Maduro is still a narco-trafficker!”

  • Latin American specialists: “This will destabilize the entire region.”

  • Response: “You’re defending a dictator!”

ACT III: THE INCONVENIENT QUESTIONS

Here are the questions nobody in power wants to answer:

On the Venezuela “Drug Crisis” Narrative:

Q: If drugs are the existential threat, why aren’t we invading Mexico, where the cartels actually operate and through which the drugs actually flow?

A: Mexico is our third-largest trading partner, and we need their cooperation on immigration. Also, they don’t have 303 billion barrels of oil. Next question.

Q: If the humanitarian crisis is the issue, why now? Maduro’s been destroying Venezuela for years. Where was the urgency in 2015? 2018? 2020?

A: [Checks oil prices] Interesting timing, isn’t it?

Q: If democracy and human rights are the goals, why do we support Saudi Arabia, a country that just murdered and dismembered a journalist, didn’t allow women to drive until recently, and executes people for witchcraft?

A: Different oil. I mean, different situation.

Q: If we’re so concerned about drug trafficking, why don’t we address the demand side—the American addiction crisis, the overprescription of opioids, the lack of treatment facilities?

A: That sounds expensive and complicated. Invading Venezuela is simpler.

Q: Wait, didn’t the administration just threaten tariffs on Canada to stop drug smuggling? If tariffs work to stop drugs, why not just tariff Venezuela instead of invading?

A: [Long pause] You’re not supposed to think about that. The tariff threat to Canada was about... look, it’s different. Canada we can tariff because we need their cooperation and they’re not really the drug problem. Venezuela we need to invade because of the drugs. Even though Mexico is the actual drug corridor. And China makes the fentanyl. But tariffs for Canada, invasion for Venezuela. See? Perfectly logical.

Q: But wait—Canada has the third-largest oil reserves in the world. 168 billion barrels. If this is really about oil, shouldn’t you be invading Canada?

A: [Nervous laughter] Canada is our ally and largest trading partner! We can’t just—

Q: The administration literally floated annexing Canada as the 51st state.

A: That was a joke! Probably. Maybe. Look, Canada is different because—

Q: Because they’re white, speak English, have a functioning military, and oh right—their massive landmass provides a crucial buffer between the United States and Russia? Especially with the Arctic becoming a strategic hotspot?

A: [Even longer pause] Canada’s geography is... strategically important for continental defense, yes. NORAD and all that. And Arctic sovereignty. And—

Q: So you need Canada as a buffer state but don’t need Venezuela for anything except their oil?

A: This is getting complicated.

Q: It’s actually very simple. Tariffs are for oil-rich countries you need for strategic reasons. Invasion is for oil-rich countries you don’t need for strategic reasons and can’t fight back.

A: You’re way too close to the pattern now. NEXT QUESTION.

On the Iran Connection:

Q: Wait, now Venezuela is connected to Iran? How does that work?

A: Same way Iraq was connected to Al Qaeda—through the magic of repetition and vague “intelligence.”

Q: But Iraq and Al Qaeda hated each other. The connection was false.

A: Correct! And it only took 20 years, a trillion dollars, and hundreds of thousands of deaths to admit it. But surely we’ve learned from that mistake.

Q: Have we?

A: [Gestures at Marco Rubio on Fox News talking about Iran’s influence in Venezuela]

Q: So what’s the actual Venezuela-Iran connection?

A: Iran and Venezuela have economic ties. They’ve traded oil technology. There are probably some Hezbollah sympathizers somewhere in Latin America. Now connect those dots! (Don’t ask for evidence of the actual dots.)

Q: That sounds... thin.

A: So did the Saddam-Al Qaeda connection. Didn’t stop us then.

Q: But why Iran specifically?

A: Because Americans already fear Iran. It’s pre-sold fear. You don’t have to explain why Iran is scary; decades of “Death to America” chants did that work already. So if Venezuela is connected to Iran, Venezuela becomes scarier by association. It’s threat laundering.

Q: Isn’t this just finding any connection to make the threat seem bigger than it is?

A: Now you’re getting it! You take a real problem (Maduro is terrible), add a real but unrelated problem (drugs), multiply by a pre-existing fear (Iran), and suddenly you’ve got an existential threat that justifies military intervention.

Q: Did this exact tactic work with Iraq?

A: It got us into a 20-year war that destabilized an entire region and created ISIS, so yes, it “worked” depending on your definition of success.

Q: And we’re doing it again?

A: Pattern recognition is apparently not a strong suit in American foreign policy.

On the Iraq Precedent:

Q: How did the Iraq War turn out?

A:

  • Cost: $2 trillion and counting

  • American casualties: 4,500 dead, 32,000 wounded

  • Iraqi civilian deaths: 200,000+ (conservative estimate)

  • Weapons of Mass Destruction found: Zero

  • Democracy established: Questionable

  • Regional stability achieved: ISIS happened

  • “Mission Accomplished” sign aged: Poorly

Q: Did anyone face consequences for being catastrophically wrong?

A: The architects of the war went on to lucrative careers in think tanks and cable news. Colin Powell, who delivered the infamous UN presentation, later called it a permanent “blot” on his record. George W. Bush paints now.

Q: So we learned from our mistakes?

A: [Gestures at Venezuela crisis] What do you think?

ACT IV: THE PATTERN

Let me show you something interesting. These are actual quote structures. Guess which is Iraq and which is Venezuela:

Quote 1: “We know he has weapons of mass destruction. We have intelligence that proves it. The threat is real and imminent.”

Quote 2: “We know the regime is facilitating drug trafficking. We have intelligence that proves it. The threat is real and imminent.”

Quote 3: “The people are suffering under a brutal dictator. America has a moral obligation to act.”

Quote 4: “Regime change is not our goal. We just want to protect American interests and promote stability.”

Quote 5: “There is credible evidence of ties between the regime and terrorist organizations that threaten Americans.”

Quote 6: “We cannot allow a hostile regime in league with our enemies to operate freely in this strategic region.”

Trick question: All six could apply to either situation.

The rhetoric is interchangeable. Change “WMDs” to “drugs,” swap “Al Qaeda” for “Iran,” and you’ve got the same speech separated by 22 years.

THE TELL

Here’s how you know you’re being sold a bill of goods:

When a politician tells you that a country poses an urgent, existential threat to Americans, and that country happens to sit on massive oil reserves, you’re watching a sequel.

The script changes. The actors change. The threat narrative changes. But the target convenience remains remarkably consistent.

The Iraq Formula:

  • Take a real problem (terrorism post-9/11)

  • Connect it to a dictator with oil (no matter how tenuous)

  • Connect that dictator to America’s enemies (Al Qaeda)

  • Make it existential (WMDs/mushroom clouds)

  • Invade

  • Discover the threat was exaggerated and the connections were false

  • Stay anyway (the oil contracts are signed)

The Venezuela Formula:

  • Take a real problem (drug crisis)

  • Connect it to a dictator with oil (no matter how tenuous)

  • Connect that dictator to America’s enemies (Iran)

  • Make it existential (American children dying)

  • Invade?

  • Discover the threat was exaggerated and the connections were false?

  • Stay anyway? (the oil contracts are ready)

We’re literally watching the same movie with different costumes.

ACT V: A MODEST PROPOSAL

Here’s a radical idea that will never happen:

If you want their oil, just say so.

“Fellow Americans, Venezuela has 303 billion barrels of oil. We’d like access to it. We’re prepared to negotiate a fair price. We can offer them technology, investment, and trade. If Maduro won’t deal, we’ll wait for a government that will. In the meantime, we’ll pursue actual solutions to the fentanyl crisis—you know, addressing the Chinese manufacturers and Mexican smuggling routes, investing in treatment, reducing demand.”

Can you imagine? Honesty in foreign policy?

Instead, we get:

  • Noble rhetoric about democracy

  • Terrifying warnings about threats

  • Urgent calls for action

  • And oh by the way, oil

It’s like watching someone rob a bank while loudly explaining they’re just there to improve security systems.

FAQ FOR AMERICANS TOO YOUNG TO REMEMBER 2003

Q: “But the drugs are real! People are dying!”

A: The terrorism threat in 2001 was real too. 9/11 happened. The WMDs were the lie used to invade Iraq. The drug crisis is real. The “Venezuela is the source” narrative is the lie being used now.

Q: “Are you defending Maduro?”

A: No. Maduro is a tyrant who’s destroyed his country. Saddam was a monster who gassed his own people. Both things can be true: they’re terrible, AND invading their countries for oil under false pretenses is also terrible.

Q: “So what should we do about Venezuela?”

A: How about not invading countries? Address the actual sources of fentanyl? Invest in treatment? Acknowledge that regime change in oil-rich countries has a 0% success rate in achieving stated goals?

Q: “Why does this keep happening?”

A: Because there’s no accountability. The people who lied us into Iraq are still respected foreign policy voices. The oil companies made billions. The defense contractors made billions. The politicians who voted for it got reelected.

Why would they stop?

THE 20-YEAR CYCLE

  • 1990: Iraq invades Kuwait (oil crisis #1)

  • 1991: Desert Storm (oil restoration #1)

  • 2003: Iraq has WMDs (oil acquisition #1)

  • 2011: Iraq War ends (technically)

  • 2014: ISIS emerges from Iraq War chaos

  • 2025: Venezuela is a narco-state (oil opportunity #2)

  • 2045: Check back for the next one

Somewhere, there’s an oil company executive with a calendar.

BREAKING: THEY’RE NOT EVEN HIDING IT ANYMORE

UPDATE - December 2025:

While writing this piece, the United States seized a Venezuelan oil tanker off Venezuela’s coast.

Let that sink in.

When Iran seizes tankers in the Persian Gulf, American politicians call it:

  • Piracy

  • An act of war

  • Proof they’re a rogue state

  • International terrorism

  • A threat to global shipping

  • Justification for military escalation

When the United States seizes a Venezuelan tanker off Venezuela’s coast, those same politicians call it:

  • Sanctions enforcement

  • Legal authority under international law

  • Protecting American interests

  • Completely normal and nothing to see here

The tanker, of course, was carrying oil.

Not drugs. Oil.

If you still think the Venezuela crisis is about narcotics or democracy or humanitarian concerns, ask yourself this: Why are we seizing their oil tankers?

Iran does this and we threaten war. We do it and call it law enforcement.

The quiet part isn’t even quiet anymore. It’s just that nobody wants to hear it.

CLOSING: A LETTER FROM YOUR NORTHERN NEIGHBOR

Dear Americans,

I’m writing this as a Canadian who’s watched American foreign policy for seven decades. I have no electoral stake in your decisions. No partisan angle. Just the perspective of someone who’s seen this movie before and is watching you queue up for the sequel.

I remember the Iraq War buildup. I watched Colin Powell’s UN presentation. I heard the warnings about mushroom clouds. I saw the “intelligence” that turned out to be fabricated. I witnessed the consequences.

Now I’m watching the same movie again, and you should be too.

When your politicians tell you they need to invade a country because of a threat to Americans, and that country happens to have massive oil reserves, you’re being sold something.

The WMD script didn’t work out so well. So now they’re trying the Drug War script. Different threat, same target convenience.

Here’s what I learned from watching Iraq from across the border:

They will lie. Not necessarily because they’re evil, but because they’ve convinced themselves the ends justify the means. “Sure, there might not be WMDs, but Saddam is terrible and we need the oil, so...”

Your media will help. Not because they’re complicit, but because “access journalism” means printing what officials say. Skeptical journalists get frozen out.

The public will believe it. Not because Americans are stupid, but because people want to believe their government is honest and their cause is just.

The consequences will be catastrophic. In ways nobody predicted and everyone will deny responsibility for.

Nobody will face accountability. The architects will write memoirs. The war profiteers will keep their profits. The politicians will move on.

And twenty years later, they’ll try it again.

You are here.

From up here in Canada, we have a saying: “We’re close enough to watch, far enough to see the pattern.”

And the pattern is clear.

If you’re going to invade Venezuela, at least be honest about why. If it’s about oil, say so. If it’s about establishing a friendly government that will give you favorable contracts, say so. If it’s about projecting power in your hemisphere, say so.

But please, spare the noble rhetoric about drugs and democracy while reaching for the oil fields.

We’ve seen this movie. We know how it ends.

The question is: are you going to buy tickets again?

Sincerely,
A Canadian Who Remembers What “Mission Accomplished” Looked Like
And Who Shares a Border With the Consequences

POSTSCRIPT: THE VIEW FROM THE 49TH PARALLEL

From up here, we watch with a mixture of concern and bewilderment.

Concern, because when America goes to war, we feel the ripple effects. Refugees. Economic disruption. The destabilization of entire regions. And yes, we share a border with you—when your foreign policy adventures go sideways, we don’t get to ignore the fallout.

Bewilderment, because we keep watching you do the same thing expecting different results.

If invading oil-rich countries under humanitarian pretenses is such a great idea, why haven’t you done it to Russia?

Oh right. They have nuclear weapons.

Funny how the “existential threats” always seem to come from countries that can’t actually fight back.

THE ONE THING NOBODY WANTS TO ADMIT

The most uncomfortable truth about the Iraq War wasn’t that the intelligence about WMDs was wrong.

It’s that the intelligence didn’t matter.

The decision to invade was made first. The justification was constructed afterward. The WMD narrative was the marketing campaign, not the reason. The Al Qaeda connection was fabricated to multiply the fear. Together, they sold a war.

And now, with Venezuela, we’re watching the same process in real time.

The decision has been made. The justification is being constructed. The drug narrative is the marketing campaign. The Iran connection is the fear multiplier.

The question isn’t whether Maduro is terrible—he is.

The question isn’t whether drugs are a crisis—they are.

The question is whether you’re going to let them use a real crisis (drugs) to justify a predetermined policy goal (regime change in an oil-rich country) by pretending the crisis and the target and Iran are all meaningfully connected in a way that requires military intervention.

Because that’s the pattern.

Same formula. Different decade. Different dictator with the same mustache. Different threat. Different enemy connection. Same oil.

And patterns, once you see them, are hard to unsee.
