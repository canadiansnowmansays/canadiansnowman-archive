The Exception Clause

The Surveillance Mosque

Part VII: How America Built a Domestic Surveillance State Targeting a Single Religion

April 29, 2026

On the morning of September 12, 2001, the infrastructure described in Part VI of this series was ready. The think tanks had their frameworks. The FBI had its precedents. The congressional committees had their witnesses. The legal architecture — the Reynolds belief-practice distinction, the Korematsu military necessity exception, the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act’s broad authority over domestic intelligence collection — was in place. What had been missing was the event that would allow the infrastructure to operate without the social and political constraints that had previously limited it.

The event had now occurred.

What followed was the most extensive domestic surveillance apparatus ever constructed by the United States government targeting a single religious community — an apparatus that operated across federal, state, and local levels, that penetrated mosques, civil society organizations, student groups, and private businesses, that placed thousands of Americans on watchlists without charge or evidence of wrongdoing, and that did all of this while maintaining, with varying degrees of plausibility, that it was not targeting religion but targeting the security threat that happened to be concentrated in a particular religious community.

The distinction collapsed under examination. The apparatus targeted Muslims. The religious community was the unit of surveillance. The mosque was the intelligence target. The Muslim civil liberties lawyer was the potential agent of a foreign ideology. The distinction between surveilling a religion and surveilling a security threat that expressed itself in religious language was the Reynolds belief-practice distinction applied at institutional scale — and it was just as hollow in 2002 as it had been in 1879.

The Legal Architecture

George W. Bush: signing of USA PATRIOT Act Pres. George W. Bush signing the USA PATRIOT Act in the East Room of the White House, Washington, D.C., October 26, 2001.

The USA PATRIOT Act, passed six weeks after September 11 with minimal congressional debate and almost no dissent, was the foundational legal instrument. Its provisions were extensive and its implications for civil liberties were substantial, but the most significant for Muslim Americans were the sections that expanded the government’s authority to conduct surveillance of people without establishing probable cause of a specific crime.

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, amended by the PATRIOT Act, allowed the government to conduct surveillance of American citizens and residents on the basis of their association with a “foreign power” or an “agent of a foreign power” — categories that the amended act interpreted broadly enough to encompass membership in organizations that had any connection, however attenuated, to foreign governments or movements. The FISA court, which approved surveillance requests in secret proceedings without adversarial representation, approved virtually every request it received.

Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act allowed the FBI to obtain “any tangible things” — records, documents, data — relevant to a terrorism investigation without a traditional warrant. The standard was not probable cause. It was relevance — a standard so broad that it effectively removed judicial constraint from the collection of records about anyone the FBI deemed potentially relevant to any investigation. It was used to collect the phone records of virtually every American, a program that was not publicly disclosed until Edward Snowden’s revelations in 2013.

The legal architecture was formally neutral — it applied to everyone, not specifically to Muslims. In practice, the targets were overwhelmingly Muslim. The FBI’s terrorism investigations after September 11 were focused almost entirely on Muslim communities. The FISA warrants were almost entirely directed at Muslim individuals and organizations. The neutrality of the legal language was the cover; the religious targeting was the content.

The FBI and the Mosque

Muslims reported self-censoring in religious settings, avoiding political discussions, and declining to attend Friday prayers. The surveillance apparatus did not need to produce prosecutions to do damage. Its existence was enough. Source: CNN

The FBI’s approach to Muslim communities after September 11 drew directly on the framework it had developed in its surveillance of the Nation of Islam and Black political organizations in the 1950s and 1960s: the mosque as organizational center, the religious community as intelligence environment, the religious leader as potential radicalization vector.

The bureau deployed informants inside mosques — people paid to attend religious services, report on sermons, identify potential extremists, and sometimes to approach community members with proposals for violent action in order to assess whether they would accept. The informant program was not targeted at specific individuals suspected of specific crimes. It was targeted at religious institutions as institutions — at mosques as potential radicalization sites regardless of any evidence that radicalization was occurring.

The consequences for targeted communities were substantial. The knowledge that informants were present — or might be present — in mosques, community centers, and Muslim student associations produced a pervasive atmosphere of surveillance that affected religious practice directly. Muslims reported self-censoring in religious contexts, avoiding discussions of political topics in mosques, declining to attend Friday prayers for fear that attendance itself might attract attention. The surveillance apparatus did not need to produce prosecutions to do damage. The knowledge of its existence was enough to chill religious expression in precisely the way the First Amendment’s free exercise clause was designed to prevent.

The NYPD’s Demographics Unit — later renamed the Zone Assessment Unit — went further than the FBI in its systematic approach. Operating from 2002 under the direction of a CIA officer seconded to the NYPD, the unit mapped Muslim communities across New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut. It compiled databases of Muslim-owned businesses, Muslim student organizations, and mosques. It assigned “rakers” — undercover officers — to spend time in Muslim neighborhoods, documenting community life. It sent officers to infiltrate Muslim student associations at universities including Yale, Columbia, and the University of Pennsylvania.

The unit operated for more than a decade before an Associated Press investigation exposed it in 2011. Its director testified that it had never generated a single lead in a terrorism investigation. It had produced, in more than a decade of operation, no actionable intelligence. What it had produced was a comprehensive map of Muslim community life in the New York metropolitan area — a map that existed not because there was evidence of threat but because the community itself was treated as a threat environment.

The No-Fly List and the Right to Travel

The Terrorist Screening Center’s consolidated watchlist, and the subset of it known as the No-Fly List, became one of the most consequential — and least constitutionally examined — instruments of post-9/11 targeting.

By 2014, the watchlist contained approximately 680,000 names. Of those, approximately 280,000 had “no recognized terrorist group affiliation” — they were not connected to any known terrorist organization but had been placed on the list through the application of “reasonable suspicion” criteria that were secret, not subject to meaningful judicial review, and extremely difficult to challenge.

Muslim Americans were disproportionately represented on the list — disproportionately enough that civil liberties organizations documented systematic patterns of targeting that bore no plausible relationship to individual evidence of wrongdoing. American citizens on the list who had never been charged with a crime were barred from boarding commercial flights. They were barred from returning to the United States after international travel. They were not told why they were on the list. They had no effective legal mechanism to challenge their placement.

The constitutional implications were serious. The right to travel — both domestically and internationally — had been recognized by the Supreme Court as a fundamental right. The government was depriving American citizens of that right on the basis of secret criteria, without charge, without notice of the specific basis for the determination, and without any meaningful opportunity to contest it.

The legal challenges took years to work through the courts. In 2014, a federal district court in Oregon ruled that the existing redress process for people on the No-Fly List was unconstitutional. The government revised the process. Civil liberties organizations continued to challenge it. The list continued to function.

The No-Fly List is the administrative state’s version of the Edmunds-Tucker Act — an instrument that strips members of a targeted religious community of a fundamental civil right on the basis of criteria that are not disclosed to the people they affect, not subject to meaningful judicial review, and applied with a targeting pattern that reflects the community’s religious identity rather than individual evidence of wrongdoing.

The Informant Trap

In a pattern documented extensively by civil liberties organizations and investigative journalists, FBI informants approached psychologically vulnerable Muslim men — men with histories of mental illness, addiction, financial desperation, or social isolation — made contact with them through mosques or community organizations, developed relationships over months or years, gradually introduced the topic of violent jihad, provided encouragement and sometimes logistical support for attack planning, and then arrested the men for conspiracy to commit terrorism.

The Human Rights Watch 2014 report Illusion of Justice documented twenty-seven such cases in detail. In virtually all of them, the government’s informant had been the most enthusiastic advocate for violence in the alleged conspiracy. In many cases, the defendants had shown no independent interest in terrorism before the informant’s approach. In several cases, the defendants were found by courts to have been mentally incompetent to stand trial.

The prosecutions were legally viable — conspiracy law does not require that the planned act would have occurred without government inducement. The men were convicted. They served sentences of ten, fifteen, twenty years in federal prison for plots that would not have existed without the FBI’s active construction of them.

This is the template at a level of granularity that the historical chapters have not required. It is not the persecution of a community through legislation or military force. It is the persecution of individuals within a community through the deliberate exploitation of their vulnerability — using the mosque as an access point, using the language of religious obligation as an inducement, and then using the resulting “conspiracy” as evidence that the community from which the target was drawn required continued surveillance.

The circularity is complete and self-reinforcing. Surveillance identifies vulnerable individuals. Informants cultivate those individuals. Prosecutions result. The prosecutions are used as evidence that the surveillance was justified. The surveillance continues.

The Constitutional Accounting

The post-9/11 surveillance apparatus has never received a comprehensive constitutional accounting. Elements of it have been challenged, modified, partially ruled unconstitutional, and revised — but the overall architecture remains in place, and its targeting of Muslim communities has not been judicially addressed as a systematic First Amendment violation.

The cases that have come closest are the NYPD Demographics Unit litigation, in which the Third Circuit Court of Appeals ruled in 2019 that Muslim plaintiffs had adequately alleged First Amendment violations by demonstrating that the surveillance had chilled their religious practice. The case was remanded for further proceedings. It has not produced a definitive ruling on the constitutionality of the underlying surveillance program.

The ACLU’s challenges to NSA surveillance, the Council on American-Islamic Relations’ challenges to FBI informant programs, the lawsuits brought by individuals on the No-Fly List — each has produced partial results, modified specific practices, and established procedural protections in specific contexts. None has produced the ruling that would address the fundamental question: whether the government violated the First Amendment by treating membership in a religious community as a basis for security concern.

That question remains open.

It remains open because the courts have been reluctant to address it directly — because addressing it directly would require either validating the surveillance apparatus explicitly, which the courts have generally declined to do, or striking it down as unconstitutional, which would have consequences for ongoing security programs that the courts have been equally reluctant to confront.

The First Amendment says Congress shall make no law prohibiting the free exercise of religion.

The government built a surveillance apparatus that treated the exercise of a specific religion as a security concern, monitored the exercise of that religion at institutional scale, and chilled religious practice across an entire community.

The constitutional question of whether that violates the First Amendment has not been definitively answered.
