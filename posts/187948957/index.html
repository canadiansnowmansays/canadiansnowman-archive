Two Americas

The Arrival

An Addendum to “Two Americas on the Same Road”

February 14, 2026

“Aloka the Peace Dog, heart blazing on his bandana, leads the monks. A rescue pup’s love walked 2,300 miles for strangers.

It’s Valentine’s Day. A day about love.

This week, nineteen Buddhist monks finished walking 2,300 miles to tell America that peace begins with five seconds of mindfulness. And this morning, the Department of Homeland Security shut down because Democrats refused to fund an agency that killed two Americans in Minneapolis and lied about it.

Two acts of love. One for strangers. One for power.

This is the story of the week they both arrived in Washington.

Caroline Gutman for The New York Times

On the morning of February 10, 2026, nineteen Buddhist monks crossed the Chain Bridge from Virginia into Washington, D.C. It was Day 108. They had walked 2,300 miles from Fort Worth, Texas, through ten states, through an unusually brutal Southern winter, through snow and ice in places that rarely see either. Bhikkhu Pannakara walked barefoot. He has walked barefoot for most of the journey. His feet on frozen pavement. His choice.

That same week, the Department of Homeland Security’s press office was celebrating what it calls “WOW.DHS.GOV” — a public database where Secretary Kristi Noem’s department posts daily arrest announcements with the breathless cadence of a game show. “ICE Arrests Criminal Illegal Alien Multiple Child Rapists, Kidnappers, and Violent Assailants.” Every day, a new headline. Every headline, a new superlative. The branding tells you everything you need to know about what this administration thinks law enforcement is: entertainment.

The monks asked for five seconds.

Noem promised all gas, no brakes.

Two Americas arrived in Washington the same week. Only one was invited.

When I wrote the original “Boots and Barefoot” essay in January, the monks were still in the Carolinas and the contrast was already stark — saffron robes versus tactical gear, peace bracelets versus administrative warrants, barefoot monks versus agents in body armor kicking down doors at dawn. But the walk wasn’t finished, and neither was the story. Now both are.

What happened in the weeks between tells you everything about the two Americas sharing the same roads.

Through brutal Southern winter and frozen roads: Saffron robes, simple flowers, unwavering steps. The barefoot path to Washington—love walking quietly in 2026

The barefoot: The monks walked through the worst winter weather the South had seen in years. They crossed into Virginia. Governor Abigail Spanberger met them and proclaimed February 2 “Walk for Peace Day in the Commonwealth of Virginia.” Their social media following exploded past 2.8 million on Facebook and 1.3 million on TikTok. Aloka the dog — with his heart-shaped mark and his own million-follower Facebook page — became arguably the most famous rescue animal in America. People flew from Utah, drove eleven hours from Maine, took trains from the North Carolina border. A woman named Britani Meadows left her brother in intensive care because her family told her to go and bring back blessings. A woman named Donzella Logan, a Christian from southern Virginia, waited two hours in below-zero wind chill after missing the monks once in South Carolina. She was certain they were on a mission chartered by Jesus, even though they were Buddhists. “We may not be speaking in the same language,” she said, “but it adds up to the same thing.”

Dawn raid force: Agents drag a resident from home in subzero cold. Wrong house, lasting trauma—the boots’ cost.

The boots: In Minneapolis, Operation Metro Surge — the largest immigration enforcement operation ever carried out on American soil — was reaching its violent crescendo. Whistleblowers inside DHS provided Congress with a secret May 2025 memorandum signed by Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons authorizing agents to enter homes without judicial warrants, in direct violation of the Fourth Amendment. ICE agents broke into the home of U.S. citizen ChongLy “Scott” Thao in St. Paul, held him at gunpoint in subfreezing temperatures while he stood in his underwear and sandals, and left without apology when they realized they had raided the wrong house. Renée Good was killed. Alex Pretti was killed. Fifty-three people died in ICE custody. One hundred and forty Democratic members of Congress co-sponsored articles of impeachment against Noem. Sixty CEOs of Minnesota-based companies — 3M, Cargill, Mayo Clinic, Target, Best Buy, UnitedHealth Group — signed an open letter calling for immediate de-escalation. And Democrats refused to fund DHS until the boots were brought to heel.

Overcrowded holding: Families on floors under foil blankets. 74% no criminal record—the boots deliver numbers, not safety

Meanwhile, seventy-four percent of the people in ICE detention had no criminal record. Ninety-three percent had never been convicted of a violent crime.

The monks were asking Americans to live five seconds with mindfulness. DHS was issuing directives to arrest three thousand people a day.

Kaylee Peters, a nursing educator from Maryland, stood on the side of a road in Virginia in below-zero wind chill, tears collecting behind her sunglasses, clutching a bouquet of yellow carnations she’d bought at a supermarket. “I just want to have a moment of calmness and peace,” she said, “and feeling like I’m enough.”

That is what the boots have taken from people. That basic sense of being enough. Of safety. Of belonging. Of not having to worry whether tonight is the night someone kicks in the door.

The barefoot are trying to give it back.

Britani Meadows, who flew from Utah while her brother lay in intensive care, said something that captured the whole journey: “It’s very out of character. I’m very logical and this is a very illogical thing to do.” Then she added: “But these were illogical times.”

She’s right. And here is what those illogical times produced in Washington the week of February 10, 2026:

On Tuesday, February 10, the monks crossed the Chain Bridge into Washington after 108 days and 2,300 miles. Nearly 3,500 people packed American University’s Bender Arena in silence. Pannakara entered with Aloka. Over a hundred Buddhist monks and nuns from across the country joined them. They walked to the Washington National Cathedral for an interfaith ceremony with Bishop Mariann Budde. The Dalai Lama sent a commendation.

Flowers in hand, crowds cheering: Monks arrive at the Capitol with bouquets of peace. Valentine’s Day reminder—love walks quietly.

On Wednesday, February 11, the monks walked barefoot through snow-covered Capitol Hill. Pannakara held a yellow rose. Hakeem Jeffries and Nancy Pelosi greeted them at the Peace Monument. Then they walked the length of the National Mall to the Lincoln Memorial for the concluding ceremony. Bhante Dam Phommasan — the monk who lost his leg in Texas — rolled to the microphone in his wheelchair and told the crowd he was cold, “but you all make my heart warm.” More than 21,000 people watched the livestream in a dozen languages.

On Thursday, February 12, two things happened. The monks boarded a bus to go home to Texas. And Tom Homan stood at the Bishop Henry Whipple Federal Building in Minneapolis and announced that Operation Metro Surge — the largest immigration enforcement operation in American history — was ending. Seventy-four days. Two American citizens dead. Ninety-six court orders violated. Four thousand people arrested. Over.

The barefoot and the boots left Washington the same day.

But here’s what the monks didn’t know — what none of us fully understood until this week: the boots never actually stopped. They just changed shoes.

On Friday, February 13, the day after Homan’s announcement, Bring Me The News reported continued ICE activity across the Twin Cities — vehicle stops, door knocks, agents smashing car windows in Eagan. The “ending” of Metro Surge appears to mean the ending of the name, not the operations.

And today — Saturday, February 14, Valentine’s Day — DHS funding lapsed at midnight. Congress left town for recess without a deal. Democrats held the line, refusing to fund DHS without restrictions on ICE conduct. Every Senate Democrat except John Fetterman voted no. Schumer called the White House’s latest offer “not serious.”

But here’s the cruel irony that should make every American furious: the shutdown doesn’t touch ICE. Not meaningfully. The One Big Beautiful Bill that Republicans passed last summer gave ICE $75 billion and CBP another $64 billion — a $139 billion war chest that operates independent of annual appropriations. Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons told Congress this week that the shutdown won’t affect immigration operations. So 23,000 DHS employees at TSA, FEMA, and the Coast Guard go without paychecks. But the boots keep walking.

Arm raised, gear heavy: ICE lines in Minneapolis streets. While monks asked for peace, these boots promised 3,000 arrests a day.

And they’re walking everywhere. Minnesota was never the only operation. Operation Midway Blitz has been running in Chicago since September. National Guard troops have been deployed to at least ten cities. ICE is conducting active operations in Los Angeles, Portland, Charlotte, Memphis, Raleigh, New Orleans, Atlanta, and Washington itself. ICE’s personnel has doubled in the past year — from roughly 10,000 officers to over 22,000. In Los Angeles County alone, a vulnerability index identified dozens of neighborhoods likely to face intensified enforcement. Mayor Karen Bass issued an executive directive blocking ICE from using city property, requiring body cameras during federal encounters. LA isn’t waiting for Minnesota’s story to become theirs.

The monks asked Americans to live five seconds with mindfulness. ICE has been directed to arrest 3,000 people a day.

In 1966 — back when America’s divisions were about Vietnam and civil rights instead of administrative warrants and deportation quotas — Nancy Sinatra warned us that these boots are made for walking, and that’s just what they’ll do. Sixty years later, she was right. The boots changed their name in Minnesota. But they’re still walking there, and in Chicago, and in Los Angeles, and in Portland and Charlotte and Memphis and Raleigh. They walk through neighborhoods at dawn. They walk into the wrong homes. They walk over constitutional rights and human dignity and the broken bodies of people who just wanted to live here. They have $75 billion and 22,000 agents and they will not stop because one operation in one state was forced to end.

But the barefoot are walking too. After 2,300 miles, it turns out more people want to follow them. Three million followers. Thousands lining frozen roads. People flying from Utah and driving eleven hours from Maine and taking trains from the North Carolina border just to stand in silence and feel something like peace.

Pannakara’s final words from the Lincoln Memorial: “The Walk for Peace is not a protest. It is not to convert. It’s a reminder that hope still exists when people are willing to care.”

The monks are home in Texas now. The DHS shutdown began today. Congress is on recess. The boots have $139 billion and a president who never backs down.

But one of these days — and the monks proved this, and the Democrats who forced Metro Surge to end proved this, and Renée Good and Alex Pretti proved this with their lives — one of these days, these boots are going to stop walking all over us.

The barefoot never will.
