The Exception Clause

The Founders’ Asterisk

Part I: How the Puritans Built a Nation on a Promise They Immediately Broke

March 18, 2026

The people who built the first permanent English settlements in New England were, by any reasonable definition, refugees. They had faced genuine harassment under the Church of England. Their worship had been surveilled, their ministers imprisoned, their congregations broken up by authorities who understood theological nonconformity as a species of political sedition. They crossed the Atlantic in leaky ships, at enormous personal risk, to reach a place where they could practice their faith without interference.

Within a generation, they were hanging people on Boston Common for practicing theirs.

This is not a footnote to the American founding story. It is the founding story — or rather, it is the part of the founding story that the more comfortable version leaves out. The Puritans were not hypocrites in the ordinary sense of people who fail to live up to their stated ideals. They were something more precise and more instructive: people who believed in religious freedom for themselves and understood, with complete theological clarity, why it could not be extended to others. That clarity — the certainty that one’s own faith is the correct one, and that tolerating incorrect faith constitutes an offense against God — is the engine that has powered American religious persecution in every generation since. The targets change. The certainty does not.

Understanding how it began requires understanding what the Puritans actually believed — not as a historical curiosity, but as the template from which everything that followed was made.

The City on a Hill and Its Walls

A classic historical portrait of Winthrop, emphasizing the Puritan leader who articulated the foundational (yet exclusionary) vision. Alamy historical collection

John Winthrop delivered his famous sermon — the one with the city on a hill — aboard the Arbella in 1630, before the Massachusetts Bay Colony had been built. It was a founding vision: a community covenanted with God, watched by the world, obligated to demonstrate that a godly society was possible. The city on a hill was not a metaphor for tolerance. It was a metaphor for exemplary righteousness. The community Winthrop envisioned was not pluralist. It was purposeful — a specific people, in covenant with a specific God, building a specific kind of social order.

The implications were architectural. A city on a hill has walls. The walls determine who is inside and who is not. For Winthrop and the Puritan leadership of Massachusetts Bay, those walls were theological. Church membership was required for full civic participation. Theological conformity was the price of belonging. Dissent was not merely wrong — it was corrosive, a crack in the covenant that endangered everyone within the walls.

This was not cynicism. It was conviction. The Puritan leadership genuinely believed that God’s favor — the protection and prosperity of the community — depended on the community’s collective faithfulness. A single dissenter tolerated was a risk to the entire enterprise. From inside that conviction, persecution was not cruelty. It was maintenance.

The logic held until it met Roger Williams.

Roger Williams and the Radical Idea

Williams arrived in the Massachusetts Bay Colony in 1631, already known as a gifted minister with inconvenient opinions. The most inconvenient was this: that civil government had no legitimate authority over the inner life of its citizens. That religious conscience and political authority existed in separate domains, and that each was corrupted by the intrusion of the other. He believed — and argued at length, with scriptural precision — that compelling someone to worship in a particular way produced not genuine faith but performance. And a society built on performed faith was not a godly society. It was a fraudulent one.

He had a second inconvenient opinion: that the colonial charters were illegitimate, because the English Crown had no rightful claim to land that belonged to the Indigenous peoples who lived on it. He had learned the Narragansett language and engaged in extended negotiations with the Narragansett and Wampanoag peoples — not as a missionary seeking conversion but as something closer to a diplomatic interlocutor. In a colonial culture that regarded Indigenous peoples primarily as obstacles or subjects, this was also not welcomed.

The General Court of Massachusetts Bay tried Williams in 1635 on charges of spreading “diverse, new, and dangerous opinions.” He was convicted and ordered to leave the colony. He fled during the winter of 1636, through snow, into territory the colonial authorities had not settled, and founded what would become Providence, Rhode Island. He obtained the land through negotiated purchase from the Narragansett people. He called the settlement a “shelter for persons distressed of conscience.”

Roger Williams negotiating with Narragansett leaders upon his arrival in what became Rhode Island (c. 1636 engraving).

Rhode Island became the first civil government in American history to formally separate church and state, and the first to extend religious toleration not merely to Protestant dissenters but to Catholics and Jews as well. It was, by the standards of the 17th century, a genuinely radical experiment.

The men who expelled Williams into the winter were themselves refugees from religious persecution. The irony was not lost on Williams. He spent the rest of his life writing about it.

The Quakers of Boston Common

If the expulsion of Roger Williams illustrates the intellectual architecture of Puritan intolerance, the treatment of the Quakers illustrates its practical limit — the point at which argument gives way to the rope.

The Religious Society of Friends emerged in England in the 1650s as a movement of radical spiritual egalitarianism. Quakers believed in the direct experience of the divine, without intermediary — no clergy, no sacraments, no formal liturgy. They refused to bow to social superiors, refused to swear oaths, refused to remove their hats in court. They were pacifists. They believed, scandalously for the era, that women could speak and preach with equal authority to men. They were, by the standards of 17th century England, comprehensively troublesome — and they were comprehensively persecuted for it.

When Quakers began arriving in Massachusetts Bay in 1656, the Puritan authorities did not receive them as fellow victims of Anglican persecution seeking the same shelter Williams had been expelled for providing. They received them as a contagion. The colonial legislature passed laws making it a crime to bring Quakers into the colony, to harbor them, or to defend their beliefs. Quakers who arrived anyway were flogged. Those who persisted had their ears cut off. Those who persisted further had their tongues bored through with hot irons. When that failed to stop them, the colony passed a law making it a capital offense for a Quaker to return after banishment.

Four Quakers tested the law. Between 1659 and 1661, Marmaduke Stephenson, William Robinson, Mary Dyer, and William Leddra were hanged on Boston Common. They had been banished. They had returned. They believed their conscience required it. The Puritan authorities believed their covenant required the hangings.

Mary Dyer had been in Massachusetts Bay before. She had been a follower and supporter of Anne Hutchinson — had walked out of church beside her during Hutchinson’s excommunication, a gesture of solidarity that the Puritan authorities noted and did not forget. After Hutchinson’s banishment, Dyer returned to England, encountered Quakerism, and came back to New England as a Friend. She was banished twice. The third time, she was hanged.

Her statue stands today outside the Massachusetts State House on Beacon Street, a short walk from Boston Common. She is depicted in contemplative repose. The plaque identifies her as a martyr to religious freedom.

The people who hanged her had crossed an ocean to escape religious persecution. They understood themselves, with complete sincerity, as its opponents.

Anne Hutchinson and the Crime of Interpretation

Anne Hutchinson arrived in Massachusetts Bay in 1634 with her husband and children, following the minister John Cotton whose preaching in England she had admired. She was widely regarded as a skilled midwife and a gifted theological thinker. She began holding meetings in her home — first for women to discuss recent sermons, then for men as well — at which she offered her own interpretations of scripture. She argued for a theology of grace: that salvation came through direct experience of God’s spirit, not through the performance of outward works or compliance with a covenant managed by the clergy. She was not subtle about her view that several of the colony’s ministers were preaching a theology of works that was, in her judgment, wrong.

She attracted a substantial following — substantial enough to alarm Winthrop and the colonial leadership, who understood her meetings as a competing center of theological authority. In 1637 she was called before the General Court on charges of having “troubled the peace of the commonwealth and the churches.”

Anne Hutchinson during her 1637 trial before the General Court of Massachusetts Bay, where she defended her theological interpretations and claimed direct revelation—leading to her banishment for challenging clerical authority and the colony’s covenant. Source: 19th-century historical engraving

The trial, presided over by Winthrop himself, was a remarkable document. When Hutchinson asked precisely which of her actions had violated which laws, the court found the question difficult to answer directly. When she demonstrated, in open court, a command of scripture that exceeded that of several of her accusers, the theological charges shifted. When she claimed direct revelation — that God had spoken to her — Winthrop found his opening. Direct revelation, unmediated by scripture or clergy, was the Quaker argument, and the Quaker argument was now a capital matter. The charge resolved into something simpler than heresy: she was a woman who had made herself into a theological authority, and that was intolerable on its face.

She was convicted and banished. She eventually settled in what is now the Bronx, in New York, where she and most of her family were killed in a conflict with the Sichtenaneck people in 1643. Winthrop, when he heard the news, recorded in his journal that it was a judgment of God.

He had crossed an ocean to escape a government that tried people for their religious opinions.

The First Amendment and What It Did Not Say

By 1789, when James Madison drafted the First Amendment, the colonies-turned-states had accumulated a century and a half of evidence about what happens when government and religion become entangled — and much of that evidence had been generated by Puritans persecuting each other and those who disagreed with them. Madison was not a religious enthusiast. He was a constitutional architect who had watched Virginia’s Anglican establishment jail Baptist preachers for preaching without an Anglican license in the 1770s, and who had intervened personally on their behalf. He understood, from direct observation, what an established religion did to dissent.

The religion clauses of the First Amendment — “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof” — were drafted with precision that reflected that understanding. No establishment. No prohibition of free exercise. Two principles working in tandem, each protecting against a distinct abuse. The drafters were not writing aspirationally. They were writing correctively, in full awareness of the history their new nation had already accumulated.

And yet the document contained, from its inception, the asterisk the introduction to this series identified.

The First Amendment applied, at ratification, to Congress — not to the states. Connecticut maintained its Congregationalist establishment until 1818. Massachusetts maintained its until 1833. The Baptist ministers Madison had helped free in Virginia could, in principle, face a Baptist establishment in Virginia had the political composition of the state legislature been different. The document prohibited a federal religious establishment while leaving the states to continue their own for decades.

The deeper asterisk was cultural rather than legal. The republic’s public life assumed a Protestant Christianity as its ambient background — in its rhetoric, its rituals, its calendar, its assumptions about what kinds of people were naturally fitted for self-government. Catholics were suspected of owing a loyalty to Rome that overrode their civic commitments. Jews were tolerated in commercial life in port cities but excluded from the higher registers of public standing. The promise said Congress shall make no law. The asterisk said: but the culture may do considerable work without Congress.

The promise was genuine. Madison meant it. The men who voted for it meant it. The asterisk was also genuine — not a conspiracy, not a cynical maneuver, but the natural consequence of a document drafted by men who could imagine religious freedom for Protestant Christians with considerable clarity and for everyone else with considerably less.

What the Founders’ Moment Tells Us

The founding period did not merely produce a history of persecution. It produced the template — the recurring structure that American religious bigotry would follow, with variations, for the next four centuries.

It also produced something that the template has never quite extinguished: the counter-tradition. Roger Williams did not simply document the Puritan failure. He built something different — a colony, a government, a legal framework that attempted to honor the principle the Puritans proclaimed and violated. Anne Hutchinson did not submit quietly. She demanded, in open court, that her accusers identify specifically which laws she had broken — a demand for procedural accountability that echoes in every subsequent civil liberties argument made by every subsequent targeted community.

The Quakers who returned to Massachusetts Bay knowing they would be hanged were not making a theological argument. They were making a constitutional one — that no government had the legitimate authority to execute them for their conscience. They made it in the only language that the Puritan establishment would register: they came back, and kept coming back, until the embarrassment of the hangings became politically unsustainable, and the law was quietly repealed.

The pattern that this series will trace is not simply a pattern of persecution. It is a pattern of persecution meeting resistance — of the asterisk being challenged, partially erased, and then reinstated in a new form against a new community. The city on a hill has been rebuilt in every generation. Its walls have been redefined in every generation. And in every generation, someone has stood outside those walls and said: the document says otherwise.

In 1636, that person was Roger Williams, expelled into a New England winter.

In 2026, the people standing outside the walls are the forty thousand Muslims in Nashville, the congregation in Columbia whose mosque was burned and rebuilt, the director of the American Muslim Advisory Committee calculating whether responding to Andy Ogles will cost her community more than staying silent.

The walls are newer. The argument is the same.
