The Iran Crisis

The Pause

Part LXIII: Day Thirty-Nine: The Ceasefire, the Unresolved, and the French Teachers Going Home

April 8, 2026

The Blue Marble — Earth photographed from space, Arabian Peninsula visible upper right. Somewhere in that image is the Strait of Hormuz, through which one-fifth of the world’s oil once moved freely. Somewhere in that image is Tehran, where Lili and her family stayed home because there was nowhere to go. Somewhere in that image is the mountain crevice in southwest Iran where an Air Force colonel hid for thirty-eight hours. On the same week this image was taken, four Artemis II astronauts flew 252,756 miles from this planet and photographed it from the far side of the moon. April 8, 2026

At 6:35 PM Eastern on Tuesday, April 7, ninety minutes before his own deadline, Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that he had agreed to a two-week ceasefire with Iran.

The power plants are still standing. The bridges are still there. The Strait of Hormuz will open — with conditions, under Iranian military coordination, at $2 million per ship. The civilization did not die on Tuesday night.

Cécile Kohler, a 41-year-old French high school literature teacher, and her partner Jacques Paris, a retired mathematics teacher in his 70s, were released from Evin Prison after nearly four years of detention and are on a plane to Paris. When the news was announced in France’s National Assembly, lawmakers rose to their feet to applaud. They are expected to land Wednesday morning.

Ali Ghamsari, the Iranian musician who had been sitting on a carpet in front of the Damavand power plant with his instrument since Monday evening, has gone home.

This is Day Thirty-Nine of the Iran War. The shooting has stopped, for now. The war has not ended. And the distance between those two sentences is the entire story of what happened on Tuesday.

I. How It Happened

The ceasefire arrived the way all of Trump’s exits arrive — through chaos, contradiction, and a face-saving formula engineered by someone else.

Pakistan’s Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif made a public appeal on Tuesday afternoon, asking Trump to extend the deadline by two weeks and asking Iran to open the strait as a goodwill gesture. Iran had, by that point, stopped engaging even in indirect talks through mediators, according to three Iranian officials — Trump’s “whole civilization will die tonight” post had, characteristically, backfired as a negotiating tactic.

Then China intervened. Beijing asked Iran to show flexibility and defuse tensions. Three Iranian officials confirmed this. The ceasefire was approved by the new supreme leader, Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei, himself.

Trump posted his agreement at 6:35 PM, ninety minutes before his own deadline. He said he was “holding off the destructive force being sent to Iran tonight” at the request of Pakistan’s prime minister. He said Iran’s 10-point proposal was “a workable basis on which to negotiate.” Three weeks ago he had called for Iran’s unconditional surrender.

Iran’s National Security Council immediately declared complete victory, saying the United States had accepted all ten Iranian points, including guarantees not to attack Iran, Iran’s control over the Strait of Hormuz, and reparations for war damages. Trump’s statement said nothing of the kind. Both sides are telling their domestic audiences what they need to hear. Neither account is accurate about the other side’s concessions.

David Sanger at the Times captured the essential reality in a single sentence: Trump backed down before he even heard from Tehran.

II. How It Began — The Origin Record

On Tuesday evening, Maggie Haberman and Jonathan Swan published previously undisclosed details from a forthcoming book, “Regime Change: Inside the Imperial Presidency of Donald Trump.” It is the inside account of how this war started, and it changes the origin story.

On February 11 — seventeen days before the war began — Benjamin Netanyahu sat across from Trump in the Situation Room and made an hour-long presentation. He argued that Iran was ripe for regime change and that a joint US-Israeli campaign could bring down the Islamic Republic. He played a video montage of figures who could lead Iran if the theocratic government fell. Among them was Reza Pahlavi. He promised near-certain victory: Iran’s missile program destroyed in weeks, Hormuz kept open, minimal retaliation. Mossad would foment an uprising inside Iran to finish the job.

Trump’s response: “Sounds good to me.”

US intelligence analysts worked through the night. Their assessment the next day was blunt. The first two objectives — killing the ayatollah and crippling Iran’s military capability — were achievable. The regime-change scenarios — popular uprising, secular replacement government — were not. CIA director Ratcliffe’s word: “farcical.” Rubio’s translation: “In other words, it’s bullshit.”

Trump absorbed the assessment and moved past it. Regime change, he said, would be “their problem.”

JD Vance was the only member of the inner circle who tried to stop it. He warned of regional chaos, munitions depletion, Hormuz closure, soaring gasoline prices, coalition fracture, and betrayal of voters who supported no new wars. His preference was no strikes at all. At the final meeting on February 26 — two days before the war — his message to Trump was: “You know I think this is a bad idea, but if you want to do it, I’ll support you.”

Everyone else deferred. Hegseth: “We’re going to have to take care of the Iranians eventually, so we might as well do it now.” Rubio preferred maximum pressure but did not argue against war. Susie Wiles worried about midterm elections but did not see it as her role to weigh in on military decisions in a large group setting. Caine flagged risks — weapons depletion, Hormuz, unpredictable Iranian response — but was so careful not to take a stand that he appeared to argue all sides simultaneously.

Trump believed it would be like Venezuela. When told Iran could shut Hormuz: dismissed it, assumed the regime would capitulate before it came to that. Tucker Carlson privately asked how he could be so sure. Trump: “Because it always is.”

The second-term echo chamber made this possible. Unlike his first-term team — Mattis, Kelly, Esper — who regarded him as a danger to be managed, the second-term circle had developed, after the 2024 comeback, the indictments, the assassination attempts, and the Maduro raid, an almost superstitious faith in his destiny and instincts. Almost nothing stood between the instinct and the act.

The ceasefire on Day Thirty-Nine is the bookend to “Sounds good to me” on February 11. Everything in between — the 1,701 dead, the 244 children, Sharif University, Mahshahr, Bushehr 75 meters from the reactor, the missing colonel, the CIA deception campaign, the C-130s blown up in the desert, the Easter Sunday post, “a whole civilization will die tonight” — flows from that sentence.

III. What the Ceasefire Is

A 14-day pause. Not a peace agreement. Not a resolution of any of the fundamental issues that produced the war on February 28.

The uranium remains in the ground — 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade material at a site in Isfahan, unaccounted for, uninspected, and under no agreed framework. Trump said last week he didn’t care about it. He said this week he cannot allow Iran to have a nuclear weapon. Both statements are on the record.

The missiles are being rebuilt. US intelligence documented Iran digging out missile bunkers within hours of strikes throughout the war. The missile arsenal that began this conflict has been degraded. It has not been eliminated. Iran’s parliament is advancing legislation to formalize legal control over the Strait of Hormuz. The IRGC is gaining institutional authority in the new government. Mojtaba Khamenei’s leadership is more hawkish than his father’s, not less.

The strait will open — under Iranian military coordination. Iran’s foreign minister said vessels may transit “via coordinating with Iran’s Armed Forces” for a period of two weeks. Iran retains fundamental control of the passageway. The toll system Iran has been developing — $2 million per ship, split with Oman, proceeds to reconstruction — has not been withdrawn. Iran’s parliament is legislating it into permanent existence.

The 40-plus nation coalition meeting in London on Tuesday — from which the United States was excluded — was discussing how to secure the strait after the war. That meeting produced no binding agreement. The security of the world’s most important energy chokepoint remains dependent on Iranian goodwill for the next fourteen days.

The ceasefire also extends to Lebanon. Israel has agreed to suspend strikes on Hezbollah for two weeks. Hezbollah has not confirmed. Iran fired missiles at Israel within an hour of the ceasefire announcement. Air raid sirens sounded across central Israel. The ceasefire is fourteen days old and it is already under strain.

III. What Was Not Resolved

The Trump administration entered this war with stated objectives. Six weeks later, the ceasefire leaves each of them in the following condition:

The nuclear program. Iran’s enrichment capability was struck in Operation Midnight Hammer in June 2025. Gabbard testified to Congress that the entrances to underground facilities were “buried and shuttered with cement.” Iran has since stated its intention to rebuild. The new leadership has drawn the obvious lesson from having been bombed while in negotiations: the only reliable deterrent is a nuclear weapon. The 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade uranium is still there.

The missile arsenal. Degraded. Not destroyed. Being rebuilt within hours of each strike, according to US intelligence reports throughout the war.

The Strait of Hormuz. Open, conditionally, under Iranian military coordination, for fourteen days. Iran retains control. Iran is legislating a permanent toll system. Robert Pape’s verdict from Monday stands: Iran is emerging from this war as a fourth center of global power, not despite the war but because of it.

Regime change. Did not happen. Mojtaba Khamenei is the supreme leader. The IRGC has consolidated power. The leadership that replaced the old guard is more hawkish, more revolutionary, more committed to resistance as an ontological identity. Karim Sadjadpour’s verdict from Tuesday morning stands: you cannot negotiate away the thing that justifies your existence.

Unconditional surrender. Three weeks ago Trump called for it. Yesterday he called the Iranian 10-point proposal — which demands sanctions relief, reparations, congressional approval of any agreement, and UN guarantees — “a workable basis on which to negotiate.”

IV. The Voices That Matter

Lili, the Tehran arts worker who asked not to use her full name: “I think Trump is under a lot of pressure and that he has lost his mind.” She and her family made no contingency plans. They were not going to flee. There was nowhere to go. She is still in Tehran tonight. The power is still on.

Mohsen Borhani, law professor at Tehran University, who read Trump’s threats and thought of Thomas Jefferson: “I wondered how it is possible for a US president to undermine and discredit all American values and 250 years of human legacy.” He is still in Tehran tonight. The bridges are still there.

Pedram Soltani, Iranian businessman, who appealed directly to Americans: “Your president has now placed not only Iran, but also America and the entire world at a tremendous risk.” He is still in Tehran tonight. The desalination plants are still running.

Cécile Kohler and Jacques Paris are on a plane to Paris.

The Iranian musician Ali Ghamsari sat at the Damavand power plant with his carpet and his instrument and his blanket through Monday night and Tuesday. “If there is no power, there will be no water,” he said. The power is still on at the Damavand plant tonight. He has gone home.

Marjorie Taylor Greene: “25TH AMENDMENT!!! We cannot kill an entire civilization. This is evil and madness.”

Tucker Carlson: “It is vile on every level. It begins with a promise to use the U.S. military to destroy civilian infrastructure in another country, which is to say to commit a war crime.”

Tom Nichols, The Atlantic, writing Tuesday afternoon before the ceasefire: “If Trump gives that order, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff should lay his stars down in front of Trump. Then, each general who gets the order should do likewise.”

General Dan Caine did not lay his stars down. The order did not come. The ceasefire arrived first.

Joe Kent, who resigned as Trump’s NCC nominee in March: “Trump believes he is threatening Iran with destruction, but it is America that now stands in danger. If he attempts to eradicate Iranian civilization, the United States will no longer be viewed as a stabilizing force in the world, but as an agent of chaos — effectively ending our status as the world’s greatest superpower.”

Senator Ron Johnson of Wisconsin, a Trump ally: “I hope and pray that President Trump is just using this as bluster.”

It was bluster. It was also policy. The distinction between those two things has collapsed so completely in this administration that no one — not the markets, not the allies, not the generals, not Iran — can reliably tell them apart anymore. That is itself a strategic liability that will outlast the ceasefire by years.

V. The Artemis Frame

Earth photographed from the Artemis II spacecraft, April 2026, as the crew made its historic flyby 252,756 miles from home — the farthest any human has traveled from the planet. On the same day their president posted that “a whole civilization will die tonight,” four astronauts were photographing our fragile blue planet from the far side of the moon. The civilization did not die. The Artemis crew is coming home. NASA

Charlie Warzel, writing in The Atlantic on Tuesday evening as the deadline approached, found the frame that this week required. Four astronauts — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, Jeremy Hansen — are returning from the farthest distance humans have ever traveled from Earth, 252,756 miles, a record that surpassed Apollo 13. They took approximately 10,000 photographs of our planet and our moon during their mission.

Koch, looking back at Earth from that distance: “You don’t see borders, you don’t see religious lines, you don’t see political boundaries. All you see is Earth, and you see that we are way more alike than we are different.”

Glover, looking at the planet: “Trust us, you look amazing, you look beautiful. From up here, you also look like one thing. Homo sapiens is all of us. No matter where you’re from or what you look like, we’re all one people.”

Trump mentioned Artemis II in the first thirty seconds of his prime-time address on April 1 and moved on. The four astronauts were 252,756 miles away taking photographs of the fragile blue planet on the same day its president posted “a whole civilization will die tonight.”

The civilization did not die. The Artemis crew is coming home. The war is paused. These facts coexist on the same Tuesday, on the same planet, under the same thin green atmosphere that the astronauts photographed from the far side of the moon.

VI. The Fourteen Days

The ceasefire is fourteen days. The clock started Tuesday evening.

What the next fourteen days must resolve, or fail to resolve:

The strait opening in practice — not just in announcement. Tankers carrying oil and LNG and fertilizer and helium for semiconductor manufacturing are backed up across the Gulf. Insurance markets will not normalize until ships actually transit safely and repeatedly. The first real test of the ceasefire is not a statement. It is a tanker.

The nuclear question. No framework exists. No inspection regime has been agreed. The 970 pounds remain. Trump said he doesn’t care about it. Trump said he cannot allow Iran to have it. Both statements cannot be true simultaneously.

The reparations and sanctions question. Iran’s 10-point proposal demands a complete lifting of all sanctions imposed since the George W. Bush administration and compensation for war damages approved by the US Congress and the UN. The US Congress will not vote for reparations to Iran. These demands have not been withdrawn.

The Hezbollah question. The Lebanon ceasefire is the most fragile element of the agreement. Iran fired missiles at Israel after the announcement. Israel has not confirmed it will hold. The 14-day window in Lebanon is narrower than the 14-day window on the main front.

And the question that Sadjadpour answered before the ceasefire was announced: what happens when a regime whose ideology is resistance, whose survival depends on isolation and conflict, is asked to normalize? The 14-day ceasefire buys time. It does not answer the question.

VII. What Remains

The ceasefire is real. The relief is real. The French teachers going home is real. The power plants standing is real.

The war is not over.

1,701 Iranian civilians are confirmed dead, including 244 children — HRANA count as of Tuesday. At least 1,500 Lebanese killed. At least 20 Israelis killed. 13 American service members killed.

Sharif University of Technology — Iran’s MIT, the alma mater of Maryam Mirzakhani, the only woman ever to win the Fields Medal — is rubble.

The Mahshahr petrochemical complex, which employed 300,000 people in Khuzestan Province, needs two years to rebuild.

Bushehr nuclear power plant was struck 75 meters from the reactor. The IAEA warned it “could cause a severe radiological accident with harmful consequences for people and the environment in Iran and beyond.”

The interceptor stockpiles of the UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Israel have been depleted to historically dangerous levels. The global production capacity to replace them is measured in years, not weeks.

Iran’s parliament is legislating permanent toll authority over the Strait of Hormuz. Robert Pape’s fourth-power argument has not been answered by a 14-day pause.

Narges Mohammadi — Nobel Peace Prize laureate, heart attack on March 24, denied hospital access for six weeks — is still in her prison cell.

Senator Kennedy of Louisiana remains the only Republican to have voted against the war.

VIII. Wednesday Morning — The Fragile First Hours

The ceasefire is less than twelve hours old and it is already under strain.

Iran fired missiles at Kuwait, the UAE, Qatar, and Bahrain in the hours after the announcement. Kuwait reported “an intense wave of hostile Iranian attacks” — 28 drones — intercepted after 8 AM local time, well after the ceasefire was supposed to have taken effect. Bahrain sounded air raid sirens. A fire broke out at a gas facility in Abu Dhabi. An oil refinery on Iran’s Lavan Island was struck, according to Iranian state media, attributed to “enemies.” It is unclear whether word of the ceasefire reached Iranian local commanders, or whether it did and was ignored.

Israel said immediately that the ceasefire does not cover Lebanon. Netanyahu contradicted Pakistan’s prime minister, who had said it extended everywhere. Israeli strikes in Lebanon continued through Wednesday morning. More than a million Lebanese remain displaced. Hezbollah told civilians not to return home. Lebanon’s military warned them the same. The Lebanon war, which began as a consequence of the Iran war on Day One, has no ceasefire.

The first ships transited Hormuz on Wednesday — two bulk carriers, one Greek-owned, one Liberia-flagged, tracked by Kpler. More than 400 tankers remain effectively stranded in the Persian Gulf. Maersk welcomed the announcement but said it was not yet making operational changes: “The cease-fire may create transit opportunities, but it does not yet provide full maritime certainty.”

The energy recovery will take months, not weeks. Ras Laffan, Qatar’s LNG export complex — 17 percent of capacity destroyed — needs years to repair the damaged units. Individual cooling structures as tall as 18-story buildings, with two-year lead times for replacement. Wells that have been shut for weeks face pressure problems, water buildup, equipment corrosion. Kuwait Petroleum’s chief executive estimated full production recovery at three to four months minimum. S&P Global Energy: “Normalization will take months, not weeks.” Société Générale now forecasts oil at $80 a barrel at end of 2026 — up from their pre-war forecast of $65. The geopolitical risk premium is now permanent.

Oil fell 13-15 percent on the ceasefire news. Brent at $95, WTI at $96. The Nikkei rose 5.4 percent. South Korea’s Kospi up 7 percent. The markets are pricing in relief. The markets are also pricing in persistent uncertainty — oil is still 30 percent above pre-war levels with a ceasefire in place.

The Danish foreign minister, Lars Lokke Rasmussen: “Is the world a better place today than yesterday? Undoubtedly. Than 40 days ago? More than doubtful.”

Spain’s prime minister Pedro Sánchez: “The momentary relief cannot make us forget the chaos, the destruction, and the lives lost. The government of Spain will not applaud those who set the world on fire just because they show up with a bucket.”

The Gulf Research Center’s Abdulaziz Sager, speaking from Saudi Arabia: “We suffer in the Gulf because he started the war. We told him the consequences. We were never consulted.”

Iran’s first vice president Mohammad Reza Aref: “The era of Iran has begun.”

Vance, in Budapest, called it “a fragile truce” and said some Iranian officials were “basically lying about the nature of the agreement.” Trump told him overnight: “The Iranians are better negotiators than they are fighters.”

Talks in Islamabad are proposed for Friday. Iran’s delegation will attend. The United States said “nothing is final” until announced by the president.

Pope Leo XIV, hours before the ceasefire, made his strongest rebuke of Trump yet — without naming him: “Today there has been this threat against the entire people of Iran. And this is truly unacceptable. There are certainly issues of international law here, but even more, it is a moral question concerning the good of the people as a whole, in its entirety.” He called for dialogue. “Come back to the table. Let’s talk. Let’s look for solutions in a peaceful way.” Hours later, Trump announced the ceasefire.

The American-born pope had more to say about the sanctity of Iranian civilization than the American president did at 8:06 AM on Tuesday.

This series began on January 12, 2026 — six weeks before the war started. It has documented every day of Operation Epic Fury in real time, twice weekly now, from a vantage point in Toronto that the war’s architects in Washington and Tel Aviv never consulted and cannot reach.

On Tuesday morning the president of the United States posted that a whole civilization would die tonight. By Tuesday evening he had taken the exit ramp, ninety minutes before his own deadline, brokered by Pakistan, enabled by China, accepted by a supreme leader who immediately declared victory.

The top ten reasons to TACO were published on Tuesday morning. Perhaps he read them. More likely he found, as this series has argued since Part I, that the TACO framework describes not a choice but a pattern — and patterns, eventually, complete themselves.

The pause is real. Fourteen days is fourteen days.

The record remains open.
