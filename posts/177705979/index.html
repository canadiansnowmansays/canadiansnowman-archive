The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

Finding Billions Everywhere Except for Food

Crystal Chandeliers, Military Pay, and Argentina—But No Money for SNAP?

November 1, 2025

The 2025 government shutdown has exposed a truth about the Trump administration that no amount of spin can obscure: when they want to move money, they can move money. When they want to exercise legal authority, they have no shortage of it. The question isn’t whether they have the power or the funds—it’s who they choose to help and who they choose to hurt.

“I Found Billions for the Military, But My Hands Are Tied on Food”

The cornerstone of the administration’s hypocrisy lies in a simple comparison: military pay versus food assistance

.The Trump administration demonstrated remarkable creativity in finding money for military payroll during the shutdown. They raided three different financial accounts to ensure service members received their October 31st paychecks, pulling $2.5 billion from military housing funds and $1.4 billion from research and development accounts. Earlier in the shutdown, the Pentagon identified approximately $8 billion in unobligated research funds to cover military pay. The administration even accepted a mysterious $130 million anonymous donation from a Trump “friend”—raising serious questions about foreign influence and the legality of such arrangements.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent openly stated that as long as the administration could keep finding money for the military, the shutdown would continue. The message was clear: we have the will and the way to move billions when we want to.

Yet when it came to the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)—which feeds 42 million Americans—the administration suddenly claimed legal helplessness. Speaker Mike Johnson insisted there was “no such legal avenue” for the president to tap into contingency funds for food assistance. Agriculture Secretary Brooke Rollins echoed this claim, arguing that by law, the contingency fund could only flow when the underlying fund was flowing.

Federal judges weren’t buying it. Two separate judges—in Rhode Island and Massachusetts—ruled that the administration was breaking the law by refusing to use the $5-6 billion in SNAP contingency funds that, by law, can only be used for SNAP. Judge John McConnell stated unequivocally: “There is no doubt that the six billion dollars in contingency funds are appropriated funds that are without a doubt necessary to carry out the program’s operation.”

The judges’ rulings expose the lie. The administration could fund SNAP. They chose not to.

The Smoking Gun: They Deleted Their Own Legal Guidance

The most damning evidence of the administration’s deception came from their own lawyers. On September 30, 2025, the White House Office of Management and Budget issued legal guidance explicitly stating that SNAP contingency funds could be used for benefits during a shutdown. This was their own determination, made by their own legal team

Then, on October 23—just days before SNAP benefits were set to lapse—that statement was quietly deleted from the USDA website.

They didn’t just change their position. They tried to erase the evidence that they ever held it. When confronted with this contradiction, the administration offered no explanation. They simply pretended their own lawyers had never said what everyone could see they had said.

This wasn’t a legal dispute. It was a deliberate manipulation of the law to inflict maximum pain.

Policy experts noted that no previous administration had ever withheld SNAP benefits during a government shutdown, despite similar legal frameworks. The contingency fund exists precisely for situations like this—it can only be used for SNAP and cannot be redirected anywhere else. Yet the administration claimed it was powerless to use money that was literally earmarked for the exact purpose they claimed they couldn’t fund.

Bobby Kogan, Senior Director of Federal Budget Policy at the Center for American Progress, called the administration’s legal arguments “completely false,” noting: “The SNAP contingency fund can only be used on SNAP. It cannot be used anywhere else. That’s what it’s for. Saying otherwise is just a false statement from the Speaker of the House.”

Kogan also revealed that the administration’s own internal guidance from less than a month earlier had explicitly stated that contingency funds could be used—a statement that was deliberately deleted from the USDA website. “They’re contradicting their own internal guidance from less than a month ago,” he said. “If SNAP benefits don’t go out November 1, it’s only because the Trump administration is illegally refusing to administer them.”

The $300 Million Ballroom That Couldn’t Wait

While millions of Americans faced the prospect of losing food assistance, while federal workers missed paychecks, while essential services ground to a halt, one thing continued without interruption: demolition of the White House East Wing to make way for Trump’s $300 million ballroom.

The ballroom project—initially estimated at $200 million but now projected to cost $300-350 million—proceeded despite the government shutdown. Construction crews tore down the historic East Wing, traditionally the base of operations for the First Lady, to build what Trump described as “the greatest ballroom anywhere in the world.”

The optics were staggering. Bulldozers smashing into the White House while food banks braced for the wave of newly desperate families. Trump holding lavish donor dinners to show off ballroom renderings while SNAP recipients wondered where their next meal would come from. The president boasting about gold-plated Corinthian columns and crystal chandeliers while 42 million Americans faced hunger.

It’s almost poetic that “Hypocrisy” echoes “Hippocrates,” the father of medicine who swore doctors to “first, do no harm.” If only this administration had taken the oath—then maybe they’d prescribe a meal instead of a shutdown.

The administration claimed the ballroom was privately funded and therefore unaffected by the shutdown. But this argument only highlighted their twisted priorities. They could find creative ways to continue construction on a vanity project. They could solicit and accept hundreds of millions in private donations for gilded entertaining spaces. Yet they couldn’t—or wouldn’t—use congressionally appropriated funds, sitting in an account designated specifically for food assistance, to feed hungry Americans.

Public opinion reflected the grotesque nature of these priorities. Polling showed 61% of Americans disapproved of Trump’s plans for the ballroom, with even 25% of Republicans expressing disapproval. The passion was overwhelmingly on the side of opposition, with 46% strongly opposed compared to just 18% strongly in favor.

Democratic Representative Suzan DelBene captured the sentiment perfectly: “Trump is more focused on tearing apart the White House to install a Mar-a-Lago style ballroom than reopening the government or making sure you can afford health care.”

The White House proceeded with this massive construction project despite not having approval from the National Capital Planning Commission, which oversees construction work on government buildings in Washington. The commission’s offices were closed because of the government shutdown—the same shutdown Trump was using to pressure Democrats while building his personal palace.

Argentina Gets $20 Billion, Anonymous Donors Get Access

The selective generosity of the Trump administration revealed itself in other ways as well. While claiming poverty when it came to SNAP, the administration somehow found $20 billion for a bailout to Argentina. While insisting there was no money to feed American families, Trump’s administration sent billions in foreign aid to other countries.

Democrats repeatedly highlighted this disparity, asking how the administration could afford international largesse while claiming inability to feed its own citizens. The answer, of course, was that it was never about ability—it was about willingness. About leverage. About using hunger as a negotiating tool.

The $130 million anonymous donation to pay troops raised even more troubling questions. Congressional Democrats demanded to know the donor’s identity and whether the gift violated the Antideficiency Act, which prohibits federal agencies from using funds that exceed appropriated amounts. Senator Chris Coons, the top Democrat on the Senate’s defense appropriations subcommittee, warned: “Using anonymous donations to fund our military raises troubling questions of whether our own troops are at risk of literally being bought and paid for by foreign powers.”

Budget experts also questioned the legality of using such donations to offset appropriations lapses. Bill Hoagland, a former Senate GOP budget aide, stated bluntly: “The Antideficiency Act is explicit that private donations cannot be used to offset a lapse in appropriations.”

Yet the administration accepted the money anyway, proving once again that when they wanted to find a way around legal obstacles, they could find it. They just reserved that creativity for priorities other than feeding hungry Americans.

Taxpayer-Funded Mockery While Americans Suffer

Even as the administration claimed poverty when it came to feeding hungry families, they found plenty of resources for partisan propaganda and mockery. The official White House social media account posted: “Walked into a haunted house, but instead of clowns, was surrounded by Democrats keeping the government shut down over free healthcare for illegal aliens.”

This wasn’t from Trump’s personal account—this was the official @WhiteHouse account, funded by taxpayers, spreading false talking points during a national crisis. The “free healthcare for illegal aliens” claim was a lie; Democrats were fighting to extend ACA subsidies for American citizens that would prevent insurance premiums from doubling for millions of families.

“Stop Calling Donald Trump a Clown”, Beg Actual Clowns July 2025

The projection was breathtaking. Trump—who has been depicted as a clown in political cartoons, street art, and social commentary for years—was calling his opponents clowns. The comparison has become so ubiquitous that in July 2025, Vice Magazine published an op-ed by Tim Cunningham, board president of Clowns Without Borders, pleading with the public to stop calling Trump a clown. Actual professional clowns argued the comparison was unfair to their profession, noting that “clowns have been uniting people in laughter, levity, and creativity” while “a clown would not order airstrikes that traumatized children, but rather would try to make those traumatized children feel just a little bit happier.” Cunningham suggested calling Trump a “buffoon” instead—still circus-adjacent, but without dragging an entire performance tradition through the mud.

Yet here was the official White House account using “clowns” as an insult while Trump built a $300 million gold-plated ballroom with crystal chandeliers and Corinthian columns, gilded the Oval Office in gold, and accepted anonymous donations of questionable legality.

Perhaps Trump should have looked in the mirror before authorizing that “haunted house” post. Professional clowns who risk their lives bringing joy to children in war zones were literally begging people not to associate them with his behavior. If anyone needed to worry about clowns, it was anyone walking into the Trump White House during the shutdown—where the real circus was happening.

If anyone was running a circus, it was the administration that found billions for military pay and luxury construction but claimed poverty when it came to feeding hungry Americans.

This was taxpayer-funded trolling while 42 million Americans faced hunger. Federal resources being used not to solve the crisis, but to score cheap political points with false information. If they had time and money for social media mockery, they had time and money to feed hungry Americans.

The Pattern: Creative Accounting When It Suits Them

The military pay situation revealed the administration’s extraordinary skill at creative accounting when motivated. Beyond the research and development funds and housing allowances, they:

  • Shifted $8 billion from military research and development to cover earlier payroll

  • Raided multiple accounts across different programs

  • Accepted anonymous private donations

  • Used a mix of legislative and Pentagon funds to piece together paychecks

  • Found workarounds for legal obstacles that supposedly prevented such moves

These actions proved beyond any doubt that when the administration wanted to find money, it could find money. When it wanted to move funds, it could move funds. When it wanted to exercise executive authority and legal creativity, it had no shortage of either.

The conclusion was inescapable: the administration chose not to use those same skills to feed hungry Americans. They chose to let SNAP lapse. They chose to ignore their own legal guidance. They chose to defy court orders—initially, at least—until judges forced their hand.

Senator Lindsey Graham let the quiet part slip when he said he sensed that “all the bad things that are going to happen over the weekend”—including the SNAP shortfall—would prompt movement toward a deal in the Senate. The suffering wasn’t an unfortunate side effect. It was the strategy.

“I Will Be Your Hero” After Engineering Your Crisis

The ultimate insult came when Trump, after judges ordered the administration to fund SNAP, posted a lengthy statement on Truth Social that perfectly captured his strategy of manufacturing crisis and then claiming credit for reluctantly solving it:

“Our Government lawyers do not think we have the legal authority to pay SNAP with certain monies we have available, and now two Courts have issued conflicting opinions on what we can and cannot do. I do NOT want Americans to go hungry just because the Radical Democrats refuse to do the right thing and REOPEN THE GOVERNMENT. Therefore, I have instructed our lawyers to ask the Court to clarify how we can legally fund SNAP as soon as possible.”

He continued: “It is already delayed enough due to the Democrats keeping the Government closed through the monthly payment date and, even if we get immediate guidance, it will unfortunately be delayed while States get the money out. If we are given the appropriate legal direction by the Court, it will BE MY HONOR to provide the funding, just like I did with Military and Law Enforcement Pay.”

Read that carefully. Trump positioned himself as the hero who would graciously feed Americans—after his administration had deliberately withheld food assistance that federal judges ruled he was legally required to provide. He claimed credit for being willing to follow a court order that he should never have forced judges to issue in the first place. He blamed Democrats for delays that his own obstruction had caused.

It was like an arsonist showing up at the fire with a garden hose and demanding praise for fighting the blaze.

Even after federal judges ordered SNAP funding, states like Pennsylvania had to sue separately to force compliance. Governor Shapiro’s lawsuit succeeded, forcing Trump to release funding he’d been legally required to provide all along.

And even the “victory” was incomplete. The emergency funding only covered 20 days of benefits, leaving millions still uncertain about their next meal. Trump’s “honor” to provide funding came with an expiration date—another crisis waiting to happen.

Judge McConnell understood the moral obscenity of the administration’s position when he ruled: “It’s clear that when compared to the millions of people that will go without funds for food versus the agency’s desire not to use contingency funds in case there’s a hurricane need, the balance of those equities clearly goes on the side of ensuring that people are fed.”

A federal judge had to explain to the President of the United States that feeding hungry people takes precedence over hypothetical future needs. That basic moral calculation apparently wasn’t obvious to the Trump administration.

“We Have Failed You”—Wait, She Means Democrats

The administration’s messaging became so tangled in blame-shifting that even Trump’s own cabinet members couldn’t keep the story straight. Agriculture Secretary Brooke Rollins told Americans: “My message to America is your government is failing you right now — we have failed you.”

It was a rare moment of apparent honesty from a Trump official. The government was indeed failing Americans. They had failed to use available funds to feed hungry people. They had failed to follow their own legal guidance. They had failed to prioritize human needs over political leverage.

But Speaker Mike Johnson immediately rushed to “clarify” what Rollins meant: “When she says ‘we have failed you,’ she means ‘we, the Democrats,’ okay?”

The desperation was palpable. Even when a Trump administration official accidentally told the truth—that the government had failed—Republicans had to immediately rewrite it to blame Democrats. They couldn’t even acknowledge failure without turning it into a partisan attack.

The American people could see through it. If Democrats had somehow “failed” Americans by not giving in to Republican demands, what did it say about Republicans that they chose to hurt millions of people rather than negotiate?

What the Money Trail Reveals

Follow the money, and you see the truth of this administration’s priorities:

  • Billions found for military pay through creative accounting and multiple fund raids

  • $130 million accepted from anonymous donors with potential foreign ties

  • $300-350 million raised for a luxury ballroom with gold columns and crystal chandeliers

  • $20 billion sent to Argentina for a bailout

  • $5-6 billion sitting unused in SNAP contingency funds while 42 million Americans face hunger

The administration proved it could move heaven and earth when it chose to. It proved it could find legal authority when it wanted to. It proved it could exercise executive power and creative problem-solving when it served its purposes.

It just chose not to do any of that for hungry Americans. Because their suffering was more valuable as leverage than their well-being was as a priority.

Trump even tried to justify this cruelty by claiming on Fox News: “When you talk about SNAP, you talk about largely Democrats. But I’m president, I want to help everybody. It’s largely Democrats, they’re hurting their own people.”

Setting aside the fact that SNAP benefits aren’t distributed by political affiliation, and that many red states like Oklahoma, Louisiana, West Virginia, and Alabama have high SNAP participation rates—Trump’s statement revealed his true calculus. He saw hungry Americans not as people in need, but as political pawns. Their hunger was Democrats’ problem. Their children’s empty plates were collateral damage in a partisan battle.

The images of hungry children with a simple message—”We are hungry but Mr. TRUMP won’t feed us”—told the story more powerfully than any policy analysis could. While Trump gilded his palace and moved billions for priorities he valued, American families faced empty cupboards because he chose not to use money that was already appropriated for exactly that purpose.

That’s not a budget constraint. That’s a moral choice.

Addendum

November 4, 2025 Update - Trump Admits the Quiet Part Out Loud

After this essay published, Trump just posted on Truth Social:

“SNAP BENEFITS... will be given only when the Radical Left Democrats open up government, which they can easily do, and not before!”

There it is. In writing. The President of the United States explicitly stating he will withhold food assistance until Democrats capitulate to his demands. Not because he lacks the legal authority - federal judges already ruled he has it. Not because the money isn’t there - $6 billion sits in the contingency fund.

He’s withholding food as a weapon.

The same man who found billions for military pay, who accepted anonymous millions from mysterious donors, who kept his $300 million ballroom construction going - that man just admitted he could feed Americans but chooses not to.

The moral obscenity of these priorities could not be clearer.

The facts in this essay are drawn from federal court rulings, congressional testimony, administration statements, and contemporary news reporting from October 2025. A companion essay examines the lies and blame-shifting that accompanied these funding decisions.
