When Democracy Fights Back

700 Boxes

Part 10: The Fulton County Raid and the Cast of Characters Behind Trump’s Election Vendetta

February 10, 2026

The Fulton County Election Hub and Operations Center in Union City, Georgia, the site of the FBI raid where agents seized ballots, machines, and records.

In Part 9, we followed the quiet campaign: the DOJ demanding personal voter data from 47 states, suing 24 of them, and building a national voter database without congressional authorization. That was the infrastructure — the blueprints and the reconnaissance.

On January 28, 2026, the vault door opened.

The Scene

Just after dawn, a convoy of FBI vehicles arrived at the Fulton County Election Hub and Operation Center in Union City, Georgia, a few miles south of Atlanta. What followed took hours. Agents moved systematically through the facility, loading sealed boxes onto waiting semitrucks. By the time they finished, they had taken everything.

Every paper ballot from the 2020 presidential election. Every voting machine tabulator tape. Every scanner image produced during the ballot count. The voter rolls. Seven hundred boxes in all.

Fulton County — home to Atlanta, the beating heart of Georgia’s Democratic vote — had just watched the federal government physically remove the evidence of an election that had been counted three times, three different ways, twice by machine and once entirely by hand, with observers from both parties watching every step. An election confirmed by a Republican Secretary of State, Brad Raffensperger, who rejected Trump’s infamous demand to “find 11,780 votes.” An election whose results had been upheld by every court that examined them. An election so thoroughly litigated that in 2023, Rudy Giuliani was ordered to pay $148 million for defaming two Fulton County election workers by falsely accusing them of fraud.

None of that mattered. The president wanted those ballots. And so the FBI took them.

The Defective Warrant

The legal basis for this seizure raises its own questions. The search warrant cited alleged violations of two federal laws — one regarding the retention of election records, the other criminalizing efforts to defraud voters. The prosecutor listed on the warrant was not Theodore Hertzberg, the U.S. Attorney for the Northern District of Georgia, who would normally oversee any federal investigation in Fulton County. It was Thomas Albus, the U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Missouri.

We’ll get to why Missouri in a moment.

Three officials familiar with the warrant told The Atlantic that the initial version presented to Fulton County officials that morning was defective — it didn’t contain the necessary information to lawfully begin the search. A corrected warrant had to be issued by the magistrate judge before agents could proceed. Current and former officials said the need for correction reflected the haste with which the operation was assembled and the institutional knowledge deficit at a Justice Department that has hemorrhaged thousands of employees since Trump’s second term began.

They were in such a rush to execute the president’s vendetta that they couldn’t even get the paperwork right.

Three weeks before the raid, on January 6 — a date that writes its own jokes — FBI agents had hand-delivered a letter to the Fulton County Board of Elections demanding the same materials. The county responded that the election board didn’t have custody of those records and the bureau would need to submit an amended request to the county clerk’s office. The bureau quickly complied. Then, rather than wait for the legal process to play out, they came back with a search warrant.

“The White House has tried to get these ballots from day one,” one DOJ official told The Atlantic, referring to the start of Trump’s second term. The raid, this person said, was “a recalibration in strategy that resulted from the president’s frustration.”

Translation: when asking didn’t work and suing was too slow, they sent the FBI.

The Missouri Connection

Attorney General Pam Bondi, who appointed Albus and used immigration enforcement as leverage in demands for voter data, including in Minnesota.

Why was a prosecutor from St. Louis running an election investigation in Atlanta?

Because Attorney General Pam Bondi had quietly appointed Thomas Albus to a special role: investigating election integrity cases nationwide. Bloomberg Law broke the story on the day of the raid. Using a federal statute — 28 U.S.C. § 515 — that permits the AG to authorize any DOJ officer to conduct legal proceedings anywhere in the country, Bondi had effectively created an unaccountable special counsel. All the power, none of the independence protections.

The choice of Albus was not random. Pull the thread and a tight network of Missouri conservatives emerges — one that has been trying to overturn the 2020 election since the night it happened.

Thomas Albus, U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Missouri, appointed to investigate election integrity nationwide and listed on the Fulton County search warrant.

Albus served as the top deputy to Eric Schmitt when Schmitt was Missouri’s Attorney General. In December 2020, Schmitt joined the Texas lawsuit filed by Ken Paxton seeking to overturn the election results in Georgia, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — an audacious, evidence-free attempt that the Supreme Court refused to hear. Schmitt is now a Republican senator from Missouri, where he praised Albus from the Senate floor as “sharp, serious, and a lifelong proponent of law and order.”

Then there’s Ed Martin. Former chairman of the Missouri Republican Party, Martin is now the DOJ’s pardon attorney. His approach to the job was captured in a single social media post upon his appointment: “No MAGA left behind.” Martin and Albus both worked at the Bryan Cave law firm earlier in their careers.

And then there’s Andrew Bailey — not the British central banker, but the former Missouri Attorney General who, while running for reelection, publicly declared that “the left stole that election.” Bailey is now one of two FBI Deputy Directors. He was at the Fulton County warehouse alongside Gabbard on the night of the raid.

Missouri’s entire conservative legal establishment, it seems, has been reassembled inside the federal government and aimed at Georgia’s ballots.

As Barbara McQuade, the University of Michigan law professor and former U.S. Attorney, wrote: “The appointment of Albus raises concerns not only about the potential for manufactured crimes but also about the risk of undermining the integrity of elections in 2026 and 2028.”

Marc Elias, the Democratic election attorney who has been fighting these battles in court, was more direct in Democracy Docket: “DOJ’s legal machinery to subvert the 2026 election is already in place.”

The True Believer

Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard, in baseball cap and sunglasses, inside the Fulton County Election Hub during the FBI’s seizure of 700 boxes of 2020 ballots on January 28, 2026. No Director of National Intelligence has ever appeared at a domestic FBI search. Four administration officials offered four different explanations for why she was there. (Photo: The New York Times)

Now we get to the strangest character in this story — and thanks to the New York Times, we now know exactly how she ended up in that parking lot.

In late January, Tulsi Gabbard — the Director of National Intelligence — was leading a meeting on Syria in the White House Situation Room. Active geopolitics. A real national security crisis requiring the attention of America’s top intelligence official. Then President Trump abruptly changed the subject.

He reminded Gabbard of an earlier conversation about something he considered more urgent: reopening and expanding the investigation of the 2020 election. “You go do that, you get it done,” Trump told her, according to people with knowledge of the meeting.

Days later, Gabbard was photographed at the Fulton County warehouse during the FBI raid — walking among the vehicles, talking on a phone, visible to agents and media alike, dressed in a baseball cap and sunglasses like someone who wanted to be incognito but forgot to leave the entourage at home. Former intelligence officials called her presence “unbelievable” and “unprecedented.”

Why? Because the Director of National Intelligence has no operational authority. No law enforcement role. The ODNI was created by a 2004 law to coordinate among the 18 intelligence agencies — not to participate in domestic criminal investigations. As retired Air Force General Michael Hayden, who led both the CIA and the NSA and served as a senior ODNI leader, put it: “She has no responsibility for that, at all.”

But Trump sent her anyway — and the reason is more damning than the act itself. According to the Times, the president has privately rebuked Attorney General Bondi multiple times for not moving fast enough on prosecutions and investigations he wants. He has “little confidence” that FBI Director Kash Patel can follow through and find evidence of wrongdoing. So he dispatched the one person White House officials describe as a “true believer” — sending his intelligence chief to do a law enforcement job because his law enforcement people weren’t performing to his satisfaction.

Gabbard’s “super power,” administration officials told the Times — and they meant this as a compliment — is “her enthusiasm for what many consider conspiracy theories and her relentless focus on attacking, and rooting out, the so-called deep state.”

The administration’s own officials just told the paper of record that the Director of National Intelligence’s greatest asset is her enthusiasm for conspiracy theories. Let that sit for a moment.

Senator Mark Warner of Virginia, Vice Chairman of the Senate Intelligence Committee, laid out the only two legitimate explanations for her presence: either Gabbard believed there was a “legitimate foreign intelligence nexus” — in which case she had violated her legal obligation to brief the intelligence committees — or she was attempting to insert the intelligence community into “a domestic political stunt designed to legitimize conspiracy theories that undermine our democracy.”

What followed was a five-day masterclass in contradictory cover stories:

Tuesday: White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said the president had “tapped” Gabbard to oversee election security as part of her duties.

Wednesday: Trump himself was asked by NBC’s Tom Llamas why Gabbard was there. His answer wandered into geopolitics: “I don’t know, but you know, uh, a lot of the cheating comes from — it’s international cheating. You have people, they say, from China trying to —”

Thursday: At the National Prayer Breakfast, Trump offered version three: “She went in — at Pam’s insistence.” Bondi wanted her there.

Sunday: Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche told CNN: “I don’t know why the director was there.” Then he added, remarkably, that she was “not part of the grand jury investigation.”

Four explanations. Five days. Not one of them consistent with the others. And none of them true — because the Times had already reported what actually happened: Trump ordered her to go from the Situation Room, bypassing his own Justice Department entirely.

Bondi wasn’t even given advance notice Gabbard would be on site. Blanche, her deputy, learned about it after the fact. The president was running a parallel investigation through his intelligence chief because his attorney general and his FBI director weren’t delivering the results he wanted fast enough.

This wasn’t interagency coordination. It was an end run.

The Phone Call

What happened the day after the raid was, in some ways, worse.

Gabbard visited the FBI’s Atlanta field office to meet with agents who had conducted the search. During the meeting, she pulled out her cellphone and called President Trump. He didn’t pick up. He called back shortly after. Gabbard put him on speakerphone.

The President of the United States then addressed the FBI agents who were conducting a criminal investigation in which he had an enormous personal stake.

He thanked them. He praised them. And then — according to three people with knowledge of the call — he started asking questions. The squad supervisor tried to field the president’s queries, running interference to keep the conversation from going further into investigative territory. One U.S. official compared the call to “a pep rally or a coach giving an encouraging halftime speech.”

In her letter to Congress, Gabbard denied that Trump asked questions or issued directives. Three sources told the New York Times otherwise.

David Laufman, who served as a senior Justice Department official in both Republican and Democratic administrations, called it “extremely dangerous to our democracy and a shocking abandonment of years of sound policy for the president to be directly involved in the conduct of domestic criminal investigations, especially one that seeks to redress his personal grievances.”

Here’s the legal problem — and it’s a gift to any defense attorney. In previous cases where Trump demanded prosecutions — against James Comey, against Letitia James — defense lawyers argued vindictive prosecution: the president wanted these people charged for personal reasons, not because of evidence. The administration’s counter-argument was always the same: it doesn’t matter what the president says publicly; the investigators and prosecutors are independent.

That argument just died on speakerphone in Atlanta.

If anyone is ever charged as a result of this investigation, defense lawyers will subpoena every agent who was in that room. They will ask: What did the president say to you? What questions did he ask? What did you understand his expectations to be? And suddenly the entire case stops being about evidence. It becomes about Trump's involvement.

As Devlin Barrett of the New York Times put it on The Daily: “If you have the president talking directly to the investigators, it suddenly becomes much harder to argue that there isn’t a connection between what the president wants and what the investigators are doing.”

Trump’s obsession with proving the 2020 election was stolen may have just ensured that no one will ever be convicted of stealing it — even in his alternative reality where the theft actually happened.

Clearing the Field

None of this happened in a vacuum. Before the raid, the field was carefully prepared.

Paul W. Brown, the Special Agent in Charge of the FBI's Atlanta field office, filed his resignation papers approximately a week before the search warrant was executed. Brown was among the last remaining SACs who had served in leadership roles under the Biden administration. Nearly all the others had been systematically forced out or replaced. An FBI spokesperson said Brown had "retired." The timing speaks for itself.

More disturbing is what Gabbard did to the oversight machinery. Last year, she fired a top lawyer in the Office of the Inspector General — the very office responsible for reviewing whistleblower complaints and providing independent oversight — and installed one of her own advisors. Senior Democrats said this violated the law.

Why does that matter? Because a whistleblower filed a complaint against Gabbard last May, alleging she withheld access to classified intelligence for political reasons. The complaint involved an NSA intercept of two foreign nationals discussing someone close to President Trump — with a connection to Iran, around the time the administration was deliberating strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities.

The Inspector General’s office — the same office where Gabbard had installed her own person — reviewed the complaint and found it “not credible.” The IG also determined that Gabbard’s decision to restrict distribution of the underlying intelligence was appropriate. Even some administration critics agreed on that narrow point.

But the timeline is damning. Federal law requires whistleblower complaints deemed urgent to be referred to Congress within 21 days. Gabbard’s office sat on this one for eight months, claiming the volume of classified details required “an extensive legal and security review.” Senator Warner’s response: “The law is clear. I think it was an effort to try to bury this whistleblower complaint.”

When copies were finally hand-delivered to the “Gang of Eight” congressional leaders this week, they arrived heavily redacted. Senator Tom Cotton, the Republican chairman of the Senate Intelligence Committee, dismissed it as politically motivated. Andrew Bakaj, the whistleblower’s attorney, responded: “Given the extensive redactions, it seems unlikely anyone could reasonably and in a non-partisan manner reach the conclusions issued by Senator Cotton.”

The pattern is consistent: fire the Atlanta SAC before the raid. Fire the IG’s lawyer before the complaint review. Install loyalists at every checkpoint. Restrict information. Delay disclosure. Redact what you can’t suppress.

It’s not incompetence. It’s preparation.

The Puerto Rico Detour

Voters at a polling station in Puerto Rico, where electronic voting machines were voluntarily handed over to ODNI for analysis last spring. The investigation cited ‘concerning’ cybersecurity issues but found no evidence of exploitation or foreign interference

If Fulton County was the main stage, Puerto Rico was the dress rehearsal.

Last spring, Gabbard’s office led an investigation into Puerto Rico’s voting machines, taking physical custody of an unspecified number of machines and copying their data. Three sources told Reuters the goal was to investigate claims that Venezuela had hacked the machines — a conspiracy theory popular among Trump supporters but never substantiated by evidence.

Gabbard’s office denied the Venezuela connection, saying the focus was on “vulnerabilities in the island’s electronic voting systems.” They claimed to have found “extremely concerning cyber security and operational deployment practices” — but provided no detailed evidence and no indication that any vulnerability had actually been exploited.

A former senior U.S. intelligence official who worked on election security called it “amateur hour.” Kevin Skoglund, an expert in voting machine cybersecurity, said the agency hadn’t “given any meaningful details” on its findings.

Puerto Rico’s congressional representative, Pablo Jose Hernandez, was characteristically direct: “There is no evidence of foreign interference in Puerto Rico’s elections. Yet the Trump administration has failed to explain why Tulsi Gabbard’s team was there in the first place.”

There’s also a beautiful irony that the reporting has mostly overlooked: Puerto Rico’s residents cannot vote in presidential elections. The ODNI was hunting for presidential election fraud in a place that doesn’t vote for president.

But the Puerto Rico operation served a purpose. It established a precedent for the intelligence community seizing voting equipment. It tested the legal theory that ODNI has “broad statutory authority” over election security. And it provided practice for the much larger operation to come in Georgia.

The Chain of Custody

Back in Fulton County, the aftermath has its own dark resonance.

The location of the seized materials is, as of this writing, unknown. Federal officials will not say where the 700 boxes were taken, or even whether they remain in Georgia. Fulton County officials have filed a court challenge demanding the return of the ballots and records, arguing that the federal government is violating rules designed to ensure ballot integrity and chain of custody.

Fulton County Commission Chairman Robb Pitts captured the nightmare scenario with devastating simplicity: “Once they left that facility last night in those FBI trucks, I don’t know where they are now. I don’t know what they’re doing with them. Are they opening the boxes? Are they stuffing other ballots into there? I have no clue.”

Commissioner Marvin Arrington Jr. went further: “The chain of custody has been broken. They could add 11,000 records or lose 11,000 records... and we don’t have any way to counter any false narrative they come up with.”

Eleven thousand. The exact margin by which Trump lost Georgia in 2020.

That’s not a coincidence. That’s a confession of what they’re afraid of — and what, if the chain of custody has truly been broken, becomes impossible to disprove.

The most investigated election in American history is now in the custody of the people who insist it was stolen. And no one can verify what they’re doing with it.
