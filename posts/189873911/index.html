When Democracy Fights Back

The Scoreboard

Parrt 16: The New York Times published a chart. Here's what it doesn't show.

March 4, 2026

By Stephen Gordon — CanadianSnowmanSays

On March 2, the New York Times published a tracker. David W. Chen’s piece, “Tracking the Battle to Reshape Congress for the Midterms,” laid out a simple chart showing which states have already redrawn their maps and which are still trying. It’s the kind of data journalism the Times does well — clean, visual, neutral. Maps with silhouettes. Colour-coded gains. A tidy little accounting of which party stands to win what.

But what the tracker doesn’t show — what no chart can show — is the machinery underneath the numbers. The court rulings that arrived at midnight. The voters turned away at the door. The conspiracy theories that rewired an entire county’s election infrastructure. The constitutional amendment that’s been blocked, unblocked, re-blocked, and unblocked again in the span of six weeks.

This is Part 16 of the “When Democracy Fights Back” series. In Part 15, I argued that redistricting and voter suppression have become a coordinated strategy — two fronts of the same war. Last night, on the first primary day of the 2026 cycle, that thesis walked into a polling station in Dallas County and got turned away.

Let’s look at the scoreboard. Then let’s look at what’s behind it.

The Numbers

Here’s where things stand as of March 4, 2026:

Maps already changed — Republican gains: Texas: +5R. Missouri: +1R. North Carolina: +1R. Ohio: +1 to 2R.

Maps already changed — Democratic gains: California: +5D. Utah: +1D.

Maps still in play: Florida: +2 to 4R (special session April 20-24). Virginia: +2 to 4D (referendum April 21). Maryland: +1D (effectively dead in the Senate).

Net effect if everything plays out: Somewhere between +1D and +4R, depending on Florida, Virginia, and the courts.

The Republicans moved first. They moved at the President’s direction. And they moved in states where they had both the legislative muscle and the legal cover to act fast. Texas signed its maps in August. Missouri followed in September. North Carolina in October. Ohio’s commission approved its plans the same month. By the time Democrats started to respond — California’s Prop 50 in November, Virginia’s amendment process beginning in October — the Republican maps were already being litigated, upheld, and locked in.

That sequencing matters. In a redistricting arms race, the first mover doesn’t just gain seats. They gain time. And time, in an election year, is the only resource that can’t be manufactured.

The Supreme Court Pattern

Here’s something I want you to notice, because I think it’s the most important structural development in this entire saga.

The Supreme Court has now intervened in redistricting disputes in three states: Texas, California, and New York. In each case, the Court sided 6-3 to preserve the existing map — the map that was already in place when the challenge was filed.

In Texas, the Court stayed a federal ruling that found the maps were an illegal racial gerrymander. In California, the Court dismissed Republican claims that the new maps illegally favoured Latino voters. And on March 2, the Court blocked a New York state judge’s order to redraw the 11th Congressional District — the only Republican-held seat in New York City.

On the surface, that looks even-handed. The Court preserved maps in a red state and a blue state. Justice Alito wrote that the New York ruling amounted to “unadorned racial discrimination.” The liberal justices dissented each time. You might call it consistency.

But here’s what consistency means in practice: it locks in whichever map exists at the moment the challenge is filed. And since Republicans drew their maps first — months before Democrats responded — the status quo is the Republican advantage. The Court isn’t picking sides. It’s freezing the battlefield at the moment when one side had already advanced and the other was still mobilizing.

That’s not a conspiracy. It’s a structural consequence of who moved first and how the Court’s emergency docket works. But the effect is the same: the party that acted fastest gets the most protection.

The April Convergence

Now look at the calendar.

April 20-24: Florida’s special legislative session on redistricting. Governor DeSantis has called legislators back to Tallahassee for the sole purpose of redrawing congressional maps. Florida currently has 20 Republican and 8 Democratic House seats. Republicans are eyeing 2 to 4 more.

April 21: Virginia’s redistricting referendum. Voters will decide whether to temporarily allow the Democratic legislature to redraw the state’s 11 congressional districts. The proposed map would create a 10-1 Democratic split — a net gain of four seats.

Sometime this spring: The Supreme Court ruling in Louisiana v. Callais, which could determine whether Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act — the provision that bars racial discrimination in voting systems — is constitutional. DeSantis has explicitly said he’s waiting for this ruling before finalizing Florida’s maps, because he believes it will give him legal cover to dismantle majority-minority districts in South Florida.

All three of these events could land in the same week. The math of the entire House of Representatives — where Republicans currently hold a 218-214 margin with three vacancies — could shift by anywhere from 2 to 8 seats based on what happens in those five days.

Virginia’s path has been extraordinary even by the standards of this series. The amendment was first considered in October 2025. It passed the General Assembly twice, as required. Then a Virginia judge blocked it on January 27. The Virginia Supreme Court allowed it to proceed on February 13. A different judge blocked it again on February 19, on entirely new grounds. And on March 2, yet another court ruled the election can go forward, with legal challenges to be resolved after the vote. Early voting begins March 6. National Democratic groups have poured nearly $20 million into the campaign.

The Republican legal strategy has been transparent: challenge every procedural step, in every available court, with the goal of running out the clock. If you can’t win the referendum, delay it past the point where new maps can be drawn, approved, and implemented before candidate filing deadlines. It’s the same strategy used in Ohio, where Republicans delayed compliance with court-ordered redistricting for years. Democracy by filibuster.

Florida presents a different problem. DeSantis isn’t fighting a court — he is the court, or close to it. The Florida Supreme Court, which includes justices he appointed, unanimously cleared his special session to proceed. A lawsuit challenging his authority was dismissed in two paragraphs. And Florida’s “Fair Districts” amendment — approved by more than 60% of voters in 2010 to ban partisan gerrymandering — has already been hollowed out by the same court, which upheld a DeSantis-backed gerrymander last July.

The question in Florida isn’t whether the maps will be redrawn. It’s how aggressively. If Louisiana v. Callais drops before the session and weakens the Voting Rights Act as expected, DeSantis could target majority-minority districts in South Florida with full constitutional cover. That’s not +2 Republican seats. That’s potentially +4.

Maryland: The Dog That Didn’t Bark

A brief word about what didn’t happen.

Maryland’s House of Delegates passed a redistricting bill on February 2, voting 99-37 to redraw the state’s maps and potentially turn a 7-1 Democratic advantage into 8-0. Governor Wes Moore pushed hard. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries flew to Annapolis in person to lobby. The pressure was intense.

Senate President Bill Ferguson said no.

His reasoning was practical, not principled: he believed a new map would be struck down by courts, could backfire by reopening the 2022 settlement that gave Democrats their current advantage, and couldn’t be implemented in time for 2026 anyway. On February 20, he declared the “window of opportunity has closed.”

Ferguson may be right about the legal risk. But the political consequence is real. Maryland’s refusal to act means Democrats leave a seat on the table — while Florida, where the legal barriers are arguably higher (a voter-approved anti-gerrymandering amendment), is barreling ahead anyway. The asymmetry tells you something about the two parties. Republicans treat redistricting as warfare. Some Democrats treat it as a policy debate.

Dallas County: The Thesis Made Flesh

And then there’s what happened last night.

In Part 15, I argued that redistricting and voter suppression have become a coordinated strategy — that the same political infrastructure that draws the maps also writes the voting rules, and that both are designed to predetermine outcomes before a single ballot is cast.

On March 3, in Dallas County, that argument stopped being theoretical.

The Dallas County Republican Party, led by former Florida congressman Allen West, made a decision months ago to abandon countywide voting centres and return to precinct-level voting for the primary. The stated reason was election security — specifically, conspiracy theories about voting machines that have been debunked repeatedly since 2020. The practical effect was that hundreds of voters — possibly thousands — showed up at locations where they’d voted in every previous election and were told they couldn’t vote there.

County election “navigators” stationed outside at least 75 polling locations reported redirecting between 50 and 100 percent of arriving voters. Think about that number. At some locations, every single person who showed up was at the wrong place. One woman in Oak Lawn told a reporter she’d walked to her usual polling station because she wanted to vote “so, so bad.” Election workers told her she needed to go to a different precinct — one she’d never heard of. She stood outside trying to figure out her options. “It felt like your self-esteem and everything is torn down,” she said.

This voter made it through. A primary voter wears a sticker after casting a ballot at a voting center in Dallas, on Tuesday. LM Otero/AP

She wasn’t alone. According to Crockett’s campaign, nearly 200 people showed up at the wrong site in Oak Lawn alone. The county elections website — the one tool voters had to find their correct precinct — crashed under the traffic. The Dallas County Democratic Party chair filed an emergency petition saying confusion was so severe the entire system was breaking down.

A judge ordered polls open until 9 PM. Then Attorney General Ken Paxton — who was simultaneously running for Senate on that same ballot — appealed the extension. The Texas Supreme Court sided with Paxton within hours, blocking the extension and ordering all ballots cast by voters who joined the line after 7 PM to be separated. Those ballots remain in legal limbo as I write this. The man running for office got to decide which votes counted.

And it wasn’t just Dallas. The same thing happened in Williamson County, north of Austin, where Republicans made the same switch to precinct-based voting. Same confusion. Same voters turned away. Same pattern.

The cost wasn’t theoretical. Political science professor Matthew Wilson of Southern Methodist University put it plainly: “Some number of people who are turned away will not end up locating or going to their assigned precinct.” Those aren’t hypothetical voters. Those are citizens who got dressed, drove or walked to the polls, waited in line, and were told to go somewhere else. Some fraction of them — we’ll never know how many — gave up and went home. Their votes don’t appear on any scoreboard. They just disappear.

Here’s the part that ties it all together: the mid-decade redistricting that Texas completed last August changed every district line in the state. Voters who might have known their old precinct had no idea where their new one was. The redistricting created the confusion. The precinct-level voting requirement — driven by conspiracy theories about voting machines that have been debunked since 2020 — weaponized it. And when a judge tried to give voters more time, the courts blocked the remedy at the request of a candidate on the ballot.

Redistricting drew the map. Voter suppression — whether intentional or recklessly indifferent — enforced it. Two fronts. One strategy. One outcome: fewer people voting.

The Dallas County Democratic Party chair, Kardal Coleman, connected the dots explicitly: “You have this mid-decade redistricting, then you also, subsequent to that, then you have a non-joint primary election. The Republicans wouldn’t come to the table and agree to have a joint election.”

The Texas Democratic Party chairman, Kendall Scudder, went further: “Both counties have spent a million dollars trying to get the word out about this change — a million dollars of taxpayer money that didn’t need to be spent. Democrats pushed for a joint election. It would have been cheaper, simpler, and more accessible for every voter in the county. Republicans said no.”

That’s not a conspiracy theory. That’s a sequence of decisions, each made by the same political party, each compounding the effect of the last. Draw the maps. Change the voting rules. Crash the system. Block the fix. Count what’s left.

What the Scoreboard Doesn’t Show

David Chen’s tracker in the Times is useful. I recommend you bookmark it. It tells you the seats, the states, the projected gains. It’s the box score.

But box scores don’t tell you about the umpire who expanded the strike zone in the third inning. They don’t tell you about the team that showed up to the field an hour early and moved the fences. They don’t tell you about the fans who drove to the stadium and found the gates locked.

The redistricting war of 2025-2026 is the most consequential mid-decade restructuring of American democracy in modern history. It was initiated by a sitting president who told one party’s state legislatures to redraw their maps, and it has now engulfed at least a dozen states, generated hundreds of millions of dollars in legal fees and campaign spending, and produced at least six Supreme Court interventions.

And it all comes down to April.

Florida redraws its maps. Virginia votes on its referendum. The Supreme Court decides whether the Voting Rights Act still means what it says. And somewhere in the background, the voters of Dallas County are still trying to figure out where they were supposed to go.
