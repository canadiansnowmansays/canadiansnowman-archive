The Counterfactual President

In Defense of the Counterfactual

Part 1: The Irony of Telling a What-If Story in Trump’s America

March 20, 2026

The architect of the Gulf of America, the peaceful January 6th, and the stolen 2020 election —signing another document into his preferred version of history. Win McNamee/Getty Images

March 20, 2026

Before we begin, let’s address the naysayers.

And there will be naysayers. There always are when someone dares to examine the road not taken. The objections are predictable, and I want to meet them head-on before the first word of analysis is written.

“We don’t live in a what-if universe.”

“Counterfactuals are just wishful thinking dressed up as journalism.”

“You can’t know what would have happened, so this is speculation, not analysis.”

“You lost. Get over it.”

These are reasonable-sounding objections. They are also, in the current American political context, almost exquisitely ironic.

Because the man whose presidency this series examines — the man whose first year back in power provides the documented baseline against which every counterfactual in this series is measured — is arguably the most prolific and aggressive alternate reality manufacturer in the history of American politics.

Donald Trump doesn’t just tolerate counterfactuals. He lives in them. He builds them. He imposes them on everyone around him, and then demands that institutions, courts, allies, and history itself ratify his preferred version of events.

So before any naysayer accuses me of trafficking in fantasy, I want to make something clear: I learned this genre from the master. I’m just doing it honestly.

Trump’s Alternate Reality Empire

Let us count the ways.

The Big Lie. The foundational alternate reality of Trump’s political career, the one from which all others flow: He won the 2020 election. He didn’t. Every court agreed — including judges he appointed. Every recount confirmed it. Every audit upheld it. The margin in key states was decisive. None of that matters in Trump’s counterfactual universe, where he won, the election was stolen, and anyone who certified the actual results was a traitor.

This isn’t a policy disagreement. This isn’t an interpretation of ambiguous evidence. This is the wholesale construction of an alternate reality — a reality in which a documented, certified, adjudicated electoral outcome simply didn’t happen — and the demand that everyone pretend to live in it.

January 6th: A Day of Love. We watched it happen. Every American with a television watched it happen. Capitol Police officers were beaten with flagpoles. Windows were smashed. Legislators fled. The Vice President was hidden. Gallows were erected on the Capitol grounds. People died.

In Trump’s alternate reality, January 6th was “a day of love.” The insurrectionists were “patriots,” “hostages,” “political prisoners.” Their violence was rebranded as peaceful protest. Their convictions — obtained through trials, with evidence, before juries — were declared fraudulent. Their pardons were acts of justice rather than the erasure of documented guilt.

The historical record of January 6th was not just disputed. It was officially rewritten, by the President of the United States, within weeks of his return to power.

January 6, 2021: documented on video by thousands of cameras, investigated by a bipartisan committee, prosecuted through the courts — then officially rebranded as ‘a day of love’ and the defendants pardoned en masse on inauguration day 2025. Wikipedia

The Map. Trump signed an executive order renaming the Gulf of Mexico the Gulf of America for all federal purposes — maps, documents, official communications. He reversed the renaming of Denali back to Mount McKinley. These aren’t trivial acts of presidential branding. They are the imposition of a preferred alternate reality onto official geography — erasing prior decisions and prior history by executive fiat. If the President can rename the Gulf of Mexico, he can rename anything. Official history becomes presidential preference.

One executive order, one renamed gulf: EO 14172 (January 20, 2025) directed federal agencies to replace ‘Gulf of Mexico’ with ‘Gulf of America’ on all official maps and documents — a small but telling act of imposed alternate reality. GoogleMaps

The 1776 Commission. Created explicitly to counter the 1619 Project — which examined America’s history through the lens of slavery’s centrality — the 1776 Commission produced a sanitized, celebratory alternate history curriculum designed for American schools. Not a rebuttal engaging with evidence. An alternative reality: a version of American history in which the uncomfortable parts are minimized, the comfortable parts are amplified, and the whole is presented as the “correct” history that students should learn instead of the documented one.

Confederate Monuments. Trump signed executive orders to protect Confederate monuments from removal, framing their preservation as protecting “our history.” But the monuments weren’t history — most were built decades after the Civil War, during the Jim Crow era, specifically to celebrate the Lost Cause narrative and intimidate Black Americans. Protecting them wasn’t preserving history. It was preserving a particular alternate reality about what the Confederacy meant and why it should be celebrated.

Scrubbing the Federal Record. Under Trump’s return to power, climate data began disappearing from federal websites. LGBTQ references were purged from government documents. DEI language was erased from institutional histories. The federal government’s documentary record — the accumulated factual foundation on which policy analysis, historical research, and public accountability depend — was selectively deleted to conform to presidential preference.

This is alternate reality construction at the archival level. It’s not enough to claim a different version of history. You erase the evidence that contradicts your preferred version so future generations can’t easily reconstruct what actually happened.

DOGE and the Oversight Demolition. When you fire the Inspectors General, defund the ethics offices, and eliminate the administrative infrastructure that documents government misconduct — as DOGE did systematically throughout year one — you’re not just enabling current impunity. You’re destroying the evidentiary record of past misconduct and ensuring that future historians will struggle to reconstruct what happened.

The demolition of oversight architecture is alternate reality construction at its most structural level: not rewriting history after the fact, but preventing the documentation that would make accurate history possible.

His Own Criminal Record. Thirty-four felony convictions became “election interference.” The jury that convicted him was “rigged.” The judges who oversaw proceedings were “corrupt.” The prosecutors who built the cases were “radical left.” A documented legal proceeding with witnesses, evidence, and verdict was rebranded as an elaborate hoax — a counterfactual in which none of it happened, in which the most thoroughly documented white-collar criminal case in American history was fiction.

The Irony Is the Point

I want to be precise about why this matters for this series.

When critics say “you can’t write a what-if series because we don’t live in a what-if universe” — they are applying a standard of factual accountability that the subject of this series has spent his entire political career demolishing.

Trump built his political movement on alternate realities. He won the 2020 election (alternate reality). January 6th was peaceful (alternate reality). His criminal convictions were fraudulent (alternate reality). Climate change is a hoax (alternate reality). The Gulf of Mexico has been renamed for federal purposes (imposed alternate reality). The 1776 Commission’s version of history is correct (constructed alternate reality).

The man who spent years insisting he won an election he lost — who pardoned people convicted by juries, whose guilt was documented on video — is somehow the guardian of factual reality against a series that asks what would have happened if his opponent had won?

The naysayers have it precisely backwards. In Trump’s America, he is the one constructing alternate realities and demanding institutional compliance. This series is grounded in documented evidence: Trump’s actual first-year record, Harris’s documented positions and governance history, and the rigorous analytical question of what different leadership would have produced.

What This Series Is Actually Doing

Let me be direct about the purpose and method of The Counterfactual President.

This series is not wishful thinking. It is not a fantasy about a perfect Harris presidency. Every essay in this series explicitly acknowledges the challenges Harris would have faced, the mistakes she would have made, the progressive disappointments and moderate compromises and Republican obstruction that would have defined her first year. We are not constructing a utopia. We are asking a specific, evidence-based question.

This series is forensic analysis. On November 5, 2024, reality diverged. One path was taken. We have now lived twelve months down that path. We can document what it produced: the specific institutions damaged, the specific alliances fractured, the specific policies implemented, the specific people harmed. That is the baseline — not hypothetical, but documented.

Against that documented baseline, we examine what Harris’s documented record — her Senate votes, her positions as Vice President, her stated policy commitments, her approach to governance — would have produced across those same twelve months. Not perfectly, not omnisciently, but with analytical rigor grounded in evidence.

The purpose is accountability. The deepest purpose of this series is to prevent the normalization that Trump’s alternate reality machine depends upon. When the abnormal is presented as normal long enough, people lose the memory of what normal looked like. They lose the ability to measure the damage because they’ve forgotten the baseline.

This series is the baseline. It says: this is what governance looks like when it functions. This is what alliances look like when they’re respected. This is what rule of law looks like when it’s upheld. This is what twelve months could have looked like if different choices had been made.

That’s not fantasy. That’s the essential act of democratic accountability: measuring what is against what could and should have been.

A Note on the Alternate Reality We Actually Inhabit

There is one final irony worth naming.

Trump’s alternate reality empire — the stolen election, the peaceful January 6th, the renamed Gulf, the sanitized history, the erased data, the pardoned convictions — is not presented as speculation. It is presented as fact. It is taught in some schools. It is repeated by elected officials. It is enforced through loyalty demands and retribution against those who contradict it.

My counterfactual series is presented honestly as analysis — as a rigorous examination of the road not taken, grounded in documented evidence, acknowledging uncertainty where uncertainty exists.

In Trump’s America, the fabricated alternate reality is called truth. The honest counterfactual analysis is called speculation.

We have, in other words, already been living in a what-if universe for years. Trump built it. He populated it. He demands we reside in it.

I’m simply offering a different one — one grounded in evidence, one that measures documented damage, one that remembers what normal looked like.

The naysayers are welcome to prefer Trump’s version. But they should be honest about which of us is actually trafficking in fantasy.

Each essay grounds its analysis in the documented record of what actually happened during Trump’s first year back. Each acknowledges what Harris would have struggled with. Each measures the gap between what was and what could have been.

That’s not a what-if universe. That’s accountability journalism.

And if Donald Trump can rename the Gulf of Mexico, I can certainly ask what the world looks like when democratic governance functions.

Let’s find out together.
