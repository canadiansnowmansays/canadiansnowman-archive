Someone Should Tell Them How Dodge Ball Works

Notes from the Bleachers, February Through Tuesday

April 19, 2026

Published: Sunday, April 19, 2026

Third piece of three this Sunday. If you've made it this far, you've already survived the White House chapel and the Golden Arches. The bleachers are the reward. Pull up a seat.

It's 12 PM. If you're Canadian, you're on your second Timmies. If you're American, you're probably paying more for it than you were in February. Either way, pull up a bleacher — someone should tell them how dodge ball works, and that someone, as usual, appears to be moi.

I have been sitting in these bleachers since February.

I did not choose these bleachers. I am Canadian. I came for the curling. But the gymnasium was already booked — had been booked, it seemed, for some time — and the game underway was not one I immediately recognized. It had the energy of sport. It had the costumes of diplomacy. It had the strategic coherence of neither.

It was, I eventually understood, dodge ball.

Allow me to explain how dodge ball works, for those who learned their geometry in the State Department. Each team occupies one half of the gymnasium. Players throw rubber balls at the opposing team. If you are hit, you are out. If you catch a thrown ball, the thrower is out. This is not tennis. This is not volleyball. This is not, despite what the Vice President of the United States told Fox News on Monday evening, a game in which one deposits the ball in the other team’s court and then holds a press conference.

There is no court. There is a ball. You throw it — you risk being caught out. You catch it — they’re out. But if you announce the ball is in their court while still holding it, you are simply begging to be drilled.

The Americans have been throwing since late February. By any honest accounting of the rules, they should have been eliminated weeks ago. Iran has caught nearly everything — not elegantly, not without cost, but caught nonetheless. This does not mean Iran is winning. It means the rules Vance chose to invoke on Monday evening make him look like a man who showed up to a dodge ball game with a tennis racket. Each caught ball eliminates the thrower. The American bench has been quietly thinning. No one on the American side has mentioned this, which is itself a kind of strategy, though not the kind that wins games.

The team captain has his own interpretation of events. He is not, he wishes it understood, throwing balls. He is healing people with them. He posted a photograph last Sunday to illustrate this point — himself in a white robe, hand extended, light falling at the angle painters reserve for the transcendent — before removing it the following morning. When asked, he explained he had been depicting a Red Cross doctor. The gymnasium chaplain, Pope Leo XIV, had already been called “weak on crime” and “terrible for foreign policy” for observing, with the patience of men who hold long institutional memory, that healing and bombardment are not the same activity. The chaplain said nothing further. He didn’t need to.

Last weekend, Vice President Vance flew to Islamabad with Messrs. Witkoff and Kushner — a delegation that inspired, in those Pakistani hallways, the particular confidence one feels watching three men argue about a restaurant menu in a language none of them speaks. They sat across from the Iranians for twenty-one hours. At the end of twenty-one hours, Vance stood before the cameras, announced that the ball was in Iran’s court, and flew home.

The ball was, in fact, in his hand. He had just given it back.

I have watched a great deal of dodge ball in my life — Canadians are formed by gymnasiums, by February, by the particular character-building misery of being chosen last — and I can say with some authority that returning the ball to the other team is not a diplomatic masterstroke. It is the single most efficient way to get hit. The Iranians accepted the ball with what their Foreign Minister called principled restraint and what the rest of us recognized as the expression of a person who cannot quite believe their luck.

Meanwhile, no one initially mentioned the janitor.

He had been working quietly since Monday morning, pushing the walls of the Iranian side of the gymnasium inward. Not dramatically. Not all at once. Just steadily, the way institutions move when they have decided something without quite announcing it. Ships were turned back. Ports went quiet. The Strait of Hormuz became, by American naval decree, a spectator area. The janitor kept pushing. The Iranian side of the gym grew smaller. The team had less room to dodge, less room to breathe.

Nobody mentioned the janitor.

The ceasefire expires Tuesday. April 21st. The school bell, in other words, rings in approximately forty-eight hours from the moment you read this. By Tuesday, either someone has agreed to something, or the gym teacher resets the clock again, or the janitor finishes pushing the walls, or some combination of all three occurs simultaneously while Vance explains on Fox News that the situation is fundamentally Iran’s fault for not catching the ball correctly.

I have watched six buzzer resets. I have watched three delegations board planes. I have watched the janitor work. I have watched the team captain declare himself winning from the back of the court, throwing at angles that suggest a relationship with geometry that is more aspirational than technical.

I have stopped flinching at the throws.

But the walls are closer than they were last week. The bell is the realest bell we have had. And Vance handed them the ball.

I am going to sit here a little longer.

Someone should bring better bleachers.
