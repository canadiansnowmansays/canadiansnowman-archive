The Great Energy Challenge

Meeting the AI Crisis Through a Renaissance of Clean Power Technologies

Part 3: The Wind Revolution - Onshore, Offshore, and Floating

June 25, 2025

Those beautiful windmills are killing birds!

Plot Twist: Trump's "Beautiful Windmills" Are Eating Coal's Lunch

Love them or hate them, those "beautiful windmills" that former President Trump likes to mock are quietly revolutionizing electricity generation. Wind generation reached a new record, however, as it exceeded coal-fired generation for two months in a row for the first time.

This isn't some future projection—it's happening now. Wind power has become so competitive that it's driving coal plants out of business through pure economics. Wind is among the cheapest sources of energy in the world and one of the two sources of renewable energy that make up 95% of all global renewable capacity growth in this decade.

The Numbers Are Stunning

Global wind capacity reached 1,136 GW, with 55 countries adding new turbines in 2024. To put this in perspective, that's more than the entire electricity generating capacity of the United States just a few decades ago.

The growth trajectory is accelerating. GWEC's Market Intelligence service sees consecutive record years through to 2030, with 138 GW of new capacity in 2025, 140 GW in 2026, 160 GW in 2027, 167 in 2028 and a leap in 2029 and 2030 to 183 GW and 194 GW respectively.

Onshore Wind: The Workhorse

Onshore wind has become the electricity generation workhorse. Onshore wind additions increase in our main-case forecast, from 74 GW in 2021 to 109 GW in 2027. The technology is mature, costs have plummeted, and installation is relatively straightforward.

The Economics Are Compelling: Modern wind turbines generate electricity at costs that fossil fuel plants simply can't match. In many regions, new wind power is cheaper than keeping existing coal plants running. This economic advantage is driving the transition faster than any policy could.

The Scale Advantage: A similar-scale nuclear plant would require only about 640 acres compared to wind farms, but wind can be deployed much faster. While nuclear takes a decade to build, wind farms can be operational in 18-24 months.

Offshore Wind: The New Frontier

If onshore wind is impressive, offshore wind is revolutionary. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), there is enough offshore wind resource globally to meet the world's entire electricity demand today – 18 times over.

The Growth Trajectory: GWEC forecasts offshore wind installations to grow from 16 GW in 2025 to 34 GW by 2030, with offshore wind's share of new capacity rising from 11.8% to 17.5% by the decade's end.

The offshore wind energy market size exceeded USD 55.9 billion in 2024 and is anticipated to grow at a CAGR of 14.6% from 2025 to 2034.

Floating Wind: Unlocking Deep Waters

The most exciting development might be floating offshore wind. The floating offshore wind energy market size exceeded USD 367.7 million in 2024 and is anticipated to grow at a CAGR of 31.5% from 2025 to 2034.

Why does this matter? Floating turbines offer the opportunity to tap into the richest offshore wind resources located in water depths exceeding 50-60 m, where seabed-fixed foundations are not practical.

This opens up vast new areas for wind development, particularly in countries with deep coastal waters like the United States West Coast, Japan, and much of the Mediterranean.

The AI Data Center Connection

Wind power has unique advantages for powering AI infrastructure. Offshore wind farms can generate massive amounts of power in concentrated locations—exactly what hyperscale data centers need. Offshore wind energy is shaping the future of global power generation, and it's perfectly positioned to meet AI's energy demands.

Some visionary companies are already exploring floating data centers powered directly by offshore wind farms, eliminating transmission losses and enabling AI infrastructure to follow the wind resource.

The Challenges Are Significant

But wind faces serious obstacles. Wind capacity additions fell by 14% to 2.6 GW amid continued supply chain, financing, and permitting challenges.

The Permitting Nightmare: Lengthy and complicated permitting processes are one of the main challenges to faster deployment of wind power plants in many parts of the world, especially in Europe. Projects can take years to get approval, even when the technology is ready to deploy.

Supply Chain Bottlenecks: The rapid growth has strained supply chains for key components like turbines, towers, and specialized installation vessels. These bottlenecks can delay projects and drive up costs.

Grid Integration: Like solar, wind's variability creates grid integration challenges. Unlike fossil fuel plants that can be turned on and off at will, wind power depends on nature's schedule. This requires sophisticated grid management and backup power sources.

Regional Winners and Losers

China led new installations, followed by the USA, Germany, India, and Brazil, which also now rank in the top five countries for total installed wind capacity. Meanwhile, the report shows strong growth in Asia-Pacific (7% y-o-y) and Africa & the Middle East (107% y-o-y), led by Egypt and Saudi Arabia. North America, LATAM, and Europe saw declines.

This geographic shift matters. Countries that move fastest on wind deployment will have major advantages in the AI economy, while those that lag risk being left behind.

Climate Change Complications

Here's a troubling wild card: Globally, at more than 80 % of studied sites, the capacity factors significantly decrease under all evaluated climate change scenarios in 2025–2054. Climate change itself might reduce wind resources in many regions, adding urgency to deployment while the resource is still robust.

Wind's Role in the Energy Future

Wind will be crucial for meeting AI energy demands. Its ability to generate massive amounts of clean electricity at competitive costs makes it indispensable. The combination of onshore wind's proven economics, offshore wind's massive resource potential, and floating wind's ability to access previously unreachable areas gives wind energy unmatched scalability.

But like solar, wind faces intermittency challenges. The AI energy crisis requires 24/7 power, which means we need technologies that complement wind's variability. This is where the next generation of clean energy technologies becomes crucial.
