The Seminarian’s Gambit

Part 3: How a Seminarian Is Reclaiming Christianity From the Religious Right

February 20, 2026

Texas Rep. James Talarico D-Austin speaks during a redistricting rally at Delco Activity Center Friday, July 25, 2025 in Austin, Texas. Ronaldo Bolaños/The Texas Tribune

CanadianSnowmanSays · Let’s Talk Texas · February 2026

In 2004, Karl Rove engineered George W. Bush’s re-election partly by putting same-sex marriage bans on the ballot in eleven states, including Ohio. The strategy was elegant in its cynicism: use a cultural wedge issue to drive evangelical turnout in swing states, then harvest the votes for the Republican ticket up and down the ballot. It worked. Bush won Ohio by two points and the presidency with it.

For twenty years, that playbook defined Republican politics in Texas and across the South. Mobilize the religious right. Frame every election as a battle between the faithful and the secular. Convince churchgoing voters that the Democratic Party is hostile to their values, their communities, and their God.

James Talarico is betting his Senate campaign on the proposition that the playbook has finally expired.

The Matthew 25 Argument

Talarico is a Presbyterian seminarian. Not was — is. He’s completing his seminary education while running for Senate and serving in the Texas House. That’s not a biographical footnote. It’s the architecture of his entire campaign.

His argument, which he laid out on Colbert and has been making on floors and stages across Texas for months, is disarmingly simple. For fifty years, the religious right — “a political movement,” he’s careful to say, not a spiritual one — convinced American Christians that the defining issues of their faith were abortion and gay marriage. Two issues, Talarico notes, that are never mentioned in the Bible. Two issues Jesus never addressed.

What Jesus did address, in Matthew 25, is how believers will be judged: by feeding the hungry, healing the sick, welcoming the stranger. Not by church attendance. Not by party affiliation. By how you treat other people.

“Don’t tell me what you believe,” Talarico says. “Show me how you treat other people, and I’ll tell you what you believe.”

This isn’t a new theological argument. Liberation theologians have been making versions of it for decades. What’s new is a Democratic Senate candidate making it the centrepiece of his campaign — in Texas, where the evangelical vote has been the bedrock of Republican dominance since the Reagan realignment.

Why It Matters Strategically

There’s a reason Texas Republicans told the New York Times‘ Michelle Cottle that they’re more worried about facing Talarico than Jasmine Crockett in the general election. It’s not because Crockett is a weak candidate — she’s a sharp communicator with a national profile. It’s because Talarico is attacking the foundation, not the house.

The Republican coalition in Texas rests on three pillars: business conservatives, rural cultural conservatives, and evangelical Christians. The first group is already uneasy about tariffs, DOGE, and economic instability. The second is reachable on kitchen-table issues, as Taylor Rehmet just demonstrated. But the third — the evangelical base — has been considered untouchable. They vote Republican because they believe God wants them to, because their pastors tell them to, because the party has spent decades embedding itself in the infrastructure of American Protestant Christianity so deeply that the two have become nearly indistinguishable.

Talarico isn’t asking those voters to abandon their faith. He’s asking them to examine whether their party has abandoned it for them.

That’s the gambit. And it’s either the most strategically brilliant move in Texas Democratic politics in a generation, or it’s a seminary thesis that sounds beautiful in interviews and doesn’t move a single vote on Election Day.

The Separation Argument

The most sophisticated part of Talarico’s case isn’t about Matthew 25. It’s about the separation of church and state — and specifically, his argument that the separation protects the church more than the state.

His grandfather was a Baptist preacher in South Texas who taught him, he says, that Christianity is “a simple religion — not an easy religion, but a simple religion — because Jesus gave us two commandments: love God and love neighbour.” Talarico’s fight against the Texas bill requiring the Ten Commandments in public schools wasn’t about secularism. It was about protecting faith from the corrosion of political power.

“When the church gets too cozy with political power,” he told Colbert, “it loses its prophetic voice — its ability to speak truth to power, its ability to imagine a completely different world.”

Colbert, a lifelong Catholic, extended the argument with the sharpest formulation of the evening: “How must it diminish God to be associated with something so small as a present political party?” And then the punchline that functions as a theological argument: “If you managed to co-opt Christianity — if you convinced everybody that Jesus was on your side — if you then lose the election, sounds like you’ve got a pretty weak Jesus.”

This is the argument that Christian nationalism has no answer to. You can call a Democrat godless. You can’t call a seminarian godless. And when that seminarian is telling you that your version of Christianity is the one betraying Jesus, the standard Republican playbook — accuse, deflect, culture-war — doesn’t work. The attack surface isn’t there.

The Furries Tell the Story

Every movement needs a parable, and Talarico has one that’s almost too perfect.

In the Texas legislature, his Republican colleagues promoted an internet hoax: that public school students were identifying as cats and that schools were providing litter boxes for them. This was, of course, fabricated. It circulated as a viral meme, got repeated on Fox News, and eventually some Texas Republican decided to write a bill to ban the practice that wasn’t happening.

Talarico, sitting on the public education committee, asked the bill’s author a simple question: “Has there been a single documented case of a school providing a litter box to students?” The author couldn’t name one. The video went viral.

It’s a funny story. Talarico tells it well. But the point he draws from it is the strategic heart of his campaign: “They want us talking about furries and bathrooms so we don’t realize that they are picking our pockets, that they are closing our schools, they are gutting our health care, and they are raising taxes on all of us while they cut taxes for their billionaire donors.”

And then the line that collapses his faith argument and his economic argument into a single sentence: “The culture wars are a smokescreen because the real fight in this country is not left versus right — it’s top versus bottom.”

That formulation — top versus bottom, not left versus right — is the most dangerous thing a Democrat can say in Texas. Not because it’s radical. Because it isn’t. Because it sounds like common sense to the machinist in Fort Worth and the veteran in El Paso and the nurse in San Antonio who’ve been voting Republican for twenty years because they were told the other side hated God. Talarico isn’t telling them they were wrong about God. He’s telling them they were conned about politics.

The Risk

The gambit has vulnerabilities, and it’s worth being honest about them.

First, there’s the authenticity question. Voters can smell a pose. If Talarico’s faith argument starts to feel like a campaign tactic rather than a genuine conviction, it will backfire spectacularly. Inauthenticity is the one sin that evangelical voters — and Texas voters generally — don’t forgive. So far, the seminary credentials and the family history feel real. But a general election campaign is a stress test for authenticity that no primary can replicate.

Second, there’s the reach question. Talarico’s argument is most powerful for moderate evangelicals and mainline Protestants who are already uncomfortable with Christian nationalism but haven’t had permission to say so. It’s less effective with the hardcore religious right, who aren’t going to be persuaded by a Democratic seminarian no matter what he says about Matthew 25. The question is how large the persuadable middle actually is in Texas. Rehmet’s data suggests it’s bigger than anyone thought. But a statewide Senate race covers very different terrain than a single Fort Worth district.

Third, there’s the primary. Talarico has to beat Jasmine Crockett first, and the Democratic primary electorate is not the general electorate. Primary voters tend to be more partisan, more ideological, more interested in a fighter than a bridge-builder. Crockett’s national profile and progressive credentials may play better in a primary than Talarico’s faith-forward centrism. He could win the argument and lose the election.

The View From the 49th Parallel

I keep coming back to something Colbert said: “You don’t want to fit the big thing into the small thing.” He was talking about God and political parties, but the principle applies more broadly.

From Canada, watching the American experiment in democratic self-governance, you see a country that keeps trying to fit big things into small containers. A pluralistic, multi-faith, multi-ethnic nation squeezed into a two-party system. A constitutional democracy squeezed into partisan gerrymandering. A religious tradition built on love of neighbour squeezed into a political movement built on fear of the other.

The containers keep cracking. Talarico is one of the people standing at the cracks, suggesting that maybe the answer isn’t a bigger container but a different way of thinking about what goes inside it.

Whether Texas voters agree on March 3 — that’s the test. But the argument itself is already loose in the world, and arguments like that don’t go back in the box.
