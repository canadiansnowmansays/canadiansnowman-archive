The $37 Trillion Question

Episode 1: The Postwar Promise - How America Built Prosperity with Fiscal Discipline (1945-1980)

May 28, 2025

A weekly series examining how America broke its postwar promise of fiscal responsibility. Each week, we trace the bipartisan policy choices that created today's debt crisis and explore evidence-based solutions that could restore fiscal sanity. This analysis is updated regularly with the latest economic and political developments.

___________________________________________________________________________

In March 2025, the United States national debt reached $37 trillion—a staggering, unsustainable sum that threatens to cripple the world's superpower. To put this in perspective: every American citizen, from newborn to retiree, now carries a $110,000 share of this burden. Interest payments alone consume 18.4% of federal tax revenue according to the Peter G. Peterson Foundation, rising toward 22.2% by 2035—a fiscal straitjacket that imperils America's future.

As a Canadian observing from across the longest undefended border in the world, I've watched this crisis unfold with growing concern. We share not just geography but economic interdependence—when America's fiscal house shakes, we all feel the tremors.

A Crisis Unfolding in Real Time

As this analysis develops through 2025, the United States Congress continues providing real-time validation of America's fiscal dysfunction. On May 22, the House passed the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" by the narrowest possible margin—215 to 214—but the Senate has yet to act on what Republicans themselves call "the greatest debt ceiling increase ever."

The bill would add an estimated $3.8-4.6 trillion to the national debt over the next decade according to Congressional Budget Office analysis, while raising the debt ceiling by $4-5 trillion. However, the Senate faces significant Republican opposition from fiscal conservatives. Senator Rand Paul warns that "There's nothing fiscally conservative about expanding the debt ceiling more than we've ever done it before." Senator Ron Johnson has signaled strong opposition to the bill in its current form, declaring it "so far off the mark" that it threatens the republic's financial foundation.

This moment crystallizes the bipartisan fiscal madness this analysis will dissect: Republicans proposing one of the largest debt ceiling increases in American history to fund tax cuts for the wealthy, while Democrats rightfully criticize fiscal irresponsibility after having added their own trillions through spending programs inadequately funded by progressive taxation. Both parties continue the destructive dance—tax cuts versus spending increases—that created the $37 trillion crisis.

The fiscal year 2025 deficit is projected at $1.9 trillion by the Congressional Budget Office, demonstrating how structural imbalances persist even during periods of economic expansion, when responsible fiscal policy would dictate surplus accumulation for future crisis response.

The Historical Arc of Fiscal Decay

This didn't happen overnight. I've traced America's fiscal trajectory from World War II's end, when the United States emerged victorious with a manageable debt burden and a tax structure that funded both prosperity and global leadership. In 1945, the national debt stood at $258 billion—112% of GDP, but with 91% top tax rates driving 4% annual growth, the postwar framework worked.

What changed? Both major parties abandoned fiscal discipline, but through different yet equally destructive paths:

Democrats built essential infrastructure—Social Security, Medicare, interstate highways, the Apollo program—but too often without securing sustainable funding. Roosevelt's New Deal, Johnson's Great Society, Obama's financial crisis response, Biden's COVID recovery: all necessary investments that improved American lives, but financed through borrowing rather than progressive taxation.

Republicans cut taxes relentlessly, particularly on the wealthy, promising that growth would compensate for lost revenue. Reagan's revolution dropped the top rate from 70% to 28%, Bush's cuts flipped Clinton's surplus into deficits, Trump's Tax Cuts and Jobs Act added $1.9 trillion to debt during economic expansion. The promised growth never materialized at promised levels.

The Canadian Perspective

Why does this matter to a Canadian? Beyond our intertwined economies, I offer something increasingly rare in American political discourse: a genuinely non-partisan perspective. I have no electoral stake in defending either party's choices, no career incentives to shade analysis toward partisan preferences.

More importantly, Canada provides a working model of fiscal sustainability. With a 53% combined top tax rate (federal and provincial), we fund universal healthcare, strong social programs, and maintain a debt-to-GDP ratio of 85%—significantly better than America's 130%. We prove that higher taxes on the wealthy can coexist with prosperity and social cohesion.

The Postwar Foundation: When America Got It Right

The postwar era from 1945-1980 demonstrates that progressive taxation and fiscal responsibility can coexist with exceptional economic performance and global leadership. This wasn't an accident—it was the result of deliberate policy choices that balanced revenue generation with strategic spending.

The 91% Rate and 4% Growth

From 1945 through the early 1960s, America maintained top marginal tax rates of 91% on incomes over $200,000 (roughly $2 million in today's dollars). Far from stifling growth, this period produced 4% annual GDP growth—the fastest sustained economic expansion in American history.

The revenue generated funded transformative investments. The Interstate Highway System, launched in 1956, required $25 billion in federal investment (over $250 billion today) that created jobs while establishing infrastructure supporting decades of subsequent growth. The GI Bill educated millions of veterans, creating the skilled workforce that drove technological innovation. Space exploration employed 400,000 people directly while spinning off technologies that birthed entire industries.

These weren't government handouts—they were strategic investments generating returns far exceeding their costs while demonstrating American technological leadership globally. The top 1% of earners paid effective rates around 45%, contributing substantially to public investments that supported the economic growth from which they benefited.

Eisenhower's Balanced Approach

Dwight Eisenhower, a Republican president, maintained these tax rates while achieving balanced budgets in 1956 and 1957. His administration understood that progressive taxation represented fiscal responsibility rather than ideological warfare. The Interstate Highway System—America's largest public works project—was funded through gasoline taxes and general revenue, not borrowing.

Eisenhower warned against the "military-industrial complex" while maintaining strong defense through efficient spending rather than blank checks. His approach combined conservative fiscal discipline with progressive revenue generation—exactly the balance America needs today.

Shared Prosperity and Social Cohesion

Progressive taxation during this era produced broadly shared prosperity that strengthened social cohesion and democratic governance. The top 1%'s income share remained stable around 10%, while middle-class incomes rose consistently throughout the period. The Gini coefficient measured a modest 0.35, indicating relatively equal income distribution.

This shared growth created positive feedback loops where economic success generated political support for continued public investment. When everyone benefited from growth, politics focused on optimizing policy rather than redistributing existing wealth through zero-sum conflicts.

High tax rates didn't prevent wealth accumulation—they channeled it toward productive investment rather than financial speculation. The postwar era produced substantial fortunes while ensuring that wealth creation served broader economic development.

When the Framework Began to Crack

The 1960s saw the first signs of the fiscal irresponsibility that would later explode into crisis. John F. Kennedy's tax cut in 1962—dropping the top rate from 91% to 70%—cost $11 billion yearly but sparked 5.8% growth in 1964. However, this success obscured a dangerous precedent: using tax cuts rather than spending discipline to stimulate growth.

Lyndon Johnson's simultaneous pursuit of the Great Society and Vietnam War—"guns and butter"—pushed spending to 20% of GDP while revenue lagged at 17.6%. This $25 billion deficit by 1968 represented the first major breakdown of postwar fiscal discipline, presaging the much larger imbalances to come.

The Nixon Shock and Dollar Devaluation

Richard Nixon's 1971 decision to end dollar convertibility to gold marked another turning point. While necessary given mounting pressures, it removed the external constraint that had disciplined American fiscal policy since Bretton Woods. Suddenly, America could finance deficits through dollar creation rather than gold backing, removing market discipline on fiscal excess.

This change, combined with Vietnam War costs and Great Society spending, established patterns that would persist: using monetary policy to finance fiscal imbalances rather than maintaining the revenue-spending balance that had characterized the postwar golden age.

The Path Forward

This crisis isn't inevitable—it's the predictable result of policy choices that prioritized short-term political gains over long-term fiscal stability. The solution lies in proven postwar policies: progressive taxation that asks the wealthy to contribute their fair share, strategic spending on infrastructure and human capital, and abandoning destructive schemes like tariff wars that add costs without addressing underlying structural deficits.

The data is clear, the path is obvious, yet the political will remains absent. Perhaps it takes an outside perspective to see what partisan blinders have obscured: America can choose fiscal sanity over political theater, but only if both parties acknowledge their contributions to this crisis and commit to evidence-based solutions.

A Personal Note

At 73, having lived through most of the economic history I'll analyze, I bring the perspective of someone who witnessed both America's postwar greatness and its gradual fiscal decay. This isn't academic theory—it's lived experience of watching two great nations (America and Canada) choose different paths and seeing the results.

My hope is that this analysis contributes to the serious conversation America desperately needs about its fiscal future. The $37 trillion debt isn't just a number—it's a mortgage on future generations that both parties helped create and both must help resolve.

The question isn't whether America can afford to fix this crisis. The question is whether it can afford not to.
