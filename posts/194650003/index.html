The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

Chapter and Verse

The President Reads About Humility Without a Trace of Irony

April 19, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

There is a particular species of performance that only religion can produce — the kind where a man who has never apologized for anything in his life sits in the Oval Office and reads aloud a passage about humbling oneself before God.

On Tuesday, April 15, 2026, President Donald Trump recorded himself reading from II Chronicles 7 for a weeklong marathon Bible reading in Washington called “America Reads the Bible.” The event, organized by Bunni Pounds — a former political consultant turned Christian activist who lost a 2018 congressional race in Texas — will feature nearly 500 participants reading the entire Bible from Genesis to Revelation at the Museum of the Bible, twelve hours a day, for seven consecutive days.

The passage Trump chose — or more likely, the passage chosen for him — contains this central verse:

“If My people, which are called by my name, shall humble themselves, and pray, and seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways; then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and will heal their land.”

Let that sit for a moment.

Humble themselves.

Turn from their wicked ways.

This was read by a man who, in the span of a single week, posted an AI-generated image of himself as Jesus Christ, attacked the pope as “weak on crime,” told reporters that God supports his war in Iran, and then — when the blasphemy backlash arrived from his own supporters — claimed the image showed him as a doctor.

A doctor. With light radiating from his hands. Surrounded by praying acolytes, fighter jets, and bald eagles.

The Week in Review

The timeline deserves to be laid out plainly, because it has the quality of satire written by someone who gave up on subtlety.

Saturday, April 11: Pope Leo XIV, the first American pope, holds a prayer vigil for peace. Without naming Trump, he denounces the “delusion of omnipotence” in the Iran war and declares that “no one can use God to justify war.”

Sunday, April 12 — Orthodox Easter: Trump posts a lengthy diatribe on Truth Social calling Pope Leo “WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy.” He claims the pope owes his papacy to Trump’s presidency. He tells reporters at Joint Base Andrews that he is “not a big fan of Pope Leo.” That same evening, he posts the AI-generated image of himself as Christ.

Monday, April 13: Backlash arrives from his own base. Riley Gaines: “A little humility would serve him well.” Megan Basham of The Daily Wire: “OUTRAGEOUS blasphemy.” The Knights Templar International demand a public apology. Even Marjorie Taylor Greene — the woman who once compared Trump to Jesus without irony — calls it blasphemous. Trump deletes the image. Claims he thought it showed him as a doctor.

Tuesday, April 14: Trump records his Bible reading from the Oval Office. The passage is about humility and repentance. That same evening, he posts another attack on Pope Leo, telling someone to “please tell Pope Leo” about Iran’s crimes — as though the leader of 1.4 billion Catholics simply hadn’t heard.

Wednesday, April 15: Trump shares yet another social media post featuring an image of himself being embraced by Jesus.

Five days. One war with the pope. One deleted act of blasphemy. One claim of divine endorsement for military action. And a Bible reading about turning from wicked ways.

The Passage and Its History

Biblical scholars — the people who actually study these texts for a living rather than weaponize them for political gain — note that II Chronicles 7 concerns a specific covenant between God and the ancient Israelites. It is not a general-purpose promise available to any nation that wraps itself in enough flags. The books of Chronicles cover centuries of Jewish history, including the reigns of David and Solomon.

But accuracy has never been the point. As Matthew D. Taylor, a visiting scholar at Georgetown’s Center on Faith and Justice, observed, this passage has been “a hallmark of the religious right” for decades. It has been extracted from its context, set to music, printed on bumper stickers, and deployed as a political instrument — the suggestion being that America is the new Israel, and that God has cut a special deal with the United States that can be activated by sufficient prayer and Republican voter turnout.

The passage appeared most memorably on January 6, 2021, when the founder of Cowboys for Trump prayed it through a bullhorn over a crowd that was simultaneously chanting “Fight for Trump!” before storming the United States Capitol.

Same passage. Same crowd. Different venue.

Now it gets the presidential seal, pre-recorded from the Oval Office, slotted into a Tuesday evening broadcast between Ben Carson and a home-school advocate named Heidi St. John. The passage has completed its journey from scripture to political prop to insurrection soundtrack to state-sanctioned spectacle. It is the same words. It has never meant less.

The Cast List

The reader list for America Reads the Bible is, as the New York Times noted, “a who’s who of Mr. Trump’s most loyal Christian supporters.” It reads less like a devotional gathering than a cabinet meeting with better lighting.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio will read. So will Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth — the same Pete Hegseth who has used scripture to justify the Iran war from the Pentagon podium. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy will read. Former Secretary of State Mike Pompeo will read. Secretary of Agriculture Brook Rollins will read. Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee will read from Genesis 12, which contains the passage Christian Zionists cite to justify unconditional support for Israel: “I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse.”

Franklin Graham will read the parable of the Good Samaritan. Whether he will then vote to fund the programs that actually help the people the parable is about remains an open question.

Governor Greg Abbott of Texas will read Isaiah 43, selected for its resonance with last summer’s deadly flooding in his state. This is the same Greg Abbott who has spent years cutting the social safety net that flood victims depend on, but never mind — the verse is about water, and the optics work.

And Susie Wiles, the White House chief of staff, will read Proverbs 31 — the one about “a wife of noble character.” She will do so in the service of an administration led by a man with three marriages, a hush-money conviction involving a pornographic film actress, and the social media habits of someone who has never read the passage in question.

Organizers say they invited dozens of Democratic members of Congress. None responded. This is being framed as Democratic godlessness rather than what it actually is: a reasonable refusal to participate in the partisan weaponization of holy text.

No Jews were invited to read. Bunni Pounds told the Colorado Springs Gazette she wanted to keep the event Christian. The irony of Christians reading a book written almost entirely by Jews while excluding Jews from the reading apparently requires a level of self-awareness the organizers do not possess.

And five members of the House of Representatives participating in the event are members of the Sharia-Free America Caucus — a group that claims American Muslims want to impose Sharia law on the nation. They will read from a book that contains Levitical codes prescribing death for adultery, blasphemy, and working on the Sabbath, and they will do so without any apparent awareness of what they are holding in their hands.

The cover commemorates 'The Day God Intervened' at his Butler, Pennsylvania rally, a claim that apparently did not extend to divine intervention on behalf of the rallygoer who died that day. But the deepest hypocrisy is not the cast list, or the passage selection, or even the deleted AI portrait. It is this: Trump has spent the past six weeks invoking God’s name to justify a war that the leader of the world’s largest Christian denomination has explicitly condemned.

When asked whether God approves of the American military campaign in Iran, Trump replied: “I do — because God is good and God wants to see people taken care of.”

Pete Hegseth has framed the war effort as divinely supported from the Pentagon, using scriptural justification for military operations. The president threatened to destroy “a whole civilization” in Iran — language Pope Leo called “truly unacceptable.”

And the pope’s response? “Jesus is the king of peace, who rejects war, whom no one can use to justify war.”

Pope Leo — the first American pope, a man from Chicago, the kind of man Trump would normally try to claim as one of his own — has become perhaps the most visible Christian critic of the administration’s marriage of faith and firepower. His response to Trump’s “weak on crime” diatribe was characteristically measured: “I have no fear of the Trump administration, or speaking out loudly of the message of the gospel.”

The Beatitudes — the core ethical teachings of the New Testament — say “Blessed are the peacemakers.” They do not say “Blessed are those who threaten to wipe out civilizations and then read the Bible on camera.”

The $2.5 Million Prayer Meeting

America Reads the Bible cost $2.5 million to produce. It will be livestreamed on Great American Pure Flix. Participants will read from the King James Version Easy Read Bible, which updates the 1611 language for a contemporary audience — because the same people who insist the Constitution must be read in its original eighteenth-century context apparently prefer their scripture with modern editing.

The White House statement praised the event as an occasion to “rededicate the United States as one Nation under God.” The event’s own website compares it to the biblical story of Ezra reading the law to the returning Israelites — a people rebuilding their nation after exile.

The comparison is instructive, though not in the way the organizers intend. Ezra read the law to people who had lost everything. He read to the broken, the displaced, the humbled. He did not read to cabinet secretaries and Instagram influencers in a museum gift shop.

What the Passage Actually Says

Return to the verse Trump read:

“If My people, which are called by my name, shall humble themselves, and pray, and seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways...”

Every clause is a rebuke of the man reading it.

Humble themselves. This is a man who has never conceded an error, never offered a genuine apology, and who — when told his AI portrait of himself as Jesus was blasphemous — claimed it was a picture of a doctor.

Pray. This is a man who has struggled publicly to name a single Bible verse, who when asked for his favorite passage said “I don’t want to get into it,” and who has called the Bible his favorite book while demonstrating no familiarity with its contents.

Seek my face. This is a man who sells branded Bibles for $60 — $60 for a text that every church in America gives away for free — with his name embossed on the cover as though he were a co-author.

Turn from their wicked ways. This is a man who, in the week preceding his Bible reading, compared himself to Christ, attacked the pope, invoked God to justify a war, and then sat in the Oval Office and read about repentance as though it applied to someone else.

It always applies to someone else.

The View from Canada

I am not a theologian. I am a retired Canadian journalist watching from Toronto as the most powerful nation on earth stages a $2.5 million Bible reading featuring a president who cannot name a favorite Testament, cabinet members who use scripture to justify bombing campaigns, and a guest list that excludes the Jewish authors of most of the book being read.

I have no quarrel with faith. I have no quarrel with the Bible. I have a quarrel with people who hold it up for cameras while violating every principle it contains.

The passage from II Chronicles ends with a promise: “then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and will heal their land.”

The condition is humility. The condition is repentance. The condition is turning from wicked ways.

The condition has not been met. It has not been attempted. It has been read aloud, on camera, from the Oval Office, by a man who thinks the whole thing is about him.

It always is.
