The Iran Crisis — A Note to My Readers

On Walter Cronkite, War Fatigue, and Why Less Will Be More

March 27, 2026

Walter Cronkite at the CBS News anchor desk, circa mid-1960s — the world map behind him, the whole planet his responsibility. "To the movement," civil rights leader Julian Bond recalled, "Cronkite was the voice of God on TV." He was, for a generation of North Americans, the man who told them what the world meant — every night, without fail, for nearly two decades. Credit: CBS Photo Archive. Source: "The Voice of God on TV: Walter Cronkite's 1960s," Newsweek, March 19, 2012.

I want to talk to you about something that has nothing to do with the Strait of Hormuz, the 10-year Treasury yield, or the TACO sequence — though all three will return on Monday.

I want to talk to you about Walter Cronkite.

I was thirteen years old when John F. Kennedy was assassinated. I was a teenager and then a young adult through the entirety of the Vietnam War. Every evening, Walter Cronkite sat down in front of America and delivered the news — the body counts, the bombing runs, the diplomatic failures, the protests, the escalations. He did it with authority and precision and a kind of steady gravity that made it feel like bearing witness was a civic obligation.

It was not pleasant. It was, in fact, exhausting in a way that took years to fully understand. The nightly accumulation of bad news — without resolution, without a visible endpoint, without any clear answer to the question of when it would stop — wore something down in the people who watched it. Not their interest. Not their concern. Something quieter than that. Their capacity to absorb one more day of the same terrible story.

During the Vietnam War, demonstrations became part of DC’s landscape—as did tear gas. Photograph by ©Leonard Freed/Magnum Photos.

Vietnam ended. The news cycles moved on. But the wars did not stop — and neither did the watching.

I was on a beach in Barbados the morning George H.W. Bush launched his air assault on Baghdad. I can still picture it: the pale early light, the water, the incongruous beauty of the setting, and the radio announcing that the bombs had started falling on Iraq. I was comfortable. I was safe. I was on vacation. And somewhere beneath the cognitive dissonance of lying on a beach while a city burned, I registered for the first time what it actually means to watch a war from a distance — the particular moral unease of being a spectator to something you cannot stop, consuming it as information while others consume it as fire and rubble and the sound of aircraft they cannot see.

A Barbados beach at dawn — the setting where the news of the Gulf War's opening air assault arrived by radio in January 1991. The incongruous beauty of the place made the moral dissonance sharper, not softer. Credit: Getty Images/iStockphoto. Source: Getty Images.

The Gulf War felt surgical and swift by comparison to Vietnam — a hundred hours of ground combat, a ceasefire, a ticker-tape parade. It felt, briefly, like America had learned something. Like the long national nightmare of unwinnable wars was over and a new era of clean, decisive military power had arrived. From a beach in Barbados, it was easy to believe that. The Iraqis who lived through the bombing had a different morning than I did. They did not get to change the channel.

Then came Kosovo. Then Afghanistan. Then Iraq again — this time without an exit, without a plan, without an honest accounting of what victory would even look like. I watched the shock and awe footage in 2003 with the same Barbados feeling — the terrible beauty of precision munitions lighting up a city skyline — and this time I knew better than to believe in the clean and decisive ending. The embedded reporters came home. The war did not. Then Libya. Then Syria. Then the long, grinding withdrawal from Afghanistan that produced twenty years of Walter Cronkite mornings and ended with the images from Kabul airport that required no commentary.

And now Iran. Thirty-two days in. The same structure: the confident opening, the unexpected complications, the escalating costs, the diplomatic improvisation, the congressional frustration, the market pressure, the question nobody wants to answer out loud — what does winning actually look like, and how do we know when we have done it?

I have been thinking about Cronkite a great deal this week. And about Barbados. And about all the mornings in between.

I began this series on January 12, 2026 — six weeks before the first bombs fell on Tehran. The opening essay was about Iran’s currency crash of December 28, 2025 — the rial collapsing to 1.4 million per dollar, the bazaars going dark, the middle class watching decades of savings evaporate in a week. I called it “The Currency Crash That Broke Iran.” I did not know then that by Part 8 I would be writing about Trump’s military options against the same country. I did not know that by Part 59 I would be writing about American paratroopers deploying to the Persian Gulf and a naval commander killed in a Bandar Abbas apartment.

I have now published across this series for seventy-five days. The war itself has run for thirty-two of them. And every morning of those thirty-two days, I have sat down and written another dispatch — another set of facts that are worse than the day before, another deadline extended, another diplomatic framework collapsed, another number added to the casualty count.

I lost subscribers this week. One never knows exactly why — people’s lives change, their reading habits shift, their inboxes fill up with things competing for their attention. But I have a theory, and I think you deserve to hear it.

I think I have been Walter Cronkite-ing you.

Not in the sense of authority or gravity — I am a retired Canadian businessman with a sardonic streak and a husky named Dior, not the most trusted man in America. But in the sense of the daily accumulation. The relentless morning delivery of the same terrible story, advancing incrementally, offering no resolution, demanding that you open the email and absorb one more day of it.

You chose to subscribe to this series. Unlike the Americans who watched Cronkite because it was the only news available, you are making an active decision every morning when you open my email. And I have been making that decision harder, not easier, by treating your inbox as a daily wire service rather than a considered analytical journal.

There is something else I have been sitting with this week — something the Barbados morning first planted in me and that thirty-two days of daily war dispatches have brought back with unexpected force. There is a moral dimension to the daily consumption of war as content. When I write about missile strikes and body counts and oil prices every morning, and you read about them over coffee, we are both doing something that the people on the receiving end of those missiles are not doing. We are processing this as information. They are processing it as their lives. The daily cadence of the war diary — however carefully reported, however analytically rigorous — risks normalizing something that should not be normal. It risks making the extraordinary routine. Cronkite understood this, eventually. It is why, on February 27, 1968, he broke his anchor’s neutrality and told America directly that the Vietnam War was a stalemate. He had absorbed enough daily accumulation to know that the daily accumulation was the story.

I am not Cronkite. But I understand, fifty-nine essays in, what he understood in 1968. The daily format was serving the news cycle. It was not serving you.

Starting next week, The Iran Crisis will publish twice weekly.

Monday mornings — a synthesis of the weekend’s developments, written with the benefit of two days’ perspective rather than under the pressure of a breaking news deadline. This will be the analytical essay — the one that draws meaning from the accumulating facts rather than simply recording them.

Friday mornings — a week-in-review, covering the Monday through Thursday arc, ending with the forward-looking question that the coming weekend will begin to answer.

The Sunday Satire continues on its own schedule — weekly, or as emergency editions when the situation demands it, as last nights “Take the Exit, Donald” demonstrated. Some weeks demand satire before Sunday arrives. I make no apologies for that.

Everything else continues. The TACO framework. The numbers blocks. The attribution to the journalists doing the actual work in Tehran and Kuwait and Khasab and Washington. The Senator Kennedy of Louisiana closing line, which will remain until he is no longer the only Republican to have voted against the war.

What changes is the pace. Two considered essays per week instead of seven exhausting ones.

I believe this will make the series better, not just more sustainable. The essays I am most proud of from the past thirty-two days — the TACO framework pieces, The Audit, the Lax Americana section, the Khasab dispatch, the satire — all share one quality: they had room to breathe. They were not written under the pressure of yesterday’s news becoming stale before I could finish the paragraph. They synthesized rather than recorded. They argued rather than tallied.

That is the series I want to write. That is, I suspect, the series you subscribed to read.

The war will still be there on Monday. The Strait of Hormuz will still be closed, or open, or somewhere in between. The April 6 deadline will have either produced a deal or produced another extension or produced something worse. The markets will have moved. The congressional Republicans will have said something in private that they will not say in public. The tanker captains will still have nothing but time.

I will be here Monday morning with all of it — synthesized, analyzed, and delivered without the accumulated weight of six days of daily recaps pressing down on every sentence.

Walter Cronkite signed off every broadcast with “And that’s the way it is.” He was right, of course. That was the way it was. But he also signed off every night — and came back the next morning, and the night after that, for years, until the weight of it became its own story.

I would rather come back twice a week with something worth reading than every morning with something you dread opening.

The Iraqis on that Baghdad morning in 1991 did not get to change the channel. The least I can do — for you, and in some small way for them — is make sure that when you choose to open this one, it is worth the choice.

Thank you for being here. See you Monday.

— Stephen CanadianSnowmanSays Toronto, March 28, 2026
