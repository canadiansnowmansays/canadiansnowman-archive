The Iran Crisis

The Exit Ramp

Part LXI: Days Thirty-Three Through Thirty-Six: The Speech That Wasn’t, the War That Is

April 3, 2026

President Trump addresses the nation on the Iran war, April 1, 2026 — the eighteenth minute of an eighteen-minute address in which he described the conflict as a “little journey to Iran,” declared regime change had occurred while denying it was ever the goal, and claimed Iran’s nuclear material was both irrelevant and under intense surveillance. Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic within the hour: “The president seems lost.” Credit: Alex Brandon/AP.

Friday, April 3, 2026

Three sentences. They were spoken on the same day, hours apart, by the same man.

To Reuters, at approximately noon on Wednesday, April 1: “That is so far underground, I don’t care about that.” He was referring to the 970 pounds of near-bomb-grade enriched uranium — enough to build ten to twelve nuclear weapons — that remains in Iran. The uranium whose existence was the stated primary justification for a war that has now killed 13 Americans, wounded nearly 300, and pushed gas above $4 a gallon.

To the nation, in prime time, at 9 p.m.: “Regime change was not our goal. We never said regime change.”

And then, four sentences later, in the same speech: “The new group is less radical and much more reasonable.”

This is the Iran war on April 1, 2026. The exit ramp is visible. The president is looking for it. The question — the only question that matters this week — is whether the ramp he is building leads anywhere, or whether it leads off the edge of something.

I. The Speech

The address lasted eighteen minutes. It was delivered from the White House. It was described by the administration as “an important update on Iran.”

David Sanger, writing in the Times, who has covered Iran’s nuclear program for twenty-five years, captured the central contradiction with precision: the entire argument for withdrawing from the JCPOA in 2018 was that leaving Iran with enrichment capacity was intolerable even if Iran was in compliance. Today’s stockpile is far larger than the one Trump called intolerable in 2018. At noon on April 1 he told Reuters it was underground and he didn’t care about it. At 9 p.m. he told the nation he had it under “intense satellite surveillance” and would strike if Iran made a move for it. Both statements cannot be true simultaneously. The nuclear material that justified the war has not been destroyed, removed, or accounted for.

The speech’s claim that Iran’s “ability to launch missiles and drones is dramatically curtailed” arrived on the same evening Iran launched what its own state-aligned media called “one of their biggest attacks yet” on Israel — fourteen wounded including six children, extensive damage in several cities. CENTCOM’s cited 90 percent reduction is a launch rate reduction, not a capability elimination. Fewer missiles launched; more of them landing. The Stimson Center’s Kelly Grieco has documented that Iran’s hit rate has more than doubled since March 10.

“Regime change was not our goal. We never said regime change.” On February 28 — Day One — Trump addressed the Iranian people in his opening broadcast: “When we are finished, take over your government. It will be yours to take. This will be probably your only chance for generations.” On Sunday March 29, aboard Air Force One: “We have had regime change.” On Monday. On Tuesday. On Wednesday night: never said it.

Edward Wong’s assessment in the Times of the five war aims stated February 28 is the speech’s plainest verdict: missiles not obliterated, navy partially destroyed, proxies still active, nuclear material still in Iran, no popular revolt, no regime change by any recognizable definition. Four of five unmet. “Nearing completion” does not describe four of five objectives unmet after thirty-three days.

One phrase will outlast the speech in the record. Trump called the Iran war “a little journey to Iran.” Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic within hours of the address: “His address did not come across as a wartime speech but instead was a disjointed series of complaints, brags, and exaggerations — along with a few outright lies — delivered by a man who looked and sounded tired.” Nichols noted the repetitions — “like nobody’s ever seen,” “decimated,” “never before” — cycling through the address like a man who had stopped believing the words. “The president seems lost.”

A little journey. Thirteen names from Dover.

II. The Ahead of Schedule

Carlos Lozada, writing in the Times, has documented what “ahead of schedule” means in Trump’s vocabulary — deployed more than 150 times across his presidency for everything from the White House ballroom renovation to the Dow Jones Industrial Average. He traced it to the Wollman ice rink in the 1980s. The one time it was genuinely earned was Operation Warp Speed, when the COVID vaccine arrived years ahead of standard development timelines. In Iran, Lozada writes, “the schedule is our most malleable illusion; in a war with shifting goals, any schedule is as good as any other.”

Trump has said the war would last four to five weeks, then said he felt it in his bones, then two to three weeks, then very shortly. Hegseth explained Tuesday that the administration “would never reveal precisely” what the timeline is. “He’s said four to six weeks, six to eight weeks, three — it could be any particular number.” A schedule that could be any particular number is not a schedule. It is a rhetorical device.

In Iran, Lozada writes, the administration’s approach resembles the technique Trump described in The Art of the Deal — instructing a crew to drive machinery back and forth on a construction site so that the visiting board of directors would think the job was well along. Any schedule, as the site was bare, was as good as any other.

III. The Fateful Choice

Paratroopers assigned to the 82nd Airborne Division walk to a C-17 before an airborne operation at Fort Bragg, North Carolina, March 13, 2025. (Pfc. Nicole Miller/U.S. Army)

While the speech was being written, the Pentagon was planning two potential ground operations. Nancy Youssef and Jonathan Lemire, writing in The Atlantic, report that military officials are planning for a possible assault on Kharg Island — Iran’s main oil hub, handling roughly 90 percent of its oil exports — and a separate Special Operations mission to seize the highly enriched uranium believed stored in tunnels beneath rubble at Isfahan and Natanz. Both require Trump’s authorization. Neither has been given.

The Kharg Island operation involves mined waters, drone and missile defense, and a prolonged US presence on Iranian territory. Brent crude, currently around $100, could surge to $150 — risking a global recession. The operation would produce the exact economic pain it is designed to relieve, at larger scale, before any benefit could materialize. “It would also mean we had to be there for a while,” Trump acknowledged Sunday.

The uranium seizure requires Special Operations forces inserted by air into mainland Iran, moving to storage sites whose entrances were damaged in last June’s bombing, against forces expected to respond quickly. It could allow the administration to claim the nuclear objective met. It would not reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

Neither option would guarantee the two-to-three-week exit the speech promised.

David Frum, speaking with Graeme Wood on The Atlantic‘s podcast, identified the structural problem: Trump “thinks of wars as exercises in destruction; he doesn’t accept that they’re exercises in politics.” Every time Trump has tried to jawbone oil markets — the Sunday night statements designed to hold down Monday morning Asian prices — he has been teaching Iran that economic pain is his pressure point. The jawboning that worked in early March stopped working by late March. “Iran can read Western media as well as any of us can,” Frum said, “and they can see the panic and terror of economic dislocation being broadcast by the Trump administration to the world.”

IV. The Diplomat Who Was Struck

On Wednesday, Kamal Kharazi — former Iranian foreign minister, head of Iran’s Foreign Policy Council, the official overseeing engagement with Pakistan for a possible meeting between Vice President Vance and Iranian officials — was injured in an attack on a residential building in Tehran. Two Iranian officials told the Times that the targeting is being viewed inside Iran as a deliberate attempt to derail diplomacy.

Iran’s President Pezeshkian released an open letter to the American people on Wednesday: “Continuing along the path of confrontation is more costly and futile than ever before.” Iranian hard-liners immediately attacked him for suggesting openness to negotiations. And then the diplomat managing the back channel was struck in his apartment building.

Trump posted Wednesday morning that Iran’s “New Regime President” had asked for a ceasefire. Iran’s Foreign Ministry responded within hours: “false and baseless.” Trump appeared unsure who he was referring to — Pezeshkian has been Iran’s president for more than a year.

The exit ramp requires Iran to walk down it. Iran cannot walk down an exit ramp whose architect has just been struck in a residential building. This is Wednesday’s operational contradiction in its starkest form: bombing the diplomat overseeing negotiations while claiming negotiations are progressing. Claiming Iran asked for a cease-fire while Iran denies it. Saying the war is almost over while sending more troops, doubling the A-10 fleet, and planning two ground assaults that haven’t been authorized yet.

V. Ahead of Schedule — Energy Edition

Jonathan Mingle, writing in the Times, has provided the most precise assessment yet of what comes next: it will be worse than what has already come. The final oil and gas deliveries that passed through Hormuz before the closure are arriving in Asia this week. The last shipments reach Europe by mid-April. After that, reserves dwindle. Oil could reach $200 a barrel if the war drags on. The IEA’s Fatih Birol has called this “the greatest global energy security threat in history” — worse than the 1970s oil crisis, worse than COVID, worse than Ukraine.

The speech attributed the gas price surge “entirely” to Iranian attacks on commercial tankers. This is partially true and substantially incomplete. Hormuz’s closure began as retaliation for the American and Israeli bombing campaign Trump ordered. The cause and the effect are not separable in the way the speech suggested.

The irony Mingle identifies is precise: the most fossil-fuel-friendly administration in recent American history has demonstrated more vividly than any climate advocate could manage that true energy security requires moving away from oil. The countries that made that transition — Pakistan’s solar boom now generates 30 percent of its electricity, up from 3 percent in 2020 — are the countries best insulated from the crisis this war created. The administration that gutted clean energy incentives has handed the world its most powerful argument for clean energy.

VI. The Hotels

Thomas Gibbons-Neff, writing in the Times, documented a story that deserves more attention than it has received: American troops relocated from bases under Iranian attack have been housed in hotels and office spaces in civilian areas across the Gulf. Human rights officials and international law experts say this may violate the laws of war.

The Pentagon’s own law of war manual states that military commanders must take reasonable steps to separate the civilian population from military objectives. Hotels in densely populated city centers are not separation. Iran has urged Gulf region civilians to report on US military troop locations off-base. Iranian strikes have since killed civilians in the UAE, Bahrain, and Kuwait near areas where American troops were relocated.

Brian Castner of Amnesty International: “It is unconscionable that U.S. forces would knowingly put civilians at risk by leaving their bases and moving to hotels in the densely populated city centers. The commanders who ordered these relocations should be investigated for violating U.S. laws of war.”

The bases lacked adequate missile defenses because the war was started without adequate preparation for a war. The troops are in hotels because of that lack of preparation. The civilians near those hotels are now targets because of where the troops are. The speech described this military operation as one of “masterful” and “brilliant” precision.

VII. Passover and the Nobel Laureate

Buying food before Passover in Bnei Brak, Israel, on Tuesday. Israelis have been wishing each other a “quiet” or “tranquil” holiday as air-raid sirens sound. Amit Elkayam for NYT

Two stories from Wednesday belong in the permanent record.

In Israel, Israelis prepared for Passover Seder under ballistic missile fire. At least 14 were wounded Wednesday morning, six of them children — including an 11-year-old girl critically injured. Synagogue services were limited to a few dozen congregants because of large-gathering bans. A hotel at the Dead Sea advertised itself as “a protected space for a quiet and pleasant stay.” Netanyahu the evening before compared the war to the ten plagues of Egypt. Isabel Kershner, writing in the Times, captured the particular weight of a Passover — the festival of freedom — spent in bomb shelters, in a country that has sent its president to negotiate while its cities absorb Iran’s largest salvos yet.

In Iran, Narges Mohammadi — Nobel Peace Prize laureate, Iran’s most prominent human rights activist — suffered a heart attack in prison on March 24. She collapsed and lost consciousness for more than an hour. Fellow inmates wrapped her in a blanket because her body had turned cold. Iranian authorities have refused to allow her to be taken to a hospital. Her life is in “imminent danger” according to her legal team. Her husband Taghi Rahmani lives in exile in Paris. Their teenage children have not seen her in years.

“We swear by the blood of our comrades. We are standing until the end.”

She is standing until the end in a prison cell, denied emergency medical care, during a war whose stated purpose includes the freedom of the Iranian people.

She was not mentioned in the eighteen-minute speech.

VIII. The Disinformation Reversal

Steven Lee Myers and Edward Wong, writing in the Times, documented a quiet reversal: the Trump administration — which dismantled the government’s foreign disinformation apparatus upon taking office in January 2025, shutting down the FBI’s disinformation teams, the ODNI’s tracking units, and the State Department’s Global Engagement Center — is now scrambling to rebuild them.

The State Department ordered every American embassy and consulate this week to push back against foreign influence campaigns. Voice of America and Radio Free Europe broadcasts are being partially restored after being shut down last year.

“The State Department is now admitting it might be a problem if foreign adversaries lie about us,” said Nina Jankowicz, who became a target of the campaign that dismantled these units.

Iran’s propaganda operation is the direct beneficiary of an administration that tore down its own defenses. Having created the vulnerability, the administration is now alarmed by it. Graeme Wood, in his conversation with Frum: “There’s a horrible vulnerability that could have been averted if there was just some faith and rapport developed between the administration and the American people, the administration and the press. And as we know, that rapport has been completely destroyed, and in the information war, that’s a big problem.”

IX. The Exit Ramp

Last Friday, March 27, this series published a satirical essay called “Take the Exit, Donald.” It built the off-ramp. It watched him drive past it. It noted that “the tanker captains in particular have had nothing but time.”

By Sunday he was announcing 20 Iranian ships as a “sign of respect.” By Tuesday markets were bouncing on exit signals. By Wednesday he was giving a prime-time address claiming core objectives were “nearing completion” — four of five unmet — while simultaneously ordering A-10 deployments, planning two ground assaults, and threatening to bomb every electric power plant in Iran if no deal materialized.

The exit ramp is still there. Trump is looking for it. Iran — which believes it is in a strong position, which is not willing to negotiate, which has just struck the diplomat overseeing the back channel — is not required to let him use it.

The April 6 deadline for the power plants arrives in two days.

X. The Morning After — And Good Friday

Good Friday US Air Force F-15 taking off from RAF Lakenheath

The speech intended to calm markets and signal an exit. Instead, oil surged 7 percent to $108 a barrel in early Thursday trading — the steepest single-day rise in three weeks. South Korea’s stock index fell 4.5 percent. Japan’s Nikkei shed 2.4 percent. Oxford Economics now forecasts oil averaging $113 a barrel in the second quarter, assuming Hormuz remains effectively closed through April. The jawboning that had briefly worked in early March stopped working entirely. The speech moved every market in precisely the wrong direction.

Iran’s Revolutionary Guards issued their own response Thursday morning. American and Israeli strikes had not decimated their missile production centers, long-range drones, air defenses, or electronic warfare systems. “The United States and Israel know nothing about our vast and strategic capabilities.” This was the morning after an eighteen-minute address declaring Iran had been “beaten and completely decimated.”

On Thursday, the United States struck the B1 highway bridge near Tehran — part of an ambitious project connecting the capital to the Caspian Sea. A US military official said it was a planned military supply route for Iran’s missile forces. The deputy governor of Alborz Province told Iranian state media that the bridge was not yet operational, that there was no military activity on it, and that the casualties — eight killed, ninety-five wounded — were villagers who had been picnicking nearby to celebrate Sizdah Bedar, the last day of the Persian new year. Trump celebrated the strike on Truth Social. “Bridges next, then Electric Power Plants!” he wrote. “Much more to follow.”

Also on Thursday, Pete Hegseth fired General Randy George, the Army’s chief of staff, effective immediately — the biggest wartime military shake-up in decades. Missy Ryan, Nancy Youssef, and Sarah Fitzpatrick, writing in The Atlantic, reported that the White House is also discussing the departure of Army Secretary Dan Driscoll. At least 50,000 troops are deployed to the Gulf region. Ground forces including from the Army could play a significant part in any Kharg Island or Isfahan operation. Hegseth has now removed the chairman of the Joint Chiefs, the chief of naval operations, and the Army chief of staff since taking office — and the war is in its fifth week.

At the United Nations Security Council, Russia, China, and France on Thursday effectively stymied a push by Arab countries to authorize military force to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. The resolution, drafted by Bahrain, remains blocked. The vote expected Friday faces the same veto arithmetic it faced Thursday.

On the toll booth: Iran’s foreign minister assured the Philippines that Philippine-flagged vessels would receive safe passage through the strait. The Philippines, which declared a national energy emergency, is the first country to receive this guarantee by name. Iran’s deputy foreign minister said Iran and Oman were drafting a protocol for joint “oversight” of Hormuz transit — even after the war. The Strait of Hormuz is not opening naturally. It is opening by nationality, by negotiation, on Iran’s terms.

Then came Good Friday morning.

An American fighter jet was shot down over Iran. US and Israeli officials confirmed it. Iranian state-affiliated media confirmed it. The fate of the crew was unclear as American officials scrambled to mount a search and rescue operation before Iranian forces could reach any survivors. An Iranian state broadcaster anchor called on local residents to capture “the enemy’s pilot or pilots” and turn them over alive to security forces in exchange for a reward. Two outlets affiliated with Iran’s Revolutionary Guards said helicopters — both Iranian and American — were searching. An American C-130 Hercules was also reported involved in the rescue attempt.

US markets were closed for Good Friday. The rest of the world was watching.

Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic after Wednesday’s address, had concluded: “The president seems lost.” On Good Friday morning, with a US pilot missing over Iran, with eight Nowruz picnickers dead on a bridge, with the Army’s chief of staff fired in the middle of a war, with oil at $108 and rising — the exit ramp that the speech promised was nowhere visible.

Suzanne Maloney, the Brookings Institution’s Iran director and one of Washington’s leading Iran scholars, spoke with Ezra Klein this week. Her verdict, delivered before the jet went down, was the clearest summary of where the war stands: “I don’t see a victory in real terms at the end of this crisis.” Iran has learned that time is on its side, that the Strait of Hormuz is its most potent weapon, and that that weapon becomes more powerful every week the closure continues. “It feels like a Suez moment in some respects,” she said. “This is the end of American global leadership.”

The exit ramp is still there. The jet is down. The pilot is missing. The markets are closed. And April 6 arrives in two days.

In Iowa, farmer Jolene Riessen is facing a $58,000 loss this season after fertilizer prices jumped $250 a ton — a third of the world’s fertilizer supply moves through the Strait of Hormuz. “If it was up to me,” she told the Times, “the war would be done yesterday.”
