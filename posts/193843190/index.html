The Asimov Gap

The Knowledge Kings

Part 27: AI has shattered the one argument that protected democracy from centralized control

April 30, 2026

Hayek argued that knowledge was dispersed through millions of individual minds — inalienable, local, impossible to aggregate. He was right for seventy years. Then the aggregation became possible. Visual by Grok AI.

In 2010, Peter Thiel addressed a conference called Libertopia in San Diego. He had already co-founded PayPal, already begun building Palantir Technologies, already started the political mentorship of a young Ohio entrepreneur named JD Vance. Speaking to the gathered libertarians, Thiel offered a strategic assessment that has aged into something close to prophecy.

“We could never win an election on getting certain things because we were in such a small minority,” Thiel said, “but maybe you could actually unilaterally change the world without having to constantly convince people and beg people and plead with people who are never going to agree with you through technological means.”

He was describing, in 2010, the theory of the world that artificial intelligence is now making operationally feasible. Technology as the alternative to politics. Change without consent. Power without persuasion.

Sixteen years later, Thiel and his allies may be close to not needing to win elections at all.

The Hole in Hayek

Friedrich Hayek, Nobel Prize in Economics, 1974. His central argument — that free markets aggregate dispersed knowledge better than any planner — became the intellectual foundation of the deregulatory consensus that governed economic policy for half a century. A 2025 Stanford-Harvard paper demonstrated that AI breaks both halves of the argument. Source: Nobel Foundation / public domain.

Friedrich Hayek won the Nobel Prize in economics, in 1974, for an argument about knowledge. The central insight of his work was elegant and, for seventy years, largely correct: free markets are superior to central planning because knowledge is inherently dispersed. No government, no matter how well-intentioned or computationally powerful, can aggregate the information embedded in millions of individual decisions, preferences, prices, and local conditions. The market does this automatically. The planner cannot. Therefore, the market must be left to operate, and central control — whether by state or corporation — will always be less efficient and ultimately less free.

This argument was used to defeat Soviet central planning. It was used to defeat price controls. It became the intellectual bedrock of the deregulatory consensus that has governed economic policy in the English-speaking world since roughly 1980.

Artificial intelligence has blown a hole through it.

Erik Brynjolfsson of Stanford and Zoë Hitzig of Harvard demonstrated, in a 2025 paper, precisely how this happened. Hayek’s argument, they showed, depends on knowledge being “inherently dispersed throughout the economy” — local, tacit, inalienable, impossible to aggregate. AI breaks both halves of that argument. It can codify local knowledge that was previously tacit and inalienable. And it can expand information-processing capacity to aggregate, interpret, and act on data at scales no human institution has ever approached.

The result, Brynjolfsson and Hitzig concluded, is that “centralized coordination and control” has become “more feasible and more efficient” — creating structural incentives for “larger average firm size, greater industry concentration and reduced local managerial autonomy.” And these forces, they warned, extend “beyond economic considerations: Centralization of economic power can lead to centralization of political power.”

Hayek assumed that the physics of knowledge protected us from the worst excesses of centralized control. The AI companies have changed the knowledge physics.

Who Holds the Knowledge

Jack Balkin, a professor of constitutional law at Yale, put the consequences plainly when asked by New York Times columnist Thomas Edsall to assess AI’s effect on democratic governance.

“Because the emerging algorithmic society runs on computing infrastructure, data collection, data analysis and prediction,” Balkin wrote, “these platform companies enjoy new forms of power unlike any we have previously seen. Their technological power allows them both to surveil, govern and control private parties and to influence the actions of governments.”

He continued: “Because the very largest platform and AI companies have become increasingly indispensable to territorial governments, they have enormous influence over government operations around the world.”

Margaret Hu, law professor and director of the Digital Democracy Lab at William and Mary, was more direct. AI, she wrote, “is definitely incentivizing the concentration of power,” and AI systems and their leaders “have the potential to manifest almost monarchical aspirations.” The AI race, she continued, is not merely an innovation competition. It is “a race for global dominance economically and culturally, and geopolitically” — fought on a “virtual battlefield for cognitive and information security and data and information ecosystems” simultaneously with a physical battlefield for AI computing infrastructure, minerals, and energy.

This series has spent twenty-six parts documenting the Asimov Gap as a governance failure. Hu and Balkin are naming the condition that makes the gap self-perpetuating: the entities that have the most to gain from ungoverned AI development are the same entities that now hold the knowledge, the infrastructure, and the political access that governance would require.

Peter Thiel saw this coming in 2010. The technology has now caught up to the vision.

The Persuasion Machine

The Yale research team found that large language models outperformed standard campaign advertisements at shifting political opinion. The voter experiences it as a conversation. The political actor deploying it experiences it as the most effective persuasion tool in the history of democratic elections.

The Asimov Gap has a political economy dimension that this series has touched on — the concentration of market power, the colonization of governance functions by private actors — but has not yet examined at the electoral level. That examination can no longer be deferred.

A team of researchers at Yale — Zhongren Chen, Joshua Kalla, and Quan Le — conducted experiments comparing two mechanisms of changing voter opinion: standard campaign advertisements, and conversations with large language models from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI. The results were unambiguous.

AI won. Large language models outperformed standard campaign advertisements at shifting political opinion, with variation across models. The researchers described this as AI “outperforming” media consultants — a clinical phrase for something that should register as a constitutional emergency.

Political advertising has been regulated, in various ways, in democratic countries for decades. The regulation is imperfect, and the history of campaign finance is largely a history of finding and exploiting its gaps. But it exists. It is premised on the idea that the mechanisms by which voters are persuaded are visible, attributable, and subject to public scrutiny.

AI-powered political persuasion is none of these things. When a large language model shifts a voter’s opinion — through a conversation that feels personal, responsive, attentive, and unjudgmental — there is no disclosure, no attribution, no regulatory framework that treats it as a campaign expenditure. The voter experiences it as a conversation. The political actor deploying it experiences it as the most effective persuasion tool in the history of democratic elections.

And then, separately: a team of researchers including a scientist from Anthropic demonstrated that the same large language models can deanonymize internet users — identifying with high precision the real identities of people who post under pseudonyms. The implications for political dissent, journalism, and activism in authoritarian contexts are obvious. The implications for political targeting in ostensibly democratic ones are only slightly less alarming.

The knowledge physics, as Brynjolfsson put it, have changed. Hayek’s dispersed knowledge protected individuals from surveillance states partly because the cost of aggregating that information was prohibitive. AI has eliminated the prohibitive cost.

The Partisan Geometry

Palantir CEO Alex Karp noted publicly that AI disruption favours "vocationally trained, working-class, often male voters" while reducing the economic power of "humanities-trained, largely Democratic voters." Anthropic's own research identified the ten occupations most exposed to AI displacement — they skew female, college-educated, and in the current American political alignment, Democratic. The technology is not a neutral force. It is a sorting machine. Visual by Grok AI.

Alex Karp, the outspoken chief executive of Palantir — a company that has profited enormously from both government surveillance contracts and the AI boom — offered an observation at CNBC that deserves more attention than it received in the AI coverage cycle.

The technology, Karp said, “disrupts humanities-trained, largely Democratic voters and makes their economic power less and increases the economic power of vocationally trained, working-class, often male voters.” He was noting what Anthropic’s own economic research had found: the ten occupations most exposed to AI displacement include computer programmers, customer service representatives, data analysts, financial analysts, information security analysts, and software quality assurance testers — a profile that skews female, college-educated, and — in the current American political alignment — Democratic.

Karp, whose company sells AI-powered surveillance and targeting tools to governments including the US Defense Department, was not celebrating this outcome. He was warning, with some urgency, that the political economy of AI disruption is not neutral. If the technology systematically reduces the economic power of one political coalition while leaving another relatively protected, the question of who governs AI development becomes a question of who governs the democracy that follows from it.

This is not yet a partisan argument. It is an observation about structural incentives. The companies building AI have every reason to build it in ways that serve their existing business models and political relationships. The workers whose livelihoods are most threatened by AI are the ones least represented in the rooms where its governance is decided. And the political coalition most exposed to AI-driven economic disruption is the one least likely to control the regulatory apparatus of the Trump administration.

Ethan Mollick, a professor at the Wharton School who has spent years studying AI’s effects on work and education, was asked by Edsall to forecast what comes next. His answer was the most honest sentence in months of AI coverage: “Nobody knows anything.”

Even him?

“Even me.”

The Enormousness Problem

Edsall, assembling these threads for a Times opinion column in March, arrived at a diagnosis that fits this series’s argument precisely.

“I am not an expert on A.I.,” he wrote, “but one key danger is that its development is overwhelming the ability of policymakers, chief executives and other political and economic players, not to mention ordinary citizens, to manage and direct A.I. in a way that protects the interests of society at large.”

He called this “enormousness” — the quality of a development so large and fast that it induces, in those who should be responding to it, “a combination of helplessness and passivity.”

This is the Asimov Gap’s psychological dimension, and it matters as much as the institutional one. Democratic governance requires that citizens and their representatives believe that governance is possible — that the gap between what exists and what should be regulated is, in principle, closeable. When the gap is too large, too technical, too fast-moving, and too entangled with the economic incentives of the most powerful actors in the system, the political response is not mobilization. It is resignation.

Thiel knew this in 2010. He was betting that technology could generate changes large enough and fast enough to outrun the democratic response. The question of whether that bet was correct is being answered, in real time, by the governance record of the past three years.

Brynjolfsson, writing about the knowledge physics that Hayek assumed would protect us, noted the danger carefully. We are concerned, he wrote, “about any large entity — be it a government or a private corporation — gaining this kind of central-planning authority.” The problem Hayek identified was real. The solution he proposed — dispersed markets — has been dissolved by the technology that the markets produced.

Isaac Asimov imagined a future in which intelligence was subject to law. He did not imagine a future in which intelligence had changed the relationship between knowledge and power so fundamentally that the architecture of law itself needed to be rebuilt from its foundations.

That future is not coming. According to the people who have thought longest and hardest about it, it is here.

“Nobody knows anything.”

Even them.
