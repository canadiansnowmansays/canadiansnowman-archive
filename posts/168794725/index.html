Episode 8: Epstein Files, The Ultimate TACO - Part 1 "The Promise and the Lie"

July 22, 2025

Donald Trump, his now wife Melania, Jeffrey Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell pose for a photo in February 2000

On the campaign trail, it was simple. Clear. Unambiguous.

"We will release the Epstein files."

Not "we'll consider it." Not "we'll study the matter." Not "it's complicated."

We will release the Epstein files.

Donald Trump said it. JD Vance said it. Future Attorney General Pam Bondi said it. Even FBI Director Kash Patel said it. The promise was so consistent, so definitive, that it became a rallying cry.

During campaign rallies and interviews, Trump repeatedly stated: "We will release the Epstein files. All of them. The American people deserve to know the truth."

JD Vance amplified the message: "Day one, we release the Epstein files. No more cover-ups, no more protecting the powerful."

During her confirmation hearings, Pam Bondi was unequivocal: "We will release the Epstein files. The American people have a right to know."

The promise tapped into something deeper than partisan politics. As Natalie Winters explained, "Epstein goes to the very foundation of MAGA" because supporters see it as proof of institutional corruption and elite criminality. According to conspiracy theory expert Mike Rothschild, "Epstein is seen as one of the major players in a global 'paedo elite' that's been trafficking children for centuries, and that Q and Trump were supposed to put an end to once and for all."

Republican Congressman Thomas Massie had already introduced the "Epstein Files Transparency Act," demanding release of "all unclassified records, documents, communications, and investigative materials" within 30 days, specifically prohibiting withholding for "embarrassment, reputational harm, or political sensitivity." Trump's supporters weren't asking for anything unreasonable - they were asking for exactly what Republican legislators were demanding through official channels.

Bottom Line Up Front: This simple promise would become the most perfect example yet of the TACO pattern in action - and the first one that became politically toxic rather than politically useful.

The Promise Phase: Crystal Clear Commitments

The promises weren't buried in policy papers or whispered to donors. They were public, emphatic, and repeated across multiple platforms and officials.

What made these promises particularly compelling was how specific they became over time. This wasn't just campaign rhetoric - it was detailed commitment.

The timeline evolved rapidly:

  • "Day one" became "within the first 100 days"

  • "First 100 days" became "as soon as we review them"

  • "As soon as we review them" became "they're on my desk"

The scope expanded similarly:

  • "Release what we can" became "release everything"

  • "Release everything" became "full transparency"

  • "Full transparency" became "the American people will see it all"

By January 2025, Attorney General Bondi had made perhaps the most specific commitment yet, stating in a press conference that the Epstein files were literally "on my desk" and that release was imminent.

The Peak of Commitment: When Promises Become Impossible to Escape

The promise had created what political scientists call "constituency lock-in" - when supporters become so invested in a particular outcome that backing down becomes politically costly. Remarkably, this was one of the few issues where Trump supporters and critics agreed. Everyone wanted transparency. Everyone wanted accountability.

The promise had achieved "commitment escalation" - each statement increased the specificity and raised the stakes. There was no graceful way to back down without looking like they were protecting someone.

Or so it seemed.

The Accommodation Phase: When Reality Bites

But then reality set in. The clear, simple promise started developing complications. The accommodation phase of the TACO pattern had begun.

Upon taking office, Trump appeared to follow through. Attorney General Bondi announced files were "on my desk" and that she was preparing to release them. FBI Director Kash Patel promised "no coverups, no missing documents and no stone left unturned."

In February 2025, Bondi released "Phase 1" of documents, with conservative influencers posing at the White House holding binders titled "The Epstein Files: Phase 1." The material contained few new revelations, drawing criticism from right-wing influencers, but Bondi promised more releases after redacting victim names.

The administration even held meetings with MAGA supporters, including conspiracy theorist Jack Posobiec, who "thought he would finally get some answers about Jeffrey Epstein." However, they were given "essentially just rehashed, already-public material."

The initial accommodations were subtle, almost reasonable:

  • "We need to be careful not to compromise ongoing investigations"

  • "We have to protect sources and methods that could be revealed"

  • "We don't want to jeopardize future prosecutions"

The language evolved rapidly over just a few weeks:

  • Week 1: "We will release the Epstein files as promised"

  • Week 2: "We're reviewing the files to ensure appropriate release"

  • Week 3: "There are legal complexities we need to address first"

  • Week 4: "We need more time to do this right"

The timeline got fuzzy:

  • "Day one" became "soon"

  • "On my desk" became "under review"

  • "Imminent" became "when appropriate"

  • "This month" became "in due course"

As the accommodations mounted, a new narrative began to emerge. Instead of simply releasing the files as promised, the administration began suggesting the real problem was external pressure:

  • "The Democrats are trying to turn this into a political weapon"

  • "The media is creating unrealistic expectations"

  • "Deep state operatives are making this more complicated than it needs to be"

The most sophisticated accommodation came in the form of calling for a special prosecutor - presented as taking the matter more seriously, not less. This reframed delay as diligence, inaction as responsibility.

Internal administration documents reveal splits over the investigation's conclusion, with officials debating how to handle mounting pressure while protecting Trump's interests.

The accommodation phase created specific expectations that Trump would ultimately be unable to meet.

The Reversal: From "On My Desk" to "It's All a Hoax"

And then came the most dramatic reversal in TACO history. Not just a policy change, not just a broken promise, but a complete redefinition of reality itself.

On July 7, 2025, the Justice Department backtracked completely. An unsigned memo claimed that after an "exhaustive review," they had found no "incriminating client list" or additional documents warranting public disclosure.

Trump's response was immediate and contradictory. In a Truth Social post, he wrote: "Based on the ridiculous amount of publicity given to Jeffrey Epstein, I have asked Attorney General Pam Bondi to produce any and all pertinent Grand Jury testimony, subject to Court approval. This SCAM, perpetuated by the Democrats, should end, right now!"

The logical contradiction was impossible to ignore: Trump was simultaneously offering to release testimony about Epstein while calling the entire investigation a "scam."

The transformation was breathtaking in its audacity:

  • The files went from "on my desk" to essentially "never existed"

  • The demand for transparency became a "Democrat scam"

  • His own supporters' expectations became evidence they were "stupid"

  • The promise to release everything became "falling for a hoax"

On July 16, Trump escalated his attacks, telling supporters they were "stupid" and "weaklings" for continuing to demand the files he had promised to release. As one analyst documented: "Instead they want to talk about the Epstein hoax, and the sad part is it's people that are really doing the Democrats' work. They're stupid people. They're stupid people."

This represents something unprecedented in TACO history: a politician publicly attacking his own supporters for believing his promises.

But Trump's 'hoax' narrative was about to face its biggest test. The Wall Street Journal was preparing to publish evidence that would contradict every aspect of his cover-up - evidence so damning that Trump would personally call the editor-in-chief to try to kill the story.
