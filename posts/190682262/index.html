The Permit

Introduction: How an Unwritten Asterisk Has Qualified the First Amendment for Four Hundred Years

March 12, 2026

Illustration by The Atlantic. Sources: Heather Diehl / Getty; Sepia Times / Getty.

The article in The Atlantic was published on March 11, 2026 — the twelfth day of an American war against Iran. Its subject was a Republican congressman from Tennessee named Andy Ogles, and it began with a fire.

On a Saturday morning in early 2008, three young men drove to the Islamic Center of Columbia, Tennessee, carrying spray paint and Molotov cocktails. They defaced the exterior walls with swastikas. They broke through the doors. They set the building on fire. The mosque — the first in Columbia, a small city about forty-five miles south of Nashville, and for a time the only Muslim house of worship between Nashville and Huntsville, Alabama — burned to its foundation. After the fire, the congregation purchased a nearby church, converted it quietly into a new mosque, and kept their plans secret to avoid a backlash.

That was eighteen years before the Atlantic article was published. In the intervening years, Nashville’s Muslim community had grown to more than forty thousand people — Kurdish and Somali refugees brought by resettlement programs, Palestinian families, physicians recruited to a region that needed doctors. The rebuilt mosque in Columbia sits less than a mile from the district office of Representative Andy Ogles.

On March 9, 2026, eleven days into Operation Epic Fury, Ogles posted on X: “Muslims don’t belong in American society. Pluralism is a lie.”

His office did not return requests for comment.

When a reporter asked Speaker Mike Johnson about the statement, Johnson said Ogles had used “different language than I would use.” He then spent the bulk of his answer validating concerns about Sharia law. No consequences followed. No committee assignments were stripped. No formal censure was introduced. The Republican Party — which in 2019 had stripped Representative Steve King of his committee assignments for defending the phrases “white nationalist” and “white supremacist” in a newspaper interview — declined to act. The mechanism that once existed for withdrawing permission for this kind of statement had been removed. In its place was something different: not explicit endorsement, but the careful non-rebuke that functions, in practice, as permission.

Ogles is not an important man. He is a backbencher in his second term who has made himself briefly useful as a data point. But the data point is illuminating. His district contains more Muslim constituents than any other in the Tennessee delegation. He represents a community of forty thousand people in Nashville, a congregation in Columbia that rebuilt its mosque once after white supremacists burned it, physicians who moved their families to Tennessee because the region needed their skills. He told all of them, on a public platform, that they do not belong in the country he was elected to serve.

And the Speaker of the House said he would have used different language.

This series is about what that moment reveals — not about Ogles specifically, but about the structure that produced him, sustained him, and declined to sanction him. It is about a promise the United States made to itself at its founding and the unwritten asterisk that has always qualified that promise. It is about four hundred years of American history in which the targets of that asterisk have changed — Quakers, Catholics, Mormons, Jews, Buddhists, Muslims — while the mechanism of persecution has remained, with variations, consistent.

Zimmytws/iStock, via Getty Images Plus

The promise is the First Amendment. Its religion clauses are among the most precisely drafted sentences in the founding documents: “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.” No establishment. No prohibition. The drafters were not careless. Several of them had personally witnessed religious persecution on American soil. James Madison had intervened on behalf of Baptist ministers jailed in Virginia for preaching without an Anglican license. They knew what they were writing and why.

The asterisk is not in the text. It is in the practice. It is in the four hundred years of American history between the drafting of that sentence and Andy Ogles’s post, during which the freedom the founders had in mind operated, in practice, as Protestant freedom — with everyone else admitted to varying degrees, on varying terms, with varying frequency of reminder that their admission was conditional.

There is a pattern in American religious persecution that this series will trace across ten parts. It has five elements, and they appear, with variations, in every chapter.

First, a dominant religious establishment — in the early period explicitly Protestant, in later periods implicitly so — defines the terms of acceptable religious identity in civic life.

Second, a religious community arrives or becomes newly visible. It is identified as theologically incompatible with American values — as foreign, as subversive, as carrying a loyalty that overrides its members’ civic commitments.

Third, the theological critique does racial and economic work simultaneously. The religion being targeted is almost never evaluated on its theological merits. It is a proxy — for ethnic anxiety, for labor competition, for the discomfort of a dominant group watching its dominance become less total. Anti-Catholic nativism in the 1840s meant Irish and Italian immigrants. Anti-Buddhist sentiment during the Japanese internment meant Japanese Americans. Anti-Muslim rhetoric today travels on racial coding that its practitioners know is more politically sustainable than overt racial targeting.

Fourth, the language of security is deployed. The targeted community is not merely theologically wrong but existentially dangerous — a threat to the children, the social order, the nation itself. This legitimates legal and extralegal action. It transforms persecution into patriotism.

Fifth, the permission comes from the top. It does not require a formal decree. It requires only that the people with the power to withdraw permission choose not to. Or, at the far end of the spectrum, choose to issue it.

Mike Johnson issued a permit on the day he validated Sharia law anxiety rather than rebuking the man who told forty thousand of his own constituents they don’t belong in America. He did not use Ogles’s language. He didn’t need to. The permit doesn’t require the signature of the issuer to be valid. It requires only their silence, or something close enough to silence that the difference is academic.

The title of this series borrows from contract law. An exception clause limits the scope of an agreement — it defines what the contract does not cover. The First Amendment is, in its essence, a contract between the American government and the people it governs. “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.” No establishment. No prohibition. The language is unqualified.

But the contract has always carried fine print. Not written into the document — the founders were careful enough to leave it out of the text. Written instead into the practice: into which communities the promise was understood to cover, and which were quietly placed outside its scope. That fine print is the exception clause. It has been applied to a rotating cast of targets for four hundred years. This series is an attempt to read it clearly.

Sabina Mohyuddin, executive director, American Muslim Advisory Council

One more detail from the Atlantic piece, and this one stayed with me. Sabina Mohyuddin, executive director of the Nashville-based American Muslim Advisory Committee, told a reporter she was torn about whether to respond publicly to Ogles at all. Her organization has been fielding calls about his attacks since before the war started. She knows the playbook — her organization formed in 2012 from a coalition that successfully fought a state bill seeking to ban “Sharia organizations” in Tennessee. She has been here before.

“He’s targeting our community and trying to gain relevance, because no one takes him seriously,” she said. “And, honestly, it’s working.”

That is the trap that the permission structure sets for its targets. Silence is unsatisfying — a sitting congressman has told your community it does not belong, and silence reads as acceptance. But response amplifies. Denunciation is the rocket fuel that makes a backbencher briefly important. The community that rebuilt its mosque once, that kept its renovation plans secret to avoid a backlash, that has watched its members be kicked out of meetings with state legislators — that community has to calculate, every time, whether the cost of speaking is higher than the cost of not speaking.

That calculation should not exist in a country whose founding document says Congress shall make no law prohibiting the free exercise of religion.

It exists because the asterisk exists.

It has always existed.

I am writing this series from Toronto — from outside the document. Canada has its own history of religious persecution, its own asterisks written into founding promises, its own recurring failures to honor what the founding language says. I am not writing from a position of national innocence. No country that existed in the 17th century has that.

But I am writing from a position of distance, which provides a particular kind of clarity. The promise the United States made is visible from here. The gap between the promise and the practice is visible from here. The mechanism by which the gap has been sustained and renewed across four centuries — the template, the five elements, the permit issued and reissued every generation — is visible from here in a way that is sometimes harder to see from inside the document.

This series will examine that mechanism across ten parts. It will begin where the American story begins — with the Puritans, who crossed an ocean to escape religious persecution and immediately began practicing it. It will move through the Know-Nothing movement’s war on Catholics, the federal government’s war on Mormons, the quiet institutional antisemitism that turned away a ship full of refugees while a genocide proceeded in Europe, the wartime fusion of racial and religious targeting that put Japanese Americans in camps, the pre-existing architecture of anti-Muslim suspicion that was waiting for September 11 to go fully public, and the decade-long dismantling of the penalty structure that used to constrain the Andy Ogleses of American politics.

It will end with a question rather than a verdict: what would it actually mean to honor the promise without the asterisk?

That question has been asked in every generation. It has been answered, partially, in every generation — by Roger Williams founding a colony on the principle of separated conscience, by the Catholics and Jews and Mormons and Muslims who went to court against governments that persecuted them and sometimes won, by the civil liberties lawyers who built legal walls around communities under surveillance.

The walls have never been permanent. The asterisk keeps finding ways back in.

Understanding how it does that — understanding the template, the mechanism, the permit — seems, in March 2026, on the twelfth day of a war, with a congressman telling his Muslim constituents they don’t belong and a speaker validating the anxiety that produced the statement, like useful work.

That is what this series is.

The Exception Clause is a ten-part series examining the history of religious persecution in America. Part I — “The Founders’ Asterisk” — examines the Puritans, Roger Williams, Mary Dyer, Anne Hutchinson, and the paradox embedded in the First Amendment: a promise of religious freedom drafted by people who had both suffered and practiced persecution within living memory.
