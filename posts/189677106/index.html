Part 8: Was the Democratic Promise Ever Realistic?

April 27, 2026

Steve Jobs at the International Design Conference, Aspen, 1983. "The question was whether we would do it great or do it so-so." Source: Aspen International Design Conference, 1983 (video still)

A CanadianSnowmanSays Series By Stephen Gordon - April 2026

The Question

Steve Jobs stood in Aspen in 1983 and made a promise: technology would distribute power to individuals, just as the fractional horsepower motor had distributed mechanical power from the factory to the kitchen. Forty-three years later, the technology he predicted exists. The distribution he promised does not.

The seven essays preceding this one have traced what happened — who kept the promise, who betrayed it, who struggled with it, who broke it. But the final question is not about individuals. It is about the promise itself.

Was the democratic promise ever realistic? Or was oligarchic capture of transformative technology inevitable — not because of bad people, but because of structural forces that no amount of good intention could overcome?

The Case for Inevitability

The case that oligarchic capture was inevitable is strong, and intellectual honesty requires stating it plainly.

Every transformative technology in modern history has followed the same pattern. The railroad was invented as a public conveyance and became the instrument of the robber barons. The telephone was a democratic miracle of connection and became the Bell monopoly. Radio was invented as a tool of open communication and became the broadcast oligopoly. Television democratized access to information and entertainment and became the most powerful instrument of mass persuasion ever created.

In every case, the pattern is the same: a technology emerges that has genuine democratic potential, early adopters use it in distributed and egalitarian ways, and then economic forces concentrate control in the hands of a few. The pattern is so consistent across technologies, across centuries, and across cultures that it begins to look less like a series of failures and more like a law of nature.

The reason is structural, not moral. Building and maintaining transformative infrastructure requires enormous capital. Capital flows to entities that can generate returns. Returns require control — of the platform, the data, the distribution channel, the user relationship. Control, by definition, is concentration. And concentration, once achieved, is self-reinforcing: the entity with control generates more capital, which enables more control, which generates more capital.

This is the gravity of power that Musk’s story illustrated in Part 5. It is not a failure of character. It is a structural incentive that operates on everyone — saints and scoundrels alike.

The builders — Berners-Lee, Torvalds, Wales — escaped this gravity by refusing to enter the market. They kept the promise by opting out of the economic system that makes concentration inevitable. But opting out has limits. The open web still exists, but most people experience the internet through proprietary platforms. Linux still runs the servers, but the services those servers provide are controlled by a handful of corporations. Wikipedia is free, but it accounts for a tiny fraction of the information people actually consume online.

The democratic commons exists. It is just not where most people live.

The Case Against Inevitability

And yet the case for inevitability, strong as it is, has a fatal flaw: it treats the absence of democratic governance as a given rather than a choice.

The railroad barons were eventually constrained by antitrust law and regulated by the Interstate Commerce Commission. The Bell monopoly was broken up. Broadcasting was regulated by the FCC. In each case, the democratic system — imperfectly, belatedly, inadequately — asserted public control over private concentrations of technological power.

The question is not whether technology naturally concentrates. It does. The question is whether democratic institutions can counterbalance that concentration. And the historical answer is: sometimes. When the political will exists. When the regulatory capacity exists. When the public understands what is at stake.

On March 26, 2026, a federal judge in San Francisco provided the most compelling evidence yet that democratic institutions can still do their job.

Judge Rita F. Lin, U.S. District Court, Northern District of California. She called the Pentagon's retaliation against Anthropic "classic illegal First Amendment retaliation" and "Orwellian." Source: U.S. District Court, Northern District of California

Judge Rita F. Lin granted Anthropic a sweeping preliminary injunction against the Pentagon’s attempt to blacklist the company for refusing to remove AI safety guardrails. Her forty-three-page opinion did not equivocate. She called the supply chain risk designation “likely both contrary to law and arbitrary and capricious.” She observed that the designation had historically been reserved for foreign adversaries and terrorists, not American companies engaged in contract disputes. And then she wrote a sentence that may define this chapter of the democratic promise: “Nothing in the governing statute supports the Orwellian notion that an American company may be branded a potential adversary and saboteur of the U.S. for expressing disagreement with the government.”

This is what democratic governance looks like when it functions. Not perfectly — two weeks later, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals declined to block the Pentagon’s blacklisting on different legal grounds, leaving the legal landscape fractured. Not swiftly — the case will likely take a year or more to resolve. Not comprehensively — one judge’s ruling does not constitute a regulatory framework.

But it is not nothing. It is the judiciary asserting that the government cannot punish a technology company for exercising its right to set ethical limits on its own products. It is a democratic institution doing precisely what democratic institutions are supposed to do: constraining the abuse of state power.

The failure of democratic governance in the digital age is not a failure of the institutions themselves. It is a failure of politics. The United States had the legal tools to prevent the platform monopolies. Antitrust law existed. Regulatory agencies existed. The precedents from the railroad era, the telephone era, and the broadcasting era were available. What was missing was the political will to apply those tools to the technology industry — a will that was systematically undermined by the industry’s lobbying, its campaign contributions, its revolving door with government, and its mythologization in American culture.

The democratic promise was betrayed not by the inevitable logic of technology but by the specific, contingent, reversible failure of democratic institutions to do their job.

This distinction matters. If oligarchic capture is inevitable — a law of technological nature — then the democratic promise was always a fantasy, and the appropriate response is resignation. But if oligarchic capture is the result of political failure — of specific decisions not to regulate, not to enforce, not to break up — then the appropriate response is political action.

Judge Lin’s ruling suggests the second. So does the fact that the Pentagon’s own contradictions — simultaneously declaring Anthropic a security threat and an essential national security asset — collapsed under judicial scrutiny. The institutions work. They just have to be used.

The AI Inflection

Artificial intelligence represents either the final confirmation of inevitability or the last chance to choose differently.

The case for inevitability in AI is overwhelming. Training frontier AI models requires billions of dollars in computing infrastructure. Only a handful of companies can afford this. The data required to train these models is concentrated in the hands of the companies that have spent two decades extracting it from users. The talent required to build these systems is concentrated in a few research labs, mostly in the San Francisco Bay Area. Everything about AI — the capital requirements, the data requirements, the talent requirements, the infrastructure requirements — pushes toward concentration.

And yet.

Dario Amodei drew a line — and the line held. Not because Amodei was more virtuous than everyone else in this series, though Part 7 made the case that his principles were genuine. The line held because democratic institutions reinforced it. A federal judge ruled that the government’s retaliation was unconstitutional. Lawyers filed briefs. Amicus curiae from former service secretaries and retired senior military officers supported Anthropic’s position. The Electronic Frontier Foundation documented the stakes. The legal system — slow, imperfect, expensive — did what it was designed to do.

And something else happened that no one predicted. The public responded. Within twenty-four hours of Anthropic’s refusal and OpenAI’s countermove, more than 1.5 million users joined the QuitGPT movement. Claude displaced ChatGPT as the number-one app on the U.S. Apple App Store. People cancelled subscriptions, switched platforms, and made their reasoning public. It was the first time in the AI era that a mass consumer response was driven not by product features but by principle.

The QuitGPT movement: 1.5 million users, one principle. For the first time in the AI era, people voted with their app stores. Source: Illustration by Grok / xAI

Was it large enough to threaten OpenAI’s dominance? No. Was it sustained? Too early to say. But it was evidence — the first significant evidence — that the democratic promise has a constituency. That people understand what is at stake and are willing to act on that understanding.

Tim Berners-Lee is still fighting for an open web. Open-source AI models — from Meta’s Llama to academic projects around the world — demonstrate that concentrated infrastructure does not necessarily require concentrated control. The European Union’s AI Act, the first comprehensive regulatory framework for artificial intelligence, reaches full enforcement in August 2026, with penalties of up to thirty-five million euros or seven percent of global revenue for violations. As of this writing, only eight of the EU’s twenty-seven member states have designated their enforcement authorities — a gap between ambition and implementation that echoes every previous attempt at democratic governance of transformative technology. But the framework exists. The law is on the books. The question, as always, is whether it will be enforced.

And then there is the Mythos paradox. In April 2026 — less than two months after the Pentagon branded Anthropic a national security threat — the White House invited Amodei in for talks. The administration wants access to Anthropic’s newest model, Mythos Preview, a cybersecurity tool so powerful that Anthropic itself restricted its release. The same government that tried to destroy Anthropic for refusing to remove safety guardrails now wants the technology that only a company committed to safety guardrails would build with such discipline.

The irony is not incidental. It is structural. Amodei’s willingness to say no — to the Pentagon, to the revenue, to the political pressure — is precisely what makes Anthropic’s technology trustworthy. A company that will remove guardrails for a contract will also cut corners on safety testing, on red-teaming, on the painstaking work of making sure a model powerful enough to breach financial systems is deployed responsibly. A company that holds its lines can be trusted with tools that require lines to be held.

Sam Altman signed the contract. He got the revenue. He lost the credibility. That trade may define the AI era as clearly as any regulatory framework.

The Canadian Observation

I have written this series from Canada, and I want to be transparent about what that perspective contributes and what it limits.

The democratic promise, as Jobs articulated it, was an American idea rooted in American values — individual empowerment, distrust of centralized authority, the frontier ethos of self-reliance. The betrayal of that promise is also, in many ways, an American story — the product of a specific regulatory environment, a specific political culture, and a specific relationship between government and industry.

From Canada, the pattern looks slightly different. Not because Canadians are wiser or more virtuous, but because we are smaller. We do not build the platforms. We use them. We do not set the rules. We live under them. And from that position — the position of a country that depends on American technology without controlling it — the stakes of the democratic promise are vivid in a way that they may not be for Americans, who can at least imagine that their government might, someday, regulate the companies headquartered within their borders.

The Anthropic–Pentagon confrontation makes this Canadian anxiety concrete. When the U.S. government tried to destroy a company for maintaining safety guardrails, the implications extended far beyond American borders. Canadians use Claude. Canadians rely on AI systems governed by American contract disputes, American court rulings, American political vendettas. Judge Lin’s ruling protects Anthropic from unconstitutional retaliation — but it does not protect Canadians from an American administration that might, next time, succeed in forcing an AI company to remove the constraints that keep its technology safe.

For Canadians, the question is sharper: if American democracy can only intermittently govern American technology, what recourse do the rest of us have?

This is not a question I can answer. But it is a question that the democratic promise, taken seriously, demands.

The Verdict

Was the democratic promise ever realistic?

Yes. The builders proved it. The open web, open-source software, Wikipedia — these are not hypotheticals. They are functioning systems that distribute power, empower individuals, and serve the public interest. They prove that the democratic model of technology works.

The promise was also betrayed. The enclosure of the digital commons, the rise of surveillance capitalism, the transformation of OpenAI, the fall of Elon Musk — these prove that the democratic model is vulnerable to capture by economic and political forces that it cannot resist without external support.

The external support is democratic governance — regulation, antitrust enforcement, public investment in open infrastructure, judicial review, and international cooperation to prevent any single country’s technology oligarchs from imposing their will on the rest of the world.

This governance has been, to date, profoundly inadequate. But the events of early 2026 have complicated the story in ways that matter.

A CEO said no and accepted the consequences. A federal judge called the government’s retaliation Orwellian and blocked it. A million and a half people switched platforms on principle. A regulatory framework covering five hundred million Europeans approaches enforcement. And the administration that tried to punish principled refusal found itself, weeks later, asking for access to the technology that only principled restraint could produce.

None of this is sufficient. One CEO’s principles cannot substitute for institutional governance — this series has demonstrated, figure by figure, that the market will not produce democratic outcomes without democratic constraints. One judge’s ruling does not constitute a regulatory framework. One consumer movement does not reverse two decades of platform monopoly. One European law does not govern the globe.

But inadequacy is not impossibility. And the distance between “catastrophically absent” and “beginning to emerge” is the distance between despair and political action.

The democratic promise can be kept only by democracy itself. By citizens who understand what is at stake — and who have now demonstrated, for the first time in the AI era, that they are willing to act on that understanding. By governments willing to govern — and by judges willing to remind governments that governance means constraining state power as well as corporate power. By international institutions capable of coordinating across borders. By a political culture that values democratic control of technology as much as it values the technology itself.

Steve Jobs was right in 1983. The technology would come. The question was whether we would do it great or do it so-so. Forty-three years later, the answer is clear: we did it so-so. We built the tools he imagined and failed to govern them as he hoped.

But the tools still exist. The democratic institutions still exist. The choice still exists. And in the early months of 2026, for the first time in a long time, the people who believe in the democratic promise started fighting for it — in courtrooms, in app stores, in the West Wing, and in the quiet, stubborn refusal of one company to pretend that what was happening was not happening.

The kids can’t wait. They never could.
