America’s 80-Year Failure to Solve Healthcare

Part 4: “What Works — How Other Countries Solved This”

December 10, 2025

Every debate about American healthcare reform eventually hits the same wall: “But America is different.”

Different size. Different diversity. Different culture. Different Constitution. Different relationship between citizen and state.

All true. And all irrelevant.

Because the countries that solved healthcare aren’t identical to each other either. Switzerland uses competing private insurance companies. Canada bans private insurance for core services. Germany combines public and private options. Singapore mandates personal savings accounts. The UK runs a fully socialized system where hospitals are government-owned and doctors are government employees.

They use radically different approaches. But they all achieve three things America doesn’t:

  1. Universal coverage: Everyone gets care

  2. Lower costs: Roughly half of American per-capita spending

  3. Better outcomes: Longer life expectancy, lower infant mortality, fewer preventable deaths

This is Part 4 of Code Blue, our investigation into America’s 80-year healthcare failure. We’ve documented the broken promises (Parts 1-2) and followed the money (Part 3). Now we examine what actually works — not as theoretical models, but as proven systems delivering care to hundreds of millions of people across wildly different countries and cultures.

The solutions exist. Let’s see how they did it.

Switzerland: Capitalism with a Conscience

If you want to argue America can’t have universal healthcare because “free markets” and “individual choice,” Switzerland destroys your case.

The Swiss Model:

  • Mandatory private insurance (no government insurance option)

  • Competing insurance companies (approximately 60 insurers)

  • Individual mandate (everyone must buy insurance)

  • Premium subsidies for lower-income families

  • Strict price controls and coverage requirements

  • No denial for pre-existing conditions

  • No profit on basic coverage (insurers can profit on supplemental plans)

Sound familiar? It should. The Affordable Care Act was partially modeled on Switzerland’s system — except Switzerland has teeth where the ACA has loopholes.

Results:

  • Coverage: 100% (essentially universal)

  • Per capita spending: $7,732 (vs. $14,885 in the US)

  • Life expectancy: 83.4 years (vs. 78.9 in the US)

  • Out-of-pocket costs: High deductibles (up to $2,500) but capped

  • Quality: Consistently ranks in top 5 globally

How it works: Every Swiss resident must purchase health insurance from a private company. The government defines a standard package that all insurers must offer — covering doctor visits, hospital stays, prescription drugs, and preventive care.

Insurers compete, but not on coverage (which is standardized) or by denying pre-existing conditions (which is illegal). They compete on premium prices, customer service, and supplemental coverage options.

Low-income families receive government subsidies to afford premiums. The government sets maximum prices for medical services and drugs. Hospitals are mostly private but operate as non-profits.

The trade-off: Switzerland has high out-of-pocket costs and expensive premiums (averaging $5,200 per year per person). But no one goes bankrupt. No one is denied care. And total costs are still half of American spending.

What America could learn: You can have private insurance, market competition, and individual choice while still achieving universal coverage — IF you regulate prices, mandate coverage, subsidize affordability, and prohibit denials for pre-existing conditions.

The ACA took half-measures toward this model. Switzerland proves the full model works.

Germany: The Public-Private Partnership

Germany invented health insurance in 1883 under Otto von Bismarck. 142 years later, it still works.

The German Model:

  • Statutory health insurance (public option) covers 88% of population

  • Private insurance option for high earners (11% of population)

  • Employer and employee split premiums 50/50

  • Premiums based on income (capped at roughly $5,000/year per person)

  • Choice of 100+ non-profit “sickness funds” (insurance providers)

  • Strict government price controls on drugs, procedures, doctor fees

  • Universal coverage mandatory

Results:

  • Coverage: 100%

  • Per capita spending: $7,383

  • Life expectancy: 81.7 years

  • Wait times: Generally short (2-4 weeks for specialists)

  • Quality: Excellent infrastructure, high patient satisfaction

How it works: Most Germans are covered by statutory health insurance — non-profit funds that operate more like utilities than insurance companies. Premiums are set as a percentage of income (around 14.6%, split between employer and employee).

High earners (roughly $70,000+ annually) can opt out and buy private insurance, which offers faster access and fancier hospital rooms but costs more.

The government negotiates prices with doctors, hospitals, and drug manufacturers. A hip replacement costs what it costs — hospitals can’t charge wildly different prices to different patients.

Doctors can work in the public system, private system, or both. Most choose public because the administrative burden is far lower.

The trade-off: Private insurance patients sometimes get faster access to specialists. Public system patients might wait 2-4 weeks for non-urgent specialist appointments. But urgent care is immediate, and no one is denied treatment.

What America could learn: You don’t have to eliminate private insurance to achieve universal coverage. Germany proves you can have a strong public option alongside private insurance — IF you regulate prices and make the public option robust enough that it covers 88% of people by choice, not mandate.

This is the “public option” model many Democrats proposed. Germany’s been doing it since 1883.

Canada: Single-Payer, Locally Run

Full disclosure: I’m Canadian. I’ve lived with this system for 74 years. So let me give you the unvarnished truth — the good and the bad.

The Canadian Model:

  • Single-payer insurance run by provinces (not federal government)

  • Private insurance prohibited for services covered by public system

  • Funded by taxes (no premiums, no deductibles, no co-pays for covered services)

  • Hospitals mostly private non-profits

  • Doctors private practitioners (paid fee-for-service by government)

  • Core coverage: hospital care, physician services, diagnostic tests

  • Supplementary coverage (dental, vision, prescription drugs) varies by province, age, and income level

Results:

  • Coverage: 100% for core medical services

  • Per capita spending: $5,511

  • Life expectancy: 82.7 years

  • Wait times: Variable (short for urgent care, longer for elective procedures)

  • Satisfaction: High for accessibility, frustration with wait times

How it works: Each province runs its own insurance plan, funded primarily through taxes. You visit any doctor or hospital, show your provincial health card, and receive care. No bills. No claims. No denials.

Doctors are private practitioners who bill the provincial government for services rendered. Fee schedules are negotiated between provincial governments and medical associations. A knee replacement in Ontario costs the same whether you’re rich or poor, old or young.

Beyond the basics: The universal system covers hospital and physician care. For dental, vision, and prescription drugs, coverage is more complex:

Seniors (like myself at 74) receive enhanced coverage through programs like Ontario Drug Benefit — covering most prescriptions with annual deductibles around $100. Vision care typically includes one comprehensive eye exam every 18 months at no charge (some specialized services cost extra). The new Canadian Dental Care Plan, rolled out in 2023-2024, now covers dental services for seniors and low-income Canadians.

Low-income residents qualify for provincial drug benefit programs, dental coverage, and vision care regardless of age.

Working-age Canadians with middle to higher incomes typically rely on employer-provided insurance or purchase private plans for dental, vision, and prescription drugs.

This creates a tiered system for supplementary services: comprehensive coverage for those who need it most (seniors, low-income), employer or private insurance for the working population. It’s not perfect, but it means the most vulnerable populations don’t go without care.

The reality: Canadian healthcare works brilliantly for acute care and chronic disease management. Heart attack? You’re in an ambulance in minutes, in surgery within the hour, and you’ll never see a bill. Cancer diagnosis? Treatment starts immediately.

But non-urgent procedures involve wait times. Hip replacement? You might wait 6-12 months. MRI for a non-urgent issue? Could be 2-3 months. Specialist appointment for non-urgent condition? 4-8 weeks is common.

The trade-off visualized:

Let’s be brutally honest about what these trade-offs actually mean:

Americans love pointing to Canadian wait times while ignoring that:

  1. The “faster” American access only applies if you’re insured AND can afford your deductible AND the procedure is in-network AND you don’t get surprise-billed

  2. Thirty million uninsured Americans have infinite wait times — they never get the procedure

  3. Americans with insurance but high deductibles often delay or skip necessary care — that’s also a wait time, just measured differently

Canadian wait times are real and frustrating. But they’re triaged by medical necessity, not ability to pay. Heart attack? Immediate care. Cancer? Treatment starts within weeks. Hip replacement that can wait? You wait.

Meanwhile, 45 million Americans couldn’t afford their prescribed medications in 2023. That’s not “wait times” — that’s denial of care.

The choice isn’t between “instant American access” and “long Canadian waits.” The choice is between:

  • Canadian system: Everyone gets care eventually, prioritized by medical need, zero bankruptcies

  • American system: Fast access if you can pay, delayed care if you’re underinsured, no care if you’re uninsured, bankruptcy if you’re unlucky

When Americans criticize Canadian wait times, they’re comparing the best-case American experience (fully insured, low deductible, in-network, no surprise bills) against the worst-case Canadian experience (non-urgent elective procedure).

Compare apples to apples: the average American experience against the average Canadian experience. Suddenly those wait times look like a pretty reasonable trade-off.

What America could learn: Single-payer works. For 40 million people spread across the second-largest country on earth, including remote northern communities, Canada delivers care to everyone at roughly half the cost of the American system.

The system also demonstrates how to layer coverage: universal access to essential services (hospital, physician care) supplemented with targeted programs for vulnerable populations (seniors, low-income) while allowing private insurance to fill gaps for others.

The wait times are real. But Americans already have wait times — plus medical bankruptcy, insurance denials, and 30 million uninsured people. Given a choice between “wait for elective hip replacement” and “go bankrupt or go without,” Canada’s trade-off looks pretty good.

And for seniors and lower-income Canadians, the system delivers comprehensive coverage — medical, prescription drugs, dental, and vision — at minimal or no direct cost.

Singapore: The Mandatory Savings Model

Singapore took a completely different path — and it’s arguably the most cost-effective system in the world.

The Singapore Model:

  • Mandatory health savings accounts (MediSave) — 8-10.5% of income

  • Catastrophic government insurance (MediShield) for major expenses

  • Government subsidies (Medifund) for poor who can’t afford care

  • Price transparency required for all providers

  • Heavy government involvement in hospital construction and management

  • Strong emphasis on preventive care and personal responsibility

Results:

  • Coverage: 100%

  • Per capita spending: $2,989 (lowest among developed nations)

  • Life expectancy: 83.2 years

  • Quality: Excellent, highly efficient

  • Satisfaction: High

How it works: Every working Singaporean contributes 8-10.5% of income to a personal MediSave account, which can only be used for healthcare. These accounts cover routine care, doctor visits, and minor procedures.

For major expenses (surgeries, cancer treatment, extended hospital stays), MediShield catastrophic insurance kicks in — a government-run insurance plan with premiums paid from MediSave accounts.

For those too poor to afford care even with these systems, Medifund provides a government safety net.

The government heavily regulates prices, requires transparent pricing, and directly operates many hospitals as efficient non-profits. The result: exceptional care at a fraction of the cost of any other developed nation.

The trade-off: This system requires high personal savings rates and strong government management. It works in Singapore partly because Singapore has a small, highly-organized population with cultural emphasis on saving and personal responsibility.

What America could learn: Health savings accounts don’t have to be libertarian pipe dreams. Singapore proves they can work as part of a comprehensive system with strong government backup for catastrophic costs and the truly poor.

Republicans love HSAs. Democrats love government safety nets. Singapore combines both.

United Kingdom: The Fully Socialized Model

The National Health Service is what Americans think of when they hear “socialized medicine.” Time to separate myth from reality.

The UK Model:

  • Fully government-run system

  • Hospitals owned and operated by NHS

  • Doctors and nurses are government employees

  • Funded entirely through taxes (no premiums, no deductibles)

  • Private insurance legal (13% of population buys it) but parallel to NHS

  • Prescription drugs: £9.90 per prescription ($12.50 USD) — that’s it

Results:

  • Coverage: 100%

  • Per capita spending: $5,387

  • Life expectancy: 81.3 years

  • Wait times: Variable, longer for elective procedures

  • Quality: Good but strained by underfunding

How it works: You get sick, you go to an NHS hospital or GP clinic, you receive care, you go home. No bills, no insurance cards, no claims. If you need a prescription, you pay £9.90 regardless of what the drug is — unless you’re a child, pregnant, elderly, or have chronic conditions (in which case it’s free).

Hospitals are government-owned. Doctors and nurses are government employees with salaries and pensions. The entire system is funded through taxes.

The reality: The NHS is beloved in the UK — but it’s also chronically underfunded. Wait times for non-urgent procedures can stretch to months or over a year. The system works well for emergency care and general practice but struggles with capacity for elective procedures.

About 13% of Brits buy private insurance for faster access to specialists and elective procedures. The private system exists parallel to the NHS — you can use both.

The trade-off: The NHS delivers care to 67 million people at about 36% of American per-capita costs. But chronic underfunding creates wait times and capacity constraints. Still, Brits consistently rank the NHS as one of their most treasured institutions.

What America could learn: Fully socialized medicine works. The NHS proves you can deliver universal healthcare through government-run hospitals with government-employed doctors. Whether Americans would accept this level of government involvement is a political question, not a practical one.

The Common Elements

Look across these five radically different systems and three elements appear in all of them:

1. Universal coverage is mandatory: No country achieved universal healthcare by making coverage optional. Switzerland mandates private insurance purchase. Canada, UK, and Germany make coverage automatic. Singapore mandates savings accounts. Optional systems leave people uninsured.

2. Government controls prices: Every successful system has government price regulation — either through direct price-setting (Canada, UK), negotiated fee schedules (Germany, Switzerland), or strong government involvement in markets (Singapore). No successful system lets providers charge whatever they want.

3. Administrative simplicity: Whether single-payer (Canada), multi-payer (Germany, Switzerland), or hybrid (Singapore), all successful systems have far less administrative complexity than the US. Doctors spend time on medicine, not insurance battles.

These aren’t liberal or conservative principles. They’re practical requirements proven across capitalist Switzerland, socialist UK, and everything in between.

What About Innovation?

The standard American objection: “But we lead the world in medical innovation! Universal healthcare would kill that!”

The data says otherwise:

Medical patents per capita (2010-2020):

  • Switzerland: 132 per million people

  • Netherlands: 89 per million

  • Sweden: 87 per million

  • United States: 84 per million

  • Germany: 81 per million

Clinical trials per capita:

  • Belgium: 258 per million people

  • United States: 212 per million

  • Switzerland: 196 per million

Nobel Prizes in Medicine (2000-2024, per capita):

  • Switzerland and UK lead

  • US ranks 4th per capita

America produces significant medical innovation — but so do countries with universal healthcare. Switzerland has both the world’s most expensive universal healthcare system AND leads in medical patents per capita. The UK’s NHS runs major research hospitals that produce breakthrough discoveries.

Innovation happens where research funding, universities, and pharmaceutical companies invest — not where people go bankrupt from medical bills.

The Path Forward

Five countries. Five different approaches. All successful. All delivering universal coverage at half the American per-capita cost. All achieving better health outcomes.

Which model should America adopt? That’s the question for Part 5.

Spoiler: The answer isn’t “pick one and copy it.” The answer is: “Combine the best elements and craft a uniquely American solution.”

Switzerland proves you can have private insurance and market competition with universal coverage. Germany proves you can have public and private options coexisting. Singapore proves you can emphasize personal responsibility while maintaining a government safety net. Canada proves single-payer can work at scale across a vast, diverse country. The UK proves fully socialized medicine delivers care at the lowest cost.

In Part 5, we’ll examine the bipartisan compromise that nobody wants to talk about — a regulated multi-payer system combining Swiss-style private competition with German-style price controls. A system that gives Republicans the market competition they want and Democrats the universal coverage they demand.

The solutions exist. The only question is whether America has the political will to implement them.
