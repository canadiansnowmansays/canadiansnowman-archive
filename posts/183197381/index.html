How Mar-a-Lago Became the Pipeline

Part 19 of The Epstein Files: Inside the Recruitment System Trump Now Can’t Deny

January 2, 2026

January 2, 2026

In 2019, aboard Air Force One, President Donald Trump made an admission that should have ended his ability to claim ignorance about Jeffrey Epstein’s activities.

Asked about Epstein recruiting at Mar-a-Lago, Trump said: “People from the spa were taken to work for Epstein.”

Not “allegedly.” Not “I heard rumors.” Taken. Past tense. Statement of fact.

Trump went on: Other employees complained. He told Epstein not to take “our people.” When Epstein did it again, he told him to get “out of here.”

That’s the President of the United States, on the record, confirming that his private club functioned as a recruiting ground for a sex trafficker. And he’s framing it as if the problem was Epstein poaching his employees, not that those employees were being sexually exploited.

Part 18 of this series documented how Trump’s “heroic ban” narrative collapses under scrutiny—how Epstein was banned from the spa, not the club; how Epstein himself said he wasn’t banned; how the membership wasn’t closed until Epstein’s 2007 guilty plea made the association toxic.

This installment examines what actually happened during those years. Not the myth Trump tells, but the machinery that former employees and newly resurfaced reporting have now documented.

Mar-a-Lago wasn’t just a backdrop in Jeffrey Epstein’s trafficking operation. For years, it was part of the supply chain.

The Recruitment Advertisement

It started with help wanted ads.

According to reporting and former employees, Mar-a-Lago advertised for spa attendants. The ads targeted young women, including college students. The job seemed legitimate: work at an exclusive club, good pay, proximity to wealth and glamour.

The dress code was specific. Multiple sources describe the spa attendant uniform as white miniskirts and tight polo shirts. This wasn’t accident. It was design.

Young women—many still teenagers, some in college, some looking for part-time work—were hired to staff the spa at one of the most exclusive clubs in Palm Beach. They wore uniforms designed to emphasize youth and sexuality. And then they were sent to Jeffrey Epstein’s house.

Not occasionally. Regularly. For years.

According to the Wall Street Journal’s reporting based on interviews with former Mar-a-Lago and Epstein employees, the spa sent its staff to Epstein’s nearby mansion for what were described as “massages, manicures, and other services.”

House calls. Off-site appointments. One-way traffic.

Epstein would visit Mar-a-Lago. But the young women who worked there would go to his house.

What Employees Knew

The employees weren’t ignorant about what these house calls meant.

According to the reporting, staff members at Mar-a-Lago warned each other about Epstein. They knew his reputation. They knew he was sexually aggressive. They knew he exposed himself. They knew these appointments felt less like spa services and more like something darker.

The Wall Street Journal uses the phrase “sexually suggestive behavior.” Former employees described encounters that were “sexually coercive and abusive.”

In plain language: These young women were being sent to a house where they would be sexually harassed and assaulted by a man the Mar-a-Lago staff knew was dangerous. And this went on for years.

Let that sink in. The warnings circulated among employees. The spa kept sending women to Epstein’s house anyway. And Trump—who owned the club, who was friends with Epstein, who attended parties with him, who flew on his plane—either didn’t know or didn’t care.

Neither possibility is exculpatory.

If Trump genuinely didn’t know that his spa was functioning as a pipeline to a sexual predator his employees were warning each other about, that reveals catastrophic negligence about what was happening at a club he personally owned and frequently visited.

If Trump did know and allowed it to continue until it threatened to become a scandal, that’s not negligence. That’s complicity.

The Eighteen-Year-Old

The arrangement continued until one young woman’s father forced it into the open.

According to the Wall Street Journal, an 18-year-old manicurist was sent to Epstein’s house for a house call appointment. When she returned, she reported to her managers that Epstein had pressured her for sex.

She told her supervisors. Her supervisors knew. And her father found out.

The reporting describes him as furious. He “raised hell.” Suddenly, there was talk of consequences. Suddenly, Epstein might face repercussions for what the spa staff had been quietly warning each other about for years.

But notice the sequence: The pipeline operated until a victim spoke up and her father made noise. Not until Trump discovered what was happening. Not until managers decided the practice was wrong. Until it threatened to become public.

This is the “incident” that supposedly prompted Trump’s heroic ban. An 18-year-old reports being pressured for sex. Her father explodes. And Mar-a-Lago’s response is to ban Epstein from the spa—not the club, as documented in Part 18—and hope that’s the end of it.

The house calls supposedly stopped in 2003, according to the Wall Street Journal’s reporting. But Epstein’s membership account remained open until October 2007. Four more years during which Epstein could attend events, visit the club, maintain his friendship with Trump.

The message this sent was clear: Sexually exploit our employees and we’ll ask you to stop using the spa. But you’re still welcome at the club. You’re still a member. You’re still a friend.

That’s not accountability. That’s accommodation with slightly tighter boundaries.

Trump’s Air Force One Confession

Trump’s 2019 comments aboard Air Force One deserve closer examination because they reveal how he thinks about what happened—and what that reveals about his culpability.

When asked about Epstein recruiting at Mar-a-Lago, Trump said:

“People from the spa were taken to work for Epstein. Other employees complained. I told Epstein, ‘Don’t take our people.’ And when he did it again, I said, ‘Out of here.’”

Parse that carefully:

“People from the spa were taken to work for Epstein.”

This confirms the pipeline. Young women who worked at Trump’s spa went to work for Epstein. Trump states this as established fact, not rumor or allegation.

“Other employees complained.”

Other employees. Not the women who were being sent to Epstein’s house—they’re described passively, as people who “were taken.” The complainants are “other employees,” suggesting staff who witnessed the pattern or heard about it secondhand.

This means the complaints didn’t just come from victims. The problem was visible enough that employees who weren’t directly affected felt compelled to raise concerns. The exploitation was that obvious.

“I told Epstein, ‘Don’t take our people.’”

Note the phrasing. Not “Stop exploiting young women.” Not “This is sexual harassment and it needs to end.” The framing is possessive: “our people.” The problem isn’t what Epstein is doing to them. It’s that he’s taking them from Trump.

It’s the language of property dispute, not moral outrage.

“And when he did it again, I said, ‘Out of here.’”

This suggests Trump gave Epstein a warning, Epstein ignored it, and only then did Trump take action. Which raises the obvious question: What happened to the young women Epstein exploited in the period between the first warning and the eventual ban?

Trump’s account makes him the hero of this story—the man who stood up to Epstein and kicked him out. But his own words reveal something else: years of accommodation, a warning that was ignored, continued access to the club and its employees, and action only after repeated violations forced his hand.

Virginia Giuffre

When reporters pressed Trump about whether Virginia Giuffre—who would later become one of Epstein’s most prominent accusers and would testify about being trafficked to Prince Andrew—was one of the Mar-a-Lago employees Epstein recruited, Trump said:

“I think she worked at the spa. I think she was one of the people.”

Then he added: “She had no complaints about us.”

This response is telling in multiple ways.

First, Trump “thinks” she worked at the spa. He’s not certain. But the vagueness isn’t exculpatory—it’s damning. It suggests there were enough young women being sent from his spa to Epstein’s operation that Trump can’t keep track of specific individuals.

Second, the claim that “she had no complaints about us” frames Mar-a-Lago as a customer service operation worried about its Yelp reviews. The issue isn’t whether Giuffre was satisfied with her employment. The issue is that she was trafficked from Trump’s club into Jeffrey Epstein’s sexual exploitation network while still a teenager.

Virginia Giuffre has stated publicly that she was working at Mar-a-Lago in 2000 when Ghislaine Maxwell recruited her. Court documents and testimony confirm she was 16 years old. Social Security records submitted during her defamation case against Maxwell showed she worked as a locker room spa attendant at Mar-a-Lago in summer 2000.

This is two years before Trump called Epstein “a terrific guy” who “likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are on the younger side” in his 2002 New York Magazine interview.

Trump knew Epstein had recruited from his club. He knew Giuffre had worked there. He knew when he praised Epstein publicly in 2002 that Epstein had already been exploiting young women from Mar-a-Lago for years.

And he called him terrific anyway.

The Machinery

What the Wall Street Journal and New York Post reporting—combined with Trump’s own admissions and court documents—now makes undeniable is that Mar-a-Lago wasn’t a passive setting in this story.

It was machinery.

The help wanted ads targeted young women and college students. The uniforms sexualized them. The house call system delivered them to Epstein’s mansion. The employees warned each other but the system continued. When victims reported abuse, the response was minimal: ban him from the spa but leave the membership active. When one victim’s father made noise, implement just enough restriction to contain the immediate scandal.

This is how trafficking operations work. Not through kidnapping and force in every case, but through systems that identify vulnerable young women, place them in positions where predators have access, and create plausible deniability for everyone involved.

The spa needed attendants. The attendants needed jobs. The club offered good pay and proximity to wealth. Epstein was a member and a friend of the owner. House calls were part of the job. If something inappropriate happened, well, did you complain to your manager? Did your father raise hell? No? Then the system continues.

This isn’t speculation. This is what former employees and reporting have now documented. And Trump’s own words confirm it.

What’s Still Hidden

Among the 5.2 million pages of Epstein-related documents the Department of Justice now claims exist—as documented in Part 17 of this series—are almost certainly records that would provide a fuller picture of Mar-a-Lago’s role.

Employment records showing exactly who worked at the spa and when. Membership records showing when Epstein’s account was actually closed. Communications between Trump, his staff, and Epstein about the house call arrangement. Complaints from employees about what was happening. Reports from the managers who were told by the 18-year-old manicurist that Epstein had pressured her for sex.

The files likely contain documents about Virginia Giuffre’s employment. About Maxwell’s recruiting activities at Mar-a-Lago. About how many other young women were sent to Epstein’s house over the years. About what Trump knew and when he knew it.

These documents are supposed to be public under the Epstein Files Transparency Act passed by Congress with overwhelming bipartisan support. They were supposed to be released by December 19, 2025. Instead, the DOJ has violated federal law every day since, claiming it needs more time to review documents it somehow didn’t know existed until after the deadline passed.

The number keeps growing—100,000 pages released, then 1 million claimed, then over 2 million discovered, now 5.2 million announced—each new revelation providing justification for continued delay.

Meanwhile, Trump has ordered the DOJ to investigate Bill Clinton’s “involvement and relationship” with Epstein while simultaneously claiming the Epstein files release is a “Democrat inspired hoax” distracting from his administration’s priorities.

The pattern is unmistakable: weaponize the files against opponents, suppress information about himself, and delay until the public loses interest or enforcement becomes impossible.

The Question That Remains

The Wall Street Journal and New York Post reporting, Trump’s Air Force One admissions, and the documented timeline have now established several facts beyond dispute:

  1. Mar-a-Lago’s spa sent young women to Epstein’s house for years

  2. Employees warned each other about Epstein’s sexual aggression

  3. An 18-year-old reported being pressured for sex

  4. The “ban” was from the spa, not the club

  5. Epstein’s membership remained active until his 2007 guilty plea

  6. Virginia Giuffre was trafficked from Mar-a-Lago

  7. Trump praised Epstein two years after knowing about the recruitment

The question that remains is simpler than the one Trump wants us to ask.

He wants us to ask: “Did Trump know Epstein was a pedophile?”

The right question is: “When Trump learned that his spa was sending young women to Epstein’s house, that employees were warning each other about sexual harassment, that an 18-year-old had been pressured for sex—what did he do?”

The answer, documented by his own former media allies and confirmed by his own words: As little as possible. Ban him from the spa. Leave the membership active. Maintain the friendship. Keep praising him publicly. Take meaningful action only when the guilty plea made the association too toxic to maintain.

That’s not heroism. That’s not moral clarity. That’s accommodation that extended years beyond the point where any reasonable person would have ended the relationship entirely.

Congress reconvenes January 5. The 5.2 million pages remain mostly sealed. The machinery that operated at Mar-a-Lago is now documented by Trump’s former allies and confirmed by his own admissions.

The test continues. The files matter. And the lies are getting harder to hide.
