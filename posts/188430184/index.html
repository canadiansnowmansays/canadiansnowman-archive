Let's Talk Texas

The Farm Team Problem

Part 4: Why Democrats Keep Lighting Money on Fire in Texas

February 25, 2026

Beto O'Rourke's 2018 Senate campaign raised over $80 million and drew crowds like this across Texas. Four years and two campaigns later, Democrats still hadn't won a single statewide office.

CanadianSnowmanSays · Let’s Talk Texas · February 2026

In 2010, the Republican State Leadership Committee spent roughly $30 million on state legislative races across the country. It wasn’t glamorous spending. No celebrity candidates. No viral moments. No breathless cable news coverage. They targeted specific seats in specific states, timed to the census year, with a single strategic objective: control the redistricting process.

It worked. Republicans flipped nearly 700 state legislative seats that November. They took control of redistricting in state after state, drew maps that locked in their advantages for a decade, and built the structural foundation that would sustain Republican power through two presidential losses and one insurrection.

Thirty million dollars. That’s less than Beto O’Rourke raised in a single quarter of his 2022 gubernatorial campaign in Texas — a race he lost by 11 points.

The difference between those two investments tells you everything about why Democrats keep losing Texas.

The Love Problem

Heather Williams, president of the Democratic Legislative Campaign Committee — the party’s campaign arm for state legislative races — put it to the New York Times with surgical precision: “Democrats like to fall in love with a candidate — or they fall in love with the possibility of Texas or Florida. When they fall in love, collectively, they put enormous sums and resources in there, whether the data tells us there is a path or not.”

The pattern is by now painfully familiar. A charismatic Democrat emerges in a red state. National media gets excited. Donors in New York and California open their chequebooks. Money floods in. The candidate becomes a national brand. And then they lose, because charisma doesn’t build the precinct-level infrastructure required to win in hostile territory — and because the money disappears the moment the election is over, leaving the state party weaker than before.

Texas has been the primary victim of this cycle. Beto O’Rourke raised over $80 million for his 2018 Senate race and came within three points of Ted Cruz — the closest a Democrat had come to winning statewide in Texas in a generation. It felt like a breakthrough. It was a mirage. Two years later, MJ Hegar raised $24 million to challenge John Cornyn and lost by ten. O’Rourke came back for the 2022 governor’s race, raised even more, and lost by 11. Each time, the national money came in like a tide, then receded, leaving behind neither infrastructure nor institutional memory.

“Groups come in, and the first thing they’ll do is a press release and then a little social media thing about, ‘We’re coming into Texas, and we’re going to flip it!’” said Matt Angle of the Lone Star Project. “They just undermine every penny they’re going to spend in the state.”

The Missing Alpha

Here’s the structural problem that no amount of money can solve by itself: Texas Democrats have not held a single statewide office since the early 1990s. That’s over thirty years without a governor, lieutenant governor, attorney general, or any of the statewide positions that serve as the natural leadership tier of a state party.

In practical terms, this means there’s no one in charge. No “alpha voice,” as Angle calls it, to referee conflicts between competing factions, coordinate between national and state operations, allocate resources strategically, or tell an out-of-state donor group that their messaging is wrong for Texas.

The party’s U.S. Senate nominee typically becomes the de facto leader for a cycle — but only for the length of the campaign. Once the election is over, win or lose, the organizational authority evaporates. There’s no continuity. No institutional memory. No one minding the store between elections.

Republicans, by contrast, have a deep bench of statewide officeholders, each with their own political operation, donor network, and media presence. When the state party needs coordination, there are multiple power centres capable of providing it. The asymmetry is staggering, and it compounds over time.

The Down-Ballot Desert

The consequences of the love problem and the leadership vacuum show up most clearly at the bottom of the ballot — which is exactly where they matter most.

For decades, Texas Democrats have been handing Republicans uncontested seats in state legislative races, local judicial races, and school board races. These aren’t trivial positions. State legislators draw congressional maps. Judges decide voting rights cases. School board members shape the educational environment for the next generation of voters. Every uncontested seat is a free win for Republicans and a lost opportunity for Democrats to build name recognition, train campaign staff, develop donor lists, and identify future candidates for higher office.

This is what Williams at the DLCC means when she says Republicans “think more strategically than Democrats about the relationship between all of the ballot levels — how they interplay and impact power and results.” Republicans understand that a state house seat is a pipeline to a congressional seat, which is a pipeline to a Senate seat. Democrats focus on the Senate seat and wonder why they don’t have anyone to run for it.

Williams’s observation cuts deeper: “Republicans are less focused on who the individual candidate is and whether they have star power and more on the results they can bring.” The Republican Party recruits candidates to fill structural needs. The Democratic Party waits for stars to emerge. One is a strategy. The other is a wish.

Blue Texas

Which brings us to the most interesting experiment in Texas Democratic politics in years — and possibly the most important one in the country.

The Blue Texas project, led in part by former state senator Wendy Davis, is an attempt to break the boom-bust cycle by building permanent infrastructure. Not for one candidate. Not for one cycle. For the long term.

The project has secured multi-year funding commitments from George Soros and at least one other major anonymous donor — not for a specific race, but for the kind of institutional capacity that Republicans have maintained in Texas for decades: data operations, field staff, candidate recruitment, voter contact programs, and coordination between the various Democratic entities that currently operate in isolated silos.

The early results are modest but structurally significant. For the first time since at least 1994, Texas Democrats have fielded candidates in every federal race and every state legislative, statewide judicial, and State Board of Education race on the 2026 ballot.

Read that sentence again. For thirty years, Democrats have been leaving seats uncontested — effectively conceding entire categories of governance to Republicans without requiring them to campaign, spend money, or defend their records. The simple act of filling every ballot line changes the competitive landscape in ways that don’t show up in a single election cycle but compound over time.

A contested race forces a Republican incumbent to spend money and time that would otherwise go to helping colleagues in competitive districts. It creates a campaign structure — volunteers, donor lists, voter data — that can be reactivated in the next cycle. It gives the local media a reason to cover both sides of an issue. And occasionally, in a wave year, a candidate who wasn’t supposed to win does.

The Canadian Comparison

I keep thinking about what Canadians can contribute to this conversation — not answers, exactly, but pattern recognition.

Canada has had its own versions of the farm team problem. The federal NDP spent decades trying to break through in Alberta, a province so conservatively dominant that its government was literally called the Progressive Conservatives for over forty years. The NDP won a shocking provincial majority in 2015 — not because of one charismatic leader, though Rachel Notley certainly was one, but because decades of local organizing, candidate development, and incremental gains in municipal and provincial races had built the infrastructure to capitalize when the conservative coalition finally fractured.

The parallel isn’t perfect. Alberta’s NDP lost power four years later, and the structural challenges in Texas are different from those in Alberta. But the principle holds: you can’t win a breakthrough election without the organizational foundation to exploit it when the opportunity arrives. The NDP had that foundation because they’d been building it for twenty years, even when nobody was paying attention.

The Quebec Liberals offer another example, though a more cautionary one. After the sponsorship scandal devastated the federal Liberal brand in Quebec, the party essentially disappeared from the province for a decade. Rebuilding required sustained investment in local riding associations, candidate recruitment, and community presence — the unglamorous work that nobody writes about and nobody funds unless someone makes a structural commitment to the long game.

Texas Democrats are trying to make that commitment now. The question is whether the party’s national donors and activists have the patience for it — or whether the next charismatic candidate will trigger another boom-bust cycle that undermines everything Blue Texas is trying to build.

What 2026 Will Tell Us

The November midterms won’t determine whether Blue Texas has succeeded. That’s a five-to-ten-year question. But they will produce data points that tell us whether the strategy is working.

Watch the margins, not just the outcomes. If Democratic candidates in historically uncontested state house races pull 40 or 42 percent instead of the zero percent they were getting by default, that’s infrastructure working. If Latino turnout ticks up in South Texas districts where Democrats are now actively campaigning for the first time in years, that’s outreach working. If the Senate race is within five points — regardless of who wins the Democratic primary — that’s a political environment that justifies sustained investment.

And watch the down-ballot judicial races. Texas elects its judges, and judicial elections have historically been low-information, straight-ticket affairs that Republicans win by default. If Blue Texas’s full-slate strategy produces any judicial upsets — even one or two — it will validate the theory that contesting every seat changes the competitive dynamics in ways that accumulate over time.

The worst-case scenario for Texas Democrats isn’t losing in November. They’ve lost before and they’ll lose again. The worst case is winning a glamorous Senate race while neglecting the structural investments that would make the next cycle’s races winnable too. That’s the Beto trap: a thrilling near-miss that sucks all the oxygen out of the room and leaves nothing behind when the cameras move on.

The best-case scenario is boring. It’s a permanent operation that recruits candidates, trains volunteers, maintains voter files, and contests every seat — cycle after cycle, whether the national media is paying attention or not. It’s the $30 million that Republicans spent in 2010, replicated on the Democratic side, sustained over years, and treated not as a campaign expense but as a capital investment.

Baby steps. But in Texas, baby steps have been bigger than the grand gestures for a very long time.
