F* With Your Algorithm Before It F*s With You

They’re All Selling You Something—Left, Right, and Center

October 18, 2025

Look, I’m Bill Maher’s biggest fanboy this side of the HBO green room. I’ve got his books dog-eared on my nightstand, his monologues queued up like therapy sessions, and yeah, I’ve defended his “both sides” schtick in more bar arguments than I care to admit. The guy’s a liberal atheist curmudgeon who roasts everyone equally, and in this polarized hellscape, that’s my kind of religion. So when he drops a truth bomb in his latest Real Time monologue, I listen. Hell, I evangelize.

I caught myself doing it again yesterday. Four articles deep into my rage-scroll, each one confirming what I already believed, each one making me angrier than the last. The algorithm had served up exactly what it knew I wanted: validation wrapped in outrage, with a side of smug superiority. I was hate-reading my way through lunch, nodding along to every predictable point, when something Bill Maher said in yesterday’s monologue hit me like a cold splash of water.

In his monologue about media manipulation, Maher called out both Fox News and liberal outlets for keeping people in “information ghettos.” But his solution was what stuck with me: “F*** with the algorithm. F*** with it.” His point was simple—if you’re a liberal, search for Morgan Wallen tickets. If you’re conservative, check out RuPaul’s Drag Race recaps. Confuse the digital overlord that thinks it has you figured out.

But here’s what Maher missed, and what I’ve been noticing more and more: It’s not just the algorithm anymore. It’s an entire influence economy built on top of it, with paid personalities across every part of the political spectrum who profit from your predictability. They’re all selling you something—left, right, and center—and the algorithm is their best sales associate.

The Algorithm-Influencer Industrial Complex

Let me explain how this unholy marriage works without getting too technical (because, like Maher, I’m not a virgin or a Bond villain on the spectrum). The algorithm is essentially a pattern-recognition machine. It watches what you click, how long you stay, what makes you comment, share, or buy. Every action teaches it more about your triggers, your biases, your weak spots.

But here’s where it gets insidious: That data doesn’t just customize your feed—it creates a blueprint for influence. Enter the new breed of content creators, podcasters, and “independent journalists” who know exactly how to push your buttons because the algorithm has already done the research for them.

The most disturbing part? The algorithm doesn’t require you to actually understand what you’re amplifying. A left-wing podcaster captured this perfectly the other day during a street interview with a twenty-something guy wearing a “Democrats are dumb” t-shirt. The kid was so confident in his message that he’d made it his literal uniform. But when the podcaster asked him to name the three branches of government, all he could manage was “Legislative” before drawing a blank.

That’s the algorithm’s dark genius: It can make you passionate about positions you don’t even understand. This guy felt strongly enough to wear his politics on his chest but couldn’t name the basic structure of the government he was criticizing. The algorithm fed him the conclusion—”Democrats bad”—without bothering with the education that might lead to informed opinion.

And before my liberal readers get too smug, remember the “Defund the Police” activists who can’t explain what community policing alternatives would actually look like, or climate activists who can’t describe the difference between weather and climate. The algorithm serves everybody pre-chewed talking points, no thinking required.

This isn’t a partisan problem—it’s an American problem. Maher showed this brilliantly with those viral TikTok videos of Americans who think humans walked on the sun or that Asia is a city. We have full-grown adults who believe Queen Elizabeth was from Egypt. The algorithm doesn’t care if you’re a conservative who can’t name the three branches of government or a liberal who thinks Europe is the world’s biggest city. It just needs you dumb enough to click, share, and buy.

The political ads Maher showcased reveal how campaigns have adapted to this reality. Conservatives just shoot guns at things they don’t like. Liberals avoid even saying they’re liberal—as if they’re embarrassed by their own brand. Both sides have given up on educating voters. Why explain policy when you can just show yourself in a hard hat pointing at things with your thumb? As one candidate literally said: “Truck. Gun. Me good.”

The algorithm rewards this race to the bottom. Complex explanations don’t go viral. Nuanced takes don’t get engagement. But “I’m going to blow away the Democrat socialist agenda” while literally shooting a gun? That’s algorithmic gold. The dumber the content, the wider it spreads.

On the left, you’ve got breadtube personalities who start with legitimate political commentary but somehow always end up selling you their branded merchandise, Patreon subscriptions, and whatever supplement their sponsor is pushing this week. “Smash capitalism,” they say, “but first, use my discount code for Athletic Greens.”

On the right, it’s Daily Wire types who begin with conservative talking points but inevitably pivot to their online store full of “liberal tears” tumblers and tactical gear you’ll never need. “Fight the woke mob,” they declare, “with this $89.99 patriot survival kit.”

And don’t get me started on the wellness influencers who’ve discovered that political conspiracy theories drive more engagement than yoga poses. Or the finance bros who realized that crypto advice mixed with libertarian politics is the perfect algorithm cocktail.

The economics are brutally simple: Every click generates data. Data enables targeted influence. Influence drives purchases—whether that’s products, subscriptions, or ideologies. Everyone’s making money except you.

The Manipulation Playbook

The playbook is so predictable it hurts. Here’s how they get you:

First, the algorithm identifies your lean. Maybe you clicked on one AOC tweet or shared one Babylon Bee article. That’s all it needs.

Second, it starts serving you content from influencers who speak your language. They feel authentic, relatable. They’re “just like you”—only with better lighting and suspicious income streams.

Third, the parasocial relationship develops. You start feeling like this person is your friend, your guide through the confusing political landscape. You trust them. You defend them in comments sections.

Fourth, the product placement begins. Sometimes it’s literal products—books, courses, supplements. Sometimes it’s ideological products—talking points, outrage topics, enemies lists. But it’s always something they’re selling.

Fifth, you become a predictable consumer, voter, and amplifier. You’re not thinking anymore; you’re just reacting according to the program.

I watched this happen in real-time with political influencers who started as genuinely independent voices. Take any popular political commentator with over 100K followers. Track their content from two years ago versus today. The transformation is almost always the same: They get sharper, angrier, more predictable. Why? Because that’s what the algorithm rewards, and that’s what sponsors pay for.

The wellness-to-QAnon pipeline? That’s algorithm-driven. The Bernie bro-to-tankie evolution? Algorithm. The libertarian-to-alt-right slide? You guessed it—algorithm, amplified by influencers who profit from pushing people further down the rabbit hole.

My Own Glass House

Time for some humility. I’m writing this essay, but I’m not above it. None of us are.

Last year, I bought a water filter because a podcast host I respect mentioned—totally organically, I’m sure—that tap water was full of chemicals. The filter cost $300. It sits unused under my sink, a monument to my suggestibility.

I’ve parroted talking points verbatim from my favorite political commentators, feeling smart and informed, only to realize later I was just a human retweet. The worst part? The moment of realization when you’re mid-argument and suddenly hear yourself sounding exactly like that YouTube video you watched last night.

There was that month I got really into a Substack writer who seemed to have everything figured out. I was quoting them at parties, sharing every article, considering their paid subscription. Then I noticed something: Their takes were getting more extreme, their enemies list longer, their certainty absolute. The algorithm had shaped them just as much as it had shaped me.

We all live in glass houses. Admitting it is the first step to doing something about it.

The Real Cost

What are we losing to this manipulation machine?

Independent thought, for starters. When was the last time you formed an opinion without checking what your favorite influencer thinks? When did you last read something that challenged your worldview without immediately seeking a debunking video?

But we’re not just losing independent thought—we’re losing basic knowledge. When political campaigns can succeed with ads that are just “Truck. Gun. Vote me,” it’s because they know their audience has been algorithmically reduced to pure emotion, no intellect required. As Maher pointed out, candidates literally say things like “I’m so conservative” over and over, or avoid saying “liberal” at all, because actual policy discussion would require voters to know things. And increasingly, they don’t.

We’re losing curiosity about “the other side.” The algorithm ensures we see opponents only at their worst, through the lens of someone who profits from making them look terrible. That neighbor with different political views? The algorithm wants you to see them as an enemy, not a human.

We’re losing money—all those influenced purchases, subscriptions, donations to causes we barely understand but feel emotionally manipulated into supporting. The political merchandise, the courses, the “fight back against [insert enemy]” fundraisers.

We’re losing our mental health to the constant outrage-validation cycle. Cortisol spikes from anger-bait. Dopamine hits from likes and shares. We’re addicts, and influencers are our dealers.

Most critically, we’re losing the ability to be citizens in a democracy. Democracy requires people who can think, evaluate, and sometimes change their minds. The algorithm-influencer complex needs predictable consumers who react on cue. When grown adults think someone walked on the sun, or can’t name basic facts about their own government, we’re not just polarized—we’re infantilized.

The Resistance Playbook: F***ing With Your Algorithm

So how do we f*** with it? Maher’s suggestions are a start—liberals searching for Morgan Wallen tickets, conservatives browsing RuPaul content. But let’s go deeper.

Try the “opposite day” experiment. For one day each week, deliberately engage with content from “the other side.” Not hate-watching—genuine engagement. Try to understand one point they’re making. Your algorithm will freak out.

Follow one “enemy” account with genuine curiosity. Not to mock or argue—to understand. Watch how quickly the algorithm tries to pull you back to your comfort zone with increasingly extreme content from “your side.”

Take a 24-hour influence fast. No podcasts, no political YouTube, no pundit Substacks. Just primary sources—actual bills, court documents, unedited speeches. It’s boring. It’s also enlightening.

Create deliberate confusion in your data profile. Search for things that don’t fit your pattern. Watch a documentary on a topic you know nothing about. Buy something completely outside your demographic. Be unpredictable.

Practical resistance:

  • Use incognito mode for politically charged searches

  • Clear your cookies and cache weekly

  • Unfollow all influencers for one month and see what happens

  • Read entire articles, not just headlines and tweets

  • When someone shares content, ask: “Who profits from me believing this?”

Most importantly, when an influencer—any influencer—tells you to buy something, ask yourself: Would I want this if they hadn’t mentioned it? The answer is usually no.

Beyond Individual Action

Personal resistance is necessary but not sufficient. We need structural change.

Algorithm transparency laws would force platforms to show us why we’re seeing what we’re seeing. Disclosure requirements would make influencers reveal their funding sources. Digital literacy education would teach kids (and adults) how to recognize manipulation.

We need platform accountability—real consequences for algorithms that radicalize for profit. We need antitrust action against tech monopolies that control what billions of people think about.

But waiting for systemic change while doing nothing personally is like waiting for climate policy while running your AC with the windows open. Start with yourself.

The Choice

Here’s the bottom line: You can remain a predictable mark, a reliable consumer of whatever flavor of influenced content your algorithm has determined you’ll swallow. You can keep letting your thoughts be shaped by people who profit from your predictability.

Or you can choose digital rebellion.

The algorithm wants you passive, scrolling, reacting, buying. It wants you sorted into neat categories—liberal, conservative, wellness warrior, crypto bro—because categories are profitable.

F*** with it. Be unsortable. Read what you’re not supposed to read. Click on what doesn’t fit your profile. Follow people who disagree with you and actually listen. Buy nothing from anyone with a discount code.

The powers that be—tech companies, influencers, political operators—depend on your predictability. Your unpredictability is revolution.

So here’s my challenge: Share this article with someone who disagrees with you politically. Not to convert them, not to prove a point, but to start a conversation about how we’re all being played.

Because whether you’re team red or team blue, team wellness or team science, team whatever—we’re all team algorithm in the end. And the house always wins.

Unless we f*** with it.

Time to choose chaos. Time to choose curiosity. Time to reclaim your own mind.

The algorithm is watching. Give it something interesting to see.
