The Iran Crisis

The Man Who Cannot Be Named

Part XXXIV: Day Seven: March 6, 2026

March 6, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays | March 6, 2026

The most intense strikes on Lebanon since the 2024 ceasefire. And on Tehran. Simultaneously. Night seven.

Shortly after 11 p.m. Thursday in Beirut, the first explosions rolled across the city like thunder. People rushed to their balconies. The bombing continued for hours. Three buildings in the Dahiya — the dense, heavily populated southern suburbs of Beirut where Hezbollah has long held sway — collapsed entirely. Thousands of people who had not yet fled despite the day’s evacuation warnings now did, at midnight, carrying thin mattresses, a few utensils, whatever they could grab as the television was still on or the Ramadan fast-breaking meal was still on the stove. By dawn, hundreds were sleeping under roundabouts. Others huddled along a seaside promenade in parked cars. A woman named Fatima Ibrahim, who had fled earlier in the day, had already asked: “Where shall we go?”

There was no answer Thursday night. There is no answer Friday morning.

At the same time the Dahiya was burning, the Israeli Air Force was conducting a “broad-scale wave of strikes” against Tehran. Dozens of residents texted the New York Times to report more than a dozen large explosions shaking the central and eastern parts of the city. Iran’s state television said the strikes targeted the compound of the assassinated Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei — bombed again, a week after he was killed there — as well as areas near the presidential palace and the National Security Council. A medical clinic, a gas station, a parking lot, two residential buildings were destroyed.

This is Day Seven. The war that was supposed to last “four to five weeks” — the outer boundary of Trump’s stated timeline — has just completed its first of them. In that week: two countries simultaneously bombed in a single night’s air campaign, Beirut and Tehran struck in parallel, as Iran launched fresh drone and missile salvos at Tel Aviv in response, sending air-raid sirens across the city before dawn Friday.

“It’s uncontrollable, it’s chaos, it’s disarray, disorder,” said UN human rights chief Volker Türk on Friday. “The world absolutely cannot afford it.”

What the world cannot afford is precisely what it is getting.

I. The Man Who Cannot Be Named

Morteza Nikoubazl /NurPhoto / Getty

There is a man in Iran who is almost certainly going to be the next supreme leader. His name has been in every newspaper on earth for a week. The IRGC wants him. The Assembly of Experts has reportedly reached a consensus around him. He is the son of the man the United States killed seven days ago.

And he cannot be announced.

Iranian officials confirmed to the New York Times on Thursday that the succession announcement is being deliberately delayed out of security concerns — specifically because Donald Trump said the candidate was “unacceptable” and could be “eliminated,” and because Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz has already promised that whoever is appointed will be “an unequivocal target for elimination.”

The United States and Israel have created a situation with no precedent in modern statecraft: a nation cannot name its own head of state because doing so would mark him for assassination before he takes office.

“Iranian officials will try to delay the announcement of the new supreme leader as much as they can, to avoid a pre-emptive strike on him,” said Sina Azodi, director of the Middle East department at George Washington University. “But things are already in motion. The process has begun, a consensus reached, and Mojtaba is strongly favored.”

Trump’s own words on Thursday made the problem structurally permanent. He told Axios that Mojtaba was “a lightweight” and “an unacceptable choice,” and that he himself needed to be “involved in the appointment, like with Delcy in Venezuela.” He told Reuters he would be “all for” Kurdish forces waging an offensive against the Iranian government. He told the IRGC, in an East Room event with the Inter Miami soccer team standing behind him, to lay down their arms or face “guaranteed death.”

The president of the United States is publicly threatening to kill Iran’s prospective supreme leader while simultaneously announcing that he intends to determine who governs a country of 92 million people — and doing so with Lionel Messi in the background.

The Atlantic’s Karim Sadjadpour, writing Thursday, identified the structural impossibility at the heart of the Venezuela analogy Trump keeps reaching for: in Venezuela, Delcy Rodríguez maintained a private back-channel to the Trump administration before Maduro’s capture. She had the authority to pivot the entire state apparatus toward a new energy partnership with the West. “The post-Khamenei landscape in Iran lacks any such singular, empowered interlocutor,” Sadjadpour writes. “Those who want to make amends with America cannot deliver, while those who may be able to deliver do not want to make amends.”

Graeme Wood in The Atlantic added the theological dimension: Mojtaba is not even a hojjat al-Islam — a journeyman cleric — let alone an ayatollah. He has no religious credentials whatsoever. One acquaintance told Wood before the war that Mojtaba was “the most dangerous man in the world” and considerably more violent and ideological than his father. An acquaintance also noted, in leaked diplomatic cables from 18 years ago, complaints that Mojtaba had grown too powerful running his father’s office. Appointing him would be, Wood writes, “a final act of self-delegitimation for a regime that already lacks legitimacy in the eyes of most Iranians.”

And yet he appears to be exactly who the IRGC wants. A man with no religious legitimacy, no public mandate, billions in allegedly laundered overseas assets, and a life expectancy — given Israel’s stated intentions — that one analyst measured in “weeks or even days.”

The succession that cannot be announced. The leader who cannot be named. The war that cannot be called a war. Day Seven has a theme.

II. Two Cities, One Night

Explosions and heavy smoke engulf parts of Tehran following U.S.-Israeli strikes targeting state compounds, including near the site where Khamenei was previously killed.

The simultaneous bombardment of Beirut and Tehran overnight was the most intense coordinated air campaign since the war began. In Beirut, the Israeli military confirmed striking 10 high-rise buildings used by Hezbollah, along with several command centers. In Tehran, the strikes targeted not military installations but the power centers of the state itself — the compound where Khamenei was killed, the area around the institutions that govern Iran.

Lebanon’s death toll reached 123 on Thursday. The country’s prime minister, Nawaf Salam, said Friday that Lebanon was being “dragged further into an abyss” and had been drawn into a conflict “we did not seek and did not choose.” Israel is now ordering villages in the Bekaa Valley — a Hezbollah bastion in eastern Lebanon — to evacuate northward, indicating the campaign is expanding beyond Beirut into a second front within Lebanon itself.

Hezbollah, for the first time, issued a warning to Israeli residents within three miles of the Lebanese border to evacuate their homes. “Your army’s aggression against Lebanese sovereignty and innocent civilians, its destruction of civilian infrastructure and its forced displacement operation will not go unanswered,” the group said. It is the first such warning Hezbollah has issued to Israelis, and it signals the organization is preparing to escalate rather than absorb.

UN refugee agency figures: 100,000 fled Tehran in the first two days. 84,000 Lebanese in collective shelters. The Lebanese PM’s phrase — “we did not seek and did not choose” — is the sentence that will define Lebanon’s position in the history books. The country had banned Hezbollah’s military activities. It had demanded Hezbollah disarm. None of it mattered. Israel struck anyway. Smotrich said the Dahiya would look like Khan Younis. The bombing has validated his threat.

The UN human rights office has now identified 13 attacks on Iranian health infrastructure, 2 verified school strikes with significant casualties, and mass displacement orders affecting hundreds of thousands across Lebanon. Volker Türk called for restraint, de-escalation, and a return to negotiations. No one in Washington or Jerusalem appears to be listening.

Iran’s President Pezeshkian posted Friday that “some countries have begun mediation efforts,” without naming them. He said Iran is committed to “lasting peace” but that mediation must address “those who underestimated the Iranian people and ignited this conflict.” China’s envoy Zhai Jun is in the region. Whether this represents a genuine diplomatic opening or a face-saving formulation ahead of more escalation, no one outside Tehran currently knows. Iran’s foreign minister said Thursday there is no reason to come to the negotiating table. The president’s Friday post is the first crack in that position. It may be nothing. It may be something. No one is positioned to find out.

III. The $50 Billion Gap

Before the war began, the United States and Israel spent at least a month preparing — assembling the largest carrier group deployment the Middle East had seen in decades. During that preparation, no one picked up a phone and called Ukraine.

This is the central revelation of the Atlantic’s Simon Shuster and Nancy Youssef investigation, published Thursday, and it belongs in the history of American military planning alongside the failure to plan for the Iraq occupation, the failure to foresee the Taliban’s speed, and every other catastrophic gap between preparation and reality that has defined the post-9/11 era.

Iranian Shahed-136 drones, the inexpensive “kamikaze” weapons launched in massive salvos, overwhelming high-cost defenses and driving the asymmetric warfare dynamic.

Ukraine has been fighting Iranian Shahed drones since the fall of 2022, when Iran sold Russia the designs. Ukrainian engineers have developed counter-drone systems — lasers, AI-enabled interceptor drones, the P1-Sun — some costing as little as $1,000, with a 90 percent success rate against Shaheds. They can produce the P1-Sun at 100,000 units per month. Oleksandr Kamyshin, Zelenskyy’s arms production adviser: “It’s our innovation. And I think it would be very useful for our partners right now in the Middle East.”

No one asked. “I have not received any direct requests,” Zelenskyy said Monday. “I have not discussed this with anyone.”

The US went to war against a country whose primary retaliatory weapon is a drone it has been firing at Ukraine for three and a half years, without consulting the country that has spent those three and a half years figuring out how to kill it cheaply and at scale.

What the US used instead: Patriot PAC-3 interceptors at $3 million or more each, against Shaheds costing $30,000. Apache helicopters. F-35 fighter jets. The most expensive counter-drone technology available, deployed against the cheapest attack drones in the world. A congressional official after a closed-door briefing: “There are not great defenses available to the U.S. military to defend against the Shahed. We have known this for a long time. We don’t have, at scale, good defenses against drones.”

Hegseth and Caine acknowledged the gap at the Pentagon briefing. Then Hegseth said the media was covering the drone deaths of six Americans “to make the president look bad.”

The preliminary Pentagon estimate of the war’s cost: $1 billion a day. The projected supplemental funding request to Congress: up to $50 billion.

Lockheed Martin produced 620 Patriot interceptors in all of 2025. Planned scaling: 2,000 per year. Iran has launched over 2,000 drones in the first week alone. The math is not difficult.

Phillips Payson O’Brien, writing in The Atlantic, identified what this looks like historically. When the Roman empire began to decline in the third century, the army still looked formidable. It had good equipment. It could march well. But it was no longer as advanced relative to its enemies as it had once been. A lone Iranian drone penetrated the US Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain and destroyed an AN/TPS-59 radar unit — a piece of hardware worth tens of millions of dollars — with a weapon that cost roughly $30,000. Three F-15E aircraft, among the most advanced the Air Force possesses, were shot down in a single friendly-fire incident over Kuwait. No crew members died — but how those planes, flying together, all came down at once has not been explained.

“The U.S. failure to deploy cheap and effective weapons against Iranian drones,” Shuster and Youssef write, “already looks like poor planning at best, and hubris at worst.”

UForce, the Ukrainian defense startup that aggregates the country’s counter-drone innovations, closed a $50 million seed round last month. Among the investors: Shield Capital, whose co-founder Raj Shah led a defense innovation unit inside the Pentagon during Trump’s first term. “Scaling this kind of proven capability is urgently relevant across the free world,” Shah said. The Pentagon, apparently, did not feel the same urgency — at least not before it started a war against the country whose drones it had been watching kill Ukrainians for years.

IV. “This War Started at a Time of Our Choosing”

Between 150,000 and 200,000 Americans live across the 14 countries now under State Department evacuation advisory. As of Thursday, the State Department had assisted 10,000. Roughly 20,000 had returned to the United States — a number that includes people who got home entirely on their own, without any government help.

Before the war began, the State Department issued no travel advisories warning Americans in the region that risk was increasing. US forces had been visibly massing for weeks. Trump had been warning publicly of a possible strike. None of this translated into a recommendation that Americans with non-essential reason to be in the Gulf, Lebanon, or surrounding countries might want to leave.

Ambassador Yael Lempert, who helped organize the evacuation of Americans from Libya in 2011, said it plainly: “This war started at a time of our choosing. It should have come as no surprise that airspace would close, and commercial flight options would be curtailed. It’s stunning there were no orders for authorized departure for nonessential U.S. government employees and family members in almost all the affected diplomatic missions — nor public recommendations to American citizens to depart — until days into the war.”

A State Department official said pre-emptively raising travel alerts would have led commercial airlines to cancel flights. The airlines canceled flights anyway, when the bombs fell and airspace closed. Over 20,000 flights to or from the Middle East have been canceled since Saturday. Qatar Airways remains entirely suspended. Kuwait, Bahrain, Iran, and Iraq airspaces remain closed. An El Al flight from Geneva circled in midair for half an hour Thursday night while air-raid sirens sounded at Ben Gurion Airport.

The consular affairs bureau is led by Mora Namdar — a career lawyer with approximately one year of State Department experience. Egypt, Iraq, Kuwait, Pakistan, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates currently lack Senate-confirmed US ambassadors. The American Foreign Service Association noted that the mass layoffs, budget cuts, and early retirements since January 2025 “have left many of these embassies and the offices that support them critically understaffed,” and that specialists in Farsi and Arabic — the languages of the countries being bombed — have been among those lost.

The hotline the State Department published — 1-202-501-4444, the number Rubio urged media to broadcast — didn’t work when it was announced. It gave an automated message saying the US government could not help get Americans out of the region. It was corrected hours later.

Jan Fluitt-Dupuy, a retiree from Washington State stranded in Abu Dhabi with her husband: days of calling the hotline produced “totally useless advice,” including instructions to leave by commercial means when commercial flights had already stopped. By Thursday, she thought she might have secured a government flight home. The timing remained unclear. She was afraid an “unusually robust barrage of attacks” audible from her hotel room might close the airspace again before it departed.

Linda Thomas-Greenfield, Biden’s UN ambassador, said she was “shocked” by the department’s failure. The Foreign Service union said the crisis “exposes real gaps in America’s diplomatic readiness.”

Trump and his officials spent years excoriating the Biden administration for the Kabul evacuation failure in August 2021. Biden’s people blamed the pandemic and the Taliban’s speed. Trump’s people had two months of visible preparation time, a war that started “at a time of our choosing,” and still left 150,000 Americans in 14 countries with a broken hotline, no pre-war travel advisories, and a consular chief with one year of experience.

V. Three Australians in the Indian Ocean

The IRIS Dena was returning from an international fleet review off Visakhapatnam, India, where it had participated in peacetime multilateral exercises celebrating freedom of navigation, when a US submarine torpedoed it in the Indian Ocean off Sri Lanka’s coast. Eighty-seven confirmed dead. Thirty-two rescued. Survivors treated at Galle National Hospital for burn injuries.

Three of the people on that US submarine were Australian.

Australia’s Prime Minister Anthony Albanese confirmed it Friday. The Australians were aboard as part of AUKUS integration — the pact under which Australia is set to receive US nuclear-powered submarine technology, and under which one in ten people on US nuclear attack submarines is currently Australian. They were present for training, Albanese said. They did not participate in “any offensive action against Iran.”

David Shoebridge, Australian Greens senator and long-standing AUKUS critic: “We have been dragged into this war without even a decision being made inside the cabinet room. This is an inevitability of AUKUS.”

Australia last year severed diplomatic ties with Iran over Iranian-orchestrated antisemitic attacks on Jewish institutions in Sydney and Melbourne. Albanese was among the first US allies to offer full public support for the operation against Iran. And now three of his citizens were on the submarine. The question of whether they participated in the kill is a distinction that may matter legally. Whether it will matter diplomatically — to the 90 sailors who died, to Iran, to the nations watching — is another question entirely.

The Indian Navy received a distress call from Sri Lanka Wednesday morning and joined the search and rescue effort, deploying maritime aircraft and ships. Prime Minister Modi has still not issued a public statement on the sinking. Iran’s foreign minister called the Dena “a guest of India’s Navy.” Modi visited Israel on February 24 and upgraded India’s relationship to a “special strategic partnership.” He is threading the needle between the country that just hosted the dead sailors and the country whose submarines killed them.

A park warden named S. Venkatesh, near the port where the fleet had docked, had shaken hands with the Iranian sailors days earlier. They had toured local landmarks, posed for photos, been served snacks. “I think more than half are dead by now,” he said.

VI. The War Comes to London

At 1 a.m. Friday, counterterrorism police arrested four men in north London on suspicion of helping Iran’s intelligence service conduct surveillance on locations and individuals linked to the Jewish community. One Iranian national, three dual British-Iranian citizens. Six other men detained in Harrow for assisting them. Searches ongoing at multiple addresses in Barnet, Watford, Wembley, and Harrow.

Britain’s MI5 director had warned last year that his organization tracked “more than 20 potentially lethal Iran-backed plots” in a single year. Britain names Iran alongside Russia and China as among the three greatest threats to the country’s security. Iranian reporters based in London have suffered physical attacks, threats, and surveillance. Scotland Yard’s counterterrorism commander said Iran has been “relying on criminal proxies — a network of groups and individuals paid for their services — to target people in Britain.”

The arrests on the seventh day of the war are not coincidental in timing. They are the domestic front of a conflict that has already spread from the Strait of Hormuz to the Indian Ocean to Beirut to London’s north suburbs. Azerbaijan is withdrawing its diplomats from Iran after drone strikes on Nakhchivan. NATO has raised alliance-wide alert levels. Pakistan’s foreign minister has reminded Iran of its mutual defense pact with Saudi Arabia. Bahrain arrested four people for posting social media videos expressing sympathy with Iranian strikes. An IRGC official appeared on state television to tell Iranians that a shoot-to-kill order has been issued for anyone returning to the streets to protest, and that they would be treated as agents of Israel.

“Nobody has spoken to you this directly,” the IRGC official said.

What is happening in London, what is happening in Azerbaijan, what happened in Sri Lanka’s coastal waters, what is happening in Iranian Kurdistan — these are not separate stories. They are the same story, which is that a war launched in the Middle East has no walls. It moves through intelligence networks, through shipping lanes, through diaspora communities, through submarine torpedo tubes, through alliances and their fine print, through the night skies over Beirut and Tehran and Tel Aviv, simultaneously, without pause.

VII. “Weeks to Months”

Qatar’s Energy Minister Saad al-Kaabi told the Financial Times something that has not received the attention it deserves: even if the fighting stopped immediately, it would take Qatar “weeks to months” to resume normal LNG deliveries.

Not days. Weeks to months. After a ceasefire that has not happened, from a conflict that shows no sign of ending.

Brent crude hit $87 Friday morning — up nearly 20 percent in one week, up over 40 percent for the year from $60 at year-end 2025. European natural gas is up 60 percent for the week. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell nearly 5 percent this week, its steepest weekly decline since Trump’s April 2025 tariff announcement. The European Central Bank is now expected to raise interest rates by a quarter point by year-end to combat war-driven inflation. Treasury Secretary Bessent announced a 30-day sanctions waiver allowing Indian refiners to buy Russian oil stranded at sea — reversing, in less than a week, the tariff and sanctions regime Trump spent six months building to stop India from buying Russian oil.

The logic is circular and clarifying. Trump went to war against Iran partly to cement energy dominance. The war has closed the Strait of Hormuz, spiked global oil prices, driven inflation expectations in both the US and Europe, forced the ECB toward rate hikes, crashed European equity markets, and compelled the administration to allow Russia — sanctioned Russia, the country Trump has been making concessions to for years — to sell more oil on the global market.

Iran’s strategy, as Sadjadpour explains it, is not to win militarily. It cannot win militarily. Its strategy is to spike global energy prices, soil the domestic political climate in the United States, and force Trump to choose between a protracted war and the pocketbooks of his voters. “Tehran’s hope,” Sadjadpour writes, “is that he will abruptly declare a hollow victory and abort the mission.”

At $87 a barrel and rising, at $3.25 gas and climbing, at 60 percent European natural gas increases in a single week — the strategy is working. Whether Trump’s pride will allow him to take the off-ramp, if one is offered, is the central question of the next several weeks. The man who said he doesn’t care about polling, who rated his own war performance “15 out of 10,” who stood with Inter Miami and told the IRGC to surrender or die — this is not a man whose psychology bends easily toward off-ramps.

But the economics do not negotiate. The 10-year Treasury yield is up the equivalent of a full Fed rate hike in one week. The S&P 500 is negative for the year. Foxconn’s chairman said Friday that if the war continues, “everyone will feel the effects of higher oil prices and raw materials.” Foxconn makes iPhones. The supply chain runs through the world Trump has set on fire.

VIII. The Humiliation of the Man Who Warned Us

J.D. Vance was not at Mar-a-Lago when the strikes began Saturday. The administration released a photo of him at the White House, flanked by a can of Diet Mountain Dew and a sullen-looking Tulsi Gabbard — the Director of National Intelligence, who during her 2020 presidential campaign sold T-shirts reading “No War With Iran.”

His X account, normally hyperactive, went silent for days.

In September 2024, Vance said that a war between Israel and Iran was “the most likely and most dangerous scenario” for provoking World War III. In another interview, he said America’s interest was “very much in not going to war with Iran. It would be a huge distraction of resources. It would be massively expensive to our country.” After Trump bombed Iran’s nuclear facilities last summer and declared success, Vance defended the action by saying: “This is not gonna be some long, drawn-out thing.”

It is, self-evidently, a long, drawn-out thing. Vance’s policy fingerprints are nowhere visible in it. The Idrees Kahloon profile in The Atlantic traces his diminishment across every portfolio he once owned — economic policy (Bessent), immigration (Miller), foreign affairs (Rubio and Witkoff). On Ukraine, Trump reversed the arms suspension Vance’s ally Elbridge Colby engineered within days. On antitrust, Vance’s former policy adviser Gail Slater lost her job. On family policy, the One Big Beautiful Bill delivered $2,200 per child rather than the $5,000 Vance had pushed.

The particular irony Kahloon identifies is precise: Vance warned that American missile interceptors needed to deter China were being burned through in Ukraine. He was right about the interceptors. He was wrong about where they’d be burned. They are being burned in a discretionary war in the Middle East — the one theater Vance explicitly said America should avoid.

The man who warned most clearly about this war is the man least able to stop it. The man who launched it stood with Messi’s soccer team and told Iran to surrender.

Conor Friedersdorf, surveying the full ledger in The Atlantic, counted the conflicts: Iran. Lebanon. Ecuador drug cartels. Somalia. Syria. Venezuela. Yemen Houthis. Nigeria Christmas Day strikes. Boat strikes in the Pacific and Caribbean that have killed at least 150 people. Ukraine — the one place the administration is trying to reduce involvement, though intelligence support continues. “The United States is now enmeshed in so many conflicts,” Friedersdorf writes, “that its foreign policy is closer to ‘world police’ than ‘America First.’”

Trump campaigned as the peace president. He complained he hadn’t won a Nobel Peace Prize. He is simultaneously conducting military operations on multiple continents, has launched the largest American military action since 2003, and is spending $1 billion a day on a war that three-quarters of Americans did not want.

IX. What Day Seven Has Established

One week in. Here is the ledger.

Beirut and Tehran bombed simultaneously in a single night. Three buildings collapsed in Dahiya. Thousands sleeping under roundabouts. The Lebanese prime minister saying his country was dragged into a war it did not choose. The Israeli finance minister promising it would look like Khan Younis. Israel now ordering Bekaa Valley villages to evacuate. Hezbollah warning Israelis within three miles of the border to leave. The first air-raid sirens in Tel Aviv in twelve hours sounding again Friday morning.

Iran’s next supreme leader cannot be announced because announcing him makes him a target. Trump says he must be involved in the choice. Iran’s foreign minister says there is nothing to discuss. Iran’s president says mediation has begun. Both cannot be equally true.

The war is costing $1 billion a day. The Pentagon may request $50 billion in supplemental funding. The US went to war against Iranian drones without calling the country that has been shooting them down cheaply for three years. Three F-15s were lost in a friendly-fire incident that remains unexplained. A $30,000 Iranian drone destroyed tens of millions of dollars of US radar equipment at the Fifth Fleet headquarters. Lockheed Martin can produce 2,000 Patriot interceptors per year. Iran has launched over 2,000 drones in seven days.

Three Australians were on the submarine. London had counterterrorism arrests at 1 a.m. Azerbaijan is withdrawing its diplomats. An IRGC official told Iranians the shoot-to-kill order has been issued for protesters. Qatar’s energy minister says it will take weeks to months to restore LNG deliveries after a ceasefire that has not happened.

150,000 Americans are in the evacuation zone. The State Department’s hotline didn’t work. The consular chief has one year of experience. Seven embassies have no Senate-confirmed ambassador. The career diplomats who know how to do this were laid off. The specialists in Farsi and Arabic are gone.

Brent crude at $87. European natural gas up 60 percent in a week. The ECB moving toward rate hikes. Trump reversing his own India sanctions policy to let Russia sell more oil.

One in four Americans supports the war.

Vance is in a photo with a Diet Mountain Dew.

“It’s uncontrollable,” said the UN human rights chief. “It’s chaos, it’s disarray, disorder, and the world absolutely cannot afford it.”

The man who cannot be named will eventually be named. The war that cannot be called a war will eventually be called one. The Strait that cannot be reopened will eventually reopen, or it won’t, and the price of that distinction will be paid at the pump, in the pension fund, in the grocery bill, by the voters whose pocketbooks Tehran is deliberately targeting as its primary strategic weapon.

Day 60 is April 28.

There are 53 days left.
