ABBA Against the Odds

Against the Odds

Part 9: The Gen Z Renaissance Why the Music Still Works

March 6, 2026

She wasn’t born when they recorded it. She wasn’t alive when they walked away. But when the algorithm served it up, she closed her eyes — and understood exactly what 100 million listeners before her already knew.
Some music doesn’t belong to a generation. It belongs to all of them.

On July 22, 2025, “Gimme! Gimme! Gimme! (A Man After Midnight)” crossed one billion streams on Spotify—45 years after its release. It became ABBA’s second song to join Spotify’s Billions Club, following “Dancing Queen” which hit the milestone in 2023.

The remarkable part wasn’t the achievement itself. Many classic songs from the 1970s and 1980s eventually reach streaming milestones as catalogs get digitized and older fans stream their favorite music.

The remarkable part was who was listening.

According to Spotify’s data, Gen Z listeners—people born between 1997 and 2012, who were literally not alive when ABBA disbanded in 1982—accounted for 50% of ABBA’s total streaming audience in 2025. Not 50% of new listeners. 50% of all listeners.

In 2025 alone, there were over 11 million first-time ABBA discoveries by Gen Z listeners on Spotify. That’s 11 million young people, most between ages 13 and 28, discovering a Swedish pop group that stopped performing before their parents were born.

This isn’t nostalgia. You can’t be nostalgic for something you never experienced. This is discovery—Gen Z finding ABBA’s music and deciding it’s good enough to stream repeatedly, add to playlists, share with friends, and incorporate into their musical identity.

Over the past five years, ABBA’s global streams have increased 150%. Their songs appear in approximately 130 million user-generated playlists. Their monthly listeners on Spotify number in the tens of millions.

A band that hasn’t released new material in decades (except the two songs for Voyage in 2021) is having their biggest streaming moment ever—driven primarily by the youngest generation of music listeners.

This is the ultimate validation of everything we’ve examined over the past eight weeks: the musical craftsmanship (Part 2), the strategic decisions (Parts 3-4), the emotional authenticity (Part 5), the business model (Part 6), the patient waiting (Part 7), and the innovative thinking (Part 8).

But it also raises the central question of this entire series: Why ABBA? Why does this music work for people born 30-40 years after it was created, who have completely different cultural references, musical vocabularies, and emotional contexts?

The TikTok Effect

Left: 1977. A disco floor, a disco ball, and a song you couldn't escape. Right: 2026. A bedroom, a screen, and the same song — found, not forced. The platform changed. The feeling didn't

The Gen Z discovery of ABBA happened primarily through TikTok.

Short-form video platform TikTok uses algorithms that recommend content based on engagement patterns rather than recency or familiarity. A song doesn’t need to be current to go viral on TikTok—it just needs to work for the content creators are making.

“Gimme! Gimme! Gimme!” found its moment on TikTok through dance videos, fashion content, and general vibes. The song’s driving disco beat, the dramatic vocal hook, the production clarity—it worked perfectly for TikTok’s short-form format.

Creators didn’t need to know anything about ABBA to use the song. They just needed to recognize it worked for their content. The algorithm recognized engagement and recommended the sound to more creators. A virtuous cycle began: more creators used it, more viewers heard it, more people sought out the full song on streaming platforms, more TikTokers discovered it through those platforms.

Several trending TikTok videos featured choreographed routines to “Gimme! Gimme! Gimme!” These went viral, accumulating millions of views and inspiring countless recreations. Each recreation introduced the song to new audiences who didn’t know ABBA but knew the TikTok trend.

This pattern repeated with other ABBA songs. “Dancing Queen” became a TikTok staple. “Mamma Mia” found new life. “Lay All Your Love on Me,” “Voulez-Vous,” and deeper catalog tracks got algorithmic boosts.

The mechanism is simple but powerful: algorithmic recommendation based on engagement quality rather than recency creates opportunities for old music to find new audiences. If the music is good enough—if it creates the emotional or aesthetic response that generates engagement—the algorithm doesn’t care when it was recorded.

ABBA’s music was good enough. Gen Z found it. The algorithm amplified it. The streams exploded.

The Absence of Baggage

One crucial factor in Gen Z’s ABBA embrace: they came to the music without cultural baggage.

For Boomers and Gen X, ABBA carried complex associations. The disco era. The sequined jumpsuits that became embarrassing in the post-disco backlash. The Eurovision origins. The perception—particularly in America—that they were lightweight pop, not “serious” music like rock or singer-songwriters.

Even ABBA’s 1990s revival among Millennials through ABBA Gold and Mamma Mia! carried some of that baggage. Loving ABBA was slightly ironic, slightly camp, slightly guilty pleasure. You loved them despite the cheese factor, not because of it.

Gen Z encountered ABBA without any of this context.

They didn’t live through disco or the disco backlash. They don’t have cultural memory of Eurovision as cheesy European spectacle. They weren’t told ABBA was embarrassing or that “real music” meant guitar-based rock. They don’t even have the Millennial relationship where ABBA is their parents’ music requiring ironic distance.

To Gen Z, ABBA is just... music. Old music, yes, but evaluated on the same terms as anything else: Does it sound good? Does it create the right mood? Does it work for what I’m doing?

And the answer, consistently, is yes.

This absence of baggage means Gen Z can appreciate ABBA purely on musical merit. The songs work or they don’t. The production quality is excellent or it isn’t. The emotional content resonates or it doesn’t.

Freed from historical context and cultural gatekeeping, Gen Z evaluated ABBA honestly—and decided the music was excellent.

What Works for Gen Z

So what specifically about ABBA’s music appeals to listeners born decades after it was created?

The production quality. ABBA’s meticulous production standards (Part 2) mean the songs don’t sound dated in the way much 1970s music does. There’s no excessive reverb, no muddy mixing, no period-specific production choices that scream “this is from 1979.” The vocals are clear. The instruments are distinct. The arrangements are clean. To modern ears conditioned by digital clarity, ABBA’s analog precision sounds contemporary.

The emotional complexity. Gen Z, dealing with climate anxiety, political instability, economic uncertainty, and social media’s mental health toll, connects with music that acknowledges complexity rather than offering simple answers. ABBA’s major/minor tension, their sophisticated lyrics about loss and resilience, their ability to be joyful while acknowledging sadness—this emotional sophistication resonates.

The vocal arrangements. In an era of auto-tuned, heavily processed vocals, Agnetha and Frida’s natural harmonies sound refreshing. Real voices, real harmonies, actual human performance—Gen Z, paradoxically, finds this “authentic” even though they’re listening to 45-year-old recordings.

The dance floor energy. ABBA’s best songs have driving rhythms and melodic hooks that work for clubs, parties, and solo dancing. Gen Z’s musical diet spans genres freely—they don’t feel constrained by genre boundaries the way previous generations did. Good dance music from any era works.

The melodic accessibility. ABBA’s melodies are memorable without being simple. You can sing along after one listen, but the songs reward repeated listening with subtle details. This accessibility matters for TikTok and casual listening while maintaining depth.

The lyrical universality. Songs about heartbreak, resilience, dancing, joy, loss, survival—these themes are timeless. Gen Z doesn’t need to understand 1970s Sweden to connect with “The Winner Takes It All.” Heartbreak in 2025 feels like heartbreak in 1980.

The absence of dated references. ABBA’s lyrics generally avoid topical references that date the songs. There are no Vietnam War references, no Watergate allusions, no period-specific slang. The songs exist in somewhat timeless emotional space.

The playlist culture compatibility. Gen Z builds playlists crossing genres and eras freely. A playlist might include ABBA, Billie Eilish, The Weeknd, Fleetwood Mac, Doja Cat, and David Bowie. This eclectic approach means old music doesn’t need to “fit” a single genre—it just needs to create the right mood.

The meme-ability. ABBA’s most dramatic moments—the “GIMME! GIMME! GIMME!” hook, the “DANCING QUEEN” explosion, the “MAMMA MIA!” exclamation—work perfectly as meme sounds and shareable moments.

What Doesn’t Matter to Gen Z

As important as what appeals to Gen Z is what they don’t care about:

They don’t care that ABBA won Eurovision. Eurovision means nothing to most American Gen Z. Even European Gen Z doesn’t view Eurovision victory as particularly prestigious—it’s campy fun, not artistic validation.

They don’t care about the sequined jumpsuits. Fashion cycling means what was embarrassing in the 1990s is retro-cool in the 2020s. Or more accurately, Gen Z doesn’t judge music by fashion.

They don’t care about genre purity. Previous generations had investment in rock vs. pop vs. disco hierarchies. Gen Z doesn’t. Good pop is good music. Period.

They don’t care that ABBA hasn’t toured in decades. They’re discovering the music through streaming, not expecting live performances. The catalog is what matters.

They don’t care about critical consensus. Rock critics spent decades dismissing ABBA as lightweight. Gen Z doesn’t read rock critics and wouldn’t care if they did. Streaming algorithms and peer recommendations matter more than critical authority.

They don’t care about authenticity narratives. Previous generations valued “authenticity”—artists writing their own songs, playing their own instruments, avoiding commercial compromises. Gen Z is more interested in whether the music works than whether it meets purity tests.

This freedom from historical baggage and cultural gatekeeping allowed Gen Z to encounter ABBA with fresh ears and make their own judgments—which turned out to be overwhelmingly positive.

The Streaming Data

One billion streams. Not because an algorithm pushed it. Because every generation keeps pressing play. ABBA didn’t chase the streaming era. The streaming era came looking for them.

The numbers tell the story:

6.7+ billion total Spotify streams for ABBA’s catalog as of late 2025. This is just Spotify—add Apple Music, YouTube Music, Amazon Music, and other platforms and total streams likely exceed 10-12 billion.

Two songs in Spotify’s Billions Club: “Dancing Queen” (2023) and “Gimme! Gimme! Gimme!” (2025). Multiple other songs approaching that threshold.

150% growth in five years. This isn’t plateau nostalgia—it’s accelerating discovery.

50% of streaming audience is Gen Z. The youngest generation is the largest cohort listening to ABBA in 2025.

130 million playlists feature ABBA songs. That’s 130 million different contexts where someone decided ABBA fit their musical moment.

Steady monthly growth. ABBA’s monthly Spotify listeners continue increasing year-over-year, driven primarily by new young listeners discovering the catalog.

These numbers aren’t good for a 50-year-old catalog. They’re good for any artist, current or historical. ABBA is competing successfully against contemporary acts for streaming attention—and winning.

The Algorithmic Advantage

Understanding ABBA’s Gen Z success requires understanding how streaming algorithms work differently from traditional music distribution.

Traditional distribution (radio, MTV, record stores) was inherently biased toward newness. Radio played current hits. MTV featured current videos. Record stores gave prime placement to new releases. Old music existed in “catalog” sections—physically separate, culturally distinct, harder to discover.

This meant old music had to overcome structural disadvantages. Unless you specifically sought out 1970s music, you wouldn’t encounter it casually.

Streaming algorithms don’t privilege newness. They privilege engagement. If a song generates strong engagement metrics—completion rates, playlist adds, replays, shares—the algorithm recommends it regardless of release date.

This creates opportunity for old music with high engagement to compete equally with new music. If “Gimme! Gimme! Gimme!” generates better engagement than a song released last week, the algorithm recommends “Gimme! Gimme! Gimme!”

ABBA benefits enormously from this algorithmic environment:

High completion rates. ABBA’s songs are 3-4 minutes, well-crafted, and engaging throughout. People listen to completion rather than skipping. Algorithms reward this.

High replay rates. ABBA’s songs reward repeated listening. People add them to regular rotation. Algorithms amplify this.

High playlist addition rates. People don’t just stream ABBA casually—they save songs to playlists for future listening. Algorithms recognize this as strong positive signal.

High sharing rates. People share ABBA songs—through TikTok, through direct playlist shares, through music discussions. Algorithms boost shared content.

Cross-generational appeal. When both a 60-year-old and a 16-year-old add “Dancing Queen” to playlists, algorithms recognize the song has unusually broad appeal and recommend it widely.

The algorithm doesn’t care that ABBA is old. It cares that ABBA generates metrics indicating high-quality content people want to hear.

The Irony of Innovation

There’s delicious irony in ABBA’s Gen Z renaissance.

In the 1970s, ABBA pioneered using video to reach audiences without physical presence (Part 3). They understood that visual media could carry their music globally without touring.

In the 2020s, algorithms and streaming are doing the same thing—carrying their music to global audiences without their involvement, without new releases, without marketing campaigns, without anything except the quality of the music itself.

ABBA spent their career finding ways to let the music do the work. Now, 40+ years after stopping, the music is doing exactly that—finding audiences, generating engagement, creating fans, building community—all without ABBA lifting a finger.

They built infrastructure (ownership of masters and publishing, Part 4) to capture value from their catalog. Now that catalog is generating value through mechanisms—streaming, algorithms, TikTok—that didn’t exist when they were active.

They made strategic decisions decades ago that are paying dividends in the 2020s through technologies they couldn’t have imagined.

That’s the ultimate validation of long-term thinking.

The Generational Handoff

What’s happening isn’t replacement—Boomers and Gen X abandoning ABBA for new music. It’s expansion—Gen Z joining existing fans to create multigenerational audience.

A grandmother, her daughter, and her granddaughter can all stream ABBA and attend ABBA Voyage together. Three generations, three completely different relationships to the music, three different discovery pathways—but all genuine fans.

The grandmother remembers buying ABBA albums in the 1970s, seeing them on TV, living through disco and the backlash. ABBA is memory, nostalgia, youth.

The daughter discovered ABBA through ABBA Gold in the 1990s, saw Mamma Mia! in theaters, has complicated relationship mixing genuine love with slight ironic distance. ABBA is guilty pleasure that became un-guilty.

The granddaughter discovered ABBA through TikTok in the 2020s, has no baggage or context, just thinks the songs are fire. ABBA is contemporary music that happens to be old.

Same music. Three completely different relationships. All valid. All generating streams, concert attendance, merchandise purchases.

This multigenerational appeal is extraordinarily rare. Most music from any era is generationally locked—meaningful to people who grew up with it, irrelevant to everyone else. The Beatles are an exception. Maybe Queen. Very few others achieve true cross-generational staying power.

ABBA has achieved it. And Gen Z’s embrace—coming after 40+ years—proves it’s not about nostalgia or historical importance. It’s about the music working on its own terms.

The Formula Validated

Gen Z’s ABBA renaissance validates every strategic decision and creative choice examined in this series:

The musical craftsmanship (Part 2) was sophisticated enough to work across generations. The major/minor tension, dual vocals, superior song construction—these weren’t tricks for 1970s audiences. They were genuinely excellent musical architecture that works for any audience.

The video innovation (Part 3) established that physical presence wasn’t necessary for connection. Gen Z streams ABBA without ever seeing them perform live—the music alone creates the relationship.

The business model (Part 4) ensured ABBA captured the value Gen Z discovery generates. Every stream, every playlist add, every TikTok use generates revenue for entities ABBA controls.

The emotional authenticity (Part 5) created songs that resonate across circumstances. Gen Z’s anxiety and heartbreak connects with ABBA’s mature emotional content.

The wilderness years patience (Part 7) allowed new generations to discover ABBA without oversaturation. They didn’t exhaust their catalog or their audience.

The avatar innovation (Part 8) gave Gen Z a way to experience ABBA “live” despite the band stopping before they were born.

Every strategic choice, every creative decision, every business structure—all of it culminates in this moment: Gen Z discovering ABBA and deciding it’s worth their time and attention.

That’s not luck. That’s the compounding effect of excellence and strategy over 50 years.

What It Means

The Gen Z renaissance isn’t just commercial success—though it certainly is that, generating tens of millions in streaming revenue annually.

It’s proof that ABBA achieved the goal stated in this series’ introduction: multi-generational cultural permanence.

They’re not a nostalgia act. They’re not a historical curiosity. They’re not music your parents liked that you tolerate.

They’re music that works. Period.

For people born in 1950, 1970, 1990, 2010—ABBA’s music creates connection, generates emotion, inspires movement, provides soundtrack to life.

That’s the rarest achievement in popular music: creating something that transcends its moment completely, that works independently of context, that survives not just decades but cultural eras.
