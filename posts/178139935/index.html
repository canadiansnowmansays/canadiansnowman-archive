When Democracy Fights Back

The National Battlefield - Both Parties Win, Democracy Loses

Part 2: How Texas and California triggered a redistricting arms race that could eliminate competition nationwide

November 5, 2025

Cartoon, “The Gerry-Mander”, 1813

The Scorecard That Damns Everyone

The morning after California’s Proposition 50 passed with 64% approval, political analysts opened their spreadsheets to calculate the potential damage. By early November 2025, Texas and California had completed their redistricting, while thirteen other states were either actively pursuing or seriously considering mid-decade map changes—potentially affecting 247 congressional seats, more than half the House.

What they found should terrify anyone who cares about democracy: If current proposals proceed as expected, both parties would win, and the American people would lose.

The Red State Offensive: Chasing Five to Eleven Seats

Texas: The Completed Template (38 Districts)

Everything begins in Texas. When President Trump tweeted “Time to redraw and WIN BIG in ‘26!” in July 2025, Governor Greg Abbott and the Republican legislature answered the call. What followed was gerrymandering as modern art form.

Using sophisticated algorithms from firms like TargetSmart, Republicans packed 40% of Texas’s Latino population into just 12 of 38 districts, draining Latino voting power from competitive suburban areas around Houston, Dallas, Austin, and San Antonio. They cracked Travis County (Austin) across five districts, ensuring the liberal capital could never elect more than one Democratic representative. Nine competitive districts were redrawn into safe Republican seats with 15-20 point advantages.

When Texas House Democrats fled the state to break quorum—literally escaping to Oklahoma and Illinois—Attorney General Ken Paxton’s response revealed the stakes: “Democrats who try and run away like cowards should be found, arrested, and brought back to the Capitol immediately.” Eventually, exhausted and facing $500-per-day fines, enough Democrats returned. The maps passed on a party-line vote with zero meaningful public input.

The result: Texas’s delegation has shifted from 25R-13D to a projected 30R-8D—five Republican seats manufactured by cartography, not voters.

North Carolina: Precision Planning (14 Districts)

North Carolina Republicans are currently advancing plans to refine their already-aggressive 2021 gerrymander with laser focus. The most brazen proposal targets the state’s 14th district, a majority-Black seat. The proposed maps would add affluent white Republican suburbs, potentially diluting Black voting majority from 52% to 44%, which would flip the district from D+8 to R+3.

The timing is particularly audacious: This comes weeks after a federal court ordered North Carolina to redraw maps for Voting Rights Act violations. Republican legislators appear to be calculating that appeals will drag past 2026 primaries.

If enacted as proposed: North Carolina would shift from 7R-7D to 10R-4D in a nearly evenly-split state.

Missouri and Ohio: Moving Forward (23 Districts Combined)

Missouri Republicans have introduced legislation targeting 80-year-old Democrat Emanuel Cleaver’s Kansas City district, which would transform a D+10 seat into a toss-up by adding deep-red rural counties. Cleaver has announced he’s watching the process closely. The proposed maps would crack St. Louis more aggressively, splitting its Black voters across three districts where they’d be overwhelmed by conservative suburban majorities.

Ohio’s story involves the state Supreme Court’s new composition. After the court’s makeup changed with new Republican-aligned justices, legislators are now moving to implement previously-blocked gerrymanders. The proposals would crack Columbus, Cleveland, and Cincinnati urban cores across multiple suburban-leaning districts.

If proposals pass: Missouri would go from 6R-2D to 7R-1D. Ohio would shift from 10R-5D to 12R-3D.

Red State Projected Scorecard:

Across Texas (completed) and proposed changes in North Carolina, Missouri, and Ohio (75 total seats):

  • Current: 48R-27D

  • Projected if all proposals pass: 59R-16D

  • Potential net gain: +11 seats through maps

The Blue State Response: Seeking Eleven Seats of Retaliation

California: The Democratic Mandate (52 Districts) - COMPLETED

California’s Proposition 50, covered extensively in Part 1, represented the most significant Democratic counter. After months of transparent public debate, 64% of voters approved maps that would pack rural Republicans into 3-4 ultra-safe districts while cracking Central Valley and inland suburban Republicans by combining them with coastal Democrats.

Exit polling revealed the motivation: 70% of Latino voters and 55% of independents cited “fairness against Texas” as their primary reason. This wasn’t blind partisanship—it was calculated reciprocity.

The completed result: California will shift from 40D-12R to 45D-7R, with a crucial sunset provision expiring in 2030.

New York: Contemplating Maximum Retaliation (26 Districts)

New York Democrats, having created relatively fair 2021 maps, watched Republicans’ Texas offensive and are now considering their response. Governor Kathy Hochul and the Democratic legislature have begun discussions about potential mid-decade changes.

The proposals under consideration would surgically alter key districts: Long Island’s Republican swing seats would absorb Brooklyn Democrats. Hudson Valley red suburbs would be split across multiple districts and combined with Democratic cities. The most controversial proposal would add left-leaning Brooklyn neighborhoods to Staten Island Republican Nicole Malliotakis’s district, potentially flipping it from R+8 to D+2.

If enacted: New York could go from 15D-11R to 20D-6R—a five-seat swing mirroring Texas exactly.

Illinois: Exploring Options (17 Districts)

Illinois Democrats already have one of the country’s most aggressive gerrymanders (14 of 17 seats despite Trump winning 41% statewide), but legislative leaders are studying whether they can squeeze harder. Proposals would combine two rural Republican districts into one R+25 fortress, then split the remainder across Chicago-area districts leaning D+8 to D+12.

If proposals advance: Illinois could shift from 14D-3R to 15D-2R.

Blue State Projected Scorecard:

Across California (completed) and potential changes in New York and Illinois (95 total seats):

  • Current: 69D-26R

  • Projected if all proposals pass: 80D-15R

  • Potential net gain: +11 seats, exactly matching potential Republican gains

The States That Can’t (or Won’t)

Not every proposed gerrymander will succeed. Florida’s Ron DeSantis wanted 2-3 more seats but faces resistance from his own party after the state Supreme Court issued pointed VRA warnings. Pennsylvania’s Democratic governor and Republican legislature remain deadlocked, leaving relatively fair 2021 maps intact. Wisconsin’s liberal-controlled state Supreme Court has ordered new maps that could flip 2-3 seats, currently under appeal to federal courts.

The Devastating Truth: If Everyone Succeeds, Democracy Loses

When you aggregate Texas and California’s completed redistricting with all proposed changes nationwide, the projected picture is both predictable and horrifying:

Total Congressional Seats Potentially Affected: 247 (56% of the House)

Projected Net Partisan Impact (if all proposals pass):

  • Republican gains from red states: +11 seats

  • Democratic gains from blue states: +11 seats

  • Net change in House balance: Essentially tied (+0 to +2R depending on Wisconsin)

The Real Impact—What Actually Matters:

  • Competitive districts nationally: Could drop from 35 to 18-20

  • Predetermined outcomes: Would rise from 72% to 84% of all House races

  • Districts where minorities have meaningful influence: Could fall from 92 to 78

Both parties can claim victory. Both would gain seats. And American voters would lose massively.

What 2026 Could Look Like

The implications if these proposals proceed are staggering:

For Voters:

  • More than 330 House districts (76%) could be decided by 10+ points before anyone campaigns

  • Only 20-25 genuinely competitive races nationwide

  • In gerrymandered districts, primaries would matter more than general elections

  • Millions would effectively have no choice in representation

For Governance:

  • Incumbents in safe seats would have zero incentive to moderate

  • Extremists would be rewarded for ideological purity over compromise

  • The median House member could be more extreme than 80% of constituents

  • Basic functions like budgets would become harder with increased polarization

For Minority Communities:

  • Black and Latino voters would have less influence in more states

  • Texas’s completed maps have already slashed competitive Latino districts from 8 to 4

  • VRA protections face hostile courts with limited enforcement

  • A generation could grow up believing their votes don’t matter—because in gerrymandered districts, they genuinely wouldn’t

For Democracy Itself: A November 2025 Brennan Center poll found:

  • 62% of Americans believe elections are “rigged” or “manipulated”

  • 71% of voters under 35 say their vote “doesn’t really matter”

  • Trust in Congress at historic low of 9% approval

  • 44% considering not voting in 2026 because “what’s the point?”

When citizens lose faith in democracy, democracy dies—not with a bang, but with a shrug.

The Collective Action Trap

Here’s the most frustrating part: Both parties are acting rationally within the current system. Democrats who don’t gerrymander in California and New York would hand Republicans a structural House advantage for a decade. Republicans who don’t gerrymander in Texas and elsewhere would leave seats on the table their base demands they capture.

Neither side can afford to disarm while the other is armed. The result is an arms race where everyone builds bigger weapons and no one is safer.

Political scientists call this a “collective action problem”—individually rational choices producing collectively catastrophic outcomes. It’s the tragedy of the commons applied to democracy itself.

Marc Elias, the Democratic Party’s most prominent voting rights attorney, captured the dilemma: “I don’t celebrate gerrymandering in any form. But I also don’t believe Democrats should unilaterally disarm while Republicans are waging maximum warfare. When the other side breaks the rules, you either fight back or you lose—and losing means losing the power to ever fix the rules.”

He’s not wrong. But he’s also describing a death spiral.

The Grim Irony of Mutual Destruction

If current proposals proceed, both parties will claim victory. Both can point to the other side’s gerrymanders and claim victimhood. Both can justify their actions as necessary responses to the opponent’s aggression.

And both would be destroying the very system they claim to protect.

The 2026 midterms could be decided not by voter preferences, campaigns, or issues, but by lines drawn in legislative chambers and approved by voters in referendums. Districts that used to swing with the electorate would lock in predetermined outcomes regardless of how people vote.

This is what democracy looks like when it’s decided by mapmakers instead of voters. And unless something fundamental changes, this is what democracy will look like for the foreseeable future.
