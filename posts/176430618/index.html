The Psychology of Us vs. Them (2000s-Present)

The Tribal Brain: Why American Politics Feels Like War

October 17, 2025

Scrolling through reactions to the X post this morning—some cheering the Jefferson quote as a call to arms for tomorrow’s “No Kings” rallies, others dismissing it as alarmist histrionics—I’m reminded that America’s divide isn’t just structural or economic. It’s deeply human, wired into the psychology of belonging and threat.

As a Canadian, where our fiercest debates (say, over Quebec sovereignty or carbon pricing) rarely descend into personal demonization, I watch Americans treat politics like a blood sport with a mix of fascination and concern. This isn’t mere partisanship; it’s tribalism on steroids, where the “other side” isn’t wrong—they’re dangerous, subhuman, existential foes. How did a nation of immigrants become so psychologically fractured? And how close are we?

The Rise of Negative Partisanship: Hating More Than Loving

Political scientists call it “negative partisanship”: voters increasingly motivated by loathing the opposition rather than enthusiasm for their own team. Pew Research tracks this shift—by 2020, 80% of Republicans and Democrats viewed the other party as a “threat to the nation’s well-being,” up from 20% in the 1990s. It’s not policy disagreement; it’s visceral disgust.

From my northern vantage, this echoes our occasional flare-ups, like the 1995 Quebec referendum, but even then, it was “us vs. them” within a shared Canadian identity. Americans? Their hatred has swallowed the center. A Trump rally chant of “Lock her up” or a progressive tweetstorm labeling conservatives “deplorables” (à la Clinton’s 2016 gaffe) isn’t debate—it’s dehumanization. This psychology turns elections into sieges, where winning means the enemy’s annihilation, not coexistence.

Political Identity as the New Religion

Here’s the kicker: Politics has eclipsed other identities. Once, you were Catholic first, Democrat second. Now, for many, party affiliation is core to selfhood—your tribe, your values, your moral compass. Social psychologist Lilliana Mason’s work shows how this fusion of partisanship with race, religion, and class creates “mega-identities.” A white evangelical Republican isn’t just voting values; they’re defending their entire worldview against coastal elites who “don’t get” flyover country.

Canadians layer identities more loosely—I’m Ontario-bred, hockey-mad, and mildly progressive, but none defines me to the exclusion of neighbors. America’s fusion makes compromise treasonous. Why negotiate with heretics? This explains the revolutionary edge in the post: When politics is identity, threats to one are threats to all, summoning Jefferson’s duty to rebel.

Confirmation Bias in the Echo Chamber Age

Our brains love being right, and digital tools exploit that. Confirmation bias—seeking info that affirms beliefs, ignoring contradictions—thrives in algorithm-fed bubbles. A 2022 study in Nature found Americans exposed to partisan news overestimate opposing views’ extremism by 50%. Conservatives think all Democrats want open borders and socialism; liberals assume every Republican is a racist misogynist.

That 1967 Greenwood conversation? Confirmation bias was there—the family clung to their “part of the family” narrative despite evidence. But pre-internet, you had to confront reality occasionally (a newspaper, a chat). Now? Algorithms serve comfort food, radicalizing subtly. A mild conservative Facebook post leads to Alex Jones; a progressive thread spirals to defund-the-police manifestos. No wonder trust in institutions craters—facts become “fake news” from enemy territory.

Fear and the Amygdala Hijack

Politics now hijacks the brain’s fear center. Neuroscientists show threat perception spikes cortisol, narrowing focus to fight-or-flight. Politicians weaponize this: Trump’s “American carnage” inauguration painted immigrants as invaders; Biden’s warnings of “MAGA extremists” frame half the country as domestic terrorists. Both tap primal survival instincts, making policy secondary to perceived annihilation.

From Canada, where fearmongering flares up—like Harper’s niqab debates or our own election-eve immigrant scares—this constant U.S. simmer feels all too familiar, just dialed up to a fever pitch we both risk catching. Our multicultural fabric teaches that difference isn’t doom—it’s dinner-party fodder. America’s history of existential threats (Civil War, McCarthyism) primed this, but 9/11 and economic shocks amplified it into permanence. Polls show 70% of Americans now fear civil unrest, turning neighbors into suspects.

Moral Foundations: Speaking Different Ethical Languages

Jonathan Haidt’s Moral Foundations Theory explains why debates devolve into shouting matches. Liberals prioritize care, fairness, and liberty (anti-oppression axis); conservatives add loyalty, authority, and sanctity (group-binding axis). A policy like affirmative action? To one side, it’s justice; to the other, betrayal of merit.

This clash isn’t stupidity—it’s incompatible worldviews. The post’s tyranny theme resonates here: What one sees as “tyranny” (federal overreach), the other views as protection (from corporate kings). Canadians blend foundations more evenly—our Charter balances individual rights with communal duties—making cross-ideological bridges easier, though we stumble too.

The Human Cost and the Path Out

This psychological brew—hatred over hope, identity fusion, biased feeds, fear porn, moral disconnect—turns democracy toxic. Friendships end over elections; families skip holidays. The “No Kings” rallies tomorrow channel righteous anger, but if unchecked, this mindset risks real violence, as January 6 showed.

Yet psychology offers hope too: Contact theory says exposure reduces prejudice. Small acts—cross-party coffees, shared projects—rewire brains toward empathy. As an outsider, I see America’s strength in its pluralism; reclaiming that could heal what structures alone can’t.

For millions of us Canadians with American relatives, this psychological warfare hits personally. Those cousins we grew up joking with, sharing family reunions and beach vacations—suddenly their Facebook posts seem alien, extreme, unreachable. We’re not just neighbors; we’re literally family. Yet even blood ties strain under algorithmic pressure designed to maximize outrage over understanding.

Here’s the thing, though: For all I know, those same American cousins of mine hold opposite political views from each other—maybe from me too. And you know what? That’s fine. I love them all the same. They’re still the kids who cannonballed into the lake with me, who taught me to catch fireflies, who argued over Monopoly rules at the cottage. Their voting patterns don’t erase our shared memories or DNA.

Take that a step further. Think of your own neighborhood—that mix of Democrats and Republicans living side by side. Strip away the yard signs and what do you have? Parents worried about their kids’ schools. Folks trying to make mortgage payments. People who shovel each other’s driveways after snowstorms (at least up here in Canada). The algorithm doesn’t show you Jim from three doors down helping his liberal neighbor fix a fence. It shows you anonymous liberals and conservatives screaming at each other online.

What if we chose to see our neighbors—and yes, even our relatives—as Jim and Sarah first, voters second? What if love for our actual cousins could teach us to see political opponents as somebody else’s cousin, somebody else’s Jim from three doors down?

Thoughts for my Last Essay

So here we stand—or rather, here Americans stand—at a crossroads. The “No Kings” rallies tomorrow represent both the problem and the potential solution. Revolutionary energy can tear down or build up. The psychology of division can be rewired for connection. Broken institutions can be reformed. But it takes choosing hope over hate, bridges over walls. As someone watching from Canada, with family on both sides of this divide, I can’t help but ask: What kind of North America do we want to leave our kids?...
