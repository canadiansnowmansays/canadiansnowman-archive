Cuba: The Island That Refused to Kneel

The Island That Refused to Kneel

Part 5: The Revolution’s Promise — and Washington’s Panic (1959–1962)

March 9, 2026

Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

Havana, January 1959. The beards, the fatigues, the microphone — and a country holding its breath. Nobody in this room knew yet what the Revolution would become. Neither did Washington. Both would spend the next sixty-five years finding out.

The first year of the Cuban Revolution was, by most measures, genuinely popular. That is a fact that tends to get lost in the decades of Cold War framing that followed, but it matters enormously for understanding what Cuba became and why it has been so resistant to change from outside.

When Fidel Castro’s rebels marched into Havana on January 1, 1959, they were welcomed by enormous crowds. Batista had fled to the Dominican Republic in the middle of the night, boarding a plane with cronies and suitcases full of cash while his security apparatus — the SIM torturers, the paramilitary killers — scattered in every direction. The celebrations that followed were not manufactured. They were the release of genuine accumulated frustration and hope: the Batista nightmare was over, and something new was beginning.

This iconic black-and-white photo of Fidel Castro riding triumphantly into Havana on January 8, 1959, atop a jeep amid cheering crowds and bearded rebels—pure euphoria, crowds waving, the Revolution’s popular arrival in full force.

What that something new would become was not yet determined. Fidel Castro was not, in January 1959, a communist. The revolutionary coalition that had brought him to power was ideologically heterogeneous — it included liberals, nationalists, social democrats, and yes, communists, but the dominant impulse was Cuban nationalism rather than Soviet-style socialism. The revolutionary program Castro announced in the first months was broadly reformist: agrarian reform to break up the great estates, expanded education and health care, reduced American economic dominance, and the restoration of the democratic promise of the 1940 constitution that Batista had destroyed.

What happened in the three years that followed — the radicalization of the Revolution, the turn toward the Soviet Union, the consolidation of one-party rule — cannot be understood apart from Washington’s reaction to it.

The First Year: Promise and Possibility

The early achievements of the Revolution were real and substantial, and they explain why Castro maintained genuine popular support through years of subsequent hardship that would have destroyed a government resting on repression alone.

The literacy campaign of 1961 sent more than 100,000 young Cubans — many of them teenagers from the cities — into the countryside to teach reading and writing to adults who had been left behind by the pre-revolutionary educational system. In the space of a single year, Cuba’s literacy rate rose from approximately 76 percent to over 96 percent — one of the most dramatic educational transformations in modern history. The campaign was not just a statistical achievement; it was a social revolution in miniature, bringing urban and rural Cuba into contact across class lines in ways that had never happened before.

Young literacy brigade volunteers (brigadistas) marching with giant pencil props in 1961—energetic, youthful, idealistic, symbolizing the social transformation and popular mobilization.

The expansion of health care was equally dramatic. Before the Revolution, Cuba had a substantial medical infrastructure concentrated in Havana and a few other cities, while the rural countryside was largely without doctors or basic medical services. The revolutionary government built rural clinics, trained new doctors, and began the systematic reorientation of the health system toward preventive care and universal access that would eventually give Cuba health indicators comparable to wealthy developed nations despite a fraction of their economic resources.

Agrarian reform broke up the largest landholdings — including many owned by American corporations — and distributed land to tenant farmers and agricultural workers. The reform was economically chaotic in some respects, disrupting the sugar industry’s production efficiency, but it addressed a genuine injustice and was enormously popular among the rural poor who had worked other men’s land for generations.

These early achievements created the base of popular support that the Revolution would draw on for decades, even as its political character became more authoritarian and its economic management increasingly problematic. Cubans who benefited from universal education and health care had reason to defend a system that had delivered something their parents had been denied, even when other aspects of that system were deeply objectionable.

Washington’s Panic

The Eisenhower administration’s reaction to the Cuban Revolution moved from wariness to hostility with a speed that reflected less a careful assessment of what the Revolution actually was than a set of Cold War reflexes that made any challenge to American dominance in the Caribbean automatically threatening.

The agrarian reform that affected American landholdings was the first flashpoint. United Fruit Company, the American agricultural giant that owned substantial Cuban sugar and agricultural land, found its properties subject to expropriation in 1959. The compensation offered — based on the declared tax value of the land, which American companies had systematically undervalued to minimize their tax obligations — was deeply inadequate by the companies’ own assessment, though of course the companies had created that problem themselves. Washington took the companies’ side.

The Cuban government’s nationalization of American-owned oil refineries in June 1960 was a more direct confrontation. When Castro asked the American and British oil companies operating in Cuba to refine Soviet crude oil (Cuba had begun purchasing oil from the Soviet Union, which offered it at favorable prices), they refused on American government instructions. Castro nationalized the refineries. The Eisenhower administration responded by cutting the Cuban sugar quota — the amount of Cuban sugar the United States would import at the preferential price guaranteed under long-standing trade agreements — by 700,000 tons. Cuba nationalized virtually all remaining American properties, worth several billion dollars, in October 1960. The Eisenhower administration imposed a comprehensive trade embargo in October 1960 and broke diplomatic relations with Cuba in January 1961.

The CIA, meanwhile, had begun planning for Castro’s removal almost immediately after the Revolution succeeded. The agency had trained Cuban exile forces in Guatemala and was preparing for an invasion. President Kennedy, who inherited both the plan and the problem, approved the operation over the objections of his more skeptical advisers.

An April 1961 file photo shows a group of CIA-backed Cuban counterrevolutionaries after their capture in the Bay of Pigs, Cuba. Miguel Vinas/AFP via Getty Images

The Bay of Pigs invasion of April 1961 was a disaster of almost comical proportions. Approximately 1,400 Cuban exiles landed on Cuba’s south coast, expecting popular support that did not materialize and American air support that Kennedy withheld at the last minute to maintain the fiction of non-involvement. Within three days the invasion force had been captured or killed. The operation’s failure was comprehensive: it did not overthrow Castro, it massively strengthened his political position by allowing him to present himself as successfully defending Cuban sovereignty against American aggression, and it humiliated the Kennedy administration internationally.

The lesson Kennedy and the CIA drew from the Bay of Pigs was not that the attempt to overthrow Castro was wrong but that it had been executed badly. Operation Mongoose, the assassination and sabotage campaign that followed, included genuinely bizarre plots against Castro’s person — exploding cigars, a poisoned wetsuit, a scheme involving his beard — alongside more serious efforts at economic sabotage and the support of internal dissidents. None of them worked.

The Missile Crisis: Cuba as Chess Piece

The Cuban Missile Crisis of October 1962 is remembered, in the American telling, as a confrontation between Kennedy and Khrushchev — thirteen days of nuclear brinkmanship that came closer to world war than any event in the Cold War. That telling is not wrong, but it omits something important: Cuba was the board, not one of the players.

The missiles arrived in Cuba because Castro, after the Bay of Pigs, reasonably concluded that the United States was committed to his government’s overthrow and that conventional Cuban military forces could not deter another invasion attempt. He requested Soviet military assistance, and Khrushchev, eager to close the strategic gap with the United States and establish a Soviet presence in the Western Hemisphere, agreed to supply not just conventional weapons but nuclear missiles.

When American U-2 spy planes discovered the missile installations under construction in October 1962, Kennedy faced the most dangerous thirteen days of the Cold War. The options his advisers considered ranged from a naval blockade to air strikes to a full invasion. The naval blockade — which the Kennedy administration called a “quarantine” to avoid the legal implications of the word “blockade” — was chosen, and Soviet ships bearing additional military supplies turned back before reaching Cuba.

U-2 reconnaissance photo from October 1962 showing Soviet missile sites in Cuba—grainy aerial view with annotations (tents, launchers, trailers)—the visual trigger for the Cuban Missile Crisis.

The resolution was negotiated between Kennedy and Khrushchev. The Soviet Union agreed to remove the missiles in exchange for an American pledge not to invade Cuba and a secret agreement to remove American Jupiter missiles from Turkey. Cuba was not consulted. Castro was not informed of the terms before they were agreed. He was furious — he had, by his own later account, been prepared for nuclear war as the price of defending Cuban sovereignty, and found the compromise deeply objectionable.

The crisis demonstrated, with unusual clarity, the position that Cuba occupied in the Cold War: a territory of strategic significance whose people’s preferences were essentially irrelevant to the decisions being made about their fate. The United States and Soviet Union had nearly destroyed the world over Cuban territory while the Cubans watched.

The Revolution Hardens

The confrontation with the United States accelerated and radicalized the Revolution in ways that had profound consequences for Cuban society. The process by which Cuba became a one-party communist state was not inevitable — it was the product of specific decisions made under specific pressures, many of which originated in Washington.

Castro declared Cuba a socialist state in April 1961, the day before the Bay of Pigs invasion — a declaration that functioned partly as a description of policy direction and partly as a pre-emptive positioning of Cuba within the Soviet bloc before the invasion could succeed. By December 1961 he had declared himself a Marxist-Leninist. The Communist Party of Cuba was formally established in 1965, consolidating the various revolutionary factions under a single political organization that has governed the island ever since.

The consolidation of one-party rule required the suppression of dissent, and the Revolutionary government moved against political opposition across the ideological spectrum. Liberals, social democrats, and independent leftists who had supported the Revolution but questioned its authoritarian direction found themselves outside the acceptable range of political opinion. Freedom of the press was eliminated as independent newspapers were nationalized. Civil society organizations were absorbed into or replaced by revolutionary mass organizations. The universities were brought under political control.

The Cuban communities that emigrated — the early waves of Batista’s allies and Cuba’s upper class, then the middle class professionals who found their properties nationalized and their futures foreclosed, then an ongoing flow of people who simply could not live under the political constraints of the new Cuba — settled primarily in Miami and built an exile community whose hostility to Castro would become one of the most powerful lobbying forces in American politics, with consequences that reach directly into the crisis unfolding in 2026.

The United States embargo, imposed in 1960, was simultaneously a punishment, a strategic calculation, and a gift to Castro’s political narrative. It gave him a genuine and credible external enemy to whom he could attribute Cuba’s economic difficulties. It strengthened the argument for revolutionary solidarity and sacrifice. And it genuinely harmed ordinary Cubans in ways that cannot be fully disentangled from the harm done by the Revolution’s own economic mismanagement — which is, of course, exactly what makes the embargo so politically useful to both sides of the argument.

By the end of 1962, the essential structure of the Cuban situation — a revolutionary government, an American embargo, a Soviet alliance, and a Cuban exile community in Miami — had been established. It would remain essentially unchanged for the next six decades.

The Cuban people who lived within that structure had not chosen it. They had chosen a revolution against Batista’s brutality and sixty years of American-sponsored corruption. What they got was more complicated, and less free, than what they had hoped for.

But they had chosen it more freely than anything that had been chosen for Cuba in the previous four and a half centuries. That is not nothing. It is also not enough.
