The Mirror — When America Became What It Feared

From The Trump Accountability Project

March 8, 2026

Prologue: The Shelters We Built

I am seventy-four years old, and I remember when building a bomb shelter seemed like ordinary household planning. In the suburbs of Toronto in the early 1960s, the conversation was not whether the Soviets might attack, but what you would stock in the basement when they did. The enemy was clear. The enemy was Moscow. And the country that stood between us and that threat—the shining, confident, sometimes reckless republic to our south—was the thing we counted on.

I remember the duck-and-cover drills. I remember the civil defence pamphlets. I remember the unspoken bargain of the Cold War: that however imperfect America might be, it stood on the right side of the line that divided democracy from authoritarian aggression. The Soviet Union invaded its neighbours, suppressed dissent, manufactured pretexts for war, and wrapped conquest in the language of liberation. America, whatever its sins, did not do that.

And now I sit in that same Toronto, sixty-three years later, reading an analysis in the New York Times that methodically documents how the President of the United States is using the Russian president’s rhetoric—not as echo, not as coincidence, but as near-verbatim parallel—to justify an undeclared war against Iran. And I think: this is what we built the shelters against.

The Uncanny Mirror

Anton Troianovski, who spent four years as the Times’ Moscow bureau chief, laid out the parallels in a March 8, 2026, analysis that reads less like opinion journalism and more like a forensic exhibit. The correspondences are not subtle.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, announcing military operations in Iran: We didn’t start this war, but under President Trump, we are finishing it.

Vladimir Putin, after invading Ukraine in 2022: We didn’t start the so-called war in Ukraine. Rather, we are trying to finish it.

The symmetry extends beyond a single quote. When Speaker Mike Johnson was asked whether the Iran campaign constituted war, he replied that he thought it was “an operation.” His Russian counterpart, Vyacheslav Volodin, the State Duma speaker, had used identical framing two months into Russia’s invasion: “This is a special military operation.” The refusal to name the thing you are doing is not a communications strategy. It is a constitutional evasion. If it is not war, it does not require a vote. If it is not war, the War Powers Resolution does not apply. If it is not war, accountability is optional.

Trump, speaking to CNN: “We haven’t even started hitting them hard.” Putin, five months into his war: “We haven’t even yet started anything in earnest.” Both statements project defiance. Both imply escalation as a feature, not a risk. Both treat the current level of destruction as a restrained opening act.

And both leaders called on the opposing military to surrender—Putin urging Ukrainian soldiers to “immediately lay down arms and go home,” Trump warning Iranian soldiers to “lay down your weapons” or “face certain death.” By the second day of each conflict, both men had escalated to calling on the civilian population to overthrow their own government: Putin telling Ukrainians to “take power into your own hands,” Trump urging Iranians to “seize this moment.”

These are not loose analogies drawn by a hostile press. These are documented, timestamped, and placed side by side by a journalist who heard both speeches from the capitals where they were delivered.

The Case for Action: Where the Rationale Has Merit

Accountability demands honesty, and honesty requires engaging with the strongest version of the argument you are examining. There were legitimate reasons to confront Iran, and failing to acknowledge them would make this essay a polemic rather than an analysis.

Iran’s nuclear program had crossed enrichment thresholds that alarmed not only the United States but Israel, the Gulf states, and European allies who had spent years trying to constrain it through diplomacy. The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action—the 2015 nuclear deal—was already fractured, partly by Trump’s own 2018 withdrawal and partly by Tehran’s subsequent acceleration of enrichment. The diplomatic track was not merely stalled; it had been dismantled.

The regime in Tehran was, as Troianovski’s own analysis acknowledged, “killing its own people in the streets.” The suppression of the Mahsa Amini protests in 2022 and 2023 demonstrated a government willing to murder its citizens to maintain clerical control. Iran’s proxy network—Hezbollah in Lebanon, the Houthis in Yemen, Shia militias across Iraq and Syria—had destabilized the broader Middle East for decades, extracting a human cost measured in hundreds of thousands of lives.

The argument that Iran posed a genuine security threat is not propaganda. It is supported by decades of intelligence assessments, by the testimony of regional allies, and by the observable behaviour of the regime itself. No serious analyst disputes that Iran was pursuing capabilities that would fundamentally alter the balance of power in the Middle East.

That is the steelman case. It deserves to be stated clearly, because what follows is not a rebuttal of the threat. It is an examination of what the response to that threat reveals about the man who ordered it.

Where the Rationale Collapses

A credible case for confronting Iran does not, by itself, justify anything Trump has done. The gap between legitimate concern and lawful, proportionate action is where accountability lives.

It does not justify waging war without congressional authorization. The Trump Accountability Project has documented in detail how Trump has conducted seven military operations without a single congressional war powers vote, and how Congress has abdicated its constitutional responsibility to check that authority. The Iran campaign is the most consequential expression of that pattern, but it is not the first. It is the culmination.

It does not explain the shifting objectives. In the space of a single week, the stated goals of the Iran campaign have migrated from destroying nuclear facilities, to eliminating Iran’s missile arsenal, to—as Trump declared on Friday—”unconditional surrender.” Members of his own cabinet have offered varying and contradictory definitions of success. When the war aim changes daily, the war is not being fought for a strategic objective. It is being fought for a political one.

It does not account for the rhetorical framework. And this is where the Troianovski analysis becomes devastating. Because the question is not whether Iran posed a threat. The question is why the President of the United States, confronting that threat, reached for the exact language used by Vladimir Putin to justify the invasion of a sovereign democracy.

The euphemism of “operation” instead of “war.” The call for opposing soldiers to surrender on day one. The demand that civilians overthrow their own government on day two. The projection of limitless escalation as strength. These are not universal features of war rhetoric. They are specific choices, drawn from a specific playbook, and they reveal a specific orientation toward power.

As Michael Kofman of the Carnegie Endowment observed, the war reflects “enduring historical lessons,” including “the need for an alignment between military means and political aims.” When those aims shift daily and the rhetoric mirrors that of an authoritarian aggressor, the misalignment is not accidental. It is structural.

The Great Satan: What the Mirror Reflects

Most of this series has examined what Trump has done to American institutions—the weaponization of the DOJ, the suppression of press access, the retribution architecture, the erosion of constitutional norms. This essay turns the lens outward, because the rest of the world is watching, and what they see completes the picture.

Iran has called America “The Great Satan” since 1979. The phrase is older than most of the soldiers Trump has deployed. For decades, it functioned as propaganda—a theocratic regime’s convenient shorthand for an adversary whose actual behaviour, however flawed, did not fit the caricature.

That gap has closed.

When the Assembly of Experts met to select Iran’s next Supreme Leader, the American bombing campaign did not weaken the hardliners. It strengthened them. Ayatollah Mohsen Heidari Alekasir addressed the dynamic directly: “Someone who is opposed by the enemy is more likely to be beneficial for Iran and Islam.” The Great Satan, he said, had done the Assembly “a kind of service” by publicly objecting to certain candidates.

This is the paradox that the Trump administration either fails to understand or chooses to ignore: American aggression does not delegitimize the regime. It is the regime’s legitimacy. Every bomb dropped validates the narrative that the clerics have maintained for nearly half a century. Opposition from the Great Satan is not a threat to Iran’s hardliners. It is a credential.

And it is not only Iran that watches. Dmytro Kuleba, Ukraine’s foreign minister at the time of Russia’s invasion, issued a warning on social media that carried the weight of lived experience: “American commentators are again talking about a ‘short war.’ They said the same about Russia’s war against Ukraine. It will be short only if Washington quietly scales down its goals, gives up on regime change in Iran, and sells a much smaller outcome as victory.”

“Breaking a large country,” he added, “is hard even for the United States.”

Pro- and anti-Kremlin Russian bloggers have already coined the term “Tehran in three days”—a sardonic reference to “Kyiv in three days,” the shorthand for the hubris that doomed Russia’s initial war plan. When your war rhetoric is being mocked by people who lived through the last version of it, the feedback is not political commentary. It is a diagnostic.

What We Were Told to Fear

Some of the Trump administration’s rhetoric on Iran has been in step with Russia’s rhetoric about its 2022 invasion of Ukraine.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

I return to the bomb shelters because the arc matters.

The Cold War was, at its core, a story about two systems of governance and the threat each posed to the other. The West told itself—told me, in my Toronto classroom, in the civil defence pamphlets, in the nightly news—that the Soviet threat was defined by specific behaviours: the manufacture of pretexts for war, the use of euphemisms to disguise aggression, the demand that conquered peoples submit, the shifting of war aims to suit domestic political needs, and the concentration of military authority in a single leader who operated beyond institutional constraint.

Each of those behaviours is now documented in the conduct of the forty-seventh President of the United States.

The manufactured pretext: overstating Iran’s missile threat to justify a campaign whose real aim is regime change. The euphemism: “operation” instead of “war,” to evade constitutional requirements. The demand for submission: calling on soldiers to surrender and civilians to revolt, as though a bombing campaign confers the moral authority to dictate governance. The shifting aims: from nuclear disarmament to unconditional surrender in the space of days. The concentration of authority: seven wars, zero congressional votes.

Maria Lipman, a Russia scholar at Northwestern University, captured the deeper resonance: “By starting a war with Iran, Trump stepped into the unknown. I think that there is more instability in store for the United States and the American people than they realize at this time.”

She could have been describing Moscow in February 2022. She was describing Washington in March 2026.

The Weight of the Record

The Trump Accountability Project is documenting, across more than forty essays, a pattern of conduct that is without precedent in American history. The retribution architecture. The weaponization of federal law enforcement. The seizure of a foreign head of state. The suppression of press access to the Pentagon. The use of war as a mechanism to bury politically damaging media coverage. And now: an undeclared war whose rhetoric mirrors, almost word for word, the justifications offered by the very autocrat that the Western alliance was built to contain.

The problem is not that Trump confronted a genuine threat. The problem is what the confrontation reveals. When a president wages war without authorization, shifts his objectives daily, demands unconditional surrender from a nation of eighty-eight million people, and frames the entire enterprise in language borrowed from Vladimir Putin’s invasion playbook, the issue is no longer policy. It is character. It is orientation. It is the question of what kind of power this president believes he holds and what kind of country he believes he leads.

I am a Canadian. I have watched this from across a border that suddenly feels less like a boundary and more like a front-row seat. My country’s relationship with the United States has been the defining fact of our national existence since Confederation. We have marched alongside America in two world wars, in Korea, in Afghanistan. We have built our economy, our security architecture, and our cultural identity in reference to the republic next door.

And I am telling you, from the perspective of a man who grew up in the shadow of those bomb shelters: when America mirrors the behaviour it once defined itself against, the rest of the world does not fail to notice. The Great Satan is not a phrase that belongs to Iran alone anymore. It is a question that every ally, every trading partner, every democracy that built its post-war order on American leadership is now being forced to ask.

Not whether America is the Great Satan. But whether America still knows the difference.
