Two Americas

Economic Fault Lines (1980s-Present)

The Uneven Ground Beneath America's Divide

October 17, 2025

My X post this morning—invoking Jefferson’s words on tyranny and rebellion amid the “No Kings” rallies tomorrow—resonates deeply with the economic undercurrents of American polarization. From a Canadian vantage point, where we’ve managed our own share of regional disparities through federal transfers and a more equitable safety net—flaws and all, like uneven implementation—America’s economic divides feel like a shared North American challenge.

These aren’t just about money; they’re about who gets left behind in the dream of prosperity, fueling resentment that manifests as revolutionary rhetoric. The seeds of this were planted decades ago, but they’ve grown into chasms that make compromise feel impossible.

Globalization’s Winners and Losers (1980s-2000s)

The neoliberal turn under Reagan and Mulroney (it crossed borders) promised prosperity for all through free trade and deregulation. In America, it delivered for coastal elites and tech hubs but devastated the industrial heartland. NAFTA in 1994 accelerated factory closures in places like Michigan and Ohio, where entire towns lost their economic anchors overnight.

Canadians felt the sting too—think auto plants in Windsor—but our social safety nets cushioned the blow a bit more consistently. America’s minimal welfare state left displaced workers with little recourse, breeding a sense of betrayal. “The system is rigged” became more than a slogan; it was a lived reality for millions who saw their jobs shipped to Mexico while Wall Street boomed.

This economic dislocation didn’t just create poverty—it created a narrative of elites versus “real Americans,” priming the ground for populist explosions on both sides of the border.

The Education Schism

By the 1990s, education emerged as the new class divider. College degrees became the ticket to the knowledge economy, but skyrocketing tuition and student debt turned higher education into a luxury good. Urban professionals in Seattle or Boston thrived in tech and finance, while rural high school graduates in Appalachia or the Rust Belt scraped by in low-wage service jobs.

From north of the border, this hits close to home—and yeah, it’s frustrating to watch. Canada’s tuition stays lower thanks to provincial subsidies, but we’re no utopia: Skyrocketing housing costs and underfunded trades programs leave plenty of young folks here feeling squeezed too. Both nations treat education like a ladder that’s getting steeper by the year.

The “diploma divide” sorted Americans into two castes: the credentialed cosmopolitans who vote blue and embrace globalization, and the non-college-educated who lean red and resent “elites.” Barack Obama captured this tension in his infamous 2008 comment about bitter small-town voters clinging to guns and religion—a rare moment of candor that alienated the very people feeling economically invisible.

Urban Boom, Rural Bust

America’s cities have become engines of innovation and wealth, drawing ambitious young people from across the country. New York, San Francisco, and Austin are global powerhouses, but their prosperity hasn’t trickled down—it flooded upward. Meanwhile, rural America hollowed out: farms consolidated, small towns shuttered, and opioid epidemics filled the void.

This geographic economic divergence amplified the “Two Americas.” Urban liberals see rural decline as a failure of adaptation; rural conservatives view it as abandonment by coastal overlords imposing unwanted change. Canada’s resource-dependent regions (Alberta oil, Atlantic fisheries) face boom-bust cycles too, and our equalization payments help redistribute, but debates still flare—showing no easy fixes.

Immigration and the Zero-Sum Myth

Economic anxiety around immigration isn’t new, but it intensified in the 1990s with waves of Latino migration. To many native-born workers, newcomers weren’t just cultural threats—they were economic competitors for jobs and resources. The 2008 financial crash, triggered by Wall Street greed, supercharged this: as millions lost homes and savings, scapegoating immigrants became an easy outlet.

Yet data shows immigrants often revitalize economies, starting businesses and filling labor gaps. The problem? Both countries’ frayed social contracts don’t always reassure displaced workers that the pie can grow. In Canada, our points-based immigration system ties newcomers to economic needs, and multiculturalism policies frame diversity as a strength—but even we grapple with integration strains. South of the border, it’s often framed as invasion, turning economic fear into cultural warfare.

The Feedback Loop: Economics Fuels Culture, and Vice Versa

These economic fault lines don’t exist in isolation—they intersect with the cultural and racial divides from earlier essays. A white working-class man in Pennsylvania losing his factory job might blame affirmative action more than trade deals. A Black entrepreneur in Atlanta thriving despite barriers might credit progressive policies, deepening partisan loyalty.

By the 2010s, this created a vicious cycle: economic pain drives populist voting, which elects leaders promising protectionism (tariffs, walls) that disrupt global supply chains, causing more pain. Trump’s 2016 win wasn’t just about Russia or emails—it was about capturing the economic despair of non-college whites in swing states, promising to “drain the swamp” of those faceless forces ruining their lives.

America’s refusal to invest in universal healthcare, affordable education, or robust retraining programs exacerbates these divides in ways that mirror our own policy gaps. We debate pipelines and carbon taxes fiercely, but rarely does it feel like two nations at war. America’s economic polarization feels existential because it’s tied to identity: succeeding in the Dream validates your Americanness; failing questions it.

As the post reminds us, when economic tyranny—unfettered corporate power, stagnant wages, hollowed communities—becomes the law of the land, rebellion feels like duty. Tomorrow’s rallies aren’t just about kings; they’re cries from those left on the fault lines, demanding the system work for them again.

Thoughts for my Next Essay

Economic desperation alone doesn’t break democracies—broken institutions do. As millions of Americans felt betrayed by the economy, they looked to their political system for answers. What they found instead were rigged rules, gerrymandered districts, and politicians more responsive to donors than voters. The very mechanisms designed by the founders to channel grievances into peaceful change had been corrupted. When the referee is crooked, is it any wonder the game turns violent?...
