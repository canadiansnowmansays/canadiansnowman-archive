Israel/Palestine: The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

The Original Sin and Its Compound Interest

Part I — Three Faiths, One City: Why Jerusalem Cannot Be Shared and Cannot Be Divided

April 27, 2026

The view that three billion people carry in their hearts and that no political settlement has ever successfully resolved. In the foreground, the ancient Jewish cemetery on the Mount of Olives — the dead of three faiths buried within sight of each other. In the centre, the Dome of the Rock rising from the Temple Mount, Islam's third holiest site built directly above Judaism's holiest ground. Behind it, the modern city that both peoples claim as their eternal capital. This is not a dispute about land. It is an argument about which human stories get to count.

By Stephen Gordon | CanadianSnowmanSays April 2026

There is a place on earth where the air itself feels contested.

Jerusalem’s Old City covers less than one square kilometre. You can walk its perimeter in under an hour. Within that hour you will pass the Western Wall, Judaism’s holiest site — the last surviving remnant of the Second Temple, destroyed by Rome in 70 CE. Directly above it, accessed by a separate ramp because the faiths cannot share an entrance, sits the Temple Mount — called Haram al-Sharif, the Noble Sanctuary, by Muslims — where the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque have stood since the 7th century, making it Islam’s third holiest site on earth. A ten-minute walk in the other direction brings you to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, built on the site Christians believe witnessed the crucifixion, burial, and resurrection of Jesus of Nazareth.

Three faiths. One square kilometre. Every claim genuine. Every claim, in the minds of its believers, absolute. Every claim, in the logic of its theology, superseding every other.

If you want to understand why the Middle East has not known genuine peace in living memory, stand in that square kilometre and turn slowly. The conflict visible on our screens today — the rubble of Gaza, the targeted cities of Lebanon, the fragile pause with Iran — is not primarily a 20th century political dispute. It is the compound interest on a debt accumulated over three thousand years of sacred geography, and the statement of account has never been settled because no party to it has ever agreed on the currency.

This series is an attempt to read that statement honestly.

The Three Claims, Briefly

Judaism’s connection to Jerusalem is the oldest and most archaeologically documented. King David established the city as his capital around 1000 BCE. His son Solomon built the First Temple on the Mount, which the Babylonians destroyed in 586 BCE. The Second Temple was built on the same site, expanded magnificently by Herod the Great, and destroyed by the Romans in 70 CE — the event that dispersed the Jewish people across the known world in what became the Diaspora. The Western Wall is what remains of that Temple’s outer retaining structure. For nearly two thousand years of exile, Jews prayed facing Jerusalem and concluded the Passover Seder with the phrase L’Shanah Haba’ah B’Yerushalayim — Next Year in Jerusalem. This is not metaphor. It is the most sustained expression of national longing in recorded human history.

Islam’s connection came later but is no less profound to its believers. The Prophet Muhammad’s Night Journey — the Isra and Mi’raj — described in the Quran and the Hadith brought him to Jerusalem before his ascent to the heavens. The Dome of the Rock, completed in 691 CE, marks the rock from which that ascent is believed to have occurred — the same rock, in Jewish tradition, where Abraham prepared to sacrifice Isaac, and where the Holy of Holies of the Temple once stood. This is not coincidence and it is not accident. It is the precise geography of contested sanctity. Al-Aqsa Mosque, adjacent to the Dome, is where the Prophet led prayers during the Night Journey. For 1.8 billion Muslims, this ground is not negotiable.

The Western Wall with Dome of the Rock above

Christianity’s claim is centred slightly westward but no less absolute. The Via Dolorosa — the Way of Suffering — winds through the Muslim Quarter and ends at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, which has been the destination of Christian pilgrimage since the Emperor Constantine’s mother Helena identified the site in the 4th century. The church itself is a study in the impossibility of Christian unity: it is divided between six Christian denominations — Roman Catholic, Greek Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Coptic, Ethiopian, and Syriac — whose historical enmity is so intense that a Muslim family, the Nuseibeh family, has held the key to the church since Saladin entrusted it to them in the 12th century because the Christians could not agree on which of them should hold it. They still hold it today.

The comedy of that arrangement should not distract from its lesson. Even within a single faith, Jerusalem produces irreconcilable claims.

View of church of the Holy Sepulchre. Jerusalem, Israel

What Abraham Does and Does Not Prove

Since we are beginning in Jerusalem and tracing backward, Abraham stands at the convergence of all three traditions and must be addressed directly.

The patriarch Abraham — Ibrahim in the Islamic tradition — is claimed as the common ancestor of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, the three Abrahamic faiths. God’s covenant with Abraham, recorded in Genesis, includes the promise of land: “To your descendants I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the Euphrates.” In Islamic tradition, Abraham rebuilt the Kaaba in Mecca with his son Ishmael, establishing the sacred geography of a different faith entirely on different ground. In Christian tradition, Abraham is the father of faith whose willingness to sacrifice Isaac prefigures God’s sacrifice of Christ.

Three billion people trace a spiritual lineage to the same man. That is a fact of staggering historical and anthropological importance. It explains the unique emotional temperature of this conflict — why it generates a heat entirely disproportionate to the land mass involved, why it has mobilized populations on every continent, why reasonable people lose their capacity for reason when Jerusalem enters the conversation.

What it does not do is settle the question of who owns a square kilometre of contested real estate in the 21st century.

I say this with full respect for faith and none for the manipulation of faith in the service of political claims. The moment a democratic government — or anyone arguing before the international community — grounds its territorial assertions in divine promise recorded in an ancient text, we have exited the domain of law and entered the domain of competing theologies, where there is no referee and no appeals court. The religious Zionist right in Israel makes exactly this argument: God gave this land to the Jews, and no United Nations resolution supersedes the Torah. Hamas makes a structurally identical argument from the other direction: this land is Islamic waqf — a religious endowment that can never be surrendered. Both arguments are, in the domain of international law and human rights, equally inadmissible.

This does not mean the arguments are not real, or not sincerely held, or without historical content. They are all three. But sincerity is not justification. The Crusaders were sincere. We will come to them shortly, and the results, as history records, were not encouraging for anyone.

Abraham will appear throughout this series because his legacy is woven into every argument made by every party to this conflict. He will not be settling it.

The City That Has Never Belonged to Anyone for Long

One of the more instructive exercises available to anyone trying to understand Jerusalem is to list its conquerors in sequence.

The Canaanites held it before David. David took it from the Jebusites around 1000 BCE. The Babylonians under Nebuchadnezzar destroyed it in 586 BCE. The Persians under Cyrus the Great allowed the Jews to return and rebuild in 538 BCE. Alexander the Great took it in 332 BCE without much resistance. The Ptolemies of Egypt held it after Alexander’s death. The Seleucid Greeks under Antiochus IV desecrated the Temple in 167 BCE, triggering the Maccabean revolt — the events commemorated by Hanukkah. The Hasmonean Jewish dynasty reclaimed it. The Romans under Pompey took it in 63 BCE. Herod the Great built his magnificent version of the Temple under Roman authority. The Romans destroyed it in 70 CE. The Byzantines built Christian churches on the ruins. The Persians conquered it in 614 CE. The Byzantines retook it in 629 CE. The Arab Muslims under Caliph Umar took it in 637 CE and built the Dome of the Rock. The Crusaders slaughtered its Muslim and Jewish population and took it in 1099 CE. Saladin retook it in 1187 CE. The Crusaders briefly held parts of it again. The Mamluks controlled it from 1250 CE. The Ottoman Turks took it in 1517 CE. The British took it from the Ottomans in 1917 CE. The State of Israel declared sovereignty over West Jerusalem in 1948, took the Old City in 1967, and declared all of Jerusalem its “eternal and undivided capital” in a 1980 Basic Law that the United Nations promptly declared null and void.

The lesson of that list is not that anyone’s claim is invalid. It is that every claim in Jerusalem’s history has been temporary, has been contested, and has been superseded by force. The city has never, in three thousand years, found a stable arrangement that all its inhabitants accepted as legitimate.

That is not a reason for despair. It is context. Anyone — politician, journalist, or prophet — who tells you they have the final solution to Jerusalem is either lying or deluded. The honest position is that the city requires a negotiated arrangement that allows all three faiths access to their sacred sites under conditions none of them fully controls. That arrangement has existed in imperfect forms before. The Ottoman millet system, which we will examine in Part III, came closer to it than anything since.

The Specific Problem of the Temple Mount

Within the larger impossibility of Jerusalem, the Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif represents the specific, most dangerous, most intractable sub-problem.

For Jews, the Temple Mount is the holiest site on earth — the location of both Temples, the site of the Holy of Holies where God’s presence dwelt. By rabbinical law, many Orthodox Jews will not set foot on the Mount because they might inadvertently tread on the site of the Holy of Holies without knowing its precise location. The Western Wall, below the Mount’s retaining structure, is where Jews pray — as close as they can come to the Temple itself.

For Muslims, the Haram al-Sharif — which has been under continuous Islamic administration since 637 CE with the brief exception of the Crusader period — is the third holiest site in Islam. The Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque are not merely religious buildings. They are the physical expression of fourteen centuries of Islamic presence in Jerusalem. Any attempt to alter the status quo on the Mount is understood by Muslims worldwide as an existential attack on Islam itself.

Temple Mount

Since 1967, when Israel captured the Old City, a peculiar arrangement has governed the Mount. Israel has sovereignty — in its own claim — but the day-to-day administration is managed by the Islamic Waqf, a Jordanian-controlled religious trust. Jews may visit the Mount but may not pray there publicly — a restriction that Israeli security enforces, uncomfortably, on Jewish religious nationalists who wish to pray at what they regard as their holiest site. This arrangement was brokered by Moshe Dayan immediately after the 1967 conquest specifically to prevent the Mount from becoming the trigger for a wider religious war.

It is an extraordinarily fragile arrangement, and it has been deliberately destabilized, repeatedly and with intent, by Israeli religious nationalists who conduct increasingly provocative visits to the Mount in numbers that make the arrangement functionally unenforceable. Itamar Ben Gvir — currently Israel’s National Security Minister, a man whose portrait of Baruch Goldstein (who massacred 29 Muslim worshippers at the Cave of the Patriarchs in 1994) hung in his living room until recently — has made visits to the Temple Mount a deliberate political provocation. Each visit is a match struck near dry timber.

The Camp David negotiations in 2000 broke down, in significant part, over the Temple Mount. Ehud Barak offered the Palestinians sovereignty over the surface of the Mount while Israel retained sovereignty over the subterranean layers — the area of the Temple below. Yasser Arafat rejected it. Whether that rejection was a catastrophic miscalculation or a principled refusal to surrender Islamic sovereignty over the third holiest site in Islam is a debate that continues to define how the left and right of Israeli-Palestinian discourse understand the Oslo era. We will examine it in detail in Part VIII.

The point here is simpler: the Temple Mount is a problem without a solution that both parties can currently accept, and every Israeli government that has armed, funded, and politically empowered the Jewish nationalist movement that views the Mount’s status as temporary has been pouring accelerant on the most dangerous fire in the world.

Why This Is Not Ancient History

A certain category of Western commentator, usually one who has never visited Jerusalem, dismisses the religious dimension of this conflict as medieval superstition that sensible people ought to be able to set aside in the interest of a rational political settlement.

This is among the more reliably useless analytical positions available.

The religious dimension of this conflict is not a remnant of the past that modernity has failed to dissolve. It is a living, intensifying, politically organized force on all sides. The settlement movement in the West Bank is not primarily driven by demand for affordable housing. It is driven by religious nationalist ideology that holds the biblical land of Judea and Samaria as divinely mandated Jewish territory. The settlers who have moved to the most dangerous, most isolated hilltops in the West Bank, who have terrorized Palestinian villages in what the Israeli military itself has documented as pogrom-like attacks — these are not property speculators. They are true believers acting on a theological conviction that their government has funded, protected, and politically rewarded for fifty years.

On the Palestinian side, Hamas’s founding charter originally called for the destruction of Israel in explicitly Islamic terms, framing the conflict as a religious duty rather than a national liberation struggle. While Hamas has moderated some of its formal language since the 2017 charter revision, its operational ideology remains rooted in Islamic resistance theology that has no room for the legitimacy of a Jewish state on Islamic land.

And in the United States — whose role as Israel’s primary enabler we will examine at length in Part XII — somewhere between 30 and 40 million evangelical Christians support Israel not primarily for strategic reasons but because they believe, as a matter of literal biblical prophecy, that Israel’s control of the Holy Land is a prerequisite for the Second Coming of Jesus Christ. This is not a fringe position. It is a major constituency of the Republican Party, and it has shaped American foreign policy in the Middle East in ways that realpolitik analysis consistently underestimates.

You cannot negotiate a political settlement in a territory where three billion people have theological convictions about the outcome. You can only manage the conflict as humanely as possible while working to change the political conditions — the settlements, the blockades, the weapons flows, the diplomatic vetoes — that make a just outcome structurally impossible. We will spend the rest of this series examining precisely how those conditions were created, who created them, and what it would take to change them.

The City and Its Mirror

Jerusalem is, finally, a mirror. What you see in it depends entirely on where you stand.

Stand at the Western Wall on a Friday evening as the Sabbath descends, thousands of Jews pressed against the ancient stone in prayer, and you see something genuinely moving — a people’s two-thousand-year conversation with exile and return, with loss and persistence, with a God who made promises and a history that tested them beyond endurance. Whatever your politics, that is a human reality that demands acknowledgment.

Stand on the roof of the Austrian Hospice in the Muslim Quarter on a Friday noon as the call to prayer rises from Al-Aqsa and thousands of worshippers move through the narrow streets toward the Haram, and you see something equally genuine — a faith fourteen centuries old expressing itself in the city where its Prophet walked, in a community that has been there continuously through every conqueror that came and went.

Stand outside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre on Easter morning as the flame of the Holy Fire is passed through the crowd and out the door to the waiting world, and you see the oldest continuous Christian community on earth marking the central event of its faith in the city where that event occurred.

None of these realities erases the others. None of them, by itself, settles anything. But all of them must be held simultaneously by anyone attempting to understand what is happening in this region — why passions run so high, why compromise feels like desecration to all parties, why outsiders proposing rational solutions are met with incomprehension and fury.

This is what it means to say Jerusalem is not just a city. It is the world’s most concentrated argument about which human stories get to count, which sufferings get weighed most heavily, which God — or whose interpretation of the same God — gets the last word.

The rest of this series follows the chain of decisions, mostly made by powerful people who bore none of their consequences, that turned that ancient argument into the specific modern catastrophe we are watching unfold in real time. It begins, in Part II, with the men who were so certain God was on their side that they crossed a continent to prove it — and who established a template for Western intervention in the Middle East that has never entirely been abandoned.

They were called Crusaders. They were wrong about almost everything. And we are still living in the world they made.
