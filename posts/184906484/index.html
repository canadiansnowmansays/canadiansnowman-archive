The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 9: December 2025-January 2026 - The Reckoning

April 3, 2026

Editor’s Note

This essay was written on January 17, 2026, in real time, as protests raged across all 31 Iranian provinces. I was tracking a story that had no ending yet. It has one now.

In the weeks after this was drafted, Khamenei ordered live fire on his own people. The massacres of January 8 and 9 — planned in advance, modelled on Tiananmen, approved by all three branches of government — left somewhere between 7,000 and 36,500 Iranians dead. The Supreme Leader later acknowledged that “thousands” were killed, while blaming American agents for the bodies.

The protests were suppressed. Over 24,000 were arrested. Then, on February 28, the strikes came.

I have added a closing section — “What Happened Next” — that the original essay could not contain. Everything before it stands as written, dated January 17, 2026. I have not changed a word of the original analysis. It earned the right to stand.

The Iran Crisis series, which began January 12, 2026, picks up where this essay ends.

— Stephen, April F2026

AUTHOR’S NOTE ON ONGOING EVENTS: This essay documents protests that began December 28, 2025 and were ongoing as of January 17, 2026. Due to Iran’s internet shutdown (imposed January 8), information verification remains challenging. Death toll estimates vary widely based on source methodology:

Death Toll Estimates (as of January 17, 2026):

Iran Human Rights (IHR, Norway-based NGO): 3,428 verified deaths (as of January 14, 2026)

Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA): 3,090 verified deaths, 22,123 arrests

UK Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper (Parliamentary statement): “may have been 2,000... may prove significantly higher”

CBS News (citing sources inside Iran): 12,000-20,000 possible

Iran International (opposition media): 12,000+ estimated

Iranian officials (unnamed, cited by Reuters): ~2,000 including protesters and security forces

Khamenei statement (January 17, 2026): “several thousands” killed (first official acknowledgment of scale)

The Current Uprising and the Regime’s Most Dangerous Moment

The Grand Bazaar, Tehran — merchants who helped birth the 1979 revolution closed their shops on December 28, 2025, the day the rial crossed 1.4 million to the dollar.

On December 28, 2025, the Iranian rial crashed to a record low: approximately 1.4 million to the US dollar. Tehran’s Grand Bazaar merchants—traditionally conservative, often regime supporters—shuttered their shops in protest.

By afternoon, demonstrations had erupted. By evening, protests were spreading to other provinces. By January 5, 2026—just eight days later—unrest had reached all 31 of Iran’s provinces and at least 108 cities.

This wasn’t 1999 students or 2009’s middle class or even 2022’s women-led movement. This was everyone, everywhere, simultaneously: merchants and workers, pensioners and youth, rural and urban, traditional strongholds and opposition areas.

The slogans left no ambiguity:

“Death to the Dictator!” (Khamenei)

“Death to Khamenei!”

“This year is the year of blood, Khamenei will be overthrown!”

“This country won’t be fixed until we bury the mullahs!”

On January 17, 2026, Khamenei broke weeks of relative silence: “Those linked to Israel and US caused massive damages and killed several thousands.”

He was admitting, for the first time, the scale of the killing—thousands dead. And he was lying, as usual, about who killed them. Sources close to Iran’s Supreme National Security Council reported the killing was ordered directly by Khamenei himself.

This is where we are now. The largest, most geographically comprehensive, most sustained protests since 1979. A regime that has killed thousands but hasn’t fractured the opposition. A Supreme Leader whose only responses are bullets, blame, and bunkers.

By every standard he established—protecting Iranians, upholding Islamic justice, serving the people—January 2026 marks the culmination of 37 years of failure.

I. THE BREAKING POINT: DECEMBER 28

The rial’s collapse to 1.4 million per USD wasn’t sudden—it was acceleration of long decline. But crossing that psychological barrier crystallized desperation into action.

Tehran’s Grand Bazaar holds symbolic weight. Bazaar merchants helped fuel the 1979 revolution. Their support (or opposition) matters. When they closed shops December 28 and marched in protest, it signaled something different: the revolution’s traditional base was rejecting the revolution’s Guardian.

Within hours, protests erupted elsewhere in Tehran. By evening, demonstrations had spread to:

  • Isfahan

  • Mashhad

  • Shiraz

  • Kermanshah

  • Tabriz

  • Dozens of smaller cities

The Speed

Unlike 2009’s gradual build or even 2019’s rapid spread, December 2025-January 2026 exploded almost instantaneously across the country. Social media (despite censorship) and messaging apps (despite restrictions) spread information faster than 2019. VPNs bypassed government controls. Videos of protests in one city inspired protests in others within hours.

The government couldn’t isolate regions or cities. By January 5, all 31 provinces had seen protests. By January 8, over 108 cities documented. By mid-January, even regime strongholds like Qom (religious center) witnessed demonstrations.

In a video shared early Saturday by the US State Department, ongoing protests can be seen across Iranian cities. The demonstrations, which began last Sunday, have since spread to 113 locations across 46 Iranian cities in 22 provinces.

The Trigger vs. The Cause

Currency crash triggered the protests, but it wasn’t the cause. The causes accumulated over 37 years:

  • 50%+ poverty

  • 60% malnourishment

  • 48% inflation

  • Daily blackouts

  • Water shortages

  • Medicine unavailability

  • June 2025 military humiliation

  • December 2024 regional empire collapse

  • Decades of repression

  • Systematic corruption

  • IRGC enrichment while people starve

  • Billions to foreign proxies while Iranians suffer

The rial crash was the spark. The fuel was 37 years of failure.

II. WHAT’S DIFFERENT THIS TIME

Every protest movement has unique characteristics. January 2026 combines elements from all previous uprisings—and adds new dimensions.

Geographic Totality

Previous protests:

  • 1999: 3 cities

  • 2009: ~10 major cities

  • 2017-18: 80-140 cities

  • 2019: 140+ cities

  • 2022-23: 100+ cities

  • 2025-26: All 31 provinces, 108+ cities (within 8 days)

This isn’t partial. This is national rejection.

Social Composition

Not just students (1999), not just middle class (2009), not just working poor (2019), not just women (2022)—but all simultaneously:

  • Bazaar merchants (traditionally conservative)

  • Industrial workers (economic backbone)

  • Pensioners (elderly, often religious)

  • Youth (Gen Z, no memory of revolution)

  • Women (continuing “Woman, Life, Freedom”)

  • University students (always present)

  • Rural residents (often regime strongholds)

When elderly pensioners join Gen Z youth in chanting “Death to Khamenei,” something fundamental has shifted.

Iranians gather while blocking a street during a protest in Tehran, Iran on Jan. 9, 2026. (MAHSA / Middle East Images / AFP via Getty Images)

Ideological Evolution

Trace slogans across decades:

1999: “Death to the dictator” (somewhat coded)

2009: “Where is my vote?” (reform-seeking)

2017-19: “Leave Syria, think about us!” (nationalist critique)

2022: “Woman, Life, Freedom” / “We don’t want Islamic Republic” (revolutionary, but specific trigger)

2025-26: Explicitly revolutionary AND explicit about target:

  • “Death to Khamenei” (direct, personal)

  • “This year is year of blood, Khamenei will be overthrown” (prediction of his fall)

  • “This country won’t be fixed until we bury the mullahs” (systemic rejection)

  • Monarchist sentiments emerging (Lion and Sun flags)

The Monarchist Turn

Something remarkable: pre-revolution symbols appearing prominently.

The Lion and Sun flag (used before 1979) waves at protests. Chants reference Reza Shah (modernizing monarch). On January 9, 2026, X (Twitter) changed Iran’s flag emoji to the Lion and Sun design.

This doesn’t necessarily mean monarchist restoration is likely or desired by majority. But it signals complete rejection of Islamic Republic’s legitimacy. Even pre-revolution symbols are preferable to current system.

The Regime Strongholds

Most significant: protests in areas previously loyal.

Qom (clerical center), Mashhad (religious city), rural areas in traditionally conservative provinces—all seeing demonstrations. When the revolution’s base abandons the revolution, the system faces existential crisis.

III. THE VIRAL DEFIANCE

One phenomenon captures the movement’s character: women burning Khamenei’s pictures.

Videos proliferated across X, Reddit, Instagram, Telegram: Iranian women, faces visible, lighting cigarettes with burning images of the Supreme Leader. Some filming themselves, narrating their defiance. Others silent, letting the act speak.

Under Iranian law, this is serious crime. Defacing Supreme Leader’s image = insulting Islamic sanctities, potentially punishable by years in prison.

But thousands of women did it anyway, filmed it, and shared it globally. The message: We don’t fear you anymore.

NDTV and multiple outlets noted this represented “direct challenge to country’s political and religious authority.” The regime’s image—literally and figuratively—going up in smoke, held by women’s hands, while they lit cigarettes (also defiance of public morality codes), all filmed and broadcast.

This wasn’t protest. This was desecration as performance art. And it was everywhere.

The Symbolism

For 43 years, Khamenei’s image appeared everywhere—schools, government offices, military bases, public spaces. His face watched Iranians constantly, reminder of authority and surveillance.

Now, Iranians were burning that face, filming it, and spreading videos. The panopticon was collapsing. The sacred was becoming profane. The feared was becoming mocked.

When authority loses the one thing it can’t survive without—fear—collapse accelerates.

IV. THE KILLING ESCALATES

Security forces responded with familiar brutality—but on unprecedented scale.

The Weapons

Documented by human rights monitors including Abdorrahman Boroumand Center:

  • Live ammunition from day one

  • DShK heavy machine guns (used against protesters)

  • Deliberate targeting of heads, eyes, genitals

  • Goal: Kill and terrorize through mutilation

A Tehran doctor told The Guardian (January 13): “Security forces are shooting to kill. We’re seeing head wounds, chest wounds, wounds to vital organs. This isn’t crowd control. This is execution.”

The Death Toll

As of January 17, 2026, estimates vary wildly:

Government claims: 2,000+ (blaming “foreign-backed terrorists”)

Human rights groups documented: 544-600+ with verification

Opposition sources: Potentially 3,000+

Khamenei’s January 17 statement acknowledged “several thousands” killed—first time giving sense of scale, though he blamed U.S. and Israel.

The Arrests

Over 2,000 detained within first three weeks. Many face torture. Some will face execution after rushed trials (pattern from 2022).

V. JANUARY 17: KHAMENEI SPEAKS

After weeks of limited public statements, Khamenei addressed the situation in a speech broadcast on Iranian state television on January 17, 2026. As reported by Al Jazeera, NPR, Bloomberg, Times of Israel, NBC News, and other major outlets:

“Those linked to Israel and US caused massive damages and killed several thousands during the protests.”

This marked the first time an Iranian official acknowledged the scale of casualties. Khamenei blamed foreign powers rather than security forces for the deaths. His admission of “several thousands” killed aligns with estimates from human rights organizations (Iran Human Rights: 3,428 verified; HRANA: 3,090 verified) but contradicts earlier official claims of only hundreds dead.

As Al Jazeera’s correspondent in Tehran noted: “One thing that is new in his speech is that for the first time he is actually giving a sense of the numbers of people killed.”

The Admission

For the first time, he acknowledged the scale: thousands killed. Previously, the government had downplayed casualties, denied systematic killing, blamed scattered incidents on “terrorists.”

Now, the Supreme Leader admitted thousands died—while blaming foreign powers.

The Accusation

“The latest anti-Iran sedition was different in that the US president personally became involved.”

He accused Donald Trump of orchestrating the protests and claimed actors linked to U.S. and Israel killed thousands of Iranians on Iranian soil.

Apply His Logic

If U.S. and Israel killed thousands of Iranians across all 31 provinces:

  • That proves total security failure

  • The Guardian failed to guard

  • Iranian sovereignty is fiction

  • Foreign powers operate at will inside Iran

If security forces killed thousands on Khamenei’s orders (what sources say):

  • He’s lying to avoid accountability

  • He’s blaming foreigners for his own massacre

  • The “Guardian” is killing those he claims to protect

Either interpretation damns him by his own standards.

The Warning

“We will not drag the country into war, but we will not let domestic or international criminals go unpunished.”

Translation: More killing coming. More arrests. More torture. More executions.

The pattern holds: Khamenei speaks, violence escalates.

VI. THE TRUMP FACTOR

President Trump has repeatedly threatened military intervention:

“All options are on table regarding Iran.”

“If Khamenei’s regime continues killing protesters, there will be consequences.”

The threats created dilemma for Iranian leadership:

  • Escalate repression → Risk U.S. military strikes

  • Allow protests to grow → Risk regime collapse

The Calculation

Iran is weak militarily (June 2025 proved that), economically (system is collapsing), and politically (legitimacy shattered).

U.S. strikes would devastate what remains. But allowing protests to succeed would end the regime entirely.

Khamenei chose bullets over reform—as always. But the choice now carries higher risk: Trump might actually strike, especially if death tolls continue climbing.

The Regional Isolation

Unlike previous protest cycles, Iran has no regional allies to provide cover:

  • Syria: Lost (Assad in Moscow)

  • Hezbollah: Devastated, can’t help

  • Russia: Focused on Ukraine, provided only words

  • China: Buys oil but won’t defend Iran militarily

  • Arab states: Many quietly support protests

Iran is alone. The “Axis of Resistance” is ash. The “Look East” strategy produced no protection.

When Trump threatens strikes, who will defend Iran? No one.

VII. THE SYSTEM SHOWS STRAIN

While elite unity appears to hold publicly, cracks are emerging:

Government Responses

  • Central bank governor replaced (panic measure)

  • New subsidy system announced (attempting to calm economic anger)

  • President Pezeshkian acknowledged “economic grievances”

  • Some reformist allies calling for “institutional reforms”

These are band-aids on hemorrhaging wounds, but they signal elite concern.

The IRGC Arrests

Security forces are arresting protesters in massive numbers—sign of desperation, not control. If the system were stable, this level of repression wouldn’t be necessary.

The more they arrest, the more they admit the scale of opposition.

The Information Battle

Despite internet restrictions and censorship, videos continue flowing out. VPNs proliferate. Satellite connections work. Iranians are more media-savvy than 2019.

The government lost the information battle before it began.

The Fatigue Factor

Security forces—Basij militia, police, even IRGC—are human. Shooting thousands of fellow Iranians, facing massive protests daily, dealing with their own families’ economic struggles...

Eventually, fatigue sets in. Defections become possible. The willingness to kill fellow citizens erodes.

We’re not there yet. But every day the protests continue, the possibility grows.

VIII. THE QUESTION THAT MATTERS

Will this time be different?

Iranians have asked that question five times now: 1999, 2009, 2017-19, 2022, and now 2025-26.

Each time, brutal repression succeeded. Each time, the regime survived. Each time, protesters were killed, arrested, terrorized into submission.

What’s Different:

  1. Timing: Post-June 2025 military humiliation, post-December 2024 regional collapse, worst economic crisis in modern Iranian history—all simultaneously

  2. Totality: All provinces, all cities, all social classes, all age groups

  3. Duration: Three weeks and counting, no sign of subsiding

  4. Elite strain: Cracks appearing in government responses

  5. International pressure: Trump threatening intervention

  6. Regional isolation: No allies to provide cover

  7. Legitimacy shattered: Even traditional supporters (bazaaris, religious cities) are rebelling

What’s The Same:

  1. IRGC loyalty: Revolutionary Guards remain (publicly) loyal

  2. Elite unity: No visible fractures at highest levels yet

  3. Willingness to kill: Security forces shooting protesters as in past

  4. Blame foreigners: Same excuse, same lies

  5. No organized opposition: Protests are organic but leaderless

The Verdict:

Too early to predict. The regime is weaker than ever—but it’s survived weaker positions before through sheer brutality.

The difference maker: Will bullets run out before protesters do? Will security forces maintain willingness to kill indefinitely? Will elite unity hold?

As of January 17, 2026, the answers aren’t clear. But the questions themselves demonstrate how precarious Khamenei’s position has become.

CLOSING: THE RECKONING ARRIVES

For 37 years, Ayatollah Khamenei accumulated absolute power, crushed dissent, blamed enemies, and survived.

Five major protest movements. Thousands killed. Tens of thousands imprisoned. Decades of repression.

And yet: January 2026 sees Iranians across all 31 provinces demanding his overthrow.

The reckoning isn’t just protests. It’s the culmination of every failure:

  • Economic: 50% poverty, 60% malnourishment, currency worthless

  • Military: June 2025 humiliation still fresh

  • Regional: December 2024 collapse still visible

  • Legitimacy: Even traditional supporters rebelling

  • Future: Succession crisis with no legitimate heir

The Guardian promised to protect the Iranian people. In January 2026, he’s killing them by the thousands.

The Guardian promised to uphold the revolution. In January 2026, protesters explicitly reject the revolution.

The Guardian promised Islamic justice. In January 2026, he admits to killing thousands while blaming foreigners.

By his own standards—protection, revolutionary values, Islamic justice—the reckoning reveals total failure.

The bunker beneath his compound awaits. The Iranian people want him to stay there—permanently.

And this time, they might not stop until he does.

WHAT HAPPENED NEXT January 18 – February 28, 2026: From Massacre to Military Strike

When I wrote the preceding pages on January 17, 2026, the outcome was not yet known. It is known now. What follows is the ending the essay could not have.

The January Massacres

On January 8 and 9, 2026, the Islamic Republic executed what investigators would later describe as a planned, systematic massacre of its own citizens. According to Iran International, which conducted a multi-stage investigation drawing on government sources, hospital records, and eyewitness testimony, at least 12,000 civilians were killed in those two days alone. Other estimates ranged from 7,000 to upwards of 36,500 dead. The regime’s own Ministry of Health put the figure at 30,000 in the first 48 hours.

The planning had begun in 2022. According to a former Ministry of Interior official, preparations included the identification of elevated sniper positions, ideological training to “prepare” security forces psychologically for killing, and the embedding of agents in protest crowds to steer movements and identify participants. The model, investigators concluded, was the 1989 Tiananmen Square massacre. Primary responsibility was attributed by The New York Times and Iran International to Khamenei himself, with the approval of Iran’s judiciary and executive.

On January 10, Khamenei told the nation that protester demands were “completely fair” — while simultaneously ordering that “rioters be put in their place.” On January 13, the Tehran prosecutor announced that an undisclosed number of protesters would be charged with moharebeh — waging war against God — an offence carrying the death penalty. By January 14, over 18,400 had been arrested. By January 18, that number had risen to 24,669.

The internet shutdown, already in place, was maintained. Information moved in fragments: a video here, a doctor’s testimony there. The full picture would take weeks to assemble. The scale, when it emerged, would place these events among the largest massacres in modern history.

The Suppression

By late January, the protests had been broken — not resolved, not addressed, but broken by force. The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace noted the pattern: targeted surveillance, mass arrests, financial retribution, fabricated narratives of foreign conspiracy, and a level of violence unprecedented in Iran’s post-revolutionary history.

Khamenei had survived. The regime had survived. The cost: somewhere between 7,000 and 36,500 of its own citizens, tens of thousands imprisoned, the last residue of revolutionary legitimacy spent.

On February 23, 2026, former President Mohammad Khatami — a reformist who had served under Khamenei for years — issued a public statement expressing sympathy for the families of victims and calling for the release of peaceful protesters. It was the clearest sign yet that even the regime’s own house was not intact.

February 28, 2026: The Strikes

On the evening of February 28, 2026, joint US-Israeli strikes hit targets across Iran. Nuclear facilities. Military infrastructure. Command and control centres. And the compound in Tehran where Ali Khamenei had taken shelter.

He was killed in the strike, along with his daughter, son-in-law, and grandson. Other senior officials died alongside him. The man who had spent decades in a bunker — who had gone underground for twelve days during the June 2025 war, who had built a defensive architecture designed to survive every threat — did not survive this one.

In Portland, Oregon, hundreds of Iranian-Americans gathered at Pioneer Courthouse Square to celebrate. One organiser told reporters: “This is something that we’ve been asking for, begging for, crying for, for 47 years, and finally it’s done.”

In Tehran, people poured into the streets. Some mourned. Others, according to witness accounts, did not.

The bunker awaits. Those words appeared in this essay’s original draft. He died in one. The metaphor held to the end.

The essay you have just read was written while he was alive, while the outcome was uncertain, while the protests still burned. It described a man who had failed every standard he set for himself — by his own words, his own promises, the revolution’s own constitution. History delivered the verdict that analysis had already reached.

Parts 10 through 13 of this series were also written before these events. They stand. Read them as they were intended: as a reckoning that arrived on schedule.

This essay uses the most conservative verified figures (3,000-3,500 range) while acknowledging that the true toll may be significantly higher. The internet blackout, which NetBlocks confirmed as unprecedented in scale for Iran, severely limits independent verification.
