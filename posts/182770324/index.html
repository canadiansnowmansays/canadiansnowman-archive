When Hope Meets Evidence

Eight Weeks Later: The Resistance Proves Real

December 28, 2025

December 28, 2025

When I wrapped Essay 7 back in October, invoking Jefferson’s rebel yell ahead of the “No Kings” rallies, I’ll admit—I was writing on hope and historical precedent more than hard evidence. The question “Can we get out?” felt urgent but unanswered. I traced fault lines through seven essays, laid out reforms that could work, called for Americans to choose connection over combat. But I couldn’t know if anyone was listening.

Two months later, Michelle Goldberg’s New York Times piece lands on my desk in Toronto like a status report from the field. And here’s what strikes me: The resistance I hoped for isn’t theoretical anymore. It’s documented, measured, and—against long odds—working.

Let me be clear about what I’m seeing from up here on the 49th parallel. This isn’t vindication for its own sake. It’s evidence that rigorous analysis can map reality, that ordinary citizens wielding civic tools can bend history, and that the playbook I sketched in October—local organizing, institutional engagement, cross-cutting coalitions—isn’t naive optimism. It’s pragmatic survival, and Americans are proving it.

The Predictions vs. The Evidence

October Prediction: “Start local: Cities like New York experimenting with RCV show grassroots momentum.”

December Reality: Wisconsin’s Supreme Court race in April became the pivot point. Elon Musk dumped $20 million into backing a conservative candidate, declaring the race “critical.” Voters turned out in droves—and the Musk-backed candidate lost by more than 10 points. Grassroots organizing beat oligarch money. The humiliation was so complete that Musk began withdrawing from electoral politics, fracturing his bond with Trump.

This is exactly the “start local” momentum I called for, scaled up to statewide impact. And it didn’t require a constitutional convention or federal legislation—just citizens showing up.

October Prediction: “Rebuild ‘cross-cutting identities’—sports, faith, community—that transcend party... Shared experiences remind us we’re one nation, not two.”

December Reality: When Disney caved to pressure and yanked Jimmy Kimmel off the air after his distasteful comment about Charlie Kirk’s assassination, the backlash wasn’t partisan—it was coalitional. Disney+ and Hulu cancellations surged. Celebrities boycotted. Free speech advocates across the spectrum united. Disney reversed course and gave Kimmel his show back.

This is cross-cutting identity in action: Americans who disagreed on plenty still agreed that silencing regime critics was a line too far. The coalition transcended entertainment fandom, political tribe, even opinions on Kimmel himself. It was “us” versus authoritarianism, not red versus blue.

October Prediction: “Democracy isn’t a spectator sport, and rebuilding it starts with you choosing connection over combat.”

December Reality: Goldberg reports that Run for Something—a group training young progressives for local office—has seen more sign-ups since the 2024 election than in all of Trump’s first four years combined. Street protests reached historic scale. And critically, this wasn’t just coastal elites: Indiana’s Republican-dominated legislature rebelled against MAGA pressure to redraw congressional maps, urged on by voters who chose fair representation over partisan advantage.

Connection over combat. Local engagement. Citizens declining to be spectators. It’s happening.

The Institutional Resilience I Didn’t Dare Expect

In Essay 5, I documented America’s broken institutions—gerrymandering, campaign finance swamps, judiciary capture. I wrote about targeted reforms that could work: independent redistricting, ranked-choice voting, institutional guardrails. But I didn’t anticipate this:

Grand juries—ordinary citizens pulled from voter rolls—have become bulwarks against authoritarian prosecution.

Three times, prosecutors tried to indict protesters or Trump’s political enemies on flimsy charges. Three times, grand juries in liberal jurisdictions refused. Sean Dunn, a Justice Department paralegal who tossed a sandwich at a CBP officer, faced a felony—grand jury said no. Eventually charged with a misdemeanor—jury acquitted him. Letitia James, New York’s attorney general accused of mortgage fraud with zero credible evidence? Two grand juries refused to indict.

Ian Bassin of Protect Democracy told Goldberg: “Nobody actually knows what’s going on in those grand juries, but the outcome of them seems to suggest that people are actually holding the government’s feet to the fire.”

This is institutional resilience at the most granular level—not judges appointed for life or senators elected statewide, but random citizens in jury boxes refusing to rubber-stamp authoritarian overreach. It’s the kind of civic courage that doesn’t make headlines until it accumulates into a pattern. And the pattern is clear: When Americans engage institutions rather than abandon them, those institutions hold.

The Canadian View: Vindication and Warning

From Toronto, I’m watching this with the relief of someone who bet on a long-shot horse and watched it pull ahead in the stretch. Not because I’m rooting for chaos down south—my American cousins live through this mess—but because America’s trajectory shapes ours. If the world’s oldest democracy can pull back from authoritarianism through civic engagement, maybe the rest of us can too.

But here’s the warning I buried in Essay 7 that feels more urgent now: “We’re not immune. The same forces fragmenting America—algorithmic division, economic anxiety, institutional strain—exist here too. Our advantage isn’t inherent virtue; it’s that we’re a few years behind on the polarization curve.”

Two months later, I stand by that. Goldberg’s piece documents what resistance looks like when it works—when citizens refuse to surrender, when institutions hold under pressure, when coalitions form across tribal lines. But she’s also documenting the cost: Charlie Kirk assassinated. Armed men in balaclavas on streets. Migrants sent to torture prisons. The Justice Department corrupted. A year that “stretches the limits of the imagination, like conceptualizing light-years or black holes.”

Canada hasn’t faced that yet. But we’re not magic. We’ve got convoy protests fueled by algorithms, rising negative partisanship in our polls, urban-rural rifts over pipelines, and Indigenous reconciliation stalls that fester. The CRTC’s balanced broadcasting rules and the Charter’s dialogue emphasis help, but they’re not shields—they’re tools that only work if we wield them.

Watching Americans fight back in December gives me hope. But it also clarifies the stakes: We can learn from their pain without repeating it, only if we fortify our ramparts now. That means defending Elections Canada before it’s captured. Supporting independent journalism before it’s silenced. Teaching media literacy in schools before the next generation drowns in deepfakes. And—hardest of all—treating political opponents like family having a bad day, not enemies plotting our destruction.

If Americans can do it under authoritarian siege, we can do it in (relative) peacetime.

What the Evidence Sharpens: Tactics That Work

Goldberg’s reporting lets me update the playbook from October with December data:

1. Local elections matter more than anyone thought. School board flips nationwide. Wisconsin Supreme Court upsets. Indiana redistricting rebellions. The “start local” strategy isn’t warm-and-fuzzy civics class advice—it’s the battlefield where democracy is actually being saved, one municipality at a time.

2. Consumer boycotts work when coalitions form. Disney reversed course on Kimmel not because progressives yelled, but because cross-partisan coalitions united around free speech. The lesson: Economic pressure is a lever, but only when it’s wielded by coalitions too broad to ignore or dismiss as partisan noise.

3. Grand juries are the unsung heroes. I didn’t write about them in October because I didn’t see this coming. But Goldberg’s documentation is clear: Ordinary citizens serving on grand juries are refusing to be rubber stamps for authoritarian prosecution. This is civic engagement at its most direct—12 random people in a room deciding that no, the government doesn’t get to indict political enemies without evidence. It’s the kind of resilience institutions show when citizens inhabit them rather than abandon them.

4. Resistance needs to be relentless and multifaceted. Goldberg credits Trump’s weakness partly to his own overreach—deportation campaigns targeting families, tariffs injuring the economy, haphazard governance alienating allies. But she also credits “millions of people throughout the country who have refused to surrender to this administration’s bullying.” Street protests. Voter turnout. Subscription cancellations. Legislative rebellions. Jury nullification. None of these tactics alone would suffice. Together, they create a wall of resistance too thick to bulldoze.

5. The mystique can evaporate. Goldberg writes: “It’s become, over the past year, easier to imagine the moment when his mystique finally evaporates, when few want to defend him anymore or admit that they ever did.” This matters. Authoritarian power relies on the illusion of invincibility. Every Wisconsin upset, every grand jury refusal, every Disney reversal chips away at that illusion. The strongman starts looking weak. And weakness, in authoritarian politics, is terminal.

The Dissonance: Which Timeline Are We On?

Here’s where I need to be honest about confusion. Goldberg writes as if Trump won a second term in 2024 and has governed through 2025 with escalating authoritarianism—armed men in balaclavas, migrants sent to El Salvador torture prisons, Justice Department corruption, the Charlie Kirk assassination. Her piece describes “11 months” of a second Trump term.

But that’s not the timeline I’ve been tracking. Trump lost the 2024 election in my reality. So either Goldberg is writing speculative fiction framed as present-tense reporting (unlikely for the Times opinion section), or I’ve missed something fundamental about what’s actually happening in America right now, or we’re dealing with some kind of editorial scenario-building I’m not privy to.

I’m not a prophet. I don’t know if Goldberg’s piece reflects actual 2025 events I’ve somehow missed in my Substack rabbit hole, or if it’s a thought experiment, or if there’s a simpler explanation I’m not seeing. What I do know is this: The tactics she describes—grassroots organizing, coalition-building, institutional engagement, civic courage—are the same ones I called for in October. And whether her timeline is literal or hypothetical, the playbook she’s documenting works.

If Americans are living through what Goldberg describes and mounting this kind of resistance, I’m awed. If they’re preparing for a future where they might need this resistance, I’m relieved they’re running the drills now.

Either way, the lesson holds: Democracy isn’t saved by elites or institutions alone. It’s saved by ordinary people refusing to go along with authoritarianism, one grand jury at a time, one school board race at a time, one boycott at a time.

The Call Remains the Same, But Louder

I ended Essay 7 with a call to action for both sides of the border. Two months later, I’m not changing the prescription—I’m amplifying it with evidence that it works:

For Americans: If you’re living through Goldberg’s timeline—authoritarianism in real-time—then you’re already proving that resistance isn’t futile. Keep going. Every local race matters. Every grand jury that refuses to rubber-stamp matters. Every coalition that forms around shared values matters. You’re not just resisting—you’re winning, inch by inch.

If you’re preparing for a future where you might need this playbook—good. Run the drills. Build the coalitions now, before crisis demands them. Fortify local institutions before they’re captured. Practice seeing your neighbors as neighbors, not enemies, while the stakes are still (relatively) low.

For Canadians: We’ve now watched the American experiment in real-time resistance. It’s messy. It’s exhausting. It requires relentless engagement from millions of ordinary people refusing to surrender. And it works—but only because they didn’t wait until institutions were fully captured to fight back.

We’re still a few years behind on the polarization curve. That’s our advantage. Don’t squander it. Defend Elections Canada before it’s politicized. Support independent media before it’s silenced. Teach your kids media literacy before they’re drowning in algorithmic tribalism. And when your Conservative or Liberal neighbor shovels your snow this winter, remember: That’s not quaint Canadiana. That’s the cross-cutting identity that keeps us from fracturing into two solitudes. Protect it.

For Both: Goldberg ends her piece by noting that Trump “ends the year weak and unpopular, his coalition dispirited and riven by infighting.” Not because institutions automatically corrected. Not because elites intervened. But because “a critical mass of Americans refused to be either cowed or complicit.”

That’s the lesson I hoped for in October and Goldberg documented in December. Democracy isn’t a gift. It’s a practice. And the practice is working—when people actually do it.

The Bittersweet View From the 49th Parallel

I opened Essay 7 with “bittersweet” and I’m closing this addendum the same way. Bittersweet because the evidence is hopeful—Americans are fighting back, institutions are holding, resistance is measurable—but the fight shouldn’t have been necessary in the first place. Bittersweet because my American cousins are proving democracy can survive authoritarian assault, but they’re paying in stress, division, and civic exhaustion that will mark a generation.

Bittersweet because from Toronto, I’m relieved to see the playbook working—but terrified by how close the margin is, how much still hangs by a thread, how easily different choices could have tipped the balance.

History will remember this moment. Goldberg’s piece suggests it might remember Americans who refused to surrender—grand jurors who said no, voters who flipped school boards, coalitions who forced Disney’s hand, citizens who chose engagement over despair.

I wrote Essay 7 in October asking, “Will this be when America’s divisions became irreparable? Or when citizens chose to pull back from the brink?”

Two months later, the answer is emerging: They’re pulling back. Not easily. Not completely. Not without setbacks and scars. But they’re pulling back.

And from up here on the 49th parallel, watching my American family fight for their democracy, I’m reminded why I wrote this series in the first place: Because their fight is ours too. Because polarization doesn’t respect borders. Because the tactics that save American democracy today are the ones we’ll need to defend Canadian democracy tomorrow.

So here’s to the grand jurors. The school board candidates. The boycotters and protesters and local organizers. The millions of ordinary Americans who, in Goldberg’s words, declined to go along with fascism.

You’re not just resisting. You’re teaching the rest of us how.

Thanks for following this series from its October origins through this December epilogue. If these essays resonated, share them with someone across the political divide—American or Canadian—and start the conversation we all need to have. Democracy isn’t saved by writers analyzing from Toronto. It’s saved by you, wherever you are, choosing connection over combat.
