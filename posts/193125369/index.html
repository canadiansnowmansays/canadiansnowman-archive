The Seven Ages of Trump

All the World's a Stage — and He's Playing Every Part

April 5, 2026

The Festival Theatre stage at the Stratford Festival, Stratford, Ontario — where Shakespeare's plays have been performed since 1953. "All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players; they have their exits and their entrances, and one man in his time plays many parts." The stage, as of this writing, is empty. The play, however, continues.

Sunday, April 5, 2026

“All the world’s a stage, And all the men and women merely players; They have their exits and their entrances, And one man in his time plays many parts, His acts being seven ages.”

— William Shakespeare, As You Like It, c. 1599

William Shakespeare, writing in approximately 1599, did not have access to Truth Social. He did not have a White House press secretary, a prime-time address from the East Room, or a Secretary of Defense who tweets “Back to the Stone Age” and considers it strategic communication. He did not have cable news, market-moving social media posts, or the singular experience of watching the leader of the free world describe a missing pilot over Iran as something that “will not affect negotiations.”

And yet.

Four hundred and twenty-seven years before Operation Epic Fury, Shakespeare wrote the most precise description of the Trump presidency that exists in any language. He put it in the mouth of Jaques, the melancholy philosopher of As You Like It, in a speech that begins: “All the world’s a stage.”

What follows is a close reading. The text is Shakespeare’s. The production is currently running at the White House, with limited engagement, and reviews have been mixed.

The First Age: The Infant

“At first, the infant, Mewling and puking in the nurse’s arms.”

The infant does not reason. The infant does not deliberate. The infant experiences a sensation — hunger, discomfort, the vague awareness that something is wrong — and responds immediately, without filter, without consideration of consequence, without any mechanism intervening between the feeling and its expression.

In the Trump universe, this is Truth Social.

“MAKE A DEAL BEFORE IT IS TOO LATE, AND THERE IS NOTHING LEFT OF WHAT STILL COULD BECOME A GREAT COUNTRY!” Posted at 11:47 PM on a Thursday, while a US pilot was missing over Iran and oil was trading at $108 a barrel.

“Time is running out — 48 hours before all Hell will reign down on them.” Posted on a Saturday morning, while the White House had called a lid, the president was not making public appearances, and his communications team was not answering questions about the missing airman.

“We are blasting Iran into oblivion or, as they say, back to the Stone Ages!!!” Three exclamation points. The infant does not edit.

The infant also does not explain. He does not frame, contextualize, or anticipate the consequences of his words for oil markets, allied governments, or the Iranian negotiators who are reading his posts as American policy. He feels, and he posts. The nurse’s arms, in this production, are the White House communications staff, who have learned that their function is not to shape the message but to survive it.

The Second Age: The Whining Schoolboy

“And then the whining schoolboy, with his satchel And shining morning face, creeping like snail Unwillingly to school.”

The whining schoolboy is the age of grievance — of the world’s persistent failure to recognize what is plainly obvious, which is that he is right, has always been right, and the homework was unfair to begin with.

Barack Hussein Obama gave them $1.7 billion in cash. Green, green cash. Took it out of the banks in Virginia, DC, and Maryland — all the cash they had — and flew it to Iran by airplane in an attempt to buy their respect and loyalty, but it didn’t work. They laughed at our president.

This was delivered in a prime-time address to the nation during an active war. The cash payment — a settlement of decades-old legal claims, negotiated by career diplomats — has been relitigated in every Trump speech on Iran since 2015. The schoolboy never stops whining about the homework that preceded him.

NATO. Europe. The allies who “weren’t there for us.” The countries that “refuse to get involved in the decapitation of Iran.” Fourteen years ago the Biden administration left office with a “dead and crippled country.” The media. The markets. The polls. France, which was “VERY UNHELPFUL.” Britain, which “refused to get involved” and, in any case, doesn’t even have a navy.

The whining schoolboy creeps unwillingly to school because school is where accountability lives. The homework is graded. The answers are either right or wrong. The teacher does not accept “many people are saying” as a citation.

In the Trump production, school is wherever the record is kept. He has spent his entire presidency creeping away from it.

The Third Age: The Lover

“And then the lover, Sighing like furnace, with a woeful ballad Made to his mistress’ eyebrow.”

The lover is the rally. The crowd. The reciprocal seduction between the performer and his audience that neither party can abandon because each has become essential to the other’s sense of reality.

The woeful ballad made to his mistress’ eyebrow is: “We’ve never been doing better than before. America is winning bigger than ever before. The strongest economy in history. Record setting investments, over $18 trillion. Fifty-three all-time record highs in just one year.”

This was delivered on April 1, 2026, as oil traded at $100 a barrel, gas crossed $4 nationally for the first time since 2022, the S&P 500 was in correction territory, and economists revised US growth forecasts downward and inflation forecasts upward.

The lover does not require his woeful ballad to be true. He requires it to be felt. The mistress’s eyebrow — the market, the base, the rally crowd — curves upward in something that resembles belief, and that is sufficient.

The tragedy of the lover’s age is not that he is insincere. It is that he has become genuinely unable to distinguish between the performance and the thing itself. When Trump says the economy is the strongest in history, he is not lying in the conventional sense. He is performing a truth that he has decided, in advance, to be true, and the performance is the evidence.

The furnace sighs. The ballad plays. The eyebrow curves.

The Fourth Age: The Soldier

“Then a soldier, Full of strange oaths and bearded like the pard, Jealous in honour, sudden and quick in quarrel, Seeking the bubble reputation Even in the cannon’s mouth.”

Operation Epic Fury. Operation Midnight Hammer. The “perfectly executed” capture of Venezuela’s president. The beautiful B-2 bombers. The A-10 Warthogs hunting fast-attack watercraft in the Strait of Hormuz. More than 12,300 targets struck. “We’ve done it all.”

The soldier’s age is the age of the TikTok airstrike video. The White House social media team splicing explosion footage with movie clips and a reference to the Proud Boys motto. Pete Hegseth tweeting “Back to the Stone Age” in his capacity as Secretary of Defense. The bubble reputation sought even in the cannon’s mouth — and, in this production, posted to X immediately afterward.

Charles Homans, writing in the Times, called it “TikTok Spartanism” — a burlesque of the warrior republic rather than a real enactment of it. The soldier’s age performed for an audience that has agreed, tacitly, not to be asked to sacrifice anything for the war it is watching. No war tax. No draft. No congressional vote. No honest accounting of objectives. Just the content. Just the cannon’s mouth, filmed in landscape mode and posted before the smoke clears.

The soldier’s age in this production has one complication that Shakespeare did not anticipate. In the actual cannon’s mouth, there is an F-15E Strike Eagle from the 494th Fighter Squadron at RAF Lakenheath. One crew member has been rescued. One is still missing. Iranian state television is calling on citizens to capture the pilot for a reward.

The bubble reputation is harder to seek when the pilot cannot be found.

The Fifth Age: The Justice

“And then the justice, In fair round belly with good capon lined, With eyes severe and beard of formal cut, Full of wise saws and modern instances; And so he plays his part.”

The justice is the age of self-appointment as the arbiter of all things. The man who has seen enough, done enough, survived enough, to pronounce on every matter with the authority of settled wisdom.

“I did many things during my two terms in office to stop the quest for nuclear weapons by Iran. First and perhaps most importantly, I killed General Qassem Soleimani.”

“I terminated Barack Hussein Obama’s Iran nuclear deal, a disaster.”

“I was so honored to do it. I was so proud to do it.”

“They made mistakes and I am correcting them.”

“Essentially, I did what no other president was willing to do.”

The justice has seen it all. He has the wise saws — “Drill, baby, drill.” “The hard part is done.” “It’ll just open up naturally.” He has the modern instances — the Wollman ice rink, the Art of the Deal, the Trump International Hotel, opened under budget and ahead of schedule, “five words.”

What the justice does not have, in this production, is a verdict that holds. The case he is making — that Iran has been beaten, decimated, eviscerated, that the war is nearing completion, that the strait will open naturally, that regime change was never the goal — has been rebutted, on the same day it was delivered, by Iran’s Revolutionary Guards, by US intelligence agencies, by the markets, by the IRGC helicopters searching for the missing pilot, and by the speaker of Iran’s Parliament.

The justice plays his part. The capon lines his belly. The eyes remain severe.

The gavel, however, has not come down on anything in particular.

The Sixth Age: The Lean and Slippered Pantaloon

President Trump delivers his prime-time address on Operation Epic Fury, White House, April 1, 2026 — the eighteenth minute of what Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic within the hour, described as "a disjointed series of complaints, brags, and exaggerations delivered by a man who looked and sounded tired." Shakespeare called the sixth age "the lean and slippered pantaloon, his big manly voice turning again toward childish treble." He was not writing about this speech. He did not need to. Credit: Alex Brandon/AP

“The sixth age shifts Into the lean and slippered pantaloon, With spectacles on nose and pouch on side, His youthful hose, well saved, a world too wide For his shrunk shank, and his big manly voice, Turning again toward childish treble, pipes And whistles in his sound.”

Tom Nichols watched the prime-time address on Wednesday, April 1, and wrote in The Atlantic within the hour. He counted the repetitions — “like nobody’s ever seen,” “decimated,” “never before.” He noted the delivery. He reached his conclusion.

“The president seems lost.”

It was, Nichols wrote, “a disjointed series of complaints, brags, and exaggerations — along with a few outright lies — delivered by a man who looked and sounded tired.”

The big manly voice, turning again toward childish treble. The hose well saved, a world too wide for the shrunk shank. The pantaloon on his slippers, padding through an eighteen-minute address that described a “little journey to Iran” — a little journey that has killed thirteen Americans, wounded nearly three hundred, closed the world’s most important energy chokepoint, fired the Army chief of staff, and left a pilot missing over a country the president declared beaten and completely decimated.

The sixth age pipes and whistles in its sound. The market, which had briefly believed the Wednesday night exit signals, surged 7 percent on Thursday morning. Not upward. Oil. The pantaloon’s hose, it turned out, was not adequate for the weather.

The Seventh Age: Sans Everything

“Last scene of all, That ends this strange eventful history, Is second childishness and mere oblivion, Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything.”

Suzanne Maloney, the Brookings Institution’s Iran director, speaking with Ezra Klein this week, delivered her verdict with the measured calm of someone who has studied this region for decades and has therefore stopped being surprised by it.

“I don’t see a victory in real terms at the end of this crisis.”

“It feels like a Suez moment in some respects. This is the end of American global leadership.”

Sans allies — the 41-nation coalition met virtually Thursday without the United States. Sans authorization — no UN resolution, no congressional vote, no multilateral framework. Sans plan — neither Trump nor Hegseth has explained how the war ends, short of battering a regime into concessions it has shown no willingness to make. Sans pilot — still missing, Saturday afternoon, as the president called a lid and posted about the 48-hour deadline.

Mere oblivion is what Maloney described when she imagined the world after a US withdrawal that leaves Iran controlling the Strait of Hormuz, charging tolls, running the regime that was supposed to collapse on Day 1 or Day 2 or Day 3. An Iran that has learned that the Strait is its most potent weapon. An Iran whose new supreme leader and IRGC-weighted government have drawn the obvious conclusion from having their nuclear sites bombed while in negotiations: the only reliable deterrent is nuclear capability itself.

The seventh age does not announce itself. It arrives in the space between the grand claims and the record. Between “beaten and completely decimated” and an IRGC spokesperson saying “you know nothing about our vast and strategic capabilities.” Between “it’ll just open up naturally” and Iran’s Parliament legislating a toll system. Between “we never said regime change” and the video from February 28, still indexed and searchable, in which the president told the Iranian people their hour of freedom was at hand.

Sans teeth. Sans eyes. Sans taste.

Sans everything.

Coda

Shakespeare’s Jaques delivers his speech and withdraws. The comedic action of As You Like It continues around him — love, reconciliation, the forest of Arden, the return to court. The melancholy philosopher sees the tragedy in every human performance. He is not wrong. He is also not the only thing happening in the play.

This is worth remembering as the exit ramp remains visible and the April 6 deadline arrives and the missing pilot is still somewhere in the mountains of Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad Province in southwest Iran.

The play continues. The stage is still set. The other players have not left.

Iran has not left. The 41 nations planning postwar Hormuz escorts have not left. The Iowa farmer who is looking at a $58,000 loss this season has not left. Narges Mohammadi, the Nobel laureate denied hospital care after a heart attack in her prison cell, has not left.

All the world’s a stage.

But not every part is his to play.
