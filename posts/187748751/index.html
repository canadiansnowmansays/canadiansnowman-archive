Operation Metro Surge Is Over. Democrats Won.

After 74 days and two American deaths, Tom Homan announced this morning it's ending. This is what happens when Democrats use power.

February 12, 2026

In Their Memory

“If you’re going to Minnesota
Be sure to wear some flowers in your hair”

The Marsh Family transformed a 1967 anthem of hope into a 2026 memorial for Renée Nicole Good and Alex Jeffrey Pretti—two Americans killed by their own government. You can listen to the Marsh Family’s tribute here.

This morning, Tom Homan stood at the Bishop Henry Whipple Federal Building in Fort Snelling and announced Operation Metro Surge is ending. Seventy-four days after it began. Thirty-six days after it killed Renée. Nineteen days after it killed Alex.

The flowers in their hair now mark more than graves. They mark victory.

At 8:00 AM Central time on Thursday, February 12, 2026, White House Border Czar Tom Homan held a press conference and announced the end of Operation Metro Surge.

“I have proposed, and President Trump has concurred, that this surge operation conclude,” Homan said. “A significant drawdown has already been underway this week and will continue through next week.”

The largest immigration enforcement operation in American history—3,000 federal agents deployed to the Minneapolis-St. Paul area, more than five times the size of the local police force—is over.

It lasted seventy-four days. It killed two American citizens. It violated at least 96 court orders. It generated more than 700 habeas corpus petitions in a single month. It sparked the first general strike in America since 1947. It cost Minnesota’s economy at least $78.8 million per week. It forced more than a dozen federal prosecutors to resign in protest.

And Democrats forced it to end.

Not through lawsuits. Not through press releases. Not through hoping Republicans would do the right thing.

Through power. Through refusing to fund an agency that murders citizens. Through holding the line until Trump had no choice but to retreat.

This is the story of how they did it.

What They Were Up Against

Operation Metro Surge began December 1, 2025. The Trump administration called it necessary to combat welfare fraud and remove “criminal illegal aliens” from Minnesota’s streets.

By January 6, 2026, DHS had deployed 2,000 additional agents, describing it as “the largest immigration operation ever carried out.” Trump announced he was readying the Army’s 11th Airborne Division to deploy to Minneapolis. The total federal presence reached approximately 3,000 agents—five times Minneapolis’s 600 police officers.

Secretary Kristi Noem repeatedly claimed ICE was removing “murderers” and “rapists” from Minnesota streets. In reality, only 5% of arrestees had records of violent crimes. Many people ICE claimed to have arrested had actually been transferred from state custody years earlier, including one individual transferred in 2003—twenty-two years before the operation began.

DHS Secretary Noem claimed on Twitter that ICE had “arrested over 10,000 criminal illegal aliens” in Minneapolis. The actual number was approximately 4,000 arrests between December 1 and February 12, and most had no criminal history.

The operation targeted Minnesota’s Somali population specifically. Trump had called Somalis “garbage.” Border Patrol Commander Gregory Bovino—the operation’s initial leader—conducted aggressive street operations, masked agents without identification, warrantless home entries, and raids at schools and churches.

On January 7, ICE Officer Jonathan Ross shot Renée Nicole Good three times. Noem called her a “domestic terrorist.” Video proved Ross positioned himself in front of Good’s vehicle as she tried to leave, then fired as she drove away.

On January 24, Border Patrol agents shot Alex Jeffrey Pretti ten times in five seconds. Noem claimed he was “brandishing” a gun and wanted to “massacre law enforcement.” Video showed Alex approaching with his phone to record them. The gun remained in his waistband. Agents pepper-sprayed him, wrestled him to the ground, and shot him while he lay motionless.

DHS didn’t notify either family of the killings. Both learned from reporters.

This was what Democrats faced: The largest federal law enforcement deployment in American history, willing to kill American citizens and lie about it, backed by a president who never backs down, supported by Republican control of Congress.

And Democrats stopped it anyway.

The Timeline of Democratic Power

January 7: Renée Good killed. Representative Robin Kelly (IL-D) announces she will file articles of impeachment against Kristi Noem.

January 14: Kelly formally introduces three articles charging obstruction of Congress, violation of public trust, and self-dealing. Within days, 70 Democrats cosponsor.

January 21: Representative Delia Ramirez (IL-D) introduces the MELT ICE Act—legislation barring DHS from using detention centers and redirecting funding to health care and housing.

January 22: House votes 220-207 to fund DHS. Seven moderate Democrats in swing districts vote yes. Two hundred Democrats vote no, refusing to fund an agency that kills citizens.

January 23: An estimated 50,000 people brave subzero temperatures for “ICE Out of Minnesota: Day of Truth & Freedom”—the first general strike in America since the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947. Hundreds of businesses close in solidarity.

January 24: Border Patrol kills Alex Jeffrey Pretti. Noem calls him someone who wanted to “massacre law enforcement.” Video proves she lied.

January 25: Governor Kathy Hochul demands Noem resign. Senator Jacky Rosen says Noem “has been an abject failure.” Trump deploys Tom Homan to Minneapolis and announces investigation into Representative Ilhan Omar—who led 100 Progressive Caucus members in pledging to vote against all ICE funding.

January 26: Border Patrol Commander Gregory Bovino stripped of command and sent back to California. Tom Homan takes over Operation Metro Surge. By now, Kelly’s impeachment articles have 140 Democratic cosponsors—nearly two-thirds of House Democrats.

January 28: Minnesota Chief U.S. District Judge Patrick Schiltz finds ICE violated at least 96 court orders in Minnesota since January 1. Senate Democrats lay out three specific demands: end roving patrols, establish uniform code of conduct with independent enforcement, require body cameras and ban masks.

January 29: Senate votes 45-55 NOT to advance funding package. Every Democrat votes no. Eight Republicans also vote no. Trump: “I think we’re getting close.”

January 30: Senate passes 71-29 a deal splitting DHS funding from broader package. Trump forced to accept two-week stopgap while negotiating Democratic restrictions. Twenty-four Democrats vote no, wanting more. February 13 deadline set.

February 2: Noem announces body cameras will be distributed to every DHS field agent nationwide, beginning with Minneapolis. Democrats demanded it. Trump accepted it.

February 4: Homan announces withdrawal of 700 agents, citing “unprecedented cooperation” from Minnesota counties. Approximately 2,000 remain.

February 10: Governor Tim Walz says he expects operation will end “in days, not weeks and months” based on conversations with Homan and White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles.

February 12, 8:00 AM: Tom Homan announces Operation Metro Surge is ending.

Seventy-four days. From launch to complete withdrawal. Because Democrats used power.

What Democrats Forced

The retreat wasn’t voluntary. Every step was forced.

Homan didn’t decide on his own to withdraw 700 agents on February 4. He did it because Democrats refused to fund DHS unless restrictions were negotiated.

Noem didn’t decide on her own to issue body cameras. She did it because Democrats made body cameras a prerequisite for continued funding.

Trump didn’t decide on his own to end Operation Metro Surge. He did it because:

  1. Democrats refused funding until restrictions were met

  2. Republicans admitted defeat (Sen. Kennedy: “I’ve never seen a political party take its best issue and turn it into its worst issue”)

  3. Public opinion collapsed (46% now support abolishing ICE entirely)

  4. The operation became legally untenable (96+ court order violations, 700+ habeas petitions in one month)

  5. Federal prosecutors resigned rather than participate in the charade

  6. Economic damage mounted ($78.8 million per week to Minnesota’s economy)

  7. The military refused (Northern Command issued stand-down order to 11th Airborne Division)

The New York Times analyzed video of both killings and reported that federal statements about both Renée and Alex were demonstrably false. DHS’s own internal review of Alex’s killing didn’t mention him brandishing a gun or wanting to “massacre law enforcement.”

Washington Post columnist Ramesh Ponnuru wrote that Noem was “either lying or speaking with an indifference to truth that is morally indistinguishable from lying—all to trash the reputation of someone her department had just killed.”

Even some Republicans broke. Several called for de-escalation and accountability. But most importantly, they stopped defending the operation publicly.

When your own party won’t defend you, you’ve lost.

The Cost of Victory

Operation Metro Surge is ending, but not before extracting an enormous cost:

Renée Nicole Good is dead. Mother of three. Poet. Writer. American citizen. Shot three times by ICE Officer Jonathan Ross while trying to leave. Called a “domestic terrorist” by her own government.

Alex Jeffrey Pretti is dead. ICU nurse at Minneapolis VA hospital. He cared for America’s veterans. American citizen. Shot ten times in five seconds while lying on the ground. Accused of wanting to “massacre law enforcement” when video shows he approached with his phone.

Both were 37 years old. Both were Minneapolis residents. Both were killed by federal agents who immediately lied. Both families learned about the deaths from reporters, not from DHS.

Hundreds remain detained. Of approximately 4,000 arrests, many people sit in detention facing deportation despite having no criminal records. Some, like Victor Navarro Govea, had been granted four-year deportation amnesty by DHS but were arrested anyway.

Refugees were targeted. Yah Eh Do Lah was welcomed as a refugee from Myanmar in 2024 with her husband and three children. She gave birth to her fourth child in Minnesota. She applied for her green card in November. ICE arrested her in January.

The legal system is overwhelmed. More than 700 habeas corpus petitions filed in one month. U.S. Attorney Daniel Rosen wrote that “this flood of new litigation imposes an enormous burden on this U.S. Attorney’s Office.” The 8th Circuit had to recruit judges from Iowa, Nebraska, Missouri, and North Dakota to handle Minnesota’s caseload.

More than a dozen federal prosecutors resigned rather than participate in covering up constitutional violations. The Justice Department’s Civil Rights Division declined to open constitutional investigations, prompting the resignations.

Minnesota’s economy was devastated. Governor Walz compared the economic impact to COVID-19. Restaurants empty. Businesses closed. Immigrant output declined. The Market at Malcolm Yards—normally bustling—sat nearly empty.

Native Americans were detained. The Washington Post reported U.S.-citizen Native Americans were stopped, questioned, and in some cases detained as part of racial profiling operations.

ICE violated at least 96 court orders since January 1. Chief Judge Schiltz documented the violations. The operation operated with systematic disregard for judicial authority.

This is what Democrats stopped. But these costs can never be recovered.

What Still Hangs in the Balance

Operation Metro Surge is ending, but critical questions remain unanswered:

Will anyone face accountability? Jonathan Ross shot Renée Good. Seven Border Patrol agents shot Alex Pretti. All remain on duty. No charges have been filed. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche announced a “civil rights inquiry” while prosecutors were resigning in protest over the investigation’s handling.

Will the February 13 funding restrictions be real? Democrats forced Trump to negotiate, but negotiations continue. Will body cameras actually be required everywhere? Will warrants be mandatory? Will independent investigations happen? Or will Trump accept symbolic changes while maintaining operational control?

Will DHS funding be cut? Even without new appropriations, ICE has $75 billion from Trump’s reconciliation bill passed last summer, with $14 billion earmarked for deportations. The February 13 deadline matters, but ICE can operate for months without new money.

Will Noem face consequences? Kelly’s impeachment articles have 140 cosponsors—nearly two-thirds of House Democrats. But Republicans control the House. Impeachment won’t succeed unless Republicans break ranks.

Will investigations be independent? Democrats demanded independent investigations of both killings. Career prosecutors resigned rather than participate in sham investigations. Will real accountability happen, or just more cover-ups?

What happens to the 4,000 people arrested? Many sit in detention. Some are being deported despite having no criminal records. Will courts release them? Will judges find the arrests unconstitutional? Or will they disappear into the deportation system?

Will the lawsuits succeed? Minnesota, Minneapolis, and St. Paul sued DHS. Individual plaintiffs filed class actions. Judge Katherine Menendez denied preliminary injunctions, ruling plaintiffs didn’t prove likelihood of success. But the cases continue. Will they ultimately constrain DHS?

How many agents actually remain? Homan announced the “conclusion” of Operation Metro Surge but didn’t specify final numbers. Normal operational footprint: 150 agents. Will DHS actually draw down to that level, or will hundreds remain permanently?

What precedent was set? Trump deployed 3,000 federal agents to one metropolitan area for 74 days, killed two citizens, violated 96 court orders, and faced no consequences beyond forced withdrawal. Can he do it again in Chicago? Los Angeles? New York? What did Democrats actually prevent?

These questions matter enormously. Because ending one operation isn’t the same as preventing the next one.

What Democrats Proved

For years, Democrats agonized over how to respond to Trump’s authoritarianism. They debated messaging. They worried about polls. They fretted over whether “Abolish ICE” would hurt them in swing districts.

Operation Metro Surge forced clarity.

When ICE killed Renée Good, Democrats could have expressed concern, demanded investigations, and hoped Republicans would do the right thing. Instead, Robin Kelly filed impeachment articles.

When ICE killed Alex Pretti, Democrats could have accepted a funding bill that moderates had negotiated for months. Instead, every Senate Democrat voted no.

When Trump sent Tom Homan to Minnesota and announced investigations into Ilhan Omar, Democrats could have moderated their demands. Instead, they expanded them.

At every decision point, Democrats chose power over politeness. Action over rhetoric. Constitutional authority over accommodation.

And it worked.

Trump retreated on immigration—his signature issue, his political identity, the base of his movement—because Democrats made clear they would shut down the government rather than fund an agency that murders citizens.

Robin Kelly’s impeachment articles, Delia Ramirez’s MELT ICE Act, Ilhan Omar’s pledge to vote against all ICE funding—these actions didn’t pass. But they demonstrated Democratic unity and resolve. They showed Trump that Democrats meant what they said.

Chuck Schumer negotiating directly with Trump forced the president to accept what his own party couldn’t: That ICE had become politically toxic. That videos of masked agents killing Americans destroyed Republican credibility faster than any Democratic attack ad.

Senator John Kennedy admitted it: “I’ve never seen a political party take its best issue and turn it into its worst issue in the period of time that it has happened in the last few weeks.”

That’s not Democratic messaging. That’s Republican surrender.

The New York Times reported the deal “amounted to a major breakthrough fueled by a sharp pivot by the president and Republicans in Congress, who have rushed in recent days to distance themselves from the chaos and violence wrought by federal agents carrying out Mr. Trump’s deportation drive.”

Republicans “rushed to distance themselves” from their own signature policy. Because Democrats forced them to defend the indefensible: An agency that kills American citizens and lies about it.

This is what using power looks like. Not debating whether to say “Abolish ICE.” Actually constraining ICE through appropriations authority.

The Lesson

Seventy-four days ago, Trump launched the largest immigration enforcement operation in American history. He deployed 3,000 federal agents to Minnesota. He threatened to deploy the 11th Airborne Division. He put Gregory Bovino—a man a federal judge later ruled “lied multiple times”—in command.

ICE killed Renée Nicole Good and called her a terrorist. ICE killed Alex Jeffrey Pretti and claimed he wanted to massacre law enforcement. Both lies were exposed by video.

Democrats said: Not one more dollar.

They filed impeachment articles. They introduced legislation to defund detention operations. They voted no on funding bills. They demanded body cameras, warrants, independent investigations. They refused to compromise.

And today, Operation Metro Surge is over.

Not because Trump wanted it to end. Not because Republicans convinced him. Not because think tanks found better messaging.

Because Democrats used the power the Constitution gives Congress: the power of the purse.

Article I, Section 9: “No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law.”

When an agency kills American citizens, Congress can withhold funding. When an administration lies about killings, Congress can refuse appropriations. When operations violate 96 court orders, Congress can demand accountability as a condition of funding.

Democrats finally used that power. And it worked.

The question now is whether this was a one-time tactical victory or a permanent strategic shift.

Will Democrats continue using appropriations power to constrain executive overreach? Or will they revert to symbolic gestures and strongly-worded letters?

Will they hold the line on February 13 and demand real restrictions? Or will they accept symbolic changes and declare victory?

Will they force accountability for Renée’s and Alex’s killings? Or will those murders go unpunished like so many before?

On February 12, 2026, Tom Homan stood in Fort Snelling and announced Operation Metro Surge is ending. He claimed it “yielded successful results” and made Minnesota “much safer.”

He’s lying. Everyone knows he’s lying. Even Republicans know he’s lying.

But he’s ending it anyway. Because Democrats forced him to.

And that’s the lesson: Power works when you actually use it.

Conclusion: The Flowers Still Mark Graves

“If you’re going to Minnesota
Be sure to wear some flowers in your hair”

In 1967, those flowers symbolized hope. In 2026, they mark the graves of two Americans killed by their own government.

Renée Nicole Good. Alex Jeffrey Pretti. Their names. Their families. We will not forget.

We will not forget that ICE killed them and lied about it.

We will not forget that DHS Secretary Kristi Noem called them terrorists.

We will not forget that it took 36 days from Renée’s death and 19 days from Alex’s death before the operation that killed them ended.

We will not forget that it ended only because Democrats refused to fund it.

We will not forget the lesson: Power unused is power wasted. Power used is victory.

Operation Metro Surge lasted 74 days. It killed two Americans. It arrested 4,000 people. It violated 96 court orders. It cost Minnesota’s economy hundreds of millions. It forced a dozen prosecutors to resign. It generated the first general strike in America since 1947.

And Democrats forced it to end by using constitutional power—the appropriations authority—that they’ve been too timid to use for years.

The flowers in their hair mark more than graves now. They mark the beginning of something different. A Democratic Party willing to use power. A Congress willing to constrain executive overreach. A moment when “No more” meant actual consequences, not just rhetoric.

Tom Homan announced this morning that Operation Metro Surge is over.

But the fight for accountability is just beginning.

February 13 is tomorrow. The DHS funding restrictions must be negotiated. Real body camera requirements. Real warrant mandates. Real independent investigations.

If Democrats hold the line, they can force real accountability.

If they cave, Renée and Alex died for nothing.

We’ll know tomorrow which Democrats we’re dealing with.

The flowers are waiting.
