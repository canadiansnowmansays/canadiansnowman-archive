The Enemies List

A History Part 1

When Justice Systems Become Weapons of Political Power

October 12, 2025

Before we can understand what’s happening in America right now, we need to understand what has happened before.

The weaponization of justice systems against political enemies isn’t new. It’s one of the oldest plays in the authoritarian playbook. And it never happens all at once.

It starts with pressure on prosecutors. Then comes the removal of resistant officials. Then the installation of loyalists. Then rushed prosecutions of political enemies over professional objections. Then the normalization of what was once unthinkable.

This pattern has played out repeatedly throughout history. In this three-part series, I’ll show you some of the most notorious examples—and why they matter for understanding our current moment.

We begin with two totalitarian regimes that perfected the art of turning courts into weapons: Stalin’s Soviet Union and Hitler’s Nazi Germany.

Stalin’s Show Trials: The Template for Political Terror

Between 1936 and 1938, Joseph Stalin conducted what became known as the Moscow Trials—a series of highly publicized show trials that systematically eliminated Old Bolsheviks and prominent Communist Party members who might pose any threat to his absolute power.

These weren’t criminal trials in any meaningful sense. They were political theater designed to legitimize purges that Stalin had already decided to carry out.

The Three Major Trials

The Trial of the Sixteen (August 1936): Grigory Zinovyev, Lev Kamenev, and fourteen others—all prominent Bolsheviks who had helped create the Soviet state—were accused of forming a “Trotskyite-Kamenevite-Zinovievite-Leftist-Counter-Revolutionary Bloc.” They were charged with plotting to assassinate Stalin and blamed for the 1934 murder of Sergei Kirov. All were found guilty. All were executed.

The Trial of the Seventeen (January 1937): Karl Radek, Grigory Pyatakov, and fifteen others were accused of being part of an “Anti-Soviet Trotskyist Center” that had committed acts of sabotage and espionage. Thirteen were executed. The remaining four received prison sentences but were later executed anyway.

The Trial of the Twenty-One (March 1938): Nikolai Bukharin, Aleksey Rykov, and nineteen others—including Genrikh Yagoda, the former NKVD chief who had helped orchestrate the earlier trials—were accused of plotting to murder Lenin as far back as 1918, of murdering Soviet writer Maxim Gorky by poison in 1936, and of conspiring with German and Japanese agents to dismember the Soviet Union. Eighteen were executed.

How the System Worked

The Moscow Trials shared several characteristics that would become hallmarks of authoritarian justice:

Forced confessions. Evidence consisted almost entirely of confessions extracted through torture, sleep deprivation, threats against family members, and psychological abuse. Defendants “confessed” to fantastical conspiracies they couldn’t possibly have committed.

Predetermined outcomes. Verdicts were decided before trials began. The proceedings were pure theater. Stalin sometimes observed trials from a hidden chamber in the courtroom to ensure they went according to plan.

Fabricated evidence. The secret police (NKVD) manufactured evidence and scripted testimony. Documents were forged. Witnesses were coached or coerced.

Public spectacle. Trials were highly publicized, broadcast on radio, covered extensively in Soviet media. The point was to demonstrate Stalin’s power and intimidate potential opposition.

Elimination of rivals. The trials successfully removed anyone who might challenge Stalin’s authority—even those who had been loyal to him, like Yagoda, who was eventually consumed by the same machinery he had helped create.

The Chief Prosecutor

Andrey Vyshinsky served as chief prosecutor for the Moscow Trials. His style became infamous: screaming at defendants, hurling insults, treating courtrooms as stages for ideological performance rather than venues for determining facts.

He called defendants “mad dogs,” “vermin,” “dregs of society,” and worse. His closing argument in the first trial demanded: “Shoot them all!”

Vyshinsky would later participate in drafting the principles for the Nuremberg trials after World War II—a historical irony that underscores how his innovations in show trials paradoxically influenced the development of international criminal law.

The Broader Context: The Great Purge

The Moscow Trials were just the visible tip of Stalin’s Great Purge (1936-1938), also known as the Great Terror. The purges extended far beyond high-ranking party officials:

Military officers: Between 1937 and 1939, thousands of Red Army officers were executed for allegedly being foreign spies. Some 35,000 officers were removed from posts, severely damaging Soviet military capability just before World War II.

Ordinary citizens: Anyone could be denounced as a counter-revolutionary or saboteur. Being late to work could be considered a crime against the state.

The NKVD itself: Even the secret police weren’t safe. Yagoda was arrested and executed. His replacement, Nikolai Yezhov, accelerated the terror—then was himself arrested and executed in 1940.

Estimates suggest 700,000 to 1.2 million people were killed during the Great Purge. The terror created an atmosphere of complete fear where nobody was safe, where today’s prosecutor could be tomorrow’s victim.

The Aftermath

It took decades to acknowledge the truth. Most defendants weren’t officially rehabilitated until 1988, fifty years after their executions. The Soviet government eventually admitted the trials were fabricated, the confessions forced, the accused innocent.

But by then, the damage was permanent. Stalin had successfully demonstrated that he could eliminate anyone, anytime, for any reason—all while maintaining the facade of legal process.

Nazi Germany’s People’s Court: Justice as Theater of Cruelty

Nazi Germany took a different approach to weaponizing justice, but the underlying logic was the same: courts exist to eliminate enemies, not to determine guilt or innocence.

The Creation of the People’s Court

In 1933, when several defendants were acquitted at the Reichstag fire trial, Hitler realized that traditional German courts might not reliably convict his enemies. So in 1934, Germany established the Volksgerichtshof—the People’s Court—to handle political crimes.

The court was explicitly designed to be a “revolutionary tribunal to persecute and intimidate political opponents of the regime.” It was, as historians later characterized it, “symbolic of the wanton criminality and corruption of the Third Reich.”

Roland Freisler: The Screaming Judge

In 1942, Roland Freisler became president of the People’s Court. He was an explicit admirer of Vyshinsky and consciously modeled his approach on Soviet show trials.

Freisler’s courtroom was a place of deliberate humiliation and terror:

Defendants’ treatment: Accused were forced to wear shabby, ill-fitting clothes without belts or suspenders, forcing them to hold up their pants while testifying. They were marched into court handcuffed to police. They were denied adequate time to prepare defenses or call witnesses.

Verbal abuse: Freisler screamed at defendants, hurling insults and personal attacks. He called Field Marshal von Witzleben’s brief Nazi salute inadequate and castigated him while the elderly officer struggled to hold up his trousers. He addressed former Colonel-General Erich Hoepner as “Schweinehund” (pig-dog) and asked what “zoological category” he belonged to.

Predetermined verdicts: Like the Moscow Trials, outcomes were decided in advance. The proceedings were theater, not justice.

Rapid proceedings: Trials were conducted at breakneck speed, sometimes lasting only a few hours from start to execution.

The Acceleration of Terror

As Nazi Germany’s military situation deteriorated, the People’s Court became even more brutal:

Exploding caseload: Death sentences increased from 99 in 1939-1940 to 929 in 1940-1941, then quadrupled to over 5,000 by 1943-1944.

Defeatism prosecutions: By 1943, the court was prosecuting people for “defeatism”—essentially, any expression of doubt about Nazi victory. In 1943 alone, the court handled about 2,500 defeatism cases.

Impossible standards: Even clearly innocent defendants could be convicted. Leopold Felsen was accused of listening to foreign broadcasts by his wife—and convicted anyway despite obvious innocence.

July 20 plot trials: After the failed assassination attempt on Hitler in July 1944, the People’s Court conducted trials of military officers and conspirators that were pure spectacle. The proceedings were filmed for propaganda purposes, showing defendants humiliated and degraded before being condemned.

The Death Toll

Between 1934 and 1945, the People’s Court is estimated to have sentenced about 5,200 people to death. When combined with other Nazi “special courts” (Sondergerichte), the total exceeds 12,000 executions for political crimes.

Freisler himself died in February 1945 when an Allied bomb hit the courthouse during an air raid. He was reportedly killed while clutching the files of a defendant he was about to condemn.

The Aftermath: Justice Denied

After World War II, virtually no judges or prosecutors from the People’s Court faced meaningful punishment:

  • Ernst Lautz, the Chief Public Prosecutor, received ten years at the Judges’ Trial in Nuremberg but was released after less than four years and granted a government pension by West Germany.

  • Oswald Rothaug, a People’s Court judge, was sentenced to life imprisonment in 1947, but his sentence was reduced to twenty years and he was released on parole in 1956.

  • Hans-Joachim Rehse, another judge, was tried in 1967, initially found guilty and sentenced to five years, but acquitted on appeal.

The West German justice system never successfully prosecuted any People’s Court judges. The men who had turned courtrooms into slaughterhouses largely escaped accountability.

The Common Pattern: Six Characteristics of Weaponized Justice

Despite their different contexts, Stalin’s Moscow Trials and Hitler’s People’s Court shared fundamental characteristics. These same patterns appear whenever justice systems are weaponized against political enemies:

1. Political Objectives Override Legal Standards

The purpose isn’t to determine guilt or innocence. It’s to eliminate enemies, intimidate opposition, and demonstrate power. Legal procedures become theater masking predetermined outcomes.

In both systems, verdicts were decided before trials began. Evidence, witnesses, and legal arguments were props in a predetermined drama, not factors in reaching a decision.

2. Removal of Institutional Barriers

Independent prosecutors, judges, and officials who might object are removed and replaced with loyalists.

Stalin replaced Yagoda with Yezhov when purges needed to accelerate. Hitler created an entirely new court system when existing courts proved insufficiently compliant. In both cases, anyone who might say “no” on principle was eliminated from the system.

3. Process as Punishment

Even if defendants are ultimately acquitted (rare), the process itself serves as punishment and deterrent. The arrest, the public humiliation, the ruined reputation, the legal costs—these inflict damage regardless of the verdict.

The spectacle of prominent figures being degraded in court sent a message to everyone else: this could be you. No position is too high. No loyalty is sufficient protection. No one is safe.

4. Chilling Effect on Opposition

The public nature of political trials creates terror far beyond the courtroom. Anyone who might consider opposing the regime sees what happens to those who do.

After the Moscow Trials, party members lived in constant fear. After the People’s Court ramped up prosecutions, Germans learned to stay silent even about obvious military disasters. The trials didn’t just eliminate specific individuals—they paralyzed entire populations.

5. Gradual Normalization

What seems shocking at first becomes routine. The first Moscow Trial in 1936 generated controversy even within the Communist Party. By 1938, mass purges were standard procedure.

Similarly, the People’s Court initially issued cautious verdicts that drew criticism from Nazi leadership for being too lenient. Within a few years, it was executing thousands annually for the most trivial “crimes.”

6. Facade of Legality

Critically, both systems maintained the appearance of legal process. There were courts, judges, prosecutors, trials, and verdicts. Documents were filed. Procedures were followed (however corrupted).

This facade of legality served multiple purposes:

  • Domestic legitimacy: Made the terror seem lawful rather than arbitrary

  • International credibility: Provided cover against foreign criticism

  • Psychological compliance: Easier for officials to participate when wrapped in legal forms

  • Historical deniability: Created paperwork suggesting proper process was followed

The trials were illegitimate, but they wore the costume of legitimacy. That costume mattered.

Why These Examples Matter

Some will object that comparing anything in modern America to Stalin or Hitler is hyperbolic or inflammatory.

But historians of authoritarianism consistently emphasize that we need to recognize warning signs early, not wait until we’ve reached the worst-case scenario.

The point isn’t to claim equivalence. The United States in 2025 is obviously not the Soviet Union in 1937 or Nazi Germany in 1943.

The point is to understand the pattern—to recognize how authoritarian regimes weaponize justice systems, how they maintain facades of legality while corrupting substance, how they normalize the unthinkable through gradual escalation.

Because if we understand the pattern, we can recognize it when it begins to emerge in our own time and place.

Because the pattern we’re seeing now in America follows this playbook with disturbing precision.
