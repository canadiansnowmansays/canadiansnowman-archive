The Guardian: Khamenei's Iran

Khamenei’s Iran

Part 1: The Bunker

January 19, 2026

Rise to Power and the Architecture of Control

ABOUT THIS SERIES

The Guardian: Khamenei’s Iran is a 13-part investigative series examining 37 years of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei’s rule as Supreme Leader of Iran (1989-present).

Methodology: This series combines:

Historical documentation (Parts 1-5): Sourced from academic research, government reports, human rights organizations, and international media covering 1989-2023

Recent events (Parts 6-9): Documentation of Operation Rising Lion (June 2025), regional proxy collapse including Assad’s fall (December 2024), economic crisis, and ongoing protests (December 2025-January 2026) based on multiple verified sources

Analytical framework (Parts 10-13): Political science analysis of succession crisis, institutional failure, and regime trajectory

Evaluative Framework: Rather than imposing external standards, this series measures Khamenei against the Islamic Revolution’s own stated principles: economic justice, political participation, national sovereignty, Islamic governance, protection of the oppressed, anti-imperialism, and preservation of revolutionary values. This “by his own standards” approach uses Khamenei’s speeches, the Iranian constitution, and revolutionary doctrine as the measuring stick.

Sources: Each essay covering recent events (Parts 6-9) includes detailed sourcing. Major claims are corroborated by multiple independent sources including human rights organizations (Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Iran Human Rights), international media (CNN, BBC, Reuters, Al Jazeera, Washington Post, Guardian), academic institutions (CSIS, RUSI, Hudson Institute), and government statements.

On Contested Numbers: Where estimates vary (particularly casualty figures from ongoing protests), this series presents the range of credible estimates, notes the methodology behind each, and uses the most conservative verified figures while acknowledging higher estimates exist.

Author: Stephen Gordon, CanadianSnowmanSays

June 2025, Tehran

Somewhere beneath the sprawling compound in central Tehran, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei spent twelve days underground. While Israeli F-35s struck nuclear facilities and American B-2 bombers dropped bunker-busters on Fordow, Iran’s Supreme Leader—the self-proclaimed Guardian of the Islamic Revolution—avoided all electronic communication and made no public appearances.

Iranian state media released no live footage. His whereabouts remained classified. Intelligence reports suggested he’d suffered a mental breakdown under the stress. Other sources claimed IRGC commanders had sidelined him entirely, withholding information about the war’s progress while they made decisions without him.

It was a fitting image for the system he had built: defensive, paranoid, sealed off from reality.

This is the story of how it came to that bunker. Not through Western analysis or liberal democratic standards, but through Khamenei’s own words, his own principles, and the revolutionary values he claimed to uphold.

By those standards—the only ones that matter for judging a man who ruled in the name of Islamic justice—what follows is a chronicle measured not in editorial judgments but in bodies, economic collapse, and the yawning gap between promise and reality.

It began with an objection.

On June 4, 1989, the day after Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini’s death, Iran’s Assembly of Experts convened to select the next Supreme Leader. The obvious candidate wasn’t qualified under the constitution—he lacked the religious credentials (marja status) required for the position. But Ali Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani, the pragmatic kingmaker, had a solution: change the constitution.

When the Assembly voted, one member objected.

Ali Khamenei—the man being elevated to absolute power—raised his hand and said: “I object to my own appointment. I am not qualified.”

It was performance. Rafsanjani had already secured the votes. The objection was theater designed to project humility while accepting a role he’d been maneuvering toward for years. Reluctance as strategy. Modesty as manipulation.

Of course, hiding reluctance behind false humility is hardly unique to authoritarian leaders. It’s a good thing democratically elected leaders—say, a certain American president during Black Lives Matter protests in June 2020—would never hide in bunkers during moments of crisis. That would be cowardly. That would demonstrate weakness at the exact moment strength was required. That would reveal a leader more concerned with self-preservation than leadership.

Good thing that never happened.

Khamenei’s objection lasted approximately thirty seconds. Then he accepted. The reluctant guardian who spent the next 37 years building absolute power, crushing dissent, and—when finally confronted with the consequences in June 2025—retreating to a bunker while his country burned above.

I. THE RELUCTANT GUARDIAN

The reluctant guardian in waiting: Khamenei beside Khomeini, before the Assembly of Experts named him Supreme Leader in 1989. The promises of 1979 would soon be measured against the man who claimed to guard them

June 4, 1989 - Assembly of Experts, Tehran

When Iran’s Assembly of Experts convened three days after Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini’s death, they faced a succession crisis. The heir apparent, Ayatollah Hussein-Ali Montazeri, had been dismissed months earlier after criticizing the regime’s mass executions of political prisoners.

Into this vacuum stepped Ali Khamenei, then serving as Iran’s president. Multiple eyewitness accounts report that when the Assembly nominated him for Supreme Leader, Khamenei buried his head in his hands and said despairingly: “I object.”

Whether genuine humility or political theater became irrelevant. What mattered was how he used it. In his first speech as Supreme Leader, Khamenei established the framework by which he asked to be judged:

“I am an individual with many faults and shortcomings. I am truly a minor seminarian. However, a responsibility has been placed on my shoulders, and I will use all my capabilities and all my faith in the Almighty in order to be able to bear this heavy responsibility.”

A humble servant. An unwanted burden. A minor cleric serving something greater than himself.

These words would echo through 37 years of speeches, repeated by supporters whenever critics questioned his accumulation of power. The reluctant guardian, they said, who never sought authority but accepted it from duty.

We will test that claim against his actions.

The objective for this series is simple: hold Ayatollah Ali Khamenei accountable not to Western democracy or UN human rights frameworks—which he dismisses as imperial interference—but to the revolutionary principles he claims to uphold, the Islamic values he invokes, and the specific promises he made.

By those standards, measured in his own words, the verdict will be clear.

II. THE WEAKNESS PROBLEM

Khamenei’s “reluctance” masked a genuine crisis: he lacked the religious authority the position demanded.

Iranian Shia tradition holds that the Supreme Leader should be a marja-e taqlid—a “source of emulation” recognized as one of the most learned religious scholars. These figures spend decades in seminaries, write authoritative texts on Islamic jurisprudence, and are accepted by other senior clerics as worthy of the title.

Ali Khamenei in 1989 was a mid-ranking hojjatoleslam, several steps below even a basic ayatollah. According to Abbas Milani, director of Iranian Studies at Stanford, the clerical establishment hastily “anointed him an ayatollah” after his selection, and in 1994 elevated him to marja—a compressed timeline that violated centuries of Shia scholarly tradition.

Multiple senior clerics questioned his qualifications. Grand Ayatollah Montazeri, who had been passed over for the position, openly disputed Khamenei’s religious credentials. One cleric reportedly said: “We didn’t overthrow a shah to be ruled by a hojatoleslam.”

This vulnerability would shape everything that followed.

The Political Calculation

According to accounts from associates of then-president Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani, the Assembly chose Khamenei partly because of his lower religious status. They believed a less-credentialed leader would be easier to manage. Rafsanjani, the most powerful political figure in Iran at the time, apparently thought he could control the younger cleric.

He fatally misread his man.

Unable to claim Khomeini’s unquestioned religious authority, Khamenei built power through other means: systematic appointment of loyalists, micromanagement of security forces, and construction of parallel power structures answerable only to him.

Mehdi Khalaji of the Washington Institute for Near East Policy documented the pattern: “He reached far down into the ranks and appointed new colonels and brigadiers. Khamenei micromanages the whole system, so everyone is loyal to him. He is hyperactive. He knows every low-ranking commander and even the names of their children.”

This wasn’t humble stewardship. This was methodical construction of a system where loyalty to Khamenei personally—not to the revolution, not to Islam, not to Iran—became the primary qualification for power.

Constitutional Consolidation

Iran’s Constitution grants the Supreme Leader sweeping authority: supreme command of armed forces, declaration of war and peace, appointment of judiciary chiefs, control of state media, and final say on all state matters.

Khamenei wielded every clause. Over three decades, he:

  • Appointed heads of state television and radio, controlling information

  • Named all Guardian Council members who vet election candidates, controlling who could run

  • Selected judiciary chiefs who determined legality, controlling justice

  • Chose ministers for interior, defense, intelligence, and foreign affairs

  • Appointed Revolutionary Guard and Basij commanders, controlling force

By 2009, reports indicated Khamenei had installed approximately 2,000 personal representatives across every level of government and society—eyes and ears ensuring compliance.

The constitutional scholar Said Amir Arjomand observed: “What was designed as a system of checks and balances became a system where one man held all the levers.”

The Question This Raises

Return to June 1989. Khamenei, head in hands: “I object.” The burden too heavy. The minor seminarian overwhelmed by responsibility.

Then examine the actions: systematic loyalty networks built over decades, micromanagement of military appointments down to knowing commanders’ children’s names, 2,000 personal representatives monitoring compliance, constitutional powers exercised without restraint, zero tolerance for challenge.

Which version is true? The humble servant or the calculating autocrat consolidating absolute power?

Perhaps the answer doesn’t matter. What matters is the system he built and what it did to Iran and Iranians.

III. THE ALLIANCE THAT DEFINED EVERYTHING

In 1989, Khamenei faced multiple vulnerabilities: weak religious credentials, uncertain political support, potential challengers from both the clerical establishment and reformist politicians.

He found his solution in the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps.

The IRGC, founded by Khomeini in 1979 as an ideological counterweight to Iran’s regular military, had emerged from the Iran-Iraq War as the country’s most powerful force. They were true believers—revolutionary zealots who valued ideological commitment over professional military competence.

And they needed a patron who would protect their interests.

According to scholars including Afshon Ostovar and Frederic Wehrey who have studied the IRGC extensively, Khamenei and the Guards formed a “symbiotic relationship.” He gave them political protection and economic opportunity. They gave him muscle and unwavering loyalty.

The 1999 Covenant

The marriage was consummated during Mohammad Khatami’s reformist presidency (1997-2005). Khatami won landslide elections on promises of political openness and rapprochement with the West—an agenda threatening both Khamenei’s authority and the IRGC’s ideological purpose.

In July 1999, when students protested the closure of a reformist newspaper, 24 IRGC commanders sent President Khatami a letter. The message was explicit: suppress the demonstrations or we will—with or without your approval. The threat of military coup hung in the air.

Khatami backed down.

Abbas Milani later wrote: “The regime clearly believed it was going to lose control, and the IRGC and the Basij saved the day. The result is that the IRGC now has the upper hand. Khamenei knows that without the IRGC he’d be out of a job in twenty-four hours.”

The revolutionary Islamic Republic—supposedly governed by clerical authority and popular will—had become a military state with religious window dressing.

The Economic Empire

The IRGC’s reward extended far beyond political protection. Over two decades, Khamenei oversaw their transformation into an economic colossus.

Khatam al-Anbiya, the IRGC’s engineering arm, became Iran’s largest contractor, securing no-bid government contracts worth billions. Setad Ejraiye Farmane Hazrate Emam, an organization officially created to manage “abandoned properties,” grew into a conglomerate worth an estimated $95 billion by 2013, controlled directly by Khamenei’s office.

IRGC-affiliated companies came to dominate oil and gas, telecommunications, ports and shipping, construction, mining, and import-export networks. By 2025, according to Iranian economists and World Bank estimates, IRGC-linked entities controlled 20-40% of Iran’s entire economy.

One statistic captures the transformation: By late 2025, the newspaper Donya-ye-Eghtesad reported that 1% of Iranians controlled 33% of the nation’s wealth—and much of that elite consisted of IRGC commanders and Khamenei-appointed officials.

The Revolutionary Betrayal

The Islamic Revolution of 1979 was fought partly over economic inequality under the Shah. Revolutionary slogans denounced monarchical corruption, concentration of wealth among elites, and the suffering of ordinary Iranians.

Popular chants from 1978-79: “Independence, Freedom, Islamic Republic!” and “Death to the Dictator!”

The revolutionaries promised economic justice, Islamic equity, and government for the people.

Forty-six years later, under the Guardian’s watch:

  • Economic inequality exceeds the Shah’s era

  • Wealth concentrates among military-clerical elites

  • Ordinary Iranians face 50%+ poverty rates

When protesters now chant “Death to the Dictator,” they mean Khamenei.

In a 2014 speech on “Resistance Economy,” Khamenei declared: “Economic justice is not just about raising GDP. It’s about equitable distribution, about ensuring every Iranian can live with dignity.”

Hold that standard. By 2025, 60% of Iranians reported malnourishment, minimum wage fell below poverty line, and IRGC commanders inhabited luxury compounds while Iranians queued for bread.

In December 2024, facing economic crisis, Khamenei ordered Iranians to “reduce consumption of food, water, energy and fuel.”

The Guardian of a revolution fought for economic justice told hungry people to eat less while maintaining the system that enriched his loyalists.

Not Western hypocrisy. Revolutionary hypocrisy, by revolutionary standards.

IV. THE CONSOLIDATION OF CONTROL

Unable to claim Khomeini’s religious authority, Khamenei constructed an alternative foundation: the doctrine of absolute leadership infallibility combined with permanent external threat.

Over three decades, state propaganda transformed Khamenei from a mid-ranking cleric into a quasi-sacred figure. His portraits appeared in every government building, school, and military base. His speeches were treated as revealed truth. Friday prayer sermons emphasized his divine selection and unchallengeable wisdom.

Constitutional scholar Javad Tabatabai noted the inversion: “Under Khomeini, the Vali-e-Faqih was accountable to the revolution’s ideals. Under Khamenei, the revolution became accountable to the Vali-e-Faqih.”

This required systematic suppression of dissent—not just in streets but in seminaries. Senior clerics who questioned Khamenei’s qualifications found funding cut, students harassed, influence marginalized. Grand Ayatollah Montazeri spent his final years under house arrest. When he died in 2009, thousands attended his funeral despite warnings. Many were later arrested.

The message: Even senior religious authority could not question the Supreme Leader.

The Enemy Everywhere

Simultaneously, Khamenei perfected the doctrine of perpetual siege. Every problem, every setback, every protest became evidence of foreign conspiracy.

“The enemy’s plots must be recognized. The enemy does not rest,” he declared during protests in 2025. Economic collapse? “The work of the enemy.” Currency crash? “Economic warfare by world arrogance.” Demonstrations? “CIA and Mossad operations.”

This rhetoric appears in countless speeches: “The enemy does not want the Iranian people to have a good life” (2023). “These protests are designed by America and the Zionist regime” (2022). “Foreign powers seek to create discord” (2009).

Never: “We made policy mistakes.” Never: “Our economic management failed.” Never: “We must reform.”

The Logic Trap

But the enemy doctrine contained a fatal flaw visible when you apply Khamenei’s own logic consistently:

If foreign enemies can orchestrate protests across 140 cities, coordinate millions of protesters, manipulate Iran’s currency, and destabilize the economy at will—then what exactly has the “Guardian” been guarding?

By his own framework, if enemies are this powerful inside Iran, he has failed utterly in his primary responsibility: protecting the Islamic Republic from foreign subversion.

Only two possibilities exist:

Option 1: The problems are domestic—economic mismanagement, political repression, corruption—and Khamenei lies about foreign control.

Option 2: Foreign powers do control events inside Iran, meaning Khamenei has presided over complete penetration by hostile intelligence services.

Either way, his own explanation indicts him.

The revolution promised sovereignty and independence. If Khamenei’s telling is accurate—that CIA and Mossad organize nationwide protests involving hundreds of thousands across all provinces—Iran is less sovereign under his guardianship than under the Shah.

V. THE PATTERN ESTABLISHED

By decade’s end, the architecture was complete:

Religious legitimacy replaced by personality cult. Political authority concentrated through appointments. Military power secured through IRGC alliance. Economic control captured by loyalist networks. Information dominance achieved through state media monopoly. Dissent framed as foreign subversion.

The “minor seminarian” had constructed the most comprehensive authoritarian system in modern Iranian history—more absolute than the Shah’s, more ideologically rigid than Khomeini’s, more economically corrupt than both combined.

What remained untested was whether this system could survive contact with the Iranian people when they demanded the revolution’s promises be kept.

The first real test came in July 1999, when university students took to the streets after closure of a reformist newspaper. The pattern established then would repeat for the next 27 years: escalating violence, systematic repression, thousands of dead Iranians—all justified as guarding the revolution, all contradicting the revolution’s founding principles, all personally ordered or approved by the man who claimed humble servanthood.

CLOSING: THE BUNKER AWAITED

In June 2025, when Israeli missiles screamed toward Tehran and American bombers obliterated nuclear facilities Khamenei had spent decades building, he had one option: retreat underground.

The physical bunker beneath his compound became the perfect metaphor—a defensive fortress designed to survive enemies real and imagined, but incapable of serving the people it claimed to protect.

The question driving this series: How did it come to that bunker?

Over the next eleven parts, we will examine his record against the standards he established: Did he uphold revolutionary principles? Did he serve as humble guardian? Did he protect the Iranian people? Did he maintain Islamic justice? Did he preserve Iranian sovereignty and dignity?

The evidence comes from his own speeches, his own decisions, the documented experiences of Iranians who lived under his guardianship.

By those measures—his measures, not ours—what follows is a chronicle not of foreign judgment but of revolutionary failure, measured in bodies, poverty, years lost, and promises systematically betrayed.

The bunker was waiting from the beginning.

It took 37 years to get there.
