THE EXCEPTION CLAUSE

The Mormon Moment

Part III: How the Federal Government Declared War on an American Church

April 1, 2026

In 1857, President Buchanan dispatched a third of the U.S. Army to Utah — the largest domestic military mobilization in American history to that point. Source: Wikimedia Commons / public domain

In the summer of 1857, the President of the United States dispatched 2,500 federal troops — roughly a third of the entire United States Army — to the Utah Territory. Their orders were to suppress what President James Buchanan had described as a rebellion against federal authority and to install a new territorial governor in place of Brigham Young, who had governed the territory since its establishment in 1850. The operation, which became known as the Utah War, was the largest domestic military mobilization in American history to that point.

No shots were fired. The confrontation ended in negotiation. Young accepted a presidential pardon — for offenses that were never precisely specified — and a new governor was installed, though Young retained effective authority over the territory’s population for years afterward. The federal troops wintered in Utah and withdrew. The episode was, as military campaigns go, something of a farce.

But the Utah War was not primarily a military event. It was a declaration of intent — a statement by the federal government that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, its leadership, its institutions, and its practices were incompatible with American civic life, and that the full coercive power of the state was available to compel conformity. The military campaign was the opening move in a legal and political war against an American religious community that would continue for more than three decades, strip its members of civil rights, dissolve its institutional structures, and ultimately force the church to abandon a central theological doctrine as the price of its survival.

It is the most thoroughgoing campaign of religious persecution conducted by the United States federal government against an American-born religious community in the nation’s history. It is also, outside of Mormon communities themselves, almost entirely forgotten.

The Church That America Made

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints was not an import. It did not arrive with Irish famine refugees or German immigrants. It was born in upstate New York in 1830, founded by Joseph Smith, who claimed to have received divine revelation and to have translated sacred scripture from golden plates shown to him by an angel. It was, from the beginning, a thoroughly American religious movement — steeped in the millennialist Protestant atmosphere of the early 19th century, shaped by the democratic and egalitarian currents of the Jacksonian era, convinced that the American continent had a specific and sacred role in the unfolding of divine history.

It was also, from the beginning, comprehensively unwelcome.

Smith’s followers were driven from New York to Ohio, from Ohio to Missouri, from Missouri to Illinois. In Missouri, Governor Lilburn Boggs issued what became known as the Extermination Order in 1838 — an executive order stating that “the Mormons must be treated as enemies, and must be exterminated or driven from the State if necessary for the public peace.” The order remained on Missouri’s books, unrepealed, until 1976. In Illinois, Smith was murdered by a mob in 1844 while awaiting trial in a jail in Carthage. The mob included members of the Illinois state militia.

The pattern of the template is visible at each stage: a religious community identified as theologically incompatible with American values; the theological critique carrying social and economic anxiety about a rapidly growing, politically cohesive group that voted as a bloc and accumulated land and influence; the language of security deployed — Mormons were described as a theocratic threat to republican self-government; and the persecution enabled from the top, with governors issuing extermination orders and militia members participating in the murder of the church’s founder.

Brigham Young governed Utah Territory from 1850. The federal government's target was not merely his leadership — it was the entire institutional structure of the church he led. Source: Library of Congress / public domain

After Smith’s death, Brigham Young led the main body of Latter-day Saints west, out of the United States entirely — into Mexican territory. They arrived in the Salt Lake Valley in 1847. The Mexican-American War concluded the following year, and the territory became American again. The Saints had crossed the country to escape the United States. The United States followed them.

The Practice That Made Them Enemies

Anti-Polygamy Political Cartoon / Edmunds-Tucker Era : The Edmunds-Tucker Act of 1887 dissolved the LDS Church, seized its assets, and stripped Utah women of the voting rights they had held since 1870. Source: Harper's Weekly / public domain

The specific theological practice that focused federal hostility on the Latter-day Saints was polygamy — the practice of plural marriage, which Smith had introduced as a doctrine in the early 1840s and which Young openly acknowledged and defended after the church’s arrival in Utah.

Polygamy was genuinely and deeply offensive to mainstream American Protestant culture, and the political response to it was not manufactured from whole cloth the way the anti-Catholic convent literature had been. The opposition was real. But understanding what the opposition was actually about requires separating the theological concern from the political one — and noting how quickly the political apparatus built to address polygamy expanded to target the entire institutional structure of the church, well beyond any plausible relationship to the marriage practice.

The Morrill Anti-Bigamy Act of 1862 was the first federal salvo — a law prohibiting plural marriage in the territories and disincorporating the LDS Church, limiting the value of church property it could hold. It was passed while the Civil War was consuming congressional attention, and enforcement was minimal for over a decade. But it established the legal framework.

The Poland Act of 1874 stripped the church-controlled probate courts of their jurisdiction over criminal and civil cases, transferring authority to federal courts where non-Mormon judges presided. The intent was explicit: to create a judicial apparatus capable of prosecuting polygamy cases that church-friendly local courts had declined to pursue.

The Edmunds Act of 1882 went further. It made polygamous cohabitation — living with more than one woman, regardless of formal marriage — a criminal offense punishable by fine and imprisonment. It disfranchised polygamists, barred them from jury service, and created a five-member federal commission with sweeping authority over Utah’s electoral machinery. Approximately twelve thousand Latter-day Saints were disfranchised under its provisions.

The Edmunds-Tucker Act of 1887 was the most sweeping. It dissolved the Corporation of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints entirely — seizing its assets for the federal government. It abolished the Perpetual Emigrating Fund Company, which had helped tens of thousands of converts emigrate to Utah. It disfranchised all women in Utah Territory, reversing the territory’s pioneering grant of women’s suffrage in 1870. It required all voters to swear an oath that they neither practiced nor believed in polygamy. It abolished the territorial militia.

This was not a law targeting a practice. This was a law targeting a church — dismantling its institutional infrastructure, seizing its assets, stripping its members of political rights, and imposing a loyalty oath that required subscribers to swear conformity not merely in conduct but in belief.

The First Amendment says Congress shall make no law prohibiting the free exercise of religion.

Congress made several.

Reynolds and the Belief-Practice Distinction

The Supreme Court's unanimous ruling in Reynolds introduced the belief-practice distinction: the government could not punish belief, only conduct. It was a right confined to the interior of the skull. Source: U.S. Supreme Court Records / public domain

The constitutional question arrived at the Supreme Court in 1879, in the case of Reynolds v. United States. George Reynolds was a secretary to Brigham Young who had agreed to serve as a test case for the constitutionality of the Morrill Anti-Bigamy Act. He was convicted of bigamy and appealed on First Amendment grounds, arguing that his plural marriages were a religious obligation and that punishing him for fulfilling that obligation violated his right to free exercise.

The Supreme Court ruled against him unanimously.

Chief Justice Morrison Waite’s opinion introduced a distinction that would do lasting damage to religious freedom jurisprudence for generations: the distinction between religious belief and religious practice. The First Amendment, Waite wrote, protected belief absolutely. It did not protect practice. Congress could not punish a man for believing in polygamy. It could punish him for practicing it. The government’s authority over conduct was not constrained by the religious motivations that produced that conduct.

The logic sounds plausible until you examine what it actually means. Free exercise of religion is, by definition, the practice of religion. A right to believe without a corresponding right to act on that belief is not a meaningful religious freedom — it is a freedom confined entirely to the interior of the skull, where it has no effect on anything. Waite’s distinction effectively held that the Free Exercise Clause protected thought but not action — which is to say, it protected nothing that a government would ever have reason to suppress.

The opinion also deployed, with considerable effect, a piece of rhetoric that the template had used before and would use again: the claim that the targeted practice was so barbarous, so incompatible with civilization, that it placed itself outside the scope of constitutional protection. Waite cited polygamy’s association with “Asiatic and African” peoples as evidence of its fundamental incompatibility with “republican institutions.” The theological objection was doing racial work, as it always does. Polygamy was not merely wrong. It was foreign. It was uncivilized. It was the kind of practice that peoples of a lesser order engaged in — and the Constitution was not designed to protect that.

Reynolds remained good law for decades. Its belief-practice distinction was applied, with varying degrees of consistency, in cases involving Native American religious practices, Jehovah’s Witnesses, and eventually Muslim Americans. The specific ruling was not formally overturned so much as gradually qualified by later decisions — but its underlying logic, that the government retains authority over religious practice when it deems that practice sufficiently threatening, has never been fully abandoned.

The Manifesto and the Price of Admission

By the late 1880s, the federal campaign had achieved its immediate objective. The church’s assets had been seized. Its leaders were in hiding or in prison. Thousands of its members had been disfranchised. The institutional infrastructure of the most cohesive religious community in the American West had been systematically dismantled.

Wilford Woodruff's 1890 Manifesto: The Manifesto was the price of admission — an institutional renunciation of plural marriage, framed as revelation, extracted by three decades of federal persecution. Source: Meridian Magazine / LDS Church Archives

In 1890, church president Wilford Woodruff issued what became known as the Manifesto — an official declaration ending the practice of plural marriage. It was not framed as a capitulation, though that is what it was. It was framed as a revelation: that God had directed the church to cease the practice in order to preserve the institution itself. The theological reasoning was genuine, as far as it went — Woodruff believed, and said, that continued resistance would result in the complete destruction of the church. He was probably right.

The Manifesto was the price of admission. The federal government had made clear that the Latter-day Saints could participate in American civic life — could be citizens, could vote, could hold property, could exist as an institution — on one condition: that they abandon the theological practice that had made them enemies. Not merely that they cease practicing it. That the church, as an institution, officially renounce it.

Utah was admitted to the Union as the 45th state in 1896, six years after the Manifesto. The anti-polygamy oath required by the Edmunds-Tucker Act was incorporated into the new state’s constitution. The price was paid. The admission was granted.

The parallel to John F. Kennedy’s 1960 Houston speech is not exact — Kennedy was not responding to a federal campaign of asset seizure and disfranchisement. But the structure is the same: a religious community, identified as incompatible with American civic life, required to perform a public renunciation of its institutional loyalty as a precondition for full participation. Kennedy renounced papal authority over his governance. The Latter-day Saints renounced plural marriage as an institutional practice. In both cases, the dominant culture set the terms; the targeted community met them; and the meeting of them was treated as evidence that the community had, finally, become acceptable.

The acceptability, in both cases, was conditional on continued conformity.

What the Mormon Moment Reveals

Salt Lake Temple, late 1800s: The Saints had crossed the continent to escape the United States. The United States followed them. Source: Utah State Historical Society / public domain

The history of federal persecution of the Latter-day Saints is instructive for the series’ argument in ways that go beyond the specific facts.

First, it demonstrates that the template operates regardless of the racial composition of the targeted community. The Latter-day Saints were, overwhelmingly, white and of Northern European descent. Anti-Mormon persecution cannot be explained primarily as racial proxy, as anti-Catholic and anti-Muslim persecution can. What it demonstrates is that the template’s core mechanism — identifying a religious community as bearing a foreign or incompatible loyalty, deploying security language to legitimate persecution, and enabling that persecution from the top — operates independently of race when the theological and political threat is sufficiently salient. Race amplifies the mechanism. It does not create it.

Second, it demonstrates what happens when the mechanism reaches its logical conclusion — when the government does not merely permit or enable persecution but actively prosecutes it through legislation, litigation, and military force. The Latter-day Saints lost, in sequence: their assets, their institutional structures, their voting rights, their women’s suffrage, their territorial militia, and ultimately the theological doctrine that had most distinguished them. What they gained, after meeting every condition the federal government imposed, was statehood and the right to be left alone — on the understanding that they would remain, institutionally, within the bounds of what the Protestant establishment considered acceptable.

Third, and most durably, the Reynolds belief-practice distinction introduced a constitutional vulnerability that subsequent targeted communities would find waiting for them. When the government argued, after September 11, that surveilling mosques was compatible with the First Amendment because it targeted conduct rather than belief, it was drawing on a logic that the Supreme Court had established in 1879. The specific case involved polygamy. The principle was general.

The Latter-day Saints have largely disappeared from the history of American religious persecution. They are today a fully integrated part of the American religious mainstream — politically influential, institutionally powerful, socially accepted. The journey from Missouri’s Extermination Order to Mitt Romney’s Senate seat took less than two centuries. The price of that journey was the Manifesto: the public, institutional renunciation of the practice that had made them enemies.

The asterisk was applied. The conditions were met. The community was admitted.

The asterisk moved on to its next target.
