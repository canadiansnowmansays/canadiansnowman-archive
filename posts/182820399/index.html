The Mueller Report – Ten Instances of Obstruction, (2017-2019)

Part 7: The Obstruction Continues

December 30, 2025

Part 6 examined the first five instances of obstruction documented in the Mueller Report: Trump’s response to reports of Russian support, his pressure on Comey regarding Flynn, his efforts to enlist intelligence officials, Comey’s firing, and Trump’s attempt to terminate Mueller himself.

These five instances established a clear pattern of corrupt intent and escalating conduct. But Trump wasn’t finished. Even after White House Counsel Don McGahn refused to fire Mueller, Trump continued his campaign to obstruct the investigation through increasingly sophisticated means—curtailing Mueller’s scope, preventing witness testimony, tampering with witnesses, and attempting to cover up his earlier obstruction.

The final five instances reveal a president who learned from his early failures. When direct orders to fire Mueller failed, Trump attempted subtler forms of interference. When that proved difficult, he turned to witness tampering and dangling pardons. And when news of his obstruction became public, he attempted to create false records to deny what he had done.

Together, all ten instances paint a portrait of sustained, deliberate obstruction of justice—conduct that would have resulted in criminal charges for any American not protected by the constitutional prohibition on indicting a sitting president.

Instance 6: Trump’s Efforts to Curtail the Special Counsel Investigation (June-December 2017)

The Public Campaign

After Don McGahn refused to fire Mueller in June 2017, Trump shifted tactics. If he couldn’t terminate the Special Counsel, perhaps he could limit Mueller’s investigative scope and undermine his credibility.

Throughout the summer and fall of 2017, Trump launched a sustained public campaign against Mueller and the investigation. His Twitter feed became a daily assault on the inquiry’s legitimacy. He called it a “witch hunt” 315 times during his presidency. He attacked Mueller personally, questioning his fairness and alleging conflicts of interest. He criticized Mueller’s team composition, calling them “angry Democrats.”

This public campaign served multiple obstructive purposes. It signaled to witnesses that cooperation with Mueller would earn Trump’s public wrath. It laid groundwork for potentially firing Mueller by establishing alleged improprieties. And it attempted to delegitimize Mueller’s eventual findings before they were issued.

The Private Pressure on Sessions

Simultaneously, Trump waged a private campaign to get Attorney General Jeff Sessions to reverse his recusal from the Russia investigation and take control of Mueller’s inquiry.

Sessions had recused himself in March 2017 after it emerged that he had failed to disclose his own contacts with Russian Ambassador Kislyak during Senate confirmation hearings. Justice Department ethics officials concluded that Sessions’s involvement in the Trump campaign created a conflict of interest requiring recusal from any investigation involving that campaign.

Trump was furious about the recusal and never forgave Sessions for it. Throughout 2017 and into 2018, Trump repeatedly pressured Sessions to “unrecuse” and take control of the Mueller investigation. Trump told Sessions that attorneys general in previous administrations had protected their presidents, citing examples like Bobby Kennedy protecting John F. Kennedy and Eric Holder protecting Barack Obama.

According to testimony provided to Mueller’s team, Trump told Sessions in December 2017: “I don’t have a lawyer.” Trump wanted Sessions to act as his personal attorney rather than as the nation’s chief law enforcement officer.

Trump also asked Sessions whether he would reassign Mueller’s investigation to a different prosecutor. When Sessions indicated that current policy would not permit such a transfer, Trump was livid.

The Attempt to Limit Mueller’s Scope

In addition to pressuring Sessions, Trump also attempted to define Mueller’s investigation narrowly to exclude examination of Trump’s conduct. In meetings with advisors, Trump argued that Mueller should only investigate Russian interference, not potential obstruction of justice by Trump or financial dealings involving the Trump Organization.

Trump directed his staff to communicate these limits to Mueller’s team. The staff understood these directives as attempts to interfere with the investigation and largely declined to carry them out, recognizing that Trump had no authority to dictate the Special Counsel’s investigative scope.

Mueller’s team found that Trump’s efforts to curtail the investigation—through public attacks, pressure on Sessions to unrecuse, and attempts to limit investigative scope—constituted obstruction because they were calculated to impede and discourage the investigation’s progress.

Instance 7: Trump’s Further Efforts to Have Sessions Take Over the Investigation (2017-2018)

The Ongoing Pressure Campaign

Trump’s frustration with Jeff Sessions’s recusal didn’t end in 2017. Throughout 2018, Trump continued demanding that Sessions reverse his recusal and take control of the Mueller investigation.

The pressure was relentless and increasingly public. Trump publicly humiliated Sessions through Twitter attacks and media interviews. In July 2017, Trump told the New York Times he wouldn’t have appointed Sessions if he had known Sessions would recuse himself. Trump called the recusal “very unfair to the president.”

This public criticism served a dual purpose: expressing Trump’s rage at Sessions while also signaling to future attorneys general what Trump expected—personal loyalty and protection from investigation rather than independent law enforcement.

The Whitaker Scheme

As 2018 progressed and Sessions refused to unrecuse, Trump began exploring ways to replace him with someone who would take control of Mueller’s investigation.

In November 2018, Trump forced Sessions to resign and immediately installed Matthew Whitaker as Acting Attorney General. Whitaker had publicly criticized the Mueller investigation before joining the Justice Department, writing in 2017 that Mueller had come “dangerously close to crossing” a “red line” by examining Trump’s finances.

Trump’s selection of Whitaker was transparently designed to install someone sympathetic to Trump who could oversee and potentially curtail Mueller’s investigation. However, career Justice Department officials and members of Congress immediately challenged Whitaker’s appointment, questioning its legality and appropriateness given his public comments about Mueller.

Whitaker ultimately did not interfere with Mueller’s investigation, and Mueller delivered his report in March 2019, less than four months into Whitaker’s tenure. But Trump’s selection of Whitaker demonstrated his continued determination to control the investigation through personnel choices.

The Corrupt Intent

Mueller’s team found that Trump’s persistent efforts to have Sessions take control of the investigation demonstrated corrupt intent. Trump wasn’t seeking proper Justice Department management—he was specifically trying to install himself a protector who would limit or end Mueller’s investigation.

The timing of Trump’s pressure on Sessions revealed his consciousness of guilt. Trump intensified his demands that Sessions unrecuse whenever the investigation took steps that threatened Trump—such as when Mueller’s team expressed interest in interviewing Trump or when Mueller secured cooperation from witnesses close to Trump.

Instance 8: Trump’s Orders to McGahn to Deny Reports About the Effort to Fire Mueller (January-February 2018)

The New York Times Report

On January 25, 2018, the New York Times published a detailed report revealing that Trump had ordered White House Counsel Don McGahn to have Mueller fired in June 2017, and that McGahn had threatened to resign rather than carry out the directive.

The story was accurate—as detailed in Instance 5 in Part 6—but it was politically devastating for Trump. It provided clear evidence of obstruction of justice and drew immediate comparisons to Nixon’s Saturday Night Massacre.

Trump needed the story to go away. His solution was to create a false record denying it ever happened.

The Order to Deny and Create False Documentation

Within days of the Times report, Trump called McGahn and told him to issue a public statement denying the story. McGahn refused, telling Trump’s staff that the story was accurate.

Trump didn’t give up. He had his staff continue pressuring McGahn to deny the report. When McGahn still refused, Trump tried a different approach: he asked McGahn to write a letter to the file “for our records” stating that Trump never ordered Mueller’s termination.

This request was extraordinary. Trump was asking his White House Counsel to create false documentation that would contradict McGahn’s truthful testimony to Mueller’s investigators. Such a letter would serve as evidence to dispute the Times report and potentially to defend Trump if obstruction charges were ever brought.

McGahn again refused. He told Trump’s advisors that he would not participate in creating a false record. McGahn’s notes from his conversations with Trump would be available to Mueller’s investigators, and any letter contradicting those notes would be obvious perjury.

The Additional Pressure

When McGahn wouldn’t budge, Trump escalated. He had then-Chief of Staff John Kelly and other officials meet with McGahn to convince him to issue a denial. Kelly himself recognized the impropriety—he told others he didn’t want to be involved in having McGahn lie.

Trump also attempted to cast doubt on McGahn’s account by suggesting to other advisors that perhaps McGahn had “misunderstood” Trump’s directive about firing Mueller. This alternative narrative—that McGahn had somehow misinterpreted clear instructions—was another attempt to create ambiguity about what Trump had actually said.

The Obstruction Analysis

Mueller’s team concluded that Trump’s efforts to get McGahn to deny the Times report and create false documentation constituted obstruction of justice. Trump’s actions met all three elements: obstructive act (directing McGahn to create a false record), nexus to official proceeding (Mueller’s ongoing investigation), and corrupt intent (Trump knew the Times story was accurate yet sought to create false evidence denying it).

This instance reveals a sophisticated form of obstruction—not just attempting to fire the investigator, but attempting to destroy evidence and create false records to cover up the attempt after it became public.

Instance 9: Trump’s Conduct Toward Flynn, Manafort, and Unknown Subjects (2017-2018)

The Pardon Dangles

Throughout Mueller’s investigation, Trump engaged in a systematic campaign of witness tampering through public statements and private communications designed to discourage cooperation with prosecutors.

The pattern was consistent: Trump praised witnesses who remained loyal and refused to cooperate with Mueller, while attacking those who chose to work with investigators. Underlying these public statements was an implicit promise—remain loyal, and Trump might pardon you. Cooperate, and face Trump’s wrath.

Michael Flynn

After Michael Flynn pleaded guilty in December 2017 to lying to the FBI, Trump’s public comments about Flynn were remarkably sympathetic. Trump called Flynn “a wonderful man” and suggested Flynn had been mistreated by the FBI. Trump’s personal attorney even communicated with Flynn’s attorney about a potential pardon, keeping open the possibility that Flynn’s cooperation might result in presidential forgiveness.

This pardon dangling served to discourage Flynn from providing full cooperation to Mueller’s team. If Flynn believed a pardon was possible, he might limit his cooperation or protect Trump in his testimony.

Trump’s calculation proved partially correct—Flynn’s cooperation with Mueller was limited, and Trump pardoned Flynn in November 2020 before sentencing.

Paul Manafort

Trump’s conduct toward his former campaign chairman Paul Manafort was even more blatant. After Manafort was indicted and jailed for witness tampering while awaiting trial, Trump repeatedly praised Manafort publicly.

Trump tweeted: “I feel very badly for Paul Manafort and his wonderful family. ‘Justice’ took a 12 year old tax case, among other things, applied tremendous pressure on him and, unlike Michael Cohen, he refused to ‘break’ - make up stories in order to get a ‘deal.’ Such respect for a brave man!”

This tweet explicitly praised Manafort for refusing to cooperate with Mueller and implicitly criticized Michael Cohen for doing so. The message to other witnesses was clear: remain silent about Trump, and you’ll be called brave and wonderful. Cooperate, and you’ll be attacked as a liar making up stories.

Trump also publicly mused about pardoning Manafort, stating in June 2018: “I do want to see people treated fairly. That’s what it’s all about.” This public statement signaled to Manafort that a pardon was possible if he continued refusing to cooperate against Trump.

The strategy worked. Manafort initially refused to provide meaningful cooperation to Mueller’s team. Even after agreeing to a cooperation agreement, prosecutors determined that Manafort had lied to them and continued protecting Trump. Trump pardoned Manafort in December 2020.

The Obstruction Analysis

Mueller concluded that Trump’s conduct toward witnesses constituted obstruction through witness tampering. Trump’s public statements praising those who didn’t cooperate, attacking those who did, and dangling the possibility of pardons were calculated to influence witness testimony and cooperation.

The corrupt intent was obvious: Trump wanted witnesses to protect him rather than tell the truth. His pardon power became a tool for obstructing justice rather than for its intended constitutional purpose.

Instance 10: Trump’s Conduct Toward Cohen (2017-2018)

Michael Cohen’s Dual Role

Michael Cohen served as Trump’s personal attorney and fixer for over a decade. Cohen was intimately familiar with Trump’s business dealings, personal conduct, and potentially criminal activity. When federal prosecutors and Mueller’s team began investigating Cohen in 2017-2018, Trump recognized the existential threat Cohen posed.

Initially, Trump and Cohen maintained a united front. Trump praised Cohen publicly, and Cohen signaled his continued loyalty. Trump’s attorney contacted Cohen’s attorney with messages of support and suggestions that Trump would “take care” of Cohen.

The Pressure to Stay Loyal

Throughout early 2018, as prosecutors closed in on Cohen, Trump maintained public support designed to discourage cooperation. Trump tweeted: “Most people will flip if the Government lets them out of trouble, even if it means lying or making up stories. Sorry, I don’t see Michael doing that.”

This tweet served multiple purposes. It defined cooperation with prosecutors as “flipping” and equated it with lying. It expressed confidence in Cohen’s loyalty. And it signaled Trump’s expectation that Cohen would remain silent.

Trump’s attorney, Rudy Giuliani, made the pardon dangling even more explicit. In media interviews, Giuliani repeatedly stated that Trump retained the authority to pardon Cohen and hadn’t ruled it out. These public statements ensured Cohen understood that loyalty could result in a pardon.

The Shift and Retaliation

In April 2018, FBI agents raided Cohen’s office, home, and hotel room, seizing records related to Cohen’s payment of hush money to Stormy Daniels and other matters. The aggressive prosecution tactics signaled that Cohen faced serious legal jeopardy.

By July 2018, Cohen had decided to cooperate with prosecutors. He retained new counsel and began providing information about Trump’s conduct, including Trump’s knowledge of the hush money payments and potential involvement in other criminal conduct.

Trump’s response to Cohen’s cooperation was immediate and vicious. Trump attacked Cohen on Twitter, calling him a “rat” and suggesting Cohen was lying to prosecutors to get a reduced sentence. Trump claimed Cohen was making up stories and couldn’t be trusted.

Trump tweeted: “If anyone is looking for a good lawyer, I would strongly suggest that you don’t retain the services of Michael Cohen!” And later: “I feel badly for Paul Manafort who served his country well...Unlike Michael Cohen who is a ‘rat.’”

The Explicit Comparison

Trump’s treatment of Cohen versus Manafort provided the clearest evidence of corrupt witness tampering. Both faced federal prosecution. Both had information potentially damaging to Trump. Manafort stayed quiet and was praised as brave; Cohen cooperated and was attacked as a rat.

The message to other potential witnesses couldn’t have been clearer: remain loyal and receive Trump’s praise and possible pardon, or cooperate and face public destruction and no mercy.

The Complete Pattern: Ten Instances of Obstruction

Examining all ten instances together reveals the scope and sophistication of Trump’s obstruction campaign:

  • Instances 1-2: Laying groundwork through public lies about Russian interference and direct pressure on Comey regarding Flynn

  • Instances 3-4: Enlisting government officials to make protective public statements and firing Comey when pressure failed

  • Instance 5: Attempting to terminate Mueller through White House Counsel

  • Instances 6-7: Shifting to subtler tactics—public attacks on Mueller, pressure on Sessions to unrecuse, attempts to limit investigative scope

  • Instance 8: Cover-up attempts through creating false records and getting McGahn to deny accurate reporting

  • Instances 9-10: Sophisticated witness tampering through pardon dangles, public praise for loyalists, and vicious attacks on cooperators

Why Mueller Didn’t Charge Trump

Mueller’s report carefully explained why it didn’t conclude that Trump committed crimes despite substantial evidence:

“The Office of Legal Counsel (OLC) has issued an opinion finding that ‘the indictment or criminal prosecution of a sitting President would impermissibly undermine the capacity of the executive branch to perform its constitutionally assigned functions’ and that ‘a sitting President is constitutionally immune from indictment and criminal prosecution.’”

Mueller respected this OLC opinion, concluding that fairness to Trump required not accusing him of crimes when Justice Department policy prohibited prosecution. However, Mueller explicitly stated: “If we had confidence after a thorough investigation of the facts that the President clearly did not commit obstruction of justice, we would so state. Based on the facts and the applicable legal standards, however, we are unable to reach that judgment.”

This wasn’t exoneration—this was a prosecutor saying he found substantial evidence of crimes but was constitutionally prohibited from charging the sitting president.

The Congressional Failure

Mueller’s report placed responsibility for accountability squarely on Congress. Mueller testified before Congress in July 2019 and was asked directly whether Trump could be charged with obstruction after leaving office. Mueller responded: “Yes.”

Yet Congress failed to act. The House of Representatives impeached Trump in December 2019, but not for the obstruction documented in the Mueller Report. Instead, they focused on Trump’s Ukraine conduct. The Senate acquitted Trump in February 2020.

After Trump left office in January 2021, no prosecutor brought obstruction charges based on the Mueller Report. The evidence documented by Mueller—evidence of ten separate instances of obstruction of justice—resulted in no criminal accountability.

The Ultimate Evasion

The Mueller Report represents perhaps the most comprehensive documentation of presidential criminality in American history. Mueller’s team conducted a thorough investigation, documented the evidence meticulously, and explicitly left the door open for Congressional impeachment and post-presidency prosecution.

Yet Trump evaded accountability through a combination of constitutional constraints, political calculation, and prosecutorial reluctance. The OLC opinion protected him while in office. The Senate’s partisan composition protected him from impeachment consequences. And prosecutors’ reluctance to bring unprecedented charges against a former president protected him after leaving office.

Trump understood the lesson: documentation doesn’t matter if no one acts on it. Evidence doesn’t matter if institutions fail to enforce accountability. And power—properly wielded—can defeat even the most meticulous investigation.

The Mueller Report should have ended Trump’s presidency. Instead, it became another chapter in his lifetime pattern of evading consequences for conduct that would have destroyed any other American’s career and freedom.
