Greenland When Allies Become Targets

When Allies Become Targets

Part 2: The Treaties Trump Forgets

January 22, 2026

Three days after the Wednesday White House meeting documented in Part 11, President Trump announced sweeping tariffs against eight European NATO allies who deployed forces to Greenland in solidarity with Denmark. Starting February 1, 2026, Denmark, Norway, Sweden, France, Germany, the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, and Finland will face 10% tariffs on all goods sent to the United States, increasing to 25% on June 1.

Trump’s statement was explicit: “This Tariff will be due and payable until such time as a Deal is reached for the Complete and Total purchase of Greenland.”

This is economic warfare for territorial conquest. Trump is weaponizing trade policy to coerce allied surrender of sovereign territory—proving that the Wednesday meeting changed nothing and that pressure will only escalate until Denmark capitulates or the crisis explodes into open conflict.

The tariffs also punish European nations for defending Denmark. Trump described European troop deployments to Greenland as “purposes unknown” and a “dangerous game”—the “purpose” is defending a NATO ally from American aggression, which Trump now treats as hostile act requiring punishment.

This confirms what Danish Foreign Minister Rasmussen said after Wednesday’s meeting: “We didn’t manage to change the American position.”

President Trump justified his Greenland ambitions last Friday with stark language: “When we own it, we defend it. You don’t defend leases the same way. You have to own it.”

This statement reveals either profound ignorance of existing agreements or deliberate misrepresentation of them. The United States doesn’t lease Greenland. It has something far more valuable: a Cold War-era defense treaty that grants American forces comprehensive military rights across the island—rights that provide everything the Alaska Purchase gave America in 1867, without requiring ownership.

Trump’s claim that ownership is necessary for effective defense collapses under scrutiny of what the United States already possesses. And his campaign to seize Greenland directly contradicts a 109-year-old treaty in which America formally recognized Danish sovereignty over the island.

These aren’t minor details. They’re the legal architecture that makes Trump’s Greenland ambitions not just strategically unnecessary but legally indefensible.

I. The 1916 Treaty: When America Recognized Danish Sovereignty

On August 4, 1916, the United States and Denmark signed the Treaty of the Danish West Indies. America paid Denmark $25 million—roughly $710 million in today’s dollars—for what are now the U.S. Virgin Islands.

The treaty’s significance extended far beyond the Caribbean. Article I contained language Denmark insisted upon:

“The right to take such measures as may be necessary for the defense of the territorial integrity of the United States is recognized by the Contracting Parties as being reserved exclusively to the United States of America... provided, however, that this reservation is not to be construed as in any way impairing the sovereignty of the Kingdom of Denmark over Greenland.“

This wasn’t accidental phrasing. It was negotiated specifically to ensure Denmark’s security over Greenland while America gained the Virgin Islands. The United States explicitly acknowledged Danish sovereignty over Greenland as treaty consideration.

That recognition has never been revoked. It remains binding international law between the United States and Denmark.

When Trump claims Greenland should belong to America, he’s not proposing something new. He’s repudiating a formal treaty commitment the United States made over a century ago—a commitment given as part of acquiring other Danish territory.

The historical irony cuts deep: America used recognition of Danish sovereignty over Greenland as bargaining chip to acquire the Virgin Islands. Now Trump wants to seize Greenland while keeping the Virgin Islands. This isn’t strategic vision. It’s treaty violation.

II. What the 1951 Agreement Actually Provides

If the 1916 treaty established the legal framework, the 1951 Defense Agreement established the operational reality: America already has everything in Greenland that ownership would provide.

Officially titled the “Agreement relating to the Defense of Greenland,” the 1951 treaty—signed April 27 in Copenhagen and still in force today—grants the United States extensive military rights while preserving Danish sovereignty.

What the Agreement Provides:

Article 1 allows the United States to “construct, install, maintain, and operate” military bases across Greenland—not just existing bases, but new ones anywhere on the island Washington chooses.

Article 2 permits stationing of American personnel without numerical limits.

Article 3 gives the U.S. military control over “landings, takeoffs, anchorages, moorings, movements, and operation of ships, aircraft, and waterborne craft” within designated defense areas.

The 2004 amendment, signed by Secretary of State Colin Powell, updated the agreement while maintaining these core rights. It explicitly recognizes Greenland as “an equal part of the Kingdom of Denmark”—a recognition that makes subsequent threats to seize Greenland even more problematic legally.

Operating Continuously Since 1951 - Everything Alaska Provided, America Already Has

In practical terms, this agreement means the United States can:

  • Build new military bases anywhere on Greenland

  • Station unlimited numbers of troops

  • Operate aircraft and naval vessels freely

  • Conduct surveillance and intelligence operations

  • Install radar, missile defense, and communications systems

  • Access Greenland’s strategic location for tracking missiles and monitoring Russian submarines

The United States currently operates Pituffik Space Base (formerly Thule Air Base) in northwestern Greenland, supporting missile warning, missile defense, and space surveillance operations. This base has operated continuously since 1951.

If the United States wanted to expand—building additional bases, deploying more troops, installing new radar systems—the 1951 agreement already permits this.

Former Danish parliamentary official Jens Adser Sorensen captured the situation perfectly: “Why don’t you use the mechanism of the defense agreement if you’re so worried about the security situation? The framework is there. It’s in place.”

Danish officials have said explicitly they would accommodate American requests for expanded military presence. “If the Americans want another military base, just say where,” a Danish lawmaker told The Atlantic, speaking on condition of anonymity. “If you want a radar, you can put it up.”

III. What Alaska Gave vs. What Trump Already Has

The Alaska Purchase gave the United States something it didn’t previously possess: sovereign control over Alaskan territory.

Before 1867, the United States had no legal rights in Alaska. Russia owned it, administered it, and controlled all access. American ships could not freely operate in Alaskan waters. American citizens could not settle on Alaskan land. The U.S. military could not establish bases. If the United States wanted these capabilities, it needed outright ownership.

The purchase provided ownership and with it came sovereign rights: the ability to make laws, control borders, exploit resources, and station military forces without external permission.

This is where the Greenland comparison collapses entirely.

Everything the Alaska Purchase gave America in terms of military access and strategic positioning, the 1951 agreement already provides in Greenland. The United States can:

  • Station forces (just as it could after buying Alaska)

  • Build bases (just as it could after buying Alaska)

  • Control movements in defense areas (just as it could after buying Alaska)

  • Conduct surveillance operations (just as it could after buying Alaska)

  • Access strategic locations (just as it could after buying Alaska)

The difference is ownership. And Trump has been explicit about why this matters to him.

He told The New York Times: “Ownership is very important. That’s what I feel is psychologically needed for success. I think that ownership gives you a thing that you can’t do, whether you’re talking about a lease or a treaty.”

Trump isn’t arguing that American security requires owning Greenland because the current agreement is insufficient for military purposes. He’s arguing that he wants to own it for “psychological” reasons—because ownership feels different than a treaty relationship.

This is the real estate developer talking, not the commander-in-chief. Trump looks at Greenland the way he looked at properties in Manhattan: it’s not enough to have access and usage rights; he needs his name on the deed.

IV. Indigenous Self-Determination in the Modern Era

Perhaps the most profound difference between Alaska in 1867 and Greenland in 2026 involves how international law treats indigenous peoples.

Alaska 1867: Indigenous Peoples as Property

The Treaty of Cession’s Article III stated: “The cession hereby made, conveys all the rights, franchises, and privileges now belonging to Russia... including the right of property in all unoccupied lands.”

Note what’s missing: any mention of Alaska Natives’ consent, rights, or wishes. The approximately 30,000 indigenous people inhabiting Alaska were transferred along with the land—explicitly treated as property rather than people with rights.

This wasn’t unusual for the 19th century. It’s unthinkable in the 21st.

Greenland 2026: Indigenous Rights as International Law

By 2026, multiple layers of international law protect indigenous peoples’ rights:

The UN Charter (1945) established principles of self-determination and equal rights for all peoples.

The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (1966) recognized: “All peoples have the right of self-determination. By virtue of that right they freely determine their political status.”

The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007) affirmed that indigenous peoples have:

  • “The right to self-determination”

  • The right to “freely determine their political status”

  • The right to “free, prior and informed consent” before measures affecting them are implemented

  • Protection against “any action which has the aim or effect of dispossessing them of their lands, territories or resources”

The United States voted in favor of this declaration. America formally endorsed these principles. They are now international law.

Greenland’s Self-Government Framework

Greenland’s 2009 Self-Government Act establishes:

Domestic Authority: Greenland controls 33 areas of governance including education, health care, social services, housing, environment, resource management, and culture.

Parliamentary Democracy: Greenland’s Inatsisartut (parliament) has 31 members elected through proportional representation, exercising legislative authority over domestic affairs.

Independence Referendum Right: Greenland can hold a referendum on independence at any time. If a majority votes for independence, Denmark must negotiate terms. This right is guaranteed by Danish law and cannot be revoked.

Resource Control: Greenland controls subsoil resources—minerals, oil, gas. Denmark relinquished this right in 2009, with revenues going to Greenland rather than Copenhagen.

Language and Identity: Greenlandic (Kalaallisut) is the official language. Schools teach in Greenlandic. Cultural preservation is constitutionally protected.

This isn’t theoretical autonomy. It’s functioning self-government with real power, democratic legitimacy, and legal protections.

What Greenlanders Actually Want

Multiple polls and statements reveal consistent preferences:

January 2026 Polling:

  • 85% oppose becoming part of the United States

  • Only 6% support American control

  • 56% support eventual independence from Denmark (but as sovereign nation, not joining U.S.)

Official Statements:

Prime Minister Jens-Frederik Nielsen (January 2026): “Greenland does not want to be owned by the United States. Greenland does not want to be governed by the United States. Greenland does not want to be part of the United States.”

Business Minister Naaja Nathanielsen (January 2026, testimony to British Parliament): “People are not sleeping, children are afraid, and it just fills everything these days. For others this might be a piece of land, but for us it’s home. My deepest dream or hope is that the people of Greenland will get a say no matter what.”

The Legal Reality

Denmark legally cannot sell Greenland without Greenlandic consent. This isn’t political preference—it’s constitutional requirement:

Danish Constitution Section 19: Territory cannot be ceded without consent of inhabitants affected.

Greenland Self-Government Act Section 21: Changes to Greenland’s constitutional status require referendum approved by Greenlandic voters.

International Law: UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples requires “free, prior and informed consent” for measures affecting indigenous peoples.

Any attempted transfer of Greenland without Greenlandic consent would violate Danish constitutional law, Greenlandic self-government law, and international human rights law.

When Mikkel Runge Olesen of the Danish Institute for International Studies said "It is impossible... Denmark does not have the authority to sell it," he was stating legal fact, not political opinion.

V. What Denmark Has Already Provided

Denmark hasn’t merely granted access—it has invested heavily in Arctic capabilities specifically benefiting American strategic interests.

$2.3 Billion Arctic Defense Investment (2023-2030):

  • Three new Arctic patrol vessels (Thetis-class) increasing Danish naval presence

  • Two long-range surveillance drones for continuous Arctic monitoring

  • Enhanced satellite capacity for communications and reconnaissance

  • 27 F-35A fighter aircraft fully compatible with American systems

  • CV90 infantry fighting vehicles for Arctic operations

  • Patriot and IRIS-T air defense systems protecting critical infrastructure

Personnel Expansion:

  • Extended conscription to women (starting 2027)

  • Increased annual conscripts from 4,700 to 7,500

  • Enhanced Arctic training programs

  • Joint exercises with American forces

Defense Spending Surge:

  • Reached 3.22% of GDP by 2025 (far exceeding NATO’s 2% target)

  • Committed to 5% of GDP by early 2030s

  • Specific allocations for Greenland defense infrastructure

Denmark passed legislation in June 2024 allowing expanded U.S. military presence on Danish soil, widening a 2023 agreement that already gave U.S. troops broad access to Danish air bases.

In other words: Denmark has provided everything a loyal ally should provide. It has increased defense spending beyond requirements, granted military access beyond what most allies permit, and demonstrated consistent willingness to accommodate American security needs.

The problem isn’t Danish unwillingness to cooperate. The problem is Trump’s insistence on ownership rather than partnership.

VI. Why “Ownership” Doesn’t Improve Security

Trump’s argument that “you don’t defend leases the same way” as ownership is demonstrably false based on American history.

The United States defended West Berlin for four decades without owning it. American troops stood ready to fight World War III to prevent Soviet seizure of a city the U.S. didn’t own, accessed through a treaty arrangement the U.S. didn’t control.

The United States has defended South Korea for 73 years through treaty commitment, not ownership. More than 35,000 American troops remain stationed there under a mutual defense treaty.

The United States maintains treaty-based defense commitments to:

  • Japan (since 1951)

  • Philippines (renewed 1951)

  • Australia (ANZUS, 1951)

  • Thailand (1954)

  • NATO allies (1949)

American troops have fought and died defending allies under treaty arrangements. Treaty commitments have proven more durable than territorial ownership—witness American abandonment of the Philippines in 1946 despite decades of colonial control, while treaty relationships with Japan and South Korea have strengthened over decades.

The idea that American forces won’t defend territory they don’t own contradicts 75 years of American alliance policy and military history.

VII. What Trump Would Gain vs. What He Would Lose

If Trump somehow seized Greenland, what would the United States gain beyond what it already possesses?

What America Would Gain:

  • Military access: Already have it (1951 treaty)

  • Strategic positioning: Already have it (Pituffik Space Base operational since 1951)

  • Ability to expand presence: Already have it (treaty permits unlimited expansion)

  • Resource development rights: Already negotiable (Denmark/Greenland open to business partnerships)

The only thing seizure would provide is ownership—the psychological satisfaction Trump explicitly cited as his motivation.

What America Would Lose:

  • NATO: The alliance would effectively end the moment U.S. forces entered Greenlandic territory against Danish wishes

  • Allied trust: Every treaty partner would recognize American commitments as worthless

  • Moral authority: The United States could never again credibly criticize territorial aggression

  • Legal credibility: 109 years of treaty recognition revealed as meaningless

  • International standing: America would be Russia—seizing territory by force from weaker neighbors

Senator Mark Kelly summarized the strategic catastrophe: “If we go and use the military against Denmark in taking their territory of Greenland, I think that’s a justification that the Chinese would like to have” to seize Taiwan.

The United States would trade functional, productive alliance relationships that already provide needed military access for ownership that satisfies Trump’s ego while destroying America’s alliance system.

VIII. The Treaty Violations Stack Up

Seizing Greenland would require Trump to violate:

1916 Treaty of the Danish West Indies - Explicit U.S. recognition of Danish sovereignty over Greenland

1951 Defense Agreement - Treaty acknowledging Danish sovereignty while granting U.S. military access

2004 Amendment - Reaffirmation recognizing Greenland as “equal part of the Kingdom of Denmark”

NATO Treaty Article 10 - Territorial integrity of member states

UN Charter Article 2(4) - Prohibition on threat or use of force against territorial integrity

UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples - Indigenous self-determination and consent requirements

Trump’s Greenland campaign doesn’t merely break new ground. It breaks treaty law the United States itself created, signed, and has honored for decades.

Conclusion: The Treaties Reveal the Truth

William Seward acquired Alaska through legitimate purchase from a willing seller in an era when such transactions were accepted practice and indigenous peoples had no legal voice.

Donald Trump wants to seize Greenland by threatening an unwilling ally, violating treaties America signed, ignoring indigenous peoples’ explicit rejection, and destroying the cooperative relationship that already provides everything ownership would add.

The difference isn’t strategic vision versus shortsightedness. It’s legitimate transaction versus treaty violation. It’s cooperative alliance versus coercive imperialism. It’s 21st-century law versus 19th-century conquest.

When Trump says “when we own it, we defend it,” he’s revealing that he doesn’t understand—or doesn’t care—that America already defends Greenland, that Denmark already cooperates fully, that the treaties already provide everything Alaska provided, and that ownership would add nothing strategic while costing everything alliance-related.

The treaties Trump forgets aren’t historical footnotes. They’re the legal framework making his Greenland campaign impossible under American law, Danish law, Greenlandic law, and international law.

Unless, of course, he’s willing to break them all.

Saturday’s protests in Nuuk—hundreds marching in freezing temperatures, Prime Minister Nielsen climbing onto a snowbank with his people, signs reading “No means no”—represented something Alaska Natives never had: legal rights, political voice, democratic self-governance, and international law protection.

The treaties that made Alaska possible are precisely the treaties that make Greenland impossible.
