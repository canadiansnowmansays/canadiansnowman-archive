The Asimov Gap

Beyond the Convergence - What the Builders Know

Part 18: Technical Realities, Timeline Uncertainties, and the Messy Path to Transformation

March 5, 2026

AI models released in 2025 and early 2026. Hundreds of models from dozens of companies across multiple countries - Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, Alibaba, DeepSeek, Mistral, and many more. The font is small because the list is massive. This is one year of development.

Look at that image.

Take a moment to actually scan it, even though the text is tiny. Count the columns. Note the company names. Notice how many models came from China (DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, Zhipu) versus the United States versus Europe.

This is what acceleration looks like. Not a chart showing exponential curves. Not a CEO prediction. But an actual inventory of AI models released in roughly 12 months.

Hundreds of models. From Google alone: Gemini 2.0 Flash, Gemini 2.0 Pro, Gemini 2.0 Flash-Lite, ShieldGemma 2, Gemma 4B, Gemma 3.1B, multiple experimental variants. From OpenAI: o3-mini, GPT-4.5 (Orion), o1-pro, half a dozen specialized models. From Anthropic: Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Claude Opus 4, Claude Sonnet 4, Claude Haiku 4.5, Claude Opus 4.5. From China: DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3, dozens of Qwen variants, Kimi models, Moonshot releases.

Every week brought new releases. New capabilities. New benchmarks. New frontiers crossed.

In Parts 14, we documented the CEO convergence of January 2026—five competing leaders all delivering the same warnings in the same month. The convergence validated the transformation. The timeline compressed from ten years to one or two. The urgency became undeniable.

But there’s another conversation happening—one that complicates the CEO narrative without contradicting it. It’s happening in the research labs where those hundreds of models get built. It’s happening on technical podcasts where people who write the actual code discuss what works and what doesn’t. It’s happening among builders who deal with reality every day: the models that break in production, the benchmarks that mislead, the capabilities that work in demos but fail at scale.

And it reveals something the CEO convergence doesn’t: the path to transformation is messier, more jagged, and less predictable than clean timelines suggest.

This matters because understanding where AI excels and where it fails—right now, not in theory—determines how you prepare. It’s the difference between panic and strategy. Between hype and reality. Between knowing which jobs disappear first and which remain surprisingly resilient.

That image above? It represents both incredible progress and profound uncertainty. Yes, capabilities are accelerating. But with hundreds of models, which ones actually matter? What can they do versus what gets claimed? Where is progress real versus overstated?

The CEOs gave us urgency. The builders give us precision.

Let’s talk about what the builders know.

The 4.5-Hour Technical Deep Dive

Just weeks after the CEO convergence, Lex Fridman hosted a marathon conversation with two of the most respected technical voices in AI: Nathan Lambert and Sebastian Raschka.

Nathan Lambert leads post-training research at the Allen Institute for AI, where his team develops the models that millions use. He wrote the definitive book on Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback—the technique that transformed GPT-3 into ChatGPT. He’s not selling products. He’s training the models, debugging the failures, and publishing the research that other labs build on.

Sebastian Raschka wrote “Build a Large Language Model From Scratch”—the book that teaches you to actually code these systems yourself, from first principles. He trains frontier models and teaches machine learning at scale. If you want to understand how transformers actually work, you read Sebastian’s work.

These aren’t salesmen pitching visions. These are builders dealing with reality—the models that break in production, the benchmarks that mislead, the capabilities that work in demos but fail at scale. They have no incentive to hype timelines or downplay limitations. Their reputations depend on accuracy, not excitement.

And over 4.5 hours, they delivered something rare in the AI discourse: technical honesty. What works. What doesn’t. Where progress is real. Where it’s overstated. What’s coming soon. What’s genuinely far away.

The conversation validates the CEO convergence on fundamentals—yes, capabilities are accelerating; yes, economic transformation is real; yes, the timeline compressed. But it also reveals crucial nuance that gets lost in executive summaries and 280-character warnings.

The builders see both the power and the limitations. And understanding both is essential for navigating what’s coming.

What the Builders Validate

Let’s start with what Nathan and Sebastian confirm, because the validation matters. This isn’t skepticism or denial. The transformation is real:

Scaling Laws Are Holding

“It’s held for 13 orders of magnitude of compute,” Nathan explains. “Why would it ever end?”

The fundamental relationship—more compute plus better data equals smarter models—remains intact. Pre-training scaling works. Reinforcement learning scaling works. Inference-time scaling (letting models “think” longer) works. All three axes continue improving.

Sebastian confirms: “You would still get a better model from more pre-training. It’s just finding the right ratio at the moment between pre-training, mid-training, post-training, and inference scaling.”

Capabilities Are Accelerating

The progress isn’t hype. Nathan describes his own experience: “I launched a project in July, and it was vibe-coded. I came back in December with Claude Opus 4.5, and it crushed all the issues. It might be a bigger model, but that progress is coming.”

Sebastian on RLVR (reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards): “I was training the Qwen 3 base model on Math 500. The base model had 15% accuracy. Just 50 steps, in a few minutes, the model went from 15% to 50% accuracy.”

Three-fold improvement in minutes. Not because the model learned new math, but because training unlocked capabilities already present from pre-training.

Economic Transformation Is Real

The survey data confirms Altman’s warnings: 80% of professional developers report programming is “more enjoyable” with AI. And here’s the kicker—senior developers ship MORE AI-generated code (over 50% of total output) than junior developers.

Why? Because seniors know how to use the tools effectively. They understand what to ask for, how to verify results, when to trust AI and when to intervene. The productivity gains aren’t theoretical—they’re happening now at companies building AI.

Nathan: “The amount of software that’ll be automated by the end of this year will be so high.” He’s watching it happen at AI2, at Anthropic, at OpenAI. The automation is real.

Infrastructure Build-Out Is Massive

“2026 is a year when very large Blackwell compute clusters—gigawatt-scale facilities—are coming online,” Nathan notes. “These were contracts signed in 2022 and 2023. It took two to three years to build these bigger clusters.”

The physical infrastructure for powerful AI is being built right now. Data centers. Nuclear power contracts. Chip fabrication. This isn’t speculative—it’s steel and silicon and power plants under construction.

So yes, the builders validate the core CEO message: transformation is real, capabilities are accelerating, economic impact is coming, infrastructure is scaling. The convergence wasn’t hype.

But.

What the Builders Complicate

Here’s where technical reality diverges from executive summaries:

Timeline Uncertainty Is Genuine

When Lex asks directly about superhuman coders and AGI timelines, the answers get nuanced fast.

Nathan: “I’d say less than ten years on the software side, but longer on research automation.” Then immediately complicates it: “It could be that they’re early by six months and make it because they were first, or maybe they’re early by eight years.”

Sebastian points to fundamental limitations: “Is it really AGI if we’re specializing it again? It’s not that different from back in the day when we had specialized algorithms.”

The uncertainty isn’t evasion—it’s honesty. When Dario says “1-2 years to powerful AI,” the builders see both possibilities: maybe he’s right, maybe the path is longer and messier than that timeline suggests. They don’t know. Nobody knows.

Capabilities Are “Jagged”

This is the critical concept that gets lost in CEO summaries. AI isn’t uniformly intelligent. It’s extraordinarily good at some things and shockingly bad at others—and the pattern doesn’t match intuition.

Sebastian: “AI is so-called jagged—excellent at some things, really bad at others.”

Nathan elaborates: “Computer use is a good example of what labs care about, and we haven’t seen a lot of progress on. We saw multiple demos in 2025—Claude can use your computer, OpenAI had Operator—and they all suck.”

Think about that. Claude can write sophisticated code, debug complex problems, and generate entire applications. But ask it to navigate a simple desktop interface that a six-year-old masters? It fails.

This jaggedness determines which jobs disappear first. Website creation? AI crushes it. Data analysis on clean datasets? Excellent. Debugging standard codebases? Very good.

But distributed systems programming? Tool integration across heterogeneous environments? Computer use requiring navigating arbitrary interfaces? Still terrible.

The middle 60% jobs most vulnerable aren’t the most complex—they’re the ones AI happens to be good at, which doesn’t correlate with difficulty the way we expect.

“Plateau This Year” Is Possible

Lex asks the uncomfortable question directly: “Is it possible we’re basically going to be plateauing this year? Not in capabilities, but in what that means for civilization?”

The answer isn’t dismissive. Sebastian: “I think what holds companies back right now is that the competition is not doing it. It’s more like a reputation thing.”

Nathan: “The next few years are definitely gonna see increased value on physical goods and events, and even more pressure on slop. The slop is only starting.”

Translation: We might get really good AI assistants that help with coding, writing, and analysis—but computer use stays broken, autonomous agents remain unreliable, and the “remote worker replacement” keeps not quite materializing. Civilization gets better autocomplete, not transformation.

It’s possible. Not certain, but possible. And preparing for that scenario looks different than preparing for Dario’s timeline.

Economic Impact Is Hard to Measure

When the conversation turns to GDP impact, the builders get skeptical fast.

Nathan: “What is GDP even made of? A lot of it is financial services. I don’t know what this is.”

Sebastian: “If everyone uses the same LLM, everyone will do the same thing. Companies want competitive advantage, and I think that is the no-way-around using some of their private data.”

The CEOs talk confidently about economic transformation. The builders see a more complex picture where measurement is genuinely unclear and distribution of benefits depends on factors nobody controls yet.

By mid-February, the pattern Lambert and Raschka described was playing out in public. The Atlantic‘s Lila Shroff reported that “Americans are living in parallel AI universes” — most of the country still thinks AI means ChatGPT, while tech hobbyists are watching agents collapse months of work into afternoons. Anthropic now reports that 90 percent of its own code is AI-generated. Microsoft is at 30 percent, with its CTO projecting 95 percent industry-wide by decade’s end. A pair of journalists used Claude Code to build a working competitor to a billion-dollar software company in under an hour.

But the jaggedness is right there in the same article. The same agents that can synthesize vast research and generate sophisticated code “struggle to do something as simple as copy and paste text from Google Docs into Substack.” When one venture capitalist asked Claude’s new consumer agent to organize his wife’s desktop, it deleted 15 years of family photos. “I need to stop and be honest with you about something important,” the bot told him. “I made a mistake.”

Brilliant and broken in the same breath. Lambert’s “they all suck” on computer use, confirmed in the wild.

Matt Shumer, an AI company CEO, compared the moment to early COVID — most Americans still oblivious to what’s coming. “The experience that tech workers have had over the past year, of watching AI go from ‘helpful tool’ to ‘does my job better than I do’, is the experience everyone else is about to have.” His essay has 80 million views.

Stanford’s Fei-Fei Li offered the counterweight: “Silicon Valley sometimes mistakes clear vision with short distance. But the journey is going to be long.”

Both things are true. The destination is real. The road is jagged.

Anil Dash, with 40 years of coding experience, captures the divide precisely: "For coders, LLMs free you from the drudgery to let you focus on the creative part. For writers, artists, and illustrators, they take away the creative part and only leave the drudgery." The jaggedness isn't just technical. It's experiential. It determines who celebrates and who mourns.

The Jagged Edge: What Works Now vs. Hype

Understanding specifically what AI does well versus poorly matters enormously for individual preparation. Here’s the current reality:

What Works NOW:

  • Code generation for standard tasks: Creating websites, data analysis scripts, API integrations, routine functions

  • Autocomplete at scale: Suggesting next lines, completing boilerplate, filling in patterns

  • Content transformation: Summarization, reformatting, style changes, translation

  • Information retrieval with synthesis: Search + summarization across multiple sources

  • Narrow tool use: Calling specific APIs, running defined commands, executing structured tasks

What Still Sucks:

  • Computer use: Navigating arbitrary desktop/web interfaces, clicking buttons, managing windows

  • Tool integration: Connecting heterogeneous systems that weren’t designed to work together

  • Distributed systems: Programming that requires coordinating multiple machines/services

  • Continual learning: Adapting to user-specific feedback without full retraining

  • Goal persistence: Maintaining complex objectives across long time horizons

Sebastian describes the gap perfectly: “If you ask today, ‘draw a flowchart of X, Y, Z,’ it’s most of the time terrible. And it is kind of like a very simple task for a human.”

But the same model can write sophisticated code, debug complex problems, and generate entire applications. The jaggedness is real and non-intuitive.

The jaggedness extends even to mathematics — the domain where AI should theoretically dominate. Terence Tao, widely considered the world’s greatest living mathematician, told The Atlantic that AI has been “systematically exploring the long tail” of unsolved problems, “knocking off the easiest ones.” But the solutions lack the journey: “AI tools are like taking a helicopter to drop you off at the site. You miss all the benefits of the journey itself.” The models can’t rate their own confidence accurately. They can’t have a real conversation with a human collaborator. And the AI companies are “obsessed with push-of-a-button, completely autonomous workflows” when what hard problems actually require is human-AI dialogue. Even the world’s greatest mathematician wants a collaborator, not a replacement. The builders of AI haven’t figured that out yet.

Practical Implication:

If your job is primarily “generate standard code” or “write reports following known patterns” or “analyze clean data,” you’re vulnerable now—not in 1-2 years.

If your job requires “integrate these five systems nobody designed to work together” or “navigate arbitrary legacy interfaces” or “maintain complex goals across months with minimal supervision,” you have more time than the CEO timelines suggest.

The transformation is uneven. Understanding where the edge is determines your strategy.

Timeline Scenarios: From Optimistic to Skeptical

Let’s be explicit about competing timelines, because the range matters:

Optimistic (Nathan’s “This Year”):

“By the end of this year, the amount of software that’ll be automated will be so high.” Specific capabilities: building websites, standing up data analysis, implementing standard features end-to-end.

Timeline: Now to 12 months for significant automation of narrow software tasks.

Middle (Sebastian’s “Jagged Progress”):

“I don’t think superhuman coder is unachievable, but because of the jagged nature, you’ll always have gaps in capabilities.”

Timeline: Continuous improvement over 3-5 years, with some tasks fully automated and others remaining surprisingly human-dependent. No clean “AGI achieved” moment.

Skeptical (Both on Research Automation):

Nathan: “I disagree with some of the AI2027 report presumptions. AI is jagged, excellent at some things, really bad at others. I don’t see automated AI research happening in that timeframe.”

Sebastian: “How do you become an expert if you never try to do the thing yourself? There will be a Goldilocks sweet spot where maybe you study two hours offline, and the rest use LLMs.”

Timeline: 10+ years for fully automated research, 5+ years for superhuman software engineering across all domains.

Why the Range Matters:

If optimistic timeline hits, you need to be ready this year. Master AI tools now, reposition immediately, build skills AI can’t replicate yet.

If middle timeline unfolds, you have 3-5 years of jagged progress—some jobs disappear fast, others remain weirdly resilient. Strategy: monitor which tasks AI conquers next, stay ahead of the automation wave.

If skeptical timeline proves accurate, you have 5-10 years before full job transformation, but incremental pressure throughout. Strategy: continuous adaptation, not panic.

The uncertainty itself is the challenge. You can’t plan precisely when nobody—including the builders—knows which scenario unfolds.

The Open Source Wildcard

One factor that might accelerate or decelerate timelines: the open source question.

Nathan spent significant time describing the Adam Project—American Truly Open Models—a push to build US-based open-weight models competitive with Chinese frontier labs like DeepSeek, Qwen, and Kimi.

Why this matters: Open models enable innovation that closed labs can’t capture. Every researcher, every startup, every tinkerer can build on open foundations. That accelerates progress unpredictably.

But it also means:

  • Capabilities diffuse faster (can’t be controlled or gated)

  • Competition intensifies (more labs racing)

  • Smaller actors can compete (lowers barriers)

  • Geopolitical dynamics shift (US vs. China on open models)

Nathan: “These Chinese companies have set things into motion that I think would potentially not have happened if they were not all releasing models.”

Translation: DeepSeek releasing R1 open-weight forced Meta, Google, and others to respond. Competition accelerates timelines—but in jagged, unpredictable ways.

The open source trajectory could compress timelines (more competition, faster iteration) or extend them (resources diffused across many actors, not concentrated in frontier labs). Another uncertainty.

The Crystal Ball

While Lambert and Raschka were explaining what AI builds, something else was happening: AI was learning to predict.

Not in the narrow sense — chess engines have calculated optimal futures for decades. This is general prediction. Geopolitics, economics, elections, weather, box office receipts, wars.

Three times a year, the forecasting platform Metaculus hosts tournaments with notoriously difficult questions. Hundreds of elite human forecasters — the kind of people who spend hours building regression models on Tour de France results while cross-referencing injury reports and fan commentary — compete for cash prizes and bragging rights. These aren’t casual guessers. They’re the best in the world at reading patterns in messy, contingent reality.

In 2024, no AI had cracked the top 100.

By the 2025 Summer Cup, a London startup called Mantic entered a prediction engine — a scaffolding of multiple large language models, each assigned different specialties, working as a team to generate forecasts. It had to predict shifts in Ukraine’s battle lines, Superman’s box-office gross, whether China would ban rare earth exports, and whether Musk and Trump would publicly attack each other within a specific window.

It placed eighth out of 500 entrants. A record for any bot.

Then it entered the Fall Cup. Placed fourth. Beat the weighted average of all human forecasters combined — proving itself wiser than the wisdom of a pretty wise crowd.

Meanwhile, Lightning Rod Labs built a model purpose-trained to predict Trump’s erratic behavior. Fed it 2,000 forecasting questions with known outcomes, let it learn from its mistakes, and tested it on fresh questions — whether Trump would meet Xi in person, whether he’d attend the Army-Navy game. It outperformed OpenAI’s most advanced models.

A machine that predicts Trump better than other machines. Think about that for a moment.

Haifeng Xu, a professor at the University of Chicago, built a benchmarking service that tests major AI models on live betting-market questions daily. “They all have different forecasting personalities,” Xu told The Atlantic. ChatGPT is conservative, perhaps too conservative. Grok and Gemini are currently more accurate.

In January 2026, Mantic entered its latest engine into the Metaculus Spring Cup. If it moves up one spot from its last finish, it becomes the first AI to medal in a major prediction tournament.

On Metaculus itself, a group of forecasters has taken to estimating when AI will outpredict elite human teams. Last January: 75 percent chance by 2030. Now: 95 percent.

The superforecasters are forecasting their own obsolescence.

Here’s why this belongs in an essay about what the builders know: Lambert and Raschka showed us that AI capabilities are jagged — brilliant at some tasks, terrible at others. Prediction is one of the brilliant ones. And prediction is power. Whoever sees the future most clearly — which markets will move, which governments will fall, which technologies will work — captures disproportionate value.

If AI prediction engines become superhuman, and they’re owned by the same handful of companies that own everything else in AI, the Asimov Gap doesn’t just widen.

It becomes permanent.

The crystal ball works. The question, as always, is who gets to hold it.

Why This Matters: Urgency + Humility

So what do we do with competing timelines, jagged capabilities, and genuine uncertainty?

First: Maintain Urgency

The builders validate that transformation is real. Sebastian: “Everything will just be amplified and amplified and amplified. I can see that continuing for a long time.”

The CEO convergence wasn’t wrong about fundamentals. Capabilities are accelerating. Economic transformation is coming. Infrastructure is being built. Jobs are being automated.

Don’t use timeline uncertainty as excuse for complacency. The middle 60% remains at risk. The preparation window is closing. The urgency is justified.

Second: Add Humility

But also recognize: the path is messier than clean narratives suggest. Nathan: “There’s a lot of obvious things to improve, and it’ll take us with the ideas we have multiple years to saturate.”

This isn’t about whether AGI arrives. It’s about how transformation unfolds—unevenly, unpredictably, with some capabilities racing ahead and others stalling unexpectedly.

Humility means preparing for multiple scenarios:

  • Some jobs disappear faster than expected (be ready)

  • Other jobs remain weirdly resilient (don’t panic prematurely)

  • Capabilities advance jaggedly (monitor which wave hits you next)

  • Timeline uncertainty persists (stay flexible)

Third: Act Anyway

The worst response is paralysis. Waiting for certainty guarantees you’re unprepared when transformation arrives—whether that’s 18 months or 5 years.

Nathan’s advice for individuals: “If you’re gonna scope the maximum possible impact with minimum compute, get very narrow and learn where the models are going. Build tools that test where models in eight months are gonna be struggling.”

Translation: Don’t try to predict everything. Pick your domain, understand AI’s current edge, position yourself just beyond where that edge is moving. When the wave hits, you’re ready.

The Real Risk Isn’t AGI Takeover

Here’s what the builders don’t spend much time on: paperclip maximizers, rogue superintelligence, AI uprising scenarios.

What they do worry about: Economic dislocation from uneven automation. Job loss concentrated in specific sectors. 996 work culture burning people out. The “middle 60%” getting crushed by forces they can’t control.

Sebastian: “I think the real cool thing here is that we have the foundation models that we can specialize. I think that’s the breakthrough at some point.”

The breakthrough isn’t AGI consciousness. It’s capability acceleration hitting the real economy in ways we’re not prepared for—socially, politically, or individually.

That’s the threat the builders see. And that’s the threat we should prepare for.

But there is one more thing the builders don't talk about — perhaps because it terrifies them. Michael Pollan's new book A World Appears maps the problem that the AI industry claims to be solving: consciousness. His conclusion is devastating. "Just about any place you push on it, the computer-as-brain metaphor breaks down." A single cortical neuron can do everything an entire deep artificial neural network can. There are 106 competing hypotheses of consciousness — "a pretty good indication that the field is flailing." The AI industry rejects this not because it's anti-scientific, but because it's anti-business. Workers who are irreplaceable are expensive. The oligarchic logic requires that humans be reducible to algorithms. Pollan demonstrates they aren't. That's not sentimentality. That's neuroscience.

Epilogue: Convergence + Complication = Strategy

Parts 14 and 15 documented the CEO convergence and its implications. This part adds essential nuance without undermining urgency.

The CEOs are right: capabilities are real, transformation is coming, timeline compressed.

The builders complicate it: path is jagged, uncertainty is genuine, some capabilities arrive fast while others stall.

Both perspectives matter.

If you only listen to CEOs, you might panic or position incorrectly—preparing for uniform transformation when reality is uneven.

If you only listen to builders’ caution, you might delay too long—missing the wave when it arrives in your specific domain.

The synthesis: Maintain urgency about transformation while building flexibility into your strategy. Prepare for fast change in domains AI conquers (code generation, content creation, data analysis) while recognizing other areas (tool integration, distributed systems, complex coordination) may remain surprisingly human-dependent.

Monitor the edge. As Nathan says: “You use it until it breaks, until you have a problem, and then you change the LLM.”

That’s the mindset: stay alert to what works, what fails, and where the capabilities are moving. When AI conquers your specific domain, you’re already positioned for what comes next.

The Asimov Gap is real. The convergence proved it.

The path forward is jagged. The builders showed it.

The choice remains ours.

Stay urgent. Stay flexible. Stay human.
