Formosa: The Beautiful Problem

Before the Wound

Part II: In which everyone claims the island and no one asks the Taiwanese

May 1, 2026

Fort Zeelandia, the Dutch East India Company’s headquarters on the island of Formosa (Taiwan), depicted in a seventeenth-century Dutch illustration by Johannes Vingboons (c. 1635). The fort stood on a sandbar near present-day Tainan, serving as the hub of Dutch trade operations between China, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Source: Nationaal Archief, The Hague

The Portuguese sailors who spotted the island in 1544 did not stop. They were on their way somewhere else, as people passing through Taiwan’s history always seem to be. But they looked at the mountains rising green and impossible from the sea and they called out, to no one in particular, Ilha Formosa — Beautiful Island — and sailed on. They left the name. The island kept it. The pattern of outsiders passing through and leaving their marks without particularly consulting the people already there had begun.

It has not stopped since.

This is the essay that goes back further than the wound Cheng Li-wun invoked in Nanjing — further than 1895, further than Japan, further than the Qing dynasty’s catastrophic defeat. It goes back to the seventeenth century, when the question of who owned Taiwan was first seriously contested. The answer, then as now, was complicated by the fact that everyone had a claim and no one had a consensus and the island itself had been home to Austronesian indigenous peoples for at least six thousand years before anyone arrived with a flag and an opinion about sovereignty.

The Siraya, the Taokas, the Ketagalan, and the other Formosan peoples — the island’s actual first residents, whose ancestors sailed from somewhere in the vicinity of modern-day China and the Philippines in the deep prehistoric past — do not appear in any of the contemporary arguments about Taiwan’s status. Beijing does not invoke them. Taipei’s independence movement does not prominently centre them. The United States certainly does not mention them. This is itself a form of information. When everyone arguing about an island agrees to forget who was there first, you are watching a contest between colonialisms rather than a principled argument about self-determination.

Keep that in mind as we proceed.

Group of Pepohoan (Plains Indigenous) men in traditional dress with hunting gear and dogs — perfect community/group scene emphasizing pre-colonial life.

The Dutch arrived in 1624. They did not exactly choose Taiwan freely. They had been attempting for years to establish a trading base near China — they wanted access to Chinese silk, porcelain, and tea for the trade routes they were building across Asia — and the Chinese were not enthusiastic about letting a European company plant itself on their coast. After a series of confrontations and negotiations with Ming dynasty officials, a governor of Fujian Province made the Dutch an offer they could not reasonably refuse: leave the Pescadores Islands, which China considered its own waters, and move instead to the island the Chinese called Daiwan, which they regarded as peripheral and somewhat barbarian. The Dutch could trade there without interference.

The Dutch accepted. They built Fort Zeelandia on a sandbar off the southwest coast — present-day Tainan — and set about making themselves useful to the commercial networks of the western Pacific. They were not there for Taiwan’s sake. They were there because Taiwan was conveniently located between the trade routes to Japan, China, and the rest of Southeast Asia. The island was, from the beginning, valued for its position rather than its intrinsic qualities — a pattern that has defined its geopolitical existence ever since.

The Dutch East India Company was not a gentle landlord. It taxed the indigenous Formosans, forced them to provide labour and deer pelts, suppressed their traditional religious practices, converted many of them to Dutch Reformed Protestantism, and established schools where children learned to read in their own languages using a romanised script invented by Dutch missionaries. Resistance from indigenous communities was met with military force. There were also Chinese settlers arriving in growing numbers from Fujian province — traders, fishermen, farmers, people fleeing the chaos on the mainland as the Ming dynasty entered its terminal collapse — and the Dutch taxed them too, and occasionally massacred them when they revolted, which they did more than once. By 1661, there were as many as fifty thousand Chinese settlers on Taiwan, dwarfing the Dutch garrison of a few hundred soldiers.

The Spanish, meanwhile, had planted themselves in the island’s north in 1626, alarmed that the Dutch presence might threaten their Philippine colony’s trade routes. They stayed sixteen years before the Dutch drove them out in 1642, leaving behind some missionaries, some fortifications, and the early sketch of Taiwan’s north-south cultural division that persists, in softened form, to the present day.

None of this activity — Dutch, Spanish, the growing Chinese settler population — involved asking the Formosans who had been there for millennia. Their lands were cleared for sugar and rice cultivation. Their communities were restructured around colonial administrative needs. Their children were educated in languages not their own. The formula was familiar to every colonised people on earth, and the outcome — demographic and cultural marginalisation so thorough that the original inhabitants became a minority in their own homeland — was equally familiar.

Born in Japan to a Chinese pirate father and a Japanese mother, he remains one of the most contested figures in East Asian history — claimed simultaneously by Chinese nationalists, Taiwanese independence advocates, and Japanese cultural historians. Koxinga arrived in April 1661 with four hundred ships and twenty-five thousand soldiers, and the world of Dutch Formosa ended in nine months.

Zheng Chenggong — Koxinga is the Fujianese pronunciation of his honorific, Guóxìngyé, Lord of the Imperial Surname — was one of history’s more extraordinary figures, and one of the more convenient ones for competing nationalisms to claim. Born in 1624 in Hirado, Japan, to a Chinese pirate-merchant father and a Japanese mother, he was half Japanese by birth and wholly Chinese by ambition, having been educated at the Imperial Academy in Nanjing and bestowed the honour of the Ming imperial surname as a mark of his loyalty to the dying dynasty. When the Ming fell to the Manchu-led Qing in 1644, Koxinga refused to accept it. He fought a decades-long resistance campaign from his coastal bases in Fujian, harrying the Qing and keeping the Ming flame alive through sheer ferocity of will.

By 1661, he needed a new base. The mainland campaign was failing. He looked south across the Taiwan Strait and decided that the island the Dutch were occupying would serve. His justification, to the extent he articulated one, was that the island had been settled by Chinese people and therefore rightly belonged to Chinese civilization — a claim that somewhat glossed over the six thousand years of Formosan habitation that preceded any Chinese presence, but was no less sincere for that.

The Dutch, who were by the standards of the day among the better military technologists on earth, held out for nine months inside Fort Zeelandia. They were outnumbered roughly forty to one. The Dutch governor Frederick Coyett — a proud, capable man who had been failed by his superiors in Batavia and by the fleet they eventually sent that was too small and too late — surrendered in February 1662. The Dutch were expelled. Coyett returned to Batavia, was tried for treason, narrowly avoided execution, and spent years in exile before writing a furious memoir titled, with magnificent restraint, The Neglected Formosa. It became a bestseller.

Koxinga died of fever in June 1662, only months after his victory, at the age of thirty-seven. He had briefly planned to invade the Philippines and expel the Spanish from Manila; that plan died with him. His son and successors held Taiwan as the Kingdom of Tungning for another twenty years, maintaining it as an anti-Qing redoubt and bringing waves of Ming loyalist settlers from the mainland who further transformed the island’s demographic character.

In 1683, the Qing finally sent a fleet under Admiral Shi Lang — who had once served under Koxinga’s father, fallen out with the family, and spent twenty years waiting for his revenge — and defeated the Zheng forces in the Battle of Penghu. Taiwan became, for the first time, formally incorporated into the Chinese empire as part of Fujian Province. The Qing debated whether it was worth keeping. Some officials recommended abandoning it as peripheral and ungovernable. Others, notably Shi Lang himself, argued that if China did not hold it, someone else would. The strategic argument won. Taiwan stayed.

Here, then, is the first lesson of Taiwan’s history, delivered by the seventeenth century with considerable clarity: this island has never had an uncontested status. Not once. The Formosans did not consent to the Dutch. The Dutch did not consent to Koxinga. Koxinga’s heirs did not consent to the Qing. The Qing debated whether the island was worth the trouble and concluded, narrowly, that geopolitics made the decision for them. Every power that has ever held Taiwan has held it over someone else’s objection, and every power that has ever lost Taiwan has lost it to force.

Everyone who mapped Formosa was also claiming it. The Island Formosa and the Pescadores, by Dutch cartographer Johannes Vingboons, c. 1640.

When Beijing argues today that Taiwan has been part of China for centuries, it is citing the Qing period as its evidence — specifically the 212 years from 1683 to 1895 during which the island was administered as part of Fujian Province and then, after 1885, as a separate province of its own. The argument has some historical weight. The Qing did govern Taiwan. Han Chinese settlers did come to dominate the island’s population, pushing the Formosan indigenous peoples into the mountains where their descendants largely remain. Cultural, linguistic, and familial ties between Taiwan and the mainland coast of Fujian became deep and genuine over those two centuries.

But the Qing were Manchu, not Han — they were themselves conquerors of China, the very dynasty whose overthrow Koxinga had devoted his life to preventing. If the Qing’s rule of Taiwan establishes Beijing’s claim, it does so only by a theory of political succession in which the People’s Republic inherits the territorial claims of every dynasty that ever held Chinese territory, however briefly, however contested. That is a theory with imperial implications that stretch rather beyond Taiwan.

When the independence movement argues that Taiwan has a distinct identity that predates the PRC and owes nothing to it, they are citing the Formosan peoples, the Dutch colonial experience, the particular Fujianese settler culture that evolved differently from the mainland over centuries of geographic separation, and the fifty years of Japanese colonialism that — whatever its brutalities — did produce a Taiwan that was in many respects more modernised and more institutionally distinct from the mainland by 1945 than it had been in 1895. This argument also has weight.

Both are doing what historical arguments always do when deployed in service of political claims: selecting the evidence that serves the conclusion, declining to dwell on the evidence that complicates it. The seventeenth century is obliging enough to supply ammunition for everyone. This is part of what makes it so useful to so many people, and so dangerous as a guide to what should happen next.

There is one more thing the seventeenth century teaches, which is perhaps the most important thing for understanding the present moment.

Taiwan’s fate has always been determined by the relative maritime power of the parties contesting it. The Dutch came because they had ships that could project power across the Pacific. Koxinga expelled them because he assembled a fleet large enough to overwhelm them at close quarters. The Qing consolidated control because they built a navy capable of defeating the Zheng remnant. The Japanese would take the island in 1895 because they had defeated China at sea in the Sino-Japanese War. The Americans would become Taiwan’s de facto guarantor after 1949 because they had the Pacific Fleet.

The question of who controls the seas around Taiwan is not a recent development in this story. It is the whole story. It was the story in 1624 when the Dutch chose the island because it sat astride the trade routes between China and Japan. It was the story in 1662 when Koxinga brought four hundred ships across the strait. It is the story now, when the People’s Liberation Army Navy has become the largest fleet in the world by number of hulls, when the United States is debating how much of its naval power to concentrate in the western Pacific, and when a 2026 National Defense Strategy outlines a “denial defence along the First Island Chain” without once mentioning the island at the chain’s centre.

The names change. The logic does not.

The Dutch looked at a green island rising from the sea and saw a trading post. Koxinga looked at it and saw a base from which to reclaim a lost dynasty. The Qing looked at it and saw a strategic liability they could not afford to abandon. The Japanese would look at it and see a colony. The Americans look at it and see a semiconductor supply chain and an unsinkable aircraft carrier. Beijing looks at it and sees unfinished business.

Nobody in four hundred years of this has looked at Taiwan and primarily seen the people who live there.

Ilha Formosa. Beautiful Island. It has been beautiful enough that everyone wants it, and the beauty of the wanting has always obscured the cost of the being wanted.
