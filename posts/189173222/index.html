UNITED STATES OF AMERICA — Exercise a High Degree of Caution

This advisory was updated following a 108-minute national broadcast on the evening of February 24, 2026.

OVERVIEW:

Having monitored last night’s American programming from the relative safety of a Canadian living room, Global Affairs Canada wishes to update its travel advisory for the United States.

Canada maintains a close and valued relationship with its southern neighbour. We share the world’s longest undefended border, deep economic ties, and a mutual appreciation for hockey — as demonstrated by the president’s generous 22-minute tribute to the U.S. men’s Olympic team, which we note with the quiet dignity of a nation that has won gold nine times.

However, Canadian travellers should be aware of the following conditions:

BORDER AND ENTRY:

Canadian travellers are advised that the U.S. Department of Homeland Security is currently in a partial government shutdown. Global Entry processing has been suspended. TSA agents are working without pay. The Coast Guard cannot guarantee search and rescue operations at full capacity.

The shutdown was precipitated by the fatal shooting of two American citizens by federal immigration agents, a detail the president’s address did not mention. The legislative impasse is nominally about immigration but is actually about whether federal agents should be required to identify themselves after shooting people. Canada considers this a settled question. America, apparently, does not.

Canadians are reminded that federal agents conducting immigration enforcement may not be wearing identification and may be masked. If you are stopped by an unidentified armed individual near the border, Global Affairs Canada recommends remaining calm, speaking politely, and reflecting on the fact that you chose this vacation destination voluntarily.

Travel by water in the Caribbean and Pacific regions is strongly discouraged. The U.S. military has conducted at least 35 strikes on boats since September 2025, resulting in at least 115 deaths. The United Kingdom has suspended intelligence-sharing related to these operations. Canada is reviewing its own protocols. The president, in his address, joked about “damaging the fishing industry.” Canadians should be aware that this was considered humorous by the United States Congress.

ECONOMY AND COMMERCE:

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled on February 20, 2026, that the president’s tariff regime was illegal. Approximately $160 billion in tariffs were collected unlawfully. Canadian businesses should be aware that replacement tariffs under Section 122 have been imposed but expire in 150 days and face their own legal challenges. The president’s own lawyers argued during the Supreme Court case that Section 122 was inadequate for this purpose. They have now been overruled by their own client.

Tariff rates on Canadian goods may change without notice, possibly mid-sentence. Canadian exporters are advised to price their goods in a range rather than a fixed amount, as one might when selling to a country whose trade policy is determined by the emotional state of a single individual on any given morning.

Canadian travellers are advised that the U.S. president claims gasoline costs below $2.30 per gallon in most states. This is not supported by AAA data, which shows a national average of $2.95. A member of the president’s own audience in Iowa corrected him in real time, shouting “$2.63!” — which was also wrong, but closer. Canadians are advised to budget based on AAA data rather than presidential enthusiasm.

The president also claimed $18 trillion in investment commitments. His own website says $9.7 trillion. Canadians engaged in business negotiations with American counterparts should be aware that the concept of “rounding” may be interpreted more liberally than expected.

HEALTH AND SAFETY:

The United States has significantly reduced its capacity for disease surveillance and response following the departure of 317,000 federal employees, many from health-related agencies. The elimination of USAID — while primarily affecting overseas operations — reflects a broader reduction in public health infrastructure that may impact domestic services.

The president’s address celebrated removing two million Americans from food assistance programs during a cost-of-living crisis. Canadians accustomed to a social safety net should calibrate expectations accordingly. The president characterized this as an achievement. The two million Americans were not available for comment as they were scrounging in back alleys.

Canadians with pre-existing medical conditions should note that the nominee for U.S. Surgeon General does not hold an active medical license, did not complete her residency, and has declined to tell the Senate whether she would encourage mothers to vaccinate their children, describing the science on vaccines and autism as “never settled.” Canadians are reminded that our Surgeon General holds a medical degree and considers vaccination settled science. We understand this is a controversial position south of the border.

The president announced healthcare reforms during his address with the energy of a man unveiling a new product line. Details were scarce. Canadians requiring medical attention during their visit should carry comprehensive travel insurance and the phone number of the nearest Canadian consulate, which — unlike certain American agencies — remains fully staffed, fully funded, and medically literate.

LEGAL ENVIRONMENT:

The American legal environment is currently in a state of flux that Global Affairs Canada finds challenging to summarize in advisory format.

The president’s signature economic policy was declared unconstitutional four days before his national address. He described this as “an unfortunate ruling” and announced he would pursue alternative legal mechanisms — the same mechanisms his own legal team had argued were insufficient during the case. Canadians trained in law should note that the American legal concept of “precedent” is undergoing what might charitably be called a “reinterpretation.”

The Department of Justice is currently led by an Attorney General who, during congressional testimony, was asked to turn around and acknowledge Epstein survivors sitting directly behind her. She declined. Canadians appearing before American legal proceedings should not expect the same standard of witness acknowledgment they may be accustomed to at home.

Federal agents conducting domestic operations may or may not be required to identify themselves, depending on the outcome of the shutdown negotiations referenced above. Canadians are advised that the phrase “I’d like to see some identification” may be interpreted differently depending on which side of the interaction you are on.

The FCC — the body responsible for regulating American broadcast media — has recently expanded its mandate to include threatening television networks whose late-night comedians make jokes the president dislikes. Canadians employed in media or entertainment should be aware that the First Amendment, while technically still in effect, is undergoing stress-testing not seen since the Nixon administration. The CRTC, Canada’s equivalent body, has to our knowledge never threatened to revoke a broadcast license over a comedian’s monologue. We acknowledge this may make us seem unsophisticated.

POLITICAL CONDITIONS:

The United States is currently governed by a president whose signature economic policy was struck down by his own Supreme Court appointees four days ago, whose party controls both chambers of Congress yet cannot fund its own Department of Homeland Security, whose most prominent government efficiency initiative has been quietly shelved, and who delivered a 108-minute national address without mentioning any of these things.

The president’s relationship with the American media continues to evolve in ways relevant to travellers’ understanding of the information environment. In September 2025, the administration pressured ABC to suspend late-night host Jimmy Kimmel over jokes about the president. The FCC chairman — a Trump appointee who authored the agency’s chapter of Project 2025 — threatened the network’s broadcast license. Kimmel was off the air for six days before public backlash forced his reinstatement. His contract was subsequently extended through 2027. Last night, while the president delivered his address, Kimmel was across town delivering his own — sharper, more accurate, and considerably shorter. The administration has not yet responded, though historically, a Truth Social post follows within hours.

The Democratic Party’s response to all of this consisted of sitting very quietly for 108 minutes and then sending the Governor of Virginia to deliver a ten-minute rebuttal about grocery prices. Canada’s Official Opposition, for all its faults, would have heckled.

Canadian travellers should understand that the American political environment is, at present, unpredictable. Policy may change via social media post. Tariffs may be imposed, struck down, and reimposed within the same news cycle. Federal agencies may or may not be operational depending on the day of the week. Late-night comedians may or may not be on the air depending on whether the FCC chairman considers their jokes offensive.

This is a new variable in American governance that previous travel advisories did not need to account for.

MEDIA AND INFORMATION:

Canadians visiting the United States should be aware that the American information environment has become difficult to navigate.

The president’s 108-minute address contained claims that contradicted his own administration’s published data (investment figures), the American Automobile Association (gasoline prices), the Bureau of Labor Statistics (inflation methodology), the Tax Foundation (tax cut rankings), and the Supreme Court of the United States (the legality of his tariffs). These contradictions were delivered with confidence to a chamber where approximately half the audience applauded each one.

Canadians seeking reliable information about American policy are advised that the most accurate summary of the State of the Union was delivered not by the president, not by the official opposition response, and not by any major news network’s analysis desk, but by a late-night comedian the administration tried to have fired five months ago. Jimmy Kimmel’s “real State of the Union” ran approximately sixty seconds and covered censorship, extrajudicial killings, gutted cancer research, pedophile protection, billionaire enrichment, and a Bible made in China selling for $99.99. The president’s version ran 108 minutes and did not cover most of these things.

Canadians may draw their own conclusions about efficiency.

Canadian travellers should also be aware that American news coverage may be influenced by factors not traditionally associated with journalism. Television networks whose parent companies have business before the federal government have, in at least one documented case, suspended programming critical of the president. The president has publicly celebrated such actions and encouraged more of them. This is relevant to Canadian travellers who rely on American media for local news, weather, and the status of their connecting flights — all of which may now be subject to editorial considerations that have nothing to do with meteorology.

RELIGIOUS AND CULTURAL TOURISM:

Canadians visiting American houses of worship should be aware that the president invoked the name of Jesus Christ during his address while simultaneously presiding over policies that have cut funding for children’s cancer research, removed food assistance from two million Americans, and resulted in the deaths of approximately 600,000 people through the elimination of foreign aid.

The president also sells a “God Bless the USA Bible” bearing his name, manufactured in China, available for $99.99. This is not satire. This is commerce.

Canadians of all faiths and none are advised that American political culture currently features a significant overlap between religious invocation and commercial enterprise that may be unfamiliar to visitors from countries where these categories are considered distinct.

ENTERTAINMENT AND SPORTING EVENTS:

Canadians attending American political events should be aware that the State of the Union address is now approximately 108 minutes long. This is four times the length of a standard Canadian Throne Speech, roughly equivalent to the runtime of Titanic, and longer than most NHL regulation games — though with fewer goals and more icing. Canadians should prepare accordingly: bring water, wear comfortable shoes, and consider the possibility that the address may still be in progress when you need to leave for your flight.

Standing ovations are mandatory for the president’s party and occur approximately every 90 seconds. They are performed with decreasing enthusiasm as the evening progresses, eventually resembling the movements of a congregation at a church service that has gone long — everyone knows when to stand, no one remembers why, and sitting down feels like a confession.

Audience participation dares are a new feature of the format. The president may ask members of Congress to stand if they agree with a statement. The statement will be phrased in a way that makes sitting appear treasonous and standing appear endorsement. There is no correct response. Canadians in the gallery should remain seated, smile politely, and pretend to be from New Zealand.

Canadian hockey fans should be prepared for extended tributes to American hockey achievements. The president devoted 22 minutes of his address to the U.S. men’s Olympic team and awarded the goaltender a Presidential Medal of Freedom — an honor previously reserved for figures such as Nelson Mandela and Mother Teresa. We recommend quiet pride, polite applause, and the inner knowledge that Canada has won gold nine times and did not need 22 minutes of a constitutional address to say so. There is no need to say this aloud. They know.

The Medal of Freedom may be awarded during the address to individuals whose lifetime achievement occurred earlier that week. Canadians should applaud respectfully and refrain from noting that the Order of Canada typically requires more than a strong regular season.

The U.S. women’s Olympic hockey team declined the president’s invitation to attend the address. They subsequently received an offer from Flavor Flav. The president responded by stating the women’s team would visit the White House “whether they like it or not.” Canadians are advised that declining presidential invitations in the current American environment may result in public coercion via national broadcast. Plan social engagements accordingly.

WHAT TO PACK:

Global Affairs Canada recommends the following items for Canadian travellers visiting the United States:

  • Valid passport (NEXUS cards are currently experiencing processing delays due to the shutdown)

  • Comprehensive travel insurance including medical, legal, and tariff-adjustment coverage

  • Cash in U.S. dollars — do not rely on the president’s assessment of purchasing power

  • A VPN — for accessing Canadian news sources that have not been subject to FCC review

  • Patience — American customs processing may be slower than usual, as agents are working without pay and morale is, by all accounts, suboptimal

  • A printed copy of your itinerary — in the event that policy changes during your flight render your original plans moot

  • Snacks — in the event you are invited to a State of the Union address, the vending machines in the Capitol are limited and the runtime is not

  • A sense of humour — though Canadians should be aware that the boundaries of acceptable humour in the United States are currently being adjudicated by the FCC on a case-by-case basis. When in doubt, joke about Canada. This remains safe. Probably.

EMERGENCY SERVICES AND CONTACTS:

In the event of an emergency, Canadian travellers should be aware that the Coast Guard is operating at reduced capacity due to the government shutdown. TSA agents are working without pay, which historically correlates with reduced attention to detail. FEMA’s readiness status is unclear following significant staff departures. The National Weather Service remains operational but has experienced staffing reductions.

If you require consular assistance, the nearest Canadian embassy or consulate remains fully staffed, fully funded, and fully operational. This is not a boast. It is simply the baseline expectation for government services in a functioning democracy, and we mention it only because the baseline has apparently shifted in our southern neighbour.

Emergency contacts:

  • Canadian Embassy, Washington, D.C.: +1 (202) 682-1740 — fully staffed, fully funded, open during government shutdowns

  • Nearest Canadian Consulate — locations in New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Atlanta, Boston, Dallas, Denver, Detroit, Miami, Minneapolis, San Francisco, and Seattle

  • Tim Hortons locations in border states — not officially designated as consular outposts, but recognized by Canadians worldwide as a place where you can sit quietly, drink a double-double, and remember what stability feels like. We do not officially endorse this characterization. We do not officially deny it either.

  • Jimmy Kimmel Live!, ABC Studios, Hollywood — not an official consulate, but currently providing the most accurate summary of American political conditions available on broadcast television. Weeknights at 11:35 PM ET. Subject to FCC approval.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS:

Q: Is it safe to travel to the United States? A: Canada continues to advise a high degree of caution. Millions of Canadians visit the United States every year without incident. However, Canadians should be aware that the definition of “incident” is expanding.

Q: Should I cancel my trip? A: That depends on your destination, your plans, and your tolerance for policy changes that may occur between the time you book your flight and the time you board it.

Q: Will my Canadian health insurance cover me in the United States? A: Canadian provincial health plans provide limited coverage abroad. Travellers are strongly advised to purchase supplemental insurance. Americans are also strongly advised to purchase supplemental insurance. The difference is that Canadians have a system to supplement.

Q: Are tariffs on Canadian goods still in effect? A: The original tariffs were struck down by the Supreme Court. Replacement tariffs have been imposed under a different legal authority. These expire in 150 days and face legal challenges. A definitive answer to your question may not be available before your question becomes irrelevant.

Q: Is the president’s claim that he ended eight wars accurate? A: We refer you to our companion publication, And the Envelope Please..., specifically the award for “Best Performance by a Number in a Role It Was Not Qualified For.”

Q: I saw the president say gasoline is below $2.30 a gallon. Should I budget for that? A: No.

A NOTE ON DURATION:

This travel advisory is approximately 3,000 words long. The president’s State of the Union address was approximately 9,000 words long. We have covered more ground in fewer words. We do not say this to boast — efficiency is simply a Canadian habit, like apologizing and winning hockey gold.

RECOMMENDATION:

Canada continues to value its relationship with the United States. We share a continent, an economy, and — until recently — a belief in the general utility of facts.

Canadian travellers are advised to exercise a high degree of caution, carry valid identification at all times, avoid Caribbean boating excursions, budget for tariff-adjusted prices that may change without warning, ensure vaccinations are current, verify that any federal agency you intend to interact with is currently funded and staffed, and remember that the legal status of the president’s economic policies may differ depending on which branch of government you ask and which day of the week you ask it.

We also remind Canadians that our border remains open, our healthcare system — while imperfect — covers everyone, our late-night comedians remain employed without FCC intervention, our Surgeon General believes in vaccines, our government is funded, our Supreme Court rulings are respected, and our State of the Union equivalent rarely exceeds 45 minutes.

Come home soon.
