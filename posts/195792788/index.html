The Tuesday Massacre

Part XXI: FFS Donald, Enough.

April 28, 2026

James Comey faces new indictment over this photograph of seashells

There are days when the architecture of authoritarian justice reveals itself not incrementally but all at once, in a single news cycle so compressed and so brazen that the only honest response is to stop and name what you are witnessing.

Tuesday, April 28, 2026 was one of those days.

By close of business, the Trump administration had simultaneously deployed the Department of Justice against a former FBI director over a photograph of seashells, and directed the Federal Communications Commission to initiate license proceedings against a broadcast network because its late-night host made a widow joke. Two targets. Two instruments of federal power. One afternoon.

The list was being worked.

The Seashells

Last May, former FBI Director James Comey posted a photo on social media of shells on a beach writing out the numbers “86 47.” When used as slang, the number 86 can refer to getting rid of or tossing something out. Trump is the 47th president. Comey captioned it simply: “Cool shell formation on my beach walk.” He deleted the post the same day. He wrote on social media that he assumed the shells represented “a political message” but “didn’t realize some folks associate those numbers with violence.” “It never occurred to me but I oppose violence of any kind so I took the post down,” he wrote.

That was a year ago. On Tuesday, the Trump Justice Department — now led by Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, who is Trump’s former personal defense lawyer — delivered its answer. A federal grand jury returned an indictment charging former FBI Director James Comey over the Instagram photo, in the Eastern District of North Carolina, where Comey has a beach house.

This is the second indictment of Comey. He was first indicted in September of last year on charges that he lied to and obstructed Congress related to 2020 testimony about whether he had authorized inside information about an investigation to be shared with a journalist. That case was dismissed after a federal judge ruled that the prosecutor who brought the indictment had been improperly appointed, having bypassed Senate confirmation.

The first case collapsed on a procedural flaw. So the administration found a new case. The new indictment follows the elevation of Todd Blanche to acting attorney general, after Trump fired Pam Bondi, in part due to frustration that she had not pursued his political opponents aggressively enough. Shortly after taking the role, Blanche said he believes President Trump has the “right” to order investigations into his political adversaries.

The legal prospects are not promising. Prosecutors will likely face a high legal bar to prove that the Instagram post constituted a “true threat,” which the Supreme Court in 2023 found required showing an individual understood their message would be perceived as threatening. With the phrase “86 47” increasingly adopted by protesters of the Trump administration, the case could carry sweeping implications for the First Amendment.

But legal merit is not the point. The point is the process. The indictment itself is the punishment — the legal fees, the public exposure, the years of proceedings before any judgment is rendered. Comey said it plainly after the first indictment was dismissed: “I know that Donald Trump will probably come after me again, and my attitude is going to be the same. I’m innocent. I am not afraid, and I believe in an independent federal judiciary — the gift from our founders that protects us from a would-be tyrant.”

The administration heard him. And tried again.

There is a coda to the Comey story that arrived the same afternoon, and it deserves its own paragraph.

Maurene Comey, James Comey’s daughter and a former federal prosecutor who had handled cases against Jeffrey Epstein, Ghislaine Maxwell, and Sean Combs — compiling more than 200 convictions across eleven criminal trials — was fired by the Trump administration in July 2025. Her lawsuit contended there was no plausible explanation for her dismissal other than her father’s identity or her perceived political beliefs. On Tuesday, Manhattan federal judge Jesse Furman ruled she may proceed with that lawsuit, finding her claim was outside the jurisdiction Congress intended for the Merit Systems Protection Board.

“No president can ignore the Constitution, the Bill of Rights and federal law and fire a career federal employee based solely on her last name,” her lawyers said.

The ruling came hours before the new Comey indictment was announced. On the same Tuesday that the Justice Department moved against the father over seashells, a federal judge handed the daughter a courtroom victory. The list runs in both directions.

The Widow Joke

Because this year’s White House Correspondents’ Dinner will not be hosted by a comedian, Jimmy Kimmel said he was delivering some of the jokes a comic might have done.Credit...ABC

On April 23, in a mock White House Correspondents’ Dinner segment, Jimmy Kimmel observed that Melania Trump had the glow of an “expectant widow.” It was, as Kimmel later explained, a joke about the age gap between a man approaching 80 and his younger wife — the kind of remark that has populated late-night television since the form existed.

The shooting led to the evacuation of the Trumps and Cabinet members in attendance, and the dinner was canceled. The joke, recorded two days before the attempt, became retrofitted with sinister intent by an administration with a genius for grievance inflation.

Trump and Melania both posted on social media demanding Kimmel’s termination. Melania Trump posted: “Kimmel’s hateful and violent rhetoric is intended to divide our country. His monologue about my family isn’t comedy — his words are corrosive and deepens the political sickness within America.”

Then came the instrument.

The FCC ordered Disney’s ABC to file license renewals for all of their licensed TV stations within 30 days — by May 28, 2026. The order is an extraordinary escalation by the Trump administration. Those ABC licenses were not slated for license review until 2028 at the earliest.

The official rationale is Disney’s DEI practices — an ongoing FCC investigation entirely unrelated to Kimmel. A source familiar with the matter told CNN that the FCC will claim the license review stems from an ongoing probe into Disney’s DEI practices, not the Kimmel controversy. No one in journalism, law, or the entertainment industry is deceived by this formulation. The FCC had not filed an early-renewal order in decades, according to a source familiar with the matter.

Brendan Carr, the chairman of the Federal Communications Commission, has repeatedly threatened to take action against broadcasters / NYT

The FCC’s enforcement powers are limited, which is why numerous analysts — and the FCC’s lone Democratic commissioner Anna Gomez — have suggested that the process is the intended punishment. Gomez was direct: “This is unprecedented, unlawful, and going nowhere. This political stunt won’t stick. Companies should challenge it head-on. The First Amendment is on their side.”

The Freedom of the Press Foundation was blunter still. “The First Amendment and the FCC’s mandate do not permit the agency to use broadcast licenses as weapons to punish broadcasters for constitutionally protected content they air,” said Seth Stern, chief of advocacy for the organization. “Brendan Carr was once a serious communications lawyer, and has repeatedly and correctly said that the FCC has no role in policing content, whether news reporting or comedians’ late-night jokes.” Stern added: “Carr’s decision to abandon his principles to kiss up to Trump to advance his career does not change the law that Carr knows full-well applies. The FCC is neither the journalism police nor the humor police. This is nothing but illegal jawboning intended to intimidate ABC into kissing the ring.”

Kimmel did not apologize. He doubled down. In his Monday night monologue, Kimmel said: “Trump is allowed to say whatever he wants to say, as are you and as am I and as are all of us, because under the First Amendment we have as Americans the right to free speech.”

The Hypocrisy That Has No Bottom

Here is what must be said plainly, because American media will largely decline to say it plainly.

The president who is deploying the DOJ against a man for photographing seashells, and the FCC against a network for airing a widow joke, is the same president who stood at a podium on January 6, 2021 and directed a mob toward the United States Capitol. People died that day. Officers were beaten. The democratic transfer of power was suspended for hours. An American vice president was hunted through the halls of his own legislature.

That president was never indicted. He was twice impeached, twice acquitted by a Republican Senate that chose party over country, and subsequently elected to a second term.

The asymmetry is not irony. It is not paradox. It is the architecture of authoritarian justice, operating exactly as designed: absolute impunity for the patriarch, maximum legal exposure for everyone who crosses him. James Comey photographed shells he found on a beach. Donald Trump incited a riot that killed people. One of them is facing his second federal indictment. The other is ordering it.

This is not whataboutism. It is the essential context without which Tuesday’s events cannot be understood.

What The List Looks Like In Operation

The Enemies List series has tracked the targets. Part XXI offers something different: a portrait of the list in active operation, on a single afternoon, with federal agencies functioning as enforcement arms.

The DOJ is the instrument for the old enemies — the FBI directors, the attorneys general, the prosecutors who once held the law above the president’s preferences. The FCC is the instrument for the cultural enemies — the networks, the comedians, the voices that reach millions of American homes each evening.

Public interest lawyer Andrew Jay Schwartzman told CNN: “This weapon certainly hasn’t been deployed against a major broadcaster in many decades.” That is true of the FCC action. It is equally true of the DOJ’s posture toward political opponents writ large. The weapons are old. Their deployment in this manner is new.

Comey told his followers after the first indictment was thrown out that he was not afraid. Disney aired Kimmel’s show Monday night without apology — the strongest defense of all, particularly because the show was briefly suspended last fall amid an earlier campaign of government pressure against ABC.

The list is long. Tuesday showed that the administration’s willingness to work it has no apparent limit.

The seashells case may collapse in court, as the first Comey indictment did. The FCC license proceedings may take years and resolve in Disney’s favor, as legal experts suggest they will. The legal standard for denying a license renewal is almost insurmountable, and a hearing and subsequent judicial review would take years, during which time the broadcaster can continue to operate as normal.

But in the interim, the message has been sent. The message is always the point.

You are on the list, or you are not. And if you are on it, the machinery will find you — in your beach house, on your late-night set, in the offices of your lawyers who will spend the next several years billing hours to a case the government knows it cannot win.

That is not justice. It is management. And on Tuesday, April 28, 2026, it was managed with uncommon efficiency.
