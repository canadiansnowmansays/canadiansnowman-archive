Now This is Fake News

Part1: How the Pentagon created actual state propaganda while claiming to fight "misinformation"

October 23, 2025

George Orwell gave us “War is Peace.” The Pentagon gave us the Department of War. Now they’re giving us the Ministry of Truth—where propaganda calls itself journalism and asking questions is a crime.

For years, “fake news” was the battle cry against mainstream media. Every critical story, every unflattering report, every piece of investigative journalism that made powerful people uncomfortable was dismissed with those two words. But last week, we witnessed something remarkable: the literal creation of fake news, dressed up as its opposite, welcomed through the Pentagon’s doors with open arms.

Nearly one week after virtually every major news organization—from the New York Times to Fox News—walked out of the Pentagon rather than sign restrictive new press rules, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced his “next generation” press corps. The roster reads like a greatest hits compilation of conspiracy theorists, defamation lawsuit defendants, and—this is not hyperbole—an outlet that received payments from Russian government propaganda operations.

Welcome to the new Pentagon press corps, where “real news” means pre-approved news, where “independent journalism” means asking softball questions, and where fighting “mainstream media lies” means replacing journalists with stenographers.

The Great Purge

The timeline is crucial to understanding just how unprecedented this moment is.

On September 18, 2025, Hegseth issued a new press policy requiring journalists to essentially seek Pentagon approval before reporting anything—even unclassified information. The policy threatened to treat journalists who asked Pentagon sources for unauthorized information as criminals engaged in “solicitation” of classified material. In an era where social media callouts for tips are standard reporting practice, the Pentagon declared that even posting “Does anyone know about X?” could be grounds for losing credentials.

Nearly every major outlet immediately recognized this as prior restraint—the very thing the Supreme Court rejected in the 1971 Pentagon Papers case. The Pentagon Press Association, alongside the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, tried negotiating. The Pentagon revised the policy on October 6, but the revisions changed nothing fundamental: reporters would still face “severe consequences” for seeking information the government hadn’t approved, and merely asking questions could be considered criminal solicitation.

On October 15, in a scene described as “like college move-out day,” dozens of reporters who had covered the Pentagon for decades—some for twenty years—packed their belongings into boxes and walked out together. Among them: The Washington Post, The New York Times, CNN, NBC, ABC, CBS, the Associated Press, Reuters, and the Wall Street Journal.

Also refusing to sign: Fox News. Let that sink in. Pete Hegseth’s former employer, President Trump’s favorite network, the outlet progressives love to hate—even they said no. So did Newsmax, the Washington Times, the Washington Examiner, and the Daily Caller. When outlets across the entire political spectrum, from MSNBC to Fox News, unanimously reject a policy, that’s not bias. That’s a constitutional crisis.

Only one outlet initially signed: One America News Network.

The Replacement Press Corps

A week later, Hegseth’s chief spokesperson Sean Parnell announced that over 60 journalists from “a broad spectrum of new media outlets” had signed on to replace them.

“New media outlets and independent journalists have created the formula to circumvent the lies of the mainstream media and get real news directly to the American people,” Parnell crowed on X. “Their reach and impact collectively are far more effective and balanced than the self-righteous media who chose to self-deport from the Pentagon.”

Let’s examine this “next generation” of Pentagon journalism.

Tim Pool’s Timcast is now credentialed to cover the U.S. military. Pool, a popular YouTuber and podcast host, was among several right-wing influencers who, according to a Justice Department indictment, received payments from Tenet Media—a company operated by Russian government-funded RT—to create politically divisive content designed to weaken the U.S. government. A literal Russian propaganda asset now has a front-row seat to Pentagon operations. One imagines Moscow is as surprised as anyone.

Pool himself seemed uncertain what to do with this access, posting that Timcast plans to attend briefings “when feasible” but “do not intend to maintain a significant presence at the Pentagon or engage in in-depth investigative reporting.” In other words: we’ll show up for the photo op, but don’t expect us to actually investigate anything.

LindellTV, Mike Lindell’s streaming service, is now part of the Pentagon press corps. Yes, that Mike Lindell—the MyPillow CEO who has spent years falsely claiming Trump won the 2020 election and who has become synonymous with election conspiracy theories. LindellTV proudly announced it would bring Pentagon coverage “without censorship, without spin.” Apparently, getting all your information pre-approved by the Pentagon doesn’t count as censorship in their definition.

Gateway Pundit received credentials despite recently settling a defamation lawsuit with Georgia election workers it falsely accused of rigging the 2020 election. The site, which filed for bankruptcy after facing multiple lawsuits over its content, has been described by Media Matters as run by “the dumbest man on the internet.” Its White House correspondent, Jordan Conradson, posted excitedly about joining the Pentagon press corps “and help restore honest journalism after agreeing to follow basic rules...something the legacy media refuses to do!”

The “basic rules” Conradson celebrates include not reporting information the Pentagon hasn’t approved—which is to say, not doing journalism at all.

Also credentialed: Human Events (edited by Jack Posobiec, who recently criticized reporters for the grave sin of yelling questions at Trump and Putin); the National Pulse (whose editor-in-chief smugly noted they won’t “divulge editorial decisions to external parties,” as if that’s comparable to The Washington Post’s editorial independence); Just the News (which is currently posting job ads seeking a Pentagon reporter because they don’t actually have one); and Turning Point USA’s Frontlines (which recently conducted a fawning interview where Pentagon press secretary Kingsley Wilson made false claims about the old policy).

The Numbers Don’t Lie

Let’s talk about “reach and impact,” since Parnell brought it up.

According to web traffic data from Similarweb, Gateway Pundit—one of the larger outlets in the new press corps—had 25 million website visits in August. RedState had fewer than 10 million. The New York Times, by comparison, had nearly 600 million visits.

These aren’t “new media” powerhouses disrupting the industry. They’re fringe outlets with a fraction of the audience of any major news organization. The claim that they’re “more effective and balanced” would be laughable if it weren’t so dangerous.

What Makes News “Fake”?

This is where we need to return to first principles. What actually makes news “fake”?

Fabricated stories? Yes. Intentional disinformation? Absolutely. Lack of editorial standards and fact-checking? Certainly. But above all, fake news is news that exists to serve power rather than scrutinize it. It’s information shaped by those in authority to maintain their authority. It’s a story told not to inform the public but to control what the public knows.

Real journalism, by contrast, has always required independence—the ability to ask questions those in power would prefer not to answer, to seek information they’d rather not share, to hold the powerful accountable even when it’s uncomfortable. This is why the First Amendment exists. Not to protect journalism that makes the government happy, but to protect journalism that makes the government squirm.

The Pentagon’s new press corps cannot, by definition, do real journalism. They have agreed not to seek unauthorized information. They have agreed not to ask questions that might lead sources to share non-approved details. They have agreed to treat the Pentagon’s preferred narratives as the boundaries of acceptable coverage.

They are, in the most literal sense, producing fake news—information designed to look like journalism while serving only to legitimize the government’s version of events.

But this isn’t just about one department’s press policy or a handful of fringe outlets gaining credentials. What’s happening at the Pentagon violates multiple established First Amendment principles—from prior restraint to viewpoint discrimination in public forums. It mirrors the exact playbook authoritarian regimes have used throughout history to replace independent journalism with state propaganda. And it’s part of a coordinated pattern across the Trump administration: the White House restricting access to critical outlets, the President suing newspapers over their coverage, and now the Pentagon simply replacing its entire press corps with outlets that won’t ask hard questions.

In Part 2, we’ll examine the constitutional crisis unfolding at the Pentagon, the historical parallels that should terrify us, the real-world consequences for military accountability, and why this broader assault on press freedom represents a fundamental threat to democracy itself.
