Thirty Seven Trillion

How Republicans and Democrats Undid America’s Postwar Promise

Epilogue: The Number Has Changed. Everything Else Has Gotten Worse

March 19, 2026

CanadianSnowmanSays March 2026

A brief note before we begin: this series is called The $37 Trillion Question. As you can see, the meme visual has been updated because as of this writing, the debt stands at $38.86 trillion. The series title, published just eight months ago, is already a historical artifact. By the time you read this sentence, it will be slightly higher. According to the Joint Economic Committee, the United States is adding $7.23 billion to the national debt every single day — $301 million per hour, $5 million per minute, $83,720 per second. At that rate, the debt will cross $39 trillion by approximately March 25, 2026. The series title, published just eight months ago, is already a historical artifact. That fact alone is the most concise summary of everything that has happened since Episode 10.

The Damage Assessment

When this series concluded in July 2025, the One Big Beautiful Bill had just passed and I characterized it as a generational act of fiscal irresponsibility. I estimated it would add $3 trillion to projected deficits. That estimate, it turns out, was conservative. The Congressional Budget Office has since concluded that debt-service costs under the OBBBA alone will total $551 billion over the 2025–2034 period, increasing the bill’s cumulative effect on the deficit to $3.0 trillion — with other analyses placing the figure as high as $3.4 trillion by 2034. When you borrow money to give tax cuts to people who don’t need them, you pay interest on that borrowing. The compounding math of fiscal recklessness is unforgiving.

CBO’s February 2026 baseline projects the federal budget deficit for fiscal year 2026 at $1.9 trillion, with federal debt rising to 120 percent of GDP by 2036. For context, the postwar peak — which this series cited as the historical high-water mark that progressive taxation helped reverse — was 106 percent of GDP in 1946. America is now on course to blow past its own wartime record, not because of a world war, but because of a tax cut for people who already own multiple homes.

By March 2026, the United States had already borrowed $1 trillion in just the first five months of fiscal year 2026, including $308 billion in February alone. Maya MacGuineas of the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget — not a progressive organization, not a Canadian, not someone prone to theatrical alarm — called it simply unsustainable. She is right.

The DOGE Farce: A Case Study in Manufactured Competence

The eight months since Episode 10 produced one of the more remarkable exercises in political theater in recent American memory. Elon Musk’s Department of Government Efficiency arrived promising to cut two trillion dollars from federal spending. It leaves having demonstrated, conclusively, that government efficiency cannot be achieved by people who don’t understand how government works.

The trajectory of DOGE’s credibility follows a predictable arc. After initially claiming $55 billion in savings, its own documentation supported only $16.5 billion. Media analysis then found that roughly half of that was a single data entry error — $8 billion recorded instead of $8 million. A Politico analysis ultimately found DOGE had cut only $1.4 billion in actual spending — and even that money couldn’t reduce the deficit because it would be returned to agencies that were legally obligated to spend it.

The crowning indignity: a DOGE employee, in a deposition, admitted the agency failed to reduce the federal deficit. One did not require an economics degree to predict this outcome. The federal budget is approximately $7.4 trillion. The portions that are actually discretionary — and therefore theoretically cuttable through executive action — represent a fraction of total spending. About 64 percent of government spending goes to Social Security, Medicare, health programs, income security, and veterans’ benefits. Add defense at 14 percent and interest on the debt at 13 percent, and the arithmetic of meaningful fiscal adjustment becomes immediately obvious: you cannot fix a multi-trillion dollar structural deficit by canceling DEI consulting contracts and terminating office leases.

Meanwhile, the costs of DOGE’s disruption accumulated in parallel with its phantom savings. The Partnership for Public Service, a nonpartisan nonprofit, estimated that the costs of firing, rehiring, and placing federal workers on paid leave totaled approximately $135 billion — a figure that, with bleak irony, nearly matched DOGE’s own inflated savings claims at the time. A Yale Budget Lab analysis found that the gutting of IRS staff would cost $8.5 billion in lost revenue in 2026 alone, as fewer auditors means fewer audits of the wealthy people most prone to creative accounting.

The DOGE experiment has produced a lesson worth preserving for future students of governance: performative efficiency is not fiscal responsibility. Firing bird flu experts and cancelling graduate research grants while adding $3.4 trillion to the debt through tax cuts is not deficit reduction. It is political stagecraft with a devastating fiscal epilogue.

The Threshold Nobody Wanted to Cross

Episode 10 warned that debt service costs were consuming over $800 billion annually. That figure has now been overtaken by events. Annual interest payments on US government debt are projected to surpass $1 trillion in 2026 — the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget calls this the new normal. To appreciate what this means: in 2020, interest payments stood at $345 billion. In six years, they have nearly tripled. CBO now projects these payments will exceed $2 trillion annually by 2036.

America is spending more servicing debt than it spends on Medicaid. More than it spends on all non-defense discretionary programs combined. More than it spends on children, infrastructure, scientific research, and diplomacy put together. Every dollar of interest is a dollar not spent on anything that might actually improve the country. It is the fiscal equivalent of paying the minimum balance on a credit card every month while continuing to charge extravagant purchases.

Former Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen said the quiet part plainly in early 2026, warning of what economists call fiscal dominance — the point at which a central bank is forced to finance deficits because the market won’t. “The preconditions for fiscal dominance are clearly strengthening,” she said, noting debt is on a steep upward trajectory toward 150 percent of GDP over the next three decades. This is not a fringe warning from the left. This is the former chair of the Federal Reserve and former Treasury Secretary using her post-government freedom to say what she could not say while in office.

The Mirror Nobody Wants to Look At

The fiscal crisis described in this series has an almost exact domestic parallel that rarely appears in the same paragraph — by accident or design. While the federal government added nearly $2 trillion to the national debt in a single fiscal year, American households were quietly replicating that logic on a personal scale, one grocery run and one minimum payment at a time.

Total household debt reached $18.8 trillion at the end of 2025, according to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York — with mortgage balances at $13.17 trillion, credit card balances at $1.28 trillion, auto loans at $1.67 trillion, and student loans at $1.66 trillion. These numbers don’t headline the way the national debt does, but they describe a population that is, in the aggregate, fiscally drowning while being told the economy is performing well.

The credit card figures are particularly diagnostic. Americans’ total credit card balance stands at $1.277 trillion as of Q4 2025 — the highest since the New York Fed began tracking in 1999. Since Q1 2021, when pandemic-era savings briefly suppressed balances, credit card debt has risen by $507 billion — a 66 percent increase in under five years. The pre-pandemic record, set in 2019, has been surpassed by $350 billion. Whatever the official economic narrative has been, the credit card ledger tells a different story about how ordinary Americans have actually been making ends meet.

The interest rate environment makes this worse in ways that compound quietly. Average APRs on cards accruing interest remain above 22 percent. At that rate, the average cardholder carrying a balance isn’t reducing principal — they’re servicing a perpetual debt machine. The structural parallel to the federal government, which is now spending more on interest than on most discretionary programs, is not coincidental. It reflects a society that has normalized debt as a substitute for income adequacy at every level, from household to sovereign.

The shift in what that debt is financing is perhaps the most revealing detail: credit cards are no longer primarily funding discretionary purchases. Rising food, housing, insurance, and utility costs — the non-negotiable expenses of simply existing — are now the primary driver of revolving balances for millions of households. Thirty-three percent of credit card debtors cite day-to-day expenses such as groceries, childcare, and utilities as their primary source of debt, up from 26 percent just three years ago. People are not charging vacations to Cancún. They are charging groceries in Ohio and electricity bills in Texas and hoping the minimum payment clears.

The New York Fed’s own researchers have described it plainly: “This is what the K-shaped economy looks like in the real world. There’s an affluent half of the population whose financial lives aren’t disrupted by momentary inconveniences.” The other half is running a personal deficit. When a government runs structural deficits because it refuses to tax the wealthy at appropriate rates, and working households run structural deficits because wages have not kept pace with the cost of living, the two phenomena are not unrelated. They are the same political choice, expressed at different scales.

The OBBBA, in this context, takes on an additional dimension of cruelty. Tax relief for top earners, delivered by a government borrowing at $7.23 billion per day, while the households represented by those senators carry $1.3 trillion in credit card debt at 22 percent interest. The arithmetic of who benefits and who pays runs in one direction, as it always has.

The Credit Rating Reckoning

In May 2025, Moody’s downgraded the United States credit rating, completing a trifecta that S&P began in 2011 and Fitch continued in 2023. All three major credit rating agencies now agree: the U.S. Treasury no longer deserves their top rating. The message is consistent — America’s ballooning debt is a serious problem and Washington has no credible plan to address it.

Markets absorbed the Moody’s downgrade with the same shrug they gave the previous two. This is partly because US Treasuries remain the global reserve asset by default, and partly because financial markets have a remarkable capacity for denial when the alternative is pricing in consequences. But the shrug is not the same as indifference. Each downgrade makes the next incremental increase in Treasury yields slightly more likely. And as Apollo’s chief economist observed, competition from an estimated $2.25 trillion in corporate bond issuance in 2026 — driven significantly by AI infrastructure spending — creates meaningful pressure on who will absorb additional Treasury supply. The bond market’s patience is not infinite. It is merely slow.

Even some unlikely voices have begun to acknowledge the obvious. Former Republican Senator Mitt Romney, not previously a champion of redistributive taxation, wrote in the New York Times at the end of 2025 that the combination of solutions to the nation’s fiscal problems will require the wealthiest Americans to contribute more. When Mitt Romney becomes the voice of progressive fiscal realism, the Overton window has moved — even if Congress hasn’t.

What the Numbers Predicted and What Happened

Episode 10 made a specific prediction worth examining in retrospect: that the OBBBA’s unpopular provisions would create political opportunities for progressive reform. That the case for change would strengthen as the evidence accumulated. That younger Americans bearing student debt, climate risk, and inherited fiscal liability would become a natural constituency for alternatives.

The evidence has accumulated. The political movement, as of March 2026, has not. Republicans who voted for the OBBBA have returned to their districts and found that tax cuts for high earners remain somewhat popular even among voters who don’t benefit from them, and that abstract deficit numbers remain less viscerally threatening than the cultural grievances that dominate their media ecosystem. The political window for reform is not yet open. The fiscal window is narrowing.

This is the uncomfortable update to Episode 10’s cautious optimism: the evidence for progressive alternatives has gotten stronger, but the political infrastructure to act on that evidence has not materially improved. The Democratic opportunity described in this series remains theoretical. The Republican failure is empirically confirmed. The gap between the two is where American fiscal policy currently resides.

The Noise and the Signal

There is a phenomenon familiar to anyone who has tried to cover complex, structural stories in an era of perpetual crisis: the important and the urgent are not the same thing, and the urgent almost always wins.

I have experienced this dynamic directly in my own work. The Epstein Files — a series documenting what the released documents actually reveal about power, complicity, and institutional failure — competes for reader attention against more immediately kinetic stories that are constantly producing new developments. The war story crowds out the accountability story. Not because readers don’t care about accountability, but because the human attention economy runs on adrenaline, and structural corruption doesn’t supply it the way missile strikes do.

The American fiscal crisis has suffered the same displacement at the level of mainstream media coverage. In the eight months since Episode 10, the story of the national debt approaching $39 trillion has shared the news cycle with a trade war, a constitutional confrontation between the executive branch and the judiciary, the unraveling of multiple alliances, two actual military conflicts demanding daily coverage, and the relentless metabolic output of an administration that seems to understand, intuitively, that volume is its own form of cover. When everything is a crisis, the slow-motion crisis — the one measured in basis points and decade-long projections — gets buried by design or by default.

This is not a new dynamic. The most consequential fiscal decisions in American history have almost always been made in low-attention windows, buried in reconciliation bills debated on holiday weekends, scored by agencies whose reports are published on Friday afternoons. The OBBBA passed in July 2025, a month when Washington correspondents are historically distracted and readers are historically elsewhere. The CBO’s February 2026 projection — $1.9 trillion deficit, debt at 120 percent of GDP by 2036, interest payments crossing $1 trillion this year — received a fraction of the coverage devoted to any single presidential social media post.

There is a feedback loop worth naming: the same administration that has generated sufficient volume of outrage to occupy the full bandwidth of political media has also, in the relative silence created by that saturation, added trillions to the debt with minimal sustained scrutiny. The chaos is not incidental to the fiscal recklessness. It is, whether by design or convenient accident, the environment in which fiscal recklessness flourishes most easily.

This series has tried to be the signal rather than the noise. The debt is $38.86 trillion and climbing. Interest payments will exceed $1 trillion this year and $2 trillion by 2036. Three in five Americans with credit card balances have been in debt for at least a year — up from 53 percent in 2024. These facts are less cinematically compelling than a missile strike or a courtroom confrontation. They are also more consequential to the daily lives of more people than almost any single event that dominated the front page this year.

The Epstein Files will still be there when the latest crisis quiets. The debt will still be there — larger — when the next manufactured emergency subsides. The question is whether the moment of collective attention required for a democratic society to act on structural problems will ever arrive, or whether the architecture of modern information environments has made that kind of sustained focus functionally impossible.

From Toronto, watching, I am not sure I know the answer. But I am certain that the question deserves more column inches than it gets.

The Revised Calculus of Hope

I ended this series with hope grounded in evidence. I am not prepared to abandon that framing, but I will update it with the honesty it deserves.

The evidence base for progressive fiscal reform is stronger than it was in July 2025. Every month of DOGE failure strengthens the case that you cannot cut your way to sustainability without touching mandatory spending and defence, and that touching mandatory spending and defence requires either courage or crisis. Every month of $1.9 trillion annual deficits strengthens the case that the OBBBA was the wrong medicine for the actual disease. The CBO now projects that the 2025 reconciliation act and administrative actions related to immigration increased projected deficits by $4.7 trillion and $0.5 trillion respectively over the next decade — partially offset, but not eliminated, by $3.0 trillion in tariff revenues that remain legally uncertain following Supreme Court scrutiny.

Over the past five years, the national debt has grown by $10.86 trillion. The accumulated weight of that number is beginning to press into public consciousness in ways that deficit projections alone never do. When interest on the debt exceeds $1 trillion — when that figure starts appearing in grocery-store-receipt journalism alongside milk prices and electricity bills — the abstraction starts to dissolve.

What this series has documented across ten episodes and this epilogue is a pattern that predates Trump, survived Clinton, accelerated under Bush, recovered marginally under Obama, and then accelerated catastrophically under the combined weight of pandemic spending and ideologically-driven tax reduction. The $37 trillion — now nearly $39 trillion — is the accumulated ledger of those choices. It is not destiny. It is not arithmetic. It is the predictable result of specific decisions made by specific people who decided that political convenience mattered more than intergenerational responsibility.

The tools for reversal remain the same as they were in July. Progressive taxation. Strategic public investment. Spending discipline applied to waste rather than to the programs that hold the social fabric together. International models — from Canada’s 53 percent combined top rate to the Nordic examples — continue to demonstrate that these choices are compatible with economic dynamism and democratic stability.

The path is still proven. The evidence is now overwhelming. The series title has become obsolete at an accelerating rate. That last fact is either the most compelling argument for urgency or the most damning evidence of failure, depending on whether you believe American democracy is still capable of voluntary course correction before the markets force an involuntary one.

From the outside, watching this unfold, I remain uncertain which it is. But I remain convinced that the question is still worth asking.
