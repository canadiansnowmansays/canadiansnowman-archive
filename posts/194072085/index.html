The Hypocrisy of Donald Trump and His Administration

The Gospel According to Trump

When the Self-Proclaimed Messiah Threatens to Wipe Out a Civilization

April 13, 2026

By Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

It is one week after Easter — the holiest day in the Christian calendar, shared this year in close proximity with Passover, the foundational Jewish celebration of liberation from bondage. Both traditions are rooted in the same bedrock moral truth: that the suffering of the innocent matters, that power must be constrained by conscience, and that no ruler — not Pharaoh, not Caesar — stands above divine judgment.

On the evening of April 12, 2026 — Orthodox Easter Sunday — Donald Trump posted an AI-generated image of himself on Truth Social. He said nothing. He needed to say nothing. The image spoke for him.

It depicted Trump in a white robe and red sash, his hand glowing with divine light as he healed a man in a hospital bed. Around him knelt a nurse, a soldier, a praying woman. In the background: bald eagles, military jets, the Statue of Liberty, the Lincoln Memorial, and soldiers ascending toward a radiant Heaven. The composition was unmistakable. It was Christ performing miracles. Except the face was Donald Trump’s.

He posted it less than an hour after publicly attacking Pope Leo XIV.

Pope Leo XIV, the first American-born pontiff, was on a flight to Algeria when reporters asked him about the post. He said he had “no fear of the Trump administration, or speaking out loudly about the message of the Gospel, which is what I believe I am here to do.” Asked directly about Trump’s Truth Social attack, Leo paused and said: “It’s ironic — the name of the site itself. Say no more.”

A Pope just destroyed a President in eleven words. Without raising his voice.

The Timing That Cannot Be Ignored

Before we examine what the image means, we must sit with what the timing means.

One week before this post, on Western Easter Sunday, Trump published a profanity-laced rant on Truth Social — one that contained no mention of Jesus, the resurrection, forgiveness, or grace. It attacked “Radical Left Lunatics,” condemned judges, and threatened further violence in Iran. He signed it: “Praise be to Allah. President DONALD J. TRUMP.”

Not a prayer. A taunt. Directed at the people his administration is bombing.

Religion Dispatches captured the theological obscenity precisely: Trump’s use of “Praise be to Allah” on Easter morning turned an Islamic prayer of sincere devotion into a weapon of mockery — the same gesture, the same logic, as the Roman soldiers placing “INRI” above the cross. They didn’t believe Jesus was the King of the Jews. That was the point. The sign was designed to humiliate, to degrade, to reduce a people’s deepest hope to a punchline.

Trump’s Easter message did the same thing to Muslim faith. And he did it on Easter morning, while the Christian world celebrated the resurrection of a man who said: “Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.”

Now, one week later, on Orthodox Easter, he posts himself as that same man.

The audacity is not incidental. It is the message.

Even MAGA Said No

What makes this image remarkable is not simply that critics condemned it. It is that the condemnation came from within his own movement.

Marjorie Taylor Greene — who spent years as one of Trump’s most devoted congressional defenders — wrote on X: “On Orthodox Easter, President Trump attacked the Pope because the Pope is rightly against Trump’s war in Iran and then he posted this picture of himself as if he is replacing Jesus.” She added: “It’s more than blasphemy. It’s an Antichrist spirit.”

Right-wing influencer Milo Yiannopoulos, no stranger to transgression himself, wrote: “We tolerated this kind of meme against our better judgment because he promised to save America and only when it was clear he didn’t actually think he was the Messiah.”

On Truth Social itself — among Trump’s most devoted supporters — users were equally appalled. “Equating yourself to Jesus? You’ll burn in hell,” one wrote. “If you call yourself a Christian and you’re not outraged by this, you really need to re-evaluate your faith,” wrote another.

When your own congregation recoils, something has broken.

The Pope He Attacked

Pope Leo XIV in Algiers on Monday. President Trump attacked him in a social media post Sunday night.Credit...Guglielmo Mangiapane/Reuters

The image appeared on Truth Social within an hour of Trump’s broadside against Pope Leo XIV — a 334-word attack published immediately after a 60 Minutes segment in which three senior American cardinals echoed the Pope’s criticism of Trump’s conduct of the Iran War.

Trump called Leo “weak on crime and terrible for foreign policy.” He claimed credit for Leo’s election, asserting the Church chose an American pope specifically to deal with Trump. He said he preferred the Pope’s brother Louis, a self-described “MAGA type,” adding: “I like his brother Louis much better than I like him.”

What had Leo done to earn this? He had said, plainly and without apology, that Trump’s threat to destroy “an entire civilization” in Iran was “truly unacceptable.” He had said: “Enough with the idolatry of self and money! Enough with the display of force!”

This is the Vicar of Christ, speaking from the Gospel. And the man who then posted himself as Christ called him weak.

The Beatitudes He Has Never Read

Let us go to the source material.

In the Sermon on the Mount — the foundational moral teaching of Christianity, delivered by the figure Trump depicted himself as — Jesus says:

“Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.” “Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God.” “Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth.”

The Iran War began on February 28, 2026, with US-Israeli strikes. Since then, Trump has threatened to destroy “an entire civilization.” He told a Fox News host on Easter Sunday morning that he was “fine with it.” His administration has threatened to blow up bridges and power plants — acts the Pope explicitly identified as violations of international law.

His Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — a man with the word “Kafir” (unbeliever, in the context of Islam as enemy) tattooed on his bicep — went further. There is a photograph that should be studied carefully: Hegseth standing at the Department of Defense podium, head bowed, hands gripping the lectern, eyes closed in apparent prayer. The seal of the Department of Defense is visible below him. It is, on its surface, an image of Christian humility — a powerful man brought low before God.

Pete Hegseth — who calls himself the Secretary of War, not Defense — prays at the Department of Defense podium in the name of Jesus Christ, blessing the US-Israeli campaign in Iran. The "Kafir" tattoo on his forearm, marking Muslims as enemies of the faith, is just out of frame. (Screenshot via social media/C-SPAN)

Except that Hegseth insists on calling himself the Secretary of War. The arm bowed in prayer carries a tattoo marking Muslims as enemies. And the prayer itself — delivered from the Pentagon, in the name of Jesus Christ, calling for battlefield victory — prompted Pope Leo’s most direct rebuke of the entire conflict: “Jesus does not listen to the prayers of those who wage war, but rejects them.”

The photograph is not an image of humility. It is an image of the precise thing Leo warned against: the Christian mission “distorted by a desire for domination, entirely foreign to the way of Jesus Christ.”

Two images define this moment in history. One man bowing his head at the Pentagon in Christ’s name, blessing a war. Another posting himself as Christ on Truth Social, healing the wounded. The Pope says Christ rejects the first. The Pope’s own faith tells us the second is something the Gospels have a specific name for.

In a homily before Easter, Leo stated that the Christian mission had been “distorted by a desire for domination, entirely foreign to the way of Jesus Christ.” He was not naming Trump. He didn’t need to. Everyone understood.

On Easter Sunday in St. Peter’s Square, before tens of thousands of the faithful, Leo called on the world to “abandon every desire for conflict, domination and power.” The same day, Trump signed off on a profanity-laced Truth Social post threatening Iran and closed it with “Praise be to Allah” — a taunt, not a prayer.

Three senior American cardinals appeared on 60 Minutes to voice their concern. Cardinal Robert McElroy said the administration had created “the possibility of war after war after war.” The Pope’s own ambassador, Cardinal Christophe Pierre, was summoned to the Pentagon in January after Leo’s state-of-the-world address — a dressing-down of the Holy See’s representative by the United States Department of War.

The Prince of Peace’s representative was called to the Department of War to be reprimanded for preaching peace. Vice President JD Vance — Catholic, and currently writing a memoir about his faith — was asked about the meeting. He initially could not identify who Cardinal Pierre was.

And then Trump posted himself as the Prince of Peace.

The Healer Who Cuts the Cure

The image shows Trump healing the sick. His hand glows. Light pours from his fingers. A nurse watches in reverence.

His administration has spent the past year dismantling the systems that actually heal the sick.

Medicaid cuts totalling hundreds of billions of dollars. The Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program — which keeps vulnerable Americans warm in winter — eliminated. SNAP food benefits weaponized as political leverage during the government shutdown (documented in earlier essays in this series). Mental health and addiction services stripped. Healthcare subsidies removed, sending premiums for working families soaring.

The man in the image heals with a touch. The man in the Oval Office signs the orders that take away the prescription.

There is a word for this in the tradition he is appropriating. It is not blasphemy. Blasphemy is the misuse of God’s name. This is something older and more dangerous: it is the performance of holiness in the service of its opposite. It is what the Gospels call — with unusual consistency — the one thing Jesus reserved his sharpest anger for.

He did not rage at tax collectors or prostitutes. He raged at those who performed righteousness for an audience while devouring widows’ houses.

“Woe to you, teachers of the law and Pharisees, you hypocrites!” — Matthew 23:13

A Pattern, Not a Moment

This essay is part of a series examining the distance between what this administration professes and what it practices. We have previously documented the financial hypocrisy — billions found for military pay and renovation of government ballrooms while food assistance was withheld from families. We documented the rhetorical hypocrisy — Trump’s own 2013 words about presidential responsibility inverted entirely by his conduct during the government shutdown. We documented the propaganda hypocrisy — a deliberate strategy of repetition over truth.

The image on Truth Social is not an aberration. It is the logical destination of a journey that began with the Bible held upside down outside St. John’s Church in June 2020, with tear gas clearing peaceful protesters from the path.

That was performance. This is escalation.

What Trump posted on Orthodox Easter 2026 is the clearest statement yet of a theological claim his movement has been making obliquely for years: that Trump is not merely a Christian leader, but a messianic figure, chosen and anointed, beyond the reach of the moral law he claims to embody.

The Pope said “enough with the idolatry of self.” Trump responded by posting an idol of himself.

A Pattern of Escalating Idolatry

Sunday’s image was not the first. It was the third.

In February 2025, Trump posted an AI image of himself wearing a crown on a magazine cover styled to resemble Time — except it was called Trump. He was likening himself to a king.

In February 2025, Trump posted an AI-generated image of himself wearing a crown on a magazine cover styled to resemble Time — retitled "Trump." It appeared days after he declared "Long live the king" on Truth Social following his elimination of New York City's congestion pricing. The White House Deputy Chief of Staff promoted it on official channels. No one resigned. (AI-generated image via Truth Social/@realDonaldTrump, February 2025)

In May 2025, days after the death of Pope Francis, Trump posted an AI image of himself dressed as the pontiff. As Catholics around the world mourned, he posted himself in the chair of St. Peter.

Days after attending Pope Francis's funeral in Rome — and while Catholic cardinals were preparing to enter a solemn conclave to elect his successor — Trump posted this AI-generated image of himself in the white cassock and papal mitre of the papacy. The White House reposted it on its official X account. When told Catholics were offended, Trump said: "Oh, they can't take a joke." Cardinal Timothy Dolan, his ally, said simply: "It wasn't good." (AI-generated image via Truth Social/@realDonaldTrump, May 2, 2025)

Now, on Orthodox Easter 2026, he has posted himself as Christ.

Each image is an escalation. Each one tests what his base will accept. The first drew criticism. The second drew outrage. The third — according to his own Truth Social followers — drew calls for him to take it down and warnings that he would “burn in hell.”

But he has not taken it down.

It is also worth noting: in the same post where he attacked Pope Leo, Trump claimed personal credit for Leo’s election. “If I wasn’t in the White House, Leo wouldn’t be in the Vatican,” he wrote. The man who posted himself as Christ also claims he appointed Christ’s representative on earth.

The Silence That Indicts

Where, in all of this, are the evangelical leaders who proclaimed Trump God’s chosen instrument?

Some voices did speak. Archbishop Paul S. Coakley, president of the U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops, said: “I am disheartened that the President chose to write such disparaging words about the Holy Father. Pope Leo is not his rival; nor is the Pope a politician. He is the Vicar of Christ who speaks from the truth of the Gospel and for the care of souls.”

American Jesuit priest and writer Father James Martin wrote: “I doubt Pope Leo XIV will lose any sleep over this, before he begins his pilgrimage to Africa tomorrow. But the rest of us should. Because it is unhinged, uncharitable and unchristian. Is there no bottom to this moral squalor?”

From within MAGA: Greene, Yiannopoulos, and appalled Truth Social users. From Catholic institutional leadership: Coakley and Martin. From the Pope himself: a quiet, devastating eleven-word reply about truth and irony.

But from the vast apparatus of American evangelical Christianity — the pastors who laid hands on Trump in the Oval Office, the Christian nationalist movement that has woven his presidency into its theology, the congressional members who open sessions with prayer and then vote to strip healthcare from the vulnerable — near silence.

And from JD Vance — Catholic, currently writing a memoir about his faith, the Vice President of the United States — nothing. He had already told us what we needed to know when he couldn’t identify the Pope’s own ambassador.

Conclusion: The Gospel He Is Writing

The Gospel According to Trump is not found in the Beatitudes. It is not found in the feeding of the five thousand, the healing of the lepers, the washing of feet, the commandment to love enemies, or the warning that it is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of heaven.

Pope Leo — who has seen all of this from Rome, from the air over Algeria, from the chair of St. Peter — put it without accusation and without drama: “I do not think that the message of the Gospel is meant to be abused in the way that some people are doing.”

Some people. We know who he means.

The Gospel According to Trump is found in an AI-generated image posted on Orthodox Easter, one week after a profanity-laced rant on Western Easter, days after attacking the Pope for opposing a war that threatens to annihilate a civilization, hours after claiming he personally put the Pope’s predecessor in the Vatican.

It is found in the gap between the glowing hand in the image and the pen that signs the Medicaid cuts.

It is found in the distance between “Blessed are the peacemakers” and the man who signs bombing orders and closes his Easter message with a taunt directed at the people being bombed.

It is found in the distance between “Blessed are the merciful” and a Defense Secretary who tattoos “unbeliever” on his arm and prays for battlefield victory in Christ’s name — a prayer, the Pope tells us, that Christ rejects.

The glass house was built a long time ago. The stones have been flying for years. What is new is that the man inside has finally posted a painting of himself as the one who said: “Let he who is without sin cast the first stone.”

He didn’t wait to see if anyone else was sinless. He picked up the stone himself.

Father Martin asked: “Is there no bottom to this moral squalor?”

On the evidence of Orthodox Easter 2026, we do not yet know the answer.
