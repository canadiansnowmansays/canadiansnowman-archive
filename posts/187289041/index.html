The Trump Accountability Project

From Housing Discrimination to Depicting Apes

Part 33: A 50-Year Pattern of Racism (1973-2026)

March 31, 2026

Bipartisan outrage erupts after President Donald Trump’s Truth Social account posts (and later deletes) a video superimposing former President Barack Obama and Michelle Obama’s faces onto apes, set to “The Lion Sleeps Tonight” (February 6, 2026). Source: Local News Live compilation of congressional and public reactions (2026).

On February 6, 2026, President Donald Trump shared a video on Truth Social depicting former President Barack Obama and former First Lady Michelle Obama as apes. The clip, set to “The Lion Sleeps Tonight,” showed their faces superimposed onto the bodies of primates. The video remained on Trump’s account for twelve hours before being deleted. The President refused to apologize, saying “No, I didn’t make a mistake.”

This was not an isolated incident. It was not even the beginning of a pattern. It was the latest manifestation of racist behavior spanning more than fifty years.

Housing Discrimination (1973)

Donald Trump’s first appearance in The New York Times was not about a successful real estate deal. It was about a federal lawsuit.

On October 15, 1973, the Civil Rights Division of the U.S. Department of Justice sued Trump Management Corporation—owned by Donald Trump and his father Fred Trump—for violating the Fair Housing Act. The complaint alleged systematic discrimination against Black people seeking to rent apartments in Trump buildings.

The evidence was damning. The DOJ had conducted testing where white and Black applicants with identical qualifications applied for apartments in Trump buildings. White applicants were told apartments were available. Black applicants were told nothing was available. Rental applications from Black applicants were marked with a “C” for “colored.” Black applicants were quoted higher rents or told that no apartments were available when apartments were, in fact, vacant.

Young Donald Trump (center left) and associates at a Trump Organization construction site in the 1970s, during the period when the DOJ sued Trump Management for systematic racial discrimination in rental practices. NPR / historical photo from the era of the 1973 federal lawsuit.

The discrimination was not the work of a rogue employee. It was systematic. It was policy. Multiple Trump properties across New York City engaged in the same practices. The pattern was clear and deliberate.

Trump’s response established a pattern that would persist for fifty years: deny everything, attack the accusers, never apologize.

Trump called the charges “absolutely ridiculous.” He claimed the government was trying to force him to rent to “welfare recipients.” His lawyer, Roy Cohn—who would later become infamous for his work with Senator Joseph McCarthy during the Red Scare—filed a countersuit against the government for $100 million, claiming the Justice Department had made false statements.

The countersuit was thrown out. In 1975, Trump Management reached a consent decree with the Justice Department. The company did not admit wrongdoing but agreed to take out advertisements stating that all people were welcome to apply for apartments, to list all vacancies with the Urban League, and to allow the New York Urban League to present qualified applicants for vacancies.

Three years later, in 1978, the DOJ charged Trump Management with continuing to discriminate, citing multiple violations of the consent decree. Black applicants were still being turned away. The practices the company had promised to end had continued. The company denied the charges but eventually agreed to additional monitoring.

Trump never acknowledged discrimination. He later claimed the consent decree was a victory because he didn’t admit guilt. In his telling, the federal government had targeted him unfairly, and he had won by settling without admitting wrongdoing.

The pattern was established: engage in racist practices, deny them when confronted, claim victory when forced to settle, never accept responsibility.

The Central Park Five (1989)

On April 19, 1989, a white woman jogging in Central Park was brutally assaulted and raped. Five Black and Latino teenagers—Antron McCray (15), Kevin Richardson (14), Yusef Salaam (15), Raymond Santana (14), and Korey Wise (16)—were arrested and charged with the crime.

The teenagers were interrogated for hours without lawyers or parents present. Four of the five eventually confessed, though they later recanted, saying the confessions were coerced. Despite the lack of physical evidence connecting them to the crime and despite the fact that DNA evidence from the scene did not match any of the five teenagers, they were convicted and sentenced to prison.

On May 1, 1989, before the trial began, Donald Trump took out full-page advertisements in four New York newspapers—The New York Times, Daily News, New York Post, and New York Newsday. The ads called for the return of the death penalty. They did not mention the Central Park Five by name, but the timing and context were unmistakable.

“I want to hate these muggers and murderers,” Trump wrote. “They should be forced to suffer... Yes, Mayor Koch, I want to hate these murderers and I always will... I am not looking to psychoanalyze or understand them, I am looking to punish them.”

The ads cost $85,000—approximately $210,000 in today’s dollars. Trump was using his personal wealth to call for the execution of five Black and Latino teenagers before they had been tried, before any evidence had been presented in court, based solely on coerced confessions.

The five teenagers were convicted. Antron McCray, Kevin Richardson, and Raymond Santana served approximately seven years in prison. Yusef Salaam served nearly seven years. Korey Wise, tried as an adult, served thirteen years.

In 2002, Matias Reyes, a convicted murderer and serial rapist serving a life sentence, confessed to being the sole attacker in the Central Park jogger case. DNA evidence confirmed his confession. The convictions of the Central Park Five were vacated. The five men, who had collectively spent more than forty years in prison for a crime they did not commit, were exonerated.

The evidence of their innocence was absolute. DNA proved it. Another man confessed. The original confessions were demonstrated to have been coerced. There was no ambiguity.

In 2014, the five men—now known as the Exonerated Five—settled a lawsuit against New York City for $41 million. Any reasonable person would acknowledge the injustice, express regret, and apologize.

Trump refused.

Full-page newspaper advertisement placed by Donald Trump on May 1, 1989, calling for the death penalty in the Central Park jogger case—published before the five Black and Latino teenagers were tried. Attribution: Historical newspaper archive / Trump’s paid advertisement (1989).

In a 2016 interview, Trump said the settlement was “a disgrace” and suggested the men were still guilty despite the DNA evidence and another man’s confession. “They admitted they were guilty,” Trump said, ignoring that the confessions were coerced and that all evidence pointed to their innocence. He claimed the case against them was “not a question of guilt or innocence.”

In 2019, after the Netflix series “When They See Us” brought renewed attention to the case, Trump doubled down. He wrote that the men were not innocent and that the settlement was “the heist of the century.” He continued to insist they were guilty despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary.

Think about what this means. Five innocent Black and Latino teenagers were imprisoned for years because of a crime they did not commit. Donald Trump called for their execution before trial. After they were exonerated by DNA evidence and another man’s confession, Trump refused to apologize. More than that, he continued to claim they were guilty decades later.

No remorse. No acknowledgment of error. No accountability. No consequences.

Casino Discrimination (1990s)

In 1992, the Trump Plaza Hotel and Casino in Atlantic City paid a $200,000 fine after the New Jersey Casino Control Commission found that managers had removed Black card dealers from tables when a high-roller requested it. The high-roller, later identified, was a known racist who didn’t want Black dealers at his table. Trump’s casino complied with the request.

The fine was the largest ever levied against an Atlantic City casino for a civil rights violation at that time. The finding was not ambiguous: Trump’s casino had discriminated based on race at the request of a patron.

A former employee of Trump’s casinos, Kip Brown, told The New Yorker in 2015 that Trump would personally instruct staff to remove Black dealers from tables when certain high-rollers were playing.

A former Trump Plaza Hotel and Casino president, John O’Donnell, wrote in his 1991 book “Trumped!” that Trump once said: “Black guys counting my money! I hate it. The only kind of people I want counting my money are short guys that wear yarmulkes every day. Those are the kind of people I want counting my money. Nobody else.”

Trump initially denied the quote. Then, in a 1997 Playboy interview, he acknowledged it: “The stuff O’Donnell wrote about me is probably true.”

The pattern continued: documented discrimination, minimal consequences, eventual admission, no apology.

The Birther Conspiracy (2011-2016)

Donald Trump speaks to reporters while promoting the “birther” conspiracy theory, repeatedly demanding to see President Obama’s birth certificate (2011–2016). Attribution: KPBS / news photo from Trump’s birther press events.

For the first thirty-eight years of his business career, Donald Trump’s racism was documented but not nationally prominent. That changed in 2011, when Trump became the most visible promoter of the “birther” conspiracy theory—the false claim that Barack Obama was not born in the United States and therefore was not eligible to be President.

Trump’s embrace of birtherism transformed him from a controversial businessman into a political figure. He gave dozens of interviews questioning Obama’s citizenship. He appeared on television programs demanding to see Obama’s birth certificate. He suggested the certificate Obama had already released was fraudulent. He claimed to have sent private investigators to Hawaii to uncover “the truth.” He hinted at revelations that never came.

The entire campaign was designed to delegitimize America’s first Black president by suggesting he was fundamentally foreign, fundamentally other, fundamentally un-American.

The birther conspiracy had no basis in fact. Obama was born in Hawaii on August 4, 1961. His birth certificate was publicly available and had been verified by Hawaii state officials. The director of the Hawaii Department of Health personally confirmed the authenticity of Obama’s birth certificate. Contemporary newspaper announcements documented his birth in Honolulu.

But facts were irrelevant to Trump’s purpose. The conspiracy theory was effective precisely because it exploited racial anxieties among certain segments of the American population who could not accept a Black president as legitimate. The birther lie was the old question “Where are you really from?” applied to the presidency—the suggestion that a Black American, no matter how successful, is not truly American.

Trump rode birtherism to political prominence. His willingness to say what other Republicans would only hint at made him a hero to a segment of the conservative base. Polls showed that a majority of Republicans either believed Obama was not born in the United States or were uncertain about it. Trump was giving voice to their doubts.

In April 2011, under sustained pressure, Obama released his long-form birth certificate. Trump claimed credit for forcing the release, as if he had performed a public service rather than promoted a racist lie for months.

In September 2016, when Trump was the Republican nominee for president, he finally acknowledged that Obama was born in the United States. But he did not apologize. Instead, he blamed Hillary Clinton for starting the conspiracy theory—another lie—and declared, “I finished it.” He took credit for resolving a controversy he had fueled.

There were no consequences. Trump won the Republican nomination and then the presidency. The birther lie, which would have disqualified any other candidate, became a credential. It proved Trump was willing to say what others wouldn’t. It demonstrated his contempt for political correctness. It showed he understood the resentments of his base.

“Shithole Countries” (2018)

President Trump during the January 2018 Oval Office immigration meeting in which he reportedly referred to Haiti, El Salvador, and African nations as “shithole countries” while praising immigrants from Norway. Attribution: NBC News / official White House pool photo from the 2018 meeting.

On January 11, 2018, President Trump met with lawmakers to discuss immigration policy. During the meeting, according to multiple people present, he referred to Haiti, El Salvador, and African nations as “shithole countries” and asked why the United States couldn’t have more immigrants from countries like Norway.

The comment was reported immediately. Trump initially issued a vague denial, claiming he had used “tough language” but not that specific phrase. Some Republican senators who were present claimed not to recall him saying it. Others remained silent.

But the denials were halfhearted and short-lived. The comment fit too perfectly with Trump’s established pattern of denigrating predominantly Black and brown nations while praising predominantly white countries.

Eventually, Trump stopped denying it altogether. At a January 2026 cabinet meeting, he openly admitted to using the phrase and defended it: “I didn’t say shithole. You did. We had a meeting and I said, why is it we only take people from shithole countries, right? Why can’t we have some people from Norway, Sweden, just a few, let’s have a few.”

The pattern in his comment was unmistakable: predominantly white countries (Norway, Sweden) were desirable sources of immigration, while predominantly Black and brown countries (Haiti, El Salvador, African nations) were not.

The countries he disparaged—Haiti, El Salvador, the nations of Africa—are home to hundreds of millions of people. Many immigrants from these countries have made extraordinary contributions to American society. Haitian immigrants have built communities across the United States. Salvadoran immigrants have revitalized neighborhoods and created businesses. African immigrants are among the most highly educated immigrant groups in America.

Trump reduced entire nations and their populations to a vulgar epithet based on race. The comment was not about immigration policy or economic development or political stability. It was about which people, from which places, he considered desirable. White people from Norway: yes. Black people from Haiti: no.

There were no consequences. Some Republicans expressed mild disapproval. The news cycle moved on. Trump remained president.

“They’re Eating the Dogs” (September 2024)

Donald Trump at the September 10, 2024 presidential debate repeats the false claim that Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio, are “eating the dogs… eating the cats… eating the pets. Attribution: NPR / debate-stage photograph (2024).

During the September 10, 2024 presidential debate against Kamala Harris, Donald Trump made a claim that shocked even veteran political observers: “In Springfield, they’re eating the dogs. The people that came in. They’re eating the cats. They’re eating the pets of the people that live there.”

Trump was referring to Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio, repeating a baseless conspiracy theory that had circulated in right-wing social media. The claim was that Haitian immigrants were stealing and eating residents’ pets.

The allegation was investigated by local police, city officials, and journalists. No evidence was found to support it. The Springfield city manager explicitly stated there were “no credible reports or specific claims of pets being harmed, injured or abused by individuals within the immigrant community.” Ohio Governor Mike DeWine, a Republican, confirmed the claims were false.

But Trump repeated the lie on national television, during a presidential debate watched by tens of millions of Americans, despite being fact-checked in real-time by the moderator.

The racist nature of the claim was obvious. It invoked old stereotypes of immigrants and people from developing nations as primitive, uncivilized, engaging in practices that horrify “normal” Americans. It specifically targeted Haitian immigrants—predominantly Black immigrants from one of the poorest nations in the Western Hemisphere—with a dehumanizing accusation designed to portray them as savage and dangerous.

The claim also had real-world consequences. Springfield, Ohio experienced a wave of bomb threats following Trump’s debate comments. Schools and government buildings were evacuated. Haitian community members reported being harassed and threatened. The city’s Haitian immigrant population, which had revitalized parts of Springfield’s economy by filling worker shortages, suddenly faced hostility and fear.

When confronted with the fact that his claims were false, Trump did not apologize. He did not acknowledge the bomb threats his rhetoric had inspired. Instead, he defended the statement, saying he was just repeating what he had heard on television.

The pattern was identical to every previous incident: make a racist claim, defend it when challenged, refuse to apologize when proven false, face no consequences.

The Pattern Across Five Decades

From 1973 to 2024, Donald Trump engaged in a consistent, documented pattern of racist behavior:

1973: His company systematically discriminated against Black people seeking housing, marking their applications with a “C” for colored and denying them apartments. When sued by the federal government, he denied wrongdoing, attacked the Justice Department, and violated the consent decree.

1989: He spent $85,000 to call for the execution of five innocent Black and Latino teenagers before trial. After they were exonerated by DNA evidence, he refused to apologize and continued to claim they were guilty for decades.

1990s: His casino removed Black dealers at the request of racist patrons. He later admitted to saying he didn’t want Black people counting his money.

2011-2016: He launched his political career on the racist birther conspiracy, spending five years claiming America’s first Black president was not American.

2018: He called predominantly Black nations “shitholes” and asked for more immigrants from white countries like Norway.

2024: He falsely claimed Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio were eating pets, triggering bomb threats against the community.

The pattern is unmistakable. The evidence is overwhelming. The timeline spans fifty-one years.

This is not political opportunism. This is not campaign rhetoric that got out of hand. This is who Trump has been throughout his adult life: someone who engages in racist behavior, denies it when confronted, attacks those who point it out, and never, ever accepts responsibility.

In each case, the specific response varied. Sometimes he denied. Sometimes he deflected. Sometimes he attacked. Sometimes he blamed subordinates. Sometimes he simply moved on.

But in each case, the outcome was the same: no apology, no accountability, no consequences.

Trump was sued by the federal government for housing discrimination and emerged to build a real estate empire. He called for executing innocent teenagers and became a celebrity businessman. He promoted birtherism and became president. He referred to Black nations as “shitholes” and remained president. He claimed Haitian immigrants ate pets and won reelection.

Fifty-one years. Dozens of incidents. Overwhelming documentation. Zero accountability.

But Trump’s pattern of racism didn’t end in 2024. In fact, during his second presidency beginning in 2025, the pattern would accelerate and intensify, culminating in a single devastating week in February 2026 when he depicted the Obamas as apes, attacked the Super Bowl halftime show as un-American, and created an America where citizens wonder if they belong in their own country.
