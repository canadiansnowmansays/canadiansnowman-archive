The Contribution Index

Turkey

Part II: The Bridge to Nowhere

March 13, 2026

Blue Mosque: The Sultan Ahmed Mosque at sunset, Istanbul — where Europe meets Asia, where Ottoman grandeur meets modern ambition, and where a nation that could have been the bridge between civilizations chose instead to build a toll booth. iStock/Getty Images

Underperformer #10

Turkey has everything a nation needs to be consequential for the right reasons. It straddles two continents. It commands the straits that connect the Black Sea to the Mediterranean. It has a young, educated population of 85 million, a G20 economy, NATO membership, and a cultural heritage that runs from the Ottoman Empire through Atatürk’s secular revolution to the crossroads of European and Middle Eastern civilization.

On paper, Turkey should be a model for how a Muslim-majority democracy can thrive in the twenty-first century. In practice, it is a cautionary tale about what happens when a nation with extraordinary potential surrenders it to one man’s ambition.

The Scorecard

The Analysis

Human Development: 27 out of 50

Turkey’s healthcare system has expanded significantly over the past two decades, and life expectancy has risen to roughly 76 years. The 7 reflects genuine progress in access — the universal health insurance system introduced in the 2000s brought millions of previously uninsured citizens into coverage. The infrastructure exists. The outcomes are improving.

But democratic governance earns a 4, and this score poisons everything else. Under President Erdoğan, Turkey has undergone one of the most dramatic democratic regressions of any NATO member state. Press freedom has collapsed — Turkey has ranked among the world’s top jailers of journalists for years. Judicial independence has been systematically dismantled, particularly following the 2016 coup attempt, which provided the pretext for a purge of tens of thousands of judges, academics, military officers, and civil servants. The constitutional referendum of 2017 concentrated executive power to a degree that renders the parliament largely ceremonial.

Gender equality earns a 4. Turkey withdrew from the Istanbul Convention — an international treaty on preventing violence against women that was named after its own largest city. Femicide rates remain alarmingly high. Women’s labour force participation is among the lowest in the OECD. Legal protections exist on paper; enforcement is another matter entirely.

Educational attainment scores a 6. Turkey has invested in expanding access, and university enrolment has risen. But quality is uneven, academic freedom has been gutted by political purges, and the Kurdish population faces persistent educational disadvantage.

Environmental Stewardship: 27 out of 50

Cappadocia: Hot air balloons over Cappadocia — Turkey's extraordinary natural and cultural heritage, a reminder of what this nation offers the world when it is not busy jailing journalists and weaponizing refugees. Source: Shutterstock

Turkey’s environmental story is one of middling performance without catastrophic failure or notable achievement. Renewable energy has expanded, particularly in hydroelectric and geothermal capacity, earning a 6. But coal remains a significant part of the energy mix, with new coal plants approved even as the rest of Europe moves away from the fuel. Emissions per capita are moderate by global standards — lower than most wealthy nations but rising rather than falling.

The biodiversity and habitat scores of 5 reflect a genuine tension: Turkey possesses remarkable ecological diversity but has prioritized development — particularly mega-infrastructure projects like the Istanbul Canal — over habitat preservation. Water quality is a growing concern, particularly in agricultural regions relying on increasingly stressed aquifers.

Systemic Behaviour: 21 out of 50

Arms exports and conflict contribution earn a 4. Turkey has intervened militarily in Syria, Libya, and northern Iraq. Its drone technology — particularly the Bayraktar TB2 — has been exported to conflict zones across Africa and the Middle East. Turkey is not in the same league as the top five arms exporters, but it has become an increasingly significant contributor to global militarisation, and its military interventions have destabilised regions rather than stabilised them.

Adherence to international law earns a 3. Turkey’s military operations in Syria operate without UN authorization. Its treatment of Kurdish populations has drawn repeated international condemnation. Its weaponization of refugee flows — threatening to open borders to flood Europe with migrants unless concessions are granted — represents a particularly cynical form of geopolitical leverage that directly undermines the international humanitarian framework.

And yet: the refugee score earns a 6, the highest on Turkey’s systemic behaviour card. Turkey hosts more refugees than any other nation on Earth — roughly 3.5 million Syrians alone. This is a genuine and enormous burden that Turkey has borne, however imperfectly, for over a decade. The score recognizes this reality while noting that Turkey’s management of the refugee population has been inconsistent, and its weaponization of those same refugees for political leverage undermines the moral credit the hosting itself would otherwise earn.

Turkey’s philanthropic tradition runs deep — the Islamic vakıf endowment system dates back centuries, and the Sabancı Foundation remains one of the region’s most significant charitable institutions. But philanthropic independence has been compromised by the same authoritarian pressures that have eroded every other civil society institution. State-affiliated charities have grown at the expense of independent ones. In Erdoğan’s Turkey, even generosity operates within political constraints.

Refugee camp: A Syrian refugee camp in southeastern Turkey — one of many housing the 3.5 million refugees that earn Turkey its highest systemic behaviour score of 6. The hosting is genuine. The weaponization of those same refugees as political leverage against Europe is what pulls the Neighbour Test to 18 out of 50. Source: UNHCR/Reuters

The Neighbour Test: 18 out of 50

Turkey under Erdoğan operates as a transactional power — leveraging its geographic position, its NATO membership, and its refugee hosting as bargaining chips rather than contributions to collective security.

Multilateral orientation earns a 4. Turkey participates in international institutions but instrumentalizes them — blocking Sweden’s NATO accession to extract concessions, threatening EU border agreements to secure financial transfers, using its UN position selectively.

Response to global crises earns a 4. Turkey’s response to the Syrian refugee crisis — hosting 3.5 million people — is genuine and earns credit in the systemic behaviour category. But its response to other global challenges has been calibrated for advantage rather than solidarity. The 2023 earthquake response, while devastating domestically, revealed infrastructure failures that years of construction-driven economic growth had papered over.

Treatment of allies earns a 3. Turkey maintains a military occupation of northern Cyprus. It threatens Greek sovereignty in the Aegean. It weaponized refugee flows against Europe. It held NATO enlargement hostage for months. These are not the actions of a partner; they are the actions of a nation that views every relationship as leverage.

Consistency of values earns a 3. A nation that jails journalists while claiming democratic governance, that withdrew from the Istanbul Convention while claiming to protect women, that suppresses Kurdish cultural rights while claiming pluralism — this is not inconsistency. It is the consistent application of a single principle: power serves itself.

Long-term stewardship earns a 4. Turkey invests in infrastructure and economic development, but optimizes for the political survival of its current leadership rather than the long-term wellbeing of its citizens or region. The mega-projects — the Istanbul Canal, the new airport, the construction boom — serve as patronage instruments and GDP boosters, not as investments in sustainable national capacity.

The bridge between continents operates a toll booth. And the toll keeps rising.

The Editorial Conclusion

Turkey scores 92 out of 200. It is the tenth-ranked underperformer — the least consequential failure on the underperformer list, but a failure nonetheless.

The tragedy of Turkey is the tragedy of squandered potential in its purest form. This is a nation that could have been the proof of concept for secular, democratic governance in the Muslim world — a bridge not just between continents but between civilizations. Under different leadership, with different choices, Turkey could plausibly appear on the top performer list. Its capacity is real. Its geographic advantages are permanent. Its population is young and increasingly educated.

Erdoğan: President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan — the man who transformed Turkey from a flawed but functioning secular democracy into a personalist autocracy, and in doing so turned the bridge between continents into a bridge to nowhere. Source: Reuters

Instead, it chose Erdoğan. And in choosing Erdoğan, it chose the path that leads here: a NATO member that jails journalists, a G20 economy that weaponizes refugees, a democracy in name that functions as an autocracy in practice.

The bridge between continents leads, for now, to nowhere.
