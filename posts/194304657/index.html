Training Wheels Nation

Essay #22: How America Chose the Hybrid Hedge While the World Built the Future

April 16, 2026

April 16, 2026 | Stephen Gordon for CanadianSnowmanSays

The Ram 1500 REV — America's answer to range anxiety. Starting at an estimated $60,000, it is an electric truck that carries a gasoline engine, for the moments when you stop trusting the electric truck. Photo courtesy Ram Trucks

There is a peculiarly American kind of progress that moves forward while facing backward. You can see it in the EREV — the Extended Range Electric Vehicle — which the automobile industry is currently presenting to the driving public as a revolution. It is, in fact, an electric car with a gasoline engine hidden inside it, for the moments when the driver stops trusting the electric car. The industry has done the automotive equivalent of selling someone a bicycle with a small petrol motor bolted to the frame, in case the pedalling becomes inconvenient.

The Ram 1500 REV, the first EREV set to debut in the American market later this year, offers roughly 150 miles of pure electric driving before a concealed gas engine quietly fires up and replenishes the battery. The whole arrangement can cover nearly 700 miles between stops. Ford recently killed its fully electric F-150 pickup and is resurrecting it — you could not make this up — as an EREV. Of the 16 EREVs scheduled to reach the American market within three years, every single one is a truck or SUV. The half-ton pickup, America’s most purchased vehicle category and the one most aggressively resistant to electrification, is now the designated beneficiary of a technology that exists primarily to manage the psychological discomfort of its buyers.

From where I sit, in a country that just negotiated a new trade relationship with China partly to let its citizens buy affordable electric cars, this looks less like progress than an elegantly engineered retreat.

The Stepping Stone Argument, and Its Floor Drain

The automotive industry’s proposition is straightforward: range anxiety is the primary obstacle to EV adoption, and the EREV dissolves it. Drive electrically most of the time, the pitch goes, and when the battery gets low on a long trip, the generator quietly saves you. Jeremy Michalek, director of the Vehicle Electrification Group at Carnegie Mellon, put the case plainly: “It takes away the range anxiety. When you want to go on a long trip, you can still put liquid fuel in it and continue to drive for longer distances.”

This is not wrong. Range anxiety is real, even if its severity is increasingly disproportionate to actual charging conditions. The psychological barrier is genuine, and there is a credible version of the argument that EREVs function as a gateway drug — that a driver who spends three years rarely triggering the gas engine will eventually wonder why they’re bothering to carry it.

The problem is structural. The stepping stone argument requires that drivers eventually step off. A backup system that never causes anxiety also never creates the pressure to change behaviour. The gas engine in an EREV isn’t just a mechanical component; it is a permission slip. It tells its owner: you don’t have to commit. And for a meaningful portion of buyers, that permission will be exercised indefinitely. The Scout Motors reservation data is instructive here — 85 percent of prospective buyers chose the EREV version over the pure electric alternative. That is not a statistic about transitional adoption. That is a statistic about revealed preference, and what it reveals is that most of these customers are not planning to step off the stone.

The Atlantic recently framed this problem with characteristic understatement: vehicles tend to stay on the road for a decade or more, meaning the EREVs sold in 2027 and 2028 will still be burning fossil fuels deep into the 2040s. Ford’s EREV F-150 launches next year. Scout won’t have its SUV until 2028, its truck even later. These are not transitional vehicles in any meaningful timeline sense. They are a generational commitment to compromise, packaged as reassurance.

The Infrastructure Race Nobody Is Winning (in America)

There is a competing explanation for why EREV adoption might be rational rather than merely timid. The charging infrastructure in the United States, while improving, remains genuinely inadequate for the driving patterns of rural and semi-rural truck buyers — the exact population the EREV is designed to court. Tesla now operates more than 3,000 Supercharger stations across the country, and competitors like IONNA and EVgo have accelerated deployment. But the Trump administration’s attempt to freeze billions in federal charging infrastructure funds — blocked by the courts, for now — illustrates the fragility of that progress. Policy that can be frozen by executive order is policy built on sand.

For American truck buyers in exurban and rural areas, the infrastructure anxiety is legitimate. The EREV addresses a real gap. The difficulty is that solving a supply-side problem with a demand-side workaround is not the same as solving it. The training wheels don’t build the cycling path.

There is also the price problem, which no one in the EREV promotional literature wants to dwell on. The Ram 1500 REV is estimated to start at around $60,000. The equivalent gas-powered Ram starts at $42,000. An EREV requires a large, expensive battery and a combustion engine with all its moving parts, which means it is both more expensive to purchase and more expensive to maintain than either a pure EV or a conventional truck. It is, in this sense, the worst of both worlds sold as the best.

The Industrial Failure Behind the Hedge

To understand the EREV as anything other than a clever product requires stepping back and asking how the world’s largest automobile market arrived at a position where it is celebrating a gas-powered electric car as a technological breakthrough.

The answer is a decade of policy incoherence and deliberate sabotage. As I detailed in Essay #21, the killing of the EV tax credit wasn’t an accident of fiscal conservatism — it was a calculated policy choice with consequences that are now being laundered through the product development budgets of the same automakers who lobbied for the transition. Honda has halted development of three new EVs. Volvo has discontinued its affordable electric SUV, citing “shifting market conditions.” The markets are shifting in part because the signals are incoherent: a government that freezes EV charging funds while simultaneously watching its trade competitors build entire fleets of premium electric vehicles has not created market conditions. It has created market confusion.

The EREV is what emerges from that confusion. It is the car industry’s attempt to split a political and cultural difference that was never supposed to exist in the first place. Ford didn’t kill the electric F-150 because the technology failed. It killed the electric F-150 because the political environment made the technology commercially unviable in the short term, and then dressed up the retreat as innovation.

Meanwhile, Tomorrow in Shanghai

The Zeekr 8X — 1,381 horsepower, 10-minute charging, Level 3 autonomy, $54,600 USD. It launches in Shanghai tomorrow. North Americans may have to wait. Zeekr / Geely Group

This is where the Canadian perspective becomes genuinely useful, because from here, the contrast is not theoretical.

On April 17 — tomorrow, as this essay publishes — Geely’s premium electric brand Zeekr officially launches the 8X in China. You may not have heard of it. You should have. When Zeekr opened pre-sales on March 16, the company recorded more than 10,000 orders in 38 minutes. Within 48 hours, that number had surpassed 30,000.

The Zeekr 8X is a plug-in hybrid SUV — but describing it that way is roughly equivalent to describing a Michelin three-star restaurant as a place that serves food. The 8X is built on a full-stack 900V high-voltage architecture, which enables a charge from 10 to 80 percent in approximately 10 minutes. Its tri-motor setup delivers 1,381 horsepower and reaches 100 km/h in 3.1 seconds. Its combined range — electric plus the hybrid powertrain — reaches up to 982 kilometres, or 610 miles. The AI-assisted chassis features five LiDAR sensors and dual Nvidia Drive Thor-U chips delivering 1,400 TOPS of onboard computing power, capable of handling simultaneous tire blowouts at highway speed and still stopping safely. It would be remiss of me if I didnt mention the air suspension, dual 16” screens, a flip down TV from the ceiling, heated or cooled massage seating front and rear along with a small fridge and a leather wrapped interior. Starting price in China is approximately $54,600 USD.

For comparison, Ram’s EREV pickup — which carries considerably less technology, considerably less range, and considerably less performance — is estimated to start at $60,000.

Zeekr is a Geely brand. Geely also owns Volvo and Polestar. Geely has already trademarked the Zeekr name in Canada. Whether the 8X specifically makes it here remains unconfirmed, but the direction is unmistakable.

The Carney Pivot and the American Contrast

In January, Prime Minister Carney struck a deal with Beijing that slashed Canada’s tariff on Chinese EVs from 100 percent to 6.1 percent, with an annual import quota of 49,000 vehicles growing to 70,000 by 2030. BYD, Chery, and Geely are all now working through Canadian regulatory approvals with expected launches before year-end. The BYD Seagull — a functional, reasonably attractive electric car — will start at approximately $22,000 Canadian. The BYD Dolphin at $35,000 CAD. These are not stripped economy cars; in their domestic market, they outsell the Tesla Model Y.

The deal is not without controversy. Ontario Premier Doug Ford called Chinese EVs “spy vehicles” and demanded a national boycott. Unifor, Canada’s largest private-sector union, warned about job losses. Washington expressed its displeasure through the usual channels. These are legitimate concerns that deserve serious engagement — the national security dimensions of connected vehicle technology are not trivial, and the displacement of Canadian auto workers is a real consequence, not a talking point.

But the policy calculus Carney made is essentially this: the clean energy transition is happening. The only question is whether Canada participates in it as a consumer with access to competitive choices, or as a protected market that pays a premium for inferior products while its trade partners move ahead. Given that the United States has spent the last year trying to convince Canada that its sovereignty is negotiable, the appeal of diversification is considerable.

The American alternative is the EREV — a $60,000 hedge on a transition that has already happened everywhere else.

What the Training Wheels Tell Us

The stepping stone argument for EREVs contains a genuine truth and a convenient evasion. The truth: some drivers, particularly those with legitimate infrastructure concerns and heavy-duty use cases, may need an intermediate technology to build trust. Towing a boat across Wyoming in 2026 with an all-electric truck is still a planning exercise that most drivers don’t want to undertake. The EREV solves that problem.

The evasion: infrastructure anxiety is a policy failure, not a technological inevitability. The charging network that would render the EREV redundant could be built. The investment decisions that would make it happen are political choices, not physical constraints. When the Trump administration tried to freeze EV charging funds, it wasn’t acknowledging reality — it was manufacturing the scarcity that the EREV is now sold to address.

Two things are genuinely clear about electric vehicles. They are dramatically cleaner over their operating lifetime. And drivers who buy them overwhelmingly don’t go back. The EREV delays the moment of that discovery for buyers who might otherwise make it. That is not a trivial delay. The 8X that launches in Shanghai tomorrow will still be on Chinese roads in 2040, burning almost no fuel. The Ram EREV that arrives in American showrooms later this year will still be stopping at gas stations in 2040, on a continent that is increasingly alone in believing this is adequate.

From the porch up here, watching America hedge its way into a fossil fuel future it could have left behind, the training wheels metaphor has its limits. Training wheels imply an intention to eventually ride the bicycle. What we’re watching looks more like a society that has decided the bicycle is optional, and is very proud of the motor it bolted to the frame.
