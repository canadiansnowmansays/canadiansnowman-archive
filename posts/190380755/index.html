The Idiocracy vs The Theocracy

A Confidential Organizational Performance Review

March 9, 2026

Apologies, I am a day late and a dollar short making it into Stephen’s Sunday Satire series. Hope you enjoy with your morning coffee.

Prepared by the Global Governance Practice, McKinsey & Company Classification: Confidential — Senior Leadership Distribution Only Do Not Forward. Do Not Post On Truth Social.

Executive Summary

At the request of no one in particular, McKinsey & Company has conducted an independent organizational performance review of two institutions currently engaged in a strategic conflict in the Middle East: the Government of the United States of America (hereafter “the Client,” or “the Idiocracy”) and the Islamic Republic of Iran (hereafter “the Counterparty,” or “the Theocracy”).

Our mandate was to assess both organizations across six core performance dimensions — Leadership Continuity, Strategic Clarity, Message Discipline, Operational Execution, Stakeholder Management, and Knowing What Winning Looks Like — and to offer recommendations for performance improvement.

Our findings were unexpected.

We wish to be transparent: we began this engagement with certain prior assumptions. The Client is the world’s pre-eminent military and economic power, commanding the most sophisticated weapons systems in human history, with a defense budget exceeding $800 billion annually. The Counterparty is a mid-sized theocratic state that has been under economic sanctions for four decades, whose GDP is smaller than the state of Texas, and whose air force consists largely of aircraft purchased before most of its pilots were born.

On paper, this should not have been a close review.

It was a close review.

We present our findings below. McKinsey & Company accepts no responsibility for what readers do with this information. We also note that our Riyadh office has temporarily relocated.

Section I: Leadership Continuity

Dimension Weight: 15% of Overall Score

The Theocracy

On February 28, the Counterparty’s supreme leader — the single most important figure in their organizational hierarchy, the commander-in-chief of their military, the final authority on all matters of state — was killed in an airstrike.

The Counterparty named a replacement in nine days.

The replacement was selected by a 88-member committee meeting virtually, as their traditional meeting venue had been bombed. Several committee members were under active threat of assassination. The committee completed its work anyway. The transition was announced, absorbed, and the organization continued operating without a detectable interruption in strategic output.

McKinsey notes this is a faster and more orderly C-suite transition than we have observed in several recent Fortune 500 engagements, none of which we are able to name due to confidentiality agreements.

Score: 8.5/10

The Idiocracy

The Client has a fully functioning leader who has not been killed. This is an advantage we wish to acknowledge.

However, the Client’s leader has publicly stated four to five different versions of the war’s objectives in ten days. He declared the war would last “four to five weeks,” then CENTCOM announced planning through September. He said “regime change” was not the goal, then said he wanted “different people” in charge. He said there would be no boots on the ground, then said “at some point maybe we will.” He said Iran had “surrendered” while Iran was simultaneously striking Dubai, Haifa, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Baghdad.

On Tuesday of the first week, the Client’s leader said of potential successors to the Counterparty’s supreme leader: “Pretty soon we’re not going to know anybody.” The Counterparty named a successor the following Sunday. The Client’s leader called the successor “unacceptable.” The committee selected him anyway, citing the Client’s objection as a reason to prefer him.

We have reviewed the transcript. We are confident we have read it correctly.

Score: 5/10

Section II: Strategic Clarity

Dimension Weight: 20% of Overall Score

The Theocracy

The Counterparty’s strategy, while not one McKinsey would necessarily endorse, is coherent and has remained consistent throughout the engagement period. It can be summarized as follows: survive, retaliate proportionally across multiple theaters, make the conflict expensive, wait.

There have been no press conferences at which the Counterparty’s leadership announced they had already won while their missiles were in the air. There have been no social media posts describing $120 oil as “a very small price to pay.” The Counterparty has not claimed the Strait of Hormuz is reopening based on the transit of a single Iranian-connected tanker that happened to have its transponder on.

The Counterparty’s strategy has a logic. McKinsey does not need to agree with the logic to recognize that it exists.

Score: 7/10

The Idiocracy

McKinsey spent considerable billable hours attempting to identify the Client’s endgame. Our findings:

The stated objective was to destroy Iran’s nuclear program. American intelligence agencies have since assessed that Iran’s primary store of highly enriched uranium may still be retrievable from the Isfahan site despite US strikes. The nuclear program may not have been destroyed.

The stated objective was regime change. The administration then said regime change was not the goal. Then said it wanted “different people.” Then named Mojtaba Khamenei as unacceptable. The Assembly of Experts named Mojtaba Khamenei anyway.

The stated objective was to keep oil prices low. Oil briefly hit $120 per barrel on Day Ten. The Client’s leader described this as a “very small price to pay,” having campaigned on the price of gasoline.

RBC Capital Markets, a financial institution not known for editorializing, wrote on Sunday: “With no clear definition of what winning looks like, it is hard to forecast whether this will be a multi-week or multi-month conflict.”

McKinsey charges $100,000 a week to tell clients what RBC Capital Markets will tell them for free.

Score: 3/10

Section III: Message Discipline

Dimension Weight: 15% of Overall Score

The Theocracy

The Counterparty’s messaging has been consistent, disciplined, and — McKinsey notes with some professional admiration — tactically sophisticated. When the Client’s leader declared Iran had “surrendered,” the Counterparty did not hold a press conference to deny it. They fired missiles at Dubai, Haifa, Kuwait, and Bahrain. The rebuttal was kinetic rather than verbal, which we acknowledge is not ideal, but is at minimum coherent.

Ali Larijani, the Counterparty’s senior national security official, described the succession announcement as proof that “the enemies’ tactics to bring the country to a dead end” had failed. This is factually defensible, concise, and on message. McKinsey has sat through C-suite communication workshops for which organizations paid $2 million and produced messaging of substantially lower quality.

Score: 7.5/10

The Idiocracy

On Saturday, the Client’s leader stood at Dover Air Force Base at the ceremony for his six dead soldiers and said the elementary school strike that killed 175 people — many of them children — was done by Iran. “They’re very inaccurate, as you know, with their munitions,” he said. The Defense Secretary, standing beside him, said: “The only side that targets civilians is Iran.”

A verified video showing a Tomahawk cruise missile — a weapon deployed exclusively by the United States military — striking the naval base beside the school was released the following day.

The Tomahawk is, per the Defense Department’s own materials, “long-range, highly accurate.”

McKinsey’s communication practice does not have a standard framework for this situation. We are developing one.

In a separate messaging initiative, the Client’s Energy Secretary cited a single tanker transiting the Strait of Hormuz as evidence that shipping was returning to normal. A shipping analyst identified the vessel as Iranian-connected. The Strait remains closed.

Score: 2/10

Section IV: Operational Execution

Dimension Weight: 20% of Overall Score

The Theocracy

The Counterparty entered the conflict having already established a diversified retaliation portfolio: ballistic missiles, drone swarms, proxy forces across seven countries, cyberattack capabilities, foreign intelligence networks, and — McKinsey was intrigued to discover — an active shortwave numbers station broadcasting encrypted messages in Farsi whose origin and recipient remain unidentified by either side.

We note that operating an unattributed Cold War-era communications network during a modern air campaign while simultaneously conducting a supreme leader succession process, managing proxy forces in Lebanon, Yemen, Iraq, and Syria, and keeping ballistic missiles in the air across six Gulf states represents a level of organizational multitasking that McKinsey’s operational excellence practice would be pleased to document in a case study, pending appropriate permissions.

The Pentagon acknowledged on Day Nine that Iran was “more prepared for war than the Trump administration anticipated,” and that its strikes had become “widespread and less predictable.” The Counterparty performed above client expectations. This is, in our experience, relatively rare.

Score: 7/10

The Idiocracy

The Client has struck over 3,000 targets, damaged or destroyed 43 Iranian ships, and conducted the most intensive air campaign of the 21st century. On purely kinetic metrics, execution has been strong.

However, the National Intelligence Council assessed before the war began that a large-scale assault would be unlikely to topple Iran’s government. The CIA assessed that killing the supreme leader would not produce regime change. The Client launched the war anyway. The government has not been toppled. The supreme leader has been replaced. Both assessments have been validated.

McKinsey defines operational success as the achievement of stated objectives. We note that the Client’s stated objectives have changed frequently enough that this definition creates measurement challenges.

We also note that the Client’s State Department did not issue mandatory departure orders for diplomats in Saudi Arabia until the embassy had been attacked multiple times, the acting chief of mission had formally requested authorization weeks earlier, and a drone had been shot down over the diplomatic quarter. There is no Senate-confirmed ambassador. The previous career ambassador was removed at the start of the administration before completing his tour, which is described in the Client’s own diplomatic community as “unusual.”

McKinsey’s human capital practice would like to schedule a separate engagement.

Score: 6/10

Section V: Stakeholder Management

Dimension Weight: 15% of Overall Score

The Theocracy

The Counterparty has, admittedly, managed to anger Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and the UAE — all of whom it needs as at least tacit non-belligerents. Qatar arrested 313 people for sharing videos of Iranian attacks. Saudi Arabia said Iran “would be the greatest loser” in any wider escalation. These are not the statements of allied stakeholders.

However, the Counterparty has maintained its relationship with Russia, which is sharing satellite intelligence on US warship positions, providing evacuation corridors for Iranian government personnel fleeing Lebanon, and whose president formally congratulated the new supreme leader within hours of the announcement. Vladimir Putin pledged “unwavering support for Tehran and solidarity with our Iranian friends.”

The Client’s leader has not addressed the Russia intelligence-sharing. He has, however, continued to speak warmly of Mr. Putin in other contexts.

Score: 5/10

The Idiocracy

The Client did not inform its Gulf allies — whose airspace, bases, and populations are now under Iranian missile attack — of the war’s timing before it began. Several of those allies are, according to reporting, “furious.”

The Client’s British ally declined to participate in strikes. The Client’s leader mocked the British Prime Minister publicly. The Prime Minister noted that British intelligence is being shared “every day” and that British and American personnel are “co-located in the same places, in the same bases,” which is a diplomatic way of saying: our people are there too, please stop mocking us.

Pakistan, a nuclear-armed state with oil reserves running out within two weeks, is operationally aligned against the Client’s position. The Client’s Marines fired on protesters at the US Consulate in Karachi.

Australia has three personnel on a submarine involved in the conflict and is now also managing a diplomatic crisis over Iran’s women’s soccer team, which is stranded in Sydney and afraid to go home.

McKinsey acknowledges that stakeholder management in a active war is inherently complex. We nonetheless note that alienating Australia via soccer is a novel escalation pathway that our frameworks did not anticipate.

Score: 4/10

Section VI: Knowing What Winning Looks Like

Dimension Weight: 15% of Overall Score

This is the most important dimension. In McKinsey’s experience, organizations that cannot define winning cannot achieve it.

The Theocracy

The Counterparty’s definition of winning is narrow, achievable, and has not changed: survive the war as a functioning state, install a successor, make the conflict expensive enough that the adversary eventually stops, and remain in power. On Day Ten, they are on track across all four metrics. Whether this constitutes a strategic victory in any larger sense is a separate question. It constitutes organizational survival, which in the circumstances is the prerequisite for every other objective.

Score: 7/10

The Idiocracy

We asked this question of the public record and received the following answers, in chronological order: destroy nuclear program; regime change; not regime change; different people; four to five weeks; one hundred days; Mojtaba is unacceptable; Mojtaba is in charge; oil prices will drop rapidly; oil is above $100; we already won; Iran struck six countries today; it’s a very MAGA thing; I’m at the highest point I’ve ever been with MAGA.

McKinsey’s strategy practice notes that these statements are not a strategy. They are a mood.

Score: 1.5/10

Overall Scorecard

Recommendations

For the Theocracy:

Continue current approach. Consider, when hostilities permit, some reform of the domestic political environment, as “Death to Mojtaba” chanting from rooftops on the night of the succession announcement suggests stakeholder alignment is incomplete. The MbS model has been proposed by at least one analyst. McKinsey has worked with MbS. We are not able to comment further.

For the Idiocracy:

McKinsey recommends, as a first step, defining what winning looks like. We recognize this is a significant ask given current organizational culture, but it is foundational to all subsequent recommendations and we are unable in good conscience to proceed without it.

As a second step, we recommend against standing at a military funeral and blaming the other side for a strike that a Tomahawk video will confirm was yours within 24 hours. Our communications practice has a module on this. It is one slide. The slide says: do not do this.

As a third step, we recommend appointing a Senate-confirmed ambassador to Saudi Arabia before the next war.

Finally, McKinsey notes that the Counterparty has been operating under severe sanctions, continuous foreign sabotage, and sustained bombing for decades, and has nonetheless managed to build a diversified retaliation portfolio, maintain proxy networks across seven countries, run a functioning intelligence apparatus, conduct an orderly leadership succession during active bombardment, and operate a shortwave numbers station whose very existence nobody on the other side can explain.

The Client has a defense budget of $800 billion.

We recommend the Client reflect on this.

McKinsey & Company is a global management consulting firm. This report was prepared for informational purposes only and does not constitute geopolitical advice. Our Toronto office is open. Our Riyadh office will reopen when conditions permit. We have no offices in Tehran, have never had offices in Tehran, and wish to be clear on this point given current circumstances.
