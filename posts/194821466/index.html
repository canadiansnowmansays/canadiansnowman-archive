The Kash Patel Files

The Perks

Part IV — Your Tax Dollars at Play

April 23, 2026

A continuing series by CanadianSnowmanSays

The K$H Challenge Coin — a CanadianSnowmanSays original. "Something kids use to pop the caps off beer bottles at a gaming meetup" — Tom Nichols, The Atlantic.

In 1993, FBI Director William Sessions was dismissed from his position. The proximate cause was an investigation by the Department of Justice’s Office of Professional Responsibility, which found that Sessions had used government aircraft for personal trips, had an FBI security fence installed at his home, and had agents run errands for his wife.

These findings were considered scandalous. They constituted a firing offence for the director of the nation’s premier law enforcement agency. They were, by the standards of 2026, quaint.

Every offence that cost William Sessions his job has been exceeded by Kash Patel — not by small margins, but by orders of magnitude. Sessions had agents run errands for his wife. Patel assigned a full-time SWAT team to escort his girlfriend. Sessions used government aircraft for personal trips. Patel used them so freely that when the flight records leaked, he fired the agent who oversaw the aviation programme. Sessions had a fence installed. Patel tried to post a classified photograph of Five Eyes intelligence chiefs on social media.

The difference between 1993 and 2026 is not the severity of the conduct. It is the absence of consequences.

The Alexis Wilkins Protection Detail

Kash Patel, Alexis Wilkins; Photo Courtesy Alexia Wilkins Instagram

Alexis Wilkins is 27 years old. She is a country singer from Weymouth, Massachusetts, who attended Belmont University, a private Christian school in Nashville. Her website says “NO UPCOMING SHOWS.” A longtime country music critic, asked to assess her abilities, gave her a 7.5 out of 10 on the national anthem. She appeared in small parts in two episodes of the sitcom Modern Family as a child.

She is also one of the most heavily protected individuals in the United States.

Soon after becoming FBI director in February 2025, Patel assigned a SWAT team — four agents and two vehicles — to protect Wilkins full-time in Nashville, where she lives, according to reporting by The New York Times. He overrode FBI advice that this unprecedented arrangement first undergo a legal review.

Nashville’s SWAT team was, in effect, converted to Wilkins’s personal detail. They were no longer conducting their own tactical operations. Other teams had to be brought in to cover their assignments because they were, as one FBI source put it, “guarding her all the time.”

The detail accompanied Wilkins to a fund-raiser at a country club in Palm Desert, California, where she sang the national anthem and posed for red carpet photographs with Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and his wife. Agents then drove her 135 miles to Los Angeles International Airport.

They accompanied her to a senior centre in Dixon, Illinois — Ronald Reagan’s boyhood home — where she sang for a few dozen young conservatives. Two SUVs stood guard outside.

They accompanied her to a hair salon in Nashville.

When Patel attended a secret Five Eyes intelligence conference in Britain and invited Wilkins to a dinner with King Charles III at Windsor Castle, British security declined to fetch her from the airport. The FBI scrambled tactical agents and an embassy vehicle to do the job instead.

The FBI spokesman said Wilkins needed full-time SWAT protection because she faced “more than a dozen active death threats” as a result of her relationship with Patel. In a later statement, he said the threats numbered in the “hundreds.”

Christopher O’Leary, a former senior executive in the FBI’s counterterrorism division, was not persuaded. “If you want to be a celebrity or a social media star, get your own security,” he said. “The inappropriateness of this cannot be overstated.”

Cabinet and congressional spouses do not routinely receive full-time government security details. After Paul Pelosi — husband of the Speaker of the House, second in the line of presidential succession — was attacked in his home with a hammer, it emerged that the Capitol Police had placed security cameras at the house but no personnel.

Alexis Wilkins, the girlfriend of the FBI director — not the spouse, the girlfriend — has a four-agent SWAT team.

The Government Jet Circuit

FBI directors must fly on government aircraft for security reasons, including required access to secure communications equipment. This is reasonable. It is also expensive. And it creates a responsibility — a moral one, if not always a legal one — to ensure that the flights serve the public interest rather than the director’s recreational calendar.

Patel’s flight record suggests he has not lost a great deal of sleep over this responsibility.

In October 2025, Patel flew on an FBI jet to State College, Pennsylvania, to attend a wrestling event where Wilkins was singing the national anthem. The pair then flew to Nashville, where Wilkins lives. That weekend, Patel took the jet to Boondoggle Ranch — a hunting resort in Texas owned by a major Republican donor.

Last summer, he flew on a government jet from Washington to Inverness, Scotland, for a getaway with friends, including former Navy SEALs, at the Carnegie Club, an exclusive golf resort.

When the flight records became public — posted on social media by Kyle Seraphin, a former FBI agent and podcaster who had initially supported Patel — the director reportedly blamed Steven Palmer, the agent who oversaw the bureau’s aviation assets, for the bad publicity.

Palmer was fired.

Senator Richard Durbin, citing a credible whistleblower, alleged that Patel’s use of one of the FBI’s two jets had prevented an elite team from quickly deploying to the mass shooting at Brown University in December 2025. The FBI denied this, saying the Boston field office responded promptly. But the allegation itself illuminates the tension at the heart of the jet problem: the FBI has limited aviation resources, and every flight hour spent transporting the director to a wrestling match or a hunting ranch is a flight hour unavailable for operational needs.

“There’s a $545 million budget cut for the bureau,” one field office leader told The New York Times. “Meanwhile, you’re spending millions of dollars just gallivanting. How do you justify that?”

The Milan Olympics

In February 2026, Patel flew to Italy for a four-day trip that he said was necessary to fortify the FBI’s relationship with European partners. An internal schedule obtained by The New York Times told a somewhat different story.

On Friday, Patel met for an hour with the head of Italy’s domestic security agency, followed by a photo opportunity to sign a cybercrime cooperation deal and an aperitivo at the interior ministry. He then flew to Milan to watch the U.S. hockey team’s victory over Slovakia.

On Saturday — one of Patel’s stated reasons for being in Milan was to oversee Olympic security — his schedule listed a single 20-minute briefing with the Olympic security team and a half-hour tour of the joint security operations centre. The period between 2 p.m. and 6:30 p.m. was reserved for “personal time/cultural activities.”

On Sunday, the schedule listed only two events: the gold medal game between the United States and Canada, and a 7 p.m. flight home — a trip of more than 4,000 miles that would typically cost tens of thousands of dollars on a government aircraft.

Kash Patel, the F.B.I. director, watching the United States and Canada in the gold medal hockey game at the Milan Olympics.Credit...Doug Mills/The New York Times

It was during the post-game celebrations that Patel was filmed chugging beer with the U.S. men’s hockey team, prompting the teetotalling president — whose brother died of alcoholism — to call and express his displeasure.

The FBI spokesman’s response to the leaked schedule was revealing in its priorities: “The leaking of his minute-by-minute schedule is a criminal act that jeopardizes security and will not be taken lightly.”

Not: the director should not have been partying at the Olympics on the taxpayer’s dime. Not: the schedule reveals an imbalance between official duties and personal leisure. But: someone will pay for letting the public see how the director spends his time.

The Five Eyes Fiasco

Every May, the heads of the intelligence agencies of the Five Eyes alliance — the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand — meet in a secure, classified conference. In 2025, the conference was held in Britain.

According to The New York Times investigation drawing on 45 current and former employees, the lead-up to Patel’s attendance was an exercise in absurdist entitlement.

His staff informed the British hosts that Patel was “unhappy because he doesn’t like meetings in office settings.” What he wanted instead was social events. Premier League football matches. Jet skiing. A helicopter tour. The British, who had organized a structured intelligence conference for the heads of five allied nations, were informed that the FBI director would prefer recreational activities.

His staff’s three priorities, according to a senior executive who witnessed the preparations, were: what his meals were, when his workouts would be, and what his entertainment would be. “The biggest plan,” the executive said, “is how he’s going to get his girlfriend in there so she can go to Windsor Castle.”

When staffers couldn’t arrange the food or exercise schedule to satisfaction, Nicole Rucker, Patel’s assistant, would “just start screaming at people: Make it happen!“

At the conference, MI5 Director Ken McCallum raised a critical issue: an FBI position in London that had been pulled. The FBI is MI5’s most important partner — a 38,000-person agency versus MI5’s 5,000. The London-based FBI official had been working on sensitive matters including embassy penetrations and technology. McCallum asked Patel to restore the position.

Patel agreed. The position would stay.

Two weeks later, he reversed himself and removed the official. The British were outraged.

And then there was the photograph. At Windsor Castle, the Five Eyes principals met with the King. A group photo was taken — one that included intelligence officials whose affiliations are nondisclosed, meaning their connection to British intelligence services is not public. The British forwarded the photo as a private keepsake with an explicit instruction: this is not to be shared.

Patel wanted to post it on social media.

His staff had to fight the directive. “They have people trying to negotiate with the Brits about whether that’s possible. They’re fighting with the director’s office, like: You cannot post this. Do not do that. And they’re arguing, He wants a picture out.“

This is not a perk. This is a security breach that was prevented not by institutional safeguards but by the physical intervention of staff members who understood what their director apparently did not: that revealing the identities of nondisclosed intelligence officials on a commercial social media platform would endanger those officials and damage an alliance that has protected the English-speaking world for eight decades.

The Challenge Coin

The FBI director's challenge coin. One side features a Punisher skull with "K$H" and flanking pistols. The other reads "Presented by the Director of the FBI — Kash Patel" above his signature. Tom Nichols of The Atlantic described it as "something kids use to pop the caps off beer bottles at a gaming meetup or a cosplay convention." Photo source: social media.

There is a tradition in the American military of exchanging challenge coins — small medallions carried by members of a unit as tokens of esteem. They are modest objects, usually the size of a silver dollar, bearing the symbols and names of military commands. They signify belonging, service, and shared sacrifice.

Patel created one for himself.

Tom Nichols, writing in The Atlantic, described what emerged: one side featured what appeared to be the symbol of the Punisher — a Marvel vigilante — with “K$H” on the forehead and spider-like imagery in the eye sockets. Pistols flanked the face. The reverse carried Patel’s signature, the FBI seal, and a depiction of a tommy gun.

“This is not a challenge coin,” Nichols wrote. “It is something kids use to pop the caps off beer bottles at a gaming meetup or a cosplay convention.”

A former senior administration official had previously sent Calabro a photo of an earlier Patel challenge coin — this one listing national-security-adjacent terms including DIRECT ACTION, SANCTIONS, HEZ/IRAN, and CYBER. “It’s just random shit,” the official said. “Half of this stuff, he wasn’t even involved in.”

The coin is trivial. It is also, in its triviality, the most efficient symbol of what Patel has brought to the FBI directorship. It is performative toughness without substance. It is branding without achievement. It is the aesthetic of authority without the discipline, the competence, or the institutional respect that authority requires.

It is — and I choose this word carefully — a toy.

The Precedent That Wasn’t

William Sessions was fired in 1993 for a fence, some flights, and some errands.

Kash Patel has a full-time SWAT team for his girlfriend, a government jet circuit that spans two continents, an Olympics junket with a 20-minute security briefing and hours of “cultural activities,” a near-breach of Five Eyes intelligence security that was prevented only by staff intervention, and a Punisher-skull bottle opener that he hands out as a symbol of the FBI’s authority.

The difference is not that the standards have changed. The standards are the same — they are written in the DOJ ethics handbook, in the inspector general’s guidelines, in the institutional norms that governed every director from Clarence Kelley to Christopher Wray.

The difference is that the enforcement mechanism has been dismantled. The attorney general who might hold Patel accountable was herself fired. The inspector general who might investigate has been watching the political winds. The congressional committee that might exercise oversight is chaired by a senator who is actively feeding Patel the names of agents to fire.

Sessions was held accountable because the system still functioned. Patel is not held accountable because the system has been captured by the people whose interests his impunity serves.

Your tax dollars are at play. And nobody is keeping score.

The perks and the purges might read as separate stories — one about corruption, the other about revenge. But they are the same story. Every SWAT agent guarding a girlfriend at a hair salon is a SWAT agent not conducting tactical operations. Every flight hour spent on a hunting ranch is a flight hour unavailable for a mass shooting response. Every senior leader forced out is a decade of institutional knowledge that leaves with them. The damage is not additive. It is exponential. And the bureau that remains — hollow, frightened, and asked to do more with less — is the subject of what comes next.
