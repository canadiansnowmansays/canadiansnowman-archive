The Uncomfortable Truth Fascism Exists in America Today

Part 1- A History

Understanding the Term Before We Use It

September 30, 2025

Charlie Chaplin released ‘The Great Dictator’ in 1940, mocking Hitler and Mussolini when Hollywood was afraid to take sides. His courage came from understanding what fascism actually was—not as an insult, but as a specific threat to democracy. This series does the same.

Over the weekend, California Governor Gavin Newsom openly called Stephen Miller a fascist. Trump and his allies, for their part, have spent years labeling Democrats as “radical left-wing” and, yes, also fascist. The accusations fly back and forth across our political divide with increasing frequency and decreasing precision.

Newsom’s call-out came days after Miller’s speech at Charlie Kirk’s memorial on September 21st—a speech that drew immediate comparisons to Nazi propaganda. Kirk, the 31-year-old co-founder of Turning Point USA, had been assassinated while speaking at a Utah college event on September 10th. At the memorial, Miller—himself the descendant of Jewish immigrants who fled persecution—declared “You thought you could kill Charlie Kirk. You have made him immortal,” echoing Joseph Goebbels’ 1932 speech invoking Nazi martyr Horst Wessel: “So our dead comrade Horst Wessel wrote, and we are fulfilling his prophecy.” As transcripts show, both speeches invoke martyred “comrades” to call for vengeance against political enemies. Both used light-versus-darkness imagery and summoned an “army” to rise up. The irony of Miller, whose own family escaped anti-Jewish violence, employing rhetoric that mirrors Nazi propaganda is not lost on observers. When Trump’s Deputy Chief of Staff for Policy delivers rhetoric that directly parallels the Nazi propaganda minister’s speeches, perhaps “fascist” is more than just an insult.

Why Precision Matters

The problem is simple: if everyone is a fascist, then no one is. The word has become so overused, so drained of specific meaning, that it now functions primarily as a way to say “I really, really don’t like you” rather than to describe an actual political ideology. This is dangerous. Not because hurt feelings matter in politics—they don’t—but because when we lose the ability to identify real authoritarianism, we become vulnerable to it.

So let’s clear this up. Let’s understand what fascism actually is, where it came from, and what it looked like in practice. Only then can we honestly assess whether anyone today deserves the label.

The Vision That Came Before: Mazzini’s Dream

To understand how fascism emerged, we need to start with what it corrupted. In the mid-1800s, the territory we now call Italy was a patchwork of kingdoms, duchies, and papal states, largely controlled by foreign empires. The people living there spoke six or seven major languages—Sicilian, Neapolitan, Venetian, Sardinian, Piedmontese, Lombard—languages as different from each other as English is from French. Less than three percent of people spoke what we now call Italian, which back then was simply called Florentine or Tuscan, named after the city of Florence and the region of Tuscany.

Into this fragmented landscape came Giuseppe Mazzini, an activist and journalist who believed these diverse peoples could unite—not through force, but through shared commitment to universal human rights. Mazzini was inspired by the French Revolution, which had overthrown a monarchy whose financing of foreign wars had led to the starvation of its own people. He envisioned a unified Italy built on equal rights for all people, equal rights for women, the end of slavery, and universal education. His vision was so compelling that Gandhi would later call him an inspiration.

Mazzini’s understanding of human rights was grounded in a sense of obligation to each other—a conscious decision by the citizens of cities to work together for the common good. Crucially, he saw the nation not as an end in itself, but as a stepping stone toward a global community. The unification of all cities against tyranny, moving toward universal human rights for all humanity.

This is why Mazzini endorsed Tuscan as the common language for Italia—not to suppress the others, but because it had been used by the great Tuscan poets and writers: Dante, who denounced the corruption of religion; Petrarch, who railed against the decadence of petty tyrants; Boccaccio, who satirized power. The unification movement was tied to an artistic spirit of rebellion and individuality, starkly opposed to monarchies and authoritarians.

Mazzini famously argued with Karl Marx about all this. Marx thought Mazzini was being naive, that he was ignoring how the corrupting power of aristocracy would divide working people along national lines. Marx believed Mazzini’s romantic vision denied the hard economic realities that actually shaped human behavior. But where they agreed was fundamental: equal rights for all people, economic justice, and the end of oppression.

Marx turned out to be right about the danger. Without continuity and unity between the working classes of the world, new rivalries between nations would emerge. Marx had even predicted that an isolated working class in stalemate with its aristocracy could easily fall victim to a charismatic authoritarian who would rise above them all, pretending to represent the people while actually safeguarding elite interests.

But Mazzini was also right that a sense of duty, sacrifice, and higher purpose would prove universally appealing, giving life meaning beyond class struggle alone.

In the end, both visions lost to what came next.

Mussolini’s Betrayal: The Birth of Fascism

Fascism was invented by a man named Benito Mussolini. He deliberately named it after the Roman fasces—an ancient emblem of authoritarian power. In Latin, “fasces” means “a bundle.” In ancient Rome, the fasces was an image of rods bundled together with an axe, representing conformity enforced through force. Strength through unity, unity through violence.

The near-homophone is fitting. Mussolini reached back to ancient Rome for gravitas, dressing up his movement in the imagery of imperial power. But what he created—what fascism has always been—is fundamentally shit: toxic, degrading, poisonous to everything it touches. The fasces bundles rods together with an axe; fascism bundles humanity's worst impulses into something equally worthless.

Mussolini was fundamentally opposed to democracy. He believed working people should not unite against wealthy interests—that was divisive, he said. It led to international solidarity between workers all over the world, which threatened the power of national elites. Mussolini believed he should get to decide who was a real Italian and who was not. Eventually, he identified “real Italians” as white, and his primary message became that white Italians were being replaced and diluted by foreign races and religious heretics who were weakening Italian culture.

Sound familiar? It should. This rhetoric has echoed through authoritarian movements ever since.

Mussolini adopted Nazi-like laws to maintain a white supremacist caste system. He targeted Roma, Jews, leftists, homosexuals—anybody outside the club. He formed paramilitary squads called the Blackshirts, who were sent to terrorize and arrest anybody who wasn’t considered a “real Italian.” He used concentration camps and mass deportations. Then he cracked down on journalists and artists who criticized him.

Mussolini exercised control over private corporations that were monopolizing every market—from food to healthcare to manufacturing. Rather than breaking up these monopolies or nationalizing them for public benefit, he fused state power with corporate power, each reinforcing the other. The corporations got guaranteed profits and protection from worker organizing; the state got funding and industrial capacity for its military ambitions.

He brought schools and universities under his control. Textbooks were rewritten to properly glorify Roman heritage, nationalism, and militarism. He struck a deal with the Catholic Church, and Catholic instruction became mandatory in schools. Education became indoctrination.

This is what fascism actually looked like: national purity enforced through violent domination, the fusion of state and corporate power, no loyalties to anything other than the nation itself. Fascism became a kind of religion, with Mussolini as its prophet.

Mussolini’s inconsistencies revealed the hollowness at fascism’s core. He could order the killing of tens of thousands of Muslims in Libya, then turn around and pose as a champion of Islam when he thought it would suit his expansionist ambitions. Truth and law were not defined by reality or principle, but by emotional strength and political expediency. Whatever served power in the moment became truth.

Fascism thrives in times of economic crisis, when people are struggling, feeling humiliated, searching for jobs and needing answers to what the hell is going on. In times like these, the ruling class—usually the actual culprits behind these crises—likes to blame scapegoats to avoid getting overthrown. Who are those scapegoats? The foreigner. The immigrant. The leftist. The “other.” All the divide-and-conquer tactics of what we now call culture wars.

That is fascism. Not as an insult, but as a historical fact.

Hitler’s Adoption: Fascism Perfected

Adolf Hitler saw what Mussolini was building and recognized a kindred spirit. Nazi Germany took every element of Italian fascism and amplified it to genocidal extremes. The rhetoric of racial purity, the paramilitary violence, the fusion of state and corporate power, the cult of the strongman leader, the rewriting of history and education, the scapegoating of minorities—all of it was there, refined into an even more efficient machine of oppression.

Hitler’s “blood and soil” ideology—the idea that a people’s identity was inseparable from their ancestral land and racial purity—became the defining characteristic of Nazism. Jews, Roma, homosexuals, the disabled, political dissidents, and anyone deemed “degenerate” or “un-German” were systematically dehumanized, stripped of rights, and ultimately murdered by the millions.

The Nazis didn’t just control the press—they created an entire propaganda apparatus under Joseph Goebbels that controlled every aspect of cultural life. They didn’t just rewrite textbooks—they created Hitler Youth to indoctrinate children from the earliest age. They didn’t just ally with corporations—they made corporate leaders complicit in slave labor and genocide.

And they didn’t just suppress opposition—they industrialized murder on a scale the world had never seen.

This was fascism in its purest, most horrifying form. Mussolini had created the template; Hitler showed what happened when that template was followed to its logical conclusion.

The Defining Characteristics

So what are the actual characteristics of fascism, based on how Mussolini defined it and how both he and Hitler practiced it?

Ultranationalism and “blood and soil” ideology: The nation and its “pure” people are elevated above all else. The leader gets to define who counts as a real member of the nation, usually along racial or ethnic lines. The narrative is always one of decline and replacement—”our people” are being weakened, diluted, or replaced by inferior outsiders.

Violent suppression of opposition: Not just winning elections or arguments, but using paramilitary forces to terrorize, assault, and eliminate political opponents. Violence becomes a tool of political control.

Scapegoating of minorities and “others”: Economic problems, social problems, cultural anxieties—all are blamed on easily identifiable outgroups. Immigrants, religious minorities, LGBTQ people, leftists, intellectuals. The “other” is presented as both weak and dangerous, inferior yet somehow capable of destroying the nation.

Fusion of state and corporate power: Not socialism (worker control) or pure capitalism (market control), but a merger where the state and big business reinforce each other. Corporations fund and support the regime; the regime protects corporate profits and suppresses worker organizing. The result is authoritarianism that serves elite economic interests.

Control of information and attack on press: Independent journalism is labeled “fake news” or “enemy of the people.” The press is either brought under state control or so thoroughly delegitimized that citizens stop trusting any source that contradicts the leader.

Rewriting of education and history: Schools become indoctrination centers. History is revised to glorify the nation and its heritage. Critical thinking is suppressed in favor of nationalist mythology.

Cult of personality and the strongman: The leader is presented as the only one who can save the nation. He is above the law, above institutions, above normal political processes. Loyalty to the leader becomes loyalty to the nation itself.

Rejection of democratic norms and institutions: When democracy produces the “wrong” results, it must be overridden. Elections are only legitimate when the fascist wins. Courts are only legitimate when they rule in the fascist’s favor. Constitutional limits are obstacles to be overcome, not principles to be respected.

Glorification of violence and militarism: Violence is not just a tool but a virtue. Military strength and the willingness to use force become central to national identity. Diplomatic solutions are for the weak.

Nostalgia for a mythical golden age: There was a time when the nation was pure, strong, respected, and great. We must return to that time by purging foreign influences and restoring traditional hierarchies. The fact that this golden age never actually existed doesn’t matter—it serves as an emotional rallying point.

This is the checklist. This is what fascism actually looked like in practice.

What the Irony Reveals

Here’s the great irony of fascism: it does not preserve cultures—it flattens them. Mussolini claimed to be protecting Italian culture, but before unification, the Italian peninsula was home to a rich diversity of languages, traditions, and regional identities. Fascism demanded conformity to a single vision, a single language, a single way of being Italian. Half the country still retains its regional languages at home, spoken in families, precisely because they resisted Mussolini’s cultural flattening.

Fascism didn’t emerge from strength—it emerged from crisis. Both Mussolini and Hitler rose to power in societies wracked by economic collapse, political instability, and wounded national pride. They offered simple answers to complex problems, strong leaders to replace weak institutions, and clear enemies to blame for societal pain.

And millions of ordinary people, desperate for stability and meaning, followed them into catastrophe.

Mussolini ended up captured by Italian socialists and communists—the very people he had spent decades terrorizing—and was hung upside down from a gas station in Milan. Hitler shot himself in a bunker as Soviet forces closed in. Their fascist empires, which were supposed to last a thousand years, collapsed in ruins.

But the ideology didn’t die with them. The tactics didn’t disappear. The appeal of a strong leader who promises to restore national greatness by purging the “others” remains potent, especially in times of anxiety and change.

Why This Matters Now

We’ve established what fascism actually is—not as an insult, but as a specific historical phenomenon with identifiable characteristics. We’ve seen how Mussolini perverted Mazzini’s democratic vision into violent authoritarianism, and how Hitler took that model to its genocidal conclusion.

Now comes the harder question: Does anyone in American politics today actually resemble this? Does anyone deserve the label that gets thrown around so casually?

That’s what we’ll examine in Part 2. We’ll take this checklist—these specific, historical characteristics of fascism—and honestly measure contemporary American politics against them. We’ll look at Trump and the MAGA movement. We’ll look at the left. We’ll see where the accusations hold water and where they’re just partisan noise.

Because if we’re going to use the word “fascist,” we should mean something specific by it. And if we’re going to accuse someone of fascism, we should be able to point to actual fascist behavior, not just policies we dislike.

The stakes are too high for anything less than honesty.
