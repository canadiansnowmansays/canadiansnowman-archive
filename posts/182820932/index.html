The Trump Accountability Project

COVID-19 Mismanagement

Part 8: The Lies That Killed (January-June 2020)

January 2, 2026

The Deadliest Lie

On February 7, 2020, Donald Trump sat down for an interview with journalist Bob Woodward. The conversation was remarkable for what Trump admitted—on tape—about the novel coronavirus spreading from China.

“This is deadly stuff,” Trump told Woodward. “You just breathe the air and that’s how it’s passed. And so that’s a very tricky one. That’s a very delicate one. It’s also more deadly than even your strenuous flus... This is more deadly. This is five per cent versus one per cent and less than one per cent. You know? So, this is deadly stuff.”

Trump understood the danger. He knew COVID-19 was airborne, highly contagious, and significantly more lethal than seasonal influenza. He had this information from his intelligence briefings and conversations with health officials. The President of the United States possessed accurate, lifesaving information about a pandemic spreading toward American shores.

What did Trump do with this knowledge? He lied. Repeatedly. Deliberately. Publicly.

The very next day, February 8, Trump tweeted: “The Coronavirus is very much under control in the USA... Stock Market starting to look very good to me!”

When Woodward asked Trump in March why he was publicly downplaying the virus when he privately understood its danger, Trump was remarkably candid: “I wanted to always play it down. I still like playing it down, because I don’t want to create a panic.”

This wasn’t incompetence. This wasn’t a president overwhelmed by an unprecedented crisis. This was deliberate deception—a conscious decision to lie to the American people about a deadly threat because the truth was politically inconvenient.

By the time Trump left office on January 20, 2021, more than 400,000 Americans were dead from COVID-19. Multiple studies have estimated that tens of thousands of those deaths were preventable with competent federal leadership and honest communication about the virus.

Trump’s COVID-19 response represents the deadliest failure of presidential leadership in modern American history. This essay examines the first six months of Trump’s pandemic response, documenting how his lies, his prioritization of political considerations over public health, and his active sabotage of mitigation efforts turned a public health crisis into a mass casualty event.

“It Will Disappear Like a Miracle” – The Public Lies (January-March 2020)

While privately admitting COVID-19 was “deadly stuff,” Trump spent months publicly minimizing the threat:

January 22: “We have it totally under control. It’s one person coming in from China, and we have it under control. It’s going to be just fine.”

February 10: “Looks like by April, you know, in theory, when it gets a little warmer, it miraculously goes away.”

February 26: “We’re going to be pretty soon at only five people. And we could be at just one or two people over the next short period of time.”

February 27: “It’s going to disappear. One day—it’s like a miracle—it will disappear.”

February 28: Trump called Democratic criticism of his response “their new hoax.”

March 9: “So last year 37,000 Americans died from the common Flu. It averages between 27,000 and 70,000 per year. Nothing is shut down, life & the economy go on. At this moment there are 546 confirmed cases of CoronaVirus, with 22 deaths. Think about that!”

Each statement was a lie. Trump knew the virus was airborne, deadly, and spreading. He knew it wouldn’t disappear in warm weather. He knew it wasn’t “just the flu.” And he lied anyway, repeatedly assuring Americans there was no need for concern while the virus spread unchecked across the country.

The Testing Sabotage – “I Don’t Want the Numbers to Go Up”

In early March, the Grand Princess cruise ship was stranded off the California coast with COVID-19 cases on board. The CDC wanted to bring infected passengers ashore for testing and treatment. Trump’s response revealed his twisted priorities:

“I like the numbers being where they are. I don’t need to have the numbers double because of one ship that wasn’t our fault.”

Trump didn’t want to test people because testing would increase the official case count—making his response look worse. This wasn’t about protecting Americans. It was about protecting his image and his re-election prospects.

The testing sabotage continued for months. The U.S. fell drastically behind other developed nations in testing capacity. Without adequate testing, Americans couldn’t know if they were infected. Without knowing infection status, they couldn’t isolate. The virus spread invisibly through communities while Trump touted low case numbers that were artificially low due to inadequate testing.

Trump’s logic was sociopathic: if we don’t test, we won’t know how many people are infected, so the numbers will look better. The fact that this approach allowed exponential spread didn’t matter—what mattered was the optics.

Hydroxychloroquine – The Miracle Cure That Wasn’t

In mid-March, Trump became fixated on hydroxychloroquine, an anti-malarial drug with no proven efficacy against COVID-19. Despite warnings from his own FDA and public health experts, Trump promoted the drug relentlessly:

March 19: “HYDROXYCHLOROQUINE & AZITHROMYCIN, taken together, have a real chance to be one of the biggest game changers in the history of medicine.”

March 21: At a press briefing, Trump said the drug had shown “very, very encouraging early results” and told Americans: “What do you have to lose?”

What did they have to lose? Their lives.

Dr. Anthony Fauci, standing next to Trump, tried to correct the record: “The answer is no” when asked if the drug was proven effective. Trump contradicted him moments later, insisting hydroxychloroquine showed “tremendous promise.”

People died taking hydroxychloroquine based on Trump’s reckless promotion. A Phoenix man died after ingesting chloroquine phosphate, a related substance used to clean fish tanks, after Trump’s repeated endorsements. His widow told NBC News: “We saw Trump on TV — every channel — and all of his buddies and that this was safe.”

Trump didn’t care. He continued promoting the drug for weeks, despite mounting evidence it was ineffective and potentially dangerous. His personal physician prescribed it for him after he was exposed to COVID-19. He claimed to be taking it prophylactically, though this was never verified.

The hydroxychloroquine debacle revealed Trump’s approach: promote miracle cures without evidence, contradict scientific experts, and prioritize wishful thinking over medical reality. When Americans died as a result, he faced no consequences.

“Liberation” – Inciting Armed Protests Against Public Health Measures

By late April, some states had implemented stay-at-home orders and business closures to slow viral transmission. These measures were working—new case growth was flattening in areas with strong mitigation.

Trump hated this. The economic slowdown threatened his re-election. So he incited rebellion against the public health measures his own administration’s CDC had recommended.

On April 17, Trump tweeted: “LIBERATE MINNESOTA!” “LIBERATE MICHIGAN!” “LIBERATE VIRGINIA!”

The tweets were deliberate incitement. Armed protesters immediately descended on state capitols, some carrying assault rifles, demanding governors lift restrictions. In Michigan, protesters stormed the capitol building, some in tactical gear, screaming at police and lawmakers.

Trump’s “liberation” tweets gave these protests presidential blessing. He was encouraging Americans to defy public health orders during a pandemic, knowing that premature reopening would accelerate viral spread and death.

Why? Because Trump believed a strong economy was essential for his re-election. Public health didn’t matter. American lives didn’t matter. What mattered was economic growth that would benefit his campaign.

The “liberation” protests led directly to premature reopenings in some states, which led directly to summer surges in cases and deaths. Trump had weaponized anti-government sentiment and Second Amendment culture to undermine public health—and Americans died as a result.

Attacking Dr. Fauci and the Experts

Dr. Anthony Fauci, director of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, became America’s most trusted voice on COVID-19. He was honest about the virus’s danger, the need for precautions, and the timeline for vaccine development. His credibility was built on decades of public health leadership through multiple epidemics.

Trump resented him for it.

By May, Trump began attacking Fauci indirectly through surrogates and retweets. Trump retweeted a post calling for Fauci’s firing. His campaign ran social media ads featuring Fauci but selectively edited his statements to make it appear he endorsed Trump’s response (Fauci objected publicly to this misuse).

Trump’s attacks on Fauci sent a clear message: trust the president, not the experts. Believe Trump’s optimistic lies, not Fauci’s honest warnings. Choose politics over science.

This wasn’t unique to Fauci. Trump attacked CDC Director Robert Redfield when Redfield warned about a difficult fall/winter. He overruled FDA scientists on hydroxychloroquine. He pushed the CDC to soften school reopening guidelines. He pressured agencies to manipulate data for political benefit.

Trump understood that expert consensus contradicted his lies. So he attacked the experts, delegitimized their expertise, and encouraged Americans to trust him instead. It was a systematic effort to replace scientific reality with political fantasy.

The Toll Through June 2020

By the end of June 2020, more than 125,000 Americans were dead from COVID-19. The United States, with 4% of the world’s population, accounted for roughly 25% of global COVID-19 deaths—a staggering failure of leadership.

Multiple studies have attempted to quantify how many deaths were preventable with better leadership:

A Lancet Commission report later concluded that roughly 40% of U.S. COVID deaths could have been prevented with competent federal response. That’s 50,000+ preventable deaths just through June 2020.

What would competent leadership have looked like?

Honest Communication: Tell Americans the truth about the virus’s danger, even when the truth is scary. Trust citizens with accurate information.

Federal Coordination: Use federal power to coordinate PPE procurement, prevent states from competing for supplies, and ensure healthcare systems had resources.

Testing Strategy: Implement aggressive testing and contact tracing to identify and isolate cases before exponential spread.

Clear Public Health Messaging: Provide consistent guidance on masks, distancing, and precautions. Model safe behavior.

Deference to Expertise: Let public health experts lead the medical response. Take their advice seriously. Don’t contradict them for political reasons.

Economic Support: Provide sufficient economic assistance so people could afford to stay home when sick or exposed.

Trump did none of this. He lied about the danger. He sabotaged testing. He promoted quack cures. He attacked experts. He incited protests against mitigation measures. He prioritized economic and political considerations over American lives at every turn.

The result: tens of thousands of preventable deaths in just the first six months.

Why No Accountability?

Trump’s COVID-19 response was catastrophically incompetent and arguably criminally negligent. He lied to Americans about a deadly threat. He undermined public health measures. He promoted dangerous treatments. And more than 400,000 Americans died by the time he left office.

Yet Trump faced no meaningful accountability. Why?

Complexity: Pandemic response involves federal, state, and local actors. Trump could blame governors, the WHO, China, or his own agencies. Responsibility was diffused enough that no single person could be held solely accountable.

Political Polarization: Republicans defended Trump reflexively. Any criticism was dismissed as partisan attack. Trump successfully made pandemic response a tribal loyalty test.

Collective Fatigue: By late 2020, Americans were exhausted. The desire to move past COVID was so strong that retrospective accountability felt less urgent than ending the crisis.

Electoral Accountability Insufficient: Trump lost the 2020 election partly due to his pandemic response. But electoral defeat isn’t accountability—it’s just replacement. He faced no legal consequences, no financial penalties, no admission of wrongdoing.

No Criminal Liability: Incompetent pandemic response, even if it causes mass death, doesn’t constitute obvious criminal conduct. Presidents can’t be prosecuted for policy failures, no matter how deadly.

The precedent is troubling: a president can lie about a pandemic, undermine public health, and preside over hundreds of thousands of preventable deaths—and face no consequences beyond losing an election.

The Pattern Continues

Trump’s COVID-19 response followed his lifelong pattern:

  1. Face a problem requiring competence and honesty

  2. Lie to protect image and political interests

  3. Attack anyone who tells the truth

  4. Use power to undermine accountability mechanisms

  5. Avoid personal consequences while victims suffer

  6. Claim vindication despite overwhelming evidence of failure

The bone spurs fraud taught Trump he could lie about danger to avoid personal sacrifice. Atlantic City taught him he could profit while others bore losses. Trump University taught him he could defraud thousands and settle without admission. The Mueller Report taught him he could obstruct justice without prosecution.

COVID-19 taught him he could lie about a pandemic, undermine public health, preside over hundreds of thousands of deaths, and escape accountability as long as he never admitted wrongdoing and maintained political support.

Each escaped accountability was practice for the next violation. By 2020, Trump had perfected the art of escaping consequences for conduct that would destroy anyone else.
