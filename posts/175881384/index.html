The Existential Threats - Courts and China’s Counterstrike

How Legal Challenges and Rare Earth Warfare Threaten Trump’s Tariff Empire

October 11, 2025

October 11, 2025

Bottom Line Up Front: In the three months since our August 7 analysis, Trump’s tariff regime faces twin existential threats. Federal courts have systematically dismantled the legal foundation for 80% of his tariffs, with the Supreme Court set to hear arguments in early November and legal experts assessing 60-80% odds Trump loses. Simultaneously, China extended the tariff truce in August only to explode the relationship in October with weaponized rare earth controls—a sophisticated mirror response to years of U.S. semiconductor warfare. Trump retaliated by threatening an additional 100% tariff on all Chinese goods effective November 1. As three critical deadlines converge in early November, the trade war Trump built on constitutional quicksand faces simultaneous legal and diplomatic collapse.

This is Part 1 of a two-part update covering August 7 - October 11, 2025. Part 2 examines the domestic chaos: de minimis closure, new tariff announcements, the government shutdown, and the November perfect storm.

The Courts Close In: IEEPA Tariffs on Trial

August 29: Unanimous Rejection of Trump’s Legal Theory

The legal foundation of Trump’s trade war crumbled on August 29, when the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit ruled 7-4 that Trump exceeded his authority under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose most of his tariffs. The decision was devastating in its clarity.

“The core Congressional power to impose taxes such as tariffs is vested exclusively in the legislative branch by the Constitution,” the majority held. “Both the Trafficking Tariffs and the Reciprocal Tariffs are unbounded in scope, amount, and duration.” The court found that IEEPA’s grant of presidential authority to “regulate” imports “does not authorize the tariffs imposed by the Executive Orders.”

Four judges in the majority went even further, determining that IEEPA doesn’t authorize any tariffs whatsoever. “The Government’s interpretation of IEEPA would be a functionally limitless delegation of Congressional taxation authority,” they wrote.

The court stayed its ruling until October 14 to allow the government time to appeal to the Supreme Court—meaning the tariffs remain in effect for now, but hang by a thread.

What the Ruling Affects—and What It Doesn’t

Critical distinction: The court ruling challenges only tariffs imposed under IEEPA, not all of Trump’s tariffs. Understanding which tariffs are at risk is essential:

IEEPA tariffs that would be struck down:

  • The “reciprocal tariffs” announced April 2 (10% baseline plus country-specific rates ranging up to 50%)

  • The “fentanyl/trafficking tariffs” on Canada, Mexico, and China (20% on China, varying rates on Canada/Mexico)

  • The current China tariff truce rates (the 10% reciprocal tariff temporarily reduced from 125%)

Tariffs NOT affected by the ruling:

  • Section 301 tariffs on China from Trump’s first term (retained and expanded by Biden, covering approximately $370 billion in Chinese goods at 7.5-25% rates)

  • Section 232 “national security” tariffs on steel (25%), aluminum (25%), copper (50%), and automobiles (25%)

  • Other Section 301 tariffs on specific sectors like Chinese maritime/logistics

This means that even if Trump loses at the Supreme Court, substantial tariffs on China would remain in place—approximately 7.5% to 25% on many Chinese goods under Section 301, plus the Section 232 tariffs. What would disappear are the sweeping IEEPA-based tariffs covering nearly all countries and the additional “fentanyl” tariffs that brought total rates on Chinese goods to 55% or higher.

For other countries beyond China, however, the impact would be dramatic: the entire structure of “reciprocal tariffs” ranging from 10% to 50% would potentially collapse, along with framework agreements with the EU, Japan, and others that were negotiated based on these IEEPA tariffs.

September 9: Supreme Court Grants Expedited Review

On September 9, the Supreme Court granted the Trump administration’s request for expedited review, consolidating two challenges: Trump v. V.O.S. Selections and Learning Resources v. Trump. Oral arguments are scheduled for the first week of November 2025, with briefs already filed.

In its September 19 opening brief, the administration argued that IEEPA’s language—drawn directly from the Trading with the Enemy Act after a 1975 case interpreted it to authorize tariffs—gives the president broad emergency powers. “Congress thus gave itself, not federal courts, primary oversight over the President’s exercise of IEEPA powers,” Solicitor General D. John Sauer wrote.

But legal experts are skeptical. Analysis from multiple sources suggests 60-80% odds that the Supreme Court will rule against Trump’s IEEPA tariffs, potentially requiring Treasury to refund over $210 billion in collected revenue. As one observer noted, “None of Trump’s three appointed justices is distinctively ‘Trumpy,’ and they have been less predictable than Republicans had hoped.”

The stakes couldn’t be higher: over 80% of Trump’s tariffs were imposed under IEEPA. A Supreme Court defeat would force a fundamental restructuring of his entire trade policy, though Section 301 and Section 232 tariffs would remain as fallback authorities. (Part 2 explores what alternative authorities Trump could use if the Court rules against him.)

China: From August Truce to October Crisis

August 12: Ninety More Days of Fragile Peace

With just hours to spare before the August 12 deadline, Trump extended the China tariff truce for another 90 days, pushing the expiration to November 10. The agreement maintained U.S. tariffs at 30% on Chinese goods (down from the April peak of 145%) and Chinese tariffs at 10% on U.S. goods (down from 125%).

However, this represents only the IEEPA “reciprocal tariffs” that were temporarily reduced. With other tariffs still in place—including the 20% IEEPA fentanyl tariff, 7.5-25% Section 301 tariffs from Trump’s first term, and 50% Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum—the actual minimum tariff rate on many Chinese goods during the “truce” remained around 55%. Hardly a return to normal trade relations.

The extension followed late-July talks in Stockholm that ended without agreement. Both sides characterized it as providing “time for negotiations,” with analysts seeing it as laying groundwork for a potential Trump-Xi summit at the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation forum in South Korea in late October.

But the truce was always tentative. As one Chinese official noted, “Chinese society is really not afraid of the United States now, and high U.S. tariffs and other levers have lost their deterrent effect on China.”

October 9: China’s Rare Earth Weapon

On October 9, China shattered any illusion of improving relations by announcing sweeping new export controls on rare earth elements—the most aggressive use yet of its dominance in minerals critical to defense, semiconductors, and clean energy.

The new rules, effective December 1, expanded China’s restricted list to 12 of 17 rare earth elements, adding holmium, erbium, thulium, europium, and ytterbium to the seven already controlled. These are all “heavy rare earths”—the most critical for defense applications including F-35 fighter jets, Virginia-class submarines, Tomahawk missiles, radar systems, and precision-guided munitions.

But the real escalation lay in the scope and enforcement mechanism. China applied a “foreign direct product rule” (FDPR) requiring licenses for any products containing over 0.1% Chinese-origin rare earths—even if processed, manufactured, or assembled in third countries. This marks the first time China deployed the FDPR, a mechanism Washington has long used to restrict semiconductor exports to China.

The implications are severe and the design is deliberate:

Military Applications Blocked: Companies with any affiliation to foreign militaries—including U.S. defense contractors—will be largely denied export licenses starting December 1. The Ministry of Commerce made clear that “any requests to use rare earths for military purposes will be automatically rejected.”

Semiconductor Supply Chains Targeted: Applications for rare earths used in advanced semiconductors (sub-14 nanometer), memory chips, or AI with potential military applications will face case-by-case review. Given that China controls approximately 90% of global rare earth processing, this threatens the entire semiconductor manufacturing ecosystem.

Technology Transfer Restrictions: China also prohibited its citizens from supporting overseas rare earth extraction, processing, or magnet manufacturing without government approval—attempting to prevent the export of Chinese expertise to help build competing supply chains.

Third-Country Transshipment Blocked: The FDPR requirement for any products containing over 0.1% Chinese rare earths is specifically designed to prevent circumvention through third countries. A company in Vietnam, Mexico, or anywhere else that uses Chinese-origin rare earths to manufacture magnets or semiconductors would need Chinese government approval to export those products—even if the final assembly occurred outside China.

This addresses a critical enforcement challenge: China’s FDPR creates extraterritorial control over products containing Chinese rare earths regardless of where final processing occurs. However, enforcement remains difficult. China would need to track rare earth flows globally and potentially audit foreign manufacturers’ supply chains. Companies might attempt to obscure the origin of rare earths through complex supply chains, but the 0.1% threshold is deliberately low to make such evasion difficult.

Trump has separately imposed 40% “transshipment penalties” under his reciprocal tariff framework to discourage routing goods through third countries. But China’s rare earth controls operate at a different level—they’re not tariffs but export licenses that China can approve or deny at its discretion.

China’s Justification: Mirror Response to Years of US Semiconductor Warfare

China’s Ministry of Commerce framed the controls as necessary to “safeguard national security and national interests,” stating: “Rare-earth-related items have dual-use properties for both civilian and military applications. Implementing export controls on them is an international practice.”

The statement claimed “some unnamed overseas bodies and individuals had transferred rare earths elements and technologies from China abroad for military or other sensitive uses which caused significant damage to its national security” and “undermined international peace and stability, and hindered global non-proliferation efforts.”

But this language is deliberately chosen—it’s virtually identical to the justifications the United States has used for years to strangle China’s semiconductor industry.

The US Technology War That Provoked This Response

To understand China’s rare earth controls, you must understand what the United States has done to China’s technology sector since 2018—a systematic campaign to deny China access to advanced chips and the tools to make them:

October 2022 - Biden’s Initial Semiconductor Assault: The Biden administration imposed sweeping export controls restricting China’s access to:

  • Advanced AI chips (Nvidia’s A100, H100, and similar GPUs)

  • Semiconductor manufacturing equipment using extreme ultraviolet (EUV) and advanced deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography

  • Electronic design automation (EDA) software tools needed to design advanced chips

  • Technology and support services from U.S. persons working in China’s chip industry

The stated goal was explicit: “limit China’s ability to obtain semiconductor manufacturing capabilities to produce ICs for uses that are contrary to U.S. national security and foreign policy interests.” In practice, this meant choking off China’s entire path to advanced chip production.

October 2023 - Closing the Loopholes: After Chinese companies and chip designers found workarounds—Nvidia created modified chips like the A800 and H800 with reduced interconnect speeds to evade controls—the Biden administration tightened restrictions further:

  • Expanded controls based on “performance density” to catch chips that met thresholds through brute-force scaling

  • Added controls on high-bandwidth memory (HBM) essential for AI applications

  • Extended restrictions extraterritorially using foreign direct product rules—if foreign-made equipment contained even a single U.S. chip, it required U.S. export approval

  • Targeted specific Chinese companies and entire categories of chip design firms

December 2024 - The Final Biden Expansion: Just before leaving office, Biden imposed even more restrictions on semiconductor manufacturing equipment, software tools, and memory chips, while adding 140 Chinese companies to the Entity List.

Coordinated Allied Pressure: The U.S. successfully pressured allies to align their export controls:

  • Netherlands agreed to restrict ASML’s advanced lithography equipment sales to China

  • Japan restricted Tokyo Electron’s semiconductor manufacturing equipment exports

  • South Korea faced pressure to limit Samsung and SK Hynix memory chip sales

Beyond Semiconductors - The Broader Tech War:

  • Huawei: Placed on Entity List in 2019, effectively banned from accessing U.S. technology, forcing Google to remove Android from Huawei phones and crippling its 5G business

  • TikTok: Banned or forced sale demanded, treating the app as a national security threat despite its popularity with 170 million American users

  • Entity List Expansions: Hundreds of Chinese companies blacklisted from buying U.S. technology

  • Outbound Investment Restrictions: Biden’s 2023 Executive Order restricting U.S. investment in Chinese AI, quantum computing, and advanced chips

  • 5G Equipment Bans: Huawei and ZTE banned from U.S. networks and pressured allies to exclude them

The Result: The U.S. used its dominance in semiconductor design, manufacturing equipment, and software tools to wage economic warfare aimed at preventing China from advancing in AI, supercomputing, and military technologies. As one CSIS analysis noted, the controls aim to “impair Chinese capabilities in AI and supercomputing by cutting off access to high-end chips” and “prevent China from designing and making its own high-end devices by blocking access to advanced Western design tools and chipmaking equipment.”

China’s Response: “What the U.S. Has Done to China”

Against this backdrop, China’s rare earth controls become clear—they’re a proportionate, mirror response using the same justifications and similar mechanisms the United States pioneered.

The official Chinese rhetoric about “dual-use” concerns, military applications, and non-proliferation deliberately echoes U.S. language. By applying a foreign direct product rule requiring licenses for any products containing over 0.1% Chinese rare earths—even if processed in third countries—China copied the exact extraterritorial mechanism the United States uses for semiconductor equipment.

As Chinese commentator Hu Xijin wrote: “What is Trump feeling wronged about? What is he angry about? He should first understand what the U.S. has done to China!”

From Beijing’s perspective, the comparison is exact:

  • The U.S. dominates semiconductors (designing 60%+ of advanced chips, controlling critical design software and manufacturing equipment)

  • China dominates rare earths (mining 60% and processing 90% of global supply)

  • The U.S. uses semiconductor controls to restrict China’s military and AI capabilities

  • China uses rare earth controls to restrict U.S./European military and technology capabilities

  • The U.S. claims “national security” justifications

  • China claims identical “national security” justifications

The timing—three weeks before the planned Trump-Xi summit and as the November 10 tariff deadline approached—demonstrates China’s intent to establish rare earths as equivalent leverage to semiconductors. Beijing is telling Washington: “You have your chokepoints, we have ours. You want to negotiate technology access? Let’s negotiate.”

This isn’t China being unreasonable or aggressive—it’s China playing by the exact rules the United States established. For years, Washington claimed the right to weaponize its technological dominance for strategic purposes. Now China is doing the same, and suddenly America discovers that leverage can cut both ways.

The Strategic Reality: China Holds the Cards

The brutal reality is that China’s rare earth dominance gives it genuine leverage:

  • China mines at least 60% and processes about 90% of the world’s rare earths

  • The United States is almost entirely dependent on China for rare earth supply, importing 70% of its rare earth compounds and metals from China between 2020-2023

  • In 2023, the U.S. imported $22.8 million worth of rare earth products from China

  • Noveon Magnetics is currently the only manufacturer of rare earth magnets in the United States

  • Even with recent investments, the U.S. is years away from establishing independent mine-to-magnet supply chains

Washington’s defense industry already struggles to keep pace with production needs while China scales up munitions manufacturing five to six times faster than the United States. The new restrictions “will only deepen these vulnerabilities, further widening the capability gap,” according to the director of the CSIS Critical Minerals Security Program.

The Department of Defense set a goal to develop a complete mine-to-magnet rare earth supply chain by 2027, but even optimistic projections show U.S. production reaching only 1,000 tons of neodymium-boron-iron magnets by 2025—less than 1% of the 138,000 tons China produces annually.

October 10: Trump’s Explosive Response

Trump’s reaction was immediate and incendiary. On October 10, he announced an additional 100% tariff on all Chinese imports starting November 1, “over and above any Tariff that they are currently paying.” He also declared the U.S. would impose export controls on “any and all critical software” on the same date.

If implemented, this would stack atop existing tariffs—potentially creating total rates of 245%+ on some Chinese goods when combined with the 10% reciprocal tariff (or 145% if the truce expires), 20% fentanyl tariff, 7.5-25% Section 301 tariffs, and 50% Section 232 steel/aluminum tariffs. This would effectively constitute a trade embargo.

Trump also cancelled the planned meeting with Xi, writing on Truth Social: “Now there seems to be no reason to do so.”

Trump characterized China’s rare earth controls as “an extraordinarily aggressive position” and “absolutely unheard of in International Trade, and a moral disgrace.” He claimed the restrictions “affects ALL Countries, without exception, and was obviously a plan devised by them years ago.”

The irony is profound: Trump complains that China’s export controls affect all countries, yet his own “reciprocal tariffs” were explicitly designed as worldwide measures affecting nearly every trading partner.

The November Crisis: Three Deadlines Collide

The blow-up creates a dangerous convergence:

November 1: Trump’s additional 100% China tariffs supposedly take effect, along with 25% tariffs on heavy trucks delayed from October 1.

Early November: Supreme Court oral arguments on IEEPA tariff authority, with legal experts assessing 60-80% odds Trump loses.

November 10: The August tariff truce expires for the second time. Without renewal, IEEPA tariffs could revert to 145%/125% or higher, while China’s rare earth restrictions take full effect December 1.

If Trump implements the additional 100% tariff on November 1, and the truce expires November 10, total rates could reach 245%—effectively a bilateral trade embargo. But with Trump cancelling the Xi meeting and China weaponizing critical mineral supply chains, the pathway to agreement looks impossibly narrow.

Conclusion to Part 1: The Foundations Crumble

The three months since August 7 have exposed the fundamental fragility of Trump’s tariff empire. Courts have systematically rejected his constitutional theories, with the Supreme Court poised to strike down 80% of his tariffs in early November. China has escalated rather than capitulated, deploying sophisticated rare earth controls that mirror—and retaliate for—years of U.S. semiconductor warfare.

Every assumption underlying Trump’s trade war now stands discredited: that IEEPA grants unlimited tariff authority, that China would back down under pressure, that America could weaponize its technological advantages without facing symmetric retaliation. The November deadline cascade will determine whether Trump’s entire tariff structure collapses under legal and diplomatic pressure, or whether he defies the courts and triggers a constitutional crisis.

Even if Trump loses at the Supreme Court, substantial Section 301 and Section 232 tariffs on China would remain. But the sweeping worldwide “reciprocal tariffs” that formed the centerpiece of his trade policy would evaporate, along with the leverage they provided in negotiations with allies and adversaries alike.
